Abstract
This thesis investigates the interaction between concrete and freezing medium during
an accelerated freeze/thaw test. To investigate this an accelerated freeze/thaw test was
carried out with two diﬀerent concrete mixes; one with only cement as binder and one
with 50% cement and 50% ﬂy ash as binder.
Each mix were cast with ﬁve diﬀerent
amounts of air entraining agent. The specimens with most air entraining agent were both
exposed to a freezing medium with NaCl, CaCl2 and urea with a comparable freezing
point. The freezing medium was in the meantime analysed to see if any development in
the concentration and pH value could be seen.
Other than that, a thermogravimetric analysis, TGA, was carried out for paste samples
comparable to the mixes used in the accelerated freeze/thaw test. Here both samples
exposed to chlorides and samples not exposed to chlorides were analysed.
An interaction of the freezing medium and the concrete was observed both by means
of the development in the concentrations in the freezing medium, the development in pH
value for the freezing medium and by means of the results from the TGA. The interaction
was diﬀerent both for the diﬀerent freezing media, but also for the two diﬀerent mixes of
concrete. Since the interaction changes the composition of the freezing media, the two
mixes will have diﬀerent basis in an accelerated freeze/thaw test.

Chapter 1
Introduction
There are many advantages in the use of concrete e.g. the good resistance of water,
the ﬂexibility of making diﬀerent shapes, and that it is one of the cheapest building
materials available [1]. This is also some of the reasons why concrete is the most widely
used material for buildings all around the world [1]. However, the use of concrete has
some disadvantages where one of the largest is the huge environmental impact.
When producing 1 ton of cement approximately 1 ton of CO2 is released to the atmo-
sphere contributing to the global warming of the planet [1]. It is therefore of interest to
reduce the amount of cement used in concrete to reduce the CO2 emission. One way of
doing that it to replace some of the cement with supplementary cementing materials such
as ﬂy ash.
When comparing the frost resistance of concrete with high amount of ﬂy ash and
concrete with only cement as binder, the concrete with high amount of ﬂy ash shows
poorer frost resistance compared to the concrete without ﬂy ash [2]. However, this poor
frost resistant is only present when salt solution is used as freezing medium [3]. Recent
studies also indicate an interaction between the freezing medium used and the concrete
when testing the frost resistance [2]. This interaction was examined by using both NaCl
and CaCl2 as freezing medium during an accelerated freeze/thaw test and observing a
diﬀerence in the amount of scaling. This indicated an interaction between the chlorides
and the cement paste since the chloride concentration is higher in CaCl2 than in NaCl.
It is therefore of interest to examine the interaction between the freezing medium and
the concrete during an accelerated freeze/thaw test. This interaction is studied in two
diﬀerent ways:
A. The composition of the freezing medium both before the freeze/thaw test and during
the test is identiﬁed by chemical analysis. This is examined by taking samples of the
freezing medium during the accelerated freeze/thaw test and making IC and ICP
on them. This way the diﬀerent ions can be identiﬁed as well as the development of

the concentration of the diﬀerent ions. This study is done on samples with diﬀerent
freezing media such as NaCl, CaCl2 and urea. Aditionally the study is done on
diﬀerent concrete compositions, one without ﬂy ash and one with 50% ﬂy ash and
50% cement. This is done to see if the interactions between the concrete and the
freezing medium depends on the freezing medium used, the concrete composition or
both.
B. The interaction is also examined by making a thermogravimetric analysis, TGA, of
both paste samples exposed to NaCl and reference paste samples not exposed to
NaCl. This study is also done on samples both with only cement and with 50% ﬂy

can be obtained and hopefully, the change registered in study A can be explained.
A change in the chemical composition of the freezing medium can thereby maybe
explain why ﬂy ash contribute to a poorer frost resistance.

2.1
Fly ash
Fly ash is a bi product from the coal-ﬁred electrical generating systems. Fly ash is mainly
made of silica glass and the particles have a spherical shape. However, a small amount of
hollow spheres such as cenospheres and plerospheres may also occur in ﬂy ash. The size
of these particles vary a lot (from < 1 −100µm), but about 50 wt-% of the particles have
a size below 20µm [1]. Looking at Figure 2.1 the particle size of ﬂy ash is approximately
the same as for Portland cement.
Figure 2.1: Particle size distribution for ﬂy ash, Portland cement and silica fume
[1]
However, since ﬂy ash has a spherical form the packing of the particles are more
compact than for edged cement particles. The ﬂy ash particles therefore acts like balls in
a ball bearing, which contributes to a better workability of the concrete. Other than that
Gebler and Klieger [4] presented in 1983 that all concretes with ﬂy ash needs more air
entraining agent than concretes without ﬂy ash. Newer research done by K.H. Pedersen
et.al.
shows that the need for more air entraining in ﬂy ash concrete is due to the
adsorption of the air entraining agent in the residual carbon in the ﬂy ash [5].

Chloride transportation in concrete
2.3.1
Chloride diﬀusion
Diﬀusion occurs when ions in either air, a liquid or in a solid is transported from a place
with high concentration to a place with lower concentration.
If a saturated concrete
specimen (with demineralised water or tap water in the pore solution) is placed in salt
water the chloride ions in the salt water will move into the pore solution due to diﬀusion.
This will contribute to chloride ingress in the concrete. The diﬀusion of a material can
be described from the solution to Fick’s 2nd law, Equation (2.1).

2.5
Frost damage in concrete structures
One of the frost damage mechanisms in concrete structures is salt frost scaling. Salt frost
scaling is a superﬁcial damage of the concrete subjected to a saline solution (often NaCl
or CaCl2) where ﬂakes of material are scaling oﬀ[11]. Since the salt scaling is superﬁcial
it does not directly aﬀect the strength of the concrete, but since it corrupts the surface
of the concrete structure it both makes it easier for moisture to enter the structure and
==PAGE:20==
10
Chapter 2. Theory
it also makes the structure less pleasing to look at [12]. Salt scaling is one of the major
durability problems regarding concrete in cold climates. However, the cause of this scaling
is still debated [12].
Frost resistance of concrete is highly dependant of the air void structure of the concrete
structure. The air entrainment helps reduce the salt scaling in two ways; it helps reduce
bleeding because the cement particles are sticking to the air voids counteracting the
cement particles from segregating from the water [13]. Other than that, air voids help
relieve the pressure in the cement matrix because of the larger volume.
For the air entraining to have the right eﬀect, the spacing factor, which is the distance
between the air voids, must not exceed the critical spacing factor of 250 −300µm [12].
In medium and high strength concrete a 1% increase in the air content will contribute
to a 5% decrease in strength [1]. Therefore, the air voids must be as small as possible,
to contribute to smaller air content while still having a spacing factor smaller than the
critical spacing factor.

Chapter 3
Materials and methods
In order to examine the interaction between concrete and freezing medium two main
experiments were carried out. An accelerated freeze/thaw test was done with both con-
crete specimens with 100% cement and concrete specimens with 50% cement and 50% ﬂy
ash with diﬀerent amounts of air entraining agent. This was done both to examine the
diﬀerence in durability between the two mixes, but also to see if they interacted diﬀerently
with the freezing medium used.
Other than that, a thermogravimetric analysis, TGA, was executed for paste samples
comparable to the mixes used for the accelerated freeze/thaw test. As for the accelerated
freeze/thaw test a mix with 100% cement and a mix with 50% cement and 50% ﬂy ash
were examined.
Here one sample from each mix was exposed to a migration test so
chlorides were drawn into the paste sample. Thus, a TGA could be done both on samples
with and without chlorides.

3.6.3
Stopping the hydration
After the migration test the hydration of the paste samples was stopped. The procedure
was based on the step-by-step procedure described in [18].
First, the paste samples from the migration test were split in half using a hammer
and a chisel and silver nitrate (AgNO3) was sprayed on the smallest half to check if the
chlorides had penetrated more than 5mm into the sample. If this was the case a 5mm
sample was cut from each end of the other half of the sample from the migration test and
from the end of one of the end pieces. These samples were then pulverised on a metal
tray using a hammer. Afterwards the powder was put into a mortar and ground to even
smaller particle sizes. After the grounding in the mortar the powder was ﬁltered through
a sieve so all particles with size larger than 0.150mm were sorted out. This procedure
was done relatively fast to avoid carbonation as much as possible. The powder was then

the CF-test is a test carried out with deionized water where the scaling is collected at
6 ± 1, 14 ± 1, 28 ± 1, 42 ± 1 and 56 cycles, which is almost the same as the accelerated
freeze/thaw test done in this report except the cycles are 12 hours instead of 24 hours.
However, the CDF-test is a test carried out with a 3% NaCl solution, as for the test carried
out in this report. In this test the test specimen is placed with the surface downwards
and the test starts with a 7 day capillary suction followed by 28 cycles of 12 hours, where
the scaling is collected after 4 ± 1, 6 ± 1, 14 ± 1 and 28 ± 1 cycles. The capillary suction
of the freezing medium means that the interaction between the freezing medium and the
concrete is started 7 days before the freeze/thaw cycles begin. This can induce a lower
chloride content than expected in the ﬁrst 4 cycles. However, the fact that the freezing
cycles are 12 hours instead of 24 hours causes a smaller interaction between the freezing
medium and the concrete specimen. This will induce a smaller change in the freezing
point and therefore maybe also diﬀerent test results if compared to the test used in this
report where the cycles are 24 hours.

4.5
Interaction between concrete and freezing medium
From both the accelerated freeze/thaw test, the analysis of the freezing media and the
TGA, an interaction between the concrete and the freezing medium is assessed.
It was seen that the scaling of the PC50-FA50 specimens were not stagnating which
is maybe due to the slower reaction in ﬂy ash concrete. Looking at the pozzolan reaction
in Section 2.2.2 it is seen than it needs calcium hydroxide, CH, to form C-S-H gel. Since
CH is formed in the hydration of C3S, C2S, and C4AF, these reactions must take place
before the pozzolan reaction can take place. This could cause a weaker paste in the ﬂy
ash concrete. From the TGA it is seen that there still are CH present in the PC50-FA50
samples, see Figure 4.19. This supports the fact that the pozzolan reaction is slower than
the hydration of the clinker minerals in the cement paste. If the pozzolan reaction was
complete no CH would be left in the PC50-FA50 samples.
Other than that, it was expected that more ettringite would be present in the PC100-
FA0 samples due to the larger amount of CH in Equation (2.4). This was also seen in the
TGA (Figure 4.19), however, the amount of ettringite was approximately the same for the
samples exposed to chlorides as for the samples not exposed to chlorides. This indicate
that the hypothesis of formation of ettringite from the excess sulfate from Friedel’s salt
formation may be rejected.
From Section 2.4 it was stated that the amount of Friedel’s salt depends on the amount
of the clinker mineral C3A since the hydration of C3A produces monosulfate which reacts
with chlorides to form Friedel’s salt. However, when looking at Figure 4.22 the amount of
Friedel’s salt is approximately the same for the PC100-FA0-MIG-1 sample and the PC50-
FA50-MIG-1 sample even though sample PC100-FA0-MIG-1 has more C3A. This indicate
that other factors also play a role in the binding of chlorides in Friedel’s salt, and that ﬂy
ash also have a role in the binding of the chlorides. It was also seen that the amount of
chlorides in the freezing medium was decreasing more for the PC100-FA0 specimens than
for the PC50-FA50 specimens, see Figure 4.6. This could also indicate a better binding

4.6
The Farø bridges
The Farø bridges were build to connect Zealand and Falster in Denmark and they were
consisting of concrete with 11% ﬂy ash in the concrete. The bridges were build in the
period 1980-1985 and in 2007 an examination of the bridges done by COWI for The Danish
Road Directorate showed that frost scaling had occurred [21]. The bridges were therefore
deemed not frost resistant and repairs had to be done by use of cathodic protection to avoid
corrosion of the reinforcement [21]. Both air entraining agents and frost-proof aggregates
were used, so it is deemed that the concrete had an acceptable air void structure. However,
cores taken out of the bridges showed medium to low air content of most of the cores [21].
This could be a contributor to the low frost resistance, and an indicator that more air
entraining agent should have been used.
It is not known if the damages would be the same if the bridges were made of concrete
with no ﬂy ash, however, the frost scaling was deemed more severe than expected which
indicates that the ﬂy ash may have contributed to the lower frost resistance.

Chapter 5
Future work
If more work should be done in connection to this study, it would be of interest to evaluate
the binding of chlorides in both concrete with ﬂy ash and concrete without ﬂy ash. It
is noted from Figure 4.24 that the amount of chlorides bound in Friedel’s salt is just a
fraction of the chlorides bound in the paste. It could therefore be of interest to investigate
if this is only chlorides adsorbed in the C-S-H gel, or if it is bound in a third way.
If would also be of interest to make an air void analysis both on the PC100-FA0 and
the PC50-FA50 specimens since the air void structure has a lot of inﬂuence on the frost
resistance. In this study it is assumed that at similar fresh air content the specimens will
have comparable air void structure, however, it is not known if this is true since no air
void analysis was carried out.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
From the study and tests presented in this report it is concluded that an interaction hap-
pens between the freezing medium and the concrete during an accelerated freeze/thaw
test. A diﬀerence in the interaction between the freezing medium and the concrete with
only cement as binder and concrete with high amount of ﬂy ash was also observed. This
diﬀerence in interaction was observed due to both analysis of the ions present in con-
centration samples of the freezing medium taken during an accelerated freeze/thaw test,
but also by measuring the pH value of these samples. This interaction contributes to a
change in the chloride concentration of the freezing media, which leads to change in the
freezing point. This indicates that specimens of diﬀerent mixes are not equally put in an
accelerated freeze/thaw test.
Other than that, the scaling of concrete with high amount of ﬂy ash was more severe
than for concrete with only cement as binder with comparable air content even though the
concrete with ﬂy ash showed higher compressive strength. The accelerated freeze/thaw
test also showed diﬀerent results depending on the freezing medium which also indicated
an interaction between the freezing medium and the concrete.
A TGA made on both samples with only cement as binder and cement with ﬂy ash
showed approximately the same amount of Friedel’s salt when ﬂy ash was present in the
concrete, which was not expected from the theory presented, since it was expected that
the amount of Friedel’s salt depends on the amount of C3A. This indicates that ﬂy ash
contribute to binding of chlorides in concrete. This was also seen in the analysis of the
freezing medium where the freezing medium in contact with the specimens containing ﬂy
ash showed a smaller decrease in chloride concentration. This indicates a binding of the
chlorides in the concrete containing ﬂy ash.
Other than that, the TGA showed the same amount of ettringite in the samples
exposed to chlorides and the samples not exposed to chlorides. It was expected to see a
larger amount of ettringite in the samples exposed to chlorides because of the excess sulfate
from the formation of Friedel’s salt. This could therefore indicate another interaction than
==PAGE:66==

the expected.
It was also seen that the chlorides bound in the Friedel’s salt is a fraction of the total
amount of bound chlorides. This indicates that the amount of chlorides bound in the
C-S-H gel is relatively large and would be of interest to study further.
