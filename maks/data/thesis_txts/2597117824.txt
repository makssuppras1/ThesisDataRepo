Abstract 
 
The 2D airfoil CFD (computational fluid dynamics) simulations of NACA63418 in two 
wind tunnel setup cases and one no-tunnel case have been processed using DTU in-
house flow solver EllipSys 2D. The simulations are performed at Reynolds number 
equals to 3E6 and 6E6 respectively and the k-omega (𝑘- 𝜔) SST eddy viscosity model 
is utilized. For each case the airfoil or the inflow is pitched to get the attack angles 
ranged from -20 deg to 20 deg. The airfoil polar and pressure distribution results of 
simulations are analysed and compared to the experimental results obtained from the 
wind tunnel test conducted in DTU Risø campus.

Chapter 1   
Introduction 
 
Wind tunnel test is wildly used in aerodynamics study. It is based on the principle of 
the relativity of motion, the model or real object is fixed in the man-made environment, 
and the flow field is created to simulate various flow conditions in the air to obtain 
experimental data. A sketch of the usual wind tunnel can be seen in figure 1.1. 
 
 
Figure 1.1: Wind tunnel sketch 
 
The flow quality in the test section is critically important. Ideally for 2D wind tunnel 
test, the static pressure is constant in the streamwise direction of the test section. Since 
the boundary layer develops along the upper and lower walls of wind tunnel, the side 
walls are inclined (have a small diffuser angle) to compensate for the boundary layer 
build-up and eliminate the effect of static pressure gradient along streamwise direction.

The side walls are inclined 1.91 deg wider in the first tunnel setup case, which is the 
same as the side walls alignment of the real wind tunnel in the lab. Furthermore, a 
straight tunnel setup and no-tunnel case are processed to make a comparison among 
these three cases.  
The NACA63418 airfoil is a very common cambered airfoil used on wind turbines, it 
belongs to NACA five-digit airfoil classification developed by the US institution 
National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA). The first digit controls the 
camber, it indicates the designed lift coefficient multiplied by 3/20. The second digit 
indicates the position of maximum camber divided by 20. The last two digits indicate 
the maximum thickness as the percentage of chord. The profile of NACA63418 can be

Chapter 2   
Theory 
 
Computational Fluid Dynamics, referred to as CFD, belongs to a branch of fluid 
mechanics. Like many other subject, fluid mechanics was first developed by means of 
theoretical analysis and experimental research. Theoretical analysis is to use 
mathematical methods to obtain quantitative results of the problem. But generally there 
are very few problems that can be solved by this method. Computational fluid dynamics 
makes up for the shortcomings of the analysis method and it has developed rapidly in 
the past decades. On the one hand, thanks to the advancement and improvement of 
computer hardware, it has provided a solid foundation. All the simulations in this paper 
are performed on the HPC cluster in DTU Risø campus. On the other hand, because 
both the theoretical analysis method and the experimental research method have many 
limitations, such as it is hard to obtain the analytical solution for some complex 
problems, and the experimental means are very difficult and expensive to be conducted. 
But the CFD method overcomes these problems well. If the CFD analysis is carried out 
in a correct and reasonable way, it can save time, effort and money to complete the 
accurate analysis of a real problem.

fluid mechanics. In 1883, British physicist Osborne Reynolds observed the flow of fluid 
in a circular tube and found that viscous fluid has two different flow states during the 
flow process, namely the laminar flow and turbulent flow. He also pointed out that in 
addition to the flow velocity u, the flow state is also related to several physical 
quantities such as the diameter d of the tube, the density ρ and the viscosity μ of the 
fluid. The expression of this dimensionless number can be seen in equation 2.1.

3.2  Computing Forces and Moments in EllipSys 
The results of the simulation in EllipSys is write as “.force” and “.pr” files. The force 
terms are expressed along x-axis direction and y-axis direction, and the moment is 
calculated around origin in the coordinate system: 
 
𝑭𝒙= 𝒙∙𝑭𝑷+ 𝒙∙𝑭𝝂                     (3.1) 
 
𝑭𝒚= 𝒚∙𝑭𝑷+ 𝒚∙𝑭𝝂                     (3.2) 
 
where 𝑭𝑷 is the pressure force vector and 𝑭𝝂 is the viscous force vector, 𝒙 and 𝒚 
are direction vectors. Since the pressure force and viscous force are projected to the 
direction along two coordinate axis at each cell, the total force along the specified 
direction equals to the summation of the pressure and viscous force components in this 
direction. 
For the cases with tunnel walls, the direction of velocity is parallel with the x-axis, so 
the 𝑭𝒙 term and 𝑭𝒚 term in the “.force” file are drag force and lift force of the airfoil 
respectively, no matter how the airfoil is pitched. As for the last column in the “.force” 
file, it is the moment calculated around the origin (0,0), named 𝑚𝑧. To shift the moment 
to the aerodynamic center (quarter chord after the leading edge on the chord line), we 
have the following equation: 
 
𝑚𝑐= 𝑚𝑧+ 0.125 ∙𝑭𝒚                    (3.3) 
 
where the number 0.125 is the distance between the aerodynamic center and the origin 
in the coordinate system. Here we ignore the moment contributed by the 𝑭𝒙 
component because the magnitude of 𝑭𝒙 is relative small compare to the magnitude 
of 𝑭𝒚. In addition, the distance factor 0.125 is the simplified number, it should be 
==PAGE:19==

0.125 ∙cos (AOA) precisely, but attack angles in the simulations is not that big, so we 
ignore the cos (AOA) term as well.

compoents) show steady patterns, the results could be estimated as reaching 
convergence.

sub-iterations equals to 5. The values of density and inlet velocity are set to 1, the chord 
length equals to 0.9[m]. To get desired Reynolds numbers (3E6 and 6E6), we only 
change the viscosity to 1.5E-7 and 3E-7 respectively.

Conclusion 
 
The NACA63418 airfoil in inclined tunnel walls, straight tunnel walls and no-tunnel 
cases is simulated at two different Reynolds numbers (3E6, 6E6) in the DTU in-house 
CFD flow solver EllipSys2D, the attack angles ranged from -20 deg to 20 deg with 2 
deg interval. The side walls of the tunnel are inclined to make sure the static pressure 
is constant in the streamwise direction. The flow field plots and the pressure results on 
the line probe in empty tunnel cases illustrate that the inclined tunnel walls reduce the 
effect of the boundary layer build-up on the region away from the walls. 
The simulations are also performed at three grid (mesh) levels, they show that the 
simulated results are not sensitive to the mesh quality at small attack angles since these 
three grid levels are fine enough to get the good results and convergence. For high attack 
angles, the mesh should be made as fine as possible with the consideration of the 
computation effort. 
The lift coefficient plots show that the flow solver could not give the good estimations 
at high attack angles. The experimental data shows that the lift coefficient take the 
largest value around 10 deg attack angle and decrease a little bit after that angle, then it 
shows a steady pattern, there is no that kind of peak point in the simulated results curve.  
At the 18 deg attack angle, the simulated lift coefficient result is about 36 percent larger 
than that value of experimental data. 
Based on the results of this thesis, further development could be conducted. The side 
walls could be inclined wider to find out the pressure distribution in the tunnel, since the present 
setup of the inclined tunnel cannot achieve the absolute constant pressure in the streamwise 
direction. And the simulations could be performed to larger iterations to get the better 
convergence state.
