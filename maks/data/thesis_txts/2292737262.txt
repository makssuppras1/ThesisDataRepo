Abstract
Containerized shipping changed the world economy in the last century by considerably
reducing the cargo transportation costs. The consequences were that the distance be-
tween producers and customers no longer played an important role in the success of a
company, with supply chains larger than ever. Container shipping itself has increased
in magnitude in recent decades, with vessels increasing in size up to 18,000 TEU capac-
ities. As the demand for container transport increases, liner shipping companies have
to focus on their competitiveness and optimize their operations. The accommodation of
larger demands calls for the operation of larger ships, yet the ability to fulﬁl demands
depends on vessels’ stowage capabilities, and therefore these play an important role at
the strategic, tactical, and operational levels. The Cargo Mix Problem determines how
relevant operational indicators such as vessel intake or cargo revenue are inﬂuenced by
the stowage characteristics of container vessels.
The problem extends the Container
Stowage Problem to a situation where the containers that will be loaded on the ship
have to be selected from a demand forecast.
Therefore, the resulting solution must
comply with all the restrictions that make a stowage plan seaworthy, namely respect-
ing the stability and stress forces of the vessel, as well as the vessel stowage capacities.
This thesis attempts to answer the question of whether a combination of mathematical
programming and heuristic assignment of containers can produce high quality solutions
for the Cargo Mix Problem within a computational time of 10 minutes. The solution
approach proposed solves a multi-port scenario and divides the problem in a 3-phase
iterative process, composed by a MIP to solve the stability component of the problem,
a heuristic procedure to assign containers to locations in the vessel, and an iterative
improvement heuristic that attempts to ﬁnd better solutions than the previous stable
solution obtained. The approach was implemented in Java using CPLEX 12.1 libraries
to solve the stability MIP model. Experiments were conducted on a benchmark vessel
with capacity of 15,708 TEU assigned to a service visiting 14 ports between Asia and
Europe. A set of 32 realistic instances was used for the experiments. The algorithm was
robust in presenting feasible solutions to the problem in running times considerably be-
low 10 minutes, yet the quality of the solutions generated can still be improved further.
Suggestions for improvement of the solution approach are presented as a motivation to
continue research in the Cargo Mix Problem.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Comparing the world economy today to the one in the early 20th century results in
identifying a huge progress in terms of product reachability. A successful company back
then was dependent on how close it was positioned to its direct customers, given that
distance would inﬂuence consumers’ choice. A company operated in a local market and
its customers would be local customers. In 1955, when a metallic box was introduced
in the shipping industry, the whole market perspective changed. These metallic boxes
are the main reason why today we can shop on-line from Asia and pay less than we
would pay at a local shop, given the signiﬁcant decrease in transportation costs. The
world economy has changed to a reality where the distance to the customers no longer
matters, with international supply chains larger than ever (Levinson, 2006).
Container transport increased in magnitude from its early days to nowadays, becoming
the backbone of goods transportation. From the 58 container boxes transported ﬁrst in
1956 (Levinson, 2006) it rose up to the 18,000 TEU capacity container vessels existing
today (Mærsk Triple E class). To understand the importance of containerized shipping
in our lives, it is an interesting fact to point out that over 70% of the world’s goods are
transported in container vessels. Furthermore, the world total container throughput in
the year of 2013 was greater than 650 million TEUs, being almost half of it concentrated
in the 20 largest ports. Containerized trade continues to grow each year, having increased
4.6% in 2013 (UNCTAD, 2014).
The increased demand for container transport forced the liner shipping companies to
become more competitive and optimize their operations. Larger ships accommodate
more demand, but how much more demand depends on the stowage capabilities of the
ships. Therefore, analysing these capabilities is important when taking decisions at all
levels, strategic, tactical and operational. At the strategic level, the stowage capabilities
may inﬂuence for example the design or purchase of container vessels. At the tactical
level, stowage capabilities may inﬂuence which container vessels should be assigned to
which service routes. Finally, at the operational level the stowage capabilities may dic-
tate which containers should be transported in order to increase the company’s revenue
or maximize the vessel’s intake. Consequently, liner shipping companies beneﬁt if these
decisions are taken with an accurate knowledge of the vessels’ stowage capabilities.
The problem addressed in this thesis, the Cargo Mix Problem (CMP), determines how
relevant operational indicators such as vessel intake or cargo revenue are inﬂuenced by

the stowage characteristics of containers and container vessels. It attempts to maxi-
mize these indicators, given the cargo demands, while respecting the stowage rules and
generating seaworthy stowage plans. In other words, it selects which containers should
be transported on the vessel so that the relevant indicator is maximized, and where
should these containers be stored in the vessel so that container stowage objectives are
minimized (e.g., overstowage, crane makespan, or ballast water).
Stowing containers in a vessel is a problem of its own, set aside selecting which cargo
should be transported. The stowing of containers is determinant for reducing operational
costs, since its conﬁguration inﬂuences the time spent at port and bunker consumption.
Stowage plans, which describe where each container should be loaded in the ship, are a
responsibility of liner shipping companies and are usually produced manually by highly
experienced employees, the stowage coordinators. A variety of factors makes it extremely
hard to produce cost-eﬃcient stowage plans, namely the dimensions of vessels (requiring
thousands of moves at each port), the relation between the decisions made at a port
and the operations at subsequent ports, or the interaction between stacking rules and
stability and stress limits (low and high level constraints). Furthermore, stowage plans
usually have to be generated a few hours before the vessel arrives at the port and
information on container load lists at subsequent ports is non-existent or scarce, having
the stowage coordinators to rely on demand forecasts.
Academically, cargo mix optimization is found mainly in revenue management literature,
but often disregards vessel stowage capabilities. On the other hand, container stowage
has been a research topic since the 1970s and the problem has been proved to be NP-
Complete. The most successful approaches decompose the problem in two phases, a
ﬁrst phase where containers are assigned to locations respecting stability constraints,
and a second phase where containers are distributed among the available slots inside
the location they were previously assigned to, respecting stacking rules. In-depth vessel
characteristics, an essential component for an accurate research, are often conﬁdential
and hard to obtain without the collaboration of an industry partner. The same applies
to accurate data on demands and stowage planning knowledge. These have been the
main barriers to research, resulting in interesting conceptual approaches that are often
tested using ﬁctional data or small scale real life problems. However, some authors have
recently published academic work where close collaboration with the industry resulted
in real life testing of the developed models (e.g., Pacino (2012) and Delgado (2013)).
1.1
Thesis question and motivation
The model proposed in Delgado (2013) solves the CMP for real instances, but due
to the amount of constraints and scale of the problem it uses an excessive amount of
computational time. This is an impediment if it is to be used in the daily operations of
a shipping company. Therefore, the use of heuristics to obtain solutions for the CMP
is justiﬁed, if these solutions have high quality and are obtained in low computational
times. Two distinct sections can be identiﬁed in the CMP model developed by Delgado
(2013): the group of constraints that handle ship stability and the group of constraints
that address the container assignment. The ﬁrst group are high-level constraints that
can easily be handled by a mathematical program. The second group of constraints are
low-level constraints that are hard to handle in a mathematical model for large scale
problems, but are easily handled heuristically. This thesis is focused on the Master Bay
2
==PAGE:15==

Planning phase of the CMP, where containers are assigned to locations in the ship. This
leads to the question this thesis attempts to answer:
”Can a combination of mathematical programming with heuristic assignment
of containers produce high quality solutions for the Cargo Mix Problem in
a representative industrial setting, within a computational time of 10 min-
utes?”
Obtaining solutions with heuristics instead of using exact methods has the underlying
idea that a trade-oﬀbetween solution quality and time spent solving the problem exists.
Using heuristics implies potentially losing some quality in order to have a solution faster.
In order to be interesting from an industrial perspective, an algorithm is imposed to
spend less than 10 minutes generating a solution. Furthermore, the solution produced
has to be good enough to justify the utilization of the algorithm, even though it will
most likely suﬀer further changes.
To the author’s knowledge, the most successful CMP research work can be found in Del-
gado (2013), where an extended master planning model with the capability of handling
variable displacement that increases scalability is presented. The approach proposed in
the here presented thesis solves a multi-port scenario and divides the problem in a 3-
phase iterative process, where information is passed between the phases. The ﬁrst phase
is a MIP that solves the stability part of the problem, generating feasible weights for
the locations. The model considers stress forces, bending moments, vessel displacement,
ballast water, and draft constraints. The second phase is a placement heuristic that at-
tempts to assign container types to locations such that the ﬁnal weights at the locations
are close to the ones calculated by the MIP. The third phase is an improvement heuristic
that suggests new weights to the MIP, based on the container type distribution found
in the second phase. The process is repeated iteratively until a seaworthy solution that
maximizes the objective is found.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This chapter presents a review on previous related work in the topic of this thesis. For
lack of abundant research in the Cargo Mix Problem (CMP), this chapter also presents
a literature review on the Container Stowage Problem (CSP), a problem that is closely
related with the CMP and that has been widely studied since the 1970’s.
2.1
Cargo Mix Problem
Literature that considers the optimization of container cargo mix is found mainly in
the area of revenue management, but often disregards the vessels’ stowage capabilities.
Tzeng and Ting (2004) present a revenue management model that optimizes the total
freight contribution (freight revenue - variable cost). The model includes the reposition-
ing of empty containers and considers four types of containers, namely 20’, 40’, 20’ reefer
and 40’ reefer. In terms of constraints, weight capacity is considered, but the vessel lay-
out is not taken into account. Vessel stability and stress forces are also disregarded,
limiting the applicability of solutions obtained. A case study with 3,350 TEU vessels is
presented, but the results in terms of runtime behaviour are omitted. Ang et al. (2007)
described the characteristics of the cargo mix problem for the carrier in a multi-period
planning horizon and formulated it as a multi-dimensional multiple knapsack problem
that maximizes the total proﬁt generated by all transported cargo in a determined time
period. Shipping capacities are only considered in terms of weight and volume. Two
heuristic algorithms are devised and tested on a variety of instances of the problem, but
there is never a guarantee that the obtained solutions are seaworthy. Later, the same
authors present in Ang et al. (2009) a two stage stochastic integer programming model
and propose a heuristic algorithm for solving it for a multi-period sea cargo mix problem
under uncertainty. Again, the objective is to maximize the expected revenue from the
cargo transported and shipping capability is modelled in the same way, so the same
conclusion follows. A revenue model for short-sea shipping was introduced by Chang
and Feng (2008), also not considering any stability constraints. In this model, only total
volume and weight capacity are considered for stowing containers in the vessel. A case
study of a Taiwan liner shipping company is presented, where the experimental results
show that the proposed model performs better than conventional slot allocation models.
Still, its applicability is limited since the solutions obtained may not be seaworthy.

The most recent and successful approach to the CMP can be found in Delgado (2013),
where the ﬁrst model with variable displacement that assigns containers to vessel slots
and that can solve real instances is presented. Besides modelling the CMP (named as
Cargo Composition Problem, CCP) as a boolean IP model, the author also presents an
extended master planning model with the capability of handling variable displacement
that increases scalability, which is the one adapted for this project and presented in
Chapter 4.
The container types considered are 20’, 40’, reefer containers and high-
cubes, and the models also consider ballast tanks, crane makespan estimations, and
stack and hatch overstowages in the objective.
All main stability and stress forces
constraints are taken into account, along with stacking constraints and rules of thumb
for handling lashing. Both models are tested on ten diﬀerent vessels ranging from 3,000
to 15,000 TEUs and in a single port and multi port scenario, with a time limit of 3,600
seconds. Single port experiments were solved for two diﬀerent objectives, namely intake
optimization and revenue maximization. The multi port approach was used to test the
impact of the number of ports in the service and number of diﬀerent container types.
Results showed that in the multi port scenario the decomposed model performs better,
solving 91.7% of all tested instances with up to 10 ports.
2.2
Container Stowage Planning
Since the CSP is a NP-complete problem strongly dependent on ship capacity (Avriel
et al., 2000), besides approaches aiming at optimality also a variety of methods using
heuristic approaches can be found in the literature, which are the ones that are usually
more relevant for the industry due to the time dependency of the problem. Two distinc-
tive approaches to the problem were identiﬁed, the ones that consider the problem as a
whole and solve it with a single model, and the ones that decompose the problem into
diﬀerent optimization models.
2.2.1
Single Models
2.2.1.1
Mathematical Programming
Mathematical programming was ﬁrst introduced to solve the CSP by Avriel and Penn
(1993), where a 0-1 binary linear programming model is presented. The model solves
a simpliﬁed problem considering a ship with a single rectangular bay, where stability
constraints are not taken into account and the objetive is to minimize overstowage. Also,
only one size of containers is considered. The author claims a successful implementation
of the model in GAMS, but no computational results are presented. However, the high
number of variables in the model is reported as a limitation for ﬁnding the optimal
solution. Two extensions of the model, to include other types of containers and consider
hatch covers, are proposed but not implemented.
Ambrosino et al. (2004) presented a basic 0-1 Linear Programming model that considers
both 20’ and 40’ ISO containers, divided in three diﬀerent weight classes. The model does
not consider special type containers, not even reefers. A peculiar fact is that overstowage
is not model as an objective but as a constraint, while the objective is to minimize time
at port. Lashing and GM constraints are dealt by forcing a weight ordering of containers
in stacks, the heavier in the bottom. Stability issues are handled in a simpliﬁed way by
6
==PAGE:19==
Chapter 2. Literature Review
forcing the weight diﬀerences front-rear a left-right to be within speciﬁed limits. Another
limitation of the model is that it only considers the current port. A case study with a 198
TEU container ship is presented, where using a preprocessing procedure the proposed
model solves 13 instances of the problem in at most 34 minutes, using a PC Pentium II.
Another mathematical programming formulation of the problem is proposed by Li et al.
(2008). The 0-1 IP model presented only takes into account 20’ and 40’ standard con-
tainers and models a multi-port scenario. Overstowage is represented as an objective
and metacentric height, heel, and trim are introduced to describe the vertical, transverse
and longitudinal distribution of the ship’s weight. However, these are calculated in a
similar fashion to the balancing constraints in Ambrosino et al. (2004). Also, the model
does not consider weight limitations. The authors mention that the model was tested in
about 1,000 randomly generated instances for a ﬁctional 800 TEU container ship, but
the computational results are not presented in the article.
2.2.1.2
Placement Heuristics
Placement heuristics have been explored to solve the CSP by a variety of authors. Sim-
ple placement heuristics were introduced by Avriel and Penn (1993) in the same work
where they developed their mathematical programming model referred in the previous
section. They present the ”Whole Column Heuristic Procedure” where containers are
assigned to stacks according to the same origin and destination.
In this way, over-
stowage is non existent since all containers of a stack are unloaded at the same port.
The original transportation matrix with the demands for O-D pairs is decomposed in
two matrices, one containing the ”whole columns” (multiples of the number of rows) and
one containing the remainder containers. An example is given for a single bay ship and
a solution with no overstowage is obtained, but in larger problems the remainder con-
tainers might need long computational times if solved with the proposed mathematical
program. Later, the same authors presented the ”Suspensory Heuristic Procedure” in
Avriel et al. (1998). Again, a single bay vessel is considered with only one type of con-
tainers and no stability constraints are taken into account. The objective is to minimize
overstowage in a multi-port problem where only loading of containers is allowed. The
procedure showed considerable improvements compared to the ”whole column” proce-
dure and solved instances with vessels of up to 1,700 TEUs, visiting up to 30 ports in
an average computation time of 30 seconds.
Another placement heuristic is proposed by Yoke et al. (2009), where the objective is to
minimize the number of re-handles (overstowage) and maximize crane intensity over a
set of ports. Stability of the vessel is not taken into account, but directions to further
work are provided to incorporate this feature. The procedure consists ﬁrstly in dividing
the vessel in blocks, each block containing all the locations above and below the hatch.
Secondly, containers are grouped by size, type and port of discharge, and are loaded
group by group into the blocks, instead of loaded one by one. Most stacking constraints
are handled at this stage, except for stability, hazardous segregation and stack weight
limits, being the last two handled in two subsequent stages of the heuristic. Experiments
are shown for a sequence of 8 ports with load and discharge operations visited by a 5,000
TEUs vessel, which was solved eﬃciently within 1 minute of computation time. However,
the feasibility of the solutions obtained is not guaranteed due to the omission of stability
constraints. Fan et al. (2010) present an extension of this heuristic approach to include
7
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 2. Literature Review
stability constraints and conclude with further directions to improve the procedure with
a swapping algorithm.
Some authors combined the use of placement heuristics with local search procedures.
Scott and Chen (1978) presented a methodology that grouped containers in special
requirement classes, using the last class for standard containers classiﬁed according to
their weight. Classes with special requirements are handled ﬁrst and assigned to slots
following a heuristic approach. Maximization of ship intake through assigning standard
containers to bays and optimization of ship stability are addressed next, solving two IP
models. A local search is then used to perform container swaps in order to solve the trim
and metacentric height. Finding a solution may not be possible, so when it happens
one container is removed from the loadlist and the process is repeated. Overstowage,
bending moment and lashing strength are not handled by the model, and due to the use of
dynamic and integer programming the computation time is expected to be considerable
(although it is not discussed in the report). A few years later, Aslidis (1984) took a
simpliﬁed version of the problem (considering only one type of containers and no special
requirements) and solved it with the objective of minimizing overstowage. A placement
heuristic is proposed for the containers, while trim is solved using ballast water and the
GM is solved with a container swaps’ local search. When a feasible solution is reached,
costless swaps for the objective value are performed to improve the GM. However, weight
capacities are not taken into account. Two instances with a 1,540 TEUs vessel in a
multi-port scenario were solved with a computational time under 30 seconds.
More recently, similar ideas were presented in Liu et al. (2011) where the Block Stowage
Heuristic from Yoke et al. (2009) is modiﬁed to obtain non-deterministic solutions. In a
ﬁrst stage, the modiﬁed procedure is used to obtain several initial solutions from which
the best in terms of overstowage and crane intensity is chosen to go through the second
stage. The second stage consists of a Tabu Search using a same destination container
swap neighbourhood to solve the stability of the vessel as a multi-objective problem.
A case study with a 5,436 TEUs container ship, 4 to 6 multi port scenario, and real
container data provided by a shipping company is presented, where six test cases are
solved.
Two of these six test cases are presented in the article in detail, reporting
computational times for both obtaining the initial solutions and the post tabu search
solutions.
The work of Yoke et al. (2009) was also improved by Low et al. (2011),
where a local search with swap neighbourhood is used to solve weight limits, trim,
and heel requirements. Pacino and Jensen (2009) presented a method to solve the slot
assignment for under deck locations of container vessels using a placement heuristic
with a 3-phase local search. The ﬁrst phase (Heuristic Placement) generates a good
initial conﬁguration where a order is enforced on the containers to load, with priority
given to reefers. Containers are stacked bottom up, prioritizing stack with the same
or greater discharge port ﬁrst, and empty stacks second. The second (Feasibility) and
third (Optimality) phases perform local searches restricting the swap neighbourhood
to only feasible conﬁgurations.
Placement limitation constraints, weight and height
constraints are taken into account by the model, while minimizing overstowage, the
number of diﬀerent destinations in a stack, the number of non-reefers in reefer slots, and
the number of stacks used. 19 industrial instances with under deck storage areas of 16
to 176 TEUs were solved to optimality taking up to 13 seconds.

2.2.1.3
Bin Packing Heuristics
The bin packing problem, where a maximum number of diﬀerent objects has to be
packed in a bin, has been related to the CSP by some authors. Sciomachen and Tanfani
(2003) adapted a three-dimensional bin packing heuristic to a container stowage problem,
where items are containers and bins are diﬀerent portions of the ship. The container
types considered are 10’, 20’, 40’, and high-cubes. The diﬀerent sections of the vessel are
ﬁlled with containers with the same destination, also taking into account their lengths.
The vessel layout is taken into account and sections with a regular form are stowed ﬁrst,
leaving oddly shaped sections for later. Stability and stacking constraints are addressed
in the loading order of containers and sections, and the algorithm tries to minimize the
total loading time, considering only one port. Computational experiments are presented
both for a 198 TEUs and a 1,800 TEUs vessel. For the ﬁrst, 18 instances of diﬀerent
size and characteristics were solved in less than 1 second. For the second, two industrial
cases were solved within 33 minutes. A few years later, Zhang et al. (2005) presented a
bin packing model for a stowage problem with a multi-port scenario, without stability
constraints. Minimization of overstowage and the number of used bays are obtained by
modelling the packing decisions as a binary decision tree traversed using bin packing
heuristics. A case study with a container ship with 895 TEUs is experimented, but no
results in terms of computational times are shown.
2.2.1.4
Genetic Algorithms
The use of genetic algorithms to solve the CSP was introduced by Todd and Sen (1997),
where a multi-criteria genetic algorithm for solving the stowage problem is presented.
The approach proposed a complete encoding of the solutions, with the solution represen-
tation vector having diﬀerent sections corresponding to each port. Each of these sections
contains integer vectors with the size of the total number of container slots in the vessel.
A problem with this approach is the generation of long vectors that cause the search
space to be very large, thus slowing the convergence of the algorithm. Additionally, the
complete encoding does not ensure the feasibility of the solutions generated with the
crossover operator. Feasibility should be ensured with a repair procedure afterwards,
but this would destroy the information inheritance mechanism that genetic algorithms
should have. A genetic algorithm with compact encoding that was able to signiﬁcantly
reduce the search space was proposed by Dubrovsky et al. (2002). Rather than consid-
ering the complete layout of the ship, the compact encoding considers only the changes
in the container layout that occur at each port, which is signiﬁcantly smaller than the
whole layout. Its parallel and non-linear search allows the algorithm to perform well
in the container stowage problem. With the objective of minimizing overstowage and
without considering stability and weight constraints, the algorithm can stow a single bay
vessel with one size containers. Trim requirements are handled heuristically. Randomly
generated instances of the problem were solved for vessels with 500 and 1,000 TEUs
visiting 5 to 14 ports. The runtime reported is of 30 minutes for an instance with 11
ports and the 1,000 TEUs vessel, using a PC Pentium II.
Genetic algorithms were further explored for the CSP by Imai et al. (2006), where a
combination between container stowage and terminal sequencing is presented. Contain-
ers are assumed to be stowed in the ship holds only and not on the upper deck, although
the authors claim that the model is easily adapted to upper deck stowage if all containers

placed on top of each hatch cover have the same destination. In order to take sequencing
into account, each container’s transportation time between yard and vessel is modelled.
Stability, trim, GM and list are taken into account, but overstowage is modelled as a
constraint. Numerical experiments are conducted in 22 randomly generated cases of
the problem with diﬀerent characteristics in terms of ship size (504 and 2,016 TEUs),
container volume to load (336 and 504 TEUs), stack arrangement (random, grouped
by weight or grouped by destination), initial ship conditions in terms of list and trim,
and ship hold arrangement (random or arranged by destination). Large computational
times are reported, taking over one hour to solve the presented instances. However, the
authors mention that removing the tournament from the genetic algorithm can reduce
running times by 7 times, but the quality of the obtained solution might not be the
same.
2.2.1.5
Constraint Programming
A constraint satisfaction approach was introduced by Ambrosino and Sciomachen (1998)
for solving the master bay planning problem. 20’ and 40’ containers are modelled, com-
prising three types (standard, carreageable and hazardous) and diﬀerent weights. A
heuristic approach is used to handle stability constraints. Branch-and-Bound is com-
bined with the backtrack algorithm for optimizing the solutions obtained. Good feasible
solutions are reported to be found within 10 minutes of computation time for a 350
TEUs vessel with containers of 2 types and 3 destinations, but no evidence of the results
is shown.
A diﬀerent constraint programming (CP) approach was suggested by Delgado et al.
(2009).
The model has the ﬁrst objective of minimizing overstowage and secondary
objectives of minimizing the number of used stacks and the number of non-reefer con-
tainers placed in reefer slots.
Most stacking constraints are taken into account, but
IMO restrictions and vessel stability are neglected. A careful description of the model is
provided, along with improvements that include extra constraints, symmetry breaking
constraints (for containers and slots), channelling constraints and estimators. Experi-
ments were conducted in a representative set of instances from diﬀerent real-life vessels
with diﬀerent sizes, conﬁgurations of containers, and discharge ports. Without estima-
tion algorithms, all instances but one were solved using at most 367 seconds (a time
limit of 30 minutes was imposed). Using the estimation algorithms, it took at most 22
seconds of computational time to solve all 17 instances. This work was further developed
in Delgado et al. (2012b), where it is extensively compared to an IP model. The per-
formance of the IP model and CP model are compared using 1 and 10 seconds runtime
limits. The CP model outperforms the IP model, solving almost all (90%) considered
instances to optimality for the 1 second runtime limit, while the IP model averages 59%
of instances solved. For the runtime limit of 10 seconds, 94% and 76% of the instances
were solved to optimality for the CP and IP models, respectively. It should be noted
that the model solves the slot planning phase of the CSP, thus stability is not considered
since it is assumed to be handled in the master planning phase. The same model is used
in Pacino (2012) in the Slot Planning Chapter.

2.2.1.6
Simulation Approaches
The use of simulation in container stowage dates back to Shields (1984), where a Monte
Carlo model is presented not only to provide decision support but also to handle the
uncertainty of cargo that compose loadlists. For each considered port, the algorithm
attempts the stowage of container groups in bays, pruning slots where these containers
could not be stowed. The three best solutions found in each port are selected according
to a ranking obtained from penalty functions. The overall best solution that includes
all ports is later determined based on the solutions for each port. The article presents
some examples of output generated by the system, but no performance evaluation is pre-
sented. A more recent approach to the CSP using simulation can be found in Azevedo
et al. (2012), in a model similar to the one by Shields (1984) but without stochasticity
considerations. The problem is represented by loading and unloading rules and is ex-
plained with the container ship represented by a graph. The rules are translated into
a computational program that simulates the required behaviour. Five diﬀerent loading
rules are explained, while only two unloading rules are represented. The approach is
to search for solutions by deciding which rules to take into account, simulating them
to generate a plan. A beam search method is then used to reduce the search space.
Experiments on 15 randomly generated instances with standard one size containers and
with 10 to 30 ports, showed that for a 1,500 TEUs vessel the computational times were
between 400 and 18,300 seconds. Another approach to solve the same representation
by rules using genetic algorithms was presented by the same authors in Azevedo et al.
(2013).
2.2.1.7
Other Approaches
Diﬀerent approaches that lie outside of the deﬁned categories in this section were also
found. Probably the most diﬀerent one is presented by Nugroho (2004), where a case-
based reasoning methodology is suggested. This method generates stowage plans based
on similar plans generated in the past. Since the method has a learning ability, the
more plans it solves the larger the plan database will become and theoretically better
solutions it will provide. The method has no randomness, since for the same input the
generated output will be the same. The section in the article devoted to experiments is
short and the results presented are inconclusive. Giemsch and Jellinghaus (2004) suggest
an approach to solve a single bay problem using a triple-step heuristic. The ﬁrst step
assigns whole columns, inspired by Avriel and Penn (1993), i.e. stowing containers into
stacks of single discharge port. A second step tries to assign the remaining containers
to stacks without causing overstowage. For the containers where this was not possible,
the third step of the heuristic calculates the best assignment for each container with a
branch-and-bound algorithm that minimizes the shifts necessary to store the container.
The authors claim to improve the results in 1038 out of the 1050 problem instances
presented in Avriel et al. (1998), but no results are presented in the report. A multi-
agent system is proposed by Yanbin et al. (2008) to solve a multi-port container stowage
problem.
The searching direction and strength of the agents are determined by the
learning machine using belief parameters. Experiments are conducted on a 900 TEUs
vessel visiting 10 ports and a running time of 16 hours is reported.

2.2.2
Multi-Level Models
A variety of authors have approached the problem by decomposing it in diﬀerent op-
timization problems. The ﬁrst decomposition of the CSP can be found in Botter and
Brinati (1992), where a mathematical model for a stowage problem with 20’ and 40’ con-
tainers in a multi port scenario is presented. The loading and unloading of containers
at each port is regarded as a succession of individual steps, being loading only started
when unloading is ﬁnished. The objective function considers both overstowage and crane
movement minimizations, since the model also considers the sequence of loading and un-
loading. Besides considering stacking constraints, the model also takes into account GM,
trim, heel angle, cutting forces and bending moments. Justiﬁed by the combinatorial
complexity of the problem, the authors propose a decomposition in two sub-problems:
an assignment problem and a sequencing problem. The ﬁrst phase presents the layout of
container cell occupation at the end of the loading and unloading phases at each port of
the route, obtained by minimizing the number of restows. The second sub-problem de-
termines the optimal sequencing of loading and unloading operations, minimizing crane
movement. Since the solution to the assignment problem is already known, the number
of variables in the sequencing phase is considerably reduced. In another eﬀort to reduce
the problem size, the authors proposed a grouping of containers into classes, according
to the same type, origin, destination and weight class. An implicit enumeration algo-
rithm is also proposed as an alternative to the mathematical model. The decomposition
is reported to solve small instances of the problem, but no evidence is presented.
Another decomposition approach was later proposed by Wilson and Roach (1999), which
divided the problem in strategic and tactical planning processes. This was the ﬁrst time
that block decomposition was introduced for the CSP, where in a ﬁrst phase generalized
containers (containers are grouped in classes) are assigned to blocks of slots instead of
speciﬁc slots, with the objective of minimizing hatch overstowage, crane utilization, and
optimizing discharge port clustering within the blocks. Blocks above and below deck are
considered, as well as hatch lids. The second phase assigns containers to speciﬁc slots
in the block they were already assigned to, minimizing overstowage, ordering container
weights and addressing pureness of stacks (attempt to compose stacks with containers
destined to the same port). In this way, the neighbourhood of the tabu search algorithm
used in the methodology is reduced to the moves in the same block. Computational ex-
periments were run in a 166 MHz Pentium with 40 MB of memory for a 688 TEUs vessel
visiting 4 ports, taking into account the physical layout of the vessel. It was reported
that the generalized solution (ﬁrst phase) was obtained in approximately 90 minutes,
while the specialized solutions (second phase) were produced in under 60 minutes. How-
ever, the results are only mentioned and no evidence is shown. The same decomposition
approach is also presented in Wilson and Roach (2000) and Wilson et al. (2001).
A solution approach to the CSP where the problem is partitioned into two sub-problems
was also presented by Kang and Kim (2002). The ﬁrst problem assigns container groups
to holds and the second determines the speciﬁc slots for the containers in each hold,
similarly to what happened in Wilson and Roach (1999). The two sub-problems are
solved iteratively using the information obtained in each other’s solutions. The pro-
cedure only takes 40’ standard containers into account. The ﬁrst sub-problem has the
objective of minimizing the number of shifting operations and the time required for crane
movements, being represented with a mathematical formulation that solves one port at
a time.
The second sub-problem is solved using a tree search enumeration for each
hold. Computational experiments were conducted on 180 randomly generated instances

that combine diﬀerent numbers of ports to visit (4, 6, and 8), diﬀerent vessel capacities
(2,500, 3,000, and 4,000 TEUs), and diﬀerent numbers of container weight groups (2
and 3). For the more complex combination, instances were solved in 640 seconds.
In the same paper where they present the 0-1 Linear Programming model, Ambrosino
et al. (2004) present a decomposed solution approach that solves the MBPP in three
phases. The ﬁrst phase is composed of a preprocessing with the purpose of tightening
the constraints of the model and reduce the feasible region for each container. In the
second phase, a prestowage procedure considers the set of available locations split into
diﬀerent partitions with respect to their bay address, which allows an easier destination
based stowage.
Finally, in the third phase the model is solved taking into account
the preprocessing and prestowage procedures. As mentioned before, instances of the
problem for a 188 TEUs container vessel could be solved within 34 minutes. A diﬀerent
decomposition approach was suggested by the same authors in Ambrosino et al. (2006),
still considering overstowage a hard constraint and minimizing the total loading time of
containers. Again, a 3-phase decompositions is suggested. In the ﬁrst phase containers
with the same destination port are assigned to bays, creating a distribution where each
bay only has containers to one destination port. A second phase assigns containers to
slots inside the previously allocated bay, by solving an IP model of considerably less
size. When the second phase is completed for all locations, a third phase is used to
improve the vessel’s stability using a swap neighbourhood. Computational experiments
were conducted on the same instances and ship as in Ambrosino et al. (2004), showing
that in 3.5 minutes this approach was able to ﬁnd solutions that lie within 1% from the
ones obtained in the previous work. Further modiﬁcations on the decomposition were
presented in Ambrosino et al. (2009). The ﬁrst and second phases remain unchanged, but
the third phase is substituted by a tabu search metaheuristic procedure that determines
a globally feasible solution in terms of stability and tries to improve it.
The use of
tabu search allowed the decomposition to solve instances of the problem for a 2,124
TEUs vessel within 74.4 seconds. A diﬀerent modiﬁcation to the decomposition was
presented in Ambrosino et al. (2010) where two new solution approaches are suggested,
a fast simple constructive loading heuristic and an ant colony optimization algorithm.
After the bay assignment is completed, the algorithm ﬁrst assigns containers to stacks
and after assigns containers to slots. Local search is performed to improve the solution.
Until convergence takes place, the ant system suﬀers a pheromone update and the whole
process is iterated. It should be noted that multi-port solutions and overstowage are
not taken into account. Computational experiments were conducted for two vessels, one
with 2,124 TEUs and other with 5,632 TEUs. Instances with 2 to 5 destination ports
were considered. For the larger vessel, the 11 considered instances were solved with an
average computing time of 139.4 seconds.
G¨um¨u¸s et al. (2008) presented a 4-stage decomposition where containers are aggregated
according to their O-D pairs. The ﬁrst stage assigns partitions of bays to each O-D pair
with the purpose of minimizing a worst case bound on the number of overstows. Crane
utilization is also considered in this stage, at an aggregate level.
The second stage
comprises an allocation decision for tiers, considering the solution from the previous
stage and hatch lids.
This step explicitly accounts for the inﬂuence of hatch covers
on the number of restows. Stage 3 is the one responsible for the assignment of slots
to containers, based on the output from the previous stage. Finally, in stage 4 each
container is assigned to a slot in a way that the hydrostatic constraints are satisﬁed.
The paper lacks of a computational experiment section, although the authors claim that
the approach has a good performance on real-life problems.

Pacino et al. (2011) presented a 2-phase approach that generates near-optimal stowage
plans. The algorithm was constructed under the premise that the maximum allowed
time for a solution to be found is 10 minutes, based on industrial requirements. The
ﬁrst phase solves the multi-port master planning problem with an IP formulation, taking
into account 20’, 40’ and reefer containers, GM stability, trim and draft limits, weight
distribution and shear forces. The objective is to minimize hatch overstowage and crane
makespan. IMO, pallet-wide and high-cube containers were left out of the model, the
same happening to bending moments and ballast tanks. The second phase (slot plan-
ning) is solved using a combination of constraint programming and local search, using
the information obtained in the ﬁrst phase. This second phase incorporates the major
stacking constraints, including stack maximum height and weight. The objective is to
minimize overstowage, cluster containers with the same destination port, minimize the
number of used stacks and attempt to leave reefer slots without containers. Computa-
tional experiments on 20 instances of the problem with vessels with capacities ranging
from 2,584 to 9,984 TEUs showed that only 4 instances could not be solved within the
speciﬁed time frame. Moreover, for the 16 instances solved within 10 minutes the pro-
cedure was able to reach results within a 2% optimality gap in less than 322 seconds. In
Pacino (2013) a diﬀerent methodology is proposed for solving the multi-port MBPP (ﬁrst
phase), using a large neighbourhood search (LNS) algorithm. This algorithm combines
the strength of mathematical modelling with the ﬂexibility of local search and consists
on iteratively destroying and constructing parts of the solution. In order to compare
the LNS algorithm with the previous IP formulation, computational experiments were
conducted in 80 industrial instances based on real stowage problems, for vessels rang-
ing from 2,000 to 10,000 TEUs capacity. The IP model was only able to ﬁnd feasible
solutions for 50 out of the 80 instances within the speciﬁed time limit (3,600 seconds),
from which only 6 were optimal, while the LNS algorithm found feasible solutions for all
instances in less than 600 seconds. Regarding the second phase of the decomposition,
a wider analysis is presented in Pacino and Jensen (2013) where it is reported that the
constraint-based local search algorithm can solve the problem in an average of 0.18 sec-
onds per bay, summing up to a total of 20 seconds for the entire ship. Furthermore, for
a set of 133 industrial instances the algorithm solved 86% of them to optimality.
Another author that solved a decomposition of the problem was Li (2012). The model
is ﬁrst presented as an IP model where the objective function is multi-objective and
considers the total loading time, the number of shifts, and the weight diﬀerence between
the more and less loaded bays. A decomposition is proposed for solving the problem, with
two diﬀerent sub-problems that are solved sequentially. The ﬁrst sub-problem assigns
groups of containers to bays based on their destination, while the second sub-problem
deﬁnes a loading pattern for containers placed in locations, according to heuristic rules.
After both sub-problems are solved and an initial solution is obtained, a tabu search
algorithm with diverse neighbourhood sizes and a diversiﬁcation strategy is used to
escape local minima. Experiments were conducted on 20 randomly generated instances
for a 600 TEUs vessel.
For the 20 instances, the tabu search algorithm averaged a
solution quality with an optimality gap of 1.95% in 36.3 seconds, while the IP model
solved in CPLEX averaged an optimality gap of 2.59% in 3187 seconds.
An approach to solve the multi-port MBPP part of the CSP using two diﬀerent MIP
models was suggested by Ambrosino et al. (2013). The ﬁrst model has the objective of
minimizing unsatisﬁed transportation demand, number of re-handles, and crane imbal-
ance. Hold and deck stacks of the same hatch are assigned containers with the same
destination and stability is considered only by means of horizontal and cross equilibrium.

The second model has the objective of minimizing the number of not served TEUs and
the number of re-handles. The diﬀerence between the models is that the second consid-
ers a TEU relaxation of the ﬁrst, where transportation demands, assignments, loading
operations and re-handles are expressed in terms of TEUs. Both models consider 20’ and
40’ containers only, with no special properties. Solutions are obtained for each model
according to simple heuristic rules that relax some of the variables. Experiments were
conducted both on real-life instances and randomly generated instances, considering two

to zero. This represents considerable saving times when compared to solving the MIP
models without heuristic rules.

Containers and Liner Shipping
Transporting commercial goods by ship is a vital backbone of nowadays society. Most of
the things we consume on a daily basis have along their route to our doorstep used one
or more seaborne routes. Depending on the type and volume of the commodities to be
transported, three main types of commercial shipping can be deﬁned: tramp shipping,
liner shipping, and industrial shipping. Tramp shipping refers to the transport of large
volumes of bulk cargo, typically raw materials like iron ore, crude oil, grain or coal.
In this mode, ships are operated in a charter fashion where the ship is either full or
empty, transporting cargo from a speciﬁc location to its destination. Liner shipping
refers to the transport of several types of cargo in the same ship, with diﬀerent origins
and destinations. Volumes are typically smaller than the ones in tramp shipping and
the cargo can be containerized, palletized or divided in other ways. Industrial shipping
refers to the case where the owner of the cargo is also the owner of the ship and designs
a strategy to minimize the total transport costs.
For being the preferred mode for
containerized transport, liner shipping will be the only one considered in this thesis.
Containerized transport is the transport of goods using containers. These are reusable
metallic boxes of standardized dimensions and diﬀerent types depending on the cargo to
be transported. Container vessels are ships specially designed for containerized trans-
port. These ships are typically assigned to services, which are ﬁxed cyclic sequences of
ports to visit where containers can be loaded and unloaded. When a vessel is assigned
to a service, it must visit all ports in the sequence even if it is only partially loaded. The
amount of containers that can be loaded in a container vessel is measured in Twenty-
foot Equivalent Unit (TEU), which is the space occupied by a 20’ container. The largest
container vessels can carry up to 18,000 TEUs (Mærsk Triple E class).
3.1
Containers
Containers are metallic boxes that have standardized dimensions, are reusable and can
be stored in high stacks due to their high resistance to vertical compression. Container-
ization improved the shipping of goods by allowing a safe, secure and faster transport.
In terms of dimensions, ISO standard containers are 20’, 40’ or 45’ long, but other less
common dimensions can be found. They are generally 8’ wide and 8.6’ tall, but some
are 9.6’ tall and are denominated high-cubes, although this last type is rare for 20’ long

3.3.2
Vessel structure and arrangement of containers
The structure of a container vessel is directly related to how containers are stored in
the vessel. Along its whole length, the vessel is divided in bays where containers can be
stored. Bays are numbered in ascending order from bow (front) to stern (back) of the
ship, being 20’ bays assigned an odd number and 40’ bays an even number. This means
that an even bay is divided in two contiguous odd bays. Vertically, bays are divided in
two sections, namely on-deck and below-deck. These are separated by hatch-covers, ﬂat
metallic water-proof structures that can support containers. Containers stored below
the hatch-covers are said to be stored in the cargo hold. Each bay is composed by a set

3.3.3
Vessel stability and stress limits
A container vessel is only allowed to leave a port when it is declared seaworthy, meaning
that all stress forces lie within the speciﬁed limits and the stability values are acceptable,
taking into account all the loading components of the ship (containers, water in the bal-
last tanks, fuel, etc.). The total weight of the ship including cargo is called displacement
and determines where the waterline will lie. This is the line where the water meets the
hull of the ship, being the plane that cuts the hull at this line called waterplane. The
depth of the immersed part of the ship (below the waterline) is called draft, which can
be calculated by determining the distance between the waterline and the bottom of the
vessel, the keel. When a vessel is loaded at is maximum displacement, the waterline
observed is called design waterline. A vessel with an evenly distributed displacement
will have a waterline parallel to the keel in both transversal and longitudinal axis.
Unlike longitudinal, transversal parallelism (even keel) is a mandatory condition for the
vessel to be deemed seaworthy. However, a container ship may be allowed to sail with
an uneven longitudinal weight distribution, meaning that the keel will not be parallel
to the waterline in this direction. The angle formed by the intersection of the waterline
and the keel is the trim angle.
In this situation, the draft will not be the same in
all sections of the ship and a distinction is made between the aft draft and fore draft,
respectively measured at the aft perpendicular(AP) and fore perpendicular(FP). Figure
3.8 illustrates these features, where W1 and W2 are two diﬀerent waterlines, the ﬁrst for
a longitudinally balanced ship and the second with a trim angle.
Figure 3.8: Trim, draft and waterlines of a ship, taken from Pacino (2012)
The trim is the diﬀerence between drafts, which takes the value of zero when the ship
is balanced longitudinally. For the vessel to be deemed seaworthy, the trim must lie
between the ship’s speciﬁc limits. A vessel with positive trim is trimming by the stern
and a vessel with negative trim is trimming by the bow. Additionally, the ports that
compose the service for a ship may limit the maximum draft in order to comply with
their depth and characteristics of quay cranes.
Vessel stability refers to the ship’s behaviour at sea, both in still water and in waves.
Calculations of vessel stability take into account the center of gravity (G) and center of
buoyancy (B) of the ship. The center of gravity is the point where the gravitational force
acts, or in other words the location of the weight of the ship. The center of buoyancy is

the center of gravity of the volume of water displaced by the ship, where the buoyancy
force is taken to act. Figure 3.9 presents three diﬀerent situations. In Figure 3.9(a) the
vessel is at even keel and G and B are along the center line, a line that crosses the ship
straight from bow to stern. Diﬀerent external forces may take action on the ship, for
instance wind and waves. When this happens, the situation presented in Figure 3.9(b)
occurs. The vessel responds to the external force and heels, changing the submerged
section of the hull and moving the center of buoyancy in the same direction of the
external force applied (B1). The metacenter (M ) of the vessel is the intersection of the
center line with a vertical line going through B2. Since both forces are misaligned a
moment is generated, the moment of statical stability, which is the moment that returns
the vessel to its initial position when the external force is no longer active. The lever
arm of this moment is called the righting lever and corresponds to the distance between
G and B2. It should be noted that G does not move because the vessel is assumed as a
whole, with no free bodies that would cause it to move.

For small heeling angles (θ), the buoyancy force may be considered vertical. In Figure

is easy to observe the direct relation between the moment of statical stability and the
initial GM for small θs, since the moment increases when GM increases. The GM of
a vessel dictates how it responds to external forces and must lie within a safety range
for the ship to be considered stable. A vessel with a large GM is stiﬀand resistant to
rolling, but when it starts rolling it achieves a considerable speed when going back to its
original position, which may harm cargo and crew. Contrarily, a vessel with a small GM
will not resist to rolling and tend to capsize before going back to the original position.
Figure 3.9(c) shows a phenomenon named listing, which refers to the reaction of the
vessel when a new object is added to it or an object already in it is moved transversally.
The consequence is that G also moves transversally and the vessel lists, in an attempt
to bring its G transversally under M. Due to stability rules, a vessel cannot be deemed
seaworthy in this conditions and an eﬀective distribution of cargo must take place for
the vessel return to the even keel state (Figure 3.9(a)).
Concerns with weight distribution in a container ship go beyond stability issues. The
forces imposed on a ship by the loading of containers stress its physical structure and
must therefore be kept within limits. For this reason, stacks of containers are limited in
terms of weight. For each stack two weight limits are deﬁned, being one referent to the
outer and one to the inner container supports.

Upward and downward forces applied on the ship also cause structural stress. Downward
forces are due to gravity and are related to the weight of the vessel and cargo. Upward
forces refer to the buoyancy and have a magnitude equal to the amount of water displaced
by the vessel. For the ship to buoy the downward and upward forces must be equal, but
the hull shape and the variation of weight along the ship leads to structural stresses.
To allow an eﬃcient distribution of weights within limits, each vessel has a set of points
where the stress limits are known, called frames. Figure 3.10 (Pacino, 2012) represents

between each pair of frames represent the resulting force acting on that section. If the
sections were allowed to move, they would be displaced accordingly to the direction of
the resulting force on that section. Since the vessel can be considered a rigid body,
sections cannot move and the resulting forces will cause shearing and bending stresses
on the structure. The shear force on a speciﬁc frame can be calculated as the integral
of forces on either side of the frame. For each section, its weight can be calculated by
summing the weights of all objects placed in that section. Calculating the buoyancy
force in a section is not so easy, since it depends on the volume of water displaced and
therefore depends on the total displacement of the ship. A way to approximate it is to
calculate the volume of the submerged part of the hull at that section, which depends
on the current draft of the vessel and the shape of the hull. Vessel hulls are generally
irregular forms which diﬃcult the calculation of volume. For that reason, ship builders
provide reference points along the hull called stations where the immersed area is given
as a function of the draft. This relation is called the bonjean curve. Calculating the
immersed volume of a section can be approximated by averaging the immersed areas
between two stations and multiplying it by the distance between the stations (extension
of the section). The bending moment at each frame is the moment forcing the section
to bend and is the integral of the moments on either side of the frame. Curves S and B
represent the shear force and bending moments, respectively.

Chapter 4
The Cargo Mix Problem
The Cargo Mix Problem (CMP) tries to evaluate how performance indicators in liner
shipping industry can be inﬂuenced by the stowage characteristics of containers and
container vessels. In this chapter, a detailed description of the CMP is given, explaining
how it can be solved and which rules it must comply with. The Container Stowage Plan-
ning (CSP) problem is closely related to the CMP, especially the Master Bay Planning
Problem (MBPP). For this reason, this chapter also presents a description of the MBPP
and its rules. Finally, the mathematical model for the CMP considered in this thesis is
presented.
4.1
Container Stowage
The CSP problem deals with the container stowing rules in a container vessel. In Chapter
3 the rules for a vessel to be deemed seaworthy were explained, but one cannot simply
store the containers freely inside the ship. Therefore, some rules must be deﬁned for
a stowage plan to be feasible. Besides the container speciﬁc rules already presented
in Chapter 3, there are stacking rules that can be deﬁned for a single stack and then
extrapolated for all other stacks in the ship.
Containers stowed in the holds are supported by the vessel structure. Due to the vessel
layout, the stacks located in the holds may have diﬀerent conﬁgurations. The hull shape
or the presence of ballast tanks inﬂuences the layout of the holds, restricting the capacity
of some stacks. This information can be found in the vessel proﬁle, a document that
gathers all information of the ship, namely the layout of the vessel, cells with access to
power, odd-slots (cells with only one TEU capacity), etc. For this reason, vessel proﬁles
are indispensable for stowage planning. Usually holds are 40’ long, so a below-deck cell
can stow either a 40’ container or two 20’ containers, excluding the layout exceptions
mentioned above. Cell guides secure containers placed in the holds, and are placed on
both ends of the stack. For this reason and also due to corner castings, 20’ containers
cannot be stowed on top of 40’ containers. Furthermore, single 20’ containers cannot be
stowed on top of a hold stack since the inward end is not secured by other container or
by corner castings. Some hold stacks also have cell guides in the 20’ position, meaning
that in these stacks only 20’ containers can be stowed.
27
==PAGE:40==
Chapter 4. The Cargo Mix Problem
The restrictions for the holds are also valid for the on-deck section, but this presents
additional constraints because the containers are not supported by the vessel structure
but by other containers. The presence of Russian stacks (stacks that have both 40’
and 20’ containers) on-deck is unusual.
Twistlocks are used to secure containers to
each other, which are connectors resistant to both horizontal and vertical forces. These
provide suﬃcient support to containers placed in the ﬁrst two tiers, above which and up
to the ﬁfth tier the use of lashing rods is necessary. These are carefully tied to the deck
and must be constantly veriﬁed to remain tight during the whole journey. Containers
above the ﬁfth tier must be secured to the lashing bridge. The stowage of 45’ containers
is only allowed in tiers above the lashing bridges, since the space between these is usually
40’ long. 20’ containers are only secured to the lashing bridge in one of their sides, being
the inward side lashed to the deck up to the ﬁfth tier. The inward side must be accessible
since the lashing must be tight at all times, thus a 40’ container cannot be stowed in
the path of 20’ containers in these conditions. Containers placed above the ﬁfth tier are
supported only by the twistlocks, and for this reason stacks on deck above the lashing
bridge can only have a tier diﬀerence of one in order to support each other.
The stability of the vessel also inﬂuences the stacking rules of containers. In container
ships with a short rolling period (large GM) the top tiers are exposed to strong forces
that stress the twistlocks and the whole stacking containers’ structure. For this reason,
the weight of containers in a stack should be descending bottom-up (light containers
on top). Another factor that inﬂuences the stacking rules is the line-of-sight. This is
an imaginary line that connects the pilot house to the sea, at a distance equal to the
smallest of 500 meters or twice the length of the ship. For visibility reasons, containers
cannot be stowed above this imaginary line.
A stowage plan is a possible assignment of containers to the slots in the container ship.
They are determined by stowage coordinators previous to the vessel’s call to the port, and
are later used to coordinate the loading and unloading operations. One of the inputs for
creating a stowage plan is the loadlist of containers, which is a list discriminating which
containers should be loaded in the vessel in that port. This list contains detailed infor-
mation on the containers, regarding dimensions, type, weight and destination. Similarly,
a release list is provided, which includes the same type of information but for containers
that are currently on the vessel and will be unloaded in that port. It also includes the
position occupied by the containers in the vessel. Demand forecasts on subsequent ports
based on historical data are also provided so that the amount and type of containers to
expect in these ports can be estimated. Since these forecasts are not always accurate,
some rules-of-thumb are adopted like for example not stowing non-reefer containers in
reefer slots, keep the number of diﬀerent destinations to a minimum in each location,
and group containers with the same destination in the same vessel sections. Information
regarding fuel, diesel, oil, and water in the ballast tanks is also provided to the stowage
coordinators, along with port information such as number of cranes available and depth.
The usual time window allowed for creating a stowage plan is six hours.
A good stowage plan minimizes the costs associated with the vessel’s call to the port.
Since the main costs associated with this activity are intrinsically related with the time
spent at the port, the overall objective is to minimize the time between arrival and
departure of the ship.
Stowage plans that allow a good crane-split (similar service
times for all cranes assigned to the ship) will allow a faster port service time.
The
minimization of overstowage is also a very important factor for minimizing the time at
port. To illustrate the importance of this factor, consider a container ship coming from
28
==PAGE:41==

Asia and arriving in Hamburg. Consider two containers, A and H, with ﬁnal destinations
Aarhus and Hamburg respectively, and consider that H is placed in a cell below A. When
the ship arrives in Hamburg, for H to be unloaded two extra moves will be performed,
since A has to be unloaded and then loaded again. In this situation it is said that A
overstows H. Furthermore, consider a diﬀerent situation where H is placed below-deck.
When arriving Hamburg, all the containers that are going to ports after Hamburg placed
above the hatch cover that gives access to H are overstowing H. This is a more costly
kind of overstowage (hatch overstowage) since all these containers have to be unloaded
along with the hatch cover in order to reach H. By looking at the given description one
could argue that containers should be stowed in port layers, being the ones to later ports
stowed in the ﬁrst tiers. This would however complicate the situation even more when
other containers are loaded in subsequent ports. For this reason, stowage coordinators
have to take into account all the subsequent ports when creating a stowage plan, in
order to minimize overstowage.
4.1.1
Master Bay Planning Problem
The CSP is often decomposed in two problems: the Master Bay Planning Problem
(MBPP) and the Slot Planning Problem (SPP). The ﬁrst assigns containers to locations
of the ship without specifying which slot or cell they will occupy. The second solves the
problem of assigning containers to slots or cells for each location. For the purpose of this
project, only the MBPP will be described, since it is the one that is more relevant for
the CMP approach proposed. The MBPP creates a general distribution of containers
along the vessel, so it is in this phase that the constraints related to the stability and
stress forces on the vessel are considered. The total weight of containers placed in the
locations described in Chapter 3 can be used for approximating the stability and stress
calculations, thus the slot positioning of containers is not relevant to the MBPP. The
problem considers all ports in the planed route and generates for each of them a plan
that minimizes overstowage and crane utilization.
4.2
Cargo Mix Problem
The purpose of the Cargo Mix Problem (CMP) is to evaluate how important performance
indicators for liner shipping companies, like for example vessel intake and cargo revenue,
are inﬂuenced by the stowage characteristics of containers and container vessels. The
evaluation is done by considering containers with speciﬁc features that are purpose of the
analysis and generating stowage plans with these containers respecting all the constraints
of the MBPP. The main diﬀerence between the MBPP part of the CSP and the CMP
is that in the CMP there is no loadlist. The analysis is based on stochastic cargo ﬂow
forecasts. The containers that ﬁgure on the stowage plan are selected according to their
gain or in order to maximize the performance indicator being measured. Citing Delgado
(2013), the CMP generates seaworthy stowage plans that optimize the gain generated
by the containers stowed.
If the objective to maximize is cargo revenue, along with the gain associated with stowing
a container the costs of stowing it and how it is stowed must be considered. This means
that containers should be stowed in the ship in a way that minimizes fuel consumptions
and time at ports. The ﬁrst can be minimized by minimizing the amount of ballast
29

water loaded in the tanks, which helps on the stability of the ship but increases its
displacement and consequently its fuel consumption. The second is minimized as it was
explained in Section 4.1, by minimizing overstowage and crane utilization.
The plans generated by the CMP must be seaworthy in order to be relevant for the
industry, thus all the rules and constraints of stowing containers and stability and stress
forces must be considered. In the CMP problem considered in this project, the following
container types were considered:
• Standard ISO containers 20’ and 40’ long;
• Reefer or non-reefer;
• High-cube or normal height.
Any combination of the above types is possible, except for high-cube 20’ containers, since
high-cubes are often 40’ long. Furthermore, the following sets of rules and assumptions
were taken into account:
• Location capacity rules in terms of volume, TEU, weight, and reefer capacities
(reefer slot and reefer cell capacities);
• Stability and stress forces, including ballast tanks, vessel displacement, buoyancy
forces, shear forces, and bending moment;
• It was assumed that the vessel will always sail at trim 0;
• The decisions made at one port inﬂuence the decisions made at subsequent ports.
This is due to the fact that at each port call the vessel already has containers
onboard.
• Overstowage was only considered implicitly and not explicitly in the objective
function;
• The objectives of ballast water minimization and hatch overstowage were not con-
sidered.
4.3

by Delgado (2013) for solving the same problem. The model was modiﬁed according
to the assumptions made for this project. Containers are modelled in types based on

The model builds on top of the model for the master bay planning problem (MBPP),
also developed in Delgado (2013). Due to having a multi port scenario, transports must

Chapter 5
Solution Approach
In this chapter the solution approach suggested to solve the CMP problem is presented,
describing in detail the diﬀerent components of the methodology and justifying the
decisions made while creating them.
5.1
Decomposition proposed
The solution approach proposed in this thesis decomposes the problem in three distinct
parts: a mathematical model to ﬁnd a distribution of location weights that respects
ship stability and stress forces at each port, a heuristic algorithm to assign containers
to the vessel’s locations respecting the weights found by the mathematical model, and
an improvement iterative process to attempt ﬁnding better solutions. The reasoning
behind this decision is related to the fact that stability constraints are easily handled in
a mathematical formulation, while the placement of containers is not (due to the high
number of variables it requires). On the other hand, stability constraints are hard to
handle heuristically, while container placement is relatively simple. Figure 5.1 shows a
schematic representation of how the three components are connected.
As it can be understood from Figure 5.1, the proposed approach iteratively exchanges
information between its diﬀerent components. As it will be explained in detail in Section
5.3, the heuristic placement of containers has several sub methods incorporated both to
ﬁnd an actual assignment of containers and to ﬁnd which weights should be passed to
the mathematical model. Each of the three components will be described in detail in
the following sections.

the service to generate a set of stable weights. The sets, constants, and variables used

The objective function (5.1) minimizes the diﬀerence between the suggested weights and
the actual weights that are decided upon. Therefore, if the suggested weights result in
a stable vessel (feasible set of weights for the model), then these weights will be the
optimal solution. The objective function in (5.1) is non-linear, and therefore a linearised
version of the objective is used instead.
The set of constraints is similar to the stability constraints explained in the model
presented in Chapter 4.3, where they are explained in greater detail. The main diﬀer-
ence is that the model presented here considers a single port, and therefore must be
solved multiple times to give feasible weights for the full service. Constraints (5.2)-(5.8),
mainly handle the variable displacement. This is done by splitting the displacement
into displacement intervals, in which the metacenter, trim and draft functions can ap-
proximated by linear functions.
These sets of constraints make sure that only one
displacement interval is chosen, and the vessel displacement is within the bounds of this
interval. Furthermore the longitudinal center of gravity is calculated, and it is ensured it
is within bounds deﬁned by the displacement interval. Constraints (5.9)-(5.12) calculate
the vertical moment and vertical center of gravity, while the transversal center of gravity
is forced to be 0. The next four constraints (5.13)-(5.17) use linearisation coeﬃcients
to calculate the metacenter, draft, trim, and bonjean. Here it is also ensured that the
trim is 0 and the metacentric height is higher than the minimum metacentric height
allowed. Constraints (5.18)-(5.21) calculate the shearing and bending, and it is ensured
that shearing and bending limits for each frame are not exceeded.

Solution approach description
The pseudo code for the entire solution approach is presented in Algorithm 1.

Although overstowage is not explicitly considered in the algorithm, the

A few notes should be made on how the placement heuristic is in line with the objectives
of the problem. First, intake is maximized by placing as many containers as possible at

in the literature review, minimizing the number of diﬀerent transports in a location is a
way of minimizing overstowage.

7.2
Placement procedure analysis
There are two possibilities for the limitations of the solution approach:
• The weights suggested to the stability MIP model inﬂuence the weights the model
will return, meaning that if the suggestion of weights is poor the model will hardly
return the weights that would provide the optimal solution;
• The placement procedure might not be performing well in terms of which contain-
ers to choose for placement.
The ﬁrst point is hard to assess, since there is no knowledge of which weights would
provide the optimal solution. Indeed the weight suggestion is completely dependent on
the previous solution obtained by the placement heuristic, so if that solution is bad then
the weights suggested will be also bad. It was decided to conduct further experiments
to evaluate the second point, the quality of the placement procedure.
A ﬁrst experiment consisted of running the solution approach once on each of the in-
stances considered so far (both original and lighter instances). For the ﬁnal solution
found in each of these, the weights at all locations at all ports were recorded. The upper
bound model was then used to ﬁnd an upper bound for a placement using these ex-
act weights. By comparing these upper bounds with the objective values obtained, the
quality of the placement procedure can be assessed. The results obtained are presented
in Table 7.10.

As expected, Table 7.10 shows that the gaps between the objective values and the upper

transports are attended (αt). A transport that visits more ports is given priority over
transports that visit less ports. This ordering allows situations where a combination of
two or more transports that don’t overlap each other but overlap a larger transport, being
that the combination of transports visits more ports than the larger transport, cannot
be loaded due to the amount of containers loaded from the larger one. A schematic
representation of this is shown in Figure 7.2.
Figure 7.2: Transport overlap situation
According to the adopted transport selection, Transport 1 has priority over transports
2 and 3 because it visits more ports. If the vessel is close to full capacity after loading
the containers belonging to Transport 1, it will not be able to store many containers
from transports 2 and 3, when in fact it would be beneﬁcial for the objective value
to transport them instead. If the optimal solution includes transporting all containers
from transports 2 and 3, the placement heuristic will never be able to reach that solution
because it will select Transport 1 ﬁrst. This becomes more evident for larger instances,
when all transports have a higher quantity of containers, which justiﬁes the higher gaps
observed in these instances.

The increased gaps shown in the table enable the conclusion that in fact transport
selection is the main reason why the solutions from the solution approach diﬀer from
the upper bounds. An interesting fact is that the time spent in the stability MIP was
equal to zero for all instances. This was expected since the initial weights are balanced
weights, which will ensure the solution created by the algorithm is within the OOBLimit
and thus not needing new MIP solves.
7.3
Experiments conclusions
It can be concluded that although the running times of the solution are very satisfactory,
the solutions obtained by it are not. On one hand, the selection of transports to be
attended is not eﬃcient, as it prioritizes transports with more port calls when a diﬀerent
combination of transports could be more eﬃcient. On the other hand, the suggestion
of weights to the stability MIP model does not guarantee a convergence towards the
optimal weight distribution, which is not known. Further methods should be developed
for obtaining better weight distributions.
Another factor that is inﬂuencing the quality of the solutions obtained is related to the
ﬁxing of locations. Locations are ﬁxed by analysing the diﬀerence between the MIP
weights and the weights after placement. If the suggested weights are not good, the
MIP weights will follow, meaning that the procedure might be ﬁxing locations that
are not worthy to be ﬁxed. This inﬂuences the ﬁnal solution obtained, since once the
locations are ﬁxed they remain ﬁxed until a stable solution is found. This leads to the
bad results in terms of ship occupancy, even when there is nominal, volume, reefer and
weight capacity remaining that can be ﬁlled.
In summary, for the solution approach to be improved in terms of ship occupancy results
and objective values obtained a diﬀerent transport selection should be considered. Fur-
thermore, improved methods for selecting the locations to ﬁx and for suggesting weights
to the stability MIP should be developed.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
This thesis aimed at examining the possibility of combining mathematical programming
with heuristics to obtain good solutions to the Cargo Mix Problem in computational
times under 10 minutes. The research question was therefore decomposed in two parts:
the quality of the solutions obtained and the runtime performance of the solution ap-
proach.
The solution approach is a 3-step decomposed methodology composed of a
mathematical MIP model to ﬁnd weight distributions that respect stability and stress
forces including ballast tanks, a heuristic placement of containers, and an iterative im-
provement procedure to attempt ﬁnding better solutions.
The experimental results showed that the algorithm was robust in presenting feasible
solutions to the problem in running times considerably below 10 minutes, yet the quality
of the solutions generated can still be improved further. The solutions obtained have
considerable gaps to the upper bounds calculated for the instances. Furthermore, the full
vessel occupancy was never reached, even when containers were still available and the
vessel was not operating under full capacities. A further investigation in the experimental
phase provided explanations for these limitations. It was concluded that the selection
of transports to be prioritized was one of the main reasons, as it prioritizes transports
with a higher number of port calls when there could be other combinations of transports
more beneﬁcial to the objective. Another reason investigated was the weight suggestion
from the heuristic to the MIP. These weights have a high dependency on the solution
approach performance, so if the placement is far from the optimal placement the weights
suggested will hardly converge to the optimal weight distributions. It was also concluded
that the weights of the containers generated inﬂuenced the performance of the algorithm.
Although the quality of the solutions obtained indicate that further improvements are
needed, the computational times observed support the availability of a time window for
these improvements. However, it should be noted that the solution approach considered
only the intake maximization objective.
When other objectives are included in the
model, for example the minimization of ballast water, overstowage, or crane makespan,
the running times will most likely increase.

Chapter 7. Conclusions
8.1
Future work
The limitations found in the solution approach, as well as the multiple objectives not
taken into account, motivate further research on the topic.
Regarding the limitations, the transport prioritization should be addressed. Instead of
prioritizing the transports that visit more ports, an idea could be prioritizing groups of
transports that do not overlap and that together visit as many ports as possible. This
should improve the results obtained without a considerable impact on the running times.
The suggestion of weights to the MIP should also be investigated in order to converge
to the optimal weight distribution. This is harder to do since there is no knowledge of
the ideal weight distributions, but the solution from the LP relaxation used in the upper
bound model could be used to have an idea. The improvement part of the solution
approach could also be analysed. Instead of re-starting with an empty vessel and using
only diﬀerent initial weights, it could start with some information about the previous
stable solution obtained. An idea would be to ﬁx some locations or even start with the
whole previous solution. Finally, it should be remembered that the parameter sensitivity
analysis was done due to time constraints, which not allowed conducting an extensive
parameter tuning. This could potentially improve the overall performance of the solution
approach.
Regarding the not considered objectives, the possibility of including them in the solution
approach should be addressed. The ballast water minimization objective could be in-
troduced in the stability MIP, which would become a multi objective model minimizing
the water in the tanks at the same time it minimizes the diﬀerence to the suggested
weights. As for overstowage probability and hatch overstowage, a swap algorithm could
be implemented to optimize these. However, this procedure should only be used once
intake is optimized, since according to the industry the intake maximization is more
important than overstowage minimization. Another modiﬁcation that should be tested
is changing the main objective to be revenue maximization instead of intake maximiza-
tion. To address this, it would most likely be favourable to change the priorities and
the transport selection parameters, since now the objective would depend on the proﬁt
made by each container type belonging to each transport.
Finally, in this thesis only one vessel and one service were analysed. Testing the solution
approach on several vessel types varying in size and characteristics, and on several
services of diﬀerent lengths and demands would be interesting. Furthermore, access to
real demand data would provide more useful results.
