Abstract
Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) has become important as quantum computing threatens
classical cryptosystems. This thesis investigates decryption failures in code-based public-key
encryption schemes, an important issue in PQC where many schemes have a small probability of
decryption error. The overall aim is to analyze how such failures affect security and to provide
formal security guarantees against adversaries exploiting these failures. The main contribution
is a proof that Alekhnovich’s code-based encryption scheme satisfies the security notion known
as Find Failing Plaintexts – Non Generic (FFP-NG), meaning that no efficient adversary, given
a specific public key, can exploit this key to cause the scheme to decrypt incorrectly with signifi-
cantly higher probability. Additionally, the thesis provides a comparative analysis with two other
post-quantum schemes. We examine the PVW lattice-based encryption scheme, summarizing
a recent proof of FFP-NG security. We also study HQC (Hamming Quasi-Cyclic), a modern
code-based scheme from the NIST PQC process, including an analysis of its decryption failure
rate and security in terms of FFP-NG. Through these contributions, the thesis deepens the
understanding of decryption failure attacks, and provides formal assurance that Alekhnovich’s
code-based scheme remains secure even when decryption is not always perfect.

1 Introduction
1 Introduction
Public-key encryption (PKE) is fundamental to digital security, enabling secure web browsing,
digital signatures, and many other protocols. PKE schemes typically rely on mathematical prob-
lems that make encryption using the public key easy but decryption, without the secret key,
hard. Classic examples of hard problems are integer factorization and the discrete logarithm
problem. However, Peter Shor showed in 1994 that quantum algorithms solve the factoring
and discrete logarithm problem in polynomial time, making these mathematical problems no
longer NP-hard in the presence of quantum computers. With significant progress being made
in quantum computing research, there is now a growing need for quantum-resistant cryptography.
To address this challenge, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has
launched a standardization effort for Post-Quantum cryptography [1].
At a PQCrypto con-
ference in February 2016, NIST announced a call for public-key quantum-resistant encryption
schemes, inviting cryptography experts worldwide to submit proposals, marking the beginning
of the NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization process. In 2024, NIST finalized its
first set of quantum-resistance encryption standards [2], selecting algorithms based on structured
lattices and hash functions. In the fourth round of the Post-Quantum Cryptography Standard-
ization Process, NIST selected four candidate algorithms for key establishment to be studied,
and in march 2025, the HQC scheme [3] was chosen as the fifth standardized post-quantum
algorithm. The code-based HQC was chosen to complement the lattice-based ML-KEM (Ky-
ber) scheme [4], as HQC’s different mathematical foundation is important in the case where a
weakness is discovered in ML-KEM [5].
To evaluate encryption security, cryptographers use security notions, allowing security guaran-
tees to be defined in a standardized way. A PKE scheme is IND-CPA secure (indistinguishability
under chosen-plaintext attack) if no adversary can distinguish the encryption of two messages,
even when the adversary gets to choose the specific plaintexts to encrypt. A stronger notion,
IND-CCA (indistinguishability under chosen-ciphertext attack) additionally allows the adver-
sary access to a decryption oracle, making IND-CCA a crucial property for real-world security
against attackers. Typically, a PKE scheme achieving IND-CPA can by transformed into an
IND-CCA secure KEM by the Fujisaki-Okamoto transform [6]. However, while many of the
most efficient PKEs have a small probability that decryption will output the wrong message or
fail to produce any message, these security notions do not account for the possibility of decryp-
tion errors.
Many efficient post-quantum schemes are only approximately correct, such that encryption and
decryption may fail with very small probability. This applies for the the PKE underlying ML-
KEM/Kyber [5], as well as BIKE [7] and HQC [3], as they include noise or error that can
occasionally cause decryption failures [8]. Such decryption failures can potentially be exploited
by an attacker, who might learn secret information by crafting ciphertexts that deliberately
induce errors, and by observing how the system reacts. To achieve schemes that are secure in
the post-quantum setting, one must not only ensure IND-CPA and IND-CCA security, but also
ensure that these potential decryption failure events cannot be exploited.
One such notion that accounts for the possibility of decryption failures is the Find Failing Plain-
1

text - Non Generic (FFP-NG) notion, introduced in [9]. In the FFP-NG game, a challenger
generates two key pairs and provides the adversary with one of the two public keys along with
access to a Failure Checking Oracle (FCO). The goal of the adversary is to determine whether
the oracle is using the same key pair as the one corresponding to the given public key or an
independent one. To achieve this, the adversary can submit a single message–randomness pair
to the FCO, and the oracle will test whether this pair triggers a decryption failure with respect
to the key pair it holds. The FFP-NG secures that, even if a scheme has a small failure rate, an
adversary cannot force or increase the likelihood of such an decryption error.
It is important to note that FFP-NG is distinct and independent of IND-CPA security.
A
scheme might be IND-CPA secure yet allow an adversary to force a decryption failure with
non-negligible probability, or vice versa. Standard IND-CPA does not guarantee anything about
the scheme in terms of decryption error, thus any approximately correct post-quantum PKE
must be analyzed for both. This has already been done for the lattice-based PVW PKE scheme
[10] in [8], securing the FFP-NG security assuming the hardness of the Learning With Errors
problem. In this thesis, we extend the investigation to the code-based setting.
The contributions of this thesis
Our main focus is on code-based PKE schemes, in particular Alekhnovich’s Cryptosystem [11].
Michael Alekhnovich introduced in 2003 a new variant of the Learning Parity with Noise (LPN)
problem, and showed that it could be used to build an IND-CPA secure encryption scheme.
Since the decryption process is based on decoding random linear codes, there is a natural prob-
ability of decryption error by design. However, to our knowledge, no prior work has proved
FFP-NG security for Alekhnovich’s scheme yet. This thesis closes this gap. We provide two
security reductions for (variants of) Alekhnovich’s PKE against FFP-NG adversaries.
• First reduction: We show that if an adversary can win the FFP-NG game against
Alekhnovich’s scheme with non-negligible advantage, then one can solve a variant of the
decoding problem with non-negligible advantage. This reduction requires a bound on the
adversary’s advantage.
• Second reduction: We modify the scheme to a variant called Alekhnovich∗which remains
IND-CPA secure, and prove FFP-NG security without extra assumptions on the adversary.
To put our contribution in context, we also investigate two other encryption schemes.
• Lattice-based PVW scheme: We summarize the PVW encryption scheme based on
the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem and its known IND-CPA security. We outline
the FFP-NG proof by [8] and highlight parallels and differences between the lattice and
code settings.
• HQC scheme: We review the HQC PKE (NIST’s new code-based algorithm) and its IND-
CPA proof. We analyze how decryption failures occur in HQC and discuss an approach
toward proving its FFP-NG property. Although we do not give a full proof for HQC, our
analysis provides insight into a possible strategy, highlights the challenges involved, and
suggests how a future reduction might proceed.

2.2 Cryptography
With the basic notations in place, we now look at the cryptographic concepts that are needed
for the rest of the thesis. We briefly review the main types of encryption schemes, including
symmetric encryption, public-key encryption, and key encapsulation mechanisms, and explain
why they are relevant in the context of post-quantum cryptography.
2.2.1 Encryption Schemes
Encryption schemes are algorithms that allow communication in a way such that only autho-
rized parties can decode the information. The information used for encryption and decryption
of a message (plaintext) are called keys, and these keys can be constructed in many different
ways. Encryption schemes are generally categorized into two groups: Symmetric and Asymmet-
ric/Public key encryption schemes.
Symmetric Encryption
In the symmetric model, the sender and receiver share a single key, which is used for both
encryption and decryption. This encryption method is fast and efficient, however since both
parties need the key beforehand to communicate, some prior communication is required. An
example of a symmetric encryption model is the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES). The
security of symmetric encryption relies mainly on the size of the key. A quantum computer can
use Grover’s algorithm to significantly reduce the attack time for guessing the key. However,
the AES-256 key space is believed to be sufficiently large for increasing the key size, making the
model quantum resistant.

Public Key Encryption (PKE)
The asymmetric or public-key encryption model uses two different keys for encryption and
decryption. A public key is used for encryption, and a secret key is used for decryption, as
shown in Figure 2. This means that anyone can send an encrypted message using the public
key, but only the receiver with access to the secret key will be able to decrypt the message.
To achieve these properties, the public key encryption schemes usually rely on mathematical
problems that are easy to compute one way (encryption is easy) but hard to compute the other
way (decryption is hard). Two widely used hard problems in modern cryptosystems are [12]:
• The factoring problem. Given n = pq find p and q, where p and q are different primes.
• The discrete logarithm problem. Given a, n and b = ax mod n, find x.
These problems are computationally hard when using large numbers, and the hardness as-
sumption of these problems forms the basis of the security of the PKE schemes.
The RSA
Cryptosystem is based on the factoring problem, while cryptosystems such as ElGamal and the
Diffie Hellmann Exchange protocol are based on the discrete logarithm problem [12].
Figure 2: Public Key Encryption model. The public key (blue) is used for encryption while the
secret key (red) is used for decryption.
Key Encapsulation Mechanism (KEM)
Although PKEs solve the issue of requiring prior key exchange between two parties, they are
generally inefficient to use due to their high computational cost. Instead, modern cryptographic
protocols often rely on Key Encapsulation Mechanisms (KEMs) to establish shared keys be-
tween parties where no prior trust has been established. As shown in Figure 3, the structure
of KEMs and PKEs can be seen to be quite similar. However, instead of encrypting messages,
KEMs are used to encapsulate and share keys. Once the keys have been shared between the two
parties, they can switch to symmetric encryption for further communication, benefiting from its
efficiency.

2.2.2 Post-Quantum Cryptography
Most public-key cryptographic algorithms in use today rely on the hardness of mathematical
problems such as the factoring problem and the discrete logarithm problem, which are assumed
to be hard for classical computers. However, in 1994, Peter Shor proposed a quantum algorithm,
known today as "Shor’s Algorithm", which has been proven to solve the factoring and discrete
logarithm problem in polynomial time, making the security of today’s most used public-key
encryption algorithms vulnerable in the presence of quantum computers.
Post-Quantum Cryptography is the area of research focused on designing algorithms that re-
main secure against adversaries equipped with quantum computers. To this end, researches
have proposed several families of quantum-resistant cryptographic constructions.
These in-
clude lattice-based, code-based, hash-based, non-commutative, multivariate, and isogeny-based
encryption systems. Developing and standardizing post-quantum cryptographic algorithms is
essential to protect the confidentiality and authenticity of digital communication as quantum
computing becomes a practical reality.

2.3.1 Introduction to Coding Theory
Coding theory is the study of encoding data or information efficiently for transmission while
minimizing errors. In this way, coding theory differs from cryptography, as it is no longer con-
cerned with secrecy, but primarily with accuracy and efficiency. When transferring information
from one place to another, the information often passes through a noisy channel. This could
be due to a lightning strike damaging a radio wave, poor materials causing electrical noise in
a circuit, or a scratch on a DVD that results in the information received differing from the in-
formation sent. Coding Theory focuses on detecting and correcting such transmission errors [14].
Before we go deeper into coding theory, we will first look at an example of how we might
detect and correct errors.
Say we want to send a single bit m ∈{0, 1} across an electrical
wire with a 1/10 probability that a bit sent through the wire gets flipped, due to some noise.

3 Find Failing Plaintext - Non Generic (FFP-NG)
3 Find Failing Plaintext - Non Generic (FFP-NG)
There are several approaches to formally evaluate the security of encryption schemes, typically
formalized through well-defined security notions. Among the most established are INDistin-
guishability under Chosen-Plaintext Attack (IND-CPA) and its stronger variant INDistinguisha-
bility under Chosen-Ciphertext Attack (IND-CCA). IND-CCA requires that no efficient adver-
sary with access to a decryption oracle should be able to distinguish which of two messages is
encrypted in a given ciphertext, even when the messages are chosen by the adversary itself. IND-
CPA, on the other hand, excludes oracle access to decryption and is therefore weaker. While
IND-CCA is naturally a more desired notion of security, it is usually much more difficult to
build an IND-CCA secure scheme than an IND-CPA secure one.
A number of transformations exist for turning a basic public-key encryption (PKE) scheme
into an IND-CCA secure scheme [6, 15]. One example is the Fujisaki-Okamoto (FO) transform
[6], which has become a go-to approach for transforming post-quantum PKE schemes into IND-
CCA secure KEMs, and in fact, all KEMs submitted to the NIST standardization process uses
a version of this transformation [9]. However, many of the most efficient post-quantum PKEs,
such as ML-KEM/Kyber [5, 16], BIKE [7] and HQC [3], are only approximately correct, mean-
ing that a decryption of an honestly generated ciphertext can fail. It is therefore important that
the FO security reductions account for approximately correct PKEs.
To address this, [9] introduced a framework for improving how decryption failures are handled
in the FO security reduction. As part of their approach, they defined a new family of security
games namely the Find Failing Plaintext (FFP) games. One member of the FFP family is the
Find Failing Plaintext - Non-Generic (FFP-NG) game, which gives a public key to the adversary
and asks it to find a message that triggers a decryption failure more likely with respect to this
key pair than with respect to an independent key pair. To ensure applicability of the analysis
in [9], FFP-NG security should be analyzed for any approximately correct post-quantum PKE,
either through cryptanalysis or a security reduction to a well-studied quantum-hard problem.
In the following, we first define the more familiar IND-CPA notion and then introduce the
FFP-NG game.
3.1 IND-CPA
The IND-CPA game is between an adversary A and a challenger. In this game, the challenger
generates a key pair (sk, pk). Adversary A gets access to the public key pk and chooses and
sends two messages m0 and m1 to the challenger. The challenger picks one of the two messages
at random, encrypts this message and sends back to A. Adversary A wins the game if they, with
only knowledge of the public key pk, guesses correctly on which message has been encrypted.

8.5 Challenges in the HQC
One of the main difficulties when working with HQC is the combinatorial complexity of its error
behavior. When we consider the relative distances of the 1-entries and how they overlap, the
analysis quickly becomes very complicated. In particular, it is hard to derive a threshold or con-
dition for when an FFP-NG adversary has succeeded in forcing a decryption error. Determining
exactly what patterns is "right" is non-trivial, because it involves analyzing all possible overlaps
and distances in the code’s cyclic structure. A natural idea as a follow-up to the reductions
for the PVW scheme and Alekhnovich’s cryptosystem, is to add an extra error vector to the
input key and give the modified key to the adversary, to detect when they are trying to force a
decryption failure.
For example, one might try to add an error vector x′ of weight 2 to the original secret ˆx of
weight t. Intuitively, the two 1s in x′ have a specific relative distance between them, which
might appear among the 1s of the new combined secret x = ˆx + x′. If the adversary attempts
to force a decryption error through their choice of randomness r, the hope is that this will be
apparent when analyzing the relation between x′ and the randomness r. However, predicting
the outcome of this trick is very difficult. When we add a vector of weight 2 to a vector of
weight t, we could potentially introduce as many as 2t new pairwise distances among the 1s in
x. It is also possible that one of the new 1s lands exactly on an existing 1 of ˆx, canceling it out.
In that case, the adversary might even try to "avoid" the added error as an attempt to enforce
a decryption error. If both new 1s fall in two zero positions, the weight increases by 2, and so
the resulting Hamming weight of x can vary from t −2 up to t + 2, making the distribution

9 Discussion and Conclusion
9 Discussion and Conclusion
In this thesis, we have studied two types of post-quantum public key encryptions schemes:
lattice-based and code-based PKEs. Our primary focus has been on the code-based Alekhnovich’s
cryptosystem as well as the Hamming Quasi-Cyclic scheme and the lattice-based PVW PKE.
Inspired by an existing FFP-NG proof for the PVW scheme [8], we constructed a similar proof
for Alekhnovich’s cryptosystem, proving FFP-NG security under certain assumptions. We also
explored the possibility of proving FFP-NG security for HQC, but a full proof was not achievable
within the scope of this project. Nonetheless, we presented an analysis of the HQC scheme that
identifies key challenges and outlines possible directions for future work. These investigations
have provided insights into the similarities and differences between code-based and lattice-based
public-key encryption schemes, particularly in how they handle decryption failures and how re-
duction proofs for FFP-NG can be constructed.
One fundamental difference between lattice-based and code-based cryptosystems lies in the
distribution of errors used in encryption. In lattice-based schemes like the PVW PKE, error
vectors are sampled from a discrete Gaussian distribution. By the smoothing parameter, these
distributions behave similarly to continuous Gaussians in many ways. Under certain assump-
tions on the lattice, it becomes relatively straightforward to work with the distributions of the
sum of two error vectors. Therefore, when we add an additional error to the input key in the
reduction, we can predict the final error distribution very accurately.
In contrast, code-based PKEs like Alekhnovich’s scheme and HQC involve error vectors in the
Hamming metric. When adding two such error vectors together, the resulting Hamming weight
can be very unpredictable. In Alekhnovich’s case, we addressed this by constructing alternative
versions of the decisional decoding problem to ensure that, with high probability, the resulting
error distribution had the desired weight. Even then, we cannot be certain that we obtain the
right distribution, and we must account for this probability. However, once we obtain a "good"
distribution, the analysis of the adversary’s strategy is in some ways simpler compared to the
lattice-based setting. While it was important for the lattice-based PKE to analyze the final error
distribution to ensure that the adversary would get an input that looks like a real key, this was
not necessary in the case of Alekhnovich’s cryptosystem, as it is very unlikely that the adversary
would distinguish the extra added 1-entry from the other 1-entries.
While both lattice-based and code-based encryption schemes rely on hard mathematical prob-
lems, their underlying assumptions differ significantly. Lattice-based schemes such as the PVW
PKE are based on the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem and benefit from worst-case to
average-case hardness guarantees. This means that breaking an average LWE-based scheme like
the PVW PKE would imply solving a worst-case instance of well-studied lattice problems such
as the Shortest Vector Problem (SVP) or the Short Integer Solution (SIS) problem [24].
Code-based schemes such as the HQC and Alekhnovich rely on the hardness of decoding ran-
dom linear codes, formulated as the Syndrome Decoding Problem and the Decisional Decoding
Problem. While there are examples of worst-case to average-case reductions for certain decod-
ing problems ([25],[26]), both cases required additional assumptions on the codes in order to
derive the reductions. Thus, the hardness assumptions behind code-based cryptosystems are
54

based more on cryptanalytic evidence than on proven worst-to-average reductions. However,
HQC and Alekhnovich’s cryptosystem have been analyzed against decoding attacks and are still
well-supported by decades of cryptanalysis. Alekhnovich’s cryptosystem can even be interpreted
as relying on an LPN-like assumption, whose hardness is quite well-studied and widely believed
to be hard. Therefore, while the theoretical guarantees are stronger in the lattice-based setting,
the practical security of code-based schemes remains well-supported.
An important direction for future work is to develop a full security proof of FFP-NG for HQC.
This is especially relevant now that HQC has been selected for standardization as a post-quantum
cryptographic scheme. While some preliminary analysis has been done, a complete proof remains
open. Continuing the analysis of the error vector is a natural next step, but due to the com-
plexity of the error vector distributions, it may be necessary to approach this numerically or
via simulation. Alternatively, a completely different strategy that avoids the combinatorics is
needed. Delivering such a proof and analysis would not only strengthen confidence in HQC but
could also provide insights applicable to other modern code-based schemes.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates how decryption failures can be formally addressed in
the code-based setting, and what a proof of FFP-NG security looks like in practice. The proof
for Alekhnovich’s encryption scheme serves as a concrete example of how these failures can be
handled in such a security analysis. While Alekhnovich’s scheme is primarily of theoretical inter-
est, this work lays the groundwork for applying similar techniques to larger and more practical
cryptosystems, such as HQC. It is crucial to consider a broad range of security notions when
evaluating post-quantum schemes, not only standard definitions like IND-CPA and IND-CCA,
but also those that explicitly account for decryption failures. Such failures can be critical in
real-world scenarios and must be accounted for to ensure the robustness of future cryptographic
standards.
