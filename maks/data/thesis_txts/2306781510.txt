ABSTRACT 
	  
Snakebite	   envenomation	   is	   a	   globally	   neglected	   health	   challenge	   causing	   hundreds	   of	  
thousands	   of	   mortalities	   and	   amputations	   worldwide	   –	   especially	   in	   poor,	   rural	   parts	   of	  
Africa	   and	   South-­‐East	   Asia.	   Currently,	   the	   only	   effective	   treatment	   against	   snakebite	  
envenomation	   is	   animal-­‐derived,	   serum-­‐based	   antivenom.	   This	   antivenom	   is	   expensive,	  
unspecific,	  and,	  upon	  administration,	  often	  the	  cause	  of	  severe	  adverse	  reactions	  due	  to	  the	  
heterologous	  origin	  of	  the	  antibodies	  present	  in	  the	  antivenom.	  For	  these	  reasons,	  there	  is	  
an	   impending	   need	   to	   bring	   antivenom	   treatment	   into	   the	   modern	   area	   of	  
biopharmaceuticals	  by	  creating	  safer,	  more	  efficacious,	  and	  cheaper	  antivenoms	  based	  on	  
biotechnological	  approaches.	  	  
In	  this	  thesis,	  toxins	  from	  the	  venom	  of	  the	  olive	  sea	  snake,	  Aipysurus	  laevis,	  were	  selected	  
as	   targets	   for	   antibody/drug	   discovery	   using	   phage	   display	   selection.	   A.	   laevis	   is	   a	   highly	  
neurotoxic	  and	  medically	  relevant	  sea	  snake,	  which	  venom	  consists	  of	  toxins	  from	  the	  three-­‐
finger	   toxin	   and	   phospholipase	   A2	   family;	   the	   two	   most	   abundant	   toxin	   families	   in	   the	  
Elapidae	  snake	  family.	  Using	  phage	  display	  technology,	  novel	  single	  chain	  variable	  fragment	  
(scFv)	  antibodies	  against	  phospholipase	  A2	  were	  discovered	  and	  evaluated.	  Binding	  affinity	  
to	   venoms	   from	   other	   snake	   species	   was	   further	   determined	   by	   cross-­‐reactivity	   studies.	  
Further	  characterization	  of	  inhibition	  properties	  of	  these	  novel	  scFv	  antibodies	  is	  pending,	  
and	   it	   is	   hope	   that	   these	   antibodies	   can	   one	   day	   be	   used	   in	   a	   modern	   recombinant	  
antivenom	  to	  treat	  A.	  laevis	  snakebite	  victims.

RESUME 
	  
Slangebid	  er	  en	  af	  verdens	  globalt	  negligerede	  sundhedsudfordringer	  med	  hundredtusindvis	  
af	  dødsfald	  og	  amputationer	  årligt	  verden	  over	  –	  særligt	  i	  fattige	  og	  fjerne	  dele	  af	  Afrika	  og	  
Sydøst	  Asien.	  Den	  eneste	  nuværende	  behandling	  mod	  slangebid	  er	  modgifte	  som	  er	  lavet	  
gennem	  immunisering	  af	  dyr.	  Disse	  modgifte	  er	  dyre,	  uspecifikke	  og	  ofte	  skyld	  i	  alvorlige	  
bivirkninger	  i	  forbindelse	  med	  behandling	  pga.	  den	  heterologe	  natur	  af	  de	  antistoffer,	  der	  er	  
i	  modgiften.	  Der	  eksisterer	  derfor	  et	  presserende	  behov	  for	  at	  udvikle	  moderne	  modgifte	  
mod	  slangebid,	  som	  er	  sikrere,	  mere	  effektive	  og	  billigere.	  Dette	  kan	  opnåes	  ved	  brugen	  af	  
moderne	  bioteknologiske	  metoder.	  
I	   denne	   opgave	   er	   toksinerne	   fra	   den	   olivengrønne	   havslange	   (Aipysurus	   laevis)	   blevet	  
udvalgt	  som	  lægemiddel	  targets	  til	  brug	  i	  fag	  display	  selektion	  mhp.	  at	  finde	  antistoffer	  mod	  
toksinerne.	   A.	   laevis	   er	   en	   medicinsk	   relevant	   havslange,	   hvis	   neurotoksiske	   gift	   primært	  
består	  at	  toksiner	  fra	  tre-­‐finger-­‐toksinfamilien	  og	  phospholipase	  A2-­‐	  familien,	  som	  er	  de	  to	  
største	  toksinfamilier	  i	  elapider.	  Ved	  brug	  af	  fag	  display	  teknologi	  er	  nye	  single	  chain	  variable	  
fragments	  (scFv)	  antistoffer	  mod	  phospholipase	  A2-­‐toksinerne	  blevet	  opdaget	  og	  evalueret.	  
Derudover	   er	   bindingsaffiniteten	   til	   gifte	   fra	   andre	   slangearter	   karakteriseret	   vha.	  
krydsreaktivitetsstudier.	   Yderligere	   karakterisering	   af	   inhiberingspotentialet	   for	   disse	   nye	  
antistoffer	   er	   i	   vente,	   og	   det	   er	   håbet,	   at	   disse	   antistoffer	   vil	   blive	   brugt	   i	   en	   moderne	  
rekombinant	  modgift	  til	  at	  behandle	  slangebidsofre,	  der	  er	  blevet	  bidt	  af	  A.	  laevis.

2 INTRODUCTION 
	  
Snake	  envenomation	  is	  considered	  one	  of	  the	  world’s	  most	  neglected	  tropical	  diseases	  by	  
the	   World	   Health	   Organization	   (WHO	   2010).	   It	   is	   estimated	   that	   between	   1.2-­‐5.5	   million	  
people	  suffer	  from	  snakebite	  envenoming,	  resulting	  in	  25,000-­‐125,000	  deaths	  and	  almost	  
400,000	  amputations	  around	  the	  world	  (Williams	  et	  al.	  2010;	  Kasturiratne	  et	  al.	  2008).	  The	  
majority	   of	   these	   victims	   are	   from	   rural	   and	   poor	   areas	   of	   Sub-­‐Saharan	   Africa	   and	  
South/Southeast	   Asia,	   who	   have	   with	   limited	   access	   to	   proper	   medical	   institutions,	   as	  
illustrated	  in	  figure	  1	  (Gutiérrez	  et	  al.	  2006;	  Kasturiratne	  et	  al.	  2008).	  For	  this	  reason,	  many	  
envenomations	  are	  simply	  not	  recorded.	  Despite	  the	  severe	  clinical	  implications	  and	  concern	  
for	   public	   health,	   it	   is	   an	   area	   highly	   neglected	   by	   international	   players	   such	   as	  
pharmaceutical	   companies,	   health	   organizations,	   and	   funding	   agencies	   (Gutiérrez	   et	   al.	  
2006;	  Williams	  et	  al.	  2010).	  
	  
	  
Figure	  1:	  Global	  occurrence	  of	  snakebites,	  amputations,	  and	  mortalities	  (Adapted	  from	  Gutiérrez	  et	  al.	  2006).	  
	  
2.1 CURRENT ANTIVENOM TREATMENT 
The	   only	   current	   treatment	   for	   snake	   envenomation	   is	   antivenom,	   produced	   through	  
immunization	  of	  large	  mammals,	  which	  upon	  administration	  can	  cause	  adverse	  side	  effects,	  
such	  as	  severe	  allergic	  reactions.	  Since	  its	  invention	  in	  1896	  (Calmette	  1896),	  this	  process

2.2 PROJECT AIM 
The	  aim	  of	  this	  project	  was	  to	  discover	  novel	  single	  chain	  variable	  fragment	  (scFv)	  antibodies	  
against	  toxins	  from	  a	  medically	  relevant	  snake	  species.	  Phage	  display	  techniques	  were	  used	  
to	  screen	  the	  Human	  Single	  Fold	  scFv	  Libraries	  I	  and	  J	  (Tomlinson	  I	  and	  J	  libraries)	  for	  novel	  
scFv	   phage	   binders.	   Binding	   affinity	   were	   evaluated	   using	   Enzyme-­‐Linked-­‐Immunosorbent	  
Assay	   (ELISA).	   Monoclonal	   scFv	   phages	   exhibiting	   the	   highest	   binding	   affinity	   were	  
sequenced	   and	   further	   characterized	   using	   competition	   assays,	   modeling,	   and	   cross-­‐
reactivity	  studies.

3 THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
	  
The	  most	  severe	  snakebite	  envenomings	  are	  caused	  by	  species	  of	  the	  Viperidae	  and	  Elapidae	  
families	  (Gutiérrez	  et	  al.	  2006).	  Species	  of	  the	  Viperidae	  family	  include	  rattlesnakes,	  lance	  
headed	  pit	  vipers	  and	  true	  vipers	  whose	  venom	  is	  often	  found	  to	  be	  cytotoxic,	  hemotoxic,	  
and	  in	  certain	  cases	  myotoxic,	  resulting	  in	  clinical	  manifestations	  such	  as	  swelling,	  blistering,	  
hemorrhage,	  and	  necrosis	  (Gutiérrez	  et	  al.	  2006).	  On	  the	  other	  hand,	  venom	  of	  species	  from	  
the	  Elapidae	  family	  (such	  as	  cobras,	  mambas,	  kraits,	  and	  sea	  snakes)	  is	  found	  to	  be	  mainly	  
neurotoxic,	   resulting	   in	   paralysis	   and,	   in	   severe	   cases,	   respiratory	   arrest	   (Gutiérrez	   et	   al.	  
2006).	  	  
In	  order	  to	  determine	  which	  snake	  venom	  or	  toxin	  to	  target	  for	  this	  thesis,	  it	  was	  relevant	  to	  
look	  at	  the	  neutralization	  potential	  of	  a	  novel	  recombinant	  antibody.	  In	  general,	  the	  human	  
immune	   system	   uses	   antibodies	   to	   neutralize	   toxins	   by	   binding	   the	   toxin	   rapidly	   and	  
inhibiting	   further	   effects	   (Murphy	   2012).	   Due	   to	   the	   high	   potency	   of	   snake	   venoms,	   it	   is	  
extremely	  important	  for	  the	  antibodies	  to	  diffuse	  into	  tissues	  and	  bind	  the	  toxin	  rapidly	  to	  
prevent	  severe	  damage	  (Gutiérrez	  et	  al.	  2006;	  Williams	  et	  al.	  2010).	  For	  this	  reason,	  viperid	  
venoms	  are	  generally	  more	  therapeutically	  difficult	  to	  target,	  as	  antitoxins	  must	  diffuse	  into	  
the	  affected	  tissues	  to	  reach	  the	  locally	  acting	  toxins	  present	  in	  viper	  venoms,	  which	  are	  the	  
medically	   relevant	   ones.	   On	   the	   other	   hand,	   systemically	   acting	   toxins	   from	   the	   Elapidae	  
family,	  need	  to	  be	  neutralized	  before	  they	  reach	  their	  target,	  which	  is	  after	  they	  have	  been	  
distributed	   by	   the	   circulatory	   system	   (Laustsen	   et	   al.	   2016).	   Furthermore,	   due	   to	   the	  
administration	  methods	  of	  snake	  antivenom	  –	  in	  which	  antitoxins	  are	  injected	  intravenously	  
–	   it	   would	   thus	   be	   more	   efficient	   to	   neutralize	   toxins	   in	   the	   circulatory	   system,	   thus	  
preventing	  the	  toxins	  from	  reaching	  their	  targets.	  Additionally,	  to	  show	  the	  possibilities	  of	  
recombinant	  antivenom,	  a	  proof-­‐of-­‐concept	  study	  would	  have	  to	  be	  carried	  out	  targeting	  a	  
medically	  relevant	  snake,	  but	  with	  a	  simple	  venom	  composition	  and	  a	  more	  direct	  route	  to	  
efficient	  antibody	  development.	  Later	  studies,	  focusing	  on	  neutralizing	  viperid	  venom,	  could	  
then	  ideally	  extrapolate	  essential	  knowledge	  from	  these	  findings.	  Therefore,	  the	  first	  focus	  
of	  this	  thesis	  was	  to	  determine	  which	  snake	  species	  from	  the	  Elapidae	  family	  to	  target,	  by	  
looking	  into	  the	  toxins	  of	  highest	  pharmacological	  relevance.

3.1 TOXINS FROM ELAPIDS OF MEDICAL RELEVANCE  
Snake	  venom	  is	  a	  complex	  mixture	  of	  pharmacologically	  acting	  nucleotides,	  proteins,	  and	  
peptides,	  exhibiting	  enzymatic	  and	  non-­‐enzymatic	  properties	  (McCleary	  &	  Kini	  2013).	  The	  
distinct	  protein	  types	  serve	  as	  means	  of	  acquiring	  and	  digesting	  prey,	  along	  with	  defense	  
against	  predators.	  Snake	  venom	  contains	  a	  wide	  array	  of	  bioactive	  proteins,	  known	  as	  toxins,

serving	  diverse	  functions,	  and	  the	  toxins	  can	  thus	  be	  grouped	  into	  different	  toxin	  families,

3.1.1 SNAKE THREE-FINGER TOXIN FAMILY  
Snake	   toxins	   of	   the	   three-­‐finger	   toxin	   (3FTx)	   family	   consist	   of	   a	   group	   of	   non-­‐enzymatic	  
polypeptides	  of	  60-­‐74	  amino	  acid	  residues	  (Dufton	  &	  Hider	  1988;	  Kini	  &	  Doley	  2010),	  and	  are	  
found	  in	  the	  venom	  of	  elapids	  and	  colubrids	  and	  reported	  in	  the	  transcriptomes	  of	  viperids	  
and	   crotalids	   (Kini	   &	   Doley	   2010).	   The	   scaffold	   is	   quite	   conserved	   between	   species,	   with	  
three	  beta-­‐stranded	  loops	  extending	  from	  a	  small	  hydrophobic	  core,	  which	  is	  cross-­‐linked	  by	  
four	   conserved	   disulphide	   bridges,	   mimicking	   three	   outstretched	   fingers	   from	   which	   the	  
family	   name	   is	   derived	   as	   seen	   in	   figure	   3	   A	   (Kini	   &	   Doley	   2010).	   Despite	   the	   conserved	  
structure,	  this	  family	  of	  toxins	  exhibits	  a	  diverse	  range	  of	  biological	  properties	  as	  seen	  in	  
table	   2.	   Alpha-­‐neurotoxins,	   for	   example,	   bind	   to	   muscle	   nicotinic	   acetylcholine	   receptors	  
(nAChRs)	   with	   high	   affinity,	   thus	   impairing	   neuromuscular	   transmission	   at	   the	   peripheral	  
nervous	   system.	   Other	   than	   antagonizing	   muscle	   nAChRs,	   biological	   activities	   of	   3FTxs	  
include	   recognition	   of	   neuronal	   nicotinic	   receptors,	   recognition	   of	   distinct	   types	   of	  
muscarinic	  receptors,	  inhibition	  of	  acetylcholinesterase,	  blocking	  of	  L-­‐type	  calcium	  channels	  
among	  many	  others,	  as	  detailed	  in	  table	  2	  (Kini	  &	  Doley	  2010;	  Tsetlin	  1999;	  Blanchet	  et	  al.	  
2014).

3.1.2 PHOSPHOLIPASE A2  
The	  Phospholipase	  A2	  (PLA2)	  family	  of	  snake	  toxins	  is	  found	  in	  abundance	  in	  both	  viperids	  
and	   elapids	   and	   has	   evolved	   from	   PLA2	   digestive	   enzymes	   (Davidson	   &	   Dennis	   1990);	   an	  
example	  the	  three	  dimensional	  structure	  is	  seen	  in	  figure	  3	  B.	  The	  catalytic	  function	  of	  PLA2	  
is	  to	  cleave	  phospholipids	  at	  the	  sn-­‐2	  position	  in	  the	  glycerol	  backbone	  and	  plays	  a	  pivotal	  
role	   in	   various	   biological	   activities.	   PLA2s	   are	   found	   in	   mammals	   and	   play	   key	   roles	   in	  
fertilization,	  cell	  proliferation,	  signal	  transduction,	  and	  membrane	  homeostasis	  (Kini	  2003).	  
In	   snake	   venom,	   however,	   PLA2	   not	   only	   plays	   a	   role	   in	   the	   digestion	   of	   prey,	   but	   also	  
exhibits	  a	  variety	  of	  pharmacological	  effects,	  with	  one	  PLA2	  isoform	  being	  the	  most	  toxic	  and

venom	  are	  known	  to	  be	  both	  basic	  and	  acidic.	  Despite	  the	  fact	  that	  PLA2	  isoforms	  share	  40-­‐

3.2  AIPYSURUS LAEVIS  
Aipysurus	  laevis	  (Olive-­‐brown	  sea	  snake,	  figure	  3)	  is	  an	  elapid	  found	  in	  the	  oceans	  of	  the	  
Indo-­‐Pacific	  region,	  figure	  3.	  A.	  laevis	  is	  the	  largest	  member	  of	  its	  genus,	  averaging	  around	  1	  
meter	   in	   length;	   however,	   specimens	   have	   been	   recorded	   to	   exceed	   two	   meters	  
(Gopalakrishnakone	   1994;	   Cogger	   1975).	   It	   typically	   resides	   in	   shallow	   marine	   habitats,	  
primarily	  coral	  reefs,	  but	  is	  also	  found	  in	  sandy,	  rocky,	  and	  mud	  bottoms,	  where	  it	  preys	  
from	  crevices	  in	  the	  ocean	  floor	  hunting	  different	  species	  of	  fish	  but	  also	  feeding	  on	  fish	  eggs	  
crabs	  and	  fish	  (McCosker	  1975;	  Voris	  &	  Voris	  1983).	  Being	  a	  very	  docile	  species,	  it	  is	  typically	  
only	  curious	  if	  approached	  by	  a	  human	  diver.	  However,	  during	  mating	  season,	  attacks	  on	  
humans	  are	  more	  prevalent,	  as	  the	  sea	  snake	  exhibits	  more	  aggressive	  behavior	  (Heatwole	  
1975;	   Gopalakrishnakone	   1994).	   Furthermore,	   A.	   laevis	   is	   often	   caught	   by	   commercial	  
trawlers	  or	  recreational	  fishermen	  handling	  nets	  as	  a	  by-­‐catch,	  making	  these	  more	  frequent	  
bite	  victims.	  A.	  laevis	  is	  extremely	  venomous,	  with	  a	  median	  lethal	  dose	  (LD50)	  of	  0.15	  mg/kg	  
(Laustsen	  et	  al.	  2015a).

3.3.1 PHAGE DISPLAY SELECTION TECHNIQUE 
Phage	  display	  is	  a	  selection	  technique	  developed	  by	  George	  Smith	  in	  1985,	  where	  peptides	  
are	  displayed	  on	  the	  surface	  of	  filamentous	  phages	  encoded	  by	  genetic	  information	  retained	  
in	  the	  genome	  of	  the	  phage	  (Smith	  1985).	  Phage	  display	  has	  been	  used	  extensively	  to	  study	  
protein-­‐protein,	   protein-­‐peptide	   or	   protein-­‐DNA	   interactions	   between	   the	   proteins	  
expressed	   on	   the	   surface	   of	   the	   phage	   and	   a	   molecule	   of	   interest	   (Smith	   1985;	   Smith	   &	  
Petrenko	  1997).	  	  
	  
Figure	   7:	   Schematic	   overview	   of	   biopanning	   steps	   involved	   in	   phage	   display	   selection.	   Phages	   are	   panned	  
against	  an	  immobilized	  antigen	  and	  specific	  clones	  are	  captured	  by	  binding	  to	  antigen	  (1).	  Well	  or	  immunotube	  
is	   washed	   to	   remove	   any	   unspecific	   binders	   (2).	   Then	   phages	   are	   eluted	   using	   trypsin	   or	   acid	   shift	   (3)	   and	  
promising	  clones	  are	  subsequently	  amplified	  in	  E.	  coli	  (4).	  This	  process	  is	  repeated	  multiple	  times	  and,	  finally,	  
polyclonal	  phage	  binders	  are	  analyzed	  using	  ELISA	  (Holt	  et	  al.	  2000).	  
	  
Phage	  display	  selection	  is	  carried	  out	  through	  cycles	  of	  biopanning	  (figure	  6).	  First	  a	  given	  
antigen	  is	  immobilized	  on	  a	  specific	  surface,	  such	  as	  immunotubes,	  microtiter	  plate	  wells,	  
BIAcore	   sensor	   chips	   or	   columns	   (Knappik	   et	   al.	   2000;	   McWhirter	   et	   al.	   2006;	   Lee	   et	   al.	  
2007).	  Then,	  phages	  expressing	  proteins	  or	  peptides	  are	  panned	  against	  the	  antigen,	  and	  
during	  incubation	  some	  of	  the	  phages	  bind	  to	  the	  given	  antigen	  target.	  After	  a	  given	  period	  
==PAGE:19==
	  
	  
19	  
of	  time,	  unspecific	  binding	  phages	  are	  washed	  away,	  leaving	  only	  high	  affinity	  binders,	  which	  
then	   are	   eluted	   by	   shifting	   the	   pH	   or	   adding	   a	   digestive	   enzyme	   (e.g.	   trypsin).	   The	   high	  
affinity	  binding	  phages	  may	  then	  be	  amplified	  in	  E.	  coli,	  and	  repeated	  rounds	  of	  panning	  
increase	   the	   amount	   of	   phages	   binding	   with	   high	   affinity/specificity	   to	   the	   antigen	   of	  
interest.	   After	   multiple	   cycles,	   monoclones	   are	   isolated	   and	   analyzed	   using	   phage	   titer	  
counts	  and	  Enzyme	  linked	  immunoabsorbent	  assay	  (ELISA),	  illustrated	  in	  figure	  7.	  The	  single	  
stranded	  DNA	  (ssDNA)	  can	  then	  be	  extracted	  from	  the	  phages	  for	  sequencing	  and	  further	  
characterization.	  One	  of	  the	  few	  drawbacks	  of	  using	  phage	  display	  selection	  is	  the	  possibility	  
of	   obtaining	   false	   positives,	   i.e.	   when	   the	   peptide	   expressed	   by	   the	   phage	   binds	   to	   the	  
surface	  material	  rather	  than	  the	  antigen.	  Thus,	  it	  is	  necessary	  to	  screen	  for	  false	  positives	  
and	  consider	  including	  one	  or	  more	  deselection	  steps	  in	  the	  process.	  
	  
Figure	  8:	  ELISA	  used	  in	  phage	  display	  technology	  to	  measure	  binding	  of	  of	  phages.	  First	  M13	  phage	  binds	  to	  
target	  antigen	  immobilized	  on	  a	  surface.	  Then	  anti-­‐M13	  antibody	  conjugated	  to	  horseradish	  peroxidase	  (HRP)	  
binds	  to	  the	  M13	  phage.	  Finally	  the	  substrate	  Ortho-­‐Phenylenediamine	  (OPD)	  is	  added,	  and	  in	  wells	  where	  
anti-­‐M13-­‐HRP	  is	  bound	  to	  the	  phage,	  the	  HRP	  cleaves	  the	  ORP	  resulting	  in	  a	  color	  shift	  that	  can	  be	  measured	  
using	  spectrophotometer.	  Both	  polyclonal	  and	  monoclonal.	  ELISAs	  can	  be	  carried	  out	  depending	  on	  whether	  it	  
is	  a	  mix	  of	  phages	  expressing	  scFvs	  or	  a	  single	  type	  of	  phage	  expressing	  scFvs.	  
	  
3.3.2 FILAMENTOUS M13 BACTERIOPHAGE 
One	  of	  the	  most	  common	  types	  of	  phages	  used	  for	  library	  construction	  is	  the	  filamentous	  
M13	   bacteriophage	   (figure	   8),	   which	   contains	   single	   stranded	   DNA	   encapsulated	   by	   coat	  
proteins	  (Smith	  1985).	  The	  majority	  of	  the	  virion	  consists	  of	  the	  coat	  protein	  pVIII,	  with	  each	  
end	  being	  capped	  with	  different	  minor	  coat	  proteins:	  in	  one	  end	  five	  copies	  each	  of	  pVII	  and	  
pIx	  and	  the	  other	  five	  copies	  each	  of	  pIII	  and	  pVI	  (Kehoe	  &	  Kay	  2005).	  The	  M13	  lifecycle	  
begins	  with	  infection	  of	  E.	  coli	  with	  pIII	  attaching	  to	  the	  pilus	  of	  the	  bacteria.	  The	  pilus	  then	  
==PAGE:20==
	  
	  
20	  
retracts,	  bringing	  the	  phage	  closer	  to	  the	  surface	  of	  the	  bacteria,	  so	  the	  single	  stranded	  DNA	  
(ssDNA)	   can	   enter	   the	   bacterial	   cell.	   This	   ssDNA	   is	   converted	   into	   double	   stranded	   DNA	  
(dsDNA)	   by	   Escherichia	   coli	   proteins	   and	   amplified	   by	   rolling	   circle	   amplification	   by	   viral	  
encoded	   proteins,	   resulting	   in	   production	   of	   phage	   DNA.	   Phage	   proteins	   then	   promote	  
assembly	  and	  secretion	  of	  viral	  particles	  from	  the	  cells,	  without	  killing	  them	  (Kehoe	  &	  Kay	  
2005).	  All	  of	  the	  five	  capsid	  proteins	  have	  been	  utilized	  for	  display	  purposes;	  however,	  the	  
most	   common	   approach	   being	   fusion	   of	   protein/peptides/antibodies	   with	   the	   terminal	  
phage	  gene	  pIII	  coat	  protein	  (Lee	  et	  al.	  2007;	  Kehoe	  &	  Kay	  2005).	  
	  
	  
Figure	  9:	  Schematic	  representation	  of	  the	  filamentous	  M13	  bacteriophage	  and	  the	  five	  different	  coat	  proteins	  
encapsulating	  the	  bacteriophage.	  
	  
Two	  strategies	  may	  be	  utilized	  to	  achieve	  this	  fusion,	  either	  the	  phage	  format	  or	  phagemid	  
format.	  In	  the	  phage	  format,	  all	  pIII	  are	  expressed	  as	  fusions	  resulting	  in	  the	  expression	  of	  all	  
five	  pIII	  proteins	  presenting	  the	  desired	  protein/peptide	  phage	  surface	  (Kehoe	  &	  Kay	  2005).	  
In	   the	   phagemid	   format,	   a	   plasmid	   –	   known	   as	   a	   phagemid	   –	   containing	   an	   antibiotic	  
resistance	  marker,	  bacterial	  and	  phage	  origins	  of	  replication,	  a	  phage-­‐packing	  signal,	  and	  the	  
phage	   gene	   from	   the	   library	   to	   be	   displayed,	   is	   used	   to	   transform	   bacteria.	   Using	   this	  
approach,	  the	  library	  is	  cloned	  into	  the	  phagemid,	  so	  it	  is	  expressed	  with	  the	  coat	  protein.	  
When	   using	   the	   phagemid	   format,	   a	   helper	   phage	   is	   required	   to	   assemble	   a	   functional	  
phage.	  The	  helper	  phage	  is	  typically	  a	  packaging-­‐defective	  phage,	  which	  is	  able	  to	  initiate	  the	  
synthesis	  of	  all	  the	  wild	  type	  phage	  proteins.	  Since	  the	  phagemid	  DNA	  carries	  the	  packing	  
signal	  and	  promoter	  for	  fusion	  proteins,	  these	  will	  be	  added	  to	  the	  cellular	  pool	  of	  phage	  
proteins,	   so	   that	   the	   secreted	   phage	   particles	   will	   display,	   and	   thus	   contain,	   the	   foreign	  
sequence	  on	  the	  coat	  protein	  (Kehoe	  &	  Kay	  2005;	  Lee	  et	  al.	  2007).	  One	  of	  the	  advantages	  of	  
==PAGE:21==
	  
	  
21	  
using	  the	  phagemid	  format	  is	  that	  production	  and	  maintenance	  of	  the	  library	  is	  maintained	  
by	  the	  bacteria,	  which	  replicate	  their	  DNA	  much	  more	  accurately	  than	  bacteriophages,	  thus	  
preserving	   the	   diversity	   of	   the	   libraries	   (Kehoe	   &	   Kay	   2005).	   Furthermore,	   using	   the	  
phagemid	  format,	  the	  majority	  of	  copies	  are	  derived	  from	  helper	  phage,	  with	  only	  a	  small	  
portion	  originating	  from	  the	  phagemid,	  consequently	  leading	  to	  monovalent	  display.	  
3.3.3 PHAGE DISPLAY LIBRARIES 
A	  variety	  of	  phage	  display	  libraries	  may	  be	  utilized	  to	  discover	  high	  affinity	  binders	  to	  a	  given	  
target	   of	   interest,	   such	   as	   synthetic	   peptide	   libraries	   (of	   varying	   length	   and	   structure),	  
antibody	  libraries	  derived	  from	  immunized	  animals,	  or	  donor	  libraries	  from	  immunized	  and	  
non-­‐immunized	  individuals	  (Lee	  et	  al.	  2007;	  Ahmad	  et	  al.	  2012).	  	  
	  
Figure	  10:	  Structure	  of	  an	  immunoglobulin	  IgG	  consisting	  of	  heavy	  and	  light	  chain	  constant	  regions	  (blue)	  and	  
variable	   regions	   (red);	   the	   latter	   of	   which	   contain	   the	   antigen	   binding	   site,	   more	   specifically	   the	   three	  
complementarity	  determining	  regions	  (CDRs)	  on	  each	  chain	  (L1,	  L2,	  and	  L3	  and	  H1,	  H2,	  and	  H3	  on	  light	  and	  
heavy	  chain,	  respectively)	  (Murphy	  2012).	  
	  
Phage	   antibody	   libraries	   can	   be	   used	   to	   produce	   a	   variety	   of	   recombinant	   monoclonal	  
antibodies	   (mAbs).	   These	   mAbs	   are	   often	   smaller	   fragments	   of	   antibodies	   consisting	   of	  
different	  formats	  of	  heavy	  and/or	  light	  chain	  constant	  and/or	  variable	  regions,	  as	  illustrated	  
in	  figure	  9	  (Murphy	  2012).	  These	  fragments	  include	  short-­‐chain	  variable	  fragments	  (scFv),	  
fragment	   antigen	   binding	   antibodies	   (Fab),	   and	   single	   domain	   antibodies	   (sdAb).	   The	  
==PAGE:22==
	  
	  
22	  
common	   motif	   for	   all	   of	   these	   fragments	   is	   that	   they	   contain	   the	   variable	   heavy	   and/or	  
variable	  light	  regions	  in	  which	  the	  complementarity	  determining	  regions	  (CDRs)	  are	  found	  in	  
figure	  10.	  These	  CDRs	  consist	  of	  unique	  amino	  acid	  sequences	  that	  bind	  with	  high	  affinity	  to	  
a	  given	  antigen.	  Additionally,	  due	  to	  the	  smaller	  size	  of	  the	  fragments,	  the	  pharmacokinetic	  
and	  pharmacodynamic	  properties	  appear	  to	  be	  improved	  (Zhao	  et	  al.	  2016).	  Particularly	  the	  
scFv,	   consisting	   of	   variable	   domains	   of	   heavy	   and	   light	   chain	   held	   together	   by	   a	   flexible	  
linker,	   has	   been	   found	   to	   have	   increased	   diffusion	   rates,	   tissue	   penetrability,	   and	   bio-­‐
distribution	  along	  with	  decreased	  immunogenicity	  (Wang	  &	  Duan	  2011).	  	  
	  
	  
Figure	  11:	  Various	  formats	  of	  recombinant	  monoclonal	  antibodies;	  Fab	  (fragment	  antigen	  binding),	  scFv	  (single-­‐
chain	  variable	  fragment),	  VL	  (Variable	  light	  chain),	  VH	  (variable	  heavy	  chain)	  CH1-­‐3	  (constant	  heavy	  chain	  domains	  
1-­‐3),	  and	  CL	  (constant	  light	  chain).	  Heavy	  and	  light	  chains	  of	  Fab	  antibodies	  and	  heavy	  chains	  of	  heavy	  chain	  
antibody	  are	  held	  together	  by	  disulfide	  bonds,	  while	  the	  heavy	  and	  light	  chains	  of	  the	  scFv	  antibody	  are	  fused	  
together	  by	  a	  flexible	  Glycine-­‐serine	  linker.	  
	  
Phage	  antibody	  libraries	  have	  been	  constructed	  from	  variable	  domains	  of	  B	  cell	  antibody	  
genes	  isolated	  from	  immunized	  animals	  such	  as	  mice,	  camel,	  sheep,	  and	  human	  (Ahmad	  et	  
al.	  2012;	  Zhao	  et	  al.	  2016).	  After	  exposure	  to	  the	  desired	  antigen,	  in	  vivo	  affinity	  maturation	  
by	  the	  host’s	  immune	  system	  will	  enrich	  the	  antibodies	  towards	  the	  given	  antigen.	  Another	  
type	   of	   antibody	   library	   is	   the	   human	   donor	   library	   that	   is	   created	   from	   non-­‐immunized	  
individuals.	  These	  libraries	  are	  based	  on	  a	  source	  of	  VH	  or	  VL	  segments	  from	  rearranged	  V-­‐
gene	   repertoires	   of	   healthy	   donors	   using	   B-­‐cells	   isolated	   from	   peripheral	   blood,	   bone	  
marrow,	  and/or	  lymphatic	  tissue	  (Zhao	  et	  al.	  2016).	  Since	  these	  immunized	  individuals	  have	  
not	   been	   introduced	   to	   the	   given	   antigen,	   their	   antibodies	   are	   not	   necessarily	   biased	  
towards	   a	   specific	   antigen,	   and	   thus	   a	   large	   library	   size	   (109-­‐1010	   phages)	   is	   necessary	   to	  
obtain	  antibodies	  with	  high	  affinity	  (Zhao	  et	  al.	  2016).	  The	  major	  advantage	  of	  using	  donor	  
==PAGE:23==
	  
	  
23	  
libraries	  is	  to	  avoid	  the	  immunogenic	  responses	  associated	  with	  using	  antibodies	  originating	  
from	  animals,	  as	  human	  proteins	  do	  not	  stimulate	  an	  immune	  response.	  	  
In	  this	  thesis,	  the	  Tomlinson	  I	  +	  J	  	  Human	  scFv	  libraries	  were	  used	  for	  phage	  display	  selection.	  
The	  Tomlinson	  I	  +	  J	  libraries	  are	  naïve	  libraries	  comprised	  of	  synthetic	  VH	  and	  VL	  phagemids	  
derived	   from	   non-­‐immunized	   human	   donors	   (Tomlinson	   et	   al.	   1992).	   These	   phagemid	  
libraries	  encode	  a	  single	  chain	  F	  variable	  (scFv)	  fused	  with	  the	  gIII	  gene	  of	  a	  library	  vector	  
contained	  in	  the	  M13	  phage.	  The	  scFv	  consist	  of	  a	  single	  polypeptide	  with	  the	  VH	  and	  VL	  
chains	  joined	  by	  a	  flexible	  Glycine	  and	  serine	  linker.	  The	  VH	  and	  VL	  framework	  sequences	  
have	  been	  diversified	  in	  vitro	  by	  randomizing	  key	  amino	  acids	  in	  the	  antigen-­‐binding	  CDRs.	  
These	   key	   amino	   acids,	   which	   have	   been	   identified	   from	   structural	   studies	   as	   being	  
important	  for	  antigen	  binding,	  were	  in	  the	  heavy	  chain	  positions;	  H50,	  H52,	  H52,	  H53,	  H55,	  
H56,	  H58,	  H95,	  H96,	  H97,	  and	  H98	  and	  in	  the	  light	  chain	  positions;	  L50,	  L53,	  L91,	  L92,	  L93,	  
L94	  and	  L96.	  The	  nominated	  codons	  in	  library	  I	  were	  altered	  to	  DVT-­‐format	  (A,	  G	  or	  T;	  A,	  C	  or	  
G;	  T)	  and	  in	  library	  J,	  NNK-­‐format	  (any	  base;	  any	  base;	  G	  or	  T)	  (Holt	  et	  al.	  2000).	  Each	  library	  
contains	  an	  estimated	  108	  different	  scFvs	  and	  studies	  have	  previously	  successfully	  extracted	  
antibodies	   against	   a	   diverse	   range	   of	   targets,	   thus	   proving	   to	   be	   a	   valuable	   resource	   in	  
antibody	  discovery	  (Holt	  et	  al.	  2000).

4 EXPERIMENTAL STRATEGY 
The	   experimental	   strategy	   of	   this	   thesis	   is	   depicted	   in	   figure	   11	   and	   described	   in	   the	  
following.	  Upon	  selection	  of	  the	  pharmacologically	  relevant	  snake	  species	  A.	  laevis,	  venom	  
was	  extracted	  (1).	  The	  individual	  toxins	  were	  then	  separated	  using	  RP-­‐HPLC	  and	  manually	  
separated	  into	  fractions	  (2).	  The	  medically	  relevant	  toxins	  from	  this	  separation	  were	  used	  as	  
antigens	  in	  phage	  display	  selection	  using	  Tomlinson	  I	  +	  J	  libraries	  to	  screen	  for	  high	  affinity	  
scFv	  antibody	  binders	  (3).	  These	  were	  evaluated	  using	  titration	  calculations	  of	  phage	  counts	  
along	   with	   ELISA	   using	   the	   target	   antigen	   and	   relevant	   controls,	   to	   find	   the	   strongest	  
binders.	  From	  the	  best	  polyclonal	  ELISAs,	  monoclonal	  phages	  were	  selected	  and	  assessed

5.2 METHODS 
5.2.1 VENOM SEPARATION 
The	  venom	  pooled	  from	  A.	  laevis	  specimens	  was	  fractionated	  by	  sequential	  RP-­‐HPLC	  under	  
conditions	   described	   previously	   (Lomonte,	   Tsai,	   et	   al.	   2014;	   Lomonte,	   Pla,	   et	   al.	   2014).	  
Venom	  (10	  mg)	  was	  dissolved	  in	  1	  ml	  of	  water	  containing	  0.1%	  trifluoroacetic	  acid	  (TFA)	  and	  
separated	   using	   RP-­‐HPLC	   (Agilent	   1200)	   on	   a	   C18-­‐column	   (4.6	   x	   250	   mm	   column,	   5	   µm	  
particle	  diameter;	  Teknokroma).	  Elution	  was	  carried	  out	  at	  1ml/min	  by	  applying	  a	  gradient	  
towards	  solution	  B	  (acetonitrile,	  containing	  0.1	  %	  TFA):	  0%	  B	  for	  5	  min,	  0-­‐15%	  B	  over	  10	  min,	  
15-­‐45%	  B	  over	  60	  min,	  45-­‐70%	  B	  over	  10	  min,	  and	  70%	  B	  over	  9	  min,	  as	  previously	  described	  
==PAGE:27==
	  
	  
27	  
(Lomonte,	  2014)	  Fractions	  of	  proteins	  were	  monitored	  at	  215	  nm	  and	  collected	  manually	  
before	   being	   frozen	   and	   lyophilized.	   	   Both	   fractions	   1	   and	   3	   were	   used	   for	   further	  
investigations.	  	  
5.2.2 PROTEIN MASS IDENTIFICATION BY TANDEM MASS SPECTROMETRY 
Fraction	   1	   was	   further	   analyzed	   by	   ESI-­‐MS/MS	   on	   a	   Q-­‐Trap®	   3200	   instrument	   (Applied	  
Biosystems).	   From	   this	   samples,	   10	   µl	   were	   loaded	   into	   metal-­‐coated	   capillary	   tips	  
(Proxeon).	   These	   were	   directly	   infused	   into	   a	   nano	   ESI-­‐source	   operated	   at	   1300V.	   The	  
generated	   ions	   were	   fragmented	   by	   collision-­‐induced	   dissociation	   using	   the	   Enhanced	  
Product	  Ion	  tool	  with	  Q0	  trapping.	  The	  settings	  for	  the	  MS/MS	  analyses	  analyses	  were:	  Q1,	  
unit	  resolution;	  collision	  energy,	  25–45	  eV;	  linear	  ion	  trap	  Q3	  fill	  time,	  250	  ms;	  and	  Q3	  scan	  
rate,	   1000	   amu/s.	   The	   CID	   spectras	   gained	   were	   manually	   interpreted.	   To	   determine	   the	  
isotope-­‐averaged	   mass	   of	   intact	   proteins	   in	   selected	   peaks	   from	   the	   RP-­‐HPLC	   separation,	  
nESI-­‐MS	   was	   also	   used.	   This	   reason	   being	   mass	   spectra	   were	   acquired	   in	   Enhanced	  
Multicharge	  mode	  in	  m/z	  nESI-­‐MS	  range	  700-­‐1700.	  Deconvolution	  was	  performed	  with	  the	  
aid	  of	  the	  Analyst®	  v.	  1.5.	  software	  (ABSciex).	  
5.2.3 BIOTINYLATION OF TOXINS

based	  on	  manufacturer’s	  instructions.	  This	  calculated	  amount	  of	  1.5	  &	  20	  mmol	  NHS-­‐PEG4-­‐
Biotin,	   respectively,	   was	   added	   to	   the	   toxin	   and	   incubated	   at	   room	   temperature	   for	   30	  
minutes.	   Non-­‐reacted	   NHS-­‐PEG4-­‐Biotin	   was	   removed	   using	   gel	   filtration	   kit	   (Sartorius)	  
according	  to	  manufacturer’s	  instructions.	  Here,	  100	  µl	  biotinylated	  toxin	  was	  mixed	  with	  400

more	  centrifuged	  at	  14,000	  x	  g	  for	  30	  min.	  This	  was	  repeated	  four	  more	  times	  with	  400	  µl

5.2.4 PHAGE DISPLAY SELECTION 
Four	  selection	  rounds	  using	  Tomlinson	  I	  +	  J	  libraries,	  consisting	  of	  coating,	  panning,	  titration	  
of	  phage,	  addition	  of	  helper	  phage,	  and	  harvesting	  of	  phage	  were	  carried	  out	  as	  described	  in	  
the	  following.	  
MaxiSorpTM	   plates	   (NUNC,	   Roskilde,	   Denmark)	   were	   coated	   with	   100	   µl	   of	   toxin	   (Alpha-­‐
neurotoxin,	  Phospholipase	  A2	  (PLA2)),	  A.	  laevis	  crude	  venom,	  or	  streptavidin	  (Sigma-­‐Aldrich)

were	   left	   with	   PBS.	   The	   following	   day,	   wells	   coated	   with	   toxin	   and	   venom	   were	   washed	  
three	  times	  with	  PBS,	  filled	  to	  the	  brim	  with	  2%	  milk	  powder	  (Fluka	  analytical)	  dissolved	  in	  
PBS	  (2%	  M-­‐PBS),	  and	  incubated	  at	  room	  temperature	  for	  two	  hours	  to	  block.	  Streptavidin-­‐
coated	  wells	  were	  blocked	  for	  one	  hour	  with	  2%	  M-­‐PBS	  and	  then	  100	  µl	  biotinylated	  toxin	  (1	  
µg)	  dissolved	  in	  2%	  M-­‐PBS	  was	  added	  and	  left	  for	  one	  hour	  to	  bind.	  After	  the	  respective	  
incubation	  time,	  all	  wells	  were	  washed	  three	  times	  with	  PBS	  and	  1013	  phages	  of	  library	  I	  and	  
J	  respectively	  were	  added	  and	  incubated	  for	  60	  minutes	  at	  room	  temperature	  with	  shaking	  
(250	  rpm)	  followed	  by	  60	  minutes	  at	  room	  temperature	  without	  shaking.	  Supernatant	  was	  
discarded	  and	  the	  plates	  were	  washed	  ten	  times	  with	  PBS	  containing	  0.1%	  Tween	  20	  (PBST,	  
Sigma-­‐Aldrich).	  Phages	  were	  eluted	  by	  adding	  100	  µl	  per	  well	  of	  trypsin-­‐PBS	  (diluted	  1:5	  in	  
PBS,	   Sigma-­‐Aldrich).	   Supernatant	   was	   transferred	   to	   a	   1.5	   ml	   microcentrifuge	   tube	  
containing	  600	  µl	  of	  E.	  coli	  TG1	  grown	  in	  2xTY	  to	  an	  OD600	  =	  0.4	  and	  was	  incubated	  at	  37°C

No	   binding	   affinity	   towards	   HSA	   was	   observed	   among	   any	   of	   the	   clones	   tested.	   HSA	   can	  
function	  as	  a	  versatile	  carrier	  protein	  in	  the	  human	  body	  and	  thus	  a	  relevant	  target	  to	  test

the	  specificity	  of	  the	  PLA2	  binders	  by	  not	  being	  promiscuous	  to	  this	  protein.	  	  
An	   interesting	   observation	   was	   made	   when	   comparing	   the	   binding	   affinity	   to	   the	   target	  
antigen	  (PLA2)	  and	  the	  size	  of	  E.	  coli	  colonies	  containing	  the	  scFv	  expressing	  phage.	  In	  the	  
first	   screening	   round,	   colonies	   with	   larger	   colony	   morphology	   were	   mainly	   picked,	   while	  
smaller	  colonies	  were	  mainly	  picked	  in	  the	  second	  screening	  round.	  This	  can	  be	  explained	  by	  
the	  fact	  that	  phage	  production	  is	  proportional	  to	  colony	  size;	  in	   essence,	  colonies	  with	   a	  
growth	  advantage	  will	  also	  produce	  more	  phages	  allowing	  the	  clone	  to	  dominate	  the	  pool	  
over	   time,	   despite	   lower	   affinity	   to	   the	   target	   antigen	   (Umlauf	   et	   al.	   2015).	   One	   of	   the	  
explanations	  for	  higher	  growth	  rates	  of	  low	  affinity	  binders	  could	  be	  that	  these	  phages	  might	  
contain	  truncated	  versions	  of	  the	  antibody	  fragment.	  Furthermore,	  it	  has	  previously	  been	  
seen	   that	   phages	   containing	   these	   truncated	   inserts	   are	   likely	   to	   emerge	   through	  
amplification	   cycles	   and	   dominate	   the	   output	   (de	   Bruin	   et	   al.	   1999).	   Expression	   of	  
recombinant	   antibodies	   by	   bacteria	   can	   in	   certain	   cases	   be	   toxic,	   and	   for	   this	   reason,	  
selective	  pressure	  can	  foster	  progressive	  deletion	  of	  sequence	  from	  scFvs	  during	  selection	  
and	  amplification	  (de	  Bruin	  et	  al.	  1999).	  A	  larger	  metabolic	  load	  would	  also	  be	  associated	  
with	  the	  expression	  of	  a	  full	  antibody	  fragment	  rather	  than	  a	  truncated	  version	  (de	  Bruin	  et	  
al.	  1999).	  	  	  This	  would	  in	  turn	  enable	  the	  E.	  coli	  colonies	  expressing	  truncated	  scFvs	  to	  have	  
higher	  growth	  rates.	  This	  difference	  emphasizes	  the	  importance	  of	  selecting	  colonies	  with	  
different	  colony	  morphology	  in	  phage	  display	  selections.	  Nevertheless,	  promising	  phagemids	  
were	  selected	  and	  further	  characterization	  of	  the	  sequence	  followed.

To	  attempt	  to	  produce	  soluble	  scFvs	  the	  E.	  coli	  strain	  HB2151	  was	  used	  to	  express	  soluble

stop	  in	  the	  CDR2	  sequence	  of	  the	  heavy	  chain	  and	  since	  HB2151	  is	  a	  non-­‐repressor	  it	  would	  
naturally	   truncate	   the	   sequence	   at	   this	   position,	   and	   thus	   no	   soluble	   scFv	   would	   be	  
produced.

It	   is	   interesting	   to	   understand	   why	   a	   stop	   codon	   even	   would	   arise	   into	   the	   CDRs	   of	   the	  
antibody,	   and	   to	   do	   this	   it	   is	   necessary	   to	   look	   into	   how	   the	   Tomlinson	   library	   J	   was	  
constructed.	  The	  semi	  synthetic	  libraries	  were	  both	  constructed	  by	  diversifying	  amino	  acids	  
in	  CDRs	  known	  for	  being	  important	  for	  interaction	  with	  antigen.	  To	  achieve	  this,	  selected

truncated	  scFv	  when	  expressed	  by	  HB2151	  (Holt	  et	  al.	  2000).	  Unfortunately,	  no	  complete

Barderas	  et	  al.	  (2006)	  came	  up	  with	  an	  alternative	  mutagenesis	  strategy	  to	  overcome	  this	  
truncation	   (Barderas	   et	   al.	   2006).	   In	   fact,	   in	   previous	   studies,	   further	   purification	   and	  
characterizations	   of	   antibodies	   was	   discontinued	   despite	   promising	   high	   affinity	   binders	  
(Wang	   et	   al.	   2015;	   Boulter-­‐Bitzer	   et	   al.	   2009;	   Yan	   et	   al.	   2004).	   Nevertheless,	   studies	   by	  
Barderas	  et	  al.	  2006,	  described	  a	  method	  to	  recover	  soluble	  and	  functional	  scFvs	  containing	  
amber	  codons,	  in	  which	  a	  PCR-­‐based	  mutagenesis	  was	  used	  to	  alter	  the	  stop	  codon	  TAG	  to	  a	  
TAA	  (Barderas	  et	  al.	  2006).	  Using	  this	  method,	  the	  stop	  codon	  contained	  between	  the	  scFv	  
and	  pIII	  is	  mutated	  into	  a	  TAA,	  thus	  the	  non-­‐repressor	  strain	  TG1	  could	  be	  used	  to	  produce	  
soluble	  scFvs,	  which	  in	  turn	  can	  be	  used	  for	  affinity	  determination	  and	  functional	  validation	  
(Barderas	  et	  al.	  2006).	  
Since	   soluble	   production	   was	   not	   applicable	   in	   this	   thesis,	   competition	   assays	   and	   cross-­‐
reactivity	  studies	  between	  other	  snake	  species	  were	  carried	  out	  using	  the	  scFv	  expressing

6.5 PERSPECTIVES FOR RECOMBINANT ANTIVENOMS  
The	   only	   current	   treatment	   for	   snake	   envenomation	   is	   still	   animal-­‐derived	   antivenom,	  
despite	  being	  a	  technique	  invented	  in	  the	  18th	  century	  (Williams	  et	  al.	  2010).	  With	  the	  high	  
risk	   of	   adverse	   effects	   from	   antivenom	   treatment	   due	   to	   the	   heterologous	   origin	   of	  
antivenom	   proteins,	   lack	   of	   specificity	   for	   the	   antivenom	   antibodies,	   batch-­‐to-­‐batch	  
variations,	  and	  high	  price	  for	  the	  current	  treatment,	  a	  need	  for	  a	  new	  strategy	  to	  create	  
safer	  and	  more	  specific	  toxin	  inhibitory	  compounds	  is	  vital	  (Laustsen	  et	  al.	  2016).	  An	  area	  of

rapid	   development	   and	   success	   within	   recent	   years	   is	   the	   utilization	   of	   phage	   display

7 CONCLUSION & PERSPECTIVES  
	  
Snakebite	  envenomation	  is	  a	  globally	  neglected	  health	  issue	  causing	  hundreds	  of	  thousands	  
of	   mortalities	   and	   amputations	   to	   victims	   in	   poor	   and	   rural	   parts	   of	   the	   world.	   The	   only	  
current	  treatment	  is	  based	  on	  animal-­‐derived	  antivenom,	  which	  is	  associated	  with	  a	  risk	  of	  
adverse	  reactions	  due	  to	  the	  heterologous	  origin	  of	  the	  proteins	  present	  in	  animal	  serum.	  
In	  the	  research	  behind	  this	  thesis,	  novel	  scFv	  binders	  against	  the	  medically	  relevant	  PLA2s	  
from	  the	  venom	  of	  A.	  laevis	  were	  discovered	  by	  phage	  display	  selection	  and	  characterized	  
using	  competition	  assays	  and	  homology	  modeling.	  Cross-­‐reactivity	  studies	  for	  these	  scFvs	  
was	  further	  performed,	  showing	  weak	  interactions	  between	  novel	  scFvs	  and	  the	  venom	  of	  
other	  snake	  species.	  This	  is	  the	  first	  time	  the	  naïve	  Tomlinson	  I	  +	  J	  libraries	  have	  been	  used	  
to	  discover	  scFvs	  capable	  of	  binding	  to	  a	  snake	  venom	  toxins,	  and	  further,	  the	  first	  time	  a	  
human	  scFv	  has	  been	  discovered	  towards	  a	  toxin	  from	  the	  PLA2	  family.	  
Thus,	  this	  thesis	  lays	  the	  foundation	  for	  a	  proof-­‐of-­‐concept	  of	  the	  possibility	  of	  employing	  
recombinant	   antibodies	   in	   snakebite	   envenoming	   therapy.	   In	   combination	   with	   an	   NTx

immunized	  library	  –	  this	  could	  be	  used	  to	  create	  a	  next	  generation	  recombinant	  antivenom.	  
Further	  research	  is	  necessary	  to	  characterize	  and	  optimize	  this	  novel	  human	  scFv.	  This	  thesis	  
has	  been	  a	  small	  step	  in	  the	  right	  direction,	  providing	  valuable	  insight	  into	  the	  possibilities	  of	  
recombinant	  antivenoms.	  Thus,	  hopefully	  in	  the	  future,	  snakebite	  envenoming	  therapies	  will
