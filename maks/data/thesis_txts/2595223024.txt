Abstract
3D Ken Burns eﬀect is an image processing that mimics real camera motion to create a video from a
static picture. To perform this eﬀect, one must generate a 3D scene from the initial image and then
hallucinate the disoccluded parts of the scene, induced by the camera motion. Therefore, we propose
improved techniques for depth estimation and image inpainting.
Depth estimation often suﬀers from object inconsistency: very diﬀerent depth values are estimated for
one object. To address this, we propose an unsupervised mask loss function that encourages the network
to produce depth maps that are smooth on the objects detected by a trained segmentation network.
Ground truth depth is not required for the computation and any image dataset can be used to do so,
which add variety to the training.
Supervised disocclusion inpainting requires RGB-D ground truth from multiple views and such datasets
are rarely made available. We introduce a supervised pre-training alongside with an unsupervised re-
ﬁnement method that only need the RGB-D from a single view.
This method uses an adversarial
discriminator based on perceptual features and a multi-scale architecure. This achieved state-of-the-art
results for color and depth inpainting. We also showcase that our reﬁnement technique is compatible
with already trained networks and that it does improve their performances.

Introduction
The Ken Burns eﬀect is a photographic eﬀect which consists into the combination of tracking shots on
a ﬁxed picture. A tracking shot is a kind of camera movement during the recording. It can be lateral to
the scene, following the motion of a character for instance. Or it could be orthogonal, in that case the
camera moves either backward or forward to the scene and creates a realistic zoom as if the spectator
was getting farther or closer. It is widely used in order to embed still images in videos: this eﬀect creates
an illusion of a movement that integrates better in a video. It is commonly used in documentaries but
also by television news when they are lacking video footage to illustrate their words. This technique was
named by the American ﬁlmmaker Ken Burns who has made an extensive use of this eﬀect. Of course,
this technique is rather easy to use but the resulting motion illusion is limited and cannot fool the
human eye. When a tracking shot is made on an actual scene, objects and background are not moving
at same speed in the ﬁeld of view. More precisely, the background of the scene seems to be ﬁxed, but
the objects in the ﬁeld of view move faster as they are closer to the camera. This is mainly because
closer objects have a bigger disparity (or parallax) than farther objects. The disparity is a measure of
how much an object moves in the ﬁeld of view when the point of view changes. When performing a
tracking shot on a still image, the objects do not move separately from the background as they all lie
on the same plane. The 3D Ken Burns is an extension of the Ken Burns eﬀect that tries to address
this limitation. The idea is to artiﬁcially create a parallax motion, i.e. diﬀerent movements for diﬀerent
objects based on their distance to the camera, from a single image. It is possible to do it manually using
photo editing software but this requires some time and dedicated skills. First, the diﬀerent objects of
the picture must be separated, then their depths must be estimated in order to determine how much
the objects should move when the camera moves. Finally, the displacement of the objects may lead to
disocclusion, e.g. some parts of the background become visible once the camera has moved. Those
areas must then be ﬁlled with content. However, Niklaus et al. [1] have made signiﬁcant progresses to
provide a fully automatic 3D Ken Burns eﬀect generator using deep learning.
In this thesis, we will mainly try to reproduce the results from Niklaus et al. [1] and improve some
of their methods. The process of creating the 3D Ken Burns eﬀect (KBE) is divided into two parts,
so is their work: depth estimation and disocclusion inpainting. We will as well address both of these
problems. Beyond the artistic eﬀect created by the 3D KBE, the methods developed to achieve it have
many applications.
First, depth estimation consists in generating a depth map from an image of a scene. A scene being
a 3D representation of the real world. For each pixel of the picture, a depth value is given by the
algorithm, i.e. an estimation of the distance between the camera and the corresponding object in the
real world. Self-driving cars need for instance to know accurately how far objects are in order to drive
safely and avoid collisions. Therefore, depth estimation is a crucial component to develop self-driving
methods. Plenty of work concerning depth estimation have been done in this perspective. One of the
most used dataset for depth estimation is the KITTI dataset, introduced by Geiger et al. [2]. Since
then, lots of work have been based and tested on this dataset. It contains stereo videos as well as depth
maps of traﬃc scenes as everything was recorded from a driving car. The completeness of the dataset
led to great variety of methods developed for depth estimation: monocular supervised estimation by
Eigen et al. [3], Fu et al. [4], Liebel and K¨orner [5], self-supervised estimation Godard et al. [6], Pillai
et al. [7] and unsupervised from stereo or videos cues Zhou et al. [8], Mahjourian et al. [9], Casser et al.
[10]. Then, depth estimation task is quite similar to 3D reconstruction. And methods developed for this
purpose could as well be used for 3D scanning such as Sinha et al. [11] have done. Also, robotics could
beneﬁt a lot from better depth estimation as spatial awareness is crucial to achieve some basic tasks
as moving, grasping objects or reacting to events. Although deep learning led to huge improvements in

depth estimation, some earlier works have also shown their eﬀectiveness. This is the case in Torralba
and Oliva [12] where authors used the image structure to infer information about depth and in Saxena
et al. [13] in which markov-random ﬁeld are trained to predict depth maps.
Secondly, image inpainting has been as well a proliﬁc ﬁeld in the past few years, especially with the rise
of deep learning. Recent methods make use of generative models based on neural networks in order
to learn how to inpaint images such as in Xie et al. [14] or Yeh et al. [15]. Recent approaches have
made signiﬁcant progress using slightly modiﬁed convolutional layers in Liu et al. [16] or adversarial

deep learning and plenty of works have been done without learning-based methods. In 2000, Bertalmio
et al. [19] set the grounds for image inpainting, and rapidly many improvements were proposed, such
as in Richard and Chang [20], Bertalmio et al. [21], Chan and Shen [22], making inpainting faster,
texture-aware and more reliable. Inpainting is mostly used for photo editing purpose. This ranges from
old pictures restoration to text or entire objects removal. These tasks are usually time consuming and
require people with solid knowledge in picture editing to be done. Besides the aesthetics and the gain in
time that inpainting can provide, it can also be used for more serious applications. In medical imagery,
some artifacts can appear in CT-scan, MRI or any other type of imagery. It might sometimes make
diagnosis harder or even wrong. In such cases, inpainting could ﬁx these artifacts, as it is done in Zhu
et al. [23], Armanious et al. [24].
In this work we will shortly present the underlying theory behind the Ken Burns eﬀect and the techniques
used to achieve it. The next chapter will introduce some existing works about depth estimation and
image inpainting that helped us during our study. It will in particular present the methods from Niklaus
et al. [1], which were the starting point for this thesis. The fourth part will explain our methods and the
extensions we made to prior works. The ﬁfth will expose our results. It will detail the experiments that
we conducted in order to test the methods developed in chapter 4. Then, the sixth chapter will discuss
the results and propose some future work in order to improve our methods further. Finally, the seventh
chapter will showcase the quality of our results through the application on the 3D Ken Burns eﬀect.

2
Theory
This chapter presents the theoretical aspects of the 3D Ken Burns Eﬀect. As mentioned in the introduc-
tion, this eﬀect requires two main components to be performed: depth estimation and image inpainting.
Each of them will be reviewed in detail, by introducing some concepts and deﬁnitions. This chapter will
also give some background knowledge about deep learning, which will be extensively used throughout
our study.
2.1
The Ken Burns eﬀect
In this section, we will give some deﬁnitions, related to the parametrization of the KBE. In the original
KBE, two cropping windows are chosen. The ﬁrst one will be the starting frame and the second the
ending frame of the resulting video (see Figure 2.1). The in-between frames are obtained by moving the
camera between the two endpoints. The aspect ratio of the crops should be the same but their sizes
can diﬀer (all the frames are resized to the same size in order to produce the resulting video). When the
starting crop is larger than the ending, this results in a zoom-in and inversely a smaller starting crop gives
a zoom-out. For the 3D KBE, it is transposed as a forward or backward camera motion, respectively. In
either case, the KBE can be parametrized by just giving the parameters about the starting and ending
crops. Hence, only the position of the centers of the initial and ﬁnal crops and their dimensions (width
and height) are required. We will call the coordinates of the centers ustart, vstart and uend, vend. These
coordinates are computed from the top left corner of the input image. The same way, hstart, wstart and
hend, wend represent the height and width of the starting and ending frames, respectively. The amount
of zoom or forward camera movement can then be retrieved by taking the ratio hstart
hend . Note that as the
aspect ratio is ﬁxed, this is equivalent to use the ratio of widths instead.
That is all the required parametrization to perform the 2D or 3D KBE. From this and the original
picture it is possible to perform it. Of course the 3D one will require more work as the 3D scene must
be estimated and then inpainted. These two tasks are, as shown in the next sections, not trivial at all
and require sophisticated methods to be done correctly.

2.2
Depth estimation
2.2.1
Problem statement
The goal of depth estimation is to ﬁnd the depth associated to each pixel of an image. More precisely,
how far the object represented by each pixel is from the camera. We can represent images and depth
maps as 2-dimensional functions:
I : [[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]] →[0, 1]3
(u, v) 7→I(u, v)
D: [[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]] →[0, +∞[
(u, v) 7→D(u, v)
Here, H and W represent the height and the width of the image, respectively. And [[0, H]] indicates the
integer set 0, 1, ..., H. Hence solving the depth estimation problem is equivalent to ﬁnding a mapping
between I and D, i.e. a function ΦD such as:
ΦD : F([[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]], [0, 1]3) →F([[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]], [0, +∞[)
I 7→D
Where F(A, B) is the set of functions that map elements from set A onto elements from set B.
It is hard though to ﬁnd a suitable mapping ΦD that correctly maps an image to its depth map. The
ﬁrst thing to note is that the search space for these mappings is huge as the images we are dealing
with are of a consequent size (the typical size used for training was 756 × 1024 pixels). It is possible to
count the number of diﬀerent possible functions ΦD. But to do so we ﬁrst need to count the number
of diﬀerent images and depth maps. We will do two approximations for this:
- pixel values for image are most of the time stored as 8-bit integers in our computers. That way,
there is, for a 3-channel (RGB) image, (28)3 ≈107 possible values for each pixel.
- even though depth is a distance and can range from 0 to ∞, it is not possible to store easily
arbitrary big values in memory. Therefore we will assume that depth is an integer between 0 and
216 −1 = 65536 ≈105. We chose this value according to the data type of the depth maps in
usual depth datasets, that is depth values are stored as 16-bit integers.
Now, we can give an estimate of the size of the images and depths sets as the number of mappings
from [[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]] to [[0, 255]]3 ([[0, 65535]] for depths):
|F([[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]], [[0, 255]]3)| ≈(107)1024×756 ≈10107
|F([[0, H −1]] × [[0, W −1]], [[0, 65535]])| ≈(105)1024×756 ≈10106
Finally, the number of possible mappings ΦD can be computed as the number of mappings between
two ﬁnite sets A and B: |F(A, B)| = |B||A|. For any input image, the mapping could return any of the
possible depth maps. Hence, there is 10106 possibilities for each image. Therefore, there are 1010610107
possibilities for choosing ΦD. This number is incredibly large. It is largely larger than the estimated
number of atoms in the universe: 1080 or the number classiﬁcation mappings for imagenet: 103×10107
(the source set has roughly the same size but the target set contains 1000 elements, one for each class).
It is therefore quite hard to ﬁnd a suitable mapping for depth estimation. Of course, multiple mappings
can be satisfactory. From a single picture it is not possible to get the exact depth as the sizes of the

Such problem is called an inverse problem, some missing data must be reconstructed from incomplete
information. Those types of problems are often hard to solve. First, because it is ill-posed: many
acceptable solutions can exist. Second, because most of the time, the solution space is huge as we have
shown above.
2.2.2
Geometric deﬁnitions
In order to get a complete overview of the problem, we will deﬁne some notations and notions that
will be used in the rest of our study. First of all, let’s deﬁne the coordinate system that we will use.
A picture is a 2-dimensional representation of a 3D scene, therefore the space we are looking at is R3
and any point of the scene can be denoted as (x, y, z) where z is the depth between the camera and
the point. In this setup, we consider a pinhole camera model, i.e. the camera is considered as a point.
This is a reasonable approximation as the dimensions of a typical camera sensor (around 1cm) are small
compared to the distance between the camera and the objects of the scene (typically between 1m to
80m, see section 3.3). For the sake of simplicity, we will consider that the camera’s position is at the
coordinates (0, 0, 0). Now, when the scene is captured by the camera, light emitted by the objects lands
on the focal plane (or image plane) of the camera. A point on this plane will be known as (u, v) and will
match with the pixel of the image (see Figure 2.3). Therefore, it is possible to go from one coordinate
system to the other with a simple transformation:

Numerous methods do not work directly with depth but with a closely related measure: disparity.
Disparity is a measure of how far an object moves in the ﬁeld of view of the camera when the point
of view is slightly changed. If an object is located at (u, v) in the image before the motion and at

with a small experiment. Position your index ﬁngers in front of you, pointing to the ceiling. One should
be close to your head and the other one should be farther away and at the same height. Now, close
one eye and move your head from left to right. The closest ﬁnger appears to move more than the other
with respect to the background. This represents disparity and an object has a bigger disparity when it
is close to the camera. This suggest that disparity is inversely proportional to depth. That is indeed the
case and the actual relation is given by the following equation.

this work, we will therefore work only with disparities, however we will refer to it as depths or disparities

The second problem that we will tackle in our work is the inpainting. When performing the 3D KBE, it

2.4
Deep learning theory
Since our methods are mainly based on Deep Learning, it is essential to provide a quick introduction to
this ﬁeld. In this section, we will try to give the uninformed reader a basic understanding of what deep
learning is and how it has been developed over the years.
2.4.1
From the perceptron to the feed-forward neural network
Deep learning is a subsection of Machine learning that has been able to achieve human-level performances
on plenty of tasks during the last decade. It outperforms humans at image recognition Krizhevsky et al.
[25], He et al. [26], medical diagnosis Rakhlin et al. [27], video games Mnih et al. [28], Silver et al.
[29, 30], language processing Collobert et al. [31], Hinton et al. [32], Radford et al. [33] and many
more. This ﬁeld was mainly developed during the 1980s but some pioneer works date back to the 1950s
with the introduction of the perceptron by Rosenblatt (1958) [34]. A perceptron is a linear classiﬁer
that was designed in order to mimic -at a smaller scale- the human brain. The idea that machines and
computers would beneﬁt from copying the brain’s mechanisms was introduced in various prior works.
Among them, McCulloch and Pitts (1943) [35] has introduced a mathematical neuron model and studied
their response to stimuli. At the same time Turing A. laid the foundations of artiﬁcial intelligence and
suggested the idea that complex tasks should rather be learned by machines than directly programmed.
The formula is now relatively well known inside the machine learning community: ”Instead of trying to
produce a programme to simulate the adult mind, why not rather try to produce one which simulates
the child’s? If this were then subjected to an appropriate course of education one would obtain the
adult brain.” Turing [36].
These ideas were used by Rosenblatt in his works. A perceptron is a set of neurons arranged in layers
and interconnected (see Figure 2.6). This kind of architecture is present in the brain and one example
is the visual cortex which is organized in multiple areas where visual signals are processed. Each neuron
of a layer is connected to all neurons from the previous layer. Each neuron has an activation level

have abrupt cliﬀs or basins.

2.4.3
Convolutional neural networks
However neural networks were still computationally intensive and quite hard to design. They were once
again put aside. It is only in the early 2010s that the deep learning made a comeback with various
success. In particular, in 2012 a neural network based method won the ImageNet competition, which is
a globally known image recognition challenge. The neural network introduced by Krizhevsky et al. [25]
for this competition outperformed all other competitors with a large margin. This network, now called
AlexNet, is based on a type of layer quite diﬀerent that the one we presented above: the convolutional
layer.
A convolutional layer is composed of a weight matrix like the regular layer (called in fact fully connected
layer) that we deﬁned with the perceptron (see section 2.4.1). The main diﬀerence is that this weight
matrix is the kernel of a convolution operation. Instead of multiplying the input vector by the weight
matrix to get the output vector, the kernel is convoluted on the input to get the output (see Figure
2.9). This operation is way cheaper in term of memory as the number of parameters is decreased a lot
compared to a fully connected layer (the size of the kernel is usually quite small compared to the size
of the input). This kind of layer is particularly well suited to work with images. Images are composed
of thousands of pixel and it is not practicable to use fully connected layers with them. In addition,
convolutional layers can work with any input size which is not the case for fully connected ones. It is
also worth to note that these layers can be extended to work with any given input dimensionality. Up to
now, we discussed input as vectors, in that case, the learnt kernel by the convolutional layers will also
be vectors. For images, the kernels will be matrices, for 3D volumes, it will be 3D tensors and so on.
That is also why convolutional layers work so well with images. Most image features are deﬁned locally,
i.e. in a 2D neighbourhood around a pixel. When an image is processed by a fully connected layer, all
this local information is lost while using a 2D convolution preserves it and propagates it through the
rest of the network.
Figure 2.9: Comparison between a fully connected layer and a convolutional layer
The kernel of a convolutional layer will act as a feature detector. Through learning, it will adapt its
weights to detect some pattern in the image. It could be for example straight lines, dots, corners, etc.
When the kernel is convoluted on such a feature on the image, it will produce a strong response in
the output image, otherwise the response will be weak. The output image will then display where that
speciﬁc feature is in the input image and will be called a feature map. An important thing to note is
that no matter where the feature is in the input image it will be detected by the layer as the kernel is
convoluted across the whole image. Fully connected layers are able to learn such pattern as well but
they are highly dependant on the location of the feature as diﬀerent weights are used for every single
pixel. This property that convolutional layers have over fully connected ones is called weight-sharing
and is the key to translation invariance of the feature detection.

Usually, multiple kernels are learnt by the same layer. Each of them is applied to the input image
and therefore create multiple feature maps. Each feature map detects one feature. When a second
convolution layer is applied, each of its kernels is applied on all the feature maps from the previous layer.
In fact the kernels are no longer 2-dimensional but 3-dimensional. This allows to combine features from
the previous layer. For instance imagine that the ﬁrst layer has kernels that are only able to react to
straight lines with diﬀerent orientations (horizontal, vertical, 45°,...). Then the second layer will be able
to react to more complex patter such as, corners or basic shapes. Finally a third layer could be able to
detect complex form such as face attributes (nose, eyes, mouth, etc.). That would then build a face
detector (see Figure 2.10).

For classiﬁcation purpose, convolutional neural networks are often composed of a few convolutional layers
followed by some fully connected ones (see Figure 2.11). This can be seen as ﬁrst a visual processing
and a feature extraction. Second, based on the extracted features, the classiﬁcation is performed. This
architecture was in particular adopted by AlexNet, mentioned above, that outperformed all method at
the 2012 ImageNet competition.

This success was mainly
supported by the development of faster computers and especially the democratization of graphical

parallelizable. GPU are composed of a great number of cores (i.e. processing unit) that can run at

when these instructions can be executed in parallel, i.e. at the same time, it is way faster to use a GPU.
Deep neural networks require a lot of matrix multiplication, both for the forward and the backward pass.
Fortunately, these operations are highly parallelizable: basically every elements of the resulting matrix
can be computed at the same time, which provide a huge gain in computation time. In addition to
GPU, improvement in memory capacity led to the creation of bigger datasets which is essential for deep

3.1
Depth estimation
3.1.1
Model-based methods
Depth estimation techniques have been developed long before deep learning. We will refer to them as
model-based methods, in contrast to learning-based methods that mostly rely on deep learning. In this
section, we will give a short presentation of these techniques and their functioning.
First of all, it is good to note that most model-based techniques use stereo -or binocular- images in
order to estimate depth maps as it is an easier problem. Monocular depth estimation made impressive
progress with deep learning but was not studied a lot before.
A few works though tried to solve
monocular depth estimation using Markov Random Field (Saxena et al. [13] and Liu et al. [40]) and
more recently with Conditional Random Field (Liu et al. [41]). These methods are performing relatively
well, but the quality of the depth maps produced is lower than with binocular depth estimation. This is
expected as monocular depth estimation is harder than binocular estimation. Of course, these methods
for monocular depth estimation are not properly model-based as Markov Random ﬁelds are learnt, but
are worth to be mentioned.
Now let’s explain the principles of the model-based methods. Naturally, all methods will diﬀer slightly,
but most of them are following a common scheme with following parts (Scharstein and Szeliski [42]):
- Matching cost computation: this step is the construction of a disparity space. For each possible
disparity (e.g. every 10cm from 1m to 10m), a matching cost function is computed between each
pixel of the left image (without loss of generality) and its matched pixel in the right image. This
can be done as it is assumed that the two stereo cameras have the same y coordinates, i.e. the
camera shift is only horizontal. Hence for each disparity, a cost map is created: a high cost means
that it is really unlikely that these pixels are matching with this disparity. A small cost means the
opposite. The cost can be interpreted as the likelihood of the matching between two pixels at a
speciﬁc disparity. Plenty of diﬀerent cost functions can be used such as the absolute diﬀerence in
Kanade et al. [43] or the square diﬀerence in Matthies et al. [44].
- Cost aggregation: once the matching cost has been computed, it is aggregated locally, by
summing or averaging. What locally means change from one method to another but most of the
time it is simply done on a shifting square window.
- Disparity computation: this part may be the one on which methods diﬀer the most. A distinction
need to be done here. Local methods: for each pixel the disparity that gives the smallest cost is
chosen. Global methods: in which the computation of disparity is slightly more complicated. An
energy measure is deﬁned to assess the quality of a depth map. This energy is deﬁned using the

3.1.2
Deep learning based methods
As for many computer vision tasks, deep learning has achieved state-of-art results for depth estimation
problems. In fact, the proportion of papers published about depth estimation and using deep learning
has been increasing quite drastically since 2015, going from almost nothing to 75% of the papers about
depth estimation last year. This can be seen in Figure 3.1. From this ﬁgure, it can also be seen that
the interest for this ﬁeld has only been growing.
Figure 3.1: Evolution of the number of published papers about depth estimation with and without the
keyword Deep learning.
There are two diﬀerent kinds of deep learning methods that have been applied for depth estimation:
supervised learning and unsupervised learning. Supervised learning is used when the target of the task
is known, i.e. here when the depth maps are available. This kind of learning has been described in
section 2.4 about deep learning theory. Unsupervised learning happens when the estimation task is
learnt without the ground truth depth maps. This is obviously harder than supervised learning, but it
could have some beneﬁts as well. It will be discussed further in section 3.1.4.
Supervised techniques
In case of supervised learning, the problem statement can be precised. One now has at his disposal a
set of images and their corresponding disparity maps {xN; dN}. The main principle of supervised depth
estimation is to train a neural network that is able to take an image xN as an input and produce the
depth map dN as output. This will be common to any supervised depth estimation method, however
there are still some degrees of freedom to adjust. Among them are the network architecture and the
loss function design. We will now present some of these that have been used in various works.
U-Net architecture introduced in Ronneberger et al. [45] is a commonly used architecture for image-to-
image translation tasks (for instance day to night translation, satellite image to plan conversion: Isola
et al. [46] or underwater color correction: Fabbri et al. [47]). Depth estimation can be seen as such.

17

This architecture has been extensively used for depth estimation in Chen et al. [48] and He et al. [49].
A U-Net is a special kind of auto-encoder network that uses only convolutional layers. The image is
ﬁrst down-sampled through multiple layers and then up-sampled in order to complete the task. To
preserve the elements from the input image, residual connections are set between same level layers in
the encoding and decoding parts, i.e. the feature maps from the encoding part are concatenated to the
same size feature maps in the decoding part (see Figure 3.2).
Another type of network used for this kind of task is the Res-Net He et al. [26]. A Res-Net is a network
that has residual connections, i.e. an identity connection that skips one or more layers in the network.
Therefore, a U-Net is a special kind of Res-Net, with only a small number of skip connections. In the
original Res-Net paper, there was a residual connection every other layers or every three layers. Each
connection was skipping the same number of layers while in the U-Net the connections are not of the
same size (see Figure 3.2). This architecture has also been used for depth estimation, for instance in Li
and Snavely [50] and Laina et al. [51], with similar results that were achieved with U-Nets.

greater than the one for B. But this penalty will not depend on the actual values of the maps. We will

not make use of it in our analysis. In the same way, a few other objectives can be encountered in the
literature. Some of them are multi-task objectives, i.e. the network is trained not only to predict depth
maps but also to perform some other tasks. Ideally, these tasks are not completely unrelated to depth
estimation and learning them could give the network a deeper understanding of the relation between
objects and therefore produce a more robust learning. Example of this can be found in Eigen and Fergus
[52] where a network is trained to three diﬀerent tasks: depth estimation, surface normal estimation
(i.e. the orientation of the diﬀerent surfaces of the scene) and semantic label predictions. The network
was not trained to perform all these tasks at the same time, but the same architecture and methods
were used to learn these three tasks. In Liebel and K¨orner [5], depth estimation is learned both as a
regression (predicting the exact depth value of each pixel) and as a classiﬁcation problem (predicting
the depth interval in which each pixel lies).
Most of these loss functions are not used on their own. Instead, they are combined linearly to form more
complex objectives. The weights of the diﬀerent components can be tweaked in order to emphasize the
training on diﬀerent aspects. However, there is no consensus on what method is working best or what
should be emphasized more during training.

work a neural network is trained to compute these potentials. The parameters of the CRF are then
learnt through an optimization process on the energy induced by the potentials.
Unsupervised and self-supervised methods
When pairs of image and depth maps are not available, supervised learning is not possible. However it
does not mean that depth estimation is impossible. There are several ways to do it using unsupervised
learning or self-supervised learning.
In fact the diﬀerence between unsupervised and self-supervised
learning is not clear. Unsupervised learning generally refers to learning structure from data without
ground truth information. In comparison, self-supervised learning corresponds to methods that are able
to generate supervision for a task through the supervision of another one. For instance, self-supervised
depth estimation can be achieved via trying to reconstruct the left image from a stereo pair only with the
right one. Depth map is used as a latent variable and is never compared to ground truth depth. However,
the reconstructed left image is compared to the real one and provide supervision for depth. Unsupervised
learning is sometimes used to refer to those techniques even if it is not exactly unsupervised. Though,
for the sake of simplicity, we will not make that distinction and refer to these method as unsupervised
depth estimation. We mentioned binocularity, but unsupervised learning can take advantage of several
cues in order to perform this task. Binocular and video cues are certainly the two most used:
- Binocular cues: some works have recently proposed methods based on binocularity cues in order
to learn depth estimation. For instance Garg et al. [55] trained a neural network in order to produce
a depth map and then used it alongside the binocular image pair to reconstruct the original image.
To be more explicit, the left image is picked, its depth map is estimated and from the map and
the right image, the left image is ﬁnally reconstructed. The quality of that reconstruction highly
depends on the quality of the depth map, which provides a strong supervisory signal. This has
been improved by Godard et al. [56] who provide a similar method for reconstructing both the left
and the right images at the same time.
- Video cues: an other widespread method is to use video cues to estimate depth. Successive frames
in a video are not exactly the same and include small movements between objects (motion) or
relative to the camera (ego-motion, i.e. camera motion). Gordon et al. [57] proposed a method
that is able to learn at the same time the depth but also the camera parameters, the motion
and the ego-motion. This is done using most of the time two neural networks, one for depth
estimation and one for camera pose estimation such as in Zhou et al. [8]. A consistency loss is
introduced so that depth, camera intrinsics and motions are consistent across multiple frames.
Some other works proposed similar methods such as Mahjourian et al. [9] and Casser et al. [10].
Though, the latter one does not use a camera pose estimation network and instead proposes a
method to estimate ego-motion and objects motions in 3D. This motion model works separately
with each object, previously segmented, and estimates the movement of object by tracking the
voxels positions of a particular object in multiple frames.
Of course these two categories cannot describe every unsupervised depth estimation methods. Some
for instance, could be classiﬁed in both categories as they are able to work both with monocular videos

3D Ken Burns Eﬀect
==PAGE:29==
and binocular image pairs. This is the case of Godard et al. [6], who essentially introduced a so-called
reprojection loss that measures how good an image is reconstructed from its stereo pair or from near
frames in a monocular video. It also proposed a masking method to remove pixels that violate the
estimated camera motion.
Afterwards, some works also explore the beneﬁts that adversarial training could yield for such a task. In
Chen et al. [58], a generative adversarial network (GAN) is trained to generate depth maps. A GAN is
a special kind of neural network introduced in Goodfellow et al. [59]. A GAN is composed of two neural
networks: a generator and a discriminator that are trained at the same time.
On the one hand, the generator tries to produce examples from a distribution, e.g. images of cats (for
more examples follow that link). In the original paper, generator is just given random noise in order to
produce examples. But following work from [60] introduced conditional GANs that were able to produce
examples within a certain class. And even further, Isola et al. [46] designed a GAN architecture called
Pix2pix that can be conditioned on image and therefore be used for image-to-image translation tasks.
On the second hand, the discriminator is given images that are either real, i.e.
from the original
distribution, or fake, i.e. generated by the generator. It must then classify each image as real or fake.
Both networks are trained together and should, theoretically, reach an equilibrium, where the generator
has learnt to produce perfect examples from the training distribution. These kind of networks can be
used to produced depth maps: the generator receives a RGB image and is asked to produce a depth map.
The discriminator is then given a RGB image and either its ground truth depth map or the generated
one and must then classify if it is real or fake. This is done in Chen et al. [58]. However, this cannot
be considered as unsupervised learning as the ground truth depth maps are required for training. But
usually, GANs are considered as unsupervised methods and works from [58] have been extended in an
unsupervised manner using binocular and video cues. Aleotti et al. [61] for instance use the generator to
produce depth maps that are used to warp and image to its stereo pair. The discriminator is then given
a pair of stereo images which can contains a fake image. This is highly similar to other unsupervised
methods using binocular cues. The main diﬀerence here is that the reconstruction loss is then learnt
instead of being manually speciﬁed. Indeed, the discriminator in a GAN can be interpreted as a learnable
loss function. Furthermore this loss function is local (according to the ﬁeld of view of the discriminator)
whereas manually deﬁned losses are most of the time pixel-wise. Pilzer et al. [62] went even further,
integrating adversarial training in Godard et al. [56] with cycle GANs. Cycle GANs (Zhu et al. [63])
are the combination of two GANs. One is trained to change images from distribution A (e.g. horses)
into images from distribution B (e.g. zebras). The other is trained for the inverse transformation. This
has been used to produce depths maps from both images of a stereo pair, producing a left and right
depth maps. These depth maps are used to warp images from left to right and the other way around.
During training, warped output from one generator are used as input to the other in order to create
a mutual dependency between the two networks. However some recent works from Groenendijk et al.
[64] have studied the eﬀect of adversarial training for depth estimation and concluded that it does not
always improve performances to add adversarial training. In cases where reconstruction losses are too
constraining (such as SSIM or disparity smoothness loss, see [64] for more detail), the adversarial loss
is not really useful to training and can even be harmful.
Finally, some works make use of other cues such as defocus cues in Gur and Wolf [65] where the authors
used a focus/defocus reconstruction loss as a supervisory signal to learn depth estimation. In a similar
fashion as Garg et al. [55] did with left/right binocularity reconstruction but with focus and defocus.
Creating focused image (i.e. an image where the objects that are in the focus plane are sharp while the
remaining are blurred) from a defocus image (i.e. an image where all objects are sharp) is an ill-posed
problem when depth is unknown. Depth can then be learned as a proxy for the ill-posed task. Therefore
this can be applied to any kind of problem which would beneﬁt from knowing the depth of the scene.

3D Ken Burns paper
Although we presented works on depth estimation from which we drew some inspiration, our study is
mainly based on Niklaus et al. [1]. They were the ﬁrst, to our knowledge, to propose a complete method
for fully automatic 3D Ken Burns eﬀect. Therefore we will expose in this section their methods and
contributions to address depth estimation from single image.
First of all, as the Ken Burns eﬀect is to be performed on a single image, depth estimation must also
be achieved on single image. Even if unsupervised methods are able to achieve this task, they required
binocular (or any other kind) cues during training and are still behind supervised techniques in terms of
performances. Therefore they chose to learn depth estimation in a supervised manner. To do so, they
used two separate neural networks. One for disparity estimation at a small scale and one for up-scaling
and reﬁnement.
Depth estimation
For depth estimation, Niklaus et al. [1] use a GridNet architecture. This was introduced by Fourure
et al. [66]. A GridNet is a generalization of the UNet architecture (see Figure 3.2). When the UNet
down-scales (or up-scales in the decoder part) the image at each step, the GridNet both down-scales
the image and keeps it at the same size. This divides the ﬂow in two at each step creating a grid
layout where each edge is a computing block. Horizontal edges keep the image at the same size while
vertical either down-scale or up-scale it (see Figure 3.3). A GridNet also contains residual connections
that allow to ﬁnd the exact computation path as in a UNet. Each block of the GridNet is composed of
two convolutional layers intertwined with parametric ReLU activation functions. Down-sampling blocks
have one of the convolutional layer with stride 2 resulting in the image size reduction while up-sampling
blocks have a bilinear up-sampling layer before other layers. These modiﬁcations, as well as removing
the batch normalization layers in the blocks, were introduced by Niklaus and Liu [67] and have proven
to perform better for image reconstruction tasks.
They chose a GridNet with 6 rows and 4 columns. This network was then trained using typical loss
functions for disparity estimation. They used a L1 data loss (see equation 3.2) as well as a multiscale
gradient loss (see equation 3.6). Both are then combined as:
Ldepth = Lgrad + 0.0001Ldata
(3.7)
In addition, a pre-trained network (VGG-19, introduced by Simonyan and Zisserman [68]) is used to
extract semantics from the image. This supplementary information about the image is inserted in the
fourth row of the network in order to make the depth estimation semantic-aware. This could provide
information about the diﬀerent objects in the picture, helping to determine their depths.
This depth estimation is not done directly on the full scale image. Instead, the input image is resized
so that its largest dimension is 512 pixels. Therefore an other step is necessary in order to produce a
full scale depth map.
Depth reﬁnement
This will be done by the depth reﬁnement network. However, before reﬁnement the authors introduced
a supplementary step: depth adjustment. The main idea of this step is to ﬁnd objects in the image
and assume that the depth is constant on these objects. Similar ideas were already introduced by Eigen
and Fergus [52], where objects orientation was learnt alongside disparities for adjustment purpose. Here
however, assuming that the depth is constant for every pixel of the object simpliﬁes a lot the adjustment.
It means that the surface’s normal are all colinear to the camera direction and are therefore known. The
only remaining thing to do is to locate objects in the image. To achieve this, they used a pre-trained

3.1.4
Limitations
Even though great progresses have been achieved on depth estimation, there are still some ﬂaws in
this method. First, as it is mentioned in Niklaus et al. [1], learning methods often struggle to estimate
accurately depth for reﬂective surfaces (e.g. car windshield) and thin objects (e.g. ﬂag pole). To our
knowledge, these issues are still largely unsolved even if noted by a wide range of works.
Another issue is the lack of variety in the training data. It is quite hard to construct a dataset for depth
estimation as it requires to get synchronized RGB images and depth maps. Many of such datasets
(see section 3.3) propose a large amount of images from diﬀerent view points but from only a small
variety of scenes. This is the case for the DIML dataset, NYUv2, Megadepth, the computer-generated
3D Ken Burns Eﬀect
23
==PAGE:32==
one from Niklaus et al. [1] and many others. That lack of variety opens the door to scene overﬁtting.
When the trained network is asked to predict depth maps from already seen scenes, it performs well
but for completely new scenes the performances drop a lot. We will show that this is indeed the case
in our discussion (see section 6.1.3). This is why unsupervised/self-supervised learning is promising for
depth estimation. It is possible to use larger datasets with a lot more variety than the RGB-D datasets
required for supervised depth learning. Of course such datasets are still not easy to build because cues
as stereo pairs or videos has to be provided anyway. But this makes the tasks much simpler than depth
collection.

Inpainting
3.2.1
Model based methods
Bertalmio et al. [19] introduced in 2000 one of the ﬁrst method for image inpainting. The main idea
behind their algorithm was to propagate the information from the outside into the area to be inpainted.
To do so, they extend the isophote lines (i.e. lines on which luminosity is constant) that reach the border
of the inpainting area. This propagation is performed using an iterative diﬀusion process, which enforces
the smoothness of the reconstruction. The diﬀusion process deﬁnes a partial diﬀerential equation (PDE)
that is solved iteratively. However this technique only propagates the information at the boundary of
the inpainting region.
Therefore textures will not be propagated.
Plenty of work proposed similar
but improved techniques such as Richard and Chang [20] that basically achieved the same results as in
Bertalmio et al. [19] but with faster computations or Chan and Shen [22] who introduced a new diﬀusion
PDE that takes into account the curvature of the isophotes. Which could provide more accurate or
realistic extensions. These techniques introduce a formalism close to ﬂuid dynamics. Some work cross
the bridge and used method to solve Navier-Stokes equations and applied it to structure propagation
for image inpainting Bertalmio et al. [70]. These techniques are called structural inpainting as they
only propagate the structural information inside the inpainted regions without texture. On the contrary,
some other methods lay emphasis on the texture. It is called texture inpainting and their main idea is
to ﬁnd the best matching texture to ﬁll in. This requires to be able to generate textures from some
exemplar. Either the exemplar is provided by the user (Igehy and Pereira [71]) or found automatically
by the algorithm such as in Efros and Leung [72]. In any case, these methods need then to be able to
synthesize texture that integrates well in the original image. This has been tackled by various works in
the past such as Efros and Leung [72], Wei and Levoy [73] or Ashikhmin [74].
However, most of those techniques present some ﬂaws: structural inpainting is integrated smoothly
in the original image as the structure is continuously propagated but texture is missing.
Textural
inpainting succeeds to reproduce complex texture of the original image but often border artifacts are
visible. Therefore it seems promising to combine the best of both worlds. This has been done for instance
by Bertalmio et al. [21]. In this work the image is decomposed in two components: the geometry and
the texture. Basically, this decomposition assumes that an image is actually the sum of two images. The
ﬁrst one is the image with smooth color (similarly as a ’cartoon’ version of the real image). The second
one contains the texture, which can be seen somehow as noise that is added to the ﬁrst image. This
decomposition was introduced by Vese and Osher [75] and was used in various following works. Since
the texture and the geometry can be dissociated, they can be inpainted separately using the existing
methods that we have described. This was also done by Elad et al. [76], Bugeau and Bertalmio [77].
However, all works do not use this decomposition and Criminisi et al. [78] proposed an unique algorithm
that both propagates structure and texture in a smooth manner inside the inpainted region.

Deep learning based methods
As for plenty of problems, deep learning has been applied to image inpainting during the last decade and
has achieved great results (see Figure 3.5, which shows state-of-the-art inpainting results made with
[17]). For instance, Xie et al. [14] introduced in 2012 an inpainting method using a denoising auto-
encoder, i.e. a neural network that ﬁrst compresses a noisy image before reconstructing it. Basically
the network learns to embed the important image features into a small space (compared to the image
size). From these information, it can then reconstruct the original image without the noise, or without
inpainting holes. This technique has the beneﬁt to work without any information about the area to
inpaint. Recently, it has been shown by Pathak et al. [79] that inpainting requires that the neural
network has a good understanding of the scene, not just visually but also semantically. The context
extractor trained for their inpainting was achieving comparable results as extractor trained for semantics
tasks such as classiﬁcation, detection or segmentation.

produce only the missing part of the original image instead of modifying the whole image. This method
is computationally lighter as the size of the patches to generate is generally quite small compared to the
overall image size. However, the missing parts of the image need to be squares (or rectangles). This is
a prohibitive restriction for our application as our inpainting network should be able to ﬁll disocclusion
holes which are unlikely to be squares. However patch based image inpainting was quite popular during
the 2000s and some recent works still make use of the old principles. Song et al. [83] trained two
separate neural networks to perform their inpainting. The ﬁrst learns to produce a patch completion at
a coarse scaled. Then each small window (typically 3x3 pixels) of the inpainted area is replaced by a
similar patch from the known part of the image. This allows to propagate complex textures from the
original image that the coarse network could not reconstruct. Afterward, the second network reﬁnes
this artwork to produce the ﬁnal inpainting. This is highly similar to the ﬁrst inpainting techniques that
were looking for best matching patch in the known parts of the image.
Some other works try to make use of external information to inpaint images. Dolhansky and Canton Fer-

scene to be inpainted). This method also works with compressed information about the reference image
which can be extracted from an other neural network. In a similar fashion, Yu et al. [81] introduced a
method that is able to work either fully automatically or following a sketch from the user in order to

For sure convolutions are essential for image-related tasks in deep learning, that cannot be refuted.
However, for some applications it seems that regular convolution kernels are not the best choice. For
instance semantic segmentation requires to get information at large scale from the input image. And for
large picture the ﬁeld of view of the network could not be enough. To address this issue, Yu and Koltun
[85] proposed to use a so-called dilated convolution kernel. These kernels are of the same size as for
regular convolutions, but instead of being multiplied to direct neighbour pixels values, it is multiplied
with pixels separated by a dilatation factor (see Figure 3.6). This allows for an exponential growth of
the ﬁeld of view of deep layers of the network while keeping the number of parameters growing linearly.
A few works did use this kind of convolution for image inpainting: Yu et al. [86] and Yu et al. [81] for
instance.

Adversarial inpainting
All the works mentioned above proposed supervised methods to solve the image inpainting problem. This
means that during training they have access to the ground truth images without missing information.
This allows to design the really eﬃcient loss functions that we deﬁned above.
However, for some
applications, ground truth is not available. In this case, the inpainting problem becomes much harder.
This has been only sparsely studied so far. Pajot et al. [87] proposed recently a method based on
conditional GANs in order to address this problem. However, using a regular GAN to learn inpainting is
not directly possible because the discriminator would not have any ground truth data to compare with,
as in an unsupervised setting, only images with holes are available. A workaround for this is to mask
out the inpainted image with a diﬀerent mask than the original image and compare those. Hence both
real and fake images are looking similar: the images are of the same scene but with diﬀerent holes.
But the authors did not stop there and added a second step to their method. Once they have masked
the inpainted image, they inpaint it again except that this time they used instead of a random latent
code, an extracted content code from the original image (it is extracted by a neural network trained
jointly). This code should represent the features visible in the original image and therefore provide
relevant information for the second inpainting. During this second inpainting process the network will
probably have to inpaint areas that were visible in the ﬁrst place. In order to constrain the training
of the content extractor, the authors introduced a latent reconstruction loss. The principle is that the
extracted code after the ﬁrst inpainting should match with the randomly latent code sampled for that
inpainting. This way, the inpainting network is obliged to use this latent code.
To our knowledge, this is the only work addressing the inpainting problem in a fully unsupervised manner.
Though, plenty of recent works use the GAN framework as additional supervision for inpainting. But
this is always in complement to supervised reconstruction losses. For instance, Yu et al. [81] used a fully
convolutional discriminator (PatchGAN) combined with spectral normalization. Spectral normalization
is a weight normalization scheme that has been introduced in the GAN framework by Miyato et al. [88].
This technique aims to enforce the Lipschitz constraint proposed by Arjovsky et al. [89]. It should in
theory stabilize the training of the GAN preventing some recurrent issues with these kind of networks
such as mode collapse (i.e. the generator learns to produce examples with a very small amount of
variability). Some others works use diﬀerent discriminators to assess diﬀerent features of the inpainting.
Yang et al. [82] trained a discriminator for assessing the quality of the inpainted content and one for the
texture. In a similar manner, Nazeri et al. [17] have one for the edge completion and one for the color
ﬁlling. Yu et al. [86] have one local discriminator that operates with small scale patches and a global
one that has a bigger ﬁeld of view. This is analogous to Song et al. [83] that have one discriminator for
the coarse inpainting network and one for the reﬁnement network.
From these various examples, it seems that adversarial training used in complement of typical recon-
struction loss can be very eﬃcient. It can be used in plenty of diﬀerent ways. Therefore, it is worth to
explore this area.
3.2.3
Joint depth and color inpainting
In order to perform the 3D Ken Burns eﬀect, color inpainting is not enough. As new views of the scene
must be generated, the depth must also be inpainted. When the disocclusion masks are ﬁlled with color
for a frame, it is necessary to know at what depth it corresponds. Otherwise, when an other frame is
generated, the algorithm does not know how to move the previously inpainted area compared to other
part of the scene.
In the literature, some works provide methods for depth inpainting. However, it is often only depth
not joint color and depth. This has been done by Zhang and Funkhouser [90]. They used CNN in
order to estimate scene boundaries and surface normal from the color image. Then they were able to
complete depth maps with a regular optimization algorithm. Similarly, Liu et al. [91] proposed a method
for depth completion without using deep learning and achieved competitive results. Nevertheless, some
3D Ken Burns Eﬀect
29
==PAGE:38==
works actually achieved color and depth inpainting. Wang et al. [92] introduced a method that was able
to complete both the depth map and the image from stereo images. This was mainly used for object
removal but it was also applied for disocclusion ﬁlling. The main idea was ﬁrst to ﬁll the depth map and
then use it for better color inpainting. First the image is segmented, then for each connected segment,
a disparity plane is chosen to minimize a cost function. And ﬁnally, the color inpainting is performed
using a model-based approach that takes the completed disparity map into account. Although this was
working well, it still does not ﬁt our problem as it requires stereo pairs of images to work. Finally, Reel
et al. [93] modiﬁed the propagation scheme introduced by Criminisi et al. [78] in order for it to take the
depth information into account. Accurate depth information should help to resolve ambiguity for color
inpainting. The depth ﬁlling is then performed in a patch matching manner. And these two steps are
performed jointly so each of them can beneﬁt from the progress of the other: depth information helps
for color inpainting and vice versa.
Very recently, Shih et al. [94] proposed a method for a joint depth and color inpainting. They mostly
extended a technique that we discussed before (Nazeri et al. [17]). EdgeConnect only inpaints color and
to do so it ﬁrst hallucinates the missing edges of an image. In Shih et al. [94], the authors used the
same technique but introduced another network for depth inpainting, also based on the inpainted edges.
However, this is not the only contribution of this work. It also introduces a speciﬁc data structure that
is extremely relevant for our problem. This data structure is a kind of layered depth image (LDI, [95]).
A LDI can be seen as a fully 4-connected graph where each node is a pixel of the image that we want
to represent. Then each node can hold any number (from 0 to many) of pixel values, i.e. each node
contains a list of RGB-D value. Hence, this allows to represent complex scenes in a very concise way. In
comparison, most papers that we discussed earlier, including Niklaus et al. [1] and our work are based on
a pointcloud representation of the scene, i.e. a set of coordinates (x, y, z) that each represents a point
of the scene, which itself correspond to a pixel (RGB) of the image. Shih et al. [94] modiﬁed the LDI
structure so each pixel directly embeds a pointer to its neighbouring pixels. This allows them to easily
disconnect some pixels. Once the LDI has been initialized, they compute the main discontinuities in the
depth map and disconnect the pixels pairs that lie on the boundaries. This way, they are able to create
a local and depth-aware context region for the inpainting. This is especially important for disocclusion
ﬁlling as the inpainted region is not at all related with the foreground object that was occluding. The
missing regions are therefore inpainted using only information that really matters, i.e. information in
the same depth-connected component of the LDI.
3.2.4
3D Ken Burns paper
We have seen plenty of techniques for color inpainting and some of them do take depth information
into account. However only a few address the problem of joint depth and color inpainting. And in most
cases the inpainting is done separately for depth and color. In Niklaus et al. [1], a method is proposed
for actual joint depth and color inpainting, in which an unique neural network is responsible for both.
It is noteworthy that this technique was designed to complete disocclusion holes, as in Shih et al. [94].
While most inpainting methods work with arbitrary masks and holes, these two are speciﬁcally trained
to ﬁll disocclusion. Therefore it is possible that they would perform poorly for arbitrary holes inpainting.
However, in order to achieve the 3D Ken Burns eﬀect, it is disocclusion holes that needs to be ﬁlled.
In Niklaus et al. [1], the authors generated a synthetic dataset using Unreal Engine. They created a
few 3D scenes and were then able to sample any view from these scenes, including color and depth
information with perfect accuracy. Hence, they were able to train their network in a fully supervised
manner. During training, they ﬁrst sample a RGB-D image from one scene, let’s call it view A. Then,
they randomly move the camera to create a shifted view of the scene (view B). Let’s denote the RGB
image and depth from view A, IA and DA respectively (of course, the same goes for view B with IB
and DB). From, IA, DA and the camera shift S, it is possible to render the image and depth map
from view B with disocclusion holes.

To do so, one must implement a Z-buﬀer. This is a very useful algorithm for 3D scene rendering, ﬁrst
introduced in Straßer [96]. Its principle is quite simple, given a scene, i.e. a set of points (x, y, z) and a
camera (its position and focal length). For each point of the set it ﬁnds the corresponding pixel in the
image plane (see Figure 2.3):
- if a point is already assigned to that pixel: compare the depth of the two points and keep the
closest one.
- if no point is assigned, then assign the point to that pixel.
That way, when two points of the scene are aligned with the camera, only the closest one is visible. The
farthest is then hidden from sight. Thus, when the camera moves, some of these alignments are broken,
making visible some previously hidden points. This is disocclusion. Hence, to render a new view, one
only needs to move the camera in the scene and then applies the Z-buﬀer algorithm.
Moving the camera in the scene is rather easy, given the camera model that we deﬁned in section
2.2.2, moving the camera only corresponds to multiply the camera model with a translation/rotation
matrix. However, as mentioned before, we constrain the movement of the camera to be only translation.
Therefore, there is an even simpler way to move the camera, that is moving the scene itself. It is actually
equivalent to apply the transformation matrix to the camera or to the points of the scene, but when
it is just a translation, one can simply add the shift to the points coordinates instead of multiply by a
translation matrix:
[xB, yB, zB] = [xA + Sx, yA, +SyzA + Sz]
(3.14)
[xA, yA, zA] are the coordinates of the point from view A, and [xB, yB, zB] the coordinates of the same
point but viewed from B. S = [Sx, Sy, Sz].
That is the formalism used in Niklaus et al. [1] and we chose to keep it in our work. For the sake of
simplicity, let’s call the overall view rendering operation χ. Hence, we have:

trained a context extractor alongside to the inpainting network. This context extractor is just a few-
layers deep CNN that extracts features for each point of the image. This context is computed on the
pair (IA, DA): CA = Ω(IA, DA) and should provide relevant information for inpainting. The context

3.3
Datasets
In order to apply such methods it is necessary to have access to suﬃcient data. Deep learning methods
are indeed extremely dependent on the amount, quality and diversity of the data used for training. In
this section, we present some datasets introduced in the last few years for depth estimation. We will
give some details about what data they contain and what are their pros and cons.
First of all, it is important to explain how those datasets are created and why it is still hard to gather
accurate depth data. Depth sensors are expensive and produce sparse depth map. These sensors often
work using a scanning laser. The principle is simple: a laser is emitted by the sensor, the light is then
reﬂected by the object encountered toward the sensor. The time between emission and reception of the
signal is measured. This gives the distance of the object. These sensors are called LiDAR sensors: Light
Detection And Ranging (see Figure 3.7). Unfortunately the scanning process can only be performed
sparsely, i.e. only a small number of points is scanned compared to the resolution of current RGB
camera. In addition, there might be some parts of the scene that will not reﬂect the light for some
reason (it can be too far away for instance). Hence, for a pair of RGB images and a depth map there is
no depth value for every pixel of the RGB image. These maps must then be completed and up-sampled
to match the RGB images. Plenty of techniques have been developed to solve this issue. Among those,
the ones from Hirschmuller [97], Schuon et al. [98] are quite similar to the model-based methods that
we described in section 3.1.1: it consists in choosing depths values that minimize a certain cost function
based on the information from the stereo images and the LiDAR sensor. This was used in particular in
order to build the KITTI dataset Geiger et al. [99].
Figure 3.7: Examples of depth cameras. From left to right, the Microsoft Kinect, used for NYUv2 and

Most of the time, RGB images are collected as stereo pairs. This is very useful for binocular depth
estimation or for unsupervised methods as we described in section 3.1.2. Plenty of datasets have been
collected using the Microsoft Kinect camera (see Figure 3.7) that embed both a stereo camera and a
depth sensor. This provides a lot of information and is easy to setup. This has been used in particular
for the NYUv1 and NYUv2 datasets Nathan Silberman and Fergus [100].
Some datasets only use
stereo pairs to compute ground truth depth. This can be done using some of the model-based methods
presented previously or deep learning based matching methods. The latter was used in particular to
produce outdoor pair of the DIML dataset (Cho et al. [101]). Some other works proposed datasets built
using multi-views 3D reconstruction Sch¨onberger and Frahm [102]. This is the case for the MegaDepth
dataset Li and Snavely [50] that make use of numerous pictures of the same places or monuments found
online to reconstruct 3D scenes and therefore depth maps. Finally, some authors also use computer
graphics and video game to create artiﬁcial 3D scenes from which they can sample RGB and depth
images. This has been done in Niklaus et al. [1] using Unreal Engine and in Huang et al. [103] where
the authors used the video game Grand Theft Auto V to create RGB-D images.
Most of these datasets contain a lot of images, however these images are often taken from only a few
diﬀerent scenes. This lack of diversity is crippling for deep learning methods. The risk of overﬁtting is
high and in practice, methods are not generalizing well on unknown scenes. Images from the dataset are

often recorded as videos with the camera moving, this creates a lots of images which are only slightly

data in training and test set in order to get an honest assessment of the generalization of the network.
Furthermore as explained before, no dataset contains the exact ground truth depth from the real world,

4
Methods
In this section, we will expose the diﬀerent methods used to perform the 3D Ken Burns eﬀect. As
the whole pipeline used to perform this eﬀect is mainly composed of two parts: depth estimation
and inpainting, we will explain separately the methods accordingly. The ﬁrst section focuses on depth
estimation while the second tackles the inpainting.
As mentioned earlier, our work is mainly based on Niklaus et al. [1]. Unfortunately, the authors did
not provide the full code that they used for their experiments. In fact, they only provided their trained
networks alongside with the code required to do a forward pass. This includes the network’s architectures
deﬁnitions and some useful functions to handle camera motion and view rendering. Here is a list of
these functions that were used in our work:
- depth_to_points: converts a depth map into a list of 3D points (i.e. pointcloud) that represents
the scene.
- process_shift: computes the camera motion in world coordinate (x, y, z) from the zoom settings
provided. The zoom settings are constituted of two rectangular windows. For each, the size and
center are provided. The ﬁrst one will be the starting point of the Ken Burns eﬀect, i.e. the ﬁrst
frame of the output video will be the original image cropped with the ﬁrst window. The second
window is for the end frame of the KBE. See section 2.1 for more details.
- render_point_cloud: renders the 2D image from a 3D pointcloud and a camera position. It also
outputs a mask for the areas in the image where disocclusion happens.
We considered re-writing these functions ourselves in PyTorch code for consistency with the rest of our
work. However, those were implemented as CUDA kernels that can be executed in parallel on GPU.
Therefore, it would have been a waste of time to re-do the same thing. Furthermore, this code has
been extensively used by Niklaus et al. [1] and the community as it was released on GitHub, therefore
it is likely to be correct. This is all the code that we used from their paper. Everything that we will
present now has been implemented from scratch in Python using the standard deep learning framework
PyTorch (Paszke et al. [104]).
4.1
Depth estimation
4.1.1
Architecture design and training choices
Low-resolution estimation
The ﬁrst step of our work was to replicate the results from Niklaus et al. [1]. This begins by replicating
the training methods they described. Therefore we used the same network architecture as in the paper:
a GridNet with 6 rows and 4 columns for the depth estimation and a UNet for the depth reﬁnement.
Images are not fed into this network at full size, instead those are down-sampled by a factor 4. This
is done mostly to reduce the memory footprint of the network during training.
GridNets are quite
expensive in term of memory usage as they contain a lot of full-scale layers and a lot of parameters.
The output disparity map is then 4 times smaller than the input image. Note also that the paper
uses some semantic extraction to improve the estimation. We kept this setup unchanged for all our
experiments. They extract features from the full scale image using a trained classiﬁcation network.
Then they input these features into the GridNet in order to enrich the provided data.
We have already presented the loss function that they used for depth estimation training in section
3.1.3. Yet, we slightly modiﬁed the weights in the function that they used. Hence, our objective, for
both estimation and reﬁnement, is:

Ldepth = Lgrad + 0.001Ldata
(4.1)
This does not yield tremendous improvements but it appeared to be working slightly better in most

3.1.2 the L1 loss that was used in the paper has one major ﬂaw: it is not scale invariant. Therefore, we
chose to replace it with a scale invariant data term (see eq. 3.4). As the range of values returned by

In order to train the networks, we used the Adam optimizer [105], with the same parameters as in the

After each optimization step, the learning rate is slightly reduced according to an exponential decay
function:
αt+1 = αt ∗λ
0 < λ < 1
αt+1 = α0 ∗λt+1
(4.3)
λ = 0.99999, called the learning decay rate, is chosen so that the learning rate is roughly divided by
3 every 100k iterations. The main beneﬁt of adding some decay is that it prevents (to some extent)
the network to be stuck in a local optima during optimization. When the learning rate is too large,
the optimization process can be stuck in a narrow valley of the objective. Progressively reducing the
learning rate ensures that at some point it will be small enough to go down that valley, no matter how
narrow it is.
Yet, with these parameters we faced convergence issues during the training. Some runs yielded correct
results while some others are completely oﬀ. Some of the training curves were going down while some
other kept relatively high values. In fact only the data term of loss was aﬀected, the gradient term was
going down anyway. But it seemed that when the gradient term was going down too fast and it tended
to prevent the other term to go down. Our hypothesis was then that the optimization was ﬁnding a
local optima that satisﬁes only the gradient term. This optima yields extremely smooth disparity maps,
which makes the gradient term small. Once these maps were too smooth it seemed impossible for the
network to learn accurate depths. In order to change that, we decided to tweak the weights of the
objective during the training. The idea was to emphasize ﬁrst the training for the data term, i.e. to
output depth maps close to ground truth. And then include the gradient term so that the maps are
smooth. To do so, we changed the weights during training according to the following functions:

Depth reﬁnement
For depth reﬁnement, we kept the same architecture as in the 3D KBE paper. The reﬁnement network

4.1.2
Mask loss
One clear limitation of the method presented by Niklaus et al. [1] lies in their depth adjustment technique
(see section 3.1.3). In order to improve their depth estimation, they used a segmentation network to
retrieve diﬀerent objects from the scene. Then, they assumed that depth is constant on these objects.
This could be a good approximation when objects are small and far from the camera. Unfortunately,
this is not true all the time and in such case their method shows its limits (see Figure 4.1).
Figure 4.1: Example of a wrongly adjusted depth map (left) compared to ground truth map (right).
The detected masks are not fully covering the object which produce error in the estimation.
In order to address these limitations, we proposed to add an unsupervised loss to the estimation training.
The key idea of this loss is to provide some supervision from object segmentation in the image. Instead of
directly modifying the estimation according to the segmentation, the network is encouraged to produce
depth maps that are smooth on the objects. We then formulate here one hypothesis: implementing
segmentation-based smoothness constraint will provide beneﬁcial supervision for the network. We will
then try to conduct some experiments in order to validate or refute this hypothesis (see Chapter 5). It
seems reasonable as objects are likely to be located in a small region of space and therefore have quite
small depth variations.
Mathematically speaking, smoothness means continuous variation, i.e. for a given function f on a space
E, f is diﬀerentiable and its derivative is continuous on E. However this does not apply directly to
images or depth maps. As deﬁned at the beginning of Chapter 3, an image is a 2D map over a discrete
set of locations (i.e. pixels). Furthermore, pixels intensities can only take discrete values, depending
on how the image is encoded. Hence, working with discrete values prevents the use of the common

deﬁnitions for continuity and diﬀerentiability. The derivative function of an image can therefore never
be continuous. Instead, we will enforce a pseudo-smoothness by constraining the values of the derivative
of the depth map. When the derivative of a continuous function is bounded, it is said to be lipschitz
continuous which is a stronger property than continuity. In fact it is exactly what we want to enforce:
bounded variations in the depth map. Of course, not on the whole depth map at once, as an object
in the foreground will have a way smaller depth than the background, meaning that at the separation
between these objects, the depth map could vary a lot. That is why this must be enforced only on
objects’ masks. The main idea is to teach the depth adjustment from [1] to the network directly. This
should also improve the scene understanding of the network as the network will need to learn how to
segment the image in order to satisfy the constraint.

4.2.1
Disocclusion mask generation
As for depth estimation, we started by implementing the training process described in Niklaus et al.
[1]. However, one huge limitation was that the authors did not provide the synthetic dataset that they
built, at the time of writing (they actually released it a few weeks before the end of this project). Even
though this might not seem important as there exists a lot of RGB-D datasets online, it is quite limiting.
Their synthetic dataset was not only a set of paired images and depth maps. It was entire scenes with
complete and accurate depth. Therefore, they were able to create any view from the scene. Some
datasets also provide multiple view of the same scene but this is mostly done using stereo camera.
Hence, the view shift is always the same and quite small. It is crucial to be able to sample random
camera views as the KBE will be performed with arbitrary motion as well. That way, the disocclusion
masks are also randomly generated and training will be more robust.
The dataset that we chose to work on does not provide free sampling from any view of the scene.
Instead, it provides the RGB images and corresponding depth maps. Just from this, one could generate
new views and disocclusion masks, but no ground truth would be available for supervision.
We will from now on adopt the same notation as in section 3.2.4. Let’s take a RGB-D image of a scene
from view A. A camera motion S is randomly sampled and the RGB-D image from view B is rendered
along with its disocclusion mask. But this mask is aligned with the generated view from B (see Figure
4.4). Therefore, we are limited in two ways:
- The generated new views and masks cannot be used with our dataset as no ground truth is
available for training.
- The generated masks cannot even be used with our images. They will not be aligned correctly as
they are rendered for the new views.
Figure 4.4: Example of disocclusion mask generated from the camera motion from view A (left and
middle) to view B (right). The mask is aligned only with view B. Without ground truth from view B,
this mask is not really useful in a supervised setting. Using it on view A would be the same as using
completely random mask, i.e. not generated by disocclusion.
However, it is not necessary to render new views to get disocclusion masks. The Z-buﬀer algorithm,
used for new view rendering, looks for visible areas of a scene from a certain view. To do so, a line
between each 3D point of the scene and the camera is drawn. These lines intersect the image plane at
some pixel location. This gives the values (color and depth) of the pixels of the rendered image. If two
lines intersect at the same pixel, only the closest points from the camera gives its value to the pixel.
The disocclusion masks then correspond to pixels of the image that have not been matched by any
point of the scene. However, the corresponding points did exist in the real-world scene, it’s just that no
information was available in the original image (from view A) for that point, i.e. this point was hidden
in the ﬁrst place and became visible from view B. Now let’s suppose that from the rendered image from
view B, one would apply an opposite camera motion −S. This will hide back the disoccluded areas and

4.2.2
Architecture and training details
We then chose the exact same architecture as in Niklaus et al. [1], i.e. a 4 by 4 GridNet. The training
is done using the same parameters as in that paper (see Appendix B). We chose the Adam optimizer
with some learning rate decay (same as for the depth estimation training). The main change that we
provided was about the loss function. Instead of using the loss function from Niklaus et al. [1], we chose
the one from Liu et al. [16]. This choice will be explained in section 5.3.1, as this loss function seems
to provide a signiﬁcant improvement compared to the original paper method.
Unfortunately, this training set-up does not allow to take full advantage of the architecture designed by
Niklaus et al. [1]. As detailed in section 3.2.4, they trained a context extractor alongside the inpainting
network. This context is supposedly extracted from view A, then warped to view B similarly as the
image and the disparity map. This warping operation generates disocclusion holes in the context as
well. The inpainting network then gets everything as an input. In our case, as we generate the masks
only with the view from A, there is no way to get a proper training for the context extractor. In order
to make use of it anyway, we chose to extract the context from the masked image from view A. This is
obviously not equivalent to compute the context from view B and then warp it to view A so it matches
the disocclusion masks on A. It is also not conceivable to give the extractor the entire image from A,
otherwise, the network would have had access to ground truth beforehand. Even if the context is then
masked, it could be possible that the extractor learns to copy ground truth information and pass it to
the inpainting network. We are aware that our solution is not optimal and does not ﬁt the problem that
we want to solve in the end. When performing the KBE, the ﬁrst image is known and context can be
extracted from there to inpaint the ﬁnal image. However, we hope that this setup will provide some
relevant training for the context extractor anyway. An other training setup will be proposed to correct
this in section 4.2.4.
4.2.3
Partial convolution
The ﬁrst thing that we tried after setting up the training baseline was to add partial convolutions
(see section 3.2.2). This kind of convolutions is particularly well suited for irregular masks inpainting.
Disocclusion masks are indeed irregular, therefore it seems promising to try partial convolutions for this
problem. The key idea behind partial convolutions is that the output of a layer is computed using only
valid pixels from the input. The set of valid pixels, or mask, is updated after each convolution and each
update shrinks a bit the masks. In the end, all the holes are therefore inpainted. We already deﬁned this
mask update in section 3.2.2. This can be applied directly to most CNN. However, for some networks,
such as the GridNet, it requires another step. We will call this step mask aggregation. In general,
CNN have a linear computational graph (see Figure 4.6). There is an input image and at each node,
a convolution layer is applied on the output of the previous layer. With partial convolutions it is quite
the same. The input is an image and the corresponding mask and each node take the output of the
previous as input (i.e. the updated image and updated mask). However, when the network does not
have a linear graph, it is not clear what should be the input of a node. In the case of the GridNet, each
node takes as input the sum of the outputs of the previous nodes horizontally and vertically. But when
it comes to partial convolution, each node outputs also a mask and it is not clear how to combine the
horizontal and vertical masks.
There are multiple ways to handle this. The masks are represented as arrays of either 0 or 1, i.e. as
boolean arrays. Hence, two boolean arrays can be combined using a simple operation: the logical AND
or the logical OR (pixel-wise). Of course, one could also choose to keep only one of the masks, but it
seems to be a poor choice as the inpainted pixels from some convolutions will be disregarded. Hence,
we are left with a choice between the AND and OR aggregation. The AND seems quite reasonable
as, for a pixel to be considered valid, it must be valid in both masks. However, this could prevent the
ﬁlling of the holes as some pixels will be inpainted but not considered as valid. The OR aggregation,
on the other hand will consider a pixel as valid if it has been inpainted by either one or the other two

4.2.4
Adversarial reﬁnement
As explained before, our dataset and training setup does not allow to train the context extractor properly.
The context should be computed on the whole image before the change of view so it provides relevant
information for inpainting. Instead, it is computed on the image with holes, which could skew the
context. In order to address this issue, we propose an unsupervised reﬁnement method. The idea is ﬁrst
to train in a supervised fashion the inpainting network (as described in section 4.2.1) and then switch
to an unsupervised regime to train the extractor correctly and to improve the inpainting quality further.
To do so, we chose to use adversarial training that we presented in section 3.2.2. Adversarial training
has been used quite a lot for image inpainting, but most of the time it is combined with reconstruction
(i.e. supervised) losses.
Adversarial color supervision
Our ﬁrst idea was to introduce a discriminator that would be trained to distinguish between images that
were inpainted and real images. In order to do so, we used a PatchGAN discriminator (Isola et al. [46]),
which is simply a fully convolutional neural network. Its main diﬀerence with a regular discriminator is
that it does not only output a single probability of the input image to be real or fake. Instead it outputs
a map where each pixel gives the probability that the corresponding patch in the input image is real or
fake. It thus provides some local supervision to the generator. Its architecture can be found in Appendix
B. We chose to use the least squares GAN (LS-GAN) objective introduced by Mao et al. [108], as it
has proved its eﬃciency and reliability. Note that there is no consensus on which adversarial objective
is the best at the time of writing. The LS-GAN objective can be written as follows:

Indeed, it has been proved that the GAN
original objective creates a mini-max game that should converge to an equilibrium where the generator
has learnt to produce samples from the training distribution [59]. However, in practice it is not that
easy and using the vanilla GAN objective often leads to convergence issues.
This is why plenty of
variants of this objective have been proposed. Some regularization tricks have also been introduced to
stabilize the training. Among them, spectral normalization and gradient clipping have been widely used
in previous works. We chose to use them as well. Note that spectral normalization could only be used
in our discriminator. The generator being pre-trained, does not support spectral norm. It modiﬁes its
weights and it results in a worse inpainting than without spectral norm. Maybe this modiﬁcation can
be oﬀset by learning, but it seems reasonable to keep the best inpainting as a starting point for the
adversarial reﬁnement. Besides, spectral normalization only in discriminator is often enough to enforce
the Lipschitz constraint that is known to stabilize training.
Yet, this was not enough to make it work. All training runs were ending the same way (see section
5.3.3): the generator takes the advantage and the discriminator does not learn anymore. This behaviour
was quite expected as the generator was already trained while the discriminator is randomly initialized
at the beginning of the adversarial training. Therefore, to balance the situation we used several tricks
so that the generator’s advantage does not break the training.
- First, the discriminator is pre-trained at the beginning, so it can keep up with the generator. In
this phase, the back-propagation is stopped for the generator so that its weights are not updated.
Hence, only the discriminator improves during this phase. In our experiments, we often chose to
compute a couple of thousand iterations as discriminator pre-training.
- Then, the learning rate of the generator is set lower than the discriminator’s one. This prevents the
generator to change too fast. This gives some time for the discriminator to adjust to generator’s
improvements. We typically used αG = 1 × 10−5 and αD = 5 × 10−5.
- In a similar manner, we also reduced the number of steps computed for the generator. This means
that a step is computed for the generator every few steps of the discriminator. This technique
has been introduced by Metz et al. [109] in order to address convergence issues with GANs. In
our experiments, we did one update of the generator every 5 updates of the discriminator.
- Finally, we have also seen that sometimes the above tricks were not enough and at some point
the generator takes the best of the discriminator. To prevent this, we stopped the training of the
generator once in while (every 10k steps) for a thousand iterations. That way, the discriminator
has some time to improve and catch up with the generator.

Perceptual discriminators
In order to ﬁll the performance gap with the trained network from Niklaus et al. [1] (which was still
performing better than our best results: see section 5.3.3), we thought about another way to improve
the discriminator further. It is well known that the perceptual loss provides relevant supervision for
image reconstruction tasks. In particular, it is known to improve the ’natural’ aspect of the images
(i.e. it looks better accordingly to a human observer). However the perceptual loss originally needs the
ground truth to be computed, which is not available in our problem. The same idea can nevertheless be
transposed to an unsupervised setting. Instead of directly matching the features of the trained network
(in our case we used VGG-16), one could use these as an input for the discriminator. Basically, this
is equivalent to use some layers of the trained network as the ﬁrst layers of the discriminator. The
discriminator can then learn to distinguish the images given the feature maps from the VGG network.
This also contributes to balancing the training between the discriminator and the generator as it can be
seen as a discriminator pre-training. The ﬁrst layers are then kept ﬁxed during the training, so that the
VGG features remain unchanged. This makes it easier for the discriminator to decide whether an image
is real or fake. See Figure 4.7b to have a glance of the discriminator architecture. To our knowledge,
only one work proposed similar ideas: Sungatullina et al. [110]. They proposed, a similar discriminator,
but with one major diﬀerence: it works at multiple scales. So far, our discriminators had all the same
ﬁeld of view (i.e. the size of the patch in the input image that corresponds to one pixel in the output
map). Plenty of other works used multiscale architectures to work with high resolution images such as
Iizuka et al. [111], Yu et al. [86] or Karras et al. [112]. It seems decisive for achieving good performance
on high resolution image reconstruction. What they did is simply build a discriminator backbone that
is based on the extracted features from the VGG network and same size feature convolutions that are
learnt. This allows to take advantage of the intermediate features from VGG instead of only the last
ones. From this backbone, three diﬀerent discriminators are then working at diﬀerent levels. Each of
them has a diﬀerent ﬁeld of view, which provides supervision at local, medium and global scales (see
Figure 4.7c).
Color and depth adversarial supervision
So far, only the RGB part of the inpainted image is used for supervision. This is obviously not optimal.
It is likely that the inpainted depth maps will be of a poor quality with such a setting. The network is
not asked to correct its depth maps at all. The problem is that in an unsupervised setting it is quite
hard to get some supervision for depth. From here two ideas were developed.
- Adversarial depth supervision: add the disparity maps into the discriminator, so that it also
classiﬁes as fake, parts of the image that have a wrong depth inpainting. All the discriminator’s
architectures that we deﬁned earlier could be extended to use the depth maps as an input.
- Unsupervised smoothness enforcement: there should be some continuity inside the depth maps
between inpainted areas and valid ones. Of course, this continuity cannot be insured everywhere.
When an object appears in a disoccluded area, it could likely be at a diﬀerent depth than both the
foreground and the background. Therefore, in such case, the continuity should not be respected.
The same way, when a part of the background is disoccluded, it should have continuity with the
background but not with the foreground (see Figure 4.8). Nevertheless, it is still important to
have some kind of smoothness inside the inpainted area as the depth maps do not vary rapidly
in most cases. We already argue in this direction in section 4.1.2. Therefore, we tried to add a
loss function that would enforce this during the training. To do so, we used the same mask loss

4.2.5
Performance assessment
As we did for the depth estimation, we need to introduce few metrics to assess the quality of our results.
As the network inpaints both the RGB image and the depth map, we need some metrics to assess both.
These metrics will be extensively used in Chapter 5, where we will present our results and experiments.
- Peak signal to noise radio (PSNR): it measures how close the reconstruction of an image y
is from the original one x. It can be used to assess the quality of the inpainting for both the
RGB image and the depth map. One just needs to consider the depth map as a gray-scale image.
PSNR is known to correlate well with human perception. This means that a good PSNR should
give good looking reconstruction, according to a human observer.

value of the PSNR stands for good reconstruction.
- Structural similarity (SSIM): this is a measure of the similarity between two images x and y.
It can be used for with both RGB or grayscale images as well. It should then be a good way to
measure how close the inpainting is from the ground truth. It is deﬁned as follows:

- Fr´echet inception distance (FID): it is a metrics introduced in Heusel et al. [113]. This is
an improvement of the Inception Score (IS) (Salimans et al. [114]) which was introduced for
its high correlation with human judgement. The IS takes advantage of a trained classiﬁcation
network (Inception-v3) and uses the prediction made by that network to assess the quality of the
reconstructed distribution. Unlike SSIM and PSNR, IS is not meant to be computed only with a
pair of ground truth and reconstructed images, but on plenty of pairs at once. This way, it can
also evaluate the quality of learnt distribution and in particular its variance. Basically, to have a
high score, the generator should produce samples with high variety (i.e. the marginal distribution
should be uniform across all classes). But also distinct samples that can be recognized easily, i.e.
with a focused label distribution. However, the IS has some ﬂaws. It does not penalize a generator
that will produce images from diﬀerent classes but with very few variance within each class. This
is mainly due to the fact that it does not compare the statistics of the learnt distribution with
real-world distribution’s ones. That is the gap the FID tries to ﬁll. For this purpose, Salimans et al.
[114] computed the mean vector and covariance matrices of the Inception network’s features maps
across all the images of a test set, let’s call it µT , CT respectively. They did the same with the
generated/reconstructed images: µR, CR. Then they deﬁned the FID from Fr´echet’s Distance:
FID = ∥µT −µR∥2
2 + Tr(CT + CR −(CT CR)1/2)
(4.19)
This metric could not assess the quality of the depth maps as it is likely that the features extracted
by the inception network will be wrong (it was not trained to classify grayscale image and even less
for depth maps). However, it is possible not to use the ground truth distribution as the reference.
This is particularly interesting for unsupervised learning as the ground truth is not available.
Instead, a similar distribution can be used. For instance, the reference distribution could be the
left images of a stereo pair while the compared distribution would be the reconstructed right
images of the pair.

5.1.2
Mask loss experiments
Let’s now focus on the core of our method for depth estimation: the mask loss. This loss function has
been presented in section 4.1.2 and a few hyper-parameters need to be selected here:
- γmask is the weight of the mask loss term inside the overall objective function (see eq. 4.7). This
determines how much the network should focus on this term of the loss compared to other. It
prioritizes the training between the diﬀerent loss terms.
- κ is the smoothness bound. Smaller values lead to greater smoothness.
- H is the set of scales at which the gradients are computed. As we mentioned earlier, changing
the scales is equivalent to setting a diﬀerent bound. Therefore, it is useless to try to ﬁnd the best
set of scales and the best bound, only one of them is important. Hence, we chose to keep the set
of scales unchanged and in all our experiments, H = {1}.
Finding the right parameters can be quite challenging as both γmask and κ can take arbitrary values.
In addition, training takes some time and it is not practicable to train until convergence on the whole
dataset for any pairs of parameters. Instead, we chose randomly a small subset of images from the
training set and train the network up to convergence for each pair value of parameters. This allowed
to train until convergence within roughly 10 minutes and therefore to repeat the experiments a couple
of time. Hence, we performed some kind of grid search to ﬁnd the best parameters couple. It turns
out to be γmasks = 0.0001 and κ = 0.1 (see Table C.1 in Appendix). This gives some intuition about
what could be the best parameters but one should not blindly trust these results. Some statistical noise
could alter the values of the metrics and therefore change the conclusion. In order to tackle this, we
chose to conduct a few more experiments to investigate the inﬂuence of these two parameters on the
performances more thoroughly. To do so, we changed only one parameter at a time, keeping the other
ﬁxed at the best value according to the grid search. This narrows down a lot the range of the search.
Hence each experiment was repeated 5 times. This allows to get the precision of the measurements
and gives a greater credibility to the results. Figure 5.2 gives a summary of the results: the means of
the log RMSE is plotted for each value of the parameter. A 95% conﬁdence interval is also plotted.
It shows the conﬁdence that one can have about these results. As the interval is relatively small, it is
quite certain that κ = 1 gives the best results. Note that this value is not the same result as from
the grid search. Such diﬀerences were expected and were the original motivation for this more reliable
experiment. For the γmasks value, nothing conclusive can be said as the conﬁdence interval is quite
wide. Even though, it seems that there is a trend that lower γmasks gives better results but this must be
considered carefully. One more thing to note is that for the two largest values of γmasks, some runs did
not converge, i.e. the validation losses were really high compared to other runs. We chose to disregard
these outliers as they would have changed a lot the mean and standard deviation. The exact values of
the mean and standard deviation for each experiment can be found in Tables C.2 and C.3 in Appendix.

more pixel in average using DeepLab v3 than Mask RCNN. However, this did not yield any signiﬁcant
improvement in the performances.
In fact, it performs almost identically as with Mask R-CNN on
ImageNet.
5.1.3
Sub-conclusion
Finally, we conducted some ﬁnal experiment with the mask loss. We chose to use the best parame-
ters found so far (except that we used ImageNet and not MegaDepth to compute the mask loss as
performances were quite the same). Then two networks were trained for a longer time than what we
did before. And unfortunately, it produces results in contradiction with what we got in the previous
experiments (see Table 5.4). Hence, the best performing network in the end is trained without mask
loss. This is obviously problematic concerning the methodology of the experiments that we conducted
as up to now, most of ours experiments gave the opposite results. This will be discussed in section 6.3.
Yet, this can be explained in several ways:
- The mask loss is wrong. It gives supervision that harms the depth estimation. As it has a small
weight during training, its eﬀect may take a long time to appear. This is quite unlikely though as
even if it does not yield the best results, the last experiment with the mask loss is quite close to
the best performances.
- The mask loss is correct but not useful: it enforces smoothness but our original hypothesis is
wrong. Depth maps should not be smooth on objects’ masks. The supervision of the mask loss is
then so small that it takes a really long time before it actually changes something in the training.
Therefore it is hard to see any relevant eﬀect at the beginning as it is basically the same as training
without this loss.
- The mask loss is correct and could be useful for training but the masks’ quality provides some
wrong supervision. In fact the masks are mostly correct and only a small area of them is oﬀ
sometimes. Therefore, this wrong supervision has a limited impact on the training. This is why
it takes a really long time to see its eﬀect.
It would be hard to ﬁgure out which of these three situations actually corresponds to reality. In section
6.1.1, we will give some research tracks that one could follow in order to either improve the mask loss
or show whether it is useful or harmful for depth estimation tasks.

In order to assess the performances of the partial convolutions, we decided to train two separate neural

it even performs worse than the baseline. This is probably due to the fact that the partial convolutions
consider all the border of a masked area the same way. However, this must not be the case as this area is
probably related to a background object but has at the same time a border with a foreground object (see
Figure 4.8). Even though, progressively ﬁlling the disocclusion hole sounds like a good idea. It forces
the network to inpaint some parts of the image early whereas it would have been better to inpaint it at
the very end. This ordering might be quite diﬃcult to learn as it would probably be quite diﬀerent from
a situation to another. This is a quite interesting result, even though partial convolutions are better
than regular ones for general image inpainting. When it comes to the speciﬁc case of disocclusion
inpainting, they seem to be unﬁt. This could shed some light on another result from this experiment:

5.3.3
Adversarial reﬁnement
Balancing tricks
Although the adversarial reﬁnement should be working nicely in theory, it was not so simple to make
it work in practice. At the beginning, training runs always produced the same results: the generator
overpowered the discriminator and found a way to create examples that were always classiﬁed as true
images but that looked nothing like natural images (see Figure 5.6). This can be easily spotted when
looking at the discriminator loss values during training. When the generator is too strong and fools
the discriminator too quickly, the discriminator adapts and ends up answering almost randomly to the
question: is it a real or a fake image? This is the best strategy that the discriminator can adopt when
the generator is too strong (looking at it from a game theory perspective). In the LS-GAN framework, it
means that EIA∼A(1 −D(IA))2 = 0.25 and EIB∼BD(G(IB))2 = 0.25. Hence, LD = 0.25. Therefore,
when the discriminator loss converges to 0.25, it means that it picks randomly whether the input image
is real or fake. The generator has learnt to fool it consistently. This phenomenon was clearly visible in
our ﬁrst experiments with adversarial training where the generator produced some strange artifacts that
confused the discriminator (see Figure 5.6).

and high frequency patterns that fool the discriminator.

Color and depth adversarial supervision
So far, we have only addressed the color inpainting, disregarding the depth channel. This obviously
does not produce satisfactory results (see Figure 5.8) for the depth inpainting as the network does not
get much supervision on it. In order to solve this issue, we tried a few things. But note that it is
now impossible to compute proper metrics (i.e. get quantitative results) as there exists no unsupervised
metrics for comparing depth maps (such as FID does for RGB images).
First, we tried to add the disparity maps into the discriminator, so that it also classiﬁes as fake, parts of
the image that have a wrong depth inpainting. Unfortunately, this did not provide much improvement
for the depth inpainting. On the contrary, it seems to worsen the depth inpainting that was learnt during
3D Ken Burns Eﬀect
63
==PAGE:72==
the supervised part of the training. This was only tested with the best discriminator (the perceptual
multi-scale one). As it was the best one, we extrapolated that the other architectures would probably
be even worse.
The second idea to improve the depth inpainting was to use a mask loss on the disocclusion mask in
order to enforce smoothness. This yields mitigated results as it seems to increase smoothness on the
whole depth map. Therefore valid parts of the map are altered and become blurry. This was ﬁxed by
adding an other loss to keep the valid parts of the maps unchanged. This addition yields good results
about disocclusion holes, as it seems to ﬁll it in a more sensible way (i.e. better integrated with the rest
of the map). However for larger holes that appear on the side of the image, it performs quite poorly.
The enforced smoothness prevents any edges to be inpainted. Some discontinuities should still be visible
as the objects that are inpainted in this area probably do not have a constant depth.
A comparison of these two diﬀerent methods can be found in Figure 5.9. The ﬁrst one is the method
from last section (i.e. only RGB supervision). The depth maps are poorly inpainted. The second was
trained with mask loss to enforce smooth depth maps on extended masks and with reconstruction losses
on the known parts of the map. This produces the best results for depth inpainting, however, it seems
to hurt the color inpainting. When computing the FID, we found that this method only achieves 43.68,
which is quite lower than the best network that we trained. Yet this may not be a problem after all. It
is possible to use both of these trained networks in order to get the best of both worlds and therefore
good color and depth inpainting at the same time. Of course, this is a dirty hack as both networks are
trained to output RGB-D inpainting and that only a part of these predictions will actually be used. It
makes it quite heavy as either both networks must be loaded in memory to perform the inpainting or
the must be done successively which make the estimation slower. In addition, using both networks for
inpainting will prevent any coherence between the color and depth inpainting. This kind of coherence
was not visible from any inpainting image that we generated. However, this should be a sign of a good
learning. It would mean that the network has understood that color and depth inpainting are linked and
that if an object is inpainted, it must also be inpainted in the depth map. To achieve the same thing,
it may have been simpler to train separately one network to inpaint the RGB channels and one for the
depth.
Halfway view color supervision
Finally, we tested our last idea about how to get some supervision about depth inpainting: use supervision
from the halfway view between the original image and the inpainting one.
Although, this sounds
promising in theory, it turns out not to be working. We encountered here the opposite of a problem
described before: the balancing between the generator and the discriminator. When we started with the
adversarial reﬁnement, the pre-trained generator was taking the advantage over the discriminator which
basically broke the learning for both networks. It required some tricks to achieve a reliable balance
between the two networks. However, in this new setup the ratio of power is inverted. The discriminator
overpowers the generator every time. This can be seen as the discriminator loss converges to 0. The
generator does not get proper feedbacks and cannot learn to improve its inpainting. In general, the FID
goes up and the resulting inpainting does not look good. This certainly happens because the quality of
the rendered view from C is highly dependent on the depth inpainting. When some points have a wrong
inpainted depth values from view B, the camera shift will make them disappear. If they have a too big
depth value, they risk to be occluded by other points. A too small depth, on the other hand, will shift
the points too much and they will occlude other points. Either cases will result in some holes in the
image: the points with a wrong depth value will not be at their right places in the image, which will
leave a hole at that place. Therefore, it is quite easy for the discriminator to ﬁnd out which image is
real of fake, it just needs to look for holes in the images. However, it is quite hard for the generator to
adapt and ﬁx these holes. The discriminator has a quite easy task to achieve while the generator needs
to solve a much harder problem. Hence, it will be quite hard to make this method work. Nevertheless,
we discuss some potential ways of making this work in section 6.2.3.

5.3.4
Sub-conclusion
In this section, we have proposed an improvement of the inpainting method proposed by Niklaus et al.
[1]. It allows to train a neural network for color and depth inpainting mostly in an unsupervised manner.
A supervised pre-training is performed for a slightly diﬀerent task. This initial training is done without
the context extractor as the network does no see the full image from the original viewpoint. Then an
adversarial reﬁnement improves the inpainting further. This second step is completely unsupervised as
the supervision only comes from the discriminator network. In the end, the performances achieved after
the reﬁnement are better than the trained network provided by Niklaus et al. [1]. It is quite noticeable
as their method was fully supervised. However, they used a synthetic dataset which certainly has less
variety than the one we used. Even if it provided accurate ground truth, it might not be that useful
for real world scenes inpainting. It is noteworthy that our supervised pre-training was really performing
worse than the trained network from the 3D KBE paper. This is expected as they are not trained for the
exact same task. Therefore when testing our network for the right task, bad performances are expected.
Inversely, the trained network from the paper does not perform very well when tested on the task which
was used for our pre-training (see Table 5.10).

Discussion
In this chapter, we will come back on multiple aspects of our methods that did not yield satisfactory
results. We will try to understand why and propose some research tracks for future improvements.
6.1
Depth estimation
6.1.1
Mask loss ﬂaws
As discussed in section 5.1, the mask loss does not produce any beneﬁcial results. We outlined three
scenarios that try to explain why the mask loss is not working. For each of them, we will now give a
bit more details and explain what would be the next steps to ﬁx this issue.
Mask loss is wrong
Our implementation of the mask loss might not actually enforce the smoothness on the masks. Even
though we spent some time checking the correctness of the loss, it is still possible that something is
not working as intended. In this case, one should probably start by repeating the small experiments
that we conducted about mask loss with fake data. Maybe our data generation process was not close
enough to real data to ensure that the mask loss properties will apply with actual images. It would be
interesting to compute the value of the mask loss with real images and ground truth depth maps. We
should probably have done so at that time but it seemed enough to test it on fake data.
Otherwise, one could try to redesign the loss function to ﬁnd another way to enforce smoothness. This
could be done for instance using a second order derivative on the depth maps, using for instance a
laplacian ﬁlter. As this is known to detect edges in pictures, this could probably give a good proxy for
smoothness.
Mask loss is correct but our hypothesis is wrong
In this case, our hypothesis, that is: ’enforcing smoothness on depth maps is beneﬁcial for training’,
is wrong. Of course, it is incorrect to enforce the smoothness everywhere on the map as the depth
map is by nature discontinuous. The problem is that a trivial minimization of the mask loss consists in
producing a depth map that is constant (and therefore smooth). It is likely that the network tends to
learn this instead of learning object segmentation. The main idea of the mask loss is to be a proxy for
the network to learn object segmentation. In order to be able to produce smooth depth maps only on
objects, a network needs to be able to detect these objects ﬁrst. This may be too hard to be learned
only with the supervision provided by the mask loss.
One way to address this issue could be to train explicitly the network in order to perform object
segmentation alongside depth estimation. Multi-task learning is quite popular in recent deep learning
works. It seems that learning closely related tasks can improve the performances of the networks. Even
though one could think that this would make it harder, learning diﬀerent tasks at the same time can
be seen as a form of regularization technique. We already presented some works using this method in
Chapter 3, such as Liebel and K¨orner [5] that learnt both depth regression and classiﬁcation at the same
time. Or Eigen and Fergus [52] who presented a method to learn depth estimation, surface orientation
and semantic labelling. Kendall et al. [116] actually designed a method that is trained to perform at the
same time depth estimation and object segmentation (and instance segmentation as well), and it seems
to improve the performances of both tasks compared to single-task methods. That is why it could be
interesting to look at this kind of methods in order to improve our depth estimation.

An other way could be to use the masks from a segmentation network as an input for the depth
estimation network. That way, it would not be required to learn object segmentation on its own and it
would be possible to enforce smoothness on these masks more easily. The network will be less likely to
enforce smoothness on the whole map as the masks will already be at its disposal. This of course will
be highly dependent on the quality of the segmentation network.
Mask loss and our hypothesis are correct
In this scenario, the problem does not come directly from our method. The mask loss supervision is, as
we have already explained, quite dependent on the quality of the masks. Therefore, it is crucial to use a
very well trained segmentation network in order to produce it. One could try to investigate further the
choice of this network as well as the dataset used to compute it. Even state-of-the-art segmentation
networks do not perform equally good on all datasets. It is therefore quite important to choose the
right combination. We probably did not spend enough time choosing our dataset for this purpose. As
described in section 5.1.2, none of the datasets that we tried for computing the mask loss was optimal.
Hence, it would be interesting to ﬁnd one that is better suited for segmentation tasks. An example of
such dataset would be COCO, introduced by Lin et al. [117]. This is in fact the training dataset of the
segmentation network that we used. That would not be an issue here, on the contrary, it will only make
the masks better and therefore the mask loss supervision more accurate. One could even go further and
directly use the ground truth masks from that dataset in order to compute the mask loss. This would
speed-up the training, as the segmentation network would not be used anymore, while improving the
accuracy of the masks.
It would be quite hard to ﬁnd out which of these scenario is the correct one and it is even possible that
none of them is. It is probable that something else is wrong and that we did not even think about it.
Yet, this section oﬀered some possible improvement methods that could be interesting to investigate.
6.1.2
Estimation issue
As mentioned in section 3.1.4, the depth estimation from Niklaus et al. [1] has also some ﬂaws. In
particular it could be quite hard to detect small objects or to accurately predict depth on reﬂective
surfaces such as glass. These issues were not directly addressed by our methods but the mask loss could
have helped to reduce them. In fact, it can be seen in Figure 5.4 that the trained paper from [1] is
not able to estimate depth of some objects like trees or road signs while our networks could. We did
not investigate these further but it would be interesting to see if this is just a struck of luck or if our
networks are actually more resilient for these kinds of estimation. Even so, it would not mean that this
is due to the mask loss because the network trained without it does not seem to have this problem
either.
6.1.3
Overﬁtting
As mentioned in section 5.1, there is quite a gap between the performances obtained on the test set
and a completely unrelated dataset. So far, this statement was only based on images comparisons, but
Table 6.1 clearly shows that the performances are quite bad on the NYUv2 dataset. To explain what is
going on, it might be relevant to give more information about the test set that we used. The test set is
composed of images of all the scenes of the DIML dataset. However, due to a technical issue we were
not able to get the entire training set, meaning that some scenes were not seen by the network during
the training. Hence, we hypothesized that most of the errors made by the network at test time were
done with images that come from unknown scenes. In order to test that, we made an other test set,
based only on the scenes present in the training set. The results of the performance assessment can
be found in Table 6.2. There is a clear gap in all the metrics between the full test set and the ﬁltered
one. This means that the network is overﬁtting to the scenes that were presented during training. It
’remembers’ those scenes and is able to provide really accurate predictions for other images. However,
when new images are presented, it struggles to get the same level of performance.

in the two datasets. The DIML test set will have a similar depth range as the training set while NYU
v2 test set could be quite diﬀerent. Even if the depth maps produced for NYU v2 look reasonable, the
range may be oﬀwhich produces wrong predictions according to ground truth. This can be called depth
range overﬁtting. Altough it is not properly overﬁtting, the symptoms are the same: the network will
perform better on training/test set from DIML than on other dataset because the range is not identical.
Figure 6.1 highlights this by showing the histograms of the depth and disparity values for both DIML
and NYUv2. There are some diﬀerences in the depth histograms, but the overall shape is quite similar.
However, it is completely diﬀerent for the disparity values, in NYUv2 they are located in a narrower band
compared to the DIML even with similar depths. This is mainly due to diﬀerent camera parameters
(namely the focal length and the baseline). This probably explains why the performances on the NYUv2
dataset are so bad and inversely why the trained network from Niklaus et al. [1] does not perform well on
DIML. This is an important drawback of working with disparities and not depth values directly. Depth
is absolute while disparity can vary from camera to camera, it is therefore harder to combine datasets
when working with disparities as the images will probably come from multiple cameras. If absolute depth
prediction is not the ultimate goal (unfortunately not for the 3D KBE), it might be interesting to use
the same camera parameters for all images, disregarding the actual camera parameters. Working with
depths directly may seem tempting but existing works mostly use disparities instead, as it seems to be
more numerically well-behaved.

In order to reduce this overﬁtting, one would need datasets with greater variance, both in terms of scenes
location and camera parameters. Having a greater number of scenes would reduce the scene overﬁtting
as the network will not be able to remember complex information about each scene. And a greater
number of camera could reduce the range overﬁtting or rather the camera overﬁtting. The network
learns to create disparity maps that have the same range as the ground truths that come from only few
cameras. With a greater parameter diversity, a network could be trained to learn these parameters and
produce disparity maps accordingly.
This is likely to happen in the next few years as a few smartphone brands (Samsung and Apple at
least) are releasing devices that embed depth sensors. It will then be possible to gather huge datasets
containing RGB-D images with great variety. This will probably improve a lot the robustness of the
depth estimation methods that still lack generalization.
6.2
Image inpainting
6.2.1
Color inpainting
Even if the results of the color inpainting are satisfactory, those could be improved a lot more. In this
section we will propose some future ideas that could help to improve our methods.
Context selection for inpainting
Unlike arbitrary holes that can generally be inpainted using the information from all sides the same way,
disocclusion should be ﬁlled only using information that is depth-related. When a foreground object
moves and reveals a disoccluded part of the background, that disocclusion should be ﬁlled only using the
information from the background, not from the foreground object. Partial convolutions do not account
3D Ken Burns Eﬀect
71
==PAGE:80==
for that and the masks shrinkage occurs from all directions at the same time. Therefore this may raise
problems for disocclusion inpainting: leakage of foreground objects in disocclusion areas. This means
that the color from foreground objects will be propagated into the disoccluded areas whereas it should
not.
A way to address it would be to dissociate foreground and background objects and inpaint only the
background. This has been done successfully by Shih et al. [94], but this work has been published at
the end of this project and could not be used to improve our methods. Another solution to this problem
would be to use gated convolutions instead of partial ones. The gated convolutions try to avoid the
systematic hole shrinkage that occurs with partial convolutions. This is done with a learnable mask
update (see section 3.2.2 for more details). This could be better suited for disocclusion inpainting as
this could allow some kind of inpainting ordering which would prevent to use the context from the
foreground to inpaint the background.
6.2.2
Depth inpainting
As we saw in section 5.3.3, it seems that depth inpainting is not working really well (compared to color
inpainting). The main drawback is that there is no consistency between the inpainted color and depth.
There is structure in the color inpainting: the edges from the known parts of the image are propagated
in the disoccluded areas. This does not happen in the depth inpainting. This could probably be ﬁxed by
explicitly learning this structure and using it as a baseline for both the color and depth inpainting. This
would in addition provide consistency. A way to do so would be to adapt the Edge Connect method
from [17]. This ﬁrst inpaints the edges inside the missing areas and then hallucinates the colors. It
would certainly be possible to use the same technique alongside our developed methods. This could
produce more structured and consistent depth inpainting.
6.2.3
Halfway view supervision
The halfway view supervision was quite an ambitious method. It would have provided fully unsupervised
depth and color supervision. However, it is not working easily. It seems that the depth inpainting is too
hard to achieve for the generator network. It produces holes in the halfway view because some inpainted
depths are too far away from ground truth. It makes it really easy for the discriminator to ﬁnd out which
image is real or fake. One could probably use some of the tricks described in section 5.3.3 in order
to slow down the discriminator training. However this is just a trick and it is likely that the balancing
parameters would change from a use case to another. It would be nice to ﬁnd a general method that
would be able to balance automatically the training between the generator and the discriminator. This
way, one could use trained network in the regular GAN framework as well as more elaborated ways of
training, such as the halfway view supervision.
One idea towards this could be to use a third neural network that would be trained at the same time in
order to balance the training. This third network would act as a conductor for the two others. It would
have access to a set of actions such as:
- perform a training step for the discriminator
- perform a training step for the generator
- adjust reconstruction loss weight (if any)
That way, the network would be able to control the amount of training each of the other networks
receives. The conductor network could be trained in a reinforcement learning fashion with positive
rewards when the learning of the other two networks is going well and negative rewards otherwise. Of
course, this is only an early sketch of what this method could look like. It would be interesting to ﬁrst
design such a method properly on a simpler problem and then try to apply it on the depth inpainting
problem using the halfway view supervision.

Another, simpler, solution could be to make the task of the discriminator harder, by applying some
random noise on the images (both real and fake) it has to classify. This would probably make the
discriminator training harder but it would give the opportunity to the generator to actually make progress.
Overﬁtting
The same way as we discuss it for depth estimation (see section 6.1.3), it is likely that the inpainting
network overﬁts the scenes from the training set. It seems to perform better on images from the DIML
test set compared to images from an unseen dataset (see Figure 5.11).
6.3
Methodological discussion
From a methodological point of view, a few things could have been done better. These remarks mostly
concern the way we tackled the issues that we encountered and explain what was wrong about them.
Depth estimation
For the depth estimation, we conducted diﬀerent experiments with the aim of showing that the mask loss
was actually useful for this problem. The preliminary results were in agreement with this and reinforced
our a priori, which was: it should work somehow. It was then quite unsettling to ﬁnd out that it was
probably not the case and that mask loss was not helpful. This is not a good way to do research. When
assessing if some hypothesis is true or false, one must put his a priori on the question aside as much
as possible. Otherwise this could bias the outcome. Our mistakes here were ﬁrst to have changed our
judgement on the question with wrong preliminary results. Secondly, to have not realized that the mask
loss was providing a very small supervision and that its eﬀect could take a really long time to inﬂuence
the training. This led to a waste of time trying to make it work anyway, instead of trying to ﬁnd other
ways to improve it or to understand what was wrong with it.
It would have been really interesting to think about the possible outcomes of these experiments in
advance in order to ﬁnd out that some early results could not exclude the possibility that the mask
loss was not working. This would probably have saved a lot of time and energy that could have been
allocated to investigate some other interesting ideas about mask loss (see Section 6.1.1) .
Inpainting
In a similar fashion, we got stuck with the halfway view supervision for quite some time. Being too
conﬁdent that a method should be working might be ineﬃcient as it blinds the mind to think for other
solutions or about why it is not working. This is a shame as understanding this could have given some
insights about how to ﬁnd a solution. This time could then has been used to implement a solution.
Instead, we were only able to sketch up a method that could provide a solution for it.

7.2
Dolly zoom
An interesting thing to note is that the 3D KBE eﬀect is done with a ﬁxed camera (the camera movement
is equivalent to scene motion). This means that the camera is still but also that its parameters are
unchanged. However, plenty of eﬀects (called In-camera eﬀects) in cinema do not follow this rule and
among them the Dolly zoom is quite close to the 3D KBE. The Dolly zoom is a technique that consists
of zooming in one direction while moving the camera in the opposite direction. This maintains the size
of the focused object in the frame constant while changing the perspective of the rest of the scene. This
eﬀect has been introduced for the ﬁrst time in the movie Vertigo by Alfred Hitchcock and is mostly used
to emphasize the confusion or the anxiety of a character. Zooming with an actual camera corresponds
to changing the focal length of the lens. The greater the focal length, the more zoomed-in is the picture.
When recording a video, one can decrease the focal length of the camera while moving forward. It is
possible to balance perfectly the two actions so that the zooming-out from the focal length decrease is
compensated by the zooming-in from the forward motion. With the 3D KBE, we can move the camera
forward, we simply have to change the focal length of the camera during the process. The thing is, how
much should we decrease the focal length to balance the camera motion? It is important to recall what

8
Conclusion
The 3D Ken Burns eﬀect is a quite challenging process as it requires both really accurate depth estimation
and realistic image inpainting. This is therefore a demanding benchmark to assess the performances
of these methods. The quality of eﬀects produced in this work is, in the end, quite satisfactory. It is
in some case better than the original paper [1]. This means that we have succeeded to propose some
improvements both for depth estimation and image inpainting. Yet, there is still room for progress as
both depth estimation and inpainting are still imperfect.
For depth estimation, we proposed an unsupervised loss function that encourages the network to produce
smooth depth maps on objects. Its main goal is to provide the network with some scene understanding by
being able to distinguish diﬀerent objects. In addition, this loss function, being completely unsupervised,
may be computed on any image dataset, hopefully providing more diverse examples to the network. Yet
promising, this idea did not yield signiﬁcant improvements. This is mainly due to the inaccuracy of the
objects segmentation masks. Those were computed by a trained neural network but it did not perform
very well on the chosen datasets. It would be interesting to investigate this idea further. In particular,
using the ground truth masks from a segmentation dataset instead of the prediction of a non-perfectly
trained network could provide interesting results.
For image inpainting, a challenging issue needed to be solved: the lack of data for the problem. In the
speciﬁc case of disocclusion inpainting, ground truth RGB-D images from multiple view are required
to use supervised methods. Some work built their own synthetic dataset to have complete sampling
freedom. Yet, these datasets have only a small variance and are seldom made public. To tackle this,
we proposed a modiﬁed Z-buﬀer algorithm that allows to create disocclusion masks only from a single
view of a scene. Our inpainting network can then be pre-trained in a supervised manner. Though,
this training was not enough to achieve satisfactory inpainting. Therefore, we introduced a reﬁnement
technique based on adversarial training. A discriminator assesses the quality of the inpainting and gives
feedback to the inpainting network. The discriminator architecture must be chosen carefully. Using a
multi-scale architecture as well as perceptual features provided great supervision. This allowed to reﬁne
the already trained networks and achieve state-of-the-art results.
We also found out that using partial convolutions might actually be harmful to perform disocclusion
inpainting. Unlike general image inpainting, disocclusion holes are not localized completely randomly in
the image. Therefore, all the parts of the known image should not be used for the reconstruction. Only
parts of the background should be used to inpaint holes in the background. Partial convolutions do not
allow this distinction and wrongly enforce color propagation into holes.
Even if the ultimate results are not ﬂawless, we proposed and tested methods that improve depth
estimation and image inpainting. Some of them led to actual improvements, some did not and some
results were inconclusive (especially for the mask loss). Yet, in either case we provided avenues for
future research in order to reﬁne these methods further.
Finally, we showcased that the 3D KBE is not the only eﬀect that could be performed using the two
buildings blocks that are depth estimation and disocclusion inpainting. Indeed, changing the camera
parameters during the eﬀect allows to mimic a Dolly zoom. It is likely that other cinematographic
eﬀects could be replicated from a still image by changing other parameters. In particular, allowing
camera rotations would increase the movement diversity and oﬀer more possibilities to the user.
