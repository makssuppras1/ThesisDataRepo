Abstract
A product conﬁguration model is a representation of products to be manu-
factured and sold.
As a result, the model contains a substantial amount of
customer related information and IP. This paper addresses privacy related prob-
lems arises, when the model has to be shared with a third party for testing and
debugging purposes, in our case the third party is Microsoft Development Cen-
ter Copenhagen (MDCC). We perform a detailed analysis of the information
a product conﬁguration model stores with the aim of identifying what mini-
mum information is required and how to anonymize the information such that
a valid testing and debugging can be carried out. we present and discuss dif-
ferent anonymization techniques ﬁtting the diﬀerent types of data stored in the
model. In addition to that, we present and assess an anonymization algorithm
that can help us preserve privacy of the customer while allowing a valid testing
and debugging. Adversary model, privacy goals and utility will be discussed
and formally deﬁned, furthermore the algorithm will be implemented to provide
a proof of concept and validate the anonymized models actually preserves the
privacy goals and utility.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Microsoft Solver Foundation is a .Net library that, among others it contains
languages and algorithms for formalizing and optimizing product conﬁguration
models of manufacturers. Such a model describes the components, attributes
and their relationship, and a description translated in diﬀerent languages to
improve the experience of the authorized users. A product conﬁguration model
is an abstract draft of all the product variants that could be conﬁgured using
it, thus the model contains sensitive information about the manufacturer, its
processes, and IP. As a result, a privacy type problem arises when a product
conﬁguration model should be shared with a third party- which is often necessary
in a collaborative work environment, for instance if there is an external service
provider performing testing and debugging. The main goal of this project thesis
is to ﬁnd a practical way to deal with this privacy problem. To do that we need
to analyze and understand the information stored in the model, and identify
what customer information exactly need to be protected and what information
need to be reviled to allow a valid testing without breaching the privacy of
the customer. In addition to that formally prove the anonymization method
preserves utility and that the method protects the sensitive information in the
model, i.e., that a clever attacker will be unable to combine information in an
unforeseen in order to infer at least parts of the sensitive information. Besides
the conceptual work on deﬁning the anonymization and its properties, there will
be a proof of concept implementation of the algorithm so that functional testing
can be performed (that the transformation does not skew the information in an

1.1
Problem statement
The purpose of the thesis project is to ﬁnd a pragmatic approach to deal with
privacy problem, that arises when product conﬁguration models are shared with
an external service provider for testing and debugging purposes. In this paper
we will explore diﬀerent possibilities to anonymize the models, such that the
anonymized models still allow the same quality of testing as their respective
original models. To do that we ﬁrst need to explore and understand what char-
acteristics of the original model the anonymized model should preserve, which
was’t clear at the beginning of this project.
After that we explore diﬀerent
possibilities to anonymize the models and discuss their advantages and disad-
vantages. At last we suggest a viable solution to the problem and test it.
1.2
Motivation
A product conﬁguration model is a representation of products to be manufac-
tured and sold. As a result, the model contains a substantial amount of customer
related information and IP. Such a model often shared with a third party for
debugging and testing purposes in our case the third party performing the de-
bugging and testing is Microsoft Development Center Copenhagen (MDCC).
MDCC as a service provider wants to create a library of anonymized models
for a testing and debugging purposes. Currently Microsoft gets a time limited
access to the customer owned model and they aren’t allowed to store or use
the models for any analytical purpose. So that, the solution beneﬁt Microsoft
by giving them more analytical freedom over the models, and the customers
(model owners) by giving them a privacy relieve as they will be able to share
anonymized version of their models instead of their original models.
1.3
Contribution
The existing literature primary deals with anonymizing personal data or alike.
To our knowledge there is’t literature dealing with privacy problems related to
product conﬁguration models. We have analyzed the data stored by the models

2.1
Product conﬁguration Models
Product manufacturers want to meet the current demand of their customers.
However, customer demands often ﬂuctuate rapidly from time to time. This
usually is a problem to the traditional manufacturers following the conﬁgure to
stock approach, as they are forced to stock a variant of each product to give
the customers more alternatives. However, this approach is expensive because
it involves higher inventory cost, unsold products and unsatisﬁed customers. In
contrary to that, a manufacturers following a conﬁgure to order approach, care-
ful analysis their products and deﬁne a product family with open speciﬁcations,
from which all the product variants can be conﬁgured by the customer. This
approach is cheaper as it reduces inventory time, unsold product variants and
forecast challenges [Mice]. A product conﬁguration model is a generic represen-
tation of products to be manufactured and can be stored in XML. As it can be
seen in ﬁg A.2, the model is is described hierarchically using a set of compo-
nents, attributes, constraints and calculations stored as elements of the model
in the XML structure.
Normally product conﬁguration models are big with
many nested elements, however in this section we will consider a toy example
to illustrate the structure of a simple product conﬁguration model.

2.1.1
Attribute types
Element Attribute type ﬁg.A.1 has four ﬁelds namely (Name, DataType, IsEnu-
meration, Hasbound). These ﬁelds are used to deﬁne the type of the attributes
used in the model to deﬁne the components. The ﬁeld Name takes a string
value and used to uniquely identify the Attribute type. The ﬁeld DataType
is a number code used to deﬁne the allowed types (Text, Boolean, Integer and
Decimal). Note that, Even though attribute types can be deﬁned to be un-
bounded Integers, texts with out ﬁxed list and decimals, MSF constraint solver
support only bounded integers, ﬁxed text lists, and Boolean values[Micf]. The
ﬁeld IsEnumeration is Boolean and takes 1 or 0. It’s set to 1 if the attribute type
holds a set of enumeration values, and 0 if not. The ﬁeld Hasbound is set to 1 if
the attribute has an upper and/or lower bounds, other wise 0. Enumeration At-
tribute types has additional element “EcoResEnumerationAttributeTypeValue”
used to deﬁne the list.
As it can be seen below the element contains ﬁelds
(OrdinalNumber, EnumerationValue and SolverValue). Ordinal number is just
an integer identiﬁer. EnumerationValue and SolverValue usually take the same
value and will be used in conditions, and constraints. More over there is an
additional element “EcoResTextValueTranslation” used to provide a readable
clariﬁcation. AttributeTypes can also have upper and lower bounds, this in-
formation is captured in the “Hasbound” ﬁeld mentioned above. In such cases
element attribute type have additional element deﬁning the bounds.

2.1.3
Components
Components are basic building blocks of product conﬁguration models. They
capture some generic properties of a product which will be conﬁgured further
during the conﬁguration process. Components are deﬁned only once and can
be used many times by linking together as sub-components[Mice]. To capture
the desired property components use attributes, constraints, sub components
and calculations. As it can be seen in ﬁg. A.5 a component has a Name and a
Reuse Enabled ﬁelds. The Name ﬁeld uses to uniquely identify the component
and the Reuse enabled ﬁeld will dictate if this component can be reused in mul-
tiple product conﬁgurations. In addition to that, a component has a category
translation element to provide a more readable description. A component con-
tains elements Attribute, Component Constraint, Calculation, Sub component,
Template Component, and Component Conﬁguration Nomenclature.
Attributes are components basic building blocks. Attributes used in constraints,
calculations and conditions to deﬁne the features of the components. An at-
tribute could be read only, Mandatory, or Hidden. As it can be seen in ﬁg. A.6,
Attributes has (Name, SolverName, AttributeType, IncludeInReuse) ﬁelds. The
ﬁeld Name uniquely identiﬁes the Attribute. The ﬁeld SolverName will be used
in Calculations, Constraints, and Conditions as a reference to the attribute.
The ﬁeld AttributeType deﬁnes the type of the attribute by referencing element
attribute type using their name. The ﬁeld IncludeInReuse dictates whether the
attribute will be reused by other components. In addition to that attributes
can contain elements EcoResAttributeTranslation and EcoResComponentCon-
trol. Element EcoResAttributeTranslation used improve the readability of the
attributes using it’s ﬁelds by providing friendly name and Description in diﬀerent
languages. Element EcoResComponentControl which also contains another ele-
ment PCExpressionConstraint deﬁnes the constraints enforced in that attribute.

Constraints are relations between variables. They put restrictions on possible
values the variables can take on with in their domain. Constraints are bidirec-
tional and can be enforced in the components through attributes. There are
diﬀerent types of constraints namely equality, dis-equality, Boolean, linear and
arithmetic [oTaEP]. Product conﬁguration models deﬁne constraints as table
constraints and expression constraints. Table constrains are global i.e. they can
be used by all the components during product conﬁguration. On the other hand,
constraints can be deﬁned locally at the Component scope called expression con-
straints. Such constraints only apply to the component and its sub components
it is deﬁned in. As it can be seen in ﬁg. A.7 element ComponentConstraint
contains ﬁelds Name and Description. Field Name uniquely identiﬁes the Com-
ponent Constraint and the Description improves its readability. In addition to
that, Component Constraint contains element PCExpressionConstraint deﬁn-
ing the expression enforced in the attributes. As mentioned above component
constraints can also be table Constraints in such cases it looks like the follow-
ing. As usual PCComponentConstraint contains name and description ﬁelds.
Field Name identiﬁes it uniquely and ﬁeld Description provides a more readable
explanation. Unlike the above mentioned component constraint this constraint
is a table Constraint, as a result it refers to the globally deﬁned table con-
straints. As it can be seen in ﬁg. A.8 element PCComponentConstraint contains
nested elements namely PCTableConstraint, PCTableConstraintColumnCate-
goryAttribute and Attribute. Element PCTableConstraint refers the globally
deﬁned table constraint by its name. Element PCTableConstraintColumnCat-
egoryAttribute refers to the columns of the globally deﬁned table constraints
and lastly the Attribute refers to the attribute where these constraints will be
enforced during the conﬁguration process.
Calculations are deﬁned at the component scope and are not reusable by other
components. Calculations are expressions with more features for example, calcu-

sions. Calculations ﬁg. A.9 has four ﬁelds (Name, Description, TargetAttribute,

scription” improves its readability. Field TargetAttribte used to refer to the
attribute where the result of the calculation will be assigned. Field Expres-
sion deﬁnes the calculation to be solved. For the calculation to be solved all
the required attributes should be available, for example in the expression below
the value of attribute “cq” should be available before we perform any operation
[Mica].
Some components can have sub components, these sub components are compo-
nents already deﬁned in the model and can be reused or called by other compo-
nents. A SubComponent element ﬁg. A.10 has ﬁelds (Name, SolverName, Type,
and ChildComponentClass). The ﬁeld Name is a unique identiﬁer. The ﬁeld

Document Similarity
An area of machine learning and data mining used to measure the similarity
between documents is document analysis. In order to be able to perform the
analysis, documents should be transformed in such away computing the similar-
ity among them is plausible. One approach is using the bag of words, here we
start by collecting all the unique words contained in the corpus (a collection of
documents). The collected set of words represent the overall vocabulary in the
corpus. Then we use the vocabulary to create a document term matrix [THM19].
From the document matrix diﬀerent methods could be used to compute the sim-
ilarity between the documents for example Jaccard, Cosine similarity, extended
Jaccard . . . etc. In this paper Jaccard will be used as a similarity measurement.
However, all document similarity measurements can be used as a valid measure-
ments. Note that in our case we are interested in measuring the similarity of
the anonymized model to the original model or vise versa, but not on measuring
the similarity among them which would require creating a vocabulary from both
models. Thus, our vocabulary should be generated using only the anonymized
model, such that model key words, key values and digits are removed. In ad-

available just represent it with 1. This will insure us our document vectors are

3.1.3
Function Transformation
Among others, product conﬁguration models contain constraints and calcula-
tions, which are Arithmetic/Boolean expressions used to describe the relation-
ship among the attributes. In this section we will be focusing in anonymiz-
ing arithmetic expressions deﬁned with in bounded integer/decimal domains.
Anonymizing expressions with out losing the relationship between the attributes
deﬁning the constraints can be done by transforming the domains of the at-
tributes, and their relationship represented using the constraint or calculations.
The most convenient transformations to be applied in the calculations and con-
straints are Scaling and shifting.
Ideally scaling can be performed using an
arbitrary positive integer diﬀerent from zero and 1 and shifting can be per-
formed using an arbitrary integer diﬀerent from zero, and the operations can
be performed in any order. Functional transformation can be viewed as a way
of masking the domains by increasing their space and shifting their location,
and then unmasking them in the constraints, and calculations where the do-
mains are used. The importance of unmasking them is to preserve the original
relationship between the questioned attributes.
The process of transforming
model M can be done in two ways. The ﬁst approach is to check the variables
in the expression and retrieve and remove their transforming constants already
present in the expression. Then transform the domains accordingly to compen-
sate the noise removed from the expressions. This approach allows us to usually
have parent functions in the calculations, such that the domains are adjusted
accordingly. For example, let’s assume we have a relation R having domains
x ∈[0, 4], and y ∈[0, 8] and a constraint 2x == y. After transformation
the domains of R’ will be x′ ∈[0, 8], and y ∈[0, 8] and the constraint will be
x == y. Unfortunately, this approach doesn’t work in constraints because of
the restriction that constraints domains can’t be decimals. So that, this ap-
proach doesn’t guarantee the expression to be parent functions. The second
approach is to apply a random scaling and shifting transformations in any order
in the original domains and then apply the inverse of the applied transformation
in the constraints and calculations to compensate that. Both approaches have
the same property, however the second approach is more ﬂexible as it doesn’t
restrict the transformation by the amount of transformation noise available in
the expressions, for example consider a case where the expression is a parent
function or relation.
Shifting Shifting is a process of relocating the domain space from its origi-
nally deﬁned location to a new random location. For instance, if the original
domain was deﬁned in the range of [0, 5] shifting the domain by 10 relocate
it to [−10, −5], or [10, 15]. Shifting has an advantage of hiding the domains
however it doesn’t hide the size of the domains. For instance, if we consider a
relation R between attributes x and y such that their domains are [0, 5] and
[0, 10] and their relation is deﬁned using an expression 2x == y. Transform-
==PAGE:34==
24

between attributes x′ and y′, such that their domains are [3, 8] and [5, 15]
and their relation is deﬁned using an expression 2x′ == y′ + 1. If we compare
the two relations, they are similar but operating in diﬀerent domains. Thus,
the domains are safely hidden using only shifting given that the original rela-
tion R is unknown to us. To re identify the original relation R from the new
relation R′ requires identifying the shifting factor 3 and 5 which are randomly
chosen integers. Note that, these factors are hidden in the expression deﬁning
the relation R′ by combining the two shifting factors. In the case where we have
expression with multiple bounded attributes we need to make sure the shifting
factors are combined together. For instance, lets assume we have a relation R
between attributes {a, b, x, y, z} deﬁned in some domain(in this example the
domains is ignored for simplicity), Such that their relation is deﬁned using a
constraint a + b = x + y + z. Transforming the relation R using a shifting
factors 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 respectively results in a relation R′ deﬁned in some domain,
with a constraint a + b = x + y + z + 20. As it can be seen in the trans-
formed expressions combining the shifting factors helps us to hide the factors
from becoming visible in the expressions. The only leaking information is the
summation of the shifting factors, in the second case for example the shifting
factors sum up to 20.
Scaling In most cases shifting can be considered as a safe approach to hide
the domains of the bounded attributes, however in rare cases we could have
customers don’t want to revile even the size of their domains for some reason.
In such cases scaling could be considered as an option, scaling can be deﬁned
as a process of resizing the size of the original domains to a new random size.
Ideally applying scaling to functions and relations would give us an interesting
transformation, such that the size of the domains and the domains are hidden
among other random fake domains. For instance, let’s assume the original do-
main was deﬁned in the range of [0, 5], scaling the domain by 5 would increase
the size of the original domain by a factor of 5, thus the new domain will be
[0,25]. Scaling can be considered as a technique of complementing shifting by
providing a way of hiding the domain size. However, in most cases scaling is
not convenient to use as a way of transforming product conﬁguration model’s
constraints and relations, this is due to the fact the conﬁgurator has to deal with
more domains resulting in performance degrade. Moreover, the third party per-
forming the test requires a detailed information regarding the domains where
the model is regressing, otherwise performing a valid test in the transformed
model would no longer be possible due to the fake domains.
Using scaling alone usually leaks some information regarding the scaling factor,
for instance in the above example a scaling factor 5 transformed the domains
to [0,25], revealing the scaling factor is a multiple of 5. However such problems
could be avoided by combining scaling and shifting as a transformation method
in the cases where we want to hide both the size of the domain and the domain
boundaries. However, the scaling factor should be somehow controlled and se-

Domain size of bounded attributes The size of domains of bounded at-

degrade doesn’t prevent a valid testing and debugging. On the other hand, if we
consider the performance degrade introduced by scaling. It’s diﬃcult to quan-

domains are involved. Theoretically, one can expect the performance degrade
to be proportional to the scaling factor, for instance if the domains are scaled
by 2 then the solver is expected to handle a domain of double size.

The following attack model assumes the auxiliary knowledge about the original

anonymized models given that he doesn’t know their respective original model.
This restriction automatically removes a substantial amount of attacks related
to size, structure, performance, keywords and expressions of the original model
the adversary would be able to perform. In the following sections we will see
what the adversary can and can’t do with focus in bounded and unbounded

4.2.1
Attack on bounded domains
Bounded domains are numeric domains with speciﬁc upper and lower bound-
aries. These domains are subjected to transformation using scaling and shifting
as a way of hiding the domain boundaries and adding fake domains. In the fol-
lowing we will discuss about the diﬀerent attacks the adversary can perform on
transformed domains. For simplicity, we will consider a single method at a time
and see whether the adversary can learn something out of it. For example, we
apply only shifting on a model with parent relation and try to reverse it. Note
that, the following attack can only be mounted in the cases where the original
model contains only parent function/relations as calculations/constraints. Note
that the attacker can only guise and doesn’t know whether his guise is right,
because of limited the auxiliary knowledge.
Shifting factor attack A shifting factor is supposed to be a perfect random
integer in constraints but a random number in calculations. The main aim of
this factor is to hide the size and the boundaries of the domains, by transforming
the domain space of the original domains to a random space. For example, let’s
assume we have domains x ∈[0, 4], and y ∈[0, 8] and a constraint 2x == y.
After transformation using shifting factors 3, 4 for x and y respectively, and
tr = x −c and Tr′ = x + c (c is a shifting factor). The transformed model M’
will look like x ∈[−3, 1], and y ∈[−4, 4] and the constraint will be 2x == y+1.
In order to reverse the M ′ to M the adversary is required to ﬁnd, the factors
by which x and y is shifted. That means the adversary should some how be
able to guise two number with a diﬀerence 1. This problem has inﬁnitely many
solutions and guessing the right solution out of the inﬁnite solutions is diﬃcult.
Thus, shifting can be considered as a safe approach to hide the upper and lower
bounds.
Scaling factor attack In the ideal case the scaling factor should be a perfect
random integer or number in constraints and calculations respectively. How-
ever, scaling inﬂuences the performance of the model, as the solver is supposed
to deal with more domains. The hiding property of scaling relies on combin-
ing the factors by simplifying them in the expressions. For example, let’s as-
sume we have domains x ∈[0, 4], and y ∈[0, 4] and a constraint x == y.
After transformation using scaling factors 4, 6 for x and y respectively, and
tr = bx and Tr′ = x/b (b is a scaling factor). The transformed model M ′ will
look like x ∈[0, 16], and y ∈[0, 24] and the constraint will be x/2 == y/3. In
order to reverse the M’ to M the adversary is required to ﬁnd, the factors by
which x and y is scaled. One may argue, these factors can be seen in the con-
straint. However, this is’t true as the adversary doesn’t have any idea whether
these factors are simpliﬁed or not. Instead the adversary is supposed to deal

with equation x/y = 2/3. This equation again has inﬁnitely many solutions
{2/3, 4/6, 6/9, . . . etc}. The above-mentioned example is the simplest case in-

other cases. In the cases where a model doesn’t contain a parent functions or

Delta attack Since we are releasing diﬀerent anonymized versions of the same
model the adversary can use size, structure, performance, keywords and expres-
sions to ﬁlter all anonymized versions of a single model from a collection of
anonymized models. Once the attacker has diﬀerent anonymized versions of a
single model the attacker might try to infer how the original model has been
evolving, by analyzing the diﬀerence between the diﬀerent anonymized version
of the model. However, the attack doesn’t revile any interesting information
regarding the evolution of the original model or any secret about the original
model domains. For instance, let’s consider the following scenario. MV 1 is ver-
sion 1 of the original model and MV 2 is version 2 of the original model. On
the other hand, MV 1′ and MV 2′ are the anonymized version of original models
MV 1 and MV 2 respectively. δ1 is the diﬀerence between the bounded domains
and expressions in MV 1 and MV 2 and δ2 is the diﬀerence between the bounded
domains and expressions in MV 1′ and MV 2′. By theorem 2.3 the relations be-

domains deﬁned in MV 1′ and the relations between the domains deﬁned in MV 2
is equivalent to the relations between the domains deﬁned in MV 2′. However,
δ2 = δ1 + the random noise. This implies the adversary steal requires to ﬁnd
the random scaling and shifting factors added. Which is as diﬃcult as trying
to learn about the domain boundaries of original model from the anonymized
version, with no access to diﬀerent anonymized versions of the original model.

Data
Classifying all attributes of product conﬁguration model into categories accord-
ing to their sensitivity is impractical. This is due to the fact the model contains
many redundant attributes having similar functionality but used in diﬀerent el-
ements with diﬀerent level of sensitivity. For example, both elements attributes
and components contain a parameter Name, which is an identiﬁer in both cases
at the element scope. But being an identiﬁer in attribute scope has not the same
sensitivity as being an identiﬁer in component scope. On the other hand, one
may argue that learning the attributes would allow as learning the components,
which are more sensitive. Another good example is elements attributes and at-
tribute types. Attribute types describe the ingredients attributes are made of,
and Attribute types are among the sensitive parameters, thus attributes should
also be considered as a sensitive parameter. The main issue here is the model
is deﬁned using a hierarchy of elements. As a result, if we deﬁne the base of the
hierarchy as a sensitive attribute all elements located higher in the hierarchy
should also be considered as sensitive to ensure the information doesn’t leak
there. The following ﬁgure can be considered as a simple overview of a model
structure.

4.6
Utility
All most all the time, there is a trade oﬀbetween privacy and utility. Ideally, we
want to remove all customer related information including the logic deﬁning the
relationship between the attributes. However, this is impossible as the utility
will degrade such that testing and solving customer related issues is’t possible
anymore. Thus, we need to ﬁnd a balance between the two requirements. In
this paper given the privacy goals 4.1 is satisﬁed the utility we want to preserve
is the following. Note that in this section original model is referring to the base
model which is the compressed version of the original model
1. The semantics of the original model, and the relation between the at-
tributes and components deﬁned in the original model using calculations,
constraints and conditions should be preserved.
2. Optionally the performance of the original base model should be preserved,
i.e. if the original model has performance issue supposed to be tested by
the third party.
Before we start discussing the above mentioned utility requirements, we will ﬁrst
discuss bounded and unbounded domains. For simplicity we will consider all do-
mains with out boundary as unbounded domains regardless of their type. This
includes numeric and non numeric enumerations, unbounded integers and deci-
mals. In the case of numeric and non-numeric enumeration domains consistency
preserving replacement 3.1.2 is applied. On the other hand, unbounded integer
and decimal domains don’t need any special treatment, since they don’t leak
any sensitive information regarding their domain space. The only leaking infor-
mation is that the attribute has an integer/decimal domain ranging (∞, −∞).
Bounded domains represent integers and decimals with upper and lower bound-
ary. Bounded domains are sensitive as a result they should be transformed such
that theorem 2.3 is satisﬁed. Note that utility requirement 1 applied to un-
bounded domains doesn’t need a formal treatment. This is due to the fact, the
statement represent non numeric value, and numeric enumerations, which could
easily be sanitized using Replacement technique 3.1.2. However, utility require-
ment 2 applies to bounded domains, and it’s considered preserved if and only if

Following is the analysis of the run time complexity of the algorithm. Once the
xml document is loaded, it will be optimally stored in a hash table, such that the
hashed name of the elements is used as a key. This gives us a complexity of O(1)
in ﬁnding and accessing the elements. The algorithm has two critical steps the
ﬁrst step is parsing the xml and the second step is manipulating the data stored
there. During the parsing step we explore elements to create a dictionary of
old and new values, as discussed in the algorithm step 1 4.7. This step requires
visiting most of the elements in the xml thus has a complexity of O(n). The
next step is manipulating the xml this step includes Replacing attribute values
using the map created in the previous step, Removing elements and attribute
values and Transforming expressions. Replacing attribute values using the hash
map generated in parsing step and removing the unnecessary elements and at-
tribute values requires visiting all the xml elements thus it has a complexity of
O(n). In addition to that, all the expressions containing a bounded attribute are
subjected for transformation step. In order to be able to transform expression,
they should be parsed ﬁrst. Parsing an expression of size m has a complexity
of O(m · log(m)). Thus, the complexity of the algorithm is O(n + m · log(m)).

Conclusion
This paper is aimed at developing an anonymization algorithm to solve privacy
related problems emerging, when customers must share their product conﬁgu-
ration models with a third party for testing and debugging. In order to be able
to do that, we made a detailed analysis of the information stored in product
conﬁguration models with the aim of determining what sensitive information a
model stores, what minimum information is required to perform a valid testing
and debugging and how can we anonymize the minimum required information
such that a valid testing and debugging can be performed. Based in the analysis
it can be concluded that the methods discussed in section methodology, namely
Compression, Replacement and Transformation could be used to successfully re-
move all customer related information and IP. Compression simply removed the
non-required elements, on the other hand Replacement keeps the required ele-
ments and attributes and replaces them with a random string such that certain
properties are preserved 4.6. By applying just the two techniques (Compression
and Replacement) we can be sure that the model doesn’t contain even a single
word typed by the customer/owner of the model, Compression and Replace-
ment actually provide a perfect anonymization. Transformation is applied to
bounded domains such that certain properties are preserved 2.3, this technique
hides the domains of the bounded attributes and their size as required. In addi-
tion to that a prototype algorithm 4.7 using the above mentioned approach as an
anonymization technique is designed and implemented with the aim of providing
a proof of concept. The functionality of the algorithm is tested by anonymiz-

ing a sample testing models and proven the validity of the approach, that the
privacy goals 4.1 are preserved and that a valid testing and debugging can be
performed using only the anonymized models 5. The algorithm discussed above
suﬃce as an anonymization approach under the assumption 4.1. However, the
approach could be extended further such that an adversary with a considerable
knowledge of the original model can’t infer any interesting information from the

Feature Improvements
Algorithm 4.7 could be further improved to ensure an adversary with a consid-
erable auxiliary knowledge of the original model doesn’t learn any thing inter-
esting from the anonymized model. One way to achieve that could be to some
how deal with the quasi identiﬁers namely model size, expressions, structure,
keywords, and key values. For instance, if we consider boolean expressions the
current approach is only suppressing the identiﬁers variables. Even though, this
method gives us a certain level of anonymization, the adversary can steal learn
the structure of the expression, which could be used together with other aux-
iliary information to identify the model. This structure should be removed to
mitigate attacks like ﬁltering models with the same boolean expression struc-
ture. To do this model boolean expressions may need to be manipulated, such
that they produce the same result as the original expression but written diﬀer-
ently. One way to do that could be by applying Demorgan rule, adding dummy
attributes, standardization, and re writing expressions like x →y to its equiv-
alent form ¬x ∨y. These techniques could be applied in random order to all
boolean expression available in the model. Demorgan rule can be used as a
method to convert operators like ∨to ∧or vise versa, and it can be used in
combination with other techniques. For instance consider a model contains an
expression x →y. The expression can be rewritten to its equivalent form ¬x∨y,
and then Demorgan rule can be applied to mask it further in to ¬x∧¬y. In ad-
dition to that one could add dummy attributes and include them in the boolean
expressions. Other method could be rewriting the boolean expressions in to

in random fashion knowing the algorithm doesn’t help the adversary reversing
