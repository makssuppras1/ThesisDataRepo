Opening statement from Author 
In order to reach the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SGDs) and to evaluate 
policies promoting clean technologies, there is a need for a robust tool to estimate the 
environmental impacts. Such tools can help with decision-making in important sectors such as 
transport and energy. Decision-making may lack accountability when we can’t measure 
environmental pressures, as there is an old saying - “What gets measured gets managed”. We 
need to use the available tools to measure the environmental impacts in a wide spectrum of 
contexts while decision-making and framing policies catering to the protection of the 
environment. To have a comprehensive understanding of the challenges faced by our planet, 
we need to include other under reported problems. We are in brink of crossing some crucial 
tipping points of our planet such as precipitated decline in biodiversity, increased ocean 
acidification, and excessive fertilizer applications rates leading to nutrients concentration 
overloading in aquatic ecosystems.  
Life cycle assessment, LCA, offers tool to analyse the whole life cycle of products and 
services, and it covers a wide range of impacts, that can be used to perform quantitative 
sustainability assessments. The impacts covered depend on the choice of method, however, 
impacts such as ‘climate change’, ‘ocean acidification’ and ‘atmospheric aerosol loading’ are 
common impacts quantified across methods. The primary focus of LCA studies have been in 
quantifying the environmental impacts, however recent research have explored methods to 
quantify the economic and societal benefits (Life Cycle Costing and Social LCAs). LCAs play 
a central role in environmental regulations in many regions and it is certified according to ISO 
14040 and ISO 14044 standards. Policy makers, scientist and industries could view the 
solutions with a life-cycle perspective and more importantly when making a decision, they 
could consider the trade-offs between the different environmental impacts based on a wider 
consultation with stakeholders and influencers.  
In order to evaluate such polices, one could use either an ex-ante or ex-post type of 
assessment. Ex-post assessment evaluates the outcomes of already implemented policies and 
ex-ante assessment evaluates the outcomes before implementing a policy. In this report, ex-
ante assessment would be used to evaluate the policy package provided to Tonga. The small 
developing island states in the South Pacific face recurring and long-term challenges in meeting 
their rising energy demands, due to high cost and fluctuations in the fossil fuel market. Having 
a single source of energy based on fossil fuels, which are being imported from the market, 
place these small island states in a vulnerable position. 
==PAGE:4==
A policy package has distinct targets and goals that are determined based on literature 
review, stakeholder consultation, and arriving at a consensus with the concerned departments 
representing the important sectors. The United Nations’ Climate Technology Centre and 
Network (CTCN) worked closely with Tongan Department of Energy to develop a Tonga 
Energy Efficiency Master Plan (TEEMP). The TEEMP policy package, to reduce the carbon 
emissions of Tonga, has a list of polices related to the transport and energy sector. These two 
sectors contribute to about 70% of the global carbon emissions. The sectors identified by the 
International Energy Agency and their respective share of CO2 emissions in the World for 
2016 are Electricity & heat production (49,04%), Transport (20,45%), Manufacturing 
industries & construction (19,96%), Residential building & commercial and public services 
(8,60%), and other sectors (1,96%) (Ritchie & Roser, 2019).  
The functional unit here is meeting the energy and transport needs of the people in 
Tonga for a given year (2018 and 2030). The system model of the transport and energy sector 
of Tonga includes important processes such as road construction and maintenance, transport 
fuel production, vehicle fleet manufacturing, fuel and vehicle shipping freights, and Tonga 
electricity. A Life cycle inventory model is built linking the processes that are needed to fulfil 
the functional unit offered. For a comprehensive assessment of the transport sector, experts 
argue the need to include infrastructure, vehicle production, and fuel production. Chester et al. 
considered an inclusive approach to model the transport sector in the US based on inventory 
available for automobiles, and concludes with 63% additional GHGs emissions associated with 
road transport over the conventional method of only considering vehicle tailpipe operation 
(Chester & Horvath, 2009). Such an approach was used to model the transport sector. The 
different processes were grouped based on life-cycle stages such as vehicle operation, 
infrastructure, and maintenance and transport fuel production, to have a comprehensive 
understanding of the different share of impacts caused by the modelled life-cycle stages. 
To evaluate a prospective policy assessment, we consider four scenarios. The Baseline 
scenario is used as a starting case to identify the hotspots in the sectors and identify important 
impact categories to focus. The Business-as-usual scenario considers the increases in 
consumption in transport and energy needs from 2018 until 2030 and includes no changes in 
technology or energy efficiencies. In brief terms, no intervention in terms of policy nor changes 
in consumer choices. This scenario is used as a reference to calculate the potential reduction in 
carbon emissions from the transport and energy sector considering reaching the targets set in 
the TEEMP. Finally, in an attempt to reach an absolute sustainable state, a transformational 
==PAGE:5==
change case is considered with changes such as phasing out diesel vehicles, electrification in 
passenger transport and 100% renewables in the energy grid.  
Transport and energy are important sectors where we are witnessing increasing scrutiny 
in terms of regulation and policies. There are also constructions of large-scale power plants and 
expansion of existing transport infrastructure in developing countries. Developing and under 
developed countries have traditionally not played an important role as a contributor of carbon 
emissions until recently, with the emergence of countries like China and India. Such countries 
have a potential to leap frog traditional methods of carbon intensive activities.  
Countries can use available tools such as LCAs to ensure the level of policy targets 
necessitated to meet their carbon emission targets. Countries and city municipalities are taking 
actions to reduce local air pollution, such as phasing out diesel vehicles and limiting its use in 
public spaces. Large scale projects such as improving public transportation, banning single use 
plastics, construction of high speed rail networks, electrification of their public and private 
transport services, building smart cities, and so forth. These are inspiring examples and cities 
alike need to pick and choose policies catering to address their local environmental concerns. 
For instance, water shortage and prolonged drought is a rising concern is cities, where the 
impacts associated with freshwater use would have relevance. In certain regions, there are 
concerns about air pollution and related health concerns, land use change in cities, rising sea 
levels and prolonged heat waves. The environmental impacts calculated such as ‘atmospheric 
aerosol loading’, ‘climate change’ and ‘ozone layer depletion’ could be used as a reference 
indicator to improve decision-making. To help with this, LCAs can provide quantitative scores 
for important impacts, and locally stakeholders and decision-makers can prioritize their 
concerns. Not every policy would bring forth the same level of benefits when having a life 
cycle approach, due to differences in energy mix, availability of resources, and rate of 
consumption and on the reference system considered to compare the impact scores.  
Some scientist argue we are leaving behind the Holocene epoch, which began some 
11,700 years ago after the last major ice age and are entering a new epoch, Anthropocene. Our 
current rate in consumption of resources has far exceeded than previously recorded. Methods 
of integrating absolute sustainability in environmental impact assessments frameworks is novel 
and has been done in a few studies and need further research and wider acceptance (Cole, 
Bailey, & New, 2014; Fanning & O’Neill, 2016) .  
This thesis work is written to continue the existing work of others contributing to the 
field of absolute environmental sustainability using tools such as LCAs. By using an illustrative 
example of a country attempting to implement policies to reduce their climate change impacts 
==PAGE:6==
and evaluating such polices with an environmental impact assessment methodology. It is a 
study of the absolute sustainability of the transport and energy sector in Tonga, and an article 
titled “Using planetary boundaries framework to evaluate transport and energy sector 
exceedance from safe operating space in LCA contexts” is written as a deliverable for my 
Master Thesis. The data needed for modelling is based on The United Nations’ Climate 
Technology Centre and Network (CTCN) report written in consultation with the Tongan 
Department of Energy. The results of this LCA study is compared with CTCN’s estimates to 
support the use of environmental impact assessments in boarder contexts. The method 
developed here using the planetary boundaries framework can add value and support to the 
Transformational Change Guidance developed for the Initiative for Climate Action 
Transparency (ICAT) Project.

Abstract 
To improve the relevance of environmental impact assessments in policy and decision-making 
by integrating the concept of absolute sustainability into traditional Life Cycle Assessment 
(LCA) contexts. A methodology to evaluate the absolute sustainability of the transport and 
energy sector of a country is developed. The environmental impacts are calculated using the 
Planetary boundaries-based Life cycle impact assessment method and the  planetary boundaries 
are downscaled to a country level to calculate the exceedance of these sectors from their share 
of safe operating space. To illustrate the method developed, a case study is used to estimate the 
environmental impacts from the transport and energy sector of Tonga. The Baseline 2018, 
scenario has exceeded its share of safe operating space using equal per capita sharing principle 
in two impact categories, ‘climate change – energy imbalance’ and ‘ocean acidification’. The 
Business-as-usual 2030, scenario has exceeded its share in five impact categories. The Tonga 
Energy Efficiency Master Plan (TEEMP) 2030, a scenario constructed using policy targets, is 
within its safe operating space for all but one impact category, ‘climate change – energy 
imbalance’. A Transformational change scenario is modelled to further reduce the impacts as 
an attempt to reach an absolute sustainable case for these sectors. A sensitivity and uncertainty 
==PAGE:12==
analysis of the model input parameters using Monte Carlo method were performed. The 
policies under the TEEMP has reduced all the environmental impacts except ‘climate change 
– energy imbalance’ to be within the share of safe operating space. The current model estimates 
a 40% reduction in kg CO2 eq in TEEMP compared to the Business-as-usual scenario and a 
65% reduction in kg CO2 eq using the Transformational change scenario.

1. 
Introduction  
There is a need to improve the relevance of environmental footprints in sustainability 
assessment frameworks (Fanning & O’Neill, 2016). An array of footprint based indicators such 
as – carbon, water, and biodiversity - have spawned to ensure accountability for the different 
environmental pressures facing the planet (Fang, Heijungs, & De Snoo, 2015). Sustainability 
assessment frameworks are used to support decision-making and policies in broad 
environmental, social, and economic contexts (Fang et al., 2015). The increased environmental 
pressures on our planet due to existing consumption and production patterns call for a more 
comprehensive system-level change (Sala, Ciuffo, & Nijkamp, 2015). System-level changes 
are needed to move away from conventional carbon-intensive sources of energy development 
toward clean technologies (Maxwell, 2013), and such practices are needed to ensure 
compliance and alignment with international agreements like the Paris Agreement (Hardisty, 
Clark, & Hynes, 2012).  
There is increasing evidence on the present state of our planet, such as the mass of the 
Greenland ice sheet and Artic sea-ice cover being near or may have already passed the tipping 
points (United Nations Convention on Climate Change, 2015). Lenton et al. describe tipping 
==PAGE:13==
points as “a critical threshold at which a tiny perturbation can qualitatively alter the state or 
development of a system.” The increasing environmental pressures on the planet have led the 
discussion to define the biophysical thresholds and revisiting these thresholds have gained 
momentum over the past decade. The recent attempts at defining and formulating such safe 
limits for the different environmental pressures have led to the planetary boundaries concept 
(Lenton, 2012; Lenton et al., 2008). The planetary boundaries (PBs) framework proposes 
environmental limits, such as ‘atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration’ and ‘atmospheric 
aerosol loading’ to define a safe operating space (SOS) within which humanity needs to limit 
its environmental pressures. The PBs recently updated by (Rockström et al., 2009), show two 
of the nine boundaries – ‘biogeochemical flows’ and ‘biosphere integrity’ to have exceeded 
the SOS significantly, terming them as in a high-risk zone, whereas the boundaries for the 
‘climate change’ and ‘land system change’ are in critical condition with an increased risk 
(Steffen et al., 2015). The concentration of greenhouse gases has been identified as a driver for 
the climate change planetary boundary, and the main sectors identified as contributing to the 
impacts are fossil fuels in energy and transport (Sterner et al., 2019). The transport and energy 
sector activities and their related pressures are identified as important drivers for the planetary 
boundaries such as ‘climate change’, ‘ocean acidification’, and ‘atmospheric aerosol loading’ 
(Sterner et al., 2019).  
To analyse the goals of policies, such as in the transport and energy sector, it should 
include the impacts occurring throughout the life cycle of the system (Sterner et al., 2019). By 
performing a Life cycle assessment (LCA), there is a comprehensive understanding of the 
different impacts related to the influence of policy. LCA as a tool is used to quantify the 
environmental impacts (or pressures) for a system. Proponents for the application of LCA in 
policy decision encourage considering LCAs at different levels (Chester & Horvath, 2009). It 
could be at a product, organizational, consumer/lifestyle, or a country level. The transport and 
energy sector contribute to about 70% of the global greenhouse gas emissions (Hellweg & 
Canals, 2014).  
There are studies published on the relevance of implementing life cycle thinking (LCT) 
into public policy assessment (Ritchie & Roser, 2019). The Life Cycle Initiative defines LCT 
as going beyond the traditional focus on just the production site, to include environmental, 
social, and economic impacts over the entire life-cycle of the system.  Many barriers have been 
identified in the application of LCT in public policy (Hauschild, Rosenbaum, & Olsen, 2017; 
Seidel, 2016). Suggestions were to have a normative approach, including stakeholders from 
the initial stage of the sustainability assessment (Cole et al., 2014; Dandres, Gaudreault, Seco, 
==PAGE:14==
& Samson, 2014). Public policymaking has been identified as a potential direct application of 
LCA (Bey, 2017). The European Union (EU) also recommends using LCT as a plausible 
approach in the future, and there is a growing need to identify the applicability of LCAs on real 
policy decisions (Hauschild et al., 2017).  
The Initiative for Climate Action Transparency (ICAT) was founded to support 
improved transparency and capacity building under the Paris Agreement. The ICAT team, 
along with UNEP-DTU Partnership, have developed a Transformational Change (TC) 
guidance (Koneczny, Bersani, Pennington, & Wolf, 2007). The purpose of the guidance is to 
assist decision-makers to assess transformational potential and the impact of policies and 
actions to reduce GHG emissions. Transformational change according to the guidance is 
defined as “A fundamental, sustained change of a system that disrupts established high-carbon 
practices and contributes to a zero-carbon society in line with the Paris Agreement’s 1.5-2 °C 
temperature goal and the UN Sustainable Development Goals Changes”.  
The aim of this study is estimating the environmental impacts of the transport and 
energy sector using the Planetary Boundaries-based Life Cycle Impact Assessment 
methodology (PB-LCIA) (UNEP DTU Partnership & WRI, 2018). It is applied to a  case study 
assessing the "Development of a Tonga Energy Efficiency Master Plan (TEEMP)"(Morten 
Walbech Ryberg, 2018). The TEEMP includes a package of policies focused on the transport 
and energy sector of Tonga. An introduction to the case study is available in ‘SM 1. 
Introduction to case study’. The transformational change potential of the TEEMP would be 
estimated, and a hypothetical transformational change scenario is modelled to help decision-
makers envisage a pathway needed to ensure absolute environmental sustainability. 
2. 
Materials and Methods 
2.1 Absolute sustainability assessment 
LCAs are used for environmental evaluation of products and systems, however, as a relative 
assessment framework in comparative assessments, i.e., to identify hotspots in the life-cycle or 
chose between two systems having a lower environmental impact. This kind of assessment 
cannot be used to indicate the absolute sustainability of either of the systems compared (Morten 
W. Ryberg et al., 2018). To convey information regarding the ability of Earth system processes 
to cope with the burdens from human activities, we need to couple traditional LCAs with 
absolute sustainability measures. Measures such as carrying capacity and planetary 
==PAGE:15==
boundaries of the Earth system are used here to support such absolute sustainability 
assessments (Bjørn, Margni, Roy, Bulle, & Hauschild, 2016). 
 PBs can aid to determine the limit and track the transgression of an individual boundary 
based on the carrying capacities of the planet . Carrying capacity is the maximum sustained 
population that can be supported indefinitely by the environment without transgressing the safe 
operating space (Fanning & O’Neill, 2016). Applying the PBs framework with SOS principles 
can emphasize the relevance of environmental footprints in evaluating outcomes of policies in 
the long term. 
 
The concept used for the analysis of transport and energy is shown in Figure 1. In this 
concept a system is considered to be within the safe operating space when the calculated 
impacts (Ii) using the PB-LCIA method are less than 100% of the threshold determined by the

level of overshoot above the safe operating space. An impact category is considered to be a 
hotspot if the exceedance value, Ei>1. The safe operating space (SOSi), where i represents each 
of the impact categories, and this can be attributed as the space allocated for human activities, 
and the exceedance values inform us the magnitude of overshoot. Share of safe operating space,

2.1.2 Planetary Boundaries-based Life Cycle Impact Assessment 
The PB-LCIA described by Ryberg et al. (2018) includes characterization models (or 
characterisation factors) for 15 impact categories considering respective global and sub-global 
PBs defined as in Steffen et al. (2015), with the exception of ‘change in biosphere integrity’ 
and ‘introduction of novel entities’ since the PBs are yet to be defined. In traditional LCA 
framework, the system (or sector) is modelled based on the life cycle of the product or service 
in consideration. As illustrated in Figure 2, the Life cycle inventory (LCI) modelling of such a 
system include elementary flow and process exchanges in the system irrespective of its 
temporal scope (when the environmental pressure occurs), and these would be integrated over 
the entire life-time as a single pulse input to the model. However, in PB-LCIA method, the 
elementary flows are based on continuous constant inputs per year needed to fulfil the 
functional unit. Having a mass per year input method has advantages compared to using 
integrated mass for the entire life-cycle as a single input. Using this method, we consider an 
infinite temporal scope, i.e., an infinite time. The results provided by this method thereby can 
be interpreted as providing the functional unit for an infinite period, and the results can be thus 
used as metrics to evaluate the absolute environmental sustainability of the system considered.

2.2 Life cycle inventory: 
The LCAs of the transport and energy sector is carried out following the ISO 14040:2006 and 
ISO 14044:2006 standards. The functional unit is the annual consumption of power (or 
electricity generated) and road transport services of the residents in Tonga. The road transport 
services considering a life-cycle approach include the road construction and maintenance, 
vehicle fleet production and maintenance, transport fuel production and distribution, tail-pipe 
emissions from vehicle kilometre travelled, non-exhaust emissions such as tire and brake wear 
and finally the shipping freight requirements of the vehicle, crude and the transport fuel used. 
The electricity consumption includes both the commercial and residential loads predicted by 
the Tonga Power Limited (TPL). The reference flow is the amount of road transport services 
and electricity needed for a given year.  The system is modelled using the software tool Simapro 
9.0 using the Ecoinvent 3.4 database. The data used to model the life cycle inventory is based 
on the CTCN’s report submitted to the Government of Tonga.  
2.2.1 System boundaries and modelling framework 
Based on International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) recommendations, the LCI 
modelling framework used is Attributional, and the multifunctional processes are handled 
using allocation when possible, with the modelling of background systems using average 
processes. The choice of processes from the Ecoinvent database was allocation (cut-off) based. 
The modelled system has processes occurring inside and some outside the boundary of Tonga. 
The road construction and maintenance, vehicle kilometre travelled, non-exhaust emissions, 
vehicle fleet maintenance, and electricity generation occur in the geographic scope of Tonga. 
However, the transport fuel production, vehicle fleet production, and shipping freight of the 
crude, fuel, and vehicles occur in other countries and the open ocean. The system for the 
transport and electricity sector, shown in Figure 3 The system boundary showing the 
foreground processes modelled for the transport and energy sector using a life-cycle approach, 
has the foreground processes needed with the following as its data input: 
1. 
Road construction and maintenance (RC&M): The length of the road network 
available in Tonga and maintenance input is based on the demand for the road 
network.  
2. 
Vehicle fleet production (VFP): Vehicle fleet data is used by dividing the 
number of vehicles with their respective lifetime 
3. 
Transport fuel production (TFP): The amount of diesel, petrol, biodiesel, and 
electricity for electric vehicles used in a year.  
==PAGE:19==
4. 
Vehicle operation: The total vehicle kilometre travelled (VKT) by the different 
types of road transport in a given year. The non-exhaust emissions account for 
- tire, road and brake - wear emissions of the vehicles. The biodiesel quantity 
needed to blend with diesel in a given year, which is based on the policy target.  
5. 
Vehicle fleet maintenance (VFM): The maintenance needs of the vehicle fleet. 
6. 
Freights: Transport fuel freight (TFF) and vehicle fleet freight (VFF)  
7. 
Power generation (TE): The energy requirements for a year modelled with an 
energy mix of diesel-based generation and wind power.

2.6 Policy modelling  
The consequences of policies under TEEMP are modelled in the system as VKT reduction, 
fuel economy improvements, idling time reduction, blend of biodiesels, electric vehicles, and 
changes to the electricity mix.  
2.6.1 VKT reduction projects 
The percentage of VKT reduction in light-duty vehicles (LDVs) by 2030 is modelled to be 
20% when compared to the Business-as-usual scenario, through policies encouraging 
consumer preferences toward walking, biking, transit, rideshare, and telecommute. This 
reduces gasoline consumption in LDVs.  
2.6.2 Fuel Economy improvements and HEVs 
Fuel economy (FE) improvements in new LDVs by 2030 is forecasted to be 30%, based on the 
premise that FE has improved by 34% between 2010 and 2017 in the United States (Steffen et 
==PAGE:21==
al., 2015). The reduction of fuel use is based on new LDVs purchased from 2019. The BAU 
fuel economy of LDVs is 10.1 L/100 km, and the target fuel economy is 7.0 L/100 km. A VKT 
of the vehicle per year of 13,120 km is used in the calculations (U.S. Department of Energy, 
2013). The number of vehicles used is assumed to reduce commensurately with VKT.  
2.6.3 HDV idle time reduction 
The heavy diesel vehicle idle time reduced in the assessment is 1 hour per weekday. The idle 
time fuel used here is 3.5 L/hour of idle time (CTCN, 2018). The fuel saved from idle time 
reduction in buses and trucks is calculated based on five days/week of operation in a year.  
2.6.4 Electric vehicles 
The number of EVs purchased is calculated based on the policy target of 10% new passenger 
vehicles purchased to be electric by 2030. The gasoline use reduced is commensurate with the 
number of EVs in use. However, it also depends on renewable energy penetration in the market 
in a given point, which has been taken from Tonga’s 2015-2025 Strategic development 
framework (Argonne National Laboratory, 2018).  
2.6.5 Biodiesel 
The biodiesel use is modelled as 10% of the diesel consumption. However, there was no 
equivalent process in the Ecoinvent database. A proxy process is used to reflect the impacts of 
bioethanol production based on biomass in Brazil.  
2.6.6 Electricity mix 
In 2018, Tonga’s electricity mix was considered to have 78% of the power from diesel being 
burned in a conventional diesel-electric generating set with an average efficiency of 4.15 kWh 
per litre of diesel and the rest due to 1-3 MW wind turbines, with line losses of about 13%. 
However, the actual energy grid in 2017 comprises of sources like solar PV and wind turbine 
(with 2.3 MW and 0.5 MW respectively) with 1 MW battery storage. In the Business-as-usual 
scenario, the same grid as 2018 is used without any changes to diesel efficiency improvements. 
Under the TEEMP scenario, we model a change in the energy grid. The actual power generated 
is reduced due to policies such as LED lighting, energy audits, passive shading, and solar water 
heater spanning in implementation until 2030.  
The final grid under TEEMP has 70% renewables due to 1-3 MW wind turbines and 
30% percent due to diesel generators with improved efficiency (diesel consumption reduced 
from 0.067 kg/kWh to 0.023 kg/kWh) and reduced line losses (10%). The TC case is modelled 
with 100% of electricity due to >3 MW wind turbines with 10% losses. The list of policies and

2.7.2 Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis 
The sensitivity in the model describes the extent to which changes in the input parameter or a 
choice of method lead to variations in the model results. A model is sensitive to such influences 
if a small change is an input parameter that results in a substantial change in the model 
outcomes. A sensitivity analysis would help identify the critical and relevant parameters, on 
which uncertainty and data quality analysis can be performed. The model has four different 
scenarios, and 40 parameters are tested for their influence on the results from the model.  
A normalized sensitivity coefficient is calculated by a taking a positive or negative  
perturbation to the parameters (+10% in this case), and the sensitivities are calculated according 
to the following equation (Ministry of Finance and National Planning, 2015), 
XIS,k = 𝛥𝐼𝑆/𝐼𝑆  
𝛥ak/ak  
where XIS,k is the normalized sensitivity coefficient for the impact scores (IS) for a change in 
an input parameter k, and ak is the default value used in the model for input parameter k, and 
𝛥ak is the change in the parameter k. IS is the resulting impact score using parameter value ak, 
and 𝛥𝐼𝑆 is the change in resulting impact score for perturbations in parameter ak. Input 
parameters perturbed by +10% have XIS,k in range of 0 to 1, where usual practice is to consider 
a parameter sensitive if average sensitivity coefficient is above 0.3. A score of 0.3 means a 3% 
increase in impacts from the reference due to a 10% increase in the input parameter. Here, a 
parameter is considered to have high sensitivity if XIS,k > 0.3, and medium sensitivity if XIS,k 
==PAGE:23==
>0.2. Uncertainty analysis is done using the Monte Carlo method, with a uniform distribution 
considered for the important input parameters identified in the sensitivity analysis. The analysis 
also includes the inherent uncertainties in the processes used in the model. The uncertainty in 
the population, electricity grid losses, the density of diesel, vehicle kilometre travelled, and 
share of biodiesel blended to diesel is considered. The details of which can be found in ‘SM 7. 
Uncertainty analysis’. A completeness and consistency check is performed to understand the 
influence of the LCA method and the results can be seen in ‘SM 8. Completeness and 
Consistency Checks’. 
A Process contribution analysis is performed to identify the important processes and 
their relative share in contributing to the different impacts considered. The important processes 
are then further divided based on the sector and their life-cycle stage to implement changes in 
order to construct an absolute sustainable TC scenario. A substance contribution analysis is 
performed to identify the critical elementary flows contributing to the impacts.

4. 
Discussions  
The results of the important impact categories show a reduction in exceedance across impact 
categories and the need for absolute sustainability demands high levels of policy targets and 
reduction in consumption since even the TC scenario has reduced impacts across categories to 
within the SoSOS except those of CC-E impacts. The PB LCIA method helps us to understand 
the long-term consequences of envisaged policy changes. The results show the advantage of 
setting ambitious targets as the CC-E impacts have increased by 49% in BAU 2030 from the 
Baseline 2018, whereas on the contrary, for a similar consumption under the TC scenario, the 
impacts have reduced by 49%. If Tonga had achieved the goals set in the TC case, it could 
avoid the exceedance by reducing the kg CO2 eq carbon emissions by 693 kg per capita 
(assuming 100,000 as the population), which is a substantial reduction considering the UN’s 
Global Carbon Project estimates of 1,307 kg per capita in 2016 for Tonga. A reduction of 693 
kg CO2 eq per capita, or about 47% from the per capita estimate in 2016 necessitates the need 
to have a transformational change.

4.1 Choice of sharing principle 
The results from the TEEMP scenario show the transport and electricity sector in Tonga are 
exceeding the allocated safe operating space in terms of equal per capita (EPC) allocation for 
climate change (193 %) and are close to their SOS for freshwater use- Basin dry (95%), 
biogeochemical flows-N (45%), ocean acidification (61%) and atmospheric aerosol loading 
(28%). However, in EPC, the allocated space is for the entire country rather than being based 
on the sector considered. Alternatively, when we assess exceedance based on Equal burden 
(EB) or CO2 emissions per capita, all the impact categories are within the allocated boundary. 
It is per the historical perspective wherein Tonga has a per capita emission of only 27% of the 
world average in 2016. Therefore, the share allocated in EB is higher than the one estimated 
on the EPC principle. The results for the TEEMP scenario, shown in Table 8, represent that 
outcome of policies in terms of addressing the different impacts. All impact categories but 
climate change (193%) are within the safe operating space.

trade-offs between different impacts and prioritize their policy actions per their country’s long-

4.3 Holistic approach toward decision-making 
The concept of planetary boundaries would enable a holistic approach toward policies, wherein 
we have already exceeded the boundaries for biosphere integrity and biogeochemical flows of 
N and P. We have also crossed the SOS for climate change and land system change (EEA, 
2018). This framework would help identify critical boundaries transgressed and prioritize the 
country’s policies to be accommodative of the global boundaries transgressed. An illustrative 
example, Figure 8, is shown as a way to interpret the results in accordance with the planetary 
boundaries framework. The graph is colour coded as follows, if exceedance, Ei>3, high risk  
and Ei<1, as below boundary and safe. The exceedance here is calculated based on the 
overshoot above the allocated SoSOS. However, in the PBs framework (right plot), the zones 
are characterized based on impacts on a global scale.

The results provided using the PB-LCIA method can be used to develop policy 
instruments to tackle the exceedance at national and international level. Steffen et al., 2015 
have identified policy instruments to adhere to each planetary boundary. In the case of Tonga, 
we find that after implementing the TEEMP, the system still has the climate change – energy 
imbalance impact category in a high-risk zone, assuming for a value of Ei>3. It is close to the 
SoSOS for ocean acidification and freshwater use – basin dry. For the climate change planetary 
boundary, it is recommended to eliminate fossil fuel subsidies as an immediate strategy and 
introduction of carbon taxes and tradable permits as an additional national strategy. Having a 
holistic approach requires decision-makers to be aware of the trade-offs and interconnectedness 
between the planetary boundaries. Sterner et al. have shown an illustrative example where if 
we need to reduce the climate change-related impacts, one could consider increased forestry, 
which may have positive and negative side effects to the other boundaries. If decision-makers 
are aware of the current state of exceedance, they could reduce climate change impacts at the 
cost of impact categories that have not been found to exceed the SoSOS. An illustration 
depicting this concept is shown in Figure 8 (bottom plot), which is adapted from Sterner et al., 
2019.  
4.4 Recommendation to reach an absolute sustainable state 
The following concept is used to understand the importance of efficiency and technology 
improvements in comparison to an absolute increase in consumption. Holdren and Ehrlich 
(Holdren, 1974), addressed the importance of sustainability assessments in conserving the 
environment and whose work resulted in the IPAT equation, or  
𝐼= 𝑃× 𝐴× 𝑇 
where (I) being the overall environmental impact, (P) population of the planet, (A) is per capita 
affluence (or material affluence per person) and (T) is the technology’s environmental 
intensity. The global population has increased nearly threefold since the 1950s with all 
indicators of affluence increasing at a higher rate (depending on the state of development), 
thereby increasing the affluence per capita, A. This, in turn, has increased overall impacts as 
the level of technological advancement needed to improve the technology intensity 
(environmental impact per material affluence) has not caught up yet with rising consumption.  
The overall impacts depend on these three fundamental parameters, and in the model, 
the population is assumed to be constant throughout the assessment period with increasing 
affluence (GDP per capita). Analysis of the relevant processes contributing to impacts in TC 
case shows the change in consumption (both electricity and transport) in order to reach the 
==PAGE:34==
absolute sustainability state as a hypothetical scenario to improve technology intensity in 
accordance to the IPAT equation. However, to decrease the consumption and to implement 
transformational technological changes would elicit societal and financial costs. These models 
can be used to find to explore an optimum level of technological advancement and consumption 
reduction needed within the geopolitical and financial capabilities of the country. However, 
quantification of the societal and economic benefits would entail further research, as this paper 
focuses on quantifying the environmental impacts. 
In order to avoid rebound effects (Holdren, 1974) while only focusing on improving 
efficiency can prove detrimental, and in order to avoid such instances, the level of technology 
changes needed could increase, further increasing the policy targets. It can be seen in the 
difference in kg CO2 eq per VKT between Baseline 2018 and BAU 2030. The values have 
increased from 0.43 to 0.59 kg CO2 eq per km driven. Thereby reducing consumption would 
be the only variable at disposal to reduce impacts since technology intensity has stayed the 
same or reduced. However, in TC scenario, they have gone down to 0.26 kg CO2 eq per km 
driven, giving certain leeway to accommodate the increase in the consumption. To increase the 
consumption whilst reducing or maintaining a constant environmental impact would 
necessitate increasing eco-efficiency, which according to World  Council of Sustainable 
Development is defined as “eco-efficiency is achieved by the delivery of competitively priced 
goods and services that satisfy human needs and bring quality of life while progressively 
reducing environmental impacts of goods and resource intensity throughout the entire life cycle 
to a level at least in line with the Earth’s estimated carrying capacity” (Font Vivanco, Kemp, 
& Van Der Voet, 2016).   
The principles and equity methods identified are solely based on equal per capita, and 
similar fractions are assumed for normalization to rest of the boundaries for the sake of 
convenience at arriving at meaningful and comparable results. The ideal way would be to find 
other local indicators similar to the population indicator used in EPC (like arable land 
availability, water availability, the current state of control variables), which could be a better 
representative for the specific boundary and concerned country. The demand and resource 
availability differ across the globe, and national boundaries could differ if spatial and regional 
differences are considered. For instance, a country could have low agriculture productivity (or 
eco-efficiency), and due to their population, they could end up having a high share of the 
boundary. However, compared to agriculture intensive countries with high productivity at the 
same time need a higher share of the boundary to feed its growing population and meet its 
export and market needs.

4.5 Limitations and future research work 
The reliability and broader acceptance of the results depend on the primary data used, 
uncertainties considered, and choice of sharing principle used. The geographic differences in 
need and availability of resources could raise arguments regarding sharing of the planetary 
boundaries. A broader consensus is needed to allocate appropriate boundaries such as 
freshwater use and biogeochemical flows, where we do not see an equal distribution in 
consumption. The distribution of such impacts indicate hotspots, were some countries consume 
and use more resources than others, and hence, they may need a higher share in the boundary 
as they could be large exporters of services and products causing such impacts. Here we see, 
for instance, countries having extensive vehicle fleet production facilities would need a high 
demand of freshwater use, and such cases can raise concerns in the allocation of the boundary 
to the residents of such a country. There is a need for further research to improve the quality of 
data used in this model, such as the vehicle kilometre travelled, which contribute to the 
important impact categories. An annual vehicle registration database providing information on 
vehicle type, fuel type, and odometer reading can improve the accuracy of data used. After 
choosing important parameters, efforts are needed to improve and track such parameters with 
greater scrutiny to reduce the uncertainties in the results. In this system, only the environmental 
impacts are estimated; however, estimating the social and economic benefits for such a system 
could add value to have a comprehensive assessment. Such measures also improve decision-
making and relevance, as it would cover the social, economic, and environmental perspective 
of the important sectors.  
5. 
Conclusion  
The illustrative case study presented here demonstrate the potential of LCA as a tool that 
support decision-making by policymakers and stakeholders in the transport and energy sector. 
However, the limitations in terms of choosing the appropriate system boundaries, such that it 
includes the life-cycle based stages is an essential factor to the successful application of 
absolute sustainability concepts. The results depend on reaching a consensus on the choice of 
sharing principle needed to allocate the global planetary boundaries. They indicate the 
processes contributing to each impact category and the efforts to mitigate such impacts will 
depend on the system considered, individual processes may lose their relevance in large 
systems. For instance, vehicle fleet production, transport fuel production, and the shipping 
freight occur outside the boundary of Tonga, and the results can depend on the extent of 
==PAGE:36==
including such processes. However, these processes are essential to provide the functional unit 
considered in the study.  
The impact categories exceeding the SoSOS identified in the Baseline scenario are 
‘ocean acidification’ and ‘climate change – energy imbalance’. The significant contributors to 
the climate change impacts were the Vehicle kilometre travelled, contributing about 46% 
percent of the total climate change impacts. The electricity generation, contributing 26% 
percent, of which 16% is due to diesel burned in the generators.  
In BAU scenario, the impact categories were ‘climate change -  energy imbalance’, 
‘ocean acidification’, ‘freshwater use – basin dry’, ‘atmospheric aerosol loading’, and 
‘biochemical flows – nitrogen’. Similar process contribution as the Baseline scenario is noticed 
for climate change and ocean acidification, with VKT and electricity generation (TE) 
contributing about 42% and 31% of the total climate change impacts respectively. The high 
voltage electricity production in the vehicle fleet production life-cycle stage contributed to 57% 
of the freshwater use – basin dry impact, and finally the diesel burned in electricity generation 
(TE), contributes about 55% and 51% of the atmospheric aerosol loading and biogeochemical 
flows – nitrogen impacts.  
In the TEEMP scenario, it is ‘climate change – energy imbalance’ impact category 
(exceedance of 3.04), with the VKT contribution about 53%, whereas the electricity generation 
impacts have reduced to about 12% from about 31% in the Business-as-usual scenario. The 
TEEMP scenario has reduced the ‘atmospheric aerosol loading’ impact by 58% in comparison 
to the BAU and reduced the exceedance by 27% in the freshwater-basin dry impact category. 
The ‘climate change-energy imbalance’ impact in the TEEMP has reduced by 40% compared 
to the BAU scenario.  
The Transformational change scenario has further reduced all the impacts, with CC-E 
reducing by 65% and AAL reducing by 75% compared to BAU. The choice of sharing principle 
to allocate the safe operating space play a crucial role in the outcome of the results, with equal 
per-capita used here in the broad context. The transport-related climate change impacts have 
increased from the Baseline to BAU by 0.43 to 0.59 kg CO2 eq per km driven (or 37%) and 
have reduced to 0.26 kg CO2 eq per km driven (or 40%) in TC scenario. The climate change 
impacts due to electricity generation have remained same in Baseline and BAU at 0.50 kg CO2 
eq per kWh and reduced to 0.16 kg CO2 eq per kWh for the TEEMP and further reduced to 
0.06 kg CO2 eq per kWh in the TC scenario. It is an 89% reduction in climate change impacts 
due to electricity generation from reaching the TEEMP strategies in terms of energy efficiency 
improvements and having a 100% wind power-based electricity grid.

2.1 Sharing principles 
The planetary boundaries for the different environmental pressures have been found and these 
boundaries are time independent, i.e., they are absolute boundaries. When considering long-
term policy plans, there needs to be caution considering the future changes in the share 
allocated since changes in population, trade, commerce and technology advancement of the 
country determine its exceedance. The principles illustrated here are based both on ethical 
choices and empirical data (see Table 9). Both these choices influence the outcome of the 
results, thereby accentuating the need to have subjectivity, scientific principles, and consensus 
to accept and apply these principles. The different sharing and equity principles identified here 
as based on a broader classification by den Elzen et al (Häyhä, Lucas, van Vuuren, Cornell, & 
Hoff, 2016). Their group have identified 4 key equity principles: 
1. Egalitarian: humans have the same right to use the atmosphere, therefore all the PBs 
are divided based on the population of the country 
2. Sovereignty and acquired rights: countries have their sovereign right to use the 
atmosphere and the current emissions indicate the status quo needed to distribute the remaining 
carbon budget. 
3. Responsibility/polluter pays: the greater the contribution toward the pollution, 
greater would be the responsibility, where the boundaries are reduced for historic and high per 
capita emission countries 
4. Capability: greater the economic and financial capacity, higher would be the burden 
to abate and mitigate emissions

2.1.1 Principle 1: Equal per capita (EPC)  
It is based on establishing an allowable level of carbon emission budget. The emission budget  
is then set to be allocated equally amongst all people (M G J Den Elzen, Berk, Lucas, Eickhout, 
& Van Vuuren, 2003). The right to use the SOS for the different planetary boundaries are 
equally divided based on the population of the country. This principle distributes the safe 
operating space irrespective to the historic emissions, per capita emission, gross domestic 
product and nationality. Under this principle, countries with higher population tend to have a 
larger share irrespective of the measures taken to improve effectiveness in energy 
consumptions and adaptation to latest technological advancements.

2.1.3 Principle 3: Equal burden (EB) 
The per capita CO2 emission of the country is used as an indicator. The per capita emission is 
compared to the world average, and the countries lying below the average get a higher share 
proportional to the difference of their per capita emission from the world average in order to 
facilitate the concept of contraction and convergence over long time (The World Bank, 2018). 
This enables the burden to abate emissions to historic high per capita emission countries and 
provides an additional share of the boundary to countries having low per capita emissions. The 
rationale assumed here being the burden to abate emissions needs to shared equally in line with 
the polluter pays principle (Meyer, 2000) considering the quantity of carbon emitted per capita. 
However, this reduces the burden on developing countries with low per capita emissions and 
high absolute emissions due to their population. Some could argue the burden needs to be with 
the largest polluter rather than based on per capita, and this principle does not consider for 
instance the economic output of high per capita emission countries.

3.2.6 EV Power consumption (TFP) 
The process used here is to estimate the impacts from electricity consumption to power the 
electric vehicles (EVs) in a given year in kWh. For the given year, the number of new EVs 
purchased is estimated, which depends on the number of new vehicles purchased. The 
petrol/gasoline used in a year (p1)is used as the primary data to calculate the following 
dependent variables. The petrol use per vehicle is assumed 1,320 L /vehicle and the diesel use 
is 1,835 L/vehicle. Based on this, the theoretical number of petrol vehicles is estimates.  
Later, based on the extent of achievement in the policies influencing the VKT, the 
corresponding theoretical number of vehicles is found. Further, 13,120 km is assumed as the 
average VKT/vehicle. The fuel economy (FE) used is 10.1 L/100 km and target FE is 7 L/100 
km. Then, the number of new electric vehicles is calculated based on the extent of 
implementation of the target of 10% electric vehicles as a share of new petrol vehicles 
purchased. Each electric vehicle is assumed to consumer 2781 kWh of electricity. Finally, the 
cumulative kWh needed is found and used as a reference flow in the process.

following modifications from the BAU scenario. 
1. 
The electricity grid is 100% wind power based, whereas the share of 
renewables in the BAU is 70%. 
2. 
The electricity mix is changed subsequently in road construction and 
maintenance, vehicle fleet production and maintenance and related 
background processes. 
==PAGE:61==
3. 
The diesel production is updated to a low impact process, ‘Diesel {CH}| 
petroleum refinery operation’ compared to average ‘Diesel {RoW}| petroleum 
refinery operation’ process used in other scenarios.   
4. 
The petrol passenger vehicle fleet is replaced with electric vehicles, and hence 
no petrol is imported as a consequence.  
5. 
In vehicle kilometre travel related processes, the choice of fuel standard is 
improved from Euro 4 to Euro 5 for all modes of transport, thereby reducing 
the associated environmental impacts.

stratospheric ozone depletion, land-system change – tropic and land-system change – global 
have exceedance values close to zero, i.e. these impacts are very near to lower bound of the 
safe operating space.

category and is presented in Figure 17. Using the PB-LCIA method, we identify the BAU 
scenario to have the maximum impact in 12 of the 13 impact categories, other than 
biogeochemical flows – regional P, for which the TC scenario has the highest impact, which is 
not an important impact category in terms of exceedance. This is as expected, since the 
reference flow needed in the BAU is higher than the other scenarios. The change in impacts 
across scenarios increases from Baseline to BAU, reduces from BAU to TEEMP and further 
in TC case. The exceptions to this is seen for freshwater use – basin dry, biogeochemical flows 
==PAGE:68==
– regional P and land-system change – tropic, where the impacts have increased in the TEEMP 
and TC compared to the Baseline. In particular, the freshwater use – basin dry impacts have 
increased since the water consumption needed to run the turbines, i.e., ‘Water, turbine use, 
unspecified natural origin’ in the TEEMP and TC scenario are higher than that of the Baseline 
and BAU. This is due to the energy mix modelled, where the share of wind power has increased 
from 30% in Baseline to 70% and 100% in the TEEMP and TC respectively, where as it 
remains the same in BAU.  
A similar comparison using the ILCD 2011 Midpoint+ method, in Figure 17, show the 
BAU scenario to have the highest impacts in 11 of the 15 impact categories, where in the rest 
TC scenario has the highest impacts. The five impact categories are ‘Human toxicity, non-
cancer effects’, ‘Human toxicity, cancer effects’, ‘Freshwater eutrophication’, ‘Freshwater 
ecotoxicity’, and ‘Water resource depletion’. These five impacts have increased gradually from 
the Baseline to the TC scenario. The contribution of the life-cycle stages toward these 
categories show the Vehicle fleet production and Tonga electricity generation has a large share 
of contribution. These contribute about 79% of the ‘Human toxicity, cancer effects’, about 75% 
of the ‘Human toxicity, non-cancer effects’ and ‘Freshwater eutrophication’.

SM 7. Uncertainty analysis 
The uncertainty is performed using the Monte Carlo analysis, and the parameters are chosen 
based on the results of the sensitivity analysis. The uncertainty in the input parameters is 
considered to be uniform distribution. The population estimates provided by UN is used as a 
reference in uncertainty of population (UNPD, 2015). The range of line losses seen in 
electricity grid supply systems is used for the electricity line losses considered in the model 
(CEER, 2017). The VKT per capita and GDP correlation estimate is to used to predict the lower 
and upper bound in the VKT per capita modelled (Ecola et al., 2014). The input in diesel density 
is based on the common range of values found in the literature (Bacha et al., 2007). The level 
of biodiesel blend in diesel is increased to 20% as a variation in the policy target. The Table 
39, show the input values considered as a lower and upper bound in the uniform distribution 
based Monte Carlo analysis. The mentioned uncertainties are modelled for the 2030 time period 
as the Baseline input is based on measured data and considered to be reliable. However, the 
inherent model uncertainties are considered for all the scenarios.

SM 8. Completeness and Consistency Checks 
Sensitivity toward the different LCA methods for the Baseline scenario are calculated as an 
additional analysis to verify the robustness in choice of method. Climate change or global 
warming is the impact category compared between the choices of method. 
 
 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
CTCN has calculated the carbon emissions and the Baseline scenario modelled here as 
can been seen from Table 41, show the model to have close estimates similar to the approach 
used by CTCN. Completeness requirement is needed to understand the extent of inclusion of 
relevant processes. LCA theory suggests the selection of system boundaries should begin with 
life-cycle components from the raw material extraction from nature (cradle) to components that 
discard waste flows back to the natural environment. The system should capture the exchanges 
from the ecosphere to techno sphere and finally the discarded and elementary wastes flows 
back to the ecosphere. This would ensure completeness in including the relevant processes 
having a cradle to grave approach in building the system boundaries. 
The policies intended to reduce the GHG emissions in the CTCN’s approach is based 
on considering only the life cycle of transport fuels. However, there is a need to understand the 
importance of having a life cycle engineering thinking into framing policies addressing the 
transport sector. The validity of the premise of this hypothesis is tested by considering an 
extensive and comprehensive system boundary inclusive of the different life cycle stages.

Closing statement from author 
The master thesis presents arguments to have an absolute sustainability perspective when 
considering decision-making and setting policy targets. The long-term goals set in the transport 
and energy sector need comprehensive tools such as LCAs to improve the communication of 
the associated environmental impacts. Using such tools can inform decision-makers of the 
environmental impacts other than climate change. The planetary boundaries framework has set 
definite and absolute boundaries for important Earth system boundaries such as climate change, 
freshwater use, ocean acidification, and atmospheric aerosol loading. It is essential to 
understand and consider such environmental pressures, as the current state of our planet 
necessitates we stay within the safe operating space of these thresholds. Crossing such limits 
takes us to uncharted territories, and the response of the Earth system, when pushed beyond 
the safe operating space is uncertain and increases the risk. We are already in the brink of 
crossing many critical tipping points.  
 
The results and discussions in this report show us the importance of having a life-cycle 
approach while modelling large systems such as transport and energy. We find that climate 
change-related impacts are not only from tail-pipe emissions of the vehicle and the electricity 
production but also from all the ancillary services such as maintenance, freight, and non-
exhaust emissions needed to fulfil the functional unit considered.  We also find important 
hotspot impacts, such as freshwater use, atmospheric aerosol loading, and ocean acidification,  
which need to be considered to have a comprehensive sustainability assessment.  
 
The analysis  provide necessary quantitative impact scores while considering trade-offs, as 
focusing on reducing a particular impact category, such as climate change, has considerable 
influences on the others. Such compromises can bring in difficulties to arrive at a conclusion 
and to overcome such differences it would be interesting to come at a weighting based single 
score so that the trade-offs and preferences of a decision-maker can be modelled. The 
Transformation change scenario indicates, despite having possible technological 
advancements, it wasn't enough to reduce the climate change impacts in the year 2030 to an 
absolute sustainable level. It presents the scope of exploring a further reduction in consumption, 
but also promoting the use of clean technologies and taking responsibility for the consequences 
of our increasing consumption levels.
