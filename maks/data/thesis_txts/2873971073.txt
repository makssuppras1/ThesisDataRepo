Abstract
Visualisation of the filling phase during a plastic injection moulding process is
one of the most powerful methods to relate a physical process to his simulated
equivalent.
The work addresses the challenges of integrating a transparent window into a
conventional injection mould while ensuring mechanical resistance and optical
clarity under process conditions.
Physical preliminary experiments were performed using an industrial injection
moulding machine, with process monitoring and part measurements, and the
results were directly compared with Moldflow simulations, enabling calibration
of process parameters and confirmation of the model’s predictive accuracy.
The new research tool was then designed considering multiple configurations,
with component selection guided by adaptability to existing equipment and cost
containment. Using the Moldflow validated simulation framework and its re-
sults, a thermo-mechanical analysis was carried out to assess stress distribution
in the sapphire window and surrounding components. This research delivers
both a validated simulation-to-experiment methodology and a fully engineered
tool design that met the required operational constraints and provided clear
visual access for advanced process monitoring.

Sommario
La visualizzazione della fase di riempimento durante un processo di stampag-
gio a iniezione di materie plastiche rappresenta uno dei metodi più efficaci per
mettere in relazione un processo fisico con il suo equivalente simulato.
Il lavoro affronta le sfide legate all’integrazione di una finestra trasparente in
uno stampo convenzionale, garantendo al contempo resistenza meccanica e
chiarezza ottica nelle condizioni di processo.
Sono stati condotti esperimenti preliminari fisici utilizzando una pressa indus-
triale per stampaggio a iniezione, con monitoraggio del processo e misurazioni
sui pezzi, e i cui risultati sono stati direttamente confrontati con le simulazioni
Moldflow, consentendo la calibrazione dei parametri di processo e la conferma
dell’accuratezza predittiva del modello.
Il nuovo strumento è quindi stato progettato considerando diverse configu-
razioni, con la selezione dei componenti guidata dall’adattabilità alle apparec-
chiature esistenti e dal contenimento dei costi.
Utilizzando il framework di
simulazione validato in Moldflow e i così ottenuti risultati, è stata condotta
un’analisi termo-meccanica per valutare la distribuzione delle tensioni nella
finestra in zaffiro e nei componenti circostanti.
Questa ricerca fornisce sia una metodologia validata per il confronto tra sim-
ulazione ed esperimento, sia il progetto di uno strumento di ricerca completa-
mente ingegnerizzato che soddisfa i vincoli operativi richiesti e garantisce un
accesso visivo chiaro per un monitoraggio avanzato del processo.

Introduction
1.1
Project background and motivation
The present project was developed as a MSc. Thesis for the degree of MSc.
in Product Innovation Engineering (Ingegneria dell’Innovazione del Prodotto)
at the University of Padova after a period of mobility at the Technical University
of Denmark (DTU) within the Mechanical Engineering Department.
The project was born from the collaboration between DTU and Tetra Pak, and
all major phases of the present work were carried out within the Lund, Sweden,
headquarters. Tetra Pak is intended for the project itself and the results obtained
from it.
Visualisation of the filling phase during a plastic Injection Moulding (IM)
process is one of the most powerful methods to relate a physical process to his
virtual equivalent.
Injection moulding is widely used in most Tetra Pak packaging machines, so the
possibility of adopting a new tool to better characterize and understand injection
moulding, especially the behaviour of polymer inside a specific cavity geometry
and its interaction when it comes into contact with other materials, represents
a useful tool to improve the development of new products and packaging ma-
chinery, potentially saving time and costs.
1
==PAGE:26==
1.2. GOAL AND OBJECTIVES
1.2
Goal and objectives
The main goal of this project is to develop a research tool that allows visual-
ization of cavity filling during IM.
The novelty of the present research project lies in three key aspects:
• The tool must be able to be used to evaluate different aspects that can affect
a moulding process such as different cavity geometries, presence of inserts
and influence of vents in the filling.
• The tool must be simple from the manufacturing point of view and adapt
as much as possible to other tools already in use.
• The project is set in an industrial context and as such it must represent a
useful tool for research and development of new products.
To virtually validate the new tool, it is necessary to validate the entire simu-
lation process used through physical tests with the supplied machine to directly
compare the virtual process with the real one. This represents the secondary
objective, but it is directly related to the first and without which it would not be
possible to obtain any reliable results.
1.3
Research questions
• What are the main solutions already adopted to visualize the polymer
flow?
• What materials and geometries were used taking into account constraints
and stresses due to the IM process?
• What physical tests help us obtain useful information to validate the sim-
ulation?
• What process settings allow us to faithfully replicate the experiments in
the simulator?
• Is it possible to adapt the new tool to the machine in use?
• Which components are best suited to the type of instrument developed,
taking into account the process in which it will be used?
• Will the design withstand the thermal and mechanical stresses of injection
moulding?
• How much will the new research tool cost?

State of the Art
2.1
Plastics Injection Moulding
During the past century, the usage of polymers and plastics has expanded
rapidly, and today plastics are among the most widely used materials.
Injection moulding is probably the most important process used to manufacture
plastic products with more than one third of all thermoplastic materials that
are injection moulded and more than half of all polymer processing equipment
used for IM. [1]
The large diffusion of this process is well represented by the global market value
of injection moulded plastics, which was valued at $335.4 billion in 2024 and is
projected to reach $439.4 billion by 2030. [2]
Some benefits of using IM for a product or part of a product are the following:
[3]
• Low cost and high efficiency for massive production quantities
• Automated process
• High shape flexibility
• High tolerances and very highly detailed features or finishes
• Light weight, high strength-to-weight ratio, excellent impact resistance
and low corrosion
• Net or near-net shape
• One plastic part instead of an assembly of multiple parts produced using
other materials
5
==PAGE:30==
2.1. PLASTICS INJECTION MOULDING
2.1.1
Injection moulding machine
Figure 2.1: Example of an Injection Moulding (IM) machine
A standard injection moulding machine consists of three primary compo-
nents: the injection unit, the clamping unit, and the mould. [3] [4]
• Injection Unit: This part is responsible for melting and injecting the plastic.
It typically includes a hopper for feeding plastic pellets, a heated barrel
and inside a screw divided into a feed section, a compression section,
and a metering section where the plastic is melted. The screw moves the
solid plastic toward the nozzle through constant rotation, during which
the plastic is turned from the solid state to melt and accumulates in front
of the screw ready to be injected. The screw also contributes to mixing and
homogenising the melt.
• Clamping Unit: This unit secures the mould halves together during injec-
tion and cooling. It consists of a moving platen, a stationary platen, and a
hydraulic or mechanical clamping mechanism that applies significant force
6
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 2. STATE OF THE ART
to keep the mould closed against injection pressure. Injection machines
are usually distinguished by the maximum clamping force (tons).
• Mould: The mould is a custom-designed tool that defines the shape of
the final part and generally includes a fixed side and a movable side plate.
It contains one or more cavities and is usually made of hardened steel
or aluminium. The basic structure of a mould is generally divided into
three types: two-platen mould, three-platen mould, and hot runner. The
decision for a particular mould structure is generally made by customers
or according to the products. Moulds are designed with guide, support,
cooling, ejection, gating, and venting systems to facilitate part formation
and removal.
2.1.2
Injection moulding process
Figure 2.2: Schematic of IM process. Adapted from [5]
The injection moulding process operates in a cyclical manner, with each cycle
producing one or more parts. The standard cycle comprises the following stages:
[4] [6]
1. Mould closing: The two (or more) parts of the mould are securely closed
by the clamping unit to prepare for injection.
2. Injection unit forward: If not, the injection unit moves forward into contact
with the mould.
7
==PAGE:32==
2.1. PLASTICS INJECTION MOULDING
3. Injection / Mould filling: Molten plastic is injected into the mould cavity
through the sprue, runners, and gates. This stage is controlled by the
speed and position of the screw. The injection pressure ensures that the
cavity is fully filled; then the cooling begins. The cooling channels in the
mould help to dissipate heat efficiently.
4. Packing and Holding: After initial filling, additional material may be
packed into the cavity to compensate for shrinkage as the plastic cools.
Holding pressure is applied to maintain the shape of the part.
5. Injection unit back: Movement of the injection unit backward. Almost no
used stage anymore.
6. Plasticating / recovery: After the piston stops its movement, the screw
rotates and is pushed backward by the plastic itself until enough melt is
collected. During this stage, the next injection shot is prepared.
7. Mould Opening and Ejection: The clamping unit opens the mould by
moving the movable platen, and ejector pins or plates push the cooled part
out of the mould cavity. The mould then closes again for the next cycle.
2.1.3
Injection moulding principal parameters
Successful injection moulding depends on the careful control of several pro-
cess parameters, including: [5] [6]
• Injection Pressure: Determines the force with which the molten plastic
is pushed into the mould cavity. High pressure ensures complete filling,
especially in complex geometries.
• Melt Temperature: Affects the viscosity and flow behaviour of the plastic.
The optimal temperature varies depending on the polymer used.
• Mould Temperature: Influences the cooling rate and quality of the finished
part. Higher mould temperatures can improve surface finish and reduce
warping.
• Injection Speed: Controls how fast the mould is filled. Fast injection may
reduce weld lines and cycle time, but increase shear stress and injection
pressure.
• Holding Pressure and Time: Crucial for managing shrinkage and dimen-
sional accuracy.
• Cooling Time: Affects cycle time and part strength. Adequate cooling is
necessary to prevent warpage, ensure complete solidification, and achieve
enough stiffness for the finished part to be ejected out of the mould.
These parameters can be set and controlled by the operator typically through
a control panel positioned on board the machine.

2.1. PLASTICS INJECTION MOULDING
This technique is particularly advantageous for producing parts that require ad-
ditional functionality, such as threaded inserts, electrical contacts, or reinforcing
elements. Insert moulding reduces assembly steps, improves the strength of the
part, and allows for more compact and durable designs. Key considerations in
insert moulding include:
• Insert Placement: Inserts must be accurately positioned and held in place
during injection.
Manual, semi-automatic, or fully automatic methods
may be used depending on production volume.
• Material Compatibility: The plastic must bond well with the insert mate-
rial. Surface treatments may be required to improve adhesion.
• Process Control: Parameters such as injection pressure, temperature, and
clamping force must be fine-tuned to prevent displacement, warpage, or
damage to the insert.
Insert moulding is commonly used in automotive, electronics, packaging
and medical industries for producing robust, multi-material components with
high precision and reliability.

The term "overmoulding" is often mistakenly used to indicate an insert mould-
ing process, which, however, refers to a process where plastic is moulded over
another moulded part. The first component is made inside an injection mould,
and it is then placed into a second mould to add the over-moulded material.
This technique combines multiple plastics for either practical or aesthetic pur-
poses.
For example, different durometer plastics might be used to mould a
softer plastic over a more rigid one to make a part easier to grip. Using multiple
coloured plastics in an overmoulded part can also distinguish the product from
other brands. Overmoulding is regularly used on the handles of tools such as
screwdrivers, power drills, or toothbrushes.

2.2
Injection moulding cavity filling
The filling phase of the cavity during an IM process is very important as a
critical. The filling performance and polymer behaviour inside the cavity are
related to the process parameters, so only a fully understood of this phase can
lead to the production of high-quality moulded parts with a lower risk of defects.
Examples of approaches used to evaluate the filling of the cavity are explained
below.
2.2.1
Short shots method
Figure 2.10: Short shots of an injection moulded dog bone made of POM [13]
The phenomenon called "short shot" is a type of defect that is caused by
under-filling of the mould cavity. It is most apparent in thinner zones or at
the end of runners of the produced part, and is mainly caused by insufficient
plastic supply or poor liquidity of the plastic itself, so that the liquid state halts
prematurely during the filling process. [6]
Short shots are also a common method used to evaluate the filling of the cavity
during an IM process, where an ever increasing fraction of the molten polymer
volume is injected into the cavity to compare the final results with virtual sim-
ulations and validate the model used. [13]

2.2. INJECTION MOULDING CAVITY FILLING
As shown in previous studies, there are two possible configurations for mould
construction that can be summarized as follows:
1. Parallel configuration: Most of research uses this type of construction, in
which the injection plane / cavity surface is parallel to the opening-closing
plane of the mould. This configuration keeps the same orientation and
main geometry as in a common injection mould. To visualise the filling
phase of the cavity, it is necessary to use a 45o mirror or high-polishing
metal surface to reflect light. An example of this configuration is the mould
in Figure 2.27
2. Perpendicular configuration: The other possibility is to design the mould
with the glass window placed on the side, perpendicular to the opening-
closing plane of the mould. This configuration allows direct filming of the
flow without using a reflective mirror. An example is the mould in Figure
2.23
The parallel configuration compared to the perpendicular one has:
• Lower design complexity
• Higher flow visualisation complexity
• Similar mould dimensions but in each cases higher than standard due to
the use of a reflective mirror (parallel) or the cavity orientation (perpendic-
ular)
• Higher percentage of commercial components, in fact for the parallel con-
figuration the half of the mould without the glass window (usually the
movable one) can be compared to a standard commercial one, so it could
be possible to use in-house mould
• Standard ejector system, while for the perpendicular is necessary a side
action
• Same video recording system
The parallel configuration represents a simpler design solution, but on the
other hand using a mirror could lead to some problems, including reflections,
artefacts, alignment difficulties, dust and dirt on mirror surface. These risks can
be reduced using a prism-shaped glass insert to avoid the problem of having an
empty zone inside the mould between the window and the mirror, with higher
costs on the glass, or using a simple assembly solution to facilitate the cleaning.

2.2.3
Correlation between physical tests and simulations:
sensors and software
Today physical tests of injected parts are used to compare results and validate
virtual models developed to describe the behaviour of the real process.
A
validated model can then be used for other virtual experiments by changing, for
example, input parameters or the geometry of the cavity to obtain the expected
results by real tests. In this way, it is possible to save money, reduce the design
and production lead time, achieve a high quality production (first time right).
In order to be able to correlate physical tests and simulations, it is important to
collect all the necessary data as output of the real IM process and use them as
input for the virtual validation or compare them with the simulations.
For the IM, thermocouples and pressure sensors or p-T sensors are widely used
to collect pressure and temperature data in the mould insert. Other sensors used
are position sensors to track, for example, the position of the injection screw, load
cells to ensure proper clamping pressure during mould closing, and pyrometers
to measure surface temperature before the ejection of solidified parts.

CHAPTER 2. STATE OF THE ART
A sensor can not only provide the information for what it was built for, but, for
example, a thermocouple can also lead to understanding at which time the melt
passes the location where the sensor is placed.
These sensors, integrated in a production process, are also useful for real-time
monitoring and, if their data are not in the right processing target window, can
be used as feedback to change the machine parameters and restore the correct
processing window.

According to the results in Table 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6 the IM process was
stable during the session of experiments run, since the values are consistent with
each other, depending on the measurement point. By increasing the injected
volume and therefore also the injection time, the values tend to become more
stable; this is because the flow front has more time to stabilise once it enters the
cavity. Thicknesses of the full-filled cavity experiment in Table 3.3 compared
with the depth of the mould in Table 3.8, the values of the right side, points
3,7,10 for the samples and 3,6 for the cavity, are similar to each other and lower
compared to the centre and right areas and this could be the explanation for
the lower length values on all the samples, including those from the short-

reduces and in case of rapid change in thickness this can cause an unwanted
short shot meaning an incomplete part. To confirm these considerations, further
measurements with more accurate measuring systems are necessary, since the
uncertainty due to the instrument used does not allow for an adequate estimate.
Looking at Table 3.2, the length and width are smaller than the real values of
the cavity in Table 3.7 and this is caused by the shrinkage of the polymer while
cooling.
On the other hand, considering the thickness in Table 3.3 and 3.4, the values are
in some cases higher than the depth of the cavity and appear in general not to
be affected by the shrinkage. This may be due to molecular orientation along
the flow direction and short cooling; indeed, according to Figure 3.6 the ejected
part is still quite hot and the runner zone is above 120oC (the recommended
ejection temperature is 103oC according to the material data sheet). An early
ejection postpones the shrinkage until after the ejection itself, so without the
geometry of the mould which acts as a constraint for the part, and this will
succeed in developing more elastic recovery in the flow direction , producing,
as a result, more shrinkage in-flow, compensated by less shrinkage in the z
direction (thickness) and less molecular orientation.[40]
If we also consider the thicknesses of the short-shots experiment in Table 3.5, it
is easy to see by the results that the thickness is related to the volume injected
inside the cavity: the higher the volume injected, the higher the values of the
thickness at points 1,2,3 common to each experiment sample. In fact, the lower
the volume injected inside the cavity, the faster the cooling of the part, and it
can be achieved entirely inside the mould within the cooling time set on the
machine, which is constant for every experiment and this resulted in a more
isotropic shrinkage.
In addiction, the constraint effect of the mould on the
polymer of a short shot is lower because the cavity is not fully filled.

used in [45]. The machine response time has been set at 0.001 s (0.02 s was the
default value set for the machine in the software data sheet for the IM used for
the experiments); otherwise, this response time directly impacts the volume of
polymer injected (higher than the volume set) and the results.

5
Conclusions and future work
The first part of the project focusses on preliminary experimental tests sub-
sequently validated by process simulations in Moldflow.
From the results obtained, it is possible to draw a series of conclusions:
1. Experiments performed with a commonly used in-house mould and the
material, HDPE, which is commonly used, were found to be stable and
affected by a small variability from the mean values, almost entirely below
1%.
2. The variety of experiments performed means having a varied and sufficient
sample of data to compare with the simulations.
3. Simulating the process in Moldflow without any specific control over the
injection speed profile can sometimes lead to rapid results that directly
match experimental results, as in the case of maximum pressure at the
injection location. However, the lack of effective control means that there
is no guarantee of a stable model that can be used with different geometries
and process parameters.
4. The first virtual DOE performed shows how, using three different velocity
profiles as controls, the results obtained are different, even though the
profiles are correlated with each other. As the mesh density increases, the
results tend to show a better correlation with the experimental data, but
the increase in computational cost is significantly greater, so it does not
justify the use of a very fine mesh to simulate the processes studied.
5. Considering the actual starting position of the ram used during the exper-
iments, the results of the first DOE are wrong; in fact, the geometry of the
machine should be implemented in addition to the part and feed system to
take into account the material compressibility and the pressure drop over
the nozzle and barrel.[43] [44]
125
==PAGE:150==
6. By including barrel geometry in the model, the obtained results are di-
rectly comparable with the experimental ones for the same process param-
eters except for the "%ram speed vs. %stroke" speed profile, which gives
erroneous results and therefore should not be used as a filling control
parameter within the software.
7. Among the results compared with the experimental data, the maximum
pressure at the injection location, the integral of the pressure profile at
the injection location, and the part weight show a maximum absolute
deviation of less than 10%. For flatness, the deviation is between 20-30%.
Again, increasing the mesh density too much does not offset the increased
computational cost. The best set of factors to use was "ram speed vs. time"
and a medium/coarse mesh density with the ram starting position equal
to the experimental one.
8. The same simulation model used for the fully filled cavity also allows for
very low absolute deviations in the short-shot experiments. The percent-
age deviations from the experimental values are below 10%, except for the
results of the smallest filled volume, where the small values used as the
denominator have a greater impact on the calculation.
The above conclusions allow us to state that we have defined a simulation
model, validated by experimental data, which can be used to simulate other
processes with the hypothesis of using the same machine used in the tests.
The conclusions reached regarding the parameters used in Moldflow and their
impact do not only represent a response to the needs of the project in question;
however, they can be considered as a general guideline on which parameters
are best to use when simulating an injection moulding process such as the one
studied.
The second part of the project deals with the development of the new tool,
and what was achieved can be summarized as follows:
1. The modelled and selected components allow, starting from the system
already in use in the machine, to adopt the greatest possible number of
commercial components and to be able to integrate part of what is already
in use.
2. The chosen solution with the camera in the moving half of the mould
was designed to allow the necessary or desired configuration to be chosen
depending on whether a thermal camera or a simple optical camera is used,
simply by replacing a limited number of components without impacting
the core of the mould where the most critical components are located.
3. Sapphire is the ideal material for the application considered in the new
tool.
Its excellent mechanical properties allow for a limited thickness,
compared to other transparent materials.
126
==PAGE:151==
CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
4. Simulations performed in Moldflow allow us to appreciate the potential
of being able to visualize the flow of the polymer in the cavity, especially
in non-standard processes such as insert moulding.
5. The results obtained in Moldflow with the new tool, using the validated
process, allow us to perform a static thermo-mechanical analysis.
6. Preliminary analyses allow to efficiently and quickly test the impact of the
window geometry on the maximum stress values obtained. The optimal
geometry can then be chosen.
7. The results obtained from the structural analysis show maximum stress
values one-third below the strength limits of the respective materials with
applied pressure 60% greater than the maximum value obtained by Mold-
flow, to account for the process variables and the approximations in the
definition of the model used for the analysis.
8. The total cost of the research tool depends heavily on the type of camera
used. Due to its features, the thermal camera increases the price by more

The tool therefore meets the initial objectives of simplicity of design, versa-
tility of use, adaptation to the machine in use and cost containment.
The future plan is to proceed with the procurement of all the components
to build this research tool.
Once done, it can be used to further refine the
simulations performed with Moldflow as well as being used to investigate in
detail phenomena such as polymer-insert interaction.
