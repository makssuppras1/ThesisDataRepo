Abstract
Higher education institutions (HEIs) are pivotal in shaping sustainable practices and have the opportunity
to serve as role models in waste management (WM), which is crucial for a circular economy.
By
prioritizing waste reduction and recycling, HEIs not only decrease their environmental footprint but also
create economic value, demonstrating the strategic importance of sustainable WM. However, HEIs lack
a standardized and universally recognized method for evaluating WM. A PhD thesis has proposed a
framework that inspires the methodological decisions taken in this study. The study is conducted in
collaboration with two departments within the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), Campus Service
(CAS), and an academic department, DTU Sustain.
This study aims to evaluate the municipal solid WM practices at DTU Lyngby Campus, focusing on
several methodologies. A field study is conducted, mapping the outdoor waste collection points (CPs)
present at the campus. Samples of municipal solid waste from DTU Sustain are characterized to gain
knowledge on the composition and impurities of the waste flows. A stakeholder analysis is performed to
identify and understand the flow of information and actions in the waste management system (WMS) at
DTU. Finally, a material flow analysis (MFA) and life cycle assessment (LCA) screening are applied to
investigate the flows of recyclables and potential environmental impacts. Furthermore, recommendations
for future work and monitoring of the WMS are discussed.
The study concludes that changes in the internal practices and network at DTU can obtain great
improvements. Waste samplings at DTU Sustain revealed that nearly 40% of the residual waste could
be recycled. Notable patterns included the presence of nearly empty collection bags and improperly
disposed of laboratory waste. The greatest discrepancies across sampling periods involved the segregated
plastics, metals, and glass. To enhance the monitoring of sorting practices and user behavior, future
efforts should expand to include waste from a wider array of departments and facilities.
The system’s users, who have low interest and influence, are not naturally engaged in the WMS.
The stakeholder network demonstrates a shortfall in interactions with the users, resulting in a lack
of communication of sorting guidelines. Enriching the network by including a more diverse range of
stakeholders in WM planning could enhance the distribution of information and practical insights.
A very small share of the plastics in the WMS is recycled into secondary materials, specifically due to
high levels of impurities and low segregation efficiencies. Enhanced sorting scenarios were examined to
mitigate the amount of residual waste, yet the predominant portion of municipal solid waste continues to
be channeled to incineration. The most favorable scenario suggests a remarkable reduction in Residual
waste, aligning with DTU’s WM goals. Additionally, the LCA screening indicates that better sorting
could reduce environmental impacts due to decreased impacts from waste treatments (i.e., waste-to-
energy incineration and reprocessing) and improve waste-to-energy plant efficiency in terms of CO2-
eq/kg.
Though providing valuable insights, the limited representativeness of the study should be acknowledged
while interpreting the results. Only one CP is investigated in the waste sampling, and most of the
subsequent analyses are based on these samplings. The results of the MFA and LCA are influenced by
the uncertainties related to assumptions made about parameter values. To gain more knowledge about
the dynamics of the model, a sensitivity analysis should be conducted.
The study concludes that DTU could enhance WM by implementing regular waste sampling and actively
involving users. It also recommends ongoing evaluation of the WMS’s environmental impacts and efforts
to further refine data quality.

Sammenfatning
Uddannelsesinstitutioner kan spille en afgørende rolle i udviklingen af bæredygtige tiltag inden for af-
faldssystemer og cirkulær økonomi. Ved at prioritere affaldsreduktion og genanvendelse mindsker uddan-
nelsesinstitutioner ikke kun deres miljøaftryk, men skaber ogs˚a økonomisk værdi og muligheder. Højere
uddannelsesinstitutioner mangler dog en standardiseret metode til evaluering af affaldssystemer. En
PhD-afhandling har fremlagt en analysetilgang, for en metodisk tilgang til evaluering af affaldssystemer.
Denne tilgang følges og undersøges i dette studie. Undersøgelsen i denne rapport er udført i samarbejde
med to afdelinger p˚a Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU): Campus Service (CAS) og det akademiske
institut, DTU Sustain.
Studiet har til form˚al at evaluere den nuværende praksis for h˚andtering af husholdningslignende affald p˚a
DTU Lyngby Campus. Dette gøres gennem en række metoder. En feltundersøgelse er blevet brugt til at
kortlægge affaldsg˚arde udendørs p˚a campus. For at opn˚a viden om sammensætningen og urenhederne
i affaldsstrømmene, blev der udført en affaldsanalyse p˚a husholdningslignende affald fra DTU Sustain.
En aktøranalyse blev lavet for at identificere og forst˚a informationsflowet i affaldssystemet p˚a DTU. Til
sidst anvendes en materialestrømsanalyse (MFA) samt en livscyklusvurdering (LCA) til at undersøge
strømmene af genanvendelige materialer og deres potentielle miljøp˚avirkninger. Anbefalinger til det
fremtidige arbejde og monitorering af affaldssystemet er ogs˚a diskuteret.
Studiet kan konkludere, at ændringer i de interne tiltag og netværk p˚a DTU kan medføre betydelige
forbedringer. Affaldsanalyser for DTU Sustain afslørede, at næsten 40% af restaffaldet bestod af genan-
vendelige materialer. Andre fund i forbindelse med affaldsanalysen inkluderede næsten tomme affald-
sposer og forkert bortskaffet laboratorieaffald. For at forbedre monitoreringen af sortering og brugerad-
færd bør fremtidige tiltag udvides til at inkludere affaldsanalyser for andre afdelinger og faciliteter p˚a
universitetet.
Brugerne af affaldssystemet, der som udgangspunkt har lav interesse og indflydelse p˚a affaldssorterin-
gen, er ikke naturligt engagerede. Interaktionerne mellem aktører viser en manglende kontakt mellem
beslutningstagere og de faktiske brugere, hvilket resulterer i manglende kommunikation af sorteringsret-
ningslinjer. Ved at involvere flere og mere forskellige aktører i planlægningen af affaldssystemerne, kan
aktørnetværket forbedres.
Ud fra MFA’en kan det blandt andet konstateres, at en meget lille del af plastikken i systemet genanven-
des til sekundære materialer, primært grundet høje niveauer af urenheder og d˚arlig sortering af brugerne.
Scenarier for forbedret sortering blev undersøgt, dog ender den overvejende del af affaldet fortsat med
at blive sendt til forbrænding i disse. Scenariet med de mest optimale sorteringsantagelser antyder en
betydelig reduktion i restaffald, der er i overensstemmelse med DTU’s m˚al for affaldsh˚andtering. Deru-
dover viser LCA-screeningen, at en forbedret sortering kan mindske miljøp˚avirkningen forbundet med
affaldsbehandlinger (f.eks. affaldsforbrænding og oparbejdning) samt forbedre forbrændingsanlæggets
effektivitet i forhold til CO2-ækvivalenter pr. kg.
P˚a trods af at studiet har fundet frem til værdifulde resultater, er det vigtigt at anerkende den be-
grænsede repræstenterbarhed studiet har.
Affaldsanalysen er baseret p˚a ´en affaldsg˚ard, og den har
dannet grundlag for de fleste efterfølgende analyser. Resultaterne fra MFA’en og LCA’en er p˚avirket
af antagelser om parameter-værdier og usikkerheder relateret til disse. For at opn˚a mere viden om
modellens p˚avirkning af usikre data bør der udføres en følsomhedsanalyse. Undersøgelsen konkluderer,
at DTU kan forbedre affaldsh˚andteringen ved at implementere regelmæssige affaldsanalyser og inddrage
brugerne fremadrettet. Derudover anbefales det løbende at evaluere affaldssystemets miljøp˚avirkninger
i forbindelse med ændringer samt at bestræbe sig p˚a at forbedre datakvaliteten.

Preface
This report is a Master’s thesis written as the final project for the environmental engineering Master’s
programme at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The thesis is conducted and written by two
master students at DTU Sustain, Line Kofod Egestad and Marie Skov Andersen. It corresponds to 30
ECTS points and was conducted in the spring of 2024 (from 8th of January to 8th of June 2024).
The master thesis conducts an environmental analysis of waste management at DTU based on a frame-
work using MFA-LCA methodologies.
A project plan was made to scope the project at an early stage. The original version of this is attached
separately in Appendix E, along with a final evaluation of the process. Images from the waste sampling
are attached separately in Appendix F.

Background
Higher education institutions (HEIs) have the power, possibility, and responsibility to educate future
generations about proper and sustainable practices. Their role extends beyond mere education; they
serve as exemplars for other industries facing similar challenges.
In this context, sustainable waste
management (WM) is critical to achieving a circular economy. In a circular economy, WM functions
as a strategic tool, aiming to minimize waste, promote recycling, and conserve valuable resources. By
efficiently managing waste, HEIs mitigate environmental impact and foster economic opportunities.
The waste hierarchy, also called the waste management hierarchy, provides guidelines for prioritizing
WM principles. At its core, prevention takes precedence, followed by reuse, recycling, recovery, and, as
a last resort, disposal (The European Parliament, 2023). While this hierarchy prioritizes circularity, it is
essential to note that there are typically environmental burdens related to waste treatment, whether it
is state-of-the-art recycling or uncontrolled landfills.
In Denmark, where 23% of solid waste is sent to waste-to-energy (WtE) plants (EPA, 2023), the
benefits of energy recovery typically outweigh the impacts of the incineration itself. These benefits
are specifically due to high efficiency in the heat utilization caused by the incineration (Bassi et al.,
2017). While this does provide a form of recovery, it ultimately leads to the loss of materials that could
have been reintroduced into the production cycle. Therefore, while energy recovery has its benefits,
it should not necessarily detract from efforts higher up in the waste hierarchy that contribute more
directly to a circular economy. From a broader perspective, in Denmark, the environmental impacts of
WM constitute ∼5% of the national emissions of CO2-eq. in 2019 for Denmark (Danish Ministry of
Environment, 2020). The largest share of these emissions originates from incineration, aligning with the
substantial share of waste directed toward incineration.
When evaluating WM, it is essential to begin by examining the waste materials in the system. The
physicochemical composition of a waste stream varies, and assuming that waste generation remains
constant is overly simplistic. Consequently, the purity and quality of waste streams also vary, ultimately
resulting in different treatment qualities. To ensure an environmentally sound treatment, it is necessary
to monitor what waste items get sorted in the first place and how they are sorted.
This study is done in collaboration with the Campus Service (CAS) of the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU) and DTU Sustain, an academic department. CAS published a WM plan for DTU in
2024, covering future initiatives towards an ambitious waste management system (WMS). The plan
evaluates current waste practices at DTU and sets future targets for recycling efficiencies (Nielsen et al.,
2024). CAS has previously engaged external contractors to assess their WMS by sampling one day’s
residual waste from the campus, specifically sorting the waste and looking into the composition. A
research study at DTU Sustain has further looked into organic waste, quantifying the potential for
source segregation compared to no sorting (Edjabou et al., 2016). Despite actions and research related
to WM at DTU, no general framework exists to monitor progress.

Throughout the report, every time a container type is mentioned by name, the reference will be noted
with the first letter capitalized (e.g., Plastics instead of plastics, and Glass, not glass). The type Ceramics

both Plastics and FBCs. When assessing waste samples, the amounts and types of missortings will be
inspected. Missortings are waste items that should have been sorted or treated differently by the users.
Whether an item is a missort depends on both internal guidelines for sorting at DTU and national and
municipal legislation.
When talking about recycling rates, it is important to distinguish between what is sent to recycling
and what actually ends up as secondary raw materials, substituting primary materials (The European
Parliament, 2024). The latter also refers to when waste meets the end-of-waste criteria (EoW), as
defined by the waste framework directive made by the European Commission. At the point of EoW,
the materials are no longer seen as waste by the general society and can be assigned value as a primary
resource instead. At this point, the EoW rate can be calculated (also known as ”reel genanvendelse”
in Danish) and represents a rate taking all efficiencies and losses throughout the waste treatment into
account (The European Parliament, 2023). For the amounts sent to recycling, these can be referred to as
segregated amounts. The total amount of generated waste can be described as segregation potentials.
The segregation potential is the amount of waste that could be segregated if all generation units,
e.g. users in the WMS, participated 100% and followed the sorting guideline accurately (Christensen,
2010). For instance, when users sort materials like plastics, a portion will be correctly segregated, while
the rest may likely end up as residual waste. Knowing the segregation potential in a system and the
actual segregated amounts enables the calculation of segregation efficiency. The segregation efficiency
is the ratio of the actually collected materials divided by the segregation potential. The perfect system,
where all waste is sorted in the correct waste bins, would hence have a 100% segregation efficiency. In
this context, and in this study, achieving 100% recycling depends on accurately sorting all recyclable
materials right from the user’s initial segregation process to the point of EoW. This allows for monitoring
of source-segregation potentials, user habits, impurities, and technological efficiencies.
The goal of an efficient WMS extends beyond merely reducing residual waste. Effective WM essentially
relies on understanding and adapting human behavior and lifestyle changes. Waste sorting is often
regarded as a secondary activity, as it is an additional activity caused by another primary activity, e.g.,
eating, using a product, etc. In large-scale WMS, e.g., in big companies or universities, the shared
responsibility proposes a challenge for the WMS, as it allows for diffusion of individual responsibility
(Katan, 2022).

Material Flow Analysis
An MFA is a quantitative method used to analyze flows of material, e.g., waste. An MFA helps to
understand resource utilization and waste streams (Brunner and Rechberger, 2017). The method is
based on the conservation of material balances, considering all inputs, stocks, and outputs related to
the system. It is a valuable tool for evaluating WMS, especially when combined with an LCA. MFAs
can be used to provide a complete characterization of WMS, giving insights into the flows of materials
(e.g., waste generation, treatment, and disposal) within the system (Brunner and Rechberger, 2017).
This includes the quantities of generated waste being recycled, utilized, incinerated, and sent to special
treatment.
In an MFA, the system boundaries must be defined, i.e., determining what the system includes and
excludes. For a process to be included as a process, some sort of action must be done within the
process. This action can be by transforming, transporting, or storing materials. Flows of materials go
between the processes, in e.g., kg/yr, and these are defined in an MFA as well (Brunner and Rechberger,
2017).
Life Cycle Analysis
An LCA is an assessment method used to evaluate environmental impacts of a product, process, service,
or system throughout its entire life cycle (Hauschild et al., 2018). Generally speaking, the analysis
consists of four iterative phases: goal and scope (G&S), life cycle inventory (LCI), life cycle impact
assessment (LCIA), and interpretation.
G&S:
Clarifies why the LCA is conducted. The scope is specified, including the functional unit (FU),
system boundaries, target audience, decision context, and other methodological choices.
LCI:
In this phase, an inventory of quantitative data on the inputs (materials, energy, etc.), outputs
(emissions, waste, etc.), and processes associated with the system is collected.
LCIA:
In the LCIA, the environmental impact is evaluated. The LCIA involves characterizing the LCI
into impact categories (e.g., global warming, acidification, eutrophication), using characteriza-
tion factors depending on the LCIA methodology (e.g., EF, ILCD, IMPACT, ReCiPe, etc.).
Interpretation: The interpretation phase aims to draw meaningful conclusions from the LCI and LCIA
results. It can involve contribution, completeness, sensitivity, and consistency checks and ad-
dress uncertainties for further research. Based on the interpretation, environmental performance
or modeling recommendations can be provided.
The results of an LCA are expressed as impacts; however, these can be both positive and negative. When
an impact is positive, it is regarded as a burden. In contrast, when an impact is negative, it represents a
saving or benefit. A saving could occur, e.g., when a recycled material is used in production, replacing
the need for virgin materials.

1.3
Literature Review
This literature review aims to gain insights into current practices and evaluation methods of WM for
HEIs and identify how previous research related to this has been conducted. The literature is analyzed
and summarized by discussing the identified research gaps within the scope of this study. The literature
was found through a systematic literature search, described in greater detail in section 2.1.
WM within HEIs has been a subject of ongoing research. While monitoring and setting targets for WM
are not novel concepts, the absence of a universally accepted assessment framework poses challenges for
HEIs seeking to optimize their WM strategies (Mac¸in et al., TBD). Generally, HEIs lack a standardized
method to assess and make decisions regarding goal-oriented WM. Despite the prevalence of WM studies,
there is an absence of research specifically describing the limitations, experiences, and best practices
encountered by HEIs during WM implementation (Salvatore et al., 2022; Silva et al., 2014).
A PhD conducted in collaboration with Istanbul Technical University and DTU addresses the challenges
in campus-related WM studies and proposes an eight-step framework for institutions developing WM
strategies (Mac¸in et al., TBD). The process begins with a preliminary step that involves defining the goal
and scope of the study. This PhD then outlines the subsequent steps: 1) Determination of primary data
sources and methods, 2) Data collection, 3) MFA and planning of new WM scenarios, 4) Background
data-completing inventory, 5) LCIA, 6) Contribution, sensitivity, and uncertainty analysis, 7) Holistic
assessment of MFA-LCA results, and 8) Deciding new WM strategy, monitoring and repeating framework
(Mac¸in et al., TBD). This structured approach is meant to ensure a thorough and systematic study of
WM strategies based on both qualitative and quantitative measures. The following sections will present
the prevailing motivation and methodology used for assessing WMS in HEIs.
Drivers for Adopting Sustainable WM
HEIs are increasingly becoming the object of studies across different fields, including WM (Jakimiuk et
al., 2023), making it interesting to explore the underlying factors that motivate HEIs toward researching
and enhancing their WM practices.
Universities can be regarded as small cities or municipalities as they typically have a dense population
and mimic a diverse range of activities such as housing, offices, production facilities, parks, dorms, staff
buildings, laboratories, and common areas (Galvan et al., 2020). Testing strategies on universities can
be used to understand the extent of WMSs and their effect. Furthermore, WM in HEIs can inform and
engage students to take part in and understand the reasoning behind recycling and waste reduction,
skills that can be used beyond campus (Moqbel, 2018).
HEIs have the obligation to act responsibly and sustainably in terms of social, economic, and environ-
mental impacts to maintain integrity. ”Doing what you preach” becomes a crucial aspect when their
primary function is to educate and convey the latest knowledge and findings within a range of scientific
fields (Armijo de Vega et al., 2008; Fissi et al., 2021). By looking at WM optimization for a HEI, it not
only benefits the institution by reducing the financial cost associated with waste handling but fosters
an environmentally conscious community (Armijo de Vega et al., 2008).
While the aspects mentioned above are the most frequently observed drivers for WM optimization in
research articles describing high-income countries, developing countries are motivated by other reasons.
In the literature, it is argued that critical goals for recycling have forced especially developing countries to
have a focused look at WM. These drivers are typically caused by external pressures, such as population

Mbama et al., 2023). Some HEIs in developing countries face challenges making a financially sustainable

(Mbama et al., 2023). Inadequate WM in these cases can cause water contamination, disease, and
pollution and risks to the local community’s overall health (Mbama et al., 2023).
Applied Approaches and Practices
Many studies focus on waste characterizations to enhance the sustainability of WMS at HEIs. These
investigations collect data such as waste generation patterns, waste composition (including material
fractions), and waste purity (contaminants and missortings) (Armijo de Vega et al., 2008; Smyth et al.,
2010; Moqbel, 2018; Mbama et al., 2023; Jakimiuk et al., 2023). By comprehending waste characteris-
tics, HEIs can tailor their management practices more effectively. However, waste characterizations are
frequently utilized as a comprehensive evaluation of a WM practice rather than a preliminary step for
improvement. Furthermore, they rarely specify the intended use of the results in subsequent operations.
Most of the researched studies include a separation of the sorted material fractions and a screening for
recycling potentials in the residual waste (Armijo de Vega et al., 2008; Smyth et al., 2010; Mbama
et al., 2023). The quantifications are often based on observations, surveys, and waste samples.
The segregated fractions vary depending on national regulations and the level of engagement, and
typically, universities segregate in a range from three to seven fractions.
Residual waste is, for all
studies, the most dominant waste type identified, often followed by organic or paper waste if segregated
separately (Silva et al., 2014; Mbama et al., 2023; Haksevenler et al., 2022; Jakimiuk et al., 2023). For
developing countries, the primary disposal method is found to be landfilling, while incineration is the
most observed treatment method in high-income countries (Mbama et al., 2023; Stephan et al., 2020).
Another popular assessment method involves analyzing environmental impacts or flows in the form of
an MFA, an LCA, or a combination thereof. Economic input-output LCA (EIO-LCA) and extended
environmental input-output LCA (EEIO-LCA) are also used, typically considering procurement data as
the primary source (Stephan et al., 2020; Graham Sustainability Institute and Michigan office of campus
sustainability, 2011; Rosenblum et al., 2000; Larsen H. et al., 2011). Typically, the LCA studies include
assessments of all university operations, while few studies focus exclusively on the WMS of the HEI. The
studies, including all university operations, find the WM to be causing a relatively small share of the
total environmental impacts, only accounting for 1-3% of the total impacts of the university activities
(Rosenblum et al., 2000; Woodhouse and Olloqui, 2014; SDU, 2019). All the selected studies include
municipal solid waste from various campus activities, and most of them, except for the EIO-LCAs,
exclude hazardous waste, as this waste type is handled separately from municipal solid waste.
As the needs in WM tend to be similar in countries close to each other, it is natural that case studies
from the same country and area are methodologically inspired by each other and can have similar findings
(Anuardo et al., 2022). As mentioned previously, a waste characterization of organic waste has also
been conducted at DTU Sustain (Edjabou et al., 2016), but the objective was to estimate generation
rates and not to look into future steps.

areas for improvement.
Research Gaps, Challenges, and Barriers
The obstacles to sustainable WM practices vary from financial issues to lacking engagement from stake-
holders. Studies often recommend large scale recycling programs for an ideal WM, however implemen-
tation of such programs are time-consuming and expensive for a university (Smyth et al., 2010; Moqbel,
2018). Some universities can be compared to a city, thus making it time-consuming and complex to
implement a plan, going from a pilot phase to full implementation. When a university administration
designates funds towards enhancing their WM, it inevitably results in a reduction of available funding
for other university priorities, including academic programs and various initiatives. It is necessary for
universities to focus on the long-term benefits of a more sustainable WM (Moqbel, 2018). The con-
sequences of not investing in WM are not only stagnation in development but a potential reduction in
treatment quality due to inadequate demand for better infrastructure.
Another challenge for universities is the uniqueness of faculties, ongoing research, and WM solutions.
The optimal WMS for a HEI is based on the culture, type of HEI, and waste generated, which is also
specific for each location and depends on several factors. The variation in waste is both geographical and
temporal, where amounts and compositions fluctuate due to events, seasons, or day-to-day activities.
This variability requires a customized WMS for each solution, ruling out a universally standardized WM
practice for all HEIs (Jakimiuk et al., 2023).

Lacking awareness of WM practices and changes is a barrier for several studies of HEIs (Salvatore et al.,
2022; Haksevenler et al., 2022; Galvan et al., 2020). This lack is especially seen for internal stakeholders
and users of the system (e.g., students and staff). Awareness by campaigns, workshops, etc., can be
conducted to engage the users, creating collaboration and a more institutionalized WM practice (Fissi et
al., 2021). However, it is important to facilitate potential campaigns or workshops, properly emphasizing
the changes that will happen and why the changes are happening (Moqbel, 2018). This ultimately
concerns behavioral psychology, and a big challenge lies in demanding behavioral changes from users
(Salvatore et al., 2022). Communication between stakeholders should be maintained continuously as
HEIs generally have a high turnover rate of users due to a high turnover rate in students and staff.
New users should be informed and engaged actively on the existing WMS when being introduced to the
university to maintain a good WM practice (Haksevenler et al., 2022).
As mentioned, HEIs vary in size, culture, users, and activities, ultimately making it difficult to compare
and get inspiration on WMS from other universities. Although universities are the subject of research
across various disciplines, and analyses of waste compositions and strategies are gaining relevance in
the context of circular economics and regulations, there is a notable scarcity of publicly available data
from Nordic universities. A large share of the identified studies came from developing countries where
researchers evaluated WMS. For Northern European countries, WM in universities could potentially
be managed by more administrative personnel, thus not publishing the findings and decision-making
process in an article format. This means that there is not necessarily a lack of Nordic assessments but
rather a lack of publicly shared assessments, limiting the available knowledge for institutions making a
similar transition.
Many of the studies lack a systematic and quantitative approach to WM evaluation, resulting in an
inconclusive and constructive status of the WM, not presenting any clear alterations for improvement.
This is why the proposal for a systematic approach by Mac¸in et al. (TBD) was presented as a guide for
institutions trying to develop a sustainable WMS with clear guidelines for monitoring.

1.4
Case Study Description
DTU is a polytechnic university with five campuses in Denmark and Greenland, the largest of which is
located in Kongens Lyngby, Denmark. Lyngby campus has more than 100 buildings used for teaching,
laboratories, companies, and dormitories. The university is divided into 18 academic departments with
a total of 6,000 employees and 13,000 students studying various degrees.
The sub-departments of CAS maintain DTU areas and facilities. CAS is in charge of all waste handling in
outdoor and common areas. Waste collected in indoor trash bins is handled by the cleaning personnel,
facilitated by the sub-department CAS Service. The present procedure for the cleaning personnel is
to collect all bags of waste along a route in one big collection bag, whereafter they have to sort
the waste into its respective containers at the outdoor collection points (CPs).
CAS Sustainability
handles the strategic planning and sustainability of the WMS. Other hands-on tasks within WM include
securing proper facilities for outdoor waste sorting and collection, all handled by the sub-department
CAS Maintenance.
The existing municipal solid WM practices include segregating in 11 waste types in containers at waste
CPs. The types are depicted in Figure 1, excluding Heat-resistant glass/Pyrex. Beyond these types,
special waste can be picked up and handled separately. The segregated waste is deposited into separate
closed containers of ranging sizes and colors with pictograms symbolizing the correct sorting. Most waste
types are collected internally by CAS Maintenance and transported to the correct handling facilities,
either by the collection and compactor (CC) or collection and loading (CL) truck. Food waste is collected
and handled by an external waste facilitator. Five container types are picked up within a regular schedule,
all seen on the top row in Figure 1. The remaining types are emptied occasionally as required.
Figure 1: Municipal solid waste pictograms of the ten container types segregated at the DTU.
As a part of the sustainability initiatives for DTU, CAS released the first-ever WM plan in 2024 (Nielsen
et al., 2024). The WM plan, among other things, sets targets for recycling efficiencies from 2024-2035.
The WM plan is the first edition and includes a roadmap to achieve the targets from 2024-2026. The
ambition of the plan includes better opportunities for initiatives and pilot projects. It plans to meet
legislation requirements on short- and long-term planning objectives up to 2035.
The WM plan does not aim to reduce the total quantity of waste generated at DTU but rather focuses
on the efficiency and potential of segregation and recycling. The targets are divided into goals for
recycling, reject, incineration, special treatment, and landfilling. Furthermore, goals for the composition
of Residual waste are set, corresponding to a maximum limit of 15% non-residual waste in Residual
waste by 2030. To achieve these goals, primary data from DTU is needed to identify the hotspots and
continuous monitoring could be used to evaluate the progress and achievements of the WM plan.

2.2.1
Mapping of Waste Collection Points
The mapping was a preliminary field study conducted to gain primary data concerning the waste col-

was conducted during two days in mid-February 2024. The waste CPs at DTU Lyngby Campus were
mapped, collecting data about the specific locations, condition of facilities, segregation options, and
container sizes to get an up-to-date overview of the outdoor waste collection facilities at DTU Lyngby.
Before the mapping, a map of known CPs managed by CAS was prepared based on location data from
CAS originating from 2021. This map contained data on CP locations. Only outdoor waste containers
used for industrial pick-up by waste facilitators and sanitation workers were included in the mapping.
This includes individual containers scattered around the campus as well as clusters of containers. Some
CPs were situated in close proximity to each other, but they were registered as distinct CPs due to them
being enclosed by separate fences.
A data input sheet was set up to log exact locations based on latitude and longitude from GPS mea-
surements. Furthermore, the available sorting types, quantity of containers for each sorting types, and
volumes of containers were documented during the fieldwork. Examples of how waste CPs are set up
can be seen in Appendix A.1.
The mapping only focused on containers belonging to CAS. This includes waste CPs near offices,
teaching facilities, laboratories, parks, and dorms. Any containers not present at the waste CPs due to
maintenance or replacement during the mapping were not considered.
For each waste CP, photos were taken and saved both locally and on the online street-level mapping tool
Mapilliary (https://www.mapillary.com) for documentation. The data sheet was filled out using Google
Maps to extract coordinates. The map of known waste CPs was used as a guideline for navigating on
campus, as the route covered most roads and yards within visible distance. However, it is possible that
single containers could have been missed during the mapping.

Data Analysis
The data from the waste samplings was treated in Excel, where the average composition of sub-fractions
for each container type was found. The sub-fractions were categorized as either missorted or correctly
sorted, and furthermore divided into four treatment categories: recycling, incineration, missorted recy-
clables, and special treatment.
In this context recycling refers to all fractions that are sent to waste facilities, whereas missorted
recyclables indicate that the sub-fraction could be recycled if sorted correctly by the user. incineration
refers to all sub-fractions that are supposed to go to incineration, and special treatment covers sub-
fractions that are not able to be either incinerated or directly recycled, ultimately requiring special
attention. In this study, special treatment also covers fractions that are utilized but for less profitable
use, such as ceramics used for pavements or concrete. Typically, landfilling will also be included as a
treatment category, but as no municipal solid waste from DTU goes directly to a landfill, this treatment
is excluded from the categorization.
To obtain a composition for modeling, the arithmetic mean was calculated, taking all samples for each
container type into consideration. However, one sampling cycle of Residual waste was deemed an outlier
and was thus removed from the dataset used for modeling. The compositions are seen in Appendix A.4.

strong if they were connected to a large number of stakeholders in the network, thus being in a cen-
tralized position. Stakeholder relations were considered weaker when they involved a stakeholder within
the network who was dissimilar and had infrequent or virtually non-existent communication patterns.
To complement the SNA, an influence-interest grid was constructed, plotting the stakeholders in a
matrix based on their level of influence and interest in the WMS at DTU. As according to Reed et al.,
2009, the four quadrants in the grid were defined as;
• High Influence - High Interest: Stakeholders that can make the biggest impact and who should
be kept engaged. These stakeholders can affect the project outcomes and are deeply interested.
Such stakeholders could be authorities within WM that make sure policies and development are
maintained.
• High Influence – Low Interest: These stakeholders hold the power to affect the project, however,
they are not interested in the daily operation.
This could be the CEO of a company who is
interested in key decisions and progress but not necessarily how it is established. However, with
high influence, they make great consultants.
• Low Influence – High Interest: Stakeholders are very interested and are beneficial to maintain a
close relationship with. Stakeholders with this position have a high level of interest in the project
but do not have direct influence. These could be interest groups that can help promote the cause
of the project.
• Low Influence – Low Interest: Stakeholders are not directly invested in the project but should be
kept informed. Stakeholders within this group have little to no interest or influence in the project
but should not be neglected. This could be users of the WMS who do not directly influence the
system but should be kept informed of changes for the WMS to function.
Inspecting, categorizing, and evaluating stakeholders in a WMS can help identify the right approach to
interact with stakeholders, ultimately leading to a more efficient WM.

municipal solid waste from DTU. Electronics and Batteries were not included in the modeling of the

significantly increases the assessment’s complexity due to the wide variety of electronic devices (e.g.,
servers, PCs, and other appliances) and battery chemistry (e.g., lithium-ion). Specific data for each type
of electronic product and battery would be needed, making the study extensively more resource-intensive.
Furthermore, as these waste items were typically found in their respective and correct container type,
the handling can be assumed to be correct, and for these fractions, that at least ensures proper and
approved treatment.
To quantify the MFA, both the amounts for each container type, as well as the composition of waste
within the container type, had to be defined. The first represents how the one tonne of waste will be
sorted into containers and, thus, how the distribution between container types will be. The composition
for each container type describes what sub-fractions are found in the flow related to that specific
container type. The amounts of the one tonne inflow of waste is based on annual waste reporting from
2022 from DTU CAS, as seen in Appendix B.1, Table 22. Waste amounts were aggregated based on
EAK codes resembling municipal solid waste. The matching of EAK codes and container types can be
seen in Appendix B.1, Table 23. This is meant to ensure that the amounts are not affected by other
types of waste, such as building and demolition waste. The codes are based on the waste facilitators’
identification of the waste batch received. The composition within the container types was based on the
waste sampling findings regarding correctly and missorted items. Missorted items would be expected to
be separated in the initial sorting process at the waste facilitators. The further technological treatment
of the waste, defining the output of materials and energy depicted in the MFA, is based on information
from stakeholders and average data from the Danish EPA and DTU (EPA, 2020; EPA and DTU Sustain,
Unpublished). The flows are color-coded based on the origin of data in the MFA. The full MFA can
be seen in Appendix B.2, Figure 26. Simplified flow diagrams for Batteries and Electronics can also be
seen in Appendix B.2.
To conduct the MFA, transfer coefficients describing the partitioning of a material within a process,
either based on segregation efficiencies, impurities, or technological efficiency, were assigned to each
process. segregation efficiencies are calculated based on the proportion of a desired material fraction
present in the correct containers and are determined by the waste characterization.
The purity of
a container type refers to the share of intended material within the container relative to its overall
quantity. Purity is also determined by the waste characterization. For the technological efficiencies, the
transfer coefficients are based on information from stakeholders and average data from the Danish EPA
and DTU (EPA, 2020; EPA and DTU Sustain, Unpublished) and refer to the reprocessing of recyclable
material. The EoW rate refers to the rate of substituting primary materials. This rate is calculated by
considering all efficiencies and losses for the system into account and is thus based on the previously
described efficiencies. The system in the MFA does not take inputs of chemical substances or gasoline
for transport into account and solely focuses on the material flows of the waste. Furthermore, energy
flows are excluded in an MFA, as no mass is linked directly to these.
The setup of treatment processes was defined in collaboration with CAS personnel and waste facilitators.
These defined processes served as system boundaries for constructing the MFA. It is important to

was in the container compared to the total amount of that specific material present in the system. This
can indicate the system’s users’ efficiency. The EoW rates are the total system efficiencies, representing
the share of materials re-entering the market, taking all system efficiencies into account.
Introduction of Scenarios
A baseline scenario was modeled, assuming composition and sorting habits as observed in the waste
sampling. Besides the baseline scenario, two additional scenarios were made to show the potential of
increased segregation of recyclables, diverting them away from the stream of Residual waste to the
correct fractions. All three scenarios have been assessed in the MFA and LCA.
The first scenario represents a realistic scenario, depicting optimized sorting by the users while not being
perfect. The second scenario depicts an optimistic scenario, evaluating the full potential of a perfect
sorting of the recyclables in Residual waste into their respective available waste fractions. The scenarios
are based on the results from the waste characterization and assumptions described in Appendix D.
The realistic scenario assumes that 80% of the food waste in the Residual waste flow from the baseline
scenario is sorted correctly into the Food waste fraction. Furthermore, it is assumed that 90% of the
identified paper and cardboard waste in Residual waste fraction can be correctly sorted, along with 95%
of the metals and ceramics discovered in Residual waste. Lastly, it is assumed that 50% of the plastics
found in Residual waste is diverted to the Plastics containers. In the optimistic scenario, it is assumed
that all waste connected to a container type is removed from the Residual waste flow and sorted into
the correct containers. Collection bags identified in Residual waste were not removed for either of the
scenarios, even though they are made of LDPE and are recyclable. No glass was found in the sampling
of residual waste, thus, no changes to these flows occur in the scenarios.
The diversion of recyclables influenced the mass of each recyclable waste flow, but the impurities were
kept constant. This means that the percentages of missortings for the container types are not altered in
the two additional scenarios, even though the added amounts from Residual waste, if correctly sorted,
could improve the purity of the recyclable waste flows. This is done based on the assumption that users
who sort waste items are aware of the materials and have deemed the sorting correct, even if it does not
correspond with the specific criteria of the container type. Furthermore, this study aims to highlight the
potential of an improvement in Residual waste, and not in an understanding of the material fractions.

The goal of the LCA is to evaluate the environmental impacts associated with the WMS of the municipal
solid waste fractions at DTU, specifically gaining insights into the relevance of the recycling potential.
Some of the methodological decisions will be presented in this section, while a comprehensive and more
technical description of the LCA-setup can be seen in Appendix C. Assumptions are listed in Appendix D.
It is important to note that the LCA functions more as a screening than an in-depth LCA, as its intended
function is guidance more than an exact analysis. This limits the use of the results, as the underlying
uncertainties will be much greater than in an LCA strictly following the ILCD or ISO guidelines. This is
further described in the G&S, seen in Appendix C.1 and C.2.
To perform an LCA, it is crucial to decide what function the studied system must fulfill, i.e. the FU of
the model. The FU in this study is:
Collection and treatment of one tonne of municipal solid waste from the Technical University of
Denmark, using waste treatment technologies available in 2024
This means that the results of the modeling (i.e. the estimated impacts of the system) will be per tonne
of municipal solid waste collected and treated. The target audience of the LCA is CAS Sustainability
and the supervisors of this study.

Examination of Uncertainty
The uncertainty of the model was investigated by changing the unit process for the heat-generation
used in the model. The results for this scenario were extracted and compared to the results of the
baseline, highlighting the influence of the heat process. Practically, the comparison was based on the
relative change (RC) between the two sets of results. Simply put, the RC is the percentage change in
the results when changing one or multiple parameters of the model. These parameters can either be
values or processes. If a parameter with high uncertainty also shows a high RC, it indicates that the
model’s results are highly influenced by this parameter. In such cases, efforts should be made to reduce
the uncertainty of this parameter, for example, by collecting more precise data or using more reliable
data sources.
Data Quality Rating
To assess the various kinds of data inputs used for the analysis, a data quality rating (DQR) was
performed based on an adapted pedigree matrix. The objective of the rating was to assess the quality
of the data used for modeling and to evaluate which data points should be further investigated to get
more reliable results.
In the study, an adapted pedigree matrix was developed based on Weidema et al., 2013, and was used
to assess the quality of data sources based on five independent data quality indicators (DQI). Each data
source is assigned a score for the DQIs ranging from one to five, with one indicating the highest quality
level.
The DQIs were chosen as reliability, completeness, temporal representativeness, geographical
representativeness, and technological representativeness. Each indicator was scored according to the
criteria described in Appendix C.5. The values of these five indicators are then used to calculate the
DQR for each data point, as seen in equation 1.
DQR =

Where DQI is all data quality indicator scores from one to five, Xw is the weakest score value obtained,
and n is the number of DQIs. The DQR for a data point can be between one and five, where a low
DQR indicates a high data quality and consequently a low uncertainty, whereas a high DQR suggests
a substantial uncertainty or potential issues with the data quality. For simplicity, the parameters were
divided into five categories for each container type: waste treatment, sorting, substitution, collection,
and transport (as seen in Appendix C.5, Table 10). The DQR was calculated for each category, and
the overall DQR for a specific container type was then determined by taking the average of the DQRs
across these five categories.

Condition of the Identified Collection Points
The layout and day-to-day use of the CPs are of high priority for CAS. The DTU WM plan for 2024-2026
has a continuous goal of satisfactory physical properties of the CPs (Nielsen et al., 2024). The physical
properties that CAS requires include spatial factors and desired conditions of CPs. As CPs should be
designed for users, including cleaning personnel, who dispose of waste, and sanitation workers who empty
containers, the entrance and space within the CP are highly important. For a large part of the mapped
CPs, the quantity of containers does not correlate with the space available. This results in containers
placed perpendicular to the back of the CP for space optimization, hiding the sorting pictograms on
the sides of the containers, making all containers appear the same (and as seen in Figure 19, Appendix
A.1). One simple way to fix this would be to put pictograms on all sides of the containers, ensuring
that no matter the position of the container, the pictograms will be visible.
Another issue regarding information is the changes in sorting guidelines not being updated for all CPs.
This creates confusion and can be misleading for the users. An example is the implementation of waste
pictograms on waste containers in 2020, which overrules the previous information plates still placed on
the fences above containers. These information plates are typically plastic sheets or simply laminated
paper with information about sorting guidelines, as seen in Appendix A.1, Figure 21. As the locations of
containers can vary within a CP, the information plates might not correspond to the container standing
in front of them. Another misleading information involves the segregation of EPS, which has previously
been sorted in Residual waste as stated by the plates, while the internal DTU website guides users to
segregate items of EPS separately. Clear information on proper waste segregation is crucial due to the
variability in waste segregation practices across different countries and municipalities within Denmark.
As DTU is an international university, it is important to provide segregation guidelines in both Danish
and English to encourage and engage all users. Due to currently incoherent guidelines, it is expected
that missortings will occur naturally in the WMS.
Based on the mapping, the distribution of containers for recyclables compared to the Residual waste
containers does not favor recycling, neither in the quantity of containers present nor in their volume.
The visual appearance of the CPs was dominated by containers for Residual waste.
To encourage
more sustainable behavior, a strategic nudging approach could prioritize containers for recyclables,
making them more dominant and conspicuous than Residual waste containers. However, this emphasis
on recyclables should not prevent users’ ability to dispose of residual waste at any point. A similar
incentive for Cardboard containers could involve encouraging users to compact cardboard before disposal.
Currently, Cardboard containers are almost exclusively large in volume, and a small knife is typically
attached to the fences at CPs for folding and flattening the boxes, as seen in Figure 21, Appendix A.1.
Downsizing the containers could further incentivize compacting; however, ensuring that these containers
do not overflow and compromise the quality of cardboard recycling by becoming wet is crucial.

Discussion of Representativeness
When addressing the representativeness of the waste samplings, a lot of aspects have already been high-
lighted. Generally, when analyzing waste sampling data, it is important to acknowledge the underlying
and inherent uncertainty related to it.
The samplings were conducted during periods that were deemed to represent periods of time with normal
workflow of both offices and student areas, but even so, varying habits and patterns are unavoidable.
The data was collected during the 13-week semester period, which limits temporal representativeness.
Furthermore, the sampling periods were organized so as not to overlap with any holiday periods or
bigger events during the semester. This also means that the results do not even represent the complete
13 weeks in the semester, as the number of users will vary greatly during these events.
One way
to mitigate this uncertainty could be to categorize similar periods in the annual cycle of DTU and
conduct waste samplings during each of the categorized periods in the cycle. This would also highlight
and help differentiate between trends in staff behavior and the effects of events. By monitoring the
waste compositions during longer time periods, potentially on a monthly basis, it would be possible to
conduct more comparative statistics or test hypotheses. This would also ensure a higher certainty in
the representativeness of the data, as well as a better insight into patterns.

Recommendations for Future Waste Samplings
Considering the scope and purpose of this study, the methodological decisions behind the waste sampling
were deemed sufficient, but if future monitoring of waste compositions is to be made, it should include
waste from various academic departments, areas, and facilities. This will improve the representativeness
and provide better insights into the spatial variation of waste composition of the campus.
More descriptive statistics could have been analyzed if more samplings had been conducted or if only one
type of waste was in focus. However, due to the limitations of the study, as well as structural challenges
during the samplings, statistics would not enlighten anything in a meaningful way. Using the arithmetic
mean could also be argued against, as compositional data has so-called ”closed” characteristics (e.g.,
not containing values below 0 or above 100). It does thus not align with classical statistics for non-
constrained data (Edjabou et al., 2017).
When sampling waste, there is always a risk of finding something unexpected. This was reflected in
multiple samplings. However, one could also say that the ”unexpected” is due to wrong expectations.
Some of the unexpected fractions were also gathered separately simply because they were found in such
a great quantity that they were remarkable. This does not necessarily mean that they were missorted.
During the waste sampling, mistakes could have been made due to difficulties determining the material
an item was composed of. An example of this is the tubes found in the Residual waste originating from
the laboratories. These were of high quality and could resemble heat-resistant glass, but they were more
often than not plastic. If one had no knowledge about this, they could have easily been sampled as

heat-resistant glass. While it is acknowledged that small mistakes could have been made, the results
from the samplings are deemed reliable. For future waste samplings, the findings in this study, as well

As established in the literature review, other HEIs have been monitoring their waste generation and
composition through different methods. This study, for instance, employed a more standardized and
robust approach by conducting a waste sampling as recommended in Edjabou et al., 2015. In contrast,
some studies find it adequate to assess waste composition by simply opening waste containers, inspecting
their contents, and estimating the composition visually. The issue with this approach is that while both
methods are considered waste characterizations, their results are seldom directly comparable. However,
the variability in methodology does have its advantages. It allows for flexibility in financial requirements,
as waste samplings are significantly more expensive than container inspections. This flexibility enables
HEIs to choose a method that best suits their budget and needs.
When interpreting the results, it is important to consider the potential impact of fluid absorption on
the weight-based percentages, as highlighted with napkins and tissues earlier. Further studies could be
conducted to quantify the effect of fluid absorption on the weight of different waste fractions and to
develop more accurate methods for waste characterization. This underlines the importance of considering
the specific characteristics of waste materials when conducting waste characterizations.

Dynamics of Stakeholder Interactions
When discussing stakeholder interactions within the network, it is important to note that the ties are
based on communication flows without distinguishing between the different types of communication.
Other flows, such as material or financial, could also have been highlighted. A strong stakeholder is
characterized by having many relationships and is more influential than other stakeholders with fewer
relations. However, the strength of the relationship depends on the nature of the information flow. For
instance, while some stakeholders may interact frequently with others, their information exchanges might
be primarily related to accounting and reporting, emphasizing more formal and bureaucratic aspects.
In contrast, other stakeholders only interact and exchange information about day-to-day needs. This
means these stakeholders do not follow a set pattern or system for information exchange.
Assuming that all stakeholders need to be equally involved and that influence and interest in a project
are important parameters for a stakeholder may not be true. External stakeholders performing a service
play a critical role in the SNA without having to communicate with DTU stakeholders. They often
have a high influence but not necessarily a high interest in the DTU WMS as a specific case. So, while
levels of interest and influence are important to look at for stakeholders, alternative criteria could be
considered.
In the process of strategic planning, a large amount of vital information is exchanged between strong
stakeholders. This information often forms the backbone of the strategies being developed. However,
while this information is crucial, it does not always encompass the practical insights and challenges that
might arise for, e.g., users of the WMS. These insights and challenges are often the result of on-the-
ground realities, which may not be fully captured during high-level strategic discussions. To enhance
the stakeholder network, it is beneficial to increase the diversity of stakeholders involved. Including
weaker ties in the stakeholder network would create access to a diverse flow of information with more
perspectives, enriching the overall knowledge. This is attempted in the waste network but could be
further enhanced by including all stakeholders involved in WM, such as CAS Service.
The stakeholder analysis reflects a momentary depiction of the informational flows identified by the
conductors of this study. Unidentified flows could exist and affect the network. However, to establish
a solid network for a WMS, it should be well-defined and transparent for the system’s users, so if the
links are not identified by this study, it could indicate a lack of transparency.

Future Considerations for LCAs of the WMS at DTU
When assessing the results of an LCA, it is important to understand both the potential and the lim-
itations. These have been highlighted to some degree, but a comprehensive sensitivity analysis could
be conducted, potentially for each sub-system, further inspecting the requirements for robust data. As
described earlier in this study, the expectations were that primary data (e.g., shares of impurities in the
waste) from waste facilitators would be crucial in the modeling and validating sampling results, but the
collected data did not necessarily align with the waste composition found during the sampling. Thus,
conducting a sensitivity analysis inspecting the sensitivity of the efficiencies used in the sorting processes
could be beneficial.
Another interesting aspect could be to conduct a break-even analysis of when it is no longer benefi-
cial for DTU to incinerate residual waste in terms of substitution, considering the transition to more
sustainable energy sources in Denmark. The analysis could be grounded in a consequential approach,
illustrating the environmental impact of each additional unit of waste incinerated. This would consider
the marginal changes in environmental burdens due to changes in the WMS at DTU instead of the
average environmental burden. This approach would provide insights into the environmental trade-offs
of continuing waste incineration versus transitioning to alternative WM strategies in the future.

This study contains several assessment methods, and while the results are useful, each methodology
used could be further investigated and at a higher level of detail. An obvious example of this is the LCA,

Enhancing WM Practices
Upon starting the study, it became evident that the CPs across DTU were not consistently updated
and did not adhere to the latest guidelines. Given that the majority of waste collected in the CPs
originates from waste bins placed inside buildings, indoor sorting could have been considered, evaluating
the correlation between sorting rates and proper sorting facilities for the users. The indoor segregation
options are expected to vary more than the segregation options in CPs. This is specifically due to
academic departments choosing their own sorting practices and designs in their facilities as a result of
the non-centralized characteristics of the WM. Unlike many other Danish universities, DTU does not
have a single department responsible for waste sorting facilities across the campus. A department like
this could potentially ensure consistent guidelines and implementation practices. However, having a
designated department for this also allows for less engagement from the academic departments. As
the stakeholder analysis shows, a more decentralized structure could be beneficial by allocating more
responsibility to all stakeholders and creating a more robust WMS. A robust waste network is essential
to ensure that the WMS functions. By providing a solid foundation for better source segregation, the
academic department can contribute to the overall success of the WMS.
At the other end of the system, waste facilitators significantly impact waste sorting outcomes. While
input quality matters, it is seen that technological and strategic planning within the WM business also
plays a crucial role. For improvement of the external part of the WMS, the waste facilitators should
document the actual recycling and improvement in the technological services provided, including more
transparency in the processes. Suppose an improved recycling technology or better monitoring is desired
from waste generators, such as DTU. In that case, the users must demand this regardless of the increased
pricing or complexity, as the market does not evolve without a demand. This development can be in
the form of monitoring waste streams, requiring transparency and reporting on recycling, improving the
EoW rates, exploring alternative treatment methods, and sharing findings and challenges among HEIs.
This can all lead to a positive impact on the waste sector.

the waste analysis conducted for DTU in 2021, all of the Residual waste from the campus was analyzed,

Indoor Sorting Challenges
Not all CPs contained options for all segregated types, which is why it is also important to focus on
streamlining the indoor segregation facilities to match the CPs. When users encounter an incomplete
indoor segregation display, waste will not be correctly sorted, affecting the overall waste purity in the
CPs. Conversely, if a container type is absent at the nearest CP, thorough sorting efforts by staff and
students may be pointless. The quality of segregation facilities impacts the user experience and incentive
to participate in sorting. Convenient waste bin locations and clear sorting displays motivate users to sort
correctly. In common areas such as HEIs, achieving consistent sorting behavior can be challenging due
to the large number of users responsible for the contents of a single waste bin. A few users adopting
incorrect sorting habits can undermine the entire system.
Users play a crucial role as they are responsible for the initial segregation of the waste. Currently,
information on WM flows through the waste network to the academic departments, striving to reach
both staff and students. As DTU is an international research-intensive university, the users come from
different waste sorting practices, if any, so an introduction to waste sorting practices could benefit the
WMS. This educational effort should focus on motivating users rather than imposing tasks, emphasizing
their role in taking responsibility. Human behavior tends to be controlled by habits, making it difficult
to affect behavioral changes in WM practices. Implementing updated guidelines and getting people to
observe and acknowledge these changes requires disrupting established patterns of waste sorting.

Optimizing WM from LCA
The results of the LCA came as no surprise, proving that diverting recyclables away from incineration will
benefit the WMS. The WMS for one tonne was also expected to lead to net savings, given the inclusion
of primary material substitution. More interestingly, it was proved quantitatively that the diversion
of recyclables would cause the incineration process itself to improve, looking at the impacts per kg.
This improvement was attributed to a reduction in the proportion of food waste sent to incineration,
thereby increasing the average energy utilization per kilogram incinerated. When the primary aim is to
enhance circularity, some may argue that an LCA could be seen as redundant. However, it can be an
effective tool to evaluate the potential benefits of prioritizing alternative treatment methods that might
have less impact than conventional methods. This specific LCA screening was intended to establish a
fundamental knowledge base and did not encompass the testing of various treatment methods.
It is widely recognized that many pivotal aspects concerning impacts are determined during the product
design phase. This encompasses impacts that the product may induce during the waste stage of its life
cycle, e.g., determining whether the products can be recycled at all. Moreover, considering the quality
of the recycled material during the design phase could potentially enhance substitution ratios. Although
an institution like DTU does not directly engage in product design, as consumers of products, they
can exert influence through their procurement policies, advocating for superior standards that consider
the waste stage of a product’s life cycle. This approach can drive the demand for products designed,
emphasizing waste minimization and effective recyclability.
Doing LCAs on a WMS differs in scope compared to performing an LCA for a product from cradle
to grave. An LCA of a WMS, such as the one conducted in this study, concentrates resources on a
single life stage, typically excluding the production and use stages where a significant proportion of
impacts often transpire. This focus allows for a more detailed analysis of the waste stage, but it also
means that potentially huge impacts from other stages are not accounted for. Therefore, while this
approach provides valuable insights into the environmental implications of WM strategies, it could be
complemented with broader LCAs that consider the entire life cycle of the products.
Even LCAs within WMS can vary significantly in scope as well, particularly in terms of whether they
account for the substitution of primary materials and to what extent. This study is based on qualified
assumptions regarding market responses and substitution materials, but the actual reality could be
different. It is also worth debating whether the benefits of material substitution should be factored into
the WMS, as the advantages of recyclables only become tangible when they are put into use within
an industry. Using the word ”benefit” could be generally misleading as it can insinuate a saving in the
form of, e.g., carbon sequestration, which is not the case here. Furthermore, if no company can utilize
the energy or material gained from a sustainable WMS, then no benefit is realized. It is not enough to
simply assess the potential benefits of a WMS in isolation; one must also consider the capacity of the
market to absorb and utilize the system’s outputs.

Overall Discussion and Recommendations for Future Work
3
RESULTS & DISCUSSION
During the literature search and initial data collection, it was evident that public data on WM at HEIs
in Nordic countries is scarce.
Substantially more data was available from developing countries.
In
these countries, residents directly experience, perceive, and witness the impacts, including health risks
associated with living near a dumpsite. As a result, the strategies derived may not be directly comparable
or applicable to the case of DTU. For DTU, the drive towards an optimized WMS is fueled more by
ambition and motivation. This motivation carries a certain privilege compared to that in developing
countries. Despite these differences, DTU has the opportunity to lead the way in a new approach. By
adopting a framework-based monitoring system, DTU could potentially establish a new benchmark for
data sharing and overall practices at HEIs.
Evaluation of framework
This study applied the framework for developing WM strategies for HEIs presented in the PhD Mac¸in
et al. (TBD) to assess its effectiveness in this context. The eight-step approach was adapted to suit
the context and constraints within the case study, although not every step was followed exactly as
presented in the framework. This evaluation aims to provide insights into the framework’s applicability
and highlight potential improvements for future use of the framework in WM strategy development.
The initial step involved comprehending the WM structure and identifying waste sources. This un-
derstanding was crucial for informed decision-making later in the process.
The framework’s second
step states the importance of collecting data on waste measures, both qualitative and quantitative,
to gain insights into the unique HEI in focus. Primary data collected by a waste characterization is
optimal for obtaining data. However, it is also a demanding process. This justifies why many other
studies include only a single sampling event or disregard it altogether, only considering the broader as-
sumptions. Regular waste quantification helps monitor the progress of waste composition and potential
issues. Qualitative measures such as the identification of internal and external stakeholders are critical
for understanding the university’s structure. Engaging these stakeholders is beneficial for creating a
comprehensive WM strategy. Once stakeholder perspectives are established, frequent updates may not
be immediately necessary. Updating the stakeholders’ records through periodic reviews could suffice for
further monitoring.
Steps 3 to 7 involve using an MFA-LCA approach. These methods are invaluable for identifying and
justifying changes in WM practices. While they describe the broader scope of the WMS, they might
not need to be updated regularly. Focusing on specific areas like residual waste sorting and recyclable
treatment methods ensures robust decision-making with sustainability and environmental impacts in
focus.
The final step involves making decisions for a new WMS and continuous monitoring.
All
stakeholders should be included in this process; thus, it was not involved in this study.
Although the framework includes extensive methods to assess the WMS for HEIs, not all steps may
be required in each iteration. Some methods, such as the LCA, may not recommend changes in the
WMS but shed light on environmental impacts and uncertainties. It also encourages decision-makers to
prioritize WM alongside other environmental factors, such as circularity. While frameworks aim to make
studies comparable, each HEI’s unique context prevents the implementation of a non-specific WMS.
However, the framework is believed to be beneficial for evaluating single HEIs, as done in this case
study, and on a larger scale. DTU can use the framework to monitor its progress further towards the
targets set by its WM plan.

Overall Discussion and Recommendations for Future Work

RESULTS & DISCUSSION
Future work
This study has limitations that affect the use and comprehensiveness of the findings. However, these
limitations can be used to make recommendations for future work.
The study focused only on waste collected from one CP in a limited time frame, representing only a
small fraction of the total waste generated at DTU. Due to these factors, the representativeness of the
findings in this study does not provide a comprehensive overview of all the waste generated across the
university, both in terms of temporal and geographical representation. Despite this, the waste sampling
gave valuable insights on a more localized scale. The waste sampling should be repeated, including
waste from multiple academic departments and different types of campus facilities, such as canteens,
dormitories, laboratories, etc. Furthermore, temporal variations could be investigated by conducting the
samplings throughout the year.
For both the MFA and LCA studies, the setup of the WMS was constrained by the availability of specific
data. This has resulted in a simplification of the waste treatment processes, not capturing the complexity
of the actual conditions, resulting in reduced accuracy. Conducting MFAs and LCAs can be beneficial
tools for future decision-making regarding reprocessing treatments, especially if DTU has to choose
between two alternatives. Therefore, it is recommended that the interpretation is expanded, including
investigations of parameter sensitivity and potentially a break-even analysis if any final decisions are to
be made from the results.
The scenarios developed for this study rely on assumptions regarding the segregation potential. However,
this requires better sorting habits by the system’s users. It will be crucial to investigate ways to include
the system’s users more interactively. The stakeholder analysis showed weak links of communication,
and the waste sampling showed a high potential for improvement. This could be by creating a more
available informational pathway or platform for students and staff, preferably in a way that is impossible
for them to misunderstand or overlook.
As the study focuses solely on the environmental impacts of sustainability, it excludes the social and
financial aspects and effects of a WMS. This exclusion can limit the findings and recommendations for
an improved WMS and overlook other factors that could indirectly impact the sustainability of a WMS.
An example of a financial aspect could be the cost of labor and equipment for monitoring WMS in
future studies.
Improving the share of correctly sorted waste at DTU is necessary to achieve the goals set in the WM
plan, and using average data from waste facilitators might not be adequate. In the future, it will be
important that DTU has a certain criticism of average data from external stakeholders and requires a
more transparent standard for data exchange.

4
CONCLUSION
4
Conclusion
The waste samplings resulted in valuable insights regarding the composition of municipal solid waste
generated at DTU Sustain. Almost 40% of the Residual waste sampled is waste that could be recycled,
and laboratory waste was consistently found in all samplings of Residual waste. Other signs of patterns
were observed as well, such as nearly empty collection bags and full bags of food waste in the Residual
waste samplings. The variation in results between the two sampling periods was highest for Plastics,
Metals, and Glass. The samplings worked as an efficient tool to monitor the segregation efficiency and
user behavior. Future samplings could include a broader range of waste from various departments and
facilities to improve representativeness.
Stakeholders involved in the WMS at DTU, both internal and external, have been identified and selected
have been interviewed. A large potential for improvement is identified in the internal network, and CAS
Sustainability is identified as the most influential stakeholder in the stakeholder analysis, and the network
is highly centralized around CAS Sustainability. Unfortunately, users of the system, who have low interest
and influence, are not naturally engaged. The stakeholder network shows a lack of interaction with the
actual users, leading to poor dissemination of sorting guidelines. Enhancing the network by involving
more diverse stakeholders could improve the flow of information and practical insights.
In the MFA, EoW rates were calculated, showing that only 6% of the Plastics generated in the system
are recycled into secondary materials.
All other fractions had EoW rates of or above 70%.
Three
scenarios were used to explore the impact of enhanced sorting of recyclables, aiming to reduce the
amount of Residual waste. The scenarios show that, despite improvements of segregation efficiencies,
the largest flow of municipal solid waste still goes to incineration sorted as Residual waste. By achieving
the segregation efficiencies of the optimistic scenario, the share of Residual waste would decrease from
58% to 35% of the total waste amount in the system. Furthermore, the optimistic scenario aligns with
the WM target of having below 15% non-residual waste in Residual waste, set by CAS Sustainability.
However, as the name indicates, this scenario is optimistic, indicating that it will be difficult to achieve
the target. The LCA screening highlights the potential impacts of the diversion of recyclable materials,
as modeled in the MFA. The improvement in sorting leads to both an overall decrease of impacts, as well
as an increased efficiency at the WtE plant measured in terms of CO2-eq/kg incinerated. Furthermore,
the results show that changing the waste composition has a high influence on the impacts of the system.
This emphasizes the need for primary data when modeling the WMS.
The study’s representativeness is constrained, mainly because it investigates just a single CP during
sampling, and a substantial portion of the analysis relies on data derived from these samplings. The
MFA and LCA have high uncertainties related to parameter values, and the sensitivity of the models
should be further investigated.
The findings of this study suggest that DTU should monitor their WM by waste samplings, include users
in a more direct way, and continue to assess the environmental impacts of the WMS, while improving
data quality. Fractions with high potentials are Plastics and Food waste. Using the eight-step framework
proposed by the PhD has functioned well as a comprehensive analysis, but conducting each step might
be excessive for annual monitoring. Furthermore, even with a standardized and streamlined framework,
comparing HEIs and their WMSs might not be possible due to their unique waste characteristics. To
accurately monitor their WMS, it is essential for DTU to continue their work with the WM plan and
establish a robust database of primary data.
By relying on primary data, DTU can tailor its WM
solutions to the specific types of waste generated on campus, leading to more effective recycling and
reduction strategies.

C.1
Goal
Intended Application and Scenarios
The intended application of this study is to compare and evaluate the environmental impacts of different
waste composition scenarios for municipal solid waste management of waste sourced from DTU. It should
be considered a screening, more than an actual LCA, and it does not follow ISO guidelines strictly. The
results are intended to be used in a descriptive nature, highlighting what could be prioritized in the
future. The study will look into three scenarios; a baseline scenario representing the current system and
composition, and two scenarios where the composition is assumed to be different, reducing waste sent
to incineration and increasing amounts sent to recycling. The composition of the scenarios is based on
the MFA, as shown in Appendix B, Table 22. The scenarios are:
Baseline
Represents a waste composition and sorting efficiency that is like the one currently
observed.
Realistic scenario Represents a realistic scenario, where most of the recyclables are sorted properly,
reducing the amount of waste going to incineration.
Optimistic scenario Represents a theoretically optimal, and thus optimistic, scenario, where all
recyclables found in Residual waste are sorted properly.
Reasons for Carrying Out the LCA Study and Decision Context
The study is conducted in a collaboration between DTU Sustain and DTU CAS (Sustainability de-
partment), and is of a descriptive nature. It will be used to gain insights into the current WMS at
DTU Lyngby Campus, calculating compositional data based on data from both DTU as a whole and
DTU Sustain. The analysis is a part of a proposed framework for institutions, also involving a waste
characterization and an MFA. The LCA will not be used directly to make decisions, but rather function
as a supporting aspect in a meta-analysis. Interactions with other systems are included in the modeling,
and following the European Commission’s International Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) guideline, the
decision context will be Situation C1, which includes existing benefits outside of the system boundaries.
Method Assumptions and Impact Limitations
The study focuses on the environmental impacts and excludes all social and economic aspects. In the
main report, only impacts on Global warming will be discussed, as it is deemed sufficient considering the
uncertainties in the model. The LCIA results should not be regarded as absolute, but rather as potential
impacts, especially since the model is limited in terms of both temporal and spatial representation. The
impact assessment is made on a global basis, using generic characterization factors, but in reality, spatial
variation could be implemented in the processes. The spatial variation within the WMS at DTU is not
analyzed as well. A big limitation is the temporal representation, as the composition and generation of
waste at DTU is expected to differ drastically throughout the semesters, exam periods, and holidays.
Because the waste composition used does not take these factors into account, the results represent a
very time-specific state of the WMS.

Target Audience and Commissioners of the Study
The target audience of the study is DTU CAS personnel and the technical supervisors related to this

The results are intended to be used internally at DTU. Other potential audiences could be
other higher education institutions trying to monitor their WMS in a methodological way, however, it

well, as the future work at DTU potentially involves a technical audience. The commissioner of the

The study contains comparative analyses, but is not intended to be disclosed to the public directly. The
comparisons will focus on the differences in impacts as a result of different sorting potentials, and not
the exact values.

Deliverables
The LCA will be presented in a summarized version in the main report, focusing on key impacts, primarily

Functional Unit
The function to be fulfilled is to collect and treat one tonne of municipal solid waste generated at DTU
Sustain, Lyngby. More specifically, the functional unit (FU) is as follows:
Collection and treatment of 1 tonne of municipal solid waste from the Technical University of
Denmark, Lyngby using waste treatment technologies available in 2024
The reference flow is one tonne of municipal solid waste sourced from DTU Sustain (containing data
on composition), as this is what the model requires to compute the impacts.
LCI Modeling Framework and System Boundaries
Due to the decision context being a situation C1, the LCI modeling framework will follow an attributional
approach, as recommended by the ILCD guidelines. This means that average processes will be used for
background processes and system expansion in case of multi-functionality. Multi-functionality will occur
in the system as both incineration and recycling of materials perform secondary functions, substituting
primary resources. The analysis includes processes from collection to end-of-life. The system boundaries
are visualized in Figure 31, 32, and 33 on the following three pages. The system is divided into eight
sub-systems, each representing a container type, but modeled together to fulfill the FU.

Representativeness of LCI Data
The representativeness of the LCI data is limited due to the time-frame of this study considering the
complexity of the system, the scope, as well as the potential level of detail. The groundwork done in
this study can be further worked on, developing better and more robust data quality for each iteration.
Treatment processes used in the modeling are expected to be somewhat incomplete in regards to external
processes, and will be an important area to get more concise and up-to-date data on in the future.
Basis of the Impact Assessment
The impacts will be modeled at midpoint level with a hierarchist (H) subjective choice management.
This is deemed sufficient with regard to the goal of the study. The software EASETECH v3.6.0 will
be used for modeling, and ReCiPe 2016 (H) will be used as the impact assessment method during the
LCIA. ReCiPe was chosen, as it is a widely used method and therefore validated, especially in terms of
Global warming, being the impact category focused on in the main report. The modeling will include all
18 impact categories due to the nature of the study, being a screening. This means that the following
impact categories are included in the modeling:

Requirements for Comparative Studies and Needs for Critical Review
According to the ISO 14040/14044 standard, the three distinct scenarios must meet the same FU, follow
equivalent methodological approaches, and any variations between the systems must be documented
(Hauschild et al., 2018 ; ISO, 2006a ; ISO, 2006b). The results of this LCA are meant to be used at
DTU internally, and not meant to be published in a journal. This also means that no critical review is
required from an external party (Hauschild et al., 2018).

C.5
Data Quality Rating
The levels for the Data Quality Rating (DQR) is highly inspired by EcoInvents Pedigree Matrix. The
quality of the gathered data has been assessed by data quality indicators based on the levels seen below.
The rating system goes from 1-5, where 1 is best.
Reliability
1. Verified data based on measurements, or data directly from the commissioner
2. Verified data based on assumptions or non-verified data based on measurements
3. Data partly based on assumptions and qualified estimates
4. Qualified estimates for the specific case
5. Non-qualified estimates, or estimates for something different but similar
Completeness
1. Data representing trends for an adequate time period and an actual technology being used
2. Data represents technological scope, but time period may not be adequate
3. Technology is different from what is wanted, but covers longer periods of time
4. Technology is different from what is wanted, and the time period is not adequate
5. The representativeness of the data is unknown
Temporal Representativeness
1. Less than 5 years difference to the desired time period
2. Less than 10 years difference to the desired time period
3. Less than 15 years difference to the desired time period
4. Less than 20 years difference to the desired time period
5. Older than 20 years or unknown difference to the desired time period
Geographical Representativeness
1. DK data
2. Data from Scandinavian countries
3. European data from countries that resemble Denmark
4. European data from a country not similar to Denmark (includes RER processes)
5. Data from other countries, not representative for Denmark
Technological Representativeness
1. The data depicts the present technology
2. Data on materials and processes for a specific site (preferably in Denmark).
3. Data on materials and processes, but not necessarily the exact same technology as intended
4. Data on materials and processes, but different technology
5. No data available, only theoretical values or values for other technologies.

ASSUMPTIONS MADE DURING THE STUDY
D
Assumptions Made During the Study
D.1
General Assumptions
• It was assumed that every single container found during the mapping was supposed to be there,
and thus represent a collection point (CP).
• All combined containers for Ceramics/Heat-resistant glass were registered as being combined, but
for the estimation of volume they have been regarded as Ceramics containers.
• The containers for Plastics, marked either ”soft” or ”hard”, have been included as regular Plastics
containers in the estimation of volume.
• During the waste sampling, it was assumed that the food waste left in the packaging consisted
primarily of food according to weight and that the weight of the packaging was negligible compared
to the actual food waste.
• If packaging consisting of multiple materials was found during sampling, the item was sorted as
the main material should have been sorted. As an example, if a glass jar with a metal lid was
found, this was classified as glass.
• Fines were included if enough waste was left on the tarp after the weighing process, and likewise,
the fines were not included nor weighed if they were deemed to be negligible.
• All bags used for collecting waste were classified as collection bags, both smaller bags from
laboratories and larger bags from common areas. A large share of these was bought with the
specific intention of being used as collection bags.
• The treatment methods assigned to each fraction are based on common knowledge and basic rec-
ommendations and tendencies for the specific waste fraction (Christensen, 2010). The anaerobic
digestion is seen as a recycling process, even though it utilizes the energy content of the waste.
D.2
LCA and MFA Modeling Assumptions
• For both MFA and LCA modeling, it is assumed that the waste generation of 1 tonne of municipal
solid waste from DTU has the same distribution among container types, as seen in the annual
reporting made for 2022, used in the WM plan (Nielsen et al., 2024).
• Ceramics were assumed to account for 10% of the waste stream with the EAK code 17-01-06
seen in the annual data from 2022, as the fraction as a whole is combined with building materials
and sanitation waste.
• The composition of sub-fractions in each container type is assumed to follow the composition
seen for the waste sampled for DTU Sustain.
• All collection bags are assumed to be made from Low-Density Polyethylene (LDPE) in the modeling
in EASETECH.
• Glass and plastic containers used for chemicals that have been properly disposed of are modeled
as packaging glass or plastic.
• The technological efficiencies in the recycling processes and market responses are assumed to
follow the estimates made in EPA, 2020, Table 4-10.

LCA and MFA Modeling Assumptions
D
ASSUMPTIONS MADE DURING THE STUDY
• It is assumed that 10% of the glass bottles sent to Kroghs are able to be reused. The remaining
are sent to recycling into cullets.
• If it was not possible to classify a fraction in EASETECH, the best match of either ”Other
combustibles” or ”Other non-combustibles” was used as a proxy instead. If there was a ”Other”-
version of the specific material, that process was used instead, such as ”Other paper items”.
• The market response for paper and cardboard are expected to be 80% due to a downcycling of
the fiber quality over time, likewise the plastic has been assigned an 80% market response due to
chemical additives and other impurities in recycled plastics, thus creating a need for virgin materials
in the use of recycled plastics. For metals, glass, and ceramics, the market response is expected
to be 100%. This high demand for metals can be attributed to the scarcity of resources and the
market’s favorable valuation of recycled metals. For glass and ceramics, they both substitute raw
materials of a lower grade, and are assumed to be more than adequate for this function, ensuring
a 1:1 substitution.
• The energy processes used in the modeling are based on ecoinvent processes for Denmark and
Europe without Schwitzerland. It is assumed that all processes use the same heat and electricity
sources throughout all sub-systems, however, it is deemed highly unlikely.
• The carton recycling of FBCs is assumed to follow the same treatment as cardboard recycling.
• The total material in a system used to calculate the sorting efficiency is assumed to consist only of
recyclables in the designated container and residual waste, thus leaving out if a recyclable material
had been sorted inaccurately as another recyclable container type.
• All residues from reprocessing are assumed to be sent to incineration.
• Reject of recyclables in reprocessing treatment is not sent to recycling in the modeling even though
metals are expected to be recycled from glass sorting etc..
• No mechanical treatment is included at transfer stations, only sorting if anything. These excluded
processes could be size reduction or compaction to bales.
• Hazardous waste and waste for special treatment in general was modeled by proxies in EASETECH,
often assumed to be composed of the main material of the identified waste items. E.g. plastic
and rubber tubes were modeled as non-recyclable plastic.
• Materials used for production of containers are not considered in the assessments in this study,
as they are assumed to be allocated to such a large quantity of waste, that the materials and
impacts are negligible compared to the included processes.
• Transportation from recycling facilities to the actual user of the recycled materials is not in-
cluded, as it was deemed a too uncertain parameter, as well as lying right at the boundary of the
background system.
• EoW rates for Plastics are assumed only based on values for plastics only, disregarding FBCs as
the majority of the container type was seen as plastics.

ASSUMPTIONS MADE DURING THE STUDY
D.3
Scenario Assumptions
• A realistic sorting improvement of food waste in residual waste is assumed to 80%.
• A realistic sorting improvement of paper and cardboard in residual waste is assumed to 90%.
• A realistic sorting improvement of metals and ceramics in residual waste is assumed to 95%.
• A realistic sorting improvement of plastics in residual waste is assumed to 50%.
• For optimal sorting all of the food waste, paper, cardboard, plastics, and metals are 100% removed
from the residual waste
• Sub-fractions noted as recyclables or non combustibles are; special glass, Collection bags, EPS,
non-recyclable plastics, garden waste and other non-combustibles and are not removed from the
optimal sorting scenario.
• The waste diverted away from incineration and towards recycling treatments is assumed to be
of the same composition as the waste sampled for that given recyclable type. This means that
the impurity of the recyclable streams will stay the same throughout the scenarios, while the
composition of Residual waste will be altered.
