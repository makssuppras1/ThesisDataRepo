Abstract
Solar system is constantly changing, and it is important for us to understand how our
climate and weather changes in response to the solar activity during both long time
scales(e.g.
the 11-year solar cycle) and short time scales(e.g.
days to weeks during
Forbush Decreases(FDs)). Solar variability causes a corresponding modulation of the
incident amount of cosmic rays in Earth’s atmosphere. Previous work by [Veretenenko
and Pudovkin, 1997], [Svensmark and Friis-Christensen, 1997], [Pall´e Bag´o and Butler,
2000], [Svensmark et al., 2016], [Harrison and Ambaum, 2010], and other researchers have
discussed this cause-eﬀect relationship from an experimental and theoretical approach.
Since 1970s, global observation of the Earth’s system by satellites are oﬀering invaluable
source of information about cloud parameters.
In this study, we used the newly calibrated PATMOS-x(Pathﬁnder Atmospheres -
Extended) dataset during the period from 1978 to present. A method for capturing the
connection between cosmic rays and meteorological measurements has been conducted by
superposition analysis of FD events for time series(36 days) and the Monte Carlo bootstrap
test to evaluate signiﬁcance level of the integrated signal for 9 days after minimum in
FD. We have reviewed results, primarily about cloud emissivity(Achieved Signiﬁcance
Level(ASL >99%), surface brightness temperature(ASL >99%), and cloud fraction(ASL
>99%). Some of the results support the proposed relationship between solar activity
and temperature. This result indicates that the amount of incident cosmic rays decrease
due to FDs, global average temperature increases [Friis-Christensen and Lassen, 1991],
[Harrison and Ambaum, 2010]. In addition, PATMOS-x parameters of cloud probability,
cloud mask, and cloud fraction, which all means cloud coverage on the Earth shows
statistically signiﬁcant signals following FDs. In some previous research, IR-detected
cloud fraction from International Satellite Cloud Climate Project(ISCCP) and combined
liquid and ice cloud fraction, eﬀective emissivity from the Moderate Resolution Imaging
Spectroradiometer(MODIS) also show connection with FDs, see [Svensmark et al., 2009],
[Svensmark et al., 2016], [Marsh and Svensmark, 2000a], Todd and Kniveton [2004]. The
relationship between the observed changes in cloud amount and the resulting solar forcing
is discussed. On the other hand, “Cloud water content” from Special Sensor Microwave
/Imager(SSM/I), “Liquid water path”, and “Optical thickness” from MODIS also showed
3
==PAGE:5==
as signiﬁcant signals by FDs, see [Svensmark et al., 2009], [Svensmark et al., 2016], however
a similar parameter about “optical thickness” and “integrated total cloud water over
whole column g/m2” from PATMOS-x dataset does not have high signiﬁcant signals by
bootstrap test with ASL of 77.03 and 92.51% respectively.
Moreover, signiﬁcant results are reported for several new cloud parameters from the
PATMOS-x dataset(e.g. cloud type, brightness temperature, measurements by diﬀerent
wavelength 0.65, 0.86, 3.75, 11.0, and 12.0 µm and others). It is necessary still more
to learn about the relationship between cosmic rays, clouds, and atmosphere for further
understanding.

Chapter 1
General Introduction
1.1
Background
The weather and climate have a critical impact on human and other animal activity on
the Earth. However, still there are many uncertainties in accurately understanding and
predicting the climate system. There are variations of solar activity(e.g. 11-years in
solar cycle) that have been recognised as a factor aﬀecting global meteorological systems
over long timescales. In 1934, Brooks mentioned that solar and thunderstorms activity
have a potential correlation [Brooks [1934]]. After that, Edward Ney further pointed
out the existence of potential eﬀects to atmospheric ionisation at the troposphere and
stratosphere caused by the modulation of cosmic rays by the varying solar cycle [Ney
[1959]]. Furthermore, Dickinson also suggested physical connections between solar activity
and atmospheric processes in terms of energy variations and ionisation, but also, noted
arguments for and against the connection. Regarding the estimated amount of energy vari-
ations which are strong enough to change the condition of the lower atmosphere, the energy
variation by solar activity is 10−4 times lower. In addition, response on the stratosphere and
mesosphere by solar activity was not yet detected [Dickinson [1975]]. Markson discussed
factors aﬀecting meteorology from various aspects, which extra-terrestrial: interplanetary
magnetic ﬁeld, lunar modulation, atmospheric electricity, the circulation, precipitation
mechanisms, and thunderstorms [Markson [1971]]. Many discussions were generated by
this topic, but, for several years, the question of how solar activity could inﬂuence climate
was overlooked until the lack of available atmospheric data and a complicated system with
many diﬀerent processes renewed interest in the subject.

1.3.1
Connection between solar activity and cosmic rays
Since these questions relating to connection between solar activity, cosmic rays and
meteorology have long been postulated, many investigations have sought to tie and
integrate previous ﬁndings.
The number of sunspots - which are a solar parameter
corresponding to 11-years and Northern Hemisphere temperature anomalies from 1861 to
1989 or the mean of the amount of sea ice from 1740 to 1970 showed similar variations in
long-term changes [Friis-Christensen and Lassen [1991]]. On the other hand, the idea of
observing weather and climate on the Earth from space by satellite was being developed as
early as 1946. After observation technology development and applications on the ground
and from space had been accelerated from the early 1980s onwards, a considerable volume
of data in relation to clouds, aerosols, and other fundamental meteorological parameters
have become available for the research and study. These created and intensiﬁed a new era
for the investigation of these suggested questions. The detrended mean variation of top-
of-the-atmosphere out-going longwave radiation(OLR) and surface air temperature(SAT)
showed a positive correlation on global area. On the other hand, detrended global mean
OLR and total cloud cover correlated negatively in mid-latitudes(22.5 degree S and 22.5
degree N), but not in the Tropics[Raval et al., 1994], [Kyle et al., 1995].
Findings of correlations between long-term Galactic Cosmic Rays(GCRs) ﬂux from
ground-based neutron monitors and global monthly cloud coverage over oceans during 1980

1.3.3
Long timescale
When considering variation in the amount of GCRs arriving in the atmosphere of the Earth
for a long timescale, changing cut-oﬀrigidity by geomagnetic ﬁeld, which limits the energy
level of cosmic rays can reach in the atmosphere also needs to be taken into account. The
geomagnetic ﬁeld is simulated and observed to understand the entire structure. Regarding
results of the estimated model, theoretically cut-oﬀrigidity is bigger near the geomagnetic
equator(above 15 GeV) and smaller at higher latitudes(1.0 GeV around geomagnetic poles
region). However, we should consider that there is a weak geomagnetic ﬁeld region called
South Atlantic Anomaly(SAA) caused by the non-concentricity of the Earth and magnetic
dipole. In addition, Geomagnetic ﬁeld has already changed polarity nine times already
during the last 3.6 × 106 year regarding paleomagnetic record [Dorman [2012], Herbst
et al. [2010], Frigo et al. [2013]]. 10Be and 14C are common radionuclides(trace elements)
produced in the Earth’s atmosphere by nuclear reactions of GCRs with atmospheric
nitrogen and oxygen. The geomagnetic ﬁeld and GCRs ﬂuxes can be estimated directly
from the production rates of these nuclides [Steinhilber et al. [2012]]. Radon also produces
a background radiation which aﬀects atmospheric ionisation at 2-3 km altitude over the
Earth’s surface. Distribution of radon on the Earth is not equal [Sloan and Wolfendale
[2008]]. Regional impact by solar cycle and cosmic rays has been pointed as one reason
which leads to the appearance of diﬀerent characteristics in distinct locations. In addition,
the periodic change of the amount of GCRs reaching the Earth is caused the galactic
spiral arms. In the galactic spiral arms, many stars exist and supernovae often take place,

1.3.4
Short timescale: Forbush Decrease
To date, the relationship between cosmic rays, clouds, aerosols, and other parameters
has been noted and discussed in relation to a long timescale, at least a few decades. A
long-term study is crucial to consider the climate scale system on the Earth. However,
diﬃculties arise in separating the eﬀects of GCRs from other factors which are potentially
able to change cloud parameters, for example, solar UV eﬀects, atmospheric circulation
changes and trends of natural climate variability.
These sometimes appear as noise
and conceal small signals by GCRs because these are also closely correlated [Todd and
Kniveton [2004], Voiculescu et al. [2006]]. Hence, there is a widely adopted approach
to distinguish these noises generated by long-time scale and signal: a sudden reduction
of GCRs coming into the Earth known as Forbush Decreases(FDs). FDs are a natural
temporal variation in cosmic radiation on a timescale of a few days with recovery of about
8 to 10 days [Forbush [1937]]. The cause of FDs is a coronal mass ejections(CMEs) from
sun, where plasma is ejected from the solar corona. When this plasma hits Earth, it can
screen GCRs. FDs’ eﬀect has been studied using diﬀerent materials and methodologies in
previous works. Svensmark et al. [2009] found a relevant connection in cloud parameters:
cloud water content from SSM/I, cloud cover from the Moderate Resolution Imaging
Spectroradiometer(MODIS) and ISCCP and the aerosol robotic network(AERONET) for
low clouds during 26 FDs events. Subsequently, a parameter about the liquid water cloud
fraction(LCF) was reanalysed. Laken et al. [2009] concluded that there was not enough
correlation with FDs events. Connections among the cloud index(CI), the cloud occurrence
frequency(Occ), the extinction data(Ext), and the Climax neutron monitor data(CNM)
are investigated over six FDs events. There are no detectable clear dependences for
links between GCRs and cloud on CI, Occ, and latitude(Ext) [Rohs et al. [2010]]. In
addition, global cloud cover average correlation(Pavg) does not show eﬀective evidence in
the independent analysis using selected FDs data based on the reduction in 10 GeV GCRs’s
intensity Calogovic et al. [2010]. In other instances, the diurnal temperature range(DTR),
which means a quantity aniticorrelated with cloudiness and indicates cloud cover is used
for the superposed epoch analysis on a set of FDs. The results show statistical signiﬁcance
for the connection, but only using high-amplitude FDs [Dragi´c et al. [2011]]. A further
relevant study by Svensmark et al. [2016] has indicated a statistically signiﬁcant association
between short-term reduction in galactic cosmic rays, which are noted as FDs events, and
physical and micro physical cloud parameters: cloud condensation nuclei changes from
AERONET, liquid water content from SSM/I, total high, and middle, IR-detected clouds

The sun

System, with the Earth and other planets orbiting around it. Therefore, it has a major
impact on the Earth’s climate and environment in terms of the evolution of all life, and
the solar daily cycle still inﬂuences our biological system.

2.1.3.1
Solar ﬂares and coronal mass ejections(CMEs)
Both solar ﬂares and CMEs are huge explosions on the sun caused when intense tangled
solar magnetic ﬁelds snap, release, and suddenly convert energy as heat and light. CMEs
event lifts a huge mass of solar plasma from the corona and creates a shockwave that
accelerates the solar wind particles(electrons, protons, and heavier particles) to extremely
high energies and speeds. The most noticeable diﬀerence between CMEs and solar ﬂares
is the spacial scale. Solar ﬂares are local events, while, CMEs have much larger eruptions
sometimes same size of the sun. These take from minutes to hours. CMEs often accompany
solar ﬂares. Solar cosmic rays are from rare CMEs that accelerate to energies of max
10 GeV for a short period of time. They can dump about 1,500 GW of electricity into
the Earths atmosphere where they can cause magnetic storms that disrupt the earth’s
ionosphere and long-range radio communication and aﬀect electrical grids. However, the
precise mechanisms between these are not yet fully understood.
2.2
Galactic cosmic rays(GCRs)
In 1912, an Austrian physicist, Victor Hess, succeeded in detecting changes in the amount
of radiations by GCRs using electroscopes placed in a balloon at an altitude of around
5,300 m. From this experiment, the previously prevailing theory that the radiation came
from the rocks of the Earth were overturned, and he conﬁrmed the radiation come from
above the atmosphere.
GCRs are high-energy particles that ﬂy around space at the speed of light from the
Galaxy. These pour into the Earth from all directions. These particles are produced and
accelerated by supernovae remnants. A supernova occurs when a larger star than our sun
ends its life with a huge explosion. In general, there are two types of process for supernovae.
First, an uncontrolled fusion of carbon and oxygen is caused in a carbon-oxygen white
dwarf when considerable mass piles up on the star when its core reaches a critical density
of 2.0 × 109g/cm3. Second, when a star’s lifetime is over, and there is insuﬃcient fuel to
release nuclear energy, the star will collapse if its iron core is massive or mainly it close to
the galactic plane and close to spiral arms. The shock from supernovae can very eﬃciently
accelerate particles.
GCRs are composed with 85% protons(nuclei of hydrogen atoms), 12% alpha parti-
cles(helium nuclei), with the remainder being electrons and the nuclei of heavier atoms
[Lal and Peters [1967]]. These cosmic rays typically have energies in the 100 MeV to
10 GeV range. In addition, neutrinos also originate from supernova remnants(SNRs)
or solar inner activity and gamma rays generated by collisions between the interstellar
mediums(ISM) or electromagnetic ﬁeld and high energy cosmic rays also arrive at Earth,
but these elementary particles do not have charge. Positrons, antiprotons, antiparticles, or

Forbush Decreases(FDs)
The FD is a notable sudden reduction of the amount of incident of GCRs to the Earth
lasting approximately one week. The FD event is correlated the occurrence of CMEs which
causes GCRs to be swept away from the Earth by the magnetic ﬁeld of the plasma solar
wind. This event was observed for the ﬁrst time in 1937 by the American physicist Scott

E. Forbush. He has noted a systematic decrease of cosmic ray ionisation at Cheltenham,
U.S.A., and Huancayo, Peru, simultaneous with a decrease of Earth’s horizontal magnetic
force(H.F.), during magnetic storms in April 1937. Later, V. F. Hess and A. Demmelmair
have conﬁrmed the existence and world-wide character of the cosmic ray decrease by

“Climate” is a word used for the average of daily weather over long time period(about

temperature, humidity, sunshine, wind velocity, phenomena such as fog, frost, and hail
storms, and other measures of the weather that occur over a long period in a particular

clouds tend to stay without reactions, and cloud process is not correspondingly active. On

layer. This absorbs solar ultraviolet radiation from the sun.

4.1
Measurement noise level among NOAA satellites
There is an assumption that the variance of any detrended parameters among diﬀerent
satellites supply the same size of instrumental noise and meteorological variation during
the period and other data from 1978 to present. This is a reasonable assumption, since
the same instrument(AVHRR) has been used on all NOAA satellites. The assumption is
tested below for two main reasons.
1. Composition of diﬀerent FDs events using measurements from NOAA satellites.
F(t), which is a globally averaged parameter for a time series needs to be combined
with F(t) from other satellites to oﬀset seasonal noises and other ﬂuctuations causing
systematical or occasional problems in the time series. NOAA 11,15,16, and 17 satellites
have been chosen for the noise level investigation because these satellites covers the period
of the ﬁve strongest FDs event occurring from Table 3.4 and are expected to use for
superposition. For example, NOAA 15 and 16 satellites had observed the Earth at same
time, and both can be used by superposing the two time series. Among satellite data with
non-overlapping time series, it is important to compare the noise level in the individual
satellites, and test if the noise levels are similar. If they are similar, then a single satellite
time series can be used as a the basis for the bootstrap test to extract random time series
in a Monte Carlo bootstrap procedure to estimate the signiﬁcance of the derived signals.
2. The basis data is set to oﬀer a bootstrap sample unit. Later, bootstrap test analysis
can be used to estimate signiﬁcance level from superposed measurements data from diﬀerent
NOAA satellites. This method uses random sampling and replaces elements from the
basis for the bootstrap test. Therefore, the dataset should be selected for reasonable and
considered ﬁtting noise levels with other NOAA satellites. The NOAA15 satellite has been
covering the period from October 26, 1998 to the present according to the PATMOS-s
data site. This is the longest operational period of the NOAA satellites and covered three
out of the ﬁve strongest FD events. Thus, the measurement NOAA-15 can be used as the

The surface temperature of the Earth is measured by satellite observation. The radiance

therefore each pixel has a 0.1 × 0.1 degree).

1. The number of each classiﬁcation of cloud is counted in the daily map ignoring
missing(NaN) values and stored data individually.
2. The count data is summed up in each latitude.
3. The summed count data at each latitude is weighted by latitudinal coeﬃcient.
4. The weighted count data are used to calculate total global average.

5.1
Comparison with previous works
From the PATMOS-x cloud dataset, the use of diﬀerent kinds of measurements allows a

5.1.1
Surface brightness and atmospheric temperature
Connections between the Earth’s temperature and solar activity have been suggested and
investigated [Shaviv, 2008]. On ground-based measurements, measuring variance of the
sun’s emission is diﬃcult because of the Earth’s atmospheric optical properties. Therefore,
without atmospheric eﬀect in space, instruments have started to measure the sun’s total
emission. It found the variance in synchrony with the solar cycle, with higher values
during active intervals and lowest during quiet intervals, as shown in Figure 5.1. However,
the maximum to minimum peak amplitude of these variations is only 1Wm−2(This is
equilibrium value), less than 0.1% of the mean value of the emission, too small to produce
even a 0.1K change in the Earth’s equivalent black body temperature Elsevier [2006].

In light of the results obtained, the following conclusions are oﬀered:
1) Cloud emissivity responded to a inverse signal to FDs with an ASL of more than
99%.
2) The surface brightness temperature derived from atmospheric brightness temperature
based on direct measurement from 3.75, 11, and 12 µm has a positive connection between
FDs with an ASL of more than 96%.
3) Cloud probability, cloud fraction, and cloud mask have been discussed as a cloud
coverage index. These parameters also show a connection between FDs, and especially
cloud probability and cloud fraction have an ASL of more than 99%. Regarding cloud
mask, the signiﬁcance level is high for “clear” pixel count, on the other hand “cloudy”
pixel count does not have as a high a pixel signal level as others.
4) While some of the analyzed parameters have been investigated with respect to FDs
before there are also some which are analyzed for the ﬁrst time, such as the cloud type,
brightness temperature, and measurements from diﬀerent wavelength 0.65, 0.86, 1.60, 3.75,
11.0, and 12.0 µm. For example, in terms of cloud classiﬁcations, these are worthy of
discussion for a more comprehensive understanding of each cloud’s behaviour in connection
to other meteorological factors. These further new results allows for an investigation of
connection between cosmic rays or solar activity.
Taken as a whole, these ﬁndings point to the important relationship between solar
activity and clouds on the Earth and support previous results conducted using diﬀerent
datasets from other instruments, see [Dragi´c et al., 2011], [Harrison and Ambaum, 2010],
[Svensmark and Friis-Christensen, 1997], Todd and Kniveton [2004], [Svensmark et al.,
2016], [Svensmark et al., 2016], [Marsh and Svensmark, 2000a], [Svensmark et al., 2009],
and others. The results of these connections, or interactions are required to ﬁt models and
evaluate physical quantity in the Earth’s system. An understanding of both solar activity
and Earth’s climate and weather mechanisms must be particularly important for future
weather and climate monitoring and predictions.

Project Description 
Overview 
     An intensity of cosmic rays reaching Earth is inversely correlated with the 11-year solar cycle of 
sunspot activity. In addition, Forbush Decrease(FDs) is a natural temporal variation in cosmic radiation at 
Earth specifically a decrease in the count rate on a timescale of a few days with recovery these for about 
8 to 10 days [Forbush, 1937] [2]. The cause of an FDs is a coronal mass ejection(CMEs) from the Sun, 
where plasma is ejected from the solar corona. If this plasma hits Earth it can screen the galactic cosmic 
rays, causing the observed reduction. This event was observed in 1937 by the American physicist Scott E. 
Forbush for the first time. He has noted a systematic decrease of cosmic ray ionization at Cheltenham, 
U.S.A., and Huancayo, Peru, simultaneous with a decrease of Earth's horizontal magnetic force (H.F.), 
during magnetic storms of April 1937. Later, V. F. Hess and A. Demmelmair have confirmed the 
existence and world-wide character of the cosmic ray decrease by observations on the Hafelekar near 
Innsbruck [VICTOR F. HESS, R. STEINMAURER & A. DEMMELMAIR 1938] [3]. The strongest FDs happened 
on 31st October in 2003 over 1987-2007 period [6]. 
     Galactic cosmic rays (GCRs) are known as a possible factor affecting cloud formation on Earth by 
ionizing the atmosphere [H. Svensmark and E. Friis-Christensen 1997] [4]. Correlations have been 
observed between Earth's cloud cover and the flux of GCRs entering the atmosphere of Earth. Indeed, 
cosmic rays have been shown form aerosols in the air (the aerosol Ångström exponent had a significant 
change during FDs.) [ J. Svensmark M. B. Enghoff  N. J. Shaviv  H. Svensmark] [5]. These aerosols will 
grow into cloud condensation nuclei(CCN) in the atmosphere and affect cloud formation indirectly. 
There are several mechanisms that cloud link GCR flux, clouds, and aerosols. These results have been 
confirmed by experimental work (ex. The CLOUD project and SKY project) [Hamish Gordon .et al] [7], [H. 
Svensmark, M.B. Enghoff] [8] and modelling of aerosols’ growths [J. Svensmark, N. J. Shaviv, M. B. 
Enghoff, H. Svensmark][9]. CLOUD has found that ions from galactic cosmic rays strongly enhance the 
production rate of pure biogenic particles – by a factor 10-100 compared with particles without ions. 
This suggests that cosmic rays may have played a more important role in aerosol and cloud formation in 
pre-industrial times compared to today’s polluted atmosphere. From SKY experiments, it has found that 
ion-induced condensation may be relevant under specific atmospheric conditions on.  
     Atmospheric aerosols’ effects on clouds and Earth’s climate are not still understood exactly due to an 
incomplete understanding of how aerosols form and grow as well as the complex relation between 
cloud condensation nuclei and cloud drops, which depends on e.g. mixing and relative humidity. A 
better understanding of clouds and aerosols and their integration with factors such as cosmic rays, 
temperatures, and solar activity will also mean an increased understanding of the Earth system and be 
helpful for human beings in managing this system. 
Scope of work 
     The goal of this project is to investigate effect of Forbush Decreases on clouds by using cloud data 
from AVHRR instruments. This data set goes back to 1979 and is still running. The data is reanalyzed 
using a new calibration method in the PATMOS-x project. In previous studies, effects of FDs were 
expected and investigated by using data from satellites and other observations of relevant parameters: 
(AERONET: Ångström exponent (cloud condensation nuclei changes), SSM/I: liquid water content, 
International Satellite Cloud Climate Project (ISCCP): total, high, and middle, IR-detected clouds over the 
oceans, Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS): cloud effective emissivity, cloud 
==PAGE:133==
2 
 
optical thickness, liquid water, cloud fraction, liquid water path, and liquid cloud effective radius) 
[Svensmark, J., M. B. Enghoff, N. Shaviv, and H. Svensmark 2016] [6]. These results showed an effect of 
Forbush decreases in all data sets (AERONET, ISCCP, MODIS, and SSM/I). In this project, another data set 
from AVHRR (and if it is possible GOES will also be considered later) is going to be used for an 
investigation of the effect of Forbush decreases by using the Monte Carlo bootstrap statistical approach 
previously used by Svensmark et al [6]. If this data set also has a significant result for FDs in the 
investigated parameters, it will increase the confidence in the previously observed effect of galactic 
cosmic rays on clouds and aerosols. Additionally, the expected effect can be investigated using an 
existing aerosol model [9]. 
Intended results 
     In this work, we are going to investigate and find one of results to promote understanding the 
relationship between solar activity and GCRs, and the cloud parameters recorded between 1979 and 
2018 by AVHRR instruments equipped on satellites.
