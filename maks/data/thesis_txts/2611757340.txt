Abstract
The targets of greenhouse gases emissions reduction in the Nordic countries have driven a
substantial increase in wind power generation. This increase in wind power is forcing the
reduction of the generation of conventional power plants, affecting the system’s operation
security. To face this security issue, power reserves design and coordination are under in-
vestigation, tanking into account the main characteristics of the Nordic Synchronous Area,
which include a generation mix with hydropower as the technology with the largest installed
capacity, and a high capacity of HVDC interconnections. In this line of investigation, and con-
sistent with the system’s characteristics, this master thesis investigates the system dynamics
linked to the security of operation of the Nordic Synchronous area. It also proposes a model
that represents the system’s dynamics and ultimately provides an estimation of the security
limits that can be applied together with reserve procurement costs for the optimal scheduling
of frequency reserves.

1.2
The Nordic System
Withing the European Area and the British Isles, 5 subsystems can be identiﬁed, divided
by synchronous areas. These systems, although interconnected, are not synchronized as
the interconnections between them are converter-based links (HVDC). These subsystems
correspond to areas of Continental Europe, the Nordic countries, the Baltics (the same as the
Russian synchronous area), Great Britain, Ireland and Northern Ireland.
In the Nordic area, Norway, Eastern Denmark1, Sweden and Finland form one synchronous
area. In this subsystem, hydro electric power plays a major role. The largest electric systems,
the Swedish and the Norwegian systems, include a large share of hydropower capacity, 39 and
88% percent, respectively, and it is directly reﬂected in the energy generation mix collected
from [1] and [4] and presented in ﬁgure 1.4b. Nuclear power also covers a signiﬁcant share of
the total electricity demand, although it is only located in Finland and Sweden.
The smallest electric system in the area, Denmark, and especially considering only the west-
ern grid of the country, has experienced a considerable development of wind power with an
electricity share of 46% in 2018. Although currently, wind penetration is most signiﬁcant in
the Danish system, the installed capacity in all four countries presents a clear and sustained
increasing trend.

1.3
Frequency Reserves
The penetration of converter-based technologies represents both an opportunity and a chal-
lenge for power systems, and concretely, for the Transmission System Operators (TSO). Evi-
dently, the decrease of the inertia of the system is not the only challenge of RES, which also
presents a high degree of variability. These two combined present a major challenge for the
4

==PAGE:15==
security and stability of the system due to the crucial principle of any power system, in which
the electricity generation and demand must be in balance at all times. The unbalance of these
two parties and lead either to the acceleration or deceleration of the synchronous generators
and all grid-connected electric motors, and that difference in rotating speed would be reﬂected
in the system’s frequency.
As the demand and certain energy sources are not completely predictable, support mecha-
nisms are in place to strike this balance at all times under the name of power reserves or
frequency services. These so-called reserves are power regulation services that are available
at all times that are required, that are sustained during a speciﬁc amount of time, and are com-
posed of different types attending essentially to the type (type of control, automatic, manual,
...) and the time range of deployment, The type and deﬁnition of the reserve are particular to
a power system. In the Nordic area, the following can be found [6]:
• Containment reserves: Including both Frequency Control Reserve for Normal Opera-
tion (FCR-N) and Frequency Control Reserve for disturbances (FCR-D). Both are char-
acterized by a speciﬁc power response to the frequency deviation and a time speciﬁca-
tion for deployment.
• Automatic and manual Frequency Restoration Reserves: Deployed in a larger time
range (300s - 15 min) and are intended to restore the frequency containment capac-
ity.
• Replacement Reserves: Deployed in an even larger time frame (45 min) and take over
the restoration reserves.
Figure 1.6: Types of reserves and deployment time-line.
In addition, the frequency containment reserves are complemented with Fast Frequency re-
serves, with a fast activation time between 0.7 and 1.3 seconds and with a predeﬁned amount,
activated when a frequency deviation threshold is met, but not dynamic towards the frequency
deviation ( as a difference from the containment reserves that are dynamic and proportional
to the frequency deviation).
There are also other frequency control schemes under investigation for emergency events,
such as the Emergency Power Control, which consist in the coordination as well of HVDC for
fast power supply.

1.4
Challenges and lines of research
Due to the reduction of inertia in a power system, the capability of the system to withstand
unbalances is reduced and the required times for frequency reserves become shorter. The
role of the reserves is to prevent the frequency of the system to reach extreme values, and
these might fail to keep the frequency within bound under extraordinary events. One of those
events that are covered by the operation plan is the trip of the largest unit of the system, the
1,450 MW Oskarshamn Nuclear power plant in Sweden. During periods of very low inertia
and after the trip of the largest power plant, the regular frequency containment reserve might
be estimated to fail to deploy the power balance fast enough to prevent an Instantaneous
Frequency Deviation (IFD) under 1 Hz, or what is the same, to keep the frequency above 49.0
Hz. In three occasions during the summer of 2018, the power unbalances of the potential trip
was reduced by reducing the load of the plant, with the associated re-dispatching costs [7].
This type of situation is one of the examples of the challenges that face power systems with
reduced inertia due to the phase-out of fossil fuel ﬁred power plants, dry years, or high pene-
tration of converter-based power generation or transmission.
The inertia and frequency reserves problem have been addressed from both dynamic and
economic points of view. The dynamic behavior of the system has been analyzed through
mathematical modeling in both frequency and time domains. Frequency domain studies apply
the Laplace transformation to the differential equations that characterize the dynamics of the
power system and allow to move between the two domains. However, the direct approach to
the dynamic analysis is not practical due to the resulting complexity of the systems conformed
by different components and yields models with high order dynamics difﬁcult to be interpreted.
For this reason, this approach is generally combined with an effort to simplify the model with
the minimum possible loss of accuracy. This combined approach allows studying the sys-
tem’s behavior in a wide range of frequencies and results especially suitable to assess control
schemes. Authors in [8], [9], [10], [11] address with this approach the proposition and evalu-
ation of control schemes facing particularities regarding the stability and security of systems
with low inertia. These authors employ simpliﬁed models that prime robustness and concern
the system’s general frequency dynamics, multimachine aggregation, and generator’s con-
trol response. Multimachine aggregation is limited to generators coupled to steam turbines
(deeply analyze in [12]) and components with very short time constant dynamics, such as
power converters, that are neglected.
The resulting simpliﬁed models yield estimated limits for the secure operation of power sys-
tems and these have been adapted as security constraints in economic optimization problems.
This application of operations research, through Mix Integer Programing, to cost optimization
of power systems complemented with dynamic constraints is developed in [13],[14],[15] and
[16]. In these, the dynamic response is linearized through piece-wise linear functions and
the operation of the system is limited to a bounded hyperspace built over the characteristic
parameters of the response and the components of the system.
Despite the relevance of hydropower in certain power systems, such as the Nordic system, it
was not modeled in the previously mentioned works. Hydropower present dynamics different
from power generation based on steam turbines and the aggregation of these two yields high-
order models difﬁcult to analyze. The hydropower dynamics were addressed individually in
[17], where it is proposed and evaluated a method to obtain an accurate simpliﬁed model of
the hydraulic turbine and penstock. This simpliﬁcation is further developed to a second-order
system in [18], where the multimachine stability is also addressed.
Directly applied to the Nordic Synchronous Area, the publications of the involved TSOs (En-

of a power system that greatly limit the possibility for real testing.
1.5
Objective and contribution
The objective of this master thesis is to develop a model for optimally selecting the frequency
services for a safe operation of a system representing the Nordic Synchronous Area. The
model consists of a Unit Commitment problem complemented with dynamic constraints ex-
tracted from a model including variable wind, hydro, thermal-steam turbine, and thermal-gas
turbine power generation, in addition to HVDC power exchange.
With the development of this model, this study proposes two main contributions. The ﬁrst one,
an academic contribution that proposes the addition of the hydropower dynamics to a multi-
technology and multi-machine stability. And the second, a contribution to the industry, with
the possibility for TSO to better understand the frequency response of power systems with a
high penetration of both hydropower and converter based technologies; and the possibility to
include the security constraints in cost-optimization MIP.

Chapter 2
Methodology
2.1
Overview
The methodology followed in this study to obtain the ﬁnal model for optimal frequency service
selection was divided into three phases:
1. Dynamic modeling: The ﬁrst phase covered the frequency and time domain analy-
sis and simpliﬁcation of a multi-machine and multi-technology power system with hy-
dro, thermal-steam turbine, thermal-gas turbine and converter-based power generation.
The accuracy of the simpliﬁcations at both component and system level was assessed
through qualitative and quantitative evaluation. From the ﬁnal dynamic model was ex-
tracted as a main output the combinations of the operation parameters, such as the
amount of reserve per technology and unit, the systems inertia, the time response of
the units, the load of the units, and the proportional control gain (droop), that ensure a
safe operation.
2. Linearization of safe operation limits: In this second phase the, ﬁnal output of the
ﬁrst one, the combination of parameters for a safe operation, was used to deﬁned a
hyperspace of safe operation points. This hyperspace is a function of the operation
parameters and linear bounds (hyperplanes) were deﬁned to establish linear limits for
the safe operation.
3. Extension of Unit Commitment problem with dynamic constraints: In the ﬁnal
phase, the linear limits were included as constraints in the Unit Commitment optimiza-

2.2
Dynamic modeling
The methodology to obtain the dynamic model addresses the system and the component
level. First, the system or component is characterized by differential equations or the individual
transfer functions that rule the dynamics. Once the component was the characterization, in
the case of differential equations, these were solved and its equivalent transfer function was
obtained by applying the Laplace transformation. The component transfer function was also
complemented with the corresponding control, saturation, and max rate limiters, continuous
and noncontinuous components that are not represented in the dynamics of hydro, steam,
or gas turbines. The equivalent transfer function was next simulated in the time domain with
Simulink, a procedure that was established as the reference behavior for comparison with the
simpliﬁed approximated models that were obtained thereafter.
The speciﬁc methodology of this section was structured in the same way for individual com-
ponents, the aggregation of components, and the complete system. In all cases, when the
equivalent transfer function was determined, a method for simpliﬁcation was applied, involving
the reduction of the transfer function to a continuous model of lower order. The resulting sim-
pliﬁcation was ﬁrst validated in a qualitative test observing the frequency response differences
between the detailed and the simpliﬁed model in their Bode Diagram and the dynamic re-
sponse to a reference perturbation. Finally, the overall model was subjected to a quantitative
characterization test.
The Bode diagram -part of the qualitative evaluation of the model approximations- appears
repeatedly in the Dynamic Modeling section (section 3) and represents the gain (called mag-
nitude in the diagram) and the switch of the phase of the output of the system when subjected
to a sinusoidal perturbation of a speciﬁc frequency. Although the perturbations that occur in
practical situations are not perfect sinusoidal perturbations, these can be decomposed into a
Fourier series of sinusoidal perturbations, when cut by a time window. With this approach,
the most relevant frequency region of the Bode diagram can be identiﬁed by obtaining the
frequencies of the sinusoidal components with the largest magnitude of the Fourier series of
a reference perturbation.
Finally, it is important to remark that the object of the dynamic study was a power system in-
cluding hydro power plants, steam turbine and gas turbine plants, and HVDC interconnections.
Additionally, the study is centered on the frequency response of the units providing frequency
reserves under the Dimensioning Event and after the frequency drops below 49.9 Hz, and
it investigates two different control schemes for the HVDC interconnections: a proportional
(droop) control to the frequency deviation and a ﬁxed ramp of power injection.

Discrete vs Continuous dynamic modeling
The dynamic model covering the hydro, steam turbine and gas turbine reserves was simpliﬁed
eliminating the noncontinuous character imposed by the saturation of the components (max
power, valve max opening) and the ramp limiters of the the servo actuators. Removing the
saturation limitation implies that the model would loss accuracy for power output larger than 1
p.u. at the units.
In addition, the maximum open rate of the servo, estimated generally as ± 0.1 p.u. also
represents a deviation of the power output from a fully proportional value to the frequency
deviation. Nevertheless, the power associated with the reserves was set as 20% of the rated
power of the units. With this assumption, the p.u. value of the rate limitation was corrected
to ±0.1/0.2 = 0.5, consequently a less restrictive value. The magnitude of this restriction can
be compared to the maximum rate of change of frequency, which, as an example, presents
a value of 0.36 p.u. for a power system of 100 GWs and power imbalance of 1,450 MW:
d∆f/dt = Pi/2H. In this case, it can be noticed that the maximum change of rate of the
power output associated with the reserve is greater than the rate of change of the proportional
response to the frequency deviation associated with the reserve. For this reason, the effect
of the max rate limitation is considered bounded and only signiﬁcant for cases with very low
inertia and large shares of reserves provided by each unit.

Chapter 5
Extended Unit Commitment Problem
In this chapter, the standard unit commitment problem was extended with the dynamic con-
straints. The dynamic constraints are included into the problem by including the bounds of
the variables α0, α1, α2 and M, and limiting the space of the possible combinations of these
variables by applying hyper-planes obtained in the previous chapter.
5.1
Reference Unit Commitment problem
The implementation of the unit commitment problem was based on the problem proposed
in [15].
This problem minimizes the total system’s cost and ﬁrst includes generation and
power balance and transmission constraints. Covering generation limits, upward and down-
ward ramps, start-up and shut-down times and costs, operation costs, wind power and curtail,
and load shedding. Furthermore, this reference also extends the problem with dynamic con-
straints, limiting the RoCoF, the frequency Nadir and the steady-state frequency deviation
for a system with frequency reserves provided by steam turbine power plants and HVDC in-
terconnections. It also models the reserve procurement with a series of post-contingency
constraints. Amongst these, bilinear constraints are necessary to model the response of the
steam turbine generators and the HVDC interconnections, and were approximated by the big
M and McCormick’s envelope methods to obtain a ﬁnal Mix Integer Linear Problem.
As part of the formulation required to implement the optimization model, the constraints as-
sociated with the power transmission, and the the dynamic constraints are further detailed in
this chapter.
In addition, a set of constraints for scheduling 600 MW of FCR-N reserves was also added.

5.5
Solution method
The resulting Extended Unit Commitment problem includes both linear and binary variables
and was solved by the application of Mix Integer Linear Programming (MILP), more specif-
ically, by the application of the branch and bound method.
This method is based on the
simplex algorithm, which is a method for pure Linear Programming (LP). However, the branch
and bound method, applies successively the simplex algorithm to reduced, or relaxed, LP
versions of the MILP. The ﬁrst solution of the ﬁrst relaxed problem would generate a series of
branches with subsets of feasible solutions. The relaxed solutions involve noninteger values
for the variables that are integers/binaries in the original MILP and are divided between those
that are greater than the most proximate integer solution and those that are lower than the
most proximate solution, for example, between >1 and > 1. Then, with this strategy, the al-
gorithm explores all feasible branches and establishes bounds for the MILP solution with the
values of the relaxed solutions. Finally, when the upper and lower bounds of a branch are the
same, the optimal solution of the MILP is found. The ramiﬁcation of branches grows with the
number of integer variables and as this process requires time and computational resources,
it constitutes a major limitation for achieving a higher degree of detail on aspects that require
integer variables to be modelled.
Other complementary strategies can be applied to the branch and bound method, driving the
algorithm to focus on speciﬁc characteristics of the problem and reducing the time of solution.
The different focuses can improve the performance of the algorithm on aspects like ﬁnding
feasible solutions, or narrowing the gap between bounds. Although the the different focuses
of the algorithm were applied to improve the solution times, a precise tuning of the algorithm
was not investigated.

5.6
Model limitations
5.6.1
Prices and costs
Prices in other Synchronous Areas and in the NSA
The power generation mix of the model was designed to emulate the generation mix of the
Nordic System and produce an approximate electricity price for the different bidding zones.
However, no corrections were executed over the model to emulate more precisely the real
prices, and the deviation of the price could lead to odd power imports and exports due to the
the model incorporating real prices for the neighbouring areas.
Reserves Prices
The reserve prices are modeled as a fraction of the operation and maintenance costs of the
equivalent reserve MW. Although this approach is useful for the total cost minimization from
the system’s perspective, it does not take into account the producers’ optimal who would adjust
bids, and hence reserve prices, to the maximum proﬁt according to electricity prices and cost
of opportunity.
Start up and shut down costs
Although the SU and SD costs have been applied for all thermal power plants, these costs
were set equal to zero for hydropower plants. This is reﬂected in the arbitrary SU and SD of
the hydro units in the results.
No difference in cost between units of the same technology
The same marginal cost was set for all units of the same technology (Coal, peat, oil, gas,
biomass, hydro, and nuclear).
5.6.2
FCR-D and FCR-N reserves distribution
Although modeled, the constraint limiting the maximum amount of reserves per bidding zone
was not implemented. These constraints involving the sum of the reserves per zone increase
signiﬁcantly the solution time, and the limitation was assumed to generate a larger number of
scenarios for comparison.
5.6.3
HVDC reserve limits
The only limitation for HVDC supplementary reserves imposed in the model was the maxi-
mum of 10% of the nominal capacity of the interconnection, but no limit associated with the
distribution per bidding zone or per country was applied.
5.6.4
Unilateral reserve support
The reserve support is only considered provided by the other synchronous areas to the Nordic
Synchronous Area. Neither is considered the frequency dynamics of the other neighbouring
systems.
5.6.5
Steady State Frequency Deviation and amount of reserves
The SSFD was calculated with the bilinear constraint proposed in [15].
The constraint is
approximated through the piecewise McCormick envelope, and its accuracy depends on the
Optimal scheduling of multiple frequency services in the Nordic system
43
==PAGE:54==
number of segments and the price deﬁnition of the range of the possible droops. In the model,
it is noticed that the SSFD is constrained to 49.59 Hz, instead of 49.5 Hz. The cause of this
limitation was identiﬁed as the error of the McCormick envelope divided into 20 segments.
The improvement of this issue was addressed through two different approaches. The ﬁrst
approach involved increasing the number of segments, and the second involved reducing the
droop’s minimum possible value. Neither of them was successfully applied as both of them
increase largely the solution time. In the ﬁrst case, the time increase was due to the addition
of new binary variables to the MIP program. In the second, the inspection of the new problem
suggested that a large number of scenarios where the system does not satisfy the SSFD
constraint was added, the solution time is increased by the optimization algorithm (Branch
and bound complemented with pre-resolution and cutting planes) solving a large number of
infeasible scenarios. Thus, it is concluded that to solve this imprecision, two possible solutions
could be investigated: increasing the envelope segments and the computational resources or
increasing the range of the possible droop values and investigating an algorithm that has a
stronger focus on identifying feasible solutions.
5.6.6
Unit operation
Unit Saturation
The model takes into account the maximum power of the units and interconnections when dis-
patching the active power and the reserves. If any component exceeds this limit, the scenario
is then unfeasible. This approach leaves out the possibility of the component to saturate, and
with it, all associated scenarios that in practice would be feasible. This limitation becomes
more relevant with larger gains of the proportional controls, or shorter droop values. This can
be specially noticed if EPC control with large proportional gains, but short EPC capacity [25].
These two combined provokes that the frequency deviation and the large control gain lead
to a large amount of reserve, that surpasses the EPC capacity. As the reserve procurement
cannot saturate in the model, the solution would be discarded as an infeasible one.
Another relevant aspect regarding unit saturation is the amount of reserve deployment during
the frequency Nadir. As the saturation of the unit is estimated considering the SSFD, the unit
might not saturate at the steady state, but exceed its maximum capacity during the maximum
frequency deviation, due to the control’s direct proportionality. Hence, the model assumes a
transitory excess of the maximum load of the components. It is important to remark, that the
dynamic model accounts for this effect and it was considered in the error range of the dynamic
constraints approximation.
Operation costs independent on unit load
Although the efﬁciency and operation cost of the units differs from partial load to full load, a
ﬁxed operation cost was considered.

Chapter 7
Discussion
Power system modeling, as today, still presents major limitations. These are complex systems
that require a great degree of detail to achieve a precise representation of the actual systems.
Although accurate models have been developed for both dynamic and economic represen-
tation of power systems, it is of key importance to bear in mind that these models apply a
narrowed scope and hold signiﬁcant limitations. And that is no different when considering the
model developed in this study.
The limitations of the the proposed model are discussed throughout the different sections
of this text. The discussion covered signiﬁcant limitations associated with electro-dynamics,
particular behaviour of control strategies, turbo machines, hydrodynamics, range of operation
of at the component and system level, and economic representation.
Nevertheless, also in this case, the scope of the model has been narrowed to improve its
accuracy while maintaining an equivalent operation of the system under study. In addition,
the accuracy of the developed model was also compared to the performance of the detailed
model of the system, also providing a likely bound for the error. An analysis that provides very
relevant information about the performance of the model, and that is not commonly found in
the related research.
Finally, taking into account the limitation of the model that was developed, it is considered the
the goal of developing a model for optimally selecting the frequency services for a safe opera-
tion of a system representing the Nordic Synchronous Area has been successfully achieved.
And with this achievement, this investigation contributes to a more detailed representation
of the power system dynamics and the possibility to include the dynamic limitations in the
optimization of the cost of the system.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The large hydropower installed capacity in the Nordic System provides a cost-effective solu-
tion for the required frequency reserves. However, the particular dynamics of this technology
makes this solution not robust enough in situations of low inertia. These situations will immi-
nently occur more frequently due to the current plans for power converter based new capacity
and the decommissioning of nuclear power plants.
If hydropower units are to be a large share of the frequency reserves of the system during
periods of low inertia, it must be complemented with a fast response technology, either FFR,
HVDC, or gas turbine units. It could also remain with a reduced share of reserves when
complemented with the not as fast reserve deployment of steam turbine technologies, such
as biomass, with a role increasing in relevance due to renewable energy targets.
Any of the mentioned technologies that can provide fast response reserves allow the safe
operation of a system with reduced inertia, with an improvement of the maximum frequency
deviation and the reduction of the amount of the required reserves. However, the reduction in
reserves leads to a steady-state frequency deviation that is more proximate to the limit after
a large generation loss. In addition, the lower possible values of inertia produce a worsening
of the maximum rate of change of frequency. However, the negative effects over the dynamic
performance were found less signiﬁcant than the improvements, and these negative effects
could be bounded for a safe operation with a high degree of conﬁdence.
It is also identiﬁed a signiﬁcant contribution to inertia management, the participation of small
power units, including hydro, biomass, and gas turbine units, that can be online or ofﬂine in
shorter periods of time, and that can remain online with lower loads, still contributing to the
inertia of the system.
The cost difference in the total cost of operation of the system with reduced inertia is deter-
mined by the capability of avoiding wind curtailment and the operating costs associated with
FFR and gas turbines, the startup and shut down costs of this last mentioned technology are
also reﬂected in the cost variation.
In addition, regarding the power exchanges between neighbouring synchronous areas, it was
evident that the added HVDC interconnection capacity increased the limit of power import and
export. The increase of the capacity enhances the possibility for incorporating wind generation
without an equivalent reduction of synchronous generation, hence, avoiding wind curtailment
due to the dynamic constraints of a system with low inertia. However, the power exports
depend on the difference in prices between the synchronous areas, and this difference can
be signiﬁcantly affected by the decommissioning of the nuclear power. On this aspect it is
