Abstract
The main science goals of the ATHENA mission is to detect X-rays which are
emitted from gas in clusters and groups of galaxies, and by observing the extreme
energetic processes which occurs close to the event horizon of black holes. This is
achieved by focusing X-rays with low and high energies at small grazing angles,
using an optic module based on the new ESA-developed Silicon Pore Optic (SPO)
technology. ATHENA is expected to operate in the spatial range of 200 eV - 12
keV.
We will investigate the performance of the X-ray reﬂecting mirror coatings, its
short and long-term stability, thermal resistance, and the use of a chromium under-
layer to balance the stress on the materials considered for ATHENA.
Thin ﬁlms are characterized by utilizing techniques such as, X-ray reﬂectometry,
atomic force microscopy and stylus proﬁlometry to determine the coating perform-
ance, evolution and stability of the X-ray mirrors. These techniques are utilized
on single- and bi-layer thin ﬁlm coatings of iridium and chromium. The results
will be analyzed in order to evaluate the eﬀect of the coating’s properties on the
general performance of the telescope.

Preface
This master thesis project was prepared at the Technical University of Denmark’s
(DTU) National Space Institute. It is a co-authored paper, written by Nis Chris-
tian Gellert and Jakob Nygaard Korman. The project was initiated in January
2019, and due to multiple complications described in the thesis, the project was
completed in January 2020. We both a have bachelors degree in Earth and Space
Physics and Engineering fom DTU. The bachelors degree focuses on physics and
data analysis, from Earth and Space observation satellites.
We joined the research group working on the development of X-ray optics, since
our bachelor thesis in spring 2017. This provided a new set of skills, that developed
an interest in the science behind X-ray optics. We have subsequently researched
in this ﬁeld-of-study, which included a special-project and this master thesis.
The European Space Agency (ESA) has put the X-ray optics research group in
charge of developing the ATHENA missions optical module coating. This thesis
will contribute in the development of the ATHENA coating.
We assume that the reader has knowledge in X-ray optics, and understands the
basics of statistical data analysis.
We thank our supervisor Dr. Finn E. Christensen, for the opportunity to work
with him in this ﬁeld of study. We thank our co-supervisor Dr. Atefeh Jafari
for consistently supporting our work as a supervisor and as a friend. We would
like to thank Dr. Desiree D. M. Ferreira for giving us the opportunity for joining
the research group, and as students delegate projects to us, that contributes to
relevant work on a larger scale. We would like to thank Dr. Sonny Massahi, Peter
L. Henriksen and Sara Svendsen, for considering us as colleagues, and assisting
us in the work we did throughout the project. We thank Dr. Brian Shortt for
supportive feedback on our work.
All work conducted in this project has been equally divided between the authors,
and the written work has been agreed upon. Working together on this project,
has improved our cooperative skills, while still being able to perform individually
on a high level. We have experienced multiple complications which has challenged
our work ethic, but through good teamwork we prevailed. The project is meant
as a pre-study, that includes multiple samples and experiments, which should be
used as a guideline for future work.

Introduction
X-ray astronomy is the branch of astronomy, which studies the astronomical ob-
jects and phenomena that emit radiation at X-ray wavelengths. It a is an import
ﬁeld of study, since it will reveal the astrophysical phenomenons hidden in the
universe, e.g. black holes, neutron stars and colliding galaxies. This will answer
important questions about the origin and evolution of the Universe [35]. Earth’s
atmosphere absorbs X-rays, and therefore instruments must be taken to high alti-
tudes in order to detect them. With modern technology, detectors are placed onto
telescopes, which are put into orbit above Earth’s atmosphere, so they can observe
a large energy range for as long as the instrument is functional.
The European Space Agency (ESA) is constructing a large X-ray telescope, the Ad-
vanced Telescope for High ENergy Astrophysics (ATHENA), with planned launch
in early 2030’s.
The ATHENA mission will address how galaxies and galactic
clusters assemble into the large scale structures that we see today, and how black
holes grow and inﬂuence the Universe [17]. The ATHENA mission has strict and
improved requirements compared to current X-ray telescopes (Chandra & XMM-
Newton). Some of the requirements for the optics are stated in table 1.1.
Table 1.1: Requirements for the ATHENA optics [42].
Parameter
Requirement

The telescope must operate within 0.2 - 12 keV with a large collecting area and
high angular resolution. The focal length is 12 m, and the diameter of the optic
module is 3 meters. For this, ATHENA will utilize Silicon Pore Optics (SPO),
which is an innovative technology that uses a set of compact individual mirror
modules, that has an excellent eﬀective area-to-mass ratio, and can achieve high
angular resolution. There is yet to be found a favorite coating material combin-
ation for ATHENA which deﬁnes the spectral range, yet recent ﬁndings indicate
that Ir, Ir/B4C and Ir/SiC are possible candidates [42][53]. An angular resolution
of 5 arcsec is required, in order to observe and separate faint overlapping astro-
nomical sources.
While using the state-of-the-art SPO mirror technology with a desired coating com-
bination, the stress induced by the coating can deform the mirrors which would
aﬀect the angular resolution of the telescope. Furthermore, the ﬁlm stress eﬀects
the durability, particularly for cracking, deformation, and reﬂective performance
[34]. It is therefore necessary to characterize and understand the residual stresses
induced from the coating.
Iridium is a prime coating candidate for the ATHENA mission, due to its superior
X-ray reﬂectance performance, though it possesses a high stress-induced compress-
ive deformation. This project investigates a method to balance the stress on the
X-ray mirror coatings for the ATHENA mission.
There are multiple methods to balance the residual stress on thin ﬁlm coatings.
1
==PAGE:14==
One approach is to counterbalance the stress by applying an under-layer with
opposite stress-induced deformation [20]. In this project, the use of chromium to
balance the stress on iridium coatings will be investigated. Chromium possesses
a tensile stress deformation, and previous investigations have demonstrated that
an under-layer with appropriate Cr thickness will counterbalance the compressive
stress integrated in iridium ﬁlms [38].
Though this technique is limited, since
the ﬁlm has a columnar micro-structure, which results in an increased surface
roughness as the ﬁlm thickness increases [28]. Another known approach to reduce
the compressive stress in iridium is done by annealing [55]. Though, ESA will use
an annealing procedure to assemble the SPO mirrors. The annealing procedure
will fuse the SPO substrates together with strong covalent bonds, in order to bond
multiple SPO mirrors together, which could have an eﬀect on the coating stress
and structure.
1.1
The ATHENA Mission
The Advanced Telescope for High-ENergy Astrophysics (ATHENA) is an X-ray
telescope designed by the European Space Agency (ESA), to explore the Cosmic
Vision science; ’The Hot and Energetic Universe’. It will mainly address two key
astrophysical questions [18]:
• How does ordinary matter assemble into the large-scale structures we see
today?
• How do black holes grow and shape the Universe?
In order to answer these questions, it is necessary to detect X-rays which is emitted
from gas in clusters and groups of galaxies, and by observing the extreme energetic
processes which occur close to the event horizon of black holes. One must under-
stand ’the Hot and Energetic Universe’, in order to answer theses questions, and
to that requires space-based observations in the X-ray part of the electromagnetic
spectrum. This is possible by combining a large X-ray telescope with new and ad-
vanced scientiﬁc instruments. The mission for ATHENA is currently in its study
phase, where the design of the entire telescope, together with its costing is being
prepared. It is within this phase, this master thesis will provide its contribution.
ATHENA will be implementing a Wolter 1 telescope design, which is built around
mirror modules to reﬂect X-rays at small grazing angles. These mirror modules will
use Silicon Pore Optics (SPO), as they have the highest quality suited for X-ray
optics. A SPO consists of a silicon wafer which has a surface ﬁgure and roughness
ideally for such telescopes. The wafers are coated with a reﬂective material suited
for high energy photons, which has led to materials like iridium or gold, due to
their high-Z number.
SPO modules are constructed by two wafer stacks integrated together by annealing,
where the ﬁrst stack will approximate the paraboloid surface of the Wolter 1 design,
and the second stack will approximate the hyperboloid of the Wolter 1. The coating
is going to determine how eﬃcient the SPO’s are able to reﬂect incoming X-ray
photons, which is why the coating plays a big part of the ATHENA project. Figure
1.1 shows a complete SPO module, with the two wafer stacks integrated together.
The complete stacking and manufacturing of the mirror modules will be conducted
at cosine [2].
2
==PAGE:15==
Figure 1.1: A complete SPO module [18].
The coating for ATHENA has yet to be decided, but diﬀerent studies are being in-
vestigated to prove which X-ray mirror coating is the most eﬃcient. The coatings
for ATHENA have been under investigation for more than a decade, and as of now
the current baseline for the ATHENA mission consists of an iridium layer with a
low density material on top [51]. The low density material is still under investig-
ation, while suggestions like B4C and SiC has been mentioned in earlier studies
[53][42]. Previous work reports the instability of using the low density material
B4C, which appears during the annealing procedure of the stacking process [45].
These studies are important in order to understand the interaction between the
X-ray photons and the coating materials, which can lead to a successful coating
composition for the mission.
This master thesis will be studying the baseline coating of iridium and will more
speciﬁcally work with the stress level of the coating. Iridium is a material that is
known to induce a very high compressive stress after coating, which will degrade
the mirror quality. X-ray mirrors would optimally have a smooth surface, in order
to specular reﬂect as many X-ray photons as possible, and it is therefore inter-
esting to study the stress level and how to improve it. Previous research made
by Anders Jakobsen [19], discusses the ability to improve the high compressive
stress as a result from iridium, by adding a layer of chromium beneath. Anders’
research was a perfect guideline into how this project was formed, by reading and
understanding the eﬀect of adding chromium to the composition.
Figure 1.2 contains data from Anders’ work, displaying the eﬀect of adding a
chromium layer underneath a bi-layer consisting of iridium and boron carbide. As
expected with no chromium layer, the iridium will give rise to a high compressive
stress. Adding a chromium layer will counter the eﬀect from iridium, and the curve
will move towards a tensile stress with increasing chromium thickness. Although
the results from Anders’ work, show a signiﬁcant change in stress values, the results
is derived from a preliminary coating design and can therefore only be used as a
guideline to this project. As a result the thesis will be working with a thickness
range of 4 nm to 42 nm chromium.

1.2
Project Objectives
In this project, the purpose is to evaluate an optimum chromium thickness to bal-
ance the iridium coatings residual stress, while ensuring low ﬁlm roughness. The
robustness of the ATHENA coating to high temperature is demonstrated, regard-
ing what eﬀect an annealing procedure will have on the stress and durability of
the ﬁlm.
Silicon substrates have been coated with a Cr/Ir bi-layer, with a variety of chro-
mium thicknesses, at the Technical University of Denmark’s National Space In-
stitute. X-ray reﬂectometry measurements have been conducted at 8.047 keV &
1.487 keV, to validate the ﬁlms structure, surfaces and interfaces. Thin ﬁlm stress
measurements are done using a Dektak Stylus proﬁler, to validate the residual
stresses.
Selected samples have undergone thermal tests, where X-ray reﬂectometry meas-
urements are made before and after heating, to detect any deformation in the
coating. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy and atomic force microscopy measure-
ments have been conducted and used as supportive analysis.

2.1
Electromagnetic Radiation
Electromagnetic radiation are bundles of energy called photons, that propagates
through space with wave- and particle-like properties. Each bundle has the en-
ergy E = hf, where h is Plancks constant, which relates the energy carried by
a photon to its frequency f = c
λ. Electromagnetic waves consists of electric and
magnetic ﬁeld components that are perpendicular to each other and to the direc-
tion of propagation, traveling at the speed of light c. Electromagnetic waves are
categorized according to their wavelength λ, where the visible spectrum ranges
from ∼400-700 nm.
X-ray binaries, black holes, cataclysmic variables, active galactic nuclei and X-ray
background are luminous in the X- and gamma-ray regions < 10 nm. The electro-
magnetic radiation is produced by thermal, blackbody or synchrotron radiation.
Far from the source of an electromagnetic wave, we often treat the wave as a plane
wave. The electric and magnetic waves are described by equation 2.1 [16].

If the wave strikes a crystal lattice in the reﬂecting surface, it may diﬀract into
many speciﬁc directions, and peaks of scattered intensity can be observed. X-
rays have the possibility to produce a diﬀraction patterns, due to their small
wavelength, being in the same order of magnitude as spacings between atomic
planes in the crystal lattice. For crystalline materials, X-rays may be reﬂected from
lattice planes separated by the distance d between the atomic planes, as shown in
ﬁgure 2.2, where they are reﬂected at an angle equal to the angle of incidence. Two
or more waves may strengthen each other if the reﬂected waves are in phase, which
is called constructive interference [13]. Bragg’s equation, equation 2.5, describes
the condition for when constructive interference occurs.

2.2
Interface and Surface Roughness
For X-ray mirrors, it is rare to achieve a perfectly ﬂat or uniformly graded interface.
It is therefore expected, that the interface has a certain degree of randomness,
which is described as ’rough’. The roughness of the interfaces will have distinctive
features, when it comes to X-ray reﬂectivity. For a completely smooth surface
and/or interface, the incident photons, will follow the law of reﬂection, Snell’s law.
For a rough surface and/or interface, the incident photons will be scattered by
the interface, and cause for non-specular reﬂection, illustrated in ﬁgure 2.3. The
expression ’interface roughness’, describes the physical uneven interface, within
the boundary layers, which will continuously change in density. This means that
the electric density is under a continuously variation, along the thickness direction
of the sample.
Figure 2.3: Non-specular reﬂection [5].
The induced roughness in the thin ﬁlm mirrors, depend on various preparation vari-
ables. As mentioned, it is rare or maybe even impossible, to achieve a perfectly
ﬂat interface, which can be related to the method of deposition on the substrates.
The deposition on the substrates can have a large eﬀect on the induced roughness,
as a result from the coating. The induced roughness is dependent on the depos-
ition parameters, materials and the substrate. The materials growth deﬁnes if the
coating is amorphous, crystalline or polycrystalline. A high induced roughness,
will result in more absorbed and non-specular reﬂection, and will have a negative
eﬀect on the reﬂective surface.

Plasma cleaning is a process introduced in order to remove impurities, and con-
taminations from the sample surface. It utilizes energetic plasma created from
gaseous species. The two gaseous species applied for cleaning is oxygen and nitro-
gen. The plasma is created by high voltages, in order to ionise the low pressure
gas. Typically, the plasma cleaning function will cause the ionised gas to bom-
bard the samples, react with the molecular bonds of the resist on the surface, and
therefore clean the sample. The Plasma Asher that is available at DTU NanoLab,
have implemented a Faraday cage, illustrated in ﬁgure 3.7, in order to reduce the
ion damage to wafers, which could result in etching the samples. Therefore, the
chamber is ﬁlled with gas, and acts as an atmosphere, which only chemically reacts
to the samples, hence there should be no etching in the samples [11].
The use of oxygen as plasma is highly eﬀective and often the ﬁrst choice for plasma
treatments [11]. It will remove the organic contaminants, by a chemical reaction,
and the plasma will support the creation of hydroxylation on the surface.

The Icon-PT performs all major AFM imaging techniques. In order to image the
possible deformation on the sample surface, the ’PeakForce QNM in air’ mode was

lateral forces by intermittently contacting the sample. When the tip approaches

then starts to withdraw, and the force decreases to the point where the tip comes

each curve represents a pixel in the produced image.
All AFM measurements are conducted on coated witness samples. During meas-
urements, cantilevers pick up contaminants from the sample surface and accumu-
late, and due to the tip fragility, they can often break. Diﬀerent cantilevers were
therefore used during the project, and training in probe and cantilever change was
completed. For each measurement, the cantilever tip was focused, and the probe
calibrated. The laser and detector was aligned, where the horizontal and vertical
deﬂections was < 0.1 V. The Icon-PT includes a digital camera, so before each
scan it was ensured that no large particles were present in the area of interest. The
surface proﬁles were acquired from three locations of each sample with a scanning
area 1 µm × 1 µm. It is helpful to have a priori knowledge of the surface to-
pography before the measurement, so some of the numerous parameters can be
adjusted to get a desired resolution. When an image is complete, it was checked
that there were no irregular artifacts from e.g broken cantilever. The Power Spec-
tral Density (PSD) function was computed using the measured data, where the
root mean square (RMS) roughness was computed by integrating the area under
the PSD curve. If the produced surface proﬁle and PSD function is satisﬁable, the
image and data is saved. In order to get a high resolution for our samples, 512
scan lines was used, and the z-scan range was reduced to 2 µm. It was uncertain
how the AFM software, NanoScope, calculated the rms roughness, as it is possible

The XPS-ThermoScientiﬁc K-Alpha model is installed in the basement underneath

The system uses a monochromated, micro-

system is under vacuum < 5 · 10−9 mbar. A take-oﬀangle of 90° with a spot size
of 400 µm was used for all measurements. All measurements are conducted on
coated witness samples.
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) is a technique that measures the ele-
mental composition within a ﬁlm. XPS spectra is obtained by illuminating a ﬁlm
with a beam of X-rays, and then measure the number of electrons emitted from
the ﬁlm, and their kinetic energies. When a material is illuminated with X-rays,
the large photon energy can interact with an atomic electron, and as product a
photoelectron is emitted. The emitted photoelectron has a kinetic energy equal to
that of the energy of the incident X-ray minus the binding energy of the electron.
Each element in the material produces characteristic photoelectrons, where the
kinetic energy directly identiﬁes each element that exists in the material. Using
a tunable beam of argon ions, the top layer of the material may be etched oﬀ,
and subsequently the exposed surface may be analyzed [6], and thereby reveal the
elemental composition through out the ﬁlm, known as a depth proﬁle.

It can be concluded from the AFM and XRR measurements, that plasma cleaning
has a large-scale eﬀect on the sample surface, regardless of the investigated re-

The three models combined, suggests a thin top layer of chromium oxide with a

for further studies, in order to investigate this matter.

though the ﬁtted roughness parameters are not strongly convergent (not sensitive
to small changes). Later XPS results on single layer chromium samples in section
4.4, support this model. Note that the intermediate 1 layer has a large rough-
ness value, compared to the substrate roughness. It is untrustworthy to have a
roughness value larger than z/2. The 3-layer model has been tested with a ﬁxed
intermediate 1 roughness of 5 ˚A, and showed resemblance to the 1-layer model in
ﬁgure A.14. This proves the diﬃculty of ﬁtting a chromium single-layer.

Chapter Summary
The conclusion of this study is that the coating pressure does have an eﬀect on
the coating, and that chromium coated single-layer samples require a complex
model to ﬁt the reﬂectance data. The chromium ﬁlm thickness decreases with

the project, stress-proﬁle measurements should also have been conducted, to see
if pressure has an eﬀect on the thin ﬁlm coating structure.

With the results from section 4.5 and section 4.4, it was possible to coat witness

material to work with, regarding the ﬁtting for single-layers. However, previous

layer was based on the study from Anders C. Jakobsen [19].

it has on the stress level in a sample consisting of Cr/Ir/B4C. Going from a 4 nm

which could balance out the compressive stress gained from the Ir layer. With the
prior-knowledge gained from Anders’ study, this bi-layer study began with three
diﬀerent witness samples coated with; 40 ˚A, 100 ˚A and 140 ˚A chromium. These
three thicknesses, can give an indication of which area should be studied further,
in order to evaluate an optimal Cr thickness.

ponds to a compressive stress curvature, and a positive stress value corresponds
to a tensile stress curvature.
Using the ﬁtted thicknesses from section 4.6.3, the stress values for the correspond-
ing stress samples have been calculated. The calculated stress for all samples, are
shown in ﬁgure 4.46. It shows the coated chromium thickness as a function of

iridium thickness ∼150 ˚A. For a chromium thickness equal to 0 ˚A, are thus the
iridium single-layer samples. Each color represents the corresponding coating run,
where coating run 2 is a reproducibility study. The idea is to generate perfect
stress-compensation, by adapting the stress-induced deformation of each type of
material. Observing ﬁgure 4.46, the measured stress on the iridium single-layer

The reproducibility study, consisted of 4 new stress samples, coated with the same
coating script used for coating run 1. Figure 4.46 shows that the measured stress
for coating run 1 and coating run 2 are not identical, but they do show the same
trend, where the stress decreases with the chromium thickness. The calculated
standard deviation (STD) for all measurements where below 50 MPa, which is
expected since the two scans average stress had to be less than 100 MPa to be
accepted. Though, it was often necessary to measure some samples multiple times,
in order to get two measurements with an average stress < 100 MPa.
The variation in the measured stress can be due to a non-homogeneous coating,
and thereby induce a diﬀerent residual stress. It can also be due to the substrate
dimensions and the used engraved platform. A sample was measured multiple
times, by periodically lowering and raising the stylus, and replacing the sample in
the engraved platform, which produced errors of ±200 MPa. As described in sec-
tion 3.1.2, the standard substrates used for performing stress analysis are perfectly
round wafers who ﬁt in a three-point substrate suspension, which is the recommen-
ded method stated in the manual [44]. If the used thin ﬁlms have a curvature, the
stylus might put enough force on the surface to move the sample vertically and/or
horizontally. This would explain why the compressive single layer iridium samples,
where the most diﬃcult samples to get an average stress < 100 MPa. All samples
had their name-tag under the sample, written with a permanent marker. This may
induce an error, though some samples were not diﬃcult to get two measurements
with an average stress < 100 MPa.

Chapter Summary
Multiple Cr/Ir bi-layer and Ir single-layer samples have been coated in four coating
runs. The chromium thickness was varied, and the iridium thickness was ﬁxed at
150 ˚A. A coating run was initiated to investigate the quality of the coating repro-
ducibility. Two coatings runs were conducted with a week apart, with the same
coating parameters. It was expected that the samples had identical coating, but
reﬂectometry measurements showed a small deviation. XPS measurements was
conducted on two Cr/Ir bi-layers with 40/150 ˚A and 140/150 ˚A. It was observed
that a higher concentration of oxygen was present in the thin bi-layer. Iridium is
know for BIF, which occurs when a crack propagates along the grain boundaries
of the material, which can lead to oxidation. A total 18 witness samples have been
coated, where it was indicated that the thicker samples induced a smearing eﬀect
on the reﬂectance curves. It was possible to apply a simple IMD model on all
the bi-layer samples and get a good ﬁt. A trend showed that the chromium and
iridium roughness increased with the chromium thickness.
A stress sample was coated for each witness sample. The measured stress on the
iridium single layer samples, show that the stress in iridium coatings are compress-
ive, and a chromium under layer counterbalances the induced stress. The stress
samples indicate an error in the coating reproducibility.

4.7
Annealing Study
During the rocket takeoﬀphase, the launch and fairing separations will induce
vibrations and shocks, that can have an impact on the spacecraft’s payload. It
is important for the ATHENA mission, that the entire SPO modules mechan-
ical strength, is high enough to withstand these eﬀects. The development of the
ATHENA mission is currently in progress, and it is shown that by using an an-
nealing procedure, the bonds between the SPO stacks can strengthen [26]. The
heating process requires that the coated SPO stacks undergo a thermal heating
in air, up to 200 °C for 50 hours, plus 2 hours of preheating [26]. The 2 hours of
preheating is in order to stabilize the temperature. The purpose of this annealing
study is to verify if the annealing procedure will have any eﬀect on the ATHENA
missions coating.
Cosine Research B.V. has developed an annealing procedure under ESA contract
[22], which uses the vacuum oven model OV-11/12. The same oven [39] was used
for this project and all thermal tests were carried out according to the given anneal-
ing procedure. In order to validate the coatings performance after the annealing
procedure, stress measurements, X-ray reﬂectometry measurements at 8.047 keV
and 1.487 keV, XPS and AFM measurements, have been conducted before and
after heating on selected samples. The bi-layer samples selected for the annealing
procedure is: CrIr-6, 9, 17 and 18. The single-layer iridium Ir10, was included for
annealing. The corresponding stress samples were annealed together with their
respective sample. The samples were selected due to their good reﬂectance curves
and low stress. Note that sample CrIr17 was chosen in order to observe if anneal-
ing had an eﬀect on the smeared reﬂectance curve. The heating procedures for the
selected stress and witness samples are illustrated in table 4.23.

X-ray Reﬂectometry: 8.047 keV
In an attempt to verify the eﬀects from annealing, in addition to LEXR, the ﬁve se-
lected witness samples were measured at DTU NanoLab after heating. Figure 4.49
contains before and after heating X-ray reﬂectance measurements for the selected
samples. All samples were measured at DTU NanoLab, since the reﬂectometer
at DTU Space was nonfunctional, and a few samples showed a large variation
compared to the measurement before heating. This was curious, because measure-
ments for sample CrIr-6 and 9 showed little-to-none variation, while sample CrIr17,
CrIr18 and Ir10 had a change in their reﬂectance curve. This result contradicts
previous studies made by us [37], which had proven the stability of iridium, while
other studies of chromium have shown no eﬀect below 400°C of annealing [47]. If
these results are correct, the annealing procedure would have a large impact on
the current baseline coating. In order to verify if these results were correct, it was
decided to remeasure sample CrIr-17, 18 and Ir10. The new measurements are
denoted After Heating (AH) 2 & 3 in ﬁgure 4.49, where now no large variation
is observed for sample CrIr17 and Ir10, compared to before heating (BH). The
large variation observed could be due to a slit size issue with the XRD at DTU
NanoLab. A small slit size will increase the bandwidth-limitation on the samples
surface, which results in increased features on the XRR curve due to less detected
non-specular reﬂection, as described in section 2. Sample CrIr18 was measured
multiple times, in order to observe if the change in the reﬂectance curve was signi-
ﬁcant. It proved to be a signiﬁcant change in reﬂectivity for CrIr18, because after
conducting 3 measurements, a variation between before and after heating was still
observed. This observation indicates that the annealing procedure could have an
eﬀect on a Cr/Ir bi-layer with a 420 ˚A Cr layer.

X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) measurements have been conducted on
sample CrIr6 after heating, which is to be compared with the unheated sample
CrIr9 from section 4.6.2. Previous research clearly showed that the heating pro-
cedure had an eﬀect on an Ir/B4C bi-layer. It was indicated that the elemental
boron and elemental carbon had evaporated from the heated sample [37].
Sample CrIr6 and CrIr9 are not from the same coating run, but are coated with the
same script, and therefor are expected to have similar thin ﬁlm coating structure.
X-ray reﬂectometry measurements at both 1.487 keV and 8.047 keV on sample
CrIr6 and CrIr9, showed very similar model features, with a ±2 ˚A deviation in
ﬁtted thickness and roughness values. This suggests that sample CrIr6 and CrIr9
are relatively similar in coating, and can be compared.
High-resolution scans for sample CrIr6 are shown in ﬁgure G.5, at ﬁve diﬀerent
depths for iridium, chromium, silicon and oxygen. The measured peaks and trends
are similar to the measured unheated sample CrIr9 from section 4.6.2. Similar to
sample CrIr9, sample CrIr6 contained surface layers with a single large peak at
∼578 eV. This peak can either indicate the Ir4p1/2-peak located at 578 eV, or
multiple chromium oxide peaks which are located in the range 576-580 eV [40].
Though, this trend was not observed on the chromium single-layer samples depth
proﬁle from section 4.4, and therefor the Ir4p1/2-peak seems more likely. These
peaks are thus not included in the depth proﬁle for the chromium atomic concen-
tration. Tables 4.20, 4.11, 4.12 and 4.13, also contain the ﬁtted binding energies
of respectfully iridium, chromium, oxygen, and silicon, for sample CrIr6. Figure
4.50 shows the elemental concentration as a function of etch time for sample CrIr6
after heating. The etch time for sample CrIr9 was 10 seconds, and the etch time
for sample CrIr6 was 6 seconds. Therefor the resolution of the depth proﬁle is
better for sample CrIr6.

Long-Term Stability
Our previous research has proven the stability of Ir single-layers after an anneal-
ing procedure, which included 50 hours + 2 hours of pre-heating at 200 °C [37]
[45]. The reﬂectometry research was conducted in 2017 at DTU Space, and pro-
duced satisﬁed results, proving that the iridium coating is a robust material, as
it is not eﬀected by the annealing procedure. This proceeds to be very important
for the ATHENA telescope, as it is critical that the mirrors continue to be stable
for the duration of the mission. To further prove the stability of the Ir coating,
two samples from the research was remeasured, with the DTU NanoLab’s 8.047
keV reﬂectometer, again in January 2019 in order to observe if the coating could
change over time. Figure 4.57 contains measurements made in 2017 and in 2019,
for sample Si6518 and Si6523. The samples are single-layers with a 100 ˚A coating
of Ir, where sample Si6518 is a reference, and Si6523 is a sample which was meas-
ured before and after annealing.
Comparing the 2017 and 2019 reﬂectance curves, observed in ﬁgure 4.57, it is
noticed that there is not a large-scale variation from 0-2°. The frequency of the
kiessig fringes are similar, with a small change in amplitude. Though at higher
gracing angles, a variation is evident. For both the 2019 measurements, the data
has shifted where a smaller kiessig fringe is present at ∼2.3°. The reason for
this feature in unknown and not expected. It can be due to a long-term instabil-
ity, or the handling of the samples in between the projects, or the reﬂectometer
equipment. As mentioned, the samples were remeasured in the early stage of this
project. It would be optimal to remeasure the samples again, though due to time,
this was not possible. No conclusion can be made solely on the reﬂectance curve.
Figure 7.6 contains the reﬂectance data and their corresponding IMD ﬁt for each
sample, where table 4.32 shows the ﬁtted parameters. The model consisted of a
single-layer iridium on a silicon substrate with a ﬁxed roughness 3 ˚A. The model
provided a good ﬁt for all data up to 2°, however for the 2019 measured data,
the model could ﬁt the kiessig fringes present at ∼2.3°. Therefore all data was
ﬁtted to 2°. Observed in table 4.32, the iridium thickness seems to be stable on
a long-term scale. The ﬁtted roughness values increase from 2017-2019, though it
can not be concluded if this increase is due to a long-term eﬀect, measuring error
or handling of the samples.

Thesis Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate an optimum chromium thickness to bal-
ance the iridium coatings residual stress, and investigate the thermal resistance
for the ATHENA mission thin ﬁlm coating.
Silicon substrates have been coated with a Cr/Ir bi-layer, with a variety of chro-
mium thicknesses, at the Technical University of Denmark’s National Space In-
stitute. X-ray reﬂectometry measurements have been conducted at 8.047 keV &
1.487 keV, to validate the ﬁlms structure, surfaces and interfaces. Thin ﬁlm stress
measurements are done using a Dektak Stylus proﬁler, to validate the residual
stresses. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy and atomic force microscopy measure-
ments been conducted and used as supportive analysis.
It can be concluded from AFM and XRR measurements, that plasma cleaning has
a positive eﬀect on the silicon substrate surface. Multiple plasma cleaning recipes
have been tested, where the recipe consisting of 200 SCCM oxygen, 70 SCCM
nitrogen at 1000 Watts for 20 minutes, proved to be most eﬃcient. The AFM
and XRR results indicate a smooth substrate surface roughness of ∼3 ˚A, after
using the most optimal recipe. The NuSTAR cleaning process was compared to
the plasma cleaning procedure, and it can be concluded that the plasma cleaning
procedure is the optimal cleaning process for silicon substrates.
X-ray reﬂectometry results at 8.047 keV, indicate that the coating pressure has
an eﬀect on the coating, and that chromium coated single-layer samples require
a complex model to ﬁt the reﬂectance data. It can be concluded that a sputter
pressure of 3.0 mTorr induces the highest reﬂectance, and is the most optimal pres-
sure for coating chromium. It is shown that with increasing coating pressure, the
thickness will decrease, while the induced roughness indicates an increase. X-ray
photoelectron spectroscopy measurements conducted on a chromium single-layer,
concludes that chromium oxidises throughout the thin ﬁlm, which supports the
complex model.
From X-ray reﬂectometry measurements, it can be concluded
that the total induced chromium single-layer roughness increases with the thick-
ness. The stress measurements shows that chromium has a tensile curvature, and
a trend indicates that the tensile stress increases with the chromium thickness.
Multiple Cr/Ir bi-layer and Ir single-layer samples have been coated in four coating
runs. The chromium thickness was varied, and the iridium thickness was ﬁxed at
150 ˚A. It can be concluded that there is a variation in the quality of the bi-layer
coating reproducibility, which requires an in-depth investigation. XPS results in
the bi-layer, shows that presence of oxygen is primarily observed in the chromium
layer. It can be concluded that a higher atomic concentration of oxygen is observed
in a thinner chromium layer, and it’s important to have a small etch time in order
to obtain a high resolution XPS depth proﬁle. It is possible to ﬁt the 8.047 keV
reﬂectance data with a simple model, though some measured sample data showed
smeared reﬂectance features, which required an extra intermediate layer. A trend
concludes that the chromium roughness increases with the thickness. The iridium
roughness is stable at ∼5 ˚A, below a chromium thickness of 200 ˚A.
The stress analysis shows that an iridium coating induces a compressive stress
on the sample. It can be concluded that by adding a tensile chromium layer un-
der a compressive iridium layer, the induced deformation of each type of material
87
==PAGE:100==
will counter-balance each other. The amount of counter-balance deformation is de-
pendant on the chromium thickness. It is indicated that the residual stress reaches
a plateau close to 0 MPa, with a chromium thickness ∼140 ˚A, and slightly in-
creases at 420 ˚A. It is suggested that the optimum Cr thickness must be above
100 ˚A for good stress, and below 200 ˚A for low Ir roughness.
Five samples were selected in order to investigate what eﬀects the annealing pro-
cedure will have on the Cr/Ir bi-layer and Ir single-layer coating. A heating pro-
cedure at 200 °C was preformed at 50 and 2 hours + 2 hours of pre-heating.
Reﬂectometry at 1.487 keV and 8.047 keV, XPS, AFM and stress measurements
were conducted before and after heating. It can be concluded that the annealing
procedure has an eﬀect on the Cr/Ir bi- and Ir single-layer’s residual stress. The
measured stress after heating, indicated that the heating procedure had induced
a tensile eﬀect in the coating. It can not be concluded if the induced stress after
heating is dependent on the chromium bi-layer thickness, since all measured values
after heating have a relatively equal amount of tensile change. AFM measurements
showed that the annealing procedure has an eﬀect on the thickest coated bi-layer
sample, with a chromium thickness ∼420 ˚A, where the surface roughness increased
after heating. The reﬂectometry measurements showed no overall large-scale vari-
ation after heating, though the thickest coated bi-layer sample had a small increase
in roughness after heating. The XPS data indicated that the heating procedure at
200 °C for 50 hours + 2 hours pre-heating, does not eﬀect a Cr/Ir bi-layer coating
structure. Though is was observed that the surface atomic oxygen concentration
increased after heating.
This project can conclude that a chromium under-layer can improve the stress per-
formance for the ATHENA base-line coating. The thin ﬁlm stress and roughness
is dependant on the chromium thickness, and it is recommended to investigate a
chromium thickness range of 100 - 200 ˚A, in order to achieve a stress free coating
with low roughness. The annealing procedure has proved to have a supplementary
tensile eﬀect on the thin ﬁlm stress. This stress eﬀect does not seem to be de-
pendent on the chromium thickness. Reﬂectometry, AFM and XPS measurements
shows no large scale variation after annealing, which approves the use of chromium
as a stress-reliever for the iridium baseline coating. This study recommends the
use of chromium to counterbalance the induced compressive stress from iridium,
while taking into account the change in stress from annealing. It should be possible
to ﬁnd an optimal chromium thickness, which utilizes the annealing procedure in
order to achieve a stress free coating.

Further Studies
For further studies, the suggested chromium thickness range should be repeated
and narrowed using more samples. Note that the suggested range is for a 150
˚A thick iridium layer, and should be repeated for the iridium baseline thickness.
The optimal chromium thickness which utilizes the annealing procedure should be
investigated, in order to achieve a stress free coating after annealing. A short- and
long-term study must be conducted to evaluate the residual stress.
It is important to state, that the software IMD does not produce errors when
utilizing its ﬁtting tool. It is therefore diﬃcult to state if the sample coatings are
signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from each other, when applying the ﬁtting tool. The ﬁtting
tool applied in IMD should be investigated further, in order to fully understand
its function, and to achieve a statistically correct analysis. Another option is to
investigate other ﬁtting tools such as ’Global Fit’. As with the IMD software,
errors are a large part of measurements, and it is used to determine if a deviation
observed is signiﬁcant. For stress measurements it was diﬃcult to conclude what
caused the large variations observed. A further study should include a statistical
analysis of measuring stress, as the samples used in this project are not the stand-
ard substrates. The equipment is easily eﬀected by large scale vibrations, which
required for multiple measurements in order to get a ﬁnal result.
This project has shown that the coating is not homogeneous, and small deviations
appear in the sample coating. It should be studied, if this deviation should be
taken into account when coating, and a statistical measurement could verify if it is
signiﬁcant. A reproducibility study should be conducted on the coating chamber,
by coating a large number of samples with the same coating. This could be done
by putting the coating chamber into full capacity over time.
Transmission electron microscope (TEM) measurements should be conducted be-
fore and after heating, to get detailed images of the internal micro-structure. Scan-
ning electron microscope (SEM) measurements is another useful technique that can
analyse the surface composition. The electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS)
technique can be utilized, which will give structural and chemical information from
a much smaller volume of the sample, compared to XPS. Energy scans should be
conducted to investigate the eﬀect of a chromium under-layer and the annealing
procedure. The Ellipsometry technique could be applied in order to investigate
the in-situ oxidisation on the sample surface.
If the ATHENA mission decides to us a low-z material top layer, the entire study
has to be repeated for the tri-layer, in order to ﬁnd the optimum chromium thick-
ness which utilizes the annealing procedure, to achieve a stress free coating. Fur-
thermore, it must be investigated what eﬀect the annealing procedure will have
on the low-z material.

% Script function: 
% Converting NanoLab XRD RAS files, to IMD compatible TXT files.
% 1. Opens RAS files in specified folder.
% 2. Multiplies counts with attenuation divided by specified scale mode.
% 3. Finds mean of background in specified range.
% 4. Subtracts mean background from counts.
% 5. Normalizes data with max(counts). 
% 6. Saves normalized background subtracted data in specified folder.
 
% -- Specify your directory with RAS files --
dir_open= 'xxx';
% -- Specify your directory for saved TXT files -- 
dir_save= 'xxx';
% -- Be aware of scale mode in RAS files --
sm=xxx; % Scale mode
% -- Specify your background range --
BGrange=xxx; % Background range
 
 
cd(dir_open)      % Initial directory
D = dir;          % Initial directory
for k=3:length(D) % Loops over all files in folder
cd(dir_open)      % Sets directory in loop
D = dir;          % Sets directory in loop
fname=D(k).name;  % Filename 
FID = fopen(fname'); % Opens file
data = textscan(FID, '%f%f%f', 'CommentStyle', '*'); % Removes header
theta = data{1,1};                  % Grazing angle
counts = (data{1,2}.*data{1,3})/sm; % (counts * attenuation) / scale mode
background=mean(counts(length(counts)-BGrange:end)); % Calculate mean of background
counts=counts-background;           % Subtracting background from counts
counts=abs(counts./(max(counts)));  % Normalizing
theta=theta/2;                      % Normalizing 2theta 
cd(dir_save)                        % Changes directory to folder where saved 'txt' 
files is
A = [theta'; counts';  zeros(length(theta),1)']; % Save to new file 
fileID =  fopen( [fname(1:end-4) '.txt'],'w' );  % Save to new file 
fprintf(fileID,'%6.2f %12.10u %6.2f\n',A);       % Save to new file 
fclose(fileID);                                  % Save to new file 
end

E |
NuSTAR Cleaning Process of Glass
The glass arrives in the coating lab placed in cleaning baskets after the pre clean.
The cleaning basket is placed in 60 °C hot acidic soap water – 20 mL dish washing
soap to 20 L water in ultrasonic for 15 minutes.
The cleaning basket is rinsed with ultra clean DI water for 5 minutes.
Place the basket in acetone in ultrasonic for 15 minutes.
Move basket to ethanol and let it stay for 15 minutes.
