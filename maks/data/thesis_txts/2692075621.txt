Abstract
This Master Thesis project has dealt with several research objectives related to Acoutic Virtual Reality (AVR).
Three AVR experiments have been designed and administered to test participants. Two of the experiments
have investigated potential relations between visual stimuli and auditory perception through subjective ratings.
Speciﬁcally, the ﬁrst experiment sought to investigate whether a selection of common room acoustic absorbers
had an eﬀect on the perceived acoustic quality beyond that of their objective acoustic properties. The second
experiment sought to investigate whether visual brightness aﬀected the perception of auditory loudness and
reverberance. The third experiment sought to identify possible improvements to an AVR model developed by
Hermes Sampedro Llopis in his Master Thesis Acoustic Virtual Reality for Architectural Design from 2018. This
was done by tracking the source localization accuracy of the test participants, and the corresponding time spent,
on both Hermes’s original model and alternative versions designed in this project.
The experiments all used Hermes’ AVR model as the foundation. This included pre-calculating all the acoustic
simulations in the room acoustic software Odeon and exporting these via convolutions with Ambisonic B-format
impulse responses, that maintain spatial information. The ambisonic wave ﬁles were then processed in real time
in Pure Data before being presented to test participants via headphones. The visual side of the experiments
were designed and handled through Unity and the use of an Oculus-Rift Virtual Reality Head-mounted Display
(VR-HMD). Furthermore, the interactions between the visuals and the auditory stimuli were handled with C
scripts in Unity linked to Pure Data via a UDP (User Data Protocol).
It was concluded, that the visual appearance of typical room acoustic absorbers had no statistically signiﬁ-
cant eﬀect upon the perceived acoustic quality. Furthermore, it was concluded that the visual brightness had
no statistically signiﬁcant eﬀect on the perception of loudness nor reverberance. However, following a more
strategic and economic prioritisation of pre-computed impulse responses, a signiﬁcant improvement to the AVR
model was demonstrated, as both the sound source localization accuracy and the time spent was reduced.

Preface
This thesis documents the collaborative work done by the co-authors Oliver Birk Savery and Thomas Stub Nay-
lor on their ﬁnal Master Thesis project under the Acoustic Technology group at the Department of Electrical
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in the spring of 2021. The project and thesis equals
a total workload of 60 ECTS points equally divided between the two co-authors. It constitutes the ﬁnal work
needed for both authors to achieve the degree of Master of Science in Acoustical Engineering.
The project is heavily involved with Acoustic Virtual Reality (AVR) and seeks to add to this relatively new
and exciting ﬁeld and investigate potential applications hereof within the building design process. The project
has drawn on an AVR model developed by Hermes Sampedro Llopis during his own Master Thesis Acoustic
Virtual Reality for Architectural Design in 2018 [1]. Whereas tools such as auralization and visualisation have
long been used within the construction and building design industry, AVR modelling has just recently made
its entrance. The possibility to work with an emerging and developing technology in the ﬁeld of architectural
acoustics, a ﬁeld which both authors are passionate about, provided a big motivation for the creation and
execution of this project. Three independent AVR experiments have been carried out involving sixteen test
participants. Two of the experiments are concerned with uncovering hypothesised interdependencies between
certain visual stimuli and auditory perception. In short, one sought to investigate whether the visual design
of some common acoustic absorber treatments aﬀected the perceived acoustic quality, the other examined the
eﬀect of visual brightness on the perception of reverberance and loudness. Finally, the third and last experi-
ment sought to investigate possible improvements to the AVR model ﬁrst developed by Hermes Sampedro Llopis.
Broadly, this report can be divided into four main sections.
Initially, the Introduction section introduces
the project itself, describing the motivation behind the study, the conclusions drawn from a previous literature
study on the subject as well as the resulting hypotheses and research objectives. Secondly, the Theory section
outlines the relevant theoretical and mathematical principals governing diﬀerent elements of the project such
as Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) systems, general room acoustics, spatial hearing, ambisonics etc. Thirdly, an
in-depth description of the three experiments can be found in the Method section, which also includes a presen-
tation of the ﬁnal AVR models, the experimental procedure and the analytical tools applied for analysing the
results. Finally, the Results and Discussion section presents results of all the experiments, which are ultimately
discussed and concluded upon in relation to the initial hypotheses and research objectives.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In broad terms this master thesis seeks to investigate the interference between visual and auditory inputs when
assessing quality or characteristics of audio. Or more concisely, how diﬀerent visual stimuli inﬂuences the way
auditory stimuli are perceived - and vice versa. For this purpose an extensive amount of work has been put into
designing and creating an audio-visual experimental setup to be administered to multiple test subjects. The
experimental setup relies heavily on Virtual Reality (VR) and faithful spatial sound scene reproduction through
the use of ambisonics. The project was preceded by a three week special course devoted to a literature study
on the subject of virtual reality and its application in the ﬁeld of acoustics, as well a study of the connection
between audition and vision.
The scope of the project has ended up being threefold. Firstly, a wish to investigate a typical acoustic treat-
ment of a commonplace room, lead to the ﬁrst of three sub-elements of the project, where an already existing
untreated meeting room was chosen as the starting point. A VR experiment was designed to investigate the
impact of the visual appearance of common acoustic absorptive treatments upon the perceived acoustic quality.
The hope was to generate some useful hands-on guidelines for room acoustic engineers and consultants on how
these treatment types have diﬀerent subliminal eﬀects on human perception of sound apart from their objective
acoustic performance. This could potentially have a big practical application in the consulting industry.
Secondly, another VR experiment was designed with the intention of investigating whether the visual brightness
of a room has an eﬀect on the perceived loudness and reverberance. The scene of this experiment consisted of
an even simpler virtual room, where the grey shade of the walls and ceiling could be altered simultaneously.
The goal was to this way test if diﬀerent shades of grey from black to white could aﬀect the perceived acoustic
characteristics of the room.
Last but not least, a signiﬁcant part of the auditory stimuli processing in both of the abovementioned experi-
ments are handled by an acoustic virtual reality (AVR) model developed by Hermes Sampedro Llopis (2018) in
his Master Thesis project [1]. Although being an elegant, powerful and highly useful model, some minor issues
have been identiﬁed in the applied interpolation method between diﬀerent spatial impulse responses when using
the model in a free-movement scene. In certain cases this will lead to spatial misinformation, causing the sound
scene reproduction to be misleading. The third and ﬁnal sub-element of this thesis has therefore been to devise
and execute a ﬁnal VR experiment, to evaluate possible additions and improvements to Hermes’ AVR model.
1.1
Motivation
The multi-modal perception nature of humans, that is the combined impression produced by interdependant
senses (or modalities), is a well-documented phenomenon and easily observable in every day life. The more
of our senses which can be applied in unison, the more complete the perception of a given environment will
2
==PAGE:14==
be. It is therefore natural to assume that what we see will have an eﬀect on what we hear. Or at least how
we perceive what we hear. And vice versa. Multiple studies have already been done on speciﬁc cases of this
phenomenon, such as The eﬀect of concert hall color on preference and auditory perception, an article by Yuxiao
Chen and Densil Cabrera published in 2020 in the Applied Acoustics journal [2]. In this study it was found,
that color has little or no eﬀect on the perceived loudness and reverberance compared to directly changing
gain or reverberation time. However, the choice of color did aﬀect the visual and auditory preferences which
were shown to be positively correlated and mutually inﬂuential. It was possible to rank the colors in regard to
visual preference from high to low, and relate this ranking to the main colour and seat colour of 100 concert
halls and opera houses around the world. It appeared, that the number of halls which employ any of these
colours display a similar trend as the average visual preference found in the study. Finally, it is concluded that
a positive inﬂuence is found in both directions between auditory preference and visual preference, meaning that
preferable sound and preferable view compliment each other. This study also utilized VR for its experimental
setup. Another study draws a similar conclusion, namely that described in The Eﬀects of Visual Input on the
Evaluation of the Acoustics in an Opera House, an article by Jin Yong Jeon et al. published in 2005 by Forum
Acusticum [3]. Although again speciﬁc to opera theaters and employing a simpler audio-visual experimental
setup than VR, the general conclusion was, that a positive visual evaluation tends to enhance acoustic quality
judgments, and a negative visual evaluation tends to decrease the acoustic quality judgments. Although just
a few examples research in the ﬁeld does seem surprisingly scarce. VR oﬀers a cost and time eﬃcient way of
increasing the body of knowledge on the subject under controlled conditions.
Although the ﬁeld of virtual reality is by no means a new technology, having been developed upon since
the mid-to-late 20th century, it is within the last 10 to 15 years that it gained a broader more mainstream
attention. As with much other new emerging technology the virtual reality industry initially catered mainly for
industries such as medical, military training etc. and others where simulation training was beneﬁcial. However,
in the last decade or so the technology has spilled over into the gaming industry giving rise to a broader public
appeal with countless companies competing for superiority by developing the most realistically immersive expe-
rience. Lately, virtual reality has also found its way into the building and construction industry. The use of VR
provides architects, consultants and engineers an extremely useful tool for visually showcasing cases to clients
before construction or in the process of renovation, which goes beyond a simple 2D image rendering. Although
acoustic auralisations have been used extensively in the architectural and construction business, allowing one
to listen to a simulated acoustic sound scene, the visual aspect of commercially available VR systems are far
more developed than the acoustics.
All in all, the possibility to work with an emerging and trending technology within the ﬁeld of acoustics, con-
struction and building design while simultaneously producing valuable knowledge for the industry has provided
a high degree of motivation to pursue the subject of this master thesis project.

2.1.3
Sound Propagation
The importance of the above theory on LTI systems and the impulse response becomes apparent when consid-
ering that, in the ﬁeld of room acoustics, rooms may be considered LTI systems. With this knowledge in mind
the measurement of a room impulse response (RIR) becomes a very basic and straight forward yet extremely
powerful tool in respect to room acoustics, theoretically containing all the information about the acoustic prop-
erties of a room (for any speciﬁc source/receiver combination, that is).
Consider the scene illustrated below in Figure 2.8. The ﬁgure conceptually illustrates how a spherical wave
propagates away from a sound source on stage following a short sound impulse in order to reach the ears of an
audience member, the receiver.
7
==PAGE:19==
Figure 2.8: Direct and reﬂected sound paths from source to listener. From [5].
The part of the wave to ﬁrst reach the receiver is naturally the one to take the most direct path without
any detours, aptly named the direct sound. Following an initial time delay gap the direct sound is followed by
components of the sound wave which have been reﬂected one or more times by the room boundaries such as
walls, ceiling and ﬂoor, and really any other object present in the room. These components are labelled early
reﬂections. These early reﬂection components are, apart from arriving later than the direct sound, weaker in
respect to intensity, illustrated in Figure 2.9 [5].
Figure 2.9: Example of the generation of an impulse response in a room, illustrating both time delay and
decrease in intensity between the direct sound and early reﬂections. From [5].
There are multiple reasons for this. First of all, the intensity is reduced as the area of the spherical wave front
increases with time (known as spherical distance attenuation). Secondly, each time a sound wave is reﬂected oﬀ
a room boundary or object in the room a fraction of the energy is absorbed and dissipated into heat. Finally,
the intensity is also attenuated as the sound wave front moves through the air.
The reﬂection of the sound wave will continue until all the energy has been absorbed due to the above-mentioned
factors, eventually dying out. This decay is what is experienced as reverberation. With the right equipment
it is possible to capture the impulse response of a room. Apart from retrieving all important information of
acoustic properties of the room from the impulse response we should recall eq. 2.1 and 2.2. Applying these
equations with a known impulse response it is possible to determine the output of a system, in this case a room,
8
==PAGE:20==
following any given input signal. This relation is particularly useful in the creation of auralizations.
2.1.4
Auralizations
Auralization (sometimes spelled auralisation) is the audio counterpart to the visual visualisation. It is a process
which allows for modelling and simulation of the acoustic sound ﬁeld of a given space or room. Basically, it
makes it possible to experience how a given input signal would sound in a known system. Two main ingredients
are necessary for the creation of an auralization: a measured (or simulated) RIR and an anechoic audio ﬁle. As
touched upon, the auralization is created by convolving the RIR and the anechoic audio ﬁle. For auralizations
played back over headphones it often preferable to use a binaural room impulse response (BRIR) that takes into
account the head, upper-body and ear geometry of a person, resulting in a more spatially realistic reproduction.
2.1.5
Reverberance and Reverberation Time
The subjective room acoustic parameter reverberance is probably the best known and most easily relatable.
Reverberance, i.e. the persistence of a sound after the sound is produced, can have both negative and positive
eﬀects. A high level of reverberance will reduce the ability to understand speech, as syllables, words and sen-
tences are smeared and will be perceived as melting together. On the other hand, a high level of reverberance
can improve the experience of certain genres of music, adding fullness to the sound and blending sound from
diﬀerent instruments and voices. Conversely, for a low level of reverberance, these experiences will be ﬂipped [5].
The subjective experience of reverberance is linked to the objective room acoustic parameter reverberation
time or T. T is deﬁned as the time it takes for the sound level in a room to decrease by 60 dB after a continuous
sound source has been stopped. A graphic representation of this deﬁnition can be found in Figure 2.10.

2.1.6
Loudness
Just as the subjective measure of reverberance indicates, how a person perceives the reverberation of a given
sound scene, the measure of loudness describes how a person perceives the strength or sound intensity. Due to
the way the human hearing works, a certain change in sound intensity does not necessarily imply a proportionate
change in the perceived loudness. This is because both the frequency and the timbre of a sound inﬂuences the
human perception of loudness.
Based on experimentally determined data Harvey Fletcher and Wilden A. Munson produced the ﬁrst set of
equal-loudness contours, since known as Fletcher-Munson curves, in the 1930’s. These curves illustrate the
sound pressure level (SPL) required in order for a listener to perceive a constant loudness, when presented
with pure steady tones over the frequency spectrum. Since then the experiments have been reﬁned and the
equal-loudness curves adjusted several times and is today standardized in the ISO 226 Standard from 2003.
Figure 2.11 below shows the equal-loudness contours of today from ISO 226:

Figure 2.11: Equal-loudness contours of ISO 226. From [7]
As loudness varies from person to person, the contours in Figure 2.11 are averages based on tests of many
normal-hearing listeners. It can be seen, that each contour is associated with a phon value, a unit used to indi-
cate an individuals perception of loudness. Per deﬁnition 40 dB SPL equals 40 phon at 1000 Hz, meaning that
for a 1000 Hz tone SPL and phon values are equal when well above hearing threshold. What the contours in
Figure 2.11 show are, that in order to achieve the same perception of loudness for diﬀerent pure tone frequencies

audible hearing threshold and the threshold of pain, respectively.

2.1.7
Acoustic Absorbers
The reason for installing acoustic absorbers can vary from situation to situation and depends greatly on the
purpose of the room in question. Whatever the situation, there are two broad classes of absorbers which can be
applied to this end. Firstly there are porous absorbers e.g. mineral wool, carpets, curtains etc. Porous absorbers
work mainly by the principal of dissipation, where absorption is caused by sound propagation which occurs in
the network of interconnected pores within the material. This results in viscous and thermal eﬀects leading
11
==PAGE:23==
to a dissipation of the acoustic energy [8]. Porous absorbers generally oﬀer relatively wide band absorption at
higher frequencies but are very ineﬀective if they are too shallow compared to the wavelength. Meaning, that
in order to obtain eﬀective absorption at low frequencies the absorbers need to be very deep. This naturally
takes up a lot of space and is very impractical [8]. This leads us to the second broad class of absorbers, namely
resonant absorbers.
Resonant absorbers are devices which exploit resonances, and can be broadly sub-divided into two types:
Helmholtz absorbers and membrane, or panel, absorbers. Both types involve the same mechanism of a mass
vibrating against a spring. In the case of Helmholtz absorbers the mass is a plug of air in the opening of
a perforated sheet. In this case, the resonance is created in the same manner as when you blow across the
opening of a bottle and a note is generated. In order to provide any absorption, damping is added to remove
sound energy, often provided by a layer of porous material placed at the neck of the perforations. In the case
of membrane absorbers the mass is a sheet of material such as rubber or plywood. Here damping is added by
placing a porous material just behind the membrane, but not directly onto the backside. In both cases the
spring is provided by the air enclosed in the cavities. The characteristics of resonant absorbers are a peak of
absorption, which can be used to target speciﬁc unwanted frequency content. By altering the materials and
degrees of perforation, thus changing the mass and the stiﬀness of the air springs, the resonant frequency of the
absorber can be tuned to ﬁt speciﬁc needs [8].
All types of absorptive materials will typically be classiﬁed by an absorption coeﬃcient α which ranges from 0
to 1. This is the same α as the one that appeared in eq. 2.7 and eq. 2.8 earlier in this section.
The application of acoustic absorbers may be many, be it reverberation control in excessively reverberant
spaces such as atria, museums, restaurants etc., noise control within working environments, echo control in lec-
ture theaters or modal control in critical listening spaces. The applications are plenty. In this study and related
experiment it was chosen to focus on a small selection of some of the most common types of absorbers which are
used today in public spaces such as meeting rooms, oﬃces, restaurants etc. but also in private homes. Namely
acoustic wall absorbers, acoustic timber cladding panels (lamellas) and heavy drapes/curtains. Ex-
amples of the three types can be seen in Figure 2.13.
Porous wall absorbers of the kind seen in Figure 2.13a have long been one of the most common type of acoustic
absorbers installed in many diﬀerent types of spaces. They come in a myriad of sizes and colours providing
high ﬂexibility to ﬁt speciﬁc needs and design criteria. In recent years customisation of such panels has become
more popular and commonplace, with the possibility of adding custom prints to the design.
Likewise, the
timber cladding panels, or lamellas, as seen in Figure 2.13b which are generally installed as a hybrid between
resonant and porous absorbers as described above, have gained popularity in both public and private buildings,
especially in Denmark. It is not uncommon in more recent constructions to ﬁnd entire rooms or sections of
a building covered with this type of absorber. Through conversation with several acoustic consultants, it has
been understood that people generally ﬁnd the visual aesthetics of such lamellas especially pleasing to the eye.
Finally, curtains or drapes as seen in Figure 2.13c have seen a rise in use as a result of the modern open oﬃce
ﬂoor planning. They provide an easy way of creating small meeting room spaces within open plan buildings.
Furthermore, they are often seen in meeting rooms in more modern oﬃce buildings, where one or more walls
are likely to be glass panels, as to comply with the open oﬃce planning. It is of course not uncommon either

2.2
Spatial Hearing and AVR Model Integration
This section covers the basic concepts of spatial hearing and how this inﬂuences the design and implementation
of the AVR model. Also, comments are given on certain challenges with the AVR model which were identiﬁed.
2.2.1
Spatial Hearing
The ability to localize the position of a source of sound in space is crucial for humans’ ability to navigate their
surrounding environment. This is true for any animal. The complex geometric features of the human torso,
head and external ear manipulates any incoming sound in terms of phase distortions, amplitude distortions and
spectrum distortions which is encoded as direction-dependant information. This information is then passed to
the brain for processing and interpretation. The whole process is commonly referred to as spatial hearing.
The three-dimensional nature of spatial hearing, and the fact that the human head is roughly spherical, makes
it appropriate to describe the 3D sound ﬁeld by a spherical coordinate system. In such a system, the position
of a point is speciﬁed by three values, in this context deﬁned as: the radial distance r of the point from a ﬁxed
13
==PAGE:25==
origin, its polar angle (or elevation) δ and its azimuth angle θ. This is illustrated in Figure 2.14.
Figure 2.14: Spherical coordinate system used for 3D sound localization.
The three dimensional head-related coordinate systems centered around the head is described by three
planes; the horizontal plane, the median plane and the frontal plane. The horizontal plane is imagined to be
parallel to the ground. The angle δ between the horizontal plane and the vector r described from the centre
of the head to the source deﬁnes the elevation. Similarly, the median plane is perpendicular to the horizontal
plane, passing through the head vertically from front to back. The angle θ between the median plane and the
vector r described from the centre of the head to the source deﬁnes the azimuth. When dealing with three
dimensional head-related coordinate systems it is custom to let δ and θ be 0◦straight in front of the head (there
are diﬀerent conventions depending on the scientiﬁc ﬁeld).
The human brain makes use of a complex combination of many diﬀerent auditory and visual cues when trying
to spatially localize a sound source. The most important cues can be categorised as follows [9]:
1. Azimuth cues
2. Elevation cues
3. Range cues
4. Reverberation and echoes
5. Visual cues
Regarding azimuth cues, i.e. cues for localization in the horizontal plane, there are two major contributors.
Namely the Interaural Time Diﬀerence (ITD) and Interaural Level Diﬀerence (ILD) collectively described by
the Duplex Theory proposed by Lord Rayleigh in 1907 [9]. The ITD describes the diﬀerence in arrival time of
a sound between the two ears in the case of the source being located anywhere other than directly in front or
behind the listener. So the sound from a source placed at any azimuth angle θ ̸= 0◦∧180◦will arrive at one
ear before the other, with a maximum diﬀerence when the source is placed directly to either the right or left
side of the listener at θ = 90◦∧270◦.
Simultaneously, the ILD describes the diﬀerence in sound pressure level which arrives at each ear in the case of
sound sources localized outside of the median plane, known as the head-shadow eﬀect. The concepts and eﬀects
of both ITD and ILD are illustrated in Figure 2.15
14
==PAGE:26==
Figure 2.15: Simpliﬁed graphical representation of ITD (left) and ILD (right). From [10].
The eﬀect of ILD is particularly prominent for higher frequencies, where the size of the head is large relative
to the wavelength of the sound. Conversely, the eﬀect of ITD is more prominent for lower frequencies, where the
waveform is slightly shifted in time (see Figure 2.15), which is easily detected by the human ears. Commonly, it
is said that ITD is dominant up to around 1500 Hz from which point on ILD becomes more dominant. This is
due to the fact, that the average distance between the ears of humans is approximately 20 cm. Below 1500 Hz
the head dimensions are comparable with the wavelengths, and will not cast a shadowing eﬀect. Even though
we talk about this cutoﬀ-frequency it is important to remember, that ITD and ILD do in fact overlap, and in
many cases it will be a combination of the two phenomena that is used for sound source localization.
Although ITD and ILD are simple yet powerful cues for sound source localization, there are certain situa-
tions where additional cues are necessary.
For example, the duplex theory is not able to diﬀerentiate the
placement of a sound source localized in the median plane, since here both ITD and ILD are zero regardless of
the sound source position. Similarly, the so-called Cone of confusion, as illustrated in Figure 2.16, describes a
cone centered on the interaural axis with the apex at the center of the head. Diﬀerent sound sources placed any
where on the periphery of the cone, will produce identical ITD and ILD, making it impossible to distinguish
between front/back and up/down localization. For this reason, additional cues are to be taken into account to
more correctly identify elevation and orientation of a given sound source [9].
15
==PAGE:27==
Figure 2.16: Schematic of the cone of confusion. From [11].
Luckily, in most every-day listening environments some degree of mobility and head movement is possible.
Head movement is particularly useful for determining whether a source is located in front of or behind a lis-
tener in ambiguous situations, seeing that the movement will increase ITD and ILD for one ear, and conversely
decrease it for the other.
What has so far been presented to describe the azimuth cues, i.e. ITD, ILD and head movement, may be
categorized as binaural cues. However, elevation cues are often said to be monaural to a higher degree. A
majority of the sound waves arriving at the ear will have been reﬂected of the head, pinna and torso of the
listener. As a result, the sound is ﬁltered producing spectral diﬀerences which are unique from ear to ear, person
to person, and even between source positions. These diﬀerences allow for the source localization in the median
and frontal plane [9].
Finally, apart from the above-mentioned cues which help determine the orientation of a source, there are a
number of cues which allows for better judgement of the distance to a source. For example the loudness of a
sound will increase as a constant-energy source approaches a listener. Furthermore, the phenomenon of motion
parallax applies seeing that if a listener translates his or her head, the change in the azimuth angle will be range
dependant. For a source far away the change will be negligible, but for a source very close to the listener an
observable change in azimuth angle will occur. Finally, the direct-to-reverberant-energy ratio plays a role in the
perception of distance to a given source. It is known, that the energy received directly from a sound source will
drop of inversely with the square of the range. In common rooms however, the sound will be reﬂected and scat-
tered multiple times from various surfaces in the room. The resulting reverberant energy that reaches the ears
does not depend much on the distance between the source and the listener. Therefore, the direct-to-reverberant
ratio is very large at close ranges, whereas it will be relatively small at long ranges [9].
There are of course simple cues such as familiarity and experience with a given source as well as visual cues,
such as actually being able to see the source producing the sound.
2.2.2
HRTF
All the above-mentioned direction and distance dependant cues are collectively categorized by the complex
response function known as the head-related transfer function (HRTF). The HRTF and the associated head-
related impulse response (HRIR) emcompass all the available auditory cues used for sound source localization.
As mentioned the functions are unique for every source position, every listener and each ear. Seeing that humans
are equipped with two ears a HRTF and HRIR usually come in pairs - one for each ear. Furthermore, these
functions allow for the reproduction and synthesis of binaural signals from a monaural source.

2.2.3
Ambisonics
Unlike many other surround sound formats, ambisonics is a full sphere surround sound format that does not
depend on a speciﬁc speaker setup. It is instead based on spherical harmonics and describes the sound ﬁeld as
a combination of plane waves. The ambisonic channels are traditionally based on the so called B-format, and
the number of channels depends on the order of the ambisonics. Zero-order ambisonics have just one channel
(”W”), corresponding to a pulsating sphere. For ﬁrst-order ambisonics three additional channels are included
(”X”, ”Y” and ”Z”), that take into account the directionality of the sound ﬁeld for three orthogonal axes.
With second-order ambisonics further ﬁve channels are added giving a total of 9 channels, and a better spacial
resolution of the sound ﬁeld. Third-order ambisonics has 16 channels and can describe a sound ﬁeld with even
more detail. In this project only second-order ambisonics have been used.
17
==PAGE:29==
Figure 2.19: Illustration of spherical harmonics for up to third order. Sorted after increasing ambisonic
channel number and aligned for symmetry.
It is important to be aware that the internal arrangement of the channels for the diﬀerent orders can vary
depending on the speciﬁc format of the ambisonics. For instance does the Furse-Malham format (FuMa) have
the channel order ”W XYZ RSTUV” while the ACN format (Ambisonic Channel Number) instead has ”W
YZX VTRSU”.
One clear advantage of the ambisonic format being based on directions rather than speaker positions, is that
it makes it easy to rotate the sound ﬁeld around diﬀerent axes with use of rotation matrices. For playback, one
does however eventually have to decode the ambisonics to a speciﬁc speaker array.
2.2.4
Hermes’ AVR model
In Hermes’ AVR model, the above mentioned advantage of easy rotation of the sound ﬁeld with ambisonics is
a key aspect. The audio in the model is a mix of ambisonic wave ﬁles that are ﬁrst rotated, then decoded to a
virtual 12-speaker array and ﬁnally synthesized to a binaural output for headphone playback. The ambisonic
wave ﬁles are based on spacial IRs for a horizontal grid of receiver points in the room model, that are then
convolved with an anechoic mono recording of any type. Each wave ﬁle thus describes the sound ﬁeld rather
accurately in one speciﬁc point, but interpolation is needed to describe the sound ﬁeld for positions between the
points. In the acoustic simulation software Odeon, one can both modify materials, source and receiver positions,
generate the ambisonic IRs, and convolve them with a dry signal, but there are also other ways to generate the
required ﬁles.
The primary part of the AVR model is a Pd-patch (.pd ﬁle for the software family of pure data), that has
access to a folder with the ambisonic wave ﬁles, and to live transmitted spacial information from a Unity model.
Via C# scripts and a UDP (User Data Protocol), Unity can communicate directly to Pd-extended, and send
values depending on position and orientation in the model (an explanation of the programs Pd and Unity are
given later in 3.1). This is used to interpolate between the discrete IR points that the wave ﬁles represent. In
Hermes’ AVR model, linear interpolation is used to cross fade the wave ﬁles when moving from one point to
another. The wave ﬁles are simply multiplied with a gain value between 1 and 0 for both of the horizontal axes
(x and z in Unity) depending linearly on the current distance from the corresponding IR point in the model. A
distance of 0 meters thus equals a gain of 1, while a distance corresponding to the grid size (or more) equals a
gain of 0. This way only the four nearest IR points will be active at once. The four gain values will at all times
reﬂect the relative position, and if added together equal one. In Figure 2.20 below, a few examples of player
positions and the resulting gain values for the wave ﬁles of the active IR points are illustrated.

After the sound ﬁeld rotation, the nine ambisonic channels are decoded to twelve speaker signals corre-

steps, from the ambisonic wave ﬁles to the binaural output, are processed in realtime, as a function of position
and head orientation. Some processing power is therefore required for running both the VR application and the
AVR model at the same time without delays.

2.2.5
Challenges with the AVR model
As described in the previous section, the AVR model is based on interpolations between simulated points in a
given room model. If the grid size is set to be very small, the spacial IRs will have smaller variations from point
to point, and the interpolations will be less critical. However, for models of a certain size, this will mean a lot
of calculations, and a large number of 9-channel wave ﬁles, which takes up a lot of memory. In order to make
the model less computationally heavy, a compromise therefore has to be made in terms of grid size, which gives
rise to a few challenges.
The challenges will primarily lie in areas with drastic changes in the spatial IRs. This will for instance be

==PAGE:32==
the case near an active source, as the dominating sound direction should shift suddenly as one moves from one
side of the source to another. With the square grid formation this is very diﬃcult to achieve, without having
an extremely dense IR grid. To illustrate some of the issues, a few examples of problematic source, grid, and
player positions are included below. The direction of the arrows indicate the perceived sound direction for the
speciﬁc IR points, and the thickness indicate the IR gain for the shown user position.
Figure 2.22: Example 1: An active source right in front of the user with the nearest IR points (receiver
positions) to the sides.
In Example 1 above, only two IR points are included, as the gain from the remaining two active IR points
will be close to zero for the illustrated user position. As the ﬁgure shows, the two dominating IRs will have
close to opposite perceptual source directions, and a mix of the two associated wave ﬁles will simulate sound
coming from the sides, and not from the actual direction towards the source.
Figure 2.23: Example 2: An active source close to the user, with the nearest IR point on the opposite side of

Example 2 demonstrates another likely case, where the nearest, and therefore gain wise dominating, IR
point is on the opposite side of the active sound source. This results in a audio mix, where the loudest element
contains spatial cues indicating a source direction that is opposite of the correct one.
For models with free movement around an active sound source, both of the examples can in principle occur
no matter the grid size, but the coarser the grid is, the larger the problematic areas will be.

Visual Considerations
In this section some general remarks are given on color coding as well as the concept of multi-sensory integration
and synesthesia.
2.3.1
Color Coding
Colour coding is a large ﬁeld and both a science and an art, respectively. There are multiple models for describ-
ing and quantifying the visible colour spectrum. The ﬁrst known example of the now well-known colour circle
21
==PAGE:33==
(or colour wheel) was presented by Sir Isaac Newton in his book Opticks published in 1704, though naturally
knowledge of colours and colour mixing had been known for millennia prior. The colour circle illustratively or-
ganizes color hues (to which we will return) around a circle, describing the relation between primary, secondary
and tertiary colours.
A colour model may be described as a visualization which depicts the colour spectrum as a multidimensional
model. Most modern models, such as the RGB (red-green-blue) colour model, are three dimensional and may
therefor be presented as 3D shapes. However, others have more dimensions, like the CMYK (cyan-magenta-
yellow-key) colour model. The RGB model may be depicted as a cube with each dimension belonging to one
of the three colours. However, this model does not provide an especially intuitive method for creating colors in
code. Some colors, like yellow (equal parts red and green), may be easy to guess, however less pure colors are
much harder to guess. All in all, people do not think of colors as mixtures of red, green and blue light [13].
As an alternative two cylindrical color models were developed during the 1970’s for computer graphical use, to
provide a more intuitive colour model to how human vision perceives colour-making attributes. These colour
models are the HSV (hue, saturation, value) colour model and HSL (hue, saturation, lightness) colour model,
respectively. The models are visualized below in Figure 2.24 along with the RGB model.
(a) RGB colour model.
(b) HSV colour model.
(c) HSL colour model.
Figure 2.24: Colour models graphical representation. From [14].
It can be noted, that two of the dimensions involved in the HSV and HSL colour models are the same,
namely the hue and saturation dimensions. Hue speciﬁes the colour portion of the model and is speciﬁed as the
angle of the colour on the RGB colour circle. Saturation on the other hand, controls the amount of colour used.
A 100 % saturated colour will result in the purest possible colour, whereas a 0 % saturated colour will result in a
grey-scale. Where the two models diﬀer are in the value (sometimes called brightness) and lightness dimensions.
The value dimension of HSV controls the brightness of a color, so that a colour with 0 % brightness is pure black
and 100 % brightness results in pure white. Conversely, the lightness dimension of HSL controls the luminosity
of the coulour. It is diﬀerent from the HSV value dimension in that the purest colour is positioned halfway
between pure white and pure black [13].
It should be noted, that the three dimensions of the HSV colour model are interdependant.
For example,
if the value dimensions is set to 0 % then the colour will be pure black regardless of the hue and saturation
levels. Similarly, if the saturation is set to 0 % then the hue will not matter, since no colour is used. Altering
the value dimension in this case, will result in a greyscale moving from pure black to pure white over shades of
grey.

2.3.2
Multi-sensory integration & Synesthesia
As touched upon in the Motivation (section 1.1) humans exhibit a strongly multi-sensory (sometimes multi-
modal) approach to perception of sensory impressions. For example the enjoyment of food and beverages rely
heavily on the senses of both taste and smell. This is what is known as multi-sensory integration, where infor-
mation from the diﬀerent sensory modalities (e.g sight, sound, taste, etc.) is integrated by the nervous system,
and experienced in synergy. In most real-life settings this ability to utilize multiple senses is extremely useful
and vital for making the most complete assessment of a given situation. However, it is not in all situations
that multi-sensory integration will enhance your perceptions. In some cases it may rather alter and confuse,
which happens in everyday life but can also be manipulated and simulated in more controlled environments.
An example of this is the well-established McGurk eﬀect [15], where the presentation of two incongruent visual
and audio stimuli leads to the perception of an entirely third audio event.
An extreme form of multi-sensory integration is the condition known as synesthesia, the word stemming from
the Greek words ”synth” (meaning ”together”) and ”ethesia” (meaning ”perception”). Synesthesia, supposed
to aﬀect somewhere between 2−4 % of the population, is a neurological condition in which information meant to
stimulate one of your senses (e.g. sound) automatically and involuntarily stimulates several of your senses (e.g.
also vision). Although there are many diﬀerent versions of the condition, a person with synesthesia (known as
a synesthete) might for example experience distinct colors or the feeling of touch on speciﬁc areas of the body
when exposed to certain sounds. A study in sound-colour synesthesia has shown, that even though synesthetes
are more precise and consistent in attributing colours to a given set of sounds relative to a control group, both
groups appear to use the same heuristics for matching the auditory and visual domains [16]. Furthermore, in
another study it was established that people tend to match lighter visual stimuli with higher pitched auditory
stimuli and vice versa [17]. A tendency which is also true for synesthetes.
The concept of multi-sensory integration seems rational, something almost self-explanatory. We use it con-
sciously and no one would voluntarily chose to go a day with any of the senses inactive. It seems natural, that
the more of the senses we can apply the more well-informed the feedback from our surroundings is. Conversely,
the experiences by synesthetes which appear to be applicable to non-synesthetes as well, describe an uncon-
scious, involuntary integration of the senses. Of course synesthesia and multi-sensory integration are not the
topics under investigation in this project, but are noteworthy observations to keep in mind.

3.1
AVR Setup
Common to the three AVR experiments was, that they all share the same software, hardware and AVR model

3.1.1
Software
Five main software were utilized across the three experiments to properly construct the experiments and inte-
grate the AVR model.
• MATLAB The well-known programming language and numeric computing environment MATLAB was
used to handle signal processing that did not have to run real-time, e.g. ambisonics format conversion,
and application of various predetermined gains, etc.
• Pure Data Pure Data (or Pd) is an open source visual programming language created by Miller Puck-
ette in the 1990’s and is a branch of the family of patcher programming languages known as Max (e.g.
Max/MSP). It is heavily used by visual artists, musicians, programmers etc. to create interactive music
and multimedia without the need for writing actual code. Due to its open-source character Pd is widely
extended by a large community of developers. The algorithmic functions in Pd are presented by visual
boxes called objects which are placed within a patching window known as a canvas. The data ﬂow, which
as a rule of thumb happens from the top of the screen to the bottom, is achieved through connections
between inlets and outlets of objects via the visible cords known as patch cords. The task performed
by each object can vary in complexity ranging from simple mathematical operations to advanced audio
or video functions such as reverberation, FFT transformations, etc. Being free to use and tailored for
real-time processing of sound ﬁles with many channels, Pd has been a good choice for the AVR model.
• Unity Unity is a world leading cross-platform game engine used extensively for development of 2D and
3D games for a variety of desktop, mobile, console and virtual reality platforms. Furthermore, the engine
24
==PAGE:36==
is also used for other interactive simulations and experiences and has been adopted by non-gaming indus-
tries such as architecture, engineering, construction, etc. The software allows for import of 3D models,
integration of C scripts and the use of various VR equipment.
• SketchUp SketchUp is a 3D modelling software used extensively in architectural, interior design and civil
and mechanical engineering businesses, amongst others. SketchUp is very intuitive and easy to use, and,
through the external plug-in SU2Odeon, the easiest way of exporting a geometrical 3D model to Odeon.
Furthermore, models created in SketchUp can be drag-and-dropped directly into Unity, providing an easy
and iterative workﬂow when building any VR scene.
• Odeon Odeon is a comprehensive software for room acoustics used for simulating and measuring the
interior acoustics of buildings, using an image-source method combined with a modiﬁed ray tracing al-
gorithm. Given a 3D-model and appropriate surface material properties it allows for the prediction and
illustration of the acoustics as well as oﬀering the possibility to listen to the simulations. Odeon is used
in this project to generate simulated B-format IRs. B-format IRs are essential to the AVR model, as they
contain directional information that is not captured by mono IRs or binaural IRs. In Odeon you have the
possibility to export both ﬁrst and second-order Ambisonics B-format IRs.

3.1.3
AVR model integration
The AVR model supplied by Hermes consisted of several C# scripts in a 2018 unity project, and a library
including a Pd-ﬁle and folders containing generic HRTFs and second-order ambisonic wave ﬁles. As the speciﬁc
scripts and ﬁles were made for another project, the essence of them had to be transferred into new scripts
made with the experiments in mind. This meant setting up Unity objects with new C# scripts attached, that
would send relevant data to the Pd-patch in terms of mix volumes, orientation, start and stop signals, active
sources etc., depending on the active scene. The new Pd-patch also had to be structured according to a new li-
brary of convolved wave ﬁles, and set up to mix the ﬁles, and respond to orientations and commands from unity.
Initially three Pd-patches where made, one for each experiment, but when the ﬁles where opened and DSP
activated, an unpleasant noise burst kept appearing. Therefore, the three patches where eventually combined
into one patch with diﬀerent subsections that could be activated depending on the active experiment. This way,
participants would not need to remove the headphones when switching experiment.
Quite some time was spent on making sure that sound directions in Unity and Pd-extended matched when
moving around and rotating the head. A third rotation matrix was added, so the Pd-patch would take into
account all three rotation axes in Unity. Furthermore, the Odeon support team were contacted in order to
conﬁrm the exported format of the convolved second-order ambisonic wave ﬁles. The Odeon ﬁles proved to be
of the ACN format, and the channels therefore had to be rearranged to match the FuMa format that the AVR
model was based on. After the format conversion in MATLAB, the sound ﬁeld still had to be rotated 90 degrees
in the azimuth plane to match up with the orientation in Unity. The convolved wave ﬁles thus ended up being
subject to four rotations. One initial 90 degree azimuth rotation, and then three rotations based on the three
rotation axes in Unity (rotation, tilt and the newly added tumble).

3.3
The three experiments
This subsection will describe in detail the experimental design and setup of the three experiments. Before going
into details of each of the experiments, the general workﬂow leading to the creation and integration of both
visual and auditory stimuli will be described, seeing that this is common for all three experiments.
3.3.1
Experimental Setup Workﬂow
In the process of designing and building the AVR experiments a somewhat common setup workﬂow has been
followed.
The initial step in setting up both the visual and auditory aspects of the experiments involves producing a
3D CAD model using SketchUp (or any similar 3D modelling software). This model can then be exported
simultaneously to Odeon via the SU2Odeon SketchUp plugin and to Unity as one object or as many individual
objects (practical for making iterative changes along the way).
After importing the model into Odeon the necessary acoustic simulations can be performed following the as-
signment of material properties to all surfaces and the placement of sources and receivers. This can be done
based on the speciﬁc needs. It is however important to remember, that the placement of sources and receivers
will determine the outcome of the simulated impulse responses, and as such should be chosen carefully to ﬁt
the needs of the experiment. There are two main desired outcomes from the acoustic simulations in Odeon.
One is to obtain the IRs of the desired source/receiver combinations. In particular it is important to obtain the
ambisonic IRs, since these contain the valuable directional information needed to faithfully recreate a spatial
3D listening environment in the experimental AVR model. The other is to obtain said ambisonic IRs in multi-
ple diﬀerent acoustic environments (i.e. alternative material properties resulting in varying objective acoustic
parameters), such that a selection of auditory stimuli can be presented to the test subjects.
Per default Odeon generates amplitude normalized IRs, so that every receiver position will have the same
maximum value. In every AVR model, but in particular those where multiple IRs are included and virtual
movement around the model is an option, the level diﬀerence between individual IRs is naturally of great im-
29
==PAGE:41==
portance. Without it the level in relation to the distance to the source will be incorrect, and the spatial sound
reproduction will be less realistic. As a work around the later versions of Odeon (from Odeon 15 and up) allows
one to export a text ﬁle containing the attenuation factors. Through the use of MATLAB or similar software,
the corresponding level of each IR can be obtained by dividing the IR by the attenuation factor. However, when
convolving anechoic ﬁles with the spatial IRs in Odeon, the exported waveﬁles will not be normalized, and thus
have correct relative volumes depending on the positions in the model.

Experiment 3 - Sound Source Localization
The ﬁnal experiment was a test of the AVR model’s accuracy in terms of sound source localization.

10 meters, and a room height of 3.5 meters (V = 210 m3). It was treated with rather absorptive materials to
achieve a relatively low reverberation time (approximately 0.5 seconds) and thereby ensure that the soundscape
would not be dominated by late reﬂections. In Figure 3.10 below, the room and the potential sources can be
seen from the start position in the experiment. Furthermore, Figure 3.11 gives a top-down illustration of the

3.4
Analytical tools
In Section 1 the governing hypotheses behind this master thesis were put forward, which for the readers sake
are repeated here:
H1: The visual appearance of common acoustic absorptive treatments inﬂuences the perceived acoustic
quality.
H2: The brightness of a room inﬂuences the perceived level of reverberance or loudness.
H3: The spatial misinformation of the AVR model can be reduced without increasing the number of
calculated IR points.
Each hypothesis formed the basis for one of the three AVR experiments previously described, without any
particular intentional interdependency between the three. Whereas the ﬁrst two of the three experiments, i.e.
the Meeting Room Experiment (Experiment 1) and the Visual Brightness Experiment (Experiment 2), pro-
duced highly subjective results as a consequence of the subjective rating scales, the third Source Localization
Experiment (Experiment 3) produced a more objective measure as the test subjects ability to accurately locate
a sound source was easily quantiﬁable. However, in all three cases it was expected to ﬁnd both diﬀerences and
possibly tendencies in the results, that could either conﬁrm or oppose our initial hypotheses. From looking at
simple plots of the results a general impression of trends in the data can be obtained, but in order to more
meaningfully determine, whether or not the results have any statistical signiﬁcance, certain analytical tools
must be applied.
Having examined the result data for all three experiments, it was concluded that none of them followed a
normal distribution. This was done by applying the Shapiro-Wilk test to the results as well as visually exam-
ining so-called qq-plots. Furthermore, the experimental design involved within-subject testing, meaning that
each test participant would experience all conditions, and researchers test the same participants repeatedly
for diﬀerences between conditions. As a result, non-parametric (i.e non-normal distributed) within-subjects
statistical tests where to be applied for the statistical analysis of the results. These will be brieﬂy described in
the following.
3.4.1
Friedman Test
The Friedman Test is a non-parametric alternative to the Repeated Measures Analysis Of Variance (ANOVA).
The test can be used to determine, whether there is a statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence between groups for
dependent variables that are either ordinal or continuous data that has violated the assumptions necessary to
use a one-way ANOVA with repeated measures. In other words, the test helps you to determine whether to
reject the null hypothesis, i.e. H0 : µ1 = µ2 (there is no diﬀerence between the means of two groups), or
accept the alternate hypothesis, i.e. H1 : µ1 ̸= µ2 (there is a diﬀerence between the means of two groups). The
number of groups can be extended depending on the data set. Although the assumption of normality, which is
associated with the ANOVA test, does not need to be fulﬁlled, there are a few other assumptions that must be
met [18]:
• The test works for one group which is measured on three or more occasions.
• Group must be a random sample from the population.
• The dependent variable must be measured on at the ordinal or continuous level.
41
==PAGE:53==
The test is a within-subjects rank test, where the dependant variables are ranked. The whole procedure
can be performed in R (a programming language for statistical computing and graphics which has been used
throughout this project). The test produces a Chi-squared test statistic and a corresponding p-value. If the
p-value is less than the chosen signiﬁcance level (commonly 0.05), then there is suﬃcient evidence that the
means between each of the conditions are not equal. In this project a signiﬁcance level of α = 0.05 has been
applied. Although the Friedman test will tell you whether or not there is statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerences
between occasions, it will not tell you exactly where these diﬀerences lie, i.e. between which occasions. To
clarify this, a post-hoc analysis is necessary.
3.4.2
Pairwise Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test
For the Friedman test the pairwaise Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test is the appropriate post-hoc test. This is a non-
parametric alternative to the two sample t-test. The test produces a p-value for all combinations, which allows
you to identify exactly where your diﬀerences lie. A correction for multiple comparisons must be applied. In
the case of this project the Bonferroni-correction has been applied.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In line with the rest of this extensive project, the conclusion will be of a threefold format.
The AVR test of H1, whether the visual appearance of common acoustic absorptive treatments aﬀects the
perceived acoustic quality, did not provide statistically signiﬁcant results that could back up the hypothesis. It
was found that there was a general visual preference towards the room design with two walls covered by lamellas,
but this did not aﬀect the audio ratings signiﬁcantly. The preferred acoustics were from the simulation of panel
absorbers on the two walls, but in general the three acoustic designs with absorptive treatment on both walls
scored higher in terms of audio ratings.
The Visual Brightness experiment did not result in signiﬁcant ﬁndings that could conﬁrm the hypothesis
H2, that the brightness of a room inﬂuences the perceived level of reverberance or loudness. As one would
expect, the two parameters, reverberation time and gain, proved to be highly correlated with reverberance and
loudness respectively, but changing the wall and ceiling color between the ﬁve diﬀerent shades of grey ranging
from very bright to very dark, did not seem to aﬀect the ratings much.
In the third experiment, the ability to locate an active sound source while moving freely in Hermes’ AVR
model was tested for two IR grid sizes and for two alternative models with diﬀerent additional components.
While the coarse grid mode for the original model by far did worst in the test, the two model alternatives both
resulted in more correct localizations than the ﬁne grid model, but with fewer IR points used. The interpolation
alternative, Grid Mode 3, with IR points surrounding the active source, even did signiﬁcantly better in terms
distance oﬀ, supporting the hypothesis H3, that the spatial misinformation of the AVR model can be reduced
without increasing the number of calculated IR points. Grid Mode 4, with a rotatable direct sound component,
could however be argued to be the best utilization of the computationally demanding IRs, having just one more
than the coarse grid model, and still scoring an average distance oﬀclose to the one for Grid Mode 3. In spite
of positive results, none of the model variations are perfect in their current state, and suggestions for future
work are therefore included in the section of same title.
Finally, this project has also been concerned with acquiring useful skills and knowledge in the world of
modern acoustics, and it has throughout the process been exiting to explore the promising ﬁeld of future AVR.

Chapter 6
Future Work
Although the results from experiment one and two provided very little evidence of visuals eﬀecting the perceived
acoustics, the topic still has many possible angles left for exploration. Other studies have found correlations,
and the ﬁeld of AVR gives endless test possibilities. Instead of panel type, one could for instance look into a
potential eﬀect of the amount of visible absorptive treatment, or the color of a certain panel type. The quality
of the visuals could also be looked into, as photo realistic renderings might have a stronger multi-sensory eﬀect.
In terms of the AVR model, especially Grid Mode 4 seems to be promising, and could likely be made even
better by looking more into the implementation of the direct component. Maybe the grid of IRs could exclude
direct sound, so they only contained reﬂected sound, and the direct component could be attenuated according
to a point source in free ﬁeld. Grid Mode 3 might also have some potential, if a more seamless interpolation
can be made for the additional IR points.
