Summary
Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) has gained a lot of popularity the recent years. This comes
along an increase in computer power and structures are becoming more ﬂexible. The former
fact makes FSI simulations feasible whereas the latter makes it necessary for many appli-
cations e.g. aerodynamical problems. Since structures may be very ﬂexible, a reasonable
interpretation of the ﬂow can only be possible when the structural response is incorporated.
Although, FSI still suﬀers for being computationally expensive and parametric FSI studies
are thus out of scope. On the light of FSI involves resolving two entirely diﬀerent physics
makes the problem very cumbersome. One of the mayor challenges in FSI algorithms is to
reduce the time-cost while maintaining a high accuracy in such a way that it can be utilized
for parametric studies and furthermore for design purposes. In order to keep the accuracy at
a conﬁdent level, one most ﬁrst and foremost ensure that the numerical methods employed
to resolve the physics individually are of high conﬁdence as well. Additionally, the coupling
sequence in time and space must also be conducted appropriately in order to achieve a reason-
able interaction between the physics. Having said this, the aim of this thesis is to investigate
novel methodologies within the FSI framework to reduce the overall time-cost, which conse-
quently may allow us to perform parametric FSI studies. Particularly, the essence and new
contribution of this thesis are a proposal of an alternative approach of the structural part of
the FSI solver shown to gain massively improved performance.
This research will set out the methodologies and numerical models to be employed for the
FSI algorithm. We will present a loosely coupling algorithm which considers a Finite Element
Method (FEM) and Finite Volume Method (FVM) to resolve the computational domains of
the structure and ﬂuid, respectively. Concise descriptions of the considered FEM and FVM
will be presented. Subsequently, this work will propose an alternative method to solve the
FEM equations, namely, by means of a Reduced Order Model (ROM) which aims to eliminate
the time-cost of the structural solver considerably. Although, ROM will be shown to only be
capable of representing, at most, linear problems. Thus, the suggested FSI-ROM approach is
strongly limited to cases where the kinematics/strains of the structure are relatively small.
Following this, we will derive a mathematical and numerical higher order reduced model
(HORM) capable of reproducing reasonable results for Linear as well as Non-Linear prob-
lems. This will be shown to induce slightly more time cost compared to the regular FSI-ROM
approach, however, it remains by far more eﬃcient than the regular FSI method based on
the Non-Linear FEM. The suggested model will be based on a Taylor approximation up to
the ﬁrst order (linearization) of the equations of motion. The linearization will be performed
around a reference deformed state of the structure.
Once the linearization is conducted,

the structural properties like internal forces and stiﬀness matrix will be evaluated, namely,
at the deformed state.
The numerical model will be derived with the option to perform
multiple linearizations during a time simulation. These will be executed according to a pre-
deﬁned threshold which controls the perturbations upon a linearization. Once the maximum
perturbation with respect to the most recent deformed state exceeds the threshold, a new
linearization will be performed and, hence, the structural properties will be updated. This
will imply that HORM is capable to resolve the structure’s response adequately regardless of
the order of non-linearity present. The newly derived model, FSI-HORM, will be tested for a
variety of case-studies demonstrating strong and robust performance. Also, the eﬃciency of
FSI-HORM will be demonstrated to be signiﬁcantly superior to a regular FSI-FEM method.
Having presented a thorough examinations of the robustness of HORM, the optimal threshold
will be determined for each FSI example. Since the size of the threshold will be shown to
have an inﬂuential impact on the time-performance, it is essential to clarify the sense of the
threshold and its corresponding size.
Therefore, various studies will be examined to illuminate the behavior of the threshold sug-
gesting that it will be highly insensitive to various parameters. Furthermore, this work will
provide a preliminary estimation of the threshold for future usage based on prior results. That
is, the characteristic length of a structure will indicate a dependency to the threshold. That
is, larger structures allow larger thresholds and visa verca. Speciﬁcally, taking the thresh-
old somewhere between 0.25% and 0.63% of the characteristic length will be shown to yield
acceptable results.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, the topic Fluid-structure interaction (FSI) is considered in a variety of technical
applications as the interaction between ﬂuid and structure is becoming more crucial and more
importantly feasible due to the increase in computer sizes and CPU. Another reason is that
structures tend to become more ﬂexible which implies that the non-linearity phenomena is
activated and the deformation of complex conﬁgurations can be very vital to simulate in a
FSI code in order to gain a reasonable understanding of the ﬂowﬁeld (Farhat and Lesoinne,
2000; Garelli et al., 2010). As FSI deals with two diﬀerent computational physics, e.g. Com-
putational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) and Finite Element Method (FEM), several complexities
have to be taken into account. See for example (Felippa et al., 2001; de Boer et al., 2007).
One of the main challenges is to keep the time-cost at a reasonable level while maintaining
the accuracy.
Fluid-structure interaction is applied in a very broad range of ﬁelds in ﬂuid and structural dy-
namics. It is being exploit to understand the blood ﬂow in heart valves (Peskin, 1972), to pre-
dict ﬂutter speed of aircraft wings (Farhat and Lesoinne, 2000), to investigate shock-induced
deformations of rocket nozzles (Garelli et al., 2010). Also, to improve the understandings of
the shock-wave boundary layer interaction in high-speed aerodynamic applications, the FSI
is becoming more crucial (Pasquariello et al., 2015b). While the wind-energy sector has been
relying on low-ﬁdelity aeroelastic codes, a fully coupled FSI simulation of wind turbine rotors
was developed by Bazilevs et al. (2011) recently. The importance of FSI for wind turbine ro-
tors was clearly demonstrated and has since then being a growing tool within the wind-energy
ﬁeld.
Research motivations and goals
This thesis deals with coupling a reduced order model (ROM) of the structural part to a
Large Eddy Simulation (LES) code in INCA1. Evidently, the LES code requires extremely

also are computationally expensive.
A signiﬁcant reduction in time-cost can be achieved by replacing the full Non-Linear FEM
model to a ROM with substantial less degrees of freedom (DOFs) (Dowell, 1996; Dowell
and Hall, 2001; Bampton and Craig, 1968). Particularly, vibration issues usually originate
from small deﬂections which make it viable for a reduction model to capture those within an
acceptable tolerance. However, large deﬂections or strains cannot be resolved by ROM since
its foundation is based on a Linear FEM model, which means that the structural properties
like stiﬀness do not vary in time.
Therefore, a wise suggestion is to couple ROM to a
Non-Linear FEM model in a interactive fashion. That is, the coupling between ROM and
the Non-Linear FEM provide the opportunity to interact forth and back between the solvers
when requested. Consequently, the structural properties can be updated arbitrarily during
a time simulation and the limitation of ROM in Non-Linear problems is avoided. Expressed
in diﬀerent terms, the linear FEM and therefore also ROM consider only one deformed state
which is the initial state of a time-simulation. That obviously means that the models (Linear
FEM and ROM) are restricted to operate in the vicinity of the state of reference, otherwise
the assumption of linearity is violated. By considering the suggested method, the deformed
state is not ﬁxed but is rather dynamical. Therefore, it is expected that this method will
provide reasonable results for Non-Linear problems. Evidently, if the structure has limited
cycles around a deformed state, then, there will be no need to return back to the FEM solver
in order update the state of reference. However, if large deﬂections are detected an update
must be performed.
A suggestion would be to deﬁne a threshold which checks when the
maximum of the actual deformations compared to the state of reference has been reached
and an update is therefore required.
The reduction model will be based on the modal coordinate reduction method (See Vahdati
et al., 1999) which is a widely used technique. It is based on mode superposition and mode
truncation method where only a small number of modes are considered to transform the
structural model from a physical to modal space. There is very little, if any, publications on
coupling this method to a direct FE model. Hence, this work contributes with suggestions
on how to interact between a full direct FEM and a reduced order model during a time
simulation.

Chapter 2
Literature review
Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) has the recent years gained a lot of attention partly due to
the raise in advancement and complexity of conﬁgurations. Although, the main reason must
be due to the raise in CPU, which ultimately makes it feasible to conduct FSI simulations.
Structures are becoming more ﬂexible and their geometrical conﬁgurations are also becoming
more complex. Therefore, phenomena like non-linearity or critical interfaces between diﬀerent
densities can be very vital to simulate in a FSI tool (Farhat and Lesoinne, 2000; Garelli et al.,
2010). There have been reported several problems and failure due to the interaction between
diﬀerent physics. The Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940) is one of the most famous examples of
large structural failure due to the interaction between ﬂuid forces and structural responses.
Multiphysics codes are of signiﬁcant importance to gain a better understanding of problems
involving various objects with diﬀerent attributes and behavior. Particularly, the interaction
between two objects, e.g. ﬂuid and structure, has become essential to comprehend (Garelli
et al., 2010). One of the main challenges in FSI is the interface between ﬂuid and structure.
That is, the mesh almost never matches at the interface between the ﬂuid and structural do-
mains. Hence, a transformation of loads between meshes is essential for the coupling routine.
(See for example Felippa et al. (2001); de Boer et al. (2007)). Another very critical challenge
is how to consider the interfaces when the boundaries move from their initial position. Flow
in realistic applications usually involves complex geometries which regularly include the mo-
tion/deformationn of bodies. This complicates the numerical modeling signiﬁcantly as the
challenges become how to construct a grid which represents the body in motion properly, and
to maintain the computational costs at a reasonable level. The Immersed boundary method
is considered in the following work and a concise review is provided in this chapter. In fact,
the latter method is believed to treat arbitrary motion and large displacements in a very
reasonable way (Sotiropoulos and Yang, 2014). Having said this, dealing with FSI problems
increase the computational cost signiﬁcantly compared to simulations with solely FEM or
CFD, since both codes have to be run in addition to the interpolation algorithms to treat
the interface properties correctly. The reason being, why it is extremely important to invest
time to optimize the computational eﬃciency. On the other hand, it is also necessary to
treat the interfaces with high accuracy which frequently grows the time-cost. Particularly,

2.1
Reduced order model
Nowadays, a lot of research are being conducted to investigate and develop reduction order
models (ROMs) in various ﬁelds. Structural dynamics, design of feedback controllers, ﬂuid
dynamics, electronics to name few. However, the ﬁrst methods of ROMs belong to structural
dynamics, which is also the focus of this review.
One of the ﬁrst reported model reductions was presented by Rayleigh (1887) which employed
the mode superposition method, where a certain number of free vibration modes are included
to compute the displacement ﬁeld. In other words, this method truncates to a certain ex-
tent the vibration modes represented in the structure, which ultimately leads to errors. This
method is typically referred to as the Mode Displacement Method or Modal Coordinate Re-
duction and has been employed extensively for aeroelastic and FSI purposes (See Dowell and
Hall, 2001; Hall et al., 1999; Dowell, 1996; Debrabandere et al., 2012). The static correction
method reported e.g. by (Besselink et al., 2013; Wilson et al., 1982; Rixen, 2001) attempts
to account for the omitted modes by including the truncated modes statically. This is done
through a more adequate representation of modal loads. In fact, the static correction method
contributes to the lower frequency range, which is in many occasions necessary due to the
insuﬃcient representation of the modal loads (Besselink et al., 2013). Evidently, one of its
major drawbacks is that it only improves the accuracy in the low frequency spectrum (Rixen,
2001). In other words, this method is not fruitful for structures with relatively high natural
frequencies. The aforementioned correction method was improved by Dickens et al. (1995)
where the correction of the load representation was derived dynamically.
The method is
referred as the Modal Truncation Argumentation Method and was shown to be superior to
its counterpart. Despite it improved the overall accuracy compared to the static correction
method, the method is also eﬀective for a broader frequency range (Dickens et al., 1995).
Another very famous and popular reduction method is indeed the Craig-Bampton method
presented by Bampton and Craig (1968).
It is also referred to as the Component Mode
Synthesis. Instead of treating the entire structure as a big FE model, the body is divided
into N substructures connected with an appropriate interface description (See Fig.
2.1).
Subsequently, the dynamics of the system is described through modal analyses where only
a small number of modes is employed. Therefore, the reduced transformation matrix, which
transforms the global system to the partitioned systems, is described with only the dominant
modes and where the residual modes are neglected.

Another typical discussion when dealing with ROM, is how the eigenmodes can be obtained.
The most obvious answer is to directly compute the eigenvectors of the dynamic system by
means of an iterative eigenvalue solver like the Arnoldi iteration. However, there has been
presented an alternative solution aiming to decrease the computational cost for computing
the eigenmodes. Wilson et al. (1982) presented an algorithm (Wilson-Ritz algorithm) which
is capable of computing a set of reasonable eigenmodes very eﬃciently. The method is based
on the well known Reyleigh-Ritz procedure but has been extended and demonstrated for
multiple degrees of freedom. Arnold et al. (1985) presented an extensive comparison between
the Wilson-Ritz algorithm and the classical eigenvectors in terms of accuracy and time cost.
It is shown that the algorithm is very eﬃcient and accurate compared to the solution based
on classical eigenvectors.

2.2
Fluid-structure interaction
The current section presents a compromised review on methods dealt with coupling the in-
terfaces between the ﬂuid and structural computational domains. There are two main areas
underlying FSI problems, namely, coupling the diﬀerent solvers in space and time. That is,
coupling in space deals with projections of ﬂuid loads to the structural mesh and structural
deformations to the ﬂuid mesh. Obviously, that is only applicable at the interface of the two
respective meshes. The second area deals with the issue on how to couple and advance two
diﬀerent systems simultaneously in time.
2.2.1
Temporal coupling of ﬂuid and structure solvers
Generally, the temporal coupling procedure in FSI algorithms is divided into two classiﬁca-
tions, namely, the monolithic and partitioned. The monolithic approach is characterized by
its feature of solving the entire interactive system at once, i.e solving a single system (Kl¨oppel
et al., 2011). Evidently, this is time-consuming, however, the technique is robust, accurate
and unconditionally stable. The partitioned approach is characterized by considering the two
sub problems separately, namely the ﬂuid and structure are treated separately. Following this,
one sub-problem receives inputs from the other sub-problem and visa verca, i.e. subsequent
results from one solver becomes the input for its counterpart solver to advance the system in
time and visa verca. In doing so, this allows us to reuse existing computational codes which
is a substantial advantage (Causin et al., 2005).

analyzes two diﬀerent methods of time integration employed in a FSI fashion. It is shown
that by choosing certain sophisticated numerical integration schemes, the temporal coupling

Having said this, van Zuijlen (2006) worked out a novel time-integration scheme where higher
order accuracy in time was realized (> 2). The scheme is based on the so-called multistage
Runga-Kutta schemes where a mixed implicit/explicit (IMEX) time integration scheme was
considered.
One of the disadvantages of the aforementioned temporal coupling method (within the parti-
tioned framework) is that the convergence when coupling in time is not guaranteed. Particu-
larly, strongly coupled (e.g. when densities of ﬂuid and solid are comparable) problems may
suﬀer from this approach. The reason being, that the latter method is referred as the loosely
coupled method. The issue of convergence has been treated by introducing sub-iterations
to the loop between the structure and ﬂuid solver until convergence has occurred (K¨uttler
and Wall, 2008). Also, it was shown by van Zuijlen (2006) that the IMEX scheme is sta-
ble for weakly coupled problems, however, sub-iterations are inevitable for strongly coupled
problems. Although, introducing sub-iterations increase the computational time signiﬁcantly
(Farhat et al., 2006). A partitioned coupling with sub-iterations is as referred as the strongly
coupled partitioned method. For compressible ﬂow a loosely coupled partitioned approach
usually suﬃces since the coupling is relatively weak. That is, the coupling conditions are fairly
converged after one time-loop i.e. no sub-iterations at each time step is required (Farhat and
Lesoinne, 2000). Examples of applied FSI can be found in the work of Garelli et al. (2010)
where the loosely coupled partitioned method is employed and in the work of Farhat and
Lesoinne (2000) where various procedures of the partitioned methods are presented and com-
pared.
One of the most critical drawbacks by employing the loosely coupled method is the artiﬁcial
added mass eﬀect which may lead to numerical instability. Indeed, that is due to the fact that
results from time tn for one sub-problem is used to compute the results of the counterpart
sub-problem at the next time step tn+1. For instance, the pressure at tn is utilized to obtain
the kinematics (displacements and velocities) at tn+1, whereas the kinematics at tn+1 will be
used to compute the pressure at tn+1. Hence, one of the subsystems lacks one time step be-
hind with regards to its input. Causin et al. (2005) derived a mathematical formulation which

2.2.2
Spacial coupling of ﬂuid and structure solvers
Transfer of loads and kinematics are essential in FSI problems. As the structure and ﬂuid
meshes almost never match, a wise consideration has to be made in order to couple the
non-matching meshes. de Boer et al. (2007) reviewed various methods used in FSI context.
The work shows that the nearest neighbor method is simplest and fastest. Despite that it
does a reasonable good job for closely non-matching meshes, it is the most inaccurate method.
Radial basis function and weighted residual methods are more sophisticated and have shown
reasonable results in a broad range of applications.
What applies for all aforementioned
methods is that the load or motion transfer is either consistent or conservative and cannot
cover both at the same time. An example can be found by Farhat et al. (1998) where the
nearest neighbor method is ﬁrstly considered as conservative and then as consistent.
An
alternative interpolation method presented by Farhat et al. (1998) employs the ﬁnite element
shape functions. This is a consistent method, however, since the number of Gauss points can
diﬀer for the evaluation of the element stiﬀness matrix and interpolation of ﬂuid loads, this
method is not conservative (See Farhat et al. (1998)).
Another method for handling non-matching meshes, which has gained a lot of popularity,
is the Mortar method introduced by Bernardi et al. (1994). This coupling method is able
to interpolate loads from one mesh to another consistently and conservatively. The method
takes a single shape Lagrange multipliers which glue or link the two sides of the interface. It
is worthwhile to emphasize that this method has Lagrange multipliers only present at one
side of the interface. Even tough the standard Mortar method is desired in terms of imposing
the interface constraints in a consistent and conservative manner, it is not competitive and
applicable for FSI simulations (Kl¨oppel et al., 2011). That is due to the coupling of master
and slave degrees of freedom which is computationally expensive. That lays in the way the
global matrix system is assembled which makes it hard to solve numerically (Kl¨oppel et al.,
2011).
As a solution, the dual Mortar method was presented by Wohlmuth (Wohlmuth,
2001) where the global system is rearranged by means of condensation. Following this, it
was shown that the global system consisted of a diagonal matrix which is signiﬁcantly easier
to solve.
The method collocates multipliers at all interface nodes.
That is, no decision
on master vs. slave nodes has to be made. That eases the computations signiﬁcantly and
makes FSI simulations with the Mortar method more feasible. The work of Kl¨oppel et al.
(2011) applies the dual mortar formulation for both monolithic and partitioned methods.

The results indicated satisfying results with reasonable computational costs. Similarly, (Ehrl

the key idea behind this method is to completely decouple the structure and ﬂuid model,

penalty method based on a penalization of any constrain violation. That is introduced since
all the constrains are removed explicitly.
The penalization term is usually introduced in
the energy equation with a user speciﬁed parameter. In fact, the method is fairly easy and
eﬃcient to implement, however, to obtain as accurate results as for the Lagrange multiplier
you need to increase the parameter to inﬁnity (Popp, 2012). On the other hand, a larger
parameter makes the system more and more ill-conditioned.
2.3
Immersed boundary method
One of the most conventional approaches for simulating FSI problems is based on the Ar-
bitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian approach where the mesh is conformed to follow the motion of
boundaries. This method has been widely used in FSI applications. However, the method
becomes very cumbersome to handle large deﬂections (Sahin and Mohseni, 2009). One alter-
nate approach is namely the immersed boundary method (IBM) where the mesh is based on
a ﬁxed Cartesian coordinate system. A concise review by Mittal and Iaccarino (2006) and by
Sotiropoulos and Yang (2014) outline various approaches of the numerous immersed bound-
ary methods. A general distinction between two fundamental approaches, the continuous and
discrete forcing technique, was clariﬁed. Both methods have several sub-approaches in which
each has its various advantages and disadvantages. In general, the continuous approach is
nothing more than introducing a source term into the continuous governing equations where
in the discrete approach the source term is added not until the equations are discretized.
One major beneﬁt is the fact that the discrete method allows us to control various properties
of the solver e.g. conservation, numerical accuracy and stability. On the other hand, the
continuous approach has the advantage that its formulation does not depend on which spatial
discretization has been employed. The discrete forcing method is also referred to as the sharp
interface method, since the interface can be described in a sharp fashion where the continuous
method smears the mesh over the interfaces.

2.3.1
Continuous forcing Approach
The basic principle behind the continuous forcing approach is that singular forces are smeared
to the grid nodes near the interface by a discrete Dirac delta function. One of the ﬁrst pub-
lished work employing the continuous forcing approach was formulated by Peskin (1972). The
objective was to derive a formulation based on the immersed boundary method to investigate
the ﬂow patterns around heart valves. This was conducted by considering a simple grid which,
consequently, is not suited to conform complex geometry but rather relies on interpolation
schemes i.e. interpolating between the non-coinciding nodes near the interface. Although,
this work can be considered for complex geometry against the payment of accuracy. That
is, the conservation of mass appeared to be violated severely. The work by Peskin (1977)
was improved by tuning the divergence and gradient operators according to the employed
interpolation scheme. The conservation of mass was shown to be improved, however, both
aforementioned methods were oriented to certain boundary types only, namely, elastic sta-
tionary boundaries. A slightly diﬀerent approach was presented by Peskin (2002) where a
smooth piecewise approximation to the Dirac delta function was considered. Following this,
the smooth function, which smears over the grid points in question, has to ensure that the total
forces remain unchanged when they are transformed from the interface to the grid nodes. By
this approach it became tangible to model moving curved grid along with the condition that
this has to be suﬃciently dense with respect to the ﬁxed Cartesian grid. That is, the solid has
to be suﬃciently dense with respect to the ﬂuid. This method is also classiﬁed as the classical
IBM. Fadlun et al. (2000) formulated an immersed boundary method based on the continuous
forcing approach applicable for three-dimensional ﬂows. The solid boundary was treated as a
sharp interface (however, it is still based on the continuous forcing approach, classiﬁed as the
direct forcing method). Contrary to Peskin (1977), this method treats problems with rigid
boundaries. The solver is capable of simulating ﬂow around complex bodies, however, the grid
representation is simple (uniform staggered) as the case for all aforementioned publications
on IBM. Hence, a simple one-dimensional interpolation scheme for the reconstruction of the
solution in the vicinity of the body is employed to account for the discontinuities between the
grid points and nodes of the body. Although, the paper demonstrates accurate results and
the algorithm is relatively fast even for high Reynolds number. However, there might be cases
(depending on the structure) where the uniform grid is not enough to capture the boundary
layer reasonably. That is due to the fact that the method implemented lacks the capability
to eﬃciently cluster the nodes where needed (Fadlun et al., 2000), e.g. ﬁne mesh near the
walls to capture the boundary layer for viscous ﬂow. Gilmanov and Sotiropoulos (2005) im-
proved the interpolation of the reconstruction worked out by Fadlun et al. (2000). In this
work, the scheme is based on a quadratic function leading to more adequate results. Another
improvement aspect with regards to the work of Fadlun et al. (2000) is how the immersed
boundary formulation can be utilized to perform Large-Eddy-Simulation (LES) in order to
capture moderately high Reynolds number ﬂows for complex geometric conﬁgurations. That
was presented by Cristallo and Verzicco (2006). Nonetheless, conducting FSI-LES simula-
tions have been concluded to not be satisfactory as the discontinuity between the grid and
nodes yielded spurious stresses. This resulted in corrupted computations of the turbulent
viscosity (Cristallo and Verzicco, 2006). A slightly modiﬁed approach has achieved signiﬁcant
improvements (Yang and Balaras, 2006), though. In that work, the ﬁelds of pressure and
velocity in proximity to the interfaces between the ﬂuid and solid have been extended such

2.3.2
Discrete forcing approach
As the conservative issue becomes vital, particularly, when considering high turbulent ﬂow
on complex conﬁgurations, it is substantially crucial to meet the conservativity requirement.
Generally, the discrete forcing approach has appeared to solve this problem (Mittal and
Iaccarino, 2006; Sotiropoulos and Yang, 2014).
LeVeque and Li (1994) proposed the immersed interface method for the elliptic equation.
It was later developed to the Stokes equation by LeVeque and Li (1997) and further to
the Navier-Stokes equation by Li and Lai (2001). The basic idea and principle lay on the
continuous forcing approach.
Similarly, singular forces are imposed on grid nodes in the
vicinity of the interface. Contrary to the continuous approach, the forces are transformed to
the grid nodes by a jump condition instead for being smeared by a delta function. That is
also the critical point of this method. Namely, its performance depends strongly on how the
jump condition is formulated. Several jump conditions suited for diﬀerent cases haven been
derived e.g. by Lai and Li (2001); Xu and Wang (2006). Application to FSI can be found in
the work of Xu and Wang (2008).
Another sharp interface method referred as the Hybrid Cartesian-Immersed Boundary method
is suggested by Mohd-Yusof (1997). This method has strong relations to the direct forcing
approach (Fadlun et al., 2000). The idea behind this method is to distribute forces to the grid
points surrounding the interface by appropriate interpolation schemes. That is, the boundary
condition is not imposed exactly at the interface but rather on the nodes closest to the
interface, which prevents modifying the ﬂuid mesh. This approach retains the sharp interface
representation as opposed to the direct forcing approach.
One dimensional schemes have
shown to be ambiguous for complex conﬁguration, the reason being, why Griﬃth et al. (2007)
proposed multidimensional schemes. However, the work was only suited for 2D problems. A
common issue with this approach is the lack of being suited to non-staggered grids. This
was worked out by Gilmanov and Sotiropoulos (2005) where it was additionally shown that
unstructured grid types can enhance the performance signiﬁcantly. Another accounted issue is
associated to the magnitude of Reynolds number. The Hybrid-Cartesian IBM requires a very
ﬁne mesh in proximity to the interface for moderately high Reynolds number (Gilmanov and
Sotiropoulos, 2005). Hence, this method tends to be computationally ineﬃcient for turbulent
ﬂow.
Following this method, (Ge and Sotiropoulos, 2007) modiﬁed the method slightly in order to
describe curvilinear background grid. That allows the grid to become more conforming to
the structure while retaining the power of the immersed boundary method. That becomes
very useful to describe shapes of arbitrarily complex conﬁgurations (Sotiropoulos and Yang,
2014). Borazjani et al. (2008) employed this method for FSI applications.
A very widespread technique to satisfy the conservative issues is by employing the so-called
cut-cell method, in which Clarke et al. (1986) where the ﬁrst to introduce the topic. The

2.4
Discussions and Conclusions
The presented review has described relevant literature and accomplished work concerning
the key elements for the objectives, namely, the coupling between ﬂuid and structure solvers
in time and space, and the boundary method. All the aforementioned subjects have to be
taken into consideration when dealing with FSI problems in general and especially with the
research goal(s) established in Chapter 1. As regards to the FSI coupling in time, there are
two diﬀerent approaches; Monolithic and partitioned. Although the monolithic is more accu-
rate and sensible, there is still a long way to make it competitive with its rival. The approach
is extremely time-consuming and a lot of coding is required as the existing solvers cannot be
reused. In other words, it requires a lot of eﬀorts. On the contrary, the partitioned method
enables us to reuse existing codes and is very fast. Particularly, the loosely coupled method
which is even faster against the payment of stability and accuracy. Although, for compressible
ﬂow the latter method is a very good approximation (Farhat et al., 2006). On the contrary,
it was shown that problems with a density ratio of the order of one, namely strongly coupled
problems, the loosely partitioned method is not suggested. That is, the eﬀect of added mass
must be taken into account either explicitly (e.g. Banks et al. (2013, 2014); Badia et al.
(2008)) or implicitly by introducing sub-iterations within each time step (K¨uttler and Wall,
2008).
Various methods to interpolate variables obtained from the structure and ﬂuid solvers have
been considered. Several approaches have the disadvantage that they must either be non-
conservative or not consistent. For example, the radial basis function and nearest neighbor
methods (de Boer et al., 2007). Other methods associated to the Lagrange multiplier ap-
proach have certain features which might be more appealing for FSI applications. That is,
the interpolation between structure and ﬂuid can be consistent and conservative simultane-
ously. However, the Mortar method formulated by (Bernardi et al., 1994) was too slow for
FSI applications. On the other hand, the dual Mortar method showed a promising feature
(Wohlmuth, 2001). The computational cost was reduced substantially and the interpolation
between the structure and ﬂuid was both consistent and conservative. Basically, one of the
main diﬀerences between the two latter methods is that the former is based on the mas-
ter/slave coupling whereas the latter was no longer based on this coupling method. A novel

approach referred as the Localized Lagrange Multiplier has indicated even better behavior,
however, there is still a way to go; it is useless for cases where the density ratio is relatively

the ﬂuid is like air.
Finally, the immersed boundary method has been presented. The rough distinction within
this method is the discrete and continuous forcing approaches. The continuous forcing method
suﬀers from a severe problem, namely, the conservative issue. That can by no means be com-
pensated when arbitrary motion is incorporated. On the other hand, the discrete forcing
method, particularly, the cut-cell method has been presented in various forms. Overall, some
publications have considered incompressible ﬂow (Meyer et al., 2010a,b; Clarke et al., 1986)
while others have considered compressible ﬂow (Ye et al., 1999; Udaykumar et al., 1996, 1997,

reported to take this into account. Among those methods is the cell-merging and mixing pro-

and ﬂuid meshes are almost never matching along the interface. Hence, appropriate inter-

The interpolation methods applied in this work are consistent but not necessarily conserva-

4.3.2
Interpolation of FV loads to FE faces
The ﬂuid forces are computed on the centroid of each cut-element, and consequently needs
to be distributed to all the adjacent FE nodes. In order to be able to interpolate the ﬂuid

When the local coordinates of the centroid position are known, the shape-function evaluated
at these local coordinates can be exploited to distribute the ﬂuid pressure from the centroid
of the cut-element to the adjacent FE nodes. Although, the main task here is to ﬁnd the
local coordinates associated to the centroid position of the cut-element. This is conducted
in an iterative fashion; a search algorithm. The search algorithm begins with a guess of kt
assuming it to be in the center of deﬁned squared box within the FE. Following this, the local
nodes’ positions of the box can be calculated as the dimensions of the box are deﬁned. In
doing so, all local nodes can be computed back to the physical coordinate system and the
minimum distance between the guessed positions of the box nodes and the centroid node xΓ
is determined. The node which is found to be closest to xΓ becomes now the new guess of kt
(in local coordinates) and a new box is deﬁned centered to the new guess. The iteration is
repeated until the distance becomes zero. In this way the local position of the centroid kΓ is
computed. The iteration process is also visualized in Fig. 4.6.

4.3.3
Temporal coupling
We employ an explicit loosely coupled scheme for the ﬂuid-structure interaction. That is, the
results from one solver is directly imposed as input for the other solver to advance the system
in time. As mentioned in Chapter 2, this can lead to the so-called added mass eﬀect. In fact,
we do not ensure that the coupling in one time step has converged, although, it is a very good
approximation for certain ﬂow conditions like compressible ﬂow (Farhat and Lesoinne, 2000).

violations (Farhat and Lesoinne, 2000). Due to the fact that one of the state-vectors always
lags one time-step behind, the time coupling is only ﬁrst-order accurate O(∆t) (Farhat et al.,

Chapter 5
High-order reduced model
In this chapter, we derive a reduced model coupled to a Non-Linear FEM solver. The pur-
pose is to derive a reduced model capable to provide satisfactory results regardless of the
order of non-linearity present in the structure. We refer to this method as the High-Order
Reduced Model (HORM). The ﬁrst section presents a numerical formulation of the HORM.
Subsequently, the implementation procedure is brieﬂy discussed and the presented case-study
in Chapter 4.4.4 is exploited to verify the numerical model in question. Following this, a cor-
rection method to HORM is introduced with the purpose of increasing the accurac. Finally,
the correction method is tested and compared against the regular HORM.

might not be necessary to employ a Non-Linear model till the small deformations take place.

be updated and a new linearization can be performed around a new reference state.

the loads adequately in the modal space. Particularly, this issue becomes signiﬁcant for low
frequency models, e.g. the considered panel in this example. Fortunately, there have been
reported various correction methods in order to compensate for the errors which might be
introduced when the governing equations are transformed to the eigenspace. Two common
methods, the Static Correction and Modal Truncation Augmentation methods, are frequently
mentioned in literature. In Dickens et al. (1995) it was clearly illustrated that the Modal Trun-
cation Augmentation method (MT) is superior to the Static Correction method for various
reasons. The two most important reasons are that MT does the correction dynamically and
is eﬀective in a broader range of frequencies. Hence, the MT correction serves our objectives
better than the Static Correction Method. Refer to (Dickens et al., 1995) for more details.

Chapter 6
Three-dimensional case-studies
The current chapter presents two three-dimensional FSI examples. The aim of this chapter
is to introduce and assess HORM for large and complicated geometrical conﬁgurations. The
ﬁrst example deals with the buckling phenomena of a thin semi-spherical structure. Buckling
is a complex Non-Linear phenomena, which certainly will illuminate the robustness of HORM.
The second example concerns a FSI simulation of a payload fairing, which geometry is based
on the VEGA rocket. The former case has been presented in Pasquariello et al. (2015a),
whereas the latter case has not been reported in literature to the author’s knowledge.
6.1
Buckling of a three-dimensional thin spherical conﬁgura-
tion
We present in this example a three-dimensional thin semi-spherical structure. The purpose
of this study is to examine the developed model’s ability to treat three-dimensional problems
with complicated structural behavior. That is, Pasquariello et al. (2015a) showed that the
example in question is highly exposed to dynamic buckling, which is a Non-Linear structural
phenomena. Moreover, an elaborated analysis of the concept of buckling was conducted in
Ramm and Wall (2004) and it was demonstrated that buckling is highly sensitive of any kind
of imperfections, resolution and modeling parameters. That also means that tiny variations
in the loads may change the buckling mode of the structure entirely. This becomes even more
sensitive for FSI problems where the ﬂuid loads themselves also are sensitive to the shape of
the deformed body. This issue was also conﬁrmed in the work of Pasquariello et al. (2015a)
where it was stressed out that various structural resolutions implied diﬀerent buckling modes.
The thin structure is hit by a shock-wave at Ma = 1.21 which begins from x = −0.05 m and
propagates throughout the computational domain until it reﬂects back again at the end of
the wall at x = 0.2 m. Details of the setup is sketched in Fig. 6.1 with all geometric details.
The ﬂow is considered inviscid and compressible with the 5rd-order WENO scheme (HLLC
ﬂux) for the ﬂux discretization.

A vital point to reclarify is that the results presented in Figs. 6.9 - 6.11 exhibit sensitivity
issues. For instance, the case with 50 eigenmodes at ϵ = 5 × 10−4 m is apparently displaying
better results compared to the cases with 75 and 100 eigenmodes at various time instances.
Theoretically, this should not happen. However, the number of eigenmodes is also a modeling
parameter which activates the sensitivity of the buckling (Pasquariello et al., 2015a; Ramm
and Wall, 2004).
Evidently, diﬀerent modes contribute diﬀerently to the buckling modes
which may cause ambiguous results in the sense that a certain case happens to give better
predictions than expected. Additionally, the sensitivity issue from the ﬂuid solver is also
present and was discussed earlier (See Chapter 5.3). This ambiguity is dislodged by including
more eigenmodes to the system. In this way the results are generally converged to an overall
expected trend. Although, the issue can still be traced. For example, with ϵ = 5.0 × 10−4 m
the diﬀerences between the cases with 75 and 100 eigenmodes do not indicate a converging
trend.
However, these variations are very small and for the majority of cases irrelevant
but illustrate some focal points regarding dynamic buckling and its sensitivity in numerical
modeling, particularly, for FSI. Having said this, it can be stressed out that by increasing the
number of eigenmodes beyond a converged estimate may not improve the results. The reason
is that other contributing factors, e.g. noise, become stronger, whereas the additional eﬀects
of increasing the number of eigenmodes are suppressed. On the other hand, to enrich HORM
with a reasonable number of eigenmodes is essential to avoid unreasonable and ambiguous
results.

of a structure.

Chapter 7
Determination of an appropriate threshold
One of the biggest question with regards to HORM is how to make a wise decision on the size
of the threshold ϵ. This is by far not straight-forward to determine. Although, in this chapter
we attempt to illuminate some new aspects of the threshold. Speciﬁcally, the ﬁrst section
carries on an investigation of the threshold’s sensitivity with regards to the amount of non-
linearity present in the system. Thus, we have decided to perform a number of simulations for
various stiﬀness properties which implicitly allow us to control the order of the Non-Linear
terms. That is, a smaller magnitude of the Young’s modulus implies larger deﬂections which
consequently results in a higher contribution of the Non-Linear terms. Also, we present few
cases with various thicknesses to understand which impacts these impose on the threshold,
and relate it to the latter study. For brevity, we have only considered the closed channel case
presented in Chapter 5.5.
Following this, we will brieﬂy present one simulation of the semi-sphere (See Chapter 6.1)
with considerably higher loads than presented previously. Likewise, we use this outcome to
seek an improved understanding of the threshold’s behavior in terms of various load cases.
Finally, we establish a thumb-rule on how to specify an appropriate threshold which is tried
on a new FSI problem.

Recommendations for future usage
In this paragraph we will brieﬂy point out few recommendations on how HORM can be
employed most eﬃciently. The overall objective and motivation were to develop a method
which aim was to make parametric FSI studies more feasible. Therefore, if the conﬁdence of
which threshold to consider is absent, conducting a couple of simulations for various threshold,
i.e.
a convergence study for ϵ, with the starting point being what was concluded in last
paragraph, should not be a hurdle. Additionally, all features which cause the ﬂowﬁeld to be
complex and time-consuming could principally be switched oﬀwhen the threshold size is being
examined, since it has already been concluded that even substantial variations of the loads
do not imply evident changes in the threshold. Therefore, once the threshold is determined
for a speciﬁc case, it can safely be kept constant while considering diﬀerent ﬂow properties
or making changes of the structural parameters. Notice that we also did dramatic variations,
which rarely would occur for realistic applications, but merely to expose the threshold’s
sensitivity for extreme situations.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The research objective and scope of this work were to develop and couple a reduced order
model (ROM) based on eigenmodes to a ﬂuid solver (INCA) based on the immersed boundary
method. The general motivation behind the objective was to be capable of performing para-
metric studies of time-resolved Fluid-Structure interaction (FSI) problems. A FSI approach
was already available in INCA, however, it was extremely ineﬃcient. The structural solver
was based on a direct Non-Linear/Linear Finite Element method (FEM). Thus, INCA-FSI
simulations for complicated problems (i.e. turbulent ﬂow, high speed ﬂow, complex conﬁgura-
tion etc.) cannot be aﬀorded to be conducted numerous times since both parts of the coupling
algorithm become extremely ineﬃcient. Hence, diminishing the CPU load of one part of the
coupling algorithm becomes essential and can be accomplished by e.g. employing a ROM for
the structural part. Although, it is a common fact that ROM cannot resolve the structural
domain when the strains become large and therefore the research goal was to couple ROM to
a Non-Linear FEM solver such that the latter limitation is prevented.
The ﬁrst part of the work dealt with presenting the mathematical and numerical methodolo-
gies of the structural and ﬂuid solvers followed by procedures to couple these computational
domains. Subsequently, an implementation of a basic ROM was suggested and coupled to
INCA. This was followed by several validations of the INCA-ROM coupling. Two well re-
ported FSI examples (linear problems) were introduced and validated with prior work and
the old FSI method in INCA. The INCA-ROM was proven to perform accurately for linear
problems. Furthermore, the performance of INCA-ROM was shown to be signiﬁcantly supe-
rior to the regular INCA-FSI method. Afterwards, a new FSI problem was proposed and the
INCA-ROM was deﬁcient since the non-linearity of the structure became signiﬁcant.
Therefore, a novel approach of the ROM was proposed aiming to capture the structural re-
sponse regardless of the magnitude of strains. That is, a high-order reduced model (HORM)
was derived and coupled to INCA and the Non-Linear FEM solver. The suggested model was
validated against the regular INCA-FSI method and prior results when applicable for various
Non-Linear FSI problems. The INCA-HORM appeared robust and performed accurately.
The suggested HORM was based on a user deﬁned threshold and therefore all Non-Linear
case-studies were followed by an examination of various thresholds. Furthermore, a massively

threshold was illustrated to be crucial in many circumstances, since lower thresholds (com-

Once the conﬁdence of the suggested method stepped in, numerous analyses were conducted
to enhance the understandings of the threshold’s behavior and how a wise estimation could
be made. It was demonstrated that the threshold is highly insensitive to the magnitude of
loads and non-linearities. In other words, the threshold is independent on the amount of de-
ﬂections present in the structure. However, it was conﬁrmed that the threshold is reliant on
the structure’s dimension. Particularly, the characteristic length was demonstrated to have
an inﬂuence on the threshold’s size. Linear scaling of the threshold and the characteristic
length were assumed and demonstrated reasonable estimations. Expressed in diﬀerent terms,
a preliminary exploration showed that the threshold could be taken between 0.25% and 0.63%
of the characteristic length. Although, the thumb-rule should be considered wisely since more
validations are necessary. Especially, several three-dimensional test cases are strongly encour-
aged for future recommendations. This is believed to contribute with strong clariﬁcations of
the threshold’s size in addition to those derived in this work.
Having said this, the research objective has been accomplished to a large extent. As the over-
all goal was to develop a FSI model making it more feasible to perform parametric studies, the
cost of conducting a couple of simulations to determine the threshold’s size is insigniﬁcant.
Additionally, since it has been demonstrated that the magnitude of loads do not have an
evident relation to the threshold, it is even possible to consider simple ﬂuid dynamic set-up
when the threshold is to be decided, and then return to a realistic case. With this in mind,
the thumb rule established in this work is believed to provide reasonable estimations.
