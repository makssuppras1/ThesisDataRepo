Abstract 
In this study the implementation of sustainable room acoustic solutions in building design 
of regular character is investigated. By regular character is meant teaching facilities, office 
environments, atriums and similar with no special acoustic demands as e.g. concert halls. 
Through collaboration with Henning Larsen Architects (HLA) this is investigated by the 
involvement in the design phase of two competition projects of relevant and different 
character: the Liget Budapest, a 48.855 m2 museum project and DTU Campus, a 6.900 m2 
student housing project.  
Recent research points towards the fact that a bad acoustic indoor climate can have 
big consequences for e.g. both health and learning ability. Nevertheless acoustics are often 
prioritised very low in the budgeting of new building projects of regular character. In the 
worst-cases scenario it can be difficult to even meet the requirements due to lack of 
acoustic consideration. As a result of this an-aesthetically, distracting and unsustainable 
solutions are often seen implemented in order to fulfil the requirements and time schedule. 
The present hypothesis is that by involving acoustic consultants as early as possible in a 
building project, more satisfying results are achieved for all parts involved. At the same 
time it is the present hypothesis that this is not the routine to involve acoustic 
consultants in the design phase of regular building projects. Therefore it is the aim this 
study to investigate how the role of an in-house acoustic advisor at HLA can affect the 
development of such embodied, sustainable and thereby innovative interior design 
solution. The investigation is conducted by interviews, project involvement and testing of 
a promising new fabric. The fabric is tested in a prototype test model designed specifically 
to fit the geometry of a ceiling unit in the Liget Budapest design proposal. It is 
concluded, in accordance with the hypothesis, that room acoustics is prioritized very low 
in the design phase and that especially the architects is in need of tools to handle acoustic 
related challenges. The tested fabric shows decent results and it is suggested that the 
approach of working with custom designing multifunctional absorbers, in the best case, 
may encourage architects to incorporate acoustics into the design phase as a creative and 
sustainable design parameter.

Chapter 1 – Introduction 
The need for thorough acoustic considerations is not only observed in the building 
industry. This problem appears particularly critical with regards to room acoustic 
design and restoration of e.g. bars and restaurants, teaching facilities, canteens etc. 
of already erected buildings. Poor initial acoustic design can lead to expensive, non-
sustainable ad hoc solutions or to the constant discomfort of the inhabitants in the 
building. Sustainable and innovative implementation of room acoustic solutions in 
building design is of primary interest in this study. The main part of the study 
treats my involvement with HLA in relation to the two projects; the Liget 
Budapest and DTU Campus. Also of particular interest are the results derived 
through interviews, social interaction and various daily tasks at HLA; all of which 
have been part of forming a bigger picture of the workflow and routines of an 
architectural company with an international format. The building program for the 
Liget Budapest and DTU Campus exemplifies an increasing demand for green 
solutions, and the potential of sustainable material-use incorporated into 
comprehensive holistic designs. The sustainable approach is therefore of very high 
priority within the profile of HLA. The study seeks to explore the question on how 
room acoustics can be considered in the different aspects of a sustainable approach. 
Although research [1]–[3] points towards the fact that poor room acoustics 
affects both health and learning ability, the issue seems to be of very low priority 
in modern building design. The low financial priority of sufficient room acoustic 
interior design means that what is often done to comply with building regulations 
are unsustainable cheap and un-aesthetic solutions such as visually dominant wall 
absorbers [4]. My approach to study this issue is to carry out research within the 
design phase of the two projects mentioned above. Furthermore, a prototype design 
is tested to potentially point towards a new direction of multifunctional absorber 
design. In terms of a sustainable approach to room acoustics HLA is interested in 
examining the possibilities of peeling off as many material layers of the interior 
room design as possible and instead consider factors like structural geometry and 
absorbing interior elements to treat the acoustics.  
It is found that considering acoustics as a design parameter by involving 
guidance as early as possible in a project is essential in order to fulfil the increasing 
demand for innovative and sustainable solutions. Furthermore the test design 
investigated in this study contain interesting perspectives in terms of awareness of 
the significance of considering acoustics an actual design parameter in the design 
phase.

1.1  Thesis Overview 
This thesis is divided into two parts. Part I is mainly treating the involvement with 
HLA and is not of a particularly technical character. Part I contains a description 
of the assigned HLA projects as well as a description of tasks related to each of 
these projects. From the involvement with the two projects a series of follow-up

studying the aspects presented in Part I.   
1.2  Problem Statement 
In cooperation with Henning Larsen Architects (HLA) it is in the interest of the 
student to observe and investigate how and to what extend room acoustic 
considerations are taken into account in the early stages of designing a building. 
The student is to be assigned to one or more specific in-house competition projects 
in the first half of the thesis timeframe and from this/these projects collect 
information and data. In the second half of the thesis timeframe this data will be 
analyzed. The goal is to map general patterns and phases in which acoustics are 
being treated and from this knowledge suggest optimizations regarding both 
requirements and design. How can acoustic demands shape the design and building 
phases so that it is not the other way around? –Both from an aesthetical, technical 
and sustainable point of view.   
The technical part of the report will focus on one or more details of relevance 
from the project involvement and should as well point towards possible further 
study. The technical part will include material testing at the facilities at DTU.

1.4  Hypothesis  
The hypothesis of this study is that the actual involvement of the acoustic engineer 
in today’s projects of regular character is insufficient in order to obtain sustainable 
and innovative solutions. Further it is expected that the study will show that the 
involvement of external acoustic consultants in general is prioritized only out of the 
need to meet building regulation requirements rather that out of an architectural 
ambition to ensure the acoustic qualities of buildings aesthetically and 
sustainability-wise. That is to say that the treatment of the acoustics and not only 
room acoustics is not considered an actual and defining design parameter in the 
early stages of a building project in the same way that e.g. daylight is. In the 
feasibility study for this project [4] it is seen that in only one of the three projects 
investigated, SDU Kolding, the room acoustics were considered beyond the level of 
verification in the design phase due to the triangular ceiling panel design 
introduced by HLA. For this reason it is expected that the study will show that the 
field of acoustics remains a peripheral and often underdefined design parameter (in 
much contemporary architecture) compared to visual design parameters.

2.6  Clarity 
Clarity describes the degree to which every detail of the performance can be 
perceived as opposed to everything being blurred together by later arriving, 
reverberant sound components. Thus, clarity is to a large extent a property 
complementary to reverberance [9]. Clarity indicates the level difference [dB] 
between the early and late arriving signal energy. This is expressed by the index of 
either C80 or C50 depending on whether the boundary between early and late 
arrivals is placed 80 ms or 50 ms after the direct sound. Generally C80 is used for 
music performances and C50 recommended for speech [6]. For this reason only C50 is 
considered in this study. The higher the ratio, the more early sound dominates and

Chapter 3 – The Acoustic Consultant 
In order to reference the involvement of an acoustic consultant this chapter 
presents the suggested ideal involvement of such according to ACG [9]. 
3.1  The (ideal) Role of the Acoustic Consultant 
In Denmark, acoustic demands for buildings including room acoustics have been 
defined either by law or by the general recommendations as listed below: 
 
 
Building legislation BR10 [13] covers the indoor acoustic climate in housing 
and schools. 
 
“Akustik I Arbejdsrum” [14] from the Danish Working Environment Service 
covers offices, workshops and industry. 
 
Local community regulations and manuals from The Environmental 
Agency [15] covers mainly noise pollution to neighbours and to the 
environment. 
 
To fulfil these laws and recommendations, acoustic consultants must have an on-
going dialogue with the client in order to plan, solve and supervise the given 
project. As suggested by ACG [9] ideally the acoustic consultant should have a 
word in the following:  
 
1. Setup of acoustic specifications. 
2. Choice of building site. 
3. Evaluations of external noise sources. 
4. Planning of allocation in the building. 
5. Design of sound isolating constructions. 
6. Handling of noise from technical installations. 
7. Choice of room sizes and shape. 
8. Choice/design of sound absorbing materials and specific acoustic materials 
as e.g. reflectors. 
9. Placing of these sound absorbing and reflective materials in the room. 
10. Design of loudspeaker systems. 
11. Supervision of the contractor.  
12. Documenting measurements. 
13. Supervision of the client in proper use and maintenance of his new 
building. 
 
==PAGE:27==

14
Of the above-mentioned involvement in particular pt. 1, 4, 7, 8 and 9 have been 
treated in regards to the Liget Budapest and DTU Campus. It is suggested [9] that 
the relationship between the parties involved in the acoustic design of the building 
can be illustrated as shown in Figure 7 [9].  
 
Figure 7 – Suggested ideal involvement of the acoustic consultant in the design of a building

Chapter 4 - Sustainability 
All work performed with and for HLA in regards to this study has been done 
within the company’s in-house sustainability department. The sustainable approach 
of this department has been very defining for the results of the involvement on the 
two projects and is the very basis for the design of the test model presented in Part 
II.  For this reason the sustainable approach of HLA is introduced in the following.  
4.1  HLA – Sustainable Approach 
With the approach that “the most sustainable energy is the energy saved” and 
“Knowledge and evidence-based design connects art, innovation and science 
creating new opportunities for building processes and planning” [16] the following 
three-layer hierarchy has been formulated: 
 
 
Reduction: Energy consumption is reduced by optimising the design, 
functional configuration and overall technical systems of the building. 
 
 
Optimization: Energy consumption is further reduced by incorporating 
components, intelligent control and energy-reducing materials supporting 
the objective. 
 
 
Production: By means of local, building-integrated energy production 
and additional energy infrastructure, a surplus of energy can be achieved.  
 
HLA’s objective of sustainability is to create value ranging from social values to 
location-determined, infrastructural, demographic and climatic values. This holistic 
approach is intended to create increased value through sustainability. Increased 
value is achieved when a majority of objectives interact and the selected tools 
support each other. In this way, qualitative and quantitative objectives will 
interact [16].

and incapable of applying simple tools regarding the implementation of acoustics in 
their work. Regarding the role of an in-house acoustic consultant it is found that 
the day-to-day guidance is a really strong tool.

Chapter 8 - Testing  
In accordance with DE/EN ISO 354:2003 [5] the Reverberation Chamber 
Measurement  has been performed in order to evaluate the acoustic performance of 
the SEFAR fabric.  
When a sound operates in an enclosed space, the level to which reverberant 
sound builds up, and the subsequent decay of reverberant sound when the source is 
stopped, are governed by the sound absorbing characteristics of the boundary 
surfaces, the air filling the space, and the objects within the space [5]. The principle 
that lies behind the reverberation chamber method is to compare the reverberation 
time of the empty reverberation chamber with the reverberation time of the (same) 
reverberation chamber with the test specimen installed. The method is primarily 
used to determine either the absorption area- or the absorption coefficients of an 
absorptive test specimen. In the tests performed for this thesis it has been of 
interest to consider the absorption area since, due to the complex geometry of the 
set-up, it has been difficult to predict the initial absorption area which is needed in 
order to calculate the absorption coefficients.  
 The reverberation chamber method is a standardized method, [5], which 
implies that clear guidelines, requirements and limits must be followed in order to 
obtain valid results. In the following, relevant requirements and guidelines are 
described. The reverberation chamber method is based on the assumption of a 
totally diffuse sound field within the chamber. In reality it is difficult to achieve a 
totally diffuse sound field in a testing chamber but the standard suggests that the 
sound field of the room is sufficiently diffuse when the acoustic absorption of the 
space no longer increases as a result of installing more acoustic diffusers in the 
chamber. In these conditions measurements are acceptable. 
The size of the test specimen depends on its physical properties but also the 
size of the reverberation chamber. In the standard it is required that the area of 
the test specimen Afabric has to be between 10 and 12 m2 for reverberation chambers 
with the volume V of 150≥V≤200 m3. If V exceeds 200 m3 S has to be increased by 
a factor of (V/200m3)2/3. These rules apply when the specimen is installed as a 
plane as e.g. the type IA-80-CL tests performed by MÜLLER-BBM. See Appendix 
D. The atmospheric test conditions of the chamber should be constant during all 
tests and the following recommendations apply: 
 
 
Relative humidity (RH) should be 30%<RH<90% 
 
The temperature should be above 15°C

8.1.3 
Uncertainties & Design Upgrades  
A physical construction detail that might have had an effect on the test results is 
the way in which the internal layers (not the top layer which was fastened with 
tape) in the metal frame have been in contact with the frame itself. Due to the fact 
that the internal layers had to be removable it was decided to let them rest on the 
frame and not fixate them which was found to be a rather complicated task. It is 
unknown whether this setup has allowed for the layers to freely vibrate and 
thereby act differently than in a design with fixed layers. As with the layers, the 
actual frame itself is not fixed to the upper plywood unit but resting on four 
wooden blocks as shown in Figure 22, right. These wooden blocks, however, is 
mounted with screws. Due to the weight of the metal frame this system is not 
considered to represent as big an uncertainty in regards to vibrations as the lighter 
inner layers.   
The mounting of the fabric is done by hand as precise as possible. All fabric is 
suspended as tight and even as possible and fixed to the frames with staples. With 
this method it has been difficult to verify and alter the mounting once performed 
and the deviation in the suspension of the fabrics might have resulted in an 
internal variation of the performance among the different pieces of fabric. In an 
upgraded design it should be considered whether to cut all side- and bottom-
covering fabric in one piece and maybe even use a drum tuning device or similar to 
secure even suspension. 
The complexity of the truncated pyramid shape made it difficult to reach a 
high accuracy of the angles when welding the physical frame. For this reason the 
metal frame is slightly lopsided, which may or may not have had an effect on the 
test results. For potential future test of similar type the frame is suggested done by 
professionals in order to minimize this uncertainty.

Chapter 9 - Discussion & Conclusion on Testing 
9.1  Discussion 
In general the reverberation chamber measurements show a noticeable difference 
regarding T between the empty condition (Test 1) and the condition with even the 
least fabric tested (Test 2) as seen in clearly in Figure 32. But what is essential to 
consider is the efficiency of the different layer designs being AT in relation to Afabric. 
This is of particular interest in terms of cost. This relationship is shown in the 
right column of Table 6. The relation shows that the highest efficiency is obtained 
in test 2 and furthermore the lowest in Test 6. When considering AT in the 
comparison of Test 2 and Test 6 in Figure 33 a significant difference is seen above 
315 Hz illustrated by the standard deviation but when considering Taverage for Test 2 
and Test 6 only a difference of 0,2 s is seen. The relatively large standard 
deviations in the low frequency regions compared to the high frequency regions, as 
shown in Figure 33, is what could be expected due to the low modal density of the 
reverberation chamber.  
 With regards to the lighting properties of Test 2 and Test 6 it is of importance 
to notice that these results are very different indicated by the dotted red 2% 
daylight factor line in Figure 34 and Figure 35 in where it is not even present in 
the simulation. This will, as mentioned, not be discussed further but including this 
aspect, the test design for Test 2 must be considered the most beneficial for the 
ceiling design of the Ludwig Museum. From a more subjective and aesthetical point 
of view the Test 2 design does however not include the “top” layer fabric which will 
change the look of the absorber quite significantly from the appearance suggested 
in Figure 16.  
What is important to comment on is the future perspectives of designing such 
custom shaped absorbers to fit the vision and concept of the given room and 
furthermore also accommodate sustainable demands. First, it seems, based on only 
this design and the related testing, that there is a potential in considering such 
units as handling both light and acoustics. The approach of a multifunctional 
absorber design could ideally also lead to a reduced use of materials. Unfortunately 
further investigation on development and detailing has not been done in this study 
but is suggested and described in section 9.3. Aesthetically there is no doubt that 
the idea of working with custom shaped absorbers, as the one designed for this 
study, allows for a greater amount of creativity in the work of the architect as also 
explained by RM. And this is important. By providing architects with such options 
hopefully the field of room acoustics is made more attractive and tangible and 
thereby considered an actual sustainable design parameter in the design phase.

9.2  Conclusion – Testing 
The test design for Test 2 is overall found to be the most beneficial design when 
considering acoustic performance, lighting performance and cost. The designs tested 
in this study is just one many proposals on the way towards an increased 
awareness on acoustics as a design parameter. Yet the approach of designing the 
acoustic interior rather than rely on standard dimensioned, prefabricated products 
seems to contain some interesting perspectives. This being said, the financial 
aspects and general awareness in this approach is what makes it difficult as 
discussed in Chapter 6.

Chapter 10 - Summation of Part I & Part II 
It is found in accordance with the hypothesis that in general acoustics are not 
considered sufficiently in the design phase in order to reach sustainable solutions. 
Based on the interviews it seems that the approach of considering acoustics an 
actual design parameter in the design phase appears both obvious and attractive to 
the architects at HLA for which reason in-house guidance on acoustics in the design 
phase may contain great perspectives. From the two projects investigated it is 
found that this guidance in particular seems efficient when being brief and regular 
on a day-day basis.  
The testing of the custom shaped absorber may have pointed towards a more 
creative way of dealing with room acoustic challenges in complex shaped buildings, 
which hopefully, if applied, can encourage architects to treat and consider acoustics 
in the design phase. In order to increase the awareness of acoustics in projects of 
regular character it is, however, essential that the field is prioritized as early as in 
the building programs for such projects. If not, as seen now, both timeframe and 
budgets does not allow for acoustics to be considered thoroughly in the design 
phase. 
9.3  Further Development & Investigation 
Of particular interest for future study is the interaction between acoustics and 
daylight as very briefly touched upon in this study. It seems there is a potential for 
integrating acoustics as a non-visible space-shaping parameter that, in interaction 
with light could be seen in the context of defining spaces within larger rooms 
without the use of physical dividers. In the extension of this approach it is of great 
interest to investigate to which degree this can control local sound pressure levels, 
clarity and reverberation time within the room and in particular to study how 
these parameters affects the users.

Appendix A - Problem Statement Feasibility Study 
Investigation on how to implement acoustics in the early design phase of 
a building  
In cooperation with Henning Larsen Architects (HLA) it is in the interest of 
the student to observe and investigate how and at to which degree acoustic 
considerations are taken into account in the early design stages: through interviews 
with both designers and architects at HLA it is the goal to first map the general 
current processes and from this knowledge suggest optimizations. How can acoustic 
demands shape the design process so that the opposite is avoided? –Both from an 
aesthetical and technical point of view. The field of study is, for comparison, three 
existing HLA projects with the focus being on teaching- and office-environments 
representing the majority of work at HLA.   
The report will serve as a feasibility study for the subsequent Mst. Thesis on 
same topic.  The student will be working within the company for the three weeks.

the given project they are more or less involved but almost always from at least the 
proposal phase in extra demanding projects. “In projects of more regular character 
our experience is that our involvement is not always prioritized in the proposal 
phase which is really unfortunate because really often this lack of guidance just add 
more and complex work later in the project which could have been avoided”. 
Q4.2+Q5.2: ACG emphasizes that an early involvement in a project always 
enables GM to provide a better consultant work. –The earlier the better. “One of 
many problems with being involved late in the process is that the architect may 
have already sold the sketch to the contractor which makes it very difficult to alter 
the design drastically if needed. It is almost impossible to then, after the sketch is 
verified, tell the contractor that the acoustic conditions have not really been 
considered properly which then again means that drastic changes have to be made”. 
ACG underlines, for the reasons just mentioned, that the acoustics must be 
considered in the design phase and that a good architect is an architect who knows 
his or hers own limitations and thereby knows when to seek technical advice. “All 
in all the most satisfied architects I have worked with were the ones that have had 
us involved throughout the project”.  
Q6.2: ACG states that GM spends a lot of time discussing the reliability of 
simulation tools. “The outputs look great and impressive but you always need your 
common sense and experience to question the results and simulations. It is very 
dangerous to blindly rely on just the theory and hence the simulations. At the 
==PAGE:87==
 
 
 
74
same time you cannot take a too technical approach in your guidance”. ACG 
suggests that the, often seen, very technical approach may very well be connected 
to the structure of the teaching at the universities. In real life it is an interaction 
between many different parameters, (technical) acoustics being only one of them.  
Q7.2: Despite its atypical character and requirements Skuespilhuset in 
Copenhagen serves as a great example. “GM worked closely together with the main 
architect from the very beginning being the program phase. Technically, many 
factors made the work complex and very time consuming but due to mutual trust 
and understanding between us and them a very satisfying result was reached in the 
end”.  
Q8.2: “To reach a goal - being a good solution to a project - the consultants 
must, through close dialogue with, among others the architect(s), reach the 
optimal, not to be called compromise, solution”. To reach such solutions the 
engineers, not only the acoustic consultants, must master many other competences 
than just technical theory, AG suggests. In terms of communicating the work of 
the acoustic consultants to whoever may be the receiver AG emphasizes that GM 
in general does not present their work in long reports with graphs and technical 
data unless it is necessary and/or required. No one from “outside”, whichever 
specific technical fields are considered, are served with results which they have to 
almost study in order to understand. “It is very important to not alienate your field 
of work to e.g. the architects!” ACG briefly mentions how he 20 years ago at DTU 
tried to make the students from DTU and the Danish School of Architecture work 
together in a course to shred light upon this exact challenge. ACG states that it 
does not have to be that comprehensive to involve the external acousticians: “We 
at GM wonder why architects don’t just call us to arrange a brief meeting or two. 
Often our notes done on half a page of paper from a brief meeting can mean a big 
difference for the architect’s overview of what to consider in order to avoid 
fundamental mistakes in terms of the acoustic design. I understand that time is of 
the essence in the design phase but I am not sure that they are aware of how big a 
difference just a very little guidance may mean. It is in the interest of GM to 
secure best initial conditions to work with regarding e.g. placement and orientation 
of the building as well as the composition of the structural design”.  
In regards to how an acoustic consultant would fit into an office the size of 
HLA AG explains that it is a rather difficult task since many demands must be 
fulfilled in order to provide the technical guidance that is needed: one must be 
creative, concise but clear, experienced, fast and have a good understanding of both 
the limitations and opportunities within the multidisciplinary span of engineering 
and architectural design. “These people are rare in Denmark” ACG adds. “And 
what is important to add is that the service, that only a few specialized companies, 
GM being one of them, are capable of providing is based on many years of 
==PAGE:88==
 
 
 
75
experience from working with a lot of different clients and projects. This may be 
hard to compete with compared to an in-house acoustic consultant that may have 
only seen the workflow of that one company. On the other hand, having an in-
house acoustic consultant that knows the company’s ways of assessing a project 
may very well also be an advantage. If the in-house consultant’s job is to provide 
guidance in the design phase I can see that it could make sense to have such a 
person assigned to the team of engineers though I doubt that one in-house acoustic 
consultant will be able to cover all needs and all types of requirements”.   
ACG is of the impression that people in general have become more aware of 
acoustic conditions as a very important factor and that this awareness has led to a 
more open dialogue. “In the old days when Henning Larsen and Jørn Utzon were 
young I think there was a tendency to consider architecture as an exalted field of 
work where, nowadays, architecture has become much more accessible”. ACG 
mentions Japan as an example where a clear line is not necessarily seen between 
architects and engineers within the big contractor companies. “I’ve visited some big 
companies in Japan and often they have both big architect offices and special 
engineering consultants placed in the same building. This is not seen in Europe. In 
the States it is very often seen that the people treating acoustics in building 
projects have a background as an architect or maybe even a musician. This is not 
seen in Europe. Especially in Germany and UK where the opposite is more likely: 
many acoustic consultants have a hardcore technical background maybe even as a 
physicist. Both the American end the European, a little generalized, version may 
have its drawbacks and benefits”. ACG exemplifies one of the drawbacks by 
mentioning the Birmingham Concert Hall where big coupled rooms, which can be 
opened and closed, are applied on the recommendation of American consultants 
without sufficient technical background. “In practice the measured difference 
between the open and closed, very expensive, coupled rooms is negligible” ACG 
says.  
COWI – Carsten Lemvigh Fogh (CLF) & Ditte Kleener (DK) 
An interview was arranged with two acoustic consultants from the engineering 
company COWI. COWI has provided external consultant work on HLA projects on 
several occasions. The interview was conducted with DK and CLF both from 
COWI’s Danish based department Acoustics and Noise. 
 
Q1.2 + Q2.2: DK explains that COWI is capable of handling all tasks as e.g. 
housing, teaching facilities, schools, institutions, renovation, noise- and vibrations 
dampening, concert halls, big- and small offices, electro acoustics, installation noise, 
noise from surroundings and room- and building acoustics. With regards to the 
electro acoustic consultant work, this is usually done in collaboration with their 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
 
76
Norwegian colleagues. “Within the COWI group we have 35 acoustic consultants all 
with an engineering background. Of these 8 are placed in Lyngby, Denmark. Others 
are placed in Odense, Århus and Aalborg. Furthermore a rather big group of 25 
acousticians are placed in Norway” CLF explains. “As explained the group in 
Norway has specialized in treating electro acoustics including AV-systems, stage 
techniques and similar mainly concerning rooms of a rather special character”. As a 
reference to projects of special character DK and CLF refers Århus Musikhus and 
Odense Musikteater.  
Q3.2: Regarding the different phases of a building project CLF explains that 
COWI sometimes in the proposal and design phase contributes with inputs in 
terms of brief verification related to cost. “On the basis of plan drawings we are, 
based on our experience, capable of providing basic guidance such as room 
placement, amount of absorption and similar points of particular acoustic focus” 
DK adds. CLF says that COWI in their daily work is very aided by the building 
regulations in where specifications for almost all room types are specified and adds 
that “...where problems can sometimes occur is when the contractors neglect the 
acoustic treatment in the budget”. “Another critical point of interest which, in the 
worst case, can lead to irreversible mistakes is the actual execution of the work” 
CLF explains. “If for instance the foundation is wrongly casted due to lack of 
experience… This is something that is impossible to correct. For the same reason 
we, if the budget allows it, try to do as much supervision in the execution phase as 
possible. -Especially on projects of special character as e.g. a concert hall. The 
supervision is normally not prioritized so much in regular building projects due to a 
limited budget”. In continuation of this DK explains about a tick-off-schedule 
developed by COWI to, along with the various phases, keep all involved parts 
updated on their respective responsibility and the initial agreements. DK 
emphasizes that since 2012 the placement of responsibility has been very clearly 
defined in the Building and Planning [30] and that today it is the consulting 
engineers who have 100% responsibility for the acoustics and adds “…up until 2012 
there was somewhat of a blurry interpretation of who were actually responsible for 
the different tasks in a building project. Without really being defined anywhere the 
responsibility tended to be distributed somewhat around 50/50 among the 
engineers and architects involved. This really raises the risk of critical 
misunderstandings that then again potentially leads to problems that in the end 
have to be solved under a difficult time pressure. This is of course not in anybody’s 
interest”.  
Q4.2: DK: “Our recent experience with thorough guidance in the project phase 
and even before the proposal phase is very good. This experience regards Pakhuset 
at Langelinje, Copenhagen [31]. By this early involvement, being a dialogue but 
also the ongoing verifications which is highly prioritized by us, the risk of mistakes 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
 
77
is minimized which of course all parts benefits from. Unfortunately the budget 
rarely allows for such early involvement in these normal types of buildings”  
Q6.2: When asked about the software used at COWI to treat the acoustics DK 
explains that in general a lot of the initial guidance is done without any software 
and just based on their experience. “…but as soon as the tasks become more 
complicated as e.g. big atriums or similar, then we use software as Odeon, Bastian 
[32], Insul [33], Cadna [34], Soundplan [35], Catt [36] etc.. When asked about the 
reliability of these programs DK suggests that it can be difficult precisely to 
determine when the outputs are reliable and when they are not since there is 
almost an endless amount of parameters to adjust. “What I normally do when 
going through these outputs is to question them with my experience and theoretical 
know-how and then try to consider whether the results seem reasonable. These 
tools are in particular very useful to initiate a dialogue with the client by clearly 
showing visual and different scenarios. Sometimes we have even simulated 
before/after conditions as audio files which, can then be played back. This is 
extremely efficient. That being said I think that the uncertainty of the actual 
physical construction work in the project phase is just as big. We have had 
examples of contractors contacting us with the assumption that gypsum boards and 
mineral wool would work equally in terms of acoustics”. CLF adds that another 
unknown factor is the data provided from the company behind the given product. 
“IF sufficient data is provided with a product one must always question this since 
the tendency from the companies is to always make their own products a little 
more fantastic than they actually are. For this reason we benefit quite a lot from 
our corporation with DTU and the students who do a lot of material testing”.  
Q7.2: DK answers “One example of very good work I believe is definitely 
worth pointing out is, as mentioned, COWI’s involvement with the almost finished 
project named Pakhuset [31] at Langelinje, Copenhagen. In this project there has 
been room in the budget to incorporate acoustic considerations from the very 
beginning of our involvement being the program phase. In this project several 
factors such as very thin plan separations and a huge window area constituted big 
challenges in terms of acoustics. The thin plan separations made it impossible to 
integrate acoustic treatment in the ceiling which we would normally do and the big 
window areas both took away potential wall area for panels and furthermore 
exposed the users even more to the low frequent noise from nearby cruise ships. My 
point is that our early involvement allowed for alternative yet acceptable solutions 
to be developed which involved quite a lot of perforated paneling”.   
Q8.2: DK says that fortunately an increased focus, on what she refers to as 
good acoustics, is seen in the recent years within the building sector and adds “In 
general I think we have come a long way over the last years. In the building 
regulations good guidelines are provided on how to set up project-specific 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
 
78
recommendations already in the program phase of a building project. Furthermore 
specific design values are suggested and, as mentioned, in general fields as both fire 
and acoustic have been given a lot more attention over the last years. I don’t see 
any urgent need for updating this procedure”.  
When asked about the relationship between architects and engineers CLF is of the 
impression that the dialogue is getting better. “…what there often seems to be 
confusion about is the budgeting. Architects have very creative visions but 
sometimes lack from actual knowledge designing with actual knowledge”.
