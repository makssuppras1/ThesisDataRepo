Summary (English) 
 
 
 
The purpose of this study is to evaluate the concept Virtual Design and Construction (VDC) by 
observational study of project cases with involvement of VDC and by interviews with people in 
the architectural, engineering and construction (AEC) industry, who are directly involved in 
construction projects with virtual designing.  
The methodology used for analysis empirical data is chosen as Leavitt’s organisational diamond 
model, which is used as an analytical structure for clearly sorting the gathered information for 
evaluation.  
Results showed the importance of early involvment of relevant project participants to brainstorm 
a design for a construction project. Competences for understanding new working methods 
assisted by modern technology showed to be very important to develop among participants. The 
most important finding included that interfaces have proved to be essential for implementation 
of VDC. With Leavitt’s model as analytic tool, interfaces were argued to be included in almost every 
aspect of a construction project. Interfacing platforms are changed with earlier involvement of 
project participants and typically task definitions are changed when easy and tangible tech-
solutions are ready to use.  
In the conclusion, the concept of VDC was found to definitely influence the processes and tasks in 
MT Højgaard, where focus has been on earlier involvement and interference with design 
suggestions instead of receiving a defined project from the architect. It was expected that cases 
would show clear links between design phases and constructing virtually, but it has been difficult 
to identify immediate effect of VDC in a structural engineers’ daily tasks. The VDC concept is still 
very diffuse and people have not really understood the concept or effects of it. MT Højgaard is 
ready to follow the modern requirement of virtual design and can see clear benefits in their 
productivity, but they need to show and tell their result to AEC people in order to change the 
general conception. Best practise suggestions are provided the reader based upon the mentioned 
challenges and perspectives to further work is commented.  
Keywords: Virtual Design and Construction, early design, IT platform, construction management.

1.1 Motivation 
As long as the history goes back a strong wish for an own home has been the main driver for 
economic development and growth. The construction industry creates the world’s physical 
infrastructure, for example homes, offices, schools, hospitals, energy sources and supporting 
systems to our daily life such as plumbing, water and waste piping, transportation and power 
distribution. However, the organisational processes throughout a construction project have their 
problems because they are fragmented due to cooperation of multiple actors with their own 
interests (Kunz & Martin, 2012). This results in long processing times and, thereby, higher 
economic expense.  
Where the focus earlier has been on building maintenance after their erection, we have in the 
recent years seen a movement of focus towards the preconstruction phase (Biilmann, 2015). 3D 
modelling of construction projects before their execution, which has become common, has been 
essential to accommodate todays governmental Better Building regulations but also a wish from 
clients to participate more and more in the progress during design and erection more and more. 
The future operation and storage of data in models has increasing focus and this necessitates 
updated computational power and sharing platforms (RIB Software AG, 2016). The opportunity 
to include greater amounts of information in models and, in particular, of sharing information in 
structural models, was the initial motivation for diving into the issues behind.  
Another important aspect of construction projects is their social context. It is a well known fact 
that communication between multiple different stakeholders during construction projects has 
been insufficient and poor (Winch, 2010). This well known problem is now being dealt with, and 
with new technology, communication can be improved. Technology are not only bringing new 
ways of communication but also can result in a new organisational design. Lack of communication 
is not only something that occurs between individuals but also between different phases of and 
different organisations involved in a construction project. Information flows between different 
sets of participants and between different project phases requires coordination and clear 
communication channels. Minimising risk of losing information can be handled within a strong 
organisation.  
The question revolving around the topic of collaboration in building processes for years has been 
“how can it be improved”? The conclusion is always the same; there are tools and methods to 
improve the collaboration but in such a heavily regulated and traditional industry as the industry, 
new and unconventional methods are hard to implement. The aim of this study is to address this 
question and to come with some conclusions, which hopefully in the future could facilitate better 
practice break with this eternal conclusion.   
Expectations from stakeholders will traditionally increase over time in a project time line. 
However, an overall interest in the projects and a desire for influence in projects before they have 
been finally decided upon have been growing the recent years. When the client wishes to 
participate more during the initial phase of a construction project, a focus on spatial 
understanding and 3D models has been essential for understanding and communicating across

tables, regardless of whether if you are experienced or a novice with respect to construction 
projects. Virtual designs have proven to be a strong tool, where time sequences, site planning and 
crew schedules have been connected through the 3D model (CIFE/CURT, 2007). Clients and all 
other persons involved have, then, an opportunity to carry out entire projects from idea to 
execution phase in virtual models before their practical execution. Virtual Design and 
Construction (VDC) is, therefore, very relevant for the construction sector right now. The concept 
of organisational VDC, firstly introduced in 2001by John Kunz and Martin Fisher, Center for 
Integrated Facility Engineering (CIFE) at Stanford University (Kunz & Martin, 2012), is a 
conceptual way of implementing VDC in an organisation. The concept has not gained wide 
acceptance in Denmark yet. However, aspects of the concept are already well known and 
implemented in many companies. The umbrella expression “VDC” for the concept as a whole is, 
therefore, explored in this thesis in order to investigate if all aspects of the concept is appropriate 
and beneficial in an organisation, both in companies and in organised construction projects.   
In recent years, the construction industry has been challenged, just as a variety of other industries. 
Like so many others, the construction industry has seen weakening financial results, and pressure 
on margins as a result of higher risk on projects due to the overall complexity, which has called 
for changes and implementation of new working methods. Disruptive innovation has in recent 
years have created new markets, where outsiders tends to disrupt an exiting market. New simpler 
methods and approaches are implementet instead of ignoring the succesfull innovative markets. 
With technology as a central platform, many companies have discovered new ways of conducting 
business where traditional interfaces are redefined. As for so many other industries, the 
importance of technology has been central for the construction industry in both design and 
execution. Unfortunately, disharmony between new and outdated software can be experienced 
and misconnections with respect to data sharing are not unusual.  Users have expectations 
regarding both software and hardware in that they expect them to function properly in both the 
design, analysis, calculation and sharing of big data. Technology has to meet these expectations in 
order to successfully create a supportive platform for virtual construction.  
Construction is one of the most important assets in Denmark with a production value of DKK 200 
billion a year representing more than 80% of the country’s real capital (Liliegreen & Jensen, 2014). 
Obviously the incentives for increasing productivity are huge. With increased productivity comes 
increased competitive capability in the market and better prices and quality as a result. The 
importance of realising the opportunities for increased productivity is, therefore, critical and the 
Danish construction sector needs to maximise its productivity in order to remain competitive and 
not be overtaken by other international competitors 
This is the background that has motivated this study. It is hoped that this thesis will contribute to 
new ways of collaboration and management within the construction sector.

1.2 Problem definition 
This master’s thesis presents the results of a research project, where a problem statement of an 
interrogative character is evaluated. Both an evaluation and suggestions of new best practice are 
given in the thesis.  
Taking as a starting point the perspectives addressed in the motivation, the main problem 
definition is: 
 
How does Virtual Design and Construction (VDC) influence the task for a 
structural engineer in a construction project in its early design phase and 
if so, how is it implemented? 
 
Naturally, a storm of questions arise from this main problem statement. These include: 
- How can the collaboration in early construction processes be improved?  
- How can information technology (IT) be an involving platform in 
construction projects and how can all participants make use of it? 
- How is VCD introduced and adapted in construction projects? 
- Under which conditions does implementation of VDC take place? 
- Who is challenged by VDC and how can these challenges be coped with? 
- What are the effects of VDC and how are they measured? 
 
 
With an existing knowledge and understanding of the underlying issues, these questions are 
investigated. Corroborating theory is gathered and data from interviews and case studies is 
analysed  in the search for answers to the defined problem.  
In order to meet the goals of this study, sub-goals are introduced. One of these is to create a full 
mapping of the Leavitt’s Diamond organisation model in relation to construction projects. In 
addition, the concept of Integrated Product Delivery in relation to Virtual Design and Construction 
is given where the current possibilities and obstructions in relation to implementation of the 
concept are addressed. Based on observational studies of cases studies, where VDC has been 
implemented, and interviews, a “best practise” in terms of applying all the aspects of VDC seen 
from a structural engineer’s perspective is proposed.  
The purpose of this study is to evaluate the concept VDC by observational study of cases, which 
have involvement of VDC and by interviews with people in the architectural, engineering and 
construction (AEC) industry, who are involved in construction projects with virtual designing. 
Leavitt’s diamond model is used as an analytical structure for clearly sorting the gathered 
information for evaluation. Best practise suggestions are provided the reader based upon the 
observed challenges.

1.3 Methodology 
In order to analyse and answer the problem statement for this thesis several methodological 
choices have been made. The study is based on a review of relevant theory together with research 
of implantation of VDC in construction cases and interviews where relevant observations and 
contemporary challenges are addressed.  
To fulfil the purpose of the study, relevant theories are introduced. These include Leavitt’s 
organisational diamond model and Integrated Product Delivery. In addition, Virtual Design and 
Construction, firstly introduced by John Kunz and Martin Fischer in 2001 as a result of their work 
at Center for Integrated Facility Engineering (CIFE) at Stanford University, has been studied both 
in its commonly used description and in its adapted version by MT Højgaard in 2012. Leavitt’s 
diamond model is selected as a theoretical tool for carefully and systematically analysing the 
gathered empirical information about VDC. Information has been gathered from books, papers, 
articles and websites to gather the latest research and the necessary theoretical background.  
Research methods include case studies, interviews and collection of project material based on 
meetings and notes. Through observations at MT Højgaard located in Søborg, empirical data and 
project cases have been gathered from both working side-by-side with employees, by workshops 
in the VDC Lab and by interviews. Actors from other construction companies have also been 
interviewed. Participation in a conference “Advanced technology and high-tech products for 
constructions” (DA: “Avanceret teknologi og højteknologiske produkter i byggeriet”) about 
advanced technology and high-tech products in construction work, held July 22th 2016 by “The 
Danish Association of Construction Clients” (DA: Bygherreforeneingen) also provided me with 
relevant contemporary knowledge.  
Interviews have been conducted with people who have first hand experience in construction 
projects and are working on a daily basis with Building Information Modelling tools as well as 
aspects of contemporary technology and virtual design on different levels. Their observations and 
personal thoughts are, therefore, depicted in the interviews as being subjective. While a precise 
reproduction of the interviews is attempted here, the interviewees comments have been 
generalised. Selected citations are given whenever direct opinions or information are needed in 
order to address the given topic.

design phases of a construction project. The focus has been kept on the benefits seen from the 
perspective of a structural engineer. However, the project has identified a need for analysing this 
complex problem from more than just only a structural engineer’s perspective. Early design 
phases and different involved participants and parameters involved in construction projects have, 
therefore, also been treated in a general sense. Based on the results of the analyses, conclusions

2.1 Leavitt’s Diamond 
Harold J. Leavitt (1922-2007) was an American psychologist with a specialisation in management. 
Among other analyses of communication and interaction, he created a “diamond model” 
describing the organisational changes in companies. The original diamond model was presented 
to the public for the first time in 1962 and published for the first time in 1964 (Leavitt, 1964), (Ry 
Nielsen & Ry, 2014). This relatively simple model was intended to provide a systematic approach 
to the analysis of rather complex organisation by identifying variables and their interactions when 
changes are made in the organisation. 
2.1.1 Leavitt’s Original model 
Leavitt’s diamond model proposes that every organisational system is made up of four interactive 
main variables; people, task, technology and structure.  When one of the main elements changes, 
the related elements are forced to affect the others as well, as represented by the arrows in Figure 
2.1.  Their mutual ability to interact decides the fate of the company’s future.  
 
Figure 2.1 Leavitt’s original diamond model (Folkmann, 2012). 
The key variables (Bright Hub Inc, 2011), (Ry Nielsen & Ry, 2014) are: 
People 
The people are the employees of the organisation. However, when analysing an organisation with 
the diamond model, work titles such as managers, engineers, contractors, architects etc. are more 
or less redundant. In addition to positions, aspects of education, skills, knowledge and 
productivity are also important. It was outlined that it is not the titles but the competences that 
the people have that make the difference.  
With changes in tasks, people will react with different work patterns than those demonstrated 
before the changes. To avoid confusion and unnecessary stress, staff should be trained prior to 
and during the process of the change in order to make them familiar with the new working 
practise.  
==PAGE:19==
 
2 . 1  L e a v i t t ’ s  D i a m o n d  
 
P a g e  | 10 
Following a change in technology, people will have to adapt to new and, given the rapid 
development on the technology market, it is likely that this will be more advanced technology than 
what they were used to. This requires extra training, so the employees can handle the equipment 
implemented in the organisation. This may also involve hiring new skilled employees or 
consultants.  
With changes in structure, a change in job roles is implied. Again, the employees may need new 
training to meet the demands of their newly defined frames of work. This new structure should 
be implemented over a longer period of time in order to limit employees stress level. Rushing a 
process like this could shake the whole foundation of the company.  
Task 
Tasks refers not only to the employees’ daily tasks as the phrasing might suggest. Tasks also 
includes the overall foundation of the company and its purpose, including the goals defined in the 
work assignments. The benefit and the relevance of the tasks are just as important as the actual 
task.  
When changing people, the labour force will change with respect to tasks. The tasks and/or goals 
will then need to be changed in order to optimise knowledge, required experience and work 
frames.  
When changing technology, tasks will change in the sense of changing the way things are done. 
New and more advanced technology invites a redefinition of efficient working methods. Thus, 
goals may be altered and tasks redefined.   
The introduction of changes in structure can result in changes of hierarchy or even in the 
merging/splitting the organisation under extreme circumstances. This will explicitly require new 
organisational structure as well as a redefinition of goals and working methods.   
Technology 
Technology refers mainly to hardware, software, equipment etc. that helps or facilitates the 
employees or the structure and tasks of the organisations. However, working methods or 
machines and programs can also be put under this category.  
In response to changes in people, technology has to, at a minimum, match the peoples’ current 
competence level. Purchsing hardware and software, wchich are preperared for future technology 
updates, can be benfifical, in order to save expensives on future purchases and limit training for 
people to understand the new implemented IT. 
When changing tasks, changes in technology might be necessary to support different daily tasks 
or additions to the existing or newly introduced working areas in the organisation. 
==PAGE:20==

With changes in structure, the technology is also compelled to change. Shifting workload and/or 
staff necessitate the automation of processes, while new software or equipment may be required 
by others in order to support the new organisational structure.  
Structure 
Organisational structure refers not only to working titles and hierarchical system. The structure 
includes communication, relationships and interactions between employees, departments and 
management levels. Responsibility placement and flow is also a part of the structure.  
With changes in people, the workload will be shifted. Altered structure may lead to both firing and 
hiring of people. When hiring people that are more qualified, lesser-qualified people may have to 
be dismissed which will lead to a redefinition of the structure and goals of the organisation as was 
also   the case when training the staff in new methods and technology. If the organisation is just 
replacing a working area with new people, a change in structure is not necessarily needed – the 
model can only be applied when changes in qualifications and empowerment are made.  
When changing tasks, this will mean changes in structure when the organisation decides to expand 
with new departments or to new fields. This will also be the case when more customer-centricity 
or transparency is introduced. All of these cases will lead to changes in workload and result in a 
redefinition of the organisation’s structure and goals.  
With changes in technology, the structure is affected by changes in the automation of processes or 
upgrading of software that can result in shifting workload among employees. New work areas may 
be created while other areas may be closed down and communication patterns might need to be 
revised.

executives and managers who exercised the model (Ry Nielsen & Ry, 2014). In the fourth edition 
of Leavitt’s publication from 1978, the model was included with slightly changes from the firstly 
introduced, hence accounting for the critics. An environmental layer was added to the model,

Environment 
Organisations cannot stand alone but must interact with their surroundings. The environmental 
influence is considered to work both ways, meaning that organisations, and individually the four 
main components within, also could affect the environment. The level of dependence and 
complexity has only increased during the last five decades due to progressed social regulations, 
globalisation and technology development.  
The environmental layer summarises the influence of components outside the organisation 
influencing the four key variables. This is e.g. manufactures, vendors, governmental legislations 
or other regulative boards. In relation to organisational changes, it is important to assess 
environmental stakeholders and their influence level.

but with respect to the fundamental model (Ry Nielsen & Ry, 2014). Their modifications are

“Framework” and a surrounding layer “History”, which is illustrated in Figure 2.3.  The framework 
consists of four extra variables; physical settings, visions/values/goals, reward systems and 
organisational culture (Bright Hub Inc, 2011), (Ry Nielsen & Ry, 2014). The new introduced

Physical settings 
As the designation suggest, the physical settings is covering both the actual geographical location, 
building layout and lastly design/décor settings involving status image. Whether the organisation 
is scattered or gathered in the same city or country, the actual location of the organisation could 
have influence on communication flows, employment options or transportation. Equally

invites to debate and conflicts or tranquillity and control. The design and style is closely linked to 
the décor. Status image and identity is often imprinted by the first impressions.  
Visions/values/goals 
This subcategory under “framework” could be argued to belong at the already defined variables. 
“values” will unmistakably overlap with “organisational culture” and “goals” are already defined 
under “tasks”. But visions, values and goals are essential to an organisation and should be treated 
separately. Regardless of the overlaps, “visions and goals” may be used as defining the dream that 
drives the organisation. “Values” are defining the fundamental corporate values.  
Reward systems 
It is often observed in organisations that the consistency between visions and behavioural 
rewards is poor. A new management who want a change in personell often underestimate or 
misunderstand the time and effort necessary to enact given change, which can result in loss in

productivity. The management group may forget or not be aware of the need for monitoring this 
loss but continues to define the specific task or employee by current results, which then would  
not seem rewardable. It should be noted that rewards are not only defined in monetary terms but 
can also take form of “perks”, e.g. free use of car and telephone, further education, increased 
influence or responsibility, participation in projects and leisure/activity memberships outside of 
the organisation. A smile or praise can, likewise, be considered as rewards.  
Organisational culture 
The organisational culture can be described as the ability to adapt to the surroundings and the 
integration of different organisational groups. It is reflected in the shared values and norms of the 
employees and the organisation can affect this culture by encouraging solidarity and mutual 
commitment. In short terms, it can be summarised as the feeling of being in the same boat. 
Generally organisational culture occurs unprovoked and “automatically” without any managerial 
objectives.  
History 
The acknowledgement of the past events of an organisation is important for its development. The 
past critical events cannot be avoided to influence choices and decisions made in the present. 
History is an underlying base layer, which affects the other variables in the organisation.  
As mentioned before, the mentioned variables are all mutual connected and the variables are 
closely connected to the environment and framework. Thus the Leavitt’s diamond helps to 
identify the sectors in an organisation, the focus should be on how changes can be manipulated to 
make flows work better for anyone and everyone who is a part of the project as well as for 
improving efficiency in the organisational system.  
2.1.4 Leavitt-Ry’s Diamond applied on construction projects 
Leavitt-Ry’s diamond model is intended to act as a descriptive tool and analyse organisational 
changes. The applicability for describing and analysing construction project is therefore necessary 
to evaluate.  
In construction projects there usually are a high number of stakeholders, both involved directly 
such as the client, architect, engineers, suppliers etc. but also indirectly such as local community 
or public activities. Users can both be involved directly or indirectly in construction projects, 
whether they are actively participating or only considered an outside party.  
The diversity of involved actors and their positions could potentially induce conflicts. Often the 
goal is common for all parties but in a construction project with multiple companies with their 
own interests to care for, e.g. financial conflicts can evolve.  
Most construction projects can be argued to be unique. However, in many respects, 
communication links, the distribution of responsibility, and hierarchical order can be considered 
to be common from project to project. In other words, the structure and tasks of a construction

to have mainstream the organisational structure.  Complex projects are, therefore, often described 
as temporary organisations, since the organisational structure and tasks can be very different 
over time. (Winch, 2010). 
Components will come and go according to their application and the resulting changes will be 
present in all components of Leavitt’s model. The obvious example are people with introduction 
and exits of contractors and engineers. Tasks will also change over time as the project progresses 
from design to execution. Technology will change in regard of different BIM tools applied in 
different phases. Structural changes will appear as change in communication patterns influenced 
by change in people and tasks.  
The framework will also change over time, especially the physical setting when changing location 
from design offices to construction site. Reward systems, vision and goals together with

With respect to the presented arguments, Leavitt’s diamond model are suitable to describe and

The concept of VDC was drafted as a result of Kunz and Martin’s work at Center for Integrated

The need for developing a technical platform arose from the idea of managing multi-disciplinary 
models. The BLIS (Building Lifecycle Interoperable Software) project was, therefore, conceived as 
a way to initiate the next logical phase in the wide-spread adoption of an objective data model 
standard for the architectural, engineering and construction (AEC) industry together with facility 
management (FM) (BLIS sharing information, 2002). It was the first great demonstration by a 
large number of software vendors to create an integrated set of project design models, which was 
based on a shared IFC-based architectural model. Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) is a neutral 
platform intended to describe construction project data and is an open file format not controlled 
by one vendor (Wikipedia, 2016). The BLIS project operated from 1999 – 2002 and demonstrated 
that CAD (computer-aided design) and analysis application programs could easily share and 
exchange data between different teams.  
While the BLIS project received great attention and publicity, the project had limited success in 
its original hope to stimulate software developer’s support of IFCs and industry use of them (Kunz 
& Martin, 2012). Kunz and Fisher had mixed experiences with use of computer application 
interoperability: it was possible to some extent, but difficult and limited.  
Kunz and Fischer committed their work to explicit semantic data that clients, consultants and 
bidders need to share. They encourage project teams to identify and commit to a standard 
vocabulary, but they do not propose defined standards of their own. It is assumed that the long-
term solution takes time to develop, and in short-term aspects project teams makes the best of 
what they have to their disposal.

(CIFE/CURT, 2007) and is briefly presented below.

tracked during the process. The CIFE/CURT survey indicated that this stage is common within the 
global AEC industry. For visualisation to work well, all stakeholder organisations need to develop 
competences to work with and interpret visualised models. The competence development is 
closely linked to organisational development of strategic investment in methods and their use, as 
well as strategic updates in partnering arrangements.

2.3.2 VDC practise  
Currently AEC practise is obviously focuses on developing value in projects. However, building 
virtual models can add to the value. Paper documentation, such as project schedules on the wall, 
binders with printed construction and finance management from computer-based outputs and 
project descriptions, do not help integration of different disciplines and changes, small or major, 
and is very time consuming. In addition, the paper document can often be hard to understand for 
all stakeholders; for example, non-users can have a difficult time understanding 2D architectural 
drawings or Gantt charts.  
Today’s VDC model is hence computer-based. The use of VDC project models can then be flexible, 
visual and interactive rather than paper-based documentation. Conducted schedules and models 
can be projected and shown to other stakeholders, who needs the information for their own area 
of responsibility. Technology aids, such as high definition screens and projection devices, enables 
social sharing and various modelling and simulation applications are now powerful and 
affordable.  
The VDC model supports use of multiple stakeholders and is designed to support a multi-
disciplinary project team. The users will include architects, engineers, contractors, owners 
representatives, users, suppliers, community representatives and governmental jurisdiction. 
Interactive VDC enables change in behaviour and reduction in decision latency; the time between 
rising a question to having a sufficient informative answer is dramatically reduced.

Visual VDC model makes the content of each project much more accessible for stakeholders than 
the traditional paper-based descriptions. Interactive 3D models are vastly more understandable 
than static 2D drawings, as people are more familiar with understanding spacious object-based. 
4D construction process animations are similarly much more understandable than traditional 
Gantt charts. Projects, where different native languages are involved, can refer to the same 
common model. Any changes in any project are implemented rapidly and changes derived 
therefrom is instantly highlighted in all other dependants. Because models can be examined with 
respect to each other, explanation-time and decision-time drops from maybe days to seconds. 
Similar, design and construction rework are likely to drops because relevant stakeholders 
experience increased ownership from greater participation in project decision-making. 
Traditional CAD models are composed of a set of lines, which the human eye interprets as spacious 
elements like columns, walls and doors. Modern 3D CAD products built object-oriented, where the 
computer modelling system recognise the objects as physical elements and appear as meaningful 
visual representations to the user. The model understands number, location and properties, 
which other applications can interpret meaningfully.

several of the interested parties was enthusiastic and eager to participate in unfolding VDC at MT 
Højgaard. Especially their “lab”-facilities, which is explained in the following subsection, was 
highly admired.  
As a part of the project, a Microsoft PowerPoint presentation explaining the journey from 2D to 
6D was made. The presentation shows specific examples on what it takes, how to do it and the 
potential for MT Højgaard in adopting VDC. Another goal with the presentation was to make the 
concept tangible for employees in MT Højgaard and external stakeholders and to demonstrate 
that it does exist in practise. In addition, a short video presentation of VDC was prepared aimed at 
clients, cooperation partners and employees. It explains why 2D operations limit opportunities 
for directing projects, controlling risks and improving productivity. With concrete examples, the 
video demonstrates what VDC involves and why it is the right path to take. The aim of both the 
presentation and the video is to demonstrate that VDC is tangible and to diminish scepticism. (MT 
Højgaard, 2016b).  
VDC lab 
The concept of a VDC lab is to gather knowledge, competences and technology in one place. The 
idea behind the lab is to ensure the communication of VDC happens smoothly and to everyone 
involved where questions can be asked from the first encounter. The process is guided and inputs 
from relevant actors can be discussed and assessed.  
The VDC lab is divided into five rooms as Figure 2.8 shows. Each one of them is labelled with a 
creative mindset where the reflectance is supposed to unfold within an area of creativity.

Workshops 
On every workshop, it has been the goal to involve as many people as possible of the organisation

objectively and professionally on multiple levels.  
On every workshop, there has been external facilitators because there was a wish for “lightening

iTWO 
RIB (Renovation In Buildings) Software AG creates, develop and offers the software “iTWO” (RIB 
Software AG, 2016). iTWO is a combination model of time, resources, planning and finance in one 
5D model for simulation and execution. The name is a combination of “(Virtual+Physical) 
Integrated”, where virtual+physical equals “TWO” and the “i” stands for “integrated” (RIB 
Software AG, 2016).  The program is based on an interactive BIM model where processes of 
planning and execution interact. Tender calculations, contracting and financial management are 
included in the model and presents a fully integrated solution for a project.  
iTWO is also a strong tool for testing different solutions. Constructional solutions inflicted by e.g. 
choosing steel or concrete can be addressed in both structural, economic and visual sense. 
Placement of e.g. staircases, reception desk, or décor can also be practical to visualise in iTWO and

visualisation can be a strong tool for discovering best solutions.  
Since iTWO is a fully integrated software with base in interactive BIM models, the software is 
highly valued as a tool for implementing VDC in construction projects.

Project progress 
After signing contract with Himmerlands Boligforening, MT Højgaard was handed 2D project

Whether if columns are “locked” to centrelines or whether if walls are “locked” to outer face or 
centre makes a huge difference in constructability, and they are common known problems.  
Models from architects can be hard to build upon since architects works with cm and not mm like 
engineers do, as TH, Rambøll mentions (TH, appendix D).

4.1.1 People: earlier involvement and understanding VDC tools 
The people in the early design phase are always the clients, their consultants and the architects. 
However, introducing implantation of VDC the importance of including other actors is explicit. 
Basically, any person who one way or the other  is relevant in the project in the total life cycle can 
be invited to the early design phase. As TH from Rambøll points out, operation and cleaning staff 
can be relevant to invite to early design workshops, in order to influence their future workdays 
(TH, appendix D). This change in early involvement are new for many of the involved people, and 
the adaption can be challenging. It requires fast transition time and willingness to take action.  
Competences for understanding modelling tools and being able to work in collaboration and with 
open file format is expected by younger generations of educated AEC people. Learning objectives 
for structural courses taught at Technical University of Denmark’s are given in a Course Bases 
catalogue. These are included in appendix E with high-lighted learning objectives which are 
important for traditional task areas. When fundamental learning objectives for understanding 
structural elements, there is a tendency of applying technical assisting tools in courses, which add 
knowledge to the basic.  
The change in work areas for structural engineers are not to be feared, since the need for statically 
construction calculations always will be needed, as NK, MT Højgaard, points out (NK, appendix D). 
In the learning objectives for becoming a structural engineer is still fundamented in basic 
understandment of simple structures before adding technology aid (appendix E). The utopian 
dream of pushing one button and the final building design pops up is far from reality and not the 
meaning of VDC. Implementing of VDC is not replacing design processes but extending the time 
and focus on the design. VDC is a tool, which have the ability to both enhance and worsen 
competences, so structural engineers can really damage constructability if they focus too much on 
the software tools without understanding the structural composition.   
Both transformation issues and comprehending issues are known in the construction industry. 
People have limited competences for understanding piles of data, where technology can store big 
data in clouds and download it into software tools in seconds. People are cautious about virtual 
design because they fear that the information, which is enriching a model, may also be lost in 
interrogation. Casper Gullach states “… the big challenge is, with the way work are done nowadays,

4.1.2 Task: intensified modelling phase 
The task for early design can be of different character, but the overall task is the same, namely to 
increase leaned and efficient construction projects by building the projects virtually before 
building on site. More specifically, the task is to find argument supporting a given design frame. 
Plans are made, challenges are identified and solutions are being modelled. The task are changed 
with VDC in the sense that more people are involved, people who historically was not involved in 
such an early phase. In order to optimise the task all people invited are asked to bring their 
knowledge and experience.  
VDC are defined by constructing virtually before constructing in real life. The task is much more 
than just conducting a 3D model. Building virtually means building the total life span for a 
construction project. Costs and time planning are vital to the projects, but also health and safety 
issues, logistics, material consumptions and future maintenance can be planned ahead. When 
multiple participants are dependent on the information associated to the model, it is important to 
model details which are of importance for other interdisciplinary participants, mainly contractors 
and suppliers. 
In the early design phase, the task has been more intensified with the introduction of VDC. 
Generally, projects are more time consuming in the design phase than previous and especially 
projects involving virtual design. It takes more “waiting” time before any results can be shown to 
the client. This is the beliefs of TH, Rambøll (TH, appendix D) and CG, SWECO has the same 
conviction: “We do have the competences and we use it on project, where it gives value. (…) There 
should be more time and cash for the consultant, which one is not quite prepared for yet… the design 
phase is still as short as it has always been” (CG, appendix D). Their conviction is that the client has 
difficulties by extending the design phase with both time and costs, especially if it is construction 
projects by the public sector. Both interviewees are convinced that public-private partnering and 
private clients are more willing to focus on the design phase because in most cases, the client is 
also the one operating and maintaining the building in the future. This makes it easier for the 
design team to convince the client of more time to design different solutions to find the best. With 
VDC, companies are investing more time and resources in the early stage of construction to have 
a long time financial benefit. This investment should be combined with a NPV (net present value) 
of the long term saving and the extra amount companies are spending on early stage.

4.1.3 Technology: high-tech solutions in projects and in companies 
Technology for early designs has been proven to have primary software and secondary software. 
For the early design stage, the most relevant software is 3D modelling tools like Autodesk Revit 
or Trimble Tekla.  Secondary software tools are e.g. finite element analysis for controlling force 
flows, collisions tools for testing constructability, walk-through 3D model functions for 
discovering obvious errors like moving a handrail and sunlight analysis together with indoor 
environment.  
Technology trends in 2016 has been evaluated by analysts from Gartner Inc., the world's leading 
information technology research and advisory company (Gartner, 2016). Especially for design 
aiding tools in construction the most relevant are technology for analysing and simulating, 
placement of systems and data in clouds, “information of everything” linked to internet search, 
sensor technology, mobile and location-aware technology, 3D-print, modularity and predictive 
analysis.  
As mentioned before, people can be challenged by new and modern technology. SWECO consider 
the variety of software tools as a good approach for aiding early design (CG, appendix D). The 
variety enables an organisation to have multiple “super users” each with their own field of 
expertise. Especially younger generations are educated in several programs and has the know-
how to understand new programs, and they know where to find help if they do not (TH, appendix 
D). It is important that actors are able to implement technology or know who to ask, if technology 
should act as a beneficial aid and not a drag.  As CEO Torben Biilmann, MT Højgaard, also expresses 
“But change is not brought about by technology alone. Technology is worthless if we do not possess 
the skills and expertise to understand it and the processes to use it… if we are to change the 
construction industry from within, we must be prepared to change ourselves.” (Biilmann, 2015). 
Implementing of VDC can affect other IT projects and initiatives within the structure of 
organisation. It is inevitable that the organisational IT landscape progresses continuously and 
with the rapid change and development in technology – both hardware and software – architects, 
consultants and contractors is most likely updated with the latest 3D modelling programs and 
other assisting virtual design programs. Implementation of the VDC model is easier when people 
are familiar with different software tools and are more likely to be willing of virtual design for the 
total construction project before erecting it in reality. Coordination and adaption between other 
IT activities and VDC should also be evident and necessary.

Other departments of a construction company can hence benefit from VDC and not only the

A discovery made by CIFE was, that the concept VDC unfolded in practise showed interaction

evident both internally and externally. 
There is not one clear standard for BIM utilised by everyone (Kragh, et al., 2014). There is no open 
standard or platform, which is used by all participants within the construction industry. Hereby 
the data exchange is only more complicated when there is no common shared standard. BIPS 
construction descriptive standards are the foundation for a common guideline and other 
standards can be used from project to project and what else is required from other people 
involved (TH, appendix D).  
4.1.4 Structure: implementation of VDC in projects and business plans 
Implementation of VDC affects the organisational structure for a design task. The structure needs 
to be clear for implementing VDC in order to make people understand the concepts and be 
encouraged to make active use of it. If the structure is lacking, the task seem overwhelming.  
MT Højgaard was not interested in a “top-down” roll-out with the change in structure. Instead, 
they prepared a very clear business plan for a three-year-implementing strategy for VDC and 
subsequently iTWO (Kragh, et al., 2014). The plan includes outlined projects within VDC and a 
corresponding timeline. Finances and purchases are also carefully integrated in order make a total 
concept covering all critical variables and areas needed to build a strong model.  
Workshop sessions during the VDC pilot project showed that even if the technical issues are 
central, the VDC team in MT Højgaard should also be focused on several other elements. These 
were establishment of a central VDC process,  incorporation of VDC in MT Højgaard’s business 
model, establish cooperation and management support for implementation, establish the 
required IT support together with  develop and contain VDC competences. This would be 
implemented in the business core of MT Højgaard parallel to the VDC implementation on several 
construction projects, which would optimise the adaption to the production facilities available.

For a construction project, where multiple stakeholders are gathered in the early design process, 
there is a risk that the individual participants are entirely looking after own interest. The VDC

This does not lower creativity, but manage the flow of ideas. The “lab” is the managing tool for MT

The new technology possibilities brings new possibilities for responsibility distribution. E.g., the

model solutions were identified and presented to the client to have the best possible platform to 
choose the most optimal solution both technically, operationally but also financially. 
New types of interfaces for actors involved are very important to identify and differentiate 
between. With virtual construction tools and access to the models from all the involved, the 
possibility for overlapping each other fields are intensified and responsibility interfaces can be 
shifted.  
4.1.5 Physical settings: labs and workshops 
Gathering people across a project also requires gathering people physically. Especially for 
implementing the VDC concept in an early design phase, requires gathering interdisciplinary 
people in a physically sense in order to create a natural workflow for inventing and discarding 
ideas and solution. MT Højgaard has dealt with this challenge by installing a VDC lab in their 
subsidiaries around the world, where people are joining each other for workshops physically and 
by video conference with the other VDC labs. Mobile VDC labs with computers, screens and virtual 
design operational systems, are built in standard containers, which can be unpacked on site 
locations. The containers fit for transportation and good lifts, so MT Højgaard can bring their VDC 
lab anywhere. They are highly appreciated for complex construction projects, where design and 
erections are controlled continually to make sure they match. The labs are inviting creativity with 
colourful décor and equipment assisting different phases during the design.  
For the structural engineers the physical setting will be their own office and in workshop facilities 
e.g. a VDC lab. After the agreed projects has found the actors, the first design workshop will be 
held accordingly to VDC concepts. Afterwards there is a natural phase where each and one 
complete their agreed task on their own, and the early design ideas are then compared and 
discussed in workshops.  
For early design phases, the most needed tools are computer BIM tools and communication 
possibilities. The physically setting therefore requires working stations with hardware and

4.1.6 Visions/values/goals: higher productivity, improved hit-rate and better quality 
As described in the theory section, the subcategory “visions/values/goals” can be argued to 
already be defined under “organisational culture” and “goals”. However, the subcategory should 
be treated as one single unit, as it is an important variable in an organisation.  
The overall vision with virtual design is to create better quality in construction projects. The 
constructability is thoroughly investigated before construction begins on site, which brings 
financial benefits and better design solutions. The dream is to redefine the construction industry 
mainstream, and to introduce more productivity by implementing the concept of Virtual Design 
and Construction (Kunz & Martin, 2012).  
Values and visions are difficult to really embrace in Denmark. There is a tendency of finding values 
and visions meaningless and nauseatingly “American”. The value of a vision is hence important to 
find in tangible actions behind, which makes the VDC concept beneficial a traditional industry. 
4.1.7 Reward systems: decision basis and increase of stakes 
Reward systems can in this context both be connected with individual stakeholders and the 
reward systems for the design project. Consistency between visons and behaviour within MT 
Højgaard is also commented.  
The construction industry have had the tradition of only to operate with one design, and 
occasionally another one to convince the client, that the first design option was the best. In public 
procurements, the competition naturally brings more designs, but in general, clients do not have 
the time, resources or energy to care for more than two or three designs. For a structural engineer 
this limits the solution field for finding optimal statically solution, since the architect and client 
already decided to choose a design solution with little room for modification.  
With modern technology assisting virtual design, the client do not have to be familiar with 
multiple designs in their depths, but design teams can show virtual tours or walk-throughs in the 
different design suggestions, which quickly gives an indication of different consequences by 
choosing one over another. Structural engineers can be involved in the design process, and 
comment different solutions involving the statically system and combined solutions, with both 
strong design and strong structural compositions, can be considered from the very start. This 
minimise the process between architects and structural engineers, where design solutions are 
send back and forth with risk of disputes and information losses. The reward is noticeable and 
results in more quality in construction projects and in managerial systems, which was discovered 
with the survey from CIFE/CURT (CIFE/CURT, 2007).

Virtual designs also opens the possibility for the client to be more picky and set op different 
parameters of what the building must be able to, e.g. it needs to be energy efficient, instead of what

focus would then be changed from certain building materials and the cost e.g. introducing a certain

The managerial structure was changed purposely with recruitment of CEO, Torben Biilmann at

(Biilmann, 2015). It was important to ensure involvement from employees instead of a “top-
down” rollout, and workshops was held with gradually more and more employees and 
departments involved. Competences within computational understanding and use of design tools 
were measured by a survey, and employees felt more involved and aware of their own 
competences (Kragh, et al., 2014).  
4.1.8 Organisation culture: adaption to VDC 
The conviction of traditional distribution of work area and responsibility may seem remaining. 
That MT Højgaard, a company of contractors, should be involved in the design phase to such 
extent, seem distant to contemporary conceptions. The consulting engineer have traditionally 
been collaborating with the architect across the design, and contractors are handed the more or 
less finalised project. MT Højgaard is highly aware on the fact, that there is no use in being the 
only ones who can operate with VDC (Kragh, et al., 2014). Public relations have helped the ripples 
in the water and other construction companies are well aware of the enrolment of VDC in MT 
Højgaard. Other companies are however, hesitating on fully commitment to VDC but settles with 
virtual design solution only if needed, e.g. for understanding a very complex corner of a building 
or if it is very strong wish from the client (TH, appendix D), (CG, appendix D).  
The early design phase of a construction project is already very inflicted by high-tech software 
and is gradually adapting more virtual devices. The ability to adapt to VDC therefore seem a rather 
easy step to take for some but an overwhelming step to take for others. MT Højgaard has taken 
the lead and feels comfortable by doing so, because they have defined a clear implementation 
strategy. Outsiders may need to be convinced, but in construction trades, the tendency is already 
moving towards new and modern technology involvement. When involvement from all 
stakeholders are moved to an earlier stage in the project, and most of them moved to the design 
phase, they feel more involved and their own stakes are increased. All stakeholders experience a 
piece of them in the final design solution, which is agreeable thought through and everyone 
involved have the same goal.

4.1.9 Environment: regulations and procurement 
There are multiple national governmental regulations, but for the observational study, the most 
relevant legislation was Information and Communication Technology (ICT) legislation from 2013 
(Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013). The legislation concerns appliance of information and communication 
tools in public construction projects and causes the client to be responsible of technology sharing. 
The legislation does not refer to one specific system, but only places the responsibility on the 
client. Open format file (IFC) and BIPS (DA: Byggeri Informationsteknologi Produktivitet 
Samarbejde) construction descriptive guidelines are usually basis for model and information 
sharing (TH, appendix D) but MT Højgaard has developed their own for usage in projects (Olsen, 
et al., 2015). This variation in ICT causes conflicts with interface platforms, when ICT are so openly 
defined and everyone involved have their own set of guidelines.  
The ICT legislation is also requesting to coordinate and share the ICT appliance for everyone 
involved (Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013, §3), which ensures information flow and communication 
between participants. However, when MT Højgaard is involved in pre-analyses they have already 
obtained knowledge about the project and have already modelled a design. Public procurements 
should happen on an objective and factual basis with no less than three project bidders 
(Bygningsstyrelsen, 2008) and all bidders should have the same basis of knowledge about the 
project case before entering procurement. MT Højgaard has already involved themselves in 
project descriptions, and they may have trouble bidding on the projects, when they are open for 
procurement. If they hold information that others do not have, there is a problem of incapacitation 
and they may be argued to be expelled from the procurement.  
This thesis do not addresses the incapacitation challenge specifically, but there are obvious 
consequences involved with holding information before procurements. Early involvement engage 
earlier knowledge of the topic, but information should be kept open sourced, for all competitors 
to evaluate. If MT Højgaard are performing a job assigned by a future client, there is nothing wrong 
in performing the pre-analyses that are asked for, but they should be published before 
procurement to equalise the level of knowledge between bidders.  
4.1.10 History: movement in focus 
The history for economic development and growth goes a long time back. However, the 
organisational processes throughout a construction project have been causing problems because 
they are fragmented due to cooperation of multiple participants with their own interests (Kunz & 
Martin, 2012). This problem should be minimized by an implementation of VDC.  
Where the historical focus have been on building maintenance after their erection, there is a 
gradual shift towards the preconstruction phase (Biilmann, 2015). 3D models of construction 
projects before their execution, have become more and more common and are essential to 
accommodate todays governmental building regulations but also a wish from clients. 
Participation in the progress during design by erection has become a valued process for the client 
and different stakeholders who see the advantages of being involved at an earlier stage in the

the project time line, but with an overall interest in the projects and a desire for influence and

Future operation of the building and storage of data in models is more and more in focus and this 
necessitates updated computational power and sharing platforms. Virtual designs have proven to

4.2 Recap 
Results showed the importance of early involvment of relevant project participants to brainstorm 
a design for a construction project. Competences for understanding new working methods 
assisted by modern technology are very important to develop among participants. People are 
expected to know their own field of experience and additionally have the know-how and 
willingness of putting themselves in a new situation of learning new methods and IT. Fundamental 
learning objectives are still in focus, but it is expected that appliance of technology assisting tools 
win more and more area in construction projects.   
The most important finding included that interfaces have proved to be essential for 
implementation of VDC. With analyse variables of Leavitt’s diamond model, interfaces can be 
argued to be included in almost every variable. New options for expanding task areas are 
supported with modern technology and especially younger generation are educated with rapidly 
change in technology both privately and professionally. Interfacing platforms are changed with 
earlier involvement of project participants and typically task definitions are changed when easy 
and tangible tech-solutions are ready to use.

5.1 Results  
The problem definition is: how does Virtual Design and Construction (VDC) influence the task for 
a structural engineer in a construction project in its early design phase and if so, how is it 
implemented?  
In order to answer the problem definition a number of supporting sub-questions was introduced, 
which are evaluated in the following.  
Collaboration in early construction processes can be improved by implementing the VDC model 
throughout projects. The VDC model provides communication tools across virtual design, where 
open file format secures free exchange in information. The VDC model suggests earlier 
involvement of all participants to ensure share of competences and knowledge at an earlier stage 
in the process. Participants gain a sense of being a part of the design solutions whether they are 
typical AEC participants or they are suppliers or operation teams. Collaboration between parties 
is improved when they feel more involved in projects. 
Information technology has been proven highly relevant for construction projects. For early 
design tasks, technology information is divided into two categories; primary and secondary 
software. Primary software is 3D modelling programs for improved spatial understanding. 3D 
models are enriched with element information, quantity and prices. Secondary software tools are 
e.g. finite element analysis programs for controlling force flows, collisions tools for testing 
constructability, walk-through 3D model functions for discovering obvious errors like wrong 
element placements. Sunlight affections and indoor environment can also be simulated before 
actual construction is erected. By introduction of Information and Communication Technology 
(ICT) legislation in 2013 causes the client to be responsible of technology sharing and typical the 
wish for sharing between all participants is great. Open format file (IFC) is typically used for model 
and information sharing.  
MT Højgaard is the first mover introducing VDC and adapt the concept in construction projects. 
VDC was firstly introduced at MT Højgaard in 2012 when organisational changes were 
implemented. A three-year implementation business model was conducted set in motion between 
2012 and 2017 with establishment of a VDC department. Pre-analysis of competences and ability 
to adapt to VDC was analysed before installing VDC concepts. When MT Højgaard knew their 
weaknesses and strengths, they involved employees in test cases provided by iTWO’s design 
company “RIB”. Next step was then to transfer learned objects to one of MT Højgaard’s own cases 
with assistance from “RIB” followed by own development in competences for new projects.  
MT Højgaard has created VDC “labs” where they control progress in VDC projects. The labs are 
encouraging creativity with colourful décor and comfortable furnishing, and the flow in creativity 
can be managed with a structured form of brainstorming. Knowledge, competences and 
technology is gathered in one place to ensure communication of VDC with workshops and 
presentations.

Challenges by introducing VDC in construction projects are many. Barriers against changes and 
fear of change can be assigned to organisational fatigue, lack of competences and unmanageable 
training. If potential problems are identified, and addressed with possible solution suggestions, 
most confusions can be dispelled if problems should occur. The organisation is more prepared 
and ready for these challenges.  
Effects of VDC are regularly compared with the initial implementation goals. VDC can lead to a 
number of organisational improvements, and it is important to be realistic about expectations. MT 
Højgaard has carefully pin pointed specific goals from which they could directly measure benefits 
of VDC implementation, see Figure 2.7. Actual goals are based on higher hit-rate, lower risk and 
accident rates and changes later in the project. During the years of implementing VDC in MT 
Højgaard’s business model, several effects of VDC were identified, like  higher hit-rate and greater 
productivity rate, but actual numbers will first appear in 2017. MT Højgaard has not many projects 
to measure on the immediate effects of VDC, which of course should be taken into account, when 
measuring different parameters, where statistics are based on few projects.  
Now that all sub-questions have been anwered, the overall problem definition is answered.  
The concept of VDC has definitely influence the processes and tasks in MT Højgaard, where focus 
has been on earlier involvement and interfear with design suggestions instead of receiving a more 
or less defined project from the architect. However, it has been difficult to identify immediate 
effect of VDC in a structural engineers’ daily tasks. It was expected that the cases would show clear 
links between design phases and constructing virtually. Interviews gave the same indication; the 
early design can have clear benefits for saving time and cost in the long run and by virtual design 
the construction have more quality and constructability. But the link in between is not clear. 
Models from pre-analysis are discarded after presentation to the client and other VDC 
participants, and the otherwise continous process is stoppet. If the project is won, new processes 
with new models are introduced and traditional processes are followed: the architect defines the 
project and MT Højgaard is modelling new 3D models based on the design description. Structural 
elements are not considered before time and cost planning, where effects of material and type 
choices are measured. The traditional process is then followed with informing the architect and 
client, new information is incorporated in the architectural design, and the new design is handed 
back to MT Højgaard and new simulations can be modelled. This loop can run again and again, and 
is definitely necessary, but the focus on information sharing in the early design is lost. MT 
Højgaard states, “We work with BIM and VDC in all phases” (MT Højgaard, 2015b) but the links 
between the phases are important as well.  
Expectations of VDC outside of MT Højgaard are utopian in the sense that people are not sure if 
the concept is useable or if it is just a puplicity stunt with a dream concept. The VDC concept is 
still very diffuse and people have not really understood the concept or effects of it. The two 
investigated cases from MT Højgaard’s portfolio show challenges and that the construction 
industry in Denmark is in general reluctant to implement an organisational change, but is willing 
to implement new manageable changes. MT Højgaard is ready to follow the modern requirement

5.2 Best practise suggestions 
Best practise suggestions on implementation of VDC are given based on observations on how MT 
Højgaard has implemented VDC in their business model and applied the concept to projects. 
Attempted suggestions on what is needed in order for MT Højgaard to succeed with VDC are given 
together with my points on what went right. 
The benefit of implementing VDC in construction projects is clearly linked to early inclusion. MT 
Højgaard has tried to expand this general observation on the market, by enforcing VDC in early 
design contexts where everything from urban placement to the final building operation can be 
simulated and different proposals can be assessed and rejected or proceeded with.  
If MT Højgaard wants this to be a success they need to point out immediate benefit for other 
collaborative partners in VDC projects. MT Højgaard is still in an implementation phase 
themselves, and is also about to find own benefits, but in order to enrol VDC throughout the 
construction sector, participants need to see obvious benefits for changing working methods. MT 
Højgaard is fully aware that they are leading the VDC concept in Denmark, and in order for the 
concept to survive and thrive other AEC participants are needed to undertake organisational 
change. They can still strive to be Best in Class, but MT Højgaard has understood the gained value 
in not being alone about implementing VDC, as the value increase when other players are on the 
field. Sparring between AEC participants will create ripple effect in knowledge and experience, 
which can create new procurement parameters where quality design solutions are evaluated to 
give value in projects in the long run, where time and costs show in the finalised project.  
The general perception is that VDC + is a change of mindset. MT Højgaard needs to break this 
perception in order to convince participants of joining the VDC concept. MT Højgaard needs to 
teach others of the tangible methods and working tools for virtual constructions before real 
constructions. People outside MT Højgaard are reluctant to VDC as they feel VDC is hard to 
implement, where implementation of new hardware, installation of software and subscription to 
a service agreement seem more manageable. Extensive organisational changes may be feared and 
MT Højgaard need to spread the word concerning their success and implementation suggestions 
in their business plan, for others AEC organisations to adapt. There is no use in being the only 
ones, who can operate with VDC, as the value of VDC only increases when number of active users 
increases.  
In order to succeed in implementing VDC in an organisation, it is important to know what has 
already been a success, what is experienced and what is still in the planning phase. MT Højgaard 
has the advantage of having enrolled VDC in offices around the world, and international 
==PAGE:65==
 
5 . 3  D i s c u s s i o n  
 
P a g e  | 56 
experiences should be shared between VDC labs. MT Højgaard has prepared company visions or 
goals, which should be fulfilled by the end of 2016. After the implementation period, concrete 
goals should be measured and compared to international results, together with surveys with focus 
on successes and failures.  
The VDC models suggest iterations for optimising design solutions, and MT Højgaard is using 
iTWO not only as a calculation tool, but as a model optimiser in regard to cost and time planning. 
However, this level in the design modelling is only reached when the client provides an almost 
defined architectural design. Design considerations, which indirectly effect the design, such as 
orientation, sunlight simulations, indoor environment, erection steps on site, are not included in 
the initial design, but incorporated later in the process like traditionally processes. Workshops 
should help incorporation of all parameters of the design in the early stages, but in reality it is not 
so. MT Højgaard should be focused on not failing the step between early design workshops and 
finalising the model due to cost and time optimisation. The case of Mundi Kindergarten had the 
right approach to a VDC project, but the process was stopped after the presentation of virtual 
design effects to the client. If a procurement to institutions in Copenhagen Municipality is initiated, 
the pre-analysis so far is most likely to be discarded and the process of receiving architectural 
design files, which are built upon, is returned to. MT Højgaard differentiate between Urban VDC 
and VDC, which could be a reason of the clear differentiations of early design simulations and 
models based on architectural designs.     
VDC provides possibility for the client to evaluate numerous informative solution proposals. 
Working with implementation of VDC in an organisation places great demands to qualifications 
within IT, BIM and knowledge of VDC. People will be more and more aware of technology solutions 
both privately and professionally, which reduce the adaption period.  
Before considering implementation of VDC, it is important, like MT Højgaard did, to conduct a 
business model of VDC enrolment. This ensures a common vision and goal, and resources or the 
lack of the same are identified. Workshops are great to follow the progress both in the 
organisational progress and employees commitment. Workshops also encourage transparency 
communication and commitment to the task. It is easier to identify consequences of changes and 
enables organisations to prioritise assignments.  
5.3 Discussion 
The following section discusses the concluded answer to the initial problem definition and 
whether the applied theory was right as an analytical tool. The empirical data and how it was 
collected is evaluated and the fulfilment of the initial sub-goals is discussed.   The first relevant 
evaluation is whether the problem formulation was adequate investigated and answered.  
The initial problem statement was how implementation of VDC could influence the task for a 
structural engineer in a construction project in its early design phase. This matter has been 
==PAGE:66==
 
5 . 3  D i s c u s s i o n  
 
P a g e  | 57 
investigated by gathering information, and hereafter analysing the information by theoretical use 
of Leavitt’s diamond model and lastly concluded with best practise suggestions.  
In order to answer the problem definition investigations of observational character was 
conducted. The observational study was chosen to take place at construction and civil engineering 
company MT Højgaard since they could provide insight in cases and work methods involving VDC. 
At MT Højgaard they have been implementing the VDC model since 2012 and have established a 
VDC department, so it was natural to ask permission for insight in cases and daily routines.  
Interviews have been conducted with first hand involved people in construction projects and who 
are familiar with technology platforms for 3D modelling and information sharing. Interviewees 
have both been from MT Højgaard and from other construction companies to obtain knowledge 
about their experience with VDC. Interviewees could have included more people, but time 
constraint for this master thesis made appointments difficult. However, interviewees outside MT 
Højgaard express general thoughts, which of course are based on their company’s beliefs. The fact 
that these interviewees share several opinions with each other and with recent publications, make 
the general idea of the topic more trustworthy.  
Based on the gained knowledge for VDC implementation, it would have been nice to have specified 
the questions in the interviews even more. It would be good to have honest thoughts on how 
interviewees felt the implementation and roll-out in MT Højgaard and interaction with e.g. the 
BIM department, the finance department and human resources.  
Observations and gathered information from interviews, articles, conferences and notes were 
systematically analysed by the organisation diamond model created by H. J Leavitt with later 
additions by Ry-Nielsen. The appliance of the variables given by the model proved to be a strong 
tool for sorting and analysing gained knowledge. Other organisational models could definitely 
prove to be strong analytical tools, and would maybe have changed the analytical approach, which 
could give different focus and another outcome.  
In order to answer the problem definition a number of sub-goals were introduced. One was to 
create a fulfilling mapping of Leavitt’s Diamond organisation model. The theory of Leavitt’s 
Diamond was thoroughly studied and presented before introducing empirical data in order to 
exploit the organisational theory for analysing the data.  
Another matter was to support the concept VDC with Leavitt’s theory, which was implemented in 
the analysis where gathered information was systematically sorted and presented with Leavitt’s 
diamond variables. The link between VDC and the implementation of its methods were supported 
by Integrated Product Delivery methods. By combining the methods this was kept transparent 
and obvious.

5.4 Perspectivation 
With hardware and software vendors and service providers selling their products aggressively, 
there is a technology push for a VDC model today. The major driver for VDC methods is a market 
of demand. Already established AEC participants have seen the value in 3D modelling and virtual 
application in a competitive market of “faster, better, cheaper” products and services. VDC 
methods and modern technology can help achieve such competitive advantages. Company’s 
tendency to engage one specific vendor of software and service would be interesting to 
investigate.  If companies feel more comfortable with the same systems, which they have used for 
years, they could be more reluctant to choose new and perhaps better technology solutions.  
Another result of VDC implementation can be increased standard solutions in construction 
projects. Mundi Kindergarten and Kildeparken 2020 are indirectly cases of standardisation in 
project models, which could indicate a tendency even though no direct indication has been found 
in cases or interviews. VDC models do however, base themselves on BIM models where 3D models 
are central. 3D models are object-oriented and non-standardised elements are both time and cost 
consuming. VDC introduces iteration models where quantity and costs are based on a 3D model 
with information parameters, which encourage merging of similar material choices to optimise 
the model concerning purchase prices, transportation units and assembling simplification for 
contractors. Unique architect models could very well end up being standardised if the client is 
more influenced by cost and time restraints. However, structural engineers could benefit from the 
increased standardisation if complex joints are reduced, which would be very interesting to 
evaluate with multiple interviewees from the AEC industry.  
The question of incapacitation challenges in procurements discussed in section 4.1.8 would be 
really interesting to dive into in future studies. There are obvious consequences involved with 
holding information other bidders are unaware of before procurements, where VDC opens for the 
possibility of being involved on a basis that corrupt the legislations on procurements. Early 
involvement engage earlier knowledge of the topic, and interface platforms are changed with 
implementation of VDC, so legislations should be changed accordingly.

Hvad er dine erfaringer med VDC? 
Man skal generelt passe på med at gøre det til mere end det er. 
Refererer til lægeverden: hvis en patient er syg, bliver vedkommende undersøgt med forskellige 
værktøjer og der bliver stillet en diagnose samt behandling til patienten. Det er det samme med 
VDC: det er et værktøj til at eksaminere en problemstilling. 
VDC må ikke erstatte design processerne.  
Beregninger, statik og konstruktionsberegninger forandres ikke, det er en drøm at man kan trykke 
på en knap og man så får det færdige resultat. 
Man skal ikke være bange for at konstruktions- og statiske beregninger går tabt, altså at folk ikke 
har noget arbejde, da man stadig skal bruge den slags kompetencer i byggebranchen. 
VDC bringer ikke selve løsningen. VDC er et værktøj, som kan forstærke kompetencer. Men en 
dårlig konstruktionsingeniør kan blive endnu dårligere med sådan et værktøj.  
Nyuddannede statikere og konstruktionsingeniører får topkarakterer i statikfag, men kan ikke løse 
simple opgaver uden et program.  
MTH bruger f.eks. Strusoft 3D til beregningsmodeller. Det er et svært program og meget 
tidskrævende. 
Virkeligheden er, at der ikke er tid og budget til oplæring. 
Opgaven Frederikskaj 2, var et godt VDC projekt, hvor alle midler blev brugt eks. dem fra 
betonforeningens hjemmeside. 
Statiske analyseprogrammer er oftest begrænsede og har fejl og mangler. Fx kan Strusoft ikke 
regne plastisk uden man snyder programmet ved at skrue på parametre, hvor man igen skal vide

hvad man gør. Men det kommer nok, og engang kan man alt det man drømmer om i dag, og bare 
trykke på en knap og få resultatet.  
Afdelingen bruger det til tilbudsfasen. Tegner i 3D og beregningsmodeller. De ansatte 
konstruktionsingeniører er jo ikke statikere, men de har værktøjerne til at give kvalificerede bud. 
Oftest starter man så forfra efter man har vundet sagen. Tidligere modeller smides ud og man 
starter på ny ud fra arkitektens model. Nu er rammerne relativt faste, hvorved man kan smide 
parametrene ind i eks. Strusoft. 
Man skal passe på med at modellere parallelt med arkitekten, da man kan ende med to vidt 
forskellige modeller som ikke er sammenkoblet. 
Hvorfor skal man bygge virtuelt i 3D først? Det kan kræve tid og økonomi. Man behøver ikke det 
store arsenal hver gang.  
Den daglige udfordring er tid, budget og honorar. Så man skal bruge VDC med en sund skepsis.  
Er drømmen lige om hjørnet? Der er i hvert fald et stykke vej endnu. 
Programmet iTWO kommer på banen efter designfasen. Design løses ikke i iTWO. 
Man burde have et program med en motor tilbage til design. Man skal jo vide en masse inden man 
kan lægge det ind i modellen, så der burde være en sammenkobling til 
konstruktionsberegningerne. Designprogrammet burde kunne håndtere alt design, altså også 
brand, ventilationsskakte, indeklima osv. 
Man har jo kvalificerede bud til konstruktionselementer opbygget i Revit, som bruges videre til 
iTWO, men man skal hele tiden beregne stabilitet og statik og vende tilbage til designet.  
Der drømmes om genbrug af modeller fra design processer.

2. Baggrund indenfor VDC 
Erfaringer med VDC? 
Brugt 3D siden 2000. Fra 2005 og frem brugt Tekla og stål+beton modelleret i 3D. Har gjort det 
længe, så har det lidt ligesom VDC er det nye sort. Rambøll laver designet og så stopper vi. Holder

Hvilke projekter / projekt-type har været oplagte at udføre som VDC projekter? Hvad gik godt / 
dårligt? 
Bella Sky. F.eks. blev svært armeret hjørner modelleret og præsenteret i 3D for at give rumlig 
forståelse. Kollisionskontrol var et stærkt værktøj, da f.eks. dørhuller og døre ikke passe sammen;

lavet modeller længe.

for de vanskelige dele og det overordnede. Noget bliver outsourcet til Indien, både billigere og 
hurtigere.

3. Definition af VDC-projekter 
Note: Da Rambøll ikke definerer deres værktøjer og metoder ud fra ”VDC”, tales der mere generelt 
om 3D/BIM og virtuelt design.  
Modellen er vigtig internt at tale sammen om. Ved flere modeller går der nogen gange 
informationer tabt. Vigtigt med pc på byggepladsen, for hurtigt at kunne se model i 3D. 
Konceptionelt design bliver diskuteret og hurtige modeller kan laves, men typisk af arkitekten. 
Sker så meget at en egentlig model må vente. Når projektforslaget er lavet, går Rambøll i gang 
med modeller, konstruktion, VVS, ventilation mv. Modellerne kører parallelt og typisk 
kollisionstjekkes løbende, for at sammenkoble dem. Til sidst sendes hovedprojektet videre til 
entreprenør. 
Hvad er de vigtigste værktøjer? 
BIPS standarder bruges som standard ”platform”, og danner base for fælles retningslinjer inden for 
hvert projekt. Hvilke andre standarder, der bruges, varierer fra projekt til projekt, alt efter hvad 
der ønskes fra andre spillere.  
Smartphone app fra Tekla er også et stærkt værktøj. Det bruges som model viewer; kan zoome ind 
på elementer, se deres parametre (hvilken type stålbjælke f.eks.) og lægge noter (skal skiftes ud 
eller der mangler noget f.eks.). Kan bruges til kvalitetstjek. Bruger det meget og er ikke bare en 
show off gadget.   
 
4. Tidligt VDC i forhold til konstruktionsingeniører 
Hvordan inddrages KI’er i VDC projekter og hvad er deres rolle? 
Vigtigt at modellere alt der er vigtigt for andre tværfagligt samt komplicerede 
konstruktionsområder.  
 
Er arbejdsmetoder klare og definerede (eller ændres de fra projekt til projekt)? 
Det varierer. De unge behersker typisk lidt af hvert og flere forskellige programmer. De har nogle 
superbrugere inden for hvert program, man altid kan spørge til råds, men ellers er det en fordel at 
kunne lidt af hvert.  
 
Kræves der specielle kompetencer for en KI’er (IT, proces, kurser mv.)? 
F.eks. når komplicerede hjørner modelleres. Der skal man have forståelse for konstruktioner, så 
man kan bruge programmet. KI skal f.eks. selv kunne flytte en søjle el.lign. i programmet. 
Ingeniører er med i begyndelsesforløbet for at kunne byde ind og vurdere konstruktionerne, hvor 
tekniske tegnere kommer på senere når der skal tegnes i 3D. Der tilbydes kurser internt.

Hvilke programmer og modeller anvendes i det tidlige design? 
Bruger Tekla, men bruger ellers de bedste programmer der findes, til den opgave der er. Er ikke

I forhold til at løse konstruktionsopgaver, hvilke fordele eller ulemper har digitalisering af projekter 
medbragt? 
Model skrottes inden hovedprojekt. Ud fra projektforslaget udarbejdes ny model. Det ville være 
bedre at bygge videre på modellen, i hvert fald egen model. Arkitektens model kan være svær at 
bygge videre på, da arkitekten typisk arbejder med cm og ikke mm som ingeniører gør. Typisk kan 
søjler f.eks. være placeret forkert i forhold til centerlinjer osv.  
Samarbejde på tværs er aldrig bedre end det fag, der er svagest. Alle skal være med. Pt. oplever TH 
at kloak, landskabsarkitekt og infrastruktur. Det er relativt nyt for dem at lave modeller, måske 
fordi de har tendens til at have nogle andre størrelsesforhold, så deres modellering af rør eller veje 
bliver lange tynde streger i et samlet projekt.  
Projekter i dag bruger mere tid på projekteringsfasen, men vil folk betale for det? Det tager 
længere tid, før bygherrer ser resultatet. Kommer an på bygherren om han er klar til det. Til 
gengæld er der større indflydelse i begyndelsen ift. senere, hvor ændringer kan koste dyrt og tage 
lang tid.  
Virtuelt byggeri kan føre andre nye ting med sig: rengøringsudbud kunne hentes hjem baseret på 
udbudmaterialet, hvor bygningen kunne ses af rengøringsfirmaet og eksempelvis gulvarealer 
kunne hentes ud af modellen.  
Eksempelvis kunne placeringen af receptionen i Rambøll hovedkontor fastsættes ud fra den 
digitale model. Hvor gave det mest mening ift. lys, walk flow osv. Indretning af kantinen blev også 
fastlagt ud fra modellen.

Værktøjer er blevet mere avanceret og ingeniører skal i dag være i stand til at bruge dem.
