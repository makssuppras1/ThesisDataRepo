Title: Risk management practices in construction projects: Case study 
Landssimareitur in the center of Reykjavik  
 
 
 
Department: DTU Management Engineering  
 
The primary objective of this master project was to learn and analyze the 
project risk management methodology and best practices in the construction 
industry. Likewise, to gain knowledge and experience in analyzing and 
evaluating different types of risks that could arise during a project’s life cycle 
and how these risks can be mitigated. The motivation for this thesis was to 
enhance the efficiency of a large engineering company in Iceland by proactive 
risk identification and mitigation. 
As those who work in the industry know, it is all too common for construction 
projects to go over budget and time schedule because of their uncertain 
environments. This can result in poor quality of deliverables and can in many 
cases be traced back to bad project management and a lack of identifying risks 
during the pre-project planning phase. The final objective of this thesis was 
therefore to provide guidelines to implementing methods and procedures in 
the project risk management process in large construction engineering projects 
and to conduct an extensive risk assessment on a project case. 
In order to achieve these objectives, several phases of the project risk 
management process were carried out. A review of tools, methods and 
standards was conducted to expand the knowledge and analyze the current 
best project risk management practices. Interviews with experienced project 
managers, project owners and designers were conducted, where the author 
gained first-hand insight from practitioners on the topic. Based on the review, 
three risk analysis tools were further thoroughly analyzed and compared. Out 
of these tools, RamRisk from Rambøll was chosen to be applied to a real-life 
project case, which is the large Landssimareitur construction project in the 
center of Reykjavik. The chosen project case is an active project at EFLA 
Consulting Engineers and entails the construction of a high-quality hotel, 
apartments, restaurants and other service entities in Iceland. In addition, 
project risk management practices at EFLA Consulting Engineers were 
reviewed and documented. 
Based on the objectives of the project, the results are mainly twofold. The 
author gained experience and knowledge of the current best project risk 
management practices through the literature review and interviews. The 
applicability of the computer-based tool in a real-life project case was 
presented to demonstrate one of the best existing practice methods in project 
risk management. To make the RamRisk tool applicable to the 
Landssimareitur case a comprehensive checklist was worked out. It contains 
over 100 risks that can occur during the project’s life cycle of a construction 
project as well as mitigation actions and procedures for these risks. In 
conclusion, the thesis demonstrates the importance of conducting project risk 
management for large construction projects and its impact on cost, schedule 
and quality.

In 2008, the financial and economic crisis had adverse impacts in Europe and it had, inevitably, 
significant impact in Iceland. The Gross Domestic Product (GDP1) of Iceland has been growing since 
2010, after a huge drop from 2007 to 2009. In these years, the construction sector, one of the engines 
of economic growth, faced several challenges. These challenges included increasing unemployment 
rate, closure of construction companies and cancelled investments. In these years, the behavior has 
also changed through the value chain, from clients to contractors. Nowadays, there is an increase in 
the tourism industry that has influenced the construction industry. With higher demand for housing, 
the construction industry has grown exponentially and went from 428.000 m3 in 2009 to 484.500 m3 
on average each year for the past 5 years just for residential buildings [1]. With a high demand of 
quality, a constant pressure to reduce the cost and increased competition on the market, the need for 
managing and monitoring project risks is high and growing.  
Nine knowledge areas are introduced by the Project Management Institute (PMI) and one of them is 
risk management [2]. Since risks in engineering projects are known to be complex, with high 
uncertainty and dynamic, risk management is essential to achieve project’s objectives. Project risk 
management is defined as a systematic process that helps identify, analyze and respond to project 
risks in time [3]. Each of these steps includes techniques and methods that can conquer and handle 
the risk. Since risks and uncertainties can harm the objectives of a project, risk assessment and 
management is a major key of project management in engineering projects to avoid or deal with 
unexpected events that can occur during the Project Life Cycle (PLC). Due to rapid development in 
engineering projects and the need to strive for success of delivering projects to clients on time, on 
budget and with high quality, risk management practice is essential from the initial stages of the PLC. 
Decisions that are made early in the PLC can have large impact on projects in later stages. Using best 
practices in risk management enables stakeholders that are involved in projects to make better 
decisions earlier and therefore meet their objectives when it comes to cost, time and quality.

leading company for almost 4 decades in project management in Iceland. EFLA is aware of the 
usefulness of project risk management practices in large engineering projects. Therefore, this master

2.1 Overview of project risk management  
Risk is defined by PMI’s Project Management Body of Knowledge as “an uncertain event or 
condition that, if it occurs, has an effect on at least one project objective. Objective can include, 
scope, schedule, cost and quality” [2]. 
Risk events can be divided into negative events and positive events. Project risk management practice 
is to minimize negative events and their impact and increase the probability and impact of positive 
events. PMBOK guide, PSFPRM and ISO have introduced processes that project risk management 
involves and can support managers to meet project objectives. The five processes mentioned below 
interact with each other and with other processes in other knowledge areas:  
• Planning risk management 
• Risk identification 
• Risk analysis 
o Qualitative  
o Quantitative 
• Plan risk response  
• Monitor and control risks

According to PMBOK guide and the PSFPRM, organizations should be committed to address risks 
throughout the life cycle of the project to be successful. To be able to manage risk systematically in 
projects, all these steps in risk management process needs to be fulfilled.  
The treatment of project risks in every phase is essential to carry out successful project management 
since many uncertainties are involved in the PLC that stretch from initial concept to a final. Project 
risk management is not a standalone framework that has no relations to project management. Project 
scheduling with critical paths, cost estimations and resource availability is assumed to be a certain 
thing. To explore this and give accurate estimates regarding timeframe, costs and resource availability 
risk analysis is performed. “Time is money,” said Benjamin Franklin, insinuating that if you do not 
spend your time right you lose money. This can be redirected to the PLC of any project. Considering 
risk at an early stage of the project along with establishing realistic expectation about cost estimates, 
time frame and resource availability, is likely to save the project owner a fair bit of money in the end. 
Furthermore, the stakeholder influence, risk and uncertainty lowers after the project time evolves as 
can be seen in Figure 1, as depicted by Project Management Institute [2], [6].

Good risk management practice enhances the value of other project management processes and 
should always be carried out on an ethical basis. Carrying out project risk management in projects 
can differ between projects, depending on issues such as the size of the project or other activities and 
should be in proportion to the project’s objectives [2].

A fundamental aspect for a successful plan risk management process is the support of all involved

Identifying risks 
To be able to deliver the project objectives, the risks need to be identified and documented early in 
the PLC. However, not all risk can be identified early because new risk events will be exposed as the 
project develops, that can affect earlier decisions. Identifying risk early on can cause changes in the 
project strategy and maximize development and risk response time. Additionally, it will increase 
efficiency and decrease the response cost. This process is iterative throughout the PLC and should be 
repeated periodically according to what was planned in the plan risk management process. As this 
plan can change during the PLC, sudden changes in the objectives of the project could also lead to 
changes in the risk management plan. 
A range of tools and techniques exist, created to identifying risks for the past, present and future. 
These three categories can be assessed with historical review, current assessments and with creativity 
techniques respectively. For identifying risks in the past, a review of what has occurred in the past in 
the current project or review of older similar projects is done. The current assessment is a thorough 
review of the current project and is purely based on that particular project. To identifying possible 
future risks, creativity techniques are used. These rely on the creativity of the stakeholders that take 
part in the risk-identification session. It is essential for the project to use one in each category, past, 
present and future e.g. identification checklist, assumptions analysis and brainstorming. 
Proper documentation is required in order to keep a solid framework of risk management. To capture 
all relevant information from the risk identification process, a risk register is conducted on the project 
objectives. Risk register includes risk descriptions, assigned risk owners, cause and effects of the risk, 
and the initial response to that risk [2][6].

Planning risk responses 
This process is critical to mitigate or take advantages of threats that have been identified, analyzed 
and prioritized into categories. The risk response plan should enhance the chances of achieving the 
project objectives in terms of budget, schedule, resources and the project’s scope. In this process, risk 
owners are assigned to risk events in their field of specialty and are responsible for mitigating the said 
risks or terminating them effectively in a timely manner. However, risk owners could also be assigned 
in other processes e.g. risk identification process. Mitigating or terminating risks with risk responses 
can also create other threats, known as secondary risk, or opportunities that could lead to changes in 
the project objective. Moreover, secondary risks must go through the same process as the initial risk 
event. Risk response planning are divided into three categories:  
• People  
• Planning 
• Analysis

Avoidance/prevention 
Avoidance strategy is used when the risk event is evaluated to have a very high impact on the project 
objective. The risk avoidance is the last resource that is used if none of the other strategies work. If a 
risk event is that critical that it has very high impact on the project objective and cannot be mitigated 
or transferred, the project management team might have to review the project’s aim and change the 
scope or the project objective. Furthermore, it could lead to cancellation of the project if no other 
options are available [8], [7].  
If the need for a major change arises in projects, Darnall and Preston suggest applying known methods 
instead of completely new methods. Since the old methods are known, the project managers are more 
likely to be familiar with them rather than the new methods and therefore more able to get the desired 
results [9].   
Few risk prevention methods exist that are meant to eliminate the risk source completely or reduce 
the probability of them occurring. Cooper, Grey and Raymond provide this list of examples in the 
book “Project risk management guidelines: managing risk in large projects and complex 
procurements” [7]:  
• More detailed planning  
• The selection of alternative approaches 
• Improving designs and systems engineering, or adopting enhanced design standards 
• Procedural change 
• Permits to work 
• Protection and safety systems 
• Preventive maintenance 
• Formal processes and quality assurance procedures 
• Operations reviews 
• Regular inspections and audits 
• Training and skills enhancement. 
 
Reduction/mitigation 
Mitigation of a risk event is linked to minimizing the impact of an event that could harm the project 
objectives. Some events are not controllable and cannot be reduced but mitigating controllable events 
==PAGE:33==
14 
 
is essential to keep the project objectives in sight. The risk events are still there, but are reduced to a 
level that can be coped with. Like for the risk avoidance, Cooper, Grey and Raymond provide this 
list of examples [7]:  
• Contingency planning 
• Engineering and structural barriers 
• Reparation or relocation of an activity and resources 
• Quality assurance 
• Contract terms and conditions 
• Regular audits and checks to detect compliance or information security breaches 
• Crisis management and disaster recovery plans 
Reducing the likelihood of a risk event happening could also include redesigning the project or 
sharing the risk with other stakeholder that have knowledge and resources about the consequences. 
Each risk event should be signed to stakeholders with the most insight, knowledge and vison. Also, 
by sharing the risks, the responsibilities are also shared between more than one stakeholder, which 
can be of a benefit to the project [9].   
Transfer 
Risk transfer is not the same as eliminating risk. Rather, it is a strategy to transfer the risk event to 
another stakeholder that has more recourses and is capable of delivering a more accurate outcome. 
This could lead to additional fees that correspond to higher costs. Common routes for transferring the 
risk were identified by Perry and Hayes in 1985 [7]:  
• Client to contractor 
• Contractor to subcontractor 
• Client, contractor, subcontractor or designer to insurer 
• Contractor or subcontractor to surety 
When risk is transferred in whole from one stakeholder to another, all the responsibilities also transfer 
with it. Uncontrollable risks, such as weather conditions, that are beyond the capabilities of the project 
management team, should be transferred to insurance companies [9]. 
Retention 
Risk events that can neither be managed, avoided, mitigated or transferred, need to be retained. Even 
though the risk cannot be managed at the time, monitoring the risk event is advised because 
sometimes the project objectives, the scope or other unforeseen event change the risk plan. In the case 
==PAGE:34==
15 
 
that a risk event occurs that is not manageable, that risk event could become manageable later on, but 
could lead to higher costs [7]. 
When project risk management team has planned the risk responses, an update of the project 
management plan is needed in terms of costs, resources, scheduling and other changes for the 
overview of the whole framework [2], [6].  
Monitoring and controlling risks 
The main objective of this process is to monitor and control the risk throughout the PLC, because by 
monitoring and controlling the risk from the beginning enhances the project risk management. Project 
stakeholders play essential role in this process by identifying new risks, monitoring and tracking other 
risks and carrying out the risk response at the appropriate time. 
To build a successful and best project risk management practice, several factors need to be aligned 
with each other. Project Management Institute presents these factors [6]: 
• Recognize the value of risk management 
• Individual commitment/responsibility 
• Good communications 
• Organizational commitment 
• Scale risk effort to projects 
• Integrate with project management 
 ISO 31000 
ISO 31000 standard is an overall framework and guidance for risk management principles. This 
standard was published in November 2009 after four years in development and with the involvement 
of many stakeholders. Around 29 representative interest groups from different countries worked on 
making this standard come true and it is therefore very recognized. This standard is designed for a 
wide range spectrum of organizational risk management and is a generic approach to risk 
management. It provides a systematic and transparent form of managing risk in organizational 
perspective, gives the foundation for risk management process and ensures common understanding 
of the organizational goals. The relationship between the risk management principles, framework and 
process can be seen in Figure 6 as depicted by ISO [10], [11].  
ISO 31000 is meant to fit every industry or sector of any type and size, since the standard provides 
generic guidelines. These 11 principles, frameworks and processes can be synchronized with future

 Challenges/Risks in project life cycle of construction projects 
The construction industry includes a lot of risk due to its complexity and nature. The PLC in the 
construction industry can be divided into four to six phases depending on which literature is reviewed, 
==PAGE:36==
17 
 
but all these phases are very similar. According to Chapman and Ward, the phases are four, 
Conceptualization, Planning, Execution and Termination phase [12]. Bennett represented the life 
cycle in six phases: Pre-project phase, Planning and design phase, Contractor selection phase, 
Contractor mobilization phase, Project operation phase and Project closeout and termination phase 
[13]. The project risk is not dependent on where the risk occurs in the PLC, it can occur in every 
phase of the PLC. But research has shown that unmanaged risk in early phases can result in problems 
in later phases, therefore it indicates that carrying out risk management procedures in early stages is 
beneficial for the project objectives. Lyon and Skitmore show in their studies that risk management 
usage is more likely to be used in the planning and execution phase [14]. In addition, Westland 
suggested that risk management practice should be carried out in every phase of the PLC and it is not 
terminated until the last phase of the project is completed [15]. 
Risk in the PLC can be divided into external and internal risks. External risks are more likely to be 
non-controllable and are e.g. currency fluctuation, change in technology, politics, climate and weather 
conditions, and natural disasters. Due to high uncertainty of external risks, the project managers need 
to forecast and be on the lookout for any changes that can harm the project objectives. Internal risks 
are more likely to be controllable, e.g. labor, plant, resources, site conditions and material, and 
subcontractors. These risks can vary between projects since no projects are the same.

 Challenges in project risk management practices  
When using risk management practice, project risk managers face several challenges. These major 
challenges have remained the same for several decades. In a fast-pace environment like today, the 
pressure of delivering quality output with increased cost and time constraints with high uncertainties 
can lead to more severe consequences. Danesh-Mand, who is the director of risk management 
department at integrated project services consultancy Aquenta consulting and president of risk 
engineering society, says that project teams need to understand and foster the project scope to be more 
realistic regarding costs and schedule [18].  
==PAGE:39==
20 
 
Risks identification, assessment and response to risk are very challenging even though ISO 31000, 
PMI and other guidelines have made a framework to serve project risk managers. Using methods, 
techniques and tools can alter and support good risk management practices. But challenges are always 
around the corner that need to be addressed, because challenges in project risk management practice 
can vary between projects, by its complexity and size. Kulikova addressed challenges in her master’s 
thesis [19], in the perspective of unpredictable risks, scoring method and the implementation of risk 
responses. Unpredictable risks in projects vary from theory and real-live situations, when complex 
projects with high degree of uncertainty cannot be predicted in real life as in theory. Scoring methods 
in project risk management practices is a limitation according to Kulikova, because of the broad use 
of scoring methods for evaluating risk, the overconfidence of the experts can influence their decisions, 
interpretation of likelihood can differ and the scoring methods regarding ranges of probability of 
occurrence can add risks. In addition to these two factors, the implementation of risk response in 
projects can also be a challenge, because it is not addressed as a separate step in risk management 
framework. For new practitioners, this implementation can be often forgotten [19].  
Danesh-Mand mentions three key challenges in effective project risk management practices: team 
culture, the scope of the project and realistic project planning [18]. These are discussed below. 
Team culture 
This factor can either work for you or against you. Having transparent and effective collaboration 
between team members and accountability that all are working on the same objective as a team and 
not as an individual can alter the project outcome. Focusing on their own needs and demands will 
decrease the likelihood of reaching the project’s objective. Since project management operates in a 
fast pace and global environment the likelihood of diversity increases. Team culture can impact 
working speed, decision making and the desire to act [18], [2].    
The scope of the project 
This challenge involves that the project team should understand the project scope with its parameters 
and look at reasons for delays and how they can impact the project delivery. The PMI definitions of 
the project scope is “the work that needs to be accomplished to deliver a product, service, or result 
with the specified features and functions” [2]. Defining the scope in projects is crucial to the project. 
==PAGE:40==
21 
 
The scope is built on deliverables, assumptions and constraints. Moreover, the scope is finalized in 
more detail when all factors are known in the planning phase [18].  
Realistic project planning  
The third and the final challenge that Danesh-Mand believes important to deliver a successful project 
risk management practice is realistic project planning. Realistic project planning involves cost, 
schedule and risk, and should be accessible as early as possible. The challenge involves that all 
participants have common understanding of the likelihood of delivering the project on time and within 
the budget with continued monitoring of risk that could occur. It is a well-known issue that contractors 
and project managers are over optimistic on their plans, which can therefore lead to overruns in cost 
and schedule. Danesh-Mand says being optimistic in every discipline area can decrease the 
confidence level of the project [18].

The numerous techniques in the planning phase of the project indicate that more time should be spent 
on strategic issues regarding risk assessment in that phase rather than the conceptualization phase. 
To be able to see the differences between these techniques and tools a brief analysis is done in this 
section. 
Brainstorming 
Brainstorming is used in risk identification, both in the conceptualization and the planning phase to 
reach a comprehensive list of risks. Additionally, it is used in the execution phase for risk response. 
This technique is used to identify risk that could happen in the project in shortest time. It is a free-
flowing conversation between relevant stakeholders in the project, such as project manager, 
designers, architects and owners. The advantages of this technique are that it can be both formal an 
informal and it allows for a creative brainstorming to identify many risk events that could happen 
during the PLC. Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) can be used as a framework as the risk are 
categorized and sharpened [21], [2].   
 
==PAGE:43==
24 
 
Cause and effect diagram 
This method is very useful to identify causes and effects of a certain risk. Risk can be positive or 
negative, and therefore it enables the experts’ considerations of all possible scenarios. Cause and 
effect diagram is a well-structured method where participants focus on a specific problem were the 
objective is to identify possible root causes and to analyze the problems so actions can be taken [21]. 
Checklist 
This technique is used to identify risk in the initial phases. Likewise, it can be used to assess the 
effectiveness of control from older projects. It is mainly based on historical information and 
knowledge from other similar projects and is developed from experience and results from past 
failures. During and at the end of each project, it is very important that project team reviews the list 
to gain more experience through lessons learned [21], [2].  
Delphi 
This technique is like the brainstorming technique, but the difference is that experts express their 
opinion individually and anonymously while knowing what other experts, unknown to them, have 
expressed during the process and can comment on their responses. This is done with iterative actions 
throughout the process until consensus is reached. It is mainly used in the conceptualization and the 
planning phases, but have been used in every phase. Delphi technique is used were and when the view 
of an experts is needed [21], [2]. 
Event Tree Analysis 
This technique is built like a tree that starts with the initial event and then calculating and rank the 
scenarios that could initiate the initial event. This analysis is usually done in the conceptualization 
and the planning phase and represents an output of qualitative and quantitative descriptions as can be 
seen in Table 4. This analysis need a technique to identify the initial event and only takes in success 
and failure states. The advantages of using this analysis is that it examines the following scenarios 
and the influence and shows it in a diagram [21].    
Risk Breakdown Matrix 
This matrix is generated from RBS and Work Breakdown Structure (WBS). It connects these two 
techniques, that have commonly four levels of increasing detail, where WBS is the basic framework 
for management of a project and RBS is a tool to structure the risk management process. Risk 
Breakdown Structure identifies and categorizes the risk using methods like brainstorming or 
==PAGE:44==
25 
 
interviews and then the lowest identified level in the RBS combined with the WBS producing the 
two-dimensional matrix. The Risk Breakdown Matrix is used in the planning phase of the PLC and 
provides a qualitative and quantitative analysis by calculating two components, the probability and 
the degree of impact [23].  
Interviewing 
Interviewing can be both semi-structured and structured interview. Semi-structured interview is akin 
to a formal conversation with guidance from the interviewer asking open-ended questions, but 
structured interviews has prepared interview questions. These interviews techniques are used to 
identify issues that can arise and identify risks from different perspective. Interviewing can be used 
throughout the PLC in different risk management process as can be seen in Table 4 and it is applicable 
when it is hard to get all stakeholder to participate in a brainstorming meeting [21]. Also, interviews 
are a technique to conduct relative information about the probability and impact of certain risks that 
could harm the project objective [2]. 
Risk mapping, risk matrix, probability and impact matrix 
This technique is to produce a level of risk or risk ranking from various risk events combining 
qualitative and semi-quantitative data. It is a two-dimensional matrix where risks are prioritized 
according to their impact on the project objective. The risk can be rated as “high”, “medium” or “low” 
importance when two factors are considered, probability and consequences. These two scale, 
consequences and the probability, can have any numerical scale, but the probability scale needs to be 
selected that spans the range of the study and needs to be as unambiguous as possible [21], [2].  
Decision tree analysis 
This technique is similar to event tree analysis in a way that is starts with an initial event and it 
represents decisions alternatives and outcomes. Additionally, it helps to select the best available 
alternatives when the outcome of actions is uncertain. This technique combines the probability and 
cost to evaluate the better alternative. It represents clear graphical vision of details like the event tree 
analysis, but this technique can be very complex if the decision tree becomes too large. To be able to 
identify the relative values of the alternate actions for the project it incorporates the monetary value 
analysis [21], [2].  
 
 
==PAGE:45==
26 
 
Expected monetary value  
As mentioned above, this analysis is combined within the Decision tree analysis. It calculates the 
average outcome of a risk event that involves scenarios that could happen. This analysis generates 
either positive or negative values. Opportunities are expressed as positive values and threats as 
negative values, when multiplying values of the outcomes by the probability of the event happening 
and then adding the products together. This analysis is used in the planning and execution phase of 
the PLC and evaluates multiple scenarios through quantitative data [2], [22].   
Expert judgement 
Expert judgement is very useful when dealing with projects that are high in uncertainty. Experts can 
be used in previously explained methods, such as brainstorming and Delphi. In the conceptualization 
and planning phases of the PLC, the experts are very valuable in identifying risks that could occur 
and affect the project objectives. These experts should be pointed out by project managers [2].    
Fault tree analysis 
This technique, like decision trees and event trees, is built up like a tree with a top event that is 
undesirable. To be able to identify the failure, a pathway of causes is structurally organized as a tree 
with OR and AND gates. The factors identified, that cause the top event to happen, can be akin to 
component failures, human error or other events. A very good understanding of the project is needed 
so no failure events are forgotten. This analysis can be used qualitatively to find the causes and 
pathways or quantitatively to calculate the probability of the top event to happen. Using the gates can 
access the possibility of two factors combined causing the failure. Additionally, fault tree analysis 
can be used to choose between design options. This systematic approach is a flexible top-down 
technique that can include many interactions, including human interactions [21]. 
Fuzzy logic 
Fuzzy logic is an advanced quantitative technique that can be applied to scheduling, control of cost 
and multi-criteria selection. This technique is used in the planning phase of the project to reduce 
human errors and provide quicker decisions. Besides, fuzzy logic analyses the risk when access to 
data and knowledge is limited. Using expert opinions in the beginning to identify the risk in this 
bottom-up technique and focusing on the relationships between key factors, can provide a strong 
foundation of risk assessment [24], [25]. 
 
==PAGE:46==
27 
 
Sensitivity analysis 
Sensitivity analysis is used to address uncertainty and find out factors that are most sensitive for risk. 
This technique is also called “what-if analysis” and it determines how sensitive the risk is to change 
if another input is inserted. Also, identifying factors that need to be accurate is one of the key elements 
of this technique. This technique is normally used in the planning and execution phases of a project 
[21].  
Monte Carlo simulation  
This technique is used if the project is complex, with high uncertainty and cannot be solved with 
analytical methods. According to the ISO 31010 standard, this technique is useful for two different 
scenarios. These are uncertainty propagation on conventional analytical model and for probability 
calculations when other techniques cannot be used [21]. Several computer-based tools use Monte 
Carlo simulations to assist with risk identification in the planning phase of a project.    
 Computer-based Risk Management tools 
Non-computer-based tool are well recognized in the industry and are used by specialists, but the need 
for computer-based tools is also there. Computer-based simulation tools are mainly applied in larger 
and complex projects that have great amount of uncertainty and risk. These techniques and tools have 
great advantage if historical data can be accessed. Monte Carlo simulation is one of the better-known 
methods used by practitioners.  
Since computer-based software tools place an essential role on large scale engineering projects, it 
was concluded that these tools needed more attention by the author. Risk management software tools 
can be divided into two categories, standalone software or built-in software (add-on).  
Using computer-based tools can both have advantages and disadvantages. The advantages are that 
they save time and eliminate the complexity of all the available techniques. These software tools 
enable a user-friendly environment for project risk managers and the latest techniques are in one 
place. Computer-based tools provide an overview of reports and continuous monitoring that risk 
managers need to control and mitigate the project risk. The disadvantages of these tools are that they 
could add unforeseen risks by inserting wrong data into the software and therefore result in an 
inaccurate report. In addition, these software tools need experienced operators to deliver right outputs 
and reports.

Primavera Risk Analysis tool 
This software tool has been analyzed by others and will be briefly introduced in this section. 
Primavera Risk Analysis tool provides a range of benefits regarding analyzes of risk impacts, project 
cost and schedule. In addition, it provides a full life-cycle approach and has the ability to identify, 
analyze, mitigate and control risks. The methodology that Primavera uses is the framework that is 
presented in PMBOK. Primavera risk management process steps are following [19][26]: 
• Schedule check 
• Risk identification 
• Develop risk model 
• Analysis and review 
• Final model and report 
==PAGE:48==

Primavera Risk Analysis tool used advanced Monte Carlo simulation to analyze the input that is 
inserted into the risk register and risk templets. Primavera analysis provides various reporting from 
the analysis such as risk histograms, tornados and scatter plots. Besides, risk managers can access 
risk through portfolio level and project level by selecting and making decisions about projects and 
project level risks through linked schedule. To be able to provide the full life-cycle approach 
Primavera Risk Analysis tool uses these risk models, task existence, estimate-, fixed-cost-, variable 
cost-, and resource uncertainty, in addition to conditional and probability branching and weather 
modeling [26].     
Even though Primavera Risk Analysis tool is considered one of the best toolset for risk management, 
some limitations and flaws have been found within the software. These are [19]: 
• Resource leveling that could lead the management team to have inaccurate schedule. 
• Conditional events representing in branches that could be modeled as a different outcome, but 
when running the simulation, it is not an option to reflect on conditional events as the tool 
does not consider conditional probabilities. 
• Weather modeling in the weather calendar cannot be changed if the distribution has been 
estimated in the beginning. 
• Cluster risk is not an option. A combination of risk that is similar in nature cannot be taken 
into account. 
• Criticality index is hard to manage when automatic resource leveling is used because it is hard 
to identify how often activities appear in the critical path and therefore the criticality index 
depends mainly on logical sequence of the activities. The flaw occurs when the criticality 
index appears on the tornado analysis and leads to inconsistency in the software. This 
inconsistency could lead project managers to make wrong decisions in critical moments in the 
PLC. 
More comprehensive analysis can be access through Kulikova’s [19] master thesis “Managing risk 
in large engineering projects; tools, standards and practices” found in the online DTU library.

According to a specialist from Rambøll, risk mangers act in an environment with limited resources 
such as people, money and more. Therefore, the analysis, evaluation and treatment of the project 
objectives need to be in a special loop, see Figure 22.  
 
Figure 22: Advice risk loop of analysis, evaluation and treatment 
The advised method of doing the analysis, evaluation and risk treatment is shown in Figure 23. When 
a risk event is analyzed for the first time, the analysis is marked “Gross.” Then the evaluation of that 
specific risk event against the risk criteria is compared. If the risk is not in the “acceptable zone” of 
the risk matrix, the risk needs to go under risk treatment. Risk managers and risk owners have an 
obligation to find a way to mitigate the risk in a way that is executed and aligned with resources and 
budget. After the first risk treatment, the risk managers analyze the risk with “Present” stage, where

resources and budget, the risk analysis is brought to “Nett” stage, where all controls and actions are 
implemented for the risk treatment. When all risk treatments have been implemented and the risk has

3.8 RiskRisk objectives 
Risk objectives of the project case Landssimareitur are assigned to cost, schedule and quality. These 
three parameters are those which impact on the project outcome. Cost, schedule and quality are each 
addressed separately, but in terms of risk objectives, in most cases, they could easily turn into cost. 
In terms of cost risks objectives, these are associated with cost of the whole project. Attempting to 
limit the project cost by focusing on each individual hazard element within a particular risk event, 
could produce extra expenditures. The objective of the risk management procedures is to minimize 
the risks related to adding time to the project phase and therefore adding time to the overall schedule 
for the project. The quality of work and performance of the construction are essential in a way that 
influences the future operation of the project. With bad quality and performance, the cost will increase 
in the future regarding maintenance and operational cost. Therefore, the objectives of this parameter 
are to ensure the quality and performance of design and construction.

One of the factors of risk management process is stakeholder management. The stakeholder 
management follows the same principle steps in risk management, i.e. identification, analysis and 
mitigation. Each stakeholder is analyzed and evaluated according to project objective. The plan is to 
identify and examine the role and expectation, determine validity of demands, their authority and 
importance for each stakeholder in the project. The use of the stakeholder management can be 
twofold. The stakeholder management can be transferred into the risk identification process, by 
identifying the main stakeholders in the project and use it as an influence when identifying risks 
associated with stakeholders. In addition, a communication plan and reaction time can be arranged 
through the analysis.

Semi-structured interviews (SSIs) are focused on groups relevant to the subject. These interviews are 
carried out for fewer people than surveys sent by e-mail or mail and for much longer period with 
open-ended questions that allow the participants to explain in detail and offer more information about 
the subject. The disadvantages of SSIs are that it is time consuming and labor intensive. In addition, 
the interviewer needs to have knowledge about the topic. Another drawback is the sample size of 
those interviewed. To be able to get quality information or data, the sample size needs to be as large 
as possible [36]. The advantages of SSIs are that they are reasonably objective, the answers are more 
detailed and participants are more honest in their answers when the interview is conducted face-to-
face [37]. 
Since the interview phase was to identify the project risk management inside EFLA, project risk

5.1 Risk identification  
Identifying risks in projects is the first step of the risk assessment process and the first step in 
the iterative project risk management. Project risk management aims to identify all knowable 
risks to the project objective. To carry out a successful project risk management, the process 
needs to be iterative because not all risks can be known from the beginning.  
Based on the interviews, literature review and expert’s judgement from EFLA Consulting, a 
checklist was made on risks that could occur during construction projects. Not all risks 
conducted were applicable to the project case Landssimareitur. The checklist for the project 
case was compounded based on these 6 sources;  
• Rambøll consulting - presentation slide [38] 
• Risk Management in Construction Projects from Contractors and Owners perspectives 
[39] 
• Significant Risk Factors in Construction Projects: Contractor’s Perception - Science 
and Engineering Research [40] 
• Critical Analysis of Risk Management and Significant Impacts of its Application on 
Sichuan Post-Earthquake Reconstruction Project [41] 
• Expert judgement inside EFLA consulting firm  
• Interviews with project case stakeholders  
From these above sources, a risk checklist was made with over 100 risks that could occur during 
projects. Rambøll consulting provided presentation slides to introduce the RamRisk tool, one 
article and two master’s thesis that used similar techniques to cover risk in the construction 
industry, by conducting a survey with many stakeholder involved. These three resources, [39], 
[40] and [41] shed light on various risk factors and their impact on projects. To gather 
information on risks in the construction industry from EFLA consultancy, an e-mail was sent 
out to 35 division managers with an open-ended Excel spreadsheet, see Appendix D, with risk 
identification and analysis premises. This attempt was not successful, because only 4 out of 35 
answered the e-mail. In spite of the lack of answers from EFLA Consultancy, the information 
that came through was very helpful for the checklist. In addition, the project case stakeholders 
also gave a great insight into risk that needs to be addressed in projects.

5.2 Risk treatment 
The approach for risk treatment is to identify actions that will decrease the impact or the 
probability of the threat, while trying to increase the impact and probability of an opportunity 
of those threats. Implementing risk treatment for the project case was done through the 
RamRisk tool options, controls and actions. Number of controls for these 19 risk were 20 as 
one risk had two controls, corresponding to 20 actions. All controls need actions related to the 
overall control. As mentioned above in Section 2.2.1.3.6 Risk treatment, actions can be 
assigned to a specific control, but control can be assigned to more than one risks and all risk 
treatment need to have assigned status. The assigned status represented where in the system 
the risk is, e.g. risk assigned “Possible” represents risk treatment that has not been planned, but 
is possible. Risk treatment assigned “Done” means it has already been implemented 
successfully.  
For the project case, it was decided to implement risk treatment to access the use of the risk

6.1 Results and discussion 
The main emphasis in this thesis was to analyze the current best practice in risk management 
in large engineering projects, to identify the best methods in risk management to control risks 
and to address the importance of implementing it as a part of the project management process. 
The thesis shows that it is important to provide systematic procedures when executing project 
risk management and follow a standard such as ISO 31000 or other Project Management 
Institute guidelines. By reviewing the current best practice tools, methods and techniques for 
project risk management, a table was constructed with tools and methods which can be used as 
a guide for practitioners in the field of risk management. This table presents when and where, 
in the risk management process and a Projects’ Life Cycle, the tools should be applied during 
construction engineering projects. When going through the literature review phase of this 
thesis, the author came across several challenges in project risk management and challenges 
due to various critical risk factors that can occur during any phase of the Project Life Cycle. 
Sticking to a time schedule, staying on budget and ensuring quality of a project can often be 
challenging if risk factors are not identified early on. 
A set of interviews were conducted to gain more knowledge and give the overview of maturity 
of risk management practice inside EFLA Consulting Engineers and for the project case 
Landssimareitur. Findings from the interviews can be interpreted into two main sections: (1) 
Maturity of risk management process at EFLA Consulting Engineers and (2) risk management 
process at Landssimareitur the project case. Looking at the maturity of risk management 
processes at EFLA, the company has been trying to implement structured risk management 
procedures among its project managers for some time. It has been some struggle, even though 
clear and concise guidelines are readily available that projects risk management procedures 
should follow. The conclusion of this research is that the risk management procedures have not 
been established among EFLA’s project managers because of a knowledge gap that must be 
reduced. Moreover, EFLA has the tend to adapt risk management to some extent in large 
projects, meaning that there is no follow up during the Project Life Cycle and therefore no 
structure way of doing risk management.  
Prior to this thesis, no risk management procedures had been established for the project case 
Landssimareitur, and there is nearly no risk management knowledge amongst the designers,

even though they are very experienced in their field of work and in engineering projects. In 
addition, they have never taken part in risk management sessions, where risks are identified 
and analyzed. One of the key findings in the interview phase was that all interviewees, after 
explanation of risk management, confirmed that risk management procedures should have 
taken place or been established throughout the Project Life Cycle.  
Since risk management relies on structured processes and tools, a review of computer-based 
current best practice tools was conducted. Therefore, the emphasis on RamRisk was taken 
further, analyzed and presented to demonstrate, in a real-life project, as one of the best existing 
practice tool in risk management for large engineering projects. RamRisk is an extensive risk 
register tool that keeps track of every risk that has been identified, as well as its causes and 
effects. 
Project risk management is carried out for Landssimareitur project case. The risk identification 
process provided 121 risks that could occur during large engineering project. Out of the 121 
risks identified, 77 were part of the risk scope of the project case Landssimareitur and were 
analyzed and evaluated further through risk matrices. Besides that, mitigation controls and 
actions were conducted on 19 risks that were evaluated in the “non-acceptable” zone and 
brought down to “acceptable” zone. The results from the analysis and evaluations showed even 
better the applicability of project risk management to reduce risks that leads to increase in cost, 
overrun schedule and lack of quality. The results pointed out that three risk events contributed 
most to cost, time schedule and quality overruns. These three risk events are: 
• Delays in approval and permits, 
• Lacking competence and resources, and 
• Time schedule. 
These risks had over 82% proportion of total cost overrun, over 70% proportion of total time 
schedule overrun and 79% proportion of quality overrun.  
6.2 Limitations 
The results presented in the previous section show the applicability and need for risk 
management in engineering projects. However, there are some weaknesses in the risk data 
collection that need to be addressed. The weaknesses are regarding EFLA loosing connection 
to the project since the project manager left the project and lack of responses from EFLA 
==PAGE:125==
104 
 
employees. Besides, literature review limitations are also acknowledged because the focus is 
on the construction sector. 
The limitation that I faced during my studies is data collection for the risk identification process 
was done through literature sources, e-mails with colleagues and documents provided at EFLA 
Consulting Engineers. The author experienced a lack of e-mail response for the risk 
identification process, where only 4 out of 35 division managers answered the e-mail. This e-
mail contained an Excel spreadsheet with a simple form to fill out common risks, mitigation 
measures, rankings, etc. If the author had had a better response the results would have been 
more comprehensive and more reliable.      
In addition, communication losses and complications regarding the project case that happened 
during the project may have affected the information and data collection for the project case. 
If the former project manager for the project case Landssimareitur would have stayed on as a 
project manager, the connection and the parallel supervision throughout the master thesis 
would have helped. The author might have had the opportunity to look into risks that happened 
during the real-life project. 
Since the author had reviewed three tools in more detail the limitation to computer-based tool 
and the comparison are mainly between those. One of the findings is that the RamRisk tool 
does not provide time schedule linkage e.g. with MS project or other project management tools. 
The tool is also missing an option of simulating and optimizing the best possible results from 
the database e.g. Monte Carlo simulation, but documents from RamRisk could be exported to 
Excel for further analysis. The tool heavily relies on expert judgement to analyze and evaluate 
the risks. It is therefore highly recommended to conduct a risk assessment in teams. 
Due to time limitations of the study, it was not possible to provide full risk management 
procedures through the Project Life Cycle. However, it would be interesting to follow the 
project in real time to see how project risk management will contribute to the product in the 
end and how the assessed risks would be handle and managed in real life.  
6.3 Future research  
In this study, the author focused mainly on construction projects in his search for literature 
sources. Other field could have been interesting to look at to gain more knowledge and other 
findings that could have contributed to the thesis.  
==PAGE:126==
105 
 
For future research, the applicability of the RamRisk tool could be examined in other fields of 
study. Since this master thesis is only looking at project risks in the construction industry for a 
specific project, there is a chance to look into other sectors of engineering.  
In addition to this, the negative risk impact on a project was examined in this master thesis, but 
it would be interesting to look into risks that have positive impact on a project like 
Landssimareitur and use the RamRisk tool, since it offers the possibility to add “Opportunity” 
matrices, etc. Also, to see if and how RamRisk would be applicable in other fields of study, 
such as finance, insurance, environmental, manufacturing etc. 
Since the author is working for an engineering and consulting company, it would be interesting 
to see how risk management is carried out for the construction companies. What kind of 
techniques, tools and methods they are using or if they are using it at all. It would be beneficial 
to analyze this and similar projects from different stakeholder’s perspective. Understanding 
risk management practice each of the stakeholders can improve overall performance and can 
identify weak spots either in communication, analysis, data or lack of risk management from 
certain sides.
