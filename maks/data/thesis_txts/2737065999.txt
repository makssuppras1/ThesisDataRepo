Abstract
Latent Variable Models (LVMs) are a pivotal statistical framework used across various scientific and industrial
domains. This class of models aims to distill intricate high-dimensional phenomena into interpretable insights
and predictive capabilities. However, the pursuit of coherent and valuable representations in Latent Variable
Models (LVMs) has been challenged by the problem of statistical identifiability since the beginning.
The problem refers to situations where, given the same data, two research groups have different conclusions
based on the learned representations but claim equivalent performance in terms of fit to the data. In statistical
terminology, this situation points to a set of parameters that all lead to the same marginal likelihood of the
data but result in different latent variables. This ambiguity undermines the trust in the latent codes, posing
challenges in decision-making scenarios reliant on these structures. To fully realize the potential of LVMs
in light of an increasing amount of high-dimensional data, it is crucial to develop practical and scalable
solutions to ensure trust and reliability in conclusions based on the learned representations.
Existing approaches primarily emphasize achieving strong identifiability of model parameters through
stringent model constraints, often at the expense of model expressiveness and performance. While recent
theoretical advancements have reframed the identifiability problem in terms of latent space distributions and
associated decoder functions, practical and fruitful implementations based on these insights are lacking.
This work addresses this gap and shifts the focus from model identifiability to the identifiability of tasks
within the latent space of LVMs. Tasks such as clustering, classification, and interpolation are usually
accomplished in the latent space while relying on a distance measure. Recognizing the centrality of distance
measures in most tasks, we propose a theoretically grounded methodology for constructing a statistically
identifiable distance measure on the manifold learned by an LVM. Leveraging principles from Riemannian
Geometry, we establish the invariance of geodesic distances under indeterminacy transformations, thereby
formulating the concept of an Identifiable Latent Metric Space. Within this framework, subsequent tasks relying
on this structure become inherently identifiable, fostering trust in the model’s outputs.
In addition to theoretical work, we present a practical framework for applying these concepts and demonstrate
their efficacy on benchmark datasets such as MNIST and CIFAR10. Experimental analysis comparing against
the conventional Euclidean distance measure statistically underscores the superiority and reliability of our
proposed approach.

Resumé
Latente Variable Modeller (LVM’er) er en betydningsfuld familie af statistiske værktøjer, der anvendes på
tværs af forskellige videnskabelige og industrielle domæner. Denne modeltype sigter mod at destillere
komplekse højdimensionelle fænomener ned til fortolkelige indsigter og grundsten til prædiktioner. Dog har
jagten på sammenhængende og værdifulde repræsentationer i Latente Variable Modeller (LVM’er) været
udfordret af problemet med statistisk identificerbarhed siden begyndelsen.
Problemet henviser til situationer, hvor to forskningsgrupper, med de samme data, drager forskellige
konklusioner baseret på de lærte repræsentationer, men hævder ækvivalent performance med hensyn til
data fit. I statistisk terminologi peger denne situation på en mængde af parametre, hvor alle fører til den
samme marginal likelihood for datasættet, men resulterer i forskellige latente variable. Denne tvetydighed
underminerer tilliden til de latente koder og skaber udfordringer i beslutningsscenarier, der er baseret på
disse strukturer. For at realisere potentialet for LVM’er i lyset af en stigende mængde højdimensionelle data,
er det afgørende at udvikle praktiske og skalerbare løsninger for at sikre tillid og tro på konklusioner baseret
på de lærte repræsentationer.
Eksisterende tilgange lægger primært vægt på at opnå stærk identificerbarhed af modelparametre gennem
strenge model begrænsninger, ofte på bekostning af modellens fleksibilitet og ydeevne. Mens nylige teoretiske
fremskridt har omformuleret identifikationsproblemet i termer af fordelinger på de latente rum og de
tilhørende dekoderfunktioner, mangler der praktiske og frugtbare implementeringer baseret på disse
indsigter.
Vores arbejde adresserer denne mangel og skifter fokus fra modelidentificerbarhed til identificerbarheden
af opgaver inden for det latente rum i LVM’er. Opgaver såsom clustering, klassifikation og interpolation
udføres normalt i det latente rum under anvendelse af et afstandsmål. Ved at anerkende afstandsmåls
centralitet i de fleste opgaver, foreslår vi en teoretisk funderet metodologi til konstruktion af et statistisk
identificerbart afstandsmål på mangfoldigheden, som er lært af en LVM. Ved at udnytte principper fra
Riemannsk Geometri etablerer vi invariansen af geodætiske afstande under transformationer der fører til
identificerbarhed problemet og formulerer dermed konceptet af et Identificerbart Latent Metrisk Rum. Inden for
dette rum bliver efterfølgende opgaver, der er afhængige rummets metrik, intrinsisk identificerbare, hvilket
styrker tilliden til modellens resultater.
Udover det teoretiske arbejde præsenterer vi en praktisk ramme til anvendelse af disse koncepter og
demonstrerer deres effektivitet på datasæt som MNIST og CIFAR10. Den eksperimentelle analyse der
sammenligner med det konventionelle Euklidiske afstandsmål statistisk understreger overlegenhed og
pålidelighed af vores foreslåede tilgang.

Introduction 1
Our experience of the natural world is a complex interplay of various
factors that go through a filter to shape our perception of reality. The
human brain is a remarkably effective system that can continuously
process extraordinary amounts of raw input and shape it into experiences
that, to us, have explicit representations that we can assess, manipulate,
and compare. This distillation of a raw stream of observational data
into a coherent representation allows us to recognize patterns, make
predictions, and act upon them. Consequently, it is natural that a vast
amount of research has been dedicated to understanding this process and
attempting to replicate it in artificial systems. Large Language Models
(LLMs) that internally rely on a learned representation of text show that
adoption of these principles can yield impressive results as demonstrated
by, e.g., Vaswani et al. [3] and Devlin et al. [4].
Figure 1.1: The Mobius strip is a 2D man-
ifold embedded in 3D space.[5]
In computer science, a formalization of this distillation process is encap-
sulated in the manifold hypothesis formulated by i.a. Bengio, Courville,
and Vincent [6], which posits that high-dimensional observational data
actually resides on a low-dimensional manifold embedded within the
high-dimensional space. Although the concept of a manifold will be rig-
orously defined later, it can be conceptualized as a set akin to the Mobius
strip illustrated in Figure 1.1. The process of recovering the manifold
using statistical methods and leveraging it for various tasks is the focal
point of related subfields of machine learning, including Latent Variable
Modeling (LVM), Representation Learning (RL), and Manifold Learning
(ML).
While the application of these techniques in LLMs is impressive and en-
joyable to experiment with, their significance extends to critical domains
such as healthcare, biology, finance, and security. Works by Detlefsen,
Hauberg, and Boomsma [7] and Landi et al. [8] exemplify the increas-
ing efficacy of these methods and their potential to impact our lives
profoundly. This capacity typically manifests through two related yet
distinct aspects: the ability to model the underlying structure of data
and uncover novel insights about the world, alongside the capability to
generate accurate predictions.
However effective the use of statistical methods for learning representa-
tions of data has shown to be, it is not without its limitations. One pivotal
issue in statistical literature pertains to the identifiability of parameters in a
model. This problem refers to situations where two sets of parameters can
fit the observational data equally well but result in different underlying
representations, posing challenges in decision-making scenarios reliant
on these structures.

The Gaussian example above illustrates the problem well and gives us
the type of transformations that result in indeterminacies. However,
indeterminacy transformations expand significantly in intricate models,
making delineating challenging. When viewed through the lens of learned
manifolds, this implies substantial divergence among them, resulting in
very different recovered representations.
Before this work, the field has been approaching this problem on a
model-by-model basis, typically imposing quite limiting constraints on
the model to eradicate indeterminacy transformations and ensure strong6

identifiability [10]. This approach results in identifiable manifolds but at
a high cost due to a lack of expressiveness of the model and an absence
of a principled way to find suitable architectures.
There is, however, an alternative approach to the problem of indetermi-
nacy. Instead of focusing on the model itself, we can focus on subsequent
uses of the learned manifold. One typical way to utilize it involves mea-
suring the relations among points in the latent space, naturally achieved
through calculating distances. Moreover, this concept of distance consti-
tutes a fundamental component in many machine learning algorithms
frequently applied in the latent space.
Therefore, it would be beneficial to have a strongly identifiable distance
measure on the manifold while avoiding limitations associated with
strong identifiability of the model itself. When used in place of the
Euclidean distance in the latent space with subsequent algorithms,
such a distance measure would allow researchers to draw trustworthy
conclusions from the learned representations.
The main contribution of this thesis is precisely such a distance measure
and proof of its strong identifiability. The resulting construction is
invariant under all indeterminacy transformations, thus not relying on
the strong identifiability of the model itself. In our work, this is done
by leveraging ideas of Riemannian Geometry as we establish that a
distance defined in terms of the Riemannian Metric remains invariant
under reparametrizations of the manifold, yielding an Identifiable Latent
Metric Space. This theoretical finding justifies utilizing geodesic distances
as reliable distance measures when operating within the latent space
recovered by a model.

for drawing scientific conclusions from the learned representations, thus
facilitating research and reliable decision-making in various domains.

2.1 Riemannian Geometry
Figure 2.1: A geodesic on a sphere. The

Our everyday experience of the physical world is primarily three-
dimensional, where the shortest path between two objects is a straight
line. The term used for this is Euclidean Geometry, which dates back to
the ancient Greeks. A first glance at non-Euclidean geometry that some
of us might have encountered is connected to the fact that our planet is
essentially a sphere, and thus, when we travel long distances in a plane,
the screen in front of us shows a curved path similar to Figure 2.1. As we
are restricted to moving in an orbit around the Earth, this is a glimpse of
Riemannian Geometry that generalizes the notion of a straight line to a
geodesic.
To be allowed into Riemannian Geometry, we must first understand
the basics of Differential Geometry and Topology. Although this will
feel rather abstract, the benefits of a rigorous definition of a manifold
together with the Riemannian constructions will be well worth the effort.
Throughout this section, some effort will be devoted to following up on
the abstract notions with concrete examples that the reader will likely be
familiar with. It will hopefully become evident that the abstract notions
are generalizations of our everyday experiences and things that we find
natural, in some sense.

2.2 Measure Theory
As a species, we are obsessed with measuring. The desire to measure
tangible things like length, weight, time, and quantity is natural to us.
However, we also measure more abstract things like the sun’s tempera-
ture, a person’s accomplishments, and a nation’s happiness. Taking the
usefulness of the former for granted and avoiding discussing the latter,
we will focus on measuring in the context of probabilities.
The goal is to construct a general notion of a measure that satisfies our
intuitive perception of measuring quantities of interest in the real world.
For example, if we traveled from point A to point B, we would expect
certain things from the total distance traveled. First and foremost, if A
and B are at the same point, we would expect the distance to be zero and
strictly positive; otherwise, it would be. Secondly, if we traveled from A
==PAGE:28==
2 Background
14
to B and then from B to C, we would expect the total distance to be the
sum of the two distances. It is also reasonable to want this intuition to be
fulfilled when measuring abstract probabilities.
Measure Theory is a field of mathematics that generalizes the notion of
measurements to abstract sets and serves as the foundation of Probability
Theory. The idea is to define a measure that assigns a number to each
set in a collection of sets in a systematic way. Although our discussion
will start with abstract notions, we will later see that this metaphysical
idea will grow into something palpable and valuable in the context of
probabilities. The reference used for most of this section is the well-known
Measures, Integrals and Martingales by Schilling [20] and the less known,
but quite special to the author, Measure Theory by Hansen [21].

that something is a collection of sets called a 𝜎-algebra.

2.3 Latent Variable Models
Many phenomena in the world are, at least perceived by us to be, uncertain
or partially uncertain. For example, the topic of many great conversations
is that the weather feels random, although dependent on climate and
season. Given that we are in Scandinavia in May, we reason about the
chance of rain as a random variable. In the setting of the previous section,
we can think of it as a discrete random variable with two outcomes: rain
or no rain, or as a real-valued random variable signifying the amount of
rain in millimeters.
Convincing ourselves that such random variables exist is an ontological
question that we will not delve into. Instead, this section will deal with
the other side of the coin: the epistemological question of how to reason
about these random variables given our observations. This is, at least,
useful when we want to avoid showing up to work all wet or painfully
resembling a lobster on vacation.
Reasoning about the random variables given observational data requires
a set of assumptions about the data-generating process. If we believe
that the weather depends on the season and the climate, we can imagine
it to be a noisy function of these variables. A simple model could be to
model the amount of rain as an average amount of rain measured for
a given season and climate during past years. The presence of noise in
this setting allows for randomness and fluctuations around the average
amount of rain we observe, as on some days, it rains, and on others, it
does not, even during a rainy season.

Well-equipped with our theoretical toolbox from the previous chapter, we

In this chapter, the aim is to give practical tools to take advantage of the

repercussions in practice, cover the process of learning the manifold and

4.1 Learning the manifold
There is a vast body of literature on learning manifolds from data, begin-
ning with linear methods such as PCA by Pearson [26] and continuing
with more sophisticated non-linear methods such as Isomap by Tenen-
baum, Silva, and Langford [27], LLE by Roweis and Saul [28], and UMAP
by McInnes, Healy, and Melville [29]. These works learn the embedding
by carefully considering the balance between the smoothness of the map
and the type of information preserved by it.
Our approach will differ from those mentioned above in that we will
use deep neural network (DNN) architecture to learn the manifold
through an Autoencoder (AE) setting, thus allowing us to learn a more
complex and non-linear embedding. As known in the Machine Learning
community, the AE architecture was initially introduced by Rumelhart,
Hinton, and Williams [30] as non-linear PCA. The framework visualized
in Figure 4.1 usually consists of an encoder that maps the input data to a
lower-dimensional latent space and a decoder that reconstructs the input
from the latent space. For optimization, a suitable cost function is chosen
to ensure that reconstructions are faithful to the input data and that the
model overall has the desired properties.

4.2.1 Gedoesic parametrized by a cubic spline
Having settled on a cubic spline as a parametrization of a geodesic, we
will now describe the construction of the spline and use it to derive the
free parameters of the resulting curve that we can use when minimizing

A cubic spline in Fig. 4.3 is a piecewise function with pieces that are
cubic polynomials. The points where the pieces meet are called the
knots, ℎ, and we want to construct a continuous spline with continuous
first and second derivatives. Individual components are polynomials,
so we only need to constrain their behavior at the knots to satisfy the
requirements. Suppose the knots are known, and we are using the splines
to interpolate a set of points. In that case, these constraints and boundary
constraints will usually give a system of linear equations that can be
solved to find the coefficients of the polynomials. In our setting, however,
we are using splines to define a path between two points, and the knots
are the unknown parameters of the problem, as well as the coefficients
of the polynomials. Furthermore, given that 𝑎, 𝑏∈ℝ𝑛, we will look to

Discussion of experiments 44
Accepting the stochasticity inherent in the optimization process when
learning the manifold from real data, the primary goal of our experiments
is to test whether the proposed method in Chapter 3 results in a distance
measure that is more robust than the standard Euclidean distance in
the latent space of a model. The secondary goal is to test whether the
uncertainty-aware geodesic distances calculated by optimizing the lower
bound introduced in Chapter 4 provide an additional improvement to
the robustness of the distance measure.
5.1 Experimental setup
To assess the robustness of the distance measures, we consider retraining
the models using different seeds on the same data and subsequently
calculating the distances between the latent representations of the same
fixed 100 pairs of data points from the test set. We repeat the same
experiment to investigate the impact of uncertainty, changing the number
of decoders in the model to allow for uncertainty-aware geodesic distance
calculations.
5.1.1 Training procedure and architectures considered
The general modeling approach and its components are introduced in
Section 4.1. The individual components, however, introduce a number
of hyperparameters, such as the number of layers in a flow and the
number of knots in the transformer RQS. The hyperparameters for the
coupling flow architectures were adopted from Durkan et al. [41] and
only slightly adjusted to encourage models to have the same performance
in terms of the reconstruction loss on the training set. The models were
trained using the reconstruction loss, where we used the Adam optimizer
introduced by Kingma and Ba [54] with settings matching Durkan et al.
[41] and trained the models using an early stopping scheme based on the
validation loss. Appendix A contains the full training and architecture
details.
In the ensemble setting, the decoders were trained end-to-end with
the encoder, where an incoming batch of data was passed through the
encoder and then split into the same number of sub-batches as the
number of decoders. This way, each decoder was trained on a different

The 100 fixed point pairs were chosen randomly, with a subset of 50

5.2 Results
Our results unquestionably show that the geodesic distance is more robust
than the Euclidean distance in the latent space of a model. This finding
is consistent across all model architectures and datasets considered and
verifies the theoretical claims made in Chapter 3.
Figure 5.1 shows the mean and standard deviation of the CVs for the
Euclidean and geodesic distances for each model architecture trained
on the MNIST dataset. The blue curve and shadow area represent the
variability of the geodesic distance measure and are consistently below
the Euclidean counterpart in red.
The variability of the Euclidean distance measure only reflects the stochas-
ticity behind the encoder, as this measure does not consider the decoder.
On the other hand, the geodesic distance measure reflects the shape of
the manifold learned by the model and is, thereby, less sensitive to the
stochasticity of the encoder. According to our result in Chapter 3, we
would expect the geodesic distance to be perfectly robust if we could
train the model to zero reconstruction error for each seed. In contrast,
the Euclidean distance would still be sensitive to the stochasticity behind
the optimization process.

5.3 Discussion of experiments
Although our findings for the primary goal of the experiments are
consistent with the theoretical claims made in Chapter 3, the body of
evidence is limited to the MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets. To make a loud
statement about the proposed method’s applicability and scalability, we
must test it on a broader range of datasets, leading to more exciting
manifolds and solving more complex tasks.
The limiting factor for more extensive and more complex experiments
is primarily the computational complexity of the chosen model archi-
tectures. During the experimental phase, this became evident as the
models need a large number of parameters, as documented in Tables A.1
and A.2 to have a reasonable performance in terms of the reconstruction
loss. In our case, reasonable is not particularly impressive either, as
shown in Appendix B where reconstructions are shown together with
the reconstruction loss on the test set. We found that MNIST reconstruc-
tions were acceptable where certain digits’ features were lost but were
perfectly readable. CIFAR10 reconstructions, on the other hand, were
less competent, with the model struggling to capture the complexity of
the images, leading to correct shapes and colors but lacking sharpness.
However, the latter conclusion is hard to make as the original images are
of poor quality for generative modeling tasks, making comparing two
unclear objects challenging and not meaningful.
Even though there is no direct doubt over the validation of our theoretical
claims, to improve our experiments and cement the findings, we must
consider more interesting and better-suited datasets and architectures that
are efficient enough to perform well, scale, and make such experiments
feasible.
The secondary goal of the experiments was to investigate uncertainty’s
impact on the geodesic distance’s robustness. Here, we saw that the
ensemble of decoders and the uncertainty-aware geodesic distances do
not significantly improve the variability of the geodesic distances until
we reach ten decoders, amplifying the computational strain. However,
this aspect is underexplored as our discussion in Section 4.2.2 opens up
several interesting questions.
The first question is to test the impact of calculating the geodesics
according to ⋆from Eq. 4.26 rather than the lower bound ⋆⋆from

The second question would investigate the impact of the number of

The third area of interest is disambiguating the regularization effect on the

subject to the multiple hypotheses testing problem, which in Statistics
literature requires careful consideration. Heavy tails challenge the nor-

experiments show a distribution of CVs of the geodesic distances more
in line with Gaussianity. The multiple hypotheses testing problem is not
addressed in our experiments, as it would require a larger sample size,
which is not feasible given the computational and time constraints. How-
ever, the results and conclusions we draw from them are not threatened
by these limitations, as the effect sizes are large and consistent across all
model architectures and datasets considered.

The main contribution of the thesis is the solution to the statistical
identifiability problem when it comes to measuring distances on the
manifolds learned by LVMs. The theoretical result in Chapter 3 is a
consequence of merging known results from Statistics and Riemannian
Geometry by translating concepts from one field into the language of
another. Thus, it is an example of how a simple reframing can lead to
new knowledge.
With the guarantees of strong identifiability of the geodesic distances
on the manifold, the next natural challenge is to consider statistical
quantities on the manifold that depend on the distances. As mentioned
in Chapter 3, a quantity of interest is the Frechet mean, a generalization
of the Euclidean mean to Riemannian manifolds, that comes as the
minimizer of the Frechet variance defined in terms of geodesic distances.
As a consequence of our theoretical result, we can now focus on analyzing
the uniqueness of the Frechet mean as a problem of the properties of the
learned manifold. Thus, analysis of the problem and practical algorithms
to check for and achieve this uniqueness are exciting directions for further
research.
Chapter 4 builds on the mathematical result and shows how the finding
can be exploited in practice. The process has two parts: first, learning the
manifold, and second, calculating the geodesic distances. In the following
sections, we will discuss our approach to both aspects.
6.1 Learning the manifold
The elephant in the room throughout this thesis since Chapter 4 is that
our Autoencoder model is not an LVM. This conceptually significant
shortcoming of our methodology is of little consequence when learning
a useful manifold in practice. Nevertheless, it makes the discussion on
theoretical guarantees from Chapter 3 and assessing the equivalency of
models in terms of the marginal likelihood of the data very challenging.
A solution to this shortcoming is available in the original text by Brehmer
and Cranmer [39] on which we based our Autoencoder modeling ap-
proach. The original task posed in the paper was using the introduced
M-flow framework to learn a manifold and estimate a probability density
on it simultaneously. However, the authors found that first learning the
manifold and then estimating a density on it worked best in practice, as
demonstrated by their experiments. The Autoencoder setting of our train-
ing approach corresponds to the first step in the original paper, whereas
the second step introduced by Brehmer and Cranmer [39] is to train
another normalizing flow in the latent space. This is done by choosing
a base density on the latent space, e.g., Gaussian, and transforming it
by a learnable diffeomorphic map, ℎ: Z →Z, which allows the use of
the change of variable formula. During this second step, the parameters
of the decoder and encoder are fixed, and only the parameters of ℎare

Having discussed the modeling approach in general, we will now turn
to the specifics of the architecture used in our implementation. While
not the main focus of our project, our experimental process showed
that using Normalizing Flow-based architectures for the decoder is not
ideal. The architecture is not expressive enough and requires heavy
overparametrization, leading to computational inefficiency. This finding
is common in the literature, where, e.g., Lee, Kim, and Yoon [33] show that
==PAGE:63==
6 Discussion
49
this model family is computationally expensive to train and evaluate and
performs poorly compared to unrestricted counterparts. The Normalizing
Flows shine in density estimation tasks, where the architecture is required
to have a computable inverse for sampling and a computable determinant
of the Jacobian for the change of variables formula. As our requirements
are lower, we can potentially use more expressive architectures that are
not as constrained. Thus, an expressive and learnable injective decoder
architecture is needed to realize the full potential of our methodology.
Chapter 4 already mentioned works on injective decoder architecture.
However, recent work by Sorrenson et al. [34] shows that using such
architectures leads to an ill-defined objective and introduces the free-form
injective flow by using an arbitrary neural network architecture. The
approach by Sorrenson et al. [34] relaxes the constraint of injectivity
but still complies with the term flow since Sorrenson et al. [34] define a
new loss function that allows for density estimation by leveraging the
generalizations of the change of variable formula to maps of different
dimensions. While relevant to consider, this approach will not comply
with our assumption of the injective decoder needed for identifiability
guarantees and, thus, is the wrong constraint to lift for our use.
Another approach is to stay with the M-flow framework but use the invert-
ible ResNet by Behrmann et al. [36] or ODE-based decoder introduced by
Chen et al. [38] instead of a Normalizing Flow architecture. This will, in
theory, increase expressivity and decrease the computational complexity
of the model. The invertible ResNet is guaranteed to be invertible on its
image, satisfying our injectivity assumption. However, inverting it is a
separate optimization problem; thus, calculating the marginal density is
potentially computationally demanding. This, in turn, is a compromise
we are willing to make, as having easy access to the marginal likelihood
is not the main focus of our work, which is first and foremost concerned
with learning a manifold and taking advantage of identifiable geodesic
distances on it.
6.2 Calculating geodesics
Calculating the geodesic distances by minimizing the energy of a
parametrized curve 𝛾is considered an industry standard. However,
finding the coefficients of the spline is usually done by solving the linear
system outlined in Chapter 4 in closed form instead of using gradient
methods in the null space of the system as we do. Moreover, our approach
to dealing with stochastic manifolds requires more thorough theoretical
and experimental treatment.
On the one hand, the experiments demonstrate its efficacy. However, our
implementation is a lower bound of the theoretically justified expected
curve energy. Our experiments were done optimizing the curve energy on
the expected manifold instead, which does not, at first glance, seem like
the correct quantity to optimize. The consequences of this discrepancy
need to be explored in more detail, giving both a theoretical justification
and practical implementation considerations in terms of, e.g., the optimal
number of samples needed for approximations.

7.1 Summary and key results
In this project, we established sufficient conditions and proved strong
identifiability of geodesic distances on the Riemannian manifold learned
by an LVM. This result leads us to establish the concept of an Identifiable
Latent Metric Space and, consequently, the identifiability of tasks relying
on its metric.1 In particular, clustering, interpolation, and classification

the Frechet mean.
tasks are identifiable within this metric space. Leaving the statistical inde-
terminacy of distances in the latent space of an LVM behind, researchers
and practitioners can now focus on learning manifolds, distributions
on them and trust the assessment of their properties using geodesic
distances.
Accompanying the theoretical result, we proposed a practical approach
to learning manifolds and calculating the geodesic distances.
To learn the manifold, we used the M-flow framework to comply with
the sufficient conditions for our result. In our case, this framework
implies using a Coupling Flow from the Normalizing Flows family,
which was effective enough to facilitate experiments that demonstrated
our theoretical guarantees. However, the process showed that relying on
Normalizing Flows for manifold learning is computationally expensive,
leading to heavily overparametrized models that are difficult to train and
do not yield the desired expressivity.
In our work, we used two methods for computing geodesic distances, both
of which minimize the energy of a parametrized curve in the latent space.
The first method is a basic gradient descent on the energy functional,
where the decoder function defines the metric. The second method is
a stochastic version of the first, where we use an ensemble of decoder
functions to estimate the uncertainty of the geodesic curve. The latter
is a novel, promising approach to learning uncertainty-aware geodesics
on stochastic manifolds and requires further theoretical justification and
practical efficacy investigation.
Both methods were found to be effective in practice, thus contributing to
the validation of our main theoretical results.
The benefit of the uncertainty-aware geodesic curves was only demon-
strated on the MNIST (3 digits) dataset when using ten or more decoders
in the ensemble. However, the method saw no experimentation on CI-
FAR10 due to computational constraints. Thus, its efficacy must be further
validated on more complex datasets and models and decoupled from
the ensemble’s regularizing effect within our training setup.

7 Conclusion
51
7.2 Outlook and future work
As Chapter 6 mentions, there are numerous paths toward further the-
oretical and practical improvements as well as hypotheses to test and
validate.
Having established theoretical guarantees of geodesic distances, we are
also interested in deriving heuristics for stronger statements about the
identifiability of Frechét means and properties related to the manifold
itself.
Moreover, another exciting direction to pursue is a theoretical and
experimental exploration of the estimation of stochastic geometries and
how it can be done with decoder ensembling.
Practical improvements must first and foremost solve the problem of
expressivity, parameter efficiency, and the associated computational infea-
sibility in large-scale applications without compromising the injectivity
constraints.
Lastly, our experimental findings must stand the test of more demanding
tasks and applications to cement the validity and usefulness of the
proposed methods.
