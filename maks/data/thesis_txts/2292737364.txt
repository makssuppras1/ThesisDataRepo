Abstract
The objective of the present thesis is ﬁnite element modelling of the block
failure mechanism. Experimental investigations of block failure in bolted gus-
set plate connections, which have been conducted at the Technical University
of Denmark, serve as the basis for simpliﬁed 2D models. The commercial
ﬁnite element software Abaqus/CAE 6.13-4 is employed in this endeavour.
Special attention is given to the eﬀects of eccentric load cases on the
formation of the failure mechanism. Plates with bolt groups containing 8
and 12 bolts are investigated with the applied load at diﬀerent eccentricities.
Furthermore, the investigation includes concentric load cases in plates with
4, 6 and 9 bolts. Additionally, bolt hole ovalisation is investigated for plates
with 1 and 2 bolt connections.
An inverse modelling approach is used for accurately determining the
material properties used in the aforementioned models. These are based on
uniaxial tensile tests conducted on coupons machined from the tested gusset
plates. A Matlab R
⃝script is created for aiding the transformation of the force
- displacement data, obtained during the tensile tests, into true stress - true
plastic strain data pairs. Which are used as input in the commercial ﬁnite
element software Abaqus/CAE 6.13-4.
The manipulation of the load - deﬂection curves includes corrections
for errors introduced by the testing machine and a weighted extrapolation
method for obtaining the true stresses after the onset of diﬀuse necking. The
methodology presented for modelling tensile tests is consistent and applicable
in both 2D and 3D modeling space. Additionally, in combination with the
script, the force - displacement curves of the ﬁnite element models may be
ﬁtted to the experimental results with little eﬀort. The mesh sensitivity
analysis conducted for the tensile tests suggests that reasonably accurate
results may be obtained with both 2D and 3D models beyond the onset of
diﬀuse necking.
The block failure mechanism is predicted successfully in all of the in-
vestigated situations, however, the force - displacement responses do not
ﬁt perfectly with the experimental results. In some cases due to the eﬀect
vii

of bolt pretension, while in others, the material deﬁnition is the limiting
factor. Nonetheless, the extrapolated true stress - true plastic strain curves
determined from the tensile tests bring improvement in all cases investigated.

General Introduction
Engineers must abide by codes and standards in practice. One such code is
DS/EN 1993-1-1 [1] applicable for steel structures in Denmark. The rules and
regulations in such documents are obtained through empirical and theoretical
investigations which are approved by committees and experts. Nonetheless,
the practice of engineering is an ever improving ﬁeld and the formulas and
requirements continually need to be updated so the design process is as
eﬃcient as possible. However, improvements are obtained with diﬃculty and
require extensive experimental and theoretical investigations.
One such attempt has been initiated with respect to block tearing. A phe-
nomena which may be encountered, but not limited to, coped or non-coped
beam-to-beam or beam-to-column connections. Indeed, both concentric and
eccentric situations are covered in the codes, however the relationships have
been found to be conservative by Nørgaard (2013) [2]. Speciﬁcally, in the
case the resultant of the forces acting on the connection has an eccentricity
with respect to the bolt group. Determining improved relations for the failure
mechanisms of such connections is paramount.
Finite element analysis has become a widely used tool in investigations. If
done properly, it can provide valuable insight for the theoretical and empirical
research. Of course, even ﬁnite element methods of analysis have codiﬁed
requirements. Annex C in DS/EN 1993-1-5 [3] describes suggestions for this
approach. The choice of software is one such provision. Abaqus/CAE of
Dassault Syst´emes is thoroughly benchmarked and is the chosen commercial
ﬁnite element software for the present study.
3
==PAGE:20==
Objective
Finite element modelling of existing block tearing experiments is the main
purpose of the thesis. The focus is on eccentric load cases. Nevertheless,
concentric load cases are also investigated.
Scope
The theoretical part of the thesis focuses on the material properties, especially
on the methods for determining the true stresses and strains after the onset
of necking. The proposed theory surrounding block tearing is also brieﬂy
presented.
Methodology for consistently modelling uniaxial tensile tests of ﬂat or plate
type specimens is suggested. With the force - displacement response as the
focal point of the investigation.
Several experiments conducted at the Technical University of Denmark are
used as the basis for the modelling of bolted connections with block tearing
as the expected failure mode.
Methodology
The material plays an important role in the ﬁnite element models, as such,
“inverse modelling”1 of tensile test experiments are carried out for the de-
termination of proper material deﬁnitions, which are used as input in the
subsequent block tearing models.
For transformation of the tensile test results into usable input a Matlab R
⃝
script is devised. A thorough description is provided.
Experimental results are used to verify the block tearing and material test
models.

Structure
The ﬁrst chapter introduces the existing experimental work on block failure
conducted at Denmark’s Technical University as well as a brief summary of
the theoretical work. This is followed by a short description of the uniaxial
tensile tests and ﬁnally the program for determination of the true stress -
true plastic strain curves used in Abaqus is presented. The second chapter
describes the tensile test models followed by a short Results section. The
block tearing models and the results are presented in chapters three and four
respectively. The ﬁnal results are discussed in chapter ﬁve and ﬁnally the
thesis is concluded in chapter six. Additionally, geometries, mesh designs,
and some results for models which are not described in the main chapters are
presented in the Appendix.

1.1
Introduction
A parametric study of block tearing under concentric loads was studied
by Alstrøm and Raaschou (2015) [5] and the eﬀect of bolt pre-load on
the resistance of gusset plates was studied by Olsen (2014) [6] with the
accompanying test report by Skov (2013) [7]. The research also includes
investigations of bolted gusset plate capacities focusing on rotational block
tearing from relatively large eccentricities by Winterskov-Andersen (2014) [8]
with the accompanying test report by Nissen (2014) [9] and rotational block
tearing from small eccentricities by Neaman and Eljaja [10].
The main focus of the aforementioned theses was theoretical and experimental
investigation. Finite element modelling was attempted in [8] and [6], however
these models are inadequately explained and require corrections. Thus, a new
set of models is created which include non-linear material, geometry and in
some cases boundary conditions. Contact modelling is also employed with
normal and tangential interaction properties for the block tearing models. Due
to the large number of experiments conducted a few representative situations
are selected for modelling. These include the following tests:
• F01, F02, F04 [6]
• A2, A4 [5]
• T1, T4 [10]

J¨onsson starting on page 165, is a brief summary of the theoretical and
experimental basis for taking into consideration the eﬀect of eccentric loading
in block failure. The goal of the theoretical investigations is to determine a
simple method for relating individual block tearing capacities of normal, shear
and moment forces while opening the possibility for interaction relationships
through simple von Mises interaction formulas. This, so called, lower bound
method is based on the assumptions that the material is rigid-plastic and that
the rigid parts are below or at yield everywhere, nevertheless the straining
is not equal along the yield bands shown in ﬁg. 1.1. This results in strain
hardening before the mechanism has fully formed, therefore a new yield stress

Machine stiﬀness
The concept of machine stiﬀness is shortly discussed. It should be noted
that in none of the studies related to modelling tensile tests that the present
author is aware of, is machine stiﬀness included. Machine stiﬀness and the
diﬃculty in determining it has been studied as early as 1971 by Hockett et al
[14]. After numerous tensile tests the study found that even with identical
specimens the resulting machine stiﬀness determined with several methods
shows signiﬁcant scatter. In order to simplify the process of determining the
machine stiﬀness for every material, and gusset plate test studied herein an
alternative is proposed.
• Assumptions:
– The testing machine suﬀers only elastic deformations;
– The initial, elastic slope of a force - displacement curve for a tensile
test may be accurately determined by ﬁnite element analysis;
Thus, the deformation in the recorded data may be decomposed into two
parts. On one hand, the deformation of the test piece δLtp and on the other,
the deformation of the machine δLm. Consequently, the total deformation
may be written as:
δLt = δLtp + δLm

referred to as the correction curves in the following.

1. A ﬁnite element code, given that Young’s Modulus is known;
2. Strain gauge measurement;
Correction curves are not limited to the aforementioned methods.
Any
measurement method of the load - deﬂection curve which does not go through

This chapter deals with the modelling of the tensile tests. The representative

2.3 Material Deﬁnition
Axisymmetric modelling
An in-depth look at the advantages of model simpliﬁcation through symme-
try is not taken. It is expected that besides the reduction in duration of
the analyses no other improvements would be achieved with axisymmetric
elements. It should also be noted that in practice the deformation of the
specimens is not perfectly symmetric, and use of symmetry only increases
the otherwise ideal nature of the models. It is the aim to achieve a result
as realistic as possible, therefore models with the full geometry of the test
pieces are preferred. Nonetheless, future work should include a thorough
investigation of the feasibility of using axisymmetric elements for modelling
tensile tests.

modelling fracture is not part of the current investigation, which makes it

to thickness ratio w

the onset of localised necking with ﬁnite element analysis. The method
may not be speciﬁcally intended for specimens with a 1:4 thickness to width
ratio, as the ones used in the current investigation. However, some evidence
of localised necking can be observed on these specimens as well, therefore
it is assumed that the method may be used to approximately predict the
point when localised necking initiates. A rigorous proof of the method is not
provided herein as it is more important to determine how well the material
deﬁnitions perform and what are the major inﬂuencers of the resulting force

assess the point at which the ﬁnite element analysis becomes unreliable, i.e.
if the appearance of signiﬁcant discontinuities, coincides with the onset of
localised necking. As such, the methodology for acquiring the local necking
criterion suggested by Hyun et al, is brieﬂy presented.
During plastic deformation in (isotropic) metals, the material is incompressible
and the strain in the three principal directions may be expressed as:
ϵ1 + ϵ2 + ϵ3 = 0
(2.1)
where, ϵ1, ϵ2 and ϵ3 are the strains in the length, width and thickness directions,
respectively.
The suggested method deﬁnes the onset of localised necking by employing
the minor -to-major strain ratio, which is written as:
ρ = ϵ2
ϵ1
(2.2)
The strain ratio ρ is constant up to the onset of local necking. Local necking
initiates when the value of the strain ratio deviates from the constant.
The strain ratio at the onset of localised necking is expressed in terms of the
strain ratio at the maximum tensile load ρFmax, the strain-hardening exponent
n and a constant c:
ρlocal = ρFmax(1 −c · n)
(2.3)
where, ρFmax = −0.5 is the strain ratio at the maximum load for isotropic
materials. Whereas c is a constant determined by Hyun et al to give less than
5% error for the value of major strain compared to the theoretical solution
given by R. Hill’s (1952) [25] theory for necking in thin strips. The theoretical
value of the major strain at localised necking in thin sheets is expressed in
terms of the strain-hardening exponent n and is written as:
ϵlocal = 2n
(2.4)
The strain hardening exponent is equal to the strain at maximum tensile load,
n = ϵF max (Considere’s criterion, see section 1.4.1). In their investigation
Hyun et al, determined the value for c for diﬀerent values of the strain
hardening exponent (0.05, 0.1, 0.15) and determined that c ∈[0.08, 0.15] give
the closest values to Hill’ theoretical prediction. However, the specimens in
the current investigation have strain-hardening exponents n ≃0.3, as such
the values for c determined by Hyun et al may not give similar accuracy in
this case.

To avoid such a situation, the maximum increment is set small enough for the
software to require fewer cutbacks in increment size when the convergence
diﬃculties begin. However, it should be adjusted only if, and when necessary
as it increases CPU time2.
2.6
Mesh design
The choice of mesh has a direct inﬂuence on the outcome of the models. As a
result, careful selection of the element type, class and mesh structure must be
made. A description of the main advantages and drawbacks of each type of
element is made in [16]. For the present investigation accuracy and eﬃciency
are considered the main goal.
During Plastic deformation in metals the material is incompressible i.e. the
volume is constant. As such, kinematic constraints are added to the elements,
i.e. the integration points are limited to constant volume. In some cases this
leads to volumetric locking as the elements cannot solve the over-constrained
nature of this situation [16]. Conclusively, some element classes are unsuitable
in modelling plasticity problems.
The most likely candidates to provide accurate results are the ﬁrst order
(CPS4) elements for the 2D models and ﬁrst order (C3D8) for the 3D models.
Because they use a constant volume strain deﬁnition volumetric locking is not

Recovering force - displacement curves
As described throughout the present study, the accuracy of the material
deﬁnition is judged with respect to the experimental force - displacement
curves. The corrected experimental curves, see section 1.4.2, serve as reference
for the comparison. The testing machine records the applied load and the
actual displacement of the piston head. The recorded piston movement does
not suggest the actual position of the wedges gripping the coupons, unlike
the simulations where the displacement is controlled precisely at the surface
of one end.
Furthermore, the nominal stress and strain are determined
based on the original gauge length. The extent of the gauge length indicates
where the stress - strain relationship is valid. In eﬀect, obtaining comparable
displacement results from the ﬁnite element models are problematic and
subjective.
In order to obtain comparable deﬂection values from the models the original
gauge length used in the experiments is employed. Similarly to the experi-
mental data the displacement values are determined relative to the limits of
the gauge length. That is to say, that the deﬂection of the nodes situated on
these limits are used to determine the elongation δL of the specimens.

2. Related to mesh design

are accurate and eﬃcient in simulating large deformations.

The present chapter focuses mainly on the modelling of the block tearing

The main assumption and incidentally the main shortcoming of the current
investigation is the fact that a generalised plane stress model is used for the
test plates. Which means that there are no stresses in the thickness direction.
This is clearly not the case in reality. As the material expands from the
pressure generated by the contact with the bolts and contracts where the
plate is elongated. Furthermore, the expanding material is constrained by the
plates in the test setup, eﬀectively increasing the stiﬀness of the connection
and the friction between the plates. However, as was seen with the tensile
test models, the eﬀect of the simpliﬁcation is only relevant after the ultimate
tensile load is reached. Consequently, for the initial design of the block tearing
models a plane stress deﬁnition is deemed suﬃcient.

the current chapter. As it is the only experiment which satisﬁes all the
selection criteria, see 1.1. Speciﬁcally, that the plate is loaded eccentrically,
the bolts are “loosely” tightened and that material tests were performed on
test pieces machined directly from the tested plates.

To the current writers knowledge, literature on modelling block tearing,

Two types of interactions are deﬁned between the contact pairs.

• The default “Hard” contact constraint enforcement method is used,

transmitted between the interacting surfaces;
2. Behaviour tangential to the contact surface
• For simulating the friction between the two surfaces in contact;
• A friction coeﬃcient µ = 0.2, chosen under the assumption that

surfaces. The Coulomb critical shear stress is determined with:
τcrit = µp

• The surfaces do not slide relative to each other until τcrit is reached;

in fact be sticking.

Theoretical development
Corrections for machine stiﬀness
The ﬁnite element models predict the initial stiﬀness of the material as
described by the constitutive model which is based exclusively on the elastic
modulus and Poisson’s ratio. As a result the elastic part of the response is
much more ideal than what is recorded in reality by the testing machine. The
correction for this discrepancy leads to better deﬁnition of the actual material
behaviour. Therefore, it should be a requirement in the post processing stage
of the experimental data.
Extrapolation Methods
Excluding the possibility of in-situ measurement of the instantaneous cross-
sectional area for determination of an accurate true stress - true strain
relationship of a specimen, the choice in theoretical approximation is limited.
While the weighted average method proved to be adequate for the materials
investigated in the present thesis, it may not be a universal approach.
Finite element modeling
Uniaxial tensile tests
The feasibility of modelling the tensile tests with further simpliﬁcations such
as symmetry, is one aspect future work should investigate. The inclusion of
failure criteria and other advanced features in the ﬁnitei element model is
another, however, these are not necessary for determining accurate behaviour
before the ultimate tensile strength is reached.
Block tearing
Further investigation of the block failure mechanism should focus on 3D
modelling with eﬀects such as bolt pretension and friction between the surfaces

4.4 Bolt hole ovalisation
The main conclusion which can be drawn from the current investigation is
that the block failure mechanism is predicted reasonably well in all cases
investigated. Especially, the deformed shapes predicted on the ﬁnite element
models, which are in very good agreement with the experiments. Considering
that the ﬁnite element models are 2D simpliﬁcations of otherwise complex
test setups, the force - displacement responses are quite well aligned with the
experimental results. More so, if it is assumed that the bolt pretension makes
the connections stiﬀer. However, not all of the models are conclusive in this
regard.
Furthermore, it is concluded that the addition of an improved material
deﬁnition enhances the response. However, the behaviour predicted is highly
dependant on the material properties and even fairly accurate constitutive
models based on tensile tests are not completely consistent. Although, this is
to be expected as generally the experiments also give scattered results.
The method for extrapolating the true stresses after the onset of diﬀuse necking
is successfully applied and the force - displacement curves for tensile test
specimens of diﬀerent sizes and material properties are ﬁtted to experimental
data. The methodology includes a correction for the elastic deformation of
the testing machine.
The method used for determining the experimental yield mechanism forces
produce signiﬁcantly lower results for the ﬁnite element models, suggesting
that the method is not directly applicable due to the very high initial stiﬀness
predicted in the simulations.
Of course, the uniqueness of the presented results should be taken into con-
sideration, as the current investigation includes several diﬀerent connections
with one speciﬁc result for each. Moreover, each individual model uses one
speciﬁc material deﬁnition. As a result, the similarity obtained between the
results may be pure coincidence. Nonetheless, the methodology employed is
consistent in simulating the formation of the block failure mechanisms.

BLOCK FAILURE IN CONNECTIONS 
Including Effects of Eccentric Loads 
Jeppe Jönsson 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU), Department of Civil Engineering, Denmark  
jej@byg.dtu.dk 
INTRODUCTION 
Block tear-out in gusset and fin plate connections used for diagonal bracing, coped and non-coped 
beams is described in several codes as a block shear failure mechanism. However in many design 
situations the load to be transferred acts eccentrically or may involve the transfer of a substantial 
moment in combination with a shear force and/or a normal force. It is believed that the codes must 
include the moment effects from for example eccentricity of the shear load by appropriately and 
directly including all the section forces acting at the center of the individual bolt group or weld 
group of the connection. 
In the present work a summary of some previous studies on block failure is given. The summary 
illustrates that no readily available tests with substantial eccentricity have been performed.  
 
Fig. 1.  Examples of gusset and fin plate connections. 
Some theoretical work leading towards capacity methods including the combined influence of 
normal force, shear force and moment on the block failure capacity of gusset or fin plate 
connections will be exemplified. In fact plasticity based methods including the influence of the 
moment have previously been briefly described in a few steel design texts and practicing structural 
engineers normally do perform some additional design checks in order to verify the moment 
transfer capacity; especially when using Danish or German traditional beam gusset plate 
connections shown to the right in Fig. 1. The focus of the present work is to develop a few simple 
standardized capacity based methods involving the individual normal force, shear force and 
moment block failure capacities and a set of relevant capacity interaction formulas with a format 
related to those already in use for cross section analysis in the Eurocodes. In the presented work we 
use a lower bound method based on the assumption of rigid plastic behavior of the plate failing in a 
block failure mode. The block is usually a C-block cut-out or an L-block cut-out, however in this 
paper only the theoretical and experimental part on the C-block cut-out are treated.  The failing 
block is surrounded by yield bands obeying the von Mises yield law. Due to varying strain 
hardening along the yield bands the final block failure mode may be calculated based on a 
maximum stress, which is a function of the material yield stress and the material ultimate stress, e.g. 
for example the mean value.  
Experimental evidence of the rotational block failure of a combined shear and moment loaded 
gusset plate connection will be briefly presented. A completely new and statically determined and 
well defined test setup will be illustrated and some preliminary experimental results will be shown. 
==PAGE:184==
In the experimental study the fin plate or equivalent web plate and overall connection has been 
designed to fail in a block failure mode with interacting shear and moment load.  
The focus of the present paper is entirely on block failure and we assume all other capacities 
(including bolt hole distances) proven to be adequate. It is the aim to illustrated the key features of 
the present research.

STUDIES ON BLOCK FAILURE 
Investigation of stresses in gusset plate connections was reported in the research bulletin by 
Whitmore in 1952 [1]. Only concentric tension forces were applied even though the authors 
expected that bending stresses would have an influence on the failure mode. The paper suggests the 
so-called Whitmore method to design against the possibility of tearing out a block of the gusset 
plate, assuming a fictive tension failure line. The length (or width) of the fictive tension failure line 
was based on a 30 degree (1:2) spreading of the forces from the first to the last bolt line, see left 
illustration in Fig. 2.  
Fig. 2. Two conventional and an unconventional yield band block mechanism.

based on the net area for the tension failure line Ant and the gross area for the shear failure area Agv 
as shown for the coped beam in Fig. 2. It is concluded that end rotation does not have a significant 
influence on the capacity. However the test setup is not representative for all types of fin or gusset 
plate beam connections.

In another paper by Huns et al. [9] it is found that the shear capacity after tension rupture happens 
almost for the ultimate material strength. This indicates that the average between ultimate and yield 
strength suggested by Driver et al. [6] is conservative. This paper also concludes that the effect of 
the length of the connection is inconclusive and should therefore not be taken into account.  
As mentioned, Topkaya suggested formulars for block shear capacity of angle connections in [7]. 
The paper contains results from FE analysis of experimental block shear tests in order to develop 
expressions that describe the actual failure. The paper suggests that the strength of the shear plane is 
dependent on the length of the connection and that the strength is dependent on a ratio between the 
yield and ultimate strength. All three formulas proposed are for concentric loads and the paper 
suggest a factor that reduces the strength by 10% if the load is eccentric.  
To sum up the findings in the literature, the crucial conclusion is that none of the papers have been 
dealing with complex loading including substantial eccentricities and general block failure. It is 
stunning that the stress distributions in equilibrium with sections forces have not been investigated; 
perhaps they have been abandoned due to the seemingly complex stress distributions. The reason 
could be that the current formulas are based on empirical research instead of relating it to a theoretic 
approach based on plasticity theory and yield bands. It seems very inappropriate just to introduce a

CAPACITY METHODS FOR RIGID-PLASTIC BLOCK FAILURE ANALYSIS 
Assuming that the material behaves as a rigid plastic material and that all material outside the yield 
bands is rigid opens the possibility of determining the work performed during deformation of the 
rigid blocks relative to each other. This leads to an upper bound for the capacity of the given load 
situation, this involves numerical integration along the yield lines and superposition of results are 
not possible. However lower bound plasticity methods may lead to the formulation of relatively 
simple approximate methods relating individual block failure capacities for normal force, shear 
force, and moment as well as the interaction of these by simple von Mises based interaction 
formulas as exemplified in the following. The lower bound methods are based on the assumption 
that the equilibrium stress fields of the rigid parts are below or at yield everywhere. Thus to find a 
good lower bound approximation we have to find effective allowable stress fields along the yield 
bands. Three basic stress fields acting along the yield band (shown without holes) on the cut-out 
block are shown in Fig. 3. The three relatively simple situations for a C-shaped cut-out correspond 
to the normal force NR, the shear force VR and the moment MR block mechanism capacities. Based 
on experimental observations it is assumed that the holes are just inside the block and the gross 
dimensions are given by hg and bg. Deductions for holes are given by the net lengths respectively 
corresponding to hn and bn. Furthermore it is assumed that the normal stresses are acting on the net 
yield band lengths and shear stresses on the gross yield band lengths. (During mechanism forming 
and yielding the bolt holes are elongated and leave an open trace). The shear force is acting at the 
shear distribution center, given by the edge eccentricity eed as shown in the central situation of Fig. 
3. The straining of the yield bands varies along each line and strain hardening will commence
before the yield mechanism has fully formed, therefore a formal yield stress fm is used. A value of 
the formal yield stress of fm=(fy+fu)/2 corresponding to the mean value of the yield stress and the 
ultimate stress is used. (The shown distributions are relevant for

CONCLUSION 
The theoretical and soon experimental background for including effects of eccentric loads in block 
failure has been presented and ought to be included or allowed for in the codes.
