Abstract
In this project the oxidation behaviour of two metallic copper manganese foams
with a 60:40 distribution and electrospun copper manganese ﬁbers spun in-house at
DTU RISØ with a 1:2 distribution were investigated.
Reduction of the electrospun
copper manganese ﬁbers was also investigated. The investigation was done with the
aim of investigating the viability, of using these material as a contact layer in a SOFC
at the interconnect cathode interface.
In-situ XRD of the copper manganese foams
showed that at 650◦C the metal phase had disappeared and been replaced with spinel
Cu1+xMn2−xO4, CuO and Mn2O3, these phases were still visible at 800◦C. The copper
oxide was observed to be covering the surface of the backbone structure of the foam,
with the other oxides gathering under the CuO and on the alloying particles. Thermo-
graviometric measurement of the foams showed they had a mass gain from oxidation of
28.5±1.16% for the one foam and a mass gain of 35.0±1.16% for the other. A uniaxial
tension test were carried out for each foam measuring the ﬂexibility of the foams at
800◦C after they had been allowed to oxidize, giving Young moduli of 753±191MPa and
392±134MPa.
Oxidation of the electrospun copper manganese ﬁbers showed that these formed
a homogeneous phase after oxidation at 800◦C, this is expected to be a spinel phase.
Reduction was carried out at 850◦C with 9% hydrogen in nitrogen and CO, the 9%
hydrogen in nitrogen showed a slight reduction of copper with the ﬁber structure main-
tained. Reduction with CO showed a larger degree of reduced copper, the ﬁber structure
had collapsed after the reduction with CO.
Van Der Pauw measurements was carried out on both of the copper manganese
foams and the electrospun copper manganese ﬁbers. Where the resistivity was measured
at 800◦C after the samples had time to oxidise, this gave resistivities of 8.66·10−4 ± 5 ·
10−5Ωm and 6.18·10−4 ±3·10−5Ωm, for the electrospun ﬁber a resistivity of 0.345·103 ±
0.1 · 103Ωm was measured, but contacting was suspected to be an issue inﬂating this
number.
From the measurements, it was concluded that the two metal foams showed prop-
erties which would make them viable as a contact material in a SOFC, while for the
electrospun ﬁbers a more reliable method is needed for measuring the resistivity.

Introduction
Currently the energy production industry relies heavily on combustion of fossil fuels
for supplying energy to the energy grid, and it is predicted that energy consumption
around the world will only keep rising, on ﬁgure 1 a prediction of the world wide need
for electrical energy can be seen[1].
Figure 1:
Prediction for the world wide de-
mand for electrical energy[1].
However this reliance on fossil fuels
have been predicted to have severe eﬀects
on the climate causing a variety of nega-
tive impacts. With rising water levels[2],
spread of infectious diseases [3] and agri-
cultural challenges[4]. To solve these chal-
lenges sustainable energy productions so-
lutions such as wind turbines, solar panels
etc. are needed. however these solutions
are associated with a ﬂuctuating energy
production, whereas solid oxide fuel cell
(SOFC) technology compliments these systems creating a stable energy output. SOFC
is a mature technology which have proven to be versatile with regards to fuel and op-
erating conditions[6], with low emissions of green house gasses[5] and high eﬃciencies
(30%-92%)[7][8].
1.1
SOFC applications and technology
The areas of interests for SOFC can be separated into two categories. stationary
SOFC systems for large scale power production and mobile SOFC systems for Axillary
power units (APU), for e.g. automotives. [8],[9]. For stationary systems micro combined
heat and power(mCHP) system have been designed with high eﬃciencies (90%)[7]. The
APU systems developed for automotive are meant to be used as a supplementary energy
source, examples of this is is AVL who have developed SOFC systems for cars reaching
an eﬃciency of 30%[8], and the DESTA project which have tested SOFC systems for
heavy duty trucks where an APU delivering 3 kW with an electrical eﬃciency of 30%
was tested[9].
A SOFC works by having a solid non-porous metal oxide electrolyte, this electrolyte
acts as a oxygen ion conductor. Surrounding the electrolyte are two porous electrodes,
the cathode and the anode, A more in-depth material explanation can be found in
appendix C. Current SOFCs mainly operate at 600-1000◦C[6], the reason for these high

temperatures is to allow for ionic conduction through the electrolyte. On the cathode
side of a SOFC an oxidant ﬂow is introduced. When the oxidant comes into contact
with the cathode interface, the oxygen molecules acquires extra electrons creating oxygen
ions. These oxygen ions then proceeds to diﬀuse through the electrolyte until it comes
into contact with the anode, here the oxygen ions reacts with the fuel which have
been introduced at the anode, the reaction between the oxidant and the fuel produces
electrons, heat and water if hydrogen is used as fuel. In order to get useful voltage
outputs from a SOFC, cells are stacked in series in what is called a stack, the cells in a
stack is electrically connected by a metallic interconnect, from this stack it is possible
to draw a current. A schematic of the working principle of a single cell and stacking is
seen on ﬁgure 2.
Figure 2: Figure depicting the workings of a single cell and how to go from a single to a stack.
Adapted from [10], [11].
The main idea behind SOFCs, is that the electrons released from the reactions at the
anode, can be used to generate a current. The potential across a single cell can be
expressed as.
∆Ucell = ∆U◦
cell −RT
nF ln(Q) −∆Uleak −ir + η(i)

With ∆U◦
cell being the cell potential at standard conditions, R is the gas constant, T
is the temperature, n is number of involved electrons in the reaction, F is the Faraday
constant and Q is the reaction quotient. ∆Uleak is the voltage loss due to leaks, ir is
the ohmic losses and η(i) is polarisation losses. Ohmic losses are especially a concern
and wished to be kept to a minimum.

State of the art SOFC
A state of the art planar SOFC could consist of these materials, for the interconnect,
cathode, anode and cathode[22].

1. INTRODUCTION
3
1. Crofer APU 22 for the metal interconnects[12],[13], this is a ferrictic steel chromium
alloy with a high conductivity and good mechanical stability, it is also resistant
towards both reducing and oxidizing atmospheres, forming a chromia oxide scale
in oxidizing surroundings, this oxide scale is a reasonable conductor[14].
2. For the anode nickel-yttria stabilized zirconia is one of the standard materials
[6], with the nickel giving good conductivity and chemical reactivity, the yttria
stabilized zirconia allows for matching TEC with the rest of the stack to minimize
thermally induced stresses.
3. For the cathode material Lanthanum strontium cobalt ferrite (LSCF) is used quite
often [15], [16].
4. Lastly for the electrolyte yttria stabilized zirconia is the most wide spread material
at operating temperatures of 800◦C since it have an excellent ionic conductivity
at this temperature[6].
In this project the focus was especially on what happens in the cathode-interconnect
interface.
During long time testing with metallic interconnects it have been observed that the Cro-
fer APU 22, forms a chromia oxide scale on top of it [17]. As time goes chromium starts
to evaporate from this oxide scale, this leads to chromium poisoning on the cathode[19]
giving polarization losses. To inhibit the diﬀusion of chromium from the interconnect
onto the cathode, a solid layer of cobalt manganese spinel is sometimes used as a coating
material on the interconnect[18]. The layer of cobalt manganese spinel have been ob-
served to inhibit the formation of the chromia oxide, by serving as barrier for the oxygen,
meanwhile it is also a good enough conductor so as not to give problems with ohmic
losses[20]. But when the cobalt manganese spinel is coated onto the interconnect, it is
needed to be contacted with the cathode, this contact needs to be mechanically stable
and not give problems with ohmic losses. Currently one of the popular contact materials
is LSCF, which have been found to be non reactive towards the cobalt manganese spinel,
while also having a reasonable conductivity[21].

1.3
Current challenges
High eﬃciencies have been achieved with SOFC systems, but the cost of these
systems have still not reached a competitive level with traditional sources of electrical
energy[26],[27]. One of the reasons why SOFC systems have not reached a competitive
level cost wise, is the low life time where a SOFC delivers at peak performance, especially
for systems undergoing thermal cycling deterioration in the output is an issue[28]. It
is expected for APU systems for automotive, that these will need to undergo several
thermal cycles, whenever the automotive is turned oﬀand on.
One of the main reason behind the deterioration of the cells, is mismatching ther-
mal expansion coeﬃcients (TEC) between the diﬀerent layers in a SOFC, this issue
is further enhanced because of the non-uniform heat proﬁles within a SOFC during
operation or thermal cycling[22], these mismatching TECs induces thermal stresses
within a cell, and in some fatal cases these can even result in delamination of the
active components[31],[29], or cracking of active components [30], giving problems with
electric conductivity. If the mismatch in TEC is large between the diﬀerent layers the
delamination can happen as quickly as 500 hours [32]. It have been observed that a
maximum gap of 200nm is allowed between the cathode and the contact layer, before
contact issues becomes a problem[33].
1.4
Overcoming the challenges
As can be seen on ﬁgure 2, the layers involved at the interconnect-cathode interface
currently are mostly rigid ceramics[23],[24]. Such materials does not allow for larger
deformations, before cracking or delaminating. So a possible solution to minimize the
eﬀects of mismatching TECs could be to insert an elastic layer, this layer would act as
a stress buﬀer being able to store stresses, giving stress relief to the surrounding layers.
An optimal material for this would be a spring like material, with a high porosity
to allow for gas transport from the interconnect to the cathode, which could absorb and
store the thermally induced stresses, also showing excellent adhesion to both the cobalt

1.5
Purpose and hypothesis
The aim of this project was to investigate if a open celled copper manganese alloy
foam, exhibits the right properties to be used as a stress relief layer between the cathode
and the interconnect, the purpose of the foam would be to minimize degradation mech-
anisms e.g. delamination and fractures of active components. The copper manganese
alloy in its metallic state is a soft metal, but when it oxidizes it have been observed by
Zielke et al.[25] that it becomes a spinel (Cu1+xMn2−xO4) structure, which have been
observed to have a reasonable conductivity at elevated temerature levels (225S/m at
750◦C[35]). These properties makes it a promising candidate as a stress relief layer.
For this project two diﬀerent copper manganese alloy foams were acquired from
Alantum, To see if these were viable for use in a SOFC, these hypothesis were pursued.
• Hypothesis 1: Does commercially manufactured copper manganese alloy foams
created by electroplating procedures, have a low enough resistivity to not give
problems with ohmic losses and are the foams ﬂexible enough to work as a stress
relief?
This was followed by another hypothesis.
• Hypothesis 2: Did one of the acquired commercial foams from Alantum outperform
the other in terms of resistivity and ﬂexibility?
The general structure of the alloyed foam is on the micrometer scale. Another
thing which was investigated in this project, was if electrospun copper manganese ﬁbers
may be a substitute for the commercial copper manganese foam. Since these ﬁbers are
on the nanometer scale, this would allow for more contact points, possibly improving
adhesion and lowering resistances.
• Hypothesis 3: Is electrospun copper manganese ﬁbers, a viable alternative to the
electroplated metal foams?

When creating a PU foam the two starting ingredients are polyol and isocyanate.
These two ingredient are mixed with water, here the water starts to react with the
isocyanate creating carbon dioxide gas, which are trapped within the mixture, these
trapped gas bubbles gives rise to hollow spaces within the mixture. The polyol and
the isocyanate then starts to polymerize creating a polymer network of polyurethane
elastomers around the gas pockets. It is the combination of the polymerization and the
production of carbon dioxide which creates a foam structure.
When the polymerization reaction has reached a satisfactory point, the foam is
cured to stop further reaction from happening and blocks of foam is cut out in the
wished sizes. Depending on what parameters and starting materials has been used the
cut foam have diﬀerent characteristics, but is normally a partial open-celled foam. To
create a fully open-celled PU foam it needs to undergo reticulation. Reticulation can be
carried out in two diﬀerent ways, either the foam is dropped down into a caustic bath

and the remaining foam between the cells is etched away, or it can undergo zapping,
here the foam is put into a vacuum chamber which is ﬁlled with a mixture of hydrogen
and oxygen, the mixture is then ignited at some point, the ﬂame wall created from this
runs through the chamber burning away the remaining foam between the cells. After
reticulation an open-celled foam is created.
After the creation of the reticulated PU foam, the wished material for the metal
backbone of the metal foam is sputtered onto the PU foam, this can be done by physical
vapour deposition (PVD). After PVD the metal sputtered PU foam undergoes electro-
plating, here the sputtered metal starts to grow on top of the PU foam, ending up with
the metal covering the whole PU foam. After the electroplating process the PU foam
is removed by heat treatment, leaving behind hollow metal ﬁbers arranged in a open-
celled foam structure. In some cases this will be the last step for the manufacturing of
a open-celled metal foam. But in several applications an alloy metal foam is required,
therefore the metal foam need to undergo an additional step. Here a powder of the
wished alloy material is added onto the metal foam, this is followed by a heat treatment
where the alloy material diﬀuses into the backbone structure creating an alloy foam. For
this project the two foams from Alantum were made with a copper backbone structure
alloyed with manganese particles.
2.2
Mechanical analysis
An important aspect of this project was to investigate if the metal foams received
from Alantum were suitable to act as stress reliefs. to analyse this some basic mechanical
theory is needed. In appendix D a short introduction to strain, stress and young modulus
is given. Metals are normally deﬁned by having a high strength, high ductility and high
toughness allowing for large deformations before breaking, whereas ceramics are deﬁned
by having a high strength, low ductility and low toughness, making ceramics a brittle
material which is usually not capable of large deformation before breaking.
This is
the case for bulk materials, but in this project the materials worked with had a foam
structure. A foam structure allows for diﬀerent deformations mechanisms, which diﬀers
from what is seen in a bulk material. Generally the foam structure allows for larger
strains and gives ﬂexibility to a material. In a bulk material the deformation of the
material is normally caused by either material stretching or compressing. But in a open
celled foam, deformation can also happen by bending of the cell walls, giving extra
freedom for deformation. On ﬁgure 7 a sketch of how a single cell within a foam can
deform dependent on if it experiences a compressive or a tensile stress.
So by going from a bulk material to a foam structure, larger deformations can be

by Bragg’s law.

400◦C where it was kept for an hour, followed by another heating with a rate of 1◦C
till 500◦C where it was kept for an hour, this was followed by a cooling with a rate of
1◦C until RT. The calcination was done to remove the polymers and acetates in the
samples to avoid excessive shrinkage while measuring the resistance so as not to lose
contact, the reason for the slow heating rates was to avoid ruining the ﬁbers in the foam
by inducing thermal tensions. After the calcination of the ES-foam it was glued on top
of an alumina plate with gold paste, the gold paste was connected to platinum wires,
this setup can be seen on ﬁgure 17c. The ES-ﬁbers then underwent the same treatment
as the metal foams. For each sample four resistance measurements were made, to get
two measurements for the vertical resistance and two measurements for the horizontal
resistance.

4.2.1
Resistance measurement on electrospun ﬁbers
A Van Der Pauw measurement was carried out for the electrospun ﬁbers. The data
for the calculations can be seen in appendix G, but in general the resistances measured
ﬂuctuated by a large margin. In the start for measuring the resistivity of the ﬁbers, the
same measurement setup as was used for the metal foams was tried, but was observed not
to work since the foam was ripped apart by the current probes, after that the setup seen
on ﬁgure 17c was used but it seems proper contacting may still be an issue. An average
horizontal resistance of 4.85±0.129MΩwas measured and a average vertical resistance
of 5.29±0.796MΩwas measured. From this a sheet resistance of 23.0±1.88MΩ. From a
sectional picture a rough estimation of the thickness of ﬁbers was measured, the porosity
of the ES-ﬁber was not calculated in this project, so it is only possible to calculate the
engineering resistivity.
A thickness of 15±5µm was measured giving an engineering
resistivity of 0.345±0.118kΩm. This is signiﬁcantly higher than what was measured for
the foams, this could be caused by contacting issues, or that the ﬁbers cracks during
measurement.
4.2.2
Summary and discussion
Based on the measurements made in this project the answer to hypothesis 3. which
asked if electrospun copper manganse ﬁbers were a viable alternative to the metal foam,
is no. Although it is observed that the ﬁbers are homogeneous after oxidation and seems
to oxidise into a homogeneous spinel structures, a too high resistivity of 0.345±0.118kΩm
was measured, which would cause large ohmic losses in a SOFC cell. It was also not
possible to reduce the oxidised ﬁbers, so the ﬁbers would have to be inserted into the
SOFC in their ceramic state where the ductility is gone making proper contacting an
issue. But it was also observed that contact to the electrospun ﬁbers was an issue, so
the resistivity measured needs to measured in another way to check up on this result.
The volume expansion seen on the sintered ﬁbers, could also have a negative eﬀect on
the ﬂexibility of the foam.

was about the investigation of the electrospun copper manganese ﬁbers.

6. OUTLOOK

6
Outlook
The next natural step for the copper manganese foams would be to test the CuMn-
2 step in a SRU setup as have been done for the CuMn-1 foam[25]. If this is observed to
perform better than the CuMn-1 foam in a SRU setup the development should continue
with the CuMn-2 foam. There are still some properties of the foams that needs to be
investigated e.g. creep behaviour at elevated temperatures, degradation during thermal
cycling, deformation with a compressive stress, long time phase stability and reactivity
with surrounding SOFC layers.
For the electrospun ﬁbers it is still possible that these can be used, if a proper
contacting method is found for measuring the conductivity of ﬁbers. Since these were
shown to form a homogeneous phase, which have the advantage of avoiding the current
needing to travel through layers of copper oxide.
Also XRD is needed to be done
on the ﬁbers, to conﬁrm if a spinel structure is formed after oxidation.

insert these in their oxidised condition into the SOFC.

Appendix C
SOFC materials
The change in the SOFC design have been driven by the research of new materials,
giving new possibilities in regards to operational temperature, performance, robustness
and lifetime. So material research is a vital part in the development and improvement
of SOFCs. For this section on materials the main reference is[62].
Electrolyte
The electrolytes purpose within a SOFC is to transport negatively charged oxygen
ions from the electrolyte-cathode interface to the electrolyte-anode interface, therefore
the electrolyte material is required to have a high ionic conductivity, but this is only the
most basic requirement for the electrolyte to be successful, other requirements include:
Low electronic conductivity, negligible fuel migration through the electrolyte, matching
TECs with the rest of the stack, stable, inert with regards to the electrodes at a variety
of diﬀerent operating conditions.
From all of these strict requirements a few materials have emerged and proven interest-
ing. The most famous of these being zirconia (ZrO2). This material was already discov-
ered in the 1890’s by Nernst[63] where he found out that in certain temperature intervals
stabilized zirconia would conduct oxygen ions. Then in 1943 Bauer and Preis proved that
this material could be used in fuel cells[64]. Currently yttria (Y2O3) stabilized zirconia
is the most widely used electrolyte for high and intermediate temperature(600-1000◦C)
SOFCs, the reason for doping the zirconia with yttria is to further enhance the ionic
conductivity. This is done by creating extra oxygen vacancies within the material. This
process can be explained by the defect equation written with Kroger-Vink notation.

(CeO2) and lanthanim gallate (LaGaO3)-based perovskites as promising candidates for
a low temperature SOFC electrolyte material.

Anode
The purpose of the anode is to facilitate the electrochemical oxidation of the fuel
used in the SOFC, and to transport electrons out to an external circuit. To fulﬁl these
purposes there are several requirements for the material used for the anode. It needs
to have good electrochemical kinetics, be a good catalyst, be porous to give a good
transport of fuel onto the anode-electrolyte interface, low ionic conductivity, matching
TEC with the rest of the stack, mechanically stable and it needs to be a good electrical
conductor. A material which fulﬁl these conditions is nickel-yttria stabilized zirconia
(Ni-YSZ). In this material the nickel serves as the electronic conductor also giving the
catalytic activity required. The YSZ allows for the anode to have a matching TEC with
the rest of the stack. Even though Ni-YSZ is the material of choice used for anodes for
many it is not perfect.
Ni-YSZ is very sensitive towards sulfur and other contaminants, so if natural gas or
similar is used for fuel, the fuel needs to be desulfurized before being introduced at the
anode. The Ni-YSZ anode is also very sensitive towards oxidation, especially at elevated
temperatures this becomes a serious concern, because of the volume changes when the
nickel undergoes oxidation, these changes in volume damages the structural integrity of
the stack. If hydrocarbons are used for fuel the Ni-YSZ material have also shown an
inclination for carbon formation.
So research is still done towards ﬁnding new materials to use as anodes, ceria anodes
are one of the possible alternatives.
Cathode
In a SOFC the purpose of the cathode is the reduction of oxygen into oxygen ions,
this reaction happens at the cathode-electrolyte interface where electrons reacts with the
oxygen to create oxygen ions. The oxygen ions can then travel through the electrolyte
by occupying oxygen vacancies in the YSZ material. The process of oxygen going from
the cathode into the electrolyte can be described by the following reaction written with
Kroger-Vink notation.

The requirements for a cathode is that it have a high reaction activity, high electrical
conductivity, thermal and chemical stability and mechanical stable at diﬀerent tem-
peratures. Some of the materials which fulﬁll these requirements are lanthanum-based

perovskites, e.g. lanthanum manganite (LaMnO3), strontium-doped lanthanum man-

fulﬁl many of the requirements for a cathode materials in a SOFC, there are problems

Interconnects
Interconnects serve the purpose of electrically connecting the diﬀerent cells in a
SOFC stack to an external electrical circuit. Interconnect materials can be separated
into two diﬀerent groups. Interconnect materials where higher operation temperatures
(900-1000◦C) are needed, these are mainly based around conductive perovskites, inter-
connect materials for lower operation temperatures are based around metallic alloys.
The ceramic interconnects mainly used are doped lanthanum and yttrium chromites.
These ceramics exhibit high electrical conductivities at elevated temperatures, they are
stable towards reducing atmospheres at high temperatures and it is possible to change
the thermo-mechanical properties to match the rest of the stack by doping them. But
these ceramics have the set-backs of not being very ductile and having a low ﬂexibility,
these properties makes it diﬃcult to achieve good connections with the surrounding ma-
terials, to overcome this conductive pastes are sometimes used for connections, but the
reliability of these are low. Also at lower temperatures the conductivity of these mate-
rials starts to fall, making them unsuitable for low-intermediate temperature SOFCs.
With the development of SOFCs, operation temperatures have been lowered opening
for the possibility of using metallic interconnects instead of ceramic interconnects. The
advantage of these are the high electrical conductivity, low material cost, easy to process
and they are ﬂexible. But it is required of the metallic interconnects, that they are able
to withstand a strong oxidizing atmosphere on the cathode side and a strong reducing
atmosphere on the anode side, also the metal should be resistant towards creep, to allow
for good contact to the cell components, this puts severe limitation onto which metals
and alloys can be used. The most commonly used metallic interconnect used is the
Crofer22 APU. This metallic interconnect is based on it developing a protective and
conductive chromium oxide, allowing for it to work under both reducing and oxidizing
atmospheres. But during long term operation the oxide evaporates, leading to chromium
poisoning of the electrodes giving polarization losses.
