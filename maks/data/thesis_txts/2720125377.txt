Abstract 
Nearly zero energy buildings demonstrate excellent heat retention capabilities due to their superior 
insulation and low air tightness. However, this attribute renders them susceptible to overheating 
events. In this thesis the performance of different solutions that are less reliant on occupant 
behavior is explored. The solutions include automatic roof windows, mechanical cooling (through 
the heat exchanger bypass) and low g-value glazing.  
To document their operation the IDA ICE indoor climate and energy dynamic simulation software 
was employed. Their performance is simulated in a dwelling with a detailed spatial and time 
description of the internal gains in order to locate the critical zones with higher precision. Likewise, 
a daylight analysis is conducted to determine the most likely glass to floor surface scenario on each 
zone and therefore the solar gains level.  
The results showcased the superior performance of automatic roof windows as their operation 
during the occupant absence results is key in minimizing the overheating hours. The low g-value 
glazing was mostly successful in flattening the high Temperatures spikes and keeping the 
overheating levels very close to the BR18 requirements. The mechanical cooling option displayed 
the highest sensitivity to parameters such as indoor door opening profile and supply air flow rates.   
The second part of the thesis deals with electricity reliant heating solutions, such as electric heaters 
and different types of heat pumps and how they perform against a hydronic room system utilizing 
a natural gas boiler. After their indoor and energy performance was confirmed to be within the 
BR18 limits, their operation was assessed from a life cycle cost perspective in various insulation 
thickness scenarios through the IDA ICE parametric analysis tools.  
In all scenarios the electric heating showed a lower life cycle cost compared to the natural gas 
boiler as a result of the minimal heating loads. Air heating solutions although they reported the 
best results from a financial and energy perspective, they failed to perform in different zone 
setpoint scenarios and proved sensitive to indoor door closing profiles.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Buildings constitute approximately 40 % of the total energy consumption in the EU. In order to 
reach the 2030 and 2050 EU climate targets the renovation and construction of nearly zero energy 
buildings (NZEB) is crucial since there are responsible for a major part of the greenhouse gases 
emissions (European Commission, 2022). 
NZEB demonstrate superior energy performance and are economically feasible while fulfilling the 
health and comfort indoor environmental quality criteria. In the current Danish building 
regulations, the residential consumption should not exceed 30 Kwh/m2/year excluding the lighting 
and equipment usage. Furthermore, if the dwelling is classified as low energy class the Domestic 
Hot Water (DHW), heating cooling and ventilation consumption is expected to be as low as 27 
Kwh/ m2/year (BR18). 
Therefore, such constructions need to demonstrate relatively low window and wall U-values and 
high airtightness. High component efficiency and heat recovery systems are essential when aiming 
for such low energy needs. On top of that, a certain percentage of the required energy has to be 
generated on-site from heat pumps, or from photovoltaic and solar thermal systems (Mazzarella, 
2016).  
This means that NZEB have excellent heat retention capabilities. Furthermore, the minimal energy 
consumption also means that heating solutions that were effective in less energy efficient buildings 
might not be cost optimal in today’s dwellings. The purpose of this thesis is to examine different 
cooling and heating proposals that are economically and energy viable in a NZEB context.  
1.2 Motivation 
Overheating issues are currently one the most reported problems in NZEB (Attila et al., 2021). 
The architects in their attempt to minimize energy consumption focus on passive solar design 
strategies to exploit the solar gains as much as possible. However, this approach might be optimal 
in buildings which are less airtight or insulated, but in a NZEB context this entails an increased 
risk of high Temperatures even during the heating season. The main reason is that in these 
residences the cooling demand is more reliant on the internal and solar gains rather than the outdoor 
Temperatures.  
Special focus must be given when describing the ventilative cooling parameters. Real factors such 
as window opening percentage, occupant presence, window and shading behavior have to be 
carefully evaluated (Videau and al., 2013). While one could accept perfect control of natural 
ventilation and get away with it in an older building, in a NZEB this will probably end up having 
dire consequences in terms of overheating. On top of that, window opening during the heating 
season can easily lead to overcooling and heating consumption increase, especially if the rebound 
effect is considered.  
On the other hand, the low thermal losses during winter reduce the hydronic room heating system’s 
utility. More flexible options, such as electric heating could be more suitable in these situations

since they do not have the embodied energy or the high installation cost of the hydronic room 
system.  
Finally, as a result of the ever-decreasing heating loads the Domestic Hot Water usage ends up 
constituting a major part of the total building energy consumption (European Commission, 2015) 
making options such as air to water heat pumps or drain water heat recovery worth considering.  
An air to water heat pump connected to the domestic hot water consumption not only is beneficial 
due to its high COP but it also enables the user to take advantage of the electricity from renewable 
energy sources.  An air to water heat pump can also warm the cold supply air by providing hot 
water in the AHU heating coil. When the low space heating demands of the nearly zero energy 
buildings are considered it raises the question if it is possible to cover partially or fully the heating 
loads through air heating. 
1.3 Aim and scope 
In this thesis, an average sized building for a 4-people Danish family with a detailed description 
of the spatial and time distribution of internal heat gains will be simulated. A presentation of the 
buildings energy systems is given. 
Then, a daylight analysis is conducted to determine the size of the glass surface area. Three cases 
are examined where the indoor zones fulfill the SDA300, SDA500 and SDA750 criteria. The 
optimal case which is going to be basis for the future simulations on overheating and heating 
solutions is selected. Factors such as daylight, heating consumption, aestheticism and structural 
restrictions are being taken into account. 
Thereafter, the effect of the building’s orientation, glazing surface area, and g-value have on the 
solar heat gains is investigated. Afterwards, ventilative solutions such as automatic roof windows 
and mechanical cooling (by bypassing the heat exchanger without utilizing the AHU cooling coil) 
are introduced. The robustness of these proposals lies on the fact that the ventilation command is 
going to be mechanical rather than manual.  
Afterwards, multiple heating solutions are explored. The solutions include electric heaters, Air to 
Water Heat Pump, ductless MiniSplit Air to Air Heat pump or a combination of these suggestions. 
These systems will be compared in terms of energy performance and thermal comfort in relation 
to a scenario where the building is being heated by a conventional hydronic system using a natural 
gas boiler.  
Subsequently, the robustness of the heating proposals would be tested in a scenario where different 
heating setpoints are implement in the different indoor zones.  
Finally, the financial feasibility of these strategies for multiple insulation thickness sizes will be 
evaluated from a 50-year life cycle perspective. The optimal insulation thickness for each specific 
heating proposal is presented. 
In conclusion, the goal of the investigation is presented in the following questions:

2. State of the Art 
Overheating solutions such as roof windows and low g-glazing value have often been implemented 
in European countries. When it comes to automatic roof windows however not a lot of literature 
can be found, especially when such a system is installed in a nearly-zero energy building.  
Regarding roof windows special focus has to be given to heiselberg et al (2017) where the effect 
of automatic windows with integrated solar shading have on overheating hours is investigated. It 
is concluded that automatic roof windows have the potential of meeting the standard requirements. 
On the other hand, no conclusions can be deducted if a manual windows opening is assumed due 
to the unpredictable occupant behavior.  
Antoni Jan Radon (2022) also showcased the advantage of automated roof windows against 
manual strategies. The results were for a single Danish family house and the focus was in the 
automatic and manual window opening algorithms. Reasonable assumptions were taken regarding 
the glass to floor surface ratio and the level of the internal heat gains although no detailed analysis 
of the windows layout and exact thermal gains distribution was conducted.  
Concerning the low g-value glazing solutions, Skarning et al. (2016) has examined different g-
values for different glass to floor ratios and its findings helped in the definition of the minimum 
required g-value glazing in this thesis.  
In relation to utilizing low supply air Temperatures (by bypassing the heat exchanger, without 
having to activate the AHU cooling coil) as a solution against overheating the existing literature is 
also limited. 
Wang et al. (2016) has investigated the effect of low supply air Temperatures in commercial 
buildings as a free cooling option. The focus was in the cost and energy savings which 
demonstrated promising results. Nevertheless, it remains unclear how a solution like this would 
perform in a single-family house and if it would be enough to keep the overheating hours lower 
than the maximum permissible limits.  
Within the field of life cycle cost of the investigated technologies (Electric Radiators, Air to Water 
Heat Pump, MiniSplit air to air heat pump) kegel et al. (2012) offers some insight. In this paper, 
the economic feasibility of different heat pump types (air to air & water to air heat pumps) assisted 
by a solar system was evaluated and compared to a natural gas boiler for a 1980’s and a nearly 
zero energy Canadian house. In both cases the heat pump demonstrated superior financial gains 
compared to the natural gas boiler. 
Furthermore, Siren et al. (2008) examined the life cycle cost of a single-family house in Finland 
where electric heating (AHU & electric radiators) is utilized to cover the space heating needs. The 
analysis was conducted for different insulation thickness levels, lifespans and varying electricity 
prices.The trend of the analysis was towards investing in high insulation thicknesses.

3.2 Distribution of Internal Heat Gains 
NZEB display high heat retention capabilities thus there is a higher chance of overheating events 
in the zones where the highest percentage of internal gains is recorded. Therefore, their equal 
distribution in the rooms is not the ideal approach. A detailed spatial and time analysis of these 
gains is required as it enables the development of a more accurate report of the overheating events. 
In IDA ICE, three internal gains categories are defined. Internal gains from occupants, lighting, 
and equipment. First the occupant gains will be described.

In IDA ICE the user is allowed to put only the metabolic activity as an input which corresponds to

3.3 Building Energy Systems 
The building is using CAV mechanical ventilation to maintain the proper air quality. Its efficiency 
is increased not only because of the high airtightness of the structure but also by utilizing the heat 
from exhaust air with a 85% heat recovery heat exchanger. A heat exchanger with 85% heat 
recovery indicates that 85% of the warm exhaust air heat is transferred to the fresh cold supply air. 
The value 85% is selected as it is the minimum dry temperature heat exchanger efficiency based 
on the BR20 standards for single dwellings. For the initial simulations a macro representing the 
AHU operation in a simplified way is used, as shown in figure 3.2. 
A macro is an automated sequence that defines how an input is regulated to a certain output. In the 
IDA ICE AHU macro, the inputs are the outdoor air (defined as AirSupply) and the exhaust air 
which is extracted from the indoor zone. Likewise, the outputs are the supply air in the indoor zone 
and the exhaust air (defined as AirExhaust) which is the air leaving the dwelling through the AHU. 
 
Figure 3.2: AHU operation macro. 
The AHU macro consists of the supply and exhaust fans, the heat exchanger and the heating coil. 
It is visible that the fans have a pressure rise of 400 Pa and operate on 0.6 efficiency which results 
in 0.66 KJ/m3 SFP (Specific fan power). The fans and the heat exchangers operation boxes on the

3.6 Orientation Choice  
When selecting the proper orientation for the building, different variables have to be taken into 
account with the most important being the visual and thermal comfort and the passive cooling 
design. Nevertheless, designers do not always have complete freedom in the selection of the 
building’s orientation. Topography restrictions or simply arguments that a certain position offers 
the highest quality view out end up having the biggest impact in the final decision.  
Thus, for the building’s orientation choice four separate orientation choices are considered North, 
East, South and West. The overheating proposals will be examined against these four different 
directions.  
Moreover, a parametric analysis on IDA ICE is conducted and the orientation effect on the space 
heating demand and the solar heat gains is investigated. The results will offer a further clarification 
on why the cooling loads are higher in certain cases, and which is the dwelling’s best orientation 
choice in general.

4. Methods – Overheating solutions 
As it was previously described no active cooling through the AHU’s cooling coil is allowed in 
single dwellings in Denmark. That, combined with the minimal heat losses of NZEB, render the 
design of robust passive cooling systems vital.   
In this master thesis three overheating treatment suggestions are considered: 
• Automatic Roof Windows  
• Mechanical Cooling or free cooling (through the heat exchanger bypass) (No roof windows 
are installed in the dwelling) 
• Glazing with Low g-value (No roof windows are installed in the dwelling) 
These suggestions will be examined for four different residence orientation scenarios (North, East, 
South and West) in order to document how they behave against different cooling loads. 
In the daylight analysis it was stated that a roof window is installed in the kitchen in order for the 
zone to fulfill the SDA500 requirements. However, in the mechanical cooling and low g-value 
glazing simulations no roof window is present. This is done in order to facilitate the comparison 
between these three proposals and document how each of these solutions behave independently of 
each other.   
It is worth mentioning that external shading was also initially considered as a passive cooling 
option, but it was decided to not be investigated thoroughly due to its big disadvantages. External 
shading although effective it is heavily reliant on the occupant’s behavior. The installation and 
maintenance cost is another factor that needs to be considered, especially if it is intended to 
implement multiple shading devices in the dwelling.  
But most importantly, external shading has a detrimental effect on the zone’s daylight. The 
viewout quality and the higher daylight illuminance are the primarily reasons, the larger glazing 
surface areas in the dining room and the living room were chosen in the daylight analysis. By 
opting for external shading, we would reject the arguments that decided this dwelling’s window 
arrangement in the first place.  
Therefore, external shading has the least priority among the other proposals. External shading 
should be the last resort in the unfortunate event where a miscalculation in the overheating report 
is discovered too late. For this reason, only the other three overheating proposals will be 
investigated thoroughly.

4.3 Mechanical Cooling 
The mechanical cooling concept depends on utilizing the dwelling’s HVAC system and supplying 
air on its outside temperature by bypassing the heat exchanger. In order to simulate the AHU 
operation a new AHU control macro is used.  
The previous one (as shown in figure 3.2) intended to maintain the supply temperature at 18 0C 
constantly. In the new AHU macro, which is described in figure 4.3, the supply temperature 
fluctuates between 12 0C and 18 0C depending on value of the return air temperature.  
==PAGE:32==
Innovative Heating and Ventilation Systems for Cost-Effective Low Energy Homes                              24 
 
Figure 4.3: AHU with Mechanical Cooling 
The AHU macro consists of the exhaust and supply fans with 0.66 SFP and the heat exchanger 
(85% heat recovery), the heating coil and a temperature sensor.  
The sensor measures the air temperature in the exhaust duct and sends a signal in the heating and 
cooling control through the heat exchanger. The heat exchanger cooling control output modifies 
the supply air temperature by adjusting the supply air flows that bypass the heat exchanger.  
In the heating coil while the Temperature remains below 20.5 0C (as 20 0C is the heating setpoint 
in each zone) the supply Temperature is maintained at 18 0C.  
In the cooling control the concept is the same. As long as the indoor air temperature is below 22 
0C, supply air of 18 0C is provided to the zone. When the indoor temperature exceeds 22 0C then 
low temperature supply air of 12 0C is provided to the zone. The cooling setpoint for the return air 
is set 1.5 degrees higher than the heating one to increase the system’s robustness.

4.4 Glazing with low g-values.  
G-value is the total solar energy transmittance which expresses the solar gain percentage that is 
transmitted through the window into the indoor zone. Windows with low g-value reflect a high 
amount of solar gains and they are a cheap solution (compared to external shading) against 
overheating. The fact that it is not occupant reliant is another advantage of this proposal.  
The best low g-value glazing is able to block the solar radiation in the infrared region while 
allowing the light in the visible area to go through. Nevertheless, when trying to achieve very low 
g-values this gradually has a negative effect on the visible Light Transmittance (which is the 
percentage of visible light that is transmitted through the window.) 
In order to decide until which point it is allowed to decrease the g-value without significantly 
affecting the zones daylight the diagrams from Skarning et al. (2016) are used as a guidance. 
 
Figure 4.4: Correlation between glass-to-floor-ratio, glazing g-value, space heating demand and 
light transmittance. (Source: Skarning et al., 2016) 
Many conclusions can be drawn from these graphs relating to overheating and space heating. 
However, the bolded black curve that sets the lower limits of the grey area is the one where special 
focus is given. This line represents the boundary for clear glazing. Hence, for 20% percent glazing-
to-floor-ratio for a glazing with U-value = 0.5, the lowest g-value for windows facing the southern 
orientation is 35%. The same g-value is selected for the eastern, western, and northern orientation.

5. Methods-Heating Solutions 
As the overheating subject has been thoroughly analyzed, the current chapter will investigate the 
challenges faced in the heating season. In this section the indoor and energy performance of 
multiple heating systems will be compared as well as their total cost from a life cycle point of 
view. The examined space heating systems are the following: 
• Natural Gas boiler (Reference Case) (Hot Water Radiators) 
• Electric Heaters (Electric Radiators) 
• Air to Water Heat Pump (Air Heating) 
• Air to Water Heat Pump (Air Heating) & Electric Radiators  
• Electric Radiators & MiniSplit Air to Air Heat Pump  
Firstly, each system is investigated separately to confirm that the BR18 thermal comfort and indoor 
air quality requirements are fulfilled. The insulation thickness in these simulations runs is fixed 
and structure’s U-values are the same as the ones presented on table 3.1.  
The heating simulations are based on the findings of the overheating analysis. Therefore, the 
building’s orientation is towards the south (the larger kitchen and living room glass surface areas 
have a southern direction) and VELFAC 200 Energy Std/Clear/Energy Std glazing is used with its 
properties shown in table 3.10. As it is demonstrated in the results of the overheating proposals, 
this exact orientation and glazing properties with the combination of two roof windows (one in the 
Kitchen and one in the living room) minimizes the heating consumption while the overheating 
hours are maintained lower than the permissible levels.

With respect to the Domestic Hot Water Consumption the following alternatives are examined. 
• Natural Gas Boiler 
• Electric Hot Water Tank Heater 
• Air to Water Heat Pump

5.3 Air to Water Heat Pump (Air Heating) System 
A centralized Air to Water Heat Pump which is produced from Nilon Compact P manufacturer is 
chosen. This specific heat pump is using a counterflow heat exchanger which takes advantage of 
the warm exhaust air and is able to perform on high temperature efficiencies. In figure 5.1 the heat 
pump operation is illustrated. 
 
Figure 5.1: Nilon Compact P air to water heat pump operation scheme. (Source: Nilon Compact

The warm exhaust air coming out of the indoor zone warms the cold outdoor air. The heating coil 
is heating the air to the desired level when the air temperature after the heat exchanger is reported 
to be lower than the permissible limits. In a scenario where the outdoor air is warm enough the 
bypass function is activated, and the air is driven directly into the rooms bypassing the heat 
exchanger. This bypass function has the possibility to be partial depending on the outdoor 
temperature values.  
The thermal energy that has not been retrieved is utilized for the Domestic Hot Water production. 
A hot water tank is used to store the thermal energy as the Air to Water Heat Pump is incapable of 
providing adequate amounts of hot water on demand.  
The heat pump has also an active cooling option by cooling the hot air through a cooling coil. 
Nevertheless, this function is not being utilized here as in Denmark no active cooling is allowed 
to decrease the temperature inside the residence.

In an ideal dwelling not all zones have the same temperature. People in bedrooms usually desire 
lower temperatures than 200C for improved sleep quality reasons, whereas in the Kitchen, the 
Living Room and the bathrooms temperatures as high 240C may be requested (Berge et al., 2016). 
One reason for this, is that the occupants spend most of the time in the kitchen or in the Living 
Room. Another explanation is the type of activities that take place in the zones such as showers 
which inherently are more pleasant on higher thermal comfort levels.

warm air and heat up the zones up to 180C. Then, if the occupants still desire higher temperatures, 
they will be able to turn on the electric radiators. Thereby, thanks to the system’s flexibility, higher 
temperatures will be able to be maintained in these two zones. The advantage of this system is its 
higher flexibility as the AHU heating coil heats up the air to 180C and thereafter the local electric 
heaters would add the supplemental heat in the desired zones.

8. Discussion 
In this chapter the thesis findings in relation to the questions asked in the beginning are evaluated. 
Q1-What can be done to realistically reduce overheating? How can we manage the 
solar gains successfully? 
The most serious overheating events are expected to occur in the eastern and southern facades. 
Nevertheless, minimizing the glass facade in the southern orientation is not advised as its higher 
solar gains during winter help in minimizing the heat demand which is key in NZEB. 
The worst facades for window placement are primarily the eastern and secondary the western 
facade. Low solar gains during winter months and high heat spikes in the summer are documented 
and it is suggested to minimize the glass surface particularly in the eastern facade when possible. 
The northern orientation demonstrated similar or greater solar gains to the eastern and western 
orientation during the winter months. At the same time the minimal solar gains in the cooling 
season minimize the risk of overheating. 
In practice fewer overheating events are recorded in the western facades due to the time 
distribution of the solar gains compared to east and south. The western solar gains start to peak 
after 16:00 in the cooling season when the manual window opening is enabled. The western solar 
gains peak is close to the internal gains peak in the kitchen but still the zone displayed lower 
overheating hours in general when facing the west. This showcases the significance of natural 
ventilation. It also proves that the systematic placement of multiple windows in the indoor zones 
during the daylight analysis to enable crossflow ventilation was the right approach.  
The bedrooms have minimal internal gains and documented negligible overheating hours in all 
overheating proposals and orientation scenarios. Thus, the designer can be more flexible when it 
comes to their placement. Placing the bedrooms towards the east allows the occupants to exploit 
the early morning sun with minimal overheating risks. Nevertheless, a placement towards the north 
equals significantly lower solar gains and Temperatures and thus improved sleep quality and it is 
the more advantageous option.  
The living room and the kitchen are the most crucial zones since it is expected to have both the 
highest internal gains and the highest glass to floor ratio percentage (mainly for view out quality 
reasons.) Thus, at least partial window placement towards colder orientations is advised in these 
zones. 
From all the overheating proposals the automatic roof windows eliminate the issue completely as 
they are able to remove the excess from the crucial zones during the occupant absence. Their 
presence alleviates any overheating miscalculations due to the occupant manual window opening. 
Their robustness was maintained even with a permanent closed-door profile and their performance 
was sustained with the implementation of a high g-value glazing (g-value=0.61) on zones with 
high internal gains and large amounts of glass surface.  
The low g-value glazing demonstrates worse results but still quite close to the BR18 standard 
requirements. While the occupants should expect a dwelling overheated above 26 0C almost every 
==PAGE:71==
63                                Innovative Heating and Ventilation Systems for Cost-Effective Low Energy Homes 
time they return home in the cooling season, temperature spikes above 27 0C are less likely to 
occur. The solution’s performance reported results very closed to the permissible limits during a 
closed-door profile. On the other hand, low g-value glazing has a detrimental effect on the heating 
demand and the potential to be the most expensive solution from a life cycle cost perspective.  
In a closed-door scenario mechanical cooling failed to meet the BR18 standards due to the 
minimum air flows supplied to the zones. However, this option should not be disregarded as it has 
the potential to eliminate manual window opening during colder periods and the overcooling this 
action entails.  
For this thesis it was assumed that the occupants would not open the windows during the heating 
season or during nighttime because the temperature is within the lower thermal comfort range and 
the air quality is maintained through the AHU. Nevertheless, a window opening (during nighttime 
for example) is still likely to occur as the human decision-making can be unpredictable. 
Mechanical cooling can tackle this issue as it can provide cold outdoor air on demand at the proper 
air velocity without any draught risk. Since the command is mechanical rather than manual no 
overcooling events are expected. The difference between the theoretical simulated heating 
consumption and the actual measured consumption is predicted to be reduced.  
The optimal solution would be a combination of automatic roof windows and a mechanical cooling 
system since the operation of the first counters the disadvantage of the latter. Automatic roof 
windows can maintain the temperature at low levels in a closed doors worst case scenario while 
mechanical cooling considerably mitigates the risk of overcooling. Both systems are also optimal 
from a heating perspective. Roof windows allow the implementation high g-value glazing and 
large southern glass surfaces while lower supply temperatures reduce the hours during which the 
AHU heating coil is utilized. 
Q3-Is electrical heating or air heating a viable alternative to hydronic room heating? 
How does the insulation thickness affect the optimal heating system choice?  
In NZEB scenario with minimum heating loads, electric heating has the potential to be an 
alternative to hydronic room heating. The electric heaters displayed lower overall life cycle cost 
thanks to their minimum capital cost compared to the natural gas case. Nevertheless, utilizing 
electric heaters for both the space heating and the domestic hot water consumption is likely to 
result in exceeding the BR18 standard requirements. A natural gas boiler or a small air to water 
heat pump could be needed for the domestic hot water consumption. This will increase the capital 
cost of the electric heater strategy but still its total life cycle cost is predicted to remain lower than 
the natural gas boiler case. 
Furthermore, due to the higher operational cost of electricity, solutions such as drain heat recovery, 
or MiniSplit air to air heat pump have the highest effect both energetically and economically and 
are highly recommended in the electric heater’s scenario.  
On the contrary, suggestions that result in energy savings have the least effect in the air to water 
heat pump (air heating) scenario. On this instance both the DHW consumption and the space 
==PAGE:72==
Innovative Heating and Ventilation Systems for Cost-Effective Low Energy Homes                              64 
heating are minimal and even increasing the insulation thickness  (which in the rest of the scenarios 
is the optimal approach) is not cost effective.  
Nevertheless, the air heating scenario demonstrated certain drawbacks. In a closed-door profile, 
temperatures up to 24 0C are recorded in the bedrooms which cancels the improved sleep quality 
due to lower temperatures concept. A window opening in such a scenario could have a disastrous 
effect on the heating consumption as most of the dwelling’s heat is supplied through the bedrooms. 
Furthermore, achieving different temperature setpoints per zone is almost impossible and this is 
the most serious disadvantage compared to the more energy flexible electric radiators.  
In general, increasing the insulation thickness has a positive effect in the total life cycle cost even 
for the natural gas boiler case. Nevertheless, the insulation increase has an upper limit after which 
the reduction in operational cost does not compensate for the increase in the insulation price. 
The electric heaters and the natural gas boiler demonstrated a similar total life cycle cost pattern 
and reported their respective lowest life cycle cost for the same amounts of insulation thickness. 
Nevertheless, as the envelope’s U-value is increased and the primary electricity consumption rises 
the pattern is expected to change and the natural gas boiler to be optimal option. This highlights 
the need for parametric analysis with other less effective insulation types. 
 
Suggestions for future work 
• An overall economic assessment of the different overheating solutions. 
• A more stochastic manual window opening profile which could document with more detail 
overheating or overcooling events is recommended. 
• Energy performance of the electric heating proposals in a time varying primary energy 
factors scheme is worth being examined. 
• Investigate the total life cycle cost of the proposed technologies for different types of 
insulation.

9. Conclusions 
Automatic roof windows are considerably the best overall answer against overheating. They 
succeeded in maintaining the overheating hours in low levels even in scenarios with high g-value 
glazing and large southern orientated windows and allow the implantation of passive heating 
strategies.  
Low g-value glazing, and mechanical cooling proved sensitive to the indoor door profiles with the 
latter displaying the worst overall results. Nonetheless, if the overcooling risks are taken into 
account a combination of automatic roof windows and mechanical cooling is recommended as the 
optimal strategy.  
Regarding the heating technologies, in NZEB where the designers strive to minimize the heating 
loads, electric heating is an option worth considering. Not only it displayed superior overall 
financial performance compared to the natural gas boiler but also it does not include the embodied 
energy of the hydronic room heating installation. 
The scenario where an air to water heat pump was utilized for air heating, reported the least energy 
consumption and lowest life cycle costs. Nevertheless, on this instance a uniformed temperature 
profile between the different zones is unavoidable.  
On the other hand, the electric heating scenario documented high levels of energy consumption 
and in lower insulation thicknesses entail the danger of exceeding the BR18 maximum standard 
requirements. Thus, the implementation of energy saving solutions such as drain water heat 
recovery or ductless MiniSplit air to air heat pump are strongly recommended both from an energy 
and a financial perspective.

Floor heating Calculations 
Regarding the bathroom floor heating, special focus is given to the floor Temperature level. The 
floor heating wires are installed 0.02m below the floor surface.  The floor Temperature is 
calculated from equation 3.3 [33] 
𝑡𝑔= 𝑡𝑜+ 𝑚𝑔𝑖∗𝑔ℎ  (3.3) 
Where   tg  is the surface floor Temperature  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 Mgi is the heat floor’s surface resistance estimated at 0.1 m2K/W  
 
 
 
 gh is the heat loss per m2 of the heated floor area in W/ m2 which is 23 W/m2 and 27 W/ 
m2 for bathrooms 1 & 2 respectively. (Assuming that at least 1/3 of the bathroom surface is not 
available). 
Therefore, in order to reach a Temperature of 20C the floor Temperature has to be increased up to 
22.3 C and 22.7 C to bathroom 1 & 2 respectively. Both of these results are lower than the 
maximum 29 C limit [44] Thus, an electric floor heating installation in the bathrooms is feasible 
even when higher bathroom Temperatures are desired.
