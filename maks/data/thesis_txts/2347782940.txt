Executive summary
The pollution of stream water by contaminant discharge from contaminated groundwater is a serious
threat that is currently targeted by the enforcement of the European Water Framework directive.
One of the major issue is that the interaction and contaminant discharge between groundwater and
surface water is a complex phenomena, and variable with time depending on hydrogeological, mete-
orological and local conditions, in addition to the chemical property of the contaminant compound
itself. To facilitate the understanding of such phenomena, diﬀerent approaches are available, and
the use of simple but robust analytical models is a ﬁrst step towards the assessment and the fate
of contaminant discharging from the groundwater to streams.
In this study, a 2D advection/dispersion analytical model was implemented based on existing tool
and literature with the objective of evaluating the contaminant concentration and mixing in streams
after being discharged from a diﬀuse plume source. Several features were added to simulate more
complex conﬁgurations, such as additional point source, background concentration and tributaries.
The model was applied at 5 diﬀerent sites in Denmark for which measured data were available
from consulting companies. The model results are in really good agreement with the measured
data in terms of concentration computed, both in longitudinal and transversal directions. The
model could be used to reﬁne the contaminant mass discharge estimation, and determine the mix-
ing length, i.e. the point where the concentration is fully mixed in the stream. The use of the model
also facilitated the interpretation of the measured concentration in the streams, and the eﬀects of
extra dilution by ditches, drain or volatilization were particularly emphasized in several of the cases.
A coupled simulation/measurement analysis was performed on the site of Raadvad over a three
month period in summer for the evaluation of possible temporal variation eﬀects. The site is char-
acterized by several contaminant hotspots and a tributary contributing signiﬁcantly to the ﬂow
rate of the stream. The measurements clearly showed the evolution and mixing of contaminant in
streams, and exhibited clear temporal variations in terms of ﬂow rate, contaminant mass discharge
to the stream, and resulting concentration. The use of the model combined with the acquired data
allowed in a simple and fast way to update the conceptual model and understanding of the site.
Some challenges remain, especially with respect to the volatilization models to be used.
On the same site of Raadvad, the model was ﬁnally used to estimate the variation of the mixing
length due to the seasonal variation of stream ﬂow rate only (annual distribution available). The
results were compared to the current available requirement. The comparison shows that the seasonal
variations are currently a challenge with respect to the mixing zone deﬁnition in which a full mixing
conditions is supposed to be achieved. Some more robust legal framework is required to take these
variations into consideration.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background of the study
The development of human activity is desperately putting pressure on the water resources, both
in groundwater and surface water. The sources of contamination are numerous and originate from
multiple sectors and activities: pesticides leaching or runoﬀfrom agriculture, increasing pressure
of urban area with discharge from pipe and sewers, or emission from waste water treatment plants
(Bedient et al., 1999; Roy and Bickerton, 2012; McKnight et al., 2012). Furthermore, the natural
water cycle induces groundwater ﬂowing towards surface water and streams, or opposite depending
on water table conﬁguration. A ﬂow from groundwater towards surface water constitutes another
source of contamination discharge if the groundwater itself is contaminated beforehand (White,
1993; Winter et al., 1998; Roy and Bickerton, 2012; Kalbus et al., 2007).
Both groundwater,
stream water and their interaction are now speciﬁcally monitored by the enforcement of the Euro-
pean Water Framework Directive (CEC, 2000). Under this legislation shall the European member
states monitor the quality of both groundwater and surface water, in order to ensure good water
quality and ecosystem health (McKnight et al., 2012).
In Denmark, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency estimates between 35000 to 40000 the
number of contaminated sites 1, while around 3000 of them pose a potential threat to surface wa-
ter (discussion with Orbicon). Evaluating the degree of threat and its likelihood is a tremendous
endeavour. Particularly, one of the key issue in the interaction of ground/stream water is its dif-
fuse character, the diﬃculty to observe it (Winter et al., 1998; KorkkaNiemi et al., 2012), and the
variability and complexity of the interaction between both (Krause et al., 2007). Indeed natural
variations in the water cycle, variations of precipitation distribution and frequency lead to diﬀerent
rate of recharge of the groundwater aquifers, modifying the hydraulic gradient and therefore the
seepage pattern to surface water bodies as underlined by Winter et al. (1998). The ﬂow condi-
tion in the stream are also naturally aﬀected as well, and will inﬂuence the resulting contaminant
concentration by enhanced or reduced dilution. Evidence and observations of seasonal eﬀects are
numerous in the literature and will be reviewed throughout this report (Anibas et al., 2011; Schmidt
et al., 2011; Sebok et al., 2014). Evaluating the eﬀect of these variations on the resulting potential
contaminant concentration can only be achieved by a study of both contaminant mass discharge
and fate in the stream.

The determination of the contaminant mass discharge and input from groundwater requires knowl-
edge on where, to which extent and how it is discharged to the stream. Several methodologies can
be used to estimate the ﬂow between the two water bodies and have been extensively described by
Kalbus et al. (2006): from direct measurement methods using seepage meters to indirect method us-
ing water temperature diﬀerence or application of Darcy’s law. The choice of methods is dependent
on spatial and temporal concerns, uncertainty, as well as practical and economical perspectives.
For example a direct tracer technic is usually time and cost consuming, while estimation of ﬂow
based on Darcy’s law are usually relatively uncertain when applied at local scale like the edge of a
stream (Devlin et al., 2012). The recent development of the Point Velocity Probe (PVP) sensors
has lead to interesting outcome for the characterization of ﬂows, both in magnitude and direction,
and at a relatively cost. These sensors have already been tested both in laboratory and ﬁeld tests
with promising results (Labaky et al., 2007; Rønde, 2014; Labaky et al., 2010).
Evaluating the mixing process is also arduous: the contaminant mixing in stream water is de-
pendent on diﬀerent mechanisms acting at diﬀerent scales and dependent on ﬂuid and stream
properties: the contaminant is transported, can diﬀuse molecularly or turbulently, or be mechan-
ically dispersed (Fischer, 1979; Hill et al., 1984). The assessment of contaminant mixing can be
performed using tracer dedicated studies as for the contaminant mass discharge determination (Hill
et al., 1984). However the results are still bounded by the site speciﬁc conditions and once again,
are costly and time consuming. Generalization via analytical and numerical models are essential
to obtain estimation of the contaminant fate in various conditions. Numerical models allow pre-
cise estimation, but require time and data collection (Sharma and Ahmad, 2014). The analytical
model allows relatively fast prediction using a limited number of parameters, but are hampered
by the complexity of the phenomena and parameters to account for. Many analytical models for
the prediction of contaminant dilution in streams use a point source formulation and a 2D advec-
tion/dispersion equation for propagation (Fischer, 1979; Rutherford, 1994; LUNG, 1995). More
complex formulations include diﬀuse and distributed sources (Socolofsky, 2016; Zhang and Zhu,
2011), as well as some volatilization terms, as recently achieved by Aisopou et al. (2015). All these
models assume a straight river of constant rectangular cross section conﬁguration. More complex
geometry with meander or variable depth have also been developed, but are practically constrained
by the availability of the data to feed the model with.
1.2
Objectives of the study
Within the context described previously, the coupling of an analytical model and data acquired
from measurement for characterizing the contaminant mass discharge oﬀers interesting perspec-
tives, and should allow a better understanding of the mixing of contaminant in stream, and also
possible time variation eﬀects. This Master thesis will therefore focus on the following objectives:
- Development of the dilution model for the evaluation and prediction of singularities related
to stream within more urban environment, i.e. drains, outlet and tributaries.
- Evaluation of contaminant discharge and in-stream mixing. This task will be performed by
application of the developed analytical model at 5 diﬀerent sites in Denmark and data already
acquired by consulting companies.

- Application of the model coupled to a thorough measurement campaign for the characteriza-
tion of mixing eﬀects in stream and mass discharge estimation.
- Possible quantiﬁcation/characterization of the seasonal variation and its consequent eﬀect on

2.1

The water balance for a given watershed is described by the general equation (Dingman, 2015):

In relatively uniform geological proﬁle, a key point to comprehend the water ﬂow between ground

These interactions are global behavior of GW/SW interaction and local variations exist within a

a strong variability of sediment properties leads to intense mixing of water and longer residence,
as well as an intense biochemical activity aﬀecting the fate of contaminant transferred between the
two water bodies (Sophocleous, 2002; Woessner, 2000; Freitas et al., 2015).
Finally, these interactions are time dependent and diﬀerent streams may be either losing or gaining
at diﬀerent periods of the year, depending on both meteorological and hydrological factors. These
time variations can be short term, e.g. bank storage phenomena after a sudden rise of stream water
that discharge to groundwater, overland or subsurface/stormﬂow, or more longer term for example
with some seasonal eﬀects as further discussed below (Dingman, 2015; Sophocleous, 2002).

2.3
Quantiﬁcation of contaminant mass discharge to stream wa-
ters
The estimation of the contaminant mass discharge is a key input parameter to evaluate the fate
and concentration of pollutant after its release downstream a source. Several methods are available
to quantify this input. A review of these methods have already been performed and will not be
addressed further in this report (Rønde, 2014; Kalbus et al., 2006). The general principle will be
brieﬂy discussed here below.
Although not oﬃcially recognized, several Environmental Protection Agencies and oﬃcial institu-
tions agreed that the contaminant mass discharge could potentially be used as a regulatory indicator
(Basu et al., 2006). Furthermore, its use for the evaluation of remedial performance assessment
(Annable, 2008; Soga et al., 2004), site characterization and risk assessment as well as contaminant
transport between groundwater et surface water is already relatively wide spread (Miljøstyrelsen,
2015; Kalbus et al., 2007).
The mass discharge is deﬁned as a mass ﬂow [M.T −1] of substance through a ﬁctive plane perpen-
dicular to the ﬂow and contaminant plume (Troldborg et al., 2010). It is calculated by the product
of a given contaminant concentration and Darcy’s velocity through this ﬁctive plane. From a prac-
tical point of view, the concentration and ﬂow velocity can vary throughout the whole cross section
of the plume, and requires some practical adjustments. In most applications, the estimation of mass
discharge is carried out by discretization of the cross section plane in sub-surfaces of quasi-constant
concentration/ﬂow velocity. The overall mass discharge is then the sum of all the discrete mass
ﬂows, as suggested by Guilbeault et al. (2005) :

clear that the higher the concentration is, the higher the resulting mass discharge will be. Similarly,

will also result in an increase of contaminant mass discharge to the stream.
2.4

In chapter 1, the evolution and changes in the interaction GW/SW due to the meteorological and
hydrological conditions were brieﬂy introduced. More studies are reviewed in this section.
The seasonal variation of groundwater ﬂow to surface water was for instance observed by Bruner
et al. (1998) in the United States.

from groundwater to the lake was varying signiﬁcantly with time and weather-related parameters:
drought in summer, thaw in spring, precipitation, as well as evapotranspiration. Similar hydraulic

2.5
Eﬀect of stream water conﬂuence and tributary
When the contaminant reaches the stream, its fate will be dependent on its intrinsic chemical prop-
erties and the water stream itself. Nevertheless, a single stream running lonely over its all stretch is
almost seen as a singularity. In many places it will or will be joined by other tributaries or channels
contributing to its ﬂow. These tributaries will have an inﬂuence on the fate of the contaminant in
the ﬁrst stream by addition of an important quantity of water that will change the dilution, as well
as a transverse ﬂow component that will inﬂuence its mixing.
The ﬂuid behavior at a conﬂuence is a complex hydraulic and ﬂuid dynamic problem that received a
substantial attention throughout the last decades, and particularly to evaluate the most inﬂuential
parameters (Itakura, 1972; Best and Reid, 1984; Roy and Roy, 1988). The general characteristics
presented in Figure 2.5.1 have been extensively described by Best (1985), and corroborated by
numerical and experimental work.
The main aspects and eﬀects of a conﬂuence are: ﬁrstly, a contraction of the main channel ﬂow
on the opposite side of the coming tributary due to the momentum imposed by the tributary ﬂow.
In some conditions, the tributary ﬂow detaches from the inner bank and induces a separation
zone downstream where water is recirculating. The limit between this recirculation area and the
contracted zone of the ﬂow is characterized by a local increase of ﬂow velocity, intense shear, tur-
bulence, and possibly large helico¨ıdal turbulent eddies. Finally downstream, a recovery area where
a relatively uniform velocity in the channel is achieved. The existence and extent of these areas are
directly dependent on diﬀerent physical parameters and especially: the discharge or momentum
ratio (i.e. ratio between ﬂow rate from tributary over main channel Qr =

3.1
Description of the site
The site chosen for the present study is the former industrial site of Raadvad, located in Zealand,
Denmark. Activities on the site of Raadvad started more than two centuries ago with the establish-
ment of a knife factory, as well as some machining, foundry and general metal work until the 1970’s
where the factory was dismantled and the land claimed by the state. Today the site is occupied
by a bronze foundry and several oﬃces, as well as a ”Naturskole” (NIRAS, 2012). The terrain is
relatively ﬂat at an average elevation of 5 m.a.s.l. (DVR90). Two channels are running through
the site and ﬂowing from an artiﬁcial lake over respective dams located just on the west side of the
site, rarely operated and technically acting as weirs (see Figure 3.1.1). These channels merge to
form a main stream, Mølle˚aen approximately 100 m east. Some pictures of the site are provided in
appendix B.

Hydrogeology
The available bore logs on site and the general geology in the region reveal a ﬁrst superﬁcial layer of
peat/gyttja down to app. 5 m.b.s, with possible sand lenses. This layer overlays an upper aquifer
(thickness unknown), and a ﬁnal clay layer approximately 10 to 20 m. thick. The primary aquifer
is a limestone located at a depth of approximately 35 m.b.s (NIRAS, 2012, appendix 2).
The previous measurements of hydraulic head highlighted an upward gradient between the deeper
and upper aquifer (roughly 1 m.), as well as between the upper one and the terrain (gyttja). In
terms of ﬂow, terrain water seems to ﬂow towards the channels in the extreme northern and south-
ern parts of the site. In the terrain area between the channels is the hydrology more complex
and subtle, and very likely varying with time (see appendix C and the hydraulic head isolines, as
observed in 2011).
Soil and groundwater contamination
Diﬀerent investigations throughout the years revealed pollution of the upper aquifer by chlorinated
compounds, as well as oil-based products. These compounds were also found in pore air analysis.
Traces of contamination by heavy metals and oil in the soil topsurface were also detected (Grontmij,
2009; NIRAS, 2012). Two main hotspots of contamination were identiﬁed at that time: a main
one located in the north part where the old knives factory was operated (denoted as A in this
study, Figure 3.1.1), and a second one south of a former bronze workshop (denoted as B). Another
really signiﬁcant concentration was also discovered by pore air and water analysis close to the
junction between the two channels in 2012, where drums were stored and likely industrial residues
were littered (denoted as C). The contamination extent at this particular place corroborates well
with additional methods revealing high level of chlorinated compounds, higher than anywhere
else on site in 2015 (Roost et al., 2016, by treecore sampling). Traces of chlorinated compounds,
particularly cisDCE and VC, have been measured in the stream downstream up to 500 m. (no
available data beyond 500 m). The highest concentrations were measured close to the northern
bank and upstream the junction. Most of the measured water samples were below the quality
criteria for fresh water downstream the junction. These measurements exhibited some variations of
contaminant concentrations with time between summer and autumn. Finally, one sample was also
taken in the lake without trace of chlorinated compounds present (Roost et al., 2016, appendix A
and B).

3.4.1
Multilevel sampling
The multilevel sampler is a collection of miniature wells allowing water sampling at diﬀerent depth
intervals. This instrument is built at DTU and used for the characterization of plume transect.
Each miniature well consists of a polyethylene line bundled around a central PVC tube acting as a
support. In this particular study, each multilevel sampler allows the sampling over a depth 1.5 m,
with a 0.25 m interval between each points.
To install the device on site, a 20 mm steel pipe was used as casing, mounted on a spear to facilitate
penetration and leave the pipe empty of soil. The casing is manually driven into the ground by
manual hammer, or electrical one when more power is necessary to reach the required depth. When
the desired depth is achieved, the casing is ﬁlled up with water to avoid upward water gradient,
the penetration head is pushed out and let in the ground as a buttee. The multisampler is put
into place and kept at that position, while the casing is removed using a manual high-lift jack or
manually if possible.

4.1
Existing model and applications
The DILVOL model presented in this chapter is a continuation of the extensive work performed
by Aisopou et al. (2015) on behalf of the Danish Environmental Agency.
This study was one
of the work package for the development of a risk assessment tool for the Danish compliance to
the European Water Framework Directive (CEC, 2000). It aimed at evaluating and assessing the
contaminant dilution in stream, aﬀected by contaminated groundwater. The output of this work
package was a simpliﬁed analytical model currently used as a screening tool for the estimation of
a fully mixed concentration downstream a contaminated site and compared to the relevant quality
criteria (Miljøstyrelsen bek. 1022). In its full form, the model is a 2D conservative model (equation
2.6.3), i.e. no volatilization and assuming no sorption, allowing the calculation of the mixing length
and where a diﬀuse plume discharges from the bank. The contaminant distribution through the
plume section can be uniform, gaussian, or step-shaped over 4 sub-sections. A conceptual schematic
of this model is presented here below.

4.4.1
Modeling of contaminant mass discharge from drain/sewer
Considering the width and the diﬀuse character of a contaminant plume, it seems reasonable to
assume any sewer or drain could be represented as a discrete point source (in comparison to the
more diﬀuse plume source). The ﬂow rate input is negligible with respect to the stream ﬂow, i.e.
no water input but just a contaminant mass discharge, and the initial momentum of the contam-
inant entering the stream is negligible as well, i.e. Vp,0 ≃0. In the far ﬁeld from this secondary
source, the full mixing in the vertical direction occurs faster than in the transverse direction and
the problem can be simpliﬁed into a 2D mixing/dilution problem. The location of the pipe under
or above water has therefore no importance: only its position with respect to longitudinal xp and
transversal coordinate yp matters for the eﬀect far downstream.

4.4.2
Modeling of water inﬂow from tributaries
The review performed in section 2.5 suggests that the water mixing at the junction of two channels
are dependent on numerous factors, both geometrical and hydrological. Intense mixing may occur
downstream from turbulent eddies in all directions and at the present time, no equivalent value of
dispersion coeﬃcient could be found. Under these circumstances, the use of the present 2D model
does not seem realistic. A dedicated study with the help of Fluid Dynamic Calculation for instance
would be required to evaluate an equivalent model.
Nevertheless, the large junction with an important ﬂow at Raadvad requires an approach allowing
the use of the current model as the development of a new one is beyond the scope of this project.
To be able to at least validate the order of magnitude in terms of contamination, a ﬁrst simpliﬁed
approach is here proposed. First of all, the tributary brings only clean water to the stream (no ad-
dition of contaminant which can be added anyway using the additional point source module). The
tributary will not aﬀect the ﬂow direction (i.e. the induced transverse velocity is not accounted for)
but modiﬁes the ﬂow rate and consequently the related quantities. Such a discontinuity can not be
treated directly by the present analytical model, deﬁned in a ﬁxed coordinate system with constant
input data. To remediate to this diﬃculty, an equivalent discharge source is introduced. Assum-

4.5
Sensitivity analysis of the DILVOL model
Prior to any calculation, the sensitivity of the analytical model to the diﬀerent variables is evaluated.
This step ensures that the maximum eﬀort will be spent in quantifying the most sensitive parameters
during the ﬁeld measurements, and also get a better understanding on how the parameters inﬂuence
the target outputs. This study is performed on the preliminary dataset for Raadvad chosen as a
reference and evaluated on the resulting mixing length and maximum concentration (see Table 6.5.1
further down for the input parameters). A One at A Time sensitivity analysis (OAT) is carried out
by linear perturbation of +/-20% of all parameters, one by one (all other parameters being kept
constant). The fully mixed conditions is neither oﬃcially nor legally deﬁned, and will be deﬁned
in this report as the concentration varying in maximum of 10% at any point of the section. The
distance between the downstream edge of the plume and where this condition is fulﬁlled corresponds
to the mixing length Lmix.
4.5.1
Quantitative assessment
The tested parameters for this ﬁrst analysis are the ﬂow rate (Q), the contaminant mass discharge
(J), the stream depth (d), the stream width (W), the plume width (Wplume) and ﬁnally the river
slope (S). The results are presented in Figure 4.5.1.

As expected, the increase in contaminant mass discharge results in an increase of maximum con-
centration, while the increase in ﬂow rate leads to more dilution and therefore lower maximum
concentration. The deviation of the output is in the same order of magnitude as the perturbation

maximum concentration in the current model with observed deviations of 2 to 3 %.

distance (app.
20% deviation from reference case), the perturbation of the stream width and
stream depth are actually leading to more important deviation, with absolute deviation of 23%
and almost 78% respectively. The increase of stream width logically extends the mixing length as
it takes longer for the contaminant to spread throughout the larger section of the stream. The non
linear eﬀect of depth is directly connected to the change in transverse mixing coeﬃcient, as seen
in equation 2.6.5 and equation 2.6.6, section 2.6. A decrease in depth (d) prevents larger turbulent
eddies to form and less velocity shear, and therefore a strong reduction in mixing. The eﬀect of
the plume width and the amount of contaminant discharge with respect to the mixing length is
relatively insigniﬁcant.

The eﬀect of the plume shape is quantiﬁed separately, as it can not be expressed quantitatively. In
this analysis only the shape of plume, i.e. the distribution of contaminant mass through the plume
width is changed. Plume width and overall mass discharge are identical. The eﬀect in terms of
relative deviation from the Gaussian plume as a reference are shown, from minimum to maximum
deviation. The diﬀerent plume shapes were presented earlier and can be visualized in Figure 4.2.2,
while the resulting eﬀect on the maximum concentration and mixing length is shown in Figure 4.5.2.
Figure 4.5.2 suggests that the plume shape has a limited impact at the centerline of the stream
both in terms of maximum concentration observed and mixing length (the eﬀect would be diﬀerent
on the bank where the plume enters). The relative deviation of these two quantities is less than 1%
of the reference Gaussian plume distribution. Only a left-skewed distribution (i.e. most of the mass

centerline. It is reasonable to assume that the shape of the plume discharging from the bank, all
others parameters kept constant, has a relatively limited inﬂuence especially on the mixing length.
Finally, a step distribution can be considered as a linear combination of uniform distribution, and
should have a limited eﬀect as well.

4.6
Summary of ﬁndings
The DILVOL model developed in the frame of this study is a 2D steady state advection-dispersion
model. It allows to take background concentration into account, as well as an additional point
source in addition to the diﬀuse plume source. A possibility of accounting for a tributary, repre-
sented as a clean water input is also included as well as the resulting change of the downstream
channel by the use of an equivalent source. It has to be kept in mind that this tributary model is
relatively simple and does not account for the change in transverse velocity at the point of junction.
The sensitivity analysis performed over the set of input parameters revealed that the maximum
concentration in the stream will be strongly dependent on the mass discharge and the ﬂow rate of
the stream, the other parameters being less sensitive. When looking at the mixing length Lmix,
it is shown that Lmix will be strongly aﬀected and increases when the depth decreases, when the
width and ﬂow rate increase, and when the slope decreases to a less extent. This inﬂuence of the
stream depth is directly linked to the variation of the dispersion coeﬃcient when the depth changes.
Finally, it is interesting to notice that the plume shape has a relatively minor inﬂuence on the two
parameters Cmax and Lmix but this remark is only valid at the centerline.
These results are in good agreement with the more exhaustive sensitivity analysis performed by
Aisopou et al. (2014) on behalf of Miljøstyrelsen both for Cmax and Lmix and on diﬀerent stream
ranges, showing the importance of the ﬂow rate, the width, and the depth. The most signiﬁcant
diﬀerence between the two studies is the slope (S) that was highlighted as the most sensitive
parameters for the determination of the mixing length Lmix. However, the variation range of (S)
was two order of magnitude, while the perturbation in this analysis was a linear variation of [+/-
20%]. This deviation should be kept in mind for possible deviation in the model results at a later
stage.

With respect to the input parameters, the mass discharge (J) is evaluated in an ”iterative” way: a
ﬁrst estimation of (J) (usually provided by the former site investigations) is used to run the model
and evaluate a mixing length - not usually available from the performed discrete stream water
sampling. Then a concentration at that particular distance is extrapolated from the measurement
and combined with the measured ﬂow rate to evaluate the contaminant mass discharge according
to the following formula:
J = Cmix · Q
(5.2.1)
This approach is relevant as it was shown previously that the mixing distance Lmix is not inﬂuenced
by the contaminant discharge.
All in all, the performed simulation correlates well with the measurement data. The ﬁrst measure-
ment point around 130 m, measured in the center of the stream exhibits an important deviation but
the eﬀect of the tributary located at approximately 50 m could partly be responsible for this devi-
ation and is currently not accounted for in the model. A full dilution is estimated approximately
190 m downstream the entrance point of the plume.

5.7
Summary of ﬁndings
The simulations carried out for several sites and conﬁguration of streams lead to comforting results
regarding the prediction of the dilution of chlorinated compounds contamination in streams. The
evolutions of concentration along the stream were well predicted, and most of the discrepancies
could be either connected to uncertainty in the input parameters, or more importantly to features
not accounted for, e.g. depth inﬂuencing the volatilization process, lack of data for tributaries,
mass input not accounted for etc. Some divergences can also be attributed to the limit of the
model itself: especially some of the deviation in the closest sections from the plume discharge could
be attributed to non fully mixed conditions as in Grindsted or Skjern for example.
The analytical calculation was an eﬃcient way to validate the contaminant discharge estimation
when this value was uncertain, using a one-step iteration: First by analytically evaluating a fully
mixed distance from an initial estimation of mass discharge, and use the assessed mixing distance
to estimate the fully mixed concentration from measurement and corresponding mass discharge
using equation 5.2.1. This approach is possible as it was shown in the sensitivity analysis that the
mass discharge had no inﬂuence on the mixing length. This approach seems actually necessary as
the data collected from stream water sampling are usually not enough to properly estimate where
the full mixing and Cmix is achieved.
It is important to notice that the ability to estimate the mixing length could not be estimated
for all sites due to a lack of measurement data for the comparison.
Indeed transverse section
measurements are required to evaluate a uniform concentration in the stream, corresponding to
this mixing length. This veriﬁcation was performed for the Grindsted validation case only, with
really good prediction of variation of concentration in the sections. This point will be addressed
with the complementary measurements performed in Raadvad and presented in the next sections.
With regard to the sensitivity analysis, it is interesting to notice that the Grindsted results for the
mixing length are good, despite the assumption on the slope parameters, highlighted as a sensitive
parameters for this estimation by Aisopou et al. (2014). This would indicate that the assumption
on the slope parameter was actually in the right range of magnitude.

6.4
Summary of measurement results in Raadvad
The diﬀerent results presented above for the period June to August show that both ﬂow and con-
taminant discharge are variable with time on Raadvad. The measurements indicate that the stream
ﬂow rate in Mølle˚aen generally decreases over the summer period. Most of the ﬂow downstream
is made by the water ﬂowing in the south channel, while the stream stretch in which hotspot A
discharges exhibits a low ﬂow rate value. The measurements of hydraulic head in the diﬀerent
monitoring wells indicate that this particular stream stretch is gaining water during the period,
which is in agreement with the qualitative assessment performed by analysis of the PVP response.
From a contamination point of view and using the stream sampling in the diﬀerent sections, it is
found that mostly cisDCE and VC compounds are found in the stream water and are discharged
from the hotspot A, but their respective proportions vary with time. The measurement highlighted
a relatively low but constant discharge of TCE from the south channel. There is a presumption
this compound is discharged from hotspot B that was so far neglected, as the TCE is already fully
diluted upstream the junction in the south channel and do not increases much along its course.
The piezometers installed 20 cm below the streambed show a water upward gradient from the
streambed to the stream close to the hotspot A and presence of PCE and degradation products,
but the concentration values measured were relatively low. The measurements do not allow to eval-
uate is these concentrations are low due to a dilution eﬀect or that contamination is not entering
via the streambed.
The overall contaminant mass discharge of the compounds evolved during the period June to
August, with a decrease of a factor 3 for cisDCE and VC overall mass for example. The tributary
has a strong dilution eﬀect in terms of concentration due to the high ﬂow from the south channel.
Once discharged into the stream, a signiﬁcant mass diminution of cisDCE and VC was observed in
August compared to June. A signiﬁcant increase in the volatilization process is suspected: either
due to favorable conditions such as increase of temperature and low depth in the upstream stretch
prior to the junction, or right downstream the junction in an intense mixing process.

6.5.5
Summary of simulation results
The simulations applied to the mixing of contaminant discharge in Raadvad are satisfactory with
respect to the modeling of extra dilution coming from the tributary. The simulations for the period
in June captured well both the evolution of concentration along the stream and the transverse
mixing, exception made of the ﬁrst sections closest to the plume. The estimation of mixing length
for the model is therefore relevant for that particular period, and estimated to occur approximately
220 m. downstream the point of entrance of the plume.
The simulations performed for the month of August resulted in more important discrepancies,
especially due to the overestimation of concentration downstream the junction connected to the
strong decrease of contaminant mass, not captured by the model. It is important to mention that
this mass loss between the two sections is not physically understood yet, whether it occurs as
a volatilization process over a whole stretch, or due to an intense mixing right downstream the
junction (although not subjectively noticed during the measurements). Nevertheless, the results
obtained for the cisDCE simulation are still encouraging, with a relatively well predicted transverse
spread, but oﬀset due to the mentioned mass loss. As it was shown that the mass discharge do not
inﬂuence strongly the mixing length, it is believed that the variation of mixing length estimated
between June and August by the model is relevant.
The diﬀerent points of discrepancy for the model will be further examined in the coming chapter.

Updated conceptual model for the Raadvad site
The diﬀerent learnings from the measurement data and simulations carried out allow to propose an
updated conceptual model with respect to the chlorinated contamination discharge to the stream
in Raadvad (Figure 6.6.1).
During the period June to August, contamination of TCE, cisDCE, VC were detected in Mølle˚aen.
cisDCE and VC originate from the plume discharging at the north bank from the hotspot A. This
discharge occurs even during dry summer periods from favorable hydraulic gradient. The seepage
area and plume width is relatively limited, and is still not clearly identiﬁed. The use of the DIL-
VOL model suggests a possible intrusion via the bank/streambed area as the slope towards the
streambed is smooth in reality. This hypothesis is backed up by some piezometers at 20 cm depth
in the streambed, indicating a small but upwards gradient from the streambed. The groundwater
may intrude from below the concrete wall, through possible fractures or by-passing the edge of
the concrete wall (section AA, Figure 6.6.2). Some contamination is certainly discharging via the
already suspected drain, but not only. Although the piezometers revealed an upwards gradient in
the suspected area, the contamination concentration measured at 20 cm. depth do not allow to
properly conﬁrm a contaminant mass discharge. Extra measurements at deeper levels and research
of seepage area could be performed.
The measurements also revealed that the majority of the TCE contamination measured in the diﬀer-
ent sections is entering the main stream by the south channel. Two known hotspots are potentially

Chapter 7
Discussion
7.1
Beneﬁt of the DILVOL model applied to diﬀerent sites
The DILVOL model developed during this project has been successfully used to estimate the evo-
lution of contaminant concentration at diﬀerent sites contaminated with chlorinated solvents in
chapter 5. It accurately simulated the evolution of the concentration along the stream resulting
from contaminant mass discharge from diﬀuse plume sources, as well as additional point sources.
Its ability to predict the spread and mixing of contaminant in the stream was also demonstrated on
the Grindsted study case thanks to more exhaustive data (section 5.5). By its simplicity and speed,
several scenarios could be tested and the importance of the volatilization process and the eﬀect of
ditches, drain, or tributary were emphasized as important parameters for the contaminant mixing
in stream. It also allowed to point out possible underestimation of contaminant mass discharge.
One of the really strong argument for the use of the DILVOL model is its ability to characterize the
lateral spread and ultimately the mixing length. This assessment is independent of the contaminant
mass discharge, as it was proved in the sensitivity analysis in section 4.5. It can therefore be carried
out beforehand for a reﬁnement of the contaminant mass discharge or for preparing sampling
campaigns for the evaluation of a fully mixed concentration. A summary of these outputs, i.e.
updated contaminant mass discharge, mixing length, and ratio mixing length/stream width is
provided here. This ratio mixing length/stream is a convenient way to express and potentially
compare the mixing length between diﬀerent stream conﬁguration (Rutherford, 1994). This ratio
ranges from 0 (the concentration is already mixed at the downstream edge of the plume) up to 26
stream width. The literature review in chapter 2 were giving order of magnitude of 100 for this
ratio, but it is not clear if the value was for Lmix or for the distance from the upstream edge of the
plume. Furthermore, this mixing length is dependent on several other parameters, as it was again
shown in the sensitivity analysis.

contaminant mixing in streams. First of all, the inclusion of the tributary in the DILVOL model
shows the important dilution eﬀect associated to it with respect to contaminant mixing.

7.3
Identiﬁed model challenges
Two main divergences were noticed when the model was applied at the diﬀerent sites: a ﬁrst
deviation was observed in the closest sections of the plume, and further downstream with an un-
derestimated mass loss.
The near-ﬁeld eﬀect and non fully mixed conditions in the vertical direction provides a reasonable
explanation for the deviation in the ﬁrst section close to the plume discharge. The deﬁnition of
the model itself and the rectangular cross section used for the calculation provides a more relevant
explanation. As illustrated in Figure 7.3.1 and previously shown when comparing to measurement
results in Figure 6.5.7, a smooth transition exists from the edge to the streambed in many stream
conﬁgurations, making the limit in between the two conﬁgurations slightly diﬃcult to deﬁne and
the best model would probably be a combination of both inputs.
For some of the tested study cases, the volatilization was pointed out as a possible explanation

Especially in the case of Raadvad, the measurements
indicated an important volatilization over the stretch between section 1 and 2, not captured by the
current model in use. All simulations were performed using the volatilization model developed by
Southworth (1979). Other volatilization models exist, and are already or could be added for the
calculation of the volatilization rate. Trapp and Harland (1995) performed a review of diﬀerent
volatilization models, and emphasized that most of them are built on semi-empirical formulations.
Consequently these models are valid on a certain range of conditions and extrapolating them at
other conditions could result in an error. The main issue is the range of application of each of these
models is not clearly and quantitatively deﬁned. Looking back at the result obtained in August in
Raadvad, it is clear that temperature and ﬂow velocity were respectively high and low at the time
of measurement. A test performed with the model developed by Langbein/Durum is carried out
in the ﬁrst stretch of the stream where the ﬂow velocity is low, which corresponds actually to the
qualitative range of application described for this model (Trapp and Harland, 1995). The results
presented in Figure 7.3.2 demonstrate a considerable better ﬁt with the measured concentration.
The issue remains to know which model is suitable and in which applications, and likely variable
with time.

7.4
Practical consequence of seasonal variation with respect to
legal compliance
Currently no clear legal framework or standardized procedure is deﬁned for the compliance of
stream water to Environmental Quality Standards (EQS). The European legislation agreed that
the compliance is veriﬁed by sampling at monitoring points and time average at diﬀerent periods
of the year, without clearly deﬁning them. Furthermore the existence of a mixing zone right down-
stream the source is acknowledged, in which an overexceeding of these EQS is allowed as long as
”it does not aﬀect the compliance of the rest of the body of surface water ” (EU, 2008, Directive
2008/105/EC). In Denmark, this mixing zone extends over 10 times the width of the stream, up
to a maximum of 100 m long (Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). Although not clearly stated, it is implied in
this mixing zone deﬁnition that the maximum concentration is reached within it, and a fully mixed
concentration is achieved right downstream.
The study case at Raadvad illustrates that this deﬁnition of the mixing zone can be challenged
in some cases. In Figure 7.4.1 is plotted again the results obtained for the cisDCE simulation in
August 2016, compared to the mixing zone deﬁnition equal at the maximum value at 100 m for
this case. It is obvious that at the edge of the mixing zone the fully mixed concentration is not
achieved (the estimated value being around 170 m. represented by the dash line).

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The main objectives of the present study was the development and the implementation of an ana-
lytical model, coupled to ﬁeld data analysis for the evaluation of seasonal and time variation.
The DILVOL model was created based on a previous work for the elaboration of a screening tool
on behalf of the Danish EPA. It is based on a 2D advection/diﬀusion analytical formulation and
accounts for the volatilization process by use of diﬀerent semi-empirical models. The DILVOL
model was further developed during this project to take into account particular features and more
realistic conﬁgurations of contaminant mass discharge and stream: it encompasses additional point
source in the form or drain/sewers, background concentration and the junction of the stream with
a tributary. This ﬁrst model of tributary does not account for the transverse velocity.
The application of the model at 5 diﬀerent contaminated sites in Denmark, with various stream
conﬁgurations produces really good results when compared to already acquired data on site. Espe-
cially, the model could be used to reﬁne the contaminant mass discharge estimation and determine
the mixing length, i.e. the point where the concentration is fully mixed into the stream. The ap-
plication on the Grindsted site characterized by high ﬂow, non uniform contaminant discharge and
additional point sources release showed good agreement especially in the prediction of the lateral
spread and mixing. This condition is required for a relevant calculation of the mixing length. The
use of the model also facilitated the interpretation of the measured concentration in the streams,
and the eﬀect of extra dilution by ditches, drain or volatilization was particularly emphasized in
several of the cases.
A coupled simulation/measurement analysis was performed on the site of Raadvad over a three
month period in summer for the evaluation of possible temporal variation eﬀects. Instrumentation
on site included monitoring of hydraulic heads, groundwater and surface water sampling as well as
investigations of the groundwater ﬂow direction by means of Point Velocity Probes.
The use of the PVPS allowed to qualitatively evaluate the ﬂow direction in the groundwater at
several locations, in agreement with the measurements of hydraulic heads at the diﬀerent wells. No
quantitative values for the ﬂow direction and velocity were assessed due to a likely non collapse of
the gyttja around the PVP and preventing proper tracer tests. Very good results were obtained
from the stream water sampling, clearly displaying the non uniform concentration distribution
through the sections close to the sources, the strong dilution of the tributary and its eﬀect prior
to a dispersion and uniform concentration is achieved. While the tributary had some eﬀects right
85
==PAGE:98==
after the junction, the ﬂow recovered and it was observed that the mixing further downstream was
mostly driven by the combined ﬂow rate. The diﬀerent acquired data, combined with the simulation
allowed to propose a new conceptual model of the site, underlining the contribution of several con-
tamination sources on site and facilitating the identiﬁcation of potential groundwater seepage areas.
Ultimately, the measurements and the corresponding simulations over a single summer period for
two diﬀerent months clearly underline some of the eﬀects of time and seasonal variations on the
contaminant mixing in streams.
Although relatively limited over the short period, the change
in ﬂow rate is directly inﬂuencing the mixing length due to an increase transport. The use of
statistical median distribution for the ﬂow rate showed that a 3 times longest mixing length could
be expected during wetter months, especially in winter for the speciﬁc case of Raadvad. On the
same site, it was also observed that the contaminant discharge was also varying with time: between
June and August the mass discharge of VC and cisDCE was reduced by almost a factor three.
The hydrological conditions are a ﬁrst explanation but other factors are probably in play, and
the possible variation of biodegradation process (not examined here) could be investigated further.
Finally the resulting concentration was also inﬂuenced by other factors. For instance in August
a strong volatilization process was observed in the ﬁrst stretch of the stream, reducing strongly
the ﬁnal value of fully mixed concentration. These time variations constitute a serious challenge
with respect to the legal framework and comparison to the relevant water quality criterias, as the
current mixing zone and monitoring point are either ﬁxed or not clearly and legally deﬁned.

Chapter 9
Outlook and future development
9.1
Investigation at Raadvad site
The following tasks are suggested for the characterisation and understanding of the contamination
discharge in Raadvad:
- The use of DILVOL model indicates that the plume could actually enters via the streambed.
While some piezometers were deployed on site, their shallow positioning did not allow to
conﬁrm this. The discharge area could be relatively restraint and heterogeneous and diﬃcult
to detect by random sampling. The measurement planned using thermal detection by the
company NIRAS will be beneﬁcial. Other possibilities include newly and innovative thermal
imagery using UAV. Finally, the TCE contaminant discharge need to be examined and the
inherent risk with respect to the water quality in Mølle˚aen evaluated. An additional sampling
at the lake close to the weirs for each channel is suggested.
- A general follow up with the measurement performed by Orbicon and the TUP project regarding
the time variation of contaminant discharge, coupled to DILVOL model could be an insightful
project for preparing a framework/procedure for the compliance with EQS and provide some
insightful dataset regarding volatilization and tributaries eﬀects.
- The observed decrease in terms of contaminant mass discharge may also be connected to an en-
hance biodegradation process at particular time periods, as shown by the litterature reviews.
These facts were not included in the present study.
9.2
DILVOL model development
With respect to the DILVOL model, the following developments and improvements can be addressed
at a next step:
- The limit of assuming a plume discharging from the bank in a rectangular cross section suggests
that the use of a streambed model input, taking into account volatilization and a possible
user deﬁned width could be a signiﬁcant improvement. Literature for the analytical solution
of such a source has not been identiﬁed yet, but the use of an equivalent distributed source
in a 2D horizontal plane is a ﬁrst possibility. Note that this implementation was initiated
during this project but not completed yet.
87
==PAGE:100==
- The tributary model implemented at that stage does not take into account the transverse velocity
induced by the tributary in the main stream. This transverse velocity has some consequences,
especially with respect to concentration distribution in the stretch of stream right after the
junction. Some more exhaustive modeling is required if some precise assessment of the con-
centration in the junction area is required. Example of study by Zoppou and Knight (1999)
with analytical solution considering a transport ﬂow in a 2 dimension domain could probably
be a good foundation. Nevertheless some more challenging integration work is required as
the transverse ﬂow in the case of the tributary is only over a small portion of the investigated
domain.
- The diﬀerent applications showed that the volatilization process can lead to signiﬁcant mass loss
between the plume discharge area and the point of fully mixed conditions. Some more work
is required to evaluate the range of application of the diﬀerent available models, which parame-
ters are actually the most important and which model to use depending on stream/meteorological
characteristics. Furthermore, the eﬀect of the slope, highlighted as a sensitive parameter has
not been investigated in the present study or at least was not seen as sensitive in the performed
application cases.
- The implementation of a 2D equivalent source model for the streambed will certainly increase
the computation time. Furthermore; it was noticed only recently that the code was actually
not optimized, also for plume and tributary calculation and could therefore be improved.

The following step-by-step procedure can be followed to built a PVP:
1. Prepare the required length of injection line and electric cable. From a practical point of
view, 1m extra for each of the tree injection lines and approximately 1.6 m for each of the
two electrical cable. gather the injection lines together using a vynil tape. Do the same with
the electric cable. mark one of the electrical cable (e.g. tape) at the two extremities to easily
distinguish them.
2. The support for the injection line is put in place and glued to the top part.
3. The injection line are passed through this support and approximately 2 cm of silicone tube as
a clamping point E.2a. Each line is then passed through one of the injection hole and roughly
positioned, i.e. the lines protrudes (E.2b). Note that the lines are fed in such a way that the
curvature is minimum (G.1b)
4. The top part of the PVP is then glued to the core part (a sanding might be required to ensure
a proper adjustement).
5. Finally, the lines are then pulled back so that the extremity of the tube is ”aﬄeurer” the port
(E.2d). The lines are locked by applying a hot wax glue from the inside (E.2e). Afterwards,
the silicone tubes are put in place and locked using plastic bracelets, ass seen on (E.2f).
Note: It is important and recommended to use a color code for each line corresponding
to port from 1 to 3. This choice is then repeated for simplicity for all PVPs
6. To ﬁnalize the port, the support grid and the mesh need to be installed E.2g. Make sure that
the support rings ﬁt the port at that point. If not, adjust by simple sanding. For each port,
cut 4 small pieces of the mesh grid that matches the size of the port hole, i.e. rough disk or
square of 4 mm (E.2h).
7. Each pieces is then piled randomly in one of the port, so that the grid overlap in a random
pattern and reduce even more the (E.2i). Spread some glue all along the side of this ring and
put it in place by pressing hard, making sure the glue does not obstruct the port.
8. Repeat this operation for each injection port.
Note: Former experience use a piece of grid folded in four, but this technics seems to
create gap between each layer of the gri net and therefore limits its ability
Note 2: The glue is extremely fast to adhere as the material is relatively porous.
9. Prepare the electric cable by stripping approximately 12 cm of each line. For each of the
individual wire, strip approximately 2 cm (E.2j).
10. For each wire, connect a length of steel wire ø 0.4 mm which constitute the active part of the
sensor. Weld them together and apply a thermal insulation (E.2k).
113
==PAGE:126==
11. Start on the wire without mark. Pass the steel wires from the inside of the core, using the
color code deﬁned in previous appendix. make a small ”hook” to facilitate the entry through
the core (E.2l), and pull through by use of the needle nose pliers (E.2m).
12. When all cables are through, pull entirely until the thermal insulator is stopped by the core
itself (E.2n).
13. Finally adjust the length by cutting the excess wire steel, letting just enough to make another
hook that will be pushed inside and lock the sensor wire in place (E.2o). The extra excess
wire can be cut or folded on the side inside the core. the wire is maintained in place by hot
wax glue applied from the inside for example as for the injection lines.
14. The same procedure is used for the second and marked cable, corresponding to the vertical
sensors and the horizontal one between.
note that the vertical sensors are more diﬃcult to hold in place, and a few discrete drop
of hot wax glue on the external part allows to keep the sensor in place while mounting
(E.2p). As previously, the cable is maintained in place using a drop of hot wax glue
(E.2q).
15. Once done, glue the bottom part to ﬁnalize the PVP E.2r

The ﬁrst injection period in June was carried out one week after the PVP installation (tracer con-
centration c = 1g/L, V = 1mL). Most of the observed time series were characterized by either
a lack of response, or what is believed to be a forced transport of the tracer, i.e. the tracer is
pushed in between the PVP and the surrounding soil (clearly visible in Figure F.3). Indeed a sharp
edge response precisely at the time of injection is observed, which indicate the tracer passed by
the sensor in less than a minute, which is considered unrealistic by water ﬂow transport in such
a media. it is assumed the one week period was insuﬃcient for the soil material to settle down
properly around the PVP.
A more exhaustive injection test was run simultaneously with the water sampling a week later
with more concentrated tracers (tracer concentration c = 10g/L, V = 2mL) . For all four PVPs,
response were detected on the horizontal sensors for some of the injection ports, highlighting a
general ﬂow direction: For example the response measured on PVP1 (upper position north side)
for an injection on port 1 indicate a ﬂow coming from the west, which is in agreement with the
water table and ﬂow movement measured previously (appendix C). However, the post processing
of the response was unsuccessful also due to a raising edge-pulse in the time response and very
likely a tracer pushed during injection instead of transport. This response type was received on all
vertical sensor up (sensor 5), as well as for most of response measured by the horizontal detection
sensors for the PVP2, 3 and 4.
These observations are assumed to be the consequence of a non settlement of the soil around the
PVPs, which is a required condition for the proper use of this device. It has to be reminded that
most of the PVP applications underlined in section 2 were done in sandy aquifer, while the media
in which the PVPs are installed in Raadvad is mostly gyttja with very diﬀerent properties. It is
still believed that the ﬂow direction plotted in the section result is relevant, as the decay in the
response are still representative of a transport.
The Data from the measurement in August were not postprocessed.
