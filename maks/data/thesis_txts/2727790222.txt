Abstract
An intumescent coating is a passive fire protection material that expands when exposed to
high temperatures creating an insulating barrier around the object it is meant to protect.
The attempt to develop a perfect model describing the process inside the intumescent
coating and the heat transfer through it has been ongoing for the past 50 years.
The main goal of this thesis was to develop a mathematical model adapted to the Coast
small-scale oven.
For this purpose, experimental data was produced for intumescent
coatings and unprotected steel in the setup of interest. CFD simulations were used to
describe the environment inside the oven.
A simple model was developed for a steel
plate in the oven, and along with the experiments conducted on bare steel and the CFD
simulations, it was used to describe the heat transfer from the oven to the sample. Based
on these results, a one-dimensional model was developed which described the intumescent
coating behavior.
The reaction in the intumescent coating was modeled as a single first-order reaction. The
expansion was modeled as a function of the reactant’s depletion and the coating’s max-
imum expansion. The heat transfer through the coating included conductivity through
the solid material and the gas in the pores. Radiation in the pores was also included. In
addition, the model considered the heat capacity of the coating and the reaction enthalpy.
The heat transfer to the coating included both convection and radiation. The heat loss
through the backplate was also included.
The model was seen to reproduce the experimental results within the experimental stan-
dard deviation. While the model was imperfect, it was a significant step in describing
the intumescent process.

Background:
Intumescent coatings are used to thermally insulate load-bearing steel structures in the
event of a fire. The thermal insulation which results from this is achieved by the ex-
pansion and foaming of the coating at elevated temperatures and the formation of a
complex multi-cellular porous char structure. The performance of such coatings is com-
monly evaluated by heating them inside a furnace with a temperature-time pre-defined by
international standards while the temperature of the substrate is monitored to asses the
coating performance. One of the significant challenges in this field is the mathematical
model and its experimental validation under standardized fires. Conductive, convective,
and radiative heat transfer affects the intumescent coating. The way the coating insu-
lates against these three mechanisms depends highly on the coatings’ expansion and the
porous structure. Usually, the heat transfer models proposed in literature do not focus
on the morphology of the intumescent char. However, its importance for the intumes-
cent coating performance. Small-scale laboratory ovens to validate results obtained from
different heat transfer models.
Purpose of the project
The purpose of this project is to validate simple heat transfer models for intumescent
coating, improve upon them and adapt them to the experimentally obtained results. The
adaptation of these models should include an assessment of heat transfer models proposed
for different porous structures. Initially, the models will be evaluated under hydrocarbon
standard fire conditions. However, the study can be broadened to other heating curves.
Content:
• Literature study on intumescent coatings, standard curves, heat transfer in porous
structures and models for intumescent coatings.
• Applying commercial intumescent coatings to steel substrates
• Testing of the coated panels in the CoaST setup
• Validation of simple models proposed in Course 88716.
• Exploration of different models proposed in literature and adaption of them to the
model proposed.

Introduction
There is no debate that the discovery of fire has been the most important discovery in
the history of humankind. Without it, humanity would never have been able to leave
the caves and bring upon the industrial revolution that has cemented our place on top
of Earth’s food chain. Nevertheless, no invention comes without its dangers. In the year
2010, over two million fire incidents happened in just the United States [1]. While the
number of fire incidents has decreased in the years since [1], the recent rapid increase in
uncontrollable wildfires due to climate change will undoubtedly cause this number to rise
again.
Fire incidents can devastate and cause significant financial loss, permanent bodily dam-
age, or even death. Several fire prevention and protection methods already exist. An
intumescent coating is a passive fire protection system [2].
Intumescent coatings are
widely used in industry and buildings as passive fire protection. They protect the load-
bearing steel support structure in case of fire [2]. If the temperature of the steel reaches
200◦C, then the steel’s structural integrity and yield strength begin decreasing and con-
tinue decreasing rapidly up to 600◦C [3]. Around 500◦C, the steel loses its structural
properties [4]. This temperature is often referred to as a critical temperature for steel.
Intumescent coatings can delay the steel from reaching this temperature by up to several
hours[4].
Intumescent coatings were initially developed in 1969 as a part of a US Navy weapons pro-
tection program called ”The weapons cook-off Improvement Program” [5]. The original
purpose was to delay the explosion of high throughput ordnance in case of fires on carrier
ships. Later this technology was made available to the public. In modern publications,
nobody mentions the military past of the intumescent coatings.
Intumescent coatings work by expanding when exposed to heat, creating an insulating
barrier around the object they are meant to protect. An illustration of an expanding
intumescent coating can be found in figure 1. As seen from the left side of the figure, the
coating contains three zones during expansion: the virgin coating, the pyrolysis zone, and
the char [6]. The reaction order can be seen on the right half of figure 1. The intumescent
coatings begin to expand in the region furthest away from the sample it is protecting,
and the expansion front continues inwards until the coating has fully reacted [7].
Figure 1: An illustration of an intumescent coating during expansion. [Original source: [6]]
Organic intumescent coatings are the most widely used [2]. A typical organic intumescent
coating will contain a carbon donor or a char former, an acid donor or catalyst, a blowing
agent, and a binder [2].
The specific choice of components varies greatly and has a
significant impact on the properties of the coating [1].

A possible explanation of the process of the expansion of the intumescent coating is
as follows. An inorganic acid is released once a part of the coating reaches a specific
temperature. The acid can then esterify the carbon donor, which would happen at a
slightly higher temperature than when the acid is released. Around the same temperature,
the material melts, creating a liquid phase. The ester then decomposes via dehydration.
The released gas causes the liquefied material to foam. As the reactants are depleted,
the medium re-solidifies forming the porous char[6].
This explanation is only one of the possible explanations, and the mechanism will change
depending on what reactants are included in the coating. Novel coatings are constantly
being developed, some of which entirely circumvent the afore-described process, such
as, for example, self-expanding coatings[6]. The number of publications on intumescent
coatings has drastically increased since 1997[6], and there is no reason to expect this
trend to stop in the near future.
Generally, the accepted method of testing is exposing a sample coated with an intumes-
cent coating to a standardized fire condition such as, for example, a UL1709 or an ISO834
heating curve and measuring the temperature of a steel sample they are protecting [3].
These tests allow for measuring the performance of the coatings before they are released.
Industry-standard testing requires large and expensive furnaces[8], which are only some-
times readily available, even at the more prominent universities. As such, the prospect
of creating models that can predict the intumescent coating performance without testing
it in large industrial furnaces is desirable.
Since shortly after the development of intumescent coatings, attempts have been made
to create a model which can describe and predict their behavior and performance. The
earliest model was published by Riccitiello et al.[9] in 1975, and their development is
ongoing.
A model has yet to be developed that can accurately predict the tempera-
ture of the sample they are protecting, the expansion of the coating, and the intra-char
temperatures. In addition, the models available in the literature are almost exclusively
one-dimensional.
This project has as its goal to attempt to develop a predictive model which will be
capable of describing the behavior and the performance of an intumescent coating. This
model will use the until now developed models as inspiration and build upon them. The
model development will be supported by data from experiments carried out in small-scale
electric laboratory furnaces in the CoaST-Oven lab. In addition, CFD software will be
utilized to assist with understanding the conditions within the electric furnace.
Currently, no other models adapted to this specific type of furnace exist.
Should a
proper model be created, it might be possible to explain the reasons for the differences
between the results obtained in small-scale electrical furnaces and large-scale industrial
furnaces. This explanation might alleviate the need for large-scale industrial furnaces,
saving companies and universities much money.
This report will consist of five parts.
The first, which can be found in section 2, is
a comprehensive literature study of the past modeling of intumescents coating.
The
second, which can be found in section 3, is the experimental part which will contain the
procedures and results of the experiments carried out to obtain data that can be used
to validate the developed models. The third part, which can be found in section 4, will
contain the CFD simulations used to describe conditions inside the electric furnace. The
fourth part, which can be found in section 5, will model the transport of energy from the
oven to the coating to create input parameters to be used in the model for the intumescent
coating. The fifth part will be presented in section 6. This part will contain modeling

Literature study
Since the first intumescent coatings were developed, a significant amount of work has
gone into attempting to provide a satisfactory mathematical description of their behavior.
The first proper model was developed in 1975 [9], and their development continues to the
present day [10]. Attempting to systematically describe the progression of every model in
chronological order could quickly become borring and unreadable. As a result, the model
description has been broken up into a range of subsections focusing on the different
aspects of the models.
Section 2.1 will contain a brief history of modeling of the intumescent coatings. After
that, the general chemistry of the intumescent reaction will be outlined in section 2.2
and basics of reaction kinetics will be given in section 2.2 while reaction rate expressions
and thermodynamic values used in the literature will be described in section 2.2. In
section 2.3, the expansion models used across the literature will be systematically de-
scribed. Subsequently, the basic heat transfer theory will be explained, and the thermal
conductivity equations used in the literature will be systematically described in sections
2.4.1 and 2.4.2, respectively. Finally, section 2.6 will summarize the most critical findings
from the literature study and contain concluding remarks.
2.1
Brief history of modeling of intumescent coatings
The first ever model describing intumescent coatings was developed by Riccitiello et al.
[9] as a collaboration between NASA and Acurex Corp. They managed to create a predic-
tive model which could foretell the measured values of the temperature of the backplate
within a precision of 20%. The simple model included the char, pyrolysis, and virgin
coating zones.
In 1985 Anderson and Buckmaster [5] developed a simple mathematical model to de-
scribe the expansion and heat convection in an expanding intumescent coating.
The
model consisted of a simple, infinitely thin moving reaction front that consumed the vir-
gin coating and converted it to char. Heat transfer through the material was modeled
using the general energy balance equations. The movement of the interface between the
char and the virgin material was determined by a critical temperature. Once the virgin
material reached this temperature, it was transformed into char. The reaction energy
was included in the consideration. The result from the modeling did not perfectly fit
any of the experimental data. However, it reflected the general trend in the temperature
of the steel plate as the function of time in the experimental results taken from another
research paper.
In 1986 Buckmaster et al. [11] iterated further on the model published a year prior.
This time, The main difference was the inclusion of gasses that evolved during the in-
tumescent reaction leaving the char layer. They reduced the problem to a simpler form
through mathematical transformation, which was then solved numerically. The authors
themselves were unconvinced by the model they had developed. Despite their uncertain-
ties, they compared their results to data obtained elsewhere in the literature. The trends
observed, such as a plateau in the temperature of the steel plate halfway through the
heating process, were, according to them, proof that their model was a step forward in
explaining and understanding the tumescence process. They concluded that the model

2.1
Brief history of modeling of intumescent coatings
required further work before it could be used.
In 1997, Butler et al. [12] created a model for the growth and heat transfer through
intumescent coatings based on three submodels. The model was generally designed to
integrate more complex submodels if such were developed. The three included submodels
were the hydrodynamic, growth rate, and Heat transfer submodels. The hydrodynamic
submodel described the movement through the liquid, including the stress on it and its
growth as it moves through different temperature gradients. The growth rate submodel
described the bubble’s growth.
It was based on the diffusion of dissolved gas to the
bubble, assuming it is alone in an infinite amount of liquid. The heat rate submodel de-
scribed the heat flux from the fire through the coating. The three submodels integrated
should be solved by the Runge-Kutta method. No solution results were provided, which
is suspected to be the result of the computational limitations of the time.
In 2001, Di Blassi et al.
[13] created a 1D model for the expansion and heat trans-
port of an intumescent coating incorporating three different zones: the unreacted zone,
the decomposition zone, and the char zone- This was achieved by discretizing the coat-
ing. In the future, this approach will be seen in the vast majority of the models. In the
unreacted zone, the heat transfer happened only by conduction. In the decomposition
zone, the chemical reactions created gas bubbles that caused swelling. They introduced a
trapped gas ratio to eliminate model expansion overprediction. Here mainly, the conduc-
tive heat transfer was modeled under the assumption that the properties were the same
as those of the unreacted coating. The char zone included both conductive and radiant
energy transport. The model resulted in a very good fit for the data. While the fit was
imperfect, it was better than any previously reported. The model identified three zones
corresponding to the previously seen steep rise in temperature in the literature, followed
by a plateau in temperature, followed by another rise. Di Blassi et al. attributed the first
zone to the heat transfer through the virgin material, the second zone to the decomposi-
tion reaction, and the third to transport through pure char.
In 2004 di Blasi [14] published an improved version of the model.
In this study, he
focused mainly on the influence of external radiative heat flux to the surface. He saw
that mainly the second zone corresponding to the coating decomposing was affected by
changes to the external heat flux for increases up to 50kW/m2. The duration of the sec-
ond zone decreases with increases in radiative heat flux to the surface. Above 50kW/m2,
internal heat transfer played a dominant role. He also observed it was also shown that
except for the lowest tested heat flux of 25kW/m2, the heating rate of the substrate will
always have two maxima, one in the first zone and one in the third.
In 2010, G. J. Griffin concluded that all the previous attempts at providing a model
that could describe the intumescent process’s behavior were not good enough. This led
him to develop his one-dimensional model for the study of intumescent coatings [15]. The
model consisted of three layers: the char, the expanding layer, and the virgin material.
The heat transfer was modeled to include conduction, convection, and radiant energy
transport in the pores. Values for escaping gas and reaction rates were taken from previ-
ous studies. A model for oxygen levels present in the surroundings was incorporated to
simulate the diffusion of oxygen into the coating as a part of the degradation of the char
layer. The expansion was modeled based on the gas evolution in the expansion stage.
Data used for comparison was generated as a part of the study using a cone calorimeter.

The models were shown to provide a good fit for the experimental results. Small devi-

radiative transport through the coating does play a significant role. Furthermore, his

In 2012, Zhang et al. [16] attempted to create a predictive model partially based on
the work of di Blasi et al. [13, 14]. The model considers three layers: virgin material,
decomposing coating, and char. The heat transfer was modeled using conduction, con-
vection, and radiant energy transport in the pores. The expansion was modeled using
chemical kinetics for gas evolution, with the mean bubble size created as a function of
the released reaction products. They were the first to consider the shrinkage of the char
layer during its decomposition. They performed their validation experiments in a cone
calorimeter. The model results fit the experimental data well, but deviations were found
between the model and the data. They concluded that further model refinement is needed
if a perfect fit is to be found. The model was slightly modified, and compared to experi-
mental data obtained from furnaces, after which it was published in a new article in 2013
[17]. The model was shown to generally reproduce the experimental data with around
20% deviation in worst cases. The bubble size prediction method was not fully correct
and needed reevaluation.
Around 2016, a divergence was observed in the approach taken to modeling intumes-
cent coatings. Some of the publications that came in the later years attempted to refine
the models further than their predecessors while adding to their complexity. Others have
been seeking to simplify the models to make them easier to use and more applicable in
practical situations.
Cirpici et al. were among those trying to refine the model further. In 2016 they [18]
created a model focused mainly on the expansion stage of an intumescent coating. It was
a model that built upon the work made by Amon et al. [19, 20] and adapted it to an intu-
mescent coating. Amon et al., back in the period between 1984 and 1986, published two
articles regarding the mathematical description of the growth of closely packed bubbles in
foams. The model made by Cirpici et al. used data for the gas evolution, also called mass
loss, from the publications by Zhang et al. [16, 17] to model their expansion. The results
were compared to data by Zhang et al. and other data in the literature. A reasonable fit
between the data and the experimental results was seen. The model appeared to predict
the expansion rate quite well in the early stages but appeared to overpredict it in the
later stages of the expansion process.

were among those who attempted to simplify the models.

the coating was defined from the final coating thickness and degree of conversion of the
solids. The energy balance was modeled as a network of thermal resistors. The decom-
position front was described using simple reaction kinetics. The heat transfer coefficient
was determined experimentally. Due to the assumptions made, Nørgaard et al. reduced

scales with the temperature using the Arrhenius equation. This was the first model of
the reacting phase as one continuous phase with the char included instead of being split
into a separate phase. There was a good agreement between the model results and the
experimental data. The study concluded that the thermal properties in the expanded
char have a large influence on the properties of the intumescent coating.
In the same year Tian et al.
[23] formulated an engineering model for the intumes-

char layer, the energy conservation of the coating layer, the energy conservation of the
substrate, the thermal degradation of the intumescent coatings, and the expansion model.
The solution process was approached sequentially, with the external heat flux serving as
input for the energy conservation of the char layer and the energy conservation of the
coating layer. The result obtained would be fed into the energy conservation of the sub-
strate; this would provide more data for the first two conservation equations, as well as
input for the degradation model, which would, in turn, provide input for the expansion
model. This would provide input for the first two conservation equations, allowing the
process to continue until the model was solved. This interesting approach allowed for
circumventing the issue of simultaneously solving all the equations associated with mod-
eling intumescent coatings. The model data fit the experimental data reasonably well,
but major deviations were observed in certain parts.
In 2022, Zhu et al. [7] constructed a predictive model for the thermal expansion and
heat transfer of the intumescent coating. They separated the process into three distinct
stages. The first stage is preheating, where only the virgin material is present. The
second stage is the expansion into the char stage, where the reaction occurs and all three
phases of virgin material, the swelling decomposition layer, and the char layer. The final
stage is the char heat transfer stage, where only the intumescent coating is present. The
models included all the standard heat transfer mechanisms present in the previously pre-
sented models. The expansion process was the function of the mass loss calculated from
a chemical reaction with the rate constants obtained using the Arrhenius expression. The
solution was calculated iteratively, starting with the incoming heat flux with the detailed
schematics, which equation provided the input for the next equation in the article. The
modeled data showed great agreement with the experimental data, with only minor de-
viations between the model and the measured experimental results.

Lucherini et al. [10] took a very different approach from the previous models, and in

they assumed that the char layer is the dominant contributor to the insulative perfor-

other states of the coating. To model the coating, they separated the coating layer into

last zone are half as thick. The growth of the char layer was created numerically by
increasing the value of N according to an empirical formula created from the maximum
coating thickness and incoming heat. The coating properties were assumed constant and
taken from other experimental studies. The results from the modeling were compared to
experimental results. It was observed that the model would be capable of predicting the
general trends but would often over or underestimate the coating thickness and, there-
fore, would give wrong temperature predictions. Furthermore, the authors concluded that
the model was flawed as the expansion rates would be highly dependent on the coating
composition and would not apply to coatings other than the one studied.

2.5.3
CoaST - fire
The CoaST – fire stands for The Hempel Foundation Coatings Science and Technology
Centre - Furnace for Intumescence Research and Exploration [44]. It is a novel machine
developed at the Technical University of Denmark with the primary purpose of being
used for intumescent coating research [44]. Figure 4 contains a model and a picture of
the CoaST-fire setup. The system’s main body consists of a furnace tube surrounded by
Figure 4: A) model of the coast fire, B) a close-up of the top of the setup, C) a close-up of a
sample model during measurement, and D) a photo of the experimental setup at DTU in building
228A. (Source:[44])
a sizeable insulating body containing various heating elements. The heating elements are
separated into three heating zones, allowing the creation of almost any desired tempera-
ture profile. Near the top of the furnace tube is an inlet that there is a gas inlet which
allows control of the gas composition within the setup. Above it is a quartz window that
allows vision of the sample as it is moved through the furnace tube. The sample can be
illuminated by a powerful LED installed in the system and recorded using a camera at
the top of the setup. The sample is propelled through the system by a steel rod on a
plate of insulating material. The metal of the sample is connected to a thermocouple.
The rate at which the sample is propelled through the furnace tube can be controlled,
allowing it to fulfill any of the time-temperature curves[44]. A significant advantage of
the CoaST-fire setup is the degree of control over the conditions the sample is exposed
to over time. Furthermore, the oxygen percentage in the oven can be easily controlled.
Contrary to a standard furnace that needs to cool off before the sample is removed, the
test can be stopped at any time, allowing for samples to be extracted at different stages
of the expansion process[44].

Experiments
This section details the experimental setup, procedure, and results of the experiments
conducted in the CoaST electric furnace. The experimental panels fall into three cate-
gories: panels without thermal protection, panels with non-reactive industrial protection,
and panels coated with an intumescent coating. Section 3.1 describes preparing the oven

3.1
Experimental procedure
This section will describe the experimental procedures for the experiments. The setup
and experimental procedure for panels without thermal protection can be found in section
3.1.1. The setup and procedure for the panel with non-reactive industrial protection are
outlined in section 3.1.2. Finally, the setup and procedure for panels coated with an
intumescent coating are presented in section 3.1.3.
3.1.1
Panels without thermal protection
This section contains the procedure for the panels without thermal protection. The main
goal of this series of experiments was to obtain data that could be used for determining
the conditions inside the oven. The panels were modified in three ways to modify their
emissivity to make it easier to split up the convective and radiant heat transfer from the
oven to the sample.
3.1.1.1
Preparing the metal plates
In order to perform the experiments, the surfaces of the metal plates were treated using
three different methods. The goal of changing the surface was to alter the emissivity of
the panels, which would allow for easier fitting of experimental data to models to extract
data about the convective and radiant parts of the heat transfer. The first type was pol-
ished to have a smooth surface. The second was coated with a high emissivity AREMCO
840-MX-1119 [46] high-temperature coating, henceforth referred to as 840MX. The man-
ufacturer reported the emissivity of this coating to be around 0.85. The third type was
coated with AREMCO Pyro-Paint 634-AS-1258 [47] for high temperatures, henceforth
referred to as 634AS. The three prepared plates can be seen in Figure 5.
The polished steel panels were polished by the workshop at KT-DTU, and no further
action was needed. The 840MX and the 634AS coatings were applied with a standard
brush onto the surface of the steel panels until the layer was deemed sufficiently thick
and uniform. Then they were left to dry at room temperature for 72 hours, after which
they were cured at 94◦C for 2 hours.
As seen in subfigure 5c, the pigment in the 634AS coating appears to have reacted with
the steel plate, causing rust to form, which in turn caused orange/brown discoloration.
Due to this and the coating’s bad coverage, while applying it, it was decided that no more
plates of this type would be prepared and no experiments would be carried out using it.
The polished steel and the 840MX coating were deemed of satisfactory quality, and it
was decided they would be used in the experiments.

3.1.3
Intumescent coating
This section will detail the experimental procedure for the steel panels treated with an
intumescent coating. The purpose of these experiments was to examine the behavior
of intumescent coatings under different fire conditions. The data obtained from these
experiments are meant to validate the models created to describe the intumescent process.
5 of the samples were coated as a part of the work on this thesis, while Ander Labaien
Etxeberria provided the remaining two.
3.1.3.1
Preparing the metal plates
The coating used for the samples was an epoxy-based intumescent coating, hereinafter
EBIC. The EBIC consists of two components: a base and a curing agent. Both compo-
nents were heated for 24 hours at 70◦C prior to application onto the steel panels. Shortly
before the application, the components were mixed in proportions of 2.5 parts base to 1
part binder by mass.
Five molds were selected. Three molds were approximately 9mm deep, and two were
approximately 6 mm deep. The inside of the molds was smeared with Vaseline, after
which the steel panels were inserted. From the 9mm molds, approximately 24g of the
coating was weighed and poured into the mold, resulting in samples with a 5mm DFT. For
the 6 mm molds, the molds were filled with the coating to the brim, and an excess coating
was scraped off the top, which would result in samples with a 3mm DFT. The coating
was spread into all the corners of the mold with a wooden spatula, and the surface was
evened with a roller. Figure 9 contains pictures from the application process. Once the
coating was applied, it was left to cure in a climate chamber at 25◦C and 50% humidity
for between 13 to 17 days. Shortly before being used, a sample would be removed from
the climate chamber and pressed out of the mold. Subsequently, the sample would be
weighted. Figure 10 contains an image of a fully cured sample with a 5mm DFT shortly
after removal from the mold. In most cases, the sample would have thin slivers of coating
hanging from the edges, which would have to be shaved off before the sample could be
used for an experiment.

As shown in figure 17, the heating rate reflects the above-mentioned trend. The samples
appear to begin heating around the same time, followed by a rapid increase in the heat-
ing rate. Around the rate of 1.5 ◦C/s, the samples begin to diverge. The heating rate
of sample 1 slows down in its increase while the heating rate of sample 2 continues to
increase rapidly. A strange decrease in the rate increase followed by a sharp peak can be
seen for both samples. The peak lies at t ≈300s for sample 2 and t ≈350s for sample 1.
The peak for sample 1 corresponds with the time at which the oven temperature should
reach a steady state according to the UL1709 heating curve, while t ≈350s is slightly
after the oven reaches the steady state temperature. This discrepancy is strange as the
temperature curves for the oven were practically identical. An exponentially decaying
heating rate follows the peak in the data until the heating rate reaches the same value for
both samples. A possible explanation for the different positions of the noticeable peaks
could be explained by a mismatch of times between the noted time of the oven start and
the actual time when the oven was started. This explanation is plausible as there have
been issues with determining the correct oven start time for sample 2. Another reason
might be that the air circulation conditions in the oven were different for run 1 and run
2. The gas circulation in the oven is induced by purely natural convection. As a result,
different lab conditions or using a different SkamoSteel mold might have significantly
affected airflow inside the oven.

growing layer of protection.
Two probable mechanisms can explain the next peak. The first is that around the begin-

contribution for the reaction to decrease, allowing the heating rate to go up. This increase
in the heating rate continues until the coating reaches a temperature at which it would
have been had the reaction not played a role, after which the rate of temperature increase
begins to decrease again. Another possibility is that the coating expands and reaches a
critical pore size at the bottom of the first valley. Upon further expansion, the pores
exceed the critical size, and any increase in the size of the pores at this stage increases
the coating’s thermal conductivity. These two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive,
and both may be present. It is also possible that neither is present and the trend results
from something else.
The last peak is generally associated with thermal oxidation of the coating [8].
3.3
Potential sources of errors
Throughout the experiments, several potential sources of errors have been identified.
Some apply to all the experiments, while others apply only to specific experiments. This
section will first discuss the general sources of errors, after which specific sources of errors
will be discussed.
It has been mentioned several times throughout section 3.2.2 that there is a significant
possibility of desynchronization between the time the experiment starts and when the
furnace is turned on. During the experiments, effort was made to ensure that a way to
synchronize those two times was available. This was mainly done by writing to file when
the oven was turned on or off. In most cases, this worked, but there have been a few cases
where the command in Labview did not go through, resulting in no start time for the
furnace. This has, in most cases, been fixed, yet uncertainty remains. This uncertainty
might slightly impact comparing the experimental results to the model outputs.
Thought all experiments, it has been observed that the SkamoSteel mold would crack.
The more times the same mold was used, the more cracks appeared. These cracks may al-
low hot air to escape the oven, which, contradictory to the initial assumption, might have
increased the temperature of the steel sample, as the hot air would heat the ScamoSteel
molds while traveling through it. The increase in the ScamoSteel temperature would lead
to lower heat loss through the back plate than in the case of uncracked mold. In extreme
cases, this might have even led to the SkamoSteel mold becoming hot enough in certain
parts to heat the sample, but this is only pure speculation.
As the only source of momentum in the oven was the density differences caused by differ-
ent temperatures in the oven, the air would move quite slowly. The slow air movement
53
==PAGE:64==
Masters thesis
3.4
Section conclusions
might have led to a different temperature near the sample than inside the oven at a given
time. Possible signs of this were observed in some of the heating-rate vs. time curves,
where the first peak in the rate curve would be slightly delayed relative time at which the
oven had reached the maximum temperature. This delay might lead future theoretical
models to overestimate the measured values slightly.
As mentioned before, the SkamoSteel mold would crack during the experiments making
it impossible to use the same mold for all the experiments. It was observed that no two
SkamoSteel molds were the same, which might have led to some of the oven conditions
changing. These changes might result in changes to the heat transfer coefficients. It is
uncertain how the heat transfer coefficient would change; therefore, it is impossible to
predict the effect of this change on the results.
As previously mentioned, the polished steel sample deformed significantly during the
experiments, meaning all the data was invalidated. Some changes were also seen in the
840MX coated samples. The surface of the 840MX was observed to have become shinier
after the experiment, which may have led to lowering the radiant component of the heat
transfer, which would, in turn, lower the recorded temperatures for the 840MX panels.
The EBIC was difficult to apply, especially when it began cooling down and becoming
much more viscous. The viscousness made it difficult to get exactly the same thickness
for all samples. In addition, it was not easy to get a perfectly leveled surface due to
the nature of the coating. In the future, a good idea might be to let the curing process
begin in an oven at a temperature between 80◦C to 90◦C where the coating would have
a chance to become more liquid, allowing it to flow out into the mold, creating a leveled
surface.
Another issue is that the coating did not expand uniformly; this might have led to the heat
transfer happening at different rates in the coating. Furthermore, the less expanded parts
might have had different physical properties from the more expanded parts. In addition,
there were gaps between the channel walls and the coating, which might complicate the
modeling process.
The mass losses measured might not have been fully correct. After samples 1 and 2 were
removed from the oven, a black spray pattern was found on the oven’s far wall, leading
to the assumption that the coating might have violently ejected parts of itself during
the intumescent process. In addition, some of the coating mass was lost because of the
coating shattering while removing them from the oven. The shattering of coatings might
also have led to expansion heights being slightly lower than their real value.
It must be stressed that all of the sources of error mentioned above are minor, except
what happened to the polished steel panel. The setup and the experimental method
used have been used many times before this project, and the results obtained using these
methods have been validated and deemed trustworthy in the past [8].

CFD-simulations
The original goal of the Computational fluid dynamics(CFD) simulations was to qualita-
tively examine the furnace’s conditions. From the theoretical correlations introduced in
the literature study, it is known that the value of the heat transfer number will vary with
temperature. It would be difficult to provide a satisfactory fit to the data using simple
curve-fitting methods. As a result of this, CFD simulations to establish the conditions
inside the furnace were performed. The CFD experiments were split into two parts. The
first was a series of a few CFD simulations to establish whether the results from the CFD
simulations could be used to determine the conditions within the oven. The second series
was meant to produce a large data sample that could be used to create an empirical
formula for the data.
4.1
Modeling of the conditions during the unprotected steel
experiment
From results previously obtained in the group, it was known that creating a transient
CFD simulation was unadvised at the current moment due to the very high computational
cost, especially if an expanding intumescent coating was to be included. As a result, it was
decided to perform the CFD as a series of steady-state simulations where the conditions
were sat to those observed at given points during one of the experiments.

5.1

A sketch of the system in question can be seen in Figure 46. It has two planes of symmetry
perpendicular to one another; therefore, the design is the same from all sides. As can be
seen from the figure, the setup consists of a short channel leading from the inside of the
oven, with a steel plate at the end of it. The walls of the quadratic channel are made out
of SkamoSteel Vip 12 HT [48]. The side length of the channels is 4.5 cm.
The metal plate at the end of the channel has a side length of 6cm and a thickness of
3mm. Only an area of 4.5 cm × 4.5 cm of the steel plate is exposed to the channel. Two
layers of 5 mm thick Fiverfrax Duraboard 120ZK [49] insulation material insulate the
backside of the plate. The steel plate and the insulation material are held in place by a
4.3 cm thick block of SkamoSteel.

Development of a model for the intumescent coat-
ing
This section delves into developing, refining, and validating a model for the expanding
intumescent coating. Model development is initiated by adapting a simple model partially
inspired by a model introduced in course 88716 to the system of interest. A sensitivity
analysis is performed to investigate the effect of input parameters on the model results.
Subsequently, the model is improved by removing the initial assumptions. At every step,
the model is validated against the experimental results, which had been presented in
section 3.2.2. At the end of the section, the results of the two final variants of the model
are presented along with the parameters used as their input.
6.1
System description
The system is highly similar to that of steel with no thermal insulation. Figure 52 contains
the system sketch.
Figure 52: Sketch of the system with a partially expanded coating
As seen from the system, it consists of an inlet channel within which the intumescent
coating will expand, creating a thermally insulating barrier between the oven and the
steel. The energy will be transferred from the oven to the surface of the coating. The
modes of energy transfer consist of the radiant and the convective component. The energy
that hits the surface of the coating is then transferred to the metal plate through the
coating. Energy will be lost through the back insulation in the same manner as it was
for the system for steel with no thermal insulation. The cross-section of the channel is
4.5 cm x 4.5 cm, and the dimensions of the steel plate are 6 cm x 6 cm x 0.3 cm.

6.8
Section conclusions
The primary purpose of this section was to develop a mathematical model which would
be able to describe the behavior of the intumescent coating in the small-scale CoaST
electric oven.
A model which can predict experimental results within the experimental standard devi-
ation for the EBIC was developed. The model contained both the convective and the
radiant heat transfer mechanism from the oven to the coating. Furthermore, the model
included heat loss through the back insulation of the sample panel.
The model also contained all three relevant heat transfer models inside the coating.
The model took into account the heat capacity of the coating and the enthalpy of the
intumescent reaction. The heat capacity lowered the predicted temperatures significantly.
Including the reaction enthalpy made the model reproduce an inflection point in the data,
which could not be replicated otherwise with the methods attempted.
Due to a lack of experimental data, specific input parameters were modified within a
range of what is reported in the literature until the model provided a resonable fit to the
data. Ultimately two final versions of the model were presented. The two versions were
set apart by the method sued to describe the convective heat transfer from the oven. The
first used a constant heat transfer number, while in the second, the heat transfer number
was dependent on the system temperatures and the coatings expansion degree. Both
models were shown to provide a good fit for the data. The model results with varying
heat transfer numbers were closer to the experimental values with all the model-provided
values within the experimental standard deviation.
It is concluded that further work is needed to refine the model before it can give accurate
predictions. In addition, experimental work is needed to provide more accurate input
parameters for the model.

7
Conclusions
This project aimed to create a mathematical model which would describe the behavior
of the intumescent coating within the CoaST small-scale electric ovens when exposed to
a hydrocarbon fire curve.
During the literature study, it was discovered that the models developed previously were
almost exclusively one-dimensional. It was concluded that based on the findings in the
literature, the process of modeling intumescent coatings could be broken down into three
main parts: the reactions, the expansion, and the heat transfer.
It was seen in the
literature that the reactions were generally modeled as either one or multiple first-order
independent reactions. The expressions for expansion would differ from publication to
publication, but they almost always depend on the extent of the reaction and the coating
expansion ratio. The heat transfer inside the coating in all the newer publications would
always include conduction, convection, and radiation. Methods were found to estimate
the furnace convective and radiant heat transfer to the coating. Furthermore, general
industry standards testing of intumescent coatings were outlined.
A series of experiments were carried out to obtain data that could be used for parameter
fitting or model validation. The experiments were performed by exposing the samples to
standardized heating curves in the CoaST electric oven and measuring the temperatures
of the back plates. Data that was deemed valid was obtained from these experiments. It
was observed that the intumescent coatings would behave differently based on their DFT
and when exposed to different heating curves. The mass loss and expansion ratios were
noted for all the intumescent coating samples. In addition to the data for the intumescent
coatings, essential data were obtained for samples inserted in the oven without thermal
protection.
Computational fluid dynamics simulations were used to describe the conditions inside the
electric furnace during the experiments. The data from the CFDs were used to develop
and new empirical model which could predict the gas velocities close to the sample during
the experiment. This model was then used to predict temperature-dependent convective
heat transfer coefficients for energy transfer from the oven to the sample.
A simple model for unprotected steel in the Coast electric oven was developed. This
model included both the radiant and the convective heat transfer mechanism from the
oven to the plate and the heat loss from the plate through its back insulation. This model
was used to estimate the oven’s heat transfer coefficient and view factor based on the
experimental data for samples with no heat protection. Two different series of fitting
were performed. In the first, the heat transfer number and the view factor were assumed
constant, and their values were found to be 64W/m2K and 0.0378, respectively. In the
second, the view factor was kept constant, and the convective heat transfer number was
modeled using the empirical co-relation developed as part of this thesis. In the second
version, the view factor was found to be 0.1008. The heat transfer number for the loss
through the backplate was calculated to be 4.3675 W/m2K.
A mathematical model describing the behavior of the intumescent coating inside of the
small-scale electric oven was developed. A single first-order reaction governed the reaction
part of the model.
The Arrhenius equation governed the rate of the reaction.
The
expansion model was a function of the conversion degree of the reactant and the maximum
coating expansion. The heat transfer model included heat conduction through the solid
material, heat conduction in the gas in the pores, and radiation between the pore walls.
Furthermore, the model considered the reaction enthalpy and the coating heat capacity.
105
==PAGE:116==
Masters thesis
Both the radiant and convective heat transfer from the oven to the coating was included
in the model.
The loss through the backplate was also included.
Due to a lack of
information about the coating used, some parameters had to be estimated by fitting the
model to the experimental data. The final presented model provided a reasonable fit to
the experimental results within the experimental standard deviation.
While the result of the fit was not perfect, the model was concluded to work. The devel-
opment of this model is a step toward understanding the process that the intumescent
coatings go through in the small-scale furnace, which over time, might be adapted to ex-
plain why the results for large-scale furnaces differ from those obtained in the small-scale
furnaces.
Unfortunately not all the aspects of the intumescent process were fully included in the
model. The reaction scheme used was too simple, and no experimental data were available
for the parameters used in the Ahrenius expression, nor was experimental data available
for the enthalpy of the reaction. Furthermore, the expansion process was greatly sim-
plified. The model also ignored thermal oxidation, which occurs in the charr at high
temperatures. In addition, no available data was produced for validation of the expan-
sion or the coating surface temperatures. Finally, due to the assumptions made, the
model cannot predict intra-char temperatures.
A lot of possible venues for future work with the topic exist. A lot of the assumptions
which were made during the model development were not corrected. Implementation of
mass loss into the model and improving the expansion model would be the easiest to im-
plement. Discretization of the coating, as seen in many literature publications, would be
an obvious next step. Experiments should be carried out to measure the coating’s phys-
ical properties, such as the reaction enthalpies and the reaction activation energies. In
addition, more experimental data should be produced to validate the model results. Fur-
thermore, revisiting the radiant heat transfer expressions and implementing the changes
of the view factor with the expansion could be desirable. In addition, the way in which
the CFD simulations were incorporated into the modeling process yielded fantastic re-
sults, and ideas on how to further include it in any future modeling have already been
created.
As expected, a perfect 3-dimensional model which predicts the experimental results with a
hundred percent accuracy was not created. Nevertheless, steps were made in the direction
of explaining the processes, and the road was paved for future work. And like every long
journey, the road to the goal is taken one step at a time.
