I 
 
 
 
 
Problem statement 
Formation damage is a problem persisting to the oil and gas industry. It has a direct 
negative economic effect by reducing the operational capacity of a well through a 
reduction of the initial permeability of the virgin rock. The subject investigated in this 
work is the impact of nanoparticle additives in drilling fluids on formation damage. 
 
 
Abstract 
The scope of this paper is to contribute knowledge to formation damage mitigation 
research. More specifically, the impact of nanoparticle drilling fluid additives on 
formation damage is investigated through formation damage induction comparison 
between a conventional fluid and a nanoparticle fluid of similar composition. The 
experiments were conducted on low permeability Berea sandstone plugs on a 
Dynamic Formation Damage Rig apparatus capable of recreating the overbalanced 
dynamic circulation conditions dominating in a wellbore annulus. The results for the 
selected case study of a TTRD well, indicated a 5.1 % mitigation of permeability 
reduction with the fluid containing the nanoparticles, suggesting the faster build-up of 
a less permeable filter cake.

II 
 
 
 
 
Summary 
During the length of a wellвҖҷs life, the hydrocarbon bearing formation is inevitably 
exposed to foreign fluids and particles, which result in permeability impairment 
known as formation damage. A number of different mechanisms can be held 
accountable for this, and each type of operation taking place in the well has its own 
dangers for causing formation damage. The present master thesis work is 
investigating the impact of nanoparticle additives вҖ“a newly introduced technology in 
the oil and gas industryвҖ“ for drilling fluids on the above mentioned problem. 
 
Four compositionally different drilling fluid samples were initially mixed. One 
conventional without nanoparticle additives and the other three with similar 
composition to the conventional except for the introduction of a nano-additive. In 
each of the non-conventional drilling fluids a different type of nanoparticles was used. 
The viscosity of the different drilling fluids was measured using a Fann viscometer 
and was successfully attempted to be regulated to the same level for all samples using 
sodium acid pyrophosphate (SAPP) to control bentonite flocculation. After viscosity 
regulation the fluid samples were subjected to a filter press test to evaluate their filter 
cake tightness through the resulting filtrate losses. 
The conventional drilling fluid sample along with the nanofluid sample that 
resulted in the least fluid losses were selected for further testing on low permeability 
sandstone plugs using a Dynamic Formation Damage Rig (DFDR) apparatus capable 
of simulating the overbalanced dynamic circulation conditions of a real well. The 
selected study case was a typical Through Tubing Rotary Drilling (TTRD) well as this 
type of wells is regularly opted for the development of minor reservoir zones and 
marginal fields, where formation damage is a key parameter for making the project 
financially sustainable. 
After running six separate four-hour long simulations on the DFDR, three of 
which using the conventional drilling fluid and three using the nanoparticle enriched 
mud, the experimental results indicated a 5.1% mitigation of permeability reduction 
using the nanoparticle additives. Finally, the results are commented upon and 
suggestions for further work and on improving the experimental procedure are being 
made.

1. Introduction 
Modern economic forecasts indicate that energy consumption will remain one of the 
worldвҖҷs largest issues for years to come. The rapid global population increase, along 
with the development and industrialization of currently underdeveloped countries, are 
expected to render this problem even more dire.  
 
Despite the fact that the easily accessible fossil fuels of oil, coal and gas 
constitute the main sources of energy today, it appears that the oil industry has 
currently reached a temporary stalemate; the recent economic crisis has resulted in 
weaker industrial activity and thus lower demand for energy. This, combined with the 
transition of the United States and Canada into major oil producers, and the refusal of 
UAE to cut down on production in order to maintain its market share, have sent the 
oil prices spiraling down to around 60 $ per barrel. 
The impact on the oil industry has been substantial. A large number of 
operations, and most prominently those taking place in high extraction cost areas, 
have been rendered economically unsustainable. A potential solution to the problem 
for the current oil price would be making these operations more cost-effective and 
increasing their financial yield. In order for these goals to be achieved, the importance 
of research must be recognized. 
Among the major operational challenges pertaining to the oil and gas industry 
is the issue of formation damage. According to Hill et.al. вҖңFormation damage reduces 
a wellвҖҷs production or injection capacity and its removal or reduction is one of the 
major goals of petroleum engineersвҖқ [1]. Formation damage has a direct negative 
economic effect by mitigating the wellвҖҷs operational capacity. Its removal can be 
costly, time consuming, and in most cases not one hundred percent effective. As such 
it is preferable that its creation is avoided or minimized in the first place. 
Several researchers have studied the problem of formation damage. They have 
managed to indicate the controlling factors and suggested a number of general 
guidelines for its mitigation. Jilani et.al. [5] have studied the effect of overbalance 
pressure on formation damage and have very interestingly established that the optimal 
overbalance pressure for formation damage mitigation should be neither excessive nor 
minimal, but at an intermediate level. The suggested mechanism is being referred to 
again more extensively later in this work. KolltveitвҖҷs [2] experiments at the 
Norwegian University of Science and Technology corroborate the trend observed by 
Jilani. Thomas et.al. [6] have studied the distribution of mud induced damage around 
horizontal boreholes to address the question of the level of damage induced at the 
bottom, middle and upper parts of the heel and toe of a horizontal well by measuring 
the fluid losses for different drill-in fluids, axial velocities and pipe rotation rates. 
According to their results, the extent of formation damage is dependent on the 
eccentricity of the pipe, and is a function of drill pipe rotational speed, the exposure 
time and the amount of drill solids in the mud.

A newly introduced technology in the oil and gas industry, is that of 
nanoparticles. Considerable research has been made on their possible applications. 
According to Jahns [20], the use of nanoparticles in drilling fluids, contributes to 
reduced mechanical friction while drilling. Mechanical friction is one of the key 
factors limiting the maximum length of modern wells. Jianying et.al. [27] have 
investigated through molecular dynamics simulation, the application of nanoparticles 
for enhanced oil recovery and improved cement integrity with promising results. 
Agarwal et.al. [29] have established that the application of a combination of 
hydrophobic nanoparticles and organically modified nanoclay assists in producing 
stable at high temperatures water-in-oil invert emulsion drilling fluids. Finally, Abdo 
et.al. [30] were able to demonstrate the effectiveness of palygorskite in nano-form for 
swiftly controlling and retaining the desired rheological properties of drilling fluids 
over a wide range of operating pressures and temperatures. 
Regarding the use of nanoparticles as additives for formation damage control, 
two contradicting theories exist. According to the first, the small size of the nano 
additives should allow them to invade deeper into the formation compared to the 
larger particles present in conventional muds, blocking the pore channels at larger 
distances from the well and thus intensifying the problem. According to the second 
theory, nanoparticles would be far too small to cause pore plugging and will be easily 
flushed out once production initiates. Furthermore, by assisting in a faster, less 
permeable external filter cake buildup and thus reducing fluid losses, nanoparticles 
could also prevent excessive solids invasion, thus reducing formation damage. 
Finally, nanoparticles may help in creating a smoother pore profile that would result 
in improved flow and ultimately, higher recovery. The effectiveness of nanoparticles 
on formation damage control is promised by a number of companies dealing in nano-
fluids [32], and some research has been conducted on this subject, particularly on 
formation damage and fluid loss reduction. Ragab et.al. [31] conducted a research on 
formation damage by investigating the fluid loss characteristics of a water-based mud 
when three differently sized types of nanoparticles were used as fluid loss additives to 
determine the nanoparticle size range that yields the lowest losses. According to the 
results, the nanofluid showed a 56% reduction in fluid loss compared to the base fluid 
for the selected nanoparticles size and concentrations. Srivatsa et.al. [33] have again 
investigated the use of nanoparticles as fluid loss additives in a surfactant-polymer 
based drilling fluid and have concluded on improved rheological and fluid loss 
properties compared to bio-polymer or surfactant based fluids and maintained that 
вҖңnanoparticles play an important role in reducing the fluid loss of the individual fluid 
systemsвҖқ [33].

2. Formation damage вҖ“ a theoretical background 
As damage in general, can be characterized any unintended impedance to the flow of 
fluids towards the surface. It may concern the formation, the gravel packs, the 
production tubing, the flow regulating valves etc. The problem of formation damage 
in specific arises as a result of foreign fluids or particles being introduced in the 
reservoir rock during the various drilling, completion, stimulation or workover well 
operations. This interference with the reservoir rock results in a reduction of its 
permeability and thus obstructs the flow of fluids towards the well, reducing its 
operating capacity [1], [2]. A description of the different formation damage types and 
the mechanisms through which it may occur during the different well operations is 
given below. Formation damage types can be categorized as to the mechanisms that 
cause them to: 
- 
Mechanical 
- 
Chemical 
- 
Biological 
- 
Thermal 
 
2.1 Formation damage types 
2.1.1 Mechanical 
 
External solids entrainment 
In the course of the drilling operation the reservoir rock is exposed for prolonged 
periods of time in an overbalanced condition to the drilling fluid. The drilling fluid, or 
drilling mud as it is most commonly referred, contains different types of particles 
(weighting agents, fluid loss control agents, lost circulation material, drilling 
generated rock material etc.) which, due to the overbalance pressure conditions, can 
enter the formation pores and block them, effectively reducing permeability and 
causing formation damage. In non-fractured reservoirs, unless the overbalance 
pressures are extreme or the permeability very high, the distance that these particles 
can travel is dependent on their size but is in general considered a near-wellbore 
problem. In the case of fractured reservoirs however, the foreign particles can travel 
quite longer distances and pose a more serious problem [2], [3], [4], [8]. 
 
Fines migration 
When production starts, the in-place formation fluid flowing towards the well, has the 
potential to dislodge rock fragments from the formation, which are carried by the 
flow. These fragments may build up in places where the pores are narrow down and 
bridge the throats obstructing the flow. As a rule, fines migration tends to be more of 
a problem in clastic formations due to their higher levels of transportable material, but

formationвҖҷs composition to evaluate the concentration of potentially transportable

phase of the reservoir fluids is set to motion that fines migration becomes an issue, as 
a result of the wetting phase being the one in contact with the fines. Thus, in a 
strongly water-wet reservoir that is at irreducible water saturation, fines migration can 
be expected to cause no problems. However, as the water saturation increases, the 
water phase becomes mobile and fines migration may become problematic [2], [3], 
[8]. 
 
Water blocking and trapping 
This type of damage is more prominent in low permeability (tight) reservoirs. If a 
large volume of drilling fluid is lost to a well when drilled with water-based mud, then 
in the near-wellbore area, the relative permeability to the hydrocarbon phase is 
significantly decreased. If the viscous forces are dominant over the capillary forces, it 
can be expected that water blocks should clear up rather shortly. If however the 
capillary forces are dominant, such as in the case of tight gas reservoirs, then water 
blocks may persist for quite prolonged periods of time [2], [3], [8]. 
  
Glazing 
This type of formation damage occurs directly on the borehole wall as a result of its 
interaction with the drill bit. If bit lubrication and the fluidвҖҷs heat abduction capacity 
are insufficient, high temperatures may develop on the borehole face resulting in 
mechanical glazing which blocks the flow of fluid towards the well. Open-hole 
completions in low permeability clastic formations as well as underbalanced drilling 
operations where aerated fluids with low thermal conductivity are used for the 
purpose of reducing the mud weight, are the most likely candidates for this type of 
damage [8], [9]. 
 
Mashing 
Mashing refers to damage caused on the borehole wall by a badly centralized rotating 
element in combination with poor hole cleaning. In this case, the rotating element 
repeatedly hits the wall, вҖңmashingвҖқ the drilled out cuttings and fines in the formation 
[2]. 
 
Geomechanical alterations 
The removal of rock material during drilling may result in alteration of the in situ 
geomechanical stress regime in the near-wellbore region. This can cause a distortion 
of the pore shape and geometry, affecting permeability [8].

Perforation induced damage 
The perforating process, though intended to create a channel between the reservoir 
pay zone and the well, results in the production of a large volume of highly mobile 
fine particles, which may reduce permeability in the near-wellbore area. The 
composition of the perforation fluid when perforating in overbalance has a significant 
impact on this type of damage [8]. 
 
2.1.2 Chemical 
 
Swelling clays 
Swelling clays are an inherent problem in sandstone reservoirs. If the sandstone 
contains reactive clays and the invading filtrateвҖҷs salinity is lower than the salinity of 
the in-place formation fluid, it can cause the clay particles to swell, blocking the pores 
and thus causing formation damage. The most known clays to exhibit such swelling 
behaviour are kaolinite, smectite (montmorillonite) and illite. Whether or not a 
sandstone contains clays that can cause a swelling problem can be defined through 
testing, and if this is the case, appropriate design of the drilling fluid can significantly 
reduce the problem [2], [3], [4]. 
 
Chemical adsorption 
Some polymers or other high molecular weight materials, commonly present in 
drilling fluids, may be adsorbed by the formation matrix and restrict flow through the 
pores simply due to their large molecular size [8]. 
 
Paraffin, asphaltene or tar deposits 
Wax or asphaltene deposits are heavy hydrocarbons that precipitate as pressure and/or 
temperature decrease. They can be potentially combined with fines migration вҖ“the 
fine particles acting as initiation sites for the heavy hydrocarbons deposition, resulting 
in mixed organic and non-organic deposits which plug the pores [2], [3]. 
 
Chemical wettability alteration 
Reservoir rocks are in the majority of cases water-wet. When drilling with oil-based 
mud, excess surfactants вҖ“which in many cases have polar adsorptive tendencies, may 
enter the formation and cause wettability reversal, which according to laboratory tests 
can result extensive production losses. It is thus important that the levels of excess 
surfactants in oil-based muds are kept as low as possible [2], [3], [4]. 
 
Emulsion blockages 
An emulsion is a mixture of two or more liquids that are normally immiscible. Again 
when drilling with oil-based systems, excess surfactants may enter the formation and 
form emulsions inside the pores that hinder the flow of formation fluids. This itself

Scale buildup 
The precipitation product of water-soluble chemicals as a result of mixing

sulfate, iron, chloride or silica [2], [3], [4]. 
 
Chemical reactions byproducts 
A large number of reactions can take place during drilling, stimulation or production 
operations, the products of which may precipitate out of solution and cause formation 
damage. Therefore, all well fluids should be lab tested before they are used in an 
operation, especially in areas with close proximity to the reservoir pay-zone [2]. 
 
2.1.3 Biological 
Anaerobic bacteria are commonly present in and around oil and gas wells. Due to 
high temperature and pressure conditions their growth is usually not stimulated. In 
operations that involve altering these conditions along with possible accidental 
introduction of nutrients in the reservoir, such as with injection of water-based fluids, 
the population growth may be favored. The bacteria population has the potential to 
cause formation damage mainly through pore plugging. The growth of sulfur-
reducing bacteria is one of the most troublesome as it results in the formation of 
hydrogen sulfide gas and causes corrosion problems to the flowlines and associated 
facilities equipment. Bacterial growth can be controlled by the use of bactericides; a 
potentially effective, but nevertheless expensive solution to the problem [2], [4], [8].   
 
2.1.4 Thermal 
Thermal formation damage is associated with operations that involve inducing high 
temperature levels in the reservoir, such as steam injection or in situ combustion. 
 
Mineral transformation 
At high temperatures, certain clay types may become water-reactive, swell and thus 
restrict flow in the pore system, reducing permeability [8]. 
 
Mineral dissolution 
Mineral solubility increases with increasing temperature. Prolonged dissolution 
conditions can lead to the release into the flow of previously encapsulated by the rock 
matrix fines, and precipitation and deposition of the dissolved minerals when the flow 
reaches lower temperature regions [8].

Formation rocks are generally rendered more water wet with increasing temperatures.

of oil through the pore space [2], [8]. 
 
2.2 Formation damage during well operations 
All the different operations taking place during the wellвҖҷs life can result in formation 
damage and each of them is associated with certain damage types that are вҖ“due to its 
nature, induced by it. In the following, the mechanisms through which formation 
damage occurs in each operation are described. 
 
2.2.1 Drilling 
The process of drilling is the first operation that disturbs the reservoir rock. It is by 
many considered the main formation damage contributor [5]. The two factors within 
the operatorвҖҷs control having an impact on formation damage during drilling are the 
overbalance pressure and mud composition. As explained later in paragraph 2.3, 
according to Jilani et al. [14], there is an optimal overbalance pressure that results to 
the lowest invasion of mud solids in the formation. Additionally the mud needs to be 
designed taking into account the composition of the formation pore fluid. 
Nevertheless, it is unavoidable that mud will also contain foreign particles, originating 
from the cuttings created by the drilling process, the fine fraction of which can cause 
considerable damage through plugging [6]. 
 
Drilling is often the most costly operation in field development. Drilling rigs 
are hired and paid for by the number of days employed and it is thus often tempting 
for economic reasons, to push for completing an operation as fast as possible. 
However increasing the rate or penetration usually comes with an increase in fluid 
losses, resulting in higher formation damage and thus in the long term is not 
economically advisable [2]. The types of formation damage associated with drilling 
operations are: 
o Plugging by mud solids invasion 
o Swelling clays 
o Wettability alterations 
o Scales 
o Deposits 
o Emulsions 
o Water blocking 
o Chemical reactions

2.2.2 Cementing 
During a cementing operation, in order for the cement to bind well enough with the 
formation rock, the filter cake needs to be thoroughly removed from the borehole 
wall. Thus the danger of cementing fluids invasion is present. As the duration of this 
operation is relatively short compared to the overall drilling process, the invasion 
depth usually ranges from a few centimeters to a few inches and thus the formation 
damage problems caused are potentially not severe. However in cases of large 
cementing fluids losses in the formation, stimulation techniques such as hydraulic 
fracturing or even drilling a sidetrack well may need to be employed in order to 
bypass the damaged zone [2]. 
 
2.2.3 Perforating 
Perforations are meant to increase the connectivity of the well to the reservoir. 
Especially for cased completions, perforating is in general the most common option 
for this purpose. Careful planning is necessary when executing this type of operation, 
as it is critical for it to be successful in both bypassing existing formation damage 
suffered by the reservoir, as well as in avoiding the induction of additional damage. 
Parameters such as the mud composition, the overbalance pressure, the size, strength 
and density of the perforating guns and the existing formation stresses are all affecting 
the success of the operation in that aspect [2]. The types of formation damage 
associated with perforating include: 
o Solids invasion 
o Plugging by formation fines and/or perforation debris 
o Fluid associated invasion 
 
2.2.4 Gravel packing 
Gravel packing is a sand control technique used in situations where experience has 
shown that sand production is to be expected. While gravel packs are not associated 
with damage to the reservoir itself, they can be worn down and deteriorate over time, 
limiting the flow of reservoir fluids in the wellbore [2]. 
 
2.2.5 Workovers 
As a workover operation is termed any kind of well intervention involving invasive 
actions, such as wireline, coiled tubing and snubbing operations or the process of 
pulling and replacing a completion set. Due to its diverse nature, all types of 
formation damage may occur during workover operations. However, it is critical, 
since at the time at which a workover operation is necessary the reservoir is usually at 
a depleted stage, that emphasis is given in fluid loss control, and ensuring proper 
design and compatibility of the workover fluids with both the pore fluid and the 
formation [2].

2.2.6 Stimulation 
The aim of stimulation operations is to increase the flow of reservoir fluids from the 
wellвҖҷs drainage area to the wellbore. Common stimulation methods include wellbore 
cleanup, acid treatment and fracturing. 
o During well cleanup operations, hydrocarbon deposits and corrosion products 
are removed from the well. In this process, highly damaging soluble material 
from the well can invade the formation along with the cleaning fluid and 
precipitate there, blocking the pores and reducing permeability. The 
overbalance pressure defines the extent of damage invasion in this case. 
o Acid treatment is commonly utilized in acid reactive reservoir rocks, such as 
chalk, to stimulate their productivity and remove acid soluble compounds like 
polymers that are used in drilling fluids as viscosifiers or calcium carbonate вҖ“a 
common drill in fluid weighting agent. Formation damage due to water 
blocking, wettability alteration and pore deposition plugging can occur in this 
process. 
o Fracturing is a well stimulation operation commonly used in tight reservoirs to 
improve their permeability. Large overbalance pressures are applied with the 
goal of fracturing the formation and propagating the fractures in considerable 
distances. When the overbalance pressure is removed, the fractures are kept 
open with the use of proppants (usually sand) that are injected into the opening 
space of the fracture to support the formation. Insufficient proppant strength 
can lead to their being crushed by the formation and is a potential source of 
damage in this case.

2.3 Filtration Theory 
As filtration is termed the mechanical process of separating the liquid and solid 
phases of a mixture by interposing a medium through which only the liquid phase 
may pass. The fluid that passes through the interposed medium is referred to as 
filtrate. In well drilling, filtration plays an important role in filter cake buildup. The 
filter cake prevents excessive fluid losses when drilling through permeable formations 
such as reservoir rocks, and by doing so prevents a number of types of formation 
damage, mitigating later operational problems. There are three basic conditions 
necessary for filtration to occur: 
o A liquid and solids slurry mixture 
o A permeable medium 
o A higher slurry pressure compared to the pressure of the fluid already present 
in the pores of the permeable medium (overbalance pressure conditions). 
 
As such, it can be understood that filtration does not occur in e.g. underbalanced 
drilling and therefore drilling fluids for such operations are designed with entirely 
different objectives in mind. 
 
As a result of the filtration process, three zones are formed in and on a 
permeable formation during drilling as shown in the drawing of Figure 1. Filtration is 
governed by DarcyвҖҷs law for flow through porous media. However, the process is 
additionally subject to the drives of the gradual permeability reduction of the porous 
medium by the filter cake being built on the formation, and also to the forces at which 
the external filter cake is subjected to during the operation.  
 
Figure 1: Filter cake zones [12] 
 
TodayвҖҷs standard practices dictate that optimal filter cake build up occurs with 
drilling fluids containing a solid particles distribution that is one third to one seventh 
of the mean pore throat size, as indicated in Figure 2 [12].

An additional important parameter, overpressure and its effect on invasion and filter 
cake build up has been argued and documented by several researchers. According to 
Jiao et.al. [13] a minimum overbalance pressure should be maintained at all times 
during drilling. This is necessary to keep the filter cake particles in place, maintaining 
low wellbore permeability and minimizing fluid losses. In conclusion, the filter cake 
thickness is determined to be a sensitive function of the drilling fluidвҖҷs rheological 
properties, overbalance pressure, and degree of shear rate caused by the flow through 
the annulus. 
Jilani et.al. [14] also supports that the degree of overpressure is an important 
parameter for formation damage control. According to the results, a too low 
overbalance pressure, would lead to a far too small concentration of particles in the 
pores to rapidly build a filter cake. In effect the bridging particles pass through the 
pores one after another, unable to reach a concentration capable of bridging the throat 
rapidly. As a result fluid losses are higher and so is the degree of formation damage. 
On the other hand, a too high overbalance pressure would rush bridging particles and 
fluids in the formation before the filter cake can fully form and would thus again lead 
to high fluid losses and extensive formation damage. Additionally, high overbalance 
pressure is against standard modern drilling practices as not only does it risk 
formation fracturing but also critically slows down the rate of penetration due to the 
static and dynamic hold down effects explained in Paragraph 3.1.2. Therefore, a 
sensible overbalance pressure is preferable to be maintained during drilling. Later 
research experiments conducted by Alikarami [15] and Kolltveit [16] in carbonate and 
sandstone core plugs respectively, corroborate the above mentioned trend. 
A common, fast and simple test for evaluating the filtration properties of a 
drilling mud or cement slurry is the filter press test: A volume of fluid placed in a 
small reservoir is subjected to pressure and filtrated through filter paper mimicking 
porous sandstone. The filtrate volume is measured in a graduated cylinder.

3. Drilling fluids engineering 
3.1 Objectives of drilling fluids 
Drilling fluids perform a number of different functions during the drilling operation, 
and they must be designed in order to simultaneously serve all of them. These 
functions are described below. 
 
3.1.1 Primary well pressure barrier and ECD control 
During overbalanced drilling, the drilling fluidвҖҷs density must be sufficiently high so 
as to exert a downhole pressure high enough to overcome the pore pressure of the 
formation and thus prevent pore fluids from entering the wellbore (situations termed 
as kicks). At the same time the exerted pressure must be lower than the formationвҖҷs 
fracture pressure so as not to fracture the formation at the casing shoe which would 
result in loss of drilling fluid. These two boundaries, the pore and fracture pressure, 
establish what is known as the drilling window, within which the pressure profile 
must be maintained at each stage of the drilling operation. Commonly this drilling 
margin is expressed as equivalent mud weight instead of pressures so as to minimize 
field calculations on the rig; the pressure gradients of pore and fracture pressures are 
expressed as the equivalent mud densities that would give these gradients, and thus it 
is more straightforward for the rigвҖҷs mud engineer to appropriately manage the 
density of the within the given window. The appropriate safety margins mud also be 
taken into account. It is preferable that the mudвҖҷs pressure gradient is maintained 
close to the pore pressure gradient as high mud density leads to lower ROP as a result 
of hold down effects. Additionally, kick situations are much easier to handle and far 
less serious than lost circulation incidents. 
The mud density can be controlled by adding weighting agents (calcium 
carbonate or, most commonly, barite) or salt [12]. 
 
An important concept that must necessarily be taken into account when 
designing a drilling operation is the equivalent circulating density (ECD). ECD 
represents the total actual bottomhole pressure exerted on the formation as a product 
of the sum of the hydrostatic mud pressure, the additional pressure increment due to 
the weight of the cuttings transported through the annulus and the effect of pressure 
drop caused by friction along the annulus [12].

3.1.2 Removal of cuttings from under the bit 
During drilling, and depending on the drill bit type the formation is being either 
crushed or sheared by the bit. It is essential in order to increase the rate of penetration 
(ROP), that the generated rock cuttings are removed from the rock surface as soon as 
possible. The opposite would mean that the drill bit is essentially grinding the same 
rock fragments many times over, and thus the drilling progress is slowed. 
 
For this purpose, effective cuttings removal is achieved by the combined 
mechanical energy from the drill bit and the hydraulic energy of the circulating 
drilling fluid, commonly referred to as jet impact force. Studies have shown that the 
rate of penetration is significantly increased with increasing bottom hole hydraulic 
energy [12].

Two final parameters of the drilling fluids impacting hole cleaning, are the fluidвҖҷs gel

such as chalk, limestone and anhydrite is related to dissolution of dissolvable material 
that can lead to problems of [12]: 
o Flocculation of the colloidal phase of the drilling fluid or clay aggregation 
o High solids content in the mud 
o Washouts 
 
In conclusion, there are three important factors that chemically affect borehole 
stability: 
o The ratio of chemical activity of the drilling fluid to the chemical activity of 
the shale, which is related to the drilling fluidвҖҷs salinity 
o The membrane efficiency 
o The ionic exchange capacity of the shale, which is related to the shaleвҖҷs 
composition 
 
3.1.5 Lost circulation control 
The drilling fluid tanks capacity on the rig is limited. Therefore, it is important, 
especially in offshore overbalanced drilling projects, that the drilling fluid losses 
when drilling through permeable formations are controlled and that they do not 
exceed an acceptable level. This can be accomplished by a rapid filter-cake buildup 
by the drilling fluid as explained in Paragraph 2.3.

3.2.4 Viscosity 
The viscosity of a fluid expresses its resistance to flow. Nowadays, it is commonly 
measured in the drilling industry with a six speed Fann-viscometer the operation of 
which is explained later in Paragraph 3.2.6. Drilling fluids that demonstrate constant 
viscosity for all shear rates are characterized as Newtonian fluids. Water or air can be

commonly however non-Newtonian fluids, meaning that their viscosity is dependent 
on the applied shear rate. 
 
3.2.5 Shear thinning effect 
Non-newtonian fluids (such as drilling mud) demonstrate a reduction of viscosity at 
high shear rates compared to lower. This phenomenon is known as shear thinning, and 
it is vital for cuttings suspension when the mud pumps are stopped, as it allows the 
drilling mud to assume gel like properties at low shear (static) conditions.

3.2.7 Yield point

3.3 Drilling fluids classification systems 
A number of different classifications for fluid systems exist with the main practical 
distinction made between water-based and oil-based fluids. A more thorough study 
however on the drilling fluid options currently available in the industry allows for the 
further division of these two main categories, concluding to nine distinct drilling fluid 
systems, of which six are water-based, two oil-based, and the last one constitutes a 
separate category where air, mist, foam or gas assume the role of the circulating 
medium. A description of these systems and their range of practical applications will 
be given in the following [34]. 
 
Non-dispersed 
This system includes spud muds, natural muds or other lightly treated fluids and finds 
application mainly in shallow wells or top hole drilling. Thinners or dispersants are 
not added to disperse drilling produced solids or clay particles.  
 
Dispersed 
At greater depths where the requirement for higher drilling fluid density becomes 
necessary, dispersed drilling fluids are used. The dispersant is typically lignites, 
tannins or lignosulfonates. These act as effective deflocculants and fluid loss 
controllers. Additionally, for the purpose of shale chemical inhibition, potassium 
containing chemicals are frequently added along with other chemicals to control the 
fluidвҖҷs properties.

Calcium treated 
Calcium treated mud systems are used in cases where shale swelling is a problem. 
Divalent cations, such as calcium or magnesium when added to freshwater, have the 
capacity to inhibit clay and shale swelling. High soluble calcium content may be used 
to prevent hole enlargement in sloughing shales. The most common ingredients of 
calcium treated mud systems are hydrated lime, gypsum and calcium chloride. The 
pH range is from 9.5 to 12 and the practical excess component concentration can vary 
from 2-4 lb/bbl for gypsum and 1-2 lb/bbl for low lime or 5-15 lb/bbl for high lime 
systems. Other specialized components may be added to control individual mud 
properties. 
 
Calcium treated muds are salt and anhydrite contamination resistant but their 
disadvantage is that they are susceptible to gelatinization and solidification at high 
temperatures. 
 
Polymer 
Long-chained, high-molecular weight polymers additives are incorporated in the mud 
as drill solids encapsulators thus preventing dispersion and shale coating for 
inhibition, or as viscosifiers and fluid loss reducers. 
 
A number of different types of polymers are available including among others 
acrylamide, cellulose and natural gum based products. Inhibiting salts such as KCl or 
NaCl are commonly used as shale stabilizers. Additionally a minimum amount of 
bentonite is added. 
These systems may be sensitive to divalent cations such as calcium or 
magnesium and have upper temperature limits below 300 oF, even though they can be 
used in wells with considerably higher bottom hole temperatures under certain 
conditions. 
 
Low solids 
In this category, fluid systems in which the amount (by volume) of solids is controlled 
are included. The total amount of solids ranges usually about 6-10% by volume. Clay 
solids are 3% or less and exhibit a ratio of drilled solids to bentonite less that 2:1. 
Non-dispersed polymer additives are commonly included as viscosifiers or bentonite 
extenders. 
The primary advantage and main motive behind the use of low solids systems 
is their capacity to significantly improve the drilling operationвҖҷs penetration rate, thus 
reducing rig costs for the operator. 
 
Saltwater systems 
A number of drilling fluids are included in this category. Saturated systems have 
typically a chloride concentration around 190.000 mg/l and are mainly used for salt 
drilling in order to avoid problems related to formation dissolution by the drilling

Oil-based 
Oil-based muds are used in a variety of applications where fluid stability and 
formation inhibition requirements are increased such as in high-temperature well 
conditions or swelling shales and additionally where hole stabilization and sticking 
pose serious problems. They could further be subdivided in two separate systems: 
 
Invert emulsion fluids are water in oil emulsions, the emulsified phase 
commonly being calcium chloride brine with oil being the continuous phase. Relaxed 
invert emulsion fluids are emulsions that have lower electrical stabilities and present 
higher fluid loss values. The salinity along with the mixing of additives are 
manipulated in order to control the rheological properties, filtration volume (fluid 
losses), and the stability of the emulsion. 
 
Typical oil based fluids consist of only oil as the liquid phase and are 
commonly used in coring operations. They tend to absorb some water from the 
formations and require higher additions of viscosifying agents. 
 
Specialized oil-based mud additives include emulsifiers and wetting agents 
(typically fatty acids and amine derivatives), high molecular weight soaps, 
surfactants, amine treated organic materials, organic clays and lime for alkalinity. 
 
Synthetic 
Oil-based muds, despite their advantages, can potentially pose an environmental 
threat. Synthetic fluids are designed specifically to mirror their performance while 
eliminating environmental hazards. The main types of synthetic fluids include esters, 
ethers, polyalpha olefins and isomerized alpha olefins. They are environmentally safe 
and can be discharged offshore, they are non-sheening and time biodegradable. 
 
Air, mist, foam and gas 
As the name suggests, four basic operations are included in this category. 
 
Dry air drilling is an underbalanced drilling technique that involves injecting 
air or nitrogen into the wellbore at velocities sufficiently high enough to remove 
cuttings and cool the drill bit.

with the produced formation water and coats the cuttings, avoiding the formation of

3.4 Drilling fluid additives classification 
A large number of different materials may be used as drilling fluid additives. Even 
though some of them are used for more than one single purpose, it is useful to classify 
them based on their function [34]. 
 
Weighting materials 
Barite, lead compounds, iron oxides, calcium carbonates and other similar high 
density products are used as weighting agents to increase the drilling fluidвҖҷs density 
and effectively control bottom hole formation pressures, formation caving and 
facilitate pulling-out-of-hole actions. 
 
Viscosifiers 
Materials such as bentonite, CMC (sodium carboxymethylcellulose), attapulgite clays 
and polymers are used to increase the drilling fluidвҖҷs viscosity to improve its hole 
cleaning and solids suspension properties. 
 
Thinners (deflocculants), dispersants 
These materials typically include tannins, different polyphosphates, lignite and 
lignosulfonates. They have the capacity of modifying the viscosity-solids content 
relationship of the drilling fluid and may be additionally used to reduce gel strength 
for the purpose of increasing the fluidвҖҷs pumpability. The principal function of a 
thinner is to function as a deflocculant, reducing the attractive forces between clay 
particles which cause high viscosity and gel strength.

The function of lubricant additives is to reduce the friction coefficient of the wellbore

These additives are intended to be spotted in a certain area where drill pipe stickage is

The function of this category of additives is to provide wellbore control when drilling

inhibitors. 
 
Corrosion inhibitors 
Corrosion inhibitors are used to control corrosion, neutralize hazardous acidic gases 
and prevent scale in drilling fluids. They are typically used along with pH controllers 
for maximized efficiency. Common corrosion inhibition additives are amine- or 
phosphate- based products or other specially formulated materials. 
 
Alkalinity, pH control additives 
These additives are used to control the degree of acidity or alkalinity of a drilling 
fluid. Typically lime, caustic soda, soda ash and bicarbonate soda along with other 
common acids and bases fall into this category. 
 
Surfactants 
Surfactants are additives that tend to reduce interfacial tension between contacting 
surfaces such as water-oil, water-solids, water-air etc. They can be emulsifiers, de-

surfaces between which the interfacial tension is desired to be reduced.

4. Nanotechnology applications in the oil and gas industry 
Nanoparticles have already found their way in technological applications for a 
number of industries, including biomaterials, medicinal sciences, and electronics. This 
chapter will attempt to describe their possible applications in the oil and gas industry, 
a field on which the potential benefits stemming from the use of nanotechnology are 
still being explored, and limited commercial options are thus far widely available. 
 
Nanoparticles are in general defined as particles ranging in size between 1 and 
100 nanometers. Their properties differ remarkably in comparison with larger 
particles and this difference is mainly a result of their large surface area to volume 
ratio. From a chemical point of view, the large surface area of the nanoparticles, 
enhances their reactivity by enhancing the rate, selectivity and efficiency of chemical 
reactions while reducing waste products and pollution. At nanoscale, substances are 
easier to dissolve in water and it has been demonstrated at NTNUвҖҷs nanomechanical 
lab by both experimental procedures and molecular dynamics simulation that particle 
size has significant impact on polymeric particles at nanometer scales, due to the 
increase of relative surface energy [27], [36]. 
 
4.1 Enhanced oil recovery 
Enhanced oil recovery (EOR) constitutes one of the most promising applications of 
nanotechnology for the oil and gas industry. The mechanisms through which this is 
accomplished include: 
o Changing the properties of the fluid 
o Rock wettability alteration 
o Advanced drag reduction 
o Strengthening sand consolidation 
o Interfacial tension reduction 
o Capillary trapped oil mobility increase 
 
TorsГҰter et al. [36], using molecular dynamics simulation of an oil-water binary 
system confined in kaolinite clay nanochannels demonstrated the effect of mixing 
hydrophilic silica nanoparticles with the system: The nanoparticles tend to move and 
adhere to the clay wall due to both the van der Waals forces and the Coulombic force 
in order to minimize surface energy. Therefore it seems that the nanoparticles are 
altering the interface properties between the fluid and the formation due to the newly 
created surface in contact with the fluid. The same phenomenon was observed for 
mixing a nanoparticle with only water or only oil.

nanochannel, cases of nanoparticles being placed in the water phase were 
investigated. Figure 18 below shows a perspective of four nanoparticles introduced in

4.2 Losses control 
Drilling fluid costs can amount to up to 18% of the total cost of a well drilling 
operation. Thus, mitigating drilling fluid circulation losses into the drilled formations 
has obvious economic advantages [31]. For this reason, drilling fluids are usually 
designed including lost circulation material, the purpose of which is to plug the losses 
zone in the formation. Lost circulation materials in the micro scale (0.1-100 Ојm) play 
an important role and are quite effective for pore sizes around 0.1 Ојm. The same 
however does not apply for smaller вҖ“up to 10 nm, characteristic pore sizes such as in 
shales. 
 
Research with nanoparticle additives has shown promising results, with many 
researchers claiming a fluid loss reduction in laboratory results in the order of 90%. 
[32]. According to Srivatsa et al. [33] silica nanoparticles exhibit very good bridging 
agent properties, similar to calcium carbonates, having however the added advantage 
of being easier to maintain at high temperatures. Moreover, they can prove very 
effective for shale drilling as, due to their small size, they are able to penetrate the 
shale pores and act as a bridging material, strengthening the formation and therefore 
additionally enhancing wellbore stability.

4.3 Rheology stabilization at HPHT conditions 
Invert emulsion drilling fluids are used when good borehole stability is essential for 
the drilling operation. This type of fluids consists of two immiscible liquid phases 
stabilized by a polymeric surfactant, and a solid phase. At high temperature conditions 
however, such as the prevailing temperatures in ultra-deep drilling, polymeric 
surfactants disintegrate causing phase separation and loss of the desired drilling fluid 
properties. 
 
Several researchers maintain that fine particle additives (hydrophobic 
nanoparticles in combination with organically modified nanoclay, or attapulgite 
nanoparticles) can be applied in order to stabilize the emulsion. This is accomplished 
by the particles absorbing on the interface between the two immiscible phases and 
preventing them from coalescing (Pickering emulsion). The resulting emulsion retains 
the desired plastic viscosity and yield stress necessary for the drilling operation to 
proceed for a wide range of temperatures [29], [30]. 
 
4.4 Cement binding 
An integral part of oil and gas wells is the cement located between the casing of the 
well and the surrounding formation. Its functions are to bond the casing to the 
formation providing well stability, support the casing, and restrict fluid flow from the 
reservoir to the upper formations. Poor cementation quality results in well problems, 
one of the most severe of which is annular gas migration: Gas enters the cemented 
zone, creating channels and has the potential of creating a flow path for formation 
fluids, including hydrocarbons. 
 
The causes behind gas migration are related to the cement hydration process. 
The water-cement reaction product occupies a smaller volume than the initial 
reactants, favoring the formation of a pore structure in the cement, which is at a later 
stage emptied as the water-occupying phase is being absorbed. This creates a pathway 
for gas migration which overtime may gradually deteriorate. 
 
In order to prevent such a situation, it is common practice for fluid loss agents 
to be added in the cement slurry in order to reduce volumetric losses. According to He 
et.al. [27], nanoparticle technology can further enhance the fluid loss agentвҖҷs effect. 
Smart nanoparticles have been developed that can utilize pressure and temperature 
variations during the cement hydration process in order to counteract the slurryвҖҷs 
hydration shrinkage phenomenon, and mitigate the formation of the pore structure 
[27], [28].

5.1 Plug preparation process 
Fifteen core plugs 3.8 cm in diameter and 5 cm in length where cut from a Berea

5.1.1 Cleaning 
The first step in preparing the plugs for the experiment, was to clean them of any 
existing traces of fluids. Despite the obtained core plugs being relatively clean to 
begin with, it was decided to apply a cleaning procedure nevertheless to affirm that 
the obtained results would be as error free as possible. 
 
The Soxhlet method was used for cleaning. The Soxhlet method is used for 
core plug cleaning from brine or oil by dissolving and extracting the pollutant with the 
use of solvents. Methanol is used as a solvent for cleaning the plugs of water and 
toluene for cleaning the plugs of oil. The main parts of the apparatus are: 
o The boiling flask containing the extraction solvent 
o The Soxhlet extractor body 
o The cooling tower or condenser

which contains a spiral structure through which water is running constantly for heat 
abduction purposes. The condensed methanol drips onto the core plugs in the

5.1.3 Porosity measurement 
Consequently, porosity measurements were taken for each plug, using helium 
porosimetry. The precise length and diameter of each plug that were used for the 
calculation of the bulk volume, as well as the porosimeter volume measurements of 
the volume of the chamber with and without the plug are given in Table 4 at 
Appendix 1. 
 
Helium porosimetry, uses principles of gas expansion as described by BoyleвҖҷs 
law in order to determine the pore volume of a plug: A known volume of helium gas 
at fixed initial pressure is isothermally expanded into an unknown void volume. After 
expansion the resulting stabilized pressure is measured. The resulting pressure is 
dependent on the unknown void volume, which can then be calculated using BoyleвҖҷs 
law (P1V1= P2V2). 
Helium is used in this process for a number of reasons: 
o It approximates ideal gas properties for most pressures and temperatures of 
interest 
o Due to the small size of its molecules it can penetrate the small pores of a rock 
o Due to its low mass - high diffusivity properties, it can be used to determine 
porosity in low permeability rocks

During the formation of montmorillonite, some of the aluminium in the lattice 
structure are replaced by lower electron valence ions, such as magnesium. This 
substitution creates negative surface charges in the lattice. These surface charges are 
partly balanced by close association of positively charged ions such as Na+, of K+. 
However the silica layers act as a barrier that prevents full neutralization of either the 
ion or the montmorillonite surface charges. This allows for the creation of strong 
attractive forces for polar water molecules that force themselves between the 
montmorillonite unit layers. Thus, the numerous montmorillonite individual sheets 
separate as more water is attracted and in the process entrap the water molecules, 
increasing the viscosity of the fluid. 
 
In the mixing process mentioned earlier, when mixing the KCl salt first, some 
of the water molecules were already occupied in neutralizing the resulting ions. Thus, 
the waterвҖҷs chemical activity (aw) was reduced at the time of bentonite introduction, 
less water molecules were able to force themselves between and separate the 
montmorillonite sheets, and therefore the viscosity was kept to reasonable levels.

5.3.2 Operational issues encountered 
A number of operational problems were encountered and had to be repaired on the 
DFDR. In this chapter these problems are outlined along with the procedure followed 
to repair them for future reference. Despite some delay that these problems caused 
with the time-schedule of starting the experiments on the rig, valuable experience was 
gained on several mechanological issues, such as pressure line connections, pump 
operations, piston placement, pressure regulating valves etc. 
 
Mud pump directional switches 
One of the switches that control the mud pumpвҖҷs movement direction needed to be 
repaired as it was malfunctioning and the cylinder was reaching the safety switch. 
This caused the pump to shut down on every second stroke. With the assistance of 
IPTвҖҷs mechanics and electrician, the switch was repaired and re-placed on the correct 
position on the pumpвҖҷs mainframe. 
 
Mud tanks oil level adjustment 
Exxsol D60 oil needed to be refilled in both the mud pump and mud tanks. For this 
purpose the pump cylinder needed to be set as close as possible to the middle position 
and oil was loaded from the oil loading container into the pump. Then oil was also 
loaded manually in the mud tanks, making sure the level of the oil was the same in 
both of them. 
Piston placement in the mud tanks 
At the start of the experiments the mud tank pistons were taken out and needed to be 
fitted back inside. This was not easy as the pistons are air tight and needed to be 
placed leaving no air gap between the piston and the oil in the mud tanks. After some 
trial and error, the successful procedure was to fill one mud tank with oil up to the 
top, place the piston on the oil surface and then set the mud pump to receive oil from 
this tank while at the same time forcing the piston down by hand. The same was done 
for the other tank. 
 
Piston removal and re-placement in the pore fluid container 
The pore fluid container piston needed to be removed in order for its O-rings to be 
greased as it was moving in bursts rather than smoothly inside the tank. The container 
also had to be cleaned before starting the experiments. The container was emptied 
from the remaining from previous experiments pore fluid and the piston was taken out 
by applying air pressure to the bottom of the container to push the heavy piston out. 
After cleaning, the piston was fitted back inside having the bottom valve open to 
evacuate air as the piston was forced downwards.

Core holder leakage 
Quite a large leakage between the core holder and the flow channel piece appeared

The O-ring was replaced with a thicker one of the same diameter to solve this issue.

runs, observed by the smell of oil on the test plug after the end of the run. Again the 
issue was a problematic O-ring this time inside the core holder which needed to be 
replaced to solve the issue. The tank containing the oil utilized for sleeve pressure 
transmission was also refilled as losing confining pressure during a test would ruin the 
experiment. 
 
5.3.3 Operational procedure 
The main testing steps on the DFDR apparatus include the initial permeability 
measurement, the four-hour long dynamic circulation period, and the final 
permeability measurement. A 5 bar overbalance pressure was selected for the tests 
here. Thus the circulation pressure was set at 8 bars while the pore pressure was 
regulated to around 3 bars.  
For improving application, repeatability and standardization, the steps of the 
applied testing procedure are described. It is assumed here that the selected mud has 
already been loaded in the two mud tanks and the mud reservoir. The tanks need to be 
turned upside down on their stand before testing initiates to facilitate the evacuation 
of air from the circulation system. It is also assumed that the selected pore fluid has 
been loaded in the pore fluid container, the piston of which should be somewhere in 
the middle of the container as the Quizix pump needs to be able to receive as well as 
deliver pore fluid during the test.

6. Discussion 
6.1 Conclusions 
The experimental results presented in Table 3, indicated a 5.1% mitigation of 
permeability reduction for the given study case when using the nanoparticle fluid. 
This suggests enhanced filter cake properties, and strengthens Ragab and NoahвҖҷs [31] 
previous results on nanofluids filtration losses attributes. The mechanism however 
through which the filter cake was improved cannot be commented upon as it could not 
be defined by the conducted experiment. Nevertheless, the conclusion of the tests 
must be that the application of nanotechnology shows promise in the field of 
formation damage control, especially when taking into consideration that the 
nanomaterial product that was used in the present work was designed with entirely 
different applications in mind. 
 
6.2 Comments 
As mentioned in Paragraph 6.1, the results indicated that the addition of the 
nanoparticle additive assisted in mitigating permeability reduction after the 
overbalanced dynamic flow simulation. However, the effect of the SAPP polymer 
which was used to bring the viscosity of the nanofluid down to the desired level 
should also be considered. Nevertheless, it is assumed here that it does not negate 
neither the resultsвҖҷ validity nor applicability, as the required polymer concentration 
was very low. If non-charged nanoparticles were possible to be selected for the test, it 
can be assumed they would not interact with the bentonite, in which case, the use of 
the polymer might have been unnecessary entirely. 
 
Furthermore, the experiment could not pinpoint the precise formation damage 
mitigation mechanism. A number of assumptions can be made on the exact filter cake 
parameter that was improved and it cannot be ruled out that some of them may hold 
true concurrently: 
o Less permeable filter cake 
o Smoother, erosion resistant external filter cake 
o Faster filter cake build up 
 
6.3 Further work 
Further experiments need to be designed and conducted to investigate the above 
possibilities. In suggesting a possible experiment to investigate the contribution of 
nanoparticles to the formation of a less permeable filter cake, a standard filter press 
test could be used. Using the filter press with a mud of typical composition a filter 
cake is formed on filter paper. Replacing then the mud above the formed filter cake 
with salt water and running the test again, the permeability of the filter cake should be

On the other hand, longer test plugs, preferably as long as is possible to fit in 
the core holder, would show the evolution over time of the internal filter cake build-
up. Once the external filter cake affected by both the overbalance pressure and the 
circulation erosion has been established and reached a thickness equilibrium, a steady 
inflow of particles from the mud towards the test plug should occur which would 
gradually reduce the plugвҖҷs permeability and therefore, also the amount of fluid 
received by the Quizix pump. Attempting to use nanoparticles in the drilling fluid in 
such an experiment, could reveal how the nanoparticles interact with the sandstone 
matrix. If higher permeability is noticed when using the nanoparticles, then this 
should indicate that they do not have a damaging effect on the formation, but rather 
facilitate flow. 
 
Finally, a simple yet interesting application of the DFDR could be testing 
plugs using plain salt water under static (as circulation would probably have no effect) 
overbalanced conditions. By measuring the permeability before and after a 
considerably extensive (maybe 12-24 hours) production simulation period, the 
permeability impairment due to fines migration already present inside the rock could 
be evaluated. Long cores are recommended for such an experiment in order to

6.4 DRDR procedure suggestions 
The actions mentioned below are suggested as an afterthought of this work, in the 
writerвҖҷs assessment, to improve the validity of similar future experiments on the 
DFDR: 
1. There was some minor concern during the experiments that the small hole opening 
in the POM inserts might get plugged by sand grains during the test. Making the 
hole slightly larger would ensure this will not happen while it would not have any 
other effect on the rest of the experiment. 
2. The total length of the three POM inserts could be made slightly shorter as during 
some of the initial experiments the test plug broke and part of it was found 
wedged in the bottom of the flow channel. 
3. The experiments could be run in higher pore and circulation pressures than the 
experiments conducted in this work as it was noticed that the pressures tend to 
fluctuate 0.2-0.3 bars. At larger pressures this fluctuation should be negligible.
