Abstract
Atrial Fibrillation (AF) is a cardiac arrhythmia that affects a significant portion of the pop-
ulation, with 1-2% of the general population experiencing this condition [1]. AF is char-
acterised by an irregular and disorganised depolarisation of the atria, which can have
serious health implications. AF is closely associated with increased mortality, as well as
an elevated risk of stroke, heart failure, and hospitalisation. The prevalence of AF is pro-
jected to increase substantially over the coming decades, which could have significant
implications for public health. One of the main challenges associated with AF is that it
often goes undiagnosed until the condition has progressed significantly. This is due in
part to the fact that AF is typically asymptomatic, meaning that people with this condition
may not realise that they have it until they are hospitalised for another related issue. Con-
sequently, it is important to screen individuals for AF before the condition becomes too
severe. Despite the importance of early detection, however, AF remains difficult to diag-
nose due to its sporadic nature, which can make it challenging to identify using traditional
diagnostic methods.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The World Health Organisation announced cardiovascular diseases Cardiovascular Dis-
eases (CVD) as the global leading cause of death. A large class of CVD comprises of
cardiac arrhythmias [1] and the most prevalent of these is Atrial Fibrillation (AF). AF is a
condition often characterised by irregular and rapid heartbeat. It currently affects around
around 2% of the global population and its pervasiveness is expected to increase rapidly in
the coming years. It is estimated that 6- 12 milion people will be affected by this condition
by 2050, which is mainly due to an ageing global population as AF is strongly correlated
with age.
AF presents symptoms such as dizziness, tiredness, and shortness of breath, which have
a lasting impact of the quality of life of those affected. This is compounded by the asso-
ciation between AF and an increased risk of heart failure, stroke, or premature death. It
is estimated that hospitalisation costs due to untreated AF reach around $26 billion in the
US [2] and hospital admissions for patients with AF have increased by 66% in the last 20
years [3]. For the majority of those affected by AF, however, they are asymptomatic often
for many years before the condition starts to worsen later in life. This makes detection
of the disease incredibly challenging. Early detection is crucial as treating the disease
earlier, before worsening symptoms appear, drastically increases the likelihood of the
disease being managed successfully, and, negates the need for more stringent therapies
such as anticoagulation [4].
Traditional methods for arrhythmia detection are very limited and often fail. The reason
is that beside from being asymptomatic, AF has very sporadic nature. Especially in an
early stage of its development AF consists just of a few beats and it is challenging and
time consuming to visually diagnose it. The major tool for diagnosis of cardiovascular
diseases is electrocardiogram (ECG), due to its availability and cost. There are now many
devices that enable patients to record their ECG signals. In order to detect AF, a long-
term continuous monitoring of ECG signals is necessary. The most common monitoring
method is Holter, which records signals for a period of 24 to 72 hours. The longer the
period of monitoring, the higher the likelihood of the detection of sporadic episodes of
AF. This is a very time consuming and not accurate process as a trained clinician must
visually inspect the results and identify the disease based on signal morphology. A use
of an accurate analytical software to automatically detect the episodes of AF, based on
ECG signals, would be provide a significant increase in efficacy. It should be able to
accurately detect the onset and offset of any AF episodes, and know the exact duration of
the episodes as well as avoid misclassification of non-AF events such as irregular regular
rhythms.
1.1
Current issues
Over the last few years, many algorithms have been developed for accurate and fast
detection of AF, and, whilst there has been success in increasing detection, it is still limited
by two main factors: first, the data utilised is often collected solely in clinical environments,

where AF may not present itself due its sporadic presentation of symptoms; and secondly,
the labelling of data is currently a manual process.
The first limitation, that publicly available ECG datasets are primarily collected in clinically
controlled environments, is challenging because patients are typically sedentary during
the collection of the data. This leads to the recorded ECG signals lacking a fidelity often
associated with more naturally occurring heart-rate pattern. A patient’s physical activity,
posture change, mental stress, and/or food intake are also known to cause morphological
changes in the signals. Most of the existing databases do not provide this important
contextual information hence, many developed algorithms are likely to overfit the data
resulting in a high false positive rates(FPR) [5], which can lead to increased patient anxiety
and requires manual verification of given diagnosis by a cardiologist.
The second limitation, the manual labelling of data, is met when developing some super-
vised ECG analysing algorithms that require preparation of large volumes of high-quality
data. This labelling of this date must be done by a cardiologist, who is expected to exam-
ine every chunk of the signals and add informative labels, so the machine learning model
can learn from it. This is a very time consuming process and is prone to mistakes resulting
in additional expenses.
1.2
Aims and objectives
This thesis proposes to addresses the two above mentioned challenges through the fol-
lowing solutions:
• Using a multisite CACHET-CADB database prepared under free-living conditions
[6]. Beside the ECG signals, this dataset contains contextual information about the
patients that further improves the quality of obtained results. According to Kumar et
al. the presence of AF in a signal can follow distinctive patterns that are discernible
in data. Very often AF signals are clustered in the morning or late evening hours
and are highly associated with external factors such as stress level, physical activity
and food intake. This information combined with a self-supervised algorithm is a
promising step towards a successful automatic AF detection.
• A self supervised deep learning algorithm that can make conclusions just from very
few labels, which, therefore, does not require a large labeled dataset. The process
of learning consists of two steps: in the first step the network learns representations
of the dataset which is being analysed and clustered according to similarities which
appear in the signals; the second step resembles traditional supervised learning,
where the knowledge about representations learnt in the previous step is transferred
to the second network, resulting in learning from fewer annotated examples.

Chapter 2
Biological Background
The heart is a muscular organ located in thorax between the lungs. It is composed of
cardiac muscle cells called myocardium. It is surrounded by a pericardial cavity, which
protects the heart, keeps it in the right position and prevents it from over-expanding when
blood volume increases. It consists of four compartments: the upper chambers: the right
and left atria and the lower chambers: ventricles and; 4 valves:
• 2 Atrioventricular valves: Tricuspid valve and Mitral valve
• 2 Semilunar valves: Pulmonic valve and Aortic valve
Valves are structures that enable blood to travel one-way through different compartments
preventing it from flowing backward. There are two phases of the cardiac cycle:
• Diastole : Deoxygenated blood from the vena cava enters the right atrium through
the antrioventicular valve. Simultaneously, oxygenated blood from the lungs travels
back into the left atrium through the pulmonary veins. When the atriums contract,
the blood is pushed into ventricles the tricuspid and mitral valves into right and left
ventricle, accordingly.
• Systole : Contraction of the ventricles allows deoxygenated blood to be pushed to
the pulmonary artery (from the right ventricle) and oxygenated blood (from the left
ventricle) to the aorta, which distributes blood throughout the body.

2.1
Cardiac action potential
The mechanical activity of the heart is caused by changes in electrical properties of the
cardiac cells. The cell membrane usually maintains a normal action potential, which has
a stable value around –80 mV. Changes of the membrane’s action potential is associated
with changes in concentration of ions such as Na+, Ca2+, K+ inside and outside of cells.
When the threshold potential is reached, the cells start firing, causing an electrical activity.
The changes of action potential that lead to normal heart rhythm consist of five phases.
Phase 0 Rapid depolarisation changes the action potential to a positive value due to inward
currents carried by Na+ or Ca2+ (which makes the cell interior more positive).
Phase 1 Early repolarisation. Some K+ channels open briefly causing an outward flow of K+
which makes the transmembrane potential return to approximately 0 mV.
Phase 2 Shortly after cells start repolarising back, there is a flat plateau phase. It is charac-
terised by a balance between Ca2+ influx and K+ efflux.
Phase 3 repolarisation back to the resting potential value caused by outward currents carried
by K+ (which keep the cell interior negative) and closing the Ca2+ channels.
Phase 4 The Na+/K+ ATPase pump stabilises the transmembrane potential. Cells remain in
this stage until the threshold potential is reached again, causing a rapid depolarisation[6].

2.2
ECG waveform
ECG represents an electrical activity obtained from heart and its controlled by a pace-
maker called the sino-atrial node (SA), located in the right atrium. It is the primary initiator
of the normal heartbeat and together with atrioventricular node (AV) and the His–Purkinje
system is responsible for orchestrating the electrical signal of the normal heart rhythm.
The signal starts with generation of the impulse by the SA, which spreads through the
atria and reaches the AV. The AV conducts slowly causing a delay between atrial and
ventricular systole in order to facilitate the filling of the ventricles by blood before they
contract. In case of SA failure, the AV can serve as an alternative peacemaker. Next,
the impulse propagates through the bundle of His and its left and right branches that give
rise to terminal Purkinje fibres. The His–Purkinje system causes rapid conduction in the

Atrial Fibrillation
Atrial fibrillation is the most common cardiac arrhythmia. It is caused by abnormal electri-
cal activity within the atria of the heart and, because of its irregularity, the blood flow has
a high chance of forming blood clots. They are dangerous as they ultimately can dislodge
and cause a stroke. For this reason, AF is the leading cardiac cause of stroke. There are
multiple risk factors associated with AF: Its prevalence has been associated with multiple
risk factors, such as:
• being male increases the probability of AF by 64%,

2.3.1
Detection of Atrial Firbilation
AF is caused by irregular propagation of electrical activity in the atria. As a result, the P-
waves in the surface of ECG which are result of atrial depolarisation pass on into fibrillatory
waves (f-waves). These further stimulate the atrioventricular (AV) node in inconsistent
way leading to production of irregular ventricular contractions. Detection of the following
three indicators is crucial for the diagnosis of Atrial Fibrillation:
• No distinct P-waves.
• Presence of abnormal atrial activity.
• Highly variable RR intervals.
Figure 2.4: ECG in case of AF
Those conditions can induce atrial remodelling, which alters the both the structural and
electrical properties of the atria and leads to the development of AF, which consists of
three stages:
• Paroxysmal AF: it begins and ends spontaneously and usually lasts from a few sec-
onds up to an entire week. The sporadic nature of AF in this stage suggests that its
prevalence is higher than clinically observed.
• Persistent AF: it lasts longer than a week and requires cardio version to restore
normal rhythm.
• Permanent AF: lasts longer than a year and does not convert to normal sinus rhythm
spontaneously.
Continuous and recurrent AF leads to remodelling- permanent changes in heart. Because
of AF the heart tissue can be damaged.

2.3.2
Mechanisms underlying Atrial Fibrilation
Under normal conditions, the SA node fires the cells regularly, resulting in a normal si-
nus rhythm with a rate of 60 to 100 beats per minute. AF is a condition, when the atria
are not beating synchronically with the ventricles. There are two important mechanisms
underlying AF:
• focal ectopic activity: in which AF originates from an area of focal activity located
very often in pulmonary veins. It is caused by a spontaneous release of Ca2+ in the
cells what leads to delayed after depolarisation (DADs) and early after depolarisation
(EADs).
DADs are the most important mechanism of focal arrhythmias and are triggered dur-
ing the action potential plateau by an inward Ca2+ influx through voltage-dependent
Ca2+ channels. It causes further Ca2+ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum
(SR) via ryanodine receptors (RyR2) and results in intracellular calcium overload
followed by large inward Na+ currents and consequently membrane depolarisation.
EADs occur during phase 2 or 3 when action potential is prolonged disabling the
cell membrane recover from Ca2+ or Na+ inward current and depolarise again.
• Reentry, which is a disorder in signal conducting causing continuous activity of the
heart. Reentry is a disorder in signal conducting causing continuous activity of the
heart. It occurs when an electric signal doesn’t cease in the end of a cycle, but
re-excites and persists in the heart [8].

Related Work
In this chapter, recent advances in AF detection have been reviewed. It summarises
the methods, results and key findings of a few algorithms proposed in the last 14 years.
This chapter consists of sections that differentiate AF detection algorithms into three cat-
egories: traditional methods that are based on feature extraction, such as RR- related,

not depend on feature extraction, such as deep learning methods. The studies evaluated
their work on following publicly available databases:
• MIT - BIH DB (MITDB) : Arrhythmia database It consists of 48 records from 47
subjects, the sampling rate is 250 Hz.
• MIT - BIH AF (AFDB) : AF database It consists of 25 records of human subjects with
mostly paroxymal atrial fibrillation, the sampling rate is 360 Hz
• MIT - BIH NSR (NSRDB): Normal Sinus Rhythm Database It consists of 48 records
from 47 subjects, the sampling rate is 360 Hz
• The Long Term AF database (LTAFDB)
3.1
Traditional statistical methods
Traditional approach towards AF detection comprises of methods that require several
steps and can be very challenging and time consuming. These methods usually rely on an
AF signal’s characteristics, such as absence of a P wave, or irregular RR intervals, which
makes them very susceptible to noise and variation of the signal. Given these challenges,
the right preprocessing is a crucial step for an accurate AF detection. It very often involves
division of the signals into smaller chunks, which are then subjected to analysis. The pre-
vious research consists of three main approaches for AF detection utilising features from
the RR intervals (RRI), atrial activity (AA), which try to detect the location of P-wave, or
both. Most of the studies utilise RRI because the low amplitude of P-wave makes it very
susceptible to noise, which makes the task very challenging, hence the RRI- based meth-
ods are preferred. AF detection based on RR intervals was used by Dash et al. [9],
Huang et al. [10], Zhou et al. [11]. The solution proposed by Dash et al. involves explo-
ration of the three statistical techniques: Root Mean Square of Successive RR Differences
(RMSSD), the Turning Points Ratio(TPR) and Shannon entropy (SE), which qualify differ-
ent characteristics of the signals, such as: variability, randomness of the time series and
complexity, respectively. The presence of AF was considered if the conditions based on
thresholds were satisfied. This approach was successful on an unseen data set, however
it was computationally expensive. There was also a transition detection delay, which was
challenged by a study carried out by Huang et al. This study examined the variability of RR
intervals and their histograms to detect AF using delta RR interval distribution difference
curve (dRDDC). For denoising purposes, only peaks that exceed certain threshold are
further used for classification and are marked as events. These events serve as onsets
for AF episodes. The classification part included histogram analysis, standard deviation

Self supervised learning
Both unsupervised learning and self-supervised learning (SSL) can be considered very
similar approaches, as both learn without labels. However, the features learnt by self su-
pervised models are much stronger as these methods gain deep insights into the statistical
properties of the data. This improvement is achieved by the ability to learn, exploit and
compare different data representations. SSL is widely used in computer vision, natural
language processing tasks and, it has shown to also be adept in signal analysis[citation].
The general idea of SSL consists of the following steps:
1. Pretext task: This step includes data augmentation and a pre-training using this
data. Data augmentation allows the modification of elements X of the raw dataset
into X’ and generate pseudo-labels Y’, which correspond to an applied transforma-
tion. As a result, pairs (X’,Y’) can be fed into the model, which then tries to minimise
the loss by learning what modification has been utilised for a particular observation.
2. Downstream Task: Knowledge about the data exploited in previous step is trans-
ferred to the second network, which then significantly improves the understanding
of modality of the dataset. This model does not require hugely annotated samples,
because of the previous step. It can generalise well using the original dataset with
few labels.
The emphasis here is on the quality of downstream task, while the pretext task is only
serving as a process of learning representation of the data. Figure 4.6 presents the work-

4.4
Preprocessing
The ECG signal amplitude is very small and weak (it ranges between 10 µV to 4 mV) and
therefore it is very prone to various disturbances. Removing the noises is very important
to improve the quality of the ECG signal, as a clear signal simplifies the diagnose cardio-
vascular problems. This section gives on overview over steps taken in order to improve
the quality of analysed ECG signals.
• Power line interference (PLI) Is the noise caused by the electromagnetic field dis-
tributed through the power supply. It can lead to P-wave distortion, causing the
wrong diagnosis of arrhythmias like AF. It appears at frequencies of 50/60 Hz. [4].
• Baseline wander it happens due to breathing, body movements, and fluctuations
in the electrode–skin interface. It is a low-frequency component with the range of
0-0.8 Hz. It can extend up to 1 Hz during stress tests [25].
• Electromyographic (EMG) interference it is a muscle disturbance that occurs by
muscle contractions, when the person moves, or is shivering. The main concern
with these interferences is caused by the spectral overlap between the ECG and
the muscle noise [25]
• Respiratory noise The chest electrodes shift in position during patient’s breathing.
This causes the change of a mutual position between the heart and the electrodes.
It results in varying amplitudes of the QRS-complexes. [25]
4.4.1
Comparison of ECG Denoising Techniques
The most common techniques for ECG noise removal are:
• Filtering
Filtering is a common technique used to remove noise from ECG signals. It involves
applying digital filters, such as low-pass filters or high-pass filters, that remove noise
at specific frequencies. They are simple and easy to implement and can remove
noise at specific frequencies however, they can also remove useful information from
the signal along with the noise. The choice of filter parameters may affect the quality
of the ECG signal [26].
26
Detecting AF without labels: self-supervised approach
==PAGE:35==
• Wavelet transform
The wavelet transform is a signal processing technique that decomposes the signal
into different frequency bands. ECG signals can be decomposed into sub-bands us-
ing wavelet transform, and the noise can be removed from the sub-bands with the
highest noise levels. This method can remove noise at specific frequency bands
preserving important features of ECG signals. The disadvantage is that it may in-
troduce artifacts in the denoised signal Requires careful selection of wavelet and
decomposition levels [27]
• Independent Component Analysis (ICA)
ICA is a technique that separates a multivariate signal into independent non-Gaussian
signals. ECG signals can be decomposed into different independent components,
and noise can be identified and removed from the ECG signal using ICA. ICA can
remove various types of noise and can separate ECG signal from artifacts and inter-
ference. Unfortunately this method requires careful selection of independent com-
ponents to remove noise effectively and is computationally expensive [28]
• Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
SVD is a technique that decomposes a matrix into singular values and vectors. ECG
signals can be decomposed using SVD, and the noise can be removed by eliminat-
ing the singular values associated with noise. SVD can provide a low-rank approx-
imation of the ECG signal, which can help to reduce noise and enhance the signal-
to-noise ratio. Additonally, it can be used in combination with other techniques to
improve the denoising performance. SVD can be computationally expensive, espe-
cially for large datasets. It assumes that the noise and the signal are uncorrelated,
which may not be the case for some types of noise. It can also remove some useful
features of the signal, leading to a loss of information. [29]

sures that the output probabilities sum up to 1, enabling them to be interpreted as class
probabilities. Each output probability represents the likelihood or confidence of the input
belonging to the corresponding class. The softmax function is used in conjunction with
a cross-entropy loss, which measures the dissimilarity between the predicted probabil-
ity distribution and the true class labels across all classes. In order to mitigate potential
overfitting, the model incorporates dropout. Additionally, batch normalization layers are
included after each convolutional layer, subject to the presence of the batch-norm argu-
ment. The trainability of the layer weights can be adjusted using the trainable argument.
For the pretext task, all weights are set to be trainable. To identify the most effective
parameter settings, various parameter values have been explored and evaluated in the
experimentation process using the tensorflow api.

F1 Score
The F1 score is defined as the harmonic mean of precision and recall. The F1 score can
be interpreted as a weighted average of these two where the weight is balanced between
the two metrics. The F1 score ranges between 0 and 1, where a score of 1 represents
a perfect model, and a score of 0 represents a model that has no accuracy. In order
to improve the model, either precision or recall can be improved. The precision-recall
tradeoff is an important concept in machine learning and data analysis. It means that in
many cases, increasing precision comes at the cost of reducing recall and vice versa.
Ideally, we would like to have high precision and high recall, but very often cases, this
is not possible. In a binary classification problem, a model which always returns positive
predictions will have a high recall, but a low precision. On the other hand, a model that
only returns positive predictions for very confident instances will have a high precision,
but a low recall. The tradeoff between precision and recall is caused because of the
difficulty of finding the right balance between false positives and false negatives. In some
applications, a high recall is preferred (for example, in medical diagnoses where missing
a positive case can be catastrophic), while in others a high precision is preferred (for
example, in spam filtering where false positives can be annoying). To balance precision
and recall, a common approach is to use the F1 score.

5.3
Discussion
The obtained results for the pretext task exhibit variations based on the specific method
employed. Notably, when considering self-supervised learning with ECG signals in con-
junction with accelerometer data, it has been observed that this approach does not lead
to good outcomes. The underlying reason behind this lies in the intrinsic characteristics
of accelerometer signals, characterized by a highly random and unstructured morphol-
ogy, similarly to noise. The presence of noise in the data causes significant challenges
and reduces the effectiveness of self-supervised learning methods too. Noise introduces
random fluctuations that obscure the underlying patterns or structure in the data. Self-
supervised learning heavily relies on extracting meaningful representations by capturing
consistent patterns or relationships within the input. However, when the dataset is noisy,
these patterns become distorted or obscured, making it difficult for the self-supervised
learning algorithm to extract reliable and informative feature. Moreover, noise can in-
troduce misleading signals, which can misguide the self-supervised learning process.
This can lead to the learning algorithm capturing noise-related patterns rather than the
true underlying structures, resulting in inaccurate representations. The efficacy of self-
supervised learning heavily relies on the presence of discernible patterns, temporal de-
pendencies, or meaningful relationships within the input data. However, accelerometer
signals, as well as noisy data inherently lack consistent patterns or dependencies that can
be effectively leveraged to learn meaningful representations.
Using only one channel data, that consists of ECG signals provides much better results.
The model is capable of learning and recognising scaled and negated signals very well,
while it has slighlly worse performance in recognising the original signal and noised sig-
nal. This could be improved by changing the amount of noise added to simplify the task.
Unfortunately, despite having the model distingiushing between four different signals, the
performance of the downstream task is not good. The model failed to acquire the ca-
pability to accurately identify episodes associated with atrial fibrillation (AF) and instead
resorted to guessing one class only. The ROC curve is straight with the AUC score of
0.5 which charactrises random guesses in binary classification. These poor results could
be due to the fact, that the training set consisted of 2118 samples only. Even for self-

Chapter 6
Conclusions
In addition to the improvement of accuracy in detection, self-supervised learning has the
potential to reduce the effort required for healthcare professionals. Healthcare systems
are, for the most part, over capacity and any automation and prediction of diagnosis could
have a significant impact. In areas with aging populations, this could have an even greater
impact as these patients often require more time-intensive examinations such as imaging
or ECGs, which adds to the overload of doctors and nurses. Employing medical profes-
sionals, therefore, to label large data sets is counter-productive and constitutes a chal-
lenge for the development of successful ML systems. It is obvious that the development
of solutions that require little (or even zero) involvement from medical professionals is
greatly preferred. Self-supervised models create the potential to overcome this challenge
and lead to more effective diagnoses of predictable conditions. It is supposed, that the
adoption of ML-driven techniques will be increased by the use of self-supervised models.
It is also likely that, due to the reduction in the time required to label these data sets,
the uptake will be more globally proportionate compared to other ML techniques. This
is because other ML techniques will require more time from clinicians, which is a luxury
that only more economically developed nations can afford. The increasing prevalence
of AF is a very important issue, which should not be ignored. The traditional methods in
detecting arrythmia very often fail, highlighting the pressing need to explore and develop
novel methodologies for its detection. Therefore adoption of slef-supervised learning tech-
niques can be very profitable and bring a novel solutions to mitigate the risks of worsening
condition and deaths for people with AF.
The main limitation of self-supervised learning is that it requires more computational power
as they often require more training iterations. Self-supervised learning also involves ad-
ditional, complicated processing steps in comparison to supervised models, such as data
augmentation. Adding in the complexity of healthcare signals, makes this a far from sim-
ple task to generate the models required because of the different modalities, baselines
and metrics involved. There is clear potential from models that learn from unstructured
data and there has already been results in the field of medicine with this approach. The
challenge is to identify which signals are best suited to generalising across different tasks,
whilst remaining efficient in labelling. Self-supervised learning would seem to the be the
most likely solution for dealing with the primary challenge associated with ML adoption in
the healthcare setting and more should be done.
Through a series of experiments, I demonstrate that the presence of noise in training data
hinders the model’s ability to learn accurate transformations, as the inherently random
morphology of noise misguides the learning process. Furthermore, the findings indicate
that incorporating accelerometer data into the model does not contribute to any improve-
ment. This can be attributed to the absence of regular patterns in accelerometer data,
which exhibits an irregular structure. The outcomes of this research emphasize the crit-
icality of utilizing clean data and underscore the limitations of noise-contaminated and
accelerometer data in self-supervised pretext task.

6.1
Limitations
There were several important limitations and challenges encountered during the study.
First of all, the performance of self-supervised learning approaches is highly sensitive to
the choice of pretext tasks and hyperparameters. Suboptimal selection of these factors
can lead to poor results or ineffective representations. Exploration and fine-tuning various
pretext tasks and hyperparameters can be time-consuming and resource-intensive. Due
to time constraints and limited computational power it was not possible to complete these
tasks in a explorative manner. The time and computational constraints encountered in this
study resulted in a more focused approach, prioritizing only specific range of transforma-
tions and hyperparameters. The achievement of this project was highly depending upon
the extent of computational power accessible. The utilization of self-supervised learning
necessitates a greater allocation of computational resources compared to conventional
deep learning methodologies, owing to the involvement of two distinct learning settings
and the execution of signal transformations within the dataset. Within the context of this
project, three distinct transformations were applied to each individual signal, thus signifi-
cantly augmenting the dataset size. Anothet limiting factor in a succesful implementation
fo self-supervised algorithm is interpretability of learned features. Self-supervised learn-
ing methods often learn complex representations that may be challenging to interpret and
understand. Unlike supervised learning, where labels provide explicit semantic meaning,
the learned representations in self-supervised learning may lack direct interpretability,
making it harder to gain insights into the learned features.
6.2
Future Research
The section reflects on the areas that could be improved and further investigated in the
future research of self-supervised learning for AF detection. First of all, a promising di-
rection for exploration involves testing of diverse transformations. The current study has
been limited to three transformations, but there are many other possibilities to consider,
such as for example: time-warping, permutations, which have been also implemented
for the purpose of this work. Unfortunately, due to limited computational resources, they
could not be tested. By studying the impact of different transformations on the learned
representations, it is possible can gain better insights into the types of transformations
that are most beneficial for specific domains or applications. Additionally, the choice of
parameters for the different transformations should be investigated, as this project only
implies one set of parameters.
Secondly, it could be beneficial to increase the sampling frequency of the ECG data. Ini-
tially, these signals were downsapled to 64 Hz in order to align them with MAcc signals,
which were available at this frequency. As this research has proven the unprofitableness
of MAcc in self-supervised learning, reducing the frequency to 64Hz is no longer neces-
sary. Too low frequency can significantly impact the results, hence experiments with a
better quality ECG signals could bring better results.
Future research can also focus on investigating different self-supervised learning options
