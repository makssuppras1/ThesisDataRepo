Abstract
Computer ﬂuid dynamics(CFD) has become a valuable tool in developing new reactor designs
and understanding the ﬂuid behaviour in current designs. Combined with kinetic models, it has
become possible to investigate the combined eﬀect of ﬂuid proﬁles and kinetic reaction mecha-
nisms.
The aim of this thesis is to create a CFD model of a Chr.
Hansen pilot scale lactic acid
bacteria (LAB) fermentation reactor that is able to predict the ﬂuid behaviour, the pH gradi-
ents during a fermentation and their combined eﬀect on the fermentation process. To do this,
a 3-D model of the pilot scale reactor was created in SolidWorks, meshed in ANSYS ICEM and
modelled in ANSYS CFX. It was found that the ﬂuid proﬁle of the model is transient in nature,
and could not accurately be predicted using a ﬁxed velocity proﬁle. The model was validated
against experimental mixing data using NaOH and H3PO4 pulses at two impeller speeds; 132
and 242 rpm. The pH was measured at six points in the reactor, which were replicated in the
CFD model. The transient 242 rpm CFD model was found to ﬁt the experimental data of pulses
with NaOH well, having a 46 s mixing time to the experimental 47 s. This was not the case for
the 132 rpm data set, where the CFD mixing time was 83 s, and the NaOH pulse was 54 s. An
error in the experimental procedure was suspected to be the cause of this discrepancy, but with
only a single data set available to compare with, certainty on the matter could not be achieved.
To simulate the LAB fermentation, a kinetic model was developed by Robert Spann, which
was combined with a pH correlation that linked the concentrations of the lactic acid and the
NH3 that was used for pH control. It was found necessary to reduce the computational time by
implementing a steady state ﬂuid proﬁle that resembled the transient mixing time. With this
simpliﬁcation, it was found that the CFD reactor model was almost able to accurately simulate
the kinetic model with 1 s time steps and an RMS of 1e-5. Testing on a simpler reactor revealed
that the issue was an accumulation of errors, which could be solved with lower time steps or
RMS values. The kinetic model was furthermore found to have a 20 min too large lag time
coeﬃcient, but was otherwise considered to accurately predict the kinetic behaviour. Similarly,
the pH correlation was found to predict the pH trends well, but it also needed to be ﬁtted to
the experimental data set’s initial pH to be fully accurate.
Overall, the CFD model has shown promise in understanding and testing LAB fermentations
and the pH gradients in LAB reactors. The high computational requirements were considered
an issue that required further work for eﬀective usage of the model. A possible solution to
this was proposed for future work, but could not be implemented within the time frame of this
thesis.

Introduction
Computer Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has in recent years gained more interest as a possible solution
to improve the design of not only the ﬂow proﬁles used in the aviation industry, but also the
reactor design of the chemical and biochemical industry. The ability to predict dead-spaces,
concentration gradients and reaction rates throughout a reactor is of particular interest to the
latter.
Because the ﬁrst implementation of the software that is used model concentration and rate
gradients in CFD happened in the early 2000’s [1], the technology is still young. This can lead
to unexpected computational issues when ﬂuid dynamics and reaction kinetics are combined.
Furthermore, implementing the reaction kinetics often require large computational resources
when dealing with fast reactions that are occurring over long periods of time or in large volumes.
This project aims to predict pH gradients, which occur in lactic acid bacteria (LAB) fermen-
tations, in a pilot scale bioreactor of Chr. Hansen. To this end, a kinetic CFD model of the
reactor was created. The ﬂuid proﬁle will be investigated and compared with experimental mix-
ing data, before an LAB kinetic model and a pH correlation will be implemented and compared
with experimental fermentation data.
This section will provide an introduction to the subjects that are covered in this project. The
covered subjects include a brief overview of the relevant CFX functions and how the turbulence
and kinetic models operate (Section 1.1), an overview of LAB fermentation principles (Section
1.2) and the methods and models used to calculate and predict mixing times (Section 1.3).

1.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics
This section explains the process of setting up ﬂuid dynamic investigations in ANSYS CFX 17.1
and some of the fundamental models that are involved in solving the ﬂuid dynamics.
1.1.1
Setting up simulations in CFX
To set up a simulation in CFX, there are ﬁve main steps that must be completed. Firstly, a
3-D model of the volume that one wishes to investigate is crafted. A mesh is then created on
the framework of this model, which is then exported to CFX. In CFX, the imported mesh’s
boundary conditions, models and simulation parameters are set up in CFX-Pre, before being
formatted to a Solver deﬁnition ﬁle. The CFX-Solver solves the deﬁned simulation and generates
a result ﬁle that can be investigated in CFX-Post. These steps will each be further explained
below.
1.1.1.1
Sketching & meshing
When sketching the desired geometry as a 3-D model, it is the non-solid parts that are given
volume. This is because the mesh requires a bounded volume to be crafted. Solid parts are only
given a volume when the heat transfer across the solid is of interest. SolidWorks is a commonly
used tool to create such 3-D models, but simple geometrical models can also be constructed in
ANSYS ICEM. In the next step, the volume is divided into a mesh of small subvolumes that
are used in the simulation process.
The mesh consists of two parts; nodes and elements. Elements are 3-dimensional blocks that
ﬁll out the inner body of the geometry. Nodes connect the corners of neighbouring elements
and are used in the simulation as the geometric reference points. Meshes are categorized as
either unstructured or structured meshes. Unstructured meshes are generated automatically
by ICEM using either tetrahedral or hexahedral-dominated elements. Structured meshes are
crafted by the user using only heaxahedral elements. The main advantage of using an unstruc-
tured mesh is the quick and easy mesh creation, but the use of tetrahedral elements often leads
to a jagged simulation result. In contrast, manually crafting a structured mesh requires more
time and eﬀort, but the process also allows the user far more freedom to ﬁt the mesh to the
needs of the simulation. The ability to ﬁt the mesh, and the use of hexahedral elements, often
leads to a lower computational time and required node density for the structured mesh than the
unstructured mesh. Figure 1.1 displays the diﬀerence between a structured and unstructured
mesh.
1.1.1.2
Preparing the simulation
With a generated mesh, the second step is to set up the simulation. Therefore, the mesh is
imported into the program ANSYS CFX-Pre. This software is used to deﬁne the simulation in
detail, e.i. what models are used, what the boundary conditions are, how it should be solved
and what should be recorded in the results. Upon completion, a deﬁnition ﬁle will be generated
that is used to solve the deﬁned problem.
The models that are used in the simulation contains sets of partial diﬀerential equations (PDE)
each model diﬀerent phenomena. Restricting the selected models to the phenomena of relevance
to the simulation is paramount to both increase accuracy and decrease computational time. The
turbulence and laminar models are of particular interest when modelling ﬂuid behaviour. The
mechanics behind them and when one should be used over others are presented in Section 1.1.2.
Boundary conditions are set for each surface of the mesh to describe what happens at the
surface. Common boundary conditions include the inlet, outlet, opening, wall, symmetry and

periodicity. Inlets, outlets and openings allow material to enter and leave the system, and the
conditions outside the system must therefore be deﬁned for each boundary condition. Walls
describe the ﬂuids interaction with an impermeable wall, i.e. the wall slip. No slip walls allow
no ﬂuid movement at the wall, and free slip walls allow unhindered ﬂuid movement at the wall.
Symmetry and periodicity are both used to decrease the computational time by limiting the
simulation volume to a fraction of the whole geometry. Symmetry can only be used when the
boundary plane mirrors both sides. Rotational periodicity allows for circular movement from
one boundary to the other, and can only be used in cases of circular periodicity.
Transient simulations are used when the interest is on what happens over time, and steady
state (SS) simulations are used when the interest is on what happens at t∞. For both, an
appropriate time step is chosen to reﬂect the changes in the simulation. The transient time step
provides the points in time that the PDE’s are solved for. The SS time step is a pseudo time
step that allows the solver to compare the conditions before and after the time step. The Root
Mean Square (RMS) of the residuals is used for to compare the diﬀerence between simulation
iterations for both the SS time steps and for the calculation of each time step in a transient
simulation. The highest allowed RMS value is a solving parameter that is set to reﬂect the
allowed margin of error. A maximum number of iterations is set to control the computational
time that the solver spends attempting to ﬁnd a solution.
As a ﬁnal step before the simulation is started, it is deﬁned what information is relevant to
save during the simulation in order to decrease to storage demand. Because of this, the data of
simulation result ﬁles can be decreased, transient result ﬁles can be limited in both frequency
and scope, and monitoring points can be set up in the simulation to monitor speciﬁc parameters
at speciﬁc locations throughout the simulation’s run. Monitor points are of particular interest
due to their ability to compare directly with experimental data recorded at the same locations,
as well as provide the user with an overview of what has occurred so far in the simulation run.

1.1.1.3
Solving the simulation
With the deﬁnition ﬁle, which contain the simulation set-up, the simulation is then solved in
CFX-Solver. A ﬁle with initial conditions can be inserted to provide a starting point, and the
problem can be partitioned to allow for a greater usage of multiple CPU cores.
Once initialized, the monitoring points, RMS values and simulation progress can be monitored.
If necessary, the simulation can be stopped at any given point. Backup ﬁles and adjustments
can also be made during the run. At the end of the simulations, a result ﬁle is created with the
speciﬁed result data.
For this project, all heavy simulations were done in a HPC cluster on DTU’s Gbar network.
1.1.1.4
Post Processing
Once the simulation has converged in the Solver, the result ﬁle can be measured and visualized
in CFD-Post. This includes measuring any recorded simulation values at any given point or
line, or introducing one or more planes to view the visual proﬁle of a parameter. For steady
state results, it is often easier to get an overview of the entire system in Post than through the
monitoring points inserted in the Solver.
1.1.2
Transport processes
The motion of liquids and gasses has been studied extensively throughout history in the ﬁeld
known as transport processes. Understanding these motion dynamics are important in order to
improve the eﬃciency of reactor and/or aviation designs. Due to their similar motion dynamics,
gasses and liquids have often been modelled collectively under the ﬂuid name.
A number of distinctions have been made between diﬀerent ﬂuids.
The most important of
these distinctions is the diﬀerence in viscosity, where ﬂuids are divided into two groups; New-
tonian and non-Newtonian ﬂuids. The Newtonian ﬂuids have a constant viscosity regardless of

1.1.3
Kinetic modelling in CFD
The ability to combine the ﬂuid dynamics of a CFD simulation with reaction kinetics allows for
concentration gradients to be observed in reactors. This is of great interest to anyone wishing to
improve upon reactor design. In CFD, the kinetic and mass conservation equations are separate
but connected to the momentum and mass conservation equations that are used to calculate
the ﬂuid ﬂow. [9]
This separation allows for a reduction in computational time and the possibility of reﬁning
the mesh in diﬀerent areas than would be relevant for the ﬂuid dynamics. If a stable steady
state velocity proﬁle can be reached, it furthermore allows for the complete absence of the ﬂuid
dynamic models during the kinetic simulation, thereby signiﬁcantly reducing the computational
requirements. [9]
There are two primary ways of including species in CFX; Additional variables and Materi-
als.
Additional variables works by inserting the specie atop the ﬂuid material.
As such, a
liquid’s incompressibility in a closed space does not need to be upheld. For a one-phase liquid
system with no openings, this allows the user to introduce matter into the system without
exerting immense pressure on it. Similarly, matter can be removed from the system without
further considerations needed. When using Materials, the user speciﬁes the mass fraction com-
position of the ﬂuid, which means that the mass balance must always be upheld. Despite this,
the Material approach does have the advantages of allowing the ﬂuid properties to change over
time and diﬀer across the mesh. It also allows the user to take advantage of the in-built Reac-
tions mechanic to model kinetic reactions. The Reactions mechanic functions by deﬁning the
reactants and products of any reaction from the Materials list, along with their stoichiometric
coeﬃcients and reaction order. The reaction rate is then deﬁned using either the Arrhenius
equation template or a user-deﬁned expression for both the forward and backwards reaction
rate. If a reaction does not conserve matter, a warning to the user will be issued. In contrast,
the Additional variables approach uses a subdomain to allow kinetics to occur as a source of
that speciﬁc specie, independent of what happens to other specie. The subdomain approach
has the advantage that it is more ﬂexible, easy to use and allows for the reaction to only take
place in certain domains, while the Materials approach often performs better for stiﬀsystems
and allows for the CFX beta stiﬀsolver function to work.
As with the ﬂuid dynamics, it is important to consider the time step in concert with the
reaction kinetics. A too large time step will distort the results, but a too small time step will
increase the computational time considerably. For an example of this, Eq. (1.25) represents
a simple reaction, and Figure 1.3 shows the results gained from utilizing diﬀerent time steps.

1.2
Lactic acid bacteria fermentations
For millennia, humanity has utilized the fermentation process to produce food and drinking
products such as alcohol, cheese and yoghurt. This is done by cultivating cells in a medium to
produce the desired product and increase the cell concentration.[15]
In this section, the principles involved in modern fermentation reactors, and the biological
process producing lactic acid from lactose, will be presented.
1.2.1
Fermentation processes
In fermentation, there are three primary operating modes available; batch, fed-batch and con-
tinuous. The batch reactor is a quasi-closed system. It is regulated by time and temperature,
with no broth medium entering or leaving the system. However, in the fermentation perspective,
a pH-controlled fermentation or an aerated system is also considered as a batch fermentation.
A continuous fermentation usually resembles a continually stirred tank reactor (CSTR) reac-
tor, where feed and products continuously ﬂow through the fermentation tank. Fed-batch lies
between these two, and it allows a feed stream to enter the system, but not products to ﬂow
out. The three approaches are illustrated in Figure 1.4.[11]
Figure 1.4: Types of fermentation reactors.
Common for nearly all fermentation reactors is that they are continuously stirred to achieve
homogeneity. This is done both to suppress local concentration and temperature pockets that
might lead the cells to perform undesirably, and to allow for a quicker corrective action of the
entire fermentation broth than would have been possible with pure diﬀusion.

1.2.2
Lactic acid bacteria
The lactic acid bacteria (LAB) has been used for centuries to ferment dairy products by con-
suming lactose. Due to their extensive use in history, the 12 genera of LAB commonly used
in food production tend to be GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe). The bacteria tend to
prefer more acidic environment and are mostly mesophiles. LAB are further categorized as
being either homofermentative or heterofermentative. Homefermentative LAB has around 95%
lactic acid end product, whereas heterofermentative also produce acetic acid, ethanol and CO2
as end products.[15]
The L. bulgaricus and S. thermophilus bacteria primarily produce lactic acid from lactose,
making them homofermentative, and they are used in the production of yoghurt. The lactose is
ﬁrst converted to glucose and galactose using the β-galactosidase enzyme. They are then con-
verted to pyruvate and ATP through the glycotic pathway, with pyruvate later being converted
to lactic acid.[16]
The optimal temperature ranges for certain strains of S. themophilus and L. bulgaricus are
35-46◦C and 42-50◦C respectively, while the optimum pH is around 5.6. However, strains can
vary from this [17]. As shown by Rault et al [18], a high pH value can lead the lactic acid dis-
sociation to favour lactate, which can degrade the LAB’s membrane integrity and thereby the
bacteria’s viability. Furthermore, Cook and Russell [19] showed that a high acidity led certain
LAB to stop growing entirely.

Determining the time required for a container of multiple specie to reach homogeneity is of
utmost importance to dynamic systems, such as fermentations in bioreactors. Fermentation
tanks require homogeneity of substrates, oxygen, pH, etc. to avoid undesired growth or product
decrease. As noted in Section 1.2.2, a signiﬁcantly acidic region could limit LAB growth, while
a signiﬁcantly basic region could lead to increased cell degradation. Cortes et al [20] further-
more showed that E. coli expressed diﬀerent gene responses when exposed to pH gradients in
an STR than when exposed to a constant pH. In cases of large substrate gradients, feast or
famine situations would occur, which has been known to cause metabolic changes in cells [21].

between diﬀerent reactor designs and impeller speeds.

2.2
Mesh size study
With the base meshes ready, the minimum size of the elements necessary to portray the system
had to be found. This was done to ease the computational requirements. To accomplish this, the
elements’ relative sizes were uniformly altered until the simulated velocity and mixing proﬁles
no longer diﬀered signiﬁcantly from a mesh with a much higher number of nodes. For the steady
state velocity proﬁles, the impeller meshes were tested separately from the reactor meshes in
Section 2.2.1 to avoid checking every combination of the impeller and reactor meshes. The mesh
size study for the reactor part was then performed in Section 2.2.2 with the chosen impeller
mesh.
2.2.1
Impeller
In the mesh size study of the impeller domain, the impeller domain was given openings at the
top and bottom and Free Slip Walls at the round sides. The Free Slip Wall at the sides was done
because it was found that simply having openings at the sides generated too many numerical
errors in the ﬁnest mesh. For the combined reactor mesh, these would all become interfaces.
Tests with the ﬁnest mesh in a simple tank showed that these numerical errors did not appear
if there was a movable liquid domain outside the interface. The setup conditions can be found
in Table 2.2. An RMS of 1e-5 was chosen to allow the simulation to continue running until the
convergence of 3 monitor points that were measuring the Velocity in Stn Frame. This would
signal steady state in conjunction with a RMS value near or below 1e-4.

In this section, the CFD model that was derived in Section 2, will be compared to experimental
mixing data at diﬀerent impeller speeds. The purpose of this comparison was to validate the
CFD model’s ﬂuid dynamics before it was used for the fermentation simulations of Section 4.
3.1
Experimental set-up
The mixing experiments were carried out by Robert Spann and Peter Nielsen on a pilot reactor
belonging to Chr. Hansen that had the same geometry as the one seen in Figure 2.1. Tap water
was used as a medium in the pilot reactor, where two impeller speed settings were tested in
the reactor. These impeller speed settings were 200 and 100 rpm. These settings were later
found to not be directly measuring the rpm of the impeller shaft. Instead, a calibration of

two ﬁxed vortexes of equivalent velocities. Similarly, when the low tracer concentration moved
up to the impeller above it, it received a richer tracer inlet stream than it would have, if the
tracer had had to travel halfway through the space between the impellers.

pH controlled fermentation simulation using CFD
With the ﬂuid dynamics validated in Section 3, the ﬂuid dynamic model could then be used
to model the fermentation process of a lactic acid bacteria fermentation in a pilot scale reactor
with the aim to predict the pH gradients during the fermentation. The kinetics of the LAB
are presented in Section 4.2. With the kinetics being dependent on the pH, a model for the
pH was needed. This model is presented in Section 4.1. The LAB kinetics and pH model were
combined with the CFD model in Section 4.3, with the results being shown in Section 4.4.

The CFD ﬂuid dynamics were set up similar to the tracer set-up in Section 2.2.2. The specie and
kinetics from Section 4.2 were implemented as Additional Variables with subdomain sources.
The maximum allowed RMS was lowered to 1e-5 for increased accuracy. The pH was calculated
using Eq. (4.5) of Section 4.1, and the NH3 was added using a modiﬁed P-controller, shown in
Eq. (4.15). The modiﬁcation was added to combine a step response controller with a normal
P-controller to avoid the P-controller’s tendency of converging on a stable error below the pH
set point.

bottom, and just outside the shaft, as shown in Figure C3. MP 3 of Figure 2.8 was used as
the pH control point. MNH3 is the molecular weight of NH3, Vtank is the volume of the tank
and kpH is the proportional gain. kpH was calibrated to be kpH=1.0 mol/L/hr by using a small
cubical system, shown in Figure F5 of Appendix F. In accordance with the experimental set-up,

Using the set-up that was described in Section 4.3, two full 6 hr simulations were simulated
with 300 rpm ﬁxed velocity proﬁles and a time step of 1 s and 5 s. The 5 s time step was found

CFD model was attributed to the Matlab model having a ﬁxed pH of 6 after it reached pH 6.
In comparison, the CFD model had pH gradients, which may have aﬀected the growth rate.

at this point. This indicated that a smaller time step or a smaller RMS value should have been
used in the CFD simulation.

pre-NH3 section. Furthermore, by studying Figure 4.5-4.7, it was clear that the Matlab kinetic
model was closely mirroring the experimental data for most of the run. If the Matlab model was
shifted by about 20 min, the model would have been matching quite well for most of the run.

Discussion
In this project, a CFD model of a lactic acid bacteria (LAB) fermentation tank was ﬁrst con-
structed and then validated with mixing experiments. Finally, it was coupled with a kinetic
model and a pH correlation that was based on the lactic acid and NH3 concentrations. The
CFD kinetic simulation was subsequently compared against experimental fermentation data.
The results of this model, their implications and possible solutions to encountered problems
will be presented below.
From Section 2.2.2, it was found that it was not possible to accurately model the ﬂuid dy-
namics of the pilot scale reactor with a ﬁxed steady state velocity proﬁle. Furthermore, it was
found that the reactor’s node density mattered less in the transient simulations than it did in
the ﬁxed steady state case with another impeller geometry, as seen in Appendix D Figure D7.
A possible reason for this could be that since the simulation is transient, minor inaccuracies
are self-correcting over time. In a transient simulation, a momentary minor error will have
been changed by the greater ﬂuid dynamic forces in the next time step. This self-correcting
nature could be used to limit the transient simulation’s increase in computational requirements
by signiﬁcantly decreasing the node density.
When comparing the experimental H3PO4 pulses, it became evident that the mixing time
proﬁles were inﬂuenced by other factors than the ﬂuid dynamics. During the initial 242 rpm
experiments, the H3PO4 mixing times continued to rise as more H3PO4 pulses were added.
This continued until the 132 rpm experiments, where the H3PO4 mixing proﬁles had stabilized.
In total, the H3PO4 pulses had a 25 s spread between the mixing times at 242 rpm. A likely
reason for this behaviour could be that H3PO4 undergoes a series of slow dissociation reactions
in the water. That the 132 rpm H3PO4 pulses were stable could be attributed to the solution
becoming saturated. This would mirror how the CO2-carbonate buﬀer was found in Appendix
A to only be a concern when the pH was between 6 and 10, or the mixing time was close to the
time for the buﬀer system to reach steady state.
Comparing the experimental NaOH pulse data sets with the CFD simulations showed that
while the 242 rpm CFD mixing proﬁle matched the experimental mixing proﬁle quite well, the
same could not be said at 132 rpm. When comparing the 242 and 132 rpm mixing times, the
CFD simulation’s mixing time rose from 46 to 83 s, while the NaOH pulses’ average mixing time
only rose from 47 to 54 s. By using the theoretical mixing model of Paul et al [22] (Eq. (1.33)),
it was possibly to predict the CFD mixing time of one impeller speed within a 2% margin of
error by using the mixing time at the other impeller speed to ﬁnd the power number Po. This
indicated that the 132 rpm experiments may not have been performed at 132 rpm. Due to the
exact impeller rpm only being measured months after the mixing experiments were performed,
a faulty rpm setting is not an impossibility.
Moving on to the LAB kinetic model, it was found that using a transient ﬂuid proﬁle in the
simulation was highly impractical due to the severe computational requirements. Computing
just 2.5 min out of a 6 hr simulation took around 300 CPU hours to compute. Due to this, it
was decided to use a steady state velocity proﬁle with a similar mixing time as a transient sim-
ulation would have had. This choice introduced a potential source of errors when studying the
eﬀects of the ﬂuid dynamics on the fermentation reaction. The estimated degree of error, which
originated from the steady state simpliﬁcation was however dwarfed by the degree of error that
originated from the computational error accumulation throughout the simulation. In a small
cubical CFD test reactor, it had been possible to recreate the one-compartment Matlab LAB
kinetic model with a 5 s time step and a maximum allowed RMS of 1e-5, but when transferring
50
==PAGE:60==
the system to the pilot scale CFD model, it was found that even a 1 s time step was too high to
achieve the same level of accuracy. This accumulation of errors led to the concentration proﬁles
of the pilot scale CFD model lagging about 10 min behind the Matlab model. A lower time step
or RMS target value would likely have solved this issue. However, given that the 1 s pilot scale
simulation used 1784 CPU hours, the computational requirements would quickly become too
great to manage. Even with the 10 min lag, the CFD simulation results were still considered
valuable due to the trends of the concentration proﬁles remaining the same as the Matlab model.
When the Matlab and CFD models were compared with the experimental results, the issue
of lag time resurfaced again. By reviewing the concentration proﬁles of Figure 4.5-4.7, it was
found that the Matlab model’s concentration proﬁles had the same trend lines as the exper-
imental data, but lagging behind. The average time lag between the experimental data and
Matlab model was estimated to be around 20 min. Due to this observation, a simple readjust-
ment of tlag would likely allow the Matlab model to match the experimental fermentation data.
Appendix G showed this assumption to be true.
Similar to the concentration curves, the pH correlation (Eq. (4.5)) was found in Figure 4.8
to match the general trend of the experimental pH measurement well. However, a longer lag
time was present than for the concentration curves due to Eq. (4.5) assuming a pH 7 at start,
whereas the experimental fermentation started at pH 6.83. Furthermore, as seen in Figure 4.12-
4.14, the pH proﬁles didn’t resemble each other after pH 6 was reached. This was likely due
to a combination of two things. Firstly, the pH control point that was used in the simulation
was MP 3 of Figure 2.8, whereas the pH control point in the fermentation experiment was
located around MP 5. This made the experimental pH control mechanism more sensitive to
changes in the NH3 concentration, and made it remain for longer in the pH>6 region than an
MP 3 control point would have been. Secondly, the CFD simulation’s pH control mechanism
was not the same as the one that was used in the Chr. Hansen pilot scale reactor. In spite
of these diﬀerences, the pH correlation showed good promise for further use. When compared
with previous indirect pH calculation methods, such as the ionic charge balance method used
by Wu [13], the correlation between NH3 and lactic acid was simpler to develop and easier to
implement for the system in question.
The implementation of a valid pH correlation allows the user to explore the pH gradients in the
fermentation rector. From Figure 4.9, it would appear that despite the signiﬁcantly diﬀerent pH
proﬁles shown in Figure 4.12 and 4.13, the lactic acid production rate remains largely constant.
This was due to the mixing occurring much faster than the slow kinetics of the LAB fermenta-
tion, as could be seen by the virtually non-existent lactic acid gradient in Figure 4.11. However,
from Figure 4.10, it was clear that there were signiﬁcant pH gradients in the reactor. This is
important because unlike their simpliﬁed mathematical models, bacteria are more susceptible
to sudden changes in their environments. Jumping from pH 7 to 6 in a matter of seconds can
cause bacteria to shift production focus or possibly die oﬀcompletely. E. coli has already been
observed by Cortes et al [20] to express diﬀerent gene responses when placed in an STR with a
pH gradient than when no gradient was present.
For real world purposes, the ability to predict the pH everywhere in a large industrial scale
reactor allows reactor designers to predict where there might be problems with the pH becom-
ing too high, too low or with too large a pH gradient. This would then allow the designers to
circumvent such problems in advance. Alternatively, the LAB strain can be engineered to ﬁt
the pH gradients in the reactor design. Furthermore, predicting the pH everywhere also allows
for testing the eﬀect of diﬀerent pH control point locations and control mechanisms before they
are implemented in industrial scale reactors.

6
Conclusion
This study aimed to design a CFD model that was capable of predicting the lactic acid bacte-
ria (LAB) fermentation in a pilot scale bioreactor by incorporating a LAB kinetic model and
a pH correlation into the ﬂuid dynamics of CFD models. The LAB kinetic model was vali-
dated against an experimental fermentation, where it was found that the simulation followed
the experimental data, but with a 20 minute time lag. Fitting the time lag coeﬃcient tlag was
considered suﬃcient to match the simulation results with the experimental results.
An indirect pH correlation was also implemented and validated in the CFD fermentation model.
The pH correlation used the NH3 and lactic acid concentrations to predict the pH. This lessened
the computational requirements quite signiﬁcantly when compared to modelling the pH directly
from the water dissociation. Furthermore, the ability to model the pH in the reactor allowed
for the study of pH gradients in the reactor.
This was considered important in large scale
bioreactor fermentations both to optimize pH control mechanisms in and to study when the
pH gradients become an issue for the viability and growth of LAB. For small scale bioreactors,
bioreactors with very low mixing times or fermentations with cells that have proven resilient to
pH and environment gradients, the simple Matlab model allows for a quicker and easier method
of modelling the fermentation system reliably.
The CFD ﬂuid dynamic model was compared and validated against experimental tracer pulse
experiments with impeller speeds of 132 and 242 rpm. It was here found that a transient ﬂuid
proﬁle is necessary to obtain consistent mixing behaviour. With the transient ﬂuid proﬁle, a
good match was achieved with the experimental 242 rpm data, where the CFD model predicted
a mixing time of 46 s to the experimental average mixing time of 47 s. At 132 rpm, the CFD
mixing time of 83 s ﬁt well with theoretical mixing models, but not with the experimental mixing
times. Without further mixing experiments at 132 rpm, it was not possible to conclude whether
an error in the set-up had been made or not. If the experimental mixing data is accurate, then
the reason for the CFD simulation’s large deviation must be identiﬁed and corrected.
The computational requirements for using a transient ﬂuid proﬁle simulation with the LAB
kinetic model were found to be much too large for practical use. By instead using a ﬁxed steady
state velocity proﬁle with the same mixing time as the transient simulation, it was possible to
reduce the computational time to a manageable degree. However, even with this simpliﬁcation,
a 6 hr simulation with 1 s time steps and 1e-5 RMS limits was still lagging 10 minutes behind
a homogeneous Matlab simulation of the same system. From tests on a small cubical CFD
test reactor, it was shown that this time lag was a problem with error accumulation during the
much larger pilot scale simulation. A smaller time step or RMS value would likely solve the
time lag issue, but with the 1 s time step simulation already requiring 1784 CPU hours, it was
not attempted.

6.1
Future work
For future work, the reduction of the CPU hours must be considered as a priority if the model
is to be used for simulating a multitude of conditions within a reasonable time period. Aside
from decreasing the node density, a possible solution to this problem could be to set up the
kinetic model by using the Materials and Reactions system in combination with CFX’s stiﬀ
solver model. This was found to be eﬀective in managing the much faster reaction rates of the
carbonate buﬀer system from Appendix A. A similar approach to fermentation kinetic models
could signiﬁcantly reduce the computation requirements. To avoid the issue of introducing mass
in a closed system, the non-reacting NH3 should still be modelled as an Additional Variable.
If successfully implemented, a signiﬁcant reduction in computational time would then allow
for further study of the current kinetic model under diﬀerent conditions and with diﬀerent pH
controller settings.
Doing further experimental mixing time and fermentation tests with the Chr. Hansen pilot
reactor at 132 and 242 rpm, as well as other impeller speeds, should also be considered. If the
current experimental data is accurate, then the CFD model will need further improvements to
get the ﬂuid proﬁle to match the reality.
Finally, if the CPU requirements are able to be brought down, a less simpliﬁed kinetic model can
be attempted implemented for increased accuracy. This expanded model could take advantage
of the access to a pH prediction to further model the impact of pH gradients on the LAB.

CFD implementation
In CFX, the regular solver would require time steps in the range of 1e-6 s to solve even a sim-
ple 5x5x1 cm cubical system, where the concentration in every node is uniform and changed
uniformly at the initiation, as was done in Matlab. This made it impossible to use as is for
a mixing system, which usually takes in excess of 30 s to reach steady state. To reduce the
computational time and mesh complexity, the cubical system was used to compare directly with
the one-compartment Matlab simulation until a usable system set-up was found.
with materials being calculated in mass fractions in CFX. ANSYS CFX has a stiﬀsolver beta
function, which allows the user to better implement fast reactions. From experimentation, it
was found that the stiﬀsolver worked best when using the Reaction mechanism to set up the
system that was described in Reaction R3-R6. The specie were set up as mass fraction by using
Materials. In order to reach a stable kinetic system with 0.1 s time steps, it was necessary
to command the stiﬀsolver to cut oﬀthe mass fractions that were used in reaction rates and
similar calculation. The optimal cut-oﬀpoint was found to be 1e-8.
To add to the complications, the stiﬀsolver is not as ﬁne as the Matlab one, so another
parameter had to be implemented to reach a stable system as quickly as possible. The stiﬀ
solver has a function that limits the mass fraction solution change between time steps, which
was found useful in limiting the fast kinetic reactions. Figure A2 shows the comparison between
the Matlab case and a CFX system with a 0.05, 0.1 and 0.2 maximum allowed solution change.
As could be noted from it, all systems were unstable in the ﬁrst 3 s, but then stabilized and
followed the Matlab proﬁle. The reason for this change from unstable to stable was that the
system took this long to reach a steady state of the two fast reactions. Beyond this, the slower
reactions changed so slowly that the fast reactions could handle the change with the stiﬀsolver.
Of the commands investigated, it was found that a maximum solution change of 0.1 was optimal
for the system in question, but that the ﬁrst 3 seconds would be close to meaningless in this
idealized system.

B
Mesh iterations
This sections details the major changes that occurred to the meshes that were used in the
simulations, as well as the quality of the mesh iterations.
B.1
Impeller
The impeller geometry was changed halfway through the project due to a discovery that the
provided geometry did not match reality. The old geometry dimensions can be seen in Figure
B1. The new dimensions are available in Figure 2.1, and the mesh iteration for it is available
on page m.
Figure B1: Sketch of old impeller dimensions.
The ﬁrst iteration of the old geometry structured impeller mesh, henceforth dubbed impeller_org,
was constructed for an exam project in the DTU course, 28831 CFD in Chem. Eng. The project
had a 5-day time limit to construct, mesh and carry out all simulations, along with a total node
limit of 500k for the entire simulation. This mesh can be seen in Figure B2. It has a total node
count of 42k, no mesh element volumes below 3 mm3 and 12.13% of the element angles in it
are below 18 degrees, though none below 4.5 degrees and only 0.348% below 9 degrees. This
iteration was rejected due in part both to the limited node count and the signiﬁcant number of
low angles, especially around the impeller blades.
k
==PAGE:76==
B.1
Impeller
Figure B2: Impeller_org structured mesh of the old geometry with 42k nodes.
The second major impeller iteration, henceforth dubbed impeller_d, was changed from the ﬁrst
iteration by having a signiﬁcantly ﬁner standard mesh of 159k nodes and expanding the boxes
around the impeller blades. It can be seen in Figure B3. The angles of this iteration has 1.273%
of the angles below 18 degrees, none below 4.5 degrees and only 0.039% below 9 degrees. 18.3%
of the mesh element volumes were below 3 mm3, with 4.32% being between 0 and 1 mm3, and
the smallest being 0.55 mm3. The iteration was rejected due to a failure to calculate transient
concentrations with the reactor_d mesh, creating numerical errors in the interface, and for
having too many small volumes.
Figure B3: Impeller_d structured mesh of the old geometry with 159k nodes.
The third and ﬁnal major iteration for the old geometry, henceforth dubbed impeller_e, was
changed from impeller_d by reducing the size of the o-grids outlining the impeller blades and
cleaning up some angles in the periphery. It can be seen in Figure B4. It has 115k nodes as a
standard. There are only 0.034% of the angles that lie below 18 degrees, and of these, none are
below 13.5 degrees. 6.26% of the element volumes lie below 3 mm3, and none below 1.63 mm3.

The following mesh is the ﬁrst iteration of the new geometry dimensions, henceforth dubbed
impeller_new. A major change from previous iterations is that the blades are no longer cut
out with o-grids, but rather directly through splitting blocks and ﬁtting the lines to the blades.
This provided the mesh seen in Figure B5, which has 352k nodes as a standard size. Of these,
11.03% of the elements have a volume below 3 mm3, with the lowest at 0.699 mm3. These are
concentrated around the edge of the shaft, as expected. Unlike previous iterations, the lowest
element angle was 36 degrees, with only 23.06% of the element angles being below 72 degrees.
This is a considerable improvement upon previous iterations of the old geometry.

B.2
Reactor
Similar to the impeller, the ﬁrst iteration of the reactor mesh was made for an exam project with
only 5 days and 500k nodes available. This iteration can be seen in Figure B6 under the name
reactor_org. It has 327k nodes, of which 0.16% of elements has a volume below 3 mm3, with
the lowest being 1.42 mm3. 0.445% of the element angles are below 18 degrees, with 0.006%
being below 4.5 degrees and 0.084% below 9 degrees. It was rejected due to the low node count
available.
Figure B6: Reactor_org structured mesh with 327k nodes.
The second major iteration of the reactor mesh, reactor_d, can be seen in Figure B7. It was
changed from the previous iteration by making it ﬁner and cleaning up some of the edge lines
at the rounded bottom. It has 660k nodes, of which 0.619% of the element volumes are below
3 mm3, with 0.011% between 0 and 1 mm3 and the lowest being 0.684 mm3. 0.342% of the
element angles are below 18 degrees, with 0.002% being below 4.5 degrees and 0.044% being
below 9 degrees. It was rejected in consort with impeller_d mesh for not being able to calculate
transient concentration simulations due to having issues with numerical errors near the interface.
The number of low angles was also considered a concern.
n
==PAGE:79==
B.2
Reactor
Figure B7: Reactor_d structured mesh with 660k nodes.
The third major iteration of the reactor mesh, reactor_e, was changed from reactor_d in a
number of ways. Its overview can be seen in Figure B8. The ﬁrst major change was made to
the baﬄes, where the o-grids were reﬁned to provide less odd angles. The diﬀerence between the
two can be seen in Figure B9. The second major diﬀerence was made by introducing another
o-grid into the top of the shaft, as seen in Figure B10. This changes provided less odd angles
and small volumes in the mesh between the top of the shaft and the top of the reactor, as the
inside of the shaft was cut out, and therefore unaﬀected. The third major change was conducted
by introducing linear spacing near the impellers and shaft, so that it was ﬁnest near them. It
was also decided to increase the number of node points on the sides of the impellers to combat
the issue of the impeller mesh and reactor meshes being too diﬀerent near the interfaces. The
latter two changes can be seen in Figure B11.
The third iteration mesh has a standard size of 940k nodes. Of these, 37.9% elements have
a volume below 3 mm3, with the lowest being 0.955 mm3, and 2.203% being below 2 mm3.
0.134% of the element angles are below 18 degrees, of which 0.001% are below 4.5 degrees and
0.024% below 9 degrees. Most of the low angles were located in the rounded arc, where the
bottom starts to ﬂatten, but the remainder of them were located near the bottom of the baﬄes.

the measuring points not converging above an RMS of 1e-6. A possible explanation as to why

combined eﬀect of the measuring points being closer to the source of the disturbance (the im-
