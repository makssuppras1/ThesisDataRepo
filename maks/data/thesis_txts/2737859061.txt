IEQ in buildings in DTU Campus Lyngby
Abstract
The TAIL rating scheme was utilized as an Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) rating tool to
investigate the IEQ in several buildings at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Through
a measurement campaign spanning 8 weeks from the beginning of March, 2024 to the end of April,
2024, a total of 5 office buildings were monitored. A new and comprehensive method of visualizing
the TAIL classification was developed, that provided a detailed view of the quality level for all the
assessed parameters.
Alongside the measurement campaign, an IEQ survey was conducted to correlate objective mea-
surements with subjective responses. Higher correlations between thermal and light sensations and
their respective objective measurements were found when compared to noise and odor sensations.
The findings highlight potential improvements, including the integration of more pollutants in the
Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) section and surveys to address glare and odor, among other parameters,
that are challenging to capture through objective measurements. Additionally, a human flourishing
survey developed by Harvard was included to study the effects of well-being on IEQ assessments. A
derived thermal scale, based on the surveyed DTU population, was also developed, which differed
from the values obtained from standard EN 16798 for the summer and winter.

1
Introduction
Nowadays people spend most of their time indoors, whether at home, in private dwellings, or at
work or school. It has been found that humans spend around 77% of time in dwellings and 14% in
their workplaces [1]. Consequently, as more time is being spent indoors, greater efforts are being
made to improve human well-being and comfort, ensuring a healthy indoor environment. Links
have been established between indoor environment, productivity and worker performance [2][3].
Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) encompasses four main components, Indoor Air Quality
(IAQ), thermal, lighting and acoustics environments [4]. The following sections will delve deeper
into the aforementioned components and their effects on productivity, performance and comfort.
1.1
Thermal environment and comfort
The ISO 7730 standard defines thermal comfort as a condition of mind which expresses satisfaction
with the thermal environment [5]. In other words, a person is thermally comfortable when they
neither feel too warm or too cold [6]. Kim and de Dear found that individuals occupying a building
tend to overlook its thermal conditions unless they are uncomfortable and the conditions are below
their expectations [7].
Six basic components factor into the human heat balance and affect thermal comfort: air tem-
perature, mean radiant temperature, air velocity, vapor pressure, metabolic rate (physical activity
level) and clothing insulation [6]. Different combinations of the aforementioned factors can result
in a similar thermal sensation [8].
There are several indices used to evaluate the thermal conditions in a space, for example, the pre-
dicted mean vote (PMV). The ISO 7730 standard incorporates the PMV [5], which is an index that
predicts the mean value of the votes of a large group of people on a 7-point thermal sensation scale,
ranging from -3 (cold) to 3 (hot), with 0 being neutral. The PMV index is based on the heat balance
of the human body that balances internal heat production with heat loss to the environment.
Several researchers tried to identify the relationship of thermal sensation votes (PMV) with the
relative performance. Experiments were done by incorporating tasks such as arithmetical calcula-
tion, typing and logical reasoning to determine the effect the thermal environment has on workers’
performance. Lan et al. [8] and Kosonen et al. [9], found the maximum productivity at a PMV of -
0.25 and -0.21 respectively, and -1 in [10]. This could indicate that optimal performance is achieved
in slightly cooler than neutral environments. Lan et al.[8] suggests to set the PMV limits in a
workplace to -0.5 to 0 instead of -0.5 to 0.5 as it is present in the standards currently. Furthermore,
Lan demonstrates that thermal discomfort led to a significant decrease in relative performance.
To further elucidate this, Niemelä et al. shows that labor productivity in a call center decreased
5%-7% when the air temperature surpassed 25°C [11]. However, finding a specific thermal range
for a group of people can be a challenge. People from different genders and age groups can judge a
certain temperature differently based on several field studies conducted on thermal comfort [12][13].
Additionally, the optimal temperature can rely on the type of task being done [14]. Some tasks
might require thermal comfort for optimal performance, while others might benefit from slight ther-

of individual temperature control systems can prove to be highly beneficial. It was found that
individual control of ±3°C was expected to improve the group mean performance somewhere from

1.2
Acoustic environment and comfort
Studies have delved into the effects of noises in offices on workers’ productivity, especially since
the acoustic environment has been found to have most impact on the productivity of workers in
university open plan offices [16]. Workers in university research offices often require better acoustic
conditions due to the complexity of their tasks. Common noise sources in offices include conversa-
tions, ringing phones, machines, keyboard typing, and general human activity [17]. Employees who
rated their satisfaction with their environment and job found that noise disturbances significantly
affected their overall satisfaction. A noise exposure test revealed that prolonged exposure to noise
decreased job satisfaction [18].
Furthermore, research investigated the cognitive, emotional, and physiological effects of high and
low noise levels on office employees [19]. The study found that participants felt more tired and less
motivated after working in high-noise environments, with memory performance declining signifi-
cantly compared to low-noise conditions. The degree of occupants’ discomfort is influenced by both
the intensity and frequency of the sound signal. Unexpected and irregular noises, such as phones
ringing and people talking, are more disturbing than constant background noise [20] [21].
1.3
IAQ and comfort
1.3.1
Pollutants
Indoor air quality (IAQ) encompasses two main groups of pollutants: biological and chemical [4].
Biological pollutants include bacteria, dust mites, endotoxins, fungi, mold, and viruses. On the
other hand, chemical pollutants consist of substances like volatile organic compounds (VOCs),
radon, carbon monoxide (CO), and nitrogen dioxide (NO2) .
Inadequate ventilation, poor humidity control, condensation are all causes for growth of bacteria,
virus, fungi and allergens in an indoor environment. There are many sources of mold growth such as
floods, condensation, plumbing leaks and mold growth from high humidity rooms such as kitchens
and bathrooms. Exposure to such pollutants is proven to be linked to asthma and allergies [4] .
VOCs are a major class of indoor pollutants, often reported as total volatile organic compounds
(TVOCs) rather than individually [22]. VOCs can be found both indoors and outdoors. Outdoor
sources are vehicle exhaust gases as well as industrial processes, and indoor sources include, but are
not limited to, building materials, consumer products, gas cooking and tobacco smoking. Exposure
to VOCs can lead to short and long-term effects. Moreover, some VOCs such as formaldehyde and
benzene have been found to be carcinogenic [23].
Formaldehyde (CH2O), a colorless and flammable gas, is emitted through processes such as biomass
combustion, such as forest and bush fires [24]. Additionally, it is industrially produced for use in

manufacturing resins, consumer products, disinfectants, and fixatives, making these products the
primary source of indoor CH2O. Resins containing CH2O are commonly used in furniture and
wooden products, while other sources include glues, paints, cleaning products, and disinfectants.
Homes with recently installed wooden furniture or that were painted or varnished within the past
12 months have been found to have higher concentrations of CH2O [25]. There was a marked
difference in CH2O concentrations between old and new homes, with significantly higher levels
detected in the new homes [26].
Radon, a gas that is both colorless and odorless, infiltrates building foundations through cracks
driven by the pressure difference between the outdoors and indoors [24]. This gas is a result of the
decay of uranium in rocks, which first transforms into radium and then releases radon. Typically,
radon levels are monitored over an extended period (3-12 months) to account for the fluctuations
in emissions caused by seasonal and daily variations.
Carbon monoxide (CO) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2) are emitted from combustion activities such
as cooking, heating, and vehicle exhausts, as well as tobacco smoke [24]. CO is a colorless, odorless
gas, undetectable by humans. In contrast, NO2 has a pungent odor detectable by humans when
its concentration exceeds 0.1 ppm.
In addition to all the aforementioned pollutants, particulate matter commonly exist in the indoor
environment. Particulate matter are inhalable particles that comprise solid and liquid particles
suspended in the atmosphere, exhibiting a diverse range of sizes and origins.
Particles with a
diameter smaller than 2.5 micrometers (PM2.5) and particles with a diameter smaller than 10
micrometers (PM10) are proven to pose health risks, as it increases cardiovascular and respiratory
diseases, diminishes lung function in both healthy and asthmatic individuals and reduces lifespans
[27] [28]. PM2.5 primarily originates from fuel or gasoline combustion, while PM10 predominantly
arises from activities such as waste incineration, construction operations, wildfires, and industrial
processes [29].
The size of the particle significantly influences the particle’s destination in the
respiratory tract and body in general [30].
For instance, PM10 can enter the upper airways,
leading to irritation and various allergic reactions in the respiratory system. PM2.5 can harm the
respiratory system by weakening the body’s defense mechanisms in the airways and affecting the
immune system’s response, potentially entering the blood stream therefore also affecting several
organ systems.
1.3.2
Sick Building Syndrome
Poor IEQ conditions have been linked causes of Sick Building Syndrome (SBS). The World Health
Organization (WHO) defines SBS as a group of symptoms that can either affect the mucous mem-
brane such as the eyes, nose and throat or general symptoms such as headaches [4]. Such symptoms
are classified as SBS symptoms when they are experienced throughout the occupancy hours and
then fade away or improve shortly after the occupant has vacated the building [31]. Common symp-
toms of SBS include eye, nose or throat irritation, dry itchy skin, headaches, mental fatigue, loss
of concentration, high frequency of airway infections, nausea and dizziness. A study conducted in
Malaysia found that mucousal and general symptoms are almost equally common being 19.6% and

18.7%, while the skin symptoms were the least frequent, with a proportion of 10.2% [32]. Similarly,
research in Japan showed that the skin symptoms were also the least common compared to the
general symptoms [33].
The aforementioned symptoms are shown to reduce the worker’s efficiency and performance as well
as increase absences and possibly lose time due to having to close down the building for repairs.
Through conducting computerized neurobehavioral tests, as well as several other tasks, such as
typing and mental arithmetic calculations, it was found that SBS has a negative effect on work
productivity [34] [35].
The building characteristics significantly affects the health of the occupants. There is a proven
link between respiratory illnesses and the buildings characteristics. Respiratory illnesses such as
common colds, influenza, adenovirus, measles etc. are transmitted through infectious aerosols. Such
transmission can be affected by the ventilation rate, rate of air changes or rate of air filtration. It
can also be affected by air temperature and humidity [14].
1.4
Lighting and Visual Comfort
Lighting in buildings constitutes 19% of the world’s energy consumption, where 25% of that is
from housing and 75% is from commercial buildings and roads [36]. Therefore, the usage of more
daylighting in indoor spaces could prove to be useful in reducing the lighting energy consumption
[37]. Daylight proves to be more visually acceptable to office workers even without a direct view
of outside [38]. However, the availability of windows in workplaces and access to daylight proves
to be crucial in a study [39], where workers were found to sleep on average 46 minutes more per
night during the working days than workers that didn’t have windows. Access to bright light during
the day also showed reduced depressive symptoms among both subjects with seasonal-depression
symptoms and those without [40]. Exposure to daylight also showed improvement of moods of
office workers around the afternoon [38].
Finding the optimal level of lighting/daylighting indoors has been researched a lot over the past
decades. The DS/EN Standard 12464 recommends certain levels of illuminance depending on the
task being done in a space [41]. In a study done in offices [42], it was found that the optimal
Daylight Factor (DF%) levels were found to be between 2% and 5%. DF% is the ratio of horizontal
indoor to outdoor daylight illumination [43]. Rooms that had a factor greater than 5% caused
dissatisfaction and discomfort as the glare and overheating problems seemed to be too disturbing.
Too low DF% also wasn’t preferable as the majority of the office workers preferred daylight over
artificial lighting, however, some liked to have a combination of both. Additionally, a large group,
73%, thought that it was very important to have a window in their work area.

TAIL rating scheme
2.1
Introduction to the scheme
Several IEQ assessment tools have been proposed and developed, yet till now there hasn’t been a
universally accepted method for evaluation. Larsen et al. [44] proposed a new tool called the IEQ
compass which provides a holistic evaluation of the thermal, acoustic, IAQ and luminous environ-
ments. It is chosen to give the four components equal weighting due to the lack of information
regarding the importance of each. The IEQ compass is developed to obtain an IEQ label in a short
amount of time (around 3 hours) without the need for surveys and measurements. Evaluations
are based on existing building information modelling, blueprints and observations made during
inspection. The compass, however, does not consider the occupancy effects to make it reflect the
IEQ potential of buildings. Danza et al. [45] aimed to bridge the gap and therefore proposed a
method for defining weights for the components involved in the IEQ assessment. This was done
by constructing a questionnaire that involved 100 participants. The results showed that the IAQ
is the most influential component weighing around 60%-63% and the visual environment being the
least influential around 7%-10%. Devitofrancesco et al. [46] proposed an assessment tool based
on the SB Method (Sustainable Building Method) and the Multi Criteria Analysis for the evalu-
ation of IEQ during the operational phase of a building. The weights of single indicators instead
reflect “technical choices”, that is “votes” assigned by experts based on three criteria: importance
of the indicator, the ability to modify the conditions defined by the indicator and the impact on
sustainability of the indicator. This tool does not incorporate surveys or feedback from occupants
regarding their indoor environment.
Several green building certifications (GBCs) such as Leadership in Energy and Environmental
Design (LEED), Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment (BREEAM) and
Deutsche Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges Bauen (DGNB) have an IEQ rating scheme [47]. Along
with the IEQ section they also focus on energy consumption, waste, and water management. How-
ever, each of them has a different weighted scoring of the IEQ parameters. For example, LEED
has 15% of its credits dedicated to IEQ. BREEAM and DGNB have 12% and 6.5% respectively.
Therefore, due to their differences, it can be difficult to compare them with each other. The rating
scheme TAIL was developed as part of the energy certification method developed by the EU AL-
DREN project [48]. The ALDREN project (ALliance for Deep RENovation in buildings) operated
from November 2017- October 2020, aimed to promote deep renovation of buildings without com-
promising the building’s IEQ and that the IEQ in the newly renovated buildings meets the health
and well-being standards as prescribed in the international standards. Furthermore, it aimed at
including a rating scheme for IEQ parameters and overall IEQ in a building. The rating scheme
TAIL incorporates four components of IEQ: thermal (T) environment, acoustic (A) environment,
indoor air (I) environment and luminous (L) environment. From the individual component rating
an overall rating is then established.

Figure 1 shows a visual representation of the TAIL rating scheme. The four quadrants represent
the four different components discussed above; thermal (T) environment, acoustic (A) environment,
indoor air (I) environment and luminous (L) environment. The figure aims to show the four different
quality levels that can be achieved. According to the scheme, the color green represents a high-
quality level that is the most desirable, yellow indicates a medium quality level, orange denotes
a moderate ordinary quality level, and red signifies a low undesirable quality level. The overall
rating is shown as a roman numeral in the center, where I denotes a desired high-quality level, II
represents a medium-quality level, III signifies an ordinary, moderate-quality level, and IV indicates
an undesirable low-quality level.
It is imperative to note that the roman numerals correspond
to the EN 16798-1 standard [49], which defines indoor environmental input parameters for the
design and assessment of the energy performance of buildings. The overall rating is determined
by the component exhibiting the poorest performance, thereby motivating enhancements in the
building’s operation in a certain area. Additionally, it serves as guidance to the building operators
by indicating which renovation strategies should be prioritized out to improve the overall IEQ in
the building.

2.2
Overall rating of the indoor environment using TAIL
Due to the difficulty of measuring every single room in a building due to time and economic
constraints, only a selection of rooms was to be chosen in each building that was to be classified
with the TAIL scheme. A calculation method was defined to determine the overall rating of a
building’s indoor environment.
The building gets a classification through a two-step process. First, a classification is given on a
room scale. This is done by first calculating the quality level of each component in TAIL, based on
the quality levels of the included parameters that are based on the 4 categories in the EN 16798-1
standard [49]. The overall IEQ rating for a room is then defined by the worst scoring component.
It is done this way to raise concern to a room that needs improvement in a certain area. Later, the

The interim rating system categorizes the quality levels of buildings based on specific ranges. Build-
ings are assigned a rating ranging from 1 to 4, with lower ratings indicating better quality. Ratings
from 1 to 1.4 fall into the green category, while those between 1.5 and 2.4 are labeled yellow. An

Similarly, the overall IEQ rating of a building is defined by the worst scoring component in TAIL.
The 2 step process ensures a TAIL rating is given to each individual room and to the building as
a whole. This aids in informing the building occupants and operators about any specific room or
wing in a building that needs improvement in any future renovation plans.
2.3
Objective
In the present work, the TAIL rating scheme was utilized to assess buildings at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) in Kongens Lyngby. More specifically, office buildings’ IEQ were
measured and assessed. Additionally, a survey was conducted among the occupants of the monitored
office buildings to assess their experience with the IEQ in their workplace. The objective of this
thesis is to critically examine the TAIL methodology and explore the integration of subjective TAIL
elements, using DTU’s buildings as a case study. This research seeks to evaluate the effectiveness of
TAIL and identify any necessary modifications. Key areas of investigation include the development
of subjective TAIL, the correlation between subjective and objective TAIL, the adequacy of current
parameters for determining thermal comfort, acoustic quality, IAQ, and luminous comfort, as well
as the potential influence of psychological factors unrelated to IEQ on subjective responses.

3.1
TAIL at DTU Lyngby Campus
Some modifications were made to the original TAIL scheme developed by Wargocki et al. [48],
since it was not possible to measure all 12 parameters included in the original TAIL. Radon was
not measured because DTU lies in a Radon class 1 area [51], that means that only 0.3%-1% of the
buildings in the area are assessed to have a radon level above 200 Bq/m3. Benzene and ventilation
rate was not measured due to the unavailability of measuring equipment. However, measuring CO2
could be a sufficient indicator for the acceptability of a space in terms of human body odor [52].
Furthermore, there was one parameter that was added to the original TAIL: particulate matter
with a diameter less than 10 micrometers (PM10).
Tables 1, 2, 3, and 4 show the threshold values for the four different TAIL quality levels. The
threshold values from table 1 for the air temperature is obtained from the EN 16798-1 standard
[49]. A different threshold exists depending on the season and whether or not mechanical cooling
is used. Table 1 includes the values for no mechanical cooling. The heating season differs from the
cooling season due to the difference of the level of clothing of the occupants. During the heating
season the occupants are expected to be clothed with around 1 clo (unit of clothing insulation) and
with 0.5 clo during the cooling season.
To qualify for classification under a certain category, the temperature should not exceed the specified
range for the respective category by ±1 °C for more than 5% of the occupation hours, and by ±2
°C for more than 1% of the occupation hours. This method resembles the approach outlined by
Level(s) [53].

3.3
Sensor Deployment
The TAIL rating scheme was developed and tested on hotel and office buildings in various loca-
tions in Europe [48]. In the present work, the TAIL rating scheme was utilized for office buildings
at DTU’s campus in Kongens Lyngby. Five buildings were monitored over the span of 8 weeks,
starting March 4th, 2024 and ending on April 26th, 2024. Sensors were deployed at the beginning
of each work week (Monday) and collected at the end of the week (Friday), with the exception
of one building where the sensors’ locations were changed every 2.5 days instead of the usual 5

working hours (8:00 AM to 5:00 PM).
During the measurement campaign, 15 Atmocube sensors were available for deployment [58]. How-
ever, in certain buildings, such as building 115, which was simultaneously measured with other
buildings for another project, fewer sensors were accessible.
To address this limitation, sensor
locations were rotated every 2.5 days instead of the usual 5 days.
Sensors were placed on the desks of the employees, that were around 0.8-1m above the ground,
positioned roughly 1m away from windows and walls and positioned near the employee, ensuring
proximity without being affected by exhaled air or occupants’ body heat. Placement near heaters
or direct sunlight was avoided. The sensors were placed around 1m away from the windows and
walls.

3.4
Daylight Factor Simulations
For assessing the luminous environment in the offices, both measurements and simulations were
carried out. Illuminance was measured using the Atmocube sensors and the daylight factor (DF%)
was simulated for all the selected offices. One-year dynamic daylight simulations were conducted
using IDA Indoor Climate and Energy. The type of sky used was an overcast sky. The TAIL
methodology [48] specifies using a type 1 or 16 model, therefore the one chosen was type 1 which
is the CIE standard overcast sky. Simulations were run with a 0.85m distance from the floor to
represent the typical desk height. Due to unavailable detailed information on the windows installed,
assumptions were made to approximate the window specifications. The simulations assumed the
use of double glazed windows with a total solar transmittance of 0.76, direct solar transmittance
of 0.7, and light transmittance of 0.8. For simplification purposes, no outdoor obstructions such
as surrounding buildings, trees etc. was added to the simulations, which would naturally lead to
higher DF% values than if obstructions were present.
3.5
Setting up the survey
A comprehensive survey was conducted among DTU employees to gauge their perception of the
indoor environmental quality within their workplaces. Occupants’ perception-based evaluations
offer valuable insights into their subjective experiences within indoor spaces. These perceptions,
which encompass feelings of comfort and satisfaction, are inherently subjective and can vary signif-
icantly among individuals. Moreover, subjective evaluations can capture nuances that quantitative
measurements might overlook. For instance, while measurements can provide insight on factors like
temperature and humidity, they might not fully capture subjective experiences. Zalejska-Jonsson
et al. relied on subjective reporting to identify causes of IAQ dissatisfaction including issues such
as odors and air stuffiness [59].
The survey implementation was planned to ensure maximum participation and meaningful feed-
back from respondents, offering the flexibility of choice between Danish and English languages.
Comprising three distinct sections, the background questions, the IEQ survey and the Flourishing
measure survey. Table 8 shows an overview of the three sections. Additionally, the question topics
are demonstrated in the column on the right.

respondents were invited to provide insights into various personal information such as age and
gender. Additionally, questions pertaining to office location and the duration of time spent within

3.5.2
IEQ Survey
The subsequent section focused on assessing IEQ, encompassing four key domains: thermal com-
fort, acoustic environment, indoor air quality, and luminous environment.
Within this section,
various question formats were employed, including binary (yes/no), multiple-choice with the option
for multiple selections. The majority of the questions in the IEQ survey are derived from a collab-
orative effort involving Frederikshavn, Favrskov, and Ringkøbing-Skjern municipalities, alongside
DTU, Aalborg University, and Transition ApS. This initiative was aimed at developing a tool,
"arbejdspladsvurderinger" (APV), to evaluate workplace environments within municipalities.
Moreover, recognizing the inherent limitations of predefined response options, the survey integrated
elaborative prompts at the conclusion of each IEQ component. These prompts provided respondents
with the opportunity to articulate their thoughts and experiences in greater detail, thereby enriching
the qualitative depth of the survey responses.
It is crucial to note that the survey aims to assess both the respondents’ sensation and perception of
the IEQ in their workplace. Sensation is defined as "the process or experience of perceiving through
the senses". While perception is defined as "the process or result of becoming aware of objects,
relationships, and events by means of the senses, which includes such activities as recognizing,
observing, and discriminating.
These activities enable organisms to organize and interpret the
stimuli received into meaningful knowledge and to act in a coordinated manner" [60]. In essence,
sensation represents the passive detection of stimuli, whereas perception involves the integration
and interpretation of sensory information into meaningful knowledge and coordinated actions [61].
Perception inquiries were placed at the beginning of each section in the survey and presented
as yes/no questions.
Sensation inquiries, on the other hand, were included at the end of each
of the four TAIL components and utilized a continuous scale to capture the nuanced spectrum
of respondents’ sensations. This approach facilitated a greater understanding of how individuals
sensed and detected stimuli in their environment. The scales employed for the four components are
shown in figures 4, 5, 6, and 7. The values shown on the scale are later used when calculating the
weighted average for each building.

3.7
Data Analysis
Various data analysis methods were employed to identify meaningful correlations and draw conclu-
sions from the data collected through both measurements and surveys. The programming language
R was used for statistical computing and data visualization. The Pearson correlation coefficient
was calculated to assess the impact of outdoor temperature on indoor temperature. Outdoor tem-
perature and RH% data were obtained from a weather station located at DTU. The mixed effects
model was used to find out how much of the effects on indoor temperatures are attributed to the
outdoor temperature and how much are attributed to the difference in buildings/occupants. The
mixed effects model is a statistical model that involves fixed and random effects. Fixed effects are
consistent across the whole population whereas random effects are the variations between clusters
of data. In this case the fixed effects were set to be the outdoor temperature and each building’s
unique characteristics are treated as random effects.
A logistic regression model was developed using survey responses to predict occupants’ dissatis-
faction with the 4 components of TAIL. The predictors were the responses to sensation questions,
which were measured on a continuous scale, as shown in Figures 4, 5, 6, and 7. The dependent
variable consisted of the responses to perception questions, which were yes/no questions, therefore
a binomial logistic regression was employed.
Hierarchical clustering was used to group the survey respondents into clusters of similar individuals.
This method begins with each respondent as an individual cluster and iteratively merges the closest
clusters until all respondents are grouped together or until a specified number of clusters is reached.
Hierarchical clustering helps in understanding the relationships between respondents by progres-
sively linking clusters based on their similarity. The optimal number of clusters was found both
by consulting the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS)/elbow method and the silhouette width
method [63]. The elbow method identifies the "elbow" point where the WCSS starts to decrease
at a slower rate. The number of clusters chosen should be such that adding another cluster does
not significantly improve the total WCSS. The silhouette width method measures how similar each
point is to its cluster; a high silhouette width indicates good clustering.
A random forest method was applied to the survey responses, specifically the overall IEQ assess-
ment, sensation responses and Flourishing measure responses. The overall IEQ assessment was used
as the target variable. The predictors included the flourishing index and the occupants’ sensations.
Random forest, a machine learning method, constructs multiple decision trees and aggregates their
predictions to enhance model accuracy. This method also provides insights into the importance of
predictors by evaluating the change in accuracy when they are included or excluded. One common
metric is the mean decrease Gini (MDG) which denotes the reduction of Gini impurity across all
trees in a random forest, and therefore, quantifies the importance of features included in the model.
The objective was to examine the effects of the predictors on the target variable. Additionally, Ex-
ploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) was conducted on the 12 questions within the flourishing measure
survey. This analysis aimed to identify the most influential factors on the overall flourishing index,
rather than simply averaging the factors. This approach allows more significant factors to be given
greater weight.

To predict occupants’ dissatisfaction, binomial logistic regression models were built for each of the
four components of TAIL, thermal, IAQ (odor), light, and acoustics. The aforementioned sensation
and perception questions were used to build the models. For each of the components, two models
were made, one using 60% of the data collected from DTU respondents to train the model and
40% to test the model. The other model made was used with 100% of data collected from DTU
respondents to train the model, and external data to test the model. Figures 40, 41, 42, and 43
illustrate the models made with 100% of the DTU data, for the thermal, acoustic, IAQ (odor), and
luminous environments, respectively.
The thermal regression plot shows a dip in the predicted percentage of dissatified occupants around
the neutral thermal sensation vote (TSV=0), with dissatisfaction increasing towards the extremes
(TSV=3 and TSV=-3). This indicates that occupants are most comfortable at neutral temperatures
and increasingly dissatisfied as conditions become warmer or cooler. For acoustics, the percentage
of respondents bothered by noise rises as the noise sensation vote (NSV) increases, reaching a
maximum of around 90% for NSV=1, which translates to "Noisy."
The odor and light regression plots are less complete because some options were never selected,
despite there being over 200 respondents participating. Nevertheless, the predicted percentage of
respondents that are dissatisfied by odor increases with higher odor sensation votes (OSV). Similar

Discussion
5.1
TAIL Rating Scheme
A feasibility test of the TAIL rating scheme was conducted using data from measurements taken
at DTU’s campus in Kongens Lyngby. The rating scheme effectively differentiated between various
IEQ categories across offices in five different buildings.
A cross-calibration of the 15 Atmocube sensors was performed to ensure data accuracy and to
critically examine the validity of the collected data. The RMSEs were calculated for each pair of
sensors to quantify the differences between the collected data. This calculation revealed a need
for correction in some parameters more than others, specifically CH2O, sound pressure level, il-
luminance, temperature, and RH%. The simple method of correcting the data was carried out in
an effort to produce more accurate results. However, to ensure even higher accuracy, calibration
against lab-grade instruments needs to be done.
Two parameters that consistently showed undesirable results across all the buildings were tempera-
ture and illuminance. Four out of the five buildings monitored received a red rating for the thermal
component, raising the question of whether the offices truly had poor thermal conditions or if the
TAIL rating scheme is overly stringent. The TAIL rating scheme included only one parameter for
the thermal component, and that is air temperature. Including more parameters that evaluate local
discomfort such as thermal asymmetry or drafts could give a better holistic picture of the thermal
environment. However, including such parameters was omitted due to the time-consuming nature
of such measurements. Additionally, evaluation of the time-series parameters (temperature, RH%,
and CO2 concentration) might be seen as a strict feature in the rating scheme. For an office to be
classified in Category I or II, the total measured time in Category III or IV, respectively, could not
exceed more than 1% of the total time
For PM2.5, PM10, and CH2O classification, the maximum value was evaluated instead of the
median value. This approach is quite strict as it does not allow for minor exceedances that might
occur throughout the day. An improvement could be made by applying the percentage of time
method, similar to that used for temperature, RH%, and CO2 concentration. Nevertheless, the
PM2.5 and PM10 concentrations were almost always in the green zone for most offices. The original
TAIL scheme [48] included 8 parameters, while the present work included 5 parameters. However,
more pollutants can be added to the TAIL scheme, as many pollutants that could exist indoors
are odorless and therefore difficult for humans to detect. Potential additions to the scheme are
polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), asbestos, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), carbon monoxide
(CO), and nitrogen dioxide (NO2). These additions may present challenges due to the difficulties
and costs involved in measuring them or the lack of guidelines regarding safe exposure limits for
such pollutants.
Two parameters where chosen to characterize the luminous environment: illuminance and daylight
factor.
Illuminance was categorized into the 4 quality levels by finding the percentage of time
illuminance fell between 300 lux and 500 lux. Most offices obtained red and orange quality levels due
to this relatively strict requirement. A potentially valuable addition to the luminous environment
Page 56 of 93
==PAGE:57==

characterization is measuring glare, as it was a common cause of dissatisfaction among survey
respondents. Glare was omitted from both the original TAIL and TAIL at DTU due to its relatively
complex measurement. However, considering its significant impact on occupant comfort, it remains
an important factor to address in future enhancements of the TAIL scheme.
The purpose of the noise measurement was to capture the background noise coming from equipment
such as ventilation systems. Noise complaints usually come from instantaneous or sudden noise
which is not fully captured from the measurements.
The overall TAIL classification assigned to a building was determined by its worst performing
component. However, alternative methods can be used to arrive at the final TAIL classification.

5.2
Seasonal effects on the TAIL classification
The measurement campaign at DTU was run in the late winter from the beginning of March to the
end of April. A question could be raised about whether the TAIL rating is influenced by the season
during which measurements are carried out and whether similar measurement campaigns need to be
conducted during different times in a year to better capture the IEQ experienced by occupants. The
TAIL process advises conducting measurements during at least heating and non-heating/cooling
season.
To investigate this further, an office (office 3) was selected from building 402 to be monitored twice,
with 7 weeks in between, to compare the TAIL classification obtained during the two periods.
Figures 57,58, 59, 60, 61, and 62 all show boxplots of this office in both periods to illustrate
the changes. Additionally, figure 46 shows the TAIL classification for the office during the two
measurement periods.
The thermal, acoustic and luminous environments maintained the same
quality levels but the IAQ changed from green to yellow due to lower RH% and a higher CH2O
concentration in April. Despite this change in classification, the deviations were only due to short-
term exceedances in both parameters. In April, the office exceeded the green limits for RH% for 6.4%
of the time, resulting in its demotion to the yellow category. Similarly, the CH2O concentration
reached a maximum that exceeded 30 µg/m3, which also led to its demotion to the yellow category.

With the exception of building 115, all buildings received a red category rating for their thermal
environment. The assessment was conducted during mid to late winter, a shoulder season when
heating systems were still active but outdoor temperatures were gradually increasing from March to
April. This prompts the question of whether indoor temperatures were influenced by outdoor con-
ditions. To investigate this, two analytical approaches were employed. Firstly, Pearson correlation
coefficients were calculated to find out more about the relationship between indoor and outdoor
parameters such as temperature and RH%. These findings are summarized in Table 10, revealing
notably weak correlations across all buildings, indicating minimal impact of outdoor conditions on
indoor thermal environments.
To further investigate this, a mixed effects model was constructed to quantify the contributions
of outdoor temperatures, building characteristics, and other factors to indoor temperature vari-
ations. The analysis revealed that approximately 25.8% of the variation in indoor temperatures
could be attributed to both outdoor temperatures and inherent differences between buildings, and
only 2.1% of the indoor temperature variance could be explained solely by outdoor temperatures.
This suggests that while outdoor temperature affects indoor thermal conditions, its influence is
relatively minor compared to the inherent characteristics of the building itself, which accounts for
approximately 23% of the variation. The model, however, only explains roughly 26% of the factors
influencing indoor temperature, leaving a significant portion unaccounted for. These unexplained
effects could stem from variations in building operation or occupancy dynamics, such as occupant
density, frequency of window/door openings, and individual preferences regarding temperature set-
tings. These factors introduce additional layers of complexity, impacting indoor thermal conditions
beyond the scope of the model’s analysis.

5.3
Combining Subjective and Objective TAIL
When correlating the four sensation votes to the corresponding objective measurement in table 12,
high correlations were found for the thermal, and light sensation, unlike the two other sensation
votes. This suggests that sensing odor and detecting pollutants are different; many pollutants are
colorless and odorless, making them undetectable by humans. A low correlation was also found
between noise sensation and measured sound pressure levels. This discrepancy is likely because
people are affected by both the frequency and intensity of sound [20][21], with loud, sudden noises
being more intrusive and harder to ignore than constant, regular noises, leading to higher discomfort.
Further investigations were done on the thermal sensation responses obtained from the survey. A
thermal scale was derived based on the conducted survey, with the green zone lying between the
summer and winter thermal scales from standard EN 16798, as shown in figures 36, 37, and 38. The
survey was conducted in late winter when outdoor conditions were warming, yet indoor heating
was still operating, leading to the derived thermal scale’s green zone location. The green zone was
narrower, spanning 1 °C, compared to the 2 °C span of the summer and winter scales, indicating
that the surveyed population at DTU was more sensitive to higher temperatures, as shown by the
higher slope in figure 39.
When comparing the PPD% values obtained from the logistic regression models to the quality levels
obtained from the objective measurements in table 16, only the thermal component had somewhat
similar results. The three other components (noise, IAQ, and light) had very low similarities. The
discrepancy for noise and IAQ, can be explained similarly to the discrepancy between the sensations
and measurements. The discrepancy in the light PPD% can be attributed to respondents being
annoyed by glare in their workplaces due to their office’s orientation, layout and absence of solar
shading.
A comprehensive understanding of IEQ can be achieved through a combination of measurements and
surveys. Considering the significant costs and time involved in a full-scale measurement campaign,
it may be advantageous to partially rely on surveys to capture data that such a campaign would
gather, but at a fraction of the cost. Surveys can effectively capture subjective aspects of IEQ,
such as glare, odors, and drafts, which objective measurements might miss.
Conversely, measurements and sensor setups in indoor spaces offer indispensable insights into factors
that cannot be detected by humans, such as colorless and odorless pollutants. These pollutants
can pose serious health risks, and the deployment of sensors ensures their presence is accurately
monitored and managed.
In future iterations of developing the TAIL rating scheme, it could be beneficial to split it into
two distinct components: Subjective TAIL and Objective TAIL. The Subjective TAIL would rely
on survey data to capture occupants’ sensations and perceptions of the indoor environment, while
the Objective TAIL would depend on precise measurements and sensor data. These two compo-
nents would complement each other, providing a holistic view of the IEQ that encompasses both
measurable data and human experience.
For instance, while sensors detect pollutants, subjec-
tive surveys could highlight areas where occupants feel uncomfortable or notice issues that sensors
cannot quantify, such as distracting noise levels. By integrating both subjective feedback and ob-

jective data, facility managers and designers could implement more effective and occupant-centered
improvements.
5.4
Impact of the Flourishing Index on IEQ
Clustering the external data survey respondents revealed that the group with a higher overall IEQ
assessment also had a higher Flourishing Index. This was not the case, however, for the DTU survey
respondents. An interesting finding was that the cluster with higher IEQ and Flourishing Index, in
the external dataset, had a higher mean age. The domain with the highest discrepancy between the
clusters was domain six: Financial and Material Stability. This is likely because the cluster with a
higher Flourishing Index was older and thus potentially more financially stable. There are notable
differences between the two datasets. The overall IEQ was much higher in the DTU dataset, which
could indicate either a better indoor climate or a population with different standards and levels of
satisfaction. Another difference is that there was a distinction between the clusters in the external
dataset, where the cluster with the higher IEQ also had a higher Flourishing Index. The DTU
dataset, on the other hand, had very similar overall IEQ assessments in both clusters. This could
indicate that the Flourishing measure may play a role in the overall IEQ assessment; however, this
is not always the case as it could be population-dependent.
From the random forest model, the predictor with the highest impact on the model was the weighted
Flourishing Index followed by the thermal sensation. When testing the model’s accuracy using
external data, it showed lower accuracy compared to using 40% of DTU data.
This increased
accuracy with exclusively data from DTU, is likely due to the homogeneity of the data, as all
responses originated from the same population, reducing variability. The variability in external
data, could be attributable to the differences in age groups, preferences, and environments among
the external respondents, which introduced more variability and complexity, thereby challenging
the model’s predictive capability.

6
Conclusions
The TAIL rating scheme was successfully applied to assess the IEQ in five office buildings at
DTU’s campus in Kongens Lyngby. The measurements were effectively completed in a timely man-
ner. Moreover, the analysis of data and assignment of ratings were carried out reproducibly. The
rating scheme effectively differentiated between various IEQ categories across offices in five different
buildings. Additionally, a comprehensive schematic for the IEQ of each building was developed,
which is easily understandable by specialists, as well as building occupants. Moreover, a survey was
conducted among the selected buildings’ occupants to assess their IEQ sensations and perceptions.
Higher correlations between the objective measurements and the subjective evaluations were found
in the thermal and luminous environments. PPD% values corresponding to orange and red quality
levels, obtained from logistic regression models, indicated that subjective evaluations capture find-
ings that objective measurements, which obtained better quality levels in some components, fail to
capture. These findings highlight the value of combining objective measurements with subjective
evaluations to gain a comprehensive understanding of IEQ. The objective and subjective elements
complemented each other, providing insights in areas the other did not.
The derived thermal scale revealed that the DTU population remained in the thermally neutral
zone within a range of temperatures that fall between the summer and winter ranges of the EN
16798 standard. Moreover, the DTU population exhibited a narrower green zone on the thermal
scale, indicating a higher sensitivity to temperature increases compared to the standard.
This
highlights that while the standards serve as a useful starting point for building designers, different
groups of people, with varying ages, genders, and preferences, necessitate tailored solutions rather
than a "one size fits all" approach.
Furthermore, the integration of the Flourishing measure survey provided a holistic perspective,
enhancing the evaluation process by considering both environmental conditions and overall occu-
pant well-being. An influence of the Flourishing Index on the overall IEQ assessment was found
through the random forest model and cluster analysis. The random forest model revealed that the
Flourishing Index was the most important predictor of the overall IEQ.
Future research could build on these results by applying the TAIL rating scheme in diverse building
types and locations to validate its broader applicability and refine it further. Additionally, more
analyses are needed to properly quantify the effect of the Flourishing Index on IEQ assessments
and determine which domains within the Flourishing Measure have the most significant impact on
occupants’ IEQ assessments.
