Abstract
In pursuing a sustainable future, the world increasingly uses renewable energy sources
to meet its growing power demands. Wind energy has emerged as a leading contender
among the various renewable options. While onshore and shallow water offshore wind
has significantly contributed to the world energy supply, offshore wind at greater waôÙ
ter depths than 60 meters remains largely untapped. The floating wind technology has
emerged as a promising solution to access wind in deep waters by harnessing the power
of wind turbines installed on floating structures at sea. Floating wind is a novel technolôÙ
ogy that still faces the challenges of lowering costs. Among these is the simplifaction of
the mooring system and the offshore operations of commissioning and decommissionôÙ
ing. Through design and innovation methods, this thesis will investigate how singleôÙpoint
mooring can simplify installation processes and lower costs while maintaining the dynamic
performance requirements of the mooring system. The thesis will iterate and develop
various concepts and test their performance and validity for the 15 MW NREL turbine
attached to the TetraSub. The thesis will prove a minimal viable product and find the
bestôÙperforming concept and its known challenges.

Introduction
As climate change becomes more and more pressing globally, governments around the
world are looking for new sustainable energy technologies to match the demand for power
in an increasingly electrified world. Most governments are currently preoccupied with harôÙ
vesting their national potential of renewables in areas like the solar and onshore wind as
the most costôÙefficient technology options available. However, there is still a vast unexôÙ
ploited potential for both offshore wind and floating wind (FOWT) in particular.
The global offshore bottomôÙfixed industry has the potential to supply sufficient energy to
support the whole world, according to IEA in their ãOffshore wind outlook 2019ã report[1].
However, sites viable for bottomôÙfixed wind energy capture are not evenly distributed,
and many countries needing electricity cannot produce their own bottomôÙfixed energy.
IEA states that the entire offshore market, including floating wind, will have a potential of
420 000 TWh per year worldwide. This is 18 times the global demand.
Today, floating wind technology is an emergent, immature, and not yet fully proven techôÙ
nology. The industry is vibrant and offers many different floater concepts. The interest
from developers and investors has increased immensely in the last few years as it beôÙ
comes clear that many markets will soon need more available sites and new technology
options. Most of the worldãs offshore wind resources are located in waters deeper than 60
meters, making it highly feasible to expand the development of Floating Offshore WindôÙ
turbine (FOWT) at these depths.
Unlike traditional offshore bottomôÙfixed wind turbines, which are limited to shallow waters,
FOWT offers several advantages, such as access to stronger and more consistent winds
in deeper waters, minimal visual impact on the coastline, and the ability to deploy turbines
in locations where fixed foundations are impractical or cost prohibitive. However, FOWT
is still an emerging technology with only about 150 MW installed capacity in a handful of
demonstrator projects, mostly in Europe.
2.1
The Floating Wind Challenges
The floating wind industry has gained considerable attention as a promising renewable
energy technology. However, several challenges inhibit its widespread deployment and
competitiveness. Key challenges include high costs, scalability, and complexity in offshore
operations[1].
The cost factor remains a significant challenge for floating wind technology. Floating wind
projects entail higher costs than traditional fixedôÙbottom offshore wind installations. To
boost the industryãs competitiveness reducing costs is imperative for enhancing the inôÙ
dustryãs viability and market competitiveness.
Due to the novelty and maturity of the structures and procedures, installation and mainôÙ
tenance pose significant challenges for FOWT. Streamlining installation procedures and
improving processes is vital for operational efficiency and cost reduction.
2.2
FOWT Moorings and the single point concept.
Efficient and reliable mooring systems are essential to fully realizing the potential of floatôÙ
ing wind farms. The Single Point Mooring (SPM) systems have emerged as a potential
technological solution for floating wind turbines to ensure stability, safety, and operational

2.3
Stiesdal
Stiesdal Offshore is a technology company focusing on developing costôÙcompetitive reôÙ

FOWT concepts to reach offshore winds at any depth. The mooring system must be roôÙ

of interest. Stiesdal Offshore is interested in exploring the opportunities and performance

2.4
Case description
This thesis evaluates the possibilities of singleôÙpoint mooring from a design and innovaôÙ
tion perspective through the development of potential concepts. The thesis subject and
preliminary description were decided between Stiesdal and DTU, creating the opportunity
for this thesis. The case description was the following: ãCreate a Single Point Mooring
concept that enables disconnection of a 15 MW TetraSub for greater service operations
to minimize complexity inôÙ and costs of service of floating windã.
2.5
The Scope of the thesis
This thesis explores the conceptualization of an SPM system and its initial positional and
monetary effects on a floating wind structure. This study will delve into the fundamental
aspects of an SPM system, including its structural components, mooring configurations,
and integration with a 15 megawatt (MW) floating wind turbine. Additionally, it will explore
the estimated gains of designing and implementing an effective SPM system, such as
functionality, installation process, and cost reductions.
The thesis will first explain the structure and methods used for the thesis, then present
essential background knowledge that leads to the project description, concept iterations,
evaluation, and validation. Then, the results will be presented, and a discussion will deôÙ
termine the favorable concept and future work. The thesis will end with a conclusion that
goes into further work.

Methodology
This section describes the methods used to structure the project and some of the main
methods that lead to the thesis results.
To achieve viable concepts and evaluate their performance, the following methods have
been utilized: literature research, interviews, development arena, storyboards, Product
Requirement Specification (PRS) lists, Structured Representation (SR) list, MVP, KPI,
workshop, categorized brainstorms, brain walks, morphology, dynamic simulations, evalôÙ
uation workshops, cost analysis, risk assessment, and Pugh.
3.1
Project structure
To structure the project, the double diamond method has been used. The doubleôÙdiamond
design process generally structures projects, focusing on problemôÙsolving while encourôÙ
aging diverging and converging design thinking[2]. The double diamond consists of four
phases to expand or constrict the project scope and idea space [3]. The project case
initially described a problem statement of designing a singleôÙpoint mooring concept for
a FOWT. To avoid the risks of creating a product that fails to generate actual value for
the company, the project ãworked backwardsã to understand where the problem definition
came from before the idea space was investigated [3].
The project deals with a high level of complexity, and discoveries are regularly made
throughout the process. Discoveries can have various effects on what a good concept
might look like, and, to accommodate this, rigidity in the criteria and concepts must be
mitigated. Therefore, the projectãs structure was kept dynamic, and ongoing changes to
performance criteria and concepts were made. An iterative approach has been used to
avoid the rigidity of the double diamond structure. Iterations enable documentation and
revision of initially determined factors, enabling corrections and changes in the projectãs
direction[2].

4.1
Offshore Fixed Structures
Offshore structures consist of offshore oil and gas platforms and offshore wind turbines[6].
These structures are fixed in different ways to the desired location. The Oil and Gas
industry has developed and used three types of ways to keep fixed structures at a certain
location: bottom fixed, dynamically positioned, and moored structures.
BottomôÙfixed structures are the most mature type of offshore structure and are widely
used by the oil and gas and wind industries in shallow waters. The cost of the structures
increases exponentially with the water depth increasing. Within the wind industry, bottomôÙ
fixed turbines are limited to 50 meters of water depth.
Dynamic Positioning (DP) is a temporary solution for mobile structures used at loading
stations. DP is the concept of not having any fixation on the structureãs location. The platôÙ
form instead has engines attached to keep it from drifting away. One study showed that,
for a wind turbine of 5 MW, the system would use about 75% of the generated energy[7].
Since offshore wind turbines are permanent installations, DP is not considered viable.

4.3.1
Anchor and mooring line installation
The mooring line components and anchors are delivered to a port near the chosen site.
The components will be stored at the port until assembly can occur. The installation reôÙ

lines and the anchors are transported onto an installation vessel and sailed out to the sea.
At the site, the anchors are the first to be installed. A messenger line carries the anchors
down to their position one by one. A Remotely Operated Underwater Vehicle (ROV) brings
visuals to the operation and disconnects the messenger line from the anchor when put in
position. The mooring lines will be attached after all the anchors have been placed. The
mooring lines are lowered into the water and then connected to the anchors by the ROV.
They are then laid out in a ãUôÙshapeã to avoid tangling and to make it easy to retrieve the
cable ends when the TetraSub is being installed. When the installation is complete, the
installation vessel returns to the port.

arrived, the assembly can commence. The TetraSub is assembled using Self Propelled
Modular Transporter (SPMT)s and a port crane. Additional technicians are present if
painting or welding is needed. The components are assembled on the port.

4.7
Mooring structures
To access offshore wind, different mooring structures have been utilized. Within 50 meôÙ
ters in depth, bottomôÙfixed offshore wind turbines are the optimal solution[23]. These
foundations are fixed either by gravity or drilling. These are the most mature offshore
wind energy capture structures, and this thesis will not explore them since they show funôÙ
damental issues when going into deeper waters. Floating wind platforms, on the other
hand, are mainly applicable at 50ôÙmeter depths and deeper.
Within fixed floating platforms, two new categories arise: Spread mooring and singleôÙpoint
mooring. Spread mooring is a system where multiple mooring lines are connected to the
floating structure at multiple locations. It is a conventional method to fix the geostatic
position of floating wind turbines and is also used in oil and gas. The TetraSub uses
spread mooring to fix its location. The downside of spread mooring is the installation and
decommissioning of the system. Hook up and disconnection of the system is complex
and dangerous due to the weather and rough site conditions that tend to be present at
the site of the FOWT.
SingleôÙpoint mooring is a concept widely used by the oil and gas industry for production,
storage, and offloading. SingleôÙpoint mooring is a system where multiple mooring lines
are connected at a single location on the floating structure to fix it to the location. More
safety factors are required at this connection point to ensure that the structure will not
drift if this part breaks. The SPM systems do bring a lot of potential in simplifying the
installation and decommissioning processes with simple hookôÙup procedures. SPM also
enables structures to passively follow the direction of the wind, which could simplify the
turbine structure and save costs.

1500 meters depth [24]. The columns on a TLP act as pontoons that create buoyancy
force while mooring lines are tensioned so that the TLP is not at a buoyant level. This
way, the structure is restrained in vertical and rotational motion while still compliant in
horizontal motion. The TLP configuration uses a significantly smaller mooring line but
requires a stronger anchor system, and installation time is also significantly higher[25].
4.8.3
SemiôÙtaut mooring
SemiôÙtaut mooring is a hybrid design of catenary and TLP mooring, where cables are
tensioned so that the mooring line does not contact the seabed while still positioned in
a spreadôÙout formation. The system does not cost as much in installation and anchor
systems as the TLP and uses less mooring line per system than the catenary system.

4.10
Weathervane Mechanism
Not all mooring systems have a weathervane effect in their design, but minimizing the
forces on the mooring lines can be desirable. Three types of mechanisms enable the
vessel to weathervane: Swivels, Universal joints, and bearings.
Swivel is a type of joint with two connection points that allow rotation in their respecôÙ
tive axis. They technically have three rotational axes. Swivel systems are often used in
dynamic and fuel cables to give the line more flexibility[24].
Universal joints consist of two rotational axes that allow rotation in any direction. They
are sturdy and only have two moving areas making them less at risk of breaking. However,
there is a risk with these locking when moving around (there are positions where specific
directions of movement will not be possible) [30].
Bearings are simple in that they are parts that only enable rotation around a particular
axisëƒ however, they become more complex in mooring systems since they have to deal
with the forces of the environment. Bearing systems are safe and at lower risk of breaking
than swivels and universal joints, but they also tend to be more expensive within mooring
designs.

SingleôÙpoint mooring systems have been widely used in the Oil and Gas industry, and,

Failures in mooring systems
It is advantageous to look at previous systems and their failures to understand further
the challenges and risks of mooring systems. The majority of mooring failures are due
to fatigue and corrosion [24]. There are many reasons for failure in mooring systems.
However, most failures occur close to the fairlead [24]. The component that failed the
most between 2001 and 2011 was the mooring chains, according to Ma KaiôÙTung in his
historical review of integrity issues for permanent mooring systems [24]. In his study, it is
apparent that most breaks occur close to connections and close to the surface or anchor.
The failures happen in these areas due to the environment being harsher there. At the end
of the line, close to the anchor, mooring lines tend to fail because the wear of touching the
seabed weakens the chain further. Failures at the surface area occur within the splashing
zone where waves apply greater forces to the systems and create more motion. This
weakens the mooring lines and sometimes ends in premature failure of components.

CALM
Advantages: The CALM system is an easy maintenance system that has matured sigôÙ

Disadvantages: The CALM system is connected with a Hawser. The system is prone

SBS
Advantages: The SBS system is similar to the SALM but uses a Yoke connection to limit
the risk of collision. The system requires minimal modification to the floating structure and
is relatively robust. The System has a storage unit which results in continuous loading.

6.1.2
Component considerations
No mooring system can be directly transferred from the oil and gas industry into the offôÙ
shore wind industry since safety risks and forces applied to the systems vary significantly.
However, many components and solutions to different subôÙfunctions can be valuable to
study and identify. Another table was constructed to showcase the advantages and disôÙ
advantages of different components and systems within mooring. Some features are
mentioned belowëƒ the full table can be found in Appendix C.
Mooring Line Spread
Spread mooring is the conventional mooring system that ensures a small mooring radius
and is safe in case of a oneôÙline breakage. The mooring design is, however, expensive
in materials and installation process.
Single line mooring is a cheap option for mooring since it only uses one mooring line
and one anchor. The disadvantage is that the cable size is difficult to manufacture, and
no oneôÙline breakage designs are incorporated. Lastly, the design has a large mooring
radius, making site utilization inefficient. The mooring radius is the area where the floating
structure can be located within the limits of the mooring system.
Fixed bottom mooring is a safe structure that works well in shallow waters. However,
the price increases exponentially when moving to deeper waters, making it impracticable
at many sites.
Mooring type
Catenary mooring is a mooring system made for medium and deep waters. They work
well even in sites with rough seabeds but require a lot of material in mooring lines.
TLP mooring Is a cheaper system that ensures a small mooring radius. It experiences
limited wear since the mooring lines do not contact the seabed directly. It has a complex
installation process and lacks a oneôÙline breakage design.
Semi Taut mooring uses less mooring line material and avoids some of the wear that
catenary mooring systems experience. It also has a smaller mooring radius but has a
more complex installation and is still more expensive than the TLP.
Connection type
Internal connection systems are safe systems where the hookôÙup procedure does not
set any manpower at risk. The system avoids the forces of waves and wind during hookôÙ
up, but they are typically expensive and complex designs. They are often relatively large
constructions.
External connection systems are also relatively safe and have a quick hookôÙup proceôÙ
dure. They are often relatively large constructions. They are sensitive to vertical motions.
Hawser connection is the simplest type of connection system. The system is cheap in
materials and is relatively easy to hook up. The system lacks a oneôÙline breakage design
and is at risk of collision between the buoy and the floating structure. This connection is
not viable for a FOWT mooring system since collisions would significantly limit the product
lifetime of both the floater and the buoy.
Rigid Arm connection is often used as a permanent construction. The system avoids
collision risks and is relatively cheap. The system must account for bending forces and
fatigue in joints.
Yoke connection is similar to the rigid arm system but also has a passive restoration
of forces due to its pendulum effect. This reduces loads on joints. It is, however, more
Conceptualization of a Single Point Mooring System for Floating Offshore Wind Turbines

==PAGE:44==
expensive to build, and fatigue issues are more present in this solution than in the rigid
arm.
Weathervane mechanism
Turret is a safe solution with a simple hookôÙup design and low maintenance. If the system
needs a weathervane effect, the bearing system must be produced within the vessel. This
is expensive and can be complex in its design.
Turntable is a cheap option for creating weathervane effects. The vessel consists of two
parts. One rigid bottom part is geostatic and connects to the mooring lines. And One
dynamic part, the turntable, is connected through bearings to the geostatic part and the
vessel to allow it to rotate with the weather. The vessel design is simple but requires a
more complex and frequent maintenance procedure.
Swivel/Universal joint are options with the weathervane mechanism disconnected from
the major structures. It is a cheap solution for standard buoys but complicates the dynamic
cable design regarding its rotation system.

At this point, various concepts had been developed, but their level of realism varied. It

7.1.1
The ALC ôÙ subsea swivel anchor
The first concept set aside was the subôÙsea swivel buoy. Even though the concept showed
promise in its simplicity of connection procedures to the TetraSub, it had significant chalôÙ

applied to the structure. The thickness of the mooring line already reached the limits of the

conditions to deploy anchors deeply into the ground.
7.1.2
The Subsea swivel buoy
The subôÙsea swivel buoy was another fully submerged buoy system. This buoy avoided
the challenges of being a single connection cable and anchor and still avoids collision
risks with the structure. However, many challenges emerge when the buoy must be fully
submersible. The buoy is designed with an internal bearing system and slip ring. HowôÙ
ever, no similar products are currently available with a waterproof bearing system that
can withstand the excessive forces that the structure will apply to the buoy, and impleôÙ
menting a slip ring system within this system would only make the system more complex.
These challenges would make the products significantly more complex, misaligned with
the Stiesdal strategy of making simple and effective products [9].
7.1.3
Internal buoy
The last concept that was set aside was the internal buoy. The internal buoy was a comôÙ
plex solution that enabled easy inspection of the buoy system and cable handling. HowôÙ
ever, it was expensive and would require many resources in manufacturing to become
scalable. The Internal buoy system integrated a moon pool in the Front tank of the TetraôÙ
Sub. The moon pool enabled a safe installation procedure but used complex mechanisms
and components to achieve this. The moon pool would be difficult to produce, and it was
important that it could be completely sealed off during rough weather if it was not to take in
water. This would become highly difficult when the system had the buoy within the moon
pool when the structure would be installed. Because of the complexity of implementing a
moon pool and the fact that it would also need modifications to the door design, it seemed
too complex and expensive compared to other potential concepts[45].

7.3
Identical design choices
Some elements in the design of the concepts were identical. The design choices are
discussed in this section.
7.3.1
Spar buoy design
The concepts all had the same challenges and initial constraints they must overcome.
The Spar buoy design was chosen because of the wide range of benefits it can produce.
The Spar system ensured that the buoy maintains an upright position at all times deôÙ
ployed. The spar system helped keep the internal bearing system and the slip ring dry.
Furthermore, it was also very beneficial during installation that the buoy stayed in the
same position since hookôÙup procedures would become significantly faster.
The Tension legged buoy system would also keep the buoy somewhat upright. This could
be a viable solution though due to the expected loads the system experiences, the system
would likely be too rigid for a singleôÙpoint mooring system at this scale. Furthermore, a
tensionôÙlegged buoy would also be limited to being placed in waters with limited tidal
effects since the bearing system must avoid submerging fully.
The third option was a submerged buoy system. The buoy would also stay upright, but
having any rotational parts underwater will produce a wide range of challenges and comôÙ
plexity to the concept. Because of this, it was discarded as an option for an industrialized
concept. This was discussed in section 6.5.2
7.3.2
Production methods
The three concepts all strive to be scalable and industrialized at an early stage of develôÙ
opment. To do so, choosing preexisting production methods already scaled up for large
productions was advantageous. Because of this, the buoy would be produced similarly to
turbine towers. This was already the production method of the Tetra models that Stiesdal
produces, so the production is favorably located together.
7.3.3
Slip ring system
As discussed at the start of the report, the Slip ring system had been blackôÙboxed to
minimize the scope of the thesis. For that reason, all concepts implemented a simplified
slipôÙring system. The slip ring system would not go into detail about its weight or dimenôÙ
sions as well as its capabilities, though the location of the slip ring system must keep it
safe from water. The bearing systems in the designs are also set to be the supporting
factor that would withstand most of the loads the system experiences.
7.3.4
Dynamic cable connection
The dynamic cable must carry highôÙvoltage power from the turbine to the energy grid.
In many instances, the cableãs core would be made of copper, which is corrosive[46].
The core was restricted to not being in contact with the seawater. This was challenging
to ensure in components at sea, especially fully submerged components. The dynamic
cable connection would consist of a conventional dryôÙmate connection. DryôÙmate connecôÙ
tions consist of a sealing component and male and female connection parts between the
two cable ends. Companies have been producing dryôÙmate connections for a long time
to connect cables at bay or on the deck of a vessel. New concepts like WetôÙmate conôÙ
nections have recently been developed that enable subsea connecting procedures [47].
The WetôÙmate connections are highly interesting concepts for the FOWT industry as they
could significantly simplify the installation phase. However, due to a lack of accessible
information and the novelty of the concepts, the wetôÙmate connections were be considôÙ
ered in this report. Instead, the four concepts will use dryôÙmate connectors to mitigate the
design challenges of the concepts.

The buoy system must attach to the mooring lines to keep its position. To solve this, the

Various mooring systems with distinct pros and cons are at our disposal. In the oil and gas

not only facilitated the observation of variations in behavior within a simulated environment
but also enabled direct comparisons between buoy designs. Furthermore, it was outside
the scope of Stiesdal Offshore to enter the market of anchor and mooring line designs.
The mooring line design would depend on the productãs environment and would therefore
match this rather than having the design of the buoy determine the design.

Conclusion
In the pursuit of a sustainable future, the world is increasingly turning towards renewable
energy sources. Among these sources, wind energy has evolved into a leading contender
to fossil fuels. While onshore and shallow water offshore wind turbines successfully harôÙ
ness wind power, the ocean winds are largely untouched. The novel technology of floating
wind has the potential to reach the wind in deeper waters. However, challenges are yet
to be matched within the floating wind. InstallationôÙ, component, and production costs
are still large, making floating wind a relatively costly solution. This thesis explored the
opportunities within singleôÙpoint mooring systems to simplify installation procedures and
reduce the costs of floating wind.
The thesis follows the structure of the double diamond by exploring the problem space,
then analyzing data synthesizing the problem definition, an MVP, and KPIs to rate the
concepts. Interviews, storyboards, development arenas, PRS lists, SR lists, MVPs, KPIs,
workshops, categorized brainstorms, brain walks, morphology matrices, simulations, inôÙ
stallation evaluations, cost analysis, WOM, and a risk evaluation were used to find and
select the best concept.
Through extensive research, a general understanding of mooring systems was estabôÙ
lished. The TetraSub was presented, and an understanding of the installation method
was obtained through interviews and the creation of storyboards. Development arenas
on different product life stages of the TetraSub helped clarify the relevant actors and their
relationship. The mooring engineers were of particular interest since they held considôÙ
erable power, communicated with all actors, and were present during all stages of the
product life.
The research from the background was analyzed, and the scope of the project was esôÙ
tablished through use of converging methods like the PRS and SR list. This lead to the
problem statement:
Make a scalable detachable weathervaning singleôÙpoint mooring system for a 15 MW
FOWT to optimize maintenance operations and the installation phase.
To limit the scope further, an MVP was developed stating:
The concept must be able to disconnect and reconnect and rotate with the wind. It must
endure the effects of a viable site by complying with the DNVôÙSTôÙ0119. It must be deôÙ
signed for industrialization and be maintenanceôÙfree
KPIs were established to rate the concepts that fulfilled the criteria of the MVP and find
the bestôÙperforming concept.
The solution space was explored with a creative workshop. The workshop was conôÙ
structed and performed with Stiesdal mooring engineers. The workshopãs goal was to
utilize the knowledge of the experts to ideate and conceptualize. This resulted in a range
of categorized ideas and six concepts later used for the first set of iterations. A range
of singleôÙpoint mooring systems from The Oil and Gas industry were analyzed and evalôÙ
uated in their ability to be utilized for floating wind. The goal of these evaluations was
to find viable solutions for floating wind. An individual brainstorm was held to propagate

the evaluation and the valuable knowledge from previous research. The brainstorm led
to 46 ideas within eight categories that could be combined into concepts. A morphology
matrix was created to structure the conceptualization and create unique concepts. Five
concepts were constructed from the morphology for further evaluation.
The Second iteration time cut down on the concepts and introduced two new concepts
that spawned from the disadvantages of other concepts. The Riser buoy was introduced
to enable a submerged buoy design that did not submerge any critical parts or show too
much complexity in its docking station. The snufferôÙless buoy was designed to avoid
redundancy in the boarding design of the snuffer buoy. The new concepts were then
tested in one of the performance parameters, the installation process.
Three interviews were conducted with mooring experts to judge the performance of the
designs and discuss their functionality. The interviews aimed to get a tangible understandôÙ

more complexity than it removed, while the external buoy required redesign to ensure
safety and simplicity in operations. According to the experts, the Riser buoy showed the
best simple and safe results. The snuffer buoy showed promise but produced risks using
cables to operate the snuffer head.
The concepts were required to comply with the MVP. To test this, the concepts were
evaluated in their installation process, simulated to test the functionality, and analyzed on
the costs of the concepts.
Buoyancy calculations were performed to ensure that the product could be buoyant and
still within the production limits of the turbine industry. The dynamic performance was
simulated with Orcaflex to test the designs in extreme conditions. The concepts were
tested in 50ôÙyear storms following the DNVôÙSTôÙ0119 certification. Both concepts showed
markedly positive results with opportunities to reduce the mooring line size and quality
to minimize costs further. The external and riser buoy showed a smaller offset than the
snuffer buoy, which could lead to a cheaper dynamic mooring cable system.
A cost analysis was then constructed to understand the potential costs and savings the
concepts could bring to a FOWT. The analysis focused on phases where the SPM conôÙ
cepts would deviate from the conventional TetraSub. The analysis aimed to showcase
the sensitivity of certain parameters and the potential savings caused by implementing
the different concepts. The concepts showed considerable promise in saving millions
of EUR per concept. In comparison to the conventional TetraSub, where maintenance
would occur once, the snuffer head would save 9,3 million EUR, The External buoy would
save 10,1 million EUR, and the Riser buoy would save 12,8 million EUR, almost costing
oneôÙthird of the conventional design.
The three final concepts were the snuffer buoy, the external buoy, and the riser buoy. The
snuffer buoy is connected with a conical ãsnufferã that is lowered and locked on top of
the buoy. The snuffer is connected to the TetraSub by rigid arms. An SOV operates the
snuffer head during installation.
The external buoy is rigidly connected to the TetraSub with two large clamps controlled
with hydraulic pistons. The buoy has a ring structure to mitigate shear forces and is
covered by polyester to protect parts during hookup.
The Riser buoy consists of a submersible buoy and an external docking station on the
TetraSub. An ROV ballasts the buoy to make it rise into the dock of the TetraSub. The
docking station is fixed to the outside of the front tank of the TetraSub.

installation procedures.

Product Requirement specification 
 
Introduction 
 
Purpose of the document 
The purpose of the document is to specify the requirements of the Single Point Mooring (SPM) system. 
Definition of SPM system 
The SPM system is defined as a system that can be pre-installed and connected to mooring lines and array 
cables connected to the seabed and that can be connected to the Floating Wind Turbine (FOWT) when the 
FOWT has been towed to the site. 
Scope of the document 
The scope of this document is to create an overview of constraints and requirements for further detailing 
and development of the SPM system. 
 
System boundaries 
The SPM system will have the following boundaries: 
Mechanical boundaries: 
ãÂ 
Interface with FOWT at the FOWT side of the interface. 
ãÂ 
Interface with mooring lines connected to anchors at the seabed, at shackle terminating the 
mooring lines. 
ãÂ 
Interface with array cables rising from the seabed at the top end of cables. 
Electrical boundaries: 
ãÂ 
Electrical interface with array cable rising from the seabed. 
ãÂ 
Electrical interface with FOWT. 
 
Solution Space 
The solution space comprises the following: 
ãÂ 
The SPM system should enable disconnection of the FOWT from the grid and the mooring system. 
ãÂ 
The SPM system must transfer the forces from the floating structure to the mooring cables. 
ãÂ 
 The SPM system should withstand a realistic set of waves in Orcaflex simulations. 
ãÂ 
The FOWT should allow weathervaning of the FOWR without cable twisting.  
ãÂ 
The mooring system must have an estimated product life of 25 years to comply with the product 
life of FOWT. 
ãÂ 
The mooring system should incorporate a suggested solution to the risks of collision between buoy 
system and FOWT.

These are constants set before calculating. The G is gativational acceleration. Rho_w is Density of 
water. Rho_s is density of steel. F_moor is the forces applied vertically by the mooring system.
Constants set as assumptions
To make the calculations some constants need to be predefined. These are defined below here.
The Diameter of the buoy will be 8 m. This is a viable size compared to the production technique.
The thickness of the buoy walls will be 30 mm as this is also viable in the production capabilities.
The mooring lines must be attached minimum 10 meters below seasurface

height below mooring lines.
The conical part between top part and bottom part is 0.5 m tall to enable the turntable part to avoid 
sticking out.
The top above surface part of the buoy has been set to 10 meters.

will be given in accordance to the coordinate system.
