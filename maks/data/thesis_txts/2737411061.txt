1
Introduction
The goal of this project is to investigate the potential of the recently released Sentinel-1 Ex-
tra Wide Swath (EW) mode Single Look Complex (SLC) products, for InSAR applications
in coastal areas of Greenland. These include motion related to landslides in snow-free ar-
eas, and ice velocities in slow moving areas of the Greenland ice sheet. In particular, it is
of interest to investigate what accuracies and spatial resolutions can be achieved with this
acquisition mode, as compared to the well established Interferometric Wide Swath (IW)
mode, and to what extent EW observations could complement IW-based motion products,
both in space and in time.
EW acquisition mode has a larger footprint and larger swath-coverage than IW-based
products. Therefore, EW products have primarily been used for amplitude-based mon-
itoring of the oceans, and in particular sea ice. SLC products, including both amplitude
and phase information, have not been necessary for the purposes of EW mode, such as
monitoring sea ice. Due to lack of presumed widespread usage of EW SLC products for
land-based motion, these have not been made available by the European Space Agency
(ESA) until April 1st 2021, even though the raw data archive is large with frequent moni-
toring in EW mode.
Motion on land has been covered by IW SLC products for InSAR interferometry only, but
ESA’s recent release of EW SLC products has opened up the possibility to use these
data for InSAR applications as well. Sentinel-1 acquires IW data on some ground tracks
and EW data on others. This reduces the revisit time with which a given area is covered.
Continuous mapping of the same areas are required, with low revisit time, for InSAR ap-
plications such as accurate ice velocity measurements of slow-moving ice or in snow-free
areas. It is investigated within this thesis, whether the large overlaps between adjacent
EW scenes can increase the accuracy of the InSAR applications obtained from IW SLC
products alone and make the coverage more complete by filling data gaps in both space
and time.
There exist, to the knowledge of the author, no other studies in Greenland investigating the
potential of EW SLC products in complementing IW SLC products for InSAR applications.
Similar studies [1], addressing questions on the potential of SLC products from EW mode,
have been made in Svalbard and Antarctica. These studies were funded by ESA and
focused on e.g. monitoring ice shelf grounding lines in the aforementioned areas. This
project takes the questions from those preliminary studies further by investigating the
potential of EW SLC data on the coastal areas of Greenland, which is an unexploited
area regarding InSAR applications from EW SLC images.
This thesis addresses investigations of the accuracies, spatial resolutions and temporal
frequency of EW SLC products in Northwest Greenland in November 2021, for mapping
ice velocities in the slow-moving areas of the Greenland ice sheet, to avoid the melting-
season. Results from interferometry and offset tracking methods have been used and
compared within this thesis. The estimated ice velocities obtained from SLC products
from EW mode have been compared against results obtained with IW SLC products.
Comparison is also done with an external velocity mosaic containing averaged ice veloc-
ities from the period 2016-2019 covering entire Greenland.
Furthermore, investigation of the potential for mapping landslides with EW SLC products
are made with interferometry in the summer-period (June-September) of 2021, covering
Karrat Fjord in the central Westgreenland.

2.1
Sentinel-1 SLC imagery
The satellite-constellation Sentinel-1 operates with a Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) at
C-band with a frequency of 5.404 GHz and hence a wavelength of approximately 5.55
cm [2]. The constellation consists of two satellites in the period between April 2016 and
December 2021, namely the Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B, each with a 12 day repeat
period meaning a temporal baseline of 6 days could be obtained with data from both
satellites in the aforementioned period [3]. In the rest of this project Sentinel-1 (S1) refers
to both satellites in the period, where they both were present and to Sentinel-1A only in
the period between April 2014 [4] and April 2016 and again from December 2021 until
now. The letter is specified after Sentinel-1 whenever distinguishing is needed.

2.2
Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR)
Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) is a method using Single-Look Complex
(SLC) imagery to create interferograms and Line-of-Sight (LoS) displacements through
phase signals and phase unwrapping. InSAR has many applications such as monitoring
deformations related to for example earthquakes and rock avalanches and is useful to
measure ice velocities in slow-moving areas [11]. Seasonal changes can also be mea-
sured by InSAR, and according to [12], areas with flow higher than 0.3 m/day are subject
to seasonal variations.
The SLC-data are acquired through Terrain Observation by Progressive Scans (TOPS).
The coupling of azimuth lines to the phase signal from Line-of-Sight is the main problem
with TOPS interferometry [11].
Compared with offset tracking, InSAR can reach more accurate results with a higher res-
olution of velocity estimates depending on which acquisition mode is used [11]. A coarser
resolution is obtained from images with wider coverage [13] as e.g. EW SLC images.
A spatial resolution better than 100 m is typically required for the best estimates of ice
displacements and velocities [13].

2.2.1
Co-registration of image pairs
In repeat-pass interferometry an image pair is formed, covering approximately the same
area from two repeating orbits with at least a little overlap [14]. Resampling (e.g. interpo-
lating) of the image pair is applied as part of the co-registration process, which converts
the geometry of the slave image to the one for the master image and align the overlap
between them. This is performed based on knowledge of the SAR position from orbital
state vectors and a Digital Elevation Model (DEM). The resampling requires some time-
consuming geometric calculations, which are only performed on a small subset of the
original image points.
2.2.2
Interferogram formation
After image co-registration of an image pair, a wrapped phase interferogram is made [15],
representing the sum of the phase variations due to flat Earth, topography changes and
motion. Flattening of the Earth is removed with an Earth ellipsoid and the topographic
variations are corrected for with a Digital Elevation Model. After corrections the phase
variations (fringes) are assumed to represent motion between the two acquisition times
of the image pair. One interferometric fringe corresponds to a displacement of around 2.8
cm, which is half of the wavelength for the C-band SAR images from Sentinel-1 as stated
in section 2.1.
Coherence and decorrelation
The normalized complex cross-correlation, γ, between two complex signals, also called
coherence [15], can be calculated as seen in equation 2.1:
γ =
⟨s1s2⟩
√
⟨s1s1⟩× ⟨s2s2⟩
, 0 ≤|γ| ≤1
(2.1)
Here s1 and s2 are the SLC-images and the overline above the signals refer to the com-
plex conjugate of that signal. A coherence (γ) of 1 means two identical images and a
coherence of 0 means completely different images, often due to noise. Decorrelation is
due to speckle, which can be statistically uncorrelated in the two images, e.g. due to
snowfall between the two acquisition times. Variations between images will reveal lower
coherence and can be due to motion. For some InSAR applications such as measuring
ice movements of an ice sheet, a short temporal baseline will provide a higher coherence
than a longer temporal baseline, since the changes due to motion are considered low and
the coverage of the measurements will be high, but it will also be more sensitive to other
error sources.
The perpendicular baseline, or normal baseline, is the distance between the SAR posi-
tions of the acquisition times. This is not a constant parameter due to the satellite orbit.
By reducing the perpendicular baseline the differences induced by the incidence angle
will decrease, since these parameters are proportional to each other [15].
Multilooking
The phase variance related to speckle can be reduced in an effective way with spatial
averaging, also known as multilooking, since speckle has a weak spatial correlation. Mul-
tilooking is therefore applied to reduce the speckle dominating the interferograms. The
images are filtered with a chosen window size in range and azimuth respectively. Av-
eraging is increased with higher multilooking parameters, but it comes with a prize of
decreasing the resolution of the final interferogram and a potential risk of smoothing out
relevant correlation due to motion.

The multilooking parameters should be chosen wisely by looking at both the standard
deviation and the coherence to get the best output resolution for the purpose of the inter-
ferogram.
Phase jumps at burst boundaries
A possible presence of phase jumps at burst boundaries is a particularity of Sentinel-1’s
Terrain Observation by Progressive Scans (TOPS) mode for interferometry. This pro-
cess, combining phase signals from azimuth and Line-of-Sight (LoS), constitutes a huge
problem, especially in the areas of overlapping bursts [11] (see section 2.1).
One of the things multilooking cannot fully eliminate is these phase jumps, which can
indicate true motion in the azimuth direction and is a matter of how it is interpreted [16].
Phase jumps often occur in the burst-overlap region and between sub-swaths in an image.
Phase jumps can be produced by many error sources, for example ionospheric scintilla-
tions [16] (spatial fluctuations [17]) or differences in the azimuth steering angles from the
antenna [16].
Refinement of the co-registered images can reduce this problem with phase discontinu-
ities in overlapping burst regions.
Burst Overlap Spectral Diversity (BO-SD) is one method and can be used in addition to
co-registering with respect to the orbit state vectors, Digital Elevation Models (DEM) and
External Velocity Mosaics (EXTVEL) [18].
Burst Overlap Multi-Aperture Interferometry (BO-MAI) is another method of reducing phase
discontinuities [11], [19] and there are many more.
Common to both methods is that the co-registration errors are corrected and thereafter
the differences between the interferometric phases and burst-overlaps are recalculated
for the azimuth displacements (along-track) [20].
2.2.3
Interferometric phase unwrapping
Next step in the InSAR process is a phase unwrapping, where the modulo 2π ambiguity
[14] from the wrapped phase interferogram are removed. The unwrapping of the phases
can be done with branch-cutting and referenced to a pixel, where no motion is detected.
Therefore the unwrapped phase interferogram is a relative interferogram depending on
the stability of the reference pixel.
2.2.4
Creating and geocoding LoS ice velocity maps
After phase unwrapping of the interferogram, the phase deformation is converted to LoS
displacements by taking the incidence angle into consideration.
The phase deformations (ϕdeformation) are scaled with a negative scaling factor to LoS
displacements (dLoS) through this simplified equation 2.2:
dLoS = −λ
4πϕdeformation
(2.2)
With this sign convention, negative values represent displacements away from the radar,
whereas positive values are displacements towards the radar along LoS.
Coherence-based weights are applied to remove pixels with lower coherence than a cho-
sen threshold to avoid speckle [11].
Velocities are obtained by dividing the displacements with the temporal baseline. The ve-
locity map in SAR geometry (range and azimuth) is geocoded [16] by interpolating the map
points in SAR geometry to geographic coordinates or to an appropriate map projection.

Baseline calibration with Ground Control Points (GCP’s)
Since the unwrapped phase measurements also include image-wide variations due to at-
mospheric propagation and orbital uncertainties, a calibration procedure based on Ground
Control Points (GCP’s) can be used. The GCP’s are chosen as pixels or points in the rel-
evant geometric area, where the value of the motion is known. The knowledge of this
exact value can be assumed to have zero motion or be obtained from external datasets.
In areas affected by ionospheric errors, GCP’s can play a crucial role in determining the
correct velocities. The statistical noise will be reduced more, if more GCP’s are used [17].
2.2.5
Fusion of velocity maps
Since InSAR only provides measurements in radar LoS, and motion is in general 3D, it is
required to combine InSAR measurement from independent geometries, to separate the
motion components. Typically only two independent geometries are available, namely
those from ascending and descending passes. Some assumption is therefore required
to derive the motion components. In the context of ice-motion for example, the assump-
tion of Surface Parallel Flow (SPF) is often used, which means that the ice velocities
are assumed to follow the surface topography and hence a DEM is required to fulfill this
assumption.
Fusion of individual velocity maps in both ascending and descending direction is therefore
used to produce a valid output mosaic of for example ice velocities in a given area [11]. The
three motion components, typically north-south, east-west and up-down, but depending
on the purpose and geometry, can be derived by solving a linear least-squares problem
(LSQ) [11].
2.3
Offset tracking
InSAR has many useful applications as described in section 2.2, but the technique also
has some limitations. For example InSAR is only sensitive to motion along LoS [20] and
are mostly applicable for slow-moving glaciers with velocities less than at least 18 m/yr
(corresponding to 0.05 m/day) [11].
Offset tracking of features and/or speckle patterns can complement InSAR measurements
with being applicable for fast-moving glaciers up to km/yr and measures displacements in
two dimensions; range (LoS) and azimuth (along-track). The method of offset tracking is
based on the amplitudes of the SLC images and image pairs. Offset tracking can help with
removing the phase contribution along-track due to azimuth misregistration and thereby
complement InSAR products further. [11]
The principles behind offset tracking are described in the next subsections.
2.3.1
Amplitude-based feature and speckle offset tracking
The general principle behind offset tracking is finding features and/or speckle patterns
and track the changes by reaching correlation peaks between two SAR intensity images,
which will create a correlation field as seen in the right part of figure 2.3 [21].
First step of creating the correlation field is to choose a sample window from one amplitude
image with a certain number of pixels in range and azimuth. These parameters are called
the window size and are described further in the next subsection. This small sample
window is conceptually moved from pixel to pixel with a certain distance between the
chosen pixels, called the sample spacing. The spatial resolution of the output is in the
best case determined by the sample spacing. [21]

Cross-correlation window size and sample spacing
For making useful correlations, the correlation field should be equally sized for all im-
ages. The size of the sample window should fully overlap the entire search window and
window size should be chosen wisely based on this. Sample windows can overlap each
other. This can bias some results if many prominent features, creating high correlation,
are present. As stated by [21] a good ratio for the window size is 1:4 between range and
azimuth for IW products due to the spatial resolution for Single Look as described in table
2.1. Window size with ratio 1:2 is likely good for EW SLC images. This will be investigated
throughout this project in section 5. When choosing too large sample window, the tech-
nique can struggle with identifying unique offsets according to [21]. This will also affect
the spatial resolution, which will be the same size as the sample window and then poorer
than if the spatial resolution was equal to the sample spacing.
The sample spacing is the distance between to adjacent cross-correlation measurements.
If the sample spacing is too low, the different prominent features cannot be distinguished
from each other. Oppositely, too large sample spacing will potentially reveal poorer spatial
resolution. In the best case the spatial resolution is the same size as the sample spacing.
In the worst case the resolution is equal to the search window size, since no or only one
prominent feature is detected within the search window size.
2.3.2
Culling
After determining the offsets from the correlation field, some outlier removal can be made
for making the results more reliable [17].
The culling technique can be used to remove outliers based on Signal-to-Noise Ratio as
a rejection criterion for outliers [21].
Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)
The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) can be used as a confidence measure for the robustness
of the velocity estimates or as a rejection criterion for outlier removal [21].

Normalised cross-correlation (NCC)
The normalised cross-correlation reach value +1 if the sample window and search window
are fully correlated with the same sign. NCC-value of -1 are reached for opposite signs
between the sample window and the search window. Usually NCC-values lie between ±1
depending on the amount and direction of the cross-correlation between the two windows.
If unfiltered SAR data is used, it encourages larger features to be matched, but if the SAR
data is filtered before culling, smaller features on the surface can be matched and tracked
[21].
2.3.3
Creating 3D ice velocity mosaics
The offsets in range and azimuth, found by the method described in section 2.3.1, are
converted to velocities and geocoded to the relevant geophysical geometry.
The advantage of creating mosaics from offset tracking is that only a single track is re-
quired, since displacements in two dimensions are already given. All LoS and azimuth
measurements on a given area from both ascending and descending tracks are though
used for offset tracking and, if needed, the SPF assumption too, to solve for the underly-
ing 3D motion [11]. InSAR method requires at least one descending and one ascending
track to create a mosaic [11]. This is not a requirement for offset tracking, but including
both descending and ascending tracks, are effective for the velocity mosaic, yielding more
accurate results.

3.5.1
Humboldt Glacier (Sermerssuaq)
The Humboldt Glacier, named Sermerssuaq in new Greenlandic [27], located in North-
west Greenland is one of the widest (100 km across [28]) and largest glaciers in Greenland
[28] and has been retreating continuously in the period 1975 to 2012 [27]. The southern
part of the glacier retreats slower than the northern part with an average velocity of around
15 m/yr for the southern part and around 147 m/yr for the northern part [27]. This differ-
ence in discharge flux is caused by the underlying structure, which is a shallow sea bed in
the southern part and bathymetry down to 300 meter in the northern part [27]. The north-
ern section is, due to the deeper bathymetry, exposed to warmer water from the Atlantic
Ocean, which increases the discharge in a positive feedback loop [28].
The Humboldt Glacier discharges into the Kane Basin. According to [14] the ice velocities
of the southern part of the glacier are less than 100 m/yr, whereas the discharge flux of
the northeastern part of the glacier is more than 140 m/yr in average, 25 km from the
calving front. These results are obtained with a mean value of the velocity errors of -0.07
m/yr and standard deviation 2.1 m/yr after the topography has been removed by using a
Digital Elevation Model, but without modeling errors due to atmospheric effects [14]. Both
results for the ice velocities for the Humboldt Glacier correspond with the velocities from
[27].

m in the period 2016-2017. The ice velocities have accelerated with magnitude 1600 m/yr

IPP processing for interferometry
This section describes how the processing algorithm from DTU Space’s Interferometric

The Digital Elevation Model (DEM) TanDEM-90 of the Greenland Ice Sheet (GrIS) is used
as an input to the interferogram-formation and to extract GCP’s for a baseline calibration
process [31]. The DEM is given in a polar stereographic projection, EPSG 3413, with
latitude of true scale of 70◦N and reference longitude of -45◦E [17], using the WGS84 as
a vertical datum.
An External Velocity Mosaic (EXTVEL) has been build through the Programme for Mon-
itoring of the Greenland Ice Sheet (PROMICE) project for the entire Greenland [17]. It
builds on IW data from offset tracking with culling of invalid pixels, being when NCC<0.05
or SNR<7 [17]. PROMICE’s External Velocity Mosaic includes averaged ice velocities in
the northern and eastern direction and the surface velocity magnitude between 2016 and
2019 in unit m/day, since this unit is most prevalent for the users of PROMICE’s products.
The EXTVEL, described above, is used to refine the co-registration of image pairs as
described in [11]. It is also used as a benchmark, against which the results from interfer-
ometry and offset tracking are compared.

Multilooking is performed by multiplying the average decimation factors in range (raDec-
Fac) and azimuth (azDecFac) with 1.5 and use these parameters as the window sizes to
average the value of each chosen pixel and reduce the speckle noise. For a decimation
factor in range of e.g. 10, the final output grid will only contain every 10th pixel in range as-
signed with the average value of the entire cross-correlated window. The corresponding
process will happen in the azimuth direction.
Different decimation factors (raDecFac and azDecFac) are tested as seen in table 4.2.
The resampling values for co-registration (resDs, resDl, patDs and patDl) are chosen
based on keeping the original relationships with the decimation factors as given for the
IPP processing algorithm:
resDs = 4 · raDecFac
resDl = 5 · azDecFac
patDs = 10 · raDecFac
patDl = 10 · azDecFac
BO-MAI refinement of burst overlaps is implemented in the IPP algorithm used in this
project (see theory section 2.2.2).
When the refinement is activated, the parameters
raPhaAv, azPhaAv, raCorAv and azCorAv are set as the following values:
raPhaAv = 24 for EW, 48 for IW
azPhaAv = 12
raCorAv = 24 for EW, 48 for IW
azCorAv = 12
The refinement are only used for IW interferometry, since most of the EW interferograms
crashed during the process due to undefined errors in the algorithm processing. Probably
the different steering angles and incidence angles between IW and EW modes are not
fully corrected for in the algorithm and this might be part of the explanation. However, a
few of the interferograms from EW mode didn’t crash during the processing and outputs
with the same clear lines between the individual bursts were seen as for the EW inter-
ferograms without refinement. Therefore, the refinement of burst-overlaps didn’t solve
the problem with burst discontinuities and don’t make such a difference for improving the
EW interferograms. Hence, the refinement is omitted from the InSAR processing of EW
interferograms.

4.1.4
Unwrapping, geocoding and calibration of interferograms (GIM)
The second main step of the IPP processing algorithm, called the Geophysical Inversion
Module (GIM), is to unwrap, geocode and calibrate the interferograms to create calibrated
ice velocity maps with their associated errors to further investigate in for example QGIS
[25] and compare it with results from QGreenland [26] and PROMICE’s EXTVEL [17].
Phase unwrapping of the wrapped interferograms is described in the theory 2.2.3.
The LoS velocities are only created for pixels with coherence higher than the threshold of
0.15. This is a relatively low value to ensure creating an output with as many valid pixels
as possible. The unit of the velocities is m/yr.
Baseline calibration is implemented with Ground Control Points (GCP’s) as described in
section 2.2.4. If a GCP-file is not specified, the algorithm extracts, by default, stationary
GCP’s from the provided DEM. The extraction of GCP’s from the DEM and EXTVEL is
described in section 4.1.3.
For geocoding of the LoS ice velocities to the right geometry, the Polar Stereographic
projection with EPSG code 3413 has been used (see details for this projection in 4.1).
Orbit state vectors from Copernicus Open Access Hub and the Digital Elevation Model,
TanDEM-90 (described in section 4.1), have been used for the calibration and geocoding
as well. An output grid-file with 100 m spacing in EPSG 3413 projection is also given for
the processing in the GIM module.

The result of the multilooking investigation shows that multilooking in the relationship 1:2

ing angles between IW and EW interferograms, which is not corrected for in the algorithm,
when processing EW SLC data. Due to different incidence angles for the sub-swaths (be-
ing smaller for the sub-swath closest to the satellite and largest furthest away from the
satellite) it might have caused these disturbances, which are not related to displacements
nor velocities, but to satellite acquisition and configuration parameters.
Based on this analysis, it seems like the parameters raDecFac = 8 pixels, azDecFac = 4
pixels and the corresponding values for co-registration and burst overlapping parameters
are the best for InSAR purposes with EW SLC images. This yields a spatial resolution
of around 80 meter × 80 meter calculated with the average incidence angle of 35◦. This
is four times as low spatial resolution as for the IW interferograms, which yields a spatial
resolution of around 40 meter × 30 meter calculated with the average incidence angle of
35◦. Probably better results with a higher spatial resolution with pixel sizes of around
60 meter × 60 meter would have been obtained by using decimation factors 6 pixels × 3
pixels for range and azimuth respectively, but these parameters have not been analysed
for InSAR in this project. Hence the values of 8 pixels × 4 pixels are chosen as the basis
for the analysis in the rest of section 4 and section 6.

4.6
Discussion
Based on these investigations of IW and EW interferometry, the EW interferograms are
good compliments to IW interferograms with similar accuracy level and due to the larger
coverage of EW SLC products. Even though the resolution of EW interferograms is lower
than for IW InSAR, the output has a similar accuracy compared to the External Velocity
Mosaic and are especially useful for measuring ice velocities for slow moving glaciers on
the Greenland ice sheet.
It is necessary to optimize the burst-overlap processing for EW interferometry to yield bet-
ter results. Decorrelation is a larger problem for EW interferometry than IW interferometry,
which affects the LoS velocity results, for especially ascending tracks, by large data gaps
in areas with faster movements.
The analysis of the previous sections 4.3-4.5 yield the conclusion, that IW and EW reveal
similar results to a sufficient degree, but the small variations due to the short temporal
baselines of six and 12 days are enlarged. Better results could be obtained by combining
results from all the tracks for a longer period, which will average the errors out. This is
described in section 6.
Similar ice velocities are shown in figure 4.12 and 4.13 between IW and EW tracks. Higher
ice velocities are generally seen for the IW tracks than the EW tracks, especially in the
areas with fast moving glaciers. These are also the areas where the decorrelation occur
in the wrapped interferograms seen in section 4.3. The decorrelation can occur further
inland for EW tracks compared to IW tracks due to lower resolution of EW interferograms.
IW interferograms can therefore be used to cover some faster moving areas, which would
be masked out in EW ice velocity maps. EW SLC images are more useful for wider InSAR
coverage in slower moving areas than IW can handle for individual tracks.

Data

5.2
Offset tracking processing
5.2.1
Resampled offset tracking (ROTR)
Offset tracking was carried out using the IPP ROTR (Resampled Offset Tracking) module
[30]. This module co-registers the input image pair assuming no motion, and then carries
out offset tracking of features and/or correlated speckle patterns in radar geometry, in both
range and azimuth, as described in the section 2.3.
To make the offset tracking results comparable with the interferometric results, the same
DEM and External Velocity Mosaic (described in section 4.1) are used as for InSAR. The
maximum and minimum temporal baselines (see table 4.1) are also included as input
parameters to the resampled offset tracking algorithm (see details in section 2.3). Fur-
thermore, resampling parameters (resDs, resDl, patDs and patDl) for the co-registration
of image pairs (section 2.2.1) are included.

5.2.3
Culling
As described in section 2.3.2 culling is important to remove outliers. Signal-to-Noise Ra-
tio (SNR) is used as a rejection criterion to remove outliers below a threshold (snrThr).
Different values (snrThr=0, snrThr=3 and snrThr=5) for this threshold have been tested.
Based on this analysis the best threshold is at snrThr=5, which is the value used for the
rest of the analyses on offset tracking (section 5).
The normalized cross-correlation (NCC) threshold (nccThr) is set to 0 meaning all offsets
from features and/or speckle patterns are taken into account.
5.2.4
Conversion to velocities and geocoding of offset tracking (GIMO)
Conversion of the offsets was carried out with the IPP GIMO module [30], which scales
the measured offsets in radar geometry to LoS and azimuth velocities in m/yr in WGS84
NSIDC Polar Stereographic projection (EPSG 3413). The process is based on pixel spac-
ings and the temporal baseline of the input image pair.
Resampling is done for regular grids with pixel spacings 200 m and 500 m respectively
as shown in the analysis in section 5.3.

5.8
Discussion
As seen in figure 5.7 the ascending EW track 046, 6-18 Nov 2021 reveal better and less
noisy results than the descending EW track 128, 6-12 Nov 2021 (figure 5.6). The reason
can be the longer temporal baseline for the ascending EW tracks or a coincidence of the
chosen data, which is just a small sample of many offset images. This clearly yields some
errors in the ice velocity measurements and affect the offset tracking results more than
the interferometric results are being affected.
The same conclusion can be drawn from the velocity estimates in section 5.5. The EW
descending tracks with 12 days temporal baseline reveal better results than the ones with
six days temporal baseline, since the uncertainty of the ice velocity estimates is divided
by the temporal baseline, so the standard deviation of the errors decreases linearly with
the temporal baseline.
Offset tracking from IW tracks reveal better results than EW tracks for both descending
and ascending tracks. For the descending tracks IW 026 and EW 128, the 12 days tempo-
ral baseline (figure 5.12b and 5.12e) show less noisy results than for the six days temporal
baselines (figure 5.12a, 5.12c, 5.12d and 5.12f). The uncertainty level of offset tracking
depends on, for feature tracking, the strength of the features which are correlated. For
speckle tracking, the uncertainty level of offset tracking is dependent on the coherence
level. The uncertainty of the measured velocity is divided by the temporal baseline. The
external velocity mosaic includes ice velocities averaged for many tracks for a time period
of four years (2016-2019), and hence the longer time scale decreases the uncertainties
and therefore reveal better results.
Another reason of the better results of estimated ice velocities, for 12 days temporal base-
lines, can be found in from which satellite (S1A or S1B), the images are acquired. The
offsets due to satellite configuration, such as timing differences between Sentinel-1A and
Sentinel-1B and differences in processing parameter updates, are avoided [11] for re-
sults obtained with 12 days temporal baseline (or a multiple hereof) compared to six days
acquisition time differences, due to SLC products obtained from the same satellite.
Differences in ice velocities measured with IW and EW are smaller than when comparing
IW and EW ice velocities respectively with PROMICE’s EXTVEL as seen in table 5.2
and 5.3. As a deduction either both ice velocities obtained from interferometry and offset
tracking reveal almost similar bad results or the PROMICE’s EXTVEL cannot be compared
Potential of Sentinel-1 EW SLC imagery for InSAR applications in Greenland
47
==PAGE:56==
with the results obtained from these analyses. PROMICE’s EXTVEL are based on offset
tracking results from IW tracks, so the ice velocity measurements from the IW tracks
were expected to show similar results as EXTVEL. This is not the case based on this
analysis, where random noise is dominating the results. Probably the uncertainties would
be decreased by increased temporal baseline. Thereby, the strength of the dominating
features will likely decrease, which will decrease the uncertainties as well. This has not
been investigated throughout this project, but is subject for future investigations with EW-
based offset tracking.
The high standard deviations (table 5.2) for both IW and EW offset tracking results sub-
tracted with the external velocity mosaic, evidence that the offset tracking processing of
either IW nor EW data reveals its full potential based on the parameters used for the al-
gorithm in this project. Other studies, with processing IW data from the same algorithm,
reveals better results for offset tracking, which indicates that errors might have occurred
somewhere during the processing made in this project. Further investigation and op-
timization of this can potentially reveal as reasonable velocity estimates from EW SLC
data as IW SLC data. This study shows EW offset tracking measurements from individual
tracks not being suitable for measuring ice velocities, neither for slow-moving nor fast-
moving motion with the small temporal baselines (six and 12 days) as investigated in this
project.
Displacement maps in both range and azimuth for EW tracks reveal really different results
and estimates of the displacements. More results from different offset tracking datasets
are needed to confirm or deny, whether this is just a coincidence with lack of suitable
correlation, or if EW offset tracking is worse than IW-based offset tracking. Based on
the results obtained in this project, EW offset tracking reveals worse measurements for
monitoring ice motion than obtained from IW products only.
Comparing table 5.2 with the statistics for ice velocities from interferometry against the ex-
ternal velocity mosaic from PROMICE from table 4.5 and 4.6, the mean-values and stan-
dard deviations from offset tracking are much higher, revealing that InSAR obtains better
velocity measurements for both IW and EW tracks. The standard deviations are more
than 3-12 times higher for offset tracking than interferometry compared with EXTVEL,
with the largest deviations occurring for EW data.

6
Ice velocity mosaics
The two previous sections (4 and 5) have focused on obtaining ice velocities from interfer-
ometry and offset tracking respectively for separate EW and IW tracks. These measure-
ments are combined to obtain ice velocity mosaics of the horizontal motion components,
which are investigated in this section.
6.1
Data and methods
The data inputs for the mosaics are the LoS velocities from interferometry (section 4) and
Los and azimuth velocities from offset tracking (section 5) for all IW and EW tracks.
The ice velocities in m/yr in radar geometry are fused to ice velocity mosaics with unit m/-
day, representing the North and East motion components, from which also the horizontal
velocity magnitude can be computed. The algorithm is based on least-squares method
and by assuming surface parallel flow (SPF), as described in section 2.2.5.
A Digital Elevation Model is required to relate the vertical velocity component to the hor-
izontal ones, using the SPF assumption. Therefore TanDEM-90 (see section 4.1) was
used in this processing step. Two grid-files covering the entire Northwest Greenland, on
100 m and 500 m grids respectively, are specified for geocoding to WGS84 NSIDC polar
stereographic projection.
16 EW interferograms are present for the fusion from both methods (InSAR and offset
tracking), meaning a total of 48 ice velocity maps from EW. Six interferograms were formed
from IW data for both methods, resulting in a maximum of 18 velocity maps, being used as
input for the formation of mosaics. The spatial coverage of the IW images are lower than
for the EW images, though with higher spatial resolution for the IW tracks than EW tracks.
Both the different number of input-images, the different spatial coverage and resolution
might be a bias for the results in this section and it has to be taken into account when
analysing the results.
The goal, throughout this section, is to reveal how much EW measurements can improve
velocity mosaics, compared to present velocity mosaics with IW data alone. Mosaics
of horizontal velocity magnitudes are compared against each method and compared to
PROMICE’s EXTVEL (described in section 4.1). Both the fused mosaics and the External
Velocity Mosaic from PROMICE have units m/day, which make them easily comparable.
6.2
Analysis of grid-sizes
A mosaic of LoS velocities, from the six IW interferograms and 16 EW interferograms, is
made for both 100 m and 500 m grid, and the three horizontal velocity components are
seen in figure 6.1. These grid sizes are chosen based on 100 m grid being suitable for
InSAR results (see section 4.2) with spatial resolution of less than 100 m for both IW and
EW tracks. Geocoding with 500 m grid spacing is suitable for the ice velocity results from
offset tracking with resolutions higher than 200 m for both IW and EW (see section 5.3).
These mosaics (figure 6.1) are only obtainable in the area of overlapping unwrapped EW
interferograms, which does not cover the fast flowing glaciers and thus the velocities are
close to zero as expected.
The results on 500 m grid have slightly larger coverage on the rocks, but less in the south
of the image, where fast moving glaciers are present.

6.6
Discussion
Mosaicking ice velocity measurements from InSAR reveals less noisy results with fewer
outliers and hence smaller uncertainties. This is valid for both IW and EW tracks, which
reach the same level of uncertainties compared to the external mosaic. Including EW
InSAR results in the mosaic should be an advantage, since it increases the coverage
without loosing measurement accuracy, as seen in figure 6.6b.
As declared in section 5, the offset tracking results obtained from both IW and EW products
in this study, are more uncertain than the external velocity mosaic compared with. The IW-
based velocity mosaic from offset tracking (figure 6.3a) were expected to yield accuracies
similar to the external velocity mosaic, as this is created based on IW-based offset tracking
alone. The mosaic of IW-based offset tracking is though better than velocity estimates
from individual tracks, which shows the advantage of mosaicking results.
An advantage of using ice velocity maps from offset tracking against interferometric ob-
tained ice velocities, is the larger spatial coverage of the offset tracking data from both
IW and EW mode. Especially EW-based offset tracking enlarges the spatial coverage as
seen in figure 6.6c. The same accuracy level are though not obtained for the mosaic with
EW offset tracking compared to the mosaic only containing IW-based InSAR and offset
tracking and EW-based InSAR measurements.
Further investigation on EW compared with IW results can be made on fusing more im-
ages from the same tracks within different seasons to mask out the biases from seasonal
variations. The larger temporal baselines will potentially add value to the mosaic. Other-
wise, more tracks can be covered and added to the mosaic to obtain better results with
larger coverage of the Greenland ice sheet. Currently, EW SLC images are not provided
further inland to the west, than the tracks already taken into account (see figure 3.1). A
few EW tracks containing EW SLC images further south are available, but these tracks

Landslides and rock avalanches in
Karrat Fjord
Another potential InSAR application of Sentinel-1’s EW SLC data could be mapping land-
slides in Greenland. This is investigated in the Karrat Fjord in the central Westgreenland,
where large landslides have occurred as described in literature in the next section.
7.1
Theory
Mapping landslides is more dependent on the spatial resolution obtained by InSAR, since
the areas covering the landslides are typically smaller than for mapping ice velocities [32].
Too coarse resolution could increase the chance of a given slope being decorrelated, since
parts of the slope affected by temporal and/or geometric decorrelation will appear in the
same resolution cell as not decorrelated neighbouring areas, which can reveal ambigu-
ous interpretations. Since the landslides in Karrat Fjord are typically seen on the rocks
towards the south, the best results for mapping landslides in the Karrat Fjord, will likely
be obtained by analysing descending tracks to avoid too much noise from foreshortening,
which will be a larger problem in ascending tracks due to the flight direction.
InSAR measurements have many advantages compared with seismic data, in situ mea-
surements and optical imagery and can complement these methods in the detection and
mapping of landslides [32]. Larger areas can be covered faster with spaceborne remote
sensing techniques such as InSAR compared to e.g. in situ measurements, which are
time-consuming and very spatially limited. InSAR also has an advantage over the op-
tical images by being able to propagate through clouds and measure even during the
dark periods in the polar regions [17]. Seismological measurements can cover rapid rock
avalanches producing strong seismic signals, but for slower landslides, InSAR can be
useful to map the deformations [32]. Therefore InSAR measurements with longer tempo-
ral baselines typically reveal better results.
7.1.1
Landslide events in Karrat Fjord
A large rock avalanche occurred at the Ummiammakku mountain the 17th of June 2017
in Karrat Fjord in the central Westgreenland [32]. It emerged a tsunami, which destroyed
a village and killed four people [33].
The coasts are important for the life in Greenland and so are the mapping of landslides,
which seem to occur more often after the 2017 event. There were no records of large
rock slopes in Greenland before the event in 2017 [32].
A main triggering factor for the landslide in 2017 seems to be permafrost degradation build
up on long time-scales due to warming of the climate [32]. The climate is being warmer
in the future and hence it becomes more important to identify signs in areas with risks of
landslides and rock avalanches to avoid these causing large accidents.

The data covers the summer months (June-September) in 2021, where the probability of
finding ice-free rocks is largest. 2021 has been chosen to investigate for two reasons.

7.5
Discussion
As stated in the previous section the resolution of EW interferograms is too poor to de-
termine landslides, even though the landslides, in this investigated area, are some of the
largest observed in Greenland, which would yield better chances of observing those.
Investigation of four interferograms with 12 days temporal baseline for EW descending
track 040 shows that the best results are obtained by the shortest perpendicular base-
line. This is not the case for IW tracks, so it might be a coincidence of results, that the
shortest baseline reveals the best EW result. However, short perpendicular baselines are
required. EW interferograms with much longer perpendicular baseline than the chosen
threshold of ± 38 m were also analysed as a comparison. All of these revealed completely
decorrelated images even in areas which seems not decorrelated in e.g. image 7.3a. The
analysis is based on sparse results with only few EW interferograms and is likely biased
by the time of acquisition. Seasonal effects have not been taken into consideration. 2021
can also be too late for mapping the deformation of the event occurred in 2017.
The worst results (larger overall decorrelation) seen in figure 7.3b can be a consequence
of the large perpendicular baseline compared with image 7.3a, but can also be caused
by the temporal baseline of 24 days revealing more indistinguishable decorrelation in the
ocean surrounding the rocks than for the 12 days EW interferogram. This is opposite of
what is obtained for the IW interferograms, where a larger area of decorrelation within the
areas of interest (white circles) is seen for a temporal baseline of 24 days compared with
12 days being an expression of larger deformations, probably based on landslides.
Patterns of landslides are found in the EW interferograms here, since other literature
described, where the landslides occurred. The indistinguishable noise for the EW inter-
ferograms makes it difficult to determine new landslides occurring in the future in other
areas. The IW interferograms show clearer patterns of the deformations being caused by
landslides.
InSAR can only measure displacements in LoS, which makes the patterns of landslide
deformations clearer in the images with IW descending tracks than IW ascending tracks.
The deformation in the ascending track occurs along the flight direction, which makes
them difficult to detect. It is therefore appropriate, that the only EW track with SLC images
covering the area of Karrat Fjord, is acquired in the descending direction.
InSAR treats the decorrelated areas equally, regardless of the deformation being caused
by physical phenomenon such as landslides or satellite acquisition biases such as fore-
shortening. Phase unwrapping, and therefore quantitative deformation measurements,
were not deemed feasible due to the low coherence.
This analysis reveals the conclusion, that the spatial resolution is crucial for mapping
landslides, and the spatial resolution that can be obtained with EW SLC interferograms
are not sufficient enough for this InSAR application. The perpendicular baseline is more
important for EW InSAR products for mapping landslides than for IW.

Summary and conclusions
This thesis investigated the potential of using SLC products from Sentinel-1’s EW ac-
quisition mode for land-based InSAR applications in Greenland, compared with the well
established IW SLC products.
The explorations were divided into four parts consisting of analyses of ice velocity es-
timates obtained with interferometry (section 4), offset tracking (section 5), ice velocity
mosaics combining all these measurements (section 6) and interferometric analysis on
mapping landslides with EW SLC images (section 7). EW SLC products in Northwest
Greenland were considered from November 2021 for the first three parts of the thesis.
SLC products acquired from EW mode from June-September 2021 was the basis of in-
vestigating landslides in the area surrounding Karrat Fjord in central Westgreenland. IW
SLC products from the same periods respectively, have been used for comparisons.
The investigations, throughout this project, on the potential of Sentinel-1 EW SLC data
yields as accurate measurements compared to IW SLC products, by using an interfero-
metric algorithm. Therefore, EW SLC imagery constitutes a good complementary to IW
SLC data for mapping velocities of slow-moving ice in Greenland with InSAR processing.
The estimates from InSAR processing, regarding ice velocities in slow-moving areas, are
obtained with spatial resolution of 80 m × 80 m using decimation factors 8 pixels × 4
pixels, in range and azimuth respectively, for spatial averaging (multilooking) to reduce
speckle. This is four times worse as the spatial resolution (40 m × 40 m) of the IW SLC
products obtained with multilooking with decimation factors 10 pixels (range) × 2 pixels
(azimuth), but the EW estimates reach good accuracies compared with IW estimates on
ice velocities regardless of this poor resolution.
The ice velocity estimates obtained from offset tracking in this project were obtained with
spatial resolution of 210 m × 200 m and concluded too noisy to match the accuracy of the
ice velocity measurements obtained from IW SLC images alone, which has a resolution of
160 m × 140 m with the parameters used for the offset tracking processing in this project.
Mosaicking of ice velocity estimates decrease the noise and increase the possibility of
obtaining accurate estimates of ice velocities for slow-moving areas on the Greenland
ice sheet. EW InSAR ice velocity measurements show an advantage in improving IW-
based mosaics, since it increases the coverage without loosing measurement accuracy.
Measurements of ice velocities obtained from EW offset tracking can add value to the ice
velocity mosaic too. Ice velocity estimates from the offset tracking algorithm and from
interferometric analysis respectively were compared against an external velocity mosaic.
The standard deviations for these comparisons are more than three times higher, in the
best cases, for offset tracking results than interferometric results. By keeping this in mind
before adding the offset tracking results to the ice velocity mosaic, the error sources,
contributing to these bad estimates from offset tracking, should be minimized. Further
investigations, out of the scope of this project, are needed to improve this.
Concerning landslide monitoring in coastal areas, EW SLC products do not have the full
potential on monitoring deformations due to landslides, since the spatial resolution is too
coarse for this purpose. Spatial resolution of 60 m × 60 m could be obtained at the
best for EW SLC products in this area with the investigated algorithms for this project.
Correspondingly, this resolution could have been obtained for the InSAR applications of
estimating ice velocities, but were omitted in section 4, since the resolution of 80 m ×
64
Potential of Sentinel-1 EW SLC imagery for InSAR applications in Greenland
==PAGE:73==
80 m were considered suitable for the ice velocity measurements. Future work with EW
SLC data could include estimating ice velocities with multilooking parameters resulting in
spatial resolution of 60 m × 60 m or better.
Measurements obtained with 12 days temporal baseline reveal better results, than than
image pairs of six days temporal baseline, since the uncertainty of the ice velocity es-
timates is divided by the temporal baseline, so the standard deviation of the errors de-
creases linearly with the temporal baseline. The current 12 days repeat-pass period for
Sentinel-1 images, after S1B died in December 2021, is therefore suitable for the InSAR
applications investigated in this thesis.
The SLC data for EW acquisition mode is currently sparse in time and extent on the
Greenland ice sheet compared with IW SLC data. Each EW SLC image has larger swath-
width than one IW SLC image and can therefore cover larger areas faster. ESA acquires
IW data on some ground tracks and EW data on others, resulting in periods lacking of raw
EW data. ESA only release few of the available raw EW images as SLC products, since
the processing is time-consuming and not worth it, if the SLC products aren’t used for any
purposes. This complicates a continuous InSAR processing with EW SLC imagery, but
can change in the future if more SLC’s are generated for the EW archive, which is highly
recommended based on the investigations from this project.
Based on the findings in this thesis, it is recommended to include EW SLC images for
InSAR applications together with IW SLC images for obtaining wider coverage in space
and time. It requires more of the raw data being made available as SLC products for EW
mode covering larger areas to implement the full potential of the improvements for e.g.
ice velocity monitoring by EW SLC’s. Furthermore, accessing and searching for EW SLC
products, shared on NASA’s Alaska Satellite Facility platform [22], in a relevant area of
interest would be easier, if the annotation of the direction for the available SLC products
from EW tracks were correct. Currently, some of the descending tracks are annotated
with ascending-label, which complicates the accessing process unnecessarily, when only
searching for data from one direction.
Finally, EW SLC imagery has great potential for InSAR applications, such as monitoring
slow-moving ice and motion on ice-free rocks in Greenland, when mosaicking velocity es-
timates from many IW and EW tracks, regardless of the four times poorer spatial resolution
obtained compared with IW SLC products. The poor resolution can be countervailed by
filling temporal data gaps for the currently IW-based monitoring. However, the relatively
poor resolution of EW SLC’s compared with IW SLC’s, makes it unsuitable to monitor
landslides in Karrat Fjord according to the data analysed within this thesis.
