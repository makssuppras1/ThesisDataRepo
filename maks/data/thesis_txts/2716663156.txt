Abstract
The process of electrodialytic recovery (EDR) shows magnificent results as a method to
harvest the finite resource phosphorus (P) from soil, sewage sludge ash (SSA), etc., while
separating it from mixed in heavy metals (Hm). The objective of this project is to create
a system to optimize this process with regards to energy consumption and labour intensity.
Evidence exist that pulsing current and reversible polarization pulses increase the energy
efficiency of the recovery process and therefore these were implemented to the system. A
combined cleaning and measurement phase was introduced to the cell conducting the recovery
process. This utilizes anodic stripping voltammetry (ASV) in order to strip off and measure
the Hm that are deposited/plated onto the electrode during the recovery phase. The linear
voltage sweep between the electrode surface and the surrounding solution, needed in order
to conduct ASV, was implemented using a switch mode power supply (SMPS) and a stable
silver chloride reference electrode (Ag/AgCl). To develop a stable SMPS for ASV the EDR
cell’s, in which the recovery takes place, impedance is measured and modelled. From the
cleaning- and measurement phase, estimates of the amounts of Cu, deposited during the
EDR process on the electrode, were successfully measured and stored using the automatic
data logging also introduced to the system. These are the initial steps, for an EDR system
that can operate efficient and automatic and that may one day be able to characterize and
measure the amounts of a wide variety of Hm in soil, SSA etc.

INTRODUCTION
4
Introduction
The starting point of this project is an already existing setup which involves a two-compartment
electrodialytic recovery (EDR) cell, used for recovery of phosphorus from soil or SSA while
simultaneously removing mixed in heavy metals. The objective of this thesis is to advance
on this already existing setup by introducing the following initiatives:
• Pulsing current control
• Reversible polarization
• Automatic datalogging
• Crude qualitatively and quantively Hm measurements
P is the 11th most common element on earth and is fundamental to all life. It is especially
vital in the production of food, as it is a key ingredient in fertilizer products [1]. At this state,
most P is obtained from mining phosphate and after having been used as fertilizer, it ends up
in rivers, lakes and oceans [1]. In many regions this causes eutrophication, which results in
under water desserts. In the U.S.A the economic consequences of this phenomenon is in the
range of billions of dollars each year which is described by Dodds et al. [2]. Furthermore, as
the quality of phosphate rock reserves declines, more energy is needed to mine and process
the P to avoid Hm such as Cadmium (Cd) and Uranium (Ur) [1]. Due to the few reserves
around the world, the phosphate rock needs to be transported far, which means that this
method of extraction, at this time, implausibly could be considered a solution in a fossil
free future [3].
It is therefore evident that another method is needed for the extraction
of P, one that focuses on recycling sediment and SSA rather than mining. The EDR cell
shows eminent results in extraction of the strongly bound phosphorous from SSA and soils as
described by Ottosen et al. [4] and [5]. The already mentioned initiatives are made in order
to sharpen this technology in order to catapult it into industry production. This project is
two fold, first a system is build in order to utilize the earlier mentioned features and then
the system is tested on the EDR cell. In the next subsections each feature is justified.
4.1
Pulsing current control
Pulsing current instead of direct current (DC) is implemented inorder to minimize the re-
sistance in the the EDR cell and thereby minimize the power loss operating it. Evidence of
this effect is investigated in detail by Sun et al. [6].
4.2
Reversible polarization
The resistance may during the recovery process increase due to oils (contained in the used soil
or SSA) being polarized and therefore attracted to and thereby moved towards the charged
electrode surface forming a oily film around the electrode. Reversible polarization pulses are
implemented in order to minimize the resistance, by removing the oils, by changing current
direction for a short moment.

It goes without saying that automatic- rather than manual data logging boost the under-

4.4

What kind and how much of a specific Hm species, that are extracted from the medium, that
are being processed, and deposited onto the electrode, will now be measured using ASV. This
thereby eliminates the need for conventional methods for measuring the deposited amounts
of Hm after a full EDR cell run. The method will furthermore cleanse the electrode, thereby
removing more labour intense methods for cleaning the electrodes.
4.5
The System
A system will be developed to utilize the above mentioned features. The pulsing current
is implemented using a step down (buck) converter controlled by a Arduino Uno micro-
controller with current mode control. The reversible polarization is implemented using a
relay build H-brdige. The data logging is achieved using a current sense amplifier, voltage
difference amplifier and the Arduino’s analog to digital converters (ADC). Lastly the ASV
is implemented using the buck converter, the Arduino in voltage-mode control and another
relay that switch the pair of conducting electrodes.

Theory
This chapter explains the principle of the EDR cell and the theory behind the new develop-
ments in the current work.
5.1
The electrodialytic recovery cell
The principle of electrodialytic recovery EDR cell is that the soil, which is placed in the
Pt-I chamber, is acidified by the electrolysis of water at the Pt-I electrode. This results in
P and Hm are desorbed from the soil and Hm electromigrating through the cation exchange
membrane (CEM) to the Pt-II chamber, which due to electrolysis of water is becoming more
and more alkaline. This is illustrated in figure 1.

O + ne−−→R
(1)

i = i0[exp(αaFηs/RT) −exp(−αcFηs/RT)]

denotes Faradays constant, R denotes the gas constant, T denotes the temperature and

and the equilibrium potential.
So in order for the current to run forward the electrode
potential has to overcome the equilibrium potential.

describing electron transfer reaction. The expression can, for small positive overpotentials

5.5
Water’s stability region
By inspecting the Pourbaix diagram it is evident that no matter the pH, if a sufficient
large negative voltage is applied across the cell, Cu will be reduced to its metallic form.
In the earlier mentioned phase 2 the potential of the Pt-II electrode will slowly be linearly
increased from negative to large potential with respect to the reference electrode Ag/AgCl-
II, in a solution of 0.01 M NaNO3 with a pH of 2. By inspecting figure 4, it is observed that
if Cu(s) is in contact with a solution of pH 2 it will create an equilibrium potential with
its ionic form Cu2+. By applying a potential V which exceeds this equilibrium potential U
of 0.6 vs. Ag/AgCl, which means creating a positive overpotential, Cu will start oxidizing,
meaning that the electrons leaving the Cu will cause a current to run in the EDR cell. The
potential point of which the oxidation process starts reveals which metal species is being
oxidized and the peak current area describes the amount of that species being oxidized.

5.7
Reference electrode
The reason to use an reference electrode and not an auxiliary electrode (e.g Pt) is that
the reference electrode, in this case Ag/AgCl KCl saturated, has a stable and well known
potential of UAg/AgCl = 199 mV vs.
Standard hydrogen electrode (SHE). An auxiliary
electrode would most likely have a potential that is a function of the different species in
the solution. The measured potential between the electrode of interest and the reference
electode V can then be translated to be referenced with respect to the SHE.
USHE = V + UAg/AgCl
(11)
This is the typical reference used in the electrochemical series [7]. When oxidation hap-
pens at a specific measured voltage this can be looked up in the electrochemical series and
thereby the metal with such a half-cell potential can be identified. The Ag/AgCl electrode
is utilized due to its robustness to change in pH, it works up till pH=13 [7], which is fine
when comparing with earlier uses of similar implementations of the EDR cell by Ottosen et
al. [5]. Furthermore it has a simple design. A downside of the Ag/AgCl electrode is that it
is leaking KCl, but in such a crude setup, as the EDR cell with such a wide variety of ions,
this should be a negligible amount.

5.8
Characterization the EDR Cell load
In order to build a control system for such an EDR-cell a well functioning power supply
must be developed and therefore it is important to know how the EDR-cell loads the power
supply. A common equivalent circuit model for an electrochemical cell is Randles circuit [7],
this circuit is illustrated in figure 8 for the case of phase 1.
Figure 8: Randles equivalent circuit for the EDR-cell in phase 1.
Vout denotes the output voltage of a given power supply, Cdl−I, Cdl−II denote the capacitance
of respectively Pt-I and Pt-II electrodes and is a function of the used solutions permittivity
and microscopic surface area [7]. Rf,O2, Rf,H2 denotes the earlier mentioned faradaic resis-
tance to the earlier mentioned half-cell reactions for the evolution of O2 and H2. Lastly, RΩ
denotes the ohmic resistance through the EDR-cell. The impedance of the EDR-cell then
becomes:
ZEDR = RΩ+ (ZCdl−I||Rf,H2) + (ZCdl−II||Rf,O2)
(12)
Where ”|| ” denote components in parallel and ”+” denote components in series. Since the
electrodes have similar dimensions their capacitance, which is a function of geometry, must
be approximately equal Cdl−I ≈Cdl−II. In order to initially operate with a simple load as
possible it is assumed that Rf,H2 ≈Rf,O2. Then the formula reduces to:
ZEDR = RΩ+ 2(ZCdl−II||Rf,O2)
(13)
The equivalent circuit becomes as illustrated in figure 9.
Figure 9: Randles simplified equivalent circuit for the EDR-cell.
This equivalent circuit will be used both to describe the load experienced by the power
supply between Pt-I and Pt-II (phase 1) and the load between Pt-II and Pt-III (phase 2). In

solution while it carries its charge to the other electrode, which would when reaching Pt-III
be reduced back to its metallic form. Lets consider the concept of ionic mobility u which
relates drift velocity s in an electric field E [7]:
s = uE
(14)
Hm usually have an ionic mobility around uHm ≤5 · 10−8 m2s−1V−1 [7] where H+ has
a mobility of uH+ = 36.2 · 10−8 m2s−1V−1 [7] and OH−has a mobility of uOH−= 20.6 ·
10−8 m2s−1V−1[7]. The drift velocity of the H+ and OH−is therefore much larger, therefore
Hm, if the solution is not stirred, will not be the primary contributor to the conduction of
current and should not during phase 2 reach the Pt-III electrode. This also means that in
order to have a solution which is diluted and has high conductivity, and therefore less ohmic
energy loss, the solution should either be acidic or alkaline.

6.5
System design
In this section the system to utilize the earlier mentioned wanted features is developed. An
ASV analysis is in principle always performed with an potentiostat based upon an operational
amplifier (op amp) [13].
The potentiostat functions by maintaining the potential of the
electrode of interest (in this case Pt-II) at a constant level with respect to a reference
electrode (in this case Ag/AgCl-II) by adjusting the current through a closed loop between
the electrode of interest and an auxiliary electrode (in this case Pt-III). A downside of
an operational amplifier as a voltage source is that these can only handle small currents.
Therefore, in this thesis, the Switch Mode Power Supply (SMPS) technology is utilized in
order to build a potentiostat, because this technology is made for high current (compared
to op amp’s) and effective voltage conversion [14]. In order to perform ASV on the Pt-II
electrode, effort has to be put into developing a SMPS power supply that is able to create
a linearly increasing voltage with respect to time. The design of the power supply will then
be with focus on the phase 2 region of operation. This is because ASV requires a very stable
power supply especially since the impedance of the EDR cell load may vary through the
ASV process. How the developed power supply then operates in phase 1 will be investigated
afterwards. The whole system contains a lab power supply, a step down converter, current
and voltage sensing, a microcontroller, a mosfet driver, relays and a difference amplifier. A
block diagram of the design is shown in figure 12 and the full circuit is found in figure 36 in
the appendix.

6.5.8

PID control is utilized for controlling the output voltage of the power supply. The require-
ments in order to obtain a stable controller is that the open loop transfer function has an
phase margin of ϕM > 60o and a gain margin of GM > 40dB [18]. The system the PID
controller is designed for, is a so called hybrid (also known as a mixed domain) system
which is a system that is both continuous and discrete. The Arduino Uno represents the
discrete system which only receives information at the sample time Ts. The buck converter
and implemented IC’s are the continuous system and they receive information continuously.

each sample value is held for one sample interval. The mathematical model describing this
phenomena is known as the Zero Order Hold (ZOH). The principle of ZOH is illustrated in
figure 19.

6.5.12
Implementing phase 1 in the power supply
For the system to become an integrated part of the EDR cell, it must be able to operate
phase 1 as well as phase 2. Using the infrastructure made developing the system for phase 2,
small modifications can make it suitable for phase 1 operation. Phase 1 is run using current
control (cc) mode so therefore the PID regulation parameters need to be changed. In order to
have a more efficient power supply, at the cost of stability, the bleeder resistor at the output
is disconnected with the relay RSW6. The p-mosfet gate does not need to be charged as fast
as for phase 2 so R3 is interchanged with R2 using the relay RSW4. The buck converter only
utilizes step down properties so the used lab power supply is also switched from V s2 = 10 V
to V s1 = 40 V using the relay RSW5. The new control loop measures the current through
R4 and amplifies it using the INA225 IC, before measuring it with the Arduino ADC and
finally the Arduino outputs a shorter or longer duty cycle inorder to control the current
magnitude. The Ziegler-Nichols [18] method is used to tune the parameters derived in eq.

Cu amounts on Pt-II electrode compared to amounts in solu-

As seen in table 2 the used soil contains 1372 mg/kg of Cu and 50 g were used in the EDR
cell which corresponds to 68.6 mg of Cu. In similar setups as by Ottosen et al. [5] about 2/5
of the Cu in the soil had made it into what corresponds to the Pt-II chamber which amounts
to 27.44 mg. It was estimated in this thesis that 1.37 mg Cu had been deposited through the
whole 80 hours EDR cell run. So what has been estimated to be deposited on the electrode
surface is probably only a small fraction of what is inside the Pt-II chamber. The actual
amount of Cu deposited on the electrode, should be investigated next with conventional
analytical methods and compared to the results obtained using ASV, inorder to legitimize
the use of the method in this experimental setup.
7.8
Oxide formation
The reason that such low amounts of Cu were found on the Pt-II electrode could be caused by
the increasing alkalinity of the solution in the Pt-II chamber during phase 1. By inspecting
the Pourbaix diagram of Cu in figure 4, it is observed that in an alkaline solution, Cu will
precipitate out of solution, if the potential has not been sufficiently lowered. It could be
argued that one should introduce a buffer solution in the Pt-II chamber to maintain a low
pH through the whole phase 1 run. This buffer solution should then be redox inactive. The
fact that oxides form is useful since then the Pt-II electrode is not plated in vast amounts,
thereby it will keep its properties as an efficient electrode (low faradaic resistance for the
electrolysis of water). Even though ASV is a smart method for measuring the amounts of
deposits on an electrode, the electrode should not intentionally be plated in this process.
But measuring the species that accidentally deposit are still very useful.
7.9
Introduction of an ASV electrode
It is definitely relevant to test and clean soil for Cu contamination, since in vast amounts it is
toxic [20]. The elements Ar [21] and Cr [21] are definitely more toxic and they two appear in
vast amounts in the used Collstrup soil (see table 2). A next step for this setup is to estimate
the amounts of these being deposited onto the Pt-II electrode. Here another electrode such
as the Hg electrode [7] should be introduced. Hg is used due to its, as mentioned in section
5.3, low exchange-current density in the reduction of H+, which is the competing reaction
during phase 2. With the Hg electrode included, phase 2 should have included an extra
first step where the Hm on the Pt-II electrode is oxidized and thereby deposited onto the
ASV Hg electrode. Afterwards the voltage should slowly be reversed from around -1 V Vs.
Ag/AgCl-II and upwards. The Hm species deposited on the Hg electrode, will one at a time
oxidize (when using a fitting scan rate), resulting in several peaks in the order seen in figure
6 without any measurable amounts of electrolysis of water. This phenomenon is already
documented by Ellis [9]. Whether this technique works on a crude setup as the EDR cell
should be investigated.

7.10
System design considerations
The choice of developing the system on the basis of a buck converter and a Arduino Uno were
most of all a decisions based on comfort and fast implementation for the purpose of making
a proof of concept. Now when evidence is found that SMPS technology can be utilized both
for running the cell and acting as a potentiostat, more consideration should be put into the
selection of the SMPS topology (most likely an AC to DC converter) and its corresponding
components. Where the component’s stress factor (CSF) [14] and the supplies efficiency
should be key design parameters. In this system the Arduino Uno both regulates the power
supply and prints measured quantities of the EDR cell. This introduces a lot of tasks for
the Arduino to process and thereby limits the speed for how well the Arduino can perform
both tasks. Therefore, in a future system these tasks could be divided into two separate
processors. This would result in a boosted phase margin and thereby stability and a fast
data printing rate.
8
Conclusion
The resistance through the operation of phase 1 and the impedance during the ASV in phase
2 were estimated. This made it possible to develop a model to understand the operation of
the EDR cell and gave the needed information in order to develop a SMPS based potentiostat.
The system was then capable of conducting pulsing current, reverse polarization, conduct
automatic data logging and utilize ASV inside water’s stability window for Cu. The system
was calibrated by conducting ASV measurements on known amounts of Cu deposited onto
the Pt-II electrode. Finally the ASV was conducted in the cleaning program of the EDR
process (phase 2) using the developed system and calibrated model. Thereby the amounts
of Cu deposited onto the Pt electrode, from a full EDR cell run, were estimated to 1.37
mg. This was done by measuring the corresponding current peak, inside water’s stability
window, and comparing it to experimental developed linear model, that relates current peak
and mass of deposited Cu. These are the initial steps for a fully automatized EDR cell.
