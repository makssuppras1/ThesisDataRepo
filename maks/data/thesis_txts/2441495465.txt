Abstract
The nature of turbulent ﬂows are rarely stationary, and as pointed out by recent theoretical study
[George, 2012], these are not supported by the general theory of turbulence. On this basis the
group was given the opportunity to test a potentially groundbreaking theory.
Through an experimental approach, this thesis sets out to identify turbulence characteristics of
a suddenly started jet. The goal of the thesis is to make an experimental foundation for future
research into the recently developed theory, by Clara M. Velte, concerning the similarity of a
suddenly started jet. The primary focus will be on the time dependency of the velocity variance
for three diﬀerent acceleration scenarios.
The experimental setup is an aquarium with a vertically mounted jet on top, a high speed camera
(Photron FASTCAM-X 1280PCI) taking images of 5µm Polyamid particles and a 2.5W continu-
ous laser mounted with light sheet optics illuminating the particles.
The variance is extracted from a PIV analysis of the high speed images. The nozzle exit Reynolds
number are of the order 28000 for three scenarios and one scenario is replicated with a Reynolds
number of 51000.
In order to determine the boundary eﬀects on the jet plume of the boundaries; a set of simulations
was performed in Star-CCM+, with varying width and length ratios compared to the experimen-
tal setup. Furthermore, three simulations were conducted, these represented replicas of the three
diﬀerent acceleration scenarios from the experiment.
The experimental setup was found to be useful in two ways, ﬁrst oﬀit showed promise in testing
the proposed theory; secondly, the experimental setup proved able to indicated if the theoretical
time exponents for the variance are predicted correctly.
The initial expectation was that the variance should follow the predicted time dependencies
u2 ∼[t3/2, t1, t0]. However, the result showed that across all scenarios, the time dependency
of the variance was near constant, u2 ∼t0.
In conclusion, the theory is either disproven or the experimental setup should be changed to ac-
count for boundary eﬀects (side and end walls). Furthermore, given the tendency of the results
the measurement window should be extended to determine the nature of the observed results.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The ﬁeld of modern ﬂuid mechanics is relatively young with its circa 300 year history starting
with the likes of Sir Isaac Newton, Blaise Pascal and David Bernoulli. Later work by Claude-
Louis Navier and Sir George Stoke formulated some of the most inﬂuential equations within ﬂuid
mechanics. About 100 years ago a new evolution in the ﬁeld of ﬂuid mechanics arose; Turbulence
Theory. This was mainly onset by Osborne Reynolds who showed the diﬀerence between laminar
and turbulent ﬂows [Reynolds, 1894]. The work was later followed with discoveries by, among
others, Sir Geoﬀrey Taylor and his statistical approach to studying velocity ﬂuctuation [Taylor,
1935] and Andrey Kolmogorov stated his theory of universal equilibrium [Kolmogorov, 1941]. The
works of Kolmogorov and others with the concept of universal equilibrium is commonly referred
to as K41. To oversimplify, K41 states that the small structures of any turbulent ﬂow would scale
identically; a universal law of turbulent kinetic energy.
Later scientiﬁc work have evolved into modelling of turbulence, many of these models are based
on K41 theory. The concept states that if all scales smaller than some limit behaves in a universal
manner, it should be possible to predict these with a model instead of performing the full calcu-
lations.
Recent theoretical studies have suggested that the concept of universal equilibrium might not be
as universal as thought, referring to not all ﬂows being stationary [George, 2012].
1.1
Project description
The purpose of this master thesis is to perform experimental investigation of a forced turbulent
jet to observe the development of turbulent characteristics comparable with a proposed theory
of self similarity and available CFD models for ﬁnite Reynolds number. In order to do this, the
project will contain the designing of an experimental setup, CFD analysis as well as comparison
of the experimental and numerical data gathered in relation to the proposed theory. The intend is
to provide insight into the coherence between well established theory (K41), experimental results
and the accuracy of numerical models for this ﬂow condition.

1.2
Project Scope
The primary focus of this thesis is to provide quality experimental data in order to make valid
indications/conclusions about the proposed theory and its validity. This means that the inﬂuence
of nozzle designs will not be considered. The experiments will, however, include diﬀerent time
dependent accelerations. Furthermore, searching and studying literature will be limited to under-
standing the proposed theory and the justiﬁcation hereof due to lack of literature available and
the main focus being experimentation.
Another limitation is the CFD simulations are used as a tool in order to indicate external in-
ﬂuences on the ﬂow and replicating the acceleration scenarios. The simulations will be used to
evaluate and discuss the experimental setup and data. It should be stressed that the CFD part
of the project is only used as a tool of indication, methods such as convergent studies for Mesh
and Time step is not prioritised.

Project research question

number? - compared with theory (K41)".

2.1
Basic laws of ﬂuid mechanics
The ﬁeld of ﬂuid mechanics consists of a variety of diﬀerent formulations of a few basic conservation
laws, the foundation of these basic laws can be derived from mass conservation, Newton’s second
law and thermodynamics giving conservation of mass, conservation of momentum and ﬁnally
conservation of energy. Due to the nature of the project the conservation laws derived is limited
to these three. These conservation laws in their various forms are used throughout ﬂuid mechanics
from laminar to turbulent ﬂows.
In this section, the conservation laws called the continuity, momentum and energy equation will
be presented. Note, several intermediate results are omitted for simplicity and temperature will
be neglected as it is not relevant in the context of this thesis. Further derivations of the basic laws
can be found in general literature such as Kundu et al. [2015].

2.2.2
Spectral Domain
One of widely used tools for analysing homogeneous turbulence is to work in the spectral domain.
This is done via Fourier Transformation (FT). The idea is that any statistically homogeneous
signal can be divided into trigonometric waves. This type of analysis essentially divides the ﬂow
into diﬀerent trigonometric waves according to the structures in the ﬂow. The parameter for
investigation is primarily energy, thus the energy spectrum is an representation of the amount of
energy present for each wave number in the ﬂow. Wave number is often assumed to corresponds
directly to the size of structures in a turbulent ﬂow as the inverse of structure length. The direct
connection between wavelength and spatial length scales are debatable but they are deﬁnitely
proportional, k ∼L−1, i.e. high wave number is small structures and vice versa.
The spectrum, see ﬁgure (2.2.1), provided by FT of a turbulent signal, is divided into three
diﬀerent regions according to the three diﬀerent phenomenons experienced by turbulence. The
three diﬀerent phenomena are turbulent production, energy is extracted from the mean ﬂow
to the turbulent ﬂow, energy transport, energy transfer between wave numbers and ﬁnally
dissipation, energy has been transferred to scales so small they now dissipates as heat.
The conventional way to describe the spectrum is done in the following way. The three regions
dividing the spectrum is based on the primary phenomenon for that region.
The ﬁrst region
is Energy Containing Range (ECR) which primarily includes turbulent production. This is the
source, i.e. energy is supplied from the mean ﬂow. Next is the Inertial Sub Range (ISR), which
emerges for ﬂows with suﬃciently high Reynolds numbers. In this range, energy is, according
to K41, being transferred from low to high wave numbers at a constant rate. Lastly there is
theUniversal Equilibrium Range (UER) which covers the ISR and above. The region above ISR is
where the energy is dissipated as heat, hence primary contributor here is the viscous dissipation.

2.8
Double Data
Obtaining a high certainty of the statistical moments, demands a high number of samples. In a
statistically stationary ﬂow, e.g. doing hotwire analysis of a jet; the time needed for experiment
is simply the time between samples, with respect to the time scales in order to get uncorrelated
data, multiplied desired amount of samples. This, for statistically stationary ﬂows, is in the order
of hours of experimentation for high accuracy. Since this thesis revolve around a stating jet, and
the means of averaging is across samples, not over time. The total experimental time increases
drastically as the water in the aquarium has to reach a quiescent state. This means that each
sample takes a few minutes to complete (depending on the setup), whereas the mentioned hotwire
experiment of a statistically stationary jet would have a sample time in the order of milliseconds.
Experimentation needed for a starting jet to gain a low statistical uncertainty would take days,
even weeks.
To circumvent this, and in order to reach as high a accuracy as possible, it is proposed to use
the obtained data twice. When measuring at 30D from nozzle exit, with e.g. 20 symmetrical
transverse data extraction points. Each transversely oﬀset point have a symmetrical partner on
the other side of the jet centreline at which the initial conditions are exactly the same. Although
the points having the exact same initial condition, they will yield diﬀerent results as the turbulent
behaviour of the ﬂow makes it a stationery random process.
The tricky part is to determine the minimum transverse distance between measurement point in
order for them to be unique.

4.4.3
Nozzle Diameter
The nozzle diameter is a compromise, primarily between three parameters.
• Aquarium size, to justiﬁably neglect the boundary inﬂuence the aquarium have to be a
certain size compared to the nozzle - hence, the goal is to decrease nozzle size.
• Reynolds number, the objective is to run at as high Reynolds number as possible because
of the nature of the experiment - hence, for this parameter the goal is to increase the nozzle
diameter.
• Measurement area, PIV is the chosen measurement method which requires a camera taking
pictures of a light sheet illuminating seeding particles. To determine the exact position of
measurement the ratio between light sheet thickness and nozzle diameter should be high.
Based on these criteria the 15mm nozzle diameter is adequate. Lowering the diameter would
cause further decrease in Reynolds number and uncertainty of the measurement position, but an
increase in diameter would require an aquarium larger than possible for this project (time and
expenses).

inserting a lot of small springs to push the piston rings toward the cylinder for a better seal. All

5.2.1
PIV
Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV), is a well known method through out ﬂuid mechanics. It is used
as a non invasive measuring method that utilize image correlation for tracking a volumetric ﬂow.
Here the goal is to cover the entire width of the jet as well as a few diameters in downstream
direction.
53
==PAGE:66==
Chapter 5. Methodology
Characterisation of starting jet
The PIV analysis relies on a unique correlation for the movement of tracer particles in each
Interrogation Area (IA). To obtain a usable image correlation, a compromise have to be made
when designing the experiment.
The compromise is between physical size of pixel, choice of
IA size, camera frame rate and ﬂow velocity. Additionally, the density of tracer particles is a
compromise between light absorption and quality image correlation.
First, consider the relation between IA size, pixel size, frame rate and ﬂow velocity. The objective
of this project was to aim for as high Reynolds number as possible to clearly be in a region where
the diﬀerent K41 assumptions would be true for a stationary jet. This means of course, maximising
the ﬂow velocity and the frame rate which is limited by tracer particles movement from one image
to the next. Here the rule of thumb is to have a velocity limit of 1/3 IA. From this, the remaining
parameters should be chosen. The frame rate is limited by the choice of camera and the choice of
laser/light sheet optics (light intensity). For the components and setup used in this project, the
limiting frame rate is 250fps. Note, later when a new light sheet optics was produced, the frame
rate limit was 500fps. This was a clear setback as the group expected to have a frame rate of at
least 1000fps. To compensate for this a new compromise had to be made; the velocity limit can
be increased by making the physical size of the object larger (physical pixel size), or increase the
IA. Increasing these two parameters lowers the resolution (scales of motion captured) of measured
ﬂow quantities.
The compromise ended with the following:
• A physical size of pixels to be 0.26mm (1280px by 1024px with object size of 330mm by
260mm).
• PIV interogation area size of 32px by 32px (8.2mm by 8.2mm).
• Frame rate at 250fps (500fps for new light sheet optics used for Scenario D - Constant
acceleration at higher velocity).
• Resulting velocity limit is 0.69m/s (1.38m/s for new light sheet optics).
Secondly, considering the tracer particle density; one must adjust the amount of tracer particles
to accommodate a usable signal for the PIV analysis. Too few particles will result in poor cross
correlation in the PIV analysis; whereas too many particles can result in too much light absorption
close to the laser and thereby varying light intensity across the image. As a rule of thumb, the
amount of tracer particles should be ﬁve to ten particles for each IA. This was ensured by adding
more and more particles until a live image showed an average of ﬁve to ten particle per 32px by
32px of the image.
A pre-made PIV tool called PIVlab was used for the experiment covered in this thesis. This tool
is available as an add on application for MATLAB.

5.5.1
Setup and boundaries
The CFD structural setup was done in Star-CCM+, with the embedded CAD tool. Here the
experimental setup was drawn in a 1:1 scale. The nozzle and water was divided by a thin wall,
which represents the cylinder-wall of the jet, which is placed under the water surface. The piston
that forces the ﬂuid out of the jet, are represented as the back wall of the cylinder. The only
diﬀerence between the CAD drawing and the real experiment is the net.
The net has been
omitted for the simulation part as it would highly increase the complexity of the mesh.
In order to minimise the computation time of the simulation, and increase the cell number for a
ﬁner simulation, the structure was reduced by 3/4. The reasoning for this ’trick’ is based on the
theoretical assumption that a round jet is uniform and does not depend on the angular direction.

Niﬂheim
The simulations proved diﬃcult to grasp in multiple areas. Firstly, a morphing mesh was to be
created together with a controllable User Field Function for the moving piston.
Secondly, administrating and understanding how Niﬂheim and the queue system worked was
challenging. Mainly because the system is Linux based, and the group being unfamiliar with
Linux.
In order to take advantage of the queue system, some of the simulations was transferred to run on
Xeon-8 nodes instead of Xeon-16 nodes or Xeon-24 nodes. It later proved that Xeon-8 only was
able to handle simulations up to 2e6 cells. Any larger the ’run’ was terminated due to memory
shortage.
Other delays was caused by multiple power shortages either from DTU or from DONG. Which
would either turn oﬀall servers and computing nodes or make the system that runs them ineﬃcient.
On multiple occasions it was experienced that post blackout the simulations would start but only
calculate 0.002s physical time, usually this would mean three to four seconds (of seven) physical
time simulated. Other times the queue would be cleared, or reset. Due to students being lower
prioritiesed in the queue system this meant restarting a 4-6 day queue period. Combined with
this a user can only have one simulation running at a time due to hitting the limit of 1000 logical
cores activated. And when the simulations are in queue for up to 7+ days, this delays everything
further. Hence, a good simulation, without power shortages still takes 2-3 weeks. With all the
issues even though three accounts continuously queuing jobs, the results were limited. A total of
ten jobs was completed over a four month period, without any problems should have taken one
and a half months.
5.5.3
Replicating Part Two of Experimentation
The second experiment was likewise performed in a CFD version to use for comparison of the
experimental values. The simulation of the second experiment had to be a detailed and improved
version of the boundary validations and the experience gained from these simulations.
Some of the changes made was to reduce the Time Step from 1ms to 0.5ms. As well as increasing
inner iterations to 25. This was to increase the precision of the results and decrease the solver
residuals for better reliability of results. Meanwhile trying to maintain a aﬀordable computation
time. These simulations was never completed in time for the hand in.

Post Analysis
The ﬁnal part in the analysis of the experimental measurements is the post analysis involving
deriving the statistical parameters, primarily variance.
Statistical Analysis
The culmination of the analysis is the derivation of the mean velocity, variance and the variability
of the two. The primary objective is to compare the temporal dependency of the variance to the
temporal dependency predicted by the theory. Two diﬀerent smoothing techniques has been used
to enable a better comparison of the resulting data, Central Averaging Scheme (CAS) and ﬁltering
via Fourier Transformation (FT).
The CAS method calculates the average of a select number of data points that resides next
to each other. The amount of points needed for a smooth variance curve for this project is around
20 points in order to create the best tendency result - this was achieved with a trial and error
approach.
The FT ﬁltering method, simply uses the FT to transform e.g.
variance to frequency space.
Then a trial and error approach was performed to ﬁnd the optimal frequency band limits. After
ﬁltering, the data are transformed back into temporal space with an inverse FT. The FT ﬁltering
is done with the MATLAB function Blackman, that utilizes the Blackman Window for such ﬁl-

Chapter 8
Discussion
In this chapter, the diﬀerent aspects of the project will be discussed. Primarily, the discussion
will revolve around the experimental setup, analysis and results.
8.1
Experimental Setup
The discussion about the experimental setup concerns several minor aspects.
Aquarium
The boundary of the aquarium, based on the rough simulations, indicated a mild inﬂuence on the
jet in the transverse direction. For neglecting the inﬂuence all together the aquarium cross section
should have been 100D by 100D or larger.
However, the inﬂuence of the bottom of the aquarium (downstream end wall) have not been fully
investigated. This inﬂuence was increased when the measurement time was delayed and when
the velocity was increased for Scenario D. The inﬂuence cannot be neglected, but the amount of
inﬂuence is unknown.
Additionally, the aquarium was made twice due to a underestimation of the stress from the water,
this caused the aquarium to shatter resulting in an eight week delay of measurements and thereby
postponed the entire project.
Jet
The jet (consisting of nozzle, cylinder, piston and motor) raises some concerns.
The motor should, for better results, be of a stronger quality and more powerful. The nozzle
velocity of Scenario B, C & D clearly shows that the motor does not deliver as expected. The
fault lies either with the motor and/or the controlling system.
Further evidence for concern can be found in the nozzle exit velocity plots. Every sample for the
nozzle test has a small exponential part within the ﬁrst half second. This might be an eﬀect of
105
==PAGE:118==

the jet not being fully bled. When ﬁlling up the aquarium, the jet was bled by manually sucking
out the air with a long glass tube, without the possibility to check if it was fully bled. Meaning
an air cushion would most likely be present to disturb the acceleration of the water inside the jet.
And thereby potentially create an uneven acceleration from nozzle exit.
Another quality risk is the seal between piston and cylinder. When this issue was observed some
improvements was made; better and more piston rings, but this did not fully solve the issue. To
counter this, water was continuously fed to the chamber above the piston. However, this was not
foolproof as this was done manually; and at a few occasions it was found that the seal had been
broken. Hence, an air cushion had formed, deeming a number of samples unusable. Ultimately
resulting in the deletion of several samples. This could have happened without being discovered,
disrupting an unknown number of samples.
Laser Optics
At the end of the experimentations, when the new light sheet optics arrived the light intensity was
higher and the quality of the laser sheet was signiﬁcantly better. This surely would have helped
the quality of the low Reynolds number results.
Furthermore, for the correct density of particles, and a larger aquarium there should have been two
lasers, from either side of the aquarium to ensure equal light intensity across the measurement area.
Camera Optics
One consequence of switching to the FASTCAM was the light sensitivity, when comparing the
two cameras, FPS-1000 could handle 2000fps whereas FASTCAM could handle 250fps for the
same light intensity and lens. The FASTCAM has a lot of settings for both camera and post
enhancement of the images. This was superﬁcially tested due to the lack of time, and the optimal
settings for the camera might not have been completely optimised for PIV analysis.
Furthermore, the lens used had trouble focusing the entire measurement area, the groups hypoth-
esis was a small misalignment of the mirrors in the lens as the bought lens was used. Though not
investigated further due to the lack time.
An optimisation not used in this project could be a Scheimpﬂug which correct distorted images
when camera and object are not orthogonal. According to Mie scattering, particles of similar size
as the wave length of light, will scatter more light at lower angles, [Zakaria and Bryanston-Cross,
2012, Hahn, 2009]. Hence, it would be possible to enhance the light intensity and correct the
distortion via the Scheimpﬂug.
Experimental Routine
Introducing the FASTCAM also meant introducing a second computer. This computer is as old
as the camera and added an unknown latency or quality of consistency. One example is the ﬂuctu-
ating save time for each sample. Extend of the inconsistency of this system is not known, however
assumed negligible.

Setup Precision
Caution should be taken when setting up an experiment as this. The quality of the interrelation of
the physical experiment was ensured primarily with a carpenters ruler, bubble level and the sense
of quality of the project group. As an example, during analysis it was found that the presumed
centreline of the jet was about a centimetre oﬀ(at 40D downstream), which was a result of the
jet being about one degree oﬀvertically. So all in all, not perfect conditions.
Furthermore, the calibration images used for calibrating the length in PIVlab has cause for concern
as these was taken with a carpenters ruler suspended in free air by hand. Hence, the measurement
was diﬀerent from time to time. Because of this, a few calibration images was compared and
an average was used for all the ﬁnal measurement, so if the calibration is incorrect, it is equally
incorrect for all data.
Water
The water used as test medium was regular tap water and proved to be a major concern. The
hypothesis of the group stated that the bacteria level of the water was inﬂuential to the data after
three hours of measurement. It was noted to us by a former professor that the tap water had
been tested at some point many years ago. And the results was that the bacteria level was 400
times above the allowed. In order to counter this; a substantial amounts of chlorine was added
to the water, cleaning the aquarium between water replacements as well as using demineralised
water. For the tap water and the demineralised water the result was the same, after three hours
of measuring the water had to be changed. Though it is not known to the full extent how much
the cleaning of the tank helped, this was continued since it is a good experimental practice to try
to minimize the unknown inﬂuences. Since the demineralised water did not help the cause is most
likely organic. The true cause is not known. A collaboration with a chemist and/or a biologist
from DTU could give an interesting project and useful knowledge in how to optimize the water
for future experimentation. Finally condensation proved an issue due to high diﬀerence in water
temperature (weather dependent) and working environment.

8.2
Analysis
The analysis of data concerns three main points; PIVlab, data sorting and statistical post analysis.
PIVlab
Performing measurements for PIV analysis involves a lot of parameters for adjustment. Adjusting
the right settings for camera, laser, light sheet optics and PIV analysis is crucial and is a minor
project in it self.
Interrogation Area (IA) was chosen as small as possible to capture the ﬁner structures of the ﬂow
without compromising the quality due to the overall velocities. This was, based on the velocity
scatter plots, see appendix (G).
Utilizing four image correlation passes results in an increase of needed CPU power to perform
these image correlations. As a result the group gained access to eight computers running PIV
continuously during the measurement period and after due to the extensive amount of accumu-
lated data, and low performing computers.
Data Sorting
The quality of the raw data was questionable, hence, sorting was needed. The level of elimination
was chosen based on several diﬀerent settings for the sorting algorithm. A reasonable setting was
found based on Scenario A data and used for all other scenarios, to maintain the same basis for
comparison. The true extend of poor results is not known as one would have to manually check

8.3
Results
This section discuss the foundation for conclusion of the comparison between experimental results
and theoretical predictions. It involves a few diﬀerent areas which should be treated individually.
Firstly it should be noted that the statistics are not as good as desired, however the results
were good enough for an indication of the validity of the theory based on experimental results.
Double Data
The Double Data (DD) technique was used for all presented data, this artiﬁcially doubled the
number of samples for a range in transverse direction around the centreline, hence improving
statistics. However, it should be noted that the group have not altered the statistical equations
to account for possible correlation between data points. Correlation has been neglected since the
closet DD points are 2.8D apart, resulting in only the largest structures would be correlated. In
conclusion, some eﬀect of correlation will be present, but it is negligible. Though no real justiﬁ-
cation exists for this conclusion.
Comparison
As observed in the variance plots for Scenario A, B and C the steep dip indicates an unknown
eﬀect on the ﬂow in the measurement area. Two main questions rose from this.
Firstly, is the measurement period at a time for which the jet ﬂow follows the similarity theory
(assuming the theory is valid)? Or is the measurement window simply triggered to early, hence
the recorded ﬂow is not fully developed.
Secondly the eﬀects on the velocity and variance, are they caused by the boundaries and/or the
end wall? The boundaries and end wall could simply be too close and thereby having a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence.
The proposed similarity theory states that one condition is constant shear; which entail a constant
velocity gradient. This has been approximated as being possible for a few regions of the velocity

Chapter 9
Conclusion
Based on the compiled results the proposed theory of self similarity is incorrect. Assuming that
the data is true, this would be the obvious conclusion since all time dependency exponent are
approximately equal and near zero. Furthermore the variability are within a reasonably margin
of 10%. An independent study to verify the data presented in this report is needed in order to be
conclusive. The most interesting about this result is the similarity of the exponents regardless of
acceleration type.
The validity of this conclusion is however questionable at best. The two primary concerns being
the chosen delay of the measurement window and the inﬂuence of the aquarium.
Firstly, the chosen measurement window may not be at the proper time for measuring in regards
of obtaining data for parameter comparison with the proposed theory. The obtained time de-
pendency of the variance may just be an implicit state of the front of the jet plume and not the
developed state at which the proposed theory predicts the ﬂow behaviour assuming it is true.
Secondly, the true inﬂuence of the aquarium is debatable. Based on the simulation, some squeeze
eﬀect from the side walls should be expected. More importantly, it is not clear to what extend the
inﬂuence from the end wall have on the ﬂow and measurement area. Six second boundary simu-
lations yielded little inﬂuence with a factor three higher centreline velocity, than the experiment.
But the experiment was increased a full second, which diﬀers from the boundary simulations.
Hence, eﬀects like the velocity dip could be a result of back ﬂow or excess pressure build up from
the end wall.
In summary, two small studies to determine the measurement window and boundary inﬂuence
is essential. This should be with a much larger temporal measurement window to determine the
validity of said measurement window. The boundary inﬂuence could be tested by decreasing the
nozzle size. A conclusion from these studies should be the foundation for a larger study in which
the goal should be to replicate the experiment with the validated/corrected measurement window
and boundary conclusion.

Future work
In this chapter, some of our ideas for improvements and future experimental/simulation projects
are presented. This project have ﬁrst of all been valuable for the group with the amount of theo-

For better simulation results, the group should have had a better understanding of Niﬂheim from

variables still remains and when the premise of the experiment is potentially groundbreaking the-
ory, more experimentations are needed to be conclusive.
Future work on this experiment is in our opinion a costly aﬀair, as the available equipment have
been pushed to the limit, e.g. extremely low F-number lens, increased light sensitivity of camera,
barely adequate motor, small aquarium, frame rate of camera just enough for the jet speed mea-

Three main areas of improvement, where the ﬁrst two are crucial, are presented below.
Firstly, a new camera is needed. The issue of light sensitivity has proven to be a result of the old
FASTCAM as it was only capable of 250fps (500fps with the new light sheet optics) whereas the
faulty FPS-1000 managed 2000fps (4000fps with the new light sheet optics), hence a new camera
would have much better light sensitivity, enabling faster frame rate prompting a higher maximum
velocity and the possibility to shrink the physical object and capture more details of the ﬂow.
This new camera would also have a faster data transfer, more internal memory. All in all, a new
and much more expensive camera would solve most of the optical problems encountered in this
project.
Secondly, the experimental setup should be improved. Here the main points are aquarium size,
piston sealing and motor power.
The motor should be over dimensioned to dismiss any quality concerns of the initial conditions of

Appendix H
Appendix - CFD Methodology
In this appendix the diﬀerent solvers, mesh conditions and others are presented.
H.1
Solvers
To obtain usable results for both a boundary test and replication of experiments within the time
frame of the project, and since simulation is not the primary focus, it was chosen to use a URANS
solver. URANS stands for Unsteady Reynolds Averaged Navier Stoke. Since the goal is to analyse
the diﬀerence between K41 software and the experiment the solvers are chosen as the following:
- Implicit Unsteady
- Mesh Morpher
- Partitioning
- Wall Distance
- Segregated Flow
- k-Omega Turbulence
- k-Omega Turbulence Viscosity
In Star-CCM+, there are two main methods for solving the ﬂuids ﬂow. The ﬁrst one is Segregated
Flow and is mostly used when there is only one ﬂuid present and the ﬂow velocity is relatively
low. The second choice is Coupled Flow, which from our knowledge is used for mixing of ﬂows
and high velocity solutions such as turbines.
The k-ω turbulence solver was chosen over k-ϵ solver due to previous acquired knowledge from a
course concerning Star-CCM+ and its applications.
163
==PAGE:176==
The Implicit Unsteady solver was chosen, since it allows for a better physical time approxima-
tion and is by the help page associated with Star-CCM+ suggested for solving ﬂows with moving
boundaries or convection/diﬀusion ﬂows.
H.2
Mesh
Meshing of the setup, was done with the following meshers: Embedded Thin Mesher, Generalized
Cylinder, Polyhedral Mesher and Surface Remesher. For some of the larger simulations The Prism
layer mesher was added in order to make a better ﬂow out of the nozzle. The Prism layer was
only provided for the cylinder walls, and not the piston (see ﬁgure (H.2.1). This setting was made
due to morphing errors, e.g. the cylinder started bending due to optimization of the wall prism
layer cell structures.
Figure H.2.1: Figure illustrates how the setting to have no prism layers on the piston wall worked.
As seen on ﬁgure (H.2.2) the prism layers have a nice even distribution around the nozzle head,
and can therefore provide a much better resolution of the ﬂow.
Figure H.2.2: Figure shows a screenshot of the piston chamber from the boundary simulation
80D_110D. Where the prism layers are shown.
In order to maintain a fair computation time, the amount of cells was kept between 1e6 and 4e6.
164
==PAGE:177==
An example of an entire plane section of the mesh have been illustrated in ﬁgure(H.2.3).
Figure H.2.3: Figure shows a mesh scene of one side of the simulation for the boundary simulation
80D_110D.
The courant number was used to check that the cell size to time step, δt, ratio. This should
ideally be is less that one to ensure that the ﬂuid particles moves less than one cell per time
step. Courant number higher than unity could entail bad resolution of the simulated ﬂow. As an
example a courant magnitude scalar scene from a boundary simulation (80D_110D) is shown in
ﬁgure (H.2.4).
Figure H.2.4: Figure shows a screenshot of a scalar scene from Star-CCM+ with magnitude
courant number at time 5.75 seconds (0.24 seconds before last acceleration step). For the boundary
simulation 80D_110D.
A problem that was expected but diﬃcult to handle, was the mesh grid for the large boundary
tests. The structures size force a higher and higher base size, otherwise the cell count would
become too large. This results in a more coarse grid and since the nozzle structure won’t be
changed, the cells inside the nozzle has to be reﬁned. The problem here being how much of the
aquarium should actually be reﬁned in order to properly resolve the actual ﬂow. Therefore three
cones was created in the nozzle and piston area. The three cones had three levels of reﬁnement.
The idea being that the primary ﬂow behaviour would be evaluated within the three cones. The
radius and length were designed by evaluating the boundary test for 140D width and 170D depth.
The three cones can be viewed in ﬁgure (H.2.3), the ﬁrst cone starts 3D from the nozzle exit
165
==PAGE:178==
inside the nozzle with 1D in radius, and then covers the next 40D downstream and ends with an
end-radius of ≈8D. The second cone starts ≈33D downstream with ≈8D radius and ends 80D
downstream with an end-radius ≈13D. The last cone starts 33D upstream with start radius ≈
20D and ends 106D downstream with end-radius ≈26D. Now the ﬁrst cone is the most important
since the ﬂow out of nozzle will aﬀect the entire ﬂow in time an experiences the largest velocities.
Therefore, this is the cone with the highest resolution and further away from the nozzle, the mesh
becomes more and more coarse.
H.3
Boundaries
The structure was divided into ﬁve diﬀerent parts: Aquarium Walls, Cylinder Walls, Piston,
Surface Water and Symmetry Wall. The Aquarium Walls, Cylinder Walls and Piston was given
the same boundary type: Wall which means it is a rigid wall that is closed for any ﬂuid. Surface
Water was given a Pressure Outlet boundary, since this is a ’free-boundary’ resembling the
water surface. The two sides Symmetry Wall that deﬁnes the 3/4 cut in the cross section was
given the boundary Symmetry Plane which main function is to mimic the same behaviour on
the other side as the one currently unfolding - similar to a mirror ﬂow, or mirror vortex.
H.4
Acceleration - User Field Function
a moving wall was used to simulate the piston moving. The movement of this wall was done by
determining the velocity of the wall as a function of time in a User Field Function.
The function is a double if statement, that deﬁnes the velocity within a certain time limit, which
results an increasing displacement of the wall. The function as it is written in Star-CCM+ can
be seen below:
[ 0 , 0 , ( $Time >0)?(($Time<6)?( −0.0117∗pow(( $Time∗$TimeStep ) , 1 ) ) : 0 ) : 0 ]
When written into e.g. a matlab script it would come out as:
Time = 0 : 7 ;
% Time variable
DeltaT = 0.0005;
% Time step
AccTime = 6;
% Time of
a c c e l e r a t i o n
[ s ]
i f
(Time > 0 && Time < AccTime)
Velocity = [0 ,0 , −0.0117∗(Time∗DeltaT ) . ^ ( 1 ) ]
e l s e
i f
Time >= 6
Velocity = [ 0 , 0 , 0 ]
end

H.5

an overview of usable features was needed. From this an initial steady solution was carried out for a jet
ﬂow. This however showed quite clearly that more computing power was needed for further analysis.
The ﬁnal simulations were computed on the Niﬂheim cluster - which resides at DTU. The exact speci-
ﬁcations and details about the cluster can be found at the Niﬂheims homepage https://wiki.fysik.
dtu.dk/niflheim. With Niﬂheim it was made possible to make more demanding simulations and try to
determine the validity of our experimental setup and replicating the experiments.
Additionally, in Star-CCM+ it is possible to activate custom made Java scripts.
Theses scripts will
preform a pre-recorded action such as meshing. This option enabled meshing with the help of Niﬂheim.
The Niﬂheim cluster runs with Linux and are operated as a user on the managing system SLURM.
With our rather limited knowledge of how CPU-clusters work and how Linux is operated it took some
time to get used to go around the system and work with it.
There was also multiple blackouts, complications and error runs with the simulations on Niﬂheim that
further delayed the results and progress of our project.
These unforeseen complications furthermore
halted the progress in improving the simulations. Hence, replication of experiments was not made, as
well as boundary test only being made for indications.
