M.Sc Thesis: Free Swell of Clay and link to mineralogy
Abstract
A series of laboratory testing conducted by GEO Copenhagen in soil samples of Paleogene origin aiming to derive
the geotechnical parameters for the design and construction of the Fehmarnbelt Fixed Link. Few sets of raw data
were available and processed in the present study in order to evaluate how the swell behavior of soils could be linked
to clay mineralogy. In more detail, X-ray diﬀraction analysis results, classiﬁcation results as well as swell pressure
results as determined from incremental loading tests in the oedometer setup were processed and further correlate.
It was observed that smectite and illite are the predominant clay minerals in the Paleogene unit, whilst kaolinite
and chlorite co-exist in smaller amounts. Clayey soils originate from the Paleogene unit are of high plasticity and as
observed from processing the available classiﬁcation results, smectite content has a signiﬁcant impact on plasticity.
Swell pressure was well correlated with the initial moisture content, and was also observed to increase deeper in
the formation. Contrary, swell pressure was less correlated to plasticity, whilst no clear relationship between clay
mineralogy and swell pressure was observed.

1
Introduction
Soils that experience considerable volume changes with changes in their moisture content are known as expansive
soils. These volume changes are either in the form of swell upon wetting, or in the form of shrinkage upon drying.
Swelling soils can have an adverse impact on foundations and constructions, or could cause poor conditions on
geotechnical projects.
Clay consists of many minerals, some of which are likely to change their volume when they come in contact with
some types of ﬂuids or when their pore ﬂuid is changed. Hence, swelling behaviour of soils is mainly attributed to
the presence of few clay minerals, which are prone to expand up to several times their volume, such as smectite
(Christidis,(2011), Foster,(1954)).
Swelling pressure, is the pressure required to maintain the soil to its initial volume when access to water or other
ﬂuid type diﬀerent from its pore ﬂuid is allowed. Additionally, swelling pressure can be deﬁned as the pressure
required to keep a constant volume when the chemical conditions are such to induce a volume increase tendency
(Kayabali et. al.,(2014), DiMaio,(2001)). Numerous studies have been published, relating the swell pressure to
few basic soil properties, such as initial moisture content, plasticity and cation exchange capacity (Anderson et.
al.,(2010), AlZubaidi et. al.,(2016), Elarabi,(2004)), whilst in several other published studies, swelling pressure is
further related to clay content (El-Sohby et. al.,(1981), Basma et. al.,(1995), Rao et. al.,(2004)).
Besides a general approach supporting that the principle cause of swelling is the presence of clay minerals, and that
swell pressure is related to clay content and few soil indices (such as moisture content and plasticity), swelling is
still a peculiar and complicated phenomenon that might be aﬀected by several factors and conditions, which are
constantly elaborated.
1.1
Aim and Objectives
The main objective of the present study is to evaluate if swell pressure is correlated with clay mineralogy and
few basic soil indices, such as the initial moisture content and plasticity, with respect to origin formation and
environment. Aiming to investigate how the swelling behaviour of clays origin from the Paleogene unit, is linked
to clay mineralogy, X-ray diﬀraction analysis results, classiﬁcation results as well as swell pressure measurements
available from GEO Copenhagen will process. An eﬀort will be made to investigate the correlation between soil
properties and mineralogy, soil properties and swell pressure and further evaluate if there is any correlation between
swell pressure and speciﬁc clay minerals, or combinations of clay minerals.
1.2
Thesis Structure
The present Thesis projects consists of 7 sections:
• Section 1 underlies the objective of this study, introduces its subject and provides an overview of its structure.
• Section 2 provides a brief theoretical background. Clay minerals properties which could be related to swelling,
as well as the structure of few clay minerals are available. Swelling mechanisms of clay and several factors
could govern swelling are also included in this section.

2
Theoretical Background
This section includes all the relevant theoretical information, necessary to support the process of the available
data as well as a basis to conduct a brief description of the experimental methods utilized. Clay properties and
clay minerals structure which are signiﬁcantly related to swelling are presented as well as few factors that govern
swelling. A theoretical approach of the swelling mechanism and the swell pressure, are also available.
2.1
Clay Mineral Properties
Clay is a type of colloidal, ﬁne-grained natural soil material that contains phyllosilicates (clay minerals) which
are able to develop plasticity when wet. Clay deposits are mostly composed of phyllosilicate minerals containing
variable amounts of water trapped into the minerals structure and according to Hathaway (1979) clay minerals can
be detrial, authigenic or diagenetic in origin. The term clay associates to two diﬀerent concepts: one of the particle
size and one of the particle composition.
2.1.1
Particle size
According to Hathaway (1979), clay minerals consist of silicate layers (phyllosilicates as mentioned earlier), that
usually occur in a particle size of 2 µm or even smaller and various shapes. However, there are few specimens of these
minerals of larger particle size, such as mica and chlorite, as well as few other minerals, known as the ”non-clay”
minerals (such as quartz, feldspar and various carbonates) occurring in a particle size signiﬁcantly smaller than 2
µm in diameter.
Knowledge of clay particles size is important, as this property of clay minerals aﬀects signiﬁcantly few other
physical properties, such as ion-exchange property, plasticity; which stands as the basic physical property of clay
materials and speciﬁc surface [10],[20].
2.1.2
Ion-exchange properties
Cation exchange capacity (CEC) is a characteristic property of all clay minerals and of paramount importance for
Smectite speciﬁcally. CEC is related to substitution in tetrahedral and octahedral sheets, which creates a charge,
and to absorption or dissociation of protons at crystal edges.
Layer charge refers as permanent when it results from structural substitution or non-permanent when it comes from
the weak bonding of the crystal edges. Either way, layer charge is controlled by the interlayer cations (Na, Ca, K,
Mg, H).
Minerals of the smectite group characterized by a high CEC. On the other hand, minerals of the Kaolin group
have small CEC, however they do have a signiﬁcant anion exchange capacity (AEC). AEC is attributed to the H+
absorption on the crystal edges and to the further formation of water molecules. That water molecules are easily
displaced or exchanged by anionic groups as their bonding is weak [10],[20].
2.1.3
Plasticity
By deﬁnition, plasticity is that property of a material that deforms under an applied stress and once this stress is
removed the material retains the given shape. Plasticity is a characteristic physical property of clay and its nature

is related to the water molecules which absorbed by the clay minerals surfaces. Thus, a rigid ﬁlm forms that links
together clay particles resulting to a coherent network of clay particles and water that has the ability to retain a
shape when a stress is applied.
According to Grimshaw (1971), plasticity is aﬀected by the type of clay minerals, the amount of water present, the
particle size, shape and distribution, the speciﬁc surface of clay particles and the presence and nature of non-clay
minerals. Although clay minerals impart strongly the property of plasticity, the presence of non-clay minerals in a
soil could reduce it.
According to Christidis (2011), in minerals with a high CEC, such as smectites, plasticity is aﬀected by the type
of the exchangeable cations. Na-smectites for example, correspond to high plasticity whereas Illite-rich clays have
relatively low plasticity.
The presence of water is crucial for the development of plasticity. When water content is signiﬁcantly low, clay solid
particles come in contact and plasticity reduces. Also, when the water content is signiﬁcantly high water molecules
interrupt solid particles connection resulting to a less rigid ﬁlm formation and thus plasticity reduces. All in all, it
can be understood that water contain should be among suﬃcient limits in order to develop plasticity. These limits
are known as the Atterbarg limits and correspond to the minimun amount of water needed to develop plasticity
(plastic limit) and the amount of water beyond of which clay deforms under its own weight (liquid limits). The
principal and methods for determination of Atterberg limits will be elaborated further in a following section.
Atterberg limits could be assumed as a good indicator of swelling potential of a soil. According to Holds and Gibs
(1956), most of the volume change occurs between the shrinkage and the liquid limit. When the moisture content is
close to the shrinkage limit, soil would have a greater potential to swell and correspondingly if the initial moisture
content is above the shrinkage limit, soil is less prone to swell.
2.1.4
Hydration
Unlike granular soils, clayey soils are strongly inﬂuenced by the presence of water. Clay particles are almost always
hydrated in the nature as clay minerals tend to absorb water molecules on their surfaces and interlayer spaces, so
it turns out that hydration is an important property of clay [45].
Clay crystals have a negative charge which is balanced by the absorption of positively charged cations such as
Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+. These cations do not occur as a single layer but they are spread over two diﬀused layers
which are jointly refer as the diﬀused double layer (DDL). DDL forms either thicker or thinner, when the afore-
mentioned cations are absorbing water molecules which surround clay crystals and held by hydrogen bonding in
clay crystal’s surfaces. The thickness of DDL inﬂuences signiﬁcantly the swelling properties of the soil as well as
its plasticity. When clay swells, particles are pushed away from each other and the natural structure breaks. That
process is controlled partially by the thickness of the DDL which is determined from its concentration to cations [45].
According to Christidis (2011), sodium absorbs around six times more water than calcium, therefore the DDL
will be thicker when sodium occurs predominately compared to water absorption by calcium which results to the
formation of a thinner DDL.

Clay hydration is distinguished in three diﬀerent models with respect to increasing water activity: (a) interlamellar

2.2
Structure of Clay Minerals
Clays are naturally occurring minerals which are formed by weathering and decomposition of igneous rocks. As
mentioned earlier in this section, clay minerals are layered silicates or phyllosilicates which can be considered to
consist of two modular units: a sheet of corner-linked tetrahedron and a sheet of edge-linked octahedra.
Tetrahedral sheets are continuous and composed of Si, Al or Fe cations. Silicon (or in some cases Al or Fe) is
located in the centre of the tetrahedra and at each of its four corners oxygen forms, as can be seen in Figure 1.
Figure 1: On the left side: a single SiO4 tetrahedron, on the right side: the sheet structure of silicon tetrahedra
arranged in a hexagonal network (source: Hathaway (1997)).
Octahedral sheets are occupied by Mg, Fe2+ cations or by trivalent cations of Al3+, Fe3+ as can be seen in Figure
2.
Figure 2: On the left side: a single octaherdal unit, on the right side: a octahedral sheet structure (source: Hathaway
(1997)).
As clay minerals are made up of a combination of the tetrahedral and octahedral sheets, they are further grouped
into categories according to the arrangement of the contained sheets in the layers. Thus, a clay mineral which
consists of one tetrahedral and one octahedral sheet is a 1:1 clay, also known as a T-O layer. Accordingly, a clay
mineral consists of one octahedral sheet (central) in between two tetrahedral sheets is a 2:1 clay or a T-O-T layer.
In Figure 3, a 1:1 layer group in which a tetrahedral sheet lies over an octahedral sheet is illustrated, as well as a
2:1 layer group in which an octahedral is linked with two tetrahedral sheets [10],[20],[28].

Figure 3: The 1:1 and the 2:1 layer. The large white circles illustrate the oxyge, the grey the hydroxyls. The small

The 1:1 layer group consists of one sheet of silicon tetrahedron combined with one sheet of Al-octahedron in a
hexagonal network. The sheets might not ﬁt properly into this hexagonal network, resulting to distortions of the
sheets and occurring to the rise of diﬀerent mineral species. The 1:1 layer structure is typical for the Kaolin group,
where Kaolinite, Halloysite, Dickite and Nacrite are members of that group.
On the other hand, the 2:1 layer group consists of two tetrahedral [SiO4] sheets with one octahedral sheet in
between, a typical structure for the most clay minerals, such as talc, illite or mica groups and smectite groups
(montmorillonite-saponite)[10],[20].
Between two successive 1:1 and 2:1 layers, there is an inter-layer, or an empty space, which is electrostatically
neutral when there is no excess charge. In case of excess charge, this inter-layer is neutralized by cations such as
Ca, Mg, Na, K, or hydrated cations and hydroxide octahedral groups. Thus, an additional octahedral sheet forms,
known as 2:1:1 clay or T-O-T-O layer. That type of structure is typical for the chlorite minerals group.
Besides the aforementioned structures of clay minerals, there are also the mixed-layered clay minerals. The
term ’mixed-layer’ does not refer to physical mixtures but refers to that clay minerals which are formed by at least
two types of intergrown layers. Diﬀerent types of layers are stacked together randomly in a regular or partially
regular manner constituting the clay mineral structure. Mixed-layer clay minerals are very common phases in many
types of clays, such as bentonites, kaolins and shales[10],[20].
All in all, based on their structures and physical properties, clay minerals are classiﬁed into four main groups as
follows [10],[19],[20],[28]:
• Kaolinite: a highly stable 1:1 silica mineral which is non-swelling, with a relatively small surface area and a
low absorptive capacity.
• Smectites: are 2:1 silica minerals which swell on wetting and shrink on drying. They have a large surface
areas and a relatively high absorptive capacity. Montmorillonite and Bentonite are inclouded in this group.

• Chlorite: a group of mixed-layer clay minerals. They most often occur in mixtures with other materials, a

2.2.1
Smectite
Smectites are clay minerals with unique physical and chemical properties. Their characteristic physical properties
are related to their very small particle size (usually smaller tha 0.5 µm) and their correspondingly large speciﬁc
surface area, as well as their cation exchange capacity. Smectite characterized by swelling properties, high plasticity,
bonding capacity and the ability to react with inorganic and organic reagents.
As for smectite’s layering structure, it forms without a speciﬁc order, almost randomly, by stacking the sheets,
one on top of the other constituting a T-O-T layer (Figure 4). Due to the ionic substitution in tetrahedral and
octahedral sheets, or less often due to some vacancies existing in the octahedral sheets, an excess layer charge is
created. That layer charge is balanced by the inter-layer cations which are exchangeable and lead to that important
property of Smectite, the so-called cation exchange capacity (CEC). Because of this property, the layer charge of
Smectite is permanent and not aﬀected by the aqueous media in which Smectite might be dispersed [10],[20].
Figure 4: Structural pattern of Smectite (source: Wilson (2013))
According to Laird (1999), the inﬂuence of the layer charge of Smectites on ion exchange reactions is attributed
to steritic eﬀects (i.e the non-bonding interactions that inﬂuence the shape and reactivity of ions and molecules)
linked to swelling. For the case of a low layer charge, the interlayer cations of Smectite are fully hydrated, leading to
remarkable swelling properties. Remarkable swelling might also noticed in the presence of polar organic compounds.
According to Christidis (2011), Smectites swell not only when added to water or to polar organic solvent, but also
when they are in contact with a vapour of solvent of a high vapour pressure.
Smectitic clays have signiﬁcantly high swelling potential based on their speciﬁc surface and their interlayer bonding
which is relatively weak. Therefore, the precense of smectite in a soil might aﬀect remarkably the swelling potential
of a soil, even when smectite co-exists with non-swelling particles.

2.2.2
Kaolinite
Kaolinite along with Dickite, Nacrite and Halloysite are 1:1 dioctahedral minerals of the Kaoline-serpenite group.
Kaolinite (Al2Si2O5(OH)4) consists of a silica and an alumina sheet bond together and in general does not process
layer charge. Because of its relatively low cation exchange capacity, replacements do not take place in Kaolinite’s
structure (Figure 5) and other variations, such as Al3+ vacancies, in alumina layer are balanced by O-OH exchange
so that the structure has no excess charge [10],[20].
Figure 5: Structural pattern of Kaolinite (source: Wilson(2013))
The relatively small cation exchange capacity of Kaolinite, is attributed to its outer surface structure that consists
of silica or alumina sheets with broken edges; as they can not ﬁt properly in the hexagonal network causing edges
breaking or distortion. Therefore, the electrochemical properties of Kaolinite depend only on its surface properties,
controlled by silica and alumina, and on broken edges bonding.
Only a small number of ionized cations could exchange at the broken edges and not between the sheets and that
dissociation of cations does not pry the clay particles apart from their constituent units when Kaolinite disperse
in water, so there is no expansion. Consequently, Kaolinite has a low swelling capacity attributed to its colloidal
surface structure and its low cation exchange capacity.
2.2.3
Illite
According to Hathaway (1997), illite is a member of mica group and it is a 2:1 structured mineral consists of
layered alumino-silicates. Analytically, an alumina octahedron is in between two silicon tetrahedron and their layer
sequence is occupied by poorly hydrated potassium cations [K+] (Figure 6) in which the swelling absence is at-
tributed. Illite’s cation exchange capacity is considered relatively low, although is higher than Kaolinite’s.
Besides its individual non-swelling behaviour, when Illite is presence in mixed layer clay soils, as for example illite-
montmorillonite clay, they could be considered as water sensitive and thus more prone on swelling, even when
montmorillonite is not dominant.

2.3
Clay Swelling: Theoretical Background
According to Foster (1954), the phenomenon of swelling is associated with the hydration of clays. Clay minerals
tend to absorb water molecules on their external surface or in their interlayer spaces and some of them are prone
to swell or to expand their volume as a direct result to changes in water content. However, not all the types of clay
swell when hydrated. For instance, the kaolinite group corresponds to little or no swelling on hydration as mentioned
earlier in this section. On the other hand, montmorillonite swells many times its dry volume when hydrated. These
diﬀerences in swelling characteristics among diﬀerent clays may be directly related to clay’s chemical composition,
structure and nature of exchangeable cations.
2.4
Swelling Mechanisms
Swelling along with plasticity and particle size, is one of the most characteristic and important properties of clay.
Two main regimes of clay swelling have been distinguished: the crystalline and the osmotic swelling.
• Crystalline swelling (or surface hydration) can occur to all types of clay minerals when exposed to brine,
aqueous solutions or whatever contains large quantities of cations. In the presence of water, interlayer cations
hydrate and force clay layers apart. Molecular layers of water absorbed in the crystal surfaces of clay (or
for the case of expansive clays, are also absorbed in the interlayer surfaces). During this entire absorption
process, water molecules surround the clay crystal structure (and create hydrogen bonding with the layers)
in such a way that the structure increases in its space and ﬁnally result to a volume increase. According
to Anderson (2010), crystalline swelling occurs step-wise (or in levels as can be seen in Figure 7). Water
molecules are absorbed via a step-wise mechanism and thus water molecules form distinct layers into crystals
interlayer region.

2.5
Swelling Process
It is important to distinguish and understand the swelling mechanism in order to study the swelling process developed
by a clay structure. In general, considering an expansive clay, such as montmorillonite or bentonite which can expand
signiﬁcantly, up to several times their original volume, swelling is a time dependent process occurring in three steps:
initial or intervoid swelling, primary and secondary swelling. Acconditg to Sivapullaiah et. al.,(1996),
swelling occurs only after the voids of the non-swelling particles are ﬁlled up with swollen clay particles.
The
magnitude of the swell within the voids, is called initial or intervoid swelling. The observable swell, after intervoid
swelling, corresponds to the so-called primary swelling and is time depended. Time swell relationships show that
swelling continues to occur for a long time after the primary swelling, and this is called secondary swelling.
2.6
Factors that control swelling
Swelling of expansive soils is aﬀected by a large number of factors due to its complex mechanism. Nevertheless, by
deﬁnition swell can occur in soils that contain clay minerals, due to their complex structure and cation exchange
properties. It is clear that clay content is of paramount importance when swelling is studied as clay mineralogy and
composition are mostly govern swelling behaviour of soils. However, swelling is also governed by many more factors
such as the dry density, the moisture content (the initial and/or the natural), the cementation which is expressed
as the calcium carbonate content, the loading history and aging as well as by the pH and temperature.

2.6.1
Moisture content and remolding
The engineering properties of expansive soils are greatly inﬂuenced by changes in their moisture content or suction.
As an overall, based on the literature review, in a clayey soil, if the moisture content increases the soil swells and
if the moisture content decreases the soil shrinks.
The expansive behaviour of clays is controlled by the intristic expansiveness, a property which is related to changes
in the water content by suction. According to Elarabi (2004), if suction of water is exerted in a soil, water will
move out of soil under stress and the soil will therefore decrease in volume. Contrary, if water is added, suction
will decrease and respectively the overall volume of soil will increase. Therefore, the overall volume change and
the initial moisture content of a soil subjected to suction change under initial conditions is vast for soils with high
intristic expansiveness compared to soils with lower intristic expansiveness at the same conditions.
Branum (1966) stated that ”swell can not occur unless water is available”. Thus swelling properties are directly
related to the amount of water that can be accepted by the soil”. Based on that theory, Branum (1966), performed
free swell tests in several specimens of clayey soil in order to evaluate how their swelling parameters are governed by
moisture content. It was observed that the increase of the initial moisture content in a compacted soil corresponds
to a decrease of the overall volume. That volume change of soil was explained on the basis of two diﬀerent concepts:
(a) the soil surface energy which is controlled by the exchangeable cations and (b) the engineering properties of the
soil.
Another eﬀort to evaluate how moisture content impacts on volume change was made by Elarabi (2004), by studying
various clay samples by performing free swell tests. From that study, it was observed that the magnitude of swelling
was inversely proportional to the initial moisture content of soil. With an increase in the initial moisture con-
tent, swelling decreased rapidly. Consequently, it was concluded that moisture content governs swelling magnitude
and the term ’swelling moisture content’ introduced as the limited moisture content at which swelling does not occur.
Another important parameter when swelling is studied is the soil disturbance. When soil samples are remolded the
swelling behaviour might be aﬀected signiﬁcantly, causing poor or not accurate results interpretation of laboratory
investigation on swelling.
AlZubaidi et al.(2016), studied the eﬀect of drying and remolding on expansive soil samples. According to that study,
the ﬁnal swell (as well as the swell pressure), increased as the initial moisture content decreased in both remolded
and undisturbed samples. As for the remolded samples, a higher swelling noticed compared to undisturbed ones.
It was conducted that swelling increases when initial dry density increases respectively, especially for the case of
remolded samples and that remolding aﬀects swelling remarkably.

Calcium carbonate content
Calcium carbonate is encountered in soils as the mineral calcite which is often included in clays.
The presence of calcium carbonate in clayey soils is one of the reasons why swelling behaviour might be restricted.
According to Rimmer et al. (1976), there are two possible ways in which swelling behaviour is aﬀected by calcium
carbonate content. The ﬁrst one corresponds to that characteristic of calcium carbonate to act like an inorganic
cement which binds adjacent clay particles together and thus swelling is prevented. The second way calcium car-

2.7
Swell Testing
Swell is deﬁned as the value of volumetric or axial strain due to the expansion process that some soil types ex-
perience. An equivalent to swell is the swelling potential, which is determined (in percentage) directly in the
laboratory by performing one-dimensional swell-consolidation tests. However, Swelling potential could also evaluate
indirectly from classiﬁcation index properties such as plasticity index, clay content and shrinkage index.
According to Zumrawi (2013), the term ”swelling potential” is determined as the ratio of increase in thickness
to the initial thickness of a soil sample compacted in a consolidation ring at optimum moisture content, under a
deﬁned pressure so that it can undergoes free swell once is soaked.
Swelling potential is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the applied load (or stress) as observed by the literature. According to
Basm et al. (1995), as soil swells, it exerts an upward force (or pressure) which could be balanced by an externally
superimposed load. In that case, if the swelling force (or the swelling pressure) is greater than the applied one, soil
expands and as result that external pressure increases and expansion decreases respectively.
2.7.1
Free Swell
A relative simple test was developed by Holtz and Gibs (1956) in order to determine the swell potential in a
soil. According to them, free swell is expressed as a percentage increase in the volume of the soil to its original vol-
ume when the tested soil sample is hold laterally thus it can only change its volume axially when is added into water.
Free Swell Test is a standardised experimental method followed by the standards ASTM D4546 (2014), also
refers as the ’single point wetting-after-loading’ on a single specimen.
According to the standards, this method is typically used for measuring one-dimensional wetting-induced swell
strain of a single intact specimen of natural soil or a single intact specimen of compacted soil. The specimen is
loaded up to a speciﬁc vertical stress which is typically the in-situ overburden stress, or otherwise it is loaded up
to a particular designed stress. The free swell strain is evaluated in the soil sample and then inundated to measure
the wetting induced strain under particular stress.
Despite Free Swell testing method has several limitations and governed by various parameters, it could consider as a
prediction of the degree of soil expansiveness. Furthermore, its measured parameters (such as the Free Swell Ratio)
could provide additional information about the mineralogy of clay in case of absence of sophisticated classiﬁcation
techniques such as X-Ray diﬀraction.

2.7.2
Swell Pressure
Likewise Free Swell Test, another procedure followed by the same standards ASTM D4546 (2014)-Method
B is followed to determine swell pressure. Swell pressure can evaluate by using an advanced oedometer setup in
oedometer tests such as the incremental loading test (IL) which was used in that project. The principals of this
method as well as an analytical description will follow in a next section of this project.
According to the standards, swell pressure corresponds to the pressure in which an expansive soil exerts, if it is not
allowed to swell or change its volume.
Swelling pressure is an important parameter for the design of structures interacting with swelling soils and must be
evaluated in order to prevent damages (such as openings and cracks). However, there is a plethora of parameters
inﬂuencing swell pressure and based on the literature review, few of them are listed below:
• Exposure of a clayey soil to a ﬂuid with diﬀerent composition from its pore ﬂuid: According to
DiMaio et al. (2001), when a clayey soil is exposed to a ﬂuid whose ionic forces are lower or whose dielectric
constant is higher than that of the pore ﬂuid, then a possible high swell pressure might occur.
• Eﬀect of the initial moisture content: Basma et al. (1995), studied ten soil samples using advanced
oedometer techniques in order to evaluate how the moisture content impacts on the swell pressure. From the
majority of the tested samples it was observed that swell pressure reduced by increasing the initial moisture
content.
• Clay content: As introduced earlier in this section, few clay minerals have a swelling ability due to their
structure, their inter-layer bonding and the cation exchange capacity. Hence, swell pressure might be strongly
related to the clay mineralogy in a soil.

3.2.3
Atterberg Limits
Although grain-size constitutes a well-determined boundary to categorize soils and study signiﬁcant soil properties,
is not enough to rely only on that in terms of classiﬁcation. The preferred way to classify ﬁne-grained soils, is
based on correlations between the grain-size and soil plasticity. That kind of correlation can only be done once the
Atterberg limits are calculated.
Prior the Atterberg limits description, is important to introduce the term consistency which stands for the strength
at which soil materials are held together, or the resistance of soils to deformation. Consistency also refers to the
strength and resistance to penetration of the soil under in situ conditions. Soil consistency is measured for wet,
moist and dry soil samples. For wet soils speciﬁcally, it is expressed as plasticity. Consistency of clay or other
types of cohesive soils is described as soft, medium, stiﬀor hard and can determine directly from unconﬁned com-
pressive strength measurements in soil samples. In that way, it is possible to group the lower values of unconﬁned
compressive strength to very soft consistency, the slightly higher values of unconﬁned compressive strength to soft
consistency and so on, up to the highest values of unconﬁned compressive strength that would correspond to hard
consistency of soil.
After a cohesive soil has been remolded, its consistency can possibly change by increasing or decreasing water con-
tent. For example, if water content of a clay slurry slightly reduce, clay passes from a liquid state through a plastic
state and if water content reduce further, clay passes to the solid state. As can be understood, water content is an
important parameter through which the liquid or plastic state of a soil is identiﬁed. Critical water content at
which the soils changes from one state to the other are known as consistency limits or Atterberg’s
limits.
Speciﬁcally, the liquid limit (wℓ), is the water content at which a soil passes from the liquid to the plastic
state and is determined by liquid limit tests. Two diﬀerent methods are available to determine the liquid limit, the
Casagrande’s Method and the falling cone method. Both methods are relatively easy, accurate and repeatable.
The plastic limit (wp), or the lower limit of the plastic state is the water content at which the soil begins to
crumble when rolled out into thin threads.
The numerical diﬀerence between the liquid limit and the plastic limit is the plasticity index (Ip).
The range of water content within a soil passes to plasticity is known as the plastic range. As the water content of
a cohesive soil approaches the lower limit of plastic range, the stiﬀness and the compaction degree of soil increases.
The following ration (Iℓ), is called the liquidity index of soil:

could indicate high compressibility of a soil and high swelling potential. A high plasticity index generally results in
a low shear strength and low plasticity index could result in a signiﬁcant change of consistency even with a small
change in the water content.

Since the main objective of this study is to link swelling of clays with mineralogy, it is remarkably important to get
an analytical overview of the mineralogy of Clays of Paleogene origin. As mentioned earlier in a previous section,
few clay minerals are prone on swelling while some others are not. Also, the presence of non-clay minerals could
impact on the swelling behaviour of soils, thus is of paramount importance to acknowledge which speciﬁc minerals
constitute the Clays of Paleogene origin. Identiﬁcation and characterization of the crystalline structure and the
mineralogical composition in bulk samples utilized by the robust technique of the X-ray diﬀraction.
The principal of this method is the diﬀraction of monochromatic X-ray radiation of know wavelength (λ) on the
lattice levels of the tested soil sample, and further on the determination of the intervals (d) of the levels of the
crystal lattice, which are unique to each mineral. X-ray study of soil samples is performed by inserting the tested
material in powder form into special containers and via speciﬁc ray diﬀractometers the analysis is performed. The
determination of the mineral phases is further derived from radio-graphs.
Analytically, in 1912 Max Von Laue was the ﬁrst to observe the X-ray diﬀraction from crystals. According to his
theory, it was noticed that as a beam of X-ray passes through a crystal, X-ray is actually striking in the crystalline

structure. In other words, Max Von Laue observed that the crystal lattices (hkl) are the diﬀraction barriers of the

3.3.1
Principles and Theory
According to Terzaghi et al.,(1996) if an applied load on a layer of a saturated soil (such as clay) increase, the layer
is compressed resulting to excess water drain out of it. This constitutes the process of consolidation, which in more
simple words is a mechanical process of gradual volume change in a soil, in response to a pressure change.
During consolidation, if a thin horizontal layer is considered, the quantity of water drains out of it, is larger that the
quantity of water that enters it. The diﬀerence between water quantities corresponds to the volume change of that
soil layer. Consolidation is produced by added pressure or load per unit of area and it is also known as consolidation
pressure increment and consists of two stages. The so-called primary consolidation stage, where the solid parts of
soil are carrying the stress and excess water caused deformation. The secondary stage of consolidation, follows the
end of the primary stage and corresponds to the compression occurred prior.
Studying the one-dimensional deformation of soil, allows the investigation of few geotechnical parameters that could
cause deformation in the ﬁeld such as the overburden loading or soil aging. It is relatively easy to impose this one-
dimensional deformation in an oedometer setup, by applying vertical stress to a specimen in successive load steps.
Each loading corresponds to a certain time frame to ensure the consolidation progress.

After all the parts of the equipment are ready for testing and have been placed properly in position on the oedome-

The specimen is further subjected to distinguished increments of vertical loading and unloading, with its top and

to the specimen up to the level where the top porous disc is submerged, whilst evaporation losses are replaced if
noticed. Also, if there is a need, extra pore water is added during the incremental loading periods in case the soil
material swells under the applied stress. For the case in which the soil material keeps swelling because of the ex-
tra added water, swelling is prevented by increasing the vertical stress to the next increment loading in the sequence.
During the incremental loading stages time is recorded routinely, as well as the axial deformation of the specimen.

and the consolidation stages are easilly observe. During the incremental loading testing, three stages are available
as follows (Terzaghi et al.,(1996)) :
• Stage I: Initial compression; primarily caused by preloading
• Stage II: Primary Consolidation, which occurs from excess pore water pressure dissipation and corresponds
to soil volume change
• Stage III: Secondary Consolidation, which occurs after the excess pore water pressure dissipation due to
plastic deformation/readjustment of soil particles.

4.1.1
Geology
As characterized by Okkels (2009), ˚Arhus port is a challenging ﬁeld as the constructions have to be founded on very
greasy, cracked tertiary plastic clay consisting the so-called Søvindmergel formation. The port of ˚Arhus is mainly
built by ﬁllings in a previous sea-covered area, in combination with dredging for the harbour basins.
Søvindmergel formation is a geological deposition of ﬁne-grained marl which layer series was deposited at the end
of the Eocene for approximately 45 to 35 million years ago and is found in most of Jutland, as well as in the eastern
part of the North Sea. The layer series of the Søvindmergel formation can only be seen in East Jutland, as in West
Jutland they are covered by younger deposits. Søvindmergel formation was deposited in a marine environment
several hundred meters deep, at the time when the North Sea was remarkably larger than today and during a
period when the global climate gradually became colder.
As mentioned earlier, Søvindmergel formation consists of very plastic cracked clay, and is characterized by its high
calcium carbonate content and therefore appears as light grey to almost white. However, the calcium carbonate
content could vary signiﬁcantly over short distances and thus almost calcium carbonate-poor layers could appear
in darker colours, or calcium carbonate-free layers could appear in even darker to black colours, embeded in the
formation.
According to Okkels (2009), calcium carbonate content increases upwards and the occurrence of poor calcareous
layers is most frequent in the lower parts of the formation. Based on biostratigraphic investigations, the high or-
ganic content of this formation soil as well as the high calcium carbonate content, come from cocoliths (plankton).
High calcium carbonate content is assumed to represent warmer periods, whilst low calcium carbonate content is
Page 33 of 65
==PAGE:42==

assumed to represent the opposite.
Based on the varying calcium carbonate content, Søvindmergel formation is possible to sub-divide further into
calcareous zones, with the oldest encountered just above the Lillebælt formation of the Paleogene unit.
With few exceptions, in most places of Jutland, Søvindmergel formation overlies Lillebælt formation and for the
˚Arhus port region speciﬁcally, deposits of the Søvindmergel formation exist exclusively.

6
Discussion
According to few published studies (Foster (1954), Sivapullaiah et. al.,(1996) and Erguler et al.,(2003)) the swelling
behaviour of expansive soils is frequently attributed to their clay mineralogy content among others. As introduced
in a previous section, smectite is a 2:1 silica mineral prone to expand several times its volume due to its high
absorptive capacity, whilst kaolinite is a highly stable 1:1 mineral with low absorptive capacity and less prone to
expand its volume. However, illite which is also a 2:1 silica mineral, corresponds to a lower absorptive capacity
than smectite but is more prone to expand than kaolinite. Due to the aforementioned properties of clay minerals,
as well as their inter-layer bonding and their cation exchange properties, clay mineralogy could link to the swelling
behaviour of soils. However in the present study that does not seem to be the case. Although the tested soil
samples are predominated by clay minerals and speciﬁcally by illite and smectite, no clear relationship between clay
mineralogy and swell pressure could observe.
In the previous section the swell pressure, as determined by incremental loading tests, plotted versus diﬀerent clay
minerals contents (see Figure 51, 52), the total clay content (see Figure 54) and a combination of clay minerals (see
Figure 51). As an overall, it was observed that the combination of smectite and illite content that increases corre-
sponds to a decrease in the swell pressure, whilst the total clay content does not correspond to a good correlation
with swell pressure. Additionally, neither when the swell pressure was plotted against plasticity (see Figure 49) a
good correlation was observed. Hence, it was decided to discuss in detail the diﬀerences between the samples that
swell the most and the samples that swell least, in terms of clay minerals content, plasticity and initial moisture
content.

swell pressure and the sample which corresponds to the lower swell pressure chosen, along with their clay mineralogy

On an eﬀort to investigate the eﬀect of drying and remolding on undisturbed expansive soils, AlZubaidi et. al.,(2016)
tested numerous clayey/silty samples classiﬁed as (CH). From that study it was concluded that the swell pressure
increased as the initial moisture content decreased for both remolded and undisturbed samples, showing a very
good correlation between initial moisture content and swell pressure.
Kayabali et. al.,(2014) studied numerous soil samples of diﬀerent levels of plasticity on their eﬀort to investigate how
swell pressure is related to simple soil indices. The outcome of their study showed that initial moisture content is
signiﬁcantly aﬀects swell pressure. However, as was mentioned to that study, despite the good correlation observed
between initial moisture content and swell pressure, values of the initial moisture content cannot be used alone to
predict swell pressure accurately. Hence, after including Atterberg limits in regression analysis they came up with
an empirical relationship out of which swell pressure is estimated from Atteberg limits and initial moisture content.
As proposed from Kayabali et. al.,(2014), their empirical relationship could be used to estimate swell pressure for
soils with plasticity index up to 60.
In the present study, after plotting the initial moisture content versus the swell pressure as measured from dedicated
swell tests, a very good correlation is observed, that comes in agreement with the results of the published studies
conducted by AlZubaidi et. al.,(2016) and Kayabali et. al.,(2014). Since the tested clay samples originate from
the Paleogene unit mostly correspond to high plasticity it was not possible to use the aforementioned empirical
relationship, in order to check if plasticity has an impact on swell pressure.
Lastly, regarding the samples whose swell pressure was not measured as an actual value, few remarks were also
made. Smectite content observed slightly higher reaching 43%, compared to samples whose swell pressure measured
accurately where the maximun smectite content was 31%. Illite content was observed remarkably lower, whilst
kaolinite/chlorite content was almost even.
The outcome of the tests where swell pressure was not measured as an actual value could not be used to evaluate
the relationship between swell pressure and clay mineralogy, since no clear correlation is available. However they
serve as good pointers when they plotted versus initial moisture content and consequently versus burial depth.

7
Conclusion
A series of laboratory tests was conducted by GEO Copenhagen in clays of Paleogene origin in order to deter-
mine their geotechnical parameters for the design and construction of the immerse tunnel project in Fehmarn Belt.
Samples originate from both oﬀshore and onshore the wider area, were tested and the outcome of these tests was
available and further processed in the present study, on an eﬀort to evaluate how swell pressure is linked to miner-
alogy and basic soil properties.
According to the X-ray diﬀraction analysis results, illite and smectite are the predominant clay minerals in the
Paleogene unit, whilst kaolinite and chlorite co-exist mostly in smaller amounts. The majority of the tested clayey
samples corresponds to high plasticity and classiﬁed as (CH). High plasticity is mainly attributed to the high smec-
tite content, whilst higher kaolinite/chlorite content found to decrease plasticity. The low calcium carbonate content
did not allow to draw any conclusion, since no correlation between calcium carbonate content and plasticity, nor
swell pressure was observed.
Swell pressure was measured by incremental loading tests in both folded and intact clays from the Røsnæs formation
and as was observed, intact clays correspond to remarkably higher swell pressure values. Swell pressure plotted
versus initial moisture content which determined prior testing, and a very good correlation was observed. In more
detail, swell pressure increased as the initial moisture content slightly decreased. Additionally, it was observed that
deeper in the formation the initial moisture content decreases slightly, hence when swell pressure plotted versus the
burial depth, a good correlation was also observed. Correspondingly, a good correlation between swell pressure and
the in-situ vertical eﬀective stress was further observed.
No clear relationship between swell pressure and plasticity was seen. The limited amount of swell pressure mea-
surements did not provide a complete understanding on how plasticity could correlate to swell pressure. However,
based only in the few available data points, it could be concluded that in the present study no correlation between
the Atterberg limits and the swell pressure exists.
The presence of clay minerals such as smectite, which is prone to expand several times its volume when it comes
in contact with water or with another ﬂuid type, is frequently related to the swelling behaviour of soils. However,
based on the results, in the present study this does not seem to be the case. No suﬃcient relationship nor a good
correlation between swell pressure and clay minerals exist. However, when the co-joint content of smectite and illite
increases, swell pressure decreases and a good correlation occurs. This might be due to the higher illite content, as
illite is less prone to swell than smectite, but still results are inconclusive and did not provide a clear understanding
on how swelling is linked to clay mineralogy. Another possible source of swelling might be the capillary pressure, but
since no evidence is available this could only be an assumption. Due to the limited amount of data and the absence
of few more usefull information (such as the grain size distribution and the element composition of minerals) it was
not possible to study in-depth how the swell behaviour of soils is correlated to mineralogy.
Although the available measured values of swell pressure were limited, they are assumed to serve as good pointers in
terms of how swell pressure is linked to mineralogy and few soil properties. All and all, it was concluded that swell
pressure is strongly related to the initial moisture content, whilst clay mineralogy is not the dominating parameter
for swell in the present study.
