Preface
The present report comprises the work conducted within the scope of an MSc thesis project
at the Department of Wind Energy during the period from October 1 to March 31, 2025.
The main analysis of this project is based on numerical simulations performed with STAR-
CCM+, a commercial Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software; while for presentation
and visualization purposes, the data was processed through Python.
Structure of the report
This report adopts a chapter-by-chapter approach, as introducing the fundamental theory was
considered essential before delving into the complexity of numerical CFD simulations. Con-
sequently, Chapter 1 outlines the importance of computing Vortex-Induced Vibrations effects
on suspension bridges, followed by the necessary background theory on fluid and structural
dynamics in Chapter 2. Chapter 3 details the comprehensive development of representative
models within StarCCM+, while a mesh and a spanwise width convergence analysis are pre-
sented in Chapters 4 and 5. Chapters 6 and 7 demonstrate the stationary simulations of vortex
shedding and the dynamic fluid-structure interaction analysis, respectively, followed by the
final conclusions.

Abstract
Suspension bridges are capable of spanning long distances, offering -besides an aesthetic appeal-
durability and longevity. In addition, they contribute to sustainability by minimizing environ-
mental disruption compared to traditional bridge designs. Consequently, the comprehensive
study of various aspects of suspension bridges is of significant importance.
The ongoing research investigates the effects of Vortex Induced Vibrations (VIVs) on suspension
bridges, by simulating the wind flow around them through CFD analysis in STAR-CCM+.
Subtracted parts of fixed, inelastic bridge geometries were utilized, and a mesh-convergence
study was performed. Various geometric widths were additionally tested, the lift coeﬀicient
deviation of which underlined the strong influence of the three-dimensional nature of the wind
flow.
A transient external flow was continuously imposed on the aforementioned fixed geometries,
where the instabilities arising from flow separation and the resulting vortex shedding determined
the frequency and the magnitude of the periodic forcing function. The vertical displacement
is more significant in VIV cases; thus, the periodic lift signal was further analyzed, and the
Strouhal number was extracted.
The next step involves the one Degree of Freedom (DoF) Dynamic Body-Fluid Interaction
(DFBI). The geometries were elastically mounted on a spring-damper system and were allowed
to interact with the flow along the vertical axis. The structures were expected to experience
the strongest excitation at natural frequencies near the Strouhal frequency.
Therefore, to
simulate a worst-case resonance scenario, the spring elastic stiffness was estimated by adopting
the latter Strouhal frequency. Under steady-state oscillations due to vortex shedding, fluid
forces counteract with the structural damping forces; hence, the vibration resembles that of an
unforced system with zero damping.
When structures vibrate, the vortex formation is also influenced by the fluid-structure inter-
action. However, because of both the significantly lower density of air compared to a bridge’s
structure and the general dominance of structural inertia, the added mass effect becomes neg-
ligible. As a result, the natural frequencies of the structure are not substantially altered, and
the Strouhal frequency remains primarily governed by the flow characteristics and the body
geometry. The extracted vertical translation signals were compared with relative experimental
measurements conducted in wind tunnels simulating the same flow conditions.
Two different bridge section geometries were investigated: one incorporating complex micro-
scale features, and a simpler model including only the main girder.The results for the former did
not align suﬀiciently with the corresponding experimental data, whereas the latter produced
an accurate prediction of vertical displacement. While these outcomes allow for preliminary
conclusions to be drawn, additional investigations are necessary. However, due to computational
and time constraints, such analyses were not conducted under the scope of this report, and are

1
Introduction
1.1
Suspension Bridges
The growing necessity for improved transportation, connectivity, and sustained economic growth
in modern societies; coupled with advancements in engineering and technology, has strength-
ened the commitment to constructing longer and safer bridges. A suspension bridge is a type
of structure in which the main load-bearing cables extend continuously from one anchorage to
another, passing over the towers. The deck is suspended below these cables by using vertical
suspender cables or rods, allowing for eﬀicient weight distribution.
One of the key advantages of suspension bridges is their ability to span exceptionally long
distances, enabling them to cross deep valleys or large bodies of water with minimal support
structures. This design minimizes environmental disruption, making them particularly suit-
able for ecologically sensitive areas. Additionally, their flexibility and resilience allow them to
withstand high winds, seismic activity, and dynamic traﬀic loads. The eﬀicient use of materials
results in a strong yet lightweight structure, enhancing both performance and cost-effectiveness.
Last but not least, their aesthetic appeal often transforms them into iconic landmarks. Hence,
these attributes make suspension bridges a preferred choice for modern infrastructure projects
with high demand in functionality and visual significance (Chen and Duan (2003)).
1.1.1
1915 Çanakkale Bridge
The 1915 Çanakkale bridge (Figure 1.1), completed in March 2022, is a record-breaking suspen-
sion bridge in Türkiye, connecting Europe with Asia as part of the ’Kınalı-Tekirdağ-Çanakkale-
Savaştepe’ Motorway Project. The main span of 2.023 kilometers makes the Çanakkale bridge
the longest suspension bridge in the world. The structure features two 334-meter-high towers
constructed in a seismically active region and was designed to withstand strong winds, seismic
activity, ship collisions, and high-density maritime traﬀic (Ilki, D. Çavunt, and Y. S. Çavunt
(2023)). The project was designed by Turkey-based Tekfen Construction and Installation and
detailed designed by Denmark-headquartered COWI A/S and by Pyunghwa Engineering Con-
sultants in South Korea (for cable design and approach bridge design packages only).
To enhance aerodynamic stability, the bridge girder was designed as a streamlined twin box
steel girder separated by a 9 meter gap, with a total width of 45 meters and three traﬀic
lanes in each direction (Kroon, Polk, and Fuglsang (2023)). As expected, the project presented
significant engineering challenges, making the post-completion studies essential for ensuring
long-term safety maintenance.

1.1.2
The Great Belt Bridge (Danish: Storebæltsbroen)
The Great Belt bridge (Figure 1.2), fully completed in 1998, was primarily designed by the
Danish companies COWI and Ramboll and connects the Danish islands of Zealand and Funen.
With the world’s sixth longest main span (1.624 kilometers) it significantly reduces travel times
and enhances connectivity within Denmark. The bridge features a streamlined trapezoidal box
girder deck, 31 meters wide, accommodating four motorway lanes and a railway, according to
Larsen (1993).
Since its completion over 25 years ago, a comprehensive database of measurements has been
compiled, leading to further implementation of modifications to reassure safety and mitigate
aerodynamic instabilities observed post-completion Larsen, Esdahl, et al. (2000). Addition-
ally, extensive experiments in wind tunnels have also taken place, providing valuable data for
simulation comparisons (Larsen and Wall (2012)).

1.2
Project Specification
The main objective of this report is to investigate the Vortex Induced Vibrations (VIV) of the
Çanakkale and the Great Belt bridge section models, which are similar to those previously
tested in experimental studies conducted in collaboration with COWI A/S. The purpose of
the experiments was the estimation of the vertical displacement of the section models when
subjected to a wind flow.
For the investigation of the VIVs, the present project focuses on Computational Fluid Dynamics
simulations performed using StarCCM+, to capture the behavior of three-dimensional vortex
shedding and its influence on the corresponding dynamic response of the models.
Accordingly, the following main objectives were considered:
1. Development of scaled-down, three-dimensional representative models for both the Çanakkale
and the Great Belt bridge sections.
2. Execution of a comprehensive mesh convergence study, combined with a spanwise length
convergence analysis, to validate and emphasize the three-dimensional characteristics of
vortex shedding.
3. Simulation of the stationary bridge-section models for the estimation of periodic aerody-
namic forces and the Strouhal number, under the assumption of negligible influence of
coupled fluid-structure interaction on the shedding frequency.
4. Simulation of dynamic body-fluid interaction aimed at estimating the maximum vertical
translation of the section models and compare it with experimental results.

2
Background Theory
2.1
Bluff Bodies - Aerodynamic Behavior
Many engineering applications involve large, non-aerodynamically designed structures. Bridges
represent a major example of such cases, since it is impossible for them to be aerodynamically
optimized. These types of structures are commonly referred to as bluff bodies, characterized
by complex aerodynamic behavior even under low-velocity flow conditions.
More precisely, a bluff body is a solid object around which the flow cannot remain fully attached,
leading to significant flow separation and the formation of a broad, turbulent wake region.
This separation is primarily caused by the adverse pressure gradients induced by the body’s
large frontal area and often sharp geometric features. Consequently, bluff bodies are subjected
to higher pressure drag compared to aerodynamically streamlined shapes. In addition, the
unsteady nature of the separated flow around bluff bodies typically induces vortex shedding,
further influencing the profile of acting aerodynamic forces.
When flow separates from the sides of a bluff body, two shear layers are formed, one on each
side. These shear layers are inherently unstable due to the velocity difference between the
high-velocity outer flow and the slower recirculating wake, where small perturbations amplify
as they are convected downstream. As these layers develop, they begin to interact dynamically.
The formation of a vortex on one side induces a velocity field that pushes the shear layer
on the opposite side closer to the centerline. This induced motion promotes the growth of
the vortex on one side while suppressing vortex development on the other, resulting in an
alternating shedding process. This self-sustained oscillation forms the well-known von Kármán
vortex street, illustrated at Figure 2.1, characterized by a staggered pattern of vortices shed
from alternating sides of the body (vonKármán (1911)).

2.2
Vortex Induced Vibrations (VIVs)
2.2.1
Physical Phenomenon
The aforementioned periodic nature of vortex shedding generates fluctuating lift forces on
the bluff body, which oscillate at the vortex shedding frequency.
As a result, the body is
influenced by the oscillating lift force, and a dynamic response is caused. As the body interacts
dynamically with the fluid, the coupling between the fluid forces and the structural motion can
further modify the vortex shedding process, potentially resulting in the lock-in phenomenon;
where the shedding frequency synchronizes with the structural natural frequency, amplifying
oscillations (Hansen (2007)).
Although VIVs typically involve limited amplitude oscillations and do not easily lead to collapse,
they can cause large displacements, induce discomfort for drivers under low wind velocity
conditions, and also reduce the fatigue life of bridges (Hu, Zhao, and Ge (2019)). Therefore,
the thorough analysis of VIVs is crucial, and their mitigation represents a critical aspect of
structural design.
2.2.2
Strouhal Number
An important dimensionless parameter within the scope of this report is the Strouhal number
(St), as it describes the oscillatory flow phenomena generated by vortex shedding. The Strouhal
Number is defined as :
St = fvLch,St
U
(2.1)
where fv, Lch,St, and U represent the vortex shedding frequency, the characteristic length,
and the free stream velocity, respectively. The characteristic length for the Strouhal number
typically reflects the dimension that governs the flow separation, and thus the vortex shedding.
In the case of bridges, the vertical height is usually employed.
The Strouhal number is strongly dependent on the Reynolds number. At very low Reynolds
numbers, the flow is governed primarily by viscous forces, preventing the occurrence of vortex
shedding.
Conversely, at very high Reynolds numbers, inertia forces become predominant,
leading to turbulent fluctuations that disrupt vortex shedding, making it irregular. Depending
on the geometry, there is a range of Reynolds numbers within which vortex shedding occurs
periodically and remains coherent.
2.2.3
Difference Between Classical Dynamics Vibrations and VIVs Experimen-
tal Approach
It is essential to distinguish between classical dynamic vibrations and vortex-induced vibrations.
In classical dynamics, free vibrations arise when a system in equilibrium is subjected to a
perturbation, such as an initial velocity; while forced vibrations occur when a system is driven
by an external oscillatory input. On the other hand, in VIVs, a forced vibration refers to an
7
==PAGE:23==
experiment in which the body is rigidly attached to an actuator and forced to move through
the fluid with a prescribed trajectory. Meanwhile, a free vortex-induced vibration refers to a
VIV experiment where the body is elastically mounted on springs - and allowed to respond
freely to the fluid forces acting upon it (Dhanak and Xiros (2016)).
The latter type of VIV experiment, involving a freely vibrating body, was conducted in wind
tunnels, and it is thus simulated in this report. The springs were tuned to equalize the vortex
shedding frequency with the natural frequency of the simulated bridge section, so that resonance
lock-in phenomenon conditions could be captured. The primary objective of this setup was to
estimate the maximum vertical displacement induced by vortex-induced vibrations.
2.3
VIV- Observations and Previous Related Work
In this section, previous investigations of the vortex-induced vibration phenomena are outlined.
To commence with, vortex-induced vibrations are a common phenomenon affecting long-span
bridges, and numerous cases have been reported worldwide. The Rio–Niterói bridge, comprising
a steel twin-box-girder, had to be closed to traﬀic for any vehicle whenever cross-winds reached
velocities of approximately 14 m/s, prior to the implementation of counter-measurements. As
mentioned in Battista and Pfeil (2000), because of inherent operational challenges during these
events, users were left frightened when crossing on the oscillating bridge. Similarly, the Trans-
Tokyo Bay Highway Crossing, which consists of a continuous steel box-girder, experienced VIVs
peaking at wind speeds of around 16-17 m/s, with maximum vibrations amplitude exceeding
50 cm (Fujino and Yoshida (2002)). Tuned Mass Dampers (TMD) were implemented in both
structures to successfully mitigate the effects.
As it is stated in Ge, Zhao, and Cao (2022), unlike the aerodynamic configuration, which
can be optimized in advance, the internal factor of a real bridge damping ratio can only be
identified after the completion of the construction. Therefore, continuous monitoring of the
bridge’s behavior and conducting further studies aimed at stability enhancement are essential.
A notable example is the Humen Bridge, discussed in the latter report, which closed for traﬀic
after 23 years of safe operation. The reason for the closure, was the occurrence of VIVs due to
the temporary installation of water-filled barriers, which changed the aerodynamic configuration
and altered the bridge’s natural frequency. Subsequent to the barrier removal, VIV phenomena
re-appeared, this time due to the corresponding reduction of the structural damping.
To
address this, a sectional model of the bridge was developed; flow suppressors were added, wind-
tunnel tests were conducted, and the final design modifications were implemented in the actual
structure, successfully minimizing the effects of VIVs. Furthermore, Hu, Zhao, and Ge (2019)
investigated the suppression of vortex-induced vibrations in a streamlined closed-box girder
through the use of additional small-scale components. The simulation results demonstrated
the flow behavior, identifying the formation of small vortices induced by the added geometries,
as well as larger vortices generated by flow separation at the leading barriers of the girder.
A significant number of VIVs-related studies have also been published concerning the Great
Belt Bridge. Larsen (1993) presents a variety of design configurations that were tested in wind
tunnels before finalizing the bridge design, with the goal of mitigating aerodynamic instabilities.
In Larsen and Wall (2012) report, it is noted that regardless of the effective VIV mitigation
by additional fitted guide vanes at the joints of the horizontal bottom plate and the lower side
panels of the box girder, the implementation and maintenance of such measures at full scale
8
==PAGE:24==
would involve a substantially high cost. Consequently, it is desirable to design a trapezoidal box
section which does not display vortex shedding excitation and thus avoid appendages. Wind
tunnel experiments revealed that trapezoidal bridge box girders can be designed to suppress
vortex shedding excitation if the angle between the lower inclined panels and vertical is chosen
to be approximately 15 deg.
This geometric modification delays early flow separation and
consequently reduces the onset and intensity of VIV effects.
A common nominator of all the above case studies is the reliance on wind tunnel testing, with
limited or absent use of computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations. As stated in Fang et
al. (2021), developing an accurate 3D model is very complicated; therefore, 2D CFD simulations
of a truss girder with two decks were carried out. These simulations successfully captured the
formation of vortices, and the critical flow-separation regions were identified; however, a direct
comparison with experimental data was not included. A 2D CFD model was also adopted for
VIV analysis of an asymmetrical composite beam bridge in Wang et al. (2019). According
to the report, although there was reasonable agreement between the aerodynamic coeﬀicients
obtained from experimental and numerical results, further investigations into simulating the
displacement response from dynamic fluid-body interaction were not conducted.
In general, studies presenting results from simulations of dynamic fluid-body interaction re-
main limited, primarily due to the complexity and high computational cost associated with 3D
models. Therefore, the present report aims to provide a comprehensive analysis of numerical
simulations related to VIV of suspension bridges.

2.5
Turbulence Modeling
The Navier-Stokes equations describe the fluid motion in terms of pressure and velocity fields.
Thus, the three velocity components (u, v, w) and the pressure (p) are the only unknowns of
the NS. Combined with the continuity equation, they comprise a system of four equations and
four unknown variables, allowing the flow parameters to be determined by solving the system.
However, particularly in turbulent flows, directly numerically solving the Navier-Stokes equa-
tions can be extremely computationally expensive, due to the numerous iterations required to
accurately capture the flow behavior. Instead, the effects of turbulence are approximated by
the use of mathematical models, by the application of which the computational cost can be
significantly reduced. There is a variety of approaches and different ways of turbulence mod-
eling (TM); however, the k −ω SST - two equation model - was employed for the purposes of
this report, combining characteristics from k −ω and k −ϵ models, which are presented in the
following sections.

3.3
Mesh Settings
The concept of a mesh as a discretization of space has been fundamental to computational
methods since the first attempts to obtain numerical solutions of partial differential equations
(Baker (2005)). Mesh quality significantly affects the accuracy and convergence of the numerical
results, particularly in the case of simulating wind flow over complex bridge geometries. A high-
quality mesh is vital for accurately capturing the vortex shedding phenomena in the wake region
downstreams of the structure, primarily associated with VIVs. This requires a very fine mesh
configuration. The excessive computational cost, however, is a strong limitation factor not
allowing excessively fine mesh, especially throughout the entire flow domain.
Therefore, different meshing techniques are necessary for various regions of the domain, alter-
nating between fine and coarse approaches depending on the local flow characteristics. A fine
mesh approach is required for regions with steep spatial gradients, whereas a coarse approach is
recommended for regions dominated by uniform flow. Hence, an optimal mesh configuration is
desired, suﬀiciently fine to capture small-scale complex phenomena where needed, while compu-
tational cost is mitigated by the coarse flow-uniformity regions. This was achieved by employing
and adjusting the StarCCM+ automated meshers described in the following sub-chapters.
Surface Re-mesher
The surface re-mesher in StarCCM+ enhances the quality of the existing surface mesh induced
from low-quality CAD imports, prior to volume meshing (Figure 3.2d below the yellow part).
This is achieved by refining or coarsening surface mesh cells to accomplish better alignment
with geometric features and represent accurately complex surfaces. Additionally, it preserves
curvature fidelity, ensuring that curved surfaces are accurately resolved.
Polyhedral Mesher
Volume meshes comprising polyhedral cells are created by StarCCM+ Polyhedral mesher. This
type of cells has the advantage of having more faces and neighboring connections, in comparison
with traditional tetrahedral or hexahedral ones, enhancing numerical stability and accelerating
convergence. Polyhedral meshers achieve better solution accuracy with fewer cells compared
with the aforementioned cell types, contributing to the reduction of the computational cost.
Furthermore, when a periodic boundary condition is used, an interface of 100% matching cells
is generated on the two boundary planes that are cyclically connected, providing accuracy in
data transfer between them.
Prism Layer Mesher
The Prism layer mesher generates layers of high aspect-ratio prismatic cells adjacent to the
wall boundaries (Figure 3.3b following the wall-surface contours. The purpose of these layers
is to accurately capture the steep spatial gradients, such as near-wall velocity profile, which is
an essential parameter for determining wall forces and flow separation - factors that directly
influence drag and lift calculations. Accurate estimation of these forces is of high importance for
this project, as the oscillations in the lift are directly linked to the vortex shedding frequency.
24
==PAGE:40==
Moreover, in this study, the k-￿SST turbulence model was used, which requires low values of
y+, thus, careful configuration of the prism layer is essential.
Surface and Volumetric Custom Controls
In addition to the automated meshers, it is crucial to manually identify regions of the flow where
complex small-scale phenomena might occur. Surface and volumetric custom controls allow for
the creation of a finer mesh configuration in these regions, enabling high-fidelity capture of
these phenomena. When meshing regions overlap, StarCCM+ automatically retains the finer
mesh configuration.
In this study, special attention was given to generating a fine -high quality- mesh in the wake
region, as shown in Figure 3.3a. This approach ensures accurate capture of vortex shedding
phenomena, which significantly influence lift and drag forces, leading to results that closely
replicate real-world conditions. An additional reason for applying this technique was the ap-
propriate visualization of the vortices.

Implicit Unsteady
The time-variant nature of the VIV phenomena requires a time-dependent solution approach.
Therefore, an unsteady model was the only applicable choice. Between the option of an explicit
or an implicit one, the latter was employed. With an implicit approach, the set of equations
needed is resolved at each time-step, increasing the computational cost of the simulations. On
the other hand, it offers stability allowing the time-step to be larger.
In this model the time step was defined at ∆t = 0.001s for accurately capturing the time-
dependent gradients that characterize the flow, while a second-order temporal discretization
was applied. StarCCM+ utilizes a second-order backward differencing scheme derived from
Taylor’s expansion, the final approximation of which is represented in Equation 3.3 for the case
of the horizontal velocity.

Segregated Flow
By activating this model, the velocity and the pressure, from the momentum governing equation,
are not solved simultaneously, but in a sequential (segregated) way. Firstly, a velocity field is
estimated based on results from the previous time step. Then, since the new velocity field
does not satisfy the mass conservation principle, the new pressure is estimated by a pressure
correction equation, and the velocity field is updated. As mentioned in Chapter 2, when a
two-equation turbulence model is activated, two additional transport equations are introduced
to the solver. These two equations are solved alongside the momentum and pressure correction
equations, updating the turbulence properties iteratively until the continuity convergence.
The segregated flow model is highly recommended in low-speed flows (Mach < 0.3) where the
flow is assumed incompressible; or more generally in cases where the coupling between the
velocity and the pressure of the flow is weak. For strong pressure-velocity coupling like shock
waves or cavitation simulations, then a more computationally expensive coupling model, where
the pressure and velocity are resolved simultaneously, is required.
Solution Interpolation
The activation of this model is essential when a segregated flow is assumed. As it is mentioned
by using the segregated flow model, the pressure and the velocity are decoupled, and resolved
sequentially. However, in StarCCM+ the pressure correction equation is solved at cell centers
(node-based values), whereas the momentum equation is solved at cell faces.
Therefore, a
way of interpolating pressure to the faces is required for the velocity field update, and this is
provided by the solution interpolation model.
In this report, the ’nearest neighbor’ interpolation technique was employed. In this way, the
pressure correction at a face is directly taken from the closest cell center without any weighting
or averaging.

the mesh, to ensure that the results are mesh-independent. In other words, further refinement
of the mesh does not lead to different results, but increases unreasonably the computational
cost. In this study, the lift signal was the parameter used to test mesh convergence for both
bridge geometries.
The mesh convergence was performed by setting an initial base-cell size and customizing most
of the meshers’ parameters through defining an individual cell size by a -relative to the initial
base- percentage.
This approach allows small changes in the initial base size to result in
significantly different mesh configurations, while also providing transparency in documenting
and interpreting the results, particularly given the complexity of the simulated geometry parts.

Stationary Simulations
The oscillating aerodynamic forces thoroughly examined in the mesh convergence analysis, arise
as a result of vortex shedding. As discussed in Chapter 2, vortex shedding occurs due to flow
separation when a bluff body is exposed to a fluid flow, with its occurrence also dependent on
the body’s geometry and the Reynolds number. In this chapter, vortex shedding is visualized
while the bridge sections remain fixed.
The primary objective of this chapter is to verify that the frequency of the oscillating lift forces
corresponds to the vortex shedding frequency of the fixed bridge sections. Establishing this
frequency is critical for accurate vortex-induced vibration (VIV) simulations, due to the fact
that it is used to tune the elastic coeﬀicient of the spring-mass system. This coeﬀicient is
subsequently used in the following chapter to predict the vibration amplitudes of the bridge
section under flow-induced excitation.

Dynamic Body-Fluid Interaction
In this chapter, the dynamic interaction between the fluid and the structure is investigated. For
that purpose, the section-bridge geometries were elastically mounted on a mass-spring system
and were constrained to interact with the flow along the vertical axis. Since the structures were
expected to experience the strongest excitation at frequencies near the Strouhal frequency, the
spring elastic coeﬀicient (K) was estimated based on that frequency, as shown in Equation 7.1.
The added mass effect on the natural frequency estimation was considered negligible, due to
the low density of the air compared to the structural. Although vortex shedding frequency can
be influenced by fluid-structure interaction, this effect was also considered negligible due to
the significant density difference between air and the structural material. Furthermore, special
consideration was given to the lock-in phenomenon.
Lock-in refers to the synchronization
between the structural natural frequency and the vortex shedding frequency, resulting in a
nearly constant oscillation frequency and often an amplified response.

Conclusions
The primary focus of this report was the investigation and simulation of vortex shedding be-
havior and flow topology around the deck sections of two suspension bridges: the Çanakkale
Bridge and the Great Belt Bridge. The initial objective was to estimate the vertical oscillations
of these sections and compare the numerical results with experimental data obtained under
identical flow conditions.
The simulations were carried out using the Computational Fluid Dynamics software STAR-
CCM+. The models were set and a mesh-convergence analysis was conducted to ensure nu-
merical accuracy. To replicate an infinite flow-domain depth, periodic interface boundaries were
applied, enabling the cyclic transfer of flow information across domain boundaries; therefore,
a span-wise depth analysis was also performed to validate this approach. Furthermore, the
k–ω SST turbulence model was employed due to its reliable performance both in the near-wall
region and in the free-stream flow.
Following mesh convergence and spanwise length analysis, aerodynamic forces were evaluated
by imposing a transient external flow around initially fixed bridge sections. It is well-established
that vortex shedding primarily excites vertical vibrations of the structure; therefore, the lift
force was the focus of further investigation. The vortex shedding frequency, which coincided
with the oscillation frequency of the lift force, was identified, and the characteristic von Kármán
vortex street was successfully visualized.
In the next phase, a dynamic fluid–structure interaction simulation was conducted, where
the bridge sections were elastically mounted in a one-degree-of-freedom spring-mass system,
allowing vertical motion only. To simulate a worst-case resonance scenario - characterized by
the lock-in phenomenon, where the vortex shedding frequency synchronizes with the structure’s
natural frequency - the spring elastic coeﬀicient was tuned such that the system’s natural
frequency matched the shedding frequency. The vortex shedding frequency was obtained from
the fixed-geometry simulations, based on the assumption that it is negligibly influenced by the
fluid-structure interaction. This assumption is justified by the significant difference in fluid and
structural densities.
The visualization of the von Kármán vortex street in the oscillating-body simulation confirmed
the validity of the aforementioned assumption, as the shedding frequency closely matched that
of the fixed-body case. The vertical translation signal was also extracted for both geometries. In
the first case, involving the Çanakkale Bridge and its small-scale geometries, the simulation re-
sults underestimated the experimental measurements by approximately an order of magnitude.
On the other hand, for the Great Belt Bridge case - where a significantly simplified geometry
including only the main girder was used - the simulation results showed good agreement with
the experimental data.
Concluding, there are strong indications that the simulation of Vortex Induced Vibrations can
64

lead to accurate results; however, not in both cases, which leaves space for additional tests and
trials, which were not conducted in this report due to the computational and time constraints.
