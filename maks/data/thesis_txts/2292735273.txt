Investigation of an optimised morphing blade concept for improved wind turbine power performance 
Abstract 
This thesis investigates the use of camber morphing active airfoil surfaces for the purpose of 
increased power and annual energy production (AEP) on the DTU 10MW reference wind 
turbine. Active surfaces such as discreet and continuous flaps have been studied and proven 
for their potential load reduction on wind turbine blades although have yet to be studied for their 
influence on power improvement. This research seeks to quantify the potential of this 
technology to increase power and AEP in low wind resource sites, and thereby contribute to 
lowering the Cost of Energy (COE) of the turbine. A wind-speed specific optimisation framework 
is developed which investigates the optimum morphing flap configuration, location and camber 
deformation range to maximise power yield and AEP. The results indicate that substantial 
improvement is attainable for the reference blade particularly at low wind speeds (above rated 
tip speed ratios) where the optimum operational range of the airfoils is extended through the 
use of morphing techniques. The camber morphing results are compared to a site-specific 
optimised twist distribution in terms of power, AEP and COE. This work should be viewed as 
supporting the case for the adoption of active surfaces by exploring an alternative potential this 
technology has to offer apart from load reduction applications.

1. Introduction 
In order to maintain competitiveness as an energy source the reduction of cost-of-energy (COE) 
is a clear driver in the wind industry. This is pushing technological development towards larger 
and more reliable turbines, as well as improved harvesting from low-yield wind sites such as 
through the use of larger blades [1], [2]. To reduce blade weight and related costs 
manufacturers are moving towards higher lift airfoils that allow shorter chord lengths, although 
with higher relative thickness to ensure the blade can still support aerodynamic and structural 
loads [1], [3], [4]. 
A key design factor for turbine blades is the reduction of loads on the blade during operation. 
Highly fluctuating aerodynamic loads caused by turbulence and wake fields, resonance of 
natural modes, yaw error, wind shear and gusts, or tower shadows result in severe fatigue and 
extreme loads on blades and turbine components [5]–[7]. Reducing these loads offers an 
effective method of decreasing the structural mass and material requirements of blades and 
other components, and therefore the COE. However, the traditional method of pitch regulation is 
limited by the increasing inertia of the blades, reaction times and inability to address local 
variations in aerodynamic loading [8]. A plethora of research has therefore focussed on 
controlling blade loads through manipulation of the blade surface [5]–[7], [9]–[12], the majority of 
this employing active surfaces such as morphing trailing edge flaps. While the potential of this 
technology has been demonstrated both experimentally and theoretically for load reduction, 
what is absent from current research is a clear understanding of its potential to increase power 
production at low power operation. 
Wind power has a long history in Europe and other higher-wind areas, however, new 
technological developments and growing demand for green energy is driving a recent 
expansion into lower wind areas such as the Chinese market [13]. With the majority of the 
world’s land mass and near-shore areas in these lower wind areas (see Figure 1), there exists a 
vast potential for the application of low-wind energy harvesting technology. The viability of blade 
morphing to increase blade energy capture and hence boost Annual Energy Production (AEP) 
at lower winds speeds could offer an additional means of exploiting low yield wind regions, 
reducing the COE and increasing the appeal of wind energy globally.

1.1 Motivation 
Seeking to fill a knowledge gap in current research into active surfaces on wind turbine blades, 
this investigation explores the use of airfoil camber morphing for the purpose of increasing 
power yield in below-rated wind speeds. To model the topological changes and their influence 
on power production the reference wind turbine designed by the Wind Energy Department at 
the Technical University of Denmark is selected as a base model [15]–[17]. This traditional 3-
bladed upwind turbine has been designed using standard rotor design methods and is 
optimised for specific operating conditions at higher wind speeds, which therefore offers an 
ideal opportunity to test the ability of morphing technology to compensate for poor performance 
in below-design operation. 
To elucidate the potential of morphing active surfaces on the turbine the following objectives are 
sought: 
• 
Development of an optimisation framework that translates perturbations in the reference 
turbine blade’s airfoil shape to the power output of the turbine 
• 
Explore the optimal shape, location and length of the morphing blade sections along the 
blade to maximise wind speed-specific power output and Annual Energy Production 
(AEP), whilst minimising structural implications 
This work will therefore aim to contribute to the growing knowledge on the use of active 
surfaces in wind turbines by offering an insight into the additional benefits these technologies 
offer in relation to increasing power production. It is prudent to highlight that any potential power 
gain from this technology must be offset against the negative impacts incurred when integrating 
this technology into wind turbine blades. A trade-off therefore exists between the greater yield of 
the turbine and the increased complexity, structural and economic costs incurred due to the 
addition of the morphing surface. However, the appeal of these technologies is supported by 
their demonstrated potential to decrease blade and turbine loads, along with structural 
requirements, which represents the primary motivation for their use. If substantial power 
increases are feasible with this technology, this may increase the economic viability of active 
morphing technology, and contribute to improving the appeal of lower-wind site applications. 
1.2 Assumptions and Limitations 
This investigation touches upon the broad field of wind turbine design and engineering such as 
blade design, airfoil design and turbine control. Consequently, some significant scope limitations 
are required in order to accomplish the objectives in the allocated period. The following 
assumptions are therefore relevant to further understand the problem and approach 
undertaken. 
1.2.1 Morphing Section 
A morphing section refers to a region of the blade which can be deformed independently of the 
rest of the blade. In the present work, morphing is achieved by continuous and smooth camber 
deformation at the leading edge (LE) and trailing edge (TE), as shown in Figure 2, which is 
more aerodynamically efficient than discrete flaps based on hinging. A literature study (section 
2.5) identified that a deformation range of up to ±8 degrees at both LE and TE was feasible with 
present technology when the length of these flaps did not exceed 20% of the airfoils chord. Also 
from prior studies, a minimum flap length of 10% chord up to a maximum of 20% chord was 
chosen to reduce the structural implications larger flaps would have on the blade.

1.2.2 Actuator Design 
The design and control of the actuation method for the morphing airfoil flaps was beyond the 
current scope and is therefore omitted. However, the following assumptions were made in 
regards to its capabilities: 
• 
The actuator is capable of deforming the LE and TE flaps over the continuous 
deformation range, not just a simple on-off position, 
• 
The flap deformation is smooth and based on the camber line only, explained in further 
detail in the methodology section (section 3.2). 
The structural requirements for deformation are similarly not considered, and the structural 
properties of the reference wind turbine blade are used for the corresponding flap sections. It 
should be recognised that the introduction of the morphing sections would significantly alter the 
structural layup and performance of the blade; however for the present objectives quantifying 
and addressing this impact was beyond the scope.  
1.2.3 Operation 
The morphing technology is assumed to only operate in below rated wind speeds, where the 
turbine controller attempts to maximise power extracted from the wind. The full rated power of 
the reference wind turbine is therefore retained here. Although the use of this technology will 
inevitably incur an increased loading on the blade and turbine it is assumed that this does not 
represent a design-critical concern as critical load cases are generally at or above rated wind 
conditions, or in non-steady turbine operation (e.g. standstill or emergency shut-down).  
For the purpose of power increase only, steady-state operation is also assumed in the absence 
of turbulence and time-varying effects on the flow around the morphing region and blade. Such 
time-varying and dynamic flow characteristics of similar morphing airfoils have been discussed 
elsewhere, see for example Troldborg [5] or Wolff [18]. It is envisaged that the control of the 
morphing blade spans would operate at a similar time scale as that of the pitch control system, 
which also reacts to changes in wind conditions.

coverage. Key results and comparisons are finally offered before conclusions are drawn.

2. State of the Art in Rotor and Airfoil Design 
The following technology review is divided into four sections; the first addresses current state of 
the art in rotor design techniques. This is followed by an explanation of the Blade Element 
Momentum (BEM) method and its use in rotor optimisation. Third, modern airfoil design 
techniques are addressed followed by an outline of research and applications of morphing blade 
technologies for wind turbines.  
2.1 Rotor Design 
A wind turbine extracts kinetic energy from wind flowing through its rotor area, shown in Figure 
3 for the prolific three-bladed upwind design. This is transferred into rotation of the blade which 
drives the generator and produces electricity.  
 
Figure 3 Wind turbine rotor indicating swept rotor area (A), taken from [19] 
The objective of the rotor design process is therefore to maximise the aerodynamic 
performance of the rotor such that the maximum torque is extracted from the wind field, whilst 
reducing structural loads and other restrictions. This procedure is inevitably subject to the 
considerations involved in the much larger process of wind turbine design. 
Wind turbine manufacturing is an extremely competitive industry and much of the methods and 
technology employed by turbine companies is highly confidential and proprietary commercial 
information. However, the majority of academic and published approaches to turbine design 
share some common features and techniques. The process could begin with, for example, the 
selection of a desired target wind climate, maximum rotor diameter and rated power, and the 
necessary hub height [20]. From this a few key choice will significantly affect the final design of 
the blade, which are elaborated further below. However, this list is non-exhaustive and is 
tailored within the scope of the current objectives and reference wind turbine.

1. Control 
The three dominant methods of controlling the power production from a wind turbine include 
stall regulation, pitch regulation and a combination of the two. A stall regulated turbine has a 
constant blade pitch angle to the incoming wind and relies on the generation of flow separation 
on the downwind side of the blade to limit the power extracted [21]. However, due to the limited 
control and adaptability to wind speeds this type of regulation can suffer from lower efficiency at 
below-design wind speeds, larger load build up on the blade and variation in the steady-state 
power.  
Alternatively, the most common contemporary control method is through pitch regulation, 
whereby the angle of the blades to the wind speed is controlled by a wind turbine controller. In 
this case, the blades can be pitched around their main axis to limit the loading and power on the 
blades, and thus the energy produced by the turbine. This affords good power control although 
requires extra complexity in the form of the pitch mechanism and control monitoring, and can 
exhibit high fluctuations in loads at high wind speeds due to pitch changes. Pitch regulation is 
commonly combined with variable speed operation, termed pitch regulated variable speed 
(PRVS) operation, which seeks to maintain the optimum tip speed ratio (TSR) below rated wind 
speed to maximise efficiency [19]. 
The active stall method is a combination of these two methods, whereby the blade is pitched at 
low wind speeds to track optimal operation and then relies on blade stall at high wind speeds. In 
this case the blade is pitched in the opposite direction to a pitch regulated turbine. 
2. Tip Speed Ratio (TSR) 
A useful and important parameter in the blade design process is the tip speed ratio, sometimes 
denoted by 𝜆, which is the ratio of the Eigen speed of the blade tip to the free wind speed. The 
selection of a target operating TSR will have a strong impact on the blade’s power efficiency, 
shaft torque generated, noise performance and blade slenderness [15]. In general, a higher 
TSR will give a narrower blade chord distribution, higher aerodynamic and centrifugal loads. 
The aerodynamic performance of the blade will become more critical also, and increased 
aerodynamic efficiency is generally attained for TSRs of up to 8, and decreasing returns are 
experienced beyond this. Modern three-bladed upwind turbines have TSRs of between 6-9 [22]. 
In the design process a target TSR can be selected by the blade designer, and the blade layout 
and properties optimised for the aerodynamic performance. As undertaken during the RWT 
design, numerous TSRs might initially be pursued and then the TSR that results in the best 
performance of the turbine or adheres to additional restrictions is selected. The choice of a 
design TSR therefore results in a blade shape and planform that is optimised for this operating 
condition. This TSR should be maintained at the site-specific mean wind speed, to ensure the 
blade is operating at maximum efficiency during the most common wind conditions.  
1. Airfoils 
The choice of control method and design TSR will influence the selection of airfoils for the 
blade. For example, if stall control is desired, the airfoils selected must have smooth and 
predictable stall characteristics if the control and power regulation is to function effectively. A 
possible starting point could be the selection of an airfoil family, which consists of numerous 
airfoils of varying thickness with similar lift and stall behaviour to ensure an even distribution of 
aerodynamic loading along the blade.

When selecting airfoil data, it is important to capture the appropriate operating conditions the 
airfoil will be subject to. The Reynold’s number, discussed further in section 2.2, is an important 
parameter governing the flow over an airfoil and the resulting aerodynamic loads. This 
parameter varies across the blade and is dependent on rotational speed, however for modern, 
large-scale turbines generally this lies within 75 000 R and 150 000 R [20]. Aerodynamic data 
should be sought from wind tunnel testing at the appropriate Reynold’s number where possible. 
This must include the effects of roughness and flow separation which is poorly modelled by 
current tools.  
Over the course of a turbine blade’s lifespan the cumulative effects of environmental 
degradation on the blade, termed blade soiling, lead to an increase in surface roughness 
particularly at the leading edge which can lead to premature boundary layer transition. This 
phenomenon is extremely site dependent, but can significantly depreciate the annual production 
of a turbine over its lifetime [23]. Thicker airfoils of t/c greater than around 24% such as those 
used on the RWT are particularly prone to performance depreciation due to soiling [24]. It is 
therefore important to emphasise the roughness performance of any airfoils selected when 
incorporating them into the design of the blade. 
Constraints 
Throughout the design process numerous considerations due to operation and integration of the 
blade in the turbine, transport, practicality and manufacturing processes are likely to constrain 
the choice of design parameters. Many such considerations are listed by Bak et al [20], and a 
few are reiterated here for their relevance to the RWT and the current morphing objectives.  
• 
Physical limits due to transportation and installation. These may include maximum 
chord and root dimensions. The chord may also be limited due to the high standstill and 
extreme loads generated on the larger surface areas of the blade, 
• 
Minimum relative thicknesses of the airfoils which is required to maintain structural load 
bearing. These loads are highest towards the root of the blade, which as shown in 
Figure 4 is increasingly structurally-dominated over aerodynamic performance, 
• 
Noise restrictions, which can be particularly pertinent for onshore-based turbines in 
closer proximity to housing. This may manifest as a limitation on the rotor speed also, 
• 
A maximum thrust force due to structural limitations, 
• 
Tower clearance must be maintained with an appropriate buffer at all times, often with 
the use of pre-bend and pre-twist, 
• 
Aero-elastic restrictions such as the natural frequency of the blade and combined 
turbine structure to prevent overlap and resonance. 
The blade design process is therefore an inherent compromise between competing and often 
different disciplines, particularly when it comes to the aerodynamic performance and structural 
design of the blade.

Tools 
In order to accurately model and design the blade and its performance on the turbine an 
appropriate set of modelling tools must be selected and validated. The selection of tools to be 
used in blade design is inherently a trade-off between accuracy of the solution and the 
computation time required. Typically, a draft or preliminary design is constructed with quicker 
tools such as the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method, and then more advanced 
processes used to refine the design [20].  
Due to its relative simplicity and far quicker solution time, the BEM method is widely employed 
in turbine design. This technique was extensively used to design the current reference wind 
turbine and is employed throughout this study. The BEM method is simple to implement and 
optimise, however suffers due to its simplistic assumptions, and can result in poor predictions of 
the load distribution and response of the blade in complex flow conditions. A number of 
improvements and additions are therefore commonly used in the method. 
A more comprehensive and advanced method of blade design involves the use of 
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) in the design procedure. CFD solves the Navier-Stokes 
equations governing the 3D fluid flow field upstream and downstream of the rotor as well as 
along the rotor geometry. Although significantly more accurate than the BEM approach, CFD 
methods incur far higher computational cost and rely on a robust mesh design for their 
accuracy, and are therefore poorly suited to optimisation and quick design routines [25].  
Other alternatives such as vortex methods and reduced methods, which involve substitution of 
the rotor for either a line (actuator line) or disk (actuator disk) loading on the fluid field, are 
promising as these can remove the burden of modelling the full geometry in CFD. However, 
actuator methods can still suffer from poor accuracy at the blade tip and limited airfoil 
representation [20].  
One recent development for blade optimisation purposes is the formation of a free-wake panel 
method by researchers at the DTU department of wind energy called MIRAS [26]. This 
approach is able to couple the blade geometry with a viscous boundary layer solver that 
accommodates rotational effects from Coriolis and centrifugal forces. This method has shown 
promising results in terms of speed and accuracy when compared to CFD simulations and 
measurements. 
2.2 Blade Element Momentum Theory 
A brief outline of the BEM method is provided below however for a full description of the model 
and its implantation see [25], [27], [28]. The purpose of the BEM approach is to predict the loads 
on the blade due to the wind, and through it the power output of the turbine. To achieve this, 
BEM combines blade element theory with momentum theory.

2.3 Airfoil Design 
Topology design for airfoils has been extensively studied for both aeronautical and wind turbine 
applications. Due to the unique aerodynamic and structural requirements for turbine blades 
there is a tendency towards ‘families’ of airfoils, with similar moment coefficients and smooth lift 
transitions which aim for steady loading over the blade [3]. The NREL family of airfoils sought to 
overcome the poor roughness insensitivity of the traditional NACA aircraft series of airfoils, 
designed using the Eppler Airfoil Design and Analysis Code [36], [37]. Also based on the NACA 
series, the FFA turbine-specific series by The Aeronautical Research Institute of Sweden were 
generated using both ISES and XFOIL codes [38]. This produced three airfoil series, the W1 
and W2 designed for stall-regulated wind turbines, and the W3 for pitch regulated turbines. The 
W3 series has been used on modern wind turbines as well as academic reference turbines [15]. 
Another series of airfoils was developed at Delft University, using a modified XFoil code called 
RFoil [39]. However breakthroughs in computational methods have allowed airfoil designers to 
couple airfoil topology with competing aerodynamic, structural and other performance 
requirements in complex optimisation routines. 
2.3.1 Typical airfoil design parameters 
Maximising of the lift-to-drag ratio was mentioned as target for airfoil choice from the BEM 
method, however many other parameters and considerations are import in airfoil design and 
selection. For a modern PRVS turbine the operating point on the lift-AOA curve varies with 
fluctuations in wind speed and rotational speed as depicted in Figure 9.

2.4 Multidisciplinary design optimization (MDO) for turbine blades and 
airfoils 
Many of the airfoil design parameters and blade planform variables have been optimised 
through multidisciplinary numerical approaches. Numerical optimisation requires solving of 
nonlinear, constrained problems defined by design variables [2]. This can include inequality and 
equality constraints on the variables, as well as restrictions on their range. Usefully stated by 
Unal [44], ‘The objective is to search the system design space to determine values of design 
variables that optimize the performance characteristic subject to system constraints’. This 
technique has been applied extensively in the aerospace industry, for example the relaunch 
vehicles that Unal refers to, and more recently to the wind turbine industry in blade and airfoil 
optimisation. These investigations highlight the trade-offs present in the selection of an 
optimisaiton algorithm including the convergence towards a solution, computational cost and 
robustness and adherence to constraints.  
2.4.1 Blades 
One of the earlier studies on the use of numerical optimisation in wind turbine blade design is 
that conducted by Fuglsang and Madsen from the Risø National Laboratory (now part of the 
Technical university of Denmark, DTU) [45]. This formed part of the development of the 
HawtOpt numerical multi-disciplinary optimization tool developed for horizontal axis wind 
turbines (see also [46]). The objective was the minimisation of the cost of energy (COE) of the 
blade, which incorporated functions for the fatigue and extreme loads and AEP. The model 
combined both sequential linear programming (SLP) and the method of feasible directions 
(MFD); where SLP proved quickest when constraints were satisfied and MFD more robust 
although with slower convergence. A semi-empirical gradient calculation method was also used 
to speed up gradient calculations. Optimisation variables included the representation of the 
planform such as chord, twist, relative thickness, and shell thickness distribution, as well as 
airfoil lift and drag characteristics and the blade pitch. When tested on a 1.5MW stall regulated 
turbine the optimisation model yielded a 3.5% decrease in the COE for traditional airfoils and 
7% when parametric airfoil characteristics were allowed. 
Also by Fuglsang, a broader application of the HawtOpt tool involved site-specific 
characteristics incorporated into the design process of wind turbines [47]. The objective function 
was again the COE, and two turbines were optimised for their performance at six sites of 
varying wind climate and terrain, including the effects of layout and localised wind velocities. 
Results indicated that the optimisation model was most useful at site with lower wind speeds 
where large reductions in the COE were achievable. 
A more recent site-specific optimisation process was investigated by Kenway and Martins using 
a multidisciplinary design feasible approach [48]. The authors couple a BEM-based 
aerodynamic model with a finite element model of the internal spar of the turbine blade. The 
XFoil panel solver was used to precompute polars for a range of NACA airfoils, which can then 
be called by the optimiser. Optimisation variables included the Reynold’s number, angle of 
attack and airfoil relative thickness. The coupled aero-structural model was optimised using the 
SNOPT sequential quadratic programming algorithm with complex-step gradients. Together with 
standard planform variables, the spar thickness, length and location as well as the relative 
thickness of the airfoils along the blade were optimised. Constraints included the Von-Mises 
stress in the spar including consideration of fatigue loads, a cost function and maximum power. 
For a 5kW fixed pitch turbine output power increases of between 3-4% were achieved.

2.4.2 Airfoils 
During airfoil optimisation, the airfoil surface can be parametised through B-Spline control points 
which then become the design variables. The optimisation process is multi-disciplinary in that it 
generally incorporates multiple aerodynamic performance restrictions, such as minimum lift 
coefficient, as well as other constraints or requirements such as structural thickness, leading 
edge smoothness, trailing edge thickness or noise performance. Whilst the majority of research 
is concerned with optimisation of aerodynamic performance at outer sections of the blade, 
increasing attention is being provided to the thicker sections of the blade [52]. 
Researchers at the Risø DTU National Laboratory for Sustainable Energy have employed 
numerical optimisation routines coupled with both panel code flow solvers and a 2D Navier-
Stokes flow solver to design multiple airfoil families [40], [41], [53]–[56]. They note that panel 
codes such as XFoil are particularly suited to optimisation routines due to the low computational 
requirements. However, XFoil’s integral boundary layer formulation is less valid for separated 
flow conditions and is therefore less suitable for near stall conditions [40]. The authors explore 
the inherent trade-off between structural stiffness and the target lift and lift-drag performance of 
an airfoil. They find that higher lift values are generally attained when the relative thickness is 
increased while the box length (cap width) is decreased. In more recent work, the authors 
utilised direct gradient-based numerical multi-point optimization with both XFoil and the CFD 
flow solver EllipSys2D to overcome the limitations panel codes suffer from for thick trailing 
edges and high relative thicknesses [41]. Their goal was high lift, high Reynolds number and

2.4.3 Direct vs indirect methods 
Two methods are normally pursued for optimisation routines. A direct approach involves solving 
of the flow field around a prescribed geometry subject to perturbations. Alternatively, an indirect 
or inverse approach involves defining a desired pressure or Mach number distribution, or 
boundary layer properties, and then solves for the requisite airfoil surface topology [3], [4] [64]. 
However this approach can lead to the prediction of unfeasible airfoil shapes [4], and can have 
difficulty in accommodating multiple and potentially conflicting design requirements [3]. The 
optimisation cases presented above all employ a direct method, which has the advantage of 
greater solution robustness [4]. However, inverse methods have the advantage that the 
transition behaviour along the airfoils surface can be tailored during the design, such as the 
location and size of separation bubbles on low Reynold’s airfoils studied by Selig [64], [65], and 
Drela [66], [67]. The choice of method depends largely on the goals of the design, and since the 
geometry is already provided for the current airfoils under investigation a direct method is 
necessary for the present work.

AOA where maximum lift is exhibited [69], [70]. Hence, in the context of the present study, it is 
expected that increasing lift authority will be achieved by larger flap deformation angles whilst 
the flow remains attached. Beyond this point, flow separation is not accounted for by potential 
flow theory and it is likely that the gain in lift will decrease and the drag on the airfoil increase 
significantly.  
Leading edge drooping can also be employed to delay flow separation and extend the 
maximum lift attainable for an airfoil. Hoerner [70] demonstrates that drooping of the LE into the 
oncoming flow can encourage the formation of the stagnation point directly on the nose, such 
that more ‘smooth’ flow results and the stall AOA is increased. This suggests that the optimum 
level of LE droop will be strongly dependent on the AOA of the incoming flow.

from these industries have been applied to wind turbine blade design [2], [71]–[73]. The authors

2.5.1 Morphing Flap Specific Studies 
Due to their significant advantages TE flaps have been widely investigated for their potential to

3. Optimisation Methodology 
The following chapter outlines the procedure undertaken for the optimisation of the blade 
planform and morphing camber topologies. This includes a discussion of the individual 
optimisation components including the flow solver used, airfoil topology representation, 
simulation tool, and the optimiser and framework employed. The tools were generally selected 
based on a balance of computational time and accuracy; however precedence was afforded to 
computational time and cost over accuracy which is evident in the choice of flow solver and 
aero-servo-elastic code. 
3.1 Flow solver 
The flow solver used in the optimisation routine is the XFoil open-source boundary-layer solver 
developed by Mark Drela [77]. This program solves the inviscid flow field through a linear-
vorticity stream function panel method, and has been extensively used in airfoil design and 
analysis for wind turbines [4], [20], [25], [40], [78]. XFoil is known to suffer from problems when 
solving separated flow, especially for thick airfoils, and predicts higher lift and lower drag than 
experimental and CFD methods [3], [4], [38], [52], [55]. One method to compensate for the 
under-prediction of drag is simply the addition of a drag penalty, such as that implemented by 
Grasso through a manual increase of drag coefficients by 10% [42], however no correction is 
applied in this work. 
Two flow cases will be investigated for each airfoil shape; an initial free transition condition in 
which the boundary layer is allowed to transition freely along the surface of the airfoil, and a 
forced transition case where separation will be induced at the leading edge as close as possible 
to the nose. Boundary-layer transition is a highly non-linear phenomenon and historically difficult 
to predict, although the 𝑒𝑁 linear approximation method used in XFoil has been validated in 2D 
flow environments [79]. Values of 𝑁𝑐𝑐𝑐𝑐 in literature range from 6, 7 or 9 depending on the 
boundary-layer turbulence intensity (TI) to be replicated, which correspond to a TI of 0.245%, 
0.161%, and 0.070% respectively [15], [32], [38]. In the present analysis a TI of 0.1% is 
assumed. During the design of the RWT it was found that CFD simulations predicted less of a 
difference between fully turbulent and free transition than those predicted with XFoil [15]. 
The effects of surface roughness and soiling are inherently difficult to predict and model for 
turbine airfoils [38], [43], although they can be approximated ‘tripping’ the boundary layer close 
to the airfoil nose. This is can be, for example, at a location of around 1% chord (0.01c) on the 
suction side and 10% chord (0.1c) on the pressure side [3], [32], [38]. For a more conservative 
approach to roughness modelling these values should be decreased to as close as possible to 
the nose whilst still achieving convergence in the solution. Due to the deformation employed at 
the leading edge in the present study values of 0.03c at the suction side and 0.08c at the 
pressure side were used. 
To capture the effect of both free transition and forced transition cases a weighting was applied 
to all polars generated by XFoil. The tripped and free transition polars were blended with a 
70%/30% ratio favouring the more conservative tripped case, which is consistent with values 
used elsewhere [80].

3.2 Topology Parametrisation 
During optimisation it is desirable to model the geometry of the airfoil with as few parameters as 
possible to reduce computation cost. Spline interpolation curves have been widely used in the 
design and optimisation of airfoil topology, the most common being B-splines or Bezier curves. 
In the current work, Bezier curves were selected for their high degree of controllability and 
gentle curvature changes [3], [41]. These allow the surface geometry of the airfoil to be 
constructed with only a few control points (CPs), which become the control variables of the 
optimisation routine and therefore can significantly reduce the computational cost during 
optimisation [3], [4], [81], [82]. 
A typical approach to replicating airfoil geometry with Bezier curves is through splitting the airfoil 
surface into upper and lower curves extending from the leading edge to the trailing edge. Initial 
estimates are provided for the control point locations, which could be at the surface of the airfoil. 
A least squares method based on the difference between the provided curve and the produced 
curve coordinates can then used to fit the control point locations to model the curve accurately. 
To maintain C1 continuity and smoothness along the leading edge the control point at the nose 
is fixed and is shared by both the upper and lower curves [41]. The trailing edge coordinates 
also represent a fixed control point for each curve. Due to the higher curvature at the leading 
edge a greater number of CPs are generally used to model this region. An example Bezier-fit to 
the FFA-W3-241 airfoil using the above procedure is shown in Figure 15 (top). 
In this case, control point 𝑥/𝑐 locations were fixed and the 𝑦/𝑐 fitted so that the curves match. 
Discrepancies are evident between the original and predicted curve, which could be improved 
by increasing the number of CPs, allowing 𝑥/𝑐 freedom, or tightening the least squares error 
tolerances (implemented here through the Scipy LeastSQ method). 
As the current task is primarily concerned with altering the leading edge and trailing edge 
geometry only, these regions were modelled based on their mean-line and the central spar 
region of the airfoil left unaltered. The leading edge and trailing edge mean-lines were fitted with 
Bezier CPs, which could then be moved to produce leading edge drooping and trailing edge flap 
displacement. This procedure is shown for the original airfoil in Figure 15 (middle). The shorter 
line length and lower curvature of the mean-line sections compared to the full surface means 
fewer CPs are necessary to capture the shape. The CPs were displaced by specifying an 
angular displacement relative to the chord length of the flap (%c), as shown in Figure 15 
(bottom). The convention employed in this work assumes positive angular deflections are 
downwards in this figure, hence increasing the camber of the airfoil. 
The order of a Bezier curves defines the degree of local control of each control point, the higher 
this value the smoother the curve [53]. Orders of between 3-5 are generally used in literature 
[4], [41], [42], [53], and an order of 5 was selected for the current study.

3.4 Optimiser Framework 
To connect and drive the optimisation and flow of data between geometry generation, flow 
solver and power simulation, the Multidisciplinary Design Analysis and Optimization 
(OpenMDAO) framework developed by the NASA Glenn Research Centre was selected [86]–
[89]. Although conceived for the design and analysis of unconventional aircraft, the OpenMDAO 
platform is highly flexible and can be applied to a wide range of optimisation problems, and has 
already been used in wind turbine optimisation applications [41], [51], [90]. The software 
package is open source and written in Python. Individual software codes and interfaces are able 
to be contained in separate components, which can then be wrapped together in an assembly. 
One or more analysis assemblies are connected through a driver for the analysis, which can 
execute assemblies in parallel.

Gradient-based optimisation 
Gradient based optimisation algorithms are widely used to solve both linear and non-linear 
optimisation problems. Linear optimisation problems may be solved by a finite number of steps, 
however non-linear problems require numerous iteration steps [92]. Gradient methods use the 
gradient or multiple gradients of the objective function to inform the selection of the next 
iteration’s input variables in an effort to reduce the value of the objective function; a process 
termed ‘gradient descent’. The adjustment in each input variable is proportional to the observed 
reduction in objective function and its gradient(s) from the previous iteration [96]. When 
approximating derivatives using finite differences, the number of function evaluations is 
proportional to the number of design variables [3], [97]. As these methods generally rely on local 
iteration values of the objective function they are prone to finding local minima and can be 
susceptible to noise in both the objective and any constraint functions [3], [4], [96]. The 
efficiency of their implementation is similarly dependent on the calculation of the objective and 
constraint functions. However, gradient methods offer numerous advantages such as their 
relative simplicity of implementation and ability to handle multiple objectives and constraints in a 
more robust manner [3], [52]. Gradient methods have been used in the optimisation of airfoils 
shown in the literature study [3], [41]. For the present case, the PSQP algorithm was initially 
adopted for the optimisation, however problems with local minima susceptibility led instead to

Constrained optimization by linear approximation (COBYLA) 
PyOpt also offers a version of Powell’s nonlinear derivative-free constrained optimization 
(COBYLA) that uses interpolation at each iteration to produce variable changes [98], [99]. This 
approach uses linear approximations to the objective and constraint functions using linear 
programming problems. Variable changes are bound by a trust-region that is narrowed until 
convergence depending on its satisfaction of the objective and constraints. Each iterate 
produces a new vector of variables that can replace existing values. The iterative procedure of 
COBYLA means that it is a type of sequential linear programming algorithm. Importantly, there 
are no smoothness assumptions for objectives and constraints and it can therefore be more 
robust when dealing with highly nonlinear problems with complex or discontinuous derivatives. 
The final accuracy of the algorithm is however limited by the linear approximations to non-linear 
functions [98].

4.3.1 Airfoils 
The airfoils used on the RWT are the FFA-W3 airfoil family. These airfoils are freely available 
and have been used in research and industry. However, limited wind tunnel testing of the airfoils

from the thinner airfoils. Gurney flaps were also installed on the 36.0%, 48.0% and 60.0%

significant uncertainty in the aerodynamic performance of these regions. Due to the limitations

To accommodate the new XFoil-generated polars in the reference profile coefficients a merging

and end of the XFoil range, shown in Figure 28, to prevent sudden spikes in coefficients and the

concern as the lift operating range for the radial sections affected remained within the -2 to 18 
degree range. A similar smoothing and merging process was undertaken for the RWT, which 
combined the CFD generated polars with a flat-plate flow solution for the extreme angles of 
attack extrapolation.

5. Wind Climate 
To quantify the power improvement offered by morphing camber technology in AEP terms a low 
resource reference wind site was selected. China was selected as an ideal location due to its 
booming wind energy uptake and relatively low wind speeds available. In 2014 alone, 23 GW of 
new wind was installed by China, representing the highest installation rate of any country in a 
year to date [13]. The global distribution of wind as a resource is extremely uneven, and one 
global evaluation found that only 13% of wind stations studied experienced average annual 
wind speeds of greater than 6.9 m/s at 80m [102]. In the present study, the wind climate of 
Southeast China that was documented in a wind energy resource atlas published by the 
National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL, USA) was used to characterise the wind 
resource available for the reference turbine [103]. In the NREL report, coastal locations were 
found to vary in annual average wind speeds of between 5.7 to 8.1 m/s, however there were 
substantial areas of moderate (5.6-6.4 m/s) wind speeds particularly inland. Due to the steady-
state nature of the power optimisations and simulations, no atmospheric turbulence was 
modelled however it should be noted that the turbulence level at a site can have a significant 
impact on both power production and blade and turbine loads. Similarly, no losses are modelled 
on the turbine and its components; although losses such as generator efficiency will also limit 
the available electrical power from the turbine and thus it’s potential AEP.

5.2 Cost of Energy (COE) 
An additional study was performed which sought to quantify the increase available in the AEP at 
both sites from blade morphing in terms of the Cost of Energy (COE) for the turbine. In this 
case, only the relative increase in the COE is of interest, and some significant and limiting 
assumptions are made to model this. 
The World Energy Council defines the COE as a ‘value that represents the total lifecycle costs 
of costs of producing a MWh of power using a specific technology’ [104]. Modelling the COE for 
a wind turbine farm is therefore a comprehensive and extremely subjective process that is 
highly dependent on the location and local conditions. This must include consideration of such 
costs as financing, installation, equipment, operating and maintenance [104], which is beyond 
the scope of the present study. 
5.2.1 CREST 
To model the changes to the COE the Cost of Renewable Energy Spreadsheet Tool (CREST) 
developed and published by NREL was selected [105], [106]. This tool is spread-sheet based 
and freely available online [107]. CREST is a powerful tool intended to assist primarily 
policymakers for renewable energy incentives, however will be used in the present context for a 
quick approximation of the change in COE for a single turbine subject to some of the morphing 
configurations explored.  
The primary output of the CREST tool is the 1 year fixed price COE of the plant modelled based 
on some user-defined inputs. The Discounted Cash Flow Model is used to produce the COE in 
cents per kilowatt-hour (¢/kWh) [105]. A key input into the plant’s operation is the Capacity 
Factor, defined as the AEP of the plant divided by maximum output of the plant, as shown in

the maximum aerodynamic power from HawcStab2 simulations (10.6MW) which does not 
include losses and inefficiencies. This value is then multiplied by the total hours in a year.

6.1 Twist Optimisation 
6.1.1 Purpose and Method 
An initial study was conducted on the performance of the RWT in lower wind speeds based on 
its planform design. The planform, introduced and shown in section 4.3, was optimised for a 
TSR of 7.5. This TSR is only approached for wind speeds between 7 and 12 m/s (see Figure 
36), resulting in below-optimal operation at other wind speeds. An interesting benchmark is then 
to compare any gains from future topology morphing to the power improvement available if the 
planform, in this the case just the twist distribution, were to be optimised at lower wind speeds.

points (CPs) [15]. For the present study, the twist at the root shall remain constant as the

6.2.1 Uniform flap deformation parameter study 
Purpose and Method 
The small number of parameters required to define the deflection at the TE and LE of the airfoil 
allows for a parameter study to be conducted. This investigated the potential power gain 
available from deflecting the LE and TE at discreet angles within the allowable deformation 
range. The deflection was assumed to be uniform across the blade span from r/R of 0.7 to 1.0, 
covering the span of the 24.1% thickness airfoils. The uniform tip was also active from 0.97 to 
1.0.  
The two parameters studied were the LE angle and TE angle, assuming a flap length of 10%c 
and 20%c for both the LE and TE (see Figure 47). The results of this exercise will help identify 
and avoid local optima in the optimisation design variable space, and thus validate the 
optimisation results. Using this method the average time taken to survey the variable space for 
each wind speed was approximately 4 hours using the DTU Jess compute cluster [100]. It will 
therefore be a target for the optimisations to not only improve on these results but also to 
reduce this time requirement.

6.4 Optimal Flap Location and Length 
6.4.1 Purpose and Method 
The literature review (section 2.5) identified that many of the flaps studied for load reduction 
potential covered only 5-20% of the blades radius. In this section, the potential of these smaller 
flaps are tested for radial lengths of 5, 7.5, 10 and 15%. The large span covered by the 24.1% 
airfoil allowed for the testing of optimal radial location as well, since the flap location could easily 
be moved by the optimiser within this span (0.7-0.97R). Due to the quantity of simulations 
required, this exercise was only performed on the shorter 10% chord flap length airfoil. 
The flap length was fixed for each simulation and the flap defined by two airfoils at its start and 
end, whose deflection was controlled by the optimiser. The first airfoil location was a design 
variable and its range limited such that the start and end of the morphing region could not 
exceed 0.7R and 0.97R respectively. The upper bound region was chosen to avoid approaching 
the high chord change at the tip. The outer airfoil location was fixed by the length of the flap and 
the first airfoil location. An alternative approach could allow the optimiser to vary the start and 
end radial locations, and specifying the difference between these two (i.e. the flap length) as an 
objective or constraint, although the present method proved simpler and reduced the design 
variables by one. The uniform tip was not modelled as this would effectively increase the length 
of the morphing region. The initial locations for the flap centres were chosen such that the outer 
airfoil location was between 0.90 and 0.95R; however the optimiser and algorithm proved 
insensitive to initial guesses. 
6.4.2 Optimum Location 
For practical application, it was assumed that the location of the flap would not change with the 
wind speed. Two main methods were therefore considered when determining the optimal 
location for a fixed flap length. Ideally, an AEP optimised approach would consist of allowing the 
optimiser to vary the flap centre location and deflection over the below-rated wind range of 4-
10m/s, and then returning the optimal location which maximises AEP. However, this would not 
provide wind speed-specific information on the ideal flap location, and would require execution 
of HawcStab2 for the entire wind range for each iterate which is time consuming. A simpler 
method was chosen which investigated the wind speeds independently for their optimal 
deflection and location. The separate optimal locations were then averaged to provide a fixed, 
mean, location, and the optimiser run again to determine the new optimal deflection at this 
location for each wind speed. 
Results 
The optimum deflection and airfoil locations are shown in Figure 83 for the 5%R and 7.5%R flap 
lengths, and Figure 84 for the 10%R and 15%R. The mean flap centre location for each case is 
depicted by the dotted line, which indicates that overall an approximate 90%R position is 
optimal. Very strong agreement on the airfoil locations is experienced by the larger 15%R flap 
across all wind speeds. Similar consistency is demonstrated by the 7.5%R case, and less so by 
the 10%R and 5%R. These differences are captured by the relative standard deviation, shown 
in Table 9, which is the standard deviation of the locations divided by the flap length. The 15%R 
has the lowest, indicating the strongest correlation between the different wind winds. The very 
low power gains available for the smaller flap locations may help explain the greater variation in 
these cases, as very slight perturbations in locations could couple with the interpolation error to 
introduce aberrant noise in the objective function.

6.4.5 Span-wise Load Distribution 
Plots of the load distribution along the blade equipped with the various morphing configurations 
are provided in Figure 92 for 4m/s and Figure 93 for 8m/s. The use of much smaller flap lengths 
introduces significant point loads on the blade at the respective airfoil locations. At low wind 
speeds, the start of the flap is characterised by a sharp decrease in span-wise axial load and an 
increase in tangential load. At the location of the second airfoil, the sharp recovery in the load 
distribution is due to the removal of the uniform tip deformation used for the 30%R (shown) and 
also the 60%R (not shown). The significant span-wise change in Figure 92 is again due to the 
pitch change at 4m/s (Appendix C.6, Figure 140). The optimal pitch angle of 0 degrees is 
maintained for the rate TSR (7-10m/s) and hence this change is not present in the distributions 
at 8m/s. The powering effect at higher wind speeds likewise results in a spike in the distributed 
loads, particularly evident in the axial force and thrust coefficient. 
These highly localised point loadings pose a significant concern for the simulation tool used. 
Being BEM based, HawcStab2 assumes that the flow and loading conditions at radial sections 
along the blade are independent of each other. However, the introduction of such high point 
loading could violate this assumption as the flow across surrounding blade sections will likely be 
affected. It is probable, then, that a more smooth distribution would result along the blade and 
thus diminish the positive power increase of the smaller flap coverage spans. The exact effect 
would have to be explored using CFD or actuator-based techniques that avoid some of the 
simplistic assumptions of the BEM model.

7. Conclusions 
This thesis sought to investigate the potential of continuous morphing camber flaps to increase 
the power output from a megawatt wind turbine blade. The incorporation of these flaps on the 
blade was assumed to be primarily for load reduction purposes; however any potential power 
increase could serve to offset the costs associated with their integration on the blade and 
turbine. Some necessary assumption were made in terms of the deformation and actuation that 
is possible for these active surfaces, which was informed by a detailed literature study of the 
field. 
An optimisation framework was developed whereby the optimal camber deflection, flap 
coverage span length and location could be modelled on the blade of the DTU 10MW reference 
wind turbine. To offer a basis of comparison and further test the optimisation process, the twist 
distribution along the blade was optimised both at single wind speeds and in terms of AEP gain. 
A model low wind resource climate was used to quantify the increase in power in terms of 
turbine AEP. Using a simplified model the improvement to the turbine’s AEP was also quantified 
in terms of the COE of the power generated. 
From this process the following results can be concluded: 
• 
The greatest power improvements for all configurations investigated are available at 
lower wind speeds, where the reference turbine is operating above design tip speed 
ratio, 
• 
At lower wind speeds, the optimiser sought to depower the tip region of the blade, whilst 
simultaneously powering the mid-section, 
• 
The site-specific optimisation of the morphing twist distribution at individual wind speed 
yields the highest power and AEP improvement of up to 0.64%, which is reduced to 
0.46% when a single AEP-optimised distribution is assumed, 
• 
Morphing camber has the greatest power authority at around 90% of the blade span, 
• 
At this radial location, the power increase and AEP available is almost linearly related to 
the blade span coverage of the flap up to a 15%R size, beyond this length decreasing 
returns are predicted which would have to be balanced against greater structural 
requirements at inboard regions, 
• 
The addition of leading edge camber deformation is able to increase the potential power 
gain and AEP from the turbine, however this small increase may not be enough to 
justify the additional and significant structural implications at the leading edge, 
• 
Increasing the region of the airfoil camber available for morphing does not guarantee 
higher power yields from the active surfaces, especially when leading edge deformation 
is not used, although can reduce the loads on the blade, 
• 
The potential gains in AEP strongly correlate to estimated reductions in the COE, 
These findings have demonstrated that increased power can be attained from the reference 
turbine whilst still maintaining desired aerodynamic design points and limits. This strongly 
suggests that if these surfaces are to be employed for the purpose of load regulation, they 
should also be investigated for power boost particularly at low wind speeds. Inevitably, this 
would incur further challenges for the controller design whilst limiting their load reduction 
authority when activated for power gain. A trade-off could take the form of higher priority for 
power gain at low wind speeds, which decreases towards design tip speed ratio and rated

power where loads become more critical on the turbine. Ultimately, a cost trade-off should be 
performed between the potential benefits of this technology, including greater AEP, COE 
reductions, load reduction and corresponding structural mass reduction, to the increased 
structural complexity, reduced structural cross-sectional area, control and maintenance 
requirements necessary for their incorporation. This is a highly turbine and potential site-
dependent process that is not easily transferable between technologies and designs.

8. Further Work 
The investigation presented here touches on the much larger fields of both active surface 
design and wind turbine design and engineering. The narrow scope employed therefore 
necessitated some significant and limiting assumptions and simplifications. The impact of these 
could be reduced by some further model improvements and modifications. Some additional 
studies would also assist to place this technology within the scope of realistic testing and 
deployment. 
8.1 Model Improvements 
1- Flap modelling in the aerodynamic layout 
A key source of uncertainty when comparing the gains from the individual configurations was 
the power decrement incurred when aerodynamic section were removed between the flap 
locations. This arose due to a limitation in the inputs for HawcStab2, which couples the 
specification of the chord length and relative thickness along with the profile coordinate file. It is 
probable that the vast majority of this decrement could be avoided by the inclusion of numerous 
polar sets corresponding to these internal radial locations, which are formed by the interpolation 
between the polars of the morphed airfoils. Hence, the chord and relative thickness definition 
would be complete. 
2- Verification of the BEM approach for small flap lengths 
The independence between radial sections on the blade assumed by the BEM model used 
resulted in concerning perturbations on the distributed blade loads. Such large increases in 
loads at the start and end of the flap region are perhaps unreasonable, and a more realistic 
distribution could be attained from alternative methods such as actuator-based or CFD 
methods. However, the computational requirements for these would perhaps precipitate their 
use as a post-process verification tool rather than integrated in the optimisation workflow. 
3- Alternative flow solver 
The use of, for example, a 2D CFD flow solver in place of the panel-based XFoil solver would 
allow for the morphing of larger relative thicknesses, and hence larger span length long the 
blade. Additionally, this would permit the generation of polars for a larger range of AOA, and 
therefore improve the robustness of the polar merging approach whilst allowing the testing of 
off-design points, for example through the introduction of turbulent wind speeds. This would also 
ensure a common flow solver is used for all polars used in the turbine model. 
4- More detailed investigation of topology representation 
A more detailed investigation of the method used to replicate the TE and LE based on the 
mean-line of the airfoil could include the influence of the morphing caber length, resulting 
deformation and camber gradient. This could study the influence on the location of separation 
along the airfoil and its sensitivity to roughness. Additionally, alternative representation methods 
could reduce the difference in predicted aerodynamic coefficients for the two flap camber 
lengths studied (10%c and 20%c).

8.2 Further Investigations 
1- Design of actuator technique 
To validate the assumptions employed in this research on the capabilities of the active surface a 
useful further study would involve the design and possibly manufacture and testing of a camber 
morphing airfoil prototype. Although this has been pursued by numerous authors as shown in 
the literature study, no investigation has taken a power-increase and detailed structural 
approach. Of particular interest would be the exploration of the ability of the remaining static 
cross-section to withstand the current blade loads and additional morphing-loads on the blade 
given the reduced structural cross-section. Wind tunnel testing would be similarly beneficial to 
validate and correct the aerodynamic coefficients of the morphing airfoil shape. 
2- Blade design incorporation  
Significant advantages would be possible if the design and use of the morphing active surfaces 
were included in the first stages of the blade design process, resulting in a morphing-specific 
blade design that is able to capitalise on the benefits of the technology. This would likely result 
in a blade that is able to maintain the optimum tip speed ratio for a longer wind speed range, 
and hence achieve higher aerodynamic efficiency. Additionally, the impact on blade loads could 
be further quantified through time-domain simulations for the impact on accumulated damage 
and lifetime loads, and modal analysis performed. A necessary aspect of this further study 
would be the design and incorporation of the control strategy, balancing the time-varying need 
for load reduction by the flaps and the mean flap displacement for power boost.

A.1. Morphing Specific Studies 
Due to their significant advantages TE flaps have been widely investigated for their potential to 
reduce blades loads at above rated and during turbulent wind operation. The small flap inertia 
allows high actuation frequencies, which is able to react far quicker than conventional load 
reduction methods such as pitch regulation [8]. If these are distributed and independently 
controlled, morphing sections are able to respond to aerodynamic loading which varies locally 
along the blade [75]. Due to the greater moment arm available at the tip, flaps at the outer 
radius tend to have the greatest potential to reduce fatigue loads [18], and have even been 
investigated to replace traditional pitch control methods [76]. 
Demonstrating the high technological cross-over between the aeronautical industry and the 
wind industry, numerous studies were conducted by FlexSys Inc. on adaptive airfoil shapes 
initially for aircraft [108]–[110], and then for wind turbine application [75]. As part of Mission 
Adaptive Compliant Wing project, a compliant continuous trailing edge flap capable of ±10 
degree deformation at 30 °/𝑠 was installed on a natural laminar flow airfoil (Figure 98). The 
system was optimised to maximise the laminar boundary region and hence lift to drag 
performance for endurance aircraft. The authors promote the benefits of smooth camber 
deformable structures over discrete hinges due to their superior control authority in terms of lift 
and lower drag [108]. A smooth transition from deformable to fixed sections of the wing span is 
also desired to reduce noise and turbulence [109]. Flight testing indicated that the flap was able 
to significantly increase lift with minimal effect on drag of the airfoil. 
 
Figure 98 The FlexSys conformal adaptive trailing edge airfoil [108]–[110]

and the NREL 5MW offshore reference wind turbines. Shown in Figure 14, the 20% chord flaps 
were modelled on the outer 25% of the blade and were assumed capable of ±10 degree 
deformation during turbine operation.

Troldborg studied both the steady-state and oscillatory behaviour of a trailing edge flap attached 
to the Risø-B1-18 airfoil [5]. Parametrisation of the topology of the flap included the study of 
±15° flap deformation angles, flap chord lengths between 5 and 20%, and varied flap actuation 
frequencies. The 2D incompressible Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) solver 
EllipSys2D was used with the 𝑘−𝜔𝑆𝑆𝑆 turbulence method. The author promotes a design 
offering sufficiently large lift control to reduce loading coupled with low drag and flap moment. 
This was achieved with a 𝑐𝑓/𝑐 ratio of 5-10%, as increasing the flap length resulted in lower L/D 
ratios and higher flap moment (although larger lift authority). Troldborg also found that a 
morphed topology is far more efficient in terms of lift than a hinged flap, although decreasing 
returns are gained when curvature is increased as drag and moment coefficients increase. The 
increase in efficiency was attributed to the greater region of attached flow at lower curvatures. 
As part of the ADAPWING project, deformable TE flaps were modelled on a 33m V66 Vestas 
blade for controlling fluctuating aerodynamic loads [6]. Using a proportional differential controller 
with the local flapwise deflections of the wing as input, the authors modelled the potential load 
reduction of numerous flap installations of different length and radial locations. The results in 
terms of flapwise root moment reduction are shown in Figure 100 below and indicate that a 
continuous 11m flap offers the highest reduction potential (63%) for flapwise root moment. For 
the separated flaps, the authors found that dividing the flaps into individually-controlled devices 
offered the greatest reduction efficiency.

In a joint collaboration between industry and academia, the UpWind project comprehensively 
studied the application of smart rotor blade technology to large wind turbines [72], [111], [112]. 
Aero-elastic modelling using a modified Bladed code on the UpWind 5MW reference turbine 
suggested that a 20% radius, 10% chord flap with ±10° deflection could alleviate the majority of 
disturbances introduced by extreme gust and direction change when actuated up to 6Hz [111]. 
The active flap was found to significantly reduce high frequency loads, which are important for 
fatigue loading.

Delft University of Technology [112]. The model was shown to reduce the standard deviation of

The potential for fatigue load reduction from TE flaps was similarly shown through control

Wind tunnel tests were also performed on a Risø-B1-18 airfoil equipped with a 9% chord

coefficient, although this was highly dependent on phase shift and could result in increased 
loads when further out of phase. 
Recent full-scale testing of a single 70cm conventional TE flap on the 13m blade of a Vestas

frequencies. Simulations were also run with the Flex5 aero-servo-elastic code; however 
discrepancies between predictions and observations were largely attributed to unknown causes.

Similar tests were undertaken by Daynes and Weaver on a 1m span (1.3m chord) NACA 63-
418 airfoil with 20% chord deformable TE [8]. The flap was divided into 4 individually controlled 
honeycomb flaps each capable of deforming to ±10°. The tests were designed to replicate the 
conditions at a 70% radial location of a 90m wind turbine blade at high wind speeds. The 
authors investigate steady-state stiffness characterisation of the blade and flap, aerodynamic 
characteristics, pressure distribution, and actuator requirements and strains for various angles 
of attack. With ±10° deflection, the flap was able to change the lift coefficient of the blade by 
±1.0. However, high flap deflection led to positive pitching moments that could result in blade 
twisting when applied to large areas of wind turbine blades. They found that actuator loads were 
almost independent of angle of attack, likely due to the smooth increase in camber towards 
trailing edge rather than traditional rigid flaps. 
More recently, Abdelrahman and Johnson studied a 20% chord, 15% radial flap on a down-
scaled turbine rig [7]. Based on measurements of the flapwise bending moment and power 
yield, the aurthors also conclude that the greatest control authority exists for flaps located as 
close to the blade tip as possible. Additionally, the load reduction capacity of the discreet flap 
was linearly related to the angle of the flap. An increase in power was demonstrated for 
increasing flap angle on the pressure side, although resulted in higher bending moment. The 
authors note this could be useful as a power boost in low wind operation. 
Wolff and colleagues investigated the optimal deformable flap geometry in terms of chord length 
and deflection angle for a typical wind turbine airfoil [18]. They utilised the 2D RANS solver 
FLOWer to design the flap in steady-state, then studied the time-varying phase shift between 
flap deflection and dynamic lift coefficient. The flap was displaced between +10° and -10°, with 
two different curve functions. Their findings suggest an optimal flap length of between 15-20% 
chord to maintain high lift authority and low aerodynamic force, without disturbing the stall 
behaviour of the foil significantly. For larger flaps, the increase in aerodynamic force is greater 
than the increase in lift gained. The phase shift in the time-varying simulations indicated that the 
trailing edge deflection leads the lift coefficient at lower angles of attack. This trend is reversed 
near the stall region, where the flap deflection induces flow separation and reduces lift. 
Similarly, an overshoot of the static lift coefficient was predicted at high AOAs which could result 
in unsteady loading. The phase shift was also found to increase when higher actuation 
frequencies were used or at lower angles of attack. 
The modelling methodologies for trailing edge flap devices was investigated by Gillebaart and 
colleagues [115]. The aero-servo-elastic response of a 2D three-degree of freedom airfoil 
equipped with a 10% chord deformable trailing edge gap was assessed. Using a simplified PD

A.3. Optimiser Algorithm Selection 
The flexibility between OpenMDAO and its integration of PyOpt solvers allows for the easy 
interchange and testing of different optimisation algorithms on the problem. This was 
undertaken for the COBYLA algorithm discussed in section 3.4.1, and for a gradient based 
algorithm. 
Sequential Quadratic Programming (PSQP) 
A gradient-based method, the Preconditioned Sequential Quadratic Programming (PSQP) 
method solves nonlinear problems by solving a sequence of sub-problems through a quadratic 
model of the objective function [91]. Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) is a version of 
Newton’s method for unconstrained optimisation (sometimes referred to as Newton-Raphson 
method), which breaks the problem down into linear problems [92]. SQP methods rely on 
quadratic Taylor expansions at each iterate. However, the basic form of SQP inherits the local 
optimality and convergence of Newton-type methods, and hence requires a globalisation 
strategy normally in the form of a filter or merit function [92].  
The PSQP solver employs the first and second (Hessian matrix) derivatives, where the Hessian 
is approximated using a Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (BFGS) variable metric update [95]. 
The local finite difference step is applied and then the gradients are calculated, which results in 
numerous objective function executions at each iterate. As the entire workflow can be executed 
one or more times during each iteration this can become computationally expensive. The 
efficiency and solution convergence is therefore sensitive to the step-size parameter. 
Comparison 
Initially, the PSQP algorithm was chosen due to presumed greater robustness particularly when 
dealing with constraints However, long convergence times and problems with local optima were 
quickly encountered which led to the exploration of alternative algorithms and the final selection 
of the COBYLA. 
To explore the sensitivity of the problem at hand to changes in the OpenMDAO derived finite 
difference gradient calculation, a study on the Jacobian was performed over a range of potential 
finite difference step sizes from 1e-20 to 100. It is known that gradient-based methods are 
highly sensitive to finite difference step size [48], and it is therefore important to elucidate the 
range of finite difference step sizes that will result in stable convergence. 
The calculated finite difference for the LE and TE angular displacement variables on two 
morphing airfoil sections is shown in Figure 107. A high instability is obvious at extremely low 
step sizes, after which fast convergence is shown towards a step size independent gradient. As 
the default finite difference in OpenMDAO is 1e-6, it is unlikely that the divergence seen at low 
step sizes would ever be tested by the optimiser. As the finite difference calculation is 
performed independently of the driver the Jacobian will not be changed when switching 
algorithm. 
The two optimisation algorithms were also tested on two flap configurations; one with two 
morphing spans at 0.7R and 0.97R (24.1% 𝑡/𝑐) and both LE and TE morphing, and the other 
with three morphing spans at 0.48R (30.1%), 0.7R and 0.97R with only the TE deformed. The 
same initial conditions of zero deflection were used for both, and the default algorithm options 
retained except for the step size of the PSQP algorithm which was reduced from 1e16 to 1.0 to 
reflect the angular deformation range possible.

B.1. 3D Correction

The 3D correction model is applied to the polars at angles of attack between maximum lift and 
deep stall. The onset of stall was defined as the angle of attack below the point at which the 
minimum lift curve occurred; in other words the end of the approximately linear regions as 
shown in Figure 110 by the dotted line. 
Deep stall occurs after rapid detachment of the flow from the airfoil surface caused by a severe 
adverse pressure gradient along the upper surface [43]. The resulting flow is highly unstable 
with strong vortex shedding. This point would normally be defined as the point at which the lift 
slope is a minimum, after the maximum lift has been achieved. However, as shown in figure, the 
limited AOA range used in XFoil due to convergence issues meant that this point was not 
achieved. Therefore, the range for polars to be corrected was instead taken from the onset of 
stall to +8 degrees AOA beyond this point. It should be noted that the RWT polars were already 
corrected in the full stall range, and hence the merging of these polars resulted in a larger 3D 
correction range than that one shown in Figure 110.
