Abstract:
It is one of Novo Nordisk’s highest priorities to pro-
vide a healthy and engaging work environment, which
also includes the removal of repetitive and unpleas-
ant work through automation. Possible ways of using
Computer Vision and Machine Learning were investi-
gated to provide a proof of concept to automate the
manual assembly of needles being used in the process
of ﬁlling insulin into glass cartridges.
The system is able to locate the so called needle hold-
ers (NHs) from a tray with a trained Mask R-CNN
segmentation model and the input from a RGB cam-
era. In a total of 6 steps the pose of the NH is iden-
tiﬁed and its orientation is corrected before it is in-
serted into an assembly rack. Once in the rack, an
aluminium needle is inserted into the NH with force
feedback from a robotic arm.
The autonomous execution of the assembly achieved
a success rate of up to 88% throughout diﬀerent
tests.

with the author.

1 Introduction
1
Introduction
A lot has happened in the ﬁeld of production and process automation since Novo Nordisk was
founded during the second industrial revolution in 1923. The company that is widely known for
their production of insulin, was already then utilizing state-of-the-art mass production practices.
Now they have production sites on 5 continents, with 169 countries having access to their products
and are the leading supplier of insulin, covering 50% of the worlds demand. This would not have
been possible without the continuous adaption of the newest technologies.[1] [2]
While Novo Nordisk is very far in the process of fully transitioning to industry 4.0, the traditional
way in which the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) is regulating medical devices, is
not yet ready to assess the use of Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) in the pharmaceutical industry.
Nonetheless, Novo Nordisk has been investigating the potential use cases of Machine Learning
(ML) and AI since 2019. Especially the packaging of ﬁnished products and the assembly of
production components promise great opportunities.[3] [4]
In 2019 the insulin giant ﬁnished the construction of their new aseptic ﬁlling and inspection
facility called APX Hillerød. It has the nickname ”factory of the future” and PVP Christian
Larsson said it was built to ”fulﬁll current and future expectations regarding compliance and
product mix”, which also enables the integration of new technologies.[5]
A main focus point of this new production site is the implementation of isolator technology,
where ﬁlling machines can be operated while being in closed containers. Even though the ﬁlling
environment is sterile and operators wear protective clothing there is always a minimal risk of
contamination. Besides regular inspection, isolating and automating the ﬁlling process reduces
this risk of to an absolute minimum.
Today, in 2022, these ﬁlling lines have been through many tests and are soon fulﬁlling the
requirements to start production for the world market. Each line is capable of ﬁlling 600 3ml
cartridges per minute by pumping the product through 36 tubes that are attached to special
ﬁlling needles. After each batch this ”needle-tube-complex” needs to be removed, disassembled,
cleaned and reassembled, which is still a mostly manual process performed by operators. The
assembly is a tedious and unpleasant process and with scalability in mind, a way of automation
needs to be found.
1.1
Context
While most other companies focus mainly on their ﬁnancial goals, Novo Nordisk puts just as much
emphasis on their social and environmental development. This triple bottom line is reﬂected in
the ten rules of the ”Novo Nordisk way”. According to rule number eight, a working environment
must be engaging and healthy. [6] This also means that they strive to eliminate repetitive and
unpleasant work. With the current advancements in technology evermore of these tasks can be
automated to leave the operators to do more enjoyable jobs.
One of these tasks is the preparation of needles and tubes for the ﬁlling of insulin into cartridges.
The so called ”needle-tube-complex” necessary for ﬁlling is assembled from four components:
• A Needle
• A Needleholder
• A Teﬂon Tube
• A Silicone Tube

1.2
Problem formulation
These four components are manually assembled in the following steps:
1. Taking the needle holder from tray/box
2. Insertion of needle holder into assembly rack
3. Taking needle from Box
4. Insertion of needle into needle holder
5. Tightening the screw of the needle holder to keep the needle in place
6. Connecting Teﬂon tube with silicon tube
7. Pulling the Teﬂon tube over the needle
8. Clamping Teﬂon tube and needle together
(a) Assembled ”needle-tube-complex”
(b) Needle holders and needle in assembly rack
Figure 1: Images of the assembled needle
1.2
Problem formulation
As APX Hillerød is about to start production it might not be feasible anymore to manually
assemble the needles and tubes necessary for ﬁlling the product. It is therefore important to
develop an automated solution.
Of the 8 steps listed in section 1.1 steps 1 to 4 are to be automated using robotics, computer
vision (CV) and AI. It is important that all needle holders (NHs) face the same way when being
placed into the rack, so that the automation process can later be continued from there.
The problem formulation can be summarized as follows:
With the aid of computer vision and machine learning, how can a system detect and locate the
components to partially assemble the needles necessary to ﬁll insulin into glass cartridges?

1.3
End user requirements
1.3
End user requirements
The end user requirements were formulated together with Novo Nordisk, more speciﬁcally Sophia
Aman Raza, and adjusted to the time frame and expectations to a masters thesis. These re-
quirements provide the basis for evaluation in the conclusion of this report.
For the three core parts of this project the system is expected to perform:
• Tray picking
– identify and diﬀerentiate all needle holders on the tray
– locate all needle holders
– not mistake other objects for needle holders
– grab the needle holder with a gripper
• Rack insertion
– insert needle holders into the rack
– assure that all screws face the same direction after insertion
∗this is important for a further automation of the assembly
• Needle insertion
– grab the needle from a ﬁxed position
– insert a needle into each needle holder
– insert the needle all the way
1.4
Assumptions
In order to reduce the complexity of the problem to ﬁt the time frame of the project, the following
assumptions of the environment were made:
• The location of the tray is ﬁxed and known
• The needle holders are randomly placed on the tray
• The needle holders lay down ﬂat and do not stand up straight
• The location of the rack is ﬁxed and known
• The location of the needle is ﬁxed in place
• The speed of the robotic arm is not of importance

2 Related work & State Of The Art
2
Related work & State Of The Art
Automation in most companies including Novo Nordisk is still mainly ﬁxed automation, where
a sequence of assembly or processing steps is executed by ﬁxed equipment with simple linear or
rotational operations. These systems are not ﬂexible but very eﬃcient at the completion of the
task they were designed for.
According to [14] an assembly task should fulﬁll as many of the following requirements as possible
for automation to make sense:
1. The product should have a high demand to reduce the development cost per unit.
2. The product design should be stable as later changes of a ﬁxed automated assembly cell
can be very expensive.
3. The number of components should be limited.
4. The product should be designed for assembly.
If none of these apply, the process would remain to be part of a manual single station cell, where
a worker uses tools or machines to assemble the product.
Since the increased availability of robotic arms which can be programmed to automate tasks
with no-code/low-code solutions, like the ones from Universal Robots (UR), the requirements
for automation have become substantially lower. And with the operators and the working envi-
ronment moving into focus, automation is not only a tool to reduce production cost and ensure
quality, but also to replace ergonomically hard and repetitive work [15].
2.1
Pick and place for assembly
Pick and place operations are automated widely in the industry. A robot picks up a part and
places it somewhere else. [26].
In the assembly of printed circuit boards (PCB), feeders assure the exact positioning of boards
and parts while robots, mainly Cartesian robots, apply a slight vacuum to the components via
their end eﬀector and place the them on the board. The robot can be programmed to assemble a
large variety of products, but all steps are predeﬁned and their is no learning process in the form
of machine learning. Also there is no visual feedback of the assembled components. Developing
feeding mechanisms is component speciﬁc and therefore extensive and expensive [16].
Figure 2: Pick an place task for PCB assembly [18]
Automated Bin picking has a diﬀerent approach to assembly tasks. Instead of feeding components
one by one, they are supplied in a box where they lay on top of each other in random orientation.

2.2
Object detection for pick and place tasks
An AI needs to control a 6-Axis robotic arm to grip the items from the bin based on the input
of a stereo camera which supplies 3D information about the environment [19]. Identifying and
picking the right component in the right orientation seems easy for a human, but is a challenging
task for an AI. The smaller the components to pick the better the camera needs to be and the
more work is necessary for development. The biggest emerging use for bin picking is within
logistics, speciﬁcally in large packaging warehouses, where a robot can sort products without
human assistance [20][21].
There is no “one ﬁts all” solution for the development of bin picking solutions yet but much
research is being done in the ﬁeld of object detection and pose estimation.
2.2
Object detection for pick and place tasks
For ﬂexible pick an place tasks, the object to be moved needs to be located. Some of the methods
make use of 2D cameras while others also use depth information to locate an object. This depends
on the size and geometry of the object aswell as the available equipment. Machine learning has
also been used to estimate the shape of an object from 2D images.
2.2.1
2D Object detection
At the time of the release of [14] in 2015 computer vision was far less established than it is
today and object recognition was based on thresholding, feature extraction and edge detection
algorithms, with subsequent classiﬁcation.
In [17] from 2014 electronic components, as resistors and capacitors, are being diﬀerentiated using
classical computer vision as thresholding, canny edge detection, dilation and erosion to remove
the background from the foreground and create blobs of each component.Here the components
used were very small but some space was left between components. The features of each blob
were then fed to an artiﬁcial neural network (ANN) to predict the object. Extracting the blobs
was successful about 83% of the time. When components were too close they merged into one
blob. The classiﬁcation on the other hand had an accuracy of 99.33%.
By using the ﬁrst version of the YOLO (you only look once) object detection in combination with
a convolutional neural network (CNN), [37] was able to locate objects in a cluttered environment
and segment them to obtain a mask. In their future work they would like to use the mask for
pose estimation, so that a robotic arm can grip the objects. This was 2017.
A possible issue with neural networks, is that they require labeled data to train a model. In the
process of developing human–machine collaboration, [38] used 3D CAD models of components
to generate labeled images. They created three datasets of images that were either be purely
synthetic, a mixture of real backgrounds with generated objects or real objects. These were used
to train their object detection model, being YOLOv3 with a Darknet-53 ConvNet backbone,
which was pre-trained on the COCO datset. The precision of the models, on real images was
100% for all objects that had the same size in the dataset as on the work bench. Resized versions
of the same object were identiﬁed less well (recall of 59.2-81.4%).
2.2.2
6D pose estimation
To grip an object, not only its location but also its 6D pose is important. There have been
diﬀerent methods used with RGB and RGB-D images.
For a pick and place task the success depends on many steps in the process. In [39] a failure
handling system for pick an place tasks was developed with a combination of 2D vision and force
feedback from the gripper. Here an RGB camera captures a scene, regions of interest (ROIs) are

suggested and an Augmented Autoencoder returns a descriptor of the 3D orientation of the object
[40]. Finally a CNN uses this descriptor and compares it to 2D renders of diﬀerent rotations
of the object to estimate its rotation. The outcome of the Autoencoder has an Intersection of
Union (IoU) of 80%. Interesting here is, especially that the pose estimation of the object is
further reﬁned by the force input of the gripper.
In a research project for automation in the food industry from 2018 [22] use an RGB-D image
of Japanese food and segment it with a Mask R-CNN. Afterwards a 3D pointcloud of the scene
is created from the depth data. A combination of the mask and depth information enable them
to identify the object in the 3D pointcloud of the scene.
In 2018 a deep learning algorithm called PoseCNN was published which takes RGB-D images
as an input and outputs the 6D pose of the recognized objects in the scene [23]. The data set
to train the network included a depth mask, an instance mask, a label and a meta ﬁle. The
meta ﬁle again included the 2D center of an object, the 3D center of an object the intrinsics of
the camera used to take the image and the 6D poses of the objects in the image. The research
achieved an average accuracy of 93% on non occluded objects and 73% on occluded ones.
While the methods named above are mainly learning based methods, Novo Nordisk themselves
had a research project in 2021, where a non learning approach to 6D pose estimation was inves-
tigated [24]. An insulin pas was located on a tray using point cloud reconstruction in order to
obtain a complete 3D representation of the object. Afterwards the point cloud was reﬁned with
the actual cad model which enabled a robotic arm to grab it successfully.
2.3
Conclusions
Many diﬀerent approaches to the detection of objects with and without depth information from
images are presented above. More classical computer vision algorithms as used in [17] require
little data and computing power to be created but are not robust to changes in the environment.
The other extreme are advanced deep learning frameworks like PoseCNN ([23]) who have promis-
ing results and adequate robustness but the creation of a custom data set is seldom worth the
eﬀort. In [25] the process of creating a solely virtual dataset for PoseCNN is described which is
not realistic for the scope of this project.

3.2
Digital image analysis
Digital image analysis aims to perform operations on an image to enhance or manipulate it in
order to extract more information [27].
3.2.1
Color spaces
By default most images are in the RGB color space, meaning that the displayed colors are a
combination of red, green and blue. For image analysis it can be of beneﬁt to transform the
image into a diﬀerent color space. The color spaces used in this project other than RGB are
grayscale and HSV.
When transforming to gray scale, the three channels from RGB get compressed into one with a
weighted average of about 30% red, 59% green and 11% blue [27].
HSV represents color by the three channels hue, saturation and value. Hue contains information
about the color spectrum (0 to 360), saturation describes the tone of a color and value describes
its intensity. The value channel can be used to ﬁlter out the light for classiﬁcation tasks.
3.2.2
Edge detection
For many image analysis tasks it is of importance to ﬁnd the boundary of an object.
This
boundary enables to distinguish an object from its background or locate certain shapes in an
image. Finding this boundary is called edge detection.
3.2.2.1
Canny Edge detection
At the beginning an image is usually blurred with a Gaussian ﬁlter to reduce noise after which
it is convolved with a Sobel kernel in X- and Y- direction.
13
==PAGE:15==

Once the Sobel kernel has been applied the edges can still be quite thick and not yet binary.
Non-maximum suppression (NMS) checks every pixel for a neighboring maximum in the direction
of the gradient, meaning normal to the edge. If a pixel is not local maximum, it is suppressed
(set to be 0).
At last, the edges with low values are dropped by thresholding and afterwards the connection of
weaker edges to stronger ones is checked with blob analysis. The result is an image with clear
edges [28].
3.2.2.2

into Hough space. A line in image space can be deﬁned by polar coordinates. When plotting r
as a function of θ with all the edge pixels of an image, then the intersects of the sinus waves will
indicate the parameters of a line in the original image. The sinus curves of all pixels are added
on top of each other and the intersections with the highest points represent the line parameters
with most points on a line.

Neural Networks for computer vision
The use of deep learning within computer vision gains popularity every year. Easier access to
knowledge and pretrained models have lowered the barrier of entry to a point where hand crafted
image processing algorithms can be outperformed by trained networks.
Speciﬁcally for this project, a binary classiﬁcation- and an instance segmentation network were

The classiﬁer veriﬁes the correct rotation of an object and an instance segmentation

3.3.1
Convolutional Neural Network for classiﬁcation
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are neural networks which are specialized on data with
grid-like shape. The input data for CNNs in this project are images where the location of a pixel
and its correlation to surrounding pixels contains information.
A CNN for classiﬁcation is expected to to take an image as an input and return probability of
the seen object belonging to one of the wanted classes. Figure 6 shows the basic elements of
a CNN for image classiﬁcation. Those are convolutional layers, pooling layers and ﬁnally fully
connected layers.
14
==PAGE:16==
3.3
Neural Networks for computer vision
Figure 6: Elements of a basic CNN for handwritten digit classiﬁcation [30]
CNNs have convolution in their name because the input is convoluted with a kernel. Assuming
the input to a CNN is an image, this means that the kernel (a matrix of size n × n) moves over
all pixels in the image and outputs a new image, a so called feature map. While moving over the
pixels, the kernel takes the weighted average, where the weights are the entries in the matrix of
the kernel
The kernel can not go over the edge of an image, which causes the output to have less pixels
than the input. It is not always necessary since a reduction in image size can be wanted, but this
can be prevented using so called padding around the image. Here for example a layer of zeros
can be added around the image [30].
After a convolution another ﬁlter called max pooling can be added, where a kernel size is deﬁned
as well as a stride. Max pooling aims to reduce the amount of data by describing an image with
its most extreme points. The kernel then moves by the amount of pixels deﬁned as the stride
and the output is the max value of the pixels in the convolution. Other ﬁlters are mean or min
pooling.
The ﬁnal layer for classiﬁcation needs to return a vector of size c with one entry for each class, so
the grid structure of the convolutional layers is ﬂattened out into a 1 dimensional vector. Fully
connected layers can then map the ﬂattened output from the convolutional layers to a vector of
size c.
After each convolutional layer an activation function is applied to the output to enable the model
to map non linearities. One of the most common activation functions is the Rectiﬁed Linear Unit
(ReLU) function which simply sets all negative inputs to 0 as in equation 7.
ReLU(X) = max(0, X)
(7)
For classiﬁcation a softmax function at the output of the CNN will convert the c real numbers
into a probability, meaning that the probability of the classiﬁcation being right can be estimated.
Often the log softmax is used because it improves numerical performance in gradient optimization

3.3.2
Important concepts for instance segmentation
In contrast to the CNN described in section 3.3.1, a segmentation network classiﬁes each pixel
in an image by the object it is a part of.
Within image segmentation there are essentially
two types of segmentation, semantic segmentation and instance segmentation. While both can
distinguish and locate diﬀerent groups of objects on a pixel basis, semantic segmentation does
not diﬀerentiate each instance of the same object. If there were for example two humans closely
side by side in an image, they would be diﬀerentiated from the rest of the image but not from
each other. An instance segmentation network would also have diﬀerentiated between the two.
For this project each needle holder, no matter how close they lay together must be diﬀerentiated.
Therefore instance segmentation is the preferred method for the task at hand.
Since its release in 2017 the Mask Region-based Convolution Neural Network (Mask R-CNN) is
with 150 Github implementations the most used framework for instance segmentation [8]. Among
other reasons detailed in section 5.4.1, its performance and the detailed available information,
the Mask-RCNN was chosen as the preferred framework.

Mask R-CNN is an extension of Faster R-CNN, a previously existing object detection framework.
In the following sections the underlying components of Mask-RCNN will be explained. Before
getting to Mask R-CNN it is important to mention some fundamental concepts of object detection
and object segmentation, which are explained in this chapter.
3.3.2.1
Single-stage and Two-stage Deep Object Detectors
Two main types of neural networks for object detection and segmentation can be distinguished.
The ﬁrst are Single-stage object detectors, like YOLO (You Only Look Once) and SSD (Singe
Shot MultiBox Detector), where one network takes an image as input and outputs bounding boxes
and classes for the objects. Two-stage object detection models like Faster R-CNN and Mask R-
CNN have one network outputting regions of interest based on their likelihood to be foreground
rather than background. In the second stage, another network performs the classiﬁcation on
the returned regions of interest. The trade-oﬀbetween the two is mainly speed for Single-stage
against precision for Two-stage models [47]. For this project the focus is on Two-stage object
segmentation out of the precision aspect.
3.3.2.2
Region of interest
A region of interest (RoI) or proposal of a bounding box, describes an area in an image where
an object might be located. The location of a bounding box is described by a 4 sized vector
containing the location of the upper left corner of the ROI as well as the boxes width and height
(x, y, w, h). RoI and bounding box are diﬀerent in that way, that the bounding box has been
ﬁltered by a classiﬁer while many RoIs are discarded throughout the pipeline. The RoIs are the
output of a Regional Proposal Network which will be explained in section 3.3.3.
3.3.2.3
Intersection of Union
For the evaluation of bounding boxes, ROIs and binary masks the Intersection of Union (IoU) is
an important metric to indicate the overlap. It is deﬁned as follows [31]:
IoU = Area of Intersection of two boxes

Non Maximum Suppression
An Regional Proposal Network will propose many thousand RoIs and a conﬁdence score. These
RoIs need to be ﬁltered again with non Maximum Suppression (NMS) because many of them
will overlap. The proposal with the highest conﬁdence score is compared to all other proposals
and those with a too high IoU are removed from the proposals. Then the proposal is saved and
the next proposal with the highest conﬁdence score is compared to the rest. This is continued
until the end. A widely used IoU threshold is 0.5.

3.3.3.1
Regional Proposal Network
A Regional Proposal Network (RPN) is a fully convolutional network that can predict object
bounds and their scores at every position in an image. Because it uses so called “anchor boxes”
of diﬀerent scales and aspect ratios, the RPN is able to eﬃciently output region proposals at
diﬀerent sizes. The RPNs classiﬁer distinguishes between fore and background as a preliminary
ﬁlter for later classiﬁcation.
Anchor boxes
At each location in an image k diﬀerently sized anchor boxes are classiﬁed simultaneously. The
standard RPN uses 9 anchors with 3 diﬀerent sizes for each of the three aspect ratios 1:1, 1:2 and
2:1. For each point of the sliding window the RPN returns an output of 4k for box proposals (x,
y, w, h) and 2k for the probability of the proposal containing an object or background. Figure
11 gives an example of a sliding window. A certain robustness to size variance is established by
the anchor boxes sliding over diﬀerently sized feature maps of the feature extraction network.

Geometric Transformations
Geometric transformations transfer the location of a pixel in the image to a new location. Hereby
the information of each pixel and its relationship to neighboring pixels is preserved, so that
geometric features of objects stay in place. In the case of instance segmentation it is important
that the same geometric transformation is applied to the image and the mask and that the
bounding boxes are adjusted too.
The simplest form of geometric transformation is the ﬂipping of images horizontally and vertically,
which is computationally eﬃcient since the order of row or column vectors are simply reversed.
Another form of geometric transformation is the rotation of all pixels around the center of the
image, thereby creating new invariant samples. Every pixel with location (x, y) is moved to
position (x′, y′) as in equation 13 [9].

3.4.2
Photometric Transformations
In contrast to geometric transformations, photometric transformations do not alter the geometry
of a sample but each pixel value is adjusted by a function [10]. When adjusting brightness or
contrast the pixel value is subjected to point processing. Generally the equation looks like in
equation 14 [12], where b adjusts brightness and a contrast resulting in a new pixel value g(x, y).
g(x, y) = a · f(x, y) + b

But in the torchvision implementation, brightness and contrast are implemented as in equation
15. A pixel value f(x, y) is multiplied by a proportion of its own value to change the brightness
proportionally by 1 −b. Then another absolute factor a can be applied to change the contrast.
g(x, y) = a · f(x, y) · (1 −b)
(15)
To adjust saturation and hue, an image ﬁrst needs to be converted to the HSI color space and
then the same proportional adjustment as in equation 15 is applied but only to speciﬁc channels
before converting the image back to RGB.
When randomly applying all four operations as random data transformations, the process is
called ColorJitter [11].

Behavior Trees
Behaviour trees (BTs) have been used in the video game industry for years. They enable the
execution of diﬀerent tasks and create structure with a control architecture that is both modular
and reactive. Modeling the tasks of a robotic arm in servo control mode using BTs simpliﬁes
adding additional checks and objectives.

A BT describes a sequence of tasks as in ﬁgure 15. The [Ø] on top is the root node, which
generates a signal at a deﬁned frequency that starts to execute the tasks below. One task is
started when the previous one returned success or failure. For behaviour trees the signal is called
a tick but it can also be the iteration of a while/for loop. Below the [Ø] is the control ﬂow node
indicating how ticks sent down to the leaves [43].
The control ﬂow nodes used in this project are Fallback/Selector [ ? ] and Sequence [ →* ].
Sequence executes all sub tasks from left to right moving to the next one when the previous one
has ﬁnished. It succeeds when all tasks succeed. Fallback/Selector also executes sub tasks from
left to right but succeeds when any one sub tasks succeeds. Then the branch to the right of the
Fallback/Selector control ﬂow node is executed.

5.2
Robot Path planning
For the execution the needle assembly the motions of the robot had to be precise and without
error. In an early stage of the project, a 3D model of the UR3e with the camera holder and the
gripper was developed to work with ROS, Rviz and MoveIt, with the aim to enable collision free
movements. But the path planning through MoveIt framework was not always collision free. The
camera holder was once snapped oﬀby a link of the robotic arm, meaning that a new camera
mount had to be printed and recalibrations had to be done. Another limitation of movements
was the cable leading from the PC to the camera, limiting the rotation of the wrist joint. Due
to the risk of false movements and Novo Nordisk not using ROS, the movements of the gripper
were deﬁned manually as linear movements from one position to another with intermediate way
points.
When linearly rotating the gripper, it took the shortest path, so a rotation of 180 °and then
reverting to the previous position would result in a 360 °rotation since the ﬁrst rotation slightly
exceeded 180 °. Therefore some intermediate positions were deﬁned in joint coordinates to assure
that the cable to the camera is not under tension.

5.4.1
Instance segmentation of NHs
In order to locate the NHs on a tray using computer vision, they need to be be reliably diﬀerenti-
ated. During initial tests, classical computer vision like thresholding and edge detection did not
prove robust enough given the local environment and its changing lighting. Therefore the next
step was to search within Deep Learning for a viable tool for the task. As mentioned earlier, for
instance segmentation the frameworks available can generally be separated in Single-Stage and
Two-Stage segmentation. Single-Stage segmentation algorithms provide faster inference while
Two-Stage frameworks provide higher localization and recognition accuracy according to [36].
Since accuracy is valued higher than inference speed for this project a Two-Stage framework
was preferred. Speciﬁcally the Mask R-CNN developed in 2017 had proven to be a widely used
instance segmentation deep learning algorithm and will be applied to the problem at hand.
In a ﬁrst step the Mask R-CNN (section 3.3.4) performs instance segmentation on the needle
holders on the tray and return a mask for each one of them. The Mask R-CNN consists of 4
parts, a CNN backbone as feature extractor, a regional proposal network, an RoI classiﬁer and
a segmentation network.
To train all these networks from scratch several thousand images would need be taken and
labeled, so resnet50, a feature extraction network pretrained on the COCO data set [41] presents
a great foundation. Even though there are no needle holders in COCO, the around 1.5 million
object instances in the data set have enabled the CNN to learn many common features in images.
PyTorch oﬀers a great pretrained implementation of Mask R-CNN with a resnet50 backbone [42].
This implementation was modiﬁed to diﬀerentiate between two classes, the NH and background.
Then the model could be trained on a custom dataset.
Three datasets were created for the training of the instance segmentation network.
Table 4
describes them with their properties and examples are shown in ﬁgure 27. The labeling process

5.4.5
Separation of close NHs
In section 5.4.3 the approach to verify the availability of space around a NH was explained. In
the future a further developed version of this system might have a vibrating table to realign the
components but for this proof of concept a method with the tools at hand was requested by the
company.
If no NH has enough space around the head to be gripped, the gripper will attempt to create
space by spreading them further out. Assuming that the NH are more in the center of the tray,
the NH which is furthest away from the center will be the target of the realignment. If there
is at least 16.5mm between the gripping locations of two needle holders, the gripper will push
down in the middle of the two NH with a set force of 2N. 16.5mm was found to be enough for
the closed gripper to push two needle holder apart.It then continues to move above the tray to
take another image and verify the outcome of the operation.
If there is not enough space to push between the two NH, the gripper will instead sweep from
the closest corner of the tray to the gripping position of the NH.

5.5
Insertion of NH into regrasping station
Now that the pose of a needle holder is known, the gripper can move over it and grip it so that
TCP and needle holder frame align.
After the needle holder is grasped by the gripper it needs to be inserted into the regrasping
station so its rotation can be determined. Depending on the quality of the mask and where the
gripper holds the NH, the NHs position in the gripper can vary. For example when the screw
which sticks out from the NH is on the side where the gripper ﬁngers pinch together the NH,
there can be some uncertainty of the exact angle of the needle holder in the gripper.
A method had to be created to compensate for these positional deviations. The approach to ﬁnd
the hole is a spiral search feedback loop. Here the robot arm moves the NH above the hard-coded
position of the insertion hole in the regrasping station and lowers it until there is a resistance.
It then moves in a spiral on a plane deﬁned by the X and Y axis of the robotbase frame while
maintaining a constant force in negative Z direction of the same frame.
Once the NH is above the hole it will be pushed in. The spiraling movement is interrupted by
one of 2 conditions. Either an insertion of 3mm or more is detected or the forces in X and Y
direction of the robot base frame exceed 12 Newton.
Due to the diameter of the hole, 3mm of insertion is suﬃcient. When the NH is released then
it will fall into place. The force threshold was chosen to be so high because of potential friction
on the surface of the regrasping station and it can withstand the force. If the NH is lowered
directly into the hole, a further insertion of 3mm might not be possible. But then the spiraling
movement will stop shortly after due to the force feedback. All force feedback was relative to
the robot base frame.
The spiraling motion of the robot is executed 500 times per second in small increments, where
at each iteration the position and forces are monitored. Figure 31 shows the spiral search as a
behaviour tree which ﬁrst exits when all “children” have succeeded. In contrast to the behaviour
trees described in [43], this one is a simpliﬁcation without a process returning the running status
when active, but instead a task is executed until completion before it moves on. If any child
returns “False” the loop begins with the ﬁrst child on the left.

From above, the rotation of the needle holder can be estimated from the slit that enables the

5.6.1
Detection of the rotation
At ﬁrst the camera is moved above the regrasping station to capture an image of the NH.
The needle holder needs to be located in the image. At the start of the project Circle Hough
Transform was used to ﬁnd it, with the intention to make the algorithm more versatile in case of
the camera being moved to a diﬀerent position. But then the parameters needed to be ﬁnetuned
anyways. Since the location of the camera above the NH is ﬁxed, the coordinates and size of the
circle was saved and applied to the image every time.
To remove unnecessary pixels before thresholding the image is masked with the circle, so that all
pixels around it become 0. Then the image is converted into gray scale, so that the NH and the
slit can be separated using binary thresholding. Here the boundary for fore- and background is
mean(imggray[imggray > 0]) · 0.9, meaning the threshold is at 90% of the mean of all non zero
pixels. The outcome is in image 36.
Figure 33: Left to right: gray scale, masked with circle, binary threshold, detected slit on original image

with atan2(). The returned angle (anglecv) is the average angle of all lines.The lines projected
on the original image are in ﬁgure 36. Depending on the sign of the returned angle, either 90°
need to be added or subtracted to make the slit parallel to the robot frames X-axis. Equation
25 describes the two cases.

5.6.2
Veriﬁcation of the orientation
Once the slit of the needle holder has the right alignment, it is not guaranteed that it does not
need an extra rotation of 180° for the screw to face towards the center of the work space.
To check this the side of the needle holder needs to be assessed so the camera is moved to face it.
Since it can be assumed that the alignment of the NH’s slit was performed correctly, the error
can only be by 180° or 0°. For the identiﬁcation a binary classiﬁer was trained to evaluate which
case it is.
The data set to train the model was made up of 238 images, that were taken in diﬀerent lighting
circumstances with the approach to crop out the needle holder (85 by 60 pixels) to train a model.
Because of the diameter of the hole in the regrasping station, there was some room for the NH
to move around. So cropping out the image at the same pixel position could result in a diﬀerent
placement of the NH in the image.
To improve this problem the location of the cropped out window was moved around by -6,-3,0,3,6
pixels in X and Y direction while maintaining the same window size. This also increased the size
of the training set to 5950 images who were split into train and test set before the augmentation
was applied. To reduce the variation of lighting in the images the crop outs are reduced to the
value channel of HSV before training.

A few machine learning algorithms like K-Nearest Neighbors and Extreme Gradient Boost models

To avoid forces that can damage the equipment, the needle holder has to be prevented from
getting stuck in the slit. When applying a downwards force on the head of the needle holder
it is not possible to fully avoid that. A way to reduce the amount of force which the needle is
subjected to is to reduce the distance that the gripper moves away from the needle while it it
stuck in the gap. Or more concrete, make the point of the needle which touches the NH, hop
over the slit when the gripper moves to the other side of the gap. So every 1
4 of a second, the
force mode pushing down is interrupted and instead the gripper is moved up by 0.3mm, which
is enough to skip over the slit. Right afterwards, the force mode is resumed and the spiraling
continues.

movement of the arm is executed. If 1
4 of a second has passed, the hopping is activated before
the normal execution continues.

Once the spiral search has been successful, the needle is inserted 4mm into the needle holder. It
now needs to be pushed in all the way so that it touches the bottom of the rack which assures
that a needle has the right length to ﬁll cartridges during the ﬁlling process. The needles may
not be damaged or bent during the process.
The ﬁrst approach was to simply grab the needle higher up the cylinder and push down with a
force that does not exceed a certain limit. But the rack prototype is not as level as the surface
of the work space, so the needle would get stuck. By increasing the force the needle would bent
or break.
Instead of grabbing the needle when pressing down the closed gripper moves down from above
the needle. This still causes the needle to bend a certain amount so every half a second the
gripper moves up 0.5mm, which allows the needle to bend back without permanent deformation.
A maximum force threshold indicates the complete insertion of the needle.

6.1
Instance detection of NHs with Mask R-CNN
The Mask R-CNN trained to detect NHs on a tray, described in section 5.4.1, showed to perform
well on the images shown in the design section. In the following its training and testing process
will be visualized and quantiﬁed.
6.1.1
Training and testing procedure
The Mask R-CNN was trained on diﬀerent data sets at diﬀerent stages. Data sets in this chapter
are named as in 4. At ﬁrst a baseline model was created which was trained for 2 epochs on
Dataset 1. From the baseline model two models were trained. One with non augmented data
and another one with augmented data to evaluate the diﬀerence in performance. They can be
referred to as augmented model and non-augmented model in the following. At ﬁrst both models
were trained on Dataset 1 for an additional 50 epochs, followed by 50 epochs on Dataset 2 and
ﬁnally 50 epochs on Dataset 3. Before the training with each data set, the data was divided into
training and validation set, 80% to 20% respectively. Every 3rd epoch the performance on the
validation set was tested and every 4th epoch a sample training image was recorded.
To see how the performance of the model behaves as the number of needle holders increases, with
little to no distance between them, an extra labeled test set was created. The test set consists
of 5 images with respectively 1,2,4,8,16 needle holder per image. Here it is assumed that the
labeling has been done correctly.
Between data sets the model was evaluated on the test set to monitor the improvement. The
model trained with augmented data is also the ﬁnal model used, so while comparing the two
models, the model in use is evaluated as well.
During training the metric was the loss, described in section 3.3.4. To determine the performance
on the test set, additionally to the loss, average IoU per detected NH and the combined mask of
all NHs for ground truth and prediction (logical AND) was evaluated.

7 Discussion
7
Discussion
7.1
Instance detection of NHs
During the training process of the two models, the one trained on non augmented data seemed
to perform better based on the training and validation loss. That was to be expected since both
models have the same architecture but the augmented data is more diverse. At the evaluation
on the test set, the model being trained on augmented data outperformed the other one though.
To determine 5 out of 6 degrees of freedom, the mask of each NH must represent their shape to
a high degree or else the algorithm from section 5.4.2 will have trouble distinguishing the head
from the neck. Even though there is no real threshold, an indicator for the quality of the mask
is the IoU. Masks of NHs with an IoU of above 0.9 represent their shape well. The closer the
IoU is to one, the better the score. Even in the cluttered environment of image 42 where 16 NHs
were close to each other, the IoU was 0.92 per needle holder.
The ground truth is drawn by hand and can also have errors that play a role in the calculation
of the IoU.
With the separation algorithm creating space around NHs on the outside of a collection of NHs,
the correct identiﬁcation of a NH is highly probable.
7.2
Insertion of NH into regrasping station
In the test, 26 NHs were placed in varying locations, orientations and with diﬀerent spacing to
each other. The camera identiﬁed the objects and the grasped them to insert them into the re
grasping station.
25 out of 26 were successfully grasped and inserted, resulting in a success rate of 96.15%. The
insertion process of the NH which was not inserted already started to deviate from the others at
the grasping. There another NH was in the way which resulted in a rotation of the NH that was
supposed to be grasped.
Potential reasons for the failure could be the segmentation of the mask, the dimensions of the
box checking the space around a NH, the eye in hand calibration or a combination of the three.
The segmentation of the mask seemed correct from the sample saved. And the NHs to the left
and to the right were picked correctly, therefore a problem with the camera calibration is unlikely.
So the algorithm for the checking of space needs to be improved to reach a 100% success rate.
7.3
Rotation of NHs
To determine the angle which a NH needs to be rotated by in order for its screw to face the right
way, a measurement needs to be both accurate and precise.
In terms of precision, the algorithm performs well with a standard deviation of 0 to 0.361°over
200 measurements. Boxplot 4 in 47 shows a standard deviation of 48.6°which is due to the fact
that an angle of 0°can also be interpreted as an angle of ± 180°. Assuming a normal distribution,
a maximum error of ± 3 standard deviations can be expected in 99.7% of cases, meaning ±
1.083°.
Determining the exact angle of a NH’s rotation was diﬃcult as the available calibration method
was rather primitive. The deviations of the angle measurements ranged from -2.45 to 0.96°,
which could also be an error of the set up.
A laser measurement of the angle would have

Just as with all other steps in the assembly, without the insertion of the NH into the rack, the

If the location of the hole is known with an uncertainty of 3mm, the probability of a successful
insertion is 91.6% (11 out of 12) and below 2.5mm it is 100% according to the small sample
size oﬀthe experiment. Changes in the size of the spiral search as well as the number of points
and how many circles would likely improve the precision. More circles could have enabled the
insertion of point 25 in ﬁgure 53.
7.5
Insertion of needle into NH
Inserting the needle into the needle holder is a precise task. The needle has a diameter of 2.2mm
needs to ﬁt into the NH whose insertion hole has a diameter of 2.5mm or less depending on the
setting of the screw. Optimally they are all the way open, but deformations of PEEK plastic
can occur over time.
In the experiment 40 insertions were performed with distances from the hole between 0 to 3mm.
The size of the spiral was set to 0-2.5mm and there were 0.48mm between the circles inside the
spiral. Out of all insertions 26 out of 40 succeeded which alone results in a success rate of 65%.
Up to a distance of 1.9mm from the hole 18 out of 18 succeeded.
Considering that the spiral for spiral search was only designed to cover distances up to 2.5mm,
1.9mm means that 76% of the distance was covered.
The exact location of the insertion hole was not deﬁned prior to the experiment. This could
have given further insights of why a hole was missed. Main reasons for the failure of insertion
are the parameters of the spiral, as number of points or circles in the spiral. Other reasons for
missing could be the 0.48mm distance between circles of the spiral or the 0.1mm gaps when the
gripper was hopping. The necessary distance to the hole to perform an insertion is unknown but
it seemed to vary between NH types.
The force feedback visualized in graph 56 was oﬀconsidering that a force of 0 is to be expected
in X- and Y-direction when no force is applied.
The graph also shows that about 25 more
measurements were made after the force started increasing in negative X-, Y-direction. A more
accurate force feedback could enable an earlier stoppage to not damage any needles.

8 Conclusion
8
Conclusion
The goal of this proof of concept was the detection and location of the components necessary
to assemble needle holders and needles. From the many possible solutions, a combination of
classical computer vision algorithms and deep learning was used to obtain the pose of the needle
holder. Hereby the RealSense D435i camera was attached to a UR3e cobot arm with the RG2
gripper from OnRobot.
As a starting point the needle holders were laying on a tray and the needles were in a ﬁxed
position on a 0.8m by 0.8m work space.
The assembly according to the requirements, was
made up of the tray picking, rack insertion and needle insertion. For the system to fulﬁll the
requirements the three tasks were divided into six tasks for the automatic assembly.
Tray picking:
At ﬁrst the arm would move the camera over the tray where needle holders are located. From
an image taken by the camera a Mask R-CNN would segment the NHs in the image after which
a computer vision algorithm would calculate the pose for 5 out of 6 degrees of freedom. This is
enough for the gripper to grasp the NH. The trained Mask R-CNN is the enabler for the pose
calculation. It is able to create masks of the needle holders on the tray with an IoU of 0.9 and
without missing an object.
The second task was the grasping of needle holders and their insertion into a so called regrasping
station where the last degree of freedom was to be calculated. The insertion is performed with
a spiral search and has a success rate of 96.15%.
At third the rotation of the needle holder in the rack was determined using edge detection.
The standard deviation when repeating the same measurement was about 0.3°and measurements
errors between -2.45 and 0.96°were observed with a mean error of 0.78%. It was then rotated to
be aligned in the right way.
Since the rotation of a NH is not unique when looking from above the fourth task was to validate
the rotation from the side. The CNN classiﬁer had an accuracy of about 100%.
Rack insertion:
Task number ﬁve was the insertion of the NH into the ﬁnal rack where the needle could be added.
The holes in this rack were smaller than in the regrasping station but the method was the same.
The success rate of insertion varied from 84.2% at an oﬀset of up to 5mm from the location of
insertion to 91.6% if the oﬀset is only 3mm.
Needle insertion:
At last the needle was to be inserted into the needle holder. A success rate of 65% was achieved
for the insertion with an uncertainty of up to 3mm of the location of the hole. For the insertions
with an oﬀset of below 1.9mm, the success rate was 100%.
Combined probability of success:
With the Mask R-CNN performing well enough to segment the NHs, the system has a minimum
probability to insert the NHs and needles into the rack of 52.5%. With lower distances to the
holes for the various insertion tasks the maximum probability for insertion lays at 88%.
The assurance that all needle holders face the same way can be assured, but the exact angle has
a relative error margin of ± 0.78%.

8.1
Improvements and future work
8.1
Improvements and future work
Tray picking:
To reduce potential collisions of the gripper with other needle holders, a new pair of gripper
ﬁngers could be designed who are pointier with rubber coating. These could then push away
other NHs while still maintaining a good grip.
Also a robotic arm with larger range like the UR5 would be beneﬁcial to ease the approach of
areas in the work space.
Rack insertion:
An algorithm needs to be developed to locate the rack, as it wont be static in a production
environment. The results from the analysis of insertions can be used as requirements for precision.
Needle insertion:
An attachment for the robotic arm with more reliable force feedback would increase the perfor-
mance of all insertions with spiral search.
