Abstract 
To model the hydrocarbon flow from the reservoir to the pipelines and eventually to the sales 
point, the problem is split at the well perforations level. This way, reservoir engineers model the 
reservoir flow independently, by assuming that the wells’ design will be to enable the produced 
quantities to arrive at the sales point. 
Well design involves the selection of the completion system and possibly some artificial lift 
method. The design should be done so that the well deliverability meets the reservoir 
deliverability, as obtained from the reservoir model, at any time during the production life of 
the reservoir. Therefore, possible installation of an artificial lift system, which might be operated 
at the later stages of the reservoir, should be taken into account in advance. 
Hydrocarbons will normally flow to surface when production begins in a virgin reservoir. As time 
passes and production continuous, it will eventually lead to a reduction of the reservoir pressure 
and an increase of the fraction of water being produced. These factors will reduce the flow of 
fluids. The remedy for this is to include some form of artificial lift, which adds the sufficient 
energy to the fluid, allowing the well to flow at an efficient rate. 
One of the most used artificial lift methods is that of gas lift. Gas lift increases oil production rate 
by injecting compressed gas into the lower section of the tubing. The operation involves the 
installation of gas lift valves on the production tubing, from which the compressed gas enters 
from surface. As soon as the valve opens, the gas enters the column and mix with the presently 
produced liquid. The main aim of the method is to reduce the density of the fluid, and as a result 
the weight of the liquid column, to achieve lifting the produced reservoir fluids to surface. 
This Master thesis, involves thirteen offshore wells in the Alpha field. Due to the reservoir 
pressure decline and increasing water cut due to injection wells, the field has stop producing, 
meaning that most of the wells are ‘’dead’’ and only three of them barely produce. The main 
objective is to design the installation of a gas lift system, which will assist production for a long 
period of time, taking into account the production and pressure forecast provided by reservoir 
engineers. 
The main concern of the project is to design the completion of each well and its artificial lift 
system in an optimal manner to minimize or even eliminate workovers. Additionally, there will 
be the need to identify the time intervals where the artificial system, per well, should be 
operated, as well as the time at which the production should be abandoned and also to identify 
the optimal gas injection rates in order to achieve an economically viable production. 
A continuous gas lift system design was created using PROSPER, based on the worst-case 
scenario, e.g. the case where the pressure difference between the reservoir and the bottom 
hole has the smaller value. After the determination of the worst-case scenario, the gas lift 
design was made and the gas lift valve positions and gas injection rates were determined.

Finally, the production system needs to be modeled as a whole (reservoir model, well model 
and pipelining to the manifolds and eventually to the sales point), using the General Allocation 
Program (GAP) so as to ensure that it will meet the reservoir model production predictions 
under full system pressure equilibration while further taking into account the injection gas 
availability. GAP run the calculations for the whole oilfield proving that under a full system 
pressure equilibration and under a specific separator pressure, the field is able to produce the 
rates calculated in PROSPER with success.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Importance of oil & gas 
The huge economic contribution of the oil and gas industry to the economy makes its future of 
critical importance to the communities worldwide. Since the Industrial Revolution, oil and gas 
have played a very important role in the economic revolution, for the majority of the world’s 
population. Today, they vitally contribute to the current global energy system. Oil-based fuels 
meet approximately 31% of the primary energy used globally while natural gas represents 21% 
of the total energy supply [1].  
However, oil is a limited source of energy. Proven reserves have shown that the available 
crude oil can sustain the energy demands of the population for many years to come. As 
OPEC reports it, the total reserves of the world are 1.3 trillion barrels of oil and it is 
approximated that 1 trillion barrels are already consumed. However, the oil demand of the 
whole world is growing as the populations needs for energy increase and oil is produced in 
bigger quantities now more than ever. Thus, the continuous discoveries of new fields, along 
with the optimization of the existing ones, has become more crucial than ever [2]. 
 
1.2 Brief Introduction to petroleum engineering 
1.2.1 Generation of hydrocarbons 
Oil and gas are of organic origin. All of the hydrocarbon deposits in sedimentary basins contain 
organic residuals of terrestrial, lake, river and marine species, the conversion of whose, under 
anaerobic conditions, leads to the formation of kerogen. Such rocks are classified into ‘’mother 
rocks’’ depending on their content of organic material. The term kerogen refers to the insoluble 
organic material of sedimentary rocks in organic solvents, water and oxygenaceous acids. It is 
formed at the stage of organic matter transfer into the geological formations but is maintained 
partly in the later stages of its transformation up to metamorphosis [3]. 
A precondition for oil formation is the precipitation and accumulation of organic material, e.g. in 
the seabed or lake, in the absence of oxygen, so that it can decompose to produce kerogen. 
Ideal conditions for this process are found in sapropels where organic matter decomposes with 
the help of anaerobic bacteria. Even at depths of up to 1000 m and temperatures of up to 50 °C, 
the organic material, incorporated in the sediment, is converted into kerogen. This diagenetic 
process is intensified by further coverage and precipitation, and by the increasing temperature 
due to the heat of the interior of the Earth. This process, known as catagenesis, takes place at 
depths of 1000 - 5000 m and temperatures of about 175 °C (Fig. 1.1). During this catagenetic 
process, the organic material is dewatered to form methane, hydrogen and liquid hydrocarbons 
with the elevated temperature. Oxygen and hydrogen escape more easily from

the ‘’mother’’ rock compared to carbon and nitrogen, thus increasing the relative ratio to 
carbon [4]. 
 
Figure 1.1: Creation of oil and gas as a function of the matrix (‘’mother’’ rock) maturation. [5] 
 
The migration of oil from the ‘’mother’’ rock is not yet fully understood. Since oil formation is 
accompanied by changes in the pressure of the rock, it is likely that micro-cracks will occur, 
which provide a passageway from the ‘’mother’’ rock to adjacent permeable systems, such as 
sedimentary rocks. It is considered that only 10% of the oil generated is discharged and stored in 
reservoirs [6]. 
 
1.2.2 Oil and gas reservoirs 
A reservoir could be defined as an accumulation of economically exploitable amounts of 
hydrocarbons in a porous sedimentary rock. The necessary conditions (Fig. 1.2) for a 
commercially viable concentration of liquid or gaseous hydrocarbons in a reservoir are [7]: 
• 
A mature ‘’mother’’ rock 
• 
A reservoir rock (permeable rock) 
• 
A migration channel between the ‘’mother’’ rock and the reservoir 
• 
An impermeable cap rock on top of the reservoir and  
• 
The ‘’mother’’ rock, the reservoir and the cap rock need to create the conditions for 
trapping the hydrocarbons 
 
==PAGE:21==
3 
 
 
Figure 1.2: Conditions of a hydrocarbon reservoir. [8] 
 
The vast majority of reservoirs are sandstones and limestones. These rock formations consist of 
a solid structure (matrix), as well as a porous space formed by the intervals of neighboring grains 
as well as by fissures and fractures in the rock. The porous space, containing liquid and gaseous 
hydrocarbons, is usually covered by water, often high in salt content (brine), which is usually a 
part of the water table of the formation [7]. 
 
1.2.3 Hydrocarbon exploration 
The chances of having an oil or gas reservoir have to be confirmed with exploration wells. The 
cost, which increase exponentially with depth, extreme environmental conditions and rising 
equipment and staff cost, require extensive scientific research before drilling. However, in spite 
of all the necessary preparation, the probability of drilling a well that will not yield economically 
exploitable hydrocarbons remains high. It is therefore necessary to study the geological history 
of the area, to take into account any conditions of formation of oil in a chronological and 
geographic context and to check whether the rocks that are under consideration are capable of 
trapping hydrocarbons. The method of seismic reflection is the main scientific technic of todays’ 
exploration activities and is in constant use and development [9]. 
 
1.2.4 Drilling for oil and gas 
The purpose of exploration drilling is to drill a well in order to find commercially exploitable oil 
or gas accumulations, safely and with proper management of the available funds. To achieve 
this goal, the cooperation of many groups and specialized companies, contractors, consultants, 
etc. is required. The wells drilled for locating and exploring the possible reserves are known as 
exploratory wells, while the wells used for production are known as production wells [9]. 
Exploration wells must be drilled in a certain way, in order to yield accurate information about 
the hydrocarbon bearing formation. This is accomplished by sampling, collecting data from a 
==PAGE:22==
4 
 
large number of geophysical measurements, conducting productivity tests and observing the 
behavior of the field. Production wells must be completed in such a way that the total cost does 
not exceed the available funds and in order to make an optimum use of production capacity [9]. 
 
Drilling the well 
In the rotary drilling method, a sharp drill bit and drill collars are employed in a fixed position at 
the end of the bottom hole assembly, cutting the rock and applying a downward force 
respectively. This bottom hole assembly is fixed on the drill string, composed of high quality 
metal drill pipes to endure the elevated pressures and temperatures. As the drilling process 
continues, new sections of drill pipe are added at the top of the hole in order for the driller to be 
able to reach the desirable depth. When the drill bit has sustained an excessive amount of wear 
and it is time for replacement, the whole drill string must be pulled out and a new drill bit to be 
placed. As the drilling process continues, the cuttings that are produced are lifted from the 
borehole by injecting the drilling mud through the drill pipe and bit nozzles. The drilling fluid is 
then collected at surface and then flows through different tanks and separators to treat the mud 
properly. Once the mud is cleaned of any unwanted residues, the cycle repeats again [10]. The 
different parts of the drilling rig are shown in Fig. 1.3 below.

1.2.5 Production of oil and gas 
Oil production is divided into three stages of recovery: primary, secondary and tertiary. 
• 
Primary recovery: Oil and gas recovery by any of the natural drive mechanisms is called 
primary recovery. This term indicates production of oil and/or gas from a reservoir that 
is drained without the need of any technical process (such as water injection or an 
artificial lift method) to complement the natural energy of the reservoir [12]. 
• 
Secondary recovery: Primary recovery is followed by the secondary one but they can 
also be conducted concurrently. Waterflooding is the most common method. 
Nonetheless, before the operation of any secondary recovery project, it should be 
confirmed that the natural recovery is inadequate, otherwise there is a risk that the 
capital investment required for a secondary recovery project may be wasted [12].  
• 
Tertiary recovery: Tertiary (enhanced) oil recovery is the third stage of hydrocarbon 
recovery that follows the primary and secondary ones. Various methods of this category 
are designed to recover the residual oil left in the reservoir after both primary and 
secondary recovery methods have been exploited to their respective economic limits 
[12]. 
 
1.2.6 Decommissioning  
The decommissioning process is associated with the removal of any mechanical structures from 
onshore and offshore environments. It is crucial for all oil companies to comprehend the full 
range of all removal methods available, as this can significantly reduce the overall cost of the 
process [13]. Offshore decommissioning involves mainly 10 steps: project management, 
extensive decommissioning planning, administrative conformity, platform preparation, well 
plugging and abandonment, conductor removal, platform removal, pipeline and power cable 
decommissioning, material disposition and site clearance [14]. 
 
1.3 Thesis content 
Although in the present oil field, studied in this Master Thesis, hydrocarbons are present in the 
reservoir, it is required to find an optimum way to restart the dead wells in order for them to 
start producing again in a profitable way. In order for this to happen, the available oil left inside 
the reservoir must gain mobility to ‘’climb’’ to surface and eventually into the separator. If this is 
not automatically possible by natural means of production (primary production) then an 
artificial lift system must be designed to assist production. One of the most used methods of 
artificial lift is the gas lift system that increases production rate by injecting compressed gas into 
the production tubing, aerating the liquid so as to reduce its density and the downhole pressure, 
and as a result to achieve a greater pressure differential within the reservoir.

However, in a large oil field with many production wells, it is not easy to design such a system 
because the quantity of the available gas lift gas is limited and because there is the need to

whole exploitation of the field rather than just of a single well. For this specific reason, this 
Master Thesis deals with, apart from the determination of the gas lift valve positioning and 
injection rates, the determination of whether the whole field is able to produce the 
hydrocarbon quantities given by the reservoir model.  
In order to achieve this central design of such a system, a thorough investigation and design of

more thorough analysis of each chapter in this Master Thesis is described in the sub-chapters

1.3.1 Well Deliverability 
The main content of Chapter 2 will consist of the basic theory and aspects of well deliverability 
and nodal analysis. Well deliverability refers to the combination of reservoir and pipeline flow 
performance. The former describes the deliverability of the reservoir while the latter the 
resistance to flow inside the tubing. This chapter will focus on fully describing the main 
characteristics of inflow and outflow performance, along with the method of Nodal Analysis.  
Nodal Analysis is a method that combines the various elements of the production network in 
order to estimate or optimize the production rates. In order to do that, it "breaks" the system 
into various nodes that separate the major system components. Nodal Analysis is based on the 
pressure continuity principle, meaning that there is only one pressure at a given node. The 
performance curve of the upstream equipment is called "Inflow Performance Curve" and the 
performance curve of the downstream equipment is called "Outflow Performance Curve." The 
intersection of these two determines the operating point, e.g. the operating flow rate and 
pressure.  
 
1.3.2 Artificial Lift 
Chapter 3 will introduce the topic of artificial lift. Hydrocarbons will normally flow to surface 
when production begins, in a virgin reservoir. As time passes and production continuous, it will 
eventually lead to a cutback in reservoir pressure and in an increase of the fraction of water 
produced (water cut), reducing the fluid flow from the well. The remedy for this problem is to 
include some form of artificial lift, which adds the sufficient energy to the fluid, allowing the well

to flow at an acceptable rate. In this chapter, a brief description of all common artificial lift 
techniques (rod pumps, electric submersible pumps, progressive cavity pumps and hydraulic 
pumps) will be given, with emphasis on the gas lift, which is the mechanism under investigation 
in the present Master Thesis. 
 
1.3.3 PROSPER 
PROSPER (described in Chapter 4 & 5) is a well performance, design and optimization program

2. Well Deliverability 
2.1 Introduction 
Production engineering consist of two main systems that are closely connected: the reservoir 
and the artificial structures, which include the bottom hole, flow-line and surface components. 
It attempts to maximize production or, in other words, optimize production in a cost-effective 
way. In the oil industry, the combination of flow in the reservoir and in the pipelines determines 
the capacity and the productivity of the well. Reservoir fluids, upon the start of production, after 
they travel within the porous medium, they begin to flow into the well, through the tubing and 
finally into the separator which is placed at surface [15]. 
The deliverability of a well, or a number of wells, refers to a combination of the reservoir (Inflow 
Performance) and the artificial structures’ deliverability to surface (Outflow Performance), as a 
function of pressure. The first describes the reservoir deliverability, e.g. the oil and/or gas 
produced from the porous medium into the wellbore, and the latter defines the capacity of 
fluids to flow from the bottom hole to surface, by taking into account any restriction or flow 
resistance into the tubing. This system analysis is known as ‘’Nodal Analysis’’ [2]. 
 
2.2 Nodal Analysis 
The technique of Nodal Analysis has been in use for many years in order to analyze production 
performance. Gilbert first developed it in 1954 while Mach, Proano and Brown developed it 
further in 1979. This method analyzes complex pipeline networks, electrical circuits and 
production pumping systems and its objective is to combine the different components of the 
production system to estimate productions rates of a single well or to optimize its total 
production [16].  
At any point within the production system, there is a certain pressure and production rate. With 
any notable change in the production network, there will be an associated change in pressure 
and/or production rate. This fact can be used in order to define a node in the production system 
at which it will be divided and investigated. This will define the conditions of pressure and 
production rate at this specific point, e.g. the producing conditions for the system under 
investigation. In Nodal Analysis, the specific point in the system (node) is selected and the 
system is divided into two parts based on this point. All components that are located upstream 
the node define the inflow part of the system, whereas the components downstream the node 
the outflow part. The most usual placements of the node are at the wellhead or at the 
perforations (bottom hole), although the latter is most commonly used in order to divide the 
system into reservoir and tubing performance, which is more easily modelled [16]. 
The pressure and/or production rate of a system can be determined if the following conditions 
are met:

1. Flow into the node must be equal to flow out of the node. 
2. The node pressure must be the same in both inflow and outflow sections of the 
network.

2.3 Inflow Performance Relationship (IPR) 
The Inflow Performance Relationship (IPR) is a mathematical expression that is used in 
production engineering to assess reservoir performance. The data required for the IPR creation 
are obtained by contacting production and pressure test in order to determine the production 
rates under various drawdown pressures. The fluid composition and behavior of its phases 
determine the shape of the curve [19]. 
 
2.3.1 Factors affecting the IPR curve 
The IPR curve is affected by the parameters associated with the reservoir. The most important 
elements affecting the IPR curve are [7]: 
1. Reservoir pressure: The higher the pressure of the reservoir, the greater the flow into 
the well, that is, it depends on the pressure difference between the reservoir and the 
bottom hole pressure of the well. 
2. Rock properties: The properties of the rock that affect the IPR curve are the porosity, 
permeability and thickness of the production zone. 
3. Fluid properties: Fluid properties, mainly viscosity and oil formation volume factor (Bo), 
are important for the formation of the IPR curve. 
4. Well Geometry: The main elements that are included in the geometry of the well and 
are affecting its efficiency are its radius, the size of the production zones, the size and 
density of the borehole, etc.

2.4 Vertical Lift Performance (VLP) 
Chapter 2.3 described reservoir deliverability. However, production rate is determined by the 
wellhead pressure (or separator pressure) and by the flow performance of the tubing. The latter 
depends on its geometry and on the properties of the reservoir fluids. Tubing analysis includes a 
relationship between tubing size, wellhead and bottom hole pressure, fluid properties and 
production rate. Understanding wellbore flow performance is crucial to engineers in order to 
design the well equipment and optimize production [20]. 
The factor determining the wells’ production is the pressure drop required for the fluid to start 
flowing from the bottom hole, through the tubing and eventually up to surface. Starting from 
the wellhead, the parameters that contribute to the bottom hole pressure are [24]: 
==PAGE:36==
18 
 
1. The backpressure at the wellhead. This is caused by fluid friction or an induced 
resistance to flow through the pipeline network.  
2. The hydrostatic gradient between the bottom hole and the wellhead. 
3. The pressure required to overcome losses due to friction forces. This depends on the 
flow velocity of the fluid, the flow regime and the viscosity, as well as the length, 
diameter and roughness of the tubing, the valves, and so on.

2.5 Multiphase flow 
Multiphase flow is the most common flow of fluids in nature. It occurs in physical and chemical 
processes, such as oil production, but also in other cases, such as distillation, wastewater 
treatment, etc. Multiphase flow is governed by the same basic principles as the one-phase flow, 
but in this case, the two phases (liquid and gas) are distributed in the pipelines in a variety of 
ways, resulting in the appearance of various types of flow regimes. The equations describing the 
multiphase flow are complex, making their exact mathematical expression difficult, since each 
phase is influenced by its own properties and conditions, and at the same time, it affects and is 
influenced by the other phase. 
 
2.5.1 Flow regimes 
As shown in Fig. 2.11, several flow regimes can be created during the multiphase flow, the most 
important of which are [24]: 
• 
Bubble flow, in which the gas phase is dispersed in the continuous liquid phase of the 
oil. 
• 
Slug flow, in which gas bubbles merge into larger ones that, at some point, begin to 
occupy most of the tubings’ cross section. The gas slugs act as an efficient mechanism 
when it comes to lifting the production liquid to surface. 
• 
Churn flow, in which larger gas bubbles become unstable and collapse, resulting in 
turbulent flow. 
• 
Annular flow, in which the gas phase becomes dominant and continuous, while the 
liquid flows into a ring that overlaps the surface of the tubing, with liquid droplets being 
entrained in the gas phase. 
==PAGE:40==
22 
 
• 
Mist flow, in which, at excessively high velocities of the gas column, shear force at the 
gas-oil interface leads to the dispersion of oil into the gas forming a "fog" state. This 
misty flow pattern occurs only at very high flow rates and in systems with high gas-oil 
ratio (GOR). 
 
 
Figure 2.11: 14Flow regimes. [30] 
 
Given that the pressure drop in a tubing is caused by a series of complex interactions of many 
factors, one of the biggest problems in the well performance analysis and design of gas lift is to 
predict the change of pressure with depth. This problem has been the subject of numerous 
studies. In the recent years, several researchers have published correlations concerning that 
prediction. The most well-known are those of Hagedorn & Brown and Orkiszewski. These 
correlations of multiphase flow are quite accurate and very useful for engineers because they 
are used to achieve the following [24]: 
• 
Selection of the most efficient pipe diameter. 
• 
Predicting the right time, during the lifetime of the oil field, in which the artificial lift 
system should begin. 
• 
Design the appropriate artificial lift mechanisms. 
• 
Determination of the wellhead pressure. 
• 
Determination of the Productivity Index (PI). 
• 
Prediction of the maximum possible production rates.

two phases (liquid and gas), eventually leading to a higher velocity of the lighter phase. 
Multiphase flow would be highly simplified if the two phases behaved like one homogeneous

3. Artificial Lift 
3.1 Introduction 
More than three out of four producing oil wells flow using some kind of artificial lift method. 
When the reservoir no longer has the sufficient energy for hydrocarbons to flow at the desired 
rates, supporting production methods can assist the production. Because reservoir pressure 
drops as production proceeds and more water is produced later in the fields’ life, artificial lift is 
commonly associated with mature oil and gas developments [32].  
In production engineering, artificial lift methods refer to the use of artificial means to increase 
the flow or to re-start dead wells. It is a set of methods, analyzed in the following sections, 
which are used to reduce the required bottom hole pressure (BHP) in order to achieve a higher 
production rate. 
In this chapter, a description of the various artificial lift mechanisms will be presented, with 
particular emphasis on the gas lift method. 
 
3.2 Artificial lift system selection 
Many criteria influence the choice of the production aid. Prior to making any decision on the 
choice of the artificial lift selection by the production engineer, a detailed study should be 
carried out, containing elements that will identify exactly what the project needs and how it 
should be implemented. Some of the most basic selection criteria are the following [33]: 
• 
Reservoir characteristics, such as depth of formation, flow characteristics, nature of 
fluids produced etc.  
• 
The location of the field, such as terrestrial or marine environment, distance from 
treatment facilities, weather phenomena etc. 
• 
The power source to be used and ease of delivery. 
• 
Operating problems, such as the presence of solids e.g. salts, asphaltenes, hydrates, 
etc., as well as the presence of corrosive gases such as hydrogen sulfide. 
• 
Financial capacity of the company and possibilities of installation and maintenance of 
the necessary equipment such as engines, compressors, pumps etc. 
 
3.3 Types of artificial lift 
The main methods of artificial lift are: 
• 
Sucker rod pumping 
• 
Electric submersible pumps (ESPs) 
• 
Gas lift 
• 
Jet hydraulic pumping systems 
==PAGE:45==
27 
 
In addition, the less frequently used methods, such as plunger lift and progressive cavity pumps, 
are becoming more and more applicable as technology evolves. All of the above methods are 
described in the following subchapters. 
 
3.3.1 Sucker rod pump 
Rod pumps are one of the most used methods of artificial lift for wells on land. Rod pumps 
comprise of a prime mover, a surface pump, a sucker rod string and a downhole pump [34], as it 
can be seen in Fig. 3.1 below. 
 
 
Figure 3.1:17Sucker rod pump system. [34] 
 
This pumping system consists of a number of components, which are installed and operate at 
both surface and underground environments. The pumping unit at surface, which is connected 
to the underground pump, consists of a drive mechanism, such as an electric motor, and a beam 
hooked to a pivotal post [35]. 
The system sets in motion the downhole components by allowing the beam to move up and 
down. The drive mechanism, V-belt drives and a gearbox run the entire surface network with a 
==PAGE:46==
28 
 
crank mechanism on it. Nonetheless, other types of surface-pumping units can be used, like 
hydraulically actuated units, or tall-tower systems [35].  
The underground pump has steel rods (usually called sucker rods) attached to it and they are 
together connected to the surface unit. A useful feature of these rods is that they can be welded 
into one large piece that can connect the surface with the downhole pump. The rods are placed 
inside the production tubing and are moved up and down by the surface-pumping unit. This 
vertical movement mobilizes the positive-displacement pump at the bottom hole. Every time 
the rods and pumps are moved up and down, a certain amount of reservoir fluids is lifted 
through the sucker-rod tubing annulus and released to surface [35]. 
Some of the major advantages of this method include the high system efficiency, the low cost of 
repair and service, the high drawdown caused by the positive displacement pump, the reduction 
of corrosion concerns due to upgraded materials, the adaptation to production changes through 
stroke length and speed changes etc. However, there are certain drawbacks that must be taken 
into consideration. The most important are the potential for tubing and rod wear, limited gas-
handling capability, limited ability of rods to handle loads and environmental concerns [36].  
 
3.3.2 Electric submersible pumps (ESPs) 
The electrical submersible pump (ESP) is a productive and reliable method for lifting moderate 
to high volumes of fluids, ranging from 150 bbl/day to as much as 150,000 bbl/day. Speed 
controllers can extend this range significantly. The ESPs’ components comprise of a multistage 
centrifugal pump, a three-phase induction motor, a seal-chamber section, a power cable and a 
surface control system [35]. 
As it was mentioned, ESP is an efficient method and, under certain conditions, more effective 
than sucker rod pumps. As shown in Fig. 3.2, it consists of the following surface and 
underground systems: 
A) Surface system: 
• 
Motor controllers. They provide power to the ESP motors and protect the downhole 
components [37]. 
• 
Transformer. A transformer is required to convert the electricity, provided via power 
lines, to match the voltage and amperage prerequisites of the ESP motor [37]. 
• 
Surface electric cable. It is connected to the underground-insulated cable and transmits 
the required power, which is transferred to the pump. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:47==
29 
 
 
Figure 3.2: 18Electric Submersible Pumps. [38] 
 
B) Underground system: 
• 
Pump. The pump consists of a series of centrifuges in a row, as shown in Fig. 3.2 (b). This 
pump works by transferring pressure to the fluid to give it the energy it needs to start 
flowing from the bottom hole to surface at a desired flowrate. This is accomplished by 
transferring kinetic energy to the fluid from the centrifugal force that develops into the 
pump, which eventually becomes dynamic energy in the form of pressure [37]. 
• 
Motor. Provides power to the pump. 
• 
Sealed electric cable. The insulated power cable transmits the required power to the 
pump motor. Mainly, it is installed on the production column, from the wellhead down 
to the pump and is designed to operate under high pressures [37]. 
• 
Gas separator. A gas separator is installed at the intake part of the pump to separate the 
gas from the fluid before it enters the pump [37]. 
 
ESPs provide a number of advantages over other artificial lift methods. They are adaptable to 
highly deviated wells and they occupy a minimum space for subsurface control and production 
facilities. Furthermore, this system is quiet, safe and leaves a smaller surface environmental 
==PAGE:48==
30 
 
footprint, something that is preferred, especially in offshore and environmentally conscious 
areas. ESPs permit increased production while handling high water cut rates and secondary 
recovery operations and they are applicable in harsh environments. However, ESPs have certain 
operational challenges that must be considered. They can handle only a small percentage of 
solid production, they contribute to high cost pulling operations and production loss may occur 
during workovers, especially in an offshore environments. Finally, they need relatively large 
casing sizes for moderate- to high-production rate equipment [37] [39]. 
 
3.3.3 Hydraulic jet pumps 
Jet pumps are becoming more and more popular in many production operations because of 
their simple downhole architecture, which includes no moving parts. Jets pumps convert the 
energy of high-velocity fluid jets into pressure, to lower downhole pressures in order to transfer 
the fluids to surface and, hence, to increase production rates. Hydraulic jet pumps operate the 
same way as jet engines. They contain a relatively narrow nozzle that converts a pressurized and 
slow moving fluid into lower pressure, fast moving fluid by adiabatic expansion. In the case of 
the pump, the thrust generated by the movement of the high-velocity fluid transfers the 
hydrocarbons to surface [40]. 
The jet pump is moved through the casing string until the depth at which the production fluid is 
reached, as shown in Fig. 3.3. A pumping system at surface moves a pressurized fluid, usually 
called power fluid (typically refined oil or mixture of oil and water), downhole to the jet pump 
where the fluid flows through the nozzle. This specific design feature provides a restriction in 
the power fluids’ flow path that causes its speed to increase [40].  
The injected power fluid that enters from the top of the pump, exits the nozzle at a higher 
velocity and thus it accelerates and mixes with the reservoir fluid. As the two fluid streams are 
combined in a short mixing tube (pump throat), the force from the increased velocity of the 
injected fluid is partially transferred to the produced reservoir fluid and increases its kinetic 
energy. The mixed fluid stream is then transferred to a diffuser that converts the kinetic energy 
to static pressure in the fluid, which is high enough to lift it to surface [40]. 
Hydraulic pumps have become more popular due to the many advantages they provide. When 
the size of the nozzle and throat are changed, a jet pump can produce oil from wells ranging 
from 1000 – 18000 ft. Because they have no moving parts, they do not wear easily and they can 
operate for many years and with minimal maintenance requirements. Furthermore, they tend to 
be more rough and tolerant of corrosive and abrasive well fluids. Jet pumps can also handle big 
volumes of free gas. Operators install the pumps via wireline or by using pressurized power fluid 
to transport them downhole [40].  
Finally, by redirecting the flow of the power fluid, technicians can bring the pump back to 
surface for repair or replacement [40]. 
==PAGE:49==
31 
 
 
Figure 3.3: 19Hydraulic jet pump configuration. [40] 
 
However, jet pumps have certain drawbacks, including the risk of cavitation at the entrance of 
the throat section, caused by the expeditious acceleration of the production fluids as they enter 
the pump. As fluid velocity increases, fluid pressure drops to its vapor pressure, creating vapor 
cavities, which result in a restriction to the flow path. These cavities may collapse as pressure 
rises in the pump, causing cavitation damage. Furthermore, the choice of oil as the power fluid 
can be debatable because it introduce a fire danger to the well site. Finally, jet pumps are prone 
to internal turbulence and friction, due to the high mixing velocities, which drops horsepower 
efficiency [40].  
 
3.3.4 Progressive cavity pumps (PCPs) 
Progressive cavity pumps are a cost-effective artificial lift method that simplifies production and 
can withstand erosion. They are increasingly used and they have adapted to a range of 
challenging operations e.g. heavy oil, high sand production, high gas content wells etc. They 
displace the reservoir fluid by transferring it through a sequence of small cavities, formed by the 
spinning of a helical rotor within a stator [41].  
==PAGE:50==
32 
 
Most PCP systems consist of a downhole PCP and a drive system, as it can be seen in Fig. 3.4. 
The downhole positive displacement pump consist of a stator and a helical rotor. The stator is a 
steel tubular construction that has an internal bonded elastomeric sleeve with a helix design on 
its inner surface [40]. 
 
Figure 3.4: 20Progressive cavity pump (PCP) system. [41] 
 
The rotor is a small-diameter, carbon steel screw that has round threads and a long pitch and it 
is run downhole at the bottom of the sucker rod string. The helix inside the stator matches the 
motor configuration while the rotor seals tightly against the stator and turns eccentrically inside 
it [41]. 
This configuration creates a set of fixed-size cavities between the rotor and the stator that 
moves as the rotor rotates and carry the production fluid through the pump in a pulsation-free 
linear flow and up to surface. The PCP drive system is located at surface and consists of a motor 
that rotates the rod string and consequently the rotor within the stator [41]. 
PCP systems deliver overall system energy efficiency of 55-75%, due to specific downhole 
designs for several field conditions. They are relatively easy to install, operate, need less 
maintenance and they are able to produce fluids containing high concentrations of sand and 
other solids. They also operate within a wide temperature range. The pumps do not contain 
reciprocating parts that may clog and they are designed to limit the rate of shear between the 
adjacent layers of the flowing production fluid. At the surface, the drive system has a simple 
==PAGE:51==
33 
 
design with a small effect on the environment and emits low noise levels. However, PCPs have 
an upper production rate limit for large-diameter pumps and significantly less for small-
diameter ones. In addition, they are sensitive to the downhole fluid environment. Paraffin 
control may also present problems in waxy production cases because the rotation movement of 
the rod string precludes the use of scrapers for paraffin removal [41]. 
 
3.4 Gas lift 
3.4.1 Historical development of gas lift 
The gas lift method is a development of the airlift, which was first introduced for ejecting oil 
from wells drilled in USA, Pennsylvania, in 1864. The use of compressed natural gas for lifting oil 
from wells, however, is recent [42]. The airlift method arrived in Texas-Louisiana Gulf Coast oil 
fields in 1900, where it found an excessive application in many oil fields. California began to use 
the method about a decade later, however, gas rather than air begun to be utilized as the lifting 
medium [43]. 
 
3.4.2 Overview of the gas lift method 
Assisting production with gas injection is one of the most effective and less expensive methods 
of enhancing production. Gas lift increases oil production rate by injecting compressed gas into 
the production tubing through the casing- tubing annulus. The operation of the method involves 
the installation of gas lift valves on the tubing, from which the compressed gas enters from 
surface. Surface compressors create the gas pressure required for the injection into the pipe. As 
soon as the valve opens, the gas infiltrates the column and mixes with the produced liquid [44]. 
Upon inserting the tubing, gas improves hydrocarbon flow in two ways: (a) the energy from the 
expansion of the gas pushes the liquid towards the surface and (b) the gas aerates the liquid so 
as to reduce its density and downhole pressure, and as a result to achieve a greater pressure 
differential within the reservoir [44].  
In the case of gas lift, well depth is not a limitation as far as the installation of the valves is 
concerned. It is suitable for offshore environments with low lifting costs for a large number of 
wells. Furthermore, it is applied in wells with relatively high bottom hole pressures and 
reservoirs with high productivity are preferred over others in the case a well has a multi-
inclination trajectory, in which the installation and operation of other artificial lift methods is 
difficult [44]. There are two types of gas lift, continuous and intermittent, analyzed in the 
following sections. In the present Master’s Thesis, the mention of the term “Gas Lift” will always 
determine the continuous gas lift system. 
 
==PAGE:52==
34 
 
3.4.3 Benefits and limitations of the gas lift method  
The most important advantages of gas lift are described in the following section [45] [46]: 
• 
The initial cost of downhole installation is usually low. 
• 
Installations can be designed for lifting initially from near the depth of the hydrocarbon-
bearing formation. The design can also incorporate lifting many thousands of barrels per 
day. 
• 
The production rate can be controlled at surface. 
• 
Sand does not affect the gas lift equipment. 
• 
The few moving parts in the system provide a long service life, compared to other 
artificial lift methods. 
• 
Operating costs are usually low. 
• 
The gas compressor is installed at surface where it can be easily inspected and repaired. 
• 
The method permits the concurrent use of wireline equipment, which is easily used and 
maintained economically.  
• 
The gas lift design leaves the tubing fully open, permitting the use of BHP surveys, sand 
sounding and bailing, production logging, etc. 
 
However, gas lift has also certain limitations that can be summarized as follows [45] [46]: 
• 
Gas must always be available at the site. If the field is short on the available gas, or if gas 
prices rise, it may be required to switch to another artificial lift method. 
• 
Corrosive gas lift gas may increase the cost of an operation if gas treatment is necessary 
before use. 
• 
The installation of a gas lift system usually requires a longer lead-time and greater 
preparation than other systems. However, the reduced operating cost of the gas lift 
system will sometimes counterbalance any added cost of the initial installation. 
• 
Relatively high backpressure may restrict production. 
• 
There is an increased complication when lifting low gravity (less than 15°API) crude oil 
because of the excessive effect of friction, gas fingering and liquid fallback.  
 
3.4.4 Continuous gas lift 
Continuous injection is the most common method of gas lift. It is a method that is usually used 
in wells with a high PI (>0.5 stb/day/psi) and high reservoir pressure [20]. 
In continuous gas lift, compressed gas, in small volumes, is injected continuously down the 
annulus, through the gas lift valve and into the tubing to aerate or lighten the liquid column, as 
it can be seen in Fig. 3.5. This way, and with the aid of the expanding injection gas, the flowing 
bottom hole pressure decreases and is therefore capable to deliver the liquids to surface [20]. 
==PAGE:53==
35 
 
 
 
Figure 3.5: 21Continuous gas lift operation. [20] 
 
To accomplish this in an effective way, it is desirable to design a network that will permit 
injection through a single valve (usually called operating valve or orifice), which must be placed 
at the greatest depth possible. It should be noted that additional valves could be placed above 
the operating valve, in predefined positions [20]. 
 
3.4.4.1 Gas lift equipment 
For the appropriate installation and operation of a gas lift system, operators must be aware of 
the equipment and the fundamentals of gas lift technology. Gas lift equipment can be divided 
into two main categories: surface and underground equipment. Regarding the surface 
equipment, a gas compressor is needed in order to achieve the desired injection pressure. It is 
also required to have a manifold system consisting of a safety valve, a gas meter and distribution 
pipes for each well. Underground equipment consists of the gas lift valves and mandrels, into 
which the valves are positioned [20]. 
 
Side Pocket Mandrels 
Side pocket mandrels allow the permanent installation of the gas lift valves. However, in most 
wells, mandrels are placed at predetermined depths on the production tubing as part of the 
permanent downhole equipment, according to the design of the operation. Today, the great 
==PAGE:54==
36 
 
majority of mandrels used in many oil fields are for wireline-retrievable valves, since they allow 
the installation (and recovery, if necessary) of gas lift valves using wireline technology [47]. 
The shape of the mandrels is oval, with an outer diameter larger than that of the production 
column, as it can be seen in Fig. 3.6.  
 
 
Figure 3.6: 22Side pocket mandrel schematic. [48] 
 
 
Gas lift valves 
The maximum benefit of a specific gas injection rate is achieved when the gas is injected as deep 
as possible within the production tubing. The gas lift valves are the tools used for this purpose, 
which are designed to open and close according to casing pressure, Injection Pressure Operating 
valve (IPO) or production pressure, Production Pressure Operated valve (PPO), described below 
in this section [24]. 
The upper gas lift valves are designed to open and close so that to allow the annulus to be 
unloaded. They have ports sized in a predetermined way, in order to allow only limited volume 
of the gas required for unloading. The operating valve is different, meaning that it is designed to 
==PAGE:55==
37 
 
allow a continuous flow of gas. In that case, a larger port is installed so that gas injection can be 
increased to the optimum value needed for the current reservoir conditions [24]. 
 
Injection Pressure Operated Valves 
The pressure of the injected gas controls the Injection Pressure Operated valve (IPO), shown in 
Fig. 3.7 (left). Usually, IPO valves are nitrogen-charged valves and are solely calibrated by 
opening the valve. The injection pressure necessary to compress the bellows of the valves is 
determined by the pre-charged nitrogen in the bellows. The gas that is injected infiltrates the 
valve and acts on the effective bellows area. As the gas lift gas pressure overcomes the 
nitrogens’ one, the bellows compress and move the stem tip off the sealed position. As result, 
the gas injected starts to flow through the opened stem tip, past the reverse-flow check valve 
and into the tubing. The drop in casing pressure, which occurs in the later stages of the 
unloading process, is used to close the valves in a correct sequence, by lowering the stem tip 
[49] [50].  
The advantage of this opening/closing procedure is that when the desired injection point is 
reached, an added casing pressure drop can be designed in order to ensure that the upper 
valves are rigidly closed and that fluctuations in tubing pressure are very unlikely to result in re-
opening the valve. The IPO valves are considered, worldwide, as the most suited unloading valve 
type when the continuous gas lift system is in use [49]. In this Masters’ Thesis, the IPO valves are 
considered. 
 
Production Pressure Operated Valves 
In the Production Pressure Operated (PPO) valves, shown in Fig. 3.7 (right), the flow path is 
inverted and thus the tubing pressure is acting on the larger area of the bellows, making the 
valve sensitive to the tubing pressure. The fluid produced infiltrates the valve and acts on the 
bellows area. The pressure necessary to compress the bellows is again determined by the pre-
charged nitrogen pressure. As production pressure gets bigger than the nitrogen one, the 
bellows compress and move the stem tip up off the sealed position and, the same way as in IPO 
valves, gas flows through opened stem tip, past the reverse-flow check valve and into the tubing 
[51]. 
The use of PPO valves is limited to dual wells, where any fluctuations in casing pressure would 
cause some sort of impedance between the strings with the IPO valve, and in cases where the 
production pressure can be considered more predictable [49]. 
 
==PAGE:56==
38 
 
 
Figure 3.7: 23IPO and PPO gas lift valves. [52] 
 
3.4.4.2 Unloading process 
As it is already mentioned, gas lift valves allow the introduction of gas into the tubing to unload 
the well and commence or amplify flow. Proper design of the valve depths, in order to ensure 
unloading, requires a thorough understanding of the unloading process [20].  
The unloading process is shown in Fig. 3.8. Initially, as it is shown in case (a), all valves are open 
because of the high tubing pressures and the fluid inside the tubing has the pressure gradient 
(Gs) of a static liquid column. The time the gas injected through the casing enters the first valve, 
Fig. 3.8 (b), a liquid–gas slug mixture with lower density is created in the tubing above the valve.  
As the slug expands, it advances the liquid column above it, in order to establish the flow to 
surface. However, this expansion can cause a reverse flow of the liquid in the wellbore and thus, 
a check valve must be installed at the end of the tubing to prevent it [20].  
 
==PAGE:57==
39 
 
 
Figure 3.8: 24Well unloading sequence. [20] 
 
As the breadth of the slug grows, it creates a pressure drawdown in the reservoir and formation 
fluids start to flow into the wellbore and up to surface. When the tubing pressure, at the level of 
the first valve, is low enough, the first valve begin to close and the gas injected is compelled to 
pass from the second one, as shown in Fig. 3.8 (c). When the gas reaches the second valve, it 
will gasify the liquid present in between the first and the second valve. This will create a further 
drawdown and cause the inflow of more formation fluids. The same process should be repeated 
until the gas enters the operating valve, as shown in Fig. 3.8 (d), and a steady-state flow is 
established, as presented in case (e). The operating valve is usually the lowermost valve in the 
tubing that never closes, however more valves can be placed below or above and used in the 
latter stages of the reservoir’s life [20].

3.4.5 Intermittent Gas Lift 
When a well is producing with a low productivity index (PI) or with low reservoir pressure, 
intermittent gas lift can be used. In both of these cases, an excessively high-pressure drop is 
required, which can result in a prohibitively high gas/liquid ratio (GLR) for the continuous gas lift 
to produce the desired flow. Intermittent gas lift operates using periodic gas injection into the 
production column. This means that gas injection takes place only for a certain period of time 
and then stops. After a certain period has passed, gas injection is repeated. This operation is 
performed with a time switch that opens the valves for a predetermined period and at regular 
intervals [49]. 
 
 
Figure 3.9: 25Intermittent flow gas lift installation. [49] 
 
The principle of intermittent gas lift is presented in Fig. 3.9. In intermittent flow, fluid is 
permitted to accumulate and build up in the tubing at the bottom of the well. At predetermined 
periods, the gas injected into the tubing is rapidly expanding under the column of liquid, which 
is, eventually, pushed up the tubing and to surface. The reoccurrence of gas injection is 
determined by the amount of time required for the slug to build up in the tubing and the length 
of the gas injection period depends on the time required to push the slug up to surface [45].

4. Model setting in PROSPER 
4.1 Introduction to PROSPER 
PROSPER is a well performance, design and optimization program which is part of the Integrated 
Production Modelling Toolkit (IPM). It is the industrial tool for modeling the management of the 
overwhelming majority of hydrocarbon deposits worldwide and it is designed to create reliable 
well models of all types, along with every one of the artificial lift methods, taking into account 
PVT characterization, well equipment and geometry, and reservoir flow (IPR) and tubing 
performance (VLP) [53]. 
This chapter will provide a detailed analysis of the models’ development for all given wells with 
the available PVT data of the reservoir fluids and the data from the well equipment and 
geometry. 
 
4.2 PROSPER principle and system analysis 
In PROSPER, each part of the system that contributes to the overall performance is modelled 
independently. Fluid properties, IPR, VLP and pressure losses in the surface gathering network 
are individually evaluated, analyzed, confirmed and calibrated against recorded data. 
In order for PROSPER to operate, a model of PVT data of reservoir fluids must first be developed 
by introducing data from laboratory tests (usually flash expansion processes), and tuned with 
PVT correlations to improve accuracy. These are correlations for black oil, such as Glaso, 
Standing, Lasater and others. Furthermore, since well potential and pressure losses from 
hydrocarbon production are also dependent on the PVT properties of the fluid, the precision of 
the system analysis rely on the accuracy of their values [53]. 
With regard to the design of the IPR curve, which depends on the characteristics of the 
reservoir, PROSPER uses reservoir inflow equations such as Darcy, Vogel, PI etc. The difference 
between Darcy and PI is that the first one involves the introduction of the characteristics of the 
reservoir (porosity, permeability, thickness, skin factor, etc.) and calculates the PI, while the 
second directly enters the PI value, if known, bypassing the characteristics of the reservoir. With 
this, PROSPER is able to calculate and plot the IPR curve, but also accurately calculate the AOF 
value (see Chapter 2) [53]. 
In order for PROSPER to calculate and represent the VLP curve, it separates the total losses of 
production pressure into loss due to friction, gravity and acceleration. The acceleration 
component is usually small except in systems involving powerful fluid expansion, however, it is 
used in all PROSPER calculations (see Chapter 2). The VLP depends directly on the geometrical 
characteristics of the well, its inclination, the mechanical components it contains, such as valves 
or flow limitations, the geothermal gradient and the average heat capacity of the fluids 
produced along the surface. The last two characteristics are needed in order for PROSPER to be 
==PAGE:60==
42 
 
able to calculate the change in the PVT properties of the fluids as they travel to surface, since 
they depend on the temperature values that exist along the entire length of the tubing. Any 
change in these values can significantly alter the VLP curve, making it one of the most significant 
factors for design or optimization processes [53]. 
After the two IPR and VLP curves have been designed and plotted, PROSPER should confirm that 
its calculations are accurate. When sufficient and accurate field data from production tests are 
provided, PROSPER adapts various complex multi-phase flow correlations (described later in this 
chapter) in order to tune them with the field data and improve its accuracy. In this way, reliable 
models are developed, which are then used to design or optimize a production system [53].

4.4 Model set up 
As described in Chapter 4.2, the goal of PROSPER is to create a mathematical model in order to 
describe and represent a wells’ behavior under different scenarios. 
The model design starts by inserting certain basic data about the well under examination, such 
as fluid type, separator stages, viscosity models, presence of hydrates, artificial lift method etc. 
Then, the PVT data of the fluid must be entered, in order for PROSPER to generate the model 
based on the fluids’ properties. Moving on the IPR section, reservoir data must be entered so 
that PROSPER can define the reservoir inflow performance. In this area, the main parts of the 
model are defined, including the IPR model, reservoir parameters such as pressure, 
temperature, water cut etc. and data specific to the selected IPR model, like skin model, sand 
control devices, viscosity data etc. In the equipment section, the wells hardware, deviation 
survey and temperature profile are generated. For this purpose, surface and downhole 
equipment data are entered, along with data for the measured depth (MD), the corresponding 
true vertical depth (TVD), bottom hole temperatures and heat capacity coefficients. Finally, the 
artificial lift data are provided, the quality control of the well is run and now PROSPER can 
design the mathematical model [53].

5. Gas Lift Design 
In this chapter, the design of the continuous gas lift system and the optimum position of the gas 
lift valves for all wells of the Alpha field, for various reservoir pressures, is analyzed. As it was 
discussed in the previous Chapter 4, no tubing sensitive analysis is needed since the flow of the 
fluids to surface is withheld due to the large pressure gradient inside the tubing. 
The design of a continuous gas lift system depends on a number of related variables, such as 
Productivity Index, GOR, static reservoir pressure, tubing size and gas injection pressure. The 
economic impact of the optimum design depends on maintaining a minimum gas injection to 
produce the reservoir fluids. This is done by designing the gas injection pressures and a 
maximum injection valve depth [54]. 
The main concern when it comes to gas lift design is the type and pressure values of the 
unloading/operating valves, in order to commence and control oil production with an economic 
gas injection rate [54]. The factors that have a strong effect on the selection of the most suitable 
gas injection pressure include [55]: 
• 
Pressure/Volume/Temperature (PVT) properties of the reservoir fluids. 
• 
Water cut 
• 
Gas injection density 
• 
Wellhead backpressure 
When performing a gas lift design, an optimum solution between the factors listed below must 
be found [56]: 
• 
Available gas lift gas 
• 
Gas injection pressure and rates 
• 
Capacity of the compressors 
• 
Maximum injection valve depth 
• 
Number of unloading valves. This has to be designed in order to handle current and 
future well conditions 
In the present Master Thesis, the available gas lift gas is given to be 8 MMscf, available for 
distribution in all wells. The gas is planned to be reinjected, due to the high initial cost, and will 
be supplemented only when, and if, needed (e.g. in cases where there is a leak or pollution of 
the gas with contaminants).  
 
5.1 Gas Injection Pressure 
Choosing the proper gas injection pressure is of great importance in a gas lift system design. In 
order to accomplish the desired production outcome, gas must be injected as near the 
producing zones as possible. The injection pressure at any depth must always be smaller than 
==PAGE:86==
68 
 
the casing pressure at the same depth in order to avoid any possible burst and hence failure of 
the operating system [35].  
In the present Master Thesis, the casing pressure for all wells is given at 1900 psig and thus, the 
injection pressure is set to be at, maximum, 1800 psig, in order to operate in a safe injection 
‘’window’’. 
The gas injection pressure required at the wellhead dictates the discharge pressure of the 
compressor. High injection pressures increase the discharge pressure requirement of the 
compressor, which means that for a given gas volume, a related increase in the compressor 
horsepower is required. Therefore, an optimum solution must be found, in order to accomplish 
the most efficient and economic production [35]. 
 
5.2 Gas Compressors  
The capacity and operation of the compressors, along with the distribution of the injected gas 
into the wells, is of crucial importance. In order for the gas lift system to be successful and 
operative, the optimum gas compressors must be chosen and installed.  The selection and 
design of the compressors must be considered carefully because of the high initial cost, which, 
usually, represents a considerable portion of the entire project [35].  
Both centrifugal and reciprocating compressors are used in gas lift operations. However, 
because of their flexibility under changeable conditions, reciprocating compressors are more 
common than centrifugal ones [35]. In the present Master Thesis, a number of reciprocating gas 
compressors (one for each well) rather than a large one will be used to compress the available 
gas into the wells. The working pressure of the compressors can be up to 2000 psig with gas 
displacement of 2,000,000 scf/day. 
 
5.3 Valve Spacing Design 
The objective of the design process is to make sure that the unloading valves are closed when 
the well is lifting from the designed operating point. Thus, it is important to incorporate certain 
forms of “design bias” into the design. There are numerous forms of bias. In this Master Thesis, 
the casing pressure bias was incorporated [49]. 
When the casing sensitive valves (IPO) are used, the casing pressure has to be decreased for the 
valves to close. This concept is included into the spacing procedure described below. To find the 
optimum valve depth, the static fluid line is projected from the objective gradient until it 
intersects the corresponding casing pressure curve (see Fig 5.1) [53]. 
 
==PAGE:87==
69 
 
 
Figure 5.1: 44Spacing procedure for casing sensitive valves. [53] 
 
The Fig. 5.1 above presents the valve spacing process where the red lines are associated with 
the different gas injection pressures at the casing head, the green lines with the static pressure 
gradient of the casing fluid and the blue curve with the casing pressure curve. The start of the 
right red line represent the kick off pressure (KOP). [2] 
The gas is injected inside the well with a designed pressure, always by taking into account the 
casing pressure limits, i.e. the pressure the casing can withstand. The depth where the red line, 
which corresponds to the pressure of the gas injected, intersects with the static pressure 
gradient inside the casing is the depth at which the first unloading valve will be placed [2]. 
When the exact placement of the first unloading valve is established, the gas injection pressure 
is gradually reduced. This can be clearly seen in Fig. 5.1 where the new gas pressure line starts 
from a lower pressure value (second red line from the right side). The second valve position is 
determined when the static pressure inside the casing intersects the new gas pressure line, 
which was created as explained before. The procedure continuous similarly until the maximum 
injection depth is reached [2]. 
 
5.4 Design Procedure 
The uncertain and constantly changing design parameters, like reservoir pressure, water cut, 
Productivity Index etc., result in a design solution that is not unique. Thereafter, the design is 
‘’stress checked’’. This is to ensure that it is robust under a range of anticipated operating

scenarios. That means that the design must be completed according to the worst-case scenario, 
as it was described in Chapter 4.

Notes on the various input parameters 
The Maximum Gas Available option sets the maximum gas that is available in order to obtain 
the maximum rate. The gas for the whole field is given to be 8 MMscf, available for distribution 
in all wells. The Maximum Gas During Unloading corresponds to the maximum gas available for 
the shallowest valve and the Flowing Top Node Pressure provides the flowing wellhead 
pressure. Since the separator of the field is not bypassed, the Unloading Top Node Pressure sets 
the same pressure as the flowing top node one. In any other case, a lower unloading pressure 
value should be entered [53]. 
==PAGE:89==

As it was explained in Chapter 5.1 above, the maximum Operating Injection Pressure was set at 
1800 psig and the Kick-off Injection Pressure at 500 psig for all wells in the oilfield. The Desired 
dP Across Valve option provides a pressure loss across the orifice to ensure the systems 
pressure stability. It is usually in the order of 100-200 psi and in the present Master Thesis was 
set to 100 psi [53]. 
The Minimum Spacing option determines the minimum spacing between two consecutive 
valves. If PROSPER calculates that the next valve has a value less than the minimum spacing one, 
the valve will not be added and the calculation will stop. The Minimum Transfer dP option is 
activated only when the option Use IPR for Unloading has been set as ‘’No’’ and is used to size 
the valve trim. In this case and as the unloading process proceeds, PROSPER calculates the 
optimum injection rate at each valve by running a sensitivity analysis on the injection rate, 
which is then used to size the valves [53].  
The Maximum Port Size option depends on the valves selected. PROSPER will select multiple 
orifice valves for high gas injection rates if the designed injection cannot pass through one valve 
of Maximum port size. Finally, the Thornhill-Craver DeRating coefficient is used to cut down the 
maximum gas rate that can pass through a specific valve [53]. 
As far as the design options are concerned, the Valve Type option was chosen to be ‘’Casing 
Sensitive’’ in order for the valve to be able to close based on the casing pressure. Furthermore, 
the Valve Settings option was set at ‘’All Valves Pvo = Gas Pressure’’ mode, which means that 
the dome pressures are set in a way that the valves open with the designed casing pressure. 
PROSPER uses the maximum dP to close valves. This method is the recommended design setting 
when designing new wells and ensures that the valves do not close before they should, 
however, it decreases the available injection pressure and may result in lower production rates. 
The Dome Pressure Correction Above 1200 psig was set to ‘’No’’, enabling PROSPER to use the 
standard API temperature correction method for all pressures. Finally, the Conformance with 
the IPR option is set to ‘’Yes’’ in order to make sure that the calculated liquid rates can be 
delivered by the reservoir [53].

Most commonly, Injection Pressure Operated (IPO) valves are used. For the purposes of the 
Master Thesis, the Camco valves are considered. Camco valves permit a continuous tubing flow 
production with a reliable operation and with a sealed communication between the tubing and 
the casing [57]. The type chosen is the normal R-20.  
This specific type enhances safety since wellbore integrity is ensured during shut-in periods, it 
reduces downtime, it lowers the cost because of versatility and efficiency and increases 
production due to the uniform operation over a series of injection pressures. Furthermore, they 
are corrosion resistant, thus they eliminate or minimize workovers [58].

The General Allocation Program (GAP) is a tool contained in the IPM toolkit and is used in order 
to achieve important functions such as modeling the surface production network or water/gas 
injection network, optimizing production, distributing gas lift gas and forecasting production. It

account the gas injection availability and separator pressure limits [59].

Conclusion 
The goal of the Master Thesis was to design the long-term optimization of a production field 
(Alpha field) by using the gas lift method of artificial lift. The problem was split into two main 
design categories using two industry production programs. The first was to design the artificial 
lift system of each ‘’dead’’ well of the Alpha field, by pre-determining the gas lift valve positions 
and gas injection rates for a long term period of 4 years, 8 months and 17 days overall. The 
second, and more challenging, was to model the production system as a whole (reservoir model, 
well model, pipelining and separator), so as to ensure that it will meet the reservoir model 
production predictions under full system pressure equilibration while further taking into 
account the injection gas availability. 
The mathematical model of each well was created using PROSPER, in order to design a 
continuous gas lift system by determining the optimum gas lift valve positions and gas injection 
rates. For this purpose, the PVT properties of the fluids and the IPR and VLP curves of each well 
were modelled with success and precision, based on available data from the reservoir prediction 
model. After the design of the production curves, the system and pressure gradient calculations 
were performed in order to verify that the wells cannot produce and to ensure that the reason 
for this problem is the high pressure gradient inside the production column. This was important 
to prove, since the conclusion that the chosen tubing ID and material are not affecting the flow 
arises and thus, no tubing sensitivity analysis is needed in order to calculate if a different ID 
value or material type will be affecting the production in a positive way. This outcome is very 
positive, since the gas lift valves should be installed in the current tubing, eliminating any 
additional cost that would emerged from an installation of a different tubing with larger ID or 
different material. 
After the pressure gradient calculations verified that the problem lied in the high density of the 
fluid (high pressure gradient), the gas lift design procedure was carried out based on the worst-
case scenario, e.g. the case where the pressure difference (ΔP) between the reservoir and the 
bottom hole has the smaller value. The gas lift valve depths and gas injection rates for each well 
of the worst-case scenario were determined without any problems and were found to fit also to 
the rest of the scenarios, e.g. the cases where the ΔP value was greater. 
The maximum depth of the valves was set to 1410 m because of the placement of the packers at 
a depth of 1420 m for all wells. The valve spacing results showed that the first unloading valve 
should be placed at, approximately for all wells, 63 m, with the second unloading valve following 
from 1083.5 m until 1104.7 m, depending on the well. The design showed that more valves are 
needed in the later stages of the fields’ life and thus some additional mandrels should be 
positioned from the beginning in order to install the valves via wireline later. Finally, the orifice 
valve results obtained pointed that the optimum position for their installation is at 1410 m and a 
relatively smaller value for the orifice mandrels needed for certain wells. 
The calculated gas injection rates were kept below 0.22 MMscf/day for all wells and for all 
production periods, ensuring that the available gas lift gas is more than adequate to provide the 
==PAGE:119==
101 
 
needed energy to the system in order to ensure production. Finally, for most wells in the later 
stages of the production, more gas injection is needed in order to ensure that they will keep 
flowing at desired rates. This is a justified outcome since in the final stage of production and 
although the reservoir pressure has increased, more water is being produced due to water 
injection, making the fluid more difficult to lift since water adds extra pressure on the bottom 
hole. 
The production system was modeled as a whole (reservoir model, well model and pipelining to 
the manifolds and eventually to the sales point), using the General Allocation Program (GAP) so 
as to ensure, as it was mentioned, that it will meet the reservoir model production predictions 
under full system pressure equilibration while further taking into account the injection gas 
availability. After the design of the oil field network, based on the production system of the 
Alpha field, GAP run the calculations, proving that under a full system pressure equilibration and 
under a specific separator pressure, the field is able to produce the rates calculated in PROSPER 
with success. For the 1st and 2nd periods, the production rates from PROSPER match the GAP 
ones perfectly, with a deviation of only 0.14 % and 1.21 % respectively, while for the 3rd and 4th 
period, the deviation between the two rates seems to increase with the former being more 
profound. 
Finally, it should be noted that although the Master Thesis was conducted with success, in 
reality, the production optimization of an oilfield using any kind of artificial lift method is a 
complicated procedure and should be conducted with care and respect to the systems’ 
constraints and  distinctiveness.
