Abstract
The honey industry generates multiple side streams each season, one of them is honey-
water. A side stream produced from rinsing equipments and the sling machine after
extraction process. The amount of beekeepers in Denmark alone is more than 5000 and
together is estimated to produce 100000 L of honey-water side stream, based on inter-
views of two local Danish beekeepers. The aim is to determine if honey water side stream
could be fermented and produced an sensory acceptable product. As this study is out-
side the harvesting season, the chosen alternative is store bought honey. Four solutions
of varying sugar concentrations (3%, 6%, 9% and 12% brix) were prepared and fermented
with Saccharomyces cerevisiae at 20◦C. the nutrient source is solely yeast extract.
All the variants achieved good ethanol yields, within the range of the theoretical ethanol
yield, 0,51g/g. This demonstrates its ability to ferment, however, sensory evaluation re-
vealed that lower brix (3% and 6%) experiments developed cheese like and sour odours,
which are considered unappealing. It is assumed to be correlated to microbial contami-
nation, also supported by the CFU analysis for experiment brix 3%. Contamination may
be due to insufficient pre inoculation pasteurization (78◦C for 6 minutes) or poor han-
dling and hygienic conditions during fermentation. In future studies fermentation with
the real side stream is recommended and further optimization is needed to increase safety
and sensory attributes.

Introduction

Introduction
Honey is a valued natural product with multiple applications in food, cosmetic and
medicinal industries (Piwowarek Kamil, 2023). However, the byproducts the honey in-
dustry produces, such as honey-water is still not utilized and considered a waste. Honey-
water is sugar rich side stream produced during cleaning of equipments after harvesting
honey. Honey’s primary use is a sweetener in food recipes, honey-water could be col-
lected for other purposes, such as a fermented beverage (Lise Hansted, 2020). Despite
its potential, honey-water side stream has not been studied as a substrate for fermented
beverages. This study addresses the gap in the research, exploring its ability to ferment
under low initial brix values. Store bought honey do not represent the actual honey-water
side stream and variations in compositions are expected.
This study investigates the potential of fermenting in different brix ranges, as the study
is outside of the honey harvesting season, the use of store-bought honey has been chosen
as an alternative. To be more precise, this study investigates whether yeast can ferment
honey-water to produce ethanol and if the final product meets sensory acceptability. The
sensory evaluation will have the role of assessing the smell and appearance of the honey-
water before and after fermentation. This provides insight into its appeal as a fermented
drink. Unfortunately, taste is not possible to include as the experiments are conducted in
a lab that is not considered food grade.

Aim

Aim
The main purpose of this master’s thesis is to:
”Investigate the fermentation ability of honey-water and evaluate the sensory attributes
(smell and appearance) of the final product produced”.
Specific objectives:
1. Investigating the potential of yeast to ferment in different low brix ranges to pro-
duce alcohol.
2. Assesing the sensory characteristics (smell and appearance) of honey-water before
and after fermentation, through expert sensory evaluation.
3. Determining if contamination occurs during fermentation, as assesed using YPD
agar plates.
The plan on accomplishing the main purpose is to design a simple fermentation setup
and using minimal ingredients. Furthermore, the fermentation conditions recommended
by the yeast manufacturer Lesaffre are followed. Different analyses methods are utilized
to investigate the different experiments, using the same yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae -
SafAle S-04).

Litterature review
The honey industry produces multiple valuable products, but byproducts, such as honey-
water is not yet utilized and is only considered as a waste. Honey-water is rich in fer-
mentable sugars produced during the extraction and processing of honey (Camilla Sch-
abert, 2024). It has potential for upscaling applications, such as fermentation. Despite the
opportunity of recycling the side stream, there is a limited research on its composition and
fermentation characteristics. This literature review explores most similar fields, includ-
ing mead production and other sugar rich side streams, to provide some background for
the potential of the honey-water as a fermentation solution. The main product from the
honey industry is honey extracted from honeycombs, which are produced by bees, con-
taining high concentration of sugars, particularly monosaccharides, fructose and glucose
(Camilla Schabert, 2024). Honey also contains traces of minerals, proteins and enzymes.
The byproducts of honey productions are beeswax, propolis and honey water. honey-
water which is undervalued in the industry (Lise Hansted, 2020). Based on the provided
information by local beekeepers on how honey-water is produced, Honey-water typi-
cally is produced during the extraction process. When honey left in the sling machine is
diluted, during cleaning of the equipments with only water. The honey-water collected
from this contains not only fermentable sugars, but also aroma compounds, which are
typically described by sensory experts as floral and sweet. The chemical composition of
the honey-water has not been systematically studied, discussions with honey producers
suggests it maintain characteristics to diluted honey, increasing its potential for fermen-
tation.
Furthermore, there are currently more than 5000 beekeepers that are members in the Dan-
ish beekeeping union (Biavlerforening, n.d.). Based on the information given by the bee-
keepers an estimation of the overall production of honey-water side stream of Danish
beekeepers, is about 100000 L each season.
The most similar solution that is sugar rich for fermentation, is mead production. Honey
is diluted with water and fermented with yeast to produce an alcoholic beverage (Starow-

5.1
Mead and hydromel
Mead is one of the oldest known alcoholic beverages with evidence in China tracing back
over 6000 years. More evidence found in northern Europe and Africa. It held cultural
Mead is often called to as honey wine, which is produced by fermenting a solution of
water and honey, some recipes includes spices, fruits and herbs (of Encyclopaedia Britan-
ica, n.d.). Based on multiple studies diluted honey can be fermented with Saccharomyces
cerevisiae, a species of yeast that metabolizes monosacharides in honey, which are glucose
and fructose. Sugars are metabolized into ethanol and carbon dioxide via metabolism
pathways (glycolysis and fermentation pathways). The fermentation begins with an aer-
obic phase for yeast propagation, followed by anaerobic conditions where ethanol is pro-
duced. As honey lack sufficient nutrients, often nitrogen source and pH buffering agents
are added (Barreiros et al., 2024). The yeast activity and final alcohol by volume (ABV)
are also influenced by other factors, such as temperature and sugar concentration.
The classification of mead type can be based on ingredients and fermentation characrac-
teristics, such as melomel meaning with fruit, metheglin with spices and hydromel mean-
ing with low ABV (Angotti, n.d.).
Furthermore, the aging process of mead allows secondary metabolites, such as esters and
higher alcohols to develop after fermentation, which enhances aroma and flavour profile
(Fortes et al., 2023).
5.2
Yeast
Yeast is eukaryotic organism and can be found everywhere, in sugar rich environments
and its spores can be transported by wind to other places, eventually covering a vast area
from where it originated from (Ydenberg and Rose, 2008). A way a yeast cell develops
through budding process, forming new genetically identical as the parent cell. Starting

5.2.1
Sugars and carbon source alternatives
The sugars in honey consists mainly of monosaccharides (fructose and glucose), which
ranging from 32-44% and 23-38% respectively. The rest that represents 5-15% are di- and
trisaccharides (sucrose, maltose, trehalose etc.). The composition of the honey of its sug-
ars varies, depending on multiple factors. Monosaccharides are consumed ”faster”, as
enzyme is needed to break down complex sugars, before consumption. Also, depend-
ing on the yeast’s genes it has been studied that the affinity and preference to a certain
sugar, depends on the genes encoding of hexose transporter and kinases (Tronchoni et al.,
2009). The function of sugars is to provide carbon source and energy for a yeast’s growth
and production of byproducts (ethanol and CO2). When sugars are consumed yeasts can

5.2.2
Glycolytic pathway
The glycolytic pathway is a fundamental process that happens in the cytoplasm in the
cells. The main function of the pathway is to provide energy and intermediate molecules
as building blocks for other metabolic pathways. Glycolytic pathway is the essential
method yeast can generate energy, alongside of the production of pyruvate (Varacallo,
2023). A 6-carbon sugar, glucose is converted to two 3 carbon molecule pyruvate. Byprod-
ucts throughout the process are energy in form of ATP and NADH electron carrier. First
phase, also called the investment phase, is energy consuming and need ATP to start. Glu-
cose gets absorbed in the yeast cell; phosphorylation is the first to occur using ATP and
hexokinase enzyme for the addition of a phosphate group to glucose. Isomerization oc-
curs afterwards trough isomerization with phosphoglucose isomerase, forming fructose-
6-phosphate. ATP is then again through phosphorylation with phosphofrectokinase-1,
which then is cleaved by aldolase to form DHAP and G3P. The investment phase ends
with another isomerization, this time with triosephosphate isomerase. Both DHAP and
G3P can transform back and forth with each other, as they are in equilibrium (Varacallo,
2023).
The next phase is called the payoff phase, where energy is produced. In the beginning
2 G3P continues the reaction where oxidation of both G3P molecules occurs getting an
electron form the reduction of NAD+. This results to formation of 2 NADH energy car-
rier molecules alongside 1,3-diphosphoglycerate formation.
Substrate level phospho-
rylation of 1,3-diphosphoglycerate is carried out by phosphoglycerate kinase enzyme
forming 3-phosphoglycerate and ATP. Isomerization of 3-phosphoglycerate forms to 2-

Fermentation
A function of fermentation is to keep the cell to produce energy without oxygen, where
pyruvate from glycolytic pathway converts into ethanol and CO2 to generate NAD+ for
glycolysis to continue. In the cytoplasm, removal of CO2 occurs using enzyme pyruvate
decarboxylase (Maicas, 2020).

5.2.4
Respiration
Aerobic respiration has its function to generate energy in the presence of oxygen. The re-
actants are oxygen and glucose or other fermentable sugars. The products are water, CO2
and ATP (adenosine triphosphate) which is cells main energy currency. From glycolysis
where glucose (sugar) goes through the 10 steps of glycolysis gaining pyruvate in the
cytoplasm, which also produces 2 ATP and 2 NADH (T and A, 2014). A reaction occurs
towards aerobic respiration, which is mostly common in the beginning of a fermentation
or when there is oxygen available. Entering the mitochondria, oxidative decarboxylation
of pyruvate and redox results to acetyl-CoA. CO2 release and NAD+ is reduced to NADH
(T and A, 2014).
Formula: Pyruvate →acetyl-CoA + CO2+NADH
The acetyl-CoA, then continues towards the citric acid cycle (also known as Krebs cycle).
In mitochondria matrix, acetyl-CoA goes through of series of reactions where end result
is the oxidation of acetyl-CoA to CO2, along side the reduction of NAD+ to NADH, FAD+
to FADH and generating ATP (T and A, 2014).
Formula (per cycle):
Acetyl-CoA →2CO2 + 3NADH + FADH2 + ATP
Inner part of the mitochondria, the two energy storage molecules donates electrons to the
electron transport chain (ETC), via redox and oxidative phosphorylation. This creates a
gradient of proton, pumping proton (H+), as electrons moves through protein complexes.

5.4
Pasteurization
The pasteurization method was discovered by a French chemist, Louis Pasteur. He dis-
covered that by applying heat treatment, spoilage microorganisms in wine may be re-
duced at temperatures less than boiling temperature (Bhadekar and Bhola, 2019). The
main purpose of pasteurization is to extend shelf life of a food product, usually in liquid
form, by inactivation of most vegetative microorganism and inhibiting their activity. This
decreases the risk of spoilage or pathogenic microorganisms to cause harm towards con-
sumers or decrease the quality of the food product (Bhadekar & Bhola, 2019; Deak, 2014).
The spores of spore forming microorganism are not affected by pasteurization alone, as
their spores are more heat resistant, needing at least 121◦C to inactivate. Furthermore,
there are also thermophile microorganisms that thrive at high temperatures, such from
Bacillus spp. that can survive at boiling points till 120◦C (Holzapfel et al., 2022).
The heat treatment depends on the temperature, as the duration of treatment required
changes accordingly to achieve the same effectiveness or PU value. PU value is a unit

Theory
less value, that quantifies fatality of the heat treatment, against vegetative cells (Bhadekar
and Bhola, 2019). The temperature pasteurization works at is below boiling temperature
(100◦C).
To estimate the temperature and duration of a pasteurization treatment, a similar treat-
ment to beer is assumed (Rachon et al., 2018). Based on the pasteurization unit (PU)
formula, PU can be calculated based on the temperature and duration.
The formula to calculate PU is:

5.5.1
Sensory perception in food
The consumption of food involves multisensory experience; it is an integration of the five
senses. The five senses interact for instance when food is served, that shapes the hedonic
responses and evaluation or judgements that concerns the quality, safety and edibility of
the food presented. In addition, the act of eating begins with the perception of sight and
smell before oral ingestion (Ohla, 2020 , Zhang et al., 2024).
The sight corresponds to vision, the ability to form images by perceiving light. Providing
cues and informs the consumer on the food’s ripeness, freshness and spoilage through
its properties, such as its shape, brightness and colour saturation. For example, before
tasting, a red apple may be perceived to be sweeter than a green apple (Ohla, 2020).
Smell is related to the olfaction system; it is the ability of perceiving smells and interpret-
ing them by the chemical signals detected from the environment. The olfaction system
involves two means, orthonasal and retronasal to established flavour. Olfactory sensors
in the olfactory epithelium are stimulated by volatile compounds released by the food,
sends signal to the olfactory cortex of the brain, which integrating the perception of odour
with taste and memory (Ohla, 2020).
Taste is the ability to perceive flavours facilitated by the receptors of the tongue, also
known as taste buds. The receptors respond to the five basic tastes: Sweet, salty, sour,
bitter and umami. When chemical compounds or molecules from the food dissolves in
saliva, interacts with chemoreceptors, which triggers neural signals via nerves to the gus-
tatory cortex. Although gustation is essential, the olfaction is what to the perception of
flavour depends on. For example, individual who are anosmic describes food as taste-
less, which indicates the interdependence between olfaction and flavour (Behrens and
Munger, 2021).
Somatosensation; provides information on the environment through physical touch, a
complex system involving tactile, in the mouth referred to as mouthfeel. Providing feed-

5.5.3
Honey’s sensory attributes
The complexity of honey’s sensory profile is varying based on bees’ source of flowers,
region they live in and honey processing conditions. Honey has a wide range of colour,
ranging from colourless to dark brown. It depends highly on which type of flower bees
harvest nectar from, weather, pollen, minerals, amino acids, phenolic compounds and
carotenoids pigments. Storage conditions where honey is stored at higher temperatures
have been studied to show its colour to darken over time. This non-enzymatic reaction
called Maillard reaction can occur when honey is stored in room temperatures and higher,
as interaction of carbonyl groups from sugars and groups from amino acids produces
melanoidins, which is characterized by having a brown colour to it. Darker honeys con-
tain higher content of polyphenols and minerals, than lighter coloured honeys. Polyphe-
nols, such as flavonoids has antioxidant effect, majority of it comes from the propolis that
bees produce with plant derived compounds and own produced substances (enzymes,
beeswax etc.) (Salonen and Ollikka, 2016).
Clarity of honey varies from clear to cloudy, which could be caused by the content of
pollen that have gotten into the honey while harvesting. Nectar source could also con-
tribute to the cloudiness, as the nectar may contains particles from the bees and flowers.
Honey with a high moisture content could appear cloudier, it is more common in freshly
harvested raw honey (Davenport, n.d.). Also, clarity of the honey is affected by the crys-
tallization of the honey, as it gives a milky appearance. The rate of crystallization is related
Page 21 of 81
==PAGE:23==

Theory
to the content of glucose in honey. The higher the glucose amount the faster honey will
crystallize, however, the crystallization may developed larger grains of crystals and are
coarser, may affect how consumers accept the overall texture (Weber et al., 2022).
Furthermore, the viscosity of honey varies from thick to more fluid and it is affected by the
temperature of the environment and its water content. In lower temperature, depending
on the amount of glucose in the honey, crystallization may occur over time, as mentioned.
The water content of honey when harvested varies from 20% to 16%, depending on the
type of honey that is harvested (Salonen and Ollikka, 2016).
This leads to the honey’s consistency, that varies from smooth, creamy to grainy (crys-
talized). The mouthfeel would be thick and on some occasions sticky in relation to its

tar would have more distinct aroma profile than others. The primary aroma notes are
fruity, floral, herbal etc. Their intensity varies from mild to strong, which is also influ-
enced by the source of nectar. Through aroma, the freshness of honey can be determined.
Generally, a clean and pleasant aroma indicates fresh honey (Salonen and Ollikka, 2016 ,
Cicha-Wojciechowicz et al., 2024a).
Taste of honey is dominated by the high amount of sugar and hence its sweetness is the
main attribute, which has varying in intensity based on the types of sugars and their
concentration. Honey’s aftertaste varies depending on the type and may leave floral or

5.5.4
Sensory attributes expected in mead and hydromel
Mead and hydromel shares similarities, however there are still some characteristics dif-
ferences. The similarities are in base-ingredient, sensory profile and fermentation. Both
primary uses water, honey and yeast. Fermentation is an alcoholic fermentation, where
both are fermented at around 20◦C, depending on the yeast chosen. Duration of fermen-

Their appearances and flavour profile are similar and depending on the type of honey
used and both can have fermented notes. Also, the versatility of both types is great, as
they both can be flavoured with additional ingredients, such as herbs, spices and fruits
(Starowicz and Granvogl, 2020b , Meintsma, n.d.).
Differences between mead and hydromel are variations in their final alcohol content, in-
tensity of flavours and duration of fermentation. A typical alcohol content of mead is

content and hydromel is diluted with more water. Consequently, would the flavour and

flavour with more complex honey aroma. While hydromel has lighter and milder honey
notes on both flavour and aroma (Reitenbach et al., 2025a , Meintsma, n.d.).
Overall, mead and hydromel shares overlapping sensory qualities, that is expressed at
almost the opposite of intensities. Mead being more intense and complex than hydromel
and needing a longer duration of fermentation and can be aged, resulting to a higher
ABV and more full body. Hydromel is fermented at a faster rate and produced for as a
seasonal drink. In this project, as acacia honey is used, it is expected to have a milder
overall sensory characteristic.

to quantify the changes of the visual characteristics. The final product was not filtered;
this means residuals and suspended solids increased difficulty to assess final clarity with
more precision. The limitations limit the objectivity and repeatability of visual evalua-

filtration protocols would increase the visual evaluation to be precise and more reliable.

Conclusion
9
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to investigate whether honey-water side stream
could effectively fermented to produce ethanol, through monitoring fermentative pro-
cess.
Key findings from analyses showed a gradual decrease in brix, density and pH over the
fermentation period. There was an increase of cell density (cell growth), supporting that
the environment supported fermentation. HPLC results confirmed sugar utilization and
ethanol production, which validates successful fermentation. However, CFU counts on
YPD agar plates revealed microbial contamination, which could have affected the fer-
mentation process and product safety. Overall, results collectively demonstrate the po-
tential of honey-water for ethanol fermentation, proven by the sugar consumed, microbial
growth and ethanol produced. However, the microbial contamination was detected and
should be addressed in future optimizations. While ethanol produced was achieved dur-
ing the fermentation periods, as it was only by analogue experiments with store bought
honey, it does not represents the outcome with the real side stream.
The final product of the experiment with lower initial brix (3% brix) exhibited undesirable
cheese- like off odour, which is not included in a desired aroma profile. This off odour
may be associated to potential contamination and stress experienced by the yeast during
fermentation. In contrast, experiments with higher brix levels have been associated with
a more favourable aroma profile. The finding suggests that with the current recipe, op-
timization of the initial brix and ensuring increase of microbial control are both crucial
to achieving a more desirable aroma profile. In relation to the final appearance of the
experiments, murkiness is present and could be filtered off to achieve a clearer product,
aligning with the similar products available in the market. Future development should
be focused on refining conditions during fermentations, including optimization of brix
level, monitoring yeast health and overall optimization of the recipe, as prevention on
developing off odours.
Furthermore, future research and in product development should be focused on opti-

Future perspective
10
Future perspective
Although this study explored the fermentation potential of a model honey solution by
mimicking a side stream by diluting store bought honey. Due to seasonal limitations it
was not possible to access the real side stream material. The focus in future work should
be on utilizing the actual side stream material produced in the honey industry. It is cru-
cial as there are natural variations in the side stream, such as sugar content, aroma com-
pounds and overall composition between sources, subsequently influencing fermentation
performances and the quality of the final product. Furthermore, future considerations in
fermenting at industrial volume size should be conducted to observe, for changes in per-
formance of the fermentation. When considering working with the actual side stream
material, consider standardizing procedures of cleaning methods to increase replicability
and the quality of the raw material collected.
To achieve complete fermentation, improvement on the nutrient source should be con-
sidered as well. Consider combining other nutrient sources, addition of vitamins and
minerals to support growth and production of ethanol. Also, consider working with ad-
ditional nutrient pitching mid fermentation.
Furthermore, the consumer study was limited to a survey on consumers’ preferences
and consumption habits, and multiple communication platforms should be considered
to increase statistical significance. Future studies should also include direct sensory eval-
uation of the final product, including panel participants and not only rely on three tested
experts. The combination of survey data with actual product testing allows for a thor-
ough understanding of consumers acceptance, resulting to a well rounded insights on
consumer preference, expectations and potential in the market.
Sensory of multiple source honey side-stream opens for exploring how aroma and flavour
profiles develop through the fermentation, when using the actual side stream material. It
may take a while to work on single type side-streams. It may have to be a combination
of side streams from different beekeepers, that have different honey with different floral
origin.
