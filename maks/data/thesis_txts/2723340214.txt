Abstract
Background: Narcolepsy is a chronic sleep disorder that affects 1 in 2000 people. Many narcoleptic patients
experience sudden loss of muscle strength known as cataplexy. Cataplexy together with daytime sleepiness
makes the disorder very exhausting for the patient’s personal life and it often reduces life quality. Narcolepsy
exists in two types, type 1 and type 2, the difference being whether it includes cataplexy (type 1) or not
(type 2). Studies have found that narcoleptic patients are more costly than other patients because of their
higher frequency of contacts to the primary healthcare system compared to other patient groups. The golden
method to diagnose narcolepsy is a MSLT. A MSLT is a very time consuming and costly method that takes
a lot of resources to do. An alternative method is PSG but has not been fully tested.
Purpose: The purpose of this thesis is to investigate if it is possible to use PSG instead of MSLT to
diagnose narcolepsy type 1 and 2 by use of an automatic algorithm and in this way improve the current
method of diagnosing narcolepsy. PSG has been tested on narcolepsy patients before but only compared to a
healthy control group and not a group which contains other sleeping disorders like insomnia, parasomnia etc.
Method: The goal of this thesis is the construct an automatic algorithm that may produce a simpler
and less expensive procedure for diagnosing narcolepsy type 1 and type 2 patients by using the information
in the PSG test instead of the more expensive MSLT test. The testing in this thesis is done by way of data
supplied by the sleeping clinic at Rigshospitalet-Glostrup.
In the thesis the proposed algorithm consists of a statistical feature calculated on hypnodensities, to-
gether with level of hypocretin-1, from 227 sleeping observations and a support vector machine classifier.
These observations were either a MSLT (110 of them were) or PSG (117 of them were). All the patients in
the database were investigated to have narcolepsy type 1 or 2. Therefore will some patient appear in both
the PSG and MSLT group. The PSG data has been divided into two groups (PSG day and PSG night)
and with the MSLT data it brings about three groups. The hypnodensities were calculated on the data
from these three groups. Hereafter three classification problems were formulated. The first classification
problem was designed to see if narcolepsy type 1 could be separated from a control group. The second
classification problem was designed to see if narcolepsy type 2 and a control group could be separated. The
last classification problem was designed to see if narcolepsy type 1, type 2, and a control group could be
separated into three groups. For each of the classification problems three classifiers were made with each of
the three modalities in the database (PSG Day, PSG Night and MSLT). The performance of each classifier
is measured by several specific quality measures.
Results: The performance of all three modalities in the first classification problem was identical. They
achieved 100% in both accuracy, sensitivity, PPV, specificity, and F1 score. The only feature used in all
three classifiers was the level of hypocretin-1, which indicates that all other features used were less important
than the level of hypocretin-1 in NT1 patients. In classification problem two and three the results were more
unclear. In all modalities the performance was low, which can have multiple reasons.
Conclusion: Based on the results from the testing in this thesis, an automatic algorithm is proposed
that looks at clinical diagnostic features in PSG. The following answer can consequently be given to the
question in the problem statement that it seems possible to improve the current diagnostic method for
narcolepsy with an automatic algorithm with the proposed features.

Baggrund: Narkolepsi er en kronisk søvnsygdom, der rammer 1 ud af 2000 mennesker. Mange narkolep-
tiske patienter oplever pludselige tab af muskelstyrke, kendt som catapleksi.
Catapleksi i forening med
pludselige anfald af søvnbehov om dagen gør sygdommen meget belastende for patienternes daglige liv og
reducerer ofte deres livskvalitet. Narkolepsi findes i to typer, type 1 og 2, hvor forskellen er, om den om-
fatter catapleksi (narkolepsi type 1) eller ej (narkolepsi type 2). Studier har vist, at narkoleptiske patienter
har større samfundsmæssige omkostninger som følge af mere hyppige kontakter med sundhedsvæsenet end
andre patientgrupper. Den gældende diagnosticeringsmetode for narkolepsi er en MSLT-test (Multiple Sleep
Latency Test). Denne test er tids- og ressourcekrævende og derfor dyr. En alternativ metode er en PSG
(Polysomnography), men dette alternativ er ikke tilstrækkelig testet.
Form˚al: Form˚alet med dette speciale er at undersøge, om det er muligt ved konstruktion af en automatisk
algoritme at forbedre den nuværende diagnosticeringsmetode for narkolepsi type 1 og type 2 ved at anvende
PSG i stedet for MSLT . PSG er tidligere blevet testet p˚a narkolepsi-patienter, men kun i sammenligning med
en kontrolgruppe af raske personer og ikke en gruppe, som indeholder patienter med andre søvnproblemer
som søvnløshed, parasomni mv.
Metode: M˚alet i dette speciale er at konstruere en automatisk algoritme, som kan udgøre en mere simpel
og billigere diagnosticeringsmetode for narkolepsi type 1 og 2 patienter gennem at udnytte informationen i
PSG-testen i stedet for den dyrere MSLT-test. Alle tests i denne rapport er sket med data, som er stillet til
r˚adighed af søvnklinikken p˚a Rigshospitalet-Glostrup.
Den automatiske algoritme, der foresl˚as i dette speciale, best˚ar af en række statistiske beregningsvariable,
der er beregnet ud fra sandsynlighederfunktioner, kaldet hypnodensiteter, ud fra 227 observationer af sovende
patienter og ved hjælp af klassifikationsmetoden Support Vector Machine (SVM). Disse søvnobservationer
var forud registreret som enten MSLT (110 stk.) eller PSG (117 stk.). Alle patienter i databasen var diag-
nosticeret med enten narkolepsi type 1 eller 2 og kan optræde i b˚ade MSLT- og PSG-gruppen. PSG-data
blev i testen underopdelt i 2 grupper (PSG dag og PSG nat), og med MSLT-gruppen blev der s˚aledes i alt
3 datagrupper. Hypnodensiteterne blev konstrueret p˚a data i disse 3 grupper. Herefter blev der opstillet
3 klassifikationsproblemer. Det første problem var at undersøge, om narkolepsi type 1 kunne adskilles fra
en kontrolgruppe.
Det andet klassifikationsproblem var tilsvarende, om narkolepsi 2 kunne adskilles fra
en kontrolgruppe. Det sidste klassifikationsproblem var at undersøge, om narkolepsi type 1, type 2 og en
kontrolgruppe kunne adskilles fra hinanden, dvs. i tre grupper. For hvert klassifikationsproblem blev der
konstrueret 3 classifikationsalgoritmer tilhørende de tre datagrupper. Præstationen for hver algoritme blev
vurderet ud fra en række specifikke kvalitetsm˚al.
Resultater: Præstationen for alle tre datagrupper i første klassifikationsproblem havde ensartet m˚alopfyldelse.
De opn˚aede 100% i b˚ade præcision, følsomhed, PPV, specificitet og F1 score . Den eneste feature, der blev
brugt i alle tre algoritmer, var niveauet for hypocretin-1 for narkolepsi type 1 patienter, hvilket tyder p˚a, at
alle andre features, der blev brugt, var mindre relevante end hypocretin-1 niveauet for disse patienter. I de
to andre klassifikationsproblemer var resultaterne mere uklare. I alle datagrupper var præstationen lave for
classifikationsalgoritmerne, hvilket kan have mange forklaringer.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Narcolepsy is a chronic sleep disorder that affects 1 in 2000 people. The classical symptoms are excessive
daytime sleepiness and hard time sleeping during the night. Many narcoleptic patients also experience sud-
den loss of muscle strength known as cataplexy[1]. When cataplexy occurs the patient can fall to the ground,
risking fall-associated injury [2]. Cataplexy together with the daytime sleepiness makes the disorder very
exhausting for the patient’s personal life and it often reduces life quality[3]. Cataplexy can vary from seconds
to minutes and the patient is mentally awake during that time, but the patient’s body is in a sleeping state.
Narcolepsy exists in two types, type 1 and type 2, the difference being whether it includes cataplexy
(type 1) or not (type 2). The reason for the development of narcolepsy together with the mechanisms that
cause the symptoms of the disorder are partially unknown. The most believed hypothesis is that the disorder
is an autoimmune disease that occurs in the hypothalamus in the brain. It is unknown what triggers this
sudden reaction from the immune system to attack the patient’s cells. The disorder often begins in childhood
and progresses as time goes by, but there is no upper age limit of getting the disease. There is no cure for
narcolepsy, only treatment of symptoms and patients have to take medicine for the rest of their life[1]. The
patients will experience a decrease in work effort together with serious disruptions in their daily life. Because
of these effects of narcolepsy on the patients the community pays a very high price in form of medicine and
loss in workforce [4][3].
Narcoleptic patients are more expensive to society than other patients[3]. When comparing the frequency
of contacts of narcoleptic patients with the frequency of an average patient control group, narcoleptic pa-
tients have more contact with the primary healthcare system (98% vs. 94%), they are more hospitalized
(25% vs. 12%), and they take medication more often (84 vs. 76%), together with the fact that 67% of
them receive public support for their medication (vs. 42% in the control group). The study also shows that
fewer narcoleptic patients receive income from employment compared to the control group. Furthermore
narcoleptic patients have a lower employment rate, and it decreases over time after receiving the diagnose of
narcolepsy. The estimated cost of a narcoleptic patient is €8,107 per year were an average control patient
cost €5,519[3]. The study thus shows that narcoleptic patients have a bigger cost to society than patients
in an average control group, which makes it important to improve the treatment of the disorder.
Because of the unknown pathology it is very difficult to come up with a treatment for narcolepsy. One
way to get a better understanding of the pathology of narcolepsy is to improve the diagnostic method of
the disorder. A studie from 2015 found that almost 50% of patients labelled with a diagnosis of narcolepsy
was incorrectly diagnosed[5]. The diagnostic method has then been updated and the new diagnostic criteria
(ICSD-3) have a higher accuracy than the previous method (ICSD-2). The golden standard for diagnosing
narcolepsy is doing a Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT) and controlling that the patient fulfills the ICSD-
3 criteria[6]. Even though the diagnostic method has been upgraded there is still room for improvement,
especially when it comes to differentiating between narcolepsy type 1 and 2[7][8].

1.1
Motivation
As mentioned above narcolepsy has a very big impact on the individual patient’s life but also on the commu-
nity in the form of cost in medicine and loss of workforce. One way to improve the life quality for patients
and decrease the cost for the community is by improving the diagnostic method. The golden method to
diagnose narcolepsy is as mentioned a MSLT. A MSLT is a very time consuming and costly method that
takes a lot of resources to do[9]. Often a test before the MSLT is done to exclude other disorders, especially
sleep disorders. This test is called Polysomnography (PSG) and is cheaper than a MSLT, which is why
many have looked into whether PSG can be used to diagnose narcolepsy[10]. Increasing healthcare expenses
in general makes it interesting to decrease the cost of diagnosing testing[11]. If a PSG test is sufficient to
diagnose narcolepsy with the same precision and accuracy as a MSLT then the diagnostic cost for narcolepsy
will fall. Machine learning algorithms are becoming increasingly more popular in the healthcare system and
therefore an obvious tool to use to investigate if PSG can replace MSLT eventually[12].
Automatic algorithms with the purpose of diagnosing narcolepsy have been present in recent years.
Several research laboratories have focused on automatic diagnosing narcolepsy from a patient group with
multiple sleep disorders like insomnia, sleep-disordered breathing (SDB), periodic leg movement disorder
(PLM) etc.[13][14]. Stephansen et al. have focused on automatic diagnosing narcolepsy type 1 (NT1) from
non-narcoleptic patients, and they achieved high accuracy and high performance by looking only on PSG
data[9]. Others again have focused on automatic diagnosing between NT1 and type 2 narcolepsy (NT2)[7].
This project will have the same focus as Stephansen et al. testing if PSG can have the same accuracy on
diagnosing narcolepsy as MSLT. A more detailed literature review can be seen in chapter three, State of
the art. Helping narcoleptic patients may also result in helping other patients groups i.e. patients with
Parkinson’s diseases, because narcoleptic symptoms have also been observed with Parkinson patients [15].
1.2
Problem statement
The purpose of this thesis is to improve the diagnostic method of narcolepsy (NT1 and NT2) from none
narcoleptic patients. Such an improvement will have two main advantages: a more accurate or simplied
diagnosis to the benefit of both the patients and society in the form of a reduction in testing expenses. The
problem statement can be summarised in this question:
Is it possible to construct an automatic algorithm that improves the current diagnostic method for
narcolepsy?
1.3
Goals and methods
To solve the above problem statement an automatic machine learning algorithm is chosen. This approach is
inspired by the methodology of recent papers with similar aims [9][7] and by the increasing use of machine
learning algorithms in the healthcare system [12]. Such algorithms can identify unknown patterns in nar-
coleptic patients that may improve diagnosing NT1 and NT2 based only on PSG data.
The proposed method in this thesis will be focusing on diagnosing narcolepsy from PSG data. The PSG
data will include registrations in two time intervals, afternoon and night. This is a new aspect compared
to former research. The proposed method will also be tested on MSLT data to verify if it is possible to
diagnose narcolepsy with a machine learning algorithm. The algorithms should also be compared to the
current golden standard for diagnosing NT1 and NT2. The golden standard will be set to have an accuracy
and positive prediction value (PPV) at 100% because it is the best diagnostic method for narcolepsy at this
point. Therefore the proposed solution in this thesis can never be better than the golden standard but has
the aim to achieve the same accuracy and PPV with a much less financial cost. The algorithm is required
to perform automatically without the mediation of a person. This is chosen because if a person is needed
to supervise the algorithm, this will not release any labour cost. The algorithm will be validated by testing.
The chosen statistical validation parameters are sensitivity, PPV and accuracy because many similar projects

Chapter 2
Clinical Background
This chapter gives some background about narcolepsy. First the pathology and etiology of the disease will
be outlined, then the diagnostic methods will be presented, PSG and MSLT, and in the end the treatment
of narcolepsy will be mentioned.
2.1
Narcolepsy
Narcolepsy is a chronic sleep disorder, where the patients experience extreme daytime drowsiness and sudden
sleep seizures. Because of these symptoms the patients often find it difficult to stay awake for longer time
periods [1]. The clinical background for narcolepsy is partially unknown. There are two types of narcolepsy,
NT1 and NT2. The two subtypes have similar clinical profiles, but differences in the level of hypocretin-1 in
the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). In NT1 the level of hypocretin-1 is measured lower than 110 pg/ml [16]. In
NT2 the level of hypocretin-1 is intermediate (between 110 and 200 pg/ml) or normal (above 200 pg/ml).
Low level of hypocretin-1 can often cause cataplexy, and it is therefore more seen in NT1 patients than
NT2 patients. Cataplexy is a sudden episode of muscle weakness while the patient is fully conscious. These
episodes are transient and are typically triggered by emotions such as laughing or crying [17].
In recent papers cataplexy has been divided into two categories, typical and atypical cataplexy. Typical
cataplexy is a seizure that happens in both brain halves (bilaterally symmetrical), it lasts less than two
minutes, and it is provoked by strong emotion, especially of positive nature. A typical cataplexy seizure has
to meet all the listed criteria under typical cataplexy in table 2.1. An atypical cataplexy seizure also meets
all the criteria for typical cataplexy but with one additional or contradiction criterion. These additional or
contradiction criteria are listed under atypical cataplexy in table 2.1 [18].

pathology and etiology. It is believed that NT1 is an autoimmune disorder where the hypocreatin-producing
neurons in the hypothalamus is damaged by the immune cells. Hypothalamus is located deep inside the brain,
see figure 2.1. The pathogenesis is believed to be that a trigger event happens, so the immune cells start
to attach the hypocreatin-producing neurons. These neurons are located in the hypothalamus and produce
hypocretins. Hypocretins are neurotransmitters, a chemical that transfer a signal from one neuron to another
neuron. Hypocretins are a specific neurotransmitter that only targets specific neurons. The target neurons
are some that promote wakefulness and suppress rapid eye movement (REM) sleep. Therefore when lacking
hypocreatin-producing neurons, as patients with NT1 do, the brain cannot regulate sleep and wakefulness
properly. These patients experience excessive daytime sleepiness, and they are unable to fully sleep at night.
In NT2 the patients’ hypocreatin-producing neurons can be reduced but it is not always the case. The
pathology for NT2 is therefore unknown and most likely a condition with many root causes (heterogeneous
disorder) [1].
2.1.1
Genetic risk factors
One of the major genetic risk factors is variations in the genes that encode major histocompatibility complex
(MHC) class II, also called human leukocyte antigen (HLA) genes. MHC molecules, in short, are used to
present an antigen (foreign particulate matter) to the T cells, which then starts a process to destroy the
cells that contain the antigen. HLA genes code for some parts of the MHC molecule. The main genetic risk
factors for narcolepsy are the HLA-DQB1*06:02 allele. HLA-DQB1*06:02 allele is a specific DNA sequence
of the HLA-DQB1 gene in the genome. The HLA-DQB1 gene codes for one of the building blocks to the
MHC class II molecule. About 86-89% (depending on the ethnic composition of the population) of patients
with NT1 carry the HLA-DQB1*06:02 allele but only 1 per 1,000 individual with the HLA-DQB1*06:02
allele develops narcolepsy. Therefore it cannot be used alone as a diagnostic measurement. The frequency
of the HLA-DQB1*06:02 allele varies from 5% to 38% in the general population. The HLA-DQB1*06:02
allele is most found in European populations (25%) and African Americans (38%) and less in Jewish and
Sardinian populations (¡5%). Patients that carry HLA-DQB1*06:02 often also carry HLA-DQA1*01:02 on
the same chromosome. This high linkage does not change the risk or slightly increase the risk of develop-
ing narcolepsy. This indicates that HLA-DQB1*06:02 is very important for the development of narcolepsy [1].
Other genetic factors are molecules that interact with the MHC molecule or regulate autoimmunity.
Variance in the genes that encode for T cells receptors alpha-chain and beta-chain, called TRA and TRB
respectively, are associated with NT1.
Also other genes that codes for the MCH class II molecule, like
variants of encoding pro-cathepsin H (CTSH) and TNFSF4, which encodes for tumour necrosis factors
ligand superfamily member 4 (which regulates immune cell life), are associated with the developing of
narcolepsy.
Variation in immune-regulatory genes is also associated with narcolepsy such as variants of
P2RY11, IL10RB-IFNAR1 and ZNF365 [1].
2.1.2

2009 the H1N1 influenza A virus spread across the world. After vaccination with Pandemrix H1N1 vaccine

in Finland, Sweden, Norway, Ireland, France and United Kingdom. Vaccinated individuals younger than
20 years of age had the highest risk of developing the disease. The Pandemrix H1N1 vaccine reportedly
increased the relatively risk by 3 to 12.7-fold but the incidence of narcolepsy remained low as only 1 out of

Other vaccines that targeted H1N1 influenza A virus were not associated with an increase in NT1 incidence.

2.2
Diagnostic methods
First the PSG method will be outlined. This is a very common method in diagnosing sleep disorders. After
that the MSLT will be presented that is the golden standard for diagnosing narcolepsy. In MSLT a PSG
is measured but it is done in a specific environment that triggers some sleep stages that can be used to
differentiate between narcolepsy and other sleep disorders.
2.2.1
Polysomnography
PSG is a multi-parameter study of sleep, and it is a diagnostic tool in sleep medicine.
It records the
physiological changes that occur during sleep, often at night. In the PSG method many body functions
are monitored including brain activity (electroencephalography (EEG)), eye movement (electrooculography
(EOG)), skeletal muscle movement (electromyography (EMG)), heart rhythm (electrocardiography (ECG)),
respiratory airflow, pulse oximetry and other parameters depending on the study. To measure the brain
activity six electrodes and two reference electrodes will often be used. All electrodes are placed on the scalp
according to the International 10-20 system. The electrodes are either placed near the frontal lobe (near the
top of the head) or over the occipital lobe (the back of the head). From these electrodes the brain activity
during sleep can be measured and scored into different sleep stages: wake, Rapid eye movement (REM), Non
Rapid eye movement 1 (N1), N2 and N3. For eye movement two electrodes are used, one electrode is placed
1 cm above the outer canthus of the right eye and the other is placed 1 cm below the outer canthus of the
left eye. The electrodes measure the movement in the vertical plane and are used to see if the patient is in
NREM or REM sleep. The skeletal muscles are also measured with electrodes. One electrode is placed on
the chin above the jawline and one below. Two electrodes are placed on each leg to measure leg movement.
The two electrodes near the chin help, like the EOG electrodes, with determine when sleep and REM sleep
occurs. The two leg electrodes are used to diagnose Periodic limb movement disorder (PLMD). In PSG only
two to three electrodes are used, despite the normal 10 electrodes that are placed on the body to measure
ECG. The electrodes used in the PSG are the ones that contain the P-wave, QRS-complex and T-wave.
The respiratory airflow is measured using a pressure transducer fitted in or near the nostrils and the pulse
oximetry measures the changes in blood oxygen level. It usually is located over the fingertip or an earlobe
[21].
2.2.2
Multiple Sleep Latency Test
MSLT is the gold standard for diagnosing narcolepsy. The purpose of the test is to detect the number of
trails with REM sleep the patients obtain and the mean value of sleep latency. The structure of the test is
that the patients go through four (but if sleep onset rapid eye movement (SOREM) occurs then five) trails
where in each trail the patient is instructed to sleep. In each trail a PSG is measured. If the patient goes
into the REM sleep stage, that trail counts as a trail with REM in it. If REM sleep stage occurs within 15
minutes of onset of sleep, then that trail contains a SOREM event [22]. Between each onset of trail, two
hours have to occur, or there should be one and a half hours between the terminal of the previous trail to
the next trail beginning. Figure 2.2 shows a diagram of the two first trails. There can be multiple reasons
to terminate the trail such as, no sleep in 20 minutes, sleeps for 15 minutes, falls in sleep but wakes up after
20 minutes of the onset of trail, as soon as the first REM epoch appears etc. The test has to begin one and
a half to three hours after the patient has woken from night sleep. It is recommended that the patient have
had more or equal to six hours of total sleep the night before. The patient is instructed to avoid the following
multiple things like, caffeine and alcohol during the day, smoking prior to each trail, vigorous activity during
the day, falling sleep between the trails, bright lights and direct sunlight etc. Another purpose of the test is
to measure the mean value of sleep latency. Sleep latency is the time between the light off in the room to
the patient is in the first sleep stage. If the mean value between all trails is less than eight minutes, then it
indicates abnormalities in the patient’s sleep pattern [23].

2.2.3
Other diagnostic methods
Besides PSG and MSLT other methods are used to help diagnose narcolepsy, like measuring hypocretin-1
levels in the brain and symptoms like the presence of cataplexy. According to the third edition of the ICSD
guidelines for diagnosing narcolepsy there are multiple criteria there must be fulfilled to be diagnosed with
narcolepsy. For NT1, the patient must meet the two criteria:
A The patient has daily periods of irrepressible need to sleep or daytime lapses into sleep occurring for
at least 3 months
B The presence of one (or both) of the following:
(a) Cataplexy and a mean sleep latency of less or equal to 8 minutes and two or more SOREMPs on
an MSLT
(b) CSF hypocretin-1 concentration, measured by immunoreactivity, is either less or equal to 100pg/mL
or < 1/3 of mean values obtained in normal subjects with the same standardized assay
For narcolepsy type 2, the patient must meet all the listed criteria:
A The patient has daily periods of irrepressible need to sleep or daytime lapses into sleep occurring for
at least 3 months
B A mean sleep latency of less or equal to 8 minutes and two or more SOREMPs are found on a MSLT
C Cataplexy is absent
D Either CSF hypocretin-1 concentration has not been measured or CSF hypocretin-1 concentration
measured by immunoreactivity is either more than 110 pg/mL or more than 1/3 of mean values
obtained in normal subjects with the same standardized assay
E The hypersomnolence and/or MSLT findings are not better explained by other causes such as in-
sufficient sleep, obstructive sleep apnea, delayed sleep phase disorder, or the effect of medication or
substances or their withdrawal.
Besides the guidelines of the ICSD-3 there is also a DSM-5 guideline to diagnose narcolepsy type 1 and 2 [6].
2.3
Treatment of Narcolepsy
Narcolepsy is treated with drugs. These drugs reduce symptoms that narcoleptic patients often experience,
like daytime sleepiness, cataplexy and night sleep disturbance. The most often used drugs are Modafinil,
Methylphenidate and Amphetamine that all reduce daytime sleepiness. Another often used drug is Antide-
pressants, these help against cataplexy. Often the patients take multiple drugs for multiple symptoms like
Amphetamine and Antidepressants for daytime sleepiness and cataplexy. Unfortunately the treatment is
often lifelong and it does not prevent the symptoms - the patient will only experience milder symptoms [4].

Purpose: To optimize the method of MSLT to make the diagnostics criteria more specific to Narcolepsy.

diagnostic criteria for NC. In the statistical analysis they compared normal distribution data with Student’s

analysis of normally distributed data, they used Pearson’s r coefficient.
They also did a comparison of
continuous measures between the four groups with a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). To find the
optimal MSLT thresholds Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was used. The significance
was accepted at p<0.05. A flowchart over the whole process can be seen in figure 3.3.

Purpose: To find other clinical features than SOREMPs that can be identified in NT1. The golden

Data Description: In this study they used the CAP database from the Sleep Disorders Centre of the
Ospedale Maggiore of Parma in Italy. The database contains 108 PSG recordings which all consist of mul-

interested in the EEG-signal, so they chose to focus on the EEG channels F4-C4 and C4-A1 which had a

Method: After the two EEG channels were extracted from the PSG data, the EEG were filtered. It

filter to analyze the data and a 38-order filter to synthesise. They obtained eight subbands in total, one for
approximation coefficients and seven for detail coefficients. From these eight subbands the Hjorth parameters
were computed. Three Hjorth parameters from each of the eight subbands gave 24 time-domain features
from a signal EEG channel. All the features were then fed into a classifier. Here they tested on multiple
classifiers and validated it with a 10-fold cross validation. A flowchart over the whole process can be seen in

The best performing study was Zhang et al.[7] using clinical features from PSG and MSLT, which is due
to a high number of patients. In spite of this the best ranked study was chosen to be Stephansen et al.[9]
for the following reasons. In Zhang et al. they only reported having one misclassification, which makes you
suspect that the model is not tested sufficiently, and that the model may be overfitting. But when looking at
the approach and methodology they use it seems like the model is tested sufficiently. The methodology they
follow in Zhang et al. [7] is less complex than the one performed by Stephansen et al. [9], indicating that
there should be a clear difference in the patterns for Narcolepsy . The main difference concerning the purpose
of the paper between Zhang et. al [7] and Stephansen et al. [9] is that Stephansen et al. classify between
healthy subjects and narcolepsy patients whereas Zhang et al. [7] classify between the two different types
of narcolepsy. Therefore the chosen approach in this thesis is to design a classifier that classifies between
different types of sleep disorders, more precise narcolepsy versus other sleep disorders.
In the newest papers (Buettner et al. [26], Sharma, Patel, et al. [13] and Sharma, Tiwari, et al. [27]) the
primary data modality is EEG signals. The performance of these three papers are significantly lower than
the two best performing papers. This indicates that EEG signals do not contain enough information with
the signal processing method available today to design an automatic diagnostic method for Narcolepsy. The
two best performing papers are using PSG and MSLT as data modalities. In PSG and MSLT, the EEG is
measured but both the PSG and the MSLT contain an EEG and a lot more information about the patients
like ECG, EMG, HLA-DQB1*06:02 positive, hypocretin-1 levels etc. The reason for using EEG alone is
that it is less time and money consuming than making a PSG or MSLT. Therefore the chosen data modality
should be either PSG or MSLT.
In Murer et al. [14] Andlauer et al. [28] and Christensen et al. [24] they use all MSLT or PSG but per-
form worse in sensitivity than all the other mentioned papers. This could indicate that the used algorithm
is not good enough because the two best performing papers use similar data modality but different algorithms.
Because a MSLT contains a PSG, then if no patterns can be found in the MSLT, no patterns can be found
in the PSG. It has been seen that it can be difficult to achieve a high sensitivity and specificity by only using
PSG features (Moore, Zheng et al. [31], Moore et al. [32] and Christensen et al. [24]). Furthermore, the data
should be divided into patient age groups, as in Kovacs et al. [29] from 2017 that found indication in MSLT
patterns for different age groups. If the MSLT data contains night measurement it should be considered to
divide the data into nighttime data and daytime data, as some features in the nighttime data can be better
distinguished from non-Narcoleptic data, as seen in Vilela et al. [30] from 2022.
The chosen approach/algorithm is similar to that is used in Stephansen et al. because they showed that
with PSG data as modality it was possible to make a classifier with high sensitivity and accuracy that could
identify NT1 patients from healthy subjects [9]. But to answer the problem statement our approach much be
designed to produce a classifier that can identify NT1 patients from other patients with sleep disorders. The
modality is both PSG and MSLT because as mentioned if the classifier cannot identify narcolepsy patients
from a MSLT then it becomes hard to identify them from a PSG.

The algorithm can be divided into a pre-processing step and a processing step. The first step in the pre-
processing step is filtering the raw signals with a high- and low-pass filter which has a cutoff frequency at 0.2
and 49 Hz respectively. Then the signals are down sampled to 100Hz. Each electrode in the same region or
modality is compared with each other, and the noisiest channel is removed, so one EOG signal and two EEG
signals are removed. Thereafter the remaining signals are encoded either with octave or cross-correlation.
The octave encoding process uses five filters to create five new signals. All the filters are lowpass filters
with cutoff frequencies of 49, 25, 12.5, 6.25 and 3.125 Hz. In the last step the signals are scaled to the 95th

percentile and log modulus transformed, meaning that 95% of all the data is between minus one and one.
Cross-correlation coding uses the principle that noise in bioelectrical signals can be seen as having the same
properties as white noise. Therefore by using cross-correlation the noise in the signals are reduced and at
the same time the low frequencies are expressed better. See figure 4.6 for a flowchart over the pre-processing
steps. After the signals are pre-processed, the signals go into the processing step, which is a neural network.

The neural network is designed with multiple layers. First a convolution layer (also called a CNN), then
a pooling layer and in the end a short long-term memory layer. The convolution layer is a classical neural
network layer with the purpose of finding any features in the input signals. It is also described as a filter
that finds features in the input signals [39]. These filters are trained specifically to find features that are
present in sleep data such as PSG and MSLT data. A more detailed description of the training process
can be found in Stephensen et al [9]. After the convolution layer, the pooling layer then down-samples the
number of features. This makes the neural network less sensitive to where the feature is located in the
feature map. Because the filters in the convolution layer overlap each other, similar features will be found.
In this way a pooling layer will reduce the number of features so fewer of the same feature are represented
and not dependent on where it is located in the feature map [40]. Next comes the short long-term memory
layer (LSTM). LSTM has the ability to remember previous data. Often a problem with memory layer is the
amount of data it needs to remember but in LSTM it only remembers a few previously timepoints. The user
can determine how mange timepoints the LSTM needs to remember and it is therefore very often used as
the last layer in classification models [41]. A flowchart showing the deep neural network design can be seen
in figure 4.7.

5.1.1
Principal Component Analysis
PCA is a very common data reduction method.
The idea behind PCA is to reduce the dimensionality
of a dataset while retaining as much as possible of the variation present in the dataset. This is done by
transforming the variables in the dataset to principal components which are uncorrelated and ordered after
which contains most of the variance in all the original variables. The method PCA transforms the dataset
is with the eigenvalues and eigenvectors [39]. In short, the method solves the problem as can be seen in
equation 5.2.
Det(A −λI) = 0
(5.2)
A represents the covariance matrix of the dataset, λ represents the eigenvalues and I represent the unit
matrix. “Det” is the determination of the matrix. After finding the eigenvalues, the eigenvectors can be
found solving equation 5.3.
Xv −λv = 0
(5.3)
X represents the dataset matrix, v represents one eigenvector and λ represent the corresponding eigenvalue.
The numerical value of the eigenvalues represents the variance in the eigenvectors, therefore by removing
the lowest values of the eigenvalues, and thereby the corresponding eigenvector, the dataset will decompose
into a lower dimensional space. PCA can be used as a feature extraction method by removing principal
components that have low variance or selecting those principal components that contain a certain amount

5.1.2
Sequential Feature Selection
Sequential Feature Selection can be divided into two methods, forward selection, and backward selection.
In Sequential Feature Selection features are selected one at a time to see if the performance of the classifier
improves. The selected features are validated by calculating the performance of the classification algorithm
therefor is a new classification algorithm calculated for each new selected feature. This method of finding
the best subset of features by testing it on the classification algorithm is called a wrapper method. PCA is
not a wrapper method because the best subset of features is determined before calculating the classification
algorithm. Sequential Feature Selection is processed in collaboration with the classification algorithm and
can only be done after choosing a classification algorithm. There are two methods to do sequential feature
selection, forward and backward. The principle in the forward selection method is that for each iteration a
feature is added to the list of features. In the beginning there are no features selected, and a new feature is
added until the performance of the classifier does not improve by adding new features. The same principle
can be seen in backward selection with the exception that all the features are selected in the beginning and
for each iteration a feature is removed from the list of features [39]. The pro and cons for choosing Sequential
Feature Selection can be seen in figure 5.3

5.1.3
Recursive Feature Elimination
As with sequential feature selection, RFE is also a wrapper method. In contrast to sequential feature selec-
tion, RFE is much faster to calculate. In RFE all the features are selected and scored in a statistical way.
Then the one feature that has the lowest score is removed and the classifier is trained again. Then the re-
maining features are scored again and the one with the lowest score is removed. This whole process continues
until the wanted number of features is reached. The number of wanted features is a primary hyperparameter
for the RFE method and the performance of the method is very dependent on this hyperparameter. To

Classification algorithm
After determining which feature selection method to use, a classification method has to be determined.

5.2.1
Support Vector Machine
SVM is a supervised machine learning method often used for classification. This implies that SVM learns the
best decision boundary between the groups/classes from a set of training data with known classes, and then
the decision boundary is evaluated on unknown test data. SVM is one of the most robust prediction methods
and therefore also very commonly used as classification algorithm. SVM is able to deal with uneven amounts
of samples from two classes. Therefore it is a good fit for the dataset used in this project. Another advantage
is that fact that the optimization problem in the SVM algorithm is convex. Meaning that no local minima
that will decrease the overall performance of the algorithm exists. Because the database was provided by
Glostrup Hospital, the database was only accessible from computers from the Capital Region of Denmark,
which had a restriction on how computational the classification algorithm should be. The SVM algorithm
has a relatively low computational burden compared to other more complex classification algorithms [39].

5.2.1.2
Non-linear kernels
A non-linear kernel function transforms the data into a higher dimensional feature space given by the kernel
function, so it is possible to linearly separate the classes in the new feature space. Instead of remembering
the placement of data points in the higher dimensional feature space, the kernel trick can be used to mapping
the data points in the new feature space. As mentioned before the kernel trick is that the coordinates of a
higher dimensional feature space can be seen as the dot product of the coordinates in kernel function. Some
of the most popular non-linear kernels are the Polynomial kernel, Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel and
Sigmoid kernel. Many other kernels also exist but these three were compared to the linear kernel to find the
best kernel. The mathematical description of the three non-linear kernels is defined by
Polynomial kernel: K(xi, xj) = (xi · xj + r)d
(5.19)
Gaussian (RBF) kernel: K(xi, xj) = exp(−γ||xi −xj||2)
(5.20)
Sigmoid kernel: K(xi, xj) = tanh(κxi · xj + r)
(5.21)
i and j represent two observations in the data [44]. In the Polynomial kernel the parameters r and d are the
coefficient and the degree of the polynomial respectively. If r is equal to zero and d is equal to one, then
the above expression becomes the linear kernel. r and d need to be optimized for the classification problem
at hand. Due to time the r and d values were set to default values chosen by the Sklearn library which
are zero and three respectively. In the rbf kernel function, gamma scales how much the distance between
individual points influences the decision boundary. If gamma has a very high value only the observations
closest to every observation have influence on the classification. This can lead to overfitting. If gamma has
a low value more observations have influence on the classification of one observation. In this thesis gamma
was set to 1/nfeatures · Xvar. In the Sigmoid Kernel r and κ are the coefficients which also need to be
optimized due to the classification problem at hand. The Sigmoid kernel follows the hyperbolic function, so
if r or κ becomes very large the hyperbolic function will always return one, and if they are infinite small
then it returns minus one. Therefore, in the extreme values of r and κ the Sigmoid kernel will overfit the
data and perform poorly on new data. In this thesis r and κ were set to the default values in the Sklearn
library which were zero and 1/nfeatures · Xvar respectively [39]. In figure 5.6 the four kernels can be seen
on the same dataset. It illustrates how the different hyperplanes look in the normal feature space.

5.2.3
Cross-validation
Cross-validation is a very commonly used method for validating a machine learning model. It is a method
that uses different portions of the data to train and test a model on different iterations. Normally a model is
first trained on some part of the dataset and then tested on the remaining part of the dataset, to see how it
performs. In cross-validation the training and test dataset is changing so the model becomes more reliable
to predict new data. Another problem that cross-validation solves is the problem of overfitting or selection
bias. Overfitting is the phenomena where a model is so trained on the training data that it also models the
random noise in the data. This makes the model perform very poorly when seeing new data that contains
random noise. Overfitting is often seen when a model achieves high performance in the training data and
low performance in the test data. By using cross-validation the model is tested on the whole dataset, so the
model will not be focused on one part of the dataset but on the whole dataset. Cross-validation is often
used in classification problems, but it can also be used in regression problems. Considering the scope of this
thesis cross-validation will only be explained with focus on the classification scenario [39].
Cross-validation can be done on many methods depending on how the dataset is structured. If the number
of observations is low, it is common to use the leave-one-out cross-validation method. This method takes
one observation out of the dataset and uses it as a test-point. This is repeated until all observations have
tried to be the test-point. The results are then combined (often averaged) over the rounds to estimate the
41

model’s predictive performance. A similar method is the leave-p-out cross-validation which does the same as
leave-one-out, but it takes p observations out as test-data. By having many observations, leave-one-out or
leave-p-out cross-validation can take a long time to do. Therefore another method can be used called k-fold
cross-validation. In k-fold cross-validation the observations are first shuffled and then the data is divided
into k-folds. The k-folds are the test-data, and the remaining data is training-data. This makes k models
where each of them has one-fold that is different from the other models’ and the remaining data is the
training-data. See figure 5.8 for an overview of the k-fold cross validation method. After all the iterations
the results are combined [39].
Figure 5.8: A 5-fold cross-validation. Source [49].
In this thesis a 10-fold cross-validation method is used to validate the models. The k-fold cross-validation
method is used because the number of observations in each classification problem is relatively high (over
50 observations). 10 is chosen because it fits with having on average 9 observations per fold. 10-fold cross-
validation was used in optimizing the hyperparameters in the classification model, optimizing the number of
selected features and validating the final classification method. Before any training or testing was done on
the data, a subset of the data was removed and only used as the final validation of the classification method.
This was done because by validating the model with k-fold cross-validation, the model will be tested on data
it has been or will be trained on. Therefore by removing a subset of the data in the beginning, the model
can be truly tested on some data that it has never seen before and that will give a much better prediction
on how the model performs on new data. Here 15% of the observations were removed before any k-fold
cross-validation happened. 15% were chosen because we wanted as much data to test on but also enough
training data to train the model on, together with having a good representation of each group in both the
test and training data.

Discussion
In this chapter the results presented in chapter six will be evaluated together with some possible explanations
of the ambiguous results.
7.1
NT1 versus group three
The classification problem between NT1 and group three had some clear results. All the three modalities
performed perfectly as mentioned in the previous chapter. The statistical parameters are all at 100% and
the patients are all correctly diagnosed as can be seen in the confusion matrix and the ROC curve for each
modality. The backward feature selection method returned only one feature as having the most influence
on the decision boundary. This feature was the level of hypocretins-1 in the CSF. Patients with low levels
of hypocretins-1 tend to have NT1 according to the findings in this project. However, this study was done
on a small set of observations. For the MSLT observations only 15 patients had NT1 and 48 patients were
in group three, and in the PSG observations only 17 had NT1 and 52 patients were in group three. There
is a clear imbalance in the number of observations between the two groups although this is compensated by
measuring F1 and PPV. However, the total number of observations is very low to conclude that the level
of hypocretins-1 is a clear biomarker for NT1. It was expected that it would have a clear impact on the
classification but not as much as it was found here. Bigger cohorts of patients with NT1 need to be tested to
clearly conclude that the level of hypocretins-1 is always a good biomarker. One way to compensate for the
low number of observations is by creating new observations from the old ones. This was not done because
this will not give any new information but only increase what is already shown in the data.
7.2
Group two versus group three
In the classification problem between group two and group three, the results were not clear. In all the
modalities the results were not nearly as good as in the previous classification problem. In the modality
PSG Day, the training results were perfect, but the test results were around 55% in all performance param-
eters. Looking at the hyperparameters for the classifier, C was found to be 50, and it is also worth noting
that the training error was very low and the test error relatively high. This indicates that the classification
algorithm is overfitting with respect to the training data and therefore experiences high testing error. In
contrast, the classification algorithm was trained on multiple hyperparameters to find the best performing,
and C equal to 50 performed better than any lower values of C and was consequently chosen.
In any case the classification algorithm does not model the underlying patterns in NT2 and hypersomnia
to clearly separate them from group three. This can be because of the low number of observations in the
dataset. A low number of observations makes it harder for the classifier to find the underlying pattern.
Another reason could be that the calculated features do not show the underlying pattern of NT2 and hy-
persomnia. The selected features were taken from Stephansen et al. who used them to find the underlying

pattern in NT1. So it was expected that it would be harder for a classifier to separate NT2 and hyper-
somnia from group 3 with these features. This could indicate that NT2 and hypersomnia and NT1 are not
as similar as first expected. A much bigger cohort of patients needs to be analyzed to confirm that is the case.
A third reason for the poor performance is the chosen classification method, SVM. Stephansen et al. used
a gaussian process (GP) classifier as classification method. In this thesis the SVM classification method was
chosen because it has a robust prediction method and because SVM is convex. The GP classifier is also a

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The thesis focuses on the possibilities for improving the diagnosing of narcolepsy which is a sleep disorder
with severe implications for the patients that consequently will rely on medical support in many ways from
the healthcare system.
The patients have daily periods of sudden sleepiness and at the same time risk
fall-associated injuries [2]. These seizures make the disorder very exhausting for the patient’s personal life
and reduce life quality[3]. Narcoleptic patients are more costly in respect to healthcare expenses than other
patient groups because of their higher frequency of contacts to the healthcare system compared to an average
patient group[3]. In addition, the unknown pathology of narcolepsy makes the treatment lifelong.
Several research laboratories have tried to improve the diagnostic method to reduce the implied costs.
Some of these focus on designing machine learning algorithms that can automatically diagnose narcolepsy by
looking at different data modalities PSG, MSLT, and MWT data[9][7]. Some of them achieved a solution that
has the potential to replace the current golden standard method (MSLT) by achieving higher performance.
From these studies the problem statement was formulated as the following
Is it possible to construct an automatic algorithm that improves the current diagnostic method for
narcolepsy?
To answer the problem statement a method inspired by both Stephansen et al. and Zhang et al. was designed
and tested. The proposed algorithm was chosen to fulfill the following requirements:
• The algorithm should automatically perform without the mediation of a person after a possible cali-
bration is processed.
• The algorithm should be tested on PSG recordings from afternoon, night and MSLT data.
• The algorithm should have a high sensitivity and a high PPV compared to the most recent papers
with a similar methodology.
All data was provided by the Sleep Clinic at Rigshospitalet-Glostrup. It included data in the form of PSG,
MSLT, and the level of hypocretin-1. The PSG data included registrations in two time-intervals, afternoon
and night, which was a new aspect compared to former research. This produced three input data modalities,
PSG Day, PSG Night and MSLT.
The database was divided into three groups depending on the disorder of the patient, see figure 4.3.
Three classification problems were designed. Classification problem one separates group one (NT1) from
group three (all other available sleep disorders). Classification problem two separates group two (NT2 and
hypersomnia) from group three, and the last classification problem separates all three groups from each
other. For each classification problem, three machine learning classifiers were designed, each with their own
input data modality. For all machine learning classifiers, the SVM method was used together with backward
feature selection. All classifiers were validated with 10-fold cross-validation and finally tested on unseen data.
52
==PAGE:64==
Pattern recognition in the MSLT for Narcolepsy type 1 patients
The performance of all three modalities in the first classification problem was identical. They achieved
100% in both accuracy, sensitivity, PPV, specificity, and F1 score. This is higher than what was achieved in
Stephansen et al. However, Stephansen et al. had a much larger cohort. The only feature used in all three
classifiers was the level of hypocretin-1, which indicates that all other features used were less important than
the level of hypocretin-1 in NT1 patients.
In classification problem two and three the results were more unclear. In all modalities the performance
was low, which can have multiple reasons like overfitting, choice of machine learning algorithm, choice of
input features or a combination of several. The most likely reason for the poor performance is the choice
of input features. The SVM classifier is not overfitting even though it achieved high performance during
training and low performance during test in all modalities and in both classification problems (two and
three). It is not overfitting because multiple hyperparameters were tested and the hyperparameters that
performed best were chosen. The choice of SVM was because of its robustness and convexity. The SVM
method succeeds in achieving high performance in the first classification problem even though a simpler
classifier method could also achieve high performance. The reason why the input features probably are the
cause of the poor performance is that the features were inspired by Stephansen et al. who only looked at
NT1 and not NT2 and because the features do not represent physiological patterns.
Returning to the problem statement, the question was, whether it is possible to construct an automatic
algorithm that improves the current diagnostic method for narcolepsy. Based on the results from the testing
in this thesis, an automatic algorithm is proposed that looks at clinical diagnostic features in PSG. The
following answer can consequently be given to the question in the problem statement that it seems possible
to improve the current diagnostic method for narcolepsy with an automatic algorithm with the proposed
features.

Abstract—Narcolepsy is a chronic sleep disorder that
affects 1 in 2000 people. The golden method to diagnose
narcolepsy is a MSLT. A MSLT is a very time consuming
and costly method that takes a lot of resources to do. An
alternative method is PSG but has not been fully tested. The
purpose of this paper is to investigate if it is possible to use
PSG instead of MSLT to diagnose narcolepsy type 1 and 2.
In the paper the proposed algorithm consists of a statistical
feature calculated on hypnodensities, together with level
of hypocretin-1, from 227 sleeping observations and a
support vector machine classifier. These observations were
either a MSLT (110 of them were) or PSG (117 of them
were). All the patients in the database were investigated to
have narcolepsy type 1 or 2. Three classification problems
were investigated. Classification problem one, separate nar-
colepsy type 1 from control group. Classification problem
two, separate narcolepsy type 2 from control group. In the
last classification all three groups were to be separated.The
performance in the first classification problem was identi-
cal. They achieved 100% in both accuracy, sensitivity, PPV,
specificity, and F1 score. In classification problem two and
three the results were more unclear. Based on the results
from the testing in this paper, an automatic algorithm is
proposed that looks at clinical diagnostic features in PSG.

B. Hypnodensity
From the raw EEG, EOG and EMG signals in the in-
put data (the MSLT’s and PSG’s), the hypnodensities are
calculated. Hypnodensities are probability functions that
show the probability for the patient to be in one specific
sleep stage. Because there are five sleep stages (Wake,
N1, N2, N3 and REM) there are five hypnodensities for
each patient.
C. Feature Extraction
Sequential Feature Selection can be divided into two
methods, forward selection, and backward selection. In
Sequential Feature Selection features are selected one
at a time to see if the performance of the classifier
improves. The selected features are validated by cal-
culating the performance of the classification algorithm
therefor is a new classification algorithm calculated for
each new selected feature. This method of finding the
best subset of features by testing it on the classification
algorithm is called a wrapper method. PCA is not a
wrapper method because the best subset of features
is determined before calculating the classification al-
gorithm. Sequential Feature Selection is processed in
collaboration with the classification algorithm and can
only be done after choosing a classification algorithm.
There are two methods to do sequential feature selection,
forward and backward. The principle in the forward
selection method is that for each iteration a feature is
==PAGE:96==
MASTER THESIS ARTICALE
3
added to the list of features. In the beginning there
are no features selected, and a new feature is added
until the performance of the classifier does not improve
by adding new features. The same principle can be
seen in backward selection with the exception that all
the features are selected in the beginning and for each
iteration a feature is removed from the list of features
[15].
D. Classification algorithm
In SVM classification the algorithm learns how to
separate two or more classes with a decision boundary.
A non-linear kernel function transforms the data into
a higher dimensional feature space given by the kernel
function, so it is possible to linearly separate the classes
in the new feature space. Instead of remembering the
placement of data points in the higher dimensional
feature space, the kernel trick can be used to mapping
the data points in the new feature space. As mentioned
before the kernel trick is that the coordinates of a higher
dimensional feature space can be seen as the dot product
of the coordinates in kernel function. Some of the most
popular non-linear kernels are the Polynomial kernel,
Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel and Sigmoid kernel.
For each of the SVM classifiers eight different values
of C (0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 5, 10, 50) were tested
and four different kernels function (Linear, Polynomial,
Sigmoid and RBF) were tested. The hyperparameters
that performed best were chosen.

In the classification problem between group two

was found to be 50, and it is also worth noting that the
training error was very low and the test error relatively
high. This indicates that the classification algorithm
is overfitting with respect to the training data and
therefore experiences high testing error. In contrast,
the classification algorithm was trained on multiple
hyperparameters to find the best performing, and C
equal to 50 performed better than any lower values of
C and was consequently chosen.
In any case the classification algorithm does not
model the underlying patterns in NT2 and hypersomnia
to clearly separate them from group three. This can
be because of the low number of observations in the
dataset. A low number of observations makes it harder
for the classifier to find the underlying pattern. Another
reason could be that the calculated features do not
show the underlying pattern of NT2 and hypersomnia.
The selected features were taken from Stephansen et

NT1. So it was expected that it would be harder for a

analyzed to confirm that is the case.
A third reason for the poor performance is the
chosen classification method, SVM. Stephansen et al.
used a gaussian process (GP) classifier as classification
method. In this thesis the SVM classification method
was chosen because it has a robust prediction method
and because SVM is convex. The GP classifier is also
a kernel method like the SVM. One of the differences
between the GP classifier and the SVM is that the GP
can predict highly calibrated probabilities concerning
possible relations between the features. GP uses the
covariance function of the data [15]. The 10 general
features used are very similar to each other so some
degree of covariance between them could be suspected.
This would be detected by the GP, but not by the
SVM. That could be the reason why Stephansen et
al. used GP and not any other classifier. Modality
PSG Day has similar results as the MSLT modality.
They both have the same high performance in the
training data and the same hyperparameters for the
classification. The MSLT modality did perform better
in the test data, with an accuracy at 73% (against PSG
Day accuracy at 56%), specificity at 100% (against
PSG Day specificity at 62%) and the F1 score at 67%
(against PSG Days F1 score at 53%), and it performed
best as to the ROC curve. Because MSLT did not
perform much better than the two other modalities, it is
difficult to conclude that MSLT is a better method to
diagnose NT2 and hypersomnia. But as mentioned in
the Background chapter MSLT is used to diagnose NT2
and hypersomnia so the underlying pattern for NT2
and hypersomnia should be present in the MSLT data.
This leads to the possible conclusion that the selected
features do not show the underlying pattern for NT2
and hypersomnia. However, it may also be that the
classification method is not able to find the underlying
patterns as already discussed for modality PSG Day.
The performance of each of the modalities is not
obvious when looking at the three confusion matrices.
As a consequence the accuracy of each modality is used
as a performance parameter. The accuracy is lowest
in the PSG Day modality and highest in the MSLT
modality, as expected for reasons already mentioned.
The accuracy for each of the modalities is relatively
low. An accuracy at 68% as the highest one is not as
high as seen in other papers.

It shows that the modality of PSG Day performs very

performs poorly when trying to separate class two

looking at the performance parameters it seems that the
classification puts almost all the observations into group
three, because the probability of guessing correctly
is highest for group two or three. This is because of
the low number of observations in group one (NT1).

V. CONCLUSION
The database was divided into three groups depending
on the disorder of the patient, see figure ??. Three
classification problems were designed. Classification
problem
one
separates
group
one
(NT1)
from
group
three
(all
other
available
sleep
disorders).
Classification problem two separates group two (NT2
and hypersomnia) from group three, and the last
classification problem separates all three groups from
each other.
For each classification problem, three
machine
learning
classifiers
were
designed,
each
with their own input data modality. For all machine
learning classifiers, the SVM method was used together
with backward feature selection. All classifiers were
validated with 10-fold cross-validation and finally tested
on unseen data.
The performance of all three modalities in the first
classification problem was identical. They achieved
100% in both accuracy, sensitivity, PPV, specificity,
and F1 score. This is higher than what was achieved
in
Stephansen
et
al.
However,
Stephansen
et
al.
had a much larger cohort. The only feature used in
all three classifiers was the level of hypocretin-1,
which indicates that all other features used were less
important than the level of hypocretin-1 in NT1 patients.
In classification problem two and three the results
were more unclear. In all modalities the performance was
low, which can have multiple reasons like overfitting,
choice of machine learning algorithm, choice of input
features or a combination of several. The most likely
reason for the poor performance is the choice of input
features. The SVM classifier is not overfitting even
though it achieved high performance during training and
low performance during test in all modalities and in
both classification problems (two and three). It is not
overfitting because multiple hyperparameters were tested
and the hyperparameters that performed best were cho-
sen. The choice of SVM was because of its robustness
and convexity. The SVM method succeeds in achieving
high performance in the first classification problem even
though a simpler classifier method could also achieve
high performance. The reason why the input features
probably are the cause of the poor performance is that
the features were inspired by Stephansen et al. who only
looked at NT1 and not NT2 and because the features do
not represent physiological patterns.
