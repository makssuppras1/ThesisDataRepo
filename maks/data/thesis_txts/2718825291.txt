Abstract
In an attempt to decarbonize hard­to­abate sectors like heavy industry and shipping,
Power­to­X, or PtX, has been proposed as a viable solution, where clean renewable en­
ergy is converted into hydrogen by means of water electrolysis and then processed into
e­fuels like methanol, ammonia or kerosene. There are now many PtX projects planned or
under construction in Denmark, which will introduce several gigawatts of electrical loads
into the Danish power system. However, despite an expected decrease in production
costs, PtX­based fuels are still expected to exceed the costs of conventional fossil­based
products. In an effort to lower the overall production cost, it is proposed that PtX plants
utilize their inherent flexibility to provide frequency reserves to the electrical grid.
The potential cost reduction by means of frequency provision is investigated for a real­
life Hybrid Renewable Energy System (HRES) for methanol production in Denmark. The
production cost is quantified by a levelized cost of methanol calculation, where the cumu­
lative capital and operational expenses over the full lifetime of the system is calculated
for every unit of produced methanol. Fixed cost levels are estimated based on the sys­
tem design and available literature, while the market­related variable costs for electricity
are minimized by a proposed mathematical model, formulating the operation and market
participation of the HRES as a cost minimization problem. In order to investigate different
aspects of the problem, three models are developed. A basic deterministic model with
perfect foresight optimizes the setpoints in relation to the day­ahead market and appli­
cable grid tariffs. This model is then expanded to place bids in three frequency reserve
markets. In the third and final model, the uncertainty of the market is addressed by for­
mulating the optimization problem as a two­stage stochastic minimization problem, where
the outcomes of reserve market participation as well as the uncertainty of the day­ahead
clearing prices influence the hourly setpoints of the HRES.
The analysis of the HRES technology and existing reserve markets indicates that the
HRES qualifies for frequency reserve provision certification of FCR, aFRR and mFRR by
the TSO, and that the ramp rate of the PtX plant limits the respective capacities that may
be provided to 1MW, 39.3MW and 39.3MW. Under perfect market foresight conditions,
the optimal control of the HRES results in total operational costs (vOPEX) of 3.19m€ and
8.32m€ in 2020 and 2021 respectively, when only participating in the day­ahead market.
The resulting LCoM are 1199€/t and 1360€/t, when applying the yearly variable cost re­
sults of 2020 and 2021, respectively. When the system is allowed to participate in the
reserve capacity markets, vOPEX results are ­1.47m€ and ­4.97m€ respectively, i.e. the
system turns a net loss into a net profit through reserve market participation. In 2021,
where both average price levels and volatility increase across most markets as compared
to 2020, this profit further increases. The additional revenue from reserve market par­
ticipation results in LCoM price reductions of 12% and 31%, when applying 2020 and
2021 vOPEX results. Under market uncertainty, LCoM reductions are still substantial.
The stochastic model achieves LCoM reductions of 7% and 23%, respectively. Current
e­methanol production is far from being cost competitive with conventionally produced
alternatives, with European methanol futures trading at 471€/t­571€/t in 2021.The above
results corroborate this fact as the lowest realized LCoM obtained by the optimization is
944€/t. The results do however indicate that there are substantial cost reductions to be re­
alized by reserve market participation and that these reductions may increase with higher
market prices and volatility. Participation in the frequency reserve capacity markets may
therefore act not only as a means of total cost reduction, but also as an insurance policy
against volatile energy markets.

Resume
I et forsøg på at afkarbonisere sektorer som skibsfragt og tungindustri, hvor direkte elek­
trificering ikke er en mulighed, er PtX blevet foreslået som et lovende alternativ. Dette
sker ved at omdanne vedvarende energi til hydrogen ved brug af electrolyse. Hydro­
gen kan blive omdannet til andre e­brændsler, som for eksempel metanol,ammoniak eller
petroleum. Der er i øjeblikket mange PtX projekter under udvikling i Danmark. Dette vil
øge forbruget i det danske elsystem med adskillige gigawatt. Selvom produktionsomkost­
ningerne for e­brændsler forventes at falde, forvented de dog stadig at være højere end
deres fossile alternativer. I et forsøg på at sænke produktionsomkostningerne, foreslås
det at udnytte fleksibiliteten i PtX anlægget, til at tilbyde frekvensreservekapacitet bal­
ancering af elnettet.
Den potentielle omkostningsreduktion ved at tilbyde reservekapacitet, vil blive undersøgt
for et konkret dansk methanol baseret Hybrid Renewable Energy System (HRES). Pro­
duktionsomkostningerne vil blive målt ved brug af Levelized Cost of Methanol(LCoM).
De faste systemomkostninger er estimerede på baggrund af systemdesign og under­
søgelser af litteraturen. De markedsrelaterede variable omkostninger som elektricitet,
minimeres ved anvendelse af en matematisk optimiseringsmodel, hvor driftomkostninger
og markedeltagelse er formuleret som et minimerinsproblem. For at undersøge flere as­
pekter opgaven udvikles tre modeller.
Første model er en deterministisk model, hvor der optimeres på systemets driftspunkter i
forhold til day­ahead markedet og dertilhørende tariffer. Modellen udviddes til deltagelse
i tre forskellige reservemarkeder. I den sidste model inkluderes usikkerheden i markedet
ved at formulere et to­stadies stokastisk minimeringsproblem, hvor udfaldet af markeds­
deltagelsen, samt usikkerheden ift. day­ahead prisen afgør systemets driftspunkter.
Analysen af HRES teknologien samt den eksisterende reservemarkedsstruktur viser, at
systemet er kvalificeret til at levere systemydelserne FCR, aFRR, mFRR i DK1. Regu­
leringstiden af PtX anlægget, er den begrænsende faktor i systemet. Systemet vil derfor
kunne levere 1MW, 39.3MW og 39,3MW i det tre forskellige markeder.
Under perfekte markedforudsætninger vil den optimale kontrol af systemet resultere i en
total driftsomkostning på 3.19 m€ i 2020, og 8.32 m€ i 2021. Dette ved kun at deltage i day­
ahead markedet. Ved deltagelse i reservemarkederne vil disse omkostninger bringes ned
til henholdsvis ­1.47 m€ i 2020, og ­4.97 m€ i 2021. Dette betyder at driftsomkostningerne
forvandles til profit ved at inkludere deltagelse på reserve markederne. I 2021 steg både
gennemsnitspriserne samt pris volatiliteten markant, for alle markederne i forhold til 2020.
Dette skabte yderligere profit, hvilket medførte et fald i LCoM på 12%, fra 1198 €/ton til
1054 €/ton i 2020. For 2021 faldt LCoM 27%, hvilket svarer til et fald fra 1360 €/ton til 994
€/ton. Ved brug af modellen hvor usikkerhed er påbelagt markedspriserne, blev LCoM
1120 €/ton i 2020, og 1041 €/ton i 2021, hvilket stadig er betydningsfuldt. Sammenlignet
med modelresultaterne fra den første model, er dette et fald i LCoM på henholdsvis 7%
og 23%.
Sammenlignes dette med markedsprisen på den konventionelt producerede metanol, er
e­metanol ikke konkurrencedygtig, da prisen i 2021 på de europæiske futures var på 471­
571 €/ton. Selvom prisreduktionerne ikke kommer på niveau med den konventionelt pro­
ducerede metanol, er der betydelig indtjening at hente. Dette viser sig også at forstærkes,
ved yderligere volatilitet i markedspriserne. Deltagelse i frekvensreservemarkederne kan
derfor muligvis benyttes til at sikre sig mod volatile markeder.

1
Introduction
In this chapter, the thesis context, scope and goals are introduced. The motivation behind
the thesis is presented in section 1.1. The scope of the project is detailed in section 1.2
along with the defined research questions. Finally, the structure of the report is presented
in section 1.3.
1.1
Motivation
Denmark is aiming for a 70% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030, compared
to 1990 levels, and to reach carbon neutrality by 2050. These goals were signed into a
legally binding law with the 2020 ”Klimaloven” [1]. In order to achieve the emission reduc­
tion targets, all electricity generation should be provided by renewable energy sources
(RES) [2].
For high­emission sectors where electrification may not be economically or practically
feasible, such as in shipping, aviation and parts of the industrial sector, emission reduc­
tions may not be achievable by electrification. Due to limitations in e.g. energy density,
the production and use of fuels based on renewable electricity, so­called e­fuels, may be
preferred. The production of e­fuels is based on the principle of Power­to­X (PtX) and is
expected to play a vital role in the decarbonization efforts of these sectors [3].
PtX is an umbrella term for technologies that typically produce hydrogen or hydrogen­
based products (X) using water electrolysis technology powered by renewable power
generation (P). The produced hydrogen can be used directly as fuel, serve as a feed­
stock in chemical and material processes or be further converted to other fuels such as
ammonia, methanol and kerosene. In this way, the renewable electricity can indirectly cut
emissions in sectors where direct electrification in not viable [4].
PtX is still at a very early stage in Denmark. Current operational electrolysis capacity is
less than 5MW, but an acceleration in the sector is expected. The Danish government has
proposed that Denmark aims for an electrolysis capacity of 4­6MW in 2030, while other
sources state that Denmark has the potential for and should target a 10MW capacity by
2030 [5].
Currently, hydrogen produced using conventional methods e.g. fossil fuel with carbon
capture (blue hydrogen) is 2­3 times cheaper than green hydrogen [6] while e.g.
e­
methanol price differences are even higher when comparing green and conventional
methanol production [7]. For e­fuels to become cost competitive with their fossil­based
alternates, the cost of hydrogen production and subsequent production of derived fuels
must therefore be reduced.
While there are undoubtedly cost reductions to be made in the technology itself through
technology development and deployment of PtX at scale, there may also be alternative
ways to reduce production costs. Seen from the point of view of the electrical grid, a PtX
plant is a large electrical load, i.e. consumer of electricity. If the load has some flexibility,
i.e. the consumption can be changed to react to grid conditions, this flexibility can be
provided to the grid operator as a service in exchange for economic compensation, which
in turn could lower the total production cost of PtX plants.
Based on the above, it is of interest to investigate the production cost of PtX plant fuels
and the potential cost reduction that the applied load flexibility may bring to such projects.

1.2
Project scope
To ensure a realistic investigation, the study is carried out in relation to a specific PtX
project currently underway in Denmark.
The chosen system is a Hybrid Renewable
Energy System (HRES) owned by European Energy, located near Kassø, Denmark.
The system consists of a 300MW solar photovoltaic plant (PV plant) connected to a PtX
plant designed for the production of methanol as a shipping fuel. The PtX plant has an
electrolyzer capacity of 52.5MW and an annual methanol production capacity of 32,000
tonnes. A high level diagram of the HRES is shown in fig. 1.1. The PV plant of the HRES
has already been built, while the PtX plant has not yet been constructed. For the purposes
of this study, the full HRES is presumed to be constructed at the same time and put into
operation in 2024.
Figure 1.1: Overview of HRES
The full HRES is considered as a single system dedicated to the production of e­methanol.
The methanol production cost measure applied in this project is the Levelized Cost of
Methanol (LCoM), considering the full lifetime cost of each unit of methanol produced.
The study focuses on reducing the operational costs related to the interaction between the
HRES, electricity markets and the electrical grid. The entire system in fig. 1.1 is consid­
ered and the existing market costs are therefore both the electricity costs of supplying the
electrolyzer from the electrical grid and the revenue generated when selling the electricity
generation from the PV plant to the grid. In order to quantify the operational costs in a
market setting and to investigate the potential cost reductions from reserve capacity mar­
ket participation, a detailed review of the existing electricity market structure relevant to
the plant location is carried out and potential opportunities for reserve capacity provisions
by the HRES are identified. To quantify the electrical flexibility of the HRES, a review of
the central PtX plant components is performed.
A mathematical model of the HRES is developed and applied to determine the optimal
operation of the system, considering the day­ahead market and frequency reserve pro­
visions, while meeting a weekly methanol demand. The model is based on well­known
methods within the field of operations research (OR) and considers the uncertainty of
market variables.
To formulate the mathematical model of the PtX plant, load range and ramping time char­
acteristics are considered. Methanol synthesis and distillation processes are heavily sim­
plified as thermodynamic and chemical processes are only peripherally within the scope
of this project.

The model results include market bids of the HRES and the operating setpoints of the
electrolyzer and PV plant on a suitable timescale, based on real­world market data. More
than one year of data is applied in order to address potential market biases. The simulation
results cover full operational years. When full year simulations are not feasible, model
results are converted to annual figures in order to perform LCoM calculations. The capital
costs and fixed operational costs are determined based on available cost estimations in
relevant literature and are considered as fixed values with no cost reduction potential.
1.3
Research question and objectives
This project seeks to answer the following research question: To what extent can the
flexibility of a HRES be utilized to create additional revenue streams in the reserve market,
ultimately reducing the production cost of e­methanol?
The research question is answered by realizing the following objectives:
1. Quantify the HRES system and load flexibility of the PtX plant
2. Investigate current state of reserve markets and identify relevant markets of partic­
ipation
3. Develop and apply a deterministic optimization model for cost minimization of the
HRES
4. Extend the optimization model to include stochasticity of market variables
5. Perform levelized cost calculation considering model results and fixed project costs

Background
2.1
Optimization review
To gain an overview of existing and state­of­the­art in optimization approaches in the
cross­field of flexible loads, electricity markets and PtX technology, a review of research
papers in these areas is performed. A select number of these is presented below, high­
lighting some of their similarities and differences.
[8] proposes an Energy Management System (EMS) model for an industrial cluster con­
sisting of renewable generation, electric battery, electrolysis plant, hydrogen storage, in­
dustrial plants and hydrogen consuming plants. The EMS optimizes the operation of flex­
ible power consuming units (the hydrogen compression,electrolysis and battery) as well
as hydrogen consuming plants (pyrolysis, methanol, hydrogen compression and ammo­
nia plants). The electrolyzer efficiency is modelled as being constant across the full load
range with a conversion ratio of 24 kg H2 per MWh consumption. No ramp rates are
applied to methanol reactor or hydrogen compression.
The optimization problem is formulated as a mixed integer linear problem (MILP). The
system is optimized with respect to an economic and a sustainable objective. In the
economic optimization the operational costs of the system are minimized, while in the
sustainable optimization, the share of green (i.e. renewable­based) hydrogen production
is maximized. As the model assumes perfect forecasts of renewable electricity generation
and day­ahead electricity prices, there is no uncertainty modelled. The perfect forecast
is however addressed by a sensitivity analysis. Each scenario is 24 hours long, where
each scenario is applied a certain weight calculated by the K­means method applied to
the day­ahead prices and renewable generation data for a full year. In addition to the
operational cost results, the investment into an ammonia plant is investigated. By letting
the model invest into the plant, additional investment cost is added and compared to the
additional cost and revenue it brings into the system. The investment is evaluated by the
internal rate of return (IRR).
[9] Investigates the flexibility and optimal operation of an Alkaline­electrolyzer­based Power­
to­Methanol plant participating in the day­ahead electricity market under uncertainty 1.
The model is based on a real­life system in Denmark and minimizes the operational costs
of the system. The model includes a detailed electrolyzer model as well as methanol
plant model, energy and material flow balances. It is formulated as an MILP and applies
an advanced data­driven robust optimization technique to handle the uncertainty of price
fluctuations, where the worst­case is optimized based on predicted prices by a neural net­
work. The model minimized the system operation throughout a full year (2016) and report
improved results compared to other, more base optimization techniques such as simple
Robust Optimization (RO) and stochastic optimization (SO). The study reports levelized
cost of methanol in the range 584­1146€/t, where a wind farm behind­the­meter (BTM) is
accounted for in the CAPEX. depending on the CO2­price and number of wind turbines in
the system. The study includes detailed modelling of the electrolyzer and methanol reac­
tor. The electrolyzer model includes a piece­wise approximation of efficiency, to capture
efficiency increased a low current (i.e. low load).
In [10] the grid­connected electrolyzer in a hydrogen refueling station is used to provide

frequency services to the grid. An optimization problem is formulated to identify the opti­
mal hourly operation of the system components as well as the sizing of sub­components
and the provided frequency reserves. The model is formulated as a probabilistic MILP
problem and is solved for one year (2020) using real­world historical data. The system is
assumed to be located in a high production area Belgium, hence European market data
is applied. The market uncertainty is represented by scenarios. A normal distribution if
applied to generate 10 scenarios for the development for each market variable. By sce­
nario combination, 100 scenarios are produces as model inputs, including the activation
of reserves.
A Grid­connected Virtual Power Plant (VPP) is modelled in [11]. The VPP consists of
sychronous generators, a pumped­storage plant (PSP), a wind power plant (WPP)and
EVs connected to bidirectional chargers in a parking lot. The VPP participates in the day­
ahead electricity market as well as a non­specified regulating reserve (RR) market with the
goal of maximizing the expected profit. The uncertain parameters in the model include the
DA price, reserve prices, reserve activation probabilities, WPP generation and the user
pattern of EVs in the parking lot are modelled using a point estimate method. The EVs
and WPP capacities are not applied in the RR market, as their size depend on stochastic
processes. Instead, the PSP is dispatched for RR activation, when needed. The mixed­
integer programming problem (MIP) is implemented in GAMS and solved for 4 scenarios,
each consiting of a 24­hour period. In the proposed method, the RR market is responsible
for >40% of total revenue.
In [12] an EV aggregator scheduling problem is addressed. Thanks to V2G capabilities in
the EVs, the aggregator can participate in day­ahead as well as the reserve market. Both
of these markets, as well as the availability of plugged in EVs, are subject to uncertainties.
The objective of the formulated optimization problem is a maximization of the aggregator
profit, by charging at lowest possible cost from day­ahead purchased energy, and pro­
viding energy back to the grid at a higher price as well as providing ancillary services
(reserves).
The day­ahead price scenarios are generated using a seasonal autoregressive integrated
moving average (SARIMA) time series model and scenario reduction is performed by a
fast backward reduction technique. The calling (activation) of the reserve being called
by the ISO is generated by comparing a random number from a uniform distribution and
a predetermined probability. The optimization problem is formulated as a mixed­integer
nonlinear problem (MINLP). Due to the sequencial realization of the stochastic processes,
the optimization is solved as two­stage stochastic programming problem and is applied
to an hourly 24­hour period.
The studies show a diverse range of approaches to handling the inherent uncertainty of
market variables as well as renewable energy production. There is however only one
study that formulates the problem as a two­stage problem, i.e. taking market outcomes
into account prior to the ”second­stage” decision. Many of the optimization problems
are formulated a MILPs while some take the non­linear MINLP approach. Although the
majority of modelled systems are not real­life existing systems, the most relevant systems
are indeed physical systems in Denmark and therefore provide great insight into the level
of detail to which existing systems are modelled. The two models are quite different in their
approach to the level of detail in the methanol plant. While [8] applies a constant efficiency
to the electrolyzer and no constraint on the methanol plant, [9] includes a detailed model
of both system components. Of the reported studies, [9] is the only one that converts
model results to an tangible economic result considering the full lifetime of the system.

Most of the presented studies solve a 24­hour simulation period, while full year of lifetime
studies are more rare. None of the presented studies apply market data from more than
1 year. While some systems act in the reserve market and others are modelled as full
PtX systems, there is no study identified where a PtX plant offers reserve capacity from
a geographically specified location.

Technical review
In this section, the technical aspects of the main system components of the PtX plant are
described. As the technical components are not the main topic of the project, a review of
the working principle of the components is performed and a comparison of the currently
available electrolyzer technologies is provided.
2.2.1
Electrolyzer
The literature describes four types of electrolyzer technologies, here referred to as elec­
trolyzers. These are alkaline electrolyzers (AE), proton exchange membrane electrolyzers
(PEME), solid oxide electrolyzers (SOE) and aninon exchange membrane electrolyzers
(AEME). Their respective cells are referred to as AEC, PEMEC, SOEC and AEMEC. The
AE is the most common type of electrolyzer with the longest industrial application [13],
PEMEs are also commercially available, while the SOEs are at a demonstration level [14].
The largest SOE facility in the world has a nominal capacity of 2.6MW [15], the largest
AE facility is at 150MW [16] and the largest PEME facility is currently at 20MW [17]. In
Denmark it is reported that the AEME technology is still at very a early stage of develop­
ment and only prototypes have been made. Therefore, there is also more available data
on the AE and PEME technologies. The focus in this review is the mature electrolyzer
technologies i.e. the AE, PEME [14] as well as the SOE while not including the AEME
technology. Currently there is no one dominant technology for all applications, as every
technology has challenges in e.g. material performance, durability and maturity which
also leads increased competition in the field [18].

Electrolyzer comparison
The main specifications of the three electrolyzer technologies are summarized in fig. 2.4.
The PEMEC and the SOEC operate at high current densities, ranging from 600­2000A/m3
for PEMEC and 300­2000A/m3 for the SOEC [23]. The current operational density of the
AEC is significantly lower, ranging from 100­400A/m3. In [21] it is reported that the current
density for the PEMEC ranges from 1000­2000A/m3, for the SOEC 300 – 1000A/m3 and
200­600A/m3 for the AEC. These sources give various ranges but all state that the AEC
has significantly lower current density than PEMEC and SOEC, resulting in a more com­
pact device for PEMEs and SOEs compared to the AE, which may prove advantageous
when volume is a constraint.
As reported in fig. 2.4 the operating temperature of the SOEC is significantly higher than
for the AEC and PEMEC. In [21] similar temperature ranges are reported which state an
operating temperature range of 60­90 °C for AEC, 50­80 °C for PEMEC and 700­1000
°C for the SOEC. This gives SOEC the advantage of operating at a higher efficiencies
than AEC and PEMEC [24]. It is even being reported that SOEC can reach 100% effi­
ciency, meaning that that all of the input energy i.e. heat and electricity goes to hydrogen
production [25].

==PAGE:24==

The operating pressure varies significantly for the different types as seen in fig. 2.4. PEM
can operate at the highest pressure followed by AEC while the SOEC only operates at
atmospheric pressure. Different operational ranges are reported in [21], where operating
pressure ranges from 1­15 bar for the SOEC, 10­30 for the AEC and 20­50 bar for PEM. In
[26] it is reported that the operational pressure only has a minor impact on the performance
of the electrolyzer. and it is rather a question of the investment cost of buying an additional
hydrogen compressor versus the degradation of the electrolyzer cell when pressurized.
Comparing the ramping flexibility of the different types in fig. 2.4 it is clear that the PEME
has the fastest ramping capability, ramping up from 0­100% in one second. Ramp rates of
0.2­20 %/sec are reported for the AE while no specific time range is reported for the SOE
other than a slow ramp time. 0­100% ramp time of both AE and PEME are reported within
less than a second to a few seconds when operated at nominal temperature in [26], while
no ramping time was reported for SOE. Due to their quick ramp rates, AE and especially
the PEMEare very well suited when connected to a variable renewable source like wind
and solar [27] and for grid stabilization [26].
The start response time in fig. 2.4 does not state if it is from a warm or cold state. However,
[26] defines cold start as a start up from ambient temperature after a long shut down.
A warm start up is defined as a start up from idle or stand­by mode i.e. at operating
temperature and pressure. From these conditions, the warm start up times are reported
at 1­5 min, < 10 s and 15 min for AE, PEME and SOE respectively. The reported cold start
up time is 1­2 hours for the AE, 5­10 minutes for PEME, while for SOE, no start up time is
reported, but the unit should be held at high operating temperature in idle mode if possible
due to thermal stress and a long start up time would be required to re­heat the unit. In
[21] similar numbers are reported, with warm start up times of 1­5 min, < 10 s and 15 min
for AE, PEME and SOE respectively are reported. The respective cold start up times are
1­2 hours, 5­10 min and multiple hours. [21] does not state the conditions for the cold and
warm start up, but the reported start up times for all technologies match the ones found in
[26], where the conditions are defined. Although different values are reported in [28] [26]
[21] it is clear that the PEME has the quickest start up times compared to the AEC and
SOEC.

for a short time period, as increased degradation at high overpotentials has to be consid­
ered [26] [23]. The AE and SOE have load ranges of 10­110% and 20­100% respectively.
[21] reports load ranges of 20­100%, 0­100% and ­100­100 % for AEC, PEM and SOEC
respectively. [26] reports a minimal load for AE of 20­25 %, while no technical minimum

3.1
Electricity markets
Since the liberalization of the European electricity sector began in 1998, electrical energy
and related products have been traded in open markets [40]. Like most liberalized power
systems, the Danish electricity market is divided into a wholesale market and a retail mar­
ket1. The wholesale market can be divided into sub­markets based on the time between a
trade and the delivery of the traded electricity: time prior to the day before delivery (before
D­1), on the day before delivery (D­1) and on the day of delivery (D), where power trading
is carried out on the forward­, day­ahead­ and intraday market, respectively.
3.1.1
Forward market
In the forward market, also referred to as the futures market, bilateral trades of energy
more than a day prior to delivery are traded between a buyer and a seller. This market
functions similarly to a classic financial or commodities market, where the seller enters
a contract to deliver a specified amount of energy within a certain time frame, at a given
time in the future. The buyer is then obligated to act as the offtaker of the energy. A
Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) is an example of a forward contract that is typically
associated with new RES projects.
3.1.2
Day­ahead market
The day­ahead (DA) market is the largest marketplace for the trade of electric energy in the
Nordic region, with trade volumes making up roughly 70% of total power consumed [41].
In February 2014, Denmark and 16 other European countries launched North­Western
Europe (NWE) Price Coupling, which has since been expanded to the Single Day­ahead
Coupling (SDAC), including most European nations. In this market, power producers
(sellers) and consumers (buyers) can trade their respective power production and con­
sumption one day ahead of the actual occurrence of the production and consumption. In
order to participate in the market, market actors are required to be a Balance Responsible
Party (BRP). As a BRP, the market actor is financially responsible for any imbalances be­
tween agreements on power production or offtake. Individual producers and consumers
also have the option of outsourcing the responsibility to another BRP. In that case, pro­
ducers/consumers inform the BRP of their expected production/consumption and the BRP
handles the market participation on their behalf. The BRPs submit the bids to a nominated
electricity market operator (NEMO), which is responsible for running the market. Nord­
Pool, EMCO and EPEX Spot are the responsible NEMOs in Denmark [42]. Finally, all
NEMOs submit the received bids and offers to the SDAC, where the centralized market
clearing optimization is executed [43]. This process is illustrated in fig. 3.1.

the minimum compensation they require for the production (bid price, €/MWh). Similarly,
power consumers bid their consumption, i.e. the amount of energy they wish to purchase
in a given hour (MWh) at a maximum price (€/MWh).
The bids are sorted in a merit­order curve starting with the lowest sales prices and high­
est purchasing prices for power production and consumption respectively. These curves
meet at the market equilibrium and the market clearing price is determined. The mar­
ket utilizes a marginal pricing scheme. All buyers and sellers in this market either pay
or receive the market clearing price, also known as the DA­price, equal to the bid price
of the marginal producer/consumer. This is a gross simplification of the SDAC clearing
mechanism, but serves the purpose of illustrating the main principle behind the market
clearing mechanism. More sophisticated bid methods are possible [44], but these are not
considered for simplicity reasons.

3.1.3
Intraday market
As the DA market closes at 12.00 the day before delivery the energy trades are locked
12­35 hours prior to the delivery of the traded energy. Imbalances are therefore difficult
to avoid. An updated wind speed forecast or a faulty generator can change a producers
ability to provide the DA obligations, while a temperature decrease may increase the con­
sumer demand for electric heating and cause the DA consumer obligations to fall short of
the required energy. These imbalances can be addressed on the intraday (XBID) market.
This market opens at 15.00 CET (D­1), three hours after the gate closure of the market
and is open until an hour before time of delivery. Therefore a producer who has sold
30MWh on the DA market between 10.00­11.00, but realizes in the morning that he can
only provide 25MWh in that time period may purchase 5MWh in the intraday market until
09.00 to avoid an imbalance.
Just as the market, Europe is pursuing a single intraday market with the Single Intraday
Coupling initiative (SIDC) [45]. Started in 2018, the initiative has now connected 23 Eu­
ropean countries in a single market. The amount of energy traded on the intraday market
is considerably smaller than on the market, but is expected to increase significantly in the
coming years as larger imbalances from intermittent renewables are to be integrated into
the power system [41].
The market operates with a pay­as­bid pricing, where the highest buy­price bid is matched
with the lowest sell­price, in a continuous settlement mechanism The same principle is
applied in the stock market [41]. The intraday market differs in that sense from the mar­
ket, where all market participants (ignoring bottlenecks) receive the same market clearing
price for energy.

Manual frequency restoration reserve

4.3
Reserve provision capability
In order to identify the reserve markets where participation by the HRES is possible, the
technical characteristics of the system must be evaluated in relation to the technical re­
quirements of prequalification specified by the TSO in [53].
The two key components determining the capabilities of the HRES in a reserve provision
context are the electrolyzer and the methanol plant, as nothing downstream from the
methanol reactor has a restrictive effect on the material flow rate.
Given the electrolyzer nominal capacity of 52.5MW and minimum load requirement of
5%, the operational range of the electrolyzer is 0.95 · 52.5MW = 49.88MW. Assuming a
continuous setpoint option, the electrolyzer can therefore provide a reserve of any value
from 0 to 49.88MW in either direction (up­ or downregulation) by varying the load setpoint
from its minimum load (2.625MW) to nominal load (52.5MW). In order to be approved as a
reserve provider, the HRES must also meet a ramp rate requirement, i.e. the rate at which
it is possible to change the consumption or generation of the asset. The electrolyzer ramp
rate of 10%/s translates to a ramp rate of 0.10 · 52.5MW · 60s/min = 315MW/min.
The methanol plant is dimensioned to follow the setpoint of the electrolyzer across the
full range. The main compressors in the system have a fixed power consumption and
therefore do not enforce any restrictions when varying the electrolyzer power setpoint.
The range of the methanol plant in a power equivalent capacity is therefore identical to
the electrolyzer range and does not infringing on the PtX plant load range. The methanol
plant ramp rate is limited to 5% of nominal flow per minute1. This equals an electrical
ramp rate of 0.05 · 52.5MW = 2.625MW/min. This reduces the ramping capability of the
electrolyzer, since the hydrogen production rate must match the hydrogen compression
rate of the compressors of the methanol plant, which in turn must match the injection rate
of hydrogen into the reactor. With the electrical range and ramp rate of the PtX plant
quantified, the requirements of each reserve type are evaluated.
mFRR is a unidirectional (asymmetrical) product, usually only purchased as upregulation,
meaning that the PtX plant only needs to be able to change demand in one direction if
providing mFRR. The maximum bid of mFRR is 50MW, but the range of the plant is limited
to 49.88MW due to the minimum load . As the full reserve must be activated within 15
minutes of activation, the maximum ramp rate of the PtX plant allows for a supply of
2.625MW/min · 15min = 39.38MW. Regulation also requires that the reserve should be
activated with a ramp rate limited to 20%/minute of the nominal capacity of the unit. The
setpoint must therefore not change by more than 0.20min−1·52.5MW = 10.5MW/min, not
inflicting on the operation of the plant. The minimum allowable bid in the mFRR market is
5MW.
The requirements for aFRR supply are similar to those of mFRR but vary in one key
aspect, as aFRR is a bidirectional (symmetrical) product. When supplying aFRR, the
setpoint of the electrolyzer must be able to change by the size of the reserve in both di­
rections. This limits the bid range to 50% of the full range of the plant, or 0.50·49.88MW =
24.94MW. The ramp rate requirements are the same as those of mFRR supply (thereby
limiting the bid size to 39.38MW). Unlike the mFRR, the aFRR does not have a reported
maximum ramp rate.
In the Nordic aFRR balancing market, aFRR is an asymmetrical product and is therefore
divided into two different products, aFRR­up and aFRR­down. Assuming that ramp rate
requirements match those of the current regulations in DK1, the PtX plant’s ability to de­

6
Mathematical optimization and
simulation techniques
Mathematical programming or mathematical optimization is applied in order to optimize
the operation of the HRES with the ultimate goal of improving the final production cost of
methanol throughout the full lifetime of the system. With the technical specifications of the
system and the considered markets defined in fig. 4.6, mathematical optimization models
are developed to optimize the operating points and market participation of the HRES.
Three models have been developed to solve three mathematical optimization problems.
The Deterministic DA model (model 1), the Deterministic Reserve Market model (model
2) and the Stochastic Reserve Market model (model 3). Model 1 demonstrates how well
the system performs when only participating in the DA market as a price­taker. Model
2 is developed to compare the performance of the system when the reserve market is
included. Lastly, a representation of the inherent market uncertainty both in the DA market
and the reserve markets is modelled in model 3. All models perform a mathematical
optimization of the HRES operation, formulated as minimization problems. The objective
is the minimization of the total variable operational expenses (vOPEX) of operating the
HRES.
Any cost (or revenue) that can be predetermined, i.e. the investment cost (CAPEX) or the
fixed yearly expenses (fOPEX), are treated separately and independently of the model in
section 7.1 and section 7.2. In the deterministic DA model only the DA market is consid­
ered whereas both the deterministic­ and stochastic sreserve market models also consider
the reserve market, where FCR, aFRR and mFRR capacity can be sold. Two different
approaches have been taken in the modelling of electrolyzer efficiency and two variations
of models 1 and 2 are therefore presented. In line with the markets of participation, the
time resolution of the model is hourly.
For all models no DA market participation is explicitly modelled with bid prices and vol­
umes. Instead, the HRES setpoints indirectly sets the traded volume as either a realized
energy import or export. The bid is completely inelastic, meaning that the bid volume is
required with no regards to the clearing price.

6.3
Stochastic optimization in the reserve market (model 3)
In order to incorporate the uncertainty of the markets, stochastic programming is applied
to the optimization problem. Due to the different GCTs and market clearing times of dif­
ferent markets, decisions will have to be made without the perfect foresight applied in
the deterministic modelling approaches of 6.1 and 6.2. For all markets, the volume and
price bid in the market, be it energy in the DA market or reserve capacity, must natu­
rally be submitted before the market is cleared in order to be considered. As the market
outcomes are not known, the acceptable payment, i.e. the bid price, for the offered prod­
uct must be submitted based on an expected market outcome. In addition, the various
markets are cleared on the same day, but at different times of the day, which leads to
decisions being made in stages as new information becomes available. Fig. 6.2 shows
the approach taken to divide the problem into stages. The first stage concerns all ac­
tion related to the reserve market. The two first events in the timeline are the GCTs of
the FCR and aFRR markets at 07.30 and 08.00, respectively. At 08.30 and 09.10, the
outcome of the market clearing is known for the FCR and aFRR market. The FCR and
aFRR bids must therefore both be submitted before any information that may influence
the HRES operation is known. The feedback from these markets does occur before the
GCT of the mFRR market. It would therefore be possible to base the mFRR bid in part on
the information received from the FCR and aFRR markets. This option has however not
been pursued. Instead, all reserve market bids are collected in the first stage. Prior to any
market feedback, all reserve bid volumes and prices are chosen. The second stage of the
optimization relates to the DA market. The DA GCT occurs after the feedback is received
from all reserve markets and the system is therefore able to react to this information with
regards to the DA market. The DA market feedback occurs at 13.00 (D­1). No action is
taken after the DA market is cleared and the optimization problem is therefore casted as
a two­stage stochastic problem.

6.4
Scenario generation
Market uncertainty in the models is represented by numerous scenarios of market prices.
Before producing these scenarios, a suitable technique should be found. This can be
done by investigating the market data for any correlation between the various markets,
as well as by checking the data for seasonality. This is explained in section 7.4.1 and
section 7.4.2, respectively. After conducting this analysis, it is clear that there is a high
correlation between the DA, aFRR and mFRR markets, while there is a low correlation
regarding the FCR market. This justifies the decision to perform time dependent sampling,
in addition to independent time sampling for comparison. The price series show a strong
daily seasonality for all markets. To preserve the seasonality as well as the correlation
between the markets, block bootstrapping is a suitable technique.
6.4.1
Block bootstrap
The principle of the block bootstrap technique is that the original data is divided into blocks
of a custom specified length whereafter it is sampled randomly to produce a series of
blocks which make up a scenario. This is illustrated in fig. 6.4. In this case, the original
data consists of an array of values from 1 to 9 which could represent time, i.e. t=1, t=2, etc.
After the original data is divided into blocks, it is then randomly sampled to constitute an
array which is used as a scenario. The number of permutations depends on the number
of blocks that are sampled and the length of the scenario. In the case of fig. 6.4, the block
length is 3 and the scenario length is 2. As 32 = 9, there are 9 possible permutations
which can be used as scenarios, all of which are equiprobable.
Figure 6.4: Block bootstrap example
In section 7.4.1 it is shown that some of the markets have a strong correlation. In order
to preserve this correlation, it is determined that the sampling should be performed time
independently as well as time dependently. In order to conduct an independent sampling,
only one data type, e.g. market or PV data, is fed to the block bootstrap algorithm at
a time, resulting in the algorithm sampling only a block of size [1 x t] at a time. When
combining the sampling of the data, all variables i.e. prices or power can be sampled
together (dependent sampling) resulting in a block of size [6 x t]. The correlation matrix
shows that the FCR data is the only market with poor correlation to all other markets
44
==PAGE:59==
(seefig. 7.9). The combined sampling should therefore consist of a [4 x t] array including
the DA price, aFRR prices and the mFRR price as the correlation is very high for these
markets. The FCR market data is sampled independently at [1 x t] as low correlation is
shown.
To determine the length of the block, i.e. the amount of consecutive hours sampled from

Scenario reduction
The generated scenarios must be reduced to a suitable number of representative scenar­

K­medoids is used as the scenario reduction technique, as it is a simple approach and it is
possible to preserve the patterns e.g. seasonality in the series. K­medoids is a clustering
algorithm and works as shown in the flow chart in fig. 6.5.

correlation between the series, the mean value of the aFRR, mFRR and DA price is taken
for each hour and is fed as an input to the clustering algorithm. In order to retrieve the
original data, another algorithm identifies the index of the original data that matches the
output of each medoid from the cluster algorithm.

Grid connection
[83] lays out the design of the new tariffing method for power producers by the Danish
TSO Energinet. The method took effect on January 1st 2023 and is therefore applied
to the project­specific costs of grid connection. The relevant costs associated with grid
connection are summarized in [84]. These costs include a grid connection contribution
(tilslutningsbidrag), which consists of a station fee (stationsbidrag) and a connection fee
(tilslutningsbidrag til nært net).
The station fee covers the TSO cost of establishing the physical connection and paying
for expansions of the transformer station busbars, etc. The fee is standardized based on
the voltage level of the grid connection. At the 400kV level, this amounts to 12mDKK.
The connection fee is geographically differentiated between areas with excess production
and excess consumption. In areas with excess production, the addition of new production
capacity will bring new requirements to the grid, either by reserving scarce grid capacity
or by requiring the TSO to expand the existing capacity. As such, connection fees in areas
with excess production are 0.328mDKK/MW as compared to 0.101mDKK/MW in areas
with excess consumption. An analysis by Green Power Denmark has categorized Danish
geozones into three categories: producer­dominated (red), consumer­dominated (green)
and neither (yellow). This is shown in fig. 7.5.

efficiency), and model parameters than cannot be varied within a continuous range (FCR
max bid).

Discussion
The vOPEX results and the corresponding LCoM have shown that both the additional
market participation and the market year applied in simulations have a significant impact
on the production cost, and that the stochastic model, bidding into the reserve market
with imperfect uncertainty, achieves significant cost reduction. Despite the cost reduc­
tions achieved by participation in the frequency reserve capacity markets, the produced
e­methanol is not within the market price range of conventionally produced methanol in
Europe and is therefore not cost competitive in an open market. All model results fall
within the e­methanol production cost range provided by IRENA [7], indicating that the
general cost estimates are within a reasonable range
In order to address the research question, the developed model required several assump­
tions and simplifications, which in turn influence the results. Some of these have been
explored in the sensitivity analysis, where the major CAPEX components were proven
to have a very high impact on the LCoM. The most cost sensitive component by far was
the PV CAPEX, where a 20% change led to a LCoM difference of 9%. Being a mature
technology, it is however unlikely that the cost of a PV plant will change dramatically in the
near­future. The second most sensitive fixed cost parameter was the PEM electrolyzer
CAPEX, which is yet to become a mature technology. It would therefore be relevant to
further investigate current cost projections of the technology. It was also demonstrated
that electrolyzer efficiency was the most sensitive parameter in the 2021 market as this
directly affected the energy consumption per produced unit of methanol. This could serve
as motivation to consider replacing the purchased technologies with more efficient alter­
natives, like high­temperature SOEC, before the technological end of life of the HRES.
The methanol demand, which throughout the investigation was assumed fixed, may be
worth revisiting, depending on the state of the market. In 2020, a higher production led
to a lower cost per unit, but this was not the case in 2021, where both increasing and
decreasing the yearly production led to an increase in LCoM, as the additional net cost
in the DA market simply surpassed the effect of an increased production. Shorter run­
ning contracts with offtakers should therefore be considered, especially in markets with
relatively high prices and volatility. Additional revenue sources that may bring down the
production cost could also be accounted for in the economic analysis, for example tthe
sale of the excess heat from the production or the sale of produced oxygen, a biprod­
uct from the electrolysis. Heat from PtX plants is well suited for integration into district
heating and could lower production costs of PtX [100]. The economic potential of the
oxygen biproduct is demonstrated in [101], where an SOEC electrolysis­based methanol
PtX­plant oxygen sales amount to 34% of revenue.
A simplified representation of the physical system and energy and reserve markets was
necessary in order to define a realistic scope for the investigation. Some of these mod­
elling choices could be reconsidered and their implications investigated for future studies.
Regarding the system itself, as the ramping time of the HRES directly affects the volume
of reserves that can be provided, it would be relevant to develop a more detailed repre­
sentation of the system components limiting the ramping time, or even applying different
ramping times reported in literature. In terms of the energy and reserve markets, the
applied market assumptions, such as using non­Danish reserve market data, mean that
the results do not precisely represent the performance of the PtX plant in Kassø. DK1
mFRR market data was applied directly, but as mFRR revenue made up the smallest part
98
==PAGE:113==
of reserve market revenue, the majority of revenue was based on proxy markets. For
the FCR market data was based on the FCR Cooperation German bidding zone. This
was done in part due to the extremely high FCR prices in the Danish zone, which were
considered unrealistic for a 25­year economic analysis. It is possible, however, that lo­
cal circumstances sustain a systemically high price area, in which case the FCR revenue
may have been underestimated. The aFRR revenue accounts for the majority of added
revenue from reserve market participation but is based on Swedish market data. It was
deemed preferable to use the Swedish data as a proxy for DK1 prices, as DK1 will soon
be incorporated into the same Nordic aFRR capacity market of which Sweden is already
a member.
Another major simplification in the modelling of the reserve market is the omission of the
energy delivery obligations that accompany the provision of reserves. In the case of FCR,
the energy delivery is associated with minor frequency deviations between 20mHz and
200mHz in the system. The energy deliver would therefore occur continuously throughout
the day anytime the frequency deviates by 20mHz or more from the nominal frequency.
Although the total energy delivery associated with this reserve type is low, the setpoint
of the electrolyzer and therefore also the compressors and methanol reactor would be
subject to short­term, transient load changes, which may cause long­term degradation
of the electrolyzer [102]. For the more energy­intense reserves (aFRR and mFRR), the
energy delivery could have a higher impact on the production schedule as well as energy
costs, as the full range of regulation was often sold in the market. If activated, the HRES
load would then go from minimum load to full load, or vice versa, which is in contrast to
the optimization program, and the change in setpoint would be subject to penalization due
to deviations from power traded in the DA market.
The potential PV production is considered to be known at all times, i.e. perfect weather
foresight is applied in the model, in order to focus on market uncertainty. In reality, the
changing weather forecasts would introduce another stage to the decision­making pro­
cess, where the PV imbalance would have to be compensated for in either an XBID or
regulating power market.
The uncertainty modelling was designed to reflect uncertainty in the energy and reserve
markets. As the scenario generation was conducted using only a single technique, an
out of sample test could validate the robustness of the model. This test, using another
scenario generation technique such as a regression­based model, would check for biases
and evaluate the stochastic part of the current model, the block bootstrapping technique.
As European market designs are realized and market development decelerates, more
representative historical data becomes available. With adequate historical data, it will be
possible to use a regression model as opposed to scenario generation to predict market
prices.
A known bias in the stochastic approach is the use of representative weeks in the simula­
tion. This was necessary to reduce simulation time. Although these weeks are weighted
with a probability indicating the representation level of the full data, representative weeks
cannot capture any extreme observations. This bias could be assessed and possibly be
reduced by individually choosing a representative week for every month. This would re­
sult in 12 representative weeks as opposed to 10, which would also be feasible regarding
simulation time. The PV data could be chosen using similar method. The two approaches
could then be compared and analyzed in similar fashion to investigate the difference.
Based on the above discussion, two themes of future work emerge which could potentially
lead to further insights into the techno­economic case of the PtX project and add additional
99
==PAGE:114==
value to the developed model. These themes of future research are outlined below.
Techno­economic case improvements
• Quantify the additional value of process heat which could be sold to district heating
networks
• Include the produced oxygen from electrolysis as a product for sale
• Investigate the effect of different demand obligations, e.g. flexible production vol­
umes
Model extensions
• Introduce a third stage variable where a roulette or other method is applied to mimic
the stochastic process of a reserve being activated
• Include the weather uncertainty
• Model the thermodynamic and chemical characteristics of methanol synthesis for
an improved understanding of the effect of setpoint changes of the electrolyzer
• Include a hydrogen storage tank to decouple electrolyzer production from the hy­
drogen injection to the reactor

Conclusion
The studied HRES has been investigated and simplified for the purpose of modelling
the system characteristics most relevant to the optimization of DA and reserve market
interaction. Including a 300MW PV plant and a PtX plant, consisting of 52.5MW PEM
water­electrolysis capacity and a methanol synthesis­ and distillation unit, the HRES is
required to deliver 32,000 tonnes of fuel­grade methanol in weekly deliveries. As the flow
into the distillation unit is assumed constant, the raw storage tank is crucial to the flexibility
of the PtX plant. The flexibility of the PEM electrolyzer technology has been established
in existing literature, with a wide load range, fast ramp rates and a non­linear efficiency
characteristic. A minimum load of 5% of nominal load and a ramp rate of 10% of nominal
load per second is applied for this specific HRES electrolyzer. Downstream from the
electrolyzer, the compressors for the inflow of hydrogen and carbon dioxide to the reactor
are modelled and accounted for in the system power consumption. The compressors
restrict the ramping capability of the PtX plant to 5% of nominal flow per minute, the
equivalent of 2.6MW/min in electrical load.
Current reserve market regulations allow for the approval of the PtX plant as a reserve
provider for all reserve products purchased in DK1, namely FCR, aFRR and mFRR. While
technically possible, th PV plant is not considered for reserve provision. The respective
market caps are 20MW, 100MW and 284MW and are all traded on different platforms.
Due to a combination of market failures, extraordinarily high prices and near­future plans
of moving to multi­country markets, FCR and aFRR market data of Germany and Swe­
den, respectively, are used as proxies for the Danish market. While the FCR in DK1 and
Germany are currently traded in a joint European market, the aFRR proxy includes daily
auctions of hourly reserve provisions, compensated by marginal pricing, as opposed to
current Danish practices of monthly or hourly auctions with a fixed reserve for the entire
period, compensated by pay­as­bid. As Danish authorities have stated their intention of
moving to the Nordic platform currently used in Sweden, the proxy is considered a more
relevant market than current Danish practices. Due to ramp rate and granularity require­
ments, the HRES qualifies of reserve volume provisions of 1MW FCR and 39.3MW of
mFRR and asymmetrical aFRR, while symmetrical aFRR provision would be limited to
24.9MW due to the load range limitations of the electrolyzer.
Optimization models formulated as MILP cost minimization problems have been devel­
oped and verified. Model variations investigate several aspects of the HRES. The deter­
ministic model, only participating in the DA market, realizes a total annual variable cost
increase of over 160% from 2020 to 2021. This is a result of dramatic increases in both
revenue from the sale of PV generation and the cost of purchased energy, which clearly
demonstrates the effect of changing market climates.
Modelling the electrolyzer efficiency as decreasing with load level leads to a smoother
transition between load levels, as compared to constant efficiency implementation. When
no loss of efficiency is present at higher loads, a more binary transition between high and
low load setpoints dominates the electrolyzer behaviour. The simplification to a constant
efficiency representation leads to DA and tariff­related cost reductions of 11% and 12% in
the 2020 and 2021 markets, respectively.
With the introduction of reserve market participation in model 2, though still applying per­
fect foresight, the revenue of the HRES increases dramatically by 121% in 2020 and 119%
101
==PAGE:116==
in 2021, with the main contributing factor being participation in the aFRR­up and aFRR­
down market. Reserve market participation also leads to a small increase in DA­related
costs (2.6% in 2020 and 0.1% in 2021).
The participation in the reserve markets results in a change in net variable operational
cost from 3.19m€ to ­1.47m€ in 2020. In 2021 these values are 8.32m€ and ­4.97m€
respectively. The market climate in 2021 with relatively high prices and higher volatility
is proven to be highly beneficial for reducing operational costs when participating in the
reserve market. This is also reflected in the resulting Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCoM).
Only participating in the DA market results in a LCoM of 1199€/t, assuming a 2020 market
throughout the 25­year lifetime of the HRES. The cost increases to 1360€/t when assum­
ing a 2021 market, an increase of 13.4%. By participating in the reserve market, not only
is the LCoM reduced in both cases, but the 2020­based LCoM is reduced by 10.4% from
2020 to 2021, from 1054€/t to 944€/t.
To represent the market structures and the inherent uncertainty in market prices, a 2­
stage stochastic MILP minimization problem has been implemented in model 3, where
reserve market bids and HRES setpoints are based on generated price scenarios, apply­
ing sampling methods based on market data inter­ and autocorrelation. The introduction
of uncertainty leads to a sub­optimal utilization of the system flexibility in the DA market
and reserve provisions.
As it was not possible to retain computational tractability for full­year simulations, a method
was developed to address this issue utilizing representative sub­intervals of the full year
data series. After identifying the sub­intervals and applying them in models 2 and 3, a
performance review comparing both model results allowed for the translation of these
results to an estimate of the outcome of the intended full year simulation, accounting for
uncertainty.
Under market uncertainty, LCoM reductions are still substantial, with a LCoM of 1120€/t
in 2020 and 1041€/t in 2021. These are respectively 7% and 23% lower than the LCoM
achieved by the model with perfect foresight, only operating in the DA market (1199€/t
and 1360€/t).
Compared to the market price of conventional methanol, the e­methanol produced by the
HRES is not cost competitive, with European methanol futures trading in the range of
471€/t to 571€/t in 2021. The results do however indicate that there are substantial cost
reductions to be realized by reserve market participation and that these reductions may
increase with increasingly higher power and reserve market prices and volatility. Partici­
pation in the frequency reserve capacity markets may therefore act not only as a means
of total cost reduction, but also as an insurance policy against volatile energy markets.
