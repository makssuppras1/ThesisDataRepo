Abstract
The increasing emphasis on sustainability in the cement and concrete industry has promp-
ted the development of innovative binders aimed at significantly reducing CO2 emis-
sions. Among these advancements, FUTURECEM®, characterized by partial substitution
of clinker with calcined clay and limestone filler, is particularly noteworthy. This thesis thor-
oughly investigates the self-desiccation phenomena in modern cement binders, focusing
specifically on FUTURECEM® and FUTURECEM® with a 5% silica fume addition, and
comparing them with traditional binders such as ordinary Portland cement (OPC).
Self-desiccation, a critical factor influencing autogenous shrinkage, arises from the re-
duction of internal relative humidity within cementitious materials due to hydration and
chemical shrinkage processes. Understanding and effectively controlling self-desiccation
is vital to mitigate early-age cracking, enhance the durability of concrete structures, and
achieve improvements in construction quality, productivity, and environmental sustain-
ability.
This research combines both experimental and theoretical methodologies to explore in-
ternal relative humidity evolution, autogenous deformation, pore structure development,
and chemical shrinkage in six binder systems tested in both paste and concrete. These
systems include three formulations (Rapid cement, FUTURECEM®, and FUTURECEM®
with 5% silica fume) at two water-to-binder ratios (w/b) of 0.32 and 0.37.
Experimental techniques include capacitive sensor measurements of internal relative hu-
midity, dilatometry (cf.ASTM-C1698-09) for assessing autogenous deformation, thermo-
gravimetric analysis to characterize hydration processes, mercury intrusion porosime-
try (MIP) for pore structure analysis, and chemical shrinkage measurements to evalu-
ate hydration dynamics. Powers’ model complements these experimental approaches,
facilitating the calculation of hydration degrees and establishing relationships with self-
desiccation. Additionally, the Kelvin equation is applied to interpret capillary pressure and
pore humidity relations, and the Arrhenius equation is used to account for the influence
of temperature on hydration kinetics.
Results indicate that FUTURECEM® exhibits autogenous deformation and internal hu-
midity loss characteristics comparable to traditional OPC formulations. The combination
of calcined clay and limestone filler provides beneficial properties that align closely with
the industry’s needs for self-desiccating concrete. The incorporation of silica fume in
FUTURECEM® at 0.37 formulation positively impacts these characteristics, offering valu-
able options for tailored concrete performance. Experimental data support theoretical
predictions, affirming FUTURECEM® as a sustainable binder with performance suitable
for practical applications.
From an industrial perspective, Unicon A/S has expressed interest in this thesis project
due to the potential of self-desiccating concrete to deliver substantial practical benefits.
These include reduced construction timelines, decreased drying and energy costs, sim-
plified moisture control documentation, improved environmental profiles, and minimized
moisturerelated risks. By leveraging FUTURECEM selfdesiccating properties, Unicon
aims to enhance product quality, productivity, and sustainability, positioning itself at the
forefront of environmentally friendly construction practices.
This research advances the understanding of selfdesiccation phenomena in modern bind-
ers, provides practical guidance for sustainable material development, and aligns with

Introduction
Self-desiccation is an internal drying phenomenon in cement-based materials caused by the hydration pro-
cess, where cement chemically reacts with water and leads to a reduction in the total volume of solids and
liquids, resulting in the formation of internal voids, particularly under sealed conditions. While self-desiccation
can occur in any concrete regardless of the water-to-cement ratio, it is especially pronounced in modern con-
cretes with low water-to-binder ratios, such as high-performance or low-clinker systems, and has become a
topic of increasing concern due to its significant influence on early-age behavior and long-term durability [1].
The primary consequence of self-desiccation is a reduction in internal relative humidity (RH) [1]. This re-
duced RH has multifaceted implications as it induces self-desiccation shrinkage, which occurs earlier than
conventional drying shrinkage. This early shrinkage compounds with thermal shrinkage from hydration heat,
significantly increasing the risk of internal micro and macro cracking during early ages. Furthermore, the
lowered RH drastically shortens the required drying time before moisture-sensitive coverings (e.g., vinyl,
linoleum, wood) can be safely applied. On the other hand, the reduced degree of saturation enhances resis-
tance to internal frost damage, particularly in young concrete.
To meet industrial needs for shorter construction cycles and reliable moisture performance, Unicon A/S,
Denmark’s largest producer of ready-mix concrete, manufactures self-desiccating concrete under the UNI-
Dry® concept [2]. These concretes are designed so that nearly all the mixing water is consumed during
hydration, eliminating excess moisture. UNI-Dry® is offered in two grades: standard (w/c ≤0.40, RH ≤85%
in 6–8 weeks) and extra-fast (w/c ≤0.35, RH ≤85% in 10–15 days), and is typically used in floor slabs
requiring moisture-sensitive finishes such as vinyl, linoleum, or hardwood. The system has been shown to
reduce drying time, save energy, and improve indoor conditions. It complies with Danish building regulations
for RH limits before floor coverings are installed. Figure 1.1 shows the typical consequences of inadequate
moisture management in concrete substrates, highlighting the practical relevance of internal RH monitoring
and self-desiccation mitigation strategies.
Figure 1.1 Examples of moisture-related flooring failures due to improper RH control in concrete substrates [3].
Traditionally, self-desiccating concrete like UNI-Dry® has been made using CEM I cement without supple-
mentary materials such as fly ash or limestone filler to ensure rapid hydration and internal RH reduction.
However, the cement industry faces growing environmental pressure, contributing 5–8% of global CO2 emis-

sions, primarily from clinker production. In response, the development of more sustainable binders has

One promising alternative is FUTURECEM®, a CEM II cement introduced by Aalborg Portland, which achieves
up to 30% CO2 savings by partially replacing clinker with a combination of calcined clay and limestone. This
ternary blend, often referred to as LC3 (limestone calcined clay cement), takes advantage of the pozzolanic

enhance microstructure development and early strength [4].
Studies show that LC3 systems allow for high clinker replacement while maintaining mechanical performance
and controlling autogenous shrinkage [4]. The presence of metakaolin or other calcined kaolinitic clays
contributes to porosity refinement and hydration kinetics, while limestone acts as both filler and reactive
component.
This thesis investigates the use of FUTURECEM® in self-desiccating concrete, addressing a key challenge:
whether a CEM II binder with slower hydration and higher moisture retention can meet the performance
expectations established by conventional CEM I-based UNI-Dry® systems. The study also includes a FU-
TURECEM variant incorporating 5% silica fume to refine the pore structure and accelerate RH reduction.
The goal is to evaluate autogenous shrinkage, internal RH development, and practical drying performance
in both paste and concrete. This research not only supports Unicon’s industrial interest in sustainable self-
desiccating concrete but also contributes academically to a better understanding of hydration-driven RH
dynamics in new binder systems.
1.1
Aim of the Project
The project further aims to clarify technical uncertainties related to field performance, including the impact
of new composite cements (clinker content 0.6–0.7) on self-desiccation, inconsistencies between field mea-
surements and simulation tools such as TorkaS, the effect of surface sealing on moisture entrapment through
moisture distribution profiles within elements, and the overall CO2 balance considering potential energy sav-
ings from eliminated forced drying. These findings will inform future design recommendations and support
the responsible use of low-clinker cements in high-performance, moisture-critical applications.

Terminology
To establish a scientifically consistent foundation for this project, this chapter defines the key terms and con-
ceptual interrelations surrounding self-desiccation. The terminology is aligned with the framework developed
by Jensen and Hansen (2001) [5] and supported by standards such as ASTM C1698-09[6].
Self-desiccation is defined as the internal drying of a sealed cement paste, caused by hydration consuming
capillary water. It is characterized by a reduction in RH without any exchange of moisture with the external
environment. This phenomenon is particularly significant in modern low w/b systems, such as those using
FUTURECEM®, where the amount of mixing water is insufficient to fully hydrate the binder. As RH decreases,
capillary tension increases and initiates shrinkage stresses that can cause cracking.
Closely related is the concept of autogenous relative humidity change. This refers to any RH change
occurring internally within a sealed and isothermal cement-based system. Self-desiccation is the most promi-
nent mechanism of such change, although RH variations due to the dissolution of salts can also occur. The
reduction in RH is thermodynamically described by the Kelvin equation, where smaller pore radii lead to
higher capillary tension and thus lower RH.
Self-desiccation shrinkage (SDS) is refers to the macroscopic contraction that concrete exhibits as its
internal relative humidity (RH) falls during hydration. It is typically observed after setting and represents the
primary mechanism of autogenous deformation in dense binder systems. It differs from chemical shrinkage,
which refers to the reduction in absolute volume during hydration reactions but is not directly observed as a
physical strain once the material has set.
Autogenous deformation is defined as the bulk, unrestrained volume change in sealed, isothermal cement
pastes or concretes. This includes shrinkage from self-desiccation, early expansion caused by salt formation
(such as ettringite), and microstructural changes. When autogenous deformation is a net contraction, it is
often called autogenous shrinkage, although that term is best used when self-desiccation is the known
cause.
It is essential to distinguish autogenous phenomena from related types of deformation. Drying shrinkage,
for example, results from water loss to the environment and only occurs in unsealed systems. Thermal
deformation arises from temperature changes and can either expand or contract the material, depending
on conditions. Both are considered non-autogenous phenomena.
The timing of measurements is also important. The term “time zero” is used to define the start of shrinkage
monitoring. This is often based on final setting time (per ASTM C403), the onset of RH drop, or the inflection
point of the strain curve. This is particularly relevant for systems where early swelling may precede shrinkage.
All definitions in this framework assume that the system is sealed and maintained at constant temperature.
This ensures that observed behaviors are intrinsic to the material itself and not the result of external heat or
moisture flows.
From a microstructural point of view, self-desiccation is driven by hydration product formation, which con-
sumes water and densifies the microstructure. As pores shrink, capillary tension rises. Below a critical w/c
ratio (typically around 0.42), the RH drop becomes significant enough to induce measurable SDS. Figure 2.1,
adapted from Jensen and Hansen, graphically summarizes these relationships. It shows that self-desiccation
is a subset of autogenous RH change, while self-desiccation shrinkage is a subset of autogenous deforma-
tion. In contrast, drying, thermal, and load-induced strains are categorized as non-autogenous phenomena.

3
Theory
The primary focus of this research is the phenomenon of self-desiccation and its impact on the early-age
behavior of cement-based materials. Self-desiccation, driven by the internal consumption of water during hy-
dration, leads to a reduction in internal relative humidity (RH), which in turn can cause significant autogenous
shrinkage in sealed, low water-to-binder (w/b) systems.
3.1
Self-desiccation
Self-desiccation is a key phenomenon influencing the early-age behavior of modern cementitious materials,
particularly those characterized by low water-to-binder (w/b) ratios and high-performance demands. It refers
to the internal drying process that occurs within a sealed cement paste due to the chemical consumption of
water during the hydration process. This process leads to a gradual decrease in internal relative humidity
(RH), resulting in capillary tension within the pore structure and potentially inducing autogenous shrinkage.
If this shrinkage is restrained, it can generate significant tensile stresses, often leading to early-age cracking.
These effects are particularly pronounced in concretes with low w/b ratios, where the available mixing water
is insufficient to fully hydrate the binder phases.
The phenomenon of self-desiccation was conceptually acknowledged by Henri Le Chatelier as early as 1900.
Le Chatelier distinguished between absolute volume changes caused by chemical shrinkage and the appar-
ent volumetric deformations observable in sealed cement paste, which we now term autogenous deformation
[7]. The concept was later quantified and expanded upon by Powers and Brownyard in the 1940s, whose
work remains foundational in cement chemistry. Their research introduced the idea of “bound” water and
developed a model to relate hydration progression to pore structure evolution and internal humidity changes.
According to their model, approximately 0.42 g of water per gram of cement is required to fully hydrate a Port-
land cement system, of which about 0.23 g becomes chemically bound and 0.19 g is retained as physically
bound gel water. Therefore, when the initial w/b ratio is below 0.42, the system inherently lacks sufficient
free water to fill all capillary pores, leading to pore desaturation and a drop in RH under sealed conditions
(Powers & Brownyard, 1946) [8].
From a mechanistic perspective, self-desiccation and self-desiccation shrinkage are directly linked to chem-
ical shrinkage during hydration [9]. The phenomenon can be conceptually illustrated sequentially:
Hydration and chemical binding
Cement reacts with water to form solid hydration products such as calcium–silicate–hydrate (C–S–H) and
portlandite (CH), consuming pore water.
Pore-liquid reduction and desaturation
The solid volume increases while the volume of pore fluid decreases, leaving capillary pores partially desat-
urated and filled with air.
Meniscus curvature and RH reduction
The shrinking liquid volume reduces the radius r of water menisci in the remaining pores. This internal drying
is captured by the Kelvin equation, which links meniscus curvature to internal RH:

The negative capillary pressure introduces tensile stress within the cement matrix, contributing directly to
autogenous shrinkage. The magnitude of shrinkage depends on the pore size distribution, hydration kinet-
ics, and mechanical stiffness of the hydrating matrix. This form of internal stress generation is often un-
derestimated but plays a pivotal role in early-age cracking in high-performance and ultra-high-performance
concretes.

3.2
Hydration process of cementitious materials
Upon mixing cementitious materials with water, a series of exothermic hydration reactions converts anhy-
drous clinker minerals into solid hydration products. These products progressively fill available pore spaces,
developing a microstructural skeleton that defines the mechanical properties, durability, and susceptibility to
self-desiccation in the hardened cement paste [10].

3.3
Chemical shrinkage
Chemical shrinkage is the foundational mechanism behind self-desiccation in cementitious materials ( sec-
tion 3.1). It refers to the reduction in absolute volume that occurs during the hydration of cement when solid
hydration products occupy less space than the original anhydrous cement and mixing water. Under sealed
conditions, this volume reduction leads to the formation of internal voids, which, in turn, initiate pore desatu-
ration and a progressive decrease in internal relative humidity (RH). This process sets the stage for capillary
pressure development, autogenous deformation, and potential early-age cracking [19, 20].
The phenomenon of chemical shrinkage was identified by Le Chatelier in 1900 and later quantified by Powers
and Brownyard in the 1940s [8]. It arises from the molecular-level reorganization during hydration reactions
( section 3.2.1). These reactions produce solid hydrates like C-S-H and CH, which have a lower combined
volume than the sum of the original solid clinker minerals and the consumed water. The net result is the
creation of voids in the microstructure that were previously filled with liquid, especially under sealed curing
[10, 11].
Chemical shrinkage involves a range of concurrent reactions, since each clinker phase and resulting hydrate
consumes water at its characteristic rate. Consequently, the overall rate of volume loss varies over time
and depends on factors such as the water-to-cement ratio, temperature, and cement fineness [21, 20, 11].
Because chemical shrinkage is directly tied to the degree of hydration, any change in reaction kinetics from
rapid early hydration of C3S and C3A to the slower conversion of C2S will affect both the timing and magnitude
of internal volume reduction [22].
Typical chemical shrinkage values are in the range of 6–8 mL per 100 g of cement for Portland cement [19,
23], but these values increase significantly in the presence of highly reactive SCMs through their high surface
areas, enhanced reactivity, and elevated water demand. For example, Jensen and Hansen (2001) reported
chemical shrinkage values of up to 22 mL/100 g for pure silica fume pastes [21, 20].
Under sealed conditions, the water lost due to chemical shrinkage is not replaced by external sources. This
leads to the formation of air-filled voids in larger pores and the gradual desaturation of the capillary pore
system. As the water menisci recede into finer pores, the radius of curvature decreases, lowering the internal
RH according to the Kelvin equation eq. (3.1) [20]. When RH falls below a critical threshold (typically ∼80%),
further hydration becomes diffusion-limited, and the capillary pressure generated across the menisci can
induce autogenous shrinkage and stress [20]. This sequence of events defines the phenomenon of self-
desiccation.
3.4
Pore size distribution
Cement paste’s pore structure undergoes continuous transformation during hydration, significantly influenc-
ing internal relative humidity (RH), ion transport, and self-desiccation. Immediately after mixing, cement paste
presents a broad pore size distribution dominated by capillary pores (> 50 nm). These large pores initially
facilitate rapid ion transport and hydration processes. However, as hydration progresses, they become pro-
gressively filled with hydration products like calcium silicate hydrate (C–S–H), calcium hydroxide (CH), and
ettringite, shifting the pore size distribution toward smaller gel pores (2–10 nm) and even finer interlayer pores
(0.5–1.0 nm) within the C–S–H structure [10].
The distribution and evolution of these pore types are significantly influenced by the water-to-binder (w/b)
ratio: higher w/b systems initially present more capillary porosity and maintain saturation longer, while lower
w/b systems have fewer and finer capillary pores, resulting in more rapid refinement and earlier RH drop. In
well-hydrated cement pastes with low water–cement ratios, capillary pores typically range from 10 to 50 nm,
whereas in mixtures with higher water–cement ratios these voids can reach sizes on the order of 3 to 5 µm
at early ages ( fig. 3.4). The pore size distribution rather than the total capillary porosity is therefore a more
meaningful indicator of paste performance: capillary voids larger than roughly 50 nm (macropores) exert the
greatest influence on compressive strength and impermeability, while finer pores (micropores) predominantly
govern drying shrinkage and creep behavior [12].

3.4.2
Water classification
In cementitious materials, water exists in three distinct forms, each playing a unique role in hydration and
self-desiccation:
Capillary Water
Capillary water refers to water present in the relatively large pores between cement
particles, typically > 50 nm in diameter [8, 11]. It is freely mobile, physically unbound, and is the first to be
removed during hydration. Capillary water serves as the main transport medium for ions and is essential for
hydration reactions. As the hydration process proceeds, capillary pores are progressively consumed and the
degree of saturation declines, particularly in low water-to-binder (w/b) systems [24].
Under sealed conditions, once chemical shrinkage leads to the formation of voids and air entry into these
pores, menisci form, generating curvature. This curvature induces capillary tension in the pore fluid and
initiates a drop in RH, as governed by Kelvin’s equation (see Section 3.3)
[20, 19]. The capillary pore
network is therefore the primary domain where self-desiccation begins.
Gel water
Gel water is physically bound within the nanostructure of the calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-
H) gel, typically located in pores ranging from approximately 2–10 nm [11]. Though more tightly held than

==PAGE:22==
capillary water, gel water still exerts a vapor pressure and contributes to internal RH. As hydration products
accumulate and densify, the total volume of gel pores increases while capillary porosity declines.
In sealed systems, the desaturation of gel pores becomes significant after capillary pores are largely con-
sumed. In modern binders with a high specific surface area, such as those containing FUTURECEM®, the
system transitions to a gel-pore, dominated structure more rapidly, intensifying RH drop [17].
Interlayer water
Interlayer water resides within the molecular layers of C-S-H, often between 0.5–1.0
nm spacing, and is strongly adsorbed to the structure. It does not participate in vapor exchange and therefore
does not contribute to RH under sealed conditions [9, 25]. However, it plays a role in long-term shrinkage
and creep under drying or thermal cycles. In early-age sealed curing, it remains stable and has little effect
on the RH evolution relevant to self-desiccation.
Water in hydrated cement paste exists in several states section 3.4.2, as first clarified by Powers and Brown-
yard [8]: capillary water, gel water, and chemically bound water. Capillary water occupies larger pores and
is weakly held, being easily available for hydration and transport processes. Gel water, by contrast, is phys-
ically adsorbed in nanometer-scale pores (gel pores) associated with the surface of calcium silicate hydrate
(C-S-H) particles, while chemically bound water is incorporated into the solid hydration products and cannot
be removed without decomposition. The interplay of these moisture states defines the physical behavior of
the material under varying environmental conditions.

Jensen and Hensen establish that relative humidity in a cement paste is the relative humidity with which
the pore water is in equilibrium. The internal relative humidity is thus an indicator of the activity of the pore
water [5]. Relative humidity (RH) is defined as the ratio of the actual partial pressure of water vapor to the
saturation vapor pressure at the same temperature, typically expressed as a percentage of the saturation
vapor pressure at that temperature.

In cement-based systems, internal RH characterizes the moisture state within the pore system and serves
as a crucial variable influencing hydration kinetics, shrinkage, cracking risk, and long-term durability [26, 10].
Moisture transport in cementitious materials occurs in both liquid and vapor phases and is governed by
gradients in RH, temperature, and capillary pressure, as well as by the evolving pore structure during hy-
dration [27]. Capillary flow, driven by pressure differences in the interconnected pore system, dominates in
the early stages or at high saturation. As drying or self-desiccation progresses and the system becomes
less saturated, vapor diffusion becomes increasingly important, driven by internal RH gradients. Water also
continuously exchanges between vapor and the adsorbed state at the pore surfaces through the processes
of adsorption and desorption, which become especially significant at low RH. The overall rates of moisture
transport are controlled by factors such as pore size distribution, degree of hydration, temperature, and the
connectivity of pores, with the relationship between moisture content and RH being described by sorption
isotherms. These isotherms, which are generally non-linear and exhibit hysteresis between drying and wet-
ting paths, are a key characteristic of cementitious materials [28].
In the project’s scope, the autogenous RH change, often called self-desiccation, refers to the reduction in
internal RH that occurs in a sealed cementitious system as a direct consequence of hydration, without any
external drying [20]. As cement hydrates, water is chemically consumed to form hydration products, leading to
a reduction in free (physically available) water and thus in the internal RH. This is especially significant in high-
performance or low water-to-cement ratio (w/c) concretes, where much of the initial water is consumed rapidly,
intensifying the self-desiccation process. The quantitative relationship between w/c, degree of hydration, and
available water is elegantly captured by the classic Powers model [8].
Capillary pressure is a primary mechanism driving the internal RH drop in cementitious materials [29]. It
arises due to the formation of curved menisci in partially filled pores during hydration or drying. According to
the Kelvin equation eq. (3.1) or eq. (3.2), capillary tension is directly related to the internal RH and the radius
of the meniscus curvature, with smaller pores resulting in higher capillary pressures and lower equilibrium
RH. Capillary pressure dominates in meso- and macropores (radii > 2 nm) and is responsible for most of the
RH decrease and autogenous shrinkage at early and intermediate hydration stages [30].

At advanced stages of hydration, as the remaining water becomes confined to the finest pores, capillary
pressure alone is insufficient to describe the system entirely. Instead, disjoining pressure becomes increas-
ingly significant [9]. Disjoining pressure is a molecular-scale repulsive force that arises when thin films of
adsorbed water between solid surfaces begin to overlap [30]. This pressure, which dominates at very low
RH, stabilizes ultra-thin water layers on pore surfaces and explains why water remains in the cement paste
even at low RH and why the internal RH in well-hydrated, sealed systems never falls to zero [28].
The release of moisture from concrete is called desorption, while the uptake of moisture is called absorption.
The relationship between the equilibrium moisture content of concrete and the surrounding relative humidity
(RH) at a constant temperature can be illustrated using desorption and absorption curves. These curves
are obtained experimentally by measuring the equilibrium moisture-to-dry-matter ratio at various RH levels,
during either drying or wetting. As shown in Figure 3, the desorption curve is typically positioned above the

desorption and absorption curves (such as point A in Figure 3). For practical purposes, a sorption isotherm—

influences RH stability, the progression of self-desiccation, and ultimately, the dimensional stability of the
material.

These mechanisms have substantial implications for the performance and durability of modern concretes.
Autogenous shrinkage resulting from self-desiccation is a primary cause of early-age cracking, especially
in high-performance, low w/c concretes. Persistent low RH and the resultant microcracking may compro-

The balance and evolution of capillary and disjoining pressures, and thus autogenous RH change and mois-

the use of mineral or chemical admixtures. As hydration progresses and the microstructure evolves, these
variables interact to determine the material’s internal RH profile and response to environmental exposure.
3.6
Moisture transport and sorption isotherms
Jensen and Hansen described graphically the evolution of water phases and internal relative humidity (RH) in
a hydrating cement paste using Powers’ model [21]. This model not only enables the calculation of the phase
volumes but also allows for the visualization of their temporal evolution in relation to the degree of hydration.
The development of phase distribution differs significantly depending on the water-to-cement (w/c) ratio and
the availability of external moisture.
Moisture transport within cementitious materials is driven by gradients in RH and capillary pressure, charac-
terized using sorption isotherms. Sorption isotherms describe the equilibrium relationship between moisture
content and RH at a given temperature, offering insights into pore structure and moisture-binding capacity.
In pastes with high w/c ratios (typically w/c > 0.42), the system contains sufficient capillary water to support
complete hydration. Under sealed conditions, meaning no moisture exchange with the environment, the
hydration proceeds with the consumption of capillary water and the formation of gel solids and gel water
( fig. 3.8 w/c = 0.6). Because the initial water content exceeds the theoretical minimum required for hydration,
the RH within the pores remains essentially at 100%. The chemical shrinkage that results from the lower
volume of the hydration products compared to the original water and cement leads to the formation of isolated
gas-filled pores but does not significantly reduce RH. The sorption isotherm in this case evolves in such a
way that the content of evaporable water decreases with hydration, while the RH stays constant [21].
In contrast, for cement pastes with low w/c ratios, typically below 0.42, and especially relevant in high-
performance concrete or low-clinker systems, capillary water becomes depleted before hydration can reach
completion. Powers’ model shows that once capillary water is exhausted, the remaining water exists as gel
water, which is physically bound and less available for continued hydration. Hydration slows dramatically
as cement particles struggle to access moisture, and eventually halts when the internal gel porosity reaches
a critical minimum threshold. This critical porosity marks the limit beyond which hydration cannot proceed
without additional water, even if unhydrated cement remains ( fig. 3.8).

3.7
Autogenous deformation
Autogenous deformation is defined as the time-dependent volume change of cementitious materials under
sealed, isothermal, and unrestrained conditions. This deformation results primarily from self-desiccation,
which is caused by chemical shrinkage during hydration, see ( fig. 3.10). As water is consumed by hydration
products, internal pores begin to desaturate, leading to a decrease in internal relative humidity (RH). The re-
sulting capillary tension in the pore solution causes the solid skeleton to contract, manifesting as autogenous
shrinkage [33].
Figure 3.10 Schematic development of volume changes, which take place in a sealed cement paste system. Before
set, chemical shrinkage and autogenous deformation may be identical. This volume reduction is referred to as setting
shrinkage and may amount to about 1%. After set, the chemical shrinkage may be 50 times larger than the autogenous
deformation on a volume basis [19].

The menisci formed at the air–water interface within partially filled pores are critical in this process ( fig. 3.11),
and are determined by the combination of chemical shrinkage and pore structure. As hydration progresses
and the volume of pore water decreases, water retreats into smaller pores, increasing the curvature of the
meniscus. According to Kelvin’s equation, this increased curvature corresponds to a lower internal RH and
a higher capillary tension, which imposes compressive stress on the solid phase. The contraction of the
skeleton due to this internal pressure results in measurable shrinkage.
Figure 3.11 Schematic representation of menisci and adsorbed water layer in capillary pores [34]
Expansion versus shrinkage
Although autogenous deformation is often associated with shrinkage, certain hydration processes can initially
induce slight expansion. For instance, the formation of ettringite (AFt) during the early hydration of C3A in
the presence of gypsum leads to crystallization pressure, causing temporary swelling in the microstructure.
This expansion is transient and typically negligible in magnitude compared to the shrinkage that follows.
As hydration progresses, ettringite may convert to monosulfate (AFm), and water is increasingly bound into
hydrates. This, combined with the continued consumption of capillary water and refinement of the pore
structure, results in a decrease in internal RH and a transition from expansion to shrinkage [11, 35].
The balance between expansion and shrinkage depends on the specific cement chemistry, sulfate availability,
curing conditions, and the presence of supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs). In systems with high
reactivity, such as those incorporating silica fume or calcined clays, shrinkage typically dominates due to
accelerated hydration and early RH drop.
3.7.1
Stress development and cracking risk
When autogenous shrinkage occurs in an unrestrained system, the resulting deformation may go unnoticed
at the macro scale. However, in practical applications, concrete is often restrained either externally (e.g., by
reinforcement or adjacent structures) or internally (e.g., by aggregate skeleton). Such restraints inhibit free
shrinkage, causing the development of internal tensile stresses within the cement paste matrix.
Meniscus formation at the pore level initiates tensile stress within the liquid phase. This tension translates
into compressive forces on the solid matrix and, in a restrained system, results in internal tensile stresses
that may exceed the early-age tensile strength of the material. The magnitude of these stresses depends on
the radius of the pores being emptied, as shown in fig. 3.12 [33].

In cement-based materials, cracking due to autogenous deformation arises when the internally generated
tensile strain exceeds the material’s tensile strain capacity, εcr (often denoted εcr). Although εcr is not typically
measured in standard autogenous shrinkage tests, it is generally understood based on early￿age concrete
behavior that ordinary mixes can only sustain tensile strains of approximately 100–200 µε (0.01–0.02%)
before microcracking initiates, while high-performance or low w/b-ratio concretes (e.g., those containing silica
fume) may exhibit even lower capacities of 60–120 µε. In practice, autogenous shrinkage in the first 24–48
hours can readily approach or exceed these values, so in modeling and design, one applies the simple
criterion: if |εautogenous, restrained| > εcr, cracking is expected. [9, 36].
Creep plays a mitigating role in stress development. At early ages, cement paste exhibits significant vis-
coelastic behavior, allowing for stress relaxation under sustained deformation. This time-dependent defor-
mation can delay or reduce cracking risk. The efficiency of stress relaxation by creep depends on binder
composition, temperature, maturity, and the presence of SCMs [36].
The critical threshold for RH drop is approximately 80%, below which hydration becomes diffusion-limited and
tensile stress accumulation accelerates. Maintaining RH above this limit through external or internal curing
is essential for controlling autogenous deformation.

4
Materials and methods
This master’s thesis examines the key physicochemical processes governing early-age behavior in cement
pastes and concretes made with low-clinker binders, with a focus on FUTURECEM®.
Three binder systems: RAPID cement (CEM I 52.5 N), FUTURECEM (CEM II/B-M (Q-LL)52.5), and FU-
TURECEM with 5 % silica fume were evaluated at water-to-binder ratios of 0.32 and 0.37, corresponding to
Unicon’s Uni-Dry and Uni-Dry Extra mixes. In binder pastes, autogenous deformation, internal relative hu-
midity (RH), and chemical shrinkage were recorded continuously for 28 days. Mercury intrusion porosimetry
and the degree of hydration were measured at 28 days.
Concrete molds cast from the same formulations were cured in a controlled chamber (RH 60 %, 20 ◦C).
Embedded RH sensors at three depths (top, mid-height, bottom) tracked humidity over three months, while
autogenous deformation was monitored for 28 days.
By combining results from both paste and concrete scales, this work clarifies how binder composition controls
the processes of self-desiccation, early-age shrinkage, and moisture redistribution and offers guidance for
designing durable, low-carbon self-desiccating concrete systems.

4.4
Sample preparation
All cement paste and concrete mixtures were prepared using standardized procedures to ensure consistency
and reproducibility across all binder systems and water-to-binder ratios. The constituents were weighed on
a precision scale with an accuracy of ±0.01 g. Before mixing, all dry binder materials were manually stirred
to ensure full homogenization.
4.4.1
Cement paste mixing
Cement pastes were mixed using a DISPERMAT CN40 high-shear mixer (see figure 4.1), which allows pre-
cise control over mixing speed and blade position. Although this method does not conform to ASTM C1738,
it was applied uniformly throughout this study to maximize consistency and repeatability across test series.
Before mixing, the mixer arm heights h1 and h2 were defined for safety and mechanical reasons:
Figure 4.1 Dispermat CN40 high-shear mixer.
• h2 is the lowest position, approximately 0.5 cm between the blade and the container bottom.
• h1 is the highest position that still allows full blade rotation without contact.
The mixing protocol followed these steps:
1. Weigh the constituents (binder, water, admixtures).
2. Add 75% of the total mixing water to the container.
3. Secure the removable mixing container into the holder and lock it with the clamp arms.
4. Lower the mixer arm to height h2 (lowest position).
5. Start mixing at 200 rpm.
6. Gradually add the cement (or binder blend) from the side during mixing.
7. Add the pre-diluted admixtures with the remaining 25% of water.
8. Increase mixing speed to 500 rpm and continue for three minutes.
9. Stop the mixer and scrape down the container walls and bottom with a clean plastic scraper.
10. Resume mixing for 1 minute at 700 rpm.

5. Place the container and sample in a microwave oven, along with a separate cup of water (to avoid
overheating).
6. Set the microwave to medium heat and dry for 10 minutes.
7. Weigh the container again.
8. Repeat drying in 5-minute intervals until the weight loss is less than 0.5 g over one drying cycle.

Standard concrete mixing procedure
The concrete mixing process followed a consistent sequence to ensure uniformity across all binder systems.
The steps were:
1. Add coarse aggregates: Place all coarse aggregates (4–8 mm and 8–16 mm) into the mixer.
2. Add binder: Introduce the cement and any supplementary cementitious materials (e.g., silica fume).
3. Add fine aggregate: Add the 0–4 mm sand to the mixer.
4. Initial water addition: Add approximately 75% of the total mixing water to the dry components and
begin mixing immediately.
5. Prepare admixture solution: Pre-mix the remaining 25% of the mixing water with the chemical ad-
mixtures (plasticizer, superplasticizer, and air-entraining agent).
6. Add admixture solution: Gradually pour the admixture solution into the mixer while mixing continues.
7. Final mixing: Continue mixing all components for 3 minutes to ensure full homogenization and uniform
consistency.
This mixing sequence was designed to minimize the premature dispersion of fines and ensure optimal inte-
gration of admixtures.
4.5
Internal relative humidity change measurements
Monitoring internal relative humidity (RH) is a key method for evaluating self-desiccation in cement-based
materials, as the reduction in RH within sealed systems directly reflects water consumption during hydration.
This process leads to autogenous shrinkage and an increased risk of early-age cracking. The phenomenon,
known as autogenous RH change, refers to the internal decrease in relative humidity of a cementitious ma-
terial sealed from external moisture exchange. It is caused by the progressive chemical binding of free water
into hydration products such as calcium-silicate-hydrate (C–S–H), which reduces capillary water content. As
a result, internal RH can decrease substantially, even in the absence of external drying, potentially inducing
self-desiccation stresses and autogenous shrinkage. In this study, RH measurements were conducted in
both cement paste and concrete to compare the internal moisture evolution and water-binding capacity of
different binder systems under isothermal, sealed conditions.
4.5.1
Measurement principle
In binder pastes, RH change was monitored using three Rotronic Hygroscope DT stations, each equipped
with Rotronic HC2A-AW sensors (HYGROMER WA-1) and WP-40S sample holders (fig. 4.5) containing
disposable PS-14 polystyrene sample cups. The sensors function by measuring the electrical impedance
of a polymer electrolyte that varies with ambient RH, providing sensitive readings for moisture conditions in
cementitious materials.

development during hydration.

4.8.1
Experimental methodology
The degree of hydration (DoH) quantifies the progression of cement hydration and is essential for evaluating
binder reactivity, microstructural development, and moisture demand. In this study, the DoH was assessed
at 28 days under sealed curing using a Loss on Ignition (LOI) method, which measures both physically and
chemically bound water in the hydrated paste.
Sample source and preparation

genous deformation test at 28 days, ensuring consistent curing and representative hydration states for each
binder system. The tubes were carefully opened, and the inner portions of the paste (unexposed to air) were
collected to avoid carbonation artifacts.

• The paste was crushed using a hammer and a steel plate.
• Crushed particles were sieved to a size of 1–2 mm to standardize particle dimensions and drying
behavior.
• The sieved material was transferred into pre-weighed glass weighing bottles, sealed immediately, and

5.2.1
Definition of Zero time for autogenous deformation measurements
In the autogenous deformation test, a consistent and accurate reference time, commonly referred to as the
“zero time,” is essential for interpreting and comparing shrinkage evolution across different binder systems.
This reference marks the point from which all measured length changes are recorded relative to the initial
length (L0) on section 4.2.
In this study, zero time was not determined using standard setting time tests, such as the Vicat method (e.g.,
ASTM C191), as those tests were not performed on the investigated binders. Instead, zero time was defined
based on the temperature development of the concrete specimens, closely linked to the RH drop.
Figure 5.11 Temperature development in concrete samples. Markers (X) indicate selected zero times for each system.
Figure 5.11 shows the evolution of temperature in concrete molds cast with FUTURECEM binders, with and
without 5% silica fume, at water-to-binder ratios of 0.32 and 0.37. The zero time for autogenous deformation
was assigned to the inflection point prior to the main temperature rise, typically located between 6 and 9
hours after mixing, depending on the binder system.
This point corresponds to the end of the dormant period and the onset of acceleration in the hydration pro-
cess. At this stage, significant microstructural changes begin to occur, leading to internal water consumption,
capillary pressure development, and the formation of initial autogenous strain. Using this approach ensures a
physically meaningful and consistent reference for all systems, particularly when comparing low-w/b mixtures
with varying kinetics.
The black X markers in Figure 5.11 indicate the assigned zero time for each system, derived from the tem-
perature curves. This method aligns with practice in other dilatometric studies where no Vicat or ultrasonic
reference is available, but thermal development provides a reliable proxy.

5.2.4
Self-desiccation as the governing mechanism
The autogenous deformation measured at both paste and concrete scales mirrors the internal RH evolution,
confirming self-desiccation as the principal driver of early-age shrinkage under sealed conditions.

==PAGE:74==
In paste systems, a strong correlation exists between the rate and magnitude of RH decline and the extent of
autogenous deformation. At w/b = 0.32, the FUTURECEM blend with 5% silica fume showed the most rapid
RH drop—falling below 85% within 7–10 days—and correspondingly the highest shrinkage (over 2000 µε).
Other pastes at 0.32 also reached lower RH levels (∼81–83%), resulting in greater capillary stresses and
larger deformations. Both RAPID and unmodified FUTURECEM pastes followed similar RH trajectories,
yet FUTURECEM consistently exhibited slightly greater shrinkage, indicating that its chemistry and reaction
kinetics amplify stress development beyond RH effects alone.
Silica fume’s influence was w/b-dependent: at 0.32, it heightened chemical shrinkage and capillary pres-
sure, thus increasing deformation, whereas at 0.37 it produced the lowest autogenous strain of all binders—
suggesting that additional mixing water or slower pozzolanic reactions buffered internal stresses.
In concrete, aggregate restraint and reduced paste volume significantly attenuated absolute shrinkage, but
the RH–deformation relationships persisted. FUTURECEM concretes shrank more than RAPID at both w/b
ratios, and silica fume stabilized shrinkage at 0.32 while having a neutral or slight reducing effect at 0.37.
Collectively, these findings might demonstrate that:
• Self-desiccation, through RH reduction and meniscus contraction, governs autogenous deformation
in low w/b binders.
• Capillary stress directly linked to internal RH is the dominant shrinkage mechanism, especially in
paste.
• Binder chemistry, chemical shrinkage potential, and pozzolanic reaction kinetics modulate each sys-
tem’s response to self-desiccation.
• Although aggregate effects mitigate deformation in concrete, the fundamental RH–shrinkage trends
remain observable.

pared to their 0.37 counterparts. Among them, FutureCem_0.32 shows the highest cumulative pore volume,
indicating the least dense matrix—likely a result of slower hydration kinetics and suboptimal particle packing
due to the lower water content. Rapid_0.32, on the other hand, demonstrates a more refined pore structure
with a marked reduction in large capillary pores, reflecting improved hydration and lower total porosity. The
most refined system at this w/b ratio is FutureCem+Silica_0.32, which exhibits the lowest cumulative intru-
sion across all pore size ranges. This indicates a highly dense matrix, attributed to the synergistic effects of
pozzolanic activity and filler action from the silica addition. The resulting microstructure shows significantly
reduced pore connectivity, particularly in the capillary range, suggesting enhanced resistance to fluid ingress
and aggressive environmental agents.
A comparison between FUTURECEM 0.32 and FUTURECEM + Silica 0.37 underscores that binder com-
position exerts a more pronounced influence on pore structure than the w/b ratio alone. Despite the lower
w/b ratio, FutureCem_0.32 displays higher cumulative porosity and a coarser pore distribution, with a sub-
stantial proportion of pores within the capillary range (0.05–10 µm). This suggests incomplete hydration and
a less compact microstructure, likely due to the low water availability and the inherently slower reactivity of
FUTURECEM. In contrast, FUTURECEM + Silica 0.37, despite having a higher w/b ratio, exhibits a signif-
icantly refined pore network dominated by pores smaller than 0.1 µm. The addition of silica enhances the
matrix through both pozzolanic reactions and improved particle packing, thereby reducing overall porosity
and shifting the critical pore size toward the finer range.

increased α, while at 0.37 w/b, it slightly decreased, suggesting pozzolanic reactivity is more active under
water-limited conditions with available calcium hydroxide (CH).
5.4.1
Chemically and physically bound water
At 0.32 w/b, FUTURECEM had the highest total physically bound water, indicating retention of more free
water within the microstructure, likely in less dense C-S-H gel. RAPID showed the highest chemically bound
water per gram of cement (0.147 g/g), followed by FUTURECEM + silica (0.136 g/g) and FUTURECEM
(0.126 g/g), confirming its superior hydration progress.
At w/b = 0.37, chemically bound water increased across all systems: RAPID (0.16 g/g), FUTURECEM (0.15
g/g), and FUTURECEM + silica (0.14 g/g). This again highlights that higher water content promotes hydration
and bound water formation. Physically bound water also increased, showing further gel development.
5.4.2
Volumetric phase composition
Using the degree of hydration results, volumetric phase compositions were calculated. For 0.32 w/b sys-
tems, FUTURECEM showed the highest unhydrated cement volume (0.230 cm3/cm3) and the lowest gel
solid fraction, consistent with its lower hydration degree. RAPID, with a higher DoH, had the highest gel
solid volume and lowest porosity. FUTURECEM + silica showed reduced unhydrated cement but included
unreacted silica, suggesting partial pozzolanic reaction.
At w/b = 0.37, all systems showed increased gel volumes and reduced unhydrated binder. FUTURECEM +
silica had the highest total gel volume, but more unreacted silica remained, reflecting its delayed pozzolanic
activity.
The degree of hydration is a critical indicator of cementitious system maturity and microstructure develop-
ment. It directly influences internal RH, autogenous shrinkage, and pore refinement. Systems with higher
α demonstrated better hydration progress and lower residual porosity. RAPID cement consistently outper-
formed FUTURECEM in hydration rate and chemically bound water, resulting in lower shrinkage and RH
loss.
Silica fume addition appeared to contribute to early densification at 0.32 w/b but had a neutral or even in-
hibitory effect at 0.37 w/b, likely due to delayed pozzolanic activity. The interaction between binder chemistry,
w/b ratio, and pozzolanic content governs the hydration kinetics and internal structure.
These findings align with established hydration theory by Powers and Brownyard (1948), and contemporary
adaptations by Taylor (1997) and Lura et al. (2007), who emphasize that total porosity alone cannot explain
performance. Instead, the size, distribution, and evolution of gel and capillary pores must be considered,
alongside DoH, for a comprehensive understanding of self-desiccation behavior.

Conclusions
This study provides a comprehensive evaluation of hydration kinetics, moisture behavior, pore structure evo-
lution, and autogenous deformation in low-clinker binder systems, with particular focus on FUTURECEM®
and its blends with silica fume. The conclusions below summarize the key scientific insights and their impli-
cations for designing sustainable, high-performance concretes.
Across all tested binder systems, internal relative humidity (RH) development, chemical shrinkage, and auto-
genous deformation consistently confirmed that self-desiccation is the dominant mechanism driving early-age
shrinkage under sealed conditions.
Despite having 29% clinker substitution, FUTURECEM demonstrated hydration kinetics and self-desiccation
behavior comparable to those of a conventional CEM I binder (RAPID). Although FUTURECEM exhibited
a slightly delayed RH decline and higher total porosity according to mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP),
its autogenous shrinkage values remained within the same range as RAPID, validating its use in practical
applications such as UNI-Dry concretes.
The relationship between degree of hydration and RH supports the theoretical premise that self-desiccation
arises from internal water consumption and pore refinement. The Kelvin equation proved effective in pre-
dicting capillary pressure trends across systems, aligning well with measured RH trajectories and offering
valuable insights into internal stress development.
Pore structure refinement, particularly the presence of gel and fine capillary pores below 0.05 µm, was found
to be a critical determinant of RH evolution and capillary pressure development. While FUTURECEM systems
with silica fume developed more refined pore networks, total porosity alone was not predictive of performance.
Instead, pore connectivity and distribution were more closely associated with RH depression and shrinkage
magnitudes.
The degree of hydration (DoH) influenced both RH and deformation but was not solely determinative. At
28 days, RAPID cement achieved higher DoH values (up to 0.67) and more chemically bound water due
to its high clinker content and early reactivity. However, FUTURECEM + silica fume, despite a lower DoH,
showed comparable shrinkage and higher gel volume, indicating that chemical shrinkage and RH loss can
be substantial even at moderate hydration if pore structure is dense and reactive.
Chemical shrinkage measurements supported this trend. FUTURECEM and especially FUTURECEM + silica
fume at w/b = 0.37 exhibited the highest chemical shrinkage values (exceeding 5.5 mL/100 g), correlating
well with RH decline and capillary tension development. These results suggest that internal void formation
from hydration reactions is central to self-desiccation behavior.
At the concrete scale, autogenous shrinkage was reduced by 80–90% compared to paste, reflecting the
mitigating effects of aggregates. However, RH development and shrinkage ranking across binder types
remained consistent with paste results. The inclusion of silica fume was again beneficial at w/b = 0.37,
improving RH stability and limiting deformation.
Although binder systems with silica content and low w/b ratios demonstrated higher autogenous shrinkage
and capillary stress, potentially increasing early-age cracking risk due to limited tensile strain capacity, this risk
may be mitigated over time. Continued hydration of calcined clay and silica fume contributes to ongoing pore
refinement and water consumption beyond 28 days, enhancing tensile resistance and stabilizing RH. Thus,
while such systems may exhibit higher cracking susceptibility in the early days, especially under restraint,
their long-term durability potential remains strong. Effective early-age stress control strategies (e.g., internal
curing, prolonged moist curing, or reduced restraint) are essential to unlock this performance.
Maturity-based time normalization and Kelvin–Laplace thermodynamic analysis proved to be powerful tools
for interpreting RH decline and associated shrinkage behavior. These approaches enabled a consistent
comparison between systems and supported the broader conclusion that internal RH and the microstructural
conditions governing it are the most reliable predictors of autogenous deformation.
Limitations of the study include the use of controlled laboratory conditions and a limited number of replicates.

Future work should incorporate broader sample sets and environmental exposure scenarios. Furthermore,
integrating RH measurements with stress and strength development over time would deepen understanding
of self-desiccation effects in structural applications.
In summary, FUTURECEM-based binders present a promising pathway toward high-performance, low-clinker
concretes. Their ability to balance rapid internal RH decline, controlled deformation, and refined microstruc-
tures while offering substantial CO2 savings positions them as viable alternatives to OPC in moisture-sensitive
or shrinkage-critical applications.

MasterCO2re™ 4010 
(tidligere MasterEase 4010) 
Superplastificeringsmiddel baseret på Intelligent Cluster System-teknologi (ICS) 
 
 
Materialebeskrivelse  
MasterCO2re™ 4010 er et innovativt superplastificerings-
middel baseret på Intelligent Cluster System-teknologi (ICS), 
som er udviklet på Master Builders Solutions forsknings- og 
udviklingslaboratorier. MasterCO2re™ 4010 forener de gode 
flydeegenskaber, forlænget bearbejdelighed, høj tidlig og sen 
styrke med enestående reologi i én enkelt løsning. 
Pumpbarheden forbedres betydeligt, så udstøbning og 
efterbehandling af betonen bliver enklere under alle 
byggeaktiviteter.  
Den avancerede teknologi er det perfekte valg til at opnå stabil 
og robust betonkvalitet ved udfordrende anvendelser af beton 
med lavt klinkerindhold og baner således vejen for en mere 
ansvarlig fremtid for byggebranchen. 
 
Anvendelsesområder 
MasterCO2re™ 4010 er specialudviklet til reoplastisk 
færdigbeton med flydende konsistens og til selvkompakterende 
beton. MasterCO2re™ 4010 tilfører betonen enestående 
reologiske egenskaber og sikrer enestående bearbejdelighed 
over tid under alle vejrforhold. 
MasterCO2re™ 4010 er chloridfri, lever op til EN 934-2 og er 
kompatibel med alle cementtyper, der overholder EN 197-1- 
og EN 197-5-standarderne. 
 
Egenskaber og fordele 
MasterCO2re™ 4010 har følgende hovedfordele:  
 Uovertruffen robusthed: Med nutidens hurtigt skiftende 
sammensætninger af cementtyper og bindemidler er 
vedvarende stabilitet og kvalitet af beton blevet en 
udfordring 
i 
den 
daglige 
drift 
af 
betonfabrikker. 
MasterCO2re™ 4010 modvirker effektivt tendensen til høj 
vandabsorbering og andre uønskede reaktioner, som 
skyldes den varierende kemiske og mineralogiske 
sammensætning af bindemidlerne. 
 Avanceret reologi: Beton med MasterCO2re™ 4010 har 
lavere viskositet og er nemmere og hurtigere at pumpe og 
udstøbe, sammenlignet med de nyeste additiver på 
markedet. Takket være den forbedrede reologi, kan 
vandindholdet yderligere reduceres uden at gå på 
kompromis med pumpbarheden og den nemme 
udstøbning, selv af betonblandinger med reduceret 
klinkerindhold.  
 Opretholdelse af bearbejdeligheden: På grund af Intelligent 
Cluster System-teknologien frigiver MasterCO2re™ 4010 
polymererne over tid og sikrer derved en ensartet  
flydeevne og bearbejdelighed under transport og 
udstøbning af betonen. MasterCO2re™ 4010 er en løsning, 
der kan bruges året rundt, fordi bearbejdeligheden over tid 
fastholdes uden forsinkelse af afbindingen og hærdningen. 
 Enestående styrke: MasterCO2re™ 4010 fremmer 
dannelsen af cementhydrater med en tættere og mere 
homogen struktur, hvilket afspejles i en optimal 
styrkeudvikling.  
 Biocidfri teknologi: MasterCO2re™ 4010 er ikke tilsat 
biocider. 
Intelligent Cluster System-teknologi (ICS)  
MasterCO2re™4010 kan, takket være Intelligent Cluster 
System (ICS), straks frigive en andel af dets frit tilgængelige 
polymerer med henblik på vandreduktion. De finindstillede 
kemiske strukturer af forskellige klynger (clusters) tilpasser 
frigivelsesmekanismen af polymererne efter cementmatricens 
egenskaber og optimerer derved både fastholdelsen af 
bearbejdelighed og hydratiseringen af cementen. På den måde 
kontrollerer MasterCO2re™ 4010 absorptionshastigheden og 
sikrer betonen en konstant høj ydeevne.  
Forenkling af produktionen af beton med 
reduceret klinkerindhold  
Reduktion af klinkerindholdet i beton er en nødvendig 
foranstaltning for at nå de nuværende mål for CO2-reduktion. 
Beton med reduceret klinkerindhold kan overordnet set 
fremstilles på tre måder: 
 Ved at skifte til cement med reduceret CO2-udledning. 
 Ved at holde cementmængden på det lavest mulige niveau, 
samtidig med at den ønskede styrke sikres. 
  Ved at anvende en stor andel af supplerende 
cementmaterialer (SMC) i betonblandingen.

MasterAir 22SB 
Anvendes til luftindblanding, hvor cement indgår som bindemiddel. Opfylder 
DS/EN206-1 og DS2426  
 
 
 
Aug. 2022 erst. juni 2020 
ANVENDELSE 
I den friske beton tilfører MasterAir 22SB mikrobobler, der 
har en smørende effekt, hvorved bearbejdeligheden for-
øges, og separations- og bleedingtendensen nedsættes. 
Den plastificerende effekt kan udnyttes til reduktion af 
vandindhold for i øvrigt uændret konsistens. 
Ligeledes bevirker luftindholdet, at den friske beton bliver 
mere klæbrig og sammenhængende, samt at der i den 
hærdnende beton opnås større tæthed, og dermed forøget 
holdbarhed. For så vidt vil luftindblanding også være gavn-
ligt til betoner, der ikke udsættes for frostpåvirkninger. 
 
EGENSKABER 
MasterAir 22SB kan anvendes til alle typer portlandce-
ment, også i kombination med puzzolaner.  
MasterAir 22SB tilfører betonen luft i form af mikrobobler, 
hvilket har til formål at bibringe den hærdnende beton en 
mikroluftporestruktur, der kan gøre betonen frostbestan-
dig.  
 
Luftindblanding medfører normalt en mindre styrkereduk-
tion, men denne reduktion kan helt eller delvist undgås 
som følge af reduktion i vandbehovet. 
 
BRUGSANVISNING 
MasterAir 22SB leveres brugsfærdig og tilsættes beton-
blandingen som en separat komponent. 
 
Produktet kan tilsættes sammen med sidste del af blande-
vandet, men bør tilsættes før eventuel anden dosering af 
tilsætningsstoffer. 
 
Må ikke tilsættes tørre materialer.

MasterAir 22SB er registreret i databasen for bygge- 
produkter, som kan indgå i Svanemærket byggeri.
