Abstract
The objective of the present thesis is to investigate the eﬀect of applying
two diﬀerent turbulence scaling methods, on the wind turbine fatigue
loads. The methods are examined here are the default HAWC2 tur-
bulence intensity scaling method, and a novel method where the αϵ2/3
Mann parameter is ﬁtted to match the variance of the theoretical Mann
model. The results reveals that the novel method underestimates the
fatigue loads with respect to the turbulence scaling method.
In addition the variation of the loads with respect to the number
of turbulence seeds considered, is examined. Recommended number of
seeds for each wind turbine examined are provided, and furthermore
recommended Mann box size are also given per respective turbine.
Finally, the reversal of the longitudinal turbulence direction and its
eﬀect on the fatigue loads is also investigated; where a reduction of loads
has been observed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
IEC Standard and Turbulence Modeling
Aeroelastic simulations are extensively used in the development of modern wind
turbines, where design loads are obtained based on a speciﬁed turbulent wind ﬂow
input. Thus, an adequate description of the structure of atmospheric turbulence is
important in order to accurately calculate the wind turbine dynamic loads. A num-
ber of simpliﬁed engineering models exist, which do not solve the full ﬂuid equations
of motion; but instead solve either simpliﬁed versions of the Navier–Stokes equa-
tion or involve empirical representation consistent with observations(Kelly, 2018) .
Such models facilitate generation of synthetic turbulence ﬁeld time series for input
to wind turbine loads calculations.
The classical concepts used for describing such turbulent ﬁelds, are the spectral
based models which include the velocity spectra, co-spectra and the cross-spectral
properties coherence and phase (Townsend, 1980; Kaimal and Finnigan, 1994). Due
to their eﬃciency and widespread industrial use, spectral models are used for the
estimation of dynamic loads on turbines through simulation of the wind ﬁeld toward
the rotor.
Commonly used models in the wind-energy industry are the models
developed by Kaimal (Kaimal et al., 1972), the ‘Sandia method’ by Veers (Veers,
1988) and the spectral-tensor model of Mann (Mann, 1994, 1998).
With that respect, the standard IEC 61400-1 (IEC 61400-1, 2019, Appendix C)
prescribes two spectral models for deﬁning the three-dimensional wind turbulence
spectra; the Kaimal-based method or the spectral turbulence model of Mann. The
ﬁrst is an adaptation of the classic Kaimal component spectra (Kaimal et al., 1972),
plus an empirical coherence model extended from Davenport (Davenport, 1962);
which is essentially the ‘Sandia method’ of Veers. However, according to the study
of Saranyasoontorn et al. (2004), the empirical coherence formulation of this model
tends to have problems at low frequencies and large separations.
The IEC 61400-1 prescribes the Mann spectral-tensor model parameters based
largely on the analysis of Mann (1994). The Mann turbulence model is charac-
terized by parameters associated with the turbulence length scale of the spectral
velocity tensor, L, an energy dissipation factor, αϵ2/3 and the turbulence anisotropy
parameter Γ. These input parameters have recommended values prescribed by the

standard the IEC 61400-1, and are chosen such that the resulting spectra in longi-

possible match to the corresponding spectra of the Kaimal model.
1.2
Mann Spectral Model
1.2.1
Description of Mann model
In the current report, the atmospheric turbulence is quantiﬁed using the Mann
spectral model described in Mann (1994, 1998). The Mann model is based on a
three-dimensional spectrum tensor representation of the turbulence, and one three-
dimensional Fast Fourier Transform, FFT, is then used to generate all three com-
ponents of turbulence simultaneously. The equation for the evolution of the Fourier
modes is deduced from the linearized Navier–Stokes equations via Rapid Distortion
Theory, RDT.
The initial conditions of the Mann model is isotropic turbulence in neutral atmo-
spheric conditions, and the energy spectrum is given based on the von Kármán
spectrum (von Kármán, 1948) presented in Eq. (1.2.1).
From the rapid distor-
tion of isotropic turbulence by a uniform mean vertical velocity shear, the three-
dimensional spectrum tensor is derived. Therefore, the method does not treat the
three turbulence components as independent, but in particular incorporates the cor-
relation between the longitudinal and vertical components resulting from Reynolds
stresses. The spectral density for any three-dimensional wavenumber vector is ﬁ-
nally derived, and all three components of turbulence are then generated simulta-
neously by summing a set of such wavenumber vectors, each with the appropriate
amplitude and a random phase. A complete overview of the underlying equations
is given in Mann (1994, 1998) and in the IEC standard (IEC 61400-1, 2019, Annex
C.3).

1.2.2
Literature Review of Mann Model Applications
From the early application of the Mann model (Mann, 1994, 1998), it was possible
to obtain site-speciﬁc model parameters via 3-D sonic anemometer measurements.
However, even before the IEC 61400-1 [Ed.3] (IEC 61400-1, 2005), it was observed
by Mann (2000), that the parameters prescribed by the standard can vary from
those found by ﬁtting spectra.
The IEC 61400-1 (IEC 61400-1, 2019, §6.3.1) prescribes spectral-tensor model pa-
rameters by setting Γ = 3.9, while setting the other two parameters based on the
height and turbine class. The prescribed length scale L increases linearly with height
up to 60m, but above that height remains constant at 42m. However, turbulence
spectra at a given site are not a constant and they vary with respect to the wind
condition characteristics such as atmospheric stability or turbulence intensity (Peña
et al., 2010; Sathe et al., 2012; Chougule et al., 2014b). More recently, Kelly (2018)
also found that for a number of sites and ﬂow regimes, L can often diverge from the
IEC 61400-1 prescribed values. The length scale L can be smaller often due to sta-
bly stratiﬁed conditions, or much larger due to, e.g., unstable conditions. Therefore,
measurements have shown that spectral characteristics corresponding to the IEC
61400-1 set of parameters rarely occur in practice. This is especially the case for
the anisotropy parameter Γ, for which the IEC 61400-1 standard recommendation
is Γ = 3.9.
Contrary to the IEC 61400-1 prescribed value of 3.9, Γ typically varies between 2 and
4, and measurements under neutral and slightly unstable conditions in Chougule
et al. (2014b) showing that a more realistic value is Γ ≈3.16. This is consistent with
the stability distributions found earlier in Kelly and Gryning (2010) and the neutral
asymptotic value of 3.1 more recently inferred by Kelly (2018). Therefore, there
is signiﬁcant correlation between the turbulence spectral parameters describing the
wind ﬁeld and atmospheric stability.

1.3. TURBULENCE INTENSITY
In general, since the Mann parameters dictate the inﬂow conditions, they can po-
tentially have a signiﬁcant impact on the turbine load response in aeroelastic sim-
ulations. The inﬂuence of the atmospheric stability in wind turbine loads has been
demonstrated by Sathe et al. (2012), where a comparison of simulation calculated
loads obtained using site-speciﬁc and IEC wind conditions, found that IEC loads
are up to 96% larger than those obtained using site-speciﬁc wind conditions.
More recent research, Dimitrov et al. (2015) suggested that turbulence is one of the
most signiﬁcant factors, where the accumulated fatigue damage increases with the
turbulence. The eﬀect of varying the spectral parameters of the Mann turbulence
model on the fatigue and extreme loads has been further demonstrated by Dimitrov
et al. (2017), where it was concluded that the use of non optimal Mann model
parameters, can inﬂuence the accuracy of the simulated wind turbine loads. With
respect to that, Pedersen et al. (2019), investigated the use of more accurate inﬂow
descriptions and concluded that it improves the accuracy of the simulated wind-
turbine fatigue loads.

1.3.2
Turbulence Intensity Scaling in HAWC2
When turbulence is generated with the Mann box, it is required to ensure that the
turbulence intensity matches the prescribed turbulence intensity at the hub height.
This can be achieved with two ways. The more accurate practice, is to change the
Mann parameters to produce a box with the correct turbulence intensity, since the
spectral tensor components of the Mann model are directly proportional to αϵ2/3.
As a result, changing this parameter inﬂuences only the variance, but not the shape
of the spectrum. Using diﬀerent values of αϵ2/3 is thus be equivalent to scaling the
variance. The other option is to scale the turbulence ﬁeld after it’s been simulated,
in order to match the prescribe turbulence intensity.
The default option in HAWC2 is scaling of the turbulence box according to speci-
ﬁed inputs, after it’s been generated (Larsen and Hansen, 2019) . This is achieved
by using the command dont_scale 0 in the sub-command block mann of the main
command block wind. The scaling is done automatically in HAWC2, by the turbu-
lence scaling factor SF, deﬁned by in Eq. (1.3.6); which is used to multiply every
values in the turbulence ﬁeld in all directions. This turbulence scaling factor is

1.3.3
Eﬀect on Wind Turbine Loads
In wind turbine design, wind turbine load simulation programs often use ﬁxed pa-
rameters of the Mann model as speciﬁed in the IEC 61400-1 and scale the resulting
turbulence ﬁeld to a target turbulence intensity. The turbulence scaling is usually
applied both when simulating normal turbulence (the NTM model in the IEC stan-
dard), or extreme turbulence (the ETM model) and as a result both the fatigue
and the ultimate design loads can be aﬀected. The fatigue and extreme loads that
the turbine experiences are predominantly caused by atmospheric conditions such
as wind shear, turbulence and gusts. Sathe et al. (2012) assessed the dependence
of wind turbine fatigue loads to atmospheric stability and the coupling of shear
and turbulence has been investigated by Dimitrov et al. (2015) and Holtslag et al.
(2016). Dimitrov et al. (2017) suggests that turbulence is the most signiﬁcant fac-
tors aﬀecting wind turbine loads, since the accumulated fatigue damage increases
with the turbulence. A change in the turbulence intensity would result in change
of the spectra of the bending moment of the various wind turbine components. In-
creasing turbulence levels will shift the spectra upward and vice versa. Therefore
changes in the spectra contribute to the fatigue of individual turbine components,
with the sensitivity of each respective component, diﬀering uniquely.
As discussed in Subsection 1.3.2, it is common practice to scale the turbulence af-
ter generation, such that the turbulence intensity in the center of the turbulence
ﬁeld matches the turbulence intensity prescribed. Although this approach is con-
venient as it ensures agreement between the prescribed target and simulated hub
height turbulence intensity, it may, however, result in energy from scales that are
not represented in the turbulence model being distributed on other frequencies Ped-
ersen et al. (2019). Furthermore, the approach is inappropriate if the center of the
turbulence ﬁeld is not representative for the whole ﬁeld.
Pedersen et al. (2019) concluded that the commonly used turbulence scaling ap-
proach, introduced a considerable variation in the simulated loads; since scaling
of the turbulence intensity, implies that boxes with much low frequency energy
are in the inertial sub-range regime such that loads are underestimated and vice
versa. Therefore, a new method is proposed here as recommended by Pedersen
et al. (2019), where the scaling of the theoretical Mann spectra variance is done by
ﬁtting the αϵ2/3 parameter in order to match the target variance.

Mann Box Spectra.
2.3.1
Introduction.
A study is presented in the following sections where the eﬀect of the Mann pa-
rameters on the generated Mann box spectra and its respective transfer function
is investigated. The Mann Box transfer function is deﬁned as the ratio of output
spectral signal from the Mann box over the input signal of the respective Theo-
retical spectrum. The output signal spectrum is computed from the cross section
representing the rotor plane directions in the lateral and vertical dimensions of
the box; since by assuming the Taylor frozen turbulence hypothesis, each of these
planes represents a time step. This is done in order to average out noise, and it is
regarded as a more computational eﬃcient method compared to averaging over a
large number of seeds; where a lot of turbulence boxes are needed to be generated.

frequency limit can be obtained.
In both cases, the noise at the lower frequency range can be reduced by considering
more realizations, i.e. turbulence seeds. By carefully combining the two strategies
together, and taking into consideration the computer memory limitations that the
Mann box imposes, a frequency range with desirable upper and lower limits can be
generated. This subject is explored in more detail in Subsection 2.3.7.
2.3.6
Eﬀect of High Frequency Compensation.
In the current section the eﬀect of high frequency compensation, hfc, is investigated.
The hfc parameter can be regarded as a logical switch and according to Larsen and
Hansen (2019). For a high frequency compensation of hfc = 0, the point velocity
represents the average velocity within a grid cell volume. On the other hand, the

2.3.7
Wind Aliazing and Extending the Frequency Range.
For any sampling interval ∆, there is a special frequency fc, called the Nyquist
critical frequency, given by fc = 1/∆. When considering the eﬀect of sampling a
continuous function that is not bandwidth limited to less than the Nyquist critical
frequency, it turns out that all of the power spectral density that lies outside of
the frequency range −fc < f < fc is spuriously moved into that range.
This
phenomenon is called aliasing. Any frequency component outside of the frequency
range (−fc, fc) is aliased (falsely translated) into that range by the very act of

HAWC2 Wind Sensor Spectra.
2.4.1
Introduction.
There is some misinterpretation regarding the correct location of the wind sensor
in the htc ﬁle, and users can easily locate the sensor in a wrong position. Therefore
the correct wind input is not considered when simulating with HAWC2, which can
lead to miscalculation of the wind turbine loads. In this section, the wind sensor
location is investigated in order to obtain a wind sensor transfer function of unity.
The wind sensor transfer function is deﬁned as the ratio of output signal from
the wind sensor spectrum component over the input signal of the respective Mann
box spectrum. The input signal spectrum is computed from a single Mann box
grid point, corresponding to the location deﬁned in the htc output block with the
command wind free_wind.

Prior to computing the transfer function, the load spectra corresponding to the
seeds considered are binned and averaged by ensuring that they lay withing the
same frequency range. Since the loads are typically with units within the order of
kN up to MN and the wind speed in ms−1, the load spectra are further normalized
by scaling them with maximum value attained. It should be pointed out, that the
normalized spectra presented here are ﬁrst plotted with the theoretical Mann uu
spectrum for comparison purposes (with the theoretical Mann spectrum also been
normalized by the peak value), and then the respective transfer function is plotted
along side.
Consequently, with the normalization carried out, the load sensor transfer function
is evaluated with the respective Mann box uu-spectrum in order to be able to
identify any meaningful peaks. The peaks in the load transfer function are then
related to the natural frequencies identiﬁed by using HAWCStab2 (Hansen et al.,
2018).
Following are the steps carried out in order to compute the load transfer functions:
1. Compute and bin spectra per seed
2. Append wavenumbers and spectra per seed in an array
3. Combine all seed wavenumbers, sort and exclude duplicate values
4. Interpolate to obtain spectra based on combined seeds wavenumber range
5. Average spectra based on number of seeds
6. Multiply averaged spectra with respective wavenumber
7. Normalize scaled spectra by max value
8. Compute transfer function based on normalized spectra

Discussion
Figs. 37 to 40 illustrate a comparison of the identiﬁed transfer function peak
wavenumber and the respective excitation, for the three wind turbines investigated.
Direct comparison between the aeroelastic behavior between the wind turbines ex-
amined here, is out of the scope of this thesis; since it requires more investigation
in the structural properties of each turbine. However, it should be noted that, con-
trary to the DTU 10MW RTW, which was aerodynamically optimized followed by
the structural design, the IEA reference wind turbines were developed by using an
integrated design tool. This was done in order to ensure a balanced aerostructural
design so as to minimize loads (Bortolotti et al., 2019).
In overall, a general trend of common mode peaks revealed, shifting to lower
wavenumbers as the turbine size increased; and as expected, due to the fact the
larger wind turbines operate at lower rotational speed, the P harmonics lay at
lower wavenumbers as the rotor size is increased. For all wind turbines examined,
the tower-base fore-aft, yaw bearing pitch and roll and shaft torsion spectra had all
peaks located at the lower wavenumber, Since these loads have low value material-
dependent Wöhler exponent, typically 3 to 4 for metal, and are more sensitive to
these low wavenumber; the fatigue loads will be increased. On the other hand the
blade loads are more sensitive to the higher wave numbers, but have a higher Wöh-
ler exponent, typically 10 to 12 for composites;hence the fatigue damage will be
lower.

Chapter 4
Turbulence Scaling
4.1
Introduction.
A novel method of turbulence scaling is investigated in this chapter where the αϵ2/3
Mann parameter is ﬁtted, in order for the area under the Mann model spectrum
(equivalent to the variance) to correspond to the turbulence intensity of the gener-
ated time series. Evaluation of the the new αϵ2/3 ﬁt method is done by comparing
the damage equivalent loads (referred to as DELs) with the traditional turbulence
intensity scaling method of HAWC2.
A DEL represents the load amplitude that would have caused the same amount
of damage for a reference number of cycles Veldkamp (2006). To determine the
DEL from a load response, the response must be ﬁrst converted to cycles and load
amplitudes using the rainﬂow counting algorithm Burton et al. (2021), after which
the DEL for that time series can be calculated by Eq. (4.1.1).

In the expression of Eq. (4.1.1), nb is the number of bins used for the cycle counting,
ni and Ri are the number of cycles and load amplitude for bin i, respectively, m
is the material-dependent Wöhler exponent (slope of the S–N curve), and Nref is
the predeﬁned number of reference cycles. The value of Nref is chosen such that
the equivalent loads correspond to a vibration with frequency of 1 Hz, i.e., for a
single 10 minutes simulation Nref = 600. The assumed Wöhler exponents were 4 for
metal components (shaft, tower) and 10 for composite components (blades). The
DELs for the diﬀerent number of seeds considered here were combined to a single
representative value by taking the mean over all seeds for each load case examined.
For all cases investigated here the recommended IEC Mann parameters, length scale
L = 33.6 m and anisotropy parameter Γ = 3.9, are chosen. Further more the high
frequency compensation is selected in order to assess the eﬀect of grid resolution
on the lowpass ﬁltering of the high frequency compensation, when interpolating the
Mann box variance at hub center.

4.2. TURBULENCE SCALING AND WIND SPECTRA.
4.2
Turbulence Scaling and Wind Spectra.
4.2.1
Turbulence Intensity Scaling in HAWC2.
The concept of turbulence scaling in HAWC2 has been introduced in Subsec-
tion 1.3.2, and a simple example is presented here with considering the IEA 3.4MW
IEC Class 3A wind turbine, with a prescribed TI = 16%, operating at 12ms−1.
This is done in order to illustrate the diﬀerence between scaling and not scaling
of the turbulence on the wind time series and respective spectra. The command
dont_scale 1 is used in the block Mann of the htc ﬁle, in order to ensure that
the speciﬁed turbulence intensity is bypassed and the generated raw turbulence
ﬁeld is used without any scaling (Larsen and Hansen, 2019). With the command
dont_scale 0, the turbulence ﬁeld in the center of the Mann box is read, and the
actual variance if calculated. Then a scaling factor is applied to the u, v and w
wind ﬁeld directions, based on Eq. (1.3.6); in order to compensate for the diﬀerence
in variance level with respect to the target variance.
Fig. 41 presents a comparison between the respective uu-spectra when scaling and
not scaling is applied.
With respect to the raw turbulence spectra with no TI

However, implementation of the TI scaling method can be problematic in terms of
load estimation, since the accuracy is dependent on the Mann box YZ grid resolution
chosen. The TI scale method ﬁrst computes the wind ﬁeld on the center of the Mann
box by interpolating the four surrounding Mann box grid points, and afterwards
calculates the variance. Therefore, depending on the Mann box grid resolution, the
method derives a scaling factor which is then applied to the wind ﬁeld components.
The implication with respect to load calculations, is that Mann boxes with very low
frequency energy are scaled down such that the loads are underestimated and vica
versa.
4.2.2

The αϵ2/3 ﬁtting method determines an αϵ2/3 value such that the area under the
theoretical Mann uu-spectrum, which is equivalent to the variance, corresponds to
the IEC prescribed turbulence intensity. In order to do so, an upper and lower
cut-oﬀfrequencies are imposed.

determined by a convergence study of the relative variance between the computed
and prescribed variance, as shown in Fig. 43; during the initial development of the

TI Scale Method and Long Simulation Times.
In Subsection 4.3.1, it was ﬁrstly mentioned that the HAWC2 default TI scaling

be attributed to the fact that longer generated wind time series will have larger
variance, hence the turbulence scaling factor is adjusted accordingly; resulting to
an underestimation of the loads. In order to conﬁrm this, a single seed is selected
and the variance at hub height σ2

scaled down, and any low frequency energy is scaled down further more, when
the applying the scaling factor to the wind ﬁeld. This consequently results in the
underestimation of the computed loads.
It seems that the reason for the increased variance in longer simulation times, is
related to the selected spectrum length scale and simulation time, according to a
unpublished manuscript by Mann (2014).
The author states that, even though
the variance is forced to agree with the target variance, the power spectral density
is on average higher than the target spectrum; and the deviation from the target
spectrum is a function of the length of the simulation divided by the length scale of
the spectrum. Therefore, it is evident that the TI scaling method can be problematic
because it can potentially increase the uncertainty on the calculated loads, if a
relation between length scale and simulation time is not established. On the other
hand, the αϵ2/3 ﬁt method is insensitive to this behavior, and hence, it can be
considered as a more reliable method for computing the loads.

Reversed Turbulence Direction
The Mann model includes uniform wind shear, where the uniform, linear wind shear
results in the deformation of eddies; ﬁrst arriving at a higher vertical location za
and then at lower vertical location zb, as shown in Fig. 61 which has been observed
in nature and LES (Chougule et al., 2014a).
Figure 61: Schematic of eddy stretching due to shear (Redrawn from Chougule et al.
(2014a); Larsen and Hansen (2019)).
In a recent publication by Wise and Bachynski (2020) studying the wake meander-
ing eﬀects on ﬂoating wind turbines, it was noted by the authors that the eddies
generated by using the IEC turbulence simulator1 are in the opposite direction com-
pared to LES generated in Jonkman et al. (2018). According to Wise and Bachynski
(2020), the authors modiﬁed the generated Mann wind ﬁeld in order to be read in
the opposite direction, so as to correct this apparent error. Therefore, this mo-
tivated the investigation of reversing the longitudinal direction of the Mann wind

loads, while the yaw-bearing loads present an approximate reduction of 2% , when
the direction of the u velocity component is reversed. On the other hand the relative
diﬀerences with respect to the shaft torsion and blade root bending moments are
less than 1%. Since the rotor is tilted, an asymmetry is created between the upwind
and the downwind part of the rotor. With the default normal direction, the eddy
ﬁrst arrives at the lower part of the rotor and the blade sweeping downwind has
a higher angle of attack than when it is sweeping upwind. Therefore the blade
lift downwind will be greater that the upwind blade lift, and a similar diﬀerential
applies to the forces normal to the rotor plane. Therefore there will be extra net
tilting and yaw moments in the horizontal and vertical diametrical axis respectively,
which which explains the diﬀerences observed.
From the aforementioned, it seems that HAWC2 has been reading the wind ﬁeld
from the wrong direction and has been overestimating the loads. Although the dif-
ference for all loads examined for the small rotor size IEA 3.4MW can be regarded
as moderate to negligable, it is expected that these diﬀerences will be more signiﬁ-
cant as the rotor size increases. This has not been conﬁrmed in the current thesis
and is left for future research.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The current report presented the comparison between the default turbulence in-
tensity scaling method and the novel αϵ2/3 ﬁtting method. The variations of the
fatigue loads has been accesses with respect to the two diﬀerent methods, taking
into consideration two diﬀerent Mann box sizes. The results indicate that the αϵ2/3
ﬁtting method estimated reduced loads in comparison to the default turbulence
scaling method for the three wind turbines examined were lower.
A very important ﬁnding of this thesis was that the default turbulence intensity
scaling method severely underestimates the loads when applied to long simulation
times. This is related to the length scale and simulation time chosen according to
Mann (2014), and requires further investigation; since the method can be problem-
atic as it can potentially increase the uncertainty on the estimated loads. On the
other hand, the αϵ2/3 ﬁt method is insensitive to this behavior, and hence, it can
be considered as a more reliable method for computing the loads.
The current study, further demonstrated, that performing simulations with more
seeds, provides a feasible option for reducing the uncertainty in the estimated fa-
tigue loads. Based on converged value of the maximum number of seeds considered,
recommended number of seeds have been derived per scaling method and wind tur-
bine. It was further observed that as the grid resolution was increased, the number
of seeds required to reach the speciﬁed tolerance limit of the assumed converged
value, was decreasing.
Furthermore, recommendations have been provided with
respect to a suitable Mann box size per respective wind turbine, for estimating fa-
tigue loads. Unfortunately, due to a technical error it was not possible to conduct
simulations with large Mann boxes, and this is left for future research.
Finally another important ﬁnding is derived. The eﬀect on the fatigue loads from
the reversal of the longitudinal direction of the generated Mann box wind ﬁeld,
has been investigated; and a moderate reduction in the tower bending and yaw
bearing loads was observed for the smaller size wind turbine. In addition, a slight
reduction in the blade root moment was also evident, and these load diﬀerences are
expected to be more severe as the rotor size increases. This indicates that HAWC2
is reading the wind ﬁeld in the wrong direction, overestimating the loads. This is a
very important ﬁnding and requires further investigation.
