Summary
Recycling and sustainability in general, are among the challenges that nowa-
days world is facing. The incredible amount of waste tyres produced every
year is a huge environmental issue: for this reason, new and different ways to
reuse tyre rubber have to be studied. This master project investigated the fea-
sibility of the use of tyre rubber as material for injection molding applications.
In order to use tyre rubber for injection molding, this material was blend with
polypropylene to create a thermoplastic elastomer (TPE). The main focus of
this thesis was the investigation of the effect that different particle sizes of
rubber powder and different ratios between ground tyre rubber (GTR) and
PP have on the final properties of the material. In particular, the mechanical
properties were investigated. When higher content of PP and smaller particle
size of rubber are used, the blend showed an improvement in strength, stiffness
and hardness. Higher elongation was achieved by using smaller rubber par-
ticles and an higher content of GTR. Moreover, the powder distribution was
also examined, showing an uneven dispersion of the rubber powder throughout
the sample. Finally, the replication rate of three blends made with different
rubber powders was studied. The microstructures did not show a good repli-
cation when the height of the squares was smaller than 40µm. This behaviour
is particularly enhanced at the end of the sample, where the content of the
rubber powder is higher.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
One of the biggest challenges of our century is the resources sustainability.
The first to raise awareness of this issue has been the United Nation Environ-
mental Program UNEP, through a study on the effect of the unsustainable
use of resources which are commons in today global economies.
Recycling and greener waste management are among the standalone goals
of both Paris Agreement and the UN 2030 Agenda for sustainable develop-
ment. The most known definition of sustainable development is the one coined
in 1987 by the Burdtland Commission:”Sustainable development is develop-
ment that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability
of future generations to meet their own needs”. It is from this perspective
that this master thesis has been developed. This project wants to give a small
contribution on the demanding task to move towards a zero waste scenario
for a brighter future.
1.1
Problem background
Every year 1,7 billions new tyres are produced and 1 billion of old tyres are
thrown away [8]. Only the European Union has a total waste of tyres which
amounts to 3,7 millions tonnes per year [6]. It is clear that this enormous
amount of ELT (End of Life Tyres) is an important environmental issue and
it is crucial to rethink the waste management policy for this type of material.
Since 1999, thanks to the Directive 99/31/CE, in the European Union the
landfill disposal of spent tyres is forbidden; this is not true for most of other
countries around the world. It is an impelling necessity to find different and
more sustainable ways to treat this huge amount of tyre waste.
Nowadays the 30% of tyres waste is still dumped in landfill, around 20%

is recycled and the rest is burned [8], indeed the rubber of tyres is frequently
used as fuel when high levels of energy are needed. It is clear how a lot of
work still needs to be done in order to achieve a zero waste scenario.
1.2
Problem formulation
Recently a lot of projects and researches have been done on the study and
development of new and different way to recycle tyres. Lately, granulate and
powders of ELT have been used in a wide range of applications: from the civil
engineering industry to the sports and leisure sector.
The main goal of this master project is to investigate the feasibility of the
use of recycled tyre rubber as an injection molding material, by blending ELT
granulate and powder with Polypropylene, creating, in this way, a Thermo-
plastic Elastomer. Special focus is posed on how the ratio between ELT and
PP, and the particle size of the ELT powder used, influence the properties
of the materials and the replication quality of micro features using injection
moulding.
1.3
Project plan
In figure 1.1 is illustrated the research plan followed during the project. The
main idea, as mentioned above, is to create a TPE by blending ground tyre
rubber (GTR) with PP and to study the properties of this material. The two
materials (GTR and PP) were first manually mixed, then extruded and injec-
tion molded. Several blends were made in order to evaluate how the properties
are changed when the dimension of the GTR powder and the ratio between
GTR and PP are modified. The investigation of the material properties fo-
cused on three main aspects: mechanical properties, powder distribution and
replication rate.

2.1
The tyre: its structure and composition
Tyre is an advanced engineering material that has to handle different condi-
tions and environments: cold, ice and snow during winter, hot during summer,
high abrasive conditions (off road or poorly maintained road), and to assure
perfect performance for a long life.
This is the reason why tyre structure
is particularly complex. Less than half of the composition of this composite
material is made of pure rubber[7], while the remaining part contains fabric,
carbon black and fillers, metals, and chemical and minerals. Not all the tyres
have the same composition, each manufacturer has its own secrete recipe and
also the percentage of the compounds used changes from one type of tyre to
another: racing cars tyres are different from normal cars tyres which differ
from the tyres of the trucks.

2 Theoretical Background
A general tyre can contain a large number of different types of rubber. A
tyre manufactured by the company Goodyear contains 30 kinds of synthetic
rubber and 8 different kinds of natural rubber [7]. While in a car tyre the
content of synthetic rubber is greater than the one of natural rubber, for the
tyres of truck it is the opposite [23]. Most of the rubber is present inside the
top tread, the part of the tyre in contact with the road that provides cush-
ioning and grips and which has to have a high wear resistance[21]. A great
amount of rubber can also be found in the Sidewall.
More than 20% of a tyre is made of carbon black widely used as reinforcing
filler, this material can also be partially replaced by clay and silica[7].
The percentage of metals change significantly in accordance with the final
use of the tyre: while car tyres are made up of metals for a 16,5% in weight,
truck ones contain 25% of metals [7]. Metal is present in the steel belt which
is placed all around the tyre to give rigidity and strength. Moreover tyre con-
tains a steel wire called beads which provides the necessary strength for the
assembly of the tyre with the wheel[21]. It is possible to completely remove
the steel from tyres and use it, thanks to its quality, as raw material for the
production of new steel.

2.2
Rubber
Even if western countries have started using rubber only recently, this material
was known and used for centuries by center and south American indigenous
populations.
Rubber is en elastomer material that can be deformed when
loaded, and it is able to return to its original shape once the external force is
removed. This ability of rubber is closely related to the concept of entropy.
Indeed, when the material is loaded, rubbers macro molecules rearrange in a
more ordered structure, decreasing the entropy. Entropy, in nature, always
increases, so, once the load is removed, in order to raise the entropy and thus
reduce Gibbs free energy, rubbers chains return to their original disorder state.
Nowadays it is possible to find rubber as natural rubber or synthetic rubber,
also just called elastomer; while synthetic rubbers are petrochemicals prod-
ucts, natural rubber is found in nature [16, 9].
Natural rubber is extracted from a plant called Hevea Brazilensis, com-
monly known as rubber tree. By making incision in the tree it is possible to
extract a white liquid called Latex, which is a colloidal of rubber particles.
In order to obtain the natural rubber from the suspension, it is necessary to
evaporate the water present in the colloidal. From a chemical point of view,
natural rubber is a polymer based on the cis-isoprene monomer; rubber is
indeed composed by long molecules of poly-cis-1,4-isoprene as shown in figure
2.3.
Figure 2.2: Structure of poly-cis-1,4-isoprene [16].
Natural rubber is formed by ordered long macro molecules chains without
physical bonds between one another. This characteristic is the reason why
natural rubber macro molecules are quite movable and, as consequence, the
material spontaneously coagulates and results unworkable [16, 13]. At first,
==PAGE:29==
2.2 Rubber
9
in the end of the eighteenth century, a rubber emulsion was created in order
to find a way to process rubber. This emulsion was used to create waterproof
products, but unfortunately the material was quite sticky and soft when the
temperature raises, like in summer, and hard and brittle during winter [13].
Another process used to enhance the workability of rubber is mastication. This
treatment consist in the mechanical degradation of the rubber chains by break-
ing the chemical bond of the macro molecules. As a result, rubber decreases
in molecular weight and in viscosity, achieving a more plastic behaviour, and
thus becoming more treatable [16].
Only between the end of the nineteenth and the beginning of the twentieth
century, the use of rubber started to spread. The increasing use of this mate-
rial can be explained by the great properties obtained due to a revolutionary
treatment process discovered, independently, by Thomas Hancock and Charles
Goodyear: the vulcanization. Thanks to this treatment, rubber can be easily
processed, it maintains its properties in different weather conditions and re-
sists to stresses and degradation, all characteristics needed for tyres [16, 9, 13].
2.2.1
Vulcanization
As explained earlier, rubber has the ability to deform when loaded and return
to its original state once the stress is removed; this is possible thanks to the
presence of forces able to withstand the deformation: these forces are strictly
related to the cross linking of the rubber macro molecules. Vulcanization is,
indeed, a cross linking process that allows the formation of chemical and phys-
ical bonding between the rubber macro molecules. In this way the rubber
chains bond together forming a big macro molecule [16, 9, 13, 1].
The most used vulcanization process is the sulfur vulcanization, which
takes advantage of the ability of sulfur to react with the functional group of
the poly-cis-1,4-isoprene creating in this way cross links between rubber macro
molecules [13].

In order to have an effective vulcanization process, sulfur is not enough
but it is also necessary to add accelerators and activators. By adding just
sulfur, the vulcanization process is really slow and it is only possible to create
a side cyclic structure but not a real cross-linking of the rubber. In particular,
while the accelerators are responsible to enhance the speed of the reaction, the
activators, like ZnO, have a crucial role in the improving of the sulfur bond
density [13, 1].
Even thought vulcanization has been used for more than a century to
crosslink natural and synthetic rubber, its chemical process is not fully un-
derstood yet. It is known that in presence of accelerators and activators the
sulfur ring is broken and can successively react with the functional group of
the rubber chains creating bonding. This is possible due to two mechanisms
that can also occurred simultaneously: the ionic and radical polymerization
[16, 9, 13, 1].

2.3
ELT - End of Life Tyre Rubber
With the ban of the European Union on the landfill disposal of tyre, a huge
amount of material had to be reused. This material cannot be considered
a general waste since, as it was explained before, tyre is composed by high
quality materials like steel, textile and rubber that are resources of energy and
renewable materials. Recently, new researches and studies have been done on
the development of new and different ways to dispose end of life tyre rubbers;
the most promising results have been achieved with the grinding of the ELT
[4].
2.3.1
The grinding process
The process to create granulates and powders from spent tyre is quite complex
due to the presence of numerous different materials inside the waste product.
As already mentioned, the main three constituents of a tyre are: rubber (nat-
ural and synthetic), metals and fabric.
From a spent tire it is possible to
obtain overall 75% of rubber, 15% of metals and the remaining 10% is made
out of fiber. In order to recycle ELT it is necessary to separate completely
these different components present inside the tyre.
The recycling of rubber is a completely automated process.
When the
tyres arrive at the recycling implants, before entering the grinding process,
they are washed to remove impurities and dirt. After the cleaning process,
tyres are shredded with the use of a chopper machine. When the tyres have
been chopped, a disc screen splits the fraction in undersized and oversized
fractions: the oversized fraction is chopped again while the undersized one
moves to the next cutting step. Once the tyres are chopped, they have to
be mechanically treated in order to create chips. Tyre chips are produced by
milling, specifically with a system of cracker mills. As soon as the chips are
coming out of the milling process, the steel part is removed with the use of a
magnet. After the removal of the steel, it is necessary to separate the textile
from the rubber. This process is done with the use of an air classifier where
the textile is sucked from the rubber crumb.
The milling process and the
separation of metals and textile are repeated several times, each time using
different cracker mills in order to achieve smaller size of rubber crumb. Then,
at the end of each milling step, after the separation of metals and textile, rub-
ber undergoes the sieving process, during which the size of the crumb rubber
==PAGE:35==
2.3 ELT - End of Life Tyre Rubber
15
is checked and the bigger pieces are reprocessed again [4, 17].
The process just explained is called ambient grinding and it is done at a
temperature around 25◦C. With this process it is possible to achieve ground
rubber until 0,2 mm of dimension: this type of granulate is called fine powder.
It is also possible to produce powder with smaller particle sizes, but in order
to do that, the rubber has to be cooled down. This process is called cryogenic
grinding.
Thanks to the cryogenic grinding it is possible to reduce the dimension of
the rubber crumb even further until a size lower than 70 µm. To decrease the
dimension of the rubber particle after the ambient grinding, the material has
to undergo another grinding process. First the grind rubber is cooled down
with the use of liquid nitrogen. When rubber is cooled down to a temperature
below its glass transition temperature (-72◦C), the material becomes harder
and it can be easily shredded in smaller pieces. In order to reduce the size
of rubber, the material is further processed by pin milling, where the crumb
rubber is put in between two rotating disks with pins: one rotating clockwise
and the other counterclockwise. In a completely similar way to the ambient
grinding, the powder undergoes a separation process to isolate metals and
textile from the rubber. The final dimension of the rubber powder is related
to the rotation speed and the overall time of the process [17].
The size of the rubber powders is not measured with the metric system,
but it is, instead, measured in mesh. As already mentioned, in order to classify
the rubber granulates and powder dimensions, a system of sieves is used. The
mesh corresponds to the number of holes per inch present in the sieve; the
finest powder that is possible to obtain with the cryogenic grinding process
is 200 mesh (200 holes per inch) which in the metric scale it corresponds to
particles with a dimension lower than 74 µm.
2.3.2
ELT applications
Recently, a special effort has been spent in finding new ways and markets to
recycle tyres. Hereinafter it is presented the state of art in the use of spent
tyre in accordance to the study carried out by European Tyre and Rubber
Manufacturers Association ETRMA [6].

Among the different applications in which ELT has been used, a promising
field seems to be the one of the cement and concrete. Over the last years new
studies and applications have been done in order to use rubber as filler in
concrete. Rubber, indeed, has the effect to lighten the final product but also
to increase the crack resistance and the deformation capacity of the concrete.
Rubber granulates are also used inside the asphalt. Modified asphalts with
rubber have shown an increase in life of the pavements until three times, opti-
mal drainage ability and good dumping, resulting in an overall noise reduction.
By taking advantage of the natural ability of rubber to absorb shocks,
ELT is widely used to create athletic tracks, pavements for playground and
synthetic sport fields. These products are totally made of rubber and the only
other material present is polyurethane as a binding agent. Beyond its capacity
to absorb shocks, rubber has indeed the ability to be flexible and offer a good
grip.
Moreover ELT is starting to be used as a damping material for train tracks.
Unfortunately the use of ELT for playground and sport fields is already a
saturated market [3]. Even if modified cements present a huge possibility for
recycling of ELT, they are not that developed due to the necessary government
and market acceptance [6]. Also the modified asphalts have a great potential
of expansion, nonetheless different modifiers from rubber are already widely
used, making difficult for ELT to enter in this market [6].
A promising field where ELT can be used is the automotive sector [6].
Most of the components on a vehicle require the presence of a thermoplastic
elastomer. By blending GTR (Ground Tyre Rubber) with a thermoplastic
it is possible to create a thermoplastic elastomer, thus making it possible to
mould ELT [6, 25, 12, 14, 24, 10].

2.4
Thermoplastic elastomer TPE
It is possible to differentiate polymers into three different families:
• Thermoplastics
• Thermosets
• Rubber and elastomers
Thermoplastic polymers present individual long chain molecules and when
heated, there is a decrease in the second bond forces that allow the motion
of the chains. Indeed thermoplastic polymers soften when the temperature is
raised, they become harder when they are cooled down and they soften again
if heated another time. The ability of this type of polymer to soften when
heated allows to easily reprocess the material. [9, 18, 5].
On the contrary, thermosets and rubbers, once cured they cannot be soft-
ened another time, making the recycling of these type of materials impossible.
Thermoset polymers are characterized by a three dimensional network that
cannot be broken during reheating. Rubbers also present a free dimensional
network, but the degree of interconnection is lower in comparison to the ther-
mosets polymers, thus allowing rubber chains to have a certain degree of
mobility [9, 18, 5].
In the 20th century a new type of polymers has been created: the Ther-
moplastic Elastomer (TPE). This type of material behaves like rubber but, it
has similar processability of thermoplastic polymers. Normally thermoplastic
elastomers are not used in applications where withstanding high temperatures
is needed. Indeed TPE, on the contrary of rubber, doesn’t show good prop-
erties when the the temperature rises; this is the reason why they are used
in application like polymer blending, footwear, adhesive and wire insulation
where resistance to high temperature is not a requirement [11].
Most of TPE are composed by two or more different polymeric phases
which can be either chemically linked together or not. Due to the type of
polymer used to create the thermoplastic elastormer, TPE can be classified in
different groups [11, 22]
• TPE-S Styrenic block copolymer
==PAGE:38==
18
2 Theoretical Background
• TPE-O Thermoplastic Olefinic Elastomer
• TPE-E Thermoplastic Polyester Elastomer
• TPE-V Thermoplastic Vulcanisate
• TPE-A Thermoplastic Amide Elastomer
• TPE-U Thermoplastic Polyurethane elastomer
The styrenic block co-polymer TPE-S is a block polymer with a struc-
ture A-B-A formed by two monomers: one is a thermoplastic, in particular
polystyrene (A) and the other is an elastomer (B). The polystyrene domain is
at the end of the chain and it is able to bond the elastomeric domain in a net-
work, similar to a vulcanization. Among this type of polymers, the most well
known is the SBS Styrene-Butadiene-Styrene co-polymer. It is a three blocks
co-polymer, its chain is indeed formed by an hard segment of polystyrene,
followed by a soft segment of polybutadiene and finally polystyrene again. In
this way the material has both the thermoplastic behaviour of the polystyrene
and the elastomeric properties of the polybutadiene. TPE-S are widely used
for adhesive footwear, bitumen modification, seals and grips [11, 22, 9].
The TPE-O thermoplastic olefinic elastomers are blends of polypropylene
and a non cross-linked rubber like EPDM. This type of polymer is mostly
used in automotive applications for bumbers and interior skin. Rubber, in-
deed, has the ability to increase the toughness of PP [11, 22]. The TPE-O
are block co-polymers formed by propylene (or ethylene) with an alpha-olefin
(an alpha-olefin is an alkene with carbon double bonds in between the first
and the second carbon of the polymeric chain). The soft phase of the copoly-
mer consists of olefin, while the polyethylene is responsible for the hard phase.
The TPE-E, thermoplastic polyester elastomer, and TPE-U are both pro-
duced by using a condensation reaction using three prepolymers: a long-chain
diol, a short-chain diol and a diacid [11]. The TPE-E is mostly used in appli-
cation where high temperatures are expected or when chemical resistance is
needed. This material is commonly used for tubes, hose, electrical wire insu-
lation, automotive applications etc. [22]. The TPE-U has good tear strength,
flex fatigue resistance and abrasion resistance; that’s why it is used in applica-
tions like athletic ski boots, soles of the shoes, industrial belting, cables and
wires [22].
==PAGE:39==
2.4 Thermoplastic elastomer TPE

Another outstanding type of thermoplastic elastomer is the TPE-V, the
thermoplastic elastomer vulcanized. This type of polymer is similar to the
TPE-O, indeed it is also a blend between PP and an elastomer (usually
EPDM). PP is often used as thermoplastic since it is easily available, cheap,
solvent resistant and it melts at high temperatures (over 165◦C). TPE-V shows
increased performances in comparison to TPE-O because the two materials
(PP and elastomer) are physically cross-linked. During the mixing of the com-
pounds the materials are dynamically vulcanized. In order to obtain the best
properties from the blend, the two polymers have to be finely dispersed; in
the case of different polarity, in order to avoid a poor adhesion and dispersion,
compatibilizers have to be used [11]. Combinations between PP and rubber
are used for bumpers for cars because these materials have good toughness,
low costs and they are flexible at low temperatures. Moreover, they are used
in window seals, pipes, automotive and appliance parts and, finally, in wire
and cable insulation [22].

In order to prepare the samples for the testing, the next step is injection

4.1
Mechanical Tests
After the extrusion of the first batch, several mechanical tests were performed.
The major aim of these tests was to evaluate how the rubber particle size and
the ratio between GTR and PP influence the final properties of the material.
In particular, tensile test, hardness test and scratch test were done for this
purpose.
4.1.1
Tensile test
The tensile tests were performed on an Instrom Universal Testing System
model 1115.
The set speed was 0,5 cm/min for the TPE blends and 0,1
cm/min for PP. The tests were carried out in accordance with the standard
EN-ISO 527:2012 part 1 and part 2 specific for the testing condition of molding
plastic. The specimens used for the tests are the dog bones type 1B from the
standard EN-ISO 527-2:2012 (see figure 4.1).

Statistical analysis
As mentioned before, the major focus of this master project is to evaluate the
influence that different powders and ratios between GTR and PP, has on the
properties of the material. In order to due that, an extremely useful tool is
the main effect plot of the ANOVA analysis.
The main effect plot shows the mean values for each category analyzed, and
it is used to see if the single category influences the studied properties. The
effect of the variables is shown with lines. In the case in which the analyzed
category does not influence the property investigated, the line in the main
effect plot is horizontal. On the other hand, if an influence is present, the line
in the plot will have a certain slope.

Discussion
From the study of the tensile test it was possible to see how both the ratio
between PP and GTR and the particle size of the powder used, influence the
mechanical properties of the material. In particular, thanks to the nature of
PP itself, higher content of this thermoplastic has the effect to increase the
overall strength and the stiffness of the material. Young’s Modulus, ultimate
strength and stress at break are mainly influenced by the ratio between PP
and GTR; nonetheless an improvement in properties can also be seen when
the powder with the smaller particle size is used (74 µm), thanks to the bet-
ter distribution between GTR and PP, as also explained by Holden [11]. The
blend that is able to withstand an higher strength and has the biggest stiffness
is the GTR74_60/40.
By looking instead at the strain at break, better results are achieved when
the content of GTR is greater, thanks to the elastic behaviour of the rubber.

between GTR and PP. The longest elongation at break was overall achieved
with the blend GTR74_80/20.
In general the wider standard deviation observed for the blends made with
the fine powder (0,2-0,8 mm) is indeed caused by the big range of the adopted

Discussion
From the analysis of the replication rate it was possible to make the following
considerations:
1. All the blends have shown a replication rate close to 1 for the microstruc-
tures from 1 to 5. For microfeatures number 6 and 7 the replication rate
deviates from 1.
2. PP is the only material that was able to replicate the microstructure of
the features number 7.
3. The overall lower replication rate of PP on the left side is caused by the
fact that the replication rate of square number 7 is smaller than 1.
4. The replication rate for the blend with 74 µm powder is higher than 1
in both left and right positions. The higher replication rate is caused by
the presence of rubber particles inside the microfeatures. The replication
rate is particularly high for the right position due to the accumulation
of rubber powder at the end of the sample.
5. Square number 6 showed an higher replication rate in the position far
from the gate, this is caused by the presence of a local higher content
of rubber powder it that area of the sample. The reason why square
number 7 has a replication rate lower than 1 is not fully clear.

Conclusions and

8.1
Conclusions
The main aim of this master project was to find a different way to recycle
tyre rubber in accordance with the goals of Paris Agreement and the United
Nation 2030 Agenda for sustainable development. The solution selected to
recycle tyre rubber was to create a TPE by mixing ground tyre rubber (GTR)
with the thermoplastic polymer PP. The focus of the thesis was on the effect
that particle size of the GTR powder and the ratio between GTR and PP used,
have on the properties of the created TPE. In particular three main aspects
were investigated: the mechanical properties, the distribution of the powder
in the injection molded samples and the replication rate.
Among the mechanical properties, the stiffness, ultimate strength, stress
and strain at break, and hardness were analyzed. An influence on the me-
chanical properties from both the GTR/PP ratio and the particle size of the
powder used was observed.
All the mechanical properties, with the exception of the strain at break,
are mainly influenced by the ratio between GTR and PP. By using an higher
content of PP, the hard phase inside the composite material, it is possible to
improve stiffness and strength of the TPE. The blends with higher contents of
PP indeed showed higher values of Young’s modulus, ultimate strength and
stress at break. The ratio between GTR and PP has an influence also on the
hardness: in particular a reduction in the hardness was observed when the
content of GTR was increased. In a lower degree, it is possible to observe an
influence from the GTR/PP ratio even for the strain at break; by increasing
the percentage of GTR in the blend it is possible to obtain higher elongation

Differently from other properties, the strain at the break is mainly influ-
enced by the variation of the particle size of the rubber powder used. Indeed
thanks to the use of the finest powder i.e. the 74 µm powder, the total elonga-
tion that the material can withstand is improved. Moreover the particle size of
the powder influences also the stiffness and the strength of the material. The
use of the 74 µm powder showed higher values of Young’s modulus, ultimate
strength and stress at break. It was not possible to observe any effect on the
hardness from the variation of the particle size of the powder used.
It has been remarkably interesting to notice how the hardness profile
throughout the sample is not constant. The hardness is indeed higher in the
center of the specimen and lower in the area far from the gate.This hardness
behaviour is directly related with the distribution of the materials inside the
sample. Indeed from the sulfur combustion test an higher content of sulfur
was observed, and thus of GTR powder, at the beginning, and in particular
in the part of the sample far from the gate, while in the center, a lower con-
tent was measured. Also from the SEM analysis of the cross sections it was
possible to notice an accumulation of PP in the surface of the central part
of the sample. Since the hardness is influenced by the the GTR/PP ratio, a
variation in the powder distribution has to influence the hardness itself.
The hardness is not the only property influenced by the powder distribu-
tion; indeed, also surface roughness is affected by the variation of the GTR
content throughout the sample.
Higher values of roughness were detected
in the area where the local content of GTR powder is higher. Indeed, as for
hardness, also surface roughness is influenced by the variation of the GTR/PP
ratio: the lower the content of PP, the higher the roughness. The effect of the
particle size of the powder is not relevant for the surface roughness of macro
products; on the other hand, when looking at surface roughness of microfea-
tures, better results were obtained using a smaller powder (74 µm powder) in
the area where the distribution between PP and GTR is more uniform (near
the gate).
From the roughness analysis it was noticed that the packing pressure dur-
ing injection molding has an effect on the powder distributions since it influ-
ences the disposition of PP.

8 Conclusions and future work
8.2
Future work
This master project has been a preliminary work in order to study the feasi-
bility of using recycle tyre rubber as material for injection molding, and to
study the main properties of the material. As explained at the beginning of
the thesis it was not used any vulcanizing or compatibilizer agent, this can be
part of future studies.
Moreover, during the thesis the optimal injection molding parameters in-
vestigated in a previous special course at DTU were used, but it is not known
yet the full process windows that can be used to injection molded the blends.
The investigation of the powder distribution was done only taking into con-
sideration the blend GTR74_80/20. It would be quite interesting to study
also the behaviour of the powders inside the other blends. In addition also
the study of the injection molding of the microstructure can be extended for
all the other missing blends.
In order to have a better understanding of both the particles distribution
and the replication rate it is necessary to run simulations.
The main focus for a future development is to identify a specific application
for this material, in order to enhance the properties of the blends in relation
to the final application.
An interesting field of development is the one of
the automotive.
Further studies need to be done in order to evaluate the
possibility to use this material in the cars industry. An interesting analysis
is the investigation of the thoughness of the material and in particular the
resistance of the blend to shock. Other promising sectors are the use of this
TPE as electrical wire insulator or in the footwear industry as material for
soles of shoes.
