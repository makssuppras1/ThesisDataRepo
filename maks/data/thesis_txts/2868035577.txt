Abstract 
Glutamate, a critical excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain, plays an essential role in synaptic activity and is 
implicated in various neurological disorders such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's diseases. Traditional biosen-
sors, which are largely two-dimensional, fail to interface effectively with the complex 3D structures found in 
modern neural models. To address this challenge, this research explores a novel approach leveraging 3D print-
ing technologies to fabricate pyrolytic carbon electrodes tailored for 3D environments and presents the design 
and development of a glutamate biosensor using three-dimensional (3D) printed pyrolytic carbon electrodes. 
The study focuses on optimizing the electro-functionalization of these electrodes for improved enzyme im-
mobilization and enhanced electron transfer kinetics. Techniques such as cyclic voltammetry, electrochemical 
impedance spectroscopy, and chronoamperometry were employed to characterize the electrodes and validate 
their functionality. A combination of ethylenediamine and 4-aminobenzoic acid was used for electrode surface 
modification, facilitating stable enzyme tethering and mediator attachment. 
Results highlight the feasibility of 3D electrodes in achieving superior electrochemical performance, repro-
ducibility, and compatibility with glutamate oxidase immobilization. The biosensor demonstrated sensitivity 
and specificity in detecting glutamate levels, while tests on hydrogel matrix adsorption underscored its poten-
tial for real-time monitoring in neural systems. By addressing challenges such as selectivity of the biosensor 
and mediator stability, this work contributes to advancing biosensor technology for integration into human-
derived neural models.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Scope and motivation of the project 
In most biosensor applications, the most common transducers employed are flat 2D electrodes. However, 
advancements in three-dimensional (3D) cellular models, including organoids from brain and other neural 
tissue constructs, need for the development of novel biosensors specifically designed to work on these com-
plex environments. Unlike 2D configurations, 3D biosensors must provide an interface that better resembles 
the biological environment interface to interact effectively with cells on a 3D structure. This need is becoming 
more obvious in the field of neurotransmitter monitoring, such as glutamate, a critical excitatory neurotrans-
mitter in the brain. A precise detection and quantification of glutamate in real time, are becoming needs to 
solve to better understand neural activity, disease mechanisms, and therapeutic interventions in 3D neural 
models. 
Conventional biosensor designs often fall short when applied to 3D cellular environments due to their limited 
ability to interact effectively with such complex structures. For instance, traditional methods for biomolecule 
immobilization, like glutaraldehyde crosslinking, are poorly suited for 3D surfaces. These techniques can 
result in non-specific coatings that obscure electrode structures and hinder targeted functionalization. Addi-
tionally, 3D carbon-based biosensors, such as pyrolytic carbon electrodes, typically have a low density of 
reactive surface groups, including carboxyl (-COOH) and amino (-NH₂), which creates obstacles for effective 
biomolecule attachment. Addressing these challenges requires advanced surface modification strategies to 
improve the functionalization potential of 3D electrodes, making them suitable for immobilizing glutamate-
specific enzymes like glutamate oxidase. 
This thesis centers on the development, functionalization, and evaluation of 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
designed to serve as transducers in glutamate biosensors. The research seeks to create a foundation for inte-
grating biosensors into sophisticated human neural models, including brain organoids and connectoids. These 
biosensors are intended to enable accurate monitoring of glutamate activity within 3D environments, provid-
ing valuable insights into neurotransmitter signaling in both normal and disease-related contexts such as Alz-
heimer and Parkinson1. 
This project is a key component of the OpenMind2 initiative, an ambitious research effort funded by the 
HORIZON-EIC-2021-PATHFINDEROPEN-01 program. OpenMind is on the path to replace traditional ani-
mal models with human-derived brain organoid systems, or "connectoids", that more accurately replicate hu-
man neural responses and avoiding the ethical constraints of animal experimentation. In the big picture of the 
whole project, the work presented in this thesis addresses the development of a glutamate biosensor aimed to 
be used to operate with the mentioned connectoids. These biosensors will facilitate real-time monitoring of 
glutamate signaling and will help to further understand the mechanics of glutamate-related diseases. 
The design and results presented in this thesis represent a simplified prototype of 3D biosensors. These find-
ings aim to inform future developments in biosensor technology, ultimately enabling their application as part 
of integrated 3D optoelectrical systems for comprehensive neurobiological research. Figure 1 provides an 
illustration of the envisioned final structure, to which the results of this study can be translated for practical 
implementation.

1.2 
 Objectives 
This final master thesis project aims to design, fabricate and characterize a 3D glutamate biosensor on a 3D 
printed pyrolytic carbon with a honeycomb internal architecture that can be used to quantify the glutamate 
concentration of a given sample (Figure 1). The electrode architecture allows evaluation of an ECM hydrogel 
on enzyme loading and electron transfer mediator tethering. To achieve this main goal, we have established 
the following objectives: 
 
A) Electro-functionalization of the pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
-Tethering of amine/carboxylic functionalities 
-Immobilization chemistries for tethering glutamate oxidase (GLOx), hydrogel and ferrocene electron 
transfer mediator 
 
B) Electrochemical characterization of the pyrolytic 3D electrodes and each of the experimental steps (electro-
functionalization/immobilization) using the following techniques: 
-Cyclic voltammetry (CV) -- Electrode behaviour and GLOx response 
-Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) -- Electrode behaviour 
-Chronoamperometry (CA) -- GLOx response under forced convection (e.g. stirring) 
 
C) Study of mass transfer through hydrogel matrix 
 
D) Study of the effect of immobilization chemistry on the enzyme kinetics 
 
E) Study of interfering substances (e.g. ascorbic acid, dopamine)

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 
Biosensor 
Biosensors have emerged as a critical tool in various scientific and industrial applications, offering the ability 
to detect and quantify specific biological substances in a rapid, reliable, and cost-effective manner. A biosen-
sor is typically defined as an analytical device that combines a biological component with a physicochemical 
detector to monitor biological, chemical, or physical reactions. This integration allows for the conversion of 
biological responses into measurable signals, which can then be analysed to provide valuable information. 
Biosensors have found widespread use in medical diagnostics, environmental monitoring and food safety, 
owing to their sensitivity, specificity, and versatility3,4. 
The key components of a biosensor include the biological recognition element, the transducer, and the detec-
tor. The biological recognition element is responsible for selectively binding to the analyte of interest, the 
transducer then converts the biochemical interaction into a measurable signal, which is typically electrical, 
optical, or thermal and, the detector processes the signal and outputs a readable result. See in Figure 2 a 
schematic representation of a biosensor. 
 
 
Figure 2. Schematic representation of the general components of a biosensor on the specific case of glutamate. Source: Cre-
ated with BioRender.com. 
 
Biosensors are generally classified into several categories based on their biorecognition elements and the na-
ture of the signal transduction mechanism. The most common types include: 
-Enzymatic biosensors: These biosensors employ enzymes as the biorecognition element, leveraging their 
ability to catalyse specific biochemical reactions. The detection of the reaction's product or the change in 
environmental conditions (such as pH or ion concentration) enables the sensor to quantify the target analyte. 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
14 
 
Enzymatic biosensors are highly specific and are particularly useful in detecting substrates such as glucose, 
lactate, or amino acids, as well as neurotransmitters like glutamate3. 
-Antibody-based biosensors (Immunosensors): These sensors utilize antibodies as biorecognition elements, 
offering high specificity for detecting antigens or other proteins. Immunosensors are used for pathogen detec-
tion, protein analysis, and immune response studies3. 
-Nucleic acid-based biosensors (Aptamer): These sensors utilize nucleic acids (DNA or RNA) to detect com-
plementary sequences in a sample. Aptamers have found applications in genetic testing, pathogen identifica-
tion, and environmental monitoring3. 
-Cell-based biosensors: In these sensors, living cells or cell receptors are used to detect changes in the envi-
ronment, such as the presence of toxins or other biological agents3. 
 
Biosensors have evolved significantly since their inception, and today, they are categorized into three gener-
ations based on the advancements in transduction mechanisms and the interaction between the biological 
recognition element and the signal transducer.  
The first generation of biosensors primarily relied on electrochemical methods, particularly amperometric and 
potentiometric techniques. In these sensors, the enzyme or biological element interacted directly with the 
analyte, producing a change in current or potential that was measurable. While these sensors were valuable 
for certain applications, they often had limitations in terms of sensitivity, stability, and selectivity, which 
prompted the development of more sophisticated biosensor designs. 
The second generation of biosensors introduced more refined transduction mechanisms, including the use of 
mediator molecules to facilitate electron transfer between the enzyme's active site and the electrode. These 
mediators improved the efficiency of the sensor and enhanced its sensitivity by overcoming limitations in 
electron transfer that were inherent in the first-generation designs. Additionally, advancements in immobili-
zation techniques, such as the use of hydrogels or other matrices, helped stabilize the biological recognition 
element, resulting in more reproducible and durable sensors. The second-generation biosensors paved the way 
for a broader range of applications by improving sensitivity and operational characteristics. 
The third generation of biosensors represents the latest advancements, with a focus on eliminating mediators 
altogether for a more direct electron transfer between the biorecognition element and the electrode. This gen-
eration often employs nanomaterials and advanced surface chemistry to enhance the interaction between the 
biological element and the transducer, leading to improved performance in terms of sensitivity, stability, and 
specificity. The third-generation biosensors are more compact, portable, and capable of real-time measure-
ments, making them particularly suited for point-of-care diagnostics and environmental monitoring5,6.  
To sum this up, biosensors are powerful analytical tools that have revolutionized the way we detect and meas-
ure biological and chemical substances. Their development has been driven by advances in materials science, 
bioengineering, and electronics, resulting in sensors that are more sensitive, reliable, and user-friendly than 
ever before. With the continuous evolution of biosensor technology, the future holds even greater potential 
for applications in medicine, environmental monitoring, food safety, and beyond. As researchers and engi-
neers continue to innovate, biosensors will undoubtedly play an increasingly important role in advancing sci-
entific discovery, improving healthcare outcomes, and addressing global challenges.

Glutamate 
Glutamate is the most abundant excitatory neurotransmitter in the central nervous system and plays a critical 
role in numerous physiological functions, including synaptic transmission, neuronal plasticity, learning, and 
memory. It is an amino acid (Figure 3) that acts on glutamate receptors, such as NMDA, AMPA, and kainite 
receptors, to mediate excitatory signalling between neurons7. Glutamate is synthesized from glutamine 
through the action of the enzyme glutaminase and is released into synaptic space in response to neuronal 
activity. After its release, glutamate binds to its receptors on neighbouring neurons, triggering various intra-
cellular signalling pathways. Once its action is complete, glutamate is taken up by surrounding glial cells and 
neurons, where it is converted back into glutamine by the enzyme glutamine synthetase. While glutamate is 
essential for normal brain function, its dysregulation is associated with various neurological disorders, includ-
ing epilepsy, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and Huntington's disease8. Excessive glutamate release, 
often resulting from neuronal injury or disease, can lead to excitotoxicity, a process in which overstimulation 
of neurons causes cell damage and death. Therefore, precise regulation of glutamate levels is critical for main-
taining brain health and function9,10.

3.1 
3D printing of electrodes 
3D printing has become a groundbreaking technology, revolutionizing fields such as engineering, healthcare, 
and manufacturing. Unlike traditional subtractive manufacturing methods, it constructs objects layer by layer, 
allowing for the creation of complex geometries and customized designs. The precision and versatility of 3D 
printing have accelerated its integration into diverse applications, including biomedicine, aerospace, and con-
sumer products16. 
The origins of 3D printing trace back to 1987, with the development of the first stereolithography (SLA) 
system by 3D Systems. Initially limited to prototype production, advances in printing methods and materials 
have since expanded its capabilities. By the 1990s, improved resin formulations enabled more intricate designs 
across a wider range of printers. Continued innovations in materials have led to the production of stronger and 
more durable components, meeting the rigorous demands of industries such as aerospace and healthcare. The 
2000s marked a significant turning point, with the advent of more affordable and accessible printers driving 
widespread adoption. Today, 3D printing is used not only for prototyping but also for manufacturing end-use 
products. With its market value projected to grow significantly, advancements in material science and printing 
technology remain key drivers of this expansion. Lower costs and increased accessibility have further democ-
ratized the technology, making it feasible for businesses of all sizes. Bioprinting and medical applications are 
set to experience substantial growth as 3D printing continues to evolve. 
The 3D printing process begins with a digital model, typically designed in CAD software and saved in STL 
file format. This format represents the object as a mesh of triangles, which facilitates slicing and manipulation. 
Slicing divides the model into thin, successive layers that the printer constructs sequentially, as it is illustrated 
in Figure 5. Parameters such as resolution, printing time, and calibration play a crucial role in determining 
the final product’s quality. Higher resolutions produce finer details but increase print time, while accurate 
calibration ensures dimensional precision by minimizing defects. Balancing these factors is essential to 
achieving optimal quality and functionality for specific applications.

Various 3D printing technologies exist, each distinguished by its underlying principles, strengths, and con-
straints. Among the most notable are: 
 
3.1.1 
Fused filament Fabrication (FFF) 
Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF), also referred to as Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM), is among the most 
prevalent 3D printing methods, known for its affordability and adaptability. The process involves feeding a 
thermoplastic filament—such as polylactic acid (PLA), acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS), or polyethylene 
terephthalate glycol (PETG)—into a heated print head, where it melts. The liquefied material is extruded 
through a nozzle onto a build platform in sequential layers, following a preprogrammed path. Each layer cools 
and solidifies, bonding to the one below, until the final 3D structure is complete. 
FFF is compatible with a wide variety of thermoplastics and can even utilize composite filaments embedded 
with materials like carbon fibber or wood. Despite its versatility and ease of use, FFF typically yields prints 
with lower resolution compared to resin-based techniques. The visible layer lines often result in a rougher 
surface finish, and the method lacks the precision needed for highly intricate or detailed designs. Nonetheless, 
its cost-efficiency and scalability make it an ideal choice for prototyping and cost-effective manufacturing17. 
 
3.1.2 
 VAT polymerization 
Vat Polymerization encompasses techniques such as Stereolithography (SLA) and Digital Light Processing 
(DLP), both recognized for their exceptional resolution and surface quality. These methods utilize a vat of 
liquid photopolymer resin, which is solidified layer by layer through exposure to a light source. In SLA, a 
concentrated laser beam selectively cures the resin by tracing each layer according to the design. Conversely, 
DLP employs a digital light projector to cure entire layers simultaneously, enabling faster production for larger 
components compared to SLA18. 
The precision of Vat Polymerization makes it particularly suited for applications demanding high accuracy, 
such as dental moulds, jewellery, and medical models. These techniques produce parts with excellent detail 
and a smooth surface finish. However, support structures are necessary for overhangs, as the liquid resin itself 
lacks structural support. Additional post-processing steps, including solvent washing and UV curing, are typ-
ically required to ensure the final parts achieve full strength and stability. While Vat Polymerization is re-
stricted to photopolymer resins, advancements have introduced specialized options, such as flexible, biocom-
patible, and high-temperature resins, broadening its range of applications19. 
Stereolithography (SLA) is the most prevalent resin-based 3D printing technology, widely acclaimed for its 
ability to create highly accurate, isotropic, and watertight prototypes and end-use components. SLA printers 
deliver parts with advanced material properties, combining speed and high resolution. Innovations in hard-
ware, software, and materials science have made SLA technology increasingly affordable and accessible, 
transforming how businesses approach prototyping, testing, and production processes. SLA parts are now 
utilized across industries for end-use products, industrial replacement components, manufacturing aids, 
==PAGE:20==

tooling, and more. Their smooth finish and precise tolerances make them ideal for multi-part assemblies, 
consumer-grade products, and final design evaluations20,21. 
SLA 3D printing relies on a light source to solidify liquid resin into three-dimensional structures by exposing 
a resin vat to light, which cures the material. Traditional SLA printers used a top-down configuration, with 
the light source positioned above the resin vat. Inverted stereolithography, introduced in 2011 by Formlabs 
co-founders, reversed this arrangement, positioning the light source beneath the vat. In this method, the cross-
section of the object is traced at the bottom-most layer, with liquid resin replenished as the build platform 
lifts, allowing new resin to flow beneath the cured layers. Key innovations, such as the development of trans-
parent and later flexible-bottomed resin tanks, facilitated the creation of larger inverted SLA printers by re-
ducing peel forces during the printing process22. 
SLA printers employ light-reactive thermoset materials known as "resins." These SLA resins polymerize when 
exposed to specific light wavelengths, forming solid structures by linking monomers and oligomers into rigid 
or flexible geometries.

3.1.3 
 PolyJet printing 
PolyJet printing is an advanced additive manufacturing technology that operates on principles like inkjet print-
ing. The process involves depositing tiny droplets of liquid photopolymer onto a build platform in precisely 
controlled layers, which are immediately cured using ultraviolet (UV) light. This rapid curing allows for con-
tinuous layer-by-layer construction, producing objects with remarkable detail and surface finishes comparable 
to those achieved through injection moulding. PolyJet technology can achieve extremely fine resolution, with 
layer thicknesses as small as 16 microns. 
A key advantage of PolyJet printing is its ability to work with multiple materials and colours simultaneously. 
This capability enables the creation of complex, multi-material components with varying mechanical proper-
ties and textures within a single print. For instance, PolyJet can combine rigid and flexible materials to produce 
objects with specific functional characteristics, such as tools featuring soft, ergonomic grips integrated with a 
rigid framework. This versatility makes the technology particularly valuable for functional prototypes, realis-
tic medical training models, product demonstrations, and educational purposes. 
However, PolyJet printing is not without its challenges. The process tends to be slower and more expensive 
compared to other 3D printing methods. These higher costs stem from the use of costly photopolymer resins 
and the need for additional support materials, which require removal during post-processing. Additionally, 
objects produced using PolyJet are less durable over time, as the cured photopolymers can degrade when 
exposed to prolonged sunlight or elevated temperatures. As a result, this technology is less suitable for long-
term applications when compared to thermoplastic-based alternatives. 
Nonetheless, PolyJet’s unparalleled resolution, ability to handle multiple materials, and capacity to produce 
highly detailed prototypes make it an indispensable tool in fields requiring complex, multi-material designs. 
Its unique strengths ensure its continued relevance for applications that prioritize precision and material ver-
satility, even with its inherent limitations24.

Pyrolytic carbon 
3.2.1 
 Carbon, carbon structure and pyrolytic carbon 
Carbon (C), a fundamental element of life, possesses a remarkable ability to form bonds with nearly all other 
elements, making it highly versatile in creating a wide range of organic molecules and compounds. This ver-
satility is attributed to its capacity to adopt multiple hybridization states—sp, sp², and sp³—which facilitate 
the formation of various bond types and lead to the emergence of diverse structural forms, known as allotropes. 
These allotropes exhibit distinct physical and chemical properties, a direct result of carbon’s bonding flexi-
bility and interactions with other elements25. 
Carbon’s allotropes include diamond, graphite, fullerenes, graphene, carbon nanotubes, amorphous carbon, 
glassy carbon, and pyrolytic carbon, each with unique structural and electronic characteristics. Diamond, with 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
21 
 
its sp³ hybridization, is famed for its extreme hardness and thermal conductivity, making it invaluable for 
applications such as cutting tools and heat dissipation systems. In contrast, graphite, characterized by sp² 
hybridization, has a layered structure that provides excellent electrical conductivity and lubricating properties. 
Other allotropes, such as graphene and carbon nanotubes, have revolutionized fields like electronics, materials 
science, and nanotechnology due to their exceptional mechanical strength and electronic properties26. 
Among these allotropes, pyrolytic carbon is particularly significant, especially for electrochemical applica-
tions. It is produced through the high-temperature pyrolysis of organic compounds in an inert atmosphere, 
resulting in a structure like graphite, with sp²-hybridized carbon atoms arranged in graphitic layers. Pyrolytic 
carbon combines the chemical stability and electrical conductivity of graphite with the biocompatibility and 
durability of glassy carbon. These attributes make it an excellent material for electrochemical systems, partic-
ularly as an electrode material. Its fabrication process also allows for precise control over structural and sur-
face properties, enhancing its versatility for advanced applications26,27. 
Pyrolytic carbon’s adaptability is particularly beneficial in biosensors and biomedical devices, where surface 
property control is crucial. The ability to modify its morphology and electrochemical characteristics during 
pyrolysis allows for tailoring its functionality to meet specific requirements. For example, in biosensors, its 
stable and conductive surface enhances signal sensitivity and accuracy. Furthermore, its biocompatibility 
makes it a suitable choice for applications such as neural interfaces, micro batteries, and other biomedical 
technologies requiring long-term stability and minimal biological reactivity27. 
Compared to other allotropes, such as glassy carbon, pyrolytic carbon offers greater flexibility in fabrication 
and performance. While glassy carbon shares similar chemical and thermal properties, it lacks the same degree 
of tunability during synthesis, limiting its application range. In contrast, pyrolytic carbon’s customizable na-
ture enables the creation of highly specialized devices for electrochemical research and practical applica-
tions26. 
The choice between pyrolytic carbon and other carbon allotropes for use in electrochemical systems depends 
on critical factors, including surface structure, bonding configurations, and the presence of functional groups. 
These characteristics significantly influence the material’s electrochemical performance in systems such as 
biosensors, fuel cells, and other devices. Pyrolytic carbon’s unique combination of stability, conductivity, 
biocompatibility, and modifiable surface properties has established its importance in both academic research 
and industrial applications. 
As a form consisting of graphene sheets, pyrolytic carbon is distinguished by its high conductivity, chemical 
inertness, and adaptability. Its fabrication process, involving controlled pyrolysis of organic precursors, en-
sures precise property control, making it particularly valuable for advanced electrochemical systems. From 
biosensors to neural interfaces and microbatteries, pyrolytic carbon plays a pivotal role in addressing the de-
mands of precision, stability, and compatibility, especially in the biomedical field28. 
 
3.2.2 
 The pyrolytic process 
Pyrolytic carbon is a specialized form of carbon produced through the thermal decomposition of organic ma-
terials in an oxygen-free environment. Unlike traditional combustion, which occurs in the presence of oxygen 
and leads to oxidation, the process of pyrolysis involves heating organic compounds to elevated tempera-
tures—typically ranging from 800°C to 2500°C—within an inert atmosphere such as nitrogen or argon. This 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
22 
 
controlled environment prevents the organic material from burning and instead facilitates its transformation 
into a solid carbon structure. The absence of external oxygen is crucial, as the oxygen required for chemical 
reactions is sourced exclusively from the precursor material. This ensures the formation of a stable, graphitic-
like structure while retaining elements of the original material’s composition. 
The pyrolysis process serves as a key thermal degradation method, converting organic polymers into glass-
like pyrolytic carbon under specific conditions. This transformation is achieved through a series of complex, 
simultaneous reactions, including hydrogen transfer, isomerization, dehydrogenation, and condensation. 
These chemical processes collectively determine the properties of the resulting carbon material, influencing 
its structural and functional characteristics29,30. 
 
3.2.2.1 Stages of Pyrolysis 
The pyrolysis of thermosetting resins, which are commonly used as precursor materials, typically occurs in 
three primary stages, each defined by its temperature range and distinct reaction mechanisms: 
a) Pre-Carbonization (T < 300°C): 
During the initial stage of pyrolysis, any unreacted monomer molecules, residual solvents, and volatile com-
ponents within the polymer precursor are removed. This purification step eliminates impurities and prepares 
the material for the subsequent phases. It also ensures that the remaining structure is sufficiently stable to 
withstand the higher temperatures involved in the next stage. 
b) Carbonization (300°C < T < 1200°C): 
In this critical phase, the organic material undergoes significant chemical and structural changes, transforming 
into a carbon-rich solid. The polymer precursors begin to break down, releasing non-carbon elements such as 
hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen in the form of gaseous byproducts. This step typically produces an amorphous 
or disordered carbon structure, depending on the precursor and the exact temperature conditions. 
 
c) Annealing (T > 1200°C): 
The final stage involves subjecting the material to even higher temperatures to enhance its crystallinity and 
structural order. Annealing allows for the reorganization of the carbon atoms into more graphitic-like layers 
by reducing defects and increasing the alignment of graphene sheets. This results in improved electrical con-
ductivity and mechanical stability, making the material suitable for advanced applications29,30. 
 
3.2.2.2 Factors Influencing Pyrolytic Carbon Properties 
The structural and functional properties of pyrolytic carbon are heavily influenced by the specific conditions 
under which it is synthesized. Key factors such as the precursor material, pyrolysis temperature, heating rate, 
and inert atmosphere play a critical role in shaping the final material29,30. 
a) Temperature: 
Higher pyrolysis temperatures generally lead to more graphitic structures, which exhibit higher electrical con-
ductivity, improved thermal properties, and reduced porosity. Conversely, lower temperatures produce more 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
23 
 
amorphous forms of pyrolytic carbon, which are characterized by increased porosity and a more disordered 
atomic arrangement. 
b) Heating Rate: 
The rate at which the material is heated during pyrolysis can also affect the final properties. Rapid heating 
may result in structural imperfections, while slower heating promotes the gradual reorganization of carbon 
atoms, yielding a more refined structure. 
c) Precursor Material: 
The type of organic precursor used in the pyrolysis process greatly influences the outcome. For example, 
thermosetting resins such as phenolic resins tend to produce carbon with high chemical stability and durability, 
while other polymers may yield materials with varying degrees of porosity, mechanical strength, or surface 
functionality. 
 
3.2.3 
Electrochemical behaviour 
The electrical conductivity of PyC is directly influenced by its graphitic content, which can be tailored through 
careful adjustment of pyrolysis conditions. Highly graphitized PyC demonstrates superior electron mobility, 
facilitating efficient charge transfer at the electrode surface. This characteristic makes it particularly well-
suited for high-power-density applications, including supercapacitors and lithium-ion batteries. Another sig-
nificant advantage of PyC lies in its adjustable surface area and porosity, which are determined by the precur-
sor material and specific pyrolysis parameters. Variations in porosity directly impact the surface area, offering 
considerable benefits for applications like biosensors, where an expanded surface area increases the number 
of active sites available for interactions with biological molecules. Enhanced porosity also improves mass 
transport within the electrode, contributing to greater efficiency in electrochemical devices. 
 
Furthermore, PyC is both chemically inert and highly resistant to corrosion, even in extreme conditions. This 
exceptional stability stems from its strong covalent carbon bonds and robust graphitic structure, which shield 
it from oxidation and other forms of degradation. Such corrosion resistance makes PyC an ideal candidate for 
use in aggressive environments, including wastewater treatment and industrial electrolysis, where materials 
are exposed to harsh chemical conditions31,32.

Carbon surface functionalization methodologies 
In the field of electrode surface modification, various techniques have been developed to introduce specific 
functional groups, enabling the attachment of biomolecules, such as antibodies, to pyrolytic carbon electrodes. 
This modification is crucial for applications in biosensing and biofuel cells, where direct interaction between 
biomolecules and electrode surfaces is necessary for targeted interactions and signal transduction. However, 
not all surface functionalization methods are suitable for the delicate structure of carbon electrodes. Among 
the techniques used to modify carbon electrodes are nitric acid oxidation, electrochemical reduction of diazo-
nium salts, and electro-oxidation.

3.3.1 
 Plasma oxygen 
Plasma oxygen treatment is a highly effective technique for enhancing the surface properties of PyC electrodes 
by introducing reactive oxygen species that generate functional groups such as carboxyl, hydroxyl, and car-
bonyl. These modifications significantly improve surface reactivity, facilitating the attachment of biomole-
cules essential for biosensing and biofuel cell applications. Unlike more aggressive methods, such as nitric 
acid oxidation, plasma treatment selectively alters the surface without compromising the underlying carbon 
structure, thereby maintaining electrode integrity. This method allows for precise control over surface char-
acteristics, enhancing wettability and improving the efficiency of biomolecule adsorption. Additionally, 
plasma oxygen treatment can be combined with complementary techniques like electro-oxidation for ad-
vanced surface customization, making it an excellent choice for bioelectronic applications33. 
 
3.3.2 
 Nitric acid oxidation 
Nitric acid oxidation is a widely used method for functionalizing PyC electrodes by incorporating carboxyl 
and hydroxyl groups onto the surface. This approach is effective in increasing surface reactivity; however, it 
is also highly aggressive and can result in substantial degradation of the carbon structure. The strong oxidative 
conditions may compromise the integrity of the electrode material, particularly in PyC and other carbon-based 
substrates. Despite these drawbacks, nitric acid oxidation remains valuable for applications requiring high 
surface reactivity, provided precautions are taken to minimize structural damage. It is best suited for scenarios 
where maintaining electrode integrity is not a primary concern34. 
 
3.3.3 
 Diazonium salts 
The electrochemical reduction of diazonium salts is a controlled method for modifying PyC electrodes by 
covalently attaching specific functional groups. In this process, aryl radicals generated from diazonium salts 
bond to the carbon surface, introducing stable functional groups. While effective, this method is primarily 
limited to aryl compounds, as aliphatic diazonium salts are unstable and unsuitable for attaching flexible, 
aliphatic linkers. Despite its limitations, the diazonium salt method is valuable for applications requiring sta-
ble, specific surface modifications, but it may not be ideal for those requiring more diverse or flexible func-
tionalization35. 
 
3.3.4 
 Electro polymerization 
Electro polymerization is a versatile technique for modifying PyC electrodes by forming a conductive polymer 
film through the electrochemical polymerization of monomers. This method allows for the introduction of a 
wide range of functional groups, depending on the chosen monomer. Electro polymerization can incorporate 
both aliphatic and aromatic compounds, providing greater flexibility compared to other methods. However, it 
can result in thick, non-uniform polymer films that may obstruct the electrode surface, reducing sensitivity 
and hindering electron transfer. Despite this, it remains a useful option for applications requiring diverse sur-
face modifications36.

3.3.5 
 Electro oxidation 
Electro-oxidation is a highly effective and adaptable technique for functionalizing PyC electrodes by attaching 
both aliphatic and aryl linkers to the surface. This process involves applying an anodic potential to a PyC 
electrode in the presence of amine-containing compounds, causing the oxidation of amine groups and their 
subsequent covalent bonding to the electrode surface. A key benefit of electro-oxidation is its gentleness com-
pared to other oxidative methods, such as nitric acid oxidation, which can degrade the carbon structure. This 
results in better preservation of the electrode's integrity, which is crucial for maintaining its performance in 
electrochemical applications. Electro-oxidation offers precise control over surface modifications, enabling the 
introduction of various functional groups, such as amino or carboxyl groups, that act as attachment sites for 
biomolecules. This makes electro-oxidation particularly well-suited for bioelectronic applications, where sta-
ble, covalent immobilization of biomolecules is essential for reliable signal transduction and electrode inter-
action. In this study, electro-oxidation was used to modify the electrode surface with amino and carboxyl 
groups via ethylenediamine (EDA) and aminobenzoic acid (ABA), respectively, facilitating the covalent im-
mobilization of electron transfer mediators and biomolecules37. 
 
3.4 
Electrochemistry 
Electrochemistry is the study of chemical reactions that involve the transfer of electrons between substances, 
focusing on the relationship between electrical energy and chemical changes at the electrode-electrolyte in-
terface. In biosensor applications, electrochemistry is crucial for detecting biological molecules by converting 
biochemical reactions into measurable electrical signals. This field is fundamental for developing diagnostic 
tools, environmental sensors, and medical monitoring systems. By understanding the movement of electrons 
and ions, it becomes possible to design electrochemical systems capable of effectively detecting specific bio-
molecules. Key techniques and concepts related to biosensors, such as the electrochemical cell, cyclic volt-
ammetry, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, and chronoamperometry, will be explored to demonstrate 
their role in detecting and analysing biological targets. 
 
3.4.1 
The electrochemical cell 
An electrochemical cell is fundamental for performing electrochemical measurements and typically employs 
a three-electrode setup: a working electrode, a reference electrode, and a counter electrode, all immersed in 
an electrolyte solution. The working electrode serves as the site where the primary electrochemical reaction 
occurs, while the reference electrode provides a stable potential against which the working electrode's poten-
tial is monitored. The counter electrode ensures the completion of the circuit by balancing the current flowing 
through the cell. This configuration is illustrated in Figure 7.

A) Working Electrode (WE): The working electrode is the location where the electrochemical process of 
interest takes place. Its potential is regulated by a potentiostat relative to the reference electrode, al-
lowing precise control over the redox reactions occurring at its surface. It is crucial that the material 
of the working electrode remains chemically inert within the potential range applied during the ex-
periment. This ensures that any detected current is solely due to the analyte and not caused by un-
wanted side reactions with the electrode material. 
 
B) Reference Electrode (RE): The reference electrode provides a stable and consistent electrochemical 
potential, acting as a reference point for measuring the working electrode's potential. It conducts min-
imal current, which reduces any voltage drop between the reference and working electrodes, thereby 
enhancing the accuracy of the working electrode's potential control. 
 
C) Counter Electrode (CE): The counter electrode completes the electrical circuit and facilitates the flow 
of current between itself and the working electrode. Its surface area is typically much larger than that 
of the working electrode (approximately ten times greater), allowing it to accommodate the full cur-
rent required without influencing the electrochemical reactions at the working electrode. To maintain 
the current balance, the counter electrode may operate at extreme potentials, though it should not 
interfere with the reaction of interest. 
 
The electrolyte solution, consisting of a solvent and a supporting electrolyte, plays a crucial role in enabling 
ion movement, which compensates for the electron flow between the electrodes. The supporting electrolyte is 
chosen for its ability to dissolve well in the solvent, remain chemically inert under experimental conditions, 
and enhance the conductivity of the solution. The solvent must be stable within the experimental potential 
range, efficiently dissolve both the analyte and supporting electrolyte, and avoid participating in any undesir-
able side reactions38.

3.4.2 
 Chronoamperometry 
Chronoamperometry (CA) is an electrochemical technique used to measure the current as a function of time 
while applying a constant potential to the working electrode. The technique involves a potential step, where 
the electrode's potential is suddenly changed to a specific value, and the current response is monitored over 
time, as it is shown in Figure 8. This allows for studying the kinetics of electrochemical reactions, particularly 
those involving diffusion-controlled processes. In CA, the current typically decays over time as the concen-
tration of electroactive species near the electrode surface decreases due to diffusion limitations38. 
 
Figure 8. Typical plot for a CA. In the x-axis the time is monitored and in the y-axis changes on the current intensity de-
tected are recorded, and those correlate with the spike of the analyte in the reaction mixture. 
 
The initial rise in current occurs as electroactive species rapidly diffuse toward the electrode, while the slower 
decay follows as the reaction becomes diffusion limited. The rate of this decay provides information about 
the diffusion coefficient of the species involved. The relationship between the current decay and time follows 
a t-1/2 behaviour in ideal diffusion-controlled reactions, which is a key feature of chronoamperometric meas-
urements39. 
CA is a versatile electrochemical technique that can be influenced by several factors, which must be carefully 
controlled to obtain reliable and accurate measurements. Understanding these influencing factors is critical 
for optimizing the technique, particularly in biosensing applications: 
a) Electrode Surface Area: the surface area of the working electrode significantly impacts the current 
response in chronoamperometry. Larger surface areas provide more sites for the electrochemical re-
action, leading to higher currents. However, if the surface area is too large, it may lead to unwanted 
side reactions that could affect the overall measurement.  
 
b) Diffusion Coefficient: the diffusion coefficient (D) of the analyte dictates the rate at which electro-
active species can move toward the electrode. Analytes with higher diffusion coefficients will produce 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
28 
 
a faster response and lead to a more distinct current decay. Conversely, analytes with low diffusion 
coefficients may result in slower responses and longer time constants.  
 
c) Concentration of Electroactive Species: the concentration of the analyte in solution directly affects 
the magnitude of the current observed during chronoamperometry. Higher concentrations result in 
larger current responses, which can improve the signal-to-noise ratio and enhance the sensitivity of 
the measurement. However, excessively high concentrations can lead to saturation effects, where the 
electrode becomes fully occupied by electroactive species and the current plateaus. 
 
d) Applied Potential: it determines the electrochemical reactions that occur at the electrode surface. 
The choice of potential depends on the redox characteristics of the analyte. An inappropriate potential 
can either fail to trigger the desired reaction or cause unwanted side reactions, reducing the specificity 
of the measurement. Therefore, the potential should be carefully selected based on the oxidation or 
reduction potential of the target species and is typically recommended to perform a Cyclic Voltam-
metry scan of the analyte to precisely identify the oxidation peaks.  
 
CA is an effective technique for detecting specific biomolecules, enzymes, or other electroactive analytes in 
biosensing applications. By applying a potential step to initiate a redox reaction, CA monitors the resulting 
current response, which is directly proportional to the concentration of the target analyte. In enzyme-based 
biosensors, for instance, the enzyme facilitates a reaction that generates an electroactive product detectable 
through CA. This method is particularly suited for diffusion-controlled processes, where analytes must reach 
the electrode surface via diffusion. However, reactions that are not diffusion-limited or occur too rapidly may 
not produce the expected current-time behaviour. 
The technique offers numerous advantages, including simplicity, high sensitivity, and the ability to provide 
real-time data on electrochemical processes. By analysing the time-dependent current response, it is possible 
to gather valuable information about analyte concentrations, diffusion behaviour, and the kinetics of biochem-
ical reactions. These attributes make CA a critical tool for developing efficient, label-free biosensors applica-
ble across a range of fields, from healthcare to environmental monitoring. In this study, the developed biosen-
sors were consistently employed as an amperometric detection method for glucose and glutamate38–40. 
 
3.4.3 
Cyclic Voltammetry 
Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) is a versatile analytical technique used to analyse reversible and irreversible redox 
reactions by systematically varying the potential applied to an electrode while recording the resulting current. 
It provides valuable insights into reaction mechanisms, electron transfer kinetics, and the thermodynamic 
behaviour of electroactive species. CV is widely utilized in the study of batteries, fuel cells, sensors, and 
electron transfer processes in biological systems. During a CV experiment, the potential is scanned at a spec-
ified rate (scan rate), usually measured in V/s. As the potential reaches the threshold for an electrochemical 
reaction, a current is generated. The forward scan typically represents the anodic (oxidation) process, while 
the reverse scan corresponds to the cathodic (reduction) process41,42.

The relationship between current and scan rate for a reversible redox reaction is governed by the Randles-
Sevcik equation (Figure 9). This equation defines the peak current (ip) for a reversible reaction, illustrating 
how the scan rate (𝜈) impacts the peak current in a CV experiment. For diffusion-controlled processes, the 
peak current is directly proportional to the square root of the scan rate, whereas for adsorption-controlled 
processes, the peak current is directly proportional to the scan rate.rate41.

the temperature in K.41 
 
In a CV, two distinct peaks are observed: the anodic peak, corresponding to oxidation, and the cathodic peak, 
corresponding to reduction. During the forward scan, as the potential increases, electroactive species near the 
electrode surface undergo oxidation, leading to an increase in current. The anodic peak represents the point at 
which the surface concentration of the species reaches a minimum. Conversely, during the reverse scan, as 
the potential decreases, the oxidized species are reduced back to their original state, generating a cathodic 
peak. The interpretation of CV data often follows either the IUPAC or US conventions, with the IUPAC 
convention being used in this study (Figure 10), were potential increases from low to high. 
 
Figure 10. IUPAC convention to report CV data. 
The shape and behaviour of a cyclic voltammogram are influenced by several critical parameters. The scan 
rate, which determines the rate of potential variation, significantly impacts both the position of the peaks and 
the magnitude of the current. At higher scan rates, peak currents increase due to the reduced time available 
for the Nernst diffusion layer to reach equilibrium. This results in a rapid consumption and replenishment of 
electroactive species within the diffusion layer. However, after the peak, the current begins to decline because 
diffusion limitations cause the reaction rate to surpass the transport rate of species to the electrode. Further 
increases in the scan rate can lead to peak distortion and greater separation between the oxidation and reduc-
tion peaks. For reversible redox processes involving freely diffusing species, the Randles-Sevcik equation 
predicts that peak current is proportional to the square root of the scan rate42–44. 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
30 
 
The diffusion coefficient, which governs the movement of electroactive species toward the electrode, is an-
other key factor. A higher diffusion coefficient facilitates faster mass transport, leading to sharper peaks and 
increased currents. In contrast, slower diffusion broadens the peaks and may influence peak separation by 
affecting reaction kinetics. The Randles-Sevcik equation is also useful for determining whether an analyte 
diffuses freely in solution or is adsorbed onto the electrode surface42. 
The concentration of electroactive species in the solution directly affects peak current. According to the 
Randles-Sevcik equation, peak current is proportional to the square root of concentration. Higher concentra-
tions yield larger peaks, improving the signal-to-noise ratio and enhancing the visibility of redox processes42. 
The potential range, defined by the minimum and maximum potential values, establishes the limits for poten-
tial cycling during cyclic voltammetry. This range determines which redox processes can occur during the 
experiment. If the redox potential of the target species falls outside this range, the reaction will not be detected. 
Choosing an appropriate potential range is crucial to capturing all relevant electrochemical reactions while 
avoiding overextension, which could result inside reactions or damage to the electrode42. 
Finally, the surface area of the electrode plays a significant role in determining the overall current response. 
A larger electrode surface area provides more sites for electron transfer, leading to a higher current. Surface 
effects, such as species adsorption onto the electrode, can also influence peak shape and separation, particu-
larly when reactions involve surface-bound intermediates42. 
 
3.4.4 
 Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) is a frequency-domain technique used to measure the imped-
ance of an electrochemical system across a wide range of frequencies. EIS is particularly effective for inves-
tigating complex processes such as charge transfer resistance, double-layer capacitance, and ion diffusion in 
materials. It is widely applied in studies related to corrosion, battery performance, and electrochemical sen-
sors, providing a detailed understanding of both surface and bulk electrochemical properties. 
In EIS, the total impedance, Z(ω), is measured as a function of the frequency (ω) of the applied alternating 
current (AC) signal. The impedance response is typically displayed in a Nyquist plot, where the real part of 
the impedance (Z′(ω)) is plotted against the imaginary part (Z′′(ω)). A typical Nyquist plot for a simple elec-
trochemical system consists of two primary regions: a semicircle at high frequencies, representing charge 
transfer resistance (Rct) and double-layer capacitance (Cdl), and a linear region at low frequencies, which is 
attributed to diffusion-controlled processes (Warburg impedance)45,46. 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
31 
 
 
Figure 11. Nyquist plot typical representation, with the imaginary part of the impedance being represented in the y-axis and 
the real part on the x-axis. 
 
As shown in Figure 11, the different components of a Nyquist plot correspond to various resistances in the 
electrochemical system. The electrode resistance refers to the inherent resistance of the electrode material 
itself, which can vary depending on the electrode material used. The electrolyte resistance represents the re-
sistance of the bulk electrolyte solution to ion movement between the working and counter electrodes, typi-
cally reflected by the high-frequency intercept on the x-axis of the Nyquist plot.  
The diffuse layer resistance, which appears in the low-frequency region of the Nyquist plot, corresponds to 
the resistance encountered by ions moving through the diffuse layer of the electrical double layer (EDL) 
formed at the electrode-electrolyte interface. As ions diffuse through this layer, they face resistance based on 
the electrolyte properties and the electrode's surface charge. The formation of the EDL occurs at the electrode 
surface, where ions from the electrolyte organize into layers (Stern and diffuse layers) to balance the charge 
on the electrode. The steep slope in this region of the Nyquist plot reflects the capacitive behaviour during the 
buildup of the EDL. Ion diffusion is represented by a low-frequency tail, which appears as a straight line with 
a slope of 1, referred to as the Warburg impedance. This line indicates the diffusion of ions from the electrolyte 
to the electrode surface. The equilibrium differential capacitance is related to the capacitance of the EDL at 
equilibrium and shows the charge storage capacity in the double layer at a given potential. A larger semicircle 
in the Nyquist plot typically indicates a higher differential capacitance43,45,47. 
Several key parameters influence EIS measurements and are essential for interpreting the data. The frequency 
range, typically spanning millihertz, plays a significant role. Low frequencies are crucial for investigating 
diffusion-controlled processes, while high frequencies provide insights into charge transfer and surface reac-
tions at the electrode-electrolyte interface. The Cdl reflects the capacity of the electrode surface to store 
charge. This parameter is closely related to the electrode surface area and the electrolyte's nature, and it is 
particularly important in systems where capacitive behaviour dominates. Rct measures the ease of electron 
transfer between the electrode and the electrolyte. A lower Rct indicates faster electron transfer kinetics, which 
is vital for applications like fuel cells and electrochemical sensors, where efficiency is key45,46. 
At low frequencies, Warburg impedance, associated with ion diffusion, becomes more prominent. This pa-
rameter is critical for systems where diffusion limits the reaction rate, such as in batteries. Warburg impedance 
is represented as a sloped line at lower frequencies in the Nyquist plot and reflects the resistance to mass 
transport. 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
32 
 
The impedance of an electrochemical system can be modelled using the Randles’ circuit, a well-known equiv-
alent circuit model in electrochemistry, especially for EIS analysis. The Randles’ circuit is used to represent 
a diffusion-controlled faradaic reaction at a planar electrode. It consists of two fundamental components: ionic 
resistance (Ri) and Cdl. In the absence of a faradaic reaction, this forms the simplest model for an electrode 
in an electrolyte. However, when a faradaic reaction occurs, Rct is introduced, representing the resistance to 
electron transfer at the electrode surface. Rct is placed in parallel with Cdl because the faradaic reaction occurs 
concurrently with the charging of the double layer45,46. 
 
Figure 12. Randles equivalent circuit. is an equivalent electrical circuit used to model the behaviour of an electrochemical 
system, representing the electrode-electrolyte interface. It consists of a solution resistance (Rs or R1), a double-layer capaci-
tance (Cdl or C1) a charge transfer resistance (Rct or R2) and a Warburg impedance (Zw or W1) which account for the dif-
fusion of electroactive species. 
 
The key feature of the equivalent Randles circuit (illustrated in Figure 12) is the assumption that the rate of 
the faradaic reaction is limited by diffusion. To account for this, the Warburg impedance (W), representing 
the resistance to mass transport, is added in series with Rct. Warburg impedance is crucial in systems where 
the electrochemical reaction is controlled by the diffusion of species to the electrode surface. More complex 
systems may require additional components in the equivalent circuit to represent multiple processes or over-
lapping reactions48. 
The Randles circuit produces a distinctive Nyquist plot, typically featuring a semicircular shape in the high-
frequency region, corresponding to Ri, Rct, and Cdl. At lower frequencies, the plot transitions into a 45° line, 
representing Warburg impedance, which dominates when diffusion becomes the limiting factor. Both Nyquist 
and Bode plots generated from the Randles circuit are valuable tools for analysing electrochemical systems, 
offering clear insights into the resistive and capacitive elements and the diffusion-controlled processes in-
volved in faradaic reactions48. 
 
2.6.4.1 Impedance and pH 
The pH of the solution plays a critical role in determining the behaviour of redox-active species and electron 
transfer processes at the electrode interface in electrochemical measurements. It influences the protonation 
and deprotonation states of molecules, which directly impact their electrochemical properties. Protonation 
refers to the addition of a proton (H⁺) to a molecule, while deprotonation is the loss of a proton. These changes 
are controlled by the pKa of the species—the pH at which half of the molecules are protonated. 
In controlled or buffered solutions, the protonation states of species are stabilized, directly affecting charge 
transfer, especially in techniques such as EIS. Surface-modified electrodes with molecules containing ioniza-
ble groups (such as carboxyl or amine groups) are particularly sensitive to pH changes. The protonation state 
of these groups depends on both the pKa of the tethered species and the pH of the electrolyte solution. For 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
33 
 
example, at a pH below the pKa of a carboxyl group, the group is protonated and neutral, while at a pH above 
the pKa, it becomes deprotonated and negatively charged. 
The protonation state influences both electron transfer kinetics and interfacial capacitance. When molecules 
are protonated, charge transfer may be easier due to reduced electrostatic repulsion, leading to lower imped-
ance. Conversely, when deprotonated, negatively charged species can repel redox-active anions in solution, 
increasing impedance and decreasing electron transfer efficiency. This protonation-dependent behaviour can 
result in significant impedance shifts, especially near the pKa of the tethered species. 
For example, the commonly used ferrocyanide/ferricyanide ([Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻/³⁻) redox system typically operates 
in a neutral or slightly basic pH environment, such as phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) at around pH 7. The 
extent of impedance change in such a system is primarily determined by the pKa of the tethered molecules, 
which governs their protonation state in the buffered solution45,49. 
 
2.6.4.2 Tethering acidic molecules 
For molecules tethered with groups that have a low pKa, such as carboxyl groups (with pKa values typically 
around 4-5), the protonation state is strongly influenced by the neutral pH of the system. At pH 7, these car-
boxyl groups will predominantly exist in their deprotonated form, carrying a negative charge. This results in 
a negatively charged interface, which can cause electrostatic repulsion with the negatively charged ferrocya-
nide and ferricyanide ions in solution. Such repulsion decreases the efficiency of electron transfer at the elec-
trode surface, leading to an increase in the Rct and, in turn, the overall impedance in EIS measurements. The 
deprotonated groups effectively create a barrier, hindering the smooth interaction between the redox species 
and the electrode surface49. 
 
2.6.4.3 Tethering basic molecules 
For molecules with a high pKa, such as amine groups (typically with pKa values around 9-10), the situation 
differs. At pH 7, these groups remain mostly protonated and carry a positive charge. Since the protonated 
amine groups do not repel the negatively charged ferrocyanide/ferricyanide ions, they may even create a more 
favourable environment for electron transfer by attracting these anionic species. The presence of positively 
charged groups near the electrode surface can enhance the efficiency of charge transfer, leading to a decrease 
in charge transfer resistance (Rct) and a reduction in overall impedance in EIS measurements. These tethered 
molecules may also function as electron mediators, promoting faster electron transfer between the electrode 
and the redox couple49.

2D electrodes 
The 2D pyrolytic carbon electrodes utilized in this research were fabricated according to methods outlined in 
previous studies50, employing SU-8, a negative epoxy-based photoresist (Figure 13). These electrodes were

The SU-8 photoresist underwent pyrolysis at elevated temperatures (900°C to 1100°C) in a nitrogen atmos-
phere, transforming the polymeric material into conductive pyrolytic carbon. This process yielded a carbon 
structure resembling glassy carbon, known for its advantageous electrochemical properties27. To enhance the 
electrodes' performance, oxygen plasma treatment was applied to the surface, improving wettability prior to 
electrochemical testing.

4.3 
Production of 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
4.3.1 
 Design  
The initial stage involves creating the model using CAD software. The model is subsequently exported in a 
3D-printable format, typically as an (.stl) file. Printer settings, such as layout and support structure specifica-
tions, are then configured. The printing process itself is carried out in an inverted SLA tank. Following print-
ing, post-processing begins with cleaning the printed components using the Form Wash to eliminate any re-
sidual resin. After washing and drying, the parts undergo post-curing in the Form Cure machine, which 
strengthens and finalizes the material structure. 
In this study, the design and fabrication of 3D electrodes were accomplished through SLA printing, a highly 
precise additive manufacturing method. The process commenced with the 3D modelling of electrode struc-
tures using SolidWorks 2023 software. The completed models were exported from SolidWorks in the stereo-
lithography (.stl) file format, a widely used standard for 3D printing. This format converts the CAD design 
into a triangular mesh that can be processed by 3D printing software. To clarify future references, the working 
area of the designed electrodes will be referred as “racket”, since it resembles this common shape. A typical 
shape for the racket electrodes produced can be appreciated in Figure 14.

4.3.2 
 Printing 
The stereolithography printing process was performed using the Form 3 Timely Elephant Formlabs SLA 
printer with High Temp V2 resin. STL files generated from SolidWorks were uploaded to PreForm Software 
V.2.18.0 (Formlabs Inc.), designed to optimize print layout, generate supports, and prepare files for printing. 
PreForm automatically slices the 3D model into layers and creates essential support structures. 
Support generation and refinement were critical, with supports carefully adjusted to avoid interference with 
electrode regions, especially inner areas. The supports were minimized while ensuring stability by tilting the 
==PAGE:38==

structures laterally and placing supports only on the sides. This editing process, though time-intensive, signif-
icantly affects print quality.  
High Temp V2 resin was used, valued for its high thermal resistance and dimensional stability, ideal for 
electrodes intended for pyrolysis. The resin, aged 2022, offers excellent accuracy, low shrinkage, and strong 
adhesion, ensuring fidelity of the printed structures. 
After printing, structures were cleaned to remove residual resin using the Form Wash system with isopropanol. 
The parts, still attached to the platform, were immersed for 6 minutes per the resin manufacturer's guidelines. 
This step ensures that leftover resin does not compromise mechanical properties or the final finish. 
Once cleaned, structures were detached from the platform using a metal spatula, and supports were manually 
snapped off. A second isopropanol wash was conducted to remove any remaining support material, followed 
by drying with compressed air to eliminate solvent residues before curing. 
 
4.3.3 
 Curing 
The final stage in the post-processing of the printed electrodes involved curing, which fully polymerizes the 
resin to strengthen the material. This was carried out in the Form Cure machine at 60°C for 10 minutes. In 
resin-based 3D printing, curing solidifies photosensitive monomers using controlled ultraviolet (UV) or high-
energy light. While the curing process begins during printing, post-curing is essential to expose the completed 
structure to additional UV light, ensuring high-resolution prints with smooth surface finishes. 
 
4.3.4 
 Pyrolysis 
The pyrolysis process employed in this study adhered to a well-established method optimized for material 
carbonization, ensuring consistent and high-quality outcomes as refined in prior research.30 The process was 
conducted using a PEO-604 furnace (ATV Technology, Germany) within a controlled nitrogen environment. 
Nitrogen was supplied at a flow rate of 20 standard litres per minute (SLM) to maintain an inert atmosphere 
suitable for pyrolysis. 
The protocol involved a carefully controlled temperature ramping sequence. The temperature was initially 
increased at a rate of 2°C per minute until reaching 340°C. This was followed by a slower ramp rate of 1°C 
per minute, continuing to 425°C. Finally, the temperature was raised at 2°C per minute to the target of 900°C. 
A dwell time of 1 hour was held at the peak temperature to allow the carbonization process to stabilize. Dwell 
time refers to the period during which the furnace maintains a constant temperature, ensuring thorough thermal 
treatment and uniform structural transformation throughout the material. 
 
4.3.5 
 Preparation for electrochemical use 
a) Plasma treatment 
The oxygen plasma treatment of the 3D-printed pyrolytic carbon electrodes was conducted using a low-pres-
sure plasma system, specifically the Diener ATTO model (Diener Electronics). The electrodes were placed 
inside the ATTO plasma chamber and exposed to oxygen plasma at 100 W power and 0.3 mbar pressure. The 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
38 
 
treatment was carried out for 10 minutes, a duration optimized to produce a high density of oxygen-containing 
functional groups, including hydroxyl, carbonyl, and carboxyl groups, on the electrode surface. 
Pure oxygen gas was introduced into the chamber throughout the process to ensure stable plasma conditions. 
After treatment, the electrodes were carefully removed and stored under ambient conditions to protect the 
newly functionalized surfaces from contamination. 
 
b) Wire attaching 
The preparation of pyrolyzed 3D carbon electrodes for electrochemical analysis involved attaching cables and 
insulating non-active regions. Cable attachment was achieved using the EPO-TEK® H20E epoxy kit, a silver-
filled, two-component epoxy system known for its high conductivity and common use in microelectronics. 
The components were mixed in a 1:1 ratio as per the manufacturer's guidelines. A drop of the mixture was 
applied to the pad section of each electrode, utilizing the pad's large surface area to ensure a strong adhesive 
bond and minimal contact resistance. The assembly was then cured at 80°C for three hours to establish a stable 
connection. 
 
c) SU-8 insulation 
To restrict electrochemical activity exclusively to the racket of the electrode, the arm and pad were insulated 
using SU-8 epoxy resin obtained from MicroChem Corp., a widely recognized supplier in the microfabrication 
industry. SU-8, a robust and chemically resistant negative photoresist commonly employed in microelectron-
ics, was used for this purpose. Insulation was carried out with a PDMS-based immersion chamber, which 
enabled controlled exposure of the electrodes to the SU-8 solution while shielding the racket-shaped working 
electrode area. After immersion, the SU-8-coated areas were hardened by exposure to UV light, ensuring 
insulation was confined to the intended regions. The electrodes were then post-baked to fully cure the SU-8. 
This insulation process eliminated interference from non-target areas, allowing for a focused examination of 
the working electrode’s electrochemical properties.

4.5 
EDA tethering 
Before initiating the electrochemical oxidation of EDA, the electrodes were thoroughly conditioned in anhy-
drous ethanol. This process was essential to remove any potential contaminants and to prevent any interference 
from water during the functionalization stage. Such a precaution ensures that the high oxidizing potential 
applied during the oxidation does not trigger water electrolysis, a reaction that could compromise the attach-
ment of EDA by disrupting the process. 
The functionalization itself was carried out by immersing the electrodes into a solution composed of 50 mM 
EDA and 100 mM lithium perchlorate (LiClO₄), with the latter serving as the supporting electrolyte. Both 
compounds were carefully dissolved in anhydrous ethanol. Using an Ag/AgCl reference electrode, a potential 
range between 0.0 V and 1.3 V was applied, with the potential being cycled at a scan rate of 10 mV/s for a 
total of three cycles. This cyclic voltammetry scan, spanning these potential limits, facilitates the oxidation of 
EDA, enabling its successful attachment to the surface of the electrode30.  
 
4.6 
ABA tethering 
To attach ABA onto the electrode surface, an electrochemical procedure was carried out using cyclic voltam-
metry in a solution of anhydrous ethanol. A 10 mM solution of ABA was prepared in anhydrous ethanol, with 
100 mM lithium perchlorate (LiClO₄) serving as the supporting electrolyte. The ABA solution was made by 
dissolving 20.57 mg of ABA in 15 mL of a 100 mM LiClO₄ solution. This LiClO₄ solution was prepared by 
dissolving 0.213 g of LiClO₄ in 20 mL of anhydrous ethanol30. 
The electrochemical attachment of ABA to the electrode was performed using cyclic voltammetry, conducted 
on a PalmSens potentiostat. The process commenced without any equilibration time, starting at 0.0 V and 
gradually cycling up to 1.3 V before returning to 0.0 V. The scan rate was set to 0.01 V/s, and the voltammetry 
was performed over a total of three cycles. Each step in the cycle had an incremental step size of 0.01 V, 
allowing for precise control of the applied potential throughout the entire process. 
For both EDA and ABA tethering, the solvent was ethanol. Ethanol can dissolve most plastic containers, so 
the tethering was carried in a glass container with lid (the lid was to avoid changes on solvent volume due to 
evaporation) with the 15mL for each tethering. The tethering set-up is the same as shown in Figure 7 on 
section “2.6.1 The electrochemical cell”.

Mediator incubation 
4.7.1 
 2,5-Dihydroxibenzaldehyde 
The attachment of 2,5-Dihydroxibenzaldehyde (DHB, Figure 15) to the electrodes was accomplished through 
reductive amination chemistry, where amine groups on the carbon electrode surface reacted with the aldehyde 
(CHO) groups present on DHB. This reaction was promoted using NaCNBH3 as the reducing agent, main-
taining a pH of 7.4. To prepare the DHB solution, a 10 mM NaCNBH3 solution in PBS was mixed, followed 
by the addition of DHB to achieve a final concentration of 50 mM. The electrodes were immersed in this DHB 
solution at pH 7.0 for a period of 24 hours at room temperature. The incubation was carried out on an orbital 
shaker set at 300 rpm to ensure continuous mixing, which was essential for optimizing the reaction on the 
electrode surface. This extended incubation period allowed the reductive amination reaction to proceed to 
completion, promoting the effective binding of DHB to the functionalized electrode. 
 
Figure 15. Chemical structure of the DHB molecule. Source: own-made figure using ChemSketch. 
Following incubation, the electrodes were thoroughly rinsed with deionized water and then washed in PBS 
on a Labline Orbit Shaker for 15 minutes. This washing step was necessary to remove any DHB that had been 
non-specifically adsorbed to the electrode surface, leaving only the covalently bound mediator for subsequent 
electrochemical analysis.  
CV measurements were then conducted in deoxygenated PBS, which was prepared by bubbling nitrogen 
through the solution for 30 minutes prior to use. Throughout the experiments, CV was employed to assess the 
efficiency of the DHB attachment to the electrode surface. Various scan rates were tested in different experi-
ments to examine the influence of scan rate on the functionalization process. The standard CV settings in-
cluded an equilibration time of 0 seconds, an initial potential of -0.6 V, and a vertex potential of 0.9 V. The 
potential was cycled between these values, returning to -0.6 V at the end of each cycle. Scan rates ranged from 
0.01 V/s to 1 V/s for the different experiments30. 
 
4.7.2 
 Ferrocene carboxylic acid 
Ferrocene carboxylic acid (FCA, Figure 16) is a widely used redox mediator in biosensor applications due to 
its stable electrochemical properties and ability to facilitate electron transfer between the biological recogni-
tion element and the electrode surface. As a ferrocene derivative, FCA contains a ferrocenyl group, which 
consists of two cyclopentadienyl rings coordinated to an iron atom, and a carboxyl group (-COOH), which is 
highly reactive and can participate in surface interactions.  
==PAGE:42==
 
 
41 
 
 
Figure 16. Chemical structure of the FCA molecule. Source: own-made figure using ChemSketch 
 
The electrochemical properties of FCA make it ideal for enhancing the sensitivity of enzymatic biosensors, 
where it acts to shuttle electrons from the active site of the enzyme to the electrode, thus improving the overall 
performance of the sensor. The attachment of FCA to the sensor surface typically occurs through its carboxyl 
group, which can form covalent or non-covalent bonds with amine groups present on the electrode surface or 
the biological recognition element, such as enzymes or antibodies.  
The interaction between the carboxyl group of FCA and the amine residues on the surface often takes place 
through the formation of amide bonds or electrostatic interactions, depending on the conditions of the sensor 
environment. This attachment not only immobilizes the FCA on the sensor surface but also positions it near 
the biorecognition element, allowing for efficient electron transfer and enhanced sensor sensitivity. The ability 
of FCA to anchor onto amine groups and its redox properties make it an essential component in improving 
the performance of biosensors, especially in the detection of analytes requiring enzymatic catalysis. 
The protocol used for the attachment of this mediator on top of the electrode surface comprises a first step in 
which a solution of 20mg/mL of FCA in ethanol is prepared and the racket region of the electrode is sub-
merged in it for 2 hours. After the incubation, the electrode is washed carefully using ethanol and right after 
is submerged in a 10mM solution of NaCNBH3 in PBS. After 5 minutes of incubation, the electrode is washed 
with PBS and stored in the same solvent. 
 
4.8 
EDC/NHS activation and enzyme attachment 
Activation of the carboxylic groups was performed using a classic EDC/NHS protocol by using an aqueous 
solution containing 200 mM EDC and 50mM NHS in 100 mM HEPES buffer (pH 6) for 25 minutes. To 
submerge the electrodes during the time of the incubation, a 96-well microplate was employed, by filling as 
many wells as electrodes needed with 300uL of the activation mixture. As rule of thumb, for each 2mL of 
HEPES employed, 0,062g of EDC and 0,012g of NHS were employed51. A schematic representation showing 
the insights of the reaction is shown in Figure 17. 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
42 
 
 
Figure 17. Chemical reaction mechanism for the EDC/NHS chemistry. Source: own-made figure using BioRender, 
ChemSketch and PowerPoint.  
 
The main objective of this research is the obtention of a biosensor for the detection of glutamate. The main 
problem of this work, is the fact that glutamate is not an electro-active compound, meaning that it won’t 
undergo a REDOX reaction by itself if a potential is applied to the solution. Therefore, the attachment of the 
glutamate oxidase (GLOx) enzyme on top of the electrode surface is a necessary condition to allow for the 
glutamate detection in solution. Additionally, and since the protocol of the tethering was first tested with 
glucose oxidase due to the relatively high cost of the glutamate related products, two biosensors were produced 
by the end of the project, having the first one glucose oxidase attached on its surface, whilst the final biosensor 
was developed by attaching glutamate oxidase. 
The attachment of the enzyme to the electrode is achieved through the EDC/NHS coupling reaction. The 
activated carboxyl groups on the electrode surface form covalent bonds with the amine groups on the enzyme, 
resulting in stable enzyme attachment. This process enhances the enzyme-electrode interaction, ensuring ef-
ficient surface functionalization while maintaining the enzyme's activity for subsequent applications. 
Different methods for the attachment of the enzyme on top of the ABA activated molecules can be used. The 
final method employed consisted of a long incubation of 24 hours at 4ºC, with specificities depending on the 
enzyme being attached. For the glucose oxidase incubation, each electrode was submerged in 300uL of an 
enzyme solution at 8kU/mL in PBS (pH=7.4), whilst in the case of glutamate oxidase, the electrodes were 
submerged in an enzyme solution at 2U/mL in PBS (pH=7.4). These concentrations were picked after an 
extensive literature review, and further optimization could be carried out to optimize it. After the 24h incuba-
tion, the electrodes were cleaned using PBS and a Pasteur pipette to let go drops very slowly and in a controlled 
manner51.  
 
4.9 
Hydrogel matrix adsorption 
Coating an electrode, onto which an enzyme has been previously attached, with a hydrogel layer offers several 
advantages, including enhanced enzyme stability, protection from environmental factors, and improved reus-
ability. Hydrogels create a protective matrix around the enzyme, preventing denaturation while allowing for 
the diffusion of substrates and products, which is crucial for maintaining enzymatic activity. Various materials 
can be used for hydrogel coatings, such as polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), agarose, or synthetic polymers like 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
43 
 
polyethylene glycol (PEG). However, chitosan stands out as a common choice due to its biocompatibility, 
biodegradability, and the ability to form strong, stable films. It is a polymer composed by β-(1→4)-linked D-
glucosamine (deacetylated unit) and N-acetyl-D-glucosamine (acetylated unit). Additionally, chitosan offers 
the benefit of being a natural polysaccharide, which promotes further enzyme stability and mimics biological 
environments, making it particularly suitable for biosensor applications. It’s easy processability and non-toxic 
nature make it a favourable option for ensuring long-term enzyme activity and efficient sensor performance 
on in vivo monitoring52. 
For the previously mentioned reasons and its availability, chitosan was used as the material for the hydrogel 
coating. Chitosan is typically purchased in the format of thin flakes obtained from crab-shells and with a purity 
higher than 99%. From literature it was decided to use a 1% (w/w) chitosan solution dissolved in DI water 
and acidified with a solution of acetic acid 30% (v/v). For a general case of 100ml final volume preparation, 
1g of chitosan was weighted and acidified with 9mL of the mentioned acid. Since chitosan is difficult to 
dissolve, additionally to the acidification, agitation at 300rpm with temperature at 60ºC was applied, obtaining 
a transparent solution after 2h of agitation. After completion, keep in the fume hood for the acetic acid content 
and label it properly53. It is indispensable to neutralize the acidity of the hydrogel to avoid the denaturalization 
of the enzyme, so the pH was adjusted with 1N NaOH solution and the pH checked using pH indicator paper54. 
The hydrogel prepared not only contained chitosan. As an innovative approach for the gel coating, the hydro-
gel formulation also included an extra amount of the enzyme and mediator used in that strategy. For the 
glucose biosensor, the hydrogel was prepared by first preparing a solution 4kU/mL of enzyme and adding 
BSA and DHB to final concentrations of 20mg/mL and 50mM respectively, all in PBS. This solution was 
posteriorly mixed 1:1 with the chitosan solution previously prepared, and the electrodes were immersed in 
this solution for 2h at room temperature30. For the glutamate biosensor, the hydrogel was prepared by first 
preparing a solution 2U/mL of enzyme and adding BSA and DHB to final concentrations of 20mg/mL and 
50mM respectively. For the FCA glutamate biosensor, no mediator was added due to the insolubility of FCA 
in water, so only the enzyme and BSA were added into the hydrogel, at the same concentration as before.  
After the 2h incubation, it is important to crosslink the chitosan chains in the gel, otherwise the individual 
monomers would remain weakly associated between them and the overall structure would lack integrity and 
stability. The crosslinker agent was chosen to be glutaraldehyde. The stock provided was at 25% (v/v) and the 
working concentration was 0.25%, so an aliquot of the mother solution was 100 times diluted by using PBS. 
300uL of the glutaraldehyde solution were disposed in a 96-microplate in as may wells as electrodes used, 
and the electrodes were submerged into the crosslinker solution for 1h. After the last incubation the electrodes 
are washed using PBS and a Pasteur pipette, letting go drops very slowly and controlled. After this, the bio-
sensor is fully prepared and can be stored at 4ºC in PBS (helpful to have a 96-microplate to store the finished 
electrodes)53.

5.2 
Electro-functionalization of the pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
The functionalization approaches utilized in this study were influenced by earlier work by Bisht et al. from 
the University of California, Irvine, which investigated diffusion-free mediator-based miniature biofuel cell 
anodes fabricated on Carbon-MEMS electrodes. This method involves the electrochemical oxidation of amine 
linkers onto the surface of the electrode. The functionalization technique is compatible with both aliphatic and 
aryl linkers, employing stable compounds that offer a solid and flexible framework for subsequent modifica-
tions.  
Throughout the functionalization procedure, two electrode types were tested: the 3D electrodes described 
earlier in the methodology section and a specific set of 2D electrodes with a "lollipop" shape. Evaluating both 
3D and 2D electrodes offered a dual perspective on the robustness and efficacy of the functionalization tech-
niques. This comparative approach was essential for two reasons: first, it allowed for the validation of the 
functionalization process across different electrode geometries, thus increasing the reliability and adaptability 
of the results. Second, as outlined in the Materials and Methods section, the fabrication of 3D electrodes is 
not trivial and requires a considerable number of resources and time, so, the readily available 2D electrodes 
were used as practical and efficient models for testing. Insights derived from these 2D models were then 
applied to the 3D electrodes. 
Previous investigation of the same research group revealed that to have anchor points for both, the redox 
mediator and the enzyme on top of the electrode surface, the presence of both EDA and ABA were necessary.

The initial phase of this study involved the electrochemical analysis of both 2D and 3D bare electrodes to 
determine their fundamental characteristics and evaluate their potential for subsequent bio-functionalization. 
As shown in Figure 21, CV was conducted on 36 2D electrodes and 21 3D electrodes under identical exper-
imental conditions. The CV parameters were set with an initial potential of -0.4 V, vertex potentials of 0.8 V 
and -0.4 V, a scan rate of 0.1 V/s, and a step size of 0.01 V. These conditions were carefully chosen to offer 
a thorough understanding of the redox behaviour and overall conductivity of the electrodes. 
The results, summarized in Table 4, reveal that both 2D and 3D electrodes display distinct oxidation and 
reduction peaks, confirming the conductive nature of the 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes and the suitability of 
the experimental setup for electrochemical analysis. The oxidation peak current for the 3D electrodes was 
notably higher (886.50 µA on average) compared to the 2D electrodes (167.26 µA on average). In the same 
manner, the reduction peak currents were -926.30 µA for the 3D electrodes and -182.95 µA for the 2D elec-
trodes. The elevated current values observed in the 3D electrodes suggest a greater surface area, likely con-
tributing to an increased number of electroactive sites available for electron transfer. Furthermore, the peak

(1812.80 µA) than in the 2D electrodes (350.21 µA). This disparity in ΔEp can be attributed to the distinct 
structural features of the 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes. The expanded surface area of the 3D electrodes not 
only provides more electroactive sites but may also influence electron transfer kinetics and internal resistance 
due to the intricate porous structure. These results are in line with previous research on 3D pyrolytic carbon 
electrodes, which have demonstrated that such structures can greatly improve electrochemical performance 
by offering a larger surface area and more efficient pathways for electron transfer.

enabling stable charge transfer across samples.

5.2.2 
 3D electrodes stability test 
To evaluate the robustness and durability of the 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes, two electrochemical experi-
ments were carried out after subjecting the electrodes to oxygen plasma treatment and insulating them with 
SU8 resin. These stability assessments are crucial to confirm that the electrodes consistently exhibit reliable 
electrochemical behaviour over extended periods, ensuring their effectiveness in repeated applications. The 
goal is for the electrodes to demonstrate minimal degradation or variation in performance. 
The first experiment focused on analysing the electrochemical behaviour of the electrodes at varying scan 
rates using CV. This approach helps elucidate the electrodes’ response to different electron transfer rates. The 
scan rates examined included 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8, and 1 V/s. By observing how the current response changes 
with scan rate, the electrode’s capability to operate under different electrochemical conditions can be assessed. 
The relationship between peak current and scan rate provides insights into diffusion-driven processes and 
reaction kinetics56. Typically, if the peak current increases proportionally with the square root of the scan rate, 
the process is diffusion-controlled, indicating that the current is predominantly determined by the diffusion 
rate of reactants to the electrode's surface. Conversely, reversible reactions are often characterized by a con-
stant peak-to-peak separation, regardless of scan rate. However, if the peak separation increases with scan 
rate, this points to slower, quasi-reversible, or irreversible reactions due to kinetic constraints41 
As the analysis here involves bare electrodes without surface modifications or mediators, the results are an-
ticipated to display a linear correlation between the peak current and the square root of the scan rate, signifying 
a diffusion-controlled process. Alternatively, a deviation from this proportionality would reflect a largely non-
Faradaic, capacitive behaviour. The results of this study can be appreciated in Figure 25.

5.2.3 
 EDA tethering 
With the electrodes characterized and their electrochemical activity validated, the initial tethering step in-
volves the attachment of EDA to the bare electrodes, as depicted in Figure 27. This functionalization was 
successfully applied to both 2D and 3D electrodes. According to prior studies, this process is expected to 
produce a single anodic peak around 0.8 V, corresponding to the oxidation potential of EDA. Consistent with 
these predictions, only an anodic peak was observed, confirming the anticipated irreversible attachment mech-
anism30. 
 
Figure 27. Schematic of the initial functionalization step where the bare electrode is modified with ethylenediamine (EDA) 
through electrochemical oxidation. Source: own-made figure using BioRender.  
 
During the three CV cycles, a progressive decline in peak current was observed for both 2D and 3D electrodes 
with each subsequent scan. This behaviour suggests surface saturation, as the electrode surface becomes fully 
coated with EDA molecules, leaving fewer active sites available for oxidation. In the first cycle, a distinct 
anodic peak signalled the initiation of EDA oxidation and attachment to the surface. By the third cycle, the 
anodic peak had diminished significantly, indicating that the functionalization process had reached its capac-
ity. This reduction in current aligns with the hypothesis that EDA molecules progressively occupy the elec-
trode surface, eventually saturating all available active sites. The consistent anodic peak in the initial cycle 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
54 
 
and the declining current in subsequent cycles strongly support the successful functionalization of the elec-
trodes with EDA. 
Figure 28 highlights notable differences between the 2D and 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes during the elec-
tro-oxidation of EDA. Notably, the current response of the 3D electrodes is significantly higher compared to 
the 2D electrodes, as seen in the cyclic voltammograms (CVs). This higher current indicates that the 3D elec-
trodes, with their larger surface area, facilitate greater oxidation of EDA. The enhanced surface area of the 3D 
structure likely improves electron transfer efficiency, resulting in higher currents and demonstrating the suit-
ability of these electrodes for applications demanding effective electron exchange. 
Another key difference is the variation in oxidation potential between the two electrode types. For the 3D 
electrodes, the anodic peak occurs near 0.8 V, consistent with previously reported values for pyrolytic carbon 
electrodes in the literature. This alignment suggests that the surface properties of the 3D electrodes are like 
those described in earlier studies, resulting in comparable electrochemical behaviour. In contrast, the oxidation 
peak for the 2D electrodes shifts to approximately 1.03 V. This shift may arise from differences in surface 
morphology or intrinsic electrochemical properties between the flat 2D electrodes and the more intricate 3D 
pyrolytic carbon structures. The higher oxidation potential observed in the 2D electrodes could indicate that 
greater energy is required to initiate oxidation, potentially due to a less favourable environment for EDA 
attachment. Nonetheless, the results confirm that EDA was successfully tethered to the surfaces of both elec-
trode types. 
The next phase of this research focuses on EIS to evaluate the effects of EDA attachment on both 2D and 3D 
electrodes. By analysing the impedance values before and after EDA functionalization, we aim to determine 
how surface modifications influence the electrochemical behaviour of these electrodes. Impedance analysis 
provides valuable insights into the nature of the surface modifications and their impact on the electrodes' 
properties. 
As discussed in the introduction, several factors influence impedance measurements, including the pH of the 
testing solvent, the acidic or basic properties of the attached compound, and the electrochemical conditions 
during tethering. In this study, a ferro/ferri solution prepared in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) was used as 
the electrolyte for EIS experiments. The PBS buffer, primarily composed of sodium and potassium phos-
phates, maintains a stable pH around 7, approximating physiological conditions. The addition of Fe(II) and 
Fe(III) cyanide complexes does not significantly alter the pH due to their weakly basic nature in this buffered 
environment. This configuration allows for impedance measurements under near-neutral pH conditions, 
providing a consistent and biologically relevant framework for the analysis.

that under the experimental conditions, where the pH of the ferroferri solution is close to the second pKa, the

surface is reduced because fewer amino groups are fully protonated at near-neutral pH57. 
This partial protonation leads to the hypothesis that EDA functionalization would result in an increase in 
impedance. The reduced positive charge of EDA could limit its interactions with negatively charged species 
in the ferroferri solution, contributing to this impedance rise. Furthermore, the unique surface area and poros-

significantly higher standard deviation of 165.55 ohms (Table 6). This substantial increase in both the mean 
impedance and its variability suggests that the EDA attachment process is not uniform, leading to notable 
differences in the amount of EDA tethered across electrode surfaces.

The incorporation of ABA was carried out to introduce carboxylic groups onto the electrode surface, thereby 
increasing the variety of functional groups available for future biomolecule conjugation. After the initial at-
tachment of EDA to provide amino functionalities, ABA was electrochemically immobilized to create car-

solution, as described in the methodology section. According to previous studies, the oxidation peak for ABA 
is anticipated to occur between 0.8 and 1 V, which indicates the electrooxidation required for covalent binding. 
During the CV process, the amine groups on ABA are oxidized under the applied positive potential, producing 
reactive intermediates, such as radicals or cationic species. These intermediates then form covalent bonds with 
the carbon surface of the electrode, ensuring the attachment of ABA. A schematic representation can be found 
in Figure 31.

A similar analysis to that of EDA can be applied to ABA in terms of impedance. ABA contains both a car-
boxylic acid group (-COOH) and an amino group (-NH2). The carboxylic acid group has a pKa of 2.38 34, 
while the amino group has a pKa of 4.85 34 at 25ºC. At a pH of 7, the carboxylic acid group is expected to be 
deprotonated, resulting in a negatively charged carboxylate (-COO⁻) group. On the other hand, the amino 
group is likely to remain neutral since the surrounding pH is above its pKa but still far from the point at which 
the amino group would be fully deprotonated58. 
Given these protonation states, the tethered ABA molecule will carry a net negative charge due to the depro-
tonated carboxyl group, which could increase impedance by introducing repulsive interactions with other 
negatively charged species in the electrolyte. This effect is expected to hinder electron transfer processes, as 
the introduction of negatively charged functional groups would likely reduce the electrode’s ability to interact 
with other negatively charged species in the solution, thereby increasing impedance. 
Considering these factors, we expect an increase in impedance following ABA attachment, like the EDA 
scenario. However, due to ABA’s acidic nature and its fully deprotonated carboxylate group at neutral pH, 
this increase may vary in magnitude. 
The EIS analysis of ABA tethering was performed to assess how the introduction of carboxyl groups affects 
the electron transfer resistance (Rct) of both 2D and 3D electrodes. As shown in Figure 35 and 36, the Rct 
values significantly increased after EDA tethering, followed by an additional rise after ABA functionalization. 
This observation aligns with our hypothesis that the introduction of carboxyl groups would raise impedance 
due to their deprotonated form (-COO⁻) at the neutral pH of the electrolyte. The increase in impedance can be 
attributed to the presence of the negatively charged carboxyl groups, which may repel other negatively charged 
species in the electrolyte, thereby reducing electron transfer efficiency. The impedance after ABA tethering 
is notably higher in the bare approach than in the prefunctionalized approach. One possible explanation is 
that, since no prior EDA is tethered, more surface area is available for ABA, creating a larger barrier to elec-
tron transfer. In any case, the rise in impedance serves as evidence that successful ABA tethering was achieved 
in both approaches using the conditions outlined in the methodology.

5.2.5 
 Study of different tethering conditions 
As previously discussed, the tethering process exhibits considerable variability, indicating that it is inherently 
inconsistent. This variability can be attributed to several factors, such as human error, the experimental setup, 
and the intrinsic differences in the electrodes produced in-house. Both 2D and 3D electrodes, being non-
industrial and handmade, inherently lack the uniformity typically found in commercially manufactured mate-
rials. To address this issue, further investigations were conducted to determine whether specific parameter 
adjustments could improve tethering consistency. The aim is to reduce variability in the process, as measured 
by the standard deviation of Rct following biomolecule tethering. A lower standard deviation would indicate 
a more uniform distribution of tethered molecules across the electrode surface, resulting in a more consistent 
tethering process. Two key parameters were explored to assess their impact on the consistency of the tethering 
process for both 2D and 3D electrodes: the scan rate and the concentration of the tethered molecule. 
Additionally, another approach was examined where both EDA and ABA were tethered simultaneously. This 
is feasible because both molecules can be tethered within the same potential window, allowing for the prepa-
ration of a solution with a specific molar ratio of ABA and EDA. This method enables the tethering of both 
amino and carboxylic groups in a single step. The simultaneous tethering strategy offers several advantages. 
First, it increases the likelihood of achieving a more homogeneous distribution of both amino and carboxylic 
groups, in contrast to sequential tethering, where one molecule may occupy a substantial portion of the elec-
trode surface, leaving fewer available sites for the subsequent molecule. Second, this approach simplifies the 
surface functionalization process, potentially reducing the number of steps needed to functionalize the elec-
trode surface. 
The results of this analysis are presented in Table 7. One of the most notable observations is the high varia-
bility in the tethering process across almost all conditions tested. This variability is reflected in the significant 
standard deviations observed, particularly for the 2D electrodes. For example, the EDA tethering on 2D elec-
trodes at a scan rate of 5 mV/sec showed a 60% condition error, while increasing the scan rate to 10 mV/sec 
resulted in an even larger condition error of 102%. This suggests that neither a higher nor lower scan rate 
consistently improves the tethering process's consistency, highlighting the inherent variability of the tethering 
mechanism. Moreover, increasing the concentration of the tethered molecules does not appear to significantly 
improve the process either, as demonstrated by the concentration-dependent results for both EDA and ABA. 
The significant variability may be attributed to several factors, including inconsistencies in the electrode sur-
face, human error during preparation, and the complexity of the tethering mechanism itself. Additionally, it is 
important to note that the electrodes used in this study were fabricated in-house, meaning they may lack the 
uniformity seen in industrially manufactured electrodes. This inherent variability further complicates the op-
timization of the tethering process and suggests that achieving more consistent surface modification may re-
quire more refined process control.

Several reasons could explain that for either of the mediator-modified electrodes there was no signal. The first 
reason, as stated previously, could be not enough GLOx covering the electrode’s surface. Most of literature

formulation is an elegant solution aiming to fully load with enough enzyme to receive a signal after the analyte 
addition, and it would be further explored on the next section.  
Another potential explanation for the lack of detection could be the concentration window selected for the 
analyte. In all this study the glutamate concentration is analysed between 0.005mM and 1mM, covering the 
typical range in which this neurotransmitter can be found in biological samples. Therefore, with a broad scope 
as the one being used, the hypothesis of not enough analyte becomes unclear, and the responsibility falls upon 
the electrode characteristics rather than this condition.  
One last arguing point could be that the approach chosen for the electrode modification was ineffective. That 
could be a reason, since the enzyme could not be able to attach on top of the ABA moiety and there wouldn’t 
be any signalling. However, this loses strength when acknowledging that the protocol used was previously 
used in the same study to immobilize glucose oxidase (GOx) on top of the same EDA/ABA modified elec-
trodes. The results for this first glucose biosensor are explained and detailed on the section “4.4.1 Glucose 
biosensing”, but the fact that glucose detection was successfully achieved, is enough to demonstrate the fea-
sibility of the approach in principle, and there is no evidence that it wouldn’t work similarly to the GLOx.  
The only conclusion that can be extracted from this data is that a first incubation of 24h with the enzyme at 
4ºC is not enough to obtain an electrode that can detect glutamate at the typical biological concentrations. In 
consequence, the coating with a hydrogel containing the enzyme was proposed as the new potential solution.

For the glutamate biosensor, it was important to compare the effect of the mediator used (FCA or DHB), in 
the final CA detection. The current responses generated by both biosensors were recorded for increasing con-

on signal intensity, stability, sensitivity, and overall suitability for practical applications in the final biosensor. 
It is important to notice that from this point all the CA detections presented are carried at 0.5V, since no 
detection was possible at neither of the oxidation peak potentials for the mediators. 0.5V was selected after 
several rounds but further optimization could be carried to further reduce it.  
The results indicate that FCA consistently produces higher current responses across all concentrations. At the 
maximum tested concentration of 1 mM, the current reached approximately 9 µA for FCA, compared to 3 µA 
for DHB. This significant difference suggests that FCA exhibits superior electron transfer kinetics, enabling 
a more effective interaction between the enzyme and the electrode surface. Additionally, the signals obtained 
with FCA were more stable and well-defined, with clear stepwise increases corresponding to each analyte 
concentration. In contrast, the responses for DHB were weaker and less distinct, particularly at lower concen-
trations, which may limit its sensitivity and accuracy in detecting trace amounts of glutamate. 
FCA biosensor demonstrated a usable dynamic range between 0.01 mM and 1.0 mM, but the larger current 
increments and higher signal resolution provided by FCA make it more suitable for quantitative measure-
ments. DHB biosensor in contrast demonstrated a usable dynamic range slightly inferior, between 0.05mM 
and 1.0mM. These characteristics highlight the superior sensitivity and reliability of FCA as a mediator. 
In conclusion, FCA is the preferred mediator for this biosensor design due to its higher sensitivity, stable and 
reproducible signal, and superior performance over the tested dynamic range. Although DHB could serve as

5.5 
Study of interferences 
 
Figure 49. Study of interferences for the FCA-glutamate biosensor for common interfering substances in the CA detection. 
The x-axis shows the time in seconds while the y-axis shows the current intensity in µA. 
 
After the demonstration of the glutamate detection and that the biosensor is functional, a common experiment 
is the interference study. In this study, the objective is to determine the selectivity of the biosensor to determine 
for what range/family/kind of molecules does it react with or to specifically now if interfering substances 
present in the media can also interfere with the read-out of the results. For these reasons, glucose, ascorbic 
acid and dopamine were investigated as potential interference molecules, and added during CA at a final 
concentration of 1mM to see how the intensity of current would vary. The results are shown in Figure 49. 
From this data, it became obvious that dopamine and ascorbic acid did provoke a response (and 2 orders of 
magnitude higher) into the current intensity when added into the solution, while glucose did not cause any 
visible signal after the data analysis. The main reason why dopamine and ascorbic acid are reacting is due to 
the high potential that ended up being chosen for the chronoamperometric detection. Both analytes are elec-
troactive, which means that they do not need the intervention of an enzyme to undergo a redox reaction, and 
they can directly react with the electrode without the need of a mediator60,61.  
This problem should have been tackled when designing a second generation biosensor like this one, since the 
addition of the mediator allows for a lower potential to be applied into the media, reducing this kind of inter-
fering reactions to its minimum. Other analytes such as glucose also need the presence of an enzyme to un-
dergo a redox process, and since GLOx does not have specificity for glucose, no reaction is being detected 
after careful investigation of the data. That gives an idea that even if this biosensor cannot distinguish the 
signal if there is an electroactive compound present, it can serve to assess concentrations in highly pure sam-
ples and in the presence of other analytes that are not electroactive.  
However, the goal in this case should be to have a selective biosensor that only reacted when glutamate was 
present on the sample. Optimization should be performed on the fabrication process of these electrodes if the 
issue of selectivity wants to be addresses. One approach is optimizing the chitosan hydrogel coating. By 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
76 
 
increasing the hydrogel’s thickness or modifying its crosslinking density, the diffusion of unwanted electro-
active substances, such as dopamine, can be minimized, allowing glutamate to be detected more distinctly. 
Other possibility could be to explore different types of hydrogels or cryogels for the electrode coating, to 
select that that allows for better removal of interferences.  
Another key enhancement is further modifying the electrode surface. Functionalizing the electrode with con-
ductive materials or incorporating nanoparticles like gold or carbon nanotubes could improve the sensor’s 
performance by increasing the total surface area, allowing for a higher concentration of enzyme to be immo-
bilized on the sensor’s surface, and increasing the current response when adding the analyte, as well as reduc-
ing the native resistance of the electrode. The enzyme load is a critical parameter, since if there is not enough 
enzyme the signal would never be high enough to be detected with the potentiostat. Increasing the enzyme 
concentration during the incubation step and in the hydrogel, formulation could be studied as a way to increase 
the current intensity in the analyte’s presence. Additionally, using molecularly imprinted polymers (MIPs) 
that specifically bind glutamate could increase the sensor’s specificity, enabling it to detect glutamate while 
excluding other electroactive compounds, although the presence of GLOx on the electrode already fulfills the 
purpose of having a specific molecule that would bind to glutamate. 
Finally, implementing advanced signal processing algorithms, such as principal component analysis (PCA), 
could help isolate the glutamate signal from noise, further improving sensor accuracy in complex media. 
Combining these strategies can significantly improve the selectivity of the glutamate biosensor in the presence 
of interfering compounds like dopamine or ascorbic acid, and further optimization carried out to improve the 
overall performance of the glutamate biosensor.

biosensors, there is no evident decrease on the CA detection performance, since the magnitude of the step-

that involved extracting the calibration curve for the data points measured, the results are shown in Figure 51 
and Figure 52 for the FCA and DHB electrodes respectively. After refining the plot and eliminating those

inal (Eo) and after one-week (Et) electrodes respectively. From Figure 52 it can be concluded that the sensi-

The sensitivity of the FCA electrode might seem to have increased over one week, but this can just be a 
mathematical issue raised from the lack of replicates during this experiment. When comparing sensitivities

DHB electrodes, the sensitivity has changed significantly more than for the FCA electrodes, accounting for a

ously done of change the mediator in the protocol from DHB to FCA for the final glutamate biosensing ap-

to be more stable under the aforementioned conditions.

6. Conclusions 
The findings presented in this thesis highlights the potential of 3D printed pyrolytic carbon electrodes to suc-
cessfully overcome the traditional challenges of the more extended 2D biosensors. The tailored architecture 
of the 3D printed electrodes allows for fabrication of complex small structures that can enhance the interface 
between the media and electrode, and therefore increasing its electrochemical performance. This study 
achieved the main goals proposed at the beginning of the project by demonstrating repeatable and effective 
enzyme immobilization strategies that could enhance electron transfer efficiency and validating the biosen-
sor’s performance through electrochemical characterization. 
A key finding of this research is the optimization of the surface functionalization protocol, particularly the use 
of electro-oxidation methods to introduce amine and carboxylic groups tested at different tethering conditions 
and rendering the current optimum solution. These modifications enabled stable and reproducible attachment 
of biomolecules, such as glutamate oxidase or ferrocene carboxylic acid, facilitating reliable signal generation 
and detection. The study also confirmed that incorporating electron transfer mediators onto the modified elec-
trode surface significantly enhanced electrochemical signals. The comparative evaluation between 2D and 3D 
electrode configurations further emphasized the advantages of the latter, including a greater surface area, im-
proved charge transfer kinetics and the better integration with hydrogel matrices thanks to the patterning and 
complex structures created.  
However, several challenges were identified during the fabrication and optimization process, which require 
further attention in future work. The pyrolysis process, while central to achieving the desired material prop-
erties of the electrodes, introduced variability in electrode geometry due to deformation. This highlights the 
critical need for refining pyrolysis protocols to ensure dimensional consistency and structural integrity. Addi-
tionally, the exploration of different mediators and the proper selection during the fabrication process is es-
sential to ensure the sensitivity. From the mediators tested, FCA exhibited the better sensitivity and stability 
regarding the glutamate biosensing, but other mediators could be explored to improve the current performance 
metrics.   
The coating of the enzyme-immobilized electrode with a hydrogel allowed for the CA detection, becoming a 
requirement for the biosensor performance. However, to improve the selectivity of the biosensor, other gel 
matrixes should be explored with different electrochemical and size properties that would allow for interfer-
ence reductions.  
The findings presented in this thesis contribute to the growing topic of research on advanced biosensors, par-
ticularly in the context of integrating 3D-printed materials with biochemical biosensors. By addressing the 
remaining technical challenges and enhancing the fabrication process, the potential for developing high-per-
formance, reproducible, and stable 3D biosensors can be fully accomplished. These advancements will 
strengthen the foundation for continued innovation in this field and ensure the reliability of future biosensor 
designs for a variety of analytical applications.
