ABSTRACT
A sustainable Power-To-Gas (P2G) system can potentially contribute to the overall efﬁciency and ﬂexibility of
the energy system by providing storage solutions and distribution of energy to the end-use sectors. Reviews of a
comprehensive regional P2G facility integrated with an energy system optimization have not yet emerged. On this
background, the least-cost pathways for hydrogen production, by the selection of an optimal location and optimal
operation of a P2G facility was examined in this study. The Italian region of Puglia was chosen due to its high
solar potential and Italy’s incentives to become a leading hydrogen nation. Through a scenario analysis, synergy
between an on-site photovoltaic (PV) system, grid network and electrolyzer were tested to ﬁnd the pathways for
cost-efﬁcient hydrogen production. In this study, a P2G facility was created in the network optimization model
OptiFlow integrated into the Balmorel energy system model. Simultaneous simulations of the least-cost routes of
hydrogen production can give information of smart energy transition pathways. Also, the simulations points to
potential barriers that may diminish the prospective of hydrogen becoming the fuel of the future. Simulations of
the scenarios analysis of the optimal operation from the P2G facility showed that the system favors a ﬂexible
cooperation between the electrolyzer, on-site PV system and grid network. 99.94 % of the feedstock electricity
to the electrolyzer came from the on-site PV system. For a constant stream of hydrogen, selection of hydrogen
generation during PV load availability supplemented by storage options was preferred over a grid-supplied hydrogen
generation. The least-cost hydrogen production pathway resulted in a Levelized Cost Of Hydrogen (LCOH) of 48.72
C/MWh. Based on a more detailed analysis of location speciﬁc hydrogen production in Puglia and sensitivity of
the investment cost of the electrolyzer reveals that the hydrogen production expenses can further be diminished
through achieving a higher utilization factor of the electrolyzer and selecting locations with a higher abundance of
solar energy.

I. INTRODUCTION
A. Background
Mitigation of climate has to proceed in order to
obtain the Paris Agreement. The Paris Agreement
aims to sustain the global warming temperature under
a level of 2 °C rise, and desirably to 1.5 °C compared
with pre-industrial levels. In order to attain these
targets, emissions must be reduced to around 45
% by 2030, in the direction of not exceeding the
1.5 °C scenario [1]. Renewable energy has a great
potential for decarbonization of the energy sector, but
are limited to their ﬂuctuating and intermittent nature
[2]. Hydrogen can act as a buffer to increase energy
system ﬂexibility, and thereby bring together the gaps
between sectors and resilience for renewable energy
in a sustainable manner [3, 4]. Incentives towards
hydrogen can grant possibilities to reach the goal
of climate neutrality by 2050 and efforts to work
towards the Paris Agreement 1.5 °C scenario [4]. The
prospective of higher employment of hydrogen in the
energy system has attracted funds and interest in the
recent years, in addition to being an essential element
in the European Commission strategy for a climate-
neutral Europe [5]. Despite the interest and potential,
renewable (green) hydrogen and low-carbon (blue)
hydrogen are currently not cost competitive with
fossil-based (grey) hydrogen. Meaning that incentives
for the development of carbon-neutral hydrogen
as regulatory framework, investments, technology
breakthrough, infrastructure and markets needs growth
to enhance the demand of low-emission hydrogen, to
further obtain the climate targets [4].
To meet the climate goals, energy systems must
incorporate a higher share of renewable energy, and
shift away from conventional energy system relying
on fossil fuels [6]. Challenges with ﬂuctuating and
intermittent electricity from renewable energy have
only occurred in the local grid with a high allocation
of non-dispatchable energy sources. However, in a
future energy system with a seemingly higher share
of renewables, this can be a reoccurring issue [2].
Policies push for system integration of technologies
for a raise in the ﬂexibility and control, and grid
resilience, which are all factors a P2G system can
provide [7]. P2G utilizes electricity as input to produce
sustainable gaseous fuels. Hydrogen is one of these
gaseous fuels, produced through a one conversion
step of electricity and a water in the electrolyzer [8].
Conversion of electricity to gaseous fuel through P2G
will be further elaborated under section I.B Literature
overview.
Integration of P2G in an energy system can make
the energy system maintaining a continuous operation,
even in tandem with rapid changes in the supply
or demand, boosting the ﬂexibility of the energy
system [9]. This characteristic is an important feature
for the energy system, as P2G can function as a
buffer for renewable energy [1, 9, 10]. With an
energy system with an increasing rate of renewable
electricity, bottlenecks of the electricity can be handled
through P2G. Also, P2G can help provide energy at
times of scares amounts of energy [11]. Moreover,
seasonal
imbalances
caused
by
non-dispatchable
energy resources, calls for a higher demand for
intermittent storage capacities [12]. Hydrogen can
act as a bond binder between large-scale integration
of renewable energy, as it offers storage options by
converting the electricity to gaseous fuels through
water electrolysis [1]. Periods with oversupply of
variable renewable electricity can be stored for times
where energy is scarce or deﬁcit [12]. Thereby,
hydrogen can play a systematic role in the transition
from fossil fuels towards renewable energy sources,
creating a ﬂexible supply mechanism of energy
transfer across sectors [1].
Hydrogen technologies exist in segments of sectors
today like the industry-, chemical- and petroleum
reﬁnery sector. Furthermore, there is already potential
for an increase in the share of hydrogen in these
segments, and hydrogen has also the potential to be
introduced into other sectors such as transportation
and heating[1]. In 2019 the global hydrogen demand
was approximately 75 Million tons (Mt), primarily
produced
from
carbon-intensive
processes.
This
resulted in emission of 800 MtCO2 from hydrogen
production [6, 13]. Today the chemical industry and
the reﬁneries are generally linked with hydrogen
employment, where hydrogen production through
water electrolysis only accounts for approximately
0.1% of the generated hydrogen [13].
Regardless of the marginal production of green
hydrogen today, there is an growing interest in
utilizing hydrogen’s ability to act as a versatile,
clean, and ﬂexible energy carrier for the future
1
==PAGE:6==
energy transition, with falling prices in the renewable
energy market and increased interest in large-scale
hydrogen projects [1, 13]. With a growing maturity
of the P2G technologies, interests in investigating
systems economic viability and potential proﬁtability
are increasing [6]. However, the investment cost of
an electrolyzer plant has been one of the largest
barriers of the development of P2G project, leading to
numerous of smaller pilot projects and limited large-
scale P2G facilities. Hence, trends and fast expansion
of the P2G market, a rapid price cut in the upcoming
years can be expected [14].
Although hydrogen has a high potential, there are
several obstacles and challenges for implementation
in the energy system. Internationally, there have
been disagreements about hydrogen’s economical
potential and future role in the energy system. The
disagreements in the existing literature comes mainly
from variations in cost predictions, conﬁgurations of
the P2G facility and which prices and values are
taken into account for analyses [14]. Investments
in future energy systems, including production and
infrastructure, is often a demanding process with high
costs and long-term investments decisions. Due to
this, it is important with a broad perspective analysis
for a long time period that will cover all demands [15].
To obtain a cost-efﬁcient P2G system it is important
to take advantage of resource availability at a given a
location. PV technology has encountered an incredible
price drop during the last decade. Conversion of
solar irradiance into electricity through a photovoltaic
effect, can be utilized for electricity generation, and
accordingly is an attractive energy source for locations
with high solar abundance. A PV system conﬁguration
can be created by consistently arranging multiple
panels [16]. As the hourly solar insolation of a loca-
tion is hard to predict, a photovoltaic system could
potentially beneﬁt the utilization of a P2G system.
However, knowledge is still missing regarding key
measures for the integration of P2G facilities based
on photovoltaic power into the energy system. Herein
the feasibility of P2G facilities, optimal location, as
well an optimized operation of a P2G facility utiliz-
ing photovoltaic power in a local integrated energy
system. Integration of P2G in a local energy system
can provide carbon-neutral production of hydrogen,
which again can contribute to an increased interest in
green hydrogen as a fuel of the future.
B. Literature overview
Studies of P2G facilities and surrounding frame-
works can give valuable insight to model structure and
implement features for the energy system modeling.
The ﬁrst process in the conversion chain of a P2G
facility is hydrogen transformation from electricity
and water, conducted through an electrolysis process.
In a secondary conversion, hydrogen can be generated
to form other hydrocarbons or chemicals such as
methane or ammonia [2, 9]. Due to hydrogen’s
low energy density, conversion to more energy
dense substances can be favorable for e.g., storage
and transport applications. Thus, all transformations
require equipment, energy, and possibly reconversions
back to hydrogen, accordingly the transformations
needs to be emphasized [17].
The support of a ﬂexible system, such as P2G,
can provide incentives to increase the integration of
variable renewables into the power system. Thus, the
availability of low-cost green hydrogen is limited, as
well as competition from other ﬂexible generation
options pose challenges [17]. The uncertainty of P2G
production method lies in the technology and cost
developments [1]. In 2021, Kakoulaki et al. found
that the main parameters determining the costs of
hydrogen are the electricity price, investment cost
and electrolyzer utilization factor [18]. Even so, the
electrolyzer cost has decreased by 60 % in the last
10 years, and price is anticipated to be halved in
2030 in comparison with 2020 values [4, 18]. Further,
the price of green hydrogen fell 45 % from 2005 to
2020 [7]. These cost declining projections suggest
that renewable hydrogen may keep pace with the
grey hydrogen prices in the future, and European
Commission predicts a breakeven point by 2030 for
locations with low-cost renewable electricity [4].
Taking advantage of the surplus electricity from
an energy system can save the cost of electricity
feedstock for the electrolyzer. The degree of curtailed
power may rise due to the increased share of
non-dispatchable power generation in the energy
mixture [19]. In 2019, Tlili et al. analyzed the
potential hydrogen production from excess renewables
electricity production in 2035. Results showed that
hydrogen
production
in
an
energy
system
with
moderate penetration of renewable energy has a
marginal energy surplus, meaning limited electricity

for hydrogen production. Leading to results of a
low electrolyzer utilization factor, inducing a rise
in the cost of hydrogen production [20]. Parallel
results were found by Estermann in 2016. He reported
that a capture of the entire excess PV electricity
for P2G production was found to be uneconomical
[10]. The requirement of a large electrolyzer capacity
would operate less than 1% of the year on full load.
In 2021 Kakoulaki et al. found that to achieve an
efﬁcient and economic operation of electrolyzer, a
load factor above 30-40% is necessary to achieve
economic advantages [18]. Moreover, in 2019, El-
Emam et al. concluded that source availability and
uninterrupted energy supply are the main factors for
lowering of the hydrogen costs [6]. Results from these
analyses [6, 10, 18, 20], solely employing the excess
electricity in the system to produce hydrogen may not
be enough to unlock the future potential for hydrogen.
In the intermediate period, before variable renewable
energy takes a signiﬁcant share in the energy system,
production of hydrogen from merely surplus energy
may not be sufﬁcient enough to make hydrogen a key
fuel for the future energy system.
Towards making the cost of low-carbon hydrogen
competitive
with
other
ﬂexible
fuels,
incentives
towards raising the demand are necessary. Kakoulaki
et al. (2021) suggests that the future European Union
(EU) policies must focus on scaling the reﬁnery
industry with clean hydrogen in the short term from
2020 to 2030, in order to increase technology, market
maturity and to lower the economical barrier of
hydrogen. On the long term, in the years 2030 to 2050,
it is expected that hydrogen demand in other sectors
than reﬁneries has increased substantially. Thereby, a
pragmatic starting point for hydrogen development
is through creating a sustainable industrial value
chain. Moreover, also discoveries in Kakoulaki et al.
(2021) showed that 84 out of 109 European regions
have over 50 % excess renewable electricity after
covering electricity- and hydrogen demand. All the
hydrogen demand was generated through a water
electrolysis. The regions not sufﬁcient enough to
cover the electricity- and hydrogen demand, usually
had the characteristic of a dense population. However,
these demand coverages issues could be possible to
solve, if green hydrogen would be transmitted and
stored across regions and countries, using pipelines or
adapted existing gas infrastructure [18].
Feeding hydrogen or synthetic methane (methane
produced through Sabatier process from green hy-
drogen) into the gas network from a P2G facility,
could link the power grid with the gas distribution
system [2]. Blending natural gas and hydrogen has
been discussed as an option for the transition phase,
until the hydrogen infrastructure has been made.
Fragiacomo et al. (2020) studied this blend, where
up to 5% of the volume is injected with hydrogen
into the natural gas network. Results from their study
led to higher cost of the gaseous mixture, where the
higher the amount of hydrogen injection, the higher
the cost of the blend [21]. Also, mixing carbon-neutral
hydrogen with carbon-dense natural gas will diminish
the value of green hydrogen. Further, European
Commission found that it is more economical to store
hydrogen and transform it back to electricity through a
fuel cell in times of electricity deﬁcit, than to blending
natural gas with hydrogen in the gas system [4].
Infrastructure is necessary if hydrogen should
function as a link between supply and demand
in the energy system [4, 22]. Hydrogen can be
transported based on networks like pipelines or in
non-network transport such as trucks or ships [4].
Ultimately, a hydrogen distribution network consisting
of pure hydrogen would be transported either from old
natural gas networks or new hydrogen pipelines [1].
Converting natural gas pipelines for transportation of
pure hydrogen is being evaluated in the Netherlands,
Spain and United Kingdom [23]. Thus, the distribution
of hydrogen will vary with resources, hydrogen market
and will evolve as market and demands grow and the
technologies are further developed [24]. Moreover,
due to the current high costs of hydrogen and synthetic
methane, cost reductions are needed before these fuels
can become substitutes for the natural gas industry
[25].

the PV modules in four different orientations to obtain

One important driver for hydrogen- or hydrogen-
based fuel storage solutions is that they are less
expensive to store than electricity storage solutions.
With an increased share of renewable load in the
electricity grid, system power imbalances can become
a challenge, and bottle needs for long-term- and
seasonal storage options. For this purpose, P2G can
be utilized as it is one of few technologies that can
convert the oversupply of electricity in the energy
system into storage. Nevertheless, hydrogen storage
is still expensive compared to other storage options
on the market, as e.g., ﬂexible generation from gas
turbines [11, 13].
To achieve a optimal operation of a P2G plant,
Gahleitner (2013) evaluated 41 P2G pilot plants.
Gahleitner concluded that elements like location,

Revision of the literature overview of the subject,
showed that numerous reports only focused on
utilizing surplus variable renewable energy as input
for hydrogen production [6, 10, 18–20]. These system
conﬁgurations may be better when the share of non-
dispatchable power have increased signiﬁcantly in
the energy system. Accordingly, this type of P2G
operation system can become more relevant in the
future. Thus, in the short term, it may be more
applicable to utilize the P2G plant for a higher amount
of Full Load Hours (FHL) to make the electrolyzer
more cost-efﬁcient, as raising the utilization factor
of the electrolyzer might lower the investment costs.
Another factor that can generate substantial cost on the
P2G system, is the price of electricity [14]. Therefore,
the proﬁtability of a P2G system can depend on the
resources of the site, and following the electricity
prices of the location. Taking consideration of these
factor for the selection of the optimal location for a
P2G facility, may be essential for the production of
cost-efﬁcient hydrogen [2].
C. Aim of the study
A P2G system can potentially contribute to the
overall efﬁciency and balance of the energy system
with energy storage and transfer of green energy to
end-users. Although there are great expectations for
the future role of P2G, regional commercial P2G
projects have not yet emerged. A next step towards
ensuring economic feasibility of P2G projects is a
system optimization, herein a smart integration of
energy systems and sectors.
On the background of limited knowledge about lo-
cal integration of a P2G facility, a system optimization
model is built to estimate the least cost of a locally
integrated P2G facility. Before the model framework
was conducted, an evaluation of the optimal location
for a P2G facility was taken into account, with the
objective to choose a location that can enhance the
potential of hydrogen in the future (see section II-A
Optimal location.
Exploration and consideration of alternative routes
of production, storage, distribution, and utilization
application
are
taken
into
account
through
the
construction of a comprehensive P2G facility energy
system model. The network-based optimization model
used in this study integrated a regional P2G facility
in the national energy system (see section II-B3
OptiFlow model network). The resulting output of the
model were made to identify the most cost-efﬁcient
pathway or pathways, for hydrogen generation in the
future.
Further, in the present study, the goal was to
achieve a better understanding of the future energy
system of the selected location, and furthermore to
construct appropriate and prudent scenarios. Therefore
simulations without the integration of the P2G facility
were also measured (see section II-D Italian energy
system). Co-jointly with the future energy system

results, trends and dominant behavior of the energy
system were sought to be identiﬁed to create scenarios
of the P2G facilities operation, with the goal of

hydrogen

Accordingly, the intent of the study were to ﬁnd the
least-cost routes for hydrogen production, to assess the
energy transition pathway, and to identify obstacles
for the development of hydrogen becoming the as fuel
of the future.
II. METHODOLOGY
A. Optimal location
Among all possible P2G conﬁgurations, hydrogen
production is the simplest and most reliable, as it has
the highest technology readiness level. Accordingly,
only hydrogen production will be considered in this
report. Existing literature of hydrogen facilities are
comprehensive, thus the output results are commonly
inﬂuenced by the local framework [5]. Parameters
effecting
the
hydrogen
cost,
such
as
electricity
generation,
taxes,
regulations
and
subsidies
are
segments that can be inﬂuenced by the location of
the hydrogen production [6]. In the literature review,
numerous reports found that the source availability can
be an inﬂuential factor for hydrogen cost reduction
[6]. Ergo, the location of the locally integrated P2G
system for this study requires sufﬁcient supply of
electricity to meet both the initial electricity demand
and electricity feedstock for hydrogen production [18].
In this study, the Balmorel model was utilized.
The Balmorel model will further be elaborated under
section II-B Model framework. Balmorel is conﬁned
to the majority of the European countries (Austria,
Belgium, Czech Republic, Denmark, Estonia, Finland,
France, Germany, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Nether-
lands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Spain, Sweden,
Switzerland and United Kingdom). Following, the
geographical scope for selection of the optimal
location are within the countries given by Balmorel.
Resources are dependent on geography. Accord-
ingly, selection of an optimal location for hydrogen
production can be determining for the P2G systems
integrations proﬁtability [18]. For this study, solar
resources have been selected. The capacity and
operating proﬁle of the P2G will be inﬂuenced by
the variation and magnitude of solar power of the site
[10]. Accordingly, to achieve low hydrogen prices a
location with high solar resources are essential.
In 2016, Castillo et al. conducted a study of the
regional potential of solar energy in the EU. The
solar insolation of geographical location is taken into
account, in addition to the sites proximity to the
power grid, land use, urban extent and population

B. Model framework
Two models are used in this study to build a
comprehensive and coherent energy system model
for estimations of the impact of locally integrated
P2G facilities in the energy system. Simulation of
6
==PAGE:11==
the electricity- and heat balances are conducted in
Balmorel, while OptiFlow integrates a P2G energy
system simultaneously on a temporal resolution for
the region of Puglia. The linkage between Balmorel
and OptiFlow on both temporal and spatial resolution
makes it possible to sell commodities from Balmorel
to OptiFlow, or vise versa, seen in ﬁg. 3.
Fig. 3: Linkage between the energy system models
Balmorel and OptiFlow.
1) Balmorel
Balmorel is an open source partial equilibrium
energy system optimization model. With its energy
balance constrains, it secures that energy supply
equals demand at every time segment and in every
geographical entity. With a deterministic energy
system model, Balmorel assumes perfect foresight,
subjected
to
technical,
physical
and
regulatory
constraints. The objective of the model is to min-
imize the total cost of the energy system. The
cost optimization represents the sum of the system
costs including operation, investment, generation,
consumption, fuel, transmission, ﬁxed and variable
Operation and Maintenance (O&M) costs, taxes and
subsidies subtracted by consumers utility, based on
endogenous investments and decommissioning. The
Balmorel model can analyze the energy markets and
covers multiple production and storage technologies
due to its supply side bottom-up approach, which is
primarily centered around the electricity- and district
heating sector [34].
Balmorel provides marginal costs for investments,
production,
storage
and
transmission
levels
by
identifying the market clearing price of a perfect
competitive market, and thereby maximizing social
welfare. Due to the high ﬂexibility of temporal- and
spatial resolution, the model is adequate for energy
system analyses with a large share of ﬂuctuating
energy. These characteristics make Balmorel a suitable
model to use for this study’s objectives [35, 36].
Balmorels mathematical formulation and codes are
implemented in the modeling language GAMS. Given
Balmorels generic and extensible model structure, new
energy commodities can be included within the model
or as an addon [34].
2) OptiFlow
To further expand the ﬂexibility of the model and
analyse speciﬁc elements in the energy system model,
addon packages in Balmorel can be implemented. The
OptiFlow model was developed as an addon for the
Balmorel model to give a comprehensive analysis of
waste handling in the energy system, through a gen-
eralized spatiotemporal network optimization model
[34, 37]. Thus, for OptiFlows speciﬁc intentional
usage, the model has been utilized in Bramstoft et
al. (in 2020) and in Lester et al. (in 2020), to model
renewable gases through a P2G operation. Because
the OptiFlow model can be used for general system
integration analyses and has previously been applied
for other P2G studies, makes it a suitable model for
this study.
As Balmorel, OptiFlow also operates in a ﬂexible
spatial-
and
temporal
resolution,
simulating
the
optimal pathways within the network. The optimized
topological network design is driven by data input.
The goal of the model is to provide a more robust
solution from the energy system model that can
identify uncertain parameters [38].
For this study, OptiFlow is used to integrate a
P2G energy system to the Balmorel model, where
the OptiFlow model is centered around hydrogen
production. An integrated system analysis framework
allows to assess under which circumstances utilization
of hydrogen is favorable in the energy system,
especially in the case of increased hydrogen demand
in the future. The methodology used in this paper can
be utilized for other regions or areas where hydrogen
is expected to become an important component for
the future energy system.

3) OptiFlow model network
The model consists of networks made out of pro-

with a Nodes and Arc terminology [37]. The outline of
the ﬂow network in OptiFlow is built to identify and
discuss hydrogen’s potential role in the future, where
a P2G facility is locally integrated into the energy
system. Through data-driven input, a P2G network
is constructed in the OptiFlow model for this study.
Representations of the nodes and arcs for the model
structure are seen in ﬁg. 4.
Electricity feedstock to the electrolyzer for hy-
drogen production can be distributed through the
grid network, where electricity is purchased from
Balmorel, or through investments of on-site PV
systems providing electricity in OptiFlow. Likewise,
the electricity generated by the PV system can be
sold directly to Balmorel. Following the production
of hydrogen via electrolysis, hydrogen can be stored,
transported, or utilized on-site. Moreover, the transport
of hydrogen can take place directly after production
from the electrolysis or after storage. The fuel cell
can be used directly after the hydrogen generation or
storage, to then be converted back to electricity and
sold to the grid network. Excess heat production is
generated in the electrolysis and the fuel cell, and is
directly sold to Balmorel.
C. Model assumptions
The future potential for green hydrogen deployment
highly depends on the cost of hydrogen. The choices
of system and infrastructure of hydrogen application
can affect the expenses. Therefore, the assessment
based on the most cost-efﬁcient option for hydrogen
will vary based on renewable resources, transportation
distances, scale of production and request for hydro-
gen [17]. With the support of the energy systems
cost optimization models, the outcome of the models
will ﬁnd the most optimal and proﬁtable production

route for hydrogen in the future. However, in order to
make these socio-economic assessments, assumptions
must be made in the OptiFlow model. Hydrogen
roadmaps are employed as guidance for the future
energy system- and model assumptions.
The P2G facility has an on-site conﬁgurated
production, where the PV system, electrolyzer, storage
and fuel cell are assumed to be located at the same site,
shown in ﬁg. 4. Options for a P2G facility installation
in every province in Puglia are given.
To obtain high input ﬂexibility in form of feedstock
electricity to the electrolyzer, possibilities of purchas-

electricity feedstock are given [6, 11]. PV-generated

Electricity is bought and sold from electricity price
predictions, simulated based on the assumption of an
elastic demand in Balmorel. This resilience can lead to
an increase in the utilization factor for the electrolyzer,
which can further lower hydrogen production costs
[13].
The option of endogenous investments of a single
axis tracking system, a ﬁxed PV power system, or
both, is a decision that is estimated by the models for
every province. To provide an alternative for various
solar energy distributions throughout the day, an
estimated hourly Typical Meteorological Year (TMY)

As the current hydrogen infrastructure is minimal,
the European Commission predicts that the early phase
deployment of hydrogen will mainly sustain close
to the demand [4]. Subsequently, in the OptiFlow
model, hydrogen utilization after production and
storage will either be on-site or transported through
a newly invested hydrogen pipeline network, or via
compressed gas trucks. Another option is to take the
opportunity to invest in power stationery fuel cells for
hydrogen conversion to electricity and thereby sell
electricity back to the grid. Options between selling
the hydrogen to Balmorel or to cover the hydrogen
demand are given. The hydrogen market prices in
this model are based on analysis of hydrogen cost
evolution in Italy, speciﬁcally for hydrogen production
through the electrolysis (see Supplementary material
section VII-E Hydrogen selling price) [12].
Hydrogen is a low density gas at room temperature,
and its gas size needs to be diminished in order to
increase its functionality. Compression of hydrogen
takes place before transportation and storage pro-
cesses, and are taken into account in the investment
cost of the mentioned technologies. The energy needed
for compression are accounted for as an overall
reduction in the energy value chain of hydrogen [39].
The only capacity installation having an upper
bound is the PV system, because sun proﬁles are
needed for a given installation capacity. Based on the
current generation of energy in the region of Puglia,
and on future predictions of PV installations in Italy,
the model is limited to a maximum annual installation
of 4.59 GW solar PV in Puglia (see Supplementary
material section VII-A PV systems) [40].
Thus, all the other technologies have no capacity
installation constraint. Therefore, the model has the
liberty to select centralized or multiple decentralized
P2G facilities within the region of Puglia.
Adoption of technology improvements and learning
rates are given for every 5’th year, with a linear
learning rate between every 10’th year, provided

Supplementary material section VII-B Technology
properties) [16, 39, 41, 42]. The technology selection
for the electrolyzer is Alkaline electrolysis, Proton
Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis and Solid
Oxide Electrolysis Cell (SOEC) [2, 18].

IV. DISCUSSION
Changes in the energy system have to emerge in
order to accomplish climate-neutrality in Europe by
2050. Hydrogen can act as a buffer to continue the
increase of renewable energy in the energy system,
along with stabilizing the intermediate nature of
renewables and providing ﬂexibility to the energy
system [13]. However, green hydrogen is not cost
competitive with conventional fuels [29]. In this study,
local integration of a P2G facility in the energy system
were conducted through the least-cost optimization
models Balmorel and OptiFlow. The objectives of the
study was to identify the most cost-efﬁcient pathways
for hydrogen production. From the scenario analysis,
evaluation of the operation between an on-site PV
system, grid network and electrolyzer were made and
resulted in a least-cost LCOH of 48.72 C/MWh in
2050 for the region of Puglia.
Comparing the resulting LCOH of the optimal
operation with the hydrogen selling prices used in
the model from Snam, a price of 50.79 C/MWh was
predicted for 2050 [12]. The International Energy
Agengy (IEA), on the other hand, predicts a cost
16
==PAGE:21==
between 32.00 C/MWh and 81.26 C/MWh in 2060
for hydrogen produced from low carbon electricity
[43]. The resulting LCOH of this study falls right
below the middle value of the IEA predictions, and are
compatible in cost for the forecasted green hydrogen
prices provided by Snam [12, 43]. Similar ﬁndings
concerning the LCOH were found by Gallardo et
al. (2021) were they evaluated Alkaline and PEM
electrolysis for a P2G system, giving an output of the
LCOH of 54 C/MWh and 45.97 C/MWh, from PEM
and Alkaline respectively. Additionally, Gallardo et
al. (2021) found that investment costs of electrolyzer
was the main contributor to the LCOH costs [44].
Compared with current literature, the resulting LCOH
in this study is compatible with other future hydrogen
cost predictions [12, 43, 44].
Findings in the scenario analysis showed that
hydrogen production in the P2G facility is dependent
on the solar availability in the energy system. In Italy
in 2050, a penetration of 36 % PV in the electricity
generation in Italy in 2050 was found, and a tendency
of declining electricity prices when PV production
increased. Subsequently, the electricity prices only
exceed LCOH of the BASE scenario in the winter
week (S06). In view of this behavior, the deviations
in the results between the BASE case and the H2PD
scenario was found to be marginal. This led the
LCOH of the H2PD to be 0.07 C/MWh higher than
the BASE case. For H29-18 and H210-16 scenarios,
the goal was to utilize the PV characteristics and
market trends for the future energy system in Italy, in
addition to minimizing the cost of grid feedstock for
hydrogen production. This aim was not accomplished.
Limiting the time for hydrogen production, but yet
fulﬁlling the hydrogen demand, led to more than
double the amount of electricity purchased from the
grid network, compared with the BASE case. Despite
the low price of purchased electricity from the grid,
when acquiring a larger quantity of electricity, the
cost rised accordingly. Another goal of the H210-
16 scenario was to reduce the curtailed energy in
the system by simultaneously producing hydrogen
at low electricity market prices. Thus, a reduction
in the curtailed energy occurred, but H210-16 had
the highest resulting LCOH of the scenarios, due to
limited production hours of hydrogen.
The results from the LCOH showed that by
constraining the PV system and grid connection
for electricity input for hydrogen generation, the
proﬁtability of hydrogen production diminish. For
electricity input to the electrolyzer, the BASE scenario
had the lowest LCOH, and the highest degree of
freedom of cooperation between the PV system and
grid. The second lowest LCOH was found for the
H2PD, this scenario had limited constraints (only in
S06) due to the high LCOH in BASE case compared
with the electricity prices in Italy.
Results from the PV2H2 scenario compared with
the BASE case, showed that is more proﬁtable to
have synergies between PV electricity sold to the
grid co-jointly with utilizing the PV electricity for
hydrogen production. Employing all the PV electricity
for hydrogen production led to a system size increase,
thus a lowering in the utilization factor of the
electrolyzer. This accompanied an oversized P2G
system, and correspondingly a rise in the LCOH due
to high investment costs of the electrolyzer.
All the scenarios selected to predominantly produce
hydrogen when PV electricity is available. This
was chosen instead of minimizing the size of the
electrolyzer and make more use of the constantly
available electricity from the grid network. As a result,
the utilization factor for the electrolyzer is 22.7 %
for the BASE case. Thus, if more grid electricity
was utilized during hours where there was no PV
availability, the utilization factor of the electrolyzer
could rise. This could be a result of increase in the
electricity prices during periods of no PV load output.
Following this, utilizing storage options may be a
more proﬁtable application for the P2G system, than
taking advantage of the grid availability.
For simulations of the excessive models, Balmorel
and OptiFlow, the complexity of the temporal- and
spatial resolution had to be limited, in order to reduce
the computational time. Hence, this can lead to inter-
rupted ﬂows in the network. E.g., the storage facility
will only be utilized on a weekly basis and the inﬂow
to the storage will be equal the outﬂow of the storage
facility in the end of a given simulated week. This
is a result of a non-continuous temporal resolution
in the model. This was also the case for the ﬁnal
consumption of hydrogen, where a constant ﬂow only
exists on a weekly basis.
One of the limitations of the model was that the
data driven input of technology was updated every
ﬁfth year in the OptiFlow model. This makes it in
some cases more proﬁtable to invest in the same
17
==PAGE:22==
technology in OptiFlow, than in Balmorel, as the
expences are lower in OptiFlow. This was seen for
the PV system, which maximized its possible capacity
installment every simulated year. Implementation of
correlating technology cost in Balmorel and OptiFlow
could give a more interacting energy system between
the P2G facility in OptiFlow, and the Italian energy
system in Balmorel.
Working with a linear model, the possibility of
discrete investments are limited. This was seen for the
hydrogen pipeline system. As the hydrogen demand
increased annually in the model, the annual installa-
tion of the pipeline capacity grew simultaneously. For
further development of the model, the pipeline system
could be given as a discrete investment.
Moreover, the hydrogen pipeline is limited to the
region of Puglia. Expansion of the pipeline system
could result in export and import of hydrogen to the
region. Thus, the possibility of selling hydrogen in
the model was present, but was not utilized in any of
the scenarios. This was especially seen for the PV2H2
scenario where the hydrogen production is higher than
the demand. For all simulated hours, the model rather
convert hydrogen back to electricity through a fuel
cell and sell the electricity, in many cases, below the
cost of producing hydrogen, instead of selling the
hydrogen to Balmorel.
Further research is needed to assess whether the
LCOH lowers with a higher hydrogen production
in Puglia. In 2018, Weidner et al. found that by
upscaling the system size for hydrogen production,
the investment costs will decrease, as it would result
in a lower share of investment cost per produced unit
[45]. Additionally, expansion of the hydrogen pipeline
system may further strengthen the economy of scale,
as the export of hydrogen from Puglia could take
place.
Results from the model showed the importance of
both a marginal system size and utilization factor for
the electrolyzer. Further research is needed to attempt
to additionally increase the utilization factor of the
electrolyzer, as it can be of great value for further
cost reduction of hydrogen generation. Furthermore,
by utilizing the same energy resource as the energy
system is dominated by for on-site electricity input
to the electrolyzer in the P2G system, may limit
the utilization factor of the electrolyzer. The peak
electricity supply of the on-site PV system often
occurred at hours of an overloaded grid network with
low electricity prices. Exploring a blend of different
on-site renewable energy sources for the P2G system,
with the aim of expanding the availability of electricity
for on-site applications and increasing the utilization
factor, could give valuable insight.
V. CONCLUSION
The aim of the study was to determine if green
hydrogen has the potential to become the fuel of
the future through studies of smart integration of
a regional P2G facility into the energy system.
The region of Puglia in Italy was selected as the
optimal location for the energy system framework of
the P2G facility. Results within provinces in Puglia
illustrated support of higher hydrogen generation and
more proﬁtable hydrogen production, for regions with
higher solar potential. From a system perspective, if
there is a great share of solar power in the energy
generation mixture it is favored to operate hydrogen
production during sun hours. Thus, in only 18% of
the timespan in 2050, the LCOE for the on-site PV
system is higher than the market electricity price in
Italy in 2050. With a low LCOE cost for the PV
system, a hydrogen production during sunlight hours
together with a storage solution, is chosen over a
grid connected solution, to provide a constant ﬂow of
hydrogen.
For the operation of the P2G facility, interaction be-
tween the PV system, electricity grid and electrolyzer
were analyzed in ﬁve different scenarios. The LCOH
results for the operation of the P2G system showed
that the LCOH increased as the operation of the
electrolyzer became more constrained. Accordingly, it
is found that it is more favorable to have a P2G facility
with a ﬂexible operation between the PV-system,
grid network and the electrolyzer. Furthermore, it
is beneﬁcial that generated PV electricity can be sold
or used for hydrogen production whenever proﬁtable,
and that the electricity input from the grid is used
to sustain a high load of the electrolyzer when the
supply from PV is limited. Hence, the main feedstock
electricity to the electrolyzer is delivered by the PV
system. This shows that the P2G system favors an
on-site PV system input instead of a grid utilization.
18
==PAGE:23==
Further, studies of location within the regions showed
that the province with the highest load output from
the solar system also had the lowest LCOH compared
with the other provinces. This implicates that the
provinces with the highest solar resources are favored
for hydrogen production. Analysis of the scenarios
and sensitivity showed the importance of a high
utilization factor of the electrolyzer, to achieve a cost-
efﬁcient hydrogen production. The results showed that
increasing the utilization factor lowers the LCOH due
to a lower cost of electrolyzer installation per unit
hydrogen produced.
Taken together, the least-cost pathways for hydro-
gen production in this study was achieved through
ﬂexible cooperation between the electrolyzer, PV
system, and the grid network. Also, there was a
focus on location selection for abundant renewable
resources and increasing the utilization factor of the
electrolyzer. These ﬁndings can hopefully be valuable
for investment, operation and location decisions for
hydrogen production through a locally integrated P2G
system in the future.

VI. METHODOLOGY
A. LCOH
To evaluate the feasibility of the project, calculations of the LCOH is employed, expressing the unit cost of
hydrogen production in the model. This feasibility tool is applied as an indicator to compare the scenarios and the
economic status of hydrogen produced in the simulations. With the assumption of annuity investment payment of
present value, expressed as a Capital Recovery Factor (CRF) [46],
CRF =
r
1 −(1 + r)−n
The r notation is the discount rate and n is the number of the operational years. For the calculation, a discount
rate of 4 % is used, and the operational years will be the lifetime of the electrolyzer. The annuity payment is used
to express the investment cost I in an annual payment for a given period n, at present value. The other/remaining
costs M are given as annual payments, accordingly don’t have the necessity to be annualized. These costs represent
costs like operation costs, electricity cost, etc. For this report, the annual costs will be the grid tariff, electricity costs
and the O&M costs of the electrolyzer. After all the costs are summarized, the costs are divided by the quantity of
hydrogen produced, q. The LCOH is expressed as [46],
LCOE = I · CRF + M
q
All the costs are multiplied with an inﬂation index, with the assumption of 2% inﬂation rate, to get the output
LCOH in present value [46]. As parts of the electricity input feedstock to the electrolyzer, is the electricity generated
from the PV system. Accordingly, the PV systems LCOE is calculated, using the same methodology as for the
LCOH. The share of electricity going to electrolyzer from the PV system is multiplied by the LCOE of the PV
system and then added as a levelized cost to the LCOH.
VII. MODEL ASSUMPTIONS
A. PV system
To display a cohesive and realistic electricity output from the solar system, a solar load proﬁle for each province
is applied in the model. The selection of the PV load sites is taken from already existing locations of solar farms
in the provinces, shown as crosses in ﬁg. 12 [32]. The solar output is taken from the European Commissions
Photovoltaic Geographical Information System (PVGIS) to estimate the system production [47]. For the ﬁxed-tilt
system, optimal azimuth and tilt are selected, while for the single-axis tracking system the inclined axis system is
chosen, where the PV panel is rotating around its own axis. The solar proﬁle values are an average of the hourly
irradiance of the sites from 2005 to 2016, to make an approximate TMY hourly data.
The annual hourly average input irradiance for a 0.45 GWp ﬁxed-tilt and single-axis tracking PV system are
given in ﬁg. 10 and ﬁg. 11, respectively.

The PV system load from the two proﬁles shows that the ﬁxed-tilt system has a more narrow electricity distribution
than the single-axis tracking system. A PV system with a ﬁxed-tilt sustains a high load when the sun is located
perpendicular to the panels. The orientation of the ﬁxed slope panels is such that the sun is perpendicular to the
panels when solar radiation is at peak. Consequently, with a optimized south oriented panels and a ﬁxed-tilt of
34°, insolation from the sun will diminish when the angle between the sun and the panels are increasing. Resulting

The panels will rotate around its own axis, keeping the angle between the sun and the panels as small as possible.
Having this panel setup will result in higher power output compared to the static panel arrangement.

C. Hydrogen demand
The highest potential for hydrogen in Italy is in the transport, building and industrial sectors. In the coming
decades, there is expected to be an increase in the demand of low-carbon hydrogen in a variety of sectors. The
hydrogen sector expansion is driven by carbon-neutrality, and eventually low-carbon hydrogen can become cost-
competitive with fossil fuels due to the evolution of hydrogen technologies. As a result of these drivers, it is
forecasted that the demand for hydrogen will increase rapidly in the coming decades [12].
Predictions of the future hydrogen demand is an uncertain parameter as development of hydrogen as an energy
carrier in the future energy transition is debatable. Different and sometimes contrasting scenarios for the possible
development of a hydrogen economy are present in the current discussions of the energy transition. Also, the
literature shows these uncertainties, where prediction of hydrogen demand varies. However, reports from the
International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), IEA and European Commission FCH JU assumes roughly six
to seven fold increase in the hydrogen demand from today till 2050, with a lower growth rate from 2020 to 2030
compared with 2030 to 2050. However, for the ambitious scenarios in the report from IEA, IRENA and European
Commission FCH JU it is predicted that the hydrogen demand is between 20 % and 24 % of the ﬁnal energy demand
in 2050, considering both European and global levels [1, 13, 23]. Snam has forecasted the hydrogen demand in
Italy until 2050. These predictions is compatible with IRENA, IEA and European Commission FCH JU forecasts,
supporting the decision to use Snam’s national speciﬁc values as the data input for the hydrogen demand in the
OptiFlow model [12].
In the OptiFlow model, only green hydrogen is generated, thereby the hydrogen demand should be limited to
only electrolysis produced hydrogen for the data input in OptiFlow. Thus, Snam’s predictions are for all types of
hydrogen production. However, using IEA’s predicts of a 60 % electrolysis producing hydrogen in 2070, whereof
today only a share of approximately 0.1 % exists for the electrolysis produced hydrogen [13]. Assuming a linear
increase from today till 2050, the hydrogen generated through water electrolysis in 2030, 2040 and 2050 will
be 16%, 27% and 38 %, respectively, of the ﬁnal hydrogen production. Furthermore, Snam’s hydrogen demand
forecast is accounted for the entire nation of Italy, and must be divided by a fraction the provinces of Puglia, to

D. Hydrogen distribution
The transportation method for hydrogen can effect the ﬁnal cost of hydrogen, thus a suitable transportation option
depends on the location, distance and amount of hydrogen transported [17].
Because of hydrogens low energy density, hydrogen must be compressed or liqueﬁed before transport. Through
compression or liquefaction of hydrogen, a larger amount of quantity can be transferred through a smaller volume
26
==PAGE:31==
[13]. For road transport, the model has only the option to used pressurized hydrogen tank trucks. Reasoning the
signiﬁcant amount of energy liqueﬁed hydrogen requires for cooling to maintain hydrogen in a liquid state, while
the pressurized hydrogen gas above 200 bar can be stored at room temperature. Hydrogen ship transport could be
an option, however, in IEA it is found that for distances above 1500 km ship transport becomes the most feasible
option. As the analysis will only be for the region of Puglia, and none of the transport routes is above 1500 km,
ship transport is not considered in OptiFlow model [17].
The distribution of hydrogen in Puglia is predicted by the European Hydrogen Backbone, which plans to retroﬁt
the existing natural gas network to deliver pure hydrogen by 2040. Utilization of renewable energy resources
from the south of Italy are predicted to be used for hydrogen production, and then the hydrogen is transported
via pipelines to the industrial clusters in the north of Italy [5, 48]. Accordingly, the construction of hydrogen
pipelines and storage facilities across regions and countries serve possibilities of distributing hydrogen to areas
with low hydrogen production potential from sites with high hydrogen production potential. Moreover, this would
also improve the hydrogen availability and infrastructure [18]. Thus, in the OptiFlow model, only distribution
pipeline system will be considered, for regional deliveries in Puliga.
As mentioned, the delivery mode options in the OptiFlow model are hydrogen transported through a new hydrogen

IX. SCENARIOS
The scenario creation is based on ﬁndings from the literature review and the results from Balmorel without the
OptiFlow addon. From the literature review, demonstrations of P2G analysis frequently concludes that the investment
of the electrolyzer and feedstock electricity costs to the electrolyzer, dominates the cost of hydrogen production
[6, 10, 18, 20]. Commonly, the high investment costs were a result of an oversized electrolyzer. Thus, with declining
prices for solar PV, electrolyzer built at locations with excellent solar resources can beneﬁt from low cost electricity.
From the Balmorel results without the cooperation of the OptiFlow model, it was discovered that Italy’s future
energy system will be dominated by PV energy. A high share of variable renewable capacity in the energy system
can lead to a higher hydrogen potential, to give balance and ﬂexibility to the system. Instead of only running the
electrolyzer on peak supply from variable renewables, the scenarios will instead attempt to take advantage of the
market dependency of variable PV electricity. To increase the availability of the electricity input to the electrolysis,
the P2G system is grid connected. Accordingly, the scenarios will be based on how to optimally operate an on-site
P2G facility, where electricity is produced from a on-site PV system or supplied from the grid network as electricity
33
==PAGE:38==
input for the water electrolyzer.
The different scenarios look into if hydrogen production is favored in a ﬂexible or non-ﬂexible system. Examining
if operations synergy between hydrogen production and solar energy can function in a coherent matter that can make
hydrogen more proﬁtable. The scenarios takes both the ﬁndings from the Italian system’s results from Balmorel
and characteristics of the solar energy distribution. Utilizing the synergies between hydrogen and electricity from
both the PV system and the grid.
For the BASE case, no constraints are given for the operation between the PV system, grid network and
electrolyzer. Accordingly, the operation will be optimized based on the least-cost through the cooperation between
Balmorel and OptiFlow.
Results from Balmorel without the OptiFlow addon showed that PV has the highest share of generation in
the energy mixture in 2050. Examining if the impact of cooperation between hydrogen production and a grid
connected PV system to sell the PV electricity when not needed for hydrogen production, is a substantial feature
for a locally integrated P2G system. Moreover, as it is forecasted a high dominance of PV in the system, the impact
of implementing additional PV power to the grid may be marginal. Accordingly, the PV2H2 scenario utilizes all the
PV electricity generated for hydrogen production. The P2G facility is connected to the grid network at all hours,
in that way hydrogen can also be produced during hours PV electricity is not available.
Balmorel’s results gave that 80% of the curtailed energy arise from solar energy and 97% of the overall curtailed
electricity in 2050 emerge in the hours between 10:00 to 16:00, where 79 % is from PV electricity. The exploitation
of these surplus tendencies is used to determine whether hydrogen production is more beneﬁcial during these hours.
Both with the motivation of zero and low electricity prices as a result of oversupply in the system pushing the
prices down, but also minimize the load of the grid. The scenario H210-16 will only allow hydrogen production
from 10:00 to 16:00, where electricity feedstock can be given both from the PV system and the grid network.
From the Balmorel results without the Optiﬂow addon it was found that attributes to the future ﬂuctuations in
the electricity prices, are dominated by the variable electricity output from PV. Zero prices of electricity are found
when the PV output is at peak, and generally, increases and decreases according to the PV load throughout the
day. Hence, the tendency of duck curves is seen for the winter season, where the electricity prices rise signiﬁcantly
around sunrise and sunset, due to marginal or no PV load giving energy deﬁcit in the PV dominated energy
system (seen in ﬁg. 5). This electricity price trend will be taken advantage of in the H29-18 scenario. The scenario
will secure that hydrogen production will not take place during Balmorel’s predictions of energy scares in the
system, and rather sell the PV electricity to the grid. In addition, this can lead to an increased revenue for the PV
system. Accordingly, hydrogen production will only occur the hours there is PV load in the energy system, and
the electrolyzer is turned on at 9:00 and turned off at 18:00. With the two aims of only producing hydrogen when
market electricity prices are low, and minimize hydrogen production in hours of scarcity of load in the energy system.
Instead of having ﬁxed hours of hydrogen production, speciﬁc hourly determination of when hydrogen should
be produced and when the electrolyzer should be turned off. In the scenario price dependent hydrogen production,
PDH2, selection of hydrogen production hours is determined by Balmorel’s market electricity price predictions. At
times when the electricity prices are higher than a given threshold, no hydrogen production will take place. Thus,
when the electricity prices are lower than the given threshold, hydrogen production is permitted. The threshold in
the PD H2 scenario will be the LCOH of the base scenario. Accordingly, when the feedstock electricity cost is
higher than the cost of producing hydrogen, no production will take place in the P2G system.
