Summary
This paper explains the procedure, decision making and all working principle of produc-
tion line using several collaborative robots with adaptive safety limitations. Production
line goal is to perform operational test of the produced equipment, it involves two tests
”Impact test” and ”Flip test”. Impact test imitates the conditions of vehicle being hit
with maximum and minimum force, from several directions. Where flip test imitates the
condition of car being flipped. Both test equipment are dangerous and can harm any-
one nearby, thus the system will be handled with three collaborative robots, conveyor
system and linear drive for failed part handling. The system must guarantee safety in
accordance with European Union and will be equipped with devices that guarantee safe
environment for nearby personnel. Safety have several layers of complexion and different
reaction to nearby bodies and equipment. To verify if system operates in set boundaries,
simulation will be performed and validated if set goals are reached.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The changes are so profound that, from the perspective of human history, there has
never been a time of greater promise or potential peril. [Sch16] Are words said by Klaus
Schwab, German engineer who understood and seen the effect of world’s progress in
almost all aspects in everyone’s life. We are at a dawn of era of great change, from first
steam engine to first production line and first programmable logical controller. We are at
the doorstep of fourth industrial revolution, a revolution of how we see mass production,
how we design our systems and how we utilize today’s technology to reach our goals.
Today, and for upcoming year, we are at a process of building a brand new mass
production plant in Lithuania.
This plant will be producing automotive electronics
parts for vehicles that will be built in the future, cars like Audi, BMW, Mercedes-Benz,
Porsche, Volvo and so on, will be equipped with parts produced in this plant. Growing
demand and regulations regarding automotive safety enable us to produce vehicle parts
with top-quality and no room for faults or errors, especially, when human life might be
at risk. For this reason, we follow the philosophy that brought us Industry 4.0.
This work is a first example project of this plant that will reduce labour cost and
increase the quality of the work. In general, we aim to achieve a completely automated
production line, operated only by robotics and conveyors, this will be a great milestone
for this plant. This will show how this production line enable companies to make a
safe work environment for operators that work in close proximity and cooperation with
robotics.
In general, process it self is simple: test the parts that are being produced by this
production line. The tests has to determine if the part was produced properly and if
this part will trigger the safety measures that enable vehicles air-bag control units to
”explode” and save lives. These parts have to be separated, good parts will continue
onto next step in production and bad parts will be stored onto storing rack, for later to
be fixed and repeat the test. What makes it complex, is the individual components that
combined, make this system. They have to work in perfect unison.

1.1
Analysis of the task
Objectives of the system is to verify if parts were produced correctly and without errors
(this product produced will save lives, so quality must be exceptionally accurate), pro-
duce number of parts to meet the demand and to make equipment as safe as possible.
This, in correlation with company vision, gives us a number of goals:
1. Customer demand - to produce 30000 parts per year, so we need to produce one
part per 105s. This gives us time constraint to meet the demand.
2. Quality - automotive regulations to produce high quality product to achieve safety
and reliability.
3. Safety - to ensure safe working environment for the staff and achieve Industry 4.0
goals.
4. Data storage - save the results of each part that was tested.
5. Low maintenance - reduce the need for maintenance by selecting high quality
equipment and from high abundance components.
6. Easy calibration and adjustment - create system in simplistic and understandable
way, so if modifications would be necessary, adjustments would be fast. This also
is useful when time comes to recycle the system.
With these goals in mind, we can design our production line and each individual
system. Extra attention will be provided to safe working environment.
In following chapters, we will discuss the inner working of the part of one of produc-
tion lines being built. What choices did we get to make and how did we solve issues
that were present during engineering process.
1.2
System description and goals
This system, currently being built, will be producing air-bag control units for various
brands of automobiles. This paper will be focused on the engineering and process behind
”Functional test” of the production line. (Refer to Figure 1.1 Production line process
flow) From where we can see how many individual systems must be incorporated for one
product to be produced. In where some parts of the system will be a standard machinery
(like SMT production), so flexibility of these products is quite neglectable, which rises
major challenges in regards with integration, communication and mechanical parts for
the production line.

Brief description of systems mentioned in: 1.1 Production line process flow:
• SMT production - ”Surface-Mount Technology”, circuit board production. This

• Flash - in this step, machinery will upload the program on to circuit board.
• Coating - the final product will be mounted in area of a vehicle that will be
influenced by fluctuating humidity and temperatures, so it is required to cover
some circuit board elements with varnish for increased resistance to oxidation.
• Depaneling - process where four circuits will be separated into individual circuits
and data matrix printed on surface of each circuit.
• Assembly - here, the circuit boards will be combined with plastic shell covered
with adhesive.
• Leak/environment test - test where we strain each part to its environmental ex-
tremes, this test imitates cold/hot atmosphere and minimum and maximum hu-
midity.
• Functional test - main subject of this paper. This test imitates vehicle in a crash,
hitting a stationary object (of being hit while stationary) and being flipped over.
• Pin-inspection - process where each contact on the part is being optically inspected
and detect if there are any missing or bent pins.
• Packing - packing into boxes, where after, AGV (Autonomous Guided Vehicle)
picks the box up and delivers it to the warehouse.

1.5.2
Safety devices
With increased system automation, robotics and with high demand of parts to be pro-
duced, consistent motion and speed can harm the surrounding machinery or people. So
to avoid any danger of that, we have to follow strict rules of machine safety implemen-
tation directives from EU.
Today there exist number of smart devices and methods helping us to achieve com-
plete safety of the equipment. Devices like light curtains and proximity scanners:
1. Light curtains
These devices detect any breach into hazardous areas. They work by emitter-receiver
principle, both light curtains are quipped with laser emitter and light receiver. An basic
principle as shown below:
Figure 1.9: Light curtain [EzS].
Every year, this technology becomes more reliable and more complex, even today,
this device has resolution to detect objects as small as 1mm and react to it under 100µs.
2. Proximity scanner
This smart-device is rather new in industry, only few years ago these devices were not
reliable, as the would react to dust or fingerprints on the scanner surface. Great inno-
vations had solved these problems, and today it is on the same reliability level as light
curtains. It uses light emission and reflection principle using proximity sensors. Most

1.5.3
Programmable Logical Controller
Programmable Logical Controllers or PLC’s brought in the third industrial revolution,
these devices were used for many years, and they have improving up until today. Most of
the brands have to follow IEC 61131 standards for PLC programming, so reliability and
utilization would be improved. In regards to bigger demands to safety, manufacturers
of PLC’s started producing controllers equipped with increased reliability and controller
cycle time.
These unique PLC’s have several processors of which one is exclusively
dedicated to safety control. As such, we will be using this kind of PLC in this project.

2.2
Robotic arm
The system utilizes three robot arm manipulators to move the product to required
stations. These robots are equipped with pneumatic actuators for grippers, proximity
sensors for sensing the presence of object in the gripper. To completely utilize the robots
potential, we need to understand the concept of how robot moves, calculates trajectory
and what other physical factors we need to account for to make the system as stable
and reliable as possible.
2.2.1
Theory of robotics
Universal robot (UR), is a robot arm with six degrees of freedom, thus its position in
space is defined by X, Y, Z, RX, RY, RZ. These dimensions define robots end effectors
offset from its base and rotation around it. Position tracking can be achieved in many
various ways, equipping robot with encoders, calculation of position from dynamic model
with tools such as Kalman filter and so on. Although, most likely these methods will not
provide Cartesian coordinates for robots tool position. UR is equipped with absolute
encoders and this provides us with angular data: θ1, θ2, θ3, θ4, θ5, θ6 for each joint. To
translate this data into more understandable Cartesian coordinate system, we have to
==PAGE:32==
18
2 System Operation
utilize such forward kinematic methods as Denavit-Hartenberg matrix that define end
effector position only with angle of each joint.
Knowing end effector position in space as a reference point, how do we plan motion
for robot to reach desired position? In similar methods as forward kinematics, we use
inverse kinematics to define target coordinates, and using robot model, we calculate
the angle of each joint for end effector to reach goal described. These are the basics
of planing the trajectory, but in real world application we need to define constants as
gravity, end effector center of mass, its weight, joint limits, speeds and so on.
Although, UR does simplify the implementation of robotics and does not require
definition of above mentioned methods, it does posses some limitation that are important
to understand.
1. Singularity - is a physical barrier, a field in space around robots base where robot
will not operate. If robot tool enters this field, using previously mentioned methods,
controller calculates angles such that robot cannot reach. In other words, velocities
calculated by regulator reaches infinity and robot joints cannot keep up.
2. Linear and Joint movements - UR possesses two main methods of motion. Joint
movement, where trajectory is calculated in single iteration, where command from
controller is to reach angles of each joint without regard to end effector travel path,
with this method movement is much faster. Linear, where trajectory is calculated
in a straight line, this method is slow because controller iterates the coordinates
to keep a straight movement of end effector, basically, it is a collection of short
joint movements. Limitation is when defining two target points, joint movement
can jerk in a way that can be dangerous and harmful.
3. Weight of end effector - we need to calculate exact weight of robot tool with and
without a payload, the dynamics wary with different setting.
4. Cycle time - UR cycle time is rather slow for motion, it is exactly 125Hz, so
operations requiring fast motion response (like safety), must be accounted for.

2.3.1
Conveyor
Conveyor in the system is designed in a way so that carriers would be placed on two
sliding belts. Belts are moving with help of DC motor and motor controller, although,
speed of motor can be controlled. Best speed was adjusted for proper carrier velocity.
Carriers are stopped when pneumatic actuator stops them at corresponding points in
the line. These points can be seen in simulation on Chapter 3.
As for control algorithm (Figure 2.9), when carrier arrives from previous production
step, it is stopped by same actuator mentioned and its presence is detected with prox-
imity sensor. This was the trigger to continue the algorithm, laser proximity scanner
detects the presence of port on the carrier. If it detects the part, 2D matrix code reader
is triggered and the code is scanned. In some cases, this code cannot be read because of
damage or bad printing, in such case, we repeat code scanning 5 times to prevent any
false-positives. Ether way, if part was detected and code read, system send signal to the
robot to move the part to the next step and if it was rejected, robot will place it in bad
part buffer. In cases that carrier came empty, this happened when part was scrapped
in previous steps, the system will move the conveyor to the end where it will wait for
parts to be placed and move on to the next step.

Buffer
Bad part buffer is designed to move up and down for easier reach for the robot. It
is equipped with servo motor and controller, so its position is always known. When
the system gets a bad part, and when it is time to place it into the buffer, the system
correlates internal bad part counter and sensor information from the buffer, each slot is
equipped with one. This is due to easy access to the parts, operator can easily remove
the bad part and influence the statistics of how many there are rejected parts. If we
correlate, statistics stay the same and we know that slot is free. Each line can hold up
to 8 parts, so when we know the number of bad parts, variable p, we check “modulus”
of the variable, so if we have 8 rejected parts, we have to move the buffer by one level.
We check 8%8 result would be 0 (in other case 9%8=1, 10%8=2 and so on), so this
mathematical model gives us a useful new variable, telling us where new line is, but
also the slot that robot should place the part. We can see how it is integrated into the
algorithm.

Machine vision
Each part that is being produced in a system has a unique 2D matrix code laser-printed
on its body. This code is unique identifier for the part and it generates several challenges
that need to be solved for the final product to be accepted.
Firs, the quality of the 2D matrix code. There exists a standard (ISO 16022) that
defines the quality of all barcode based prints. Following it, we know that there exist
A to E quality barcodes, A being the highest quality and E the lowest, a comparison
can be seen in the Figure 2.11. This influences how we design our system. Printing
and releasing system with highest quality 2D matrix is not a difficult task, the challenge
appears when the buyer of the produced part cannot read the matrix code, resulting in
rejected part at customer side. For this issue, we have asked the supplier to provide a
”perfect sample”, a part that they produce on their own in laboratory, to fit all of their
requirements.

2.4.2
Traceability system
Traceability, or ”Manufacturing Execution System” (MES) is a method to record all the
steps that were taken to manufacture a single part. This is one of the points of optimiza-
tion of manufacturing process, each part produced will contain information like: each
single component batch, results of the tests and date and time of each process that was
taken. This methodology enables the factory to trace all the processes, equipment and
if some parts were to fail, see in which step they failed and prevent further failures. This
system is equipped with MES function. Here, before testing takes place, we scan parts
2D code which retrieves all the information from the server via TCP/IP communication.
This information consists of variables for the tests to be performed and if the part is
valid for it. After the tests were performed, system records all the results and when
the part is placed back into the conveyor, sends this information to the server. In here,
TCP/IP client is the PLC and server is a dedicated PC to recording all the information.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
Today, most of routine occupations, such as pick and place, optical inspections and so
on are replaced with automation, robotics and other smart solutions. This revolution
produces less waste, as less mistakes are made while in massive production. Not to
mention, that it also prevent any harmful illness that comes with overloading operator
with routine tasks over long time period. This production line is and excellent example
how a lot of tasks needs to be performed and how automation is used to save space.
Production lines like these, are a huge step towards completely automated factory, what
is the goal of 4’th industrial revolution.
The system contains many individual and complex components, from basic sensors,
actuators to robots, drives, scanners and controllers. Every component needs to work
properly and without faults for the system to behave as predicted. With this many
components, system complexity rises many times, but it also guarantees absolute safety
to the surrounding equipment and operators.
Currently, this system is in development state and it is around 90% done. Simula-
tion helped us to prove that the system will be keeping up with the demand, and if
required, could work with greater load if required. While at the beginning of the de-
velopment, variations such as more concentrated production line, where all equipment
would be stored in a smaller area were suggested. These ideas were withdrawn because
maintenance work would be much harder to perform, thus it was chosen that the current
production line design was the most optimal.
While this production line is not yet done, we foresee that when in production, some
modifications and optimization might be required. It is hard to predict what problems
might appear, but with observing the system and data, we might be able to reduce cycle
time. This will be important if this production line produce more parts in previous steps,
thus clogging the system. This is a small chance of that, but it must be considered non
the less. After this specific product will finish its production (this will be after 5-7 years),
the system will be recycled into a new system, depending on what is required at the
time. Around 80% of the components can be used again. Robots, axes, scanners and
PLC can be reprogrammed and with a proper maintenance plan will be used for many
years to come.
