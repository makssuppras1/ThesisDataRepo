ABSTRACT
D
istrict cooling is an interesting area since the demand for cooling around the globe
is increasing and according to the European Union, by 2030 the energy used to cool
buildings across the Europe is likely to increase by 72%, while the energy used to heat
the buildings will fall by 30%. On a concern that the waste heat energy produced during the
summer period is cheap increases the potential for cooling by utilizing that heat energy as
source through the process known as absorption cooling and this is in other also minimizes
the CO2 emissions. This thesis project is based on the analysis and feasibility of the absorption
chiller using the district heating as a heat source to sufﬁce the estimated cooling demand in
Copenhagen.
An EES model is developed to investigate the technical and economical feasibility of the single-
effect water/Li-Br absorption chiller (ABC) to adequate the cooling demand. Similarly, a vapour
compression chiller (VCR) model is made using the Pack Pro software to have comparison
with the absorption chiller to decide upon the most economically feasible technology for the
chosen region of Copenhagen. In addition, the different optimized conﬁgurations of absorption
chiller are modelled and accessed from an economic perspective.
The simulation of the model in both the cases, absorption chiller and vapour compression
chiller considers only summer (June, July and August) as the operational period. In economical
analysis of the system, the Annual Capital Cost (ACC) is calculated based on the peak load
capacity of two technologies whereas, the Annual Operating Cost (AOC) consider the hourly
cooling load proﬁle.
The Co-efﬁcient of Performance (COP) for the base case absorption chiller is found to be 0.724
for a Total Annual Cost (TAC) of the system at 319098.38 EUR to meet the estimated cooling
demand of 5910 kW. The vapour compression chiller out performs the absorption chiller with
the COP of 3.77 and at a TAC of 271776.65 EUR. The optimized cases scenarios for 100/90,
100/80, 100/70 and 100/60 yields the COP of 0.729, 0.740, 0.756 and 0.790 respectively at
the TAC of 295886.93 EUR, 305717.40 EUR, 322652.86EUR and 358242.75 EUR respectively.
Even upon cost optimization of the absorption chiller conﬁguration, economical beneﬁt of
vapour compression is still higher than all absorption chiller systems at average heat price of
41 DKK/GJ for the year 2018. With reference to the future heat prices, the optimized 100/90
absorption chiller conﬁguration will be a promising cool technique among other conﬁgurations
and have a better economical feasibility than the vapour compression chiller.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
I
n the current century we are facing the biggest environmental challenge our species has
ever seen. No matter what we are passionate about, something we care about will be
affected by climate change [1]. The Fifth Assessment Report from the Intergovernmental
Panel on Climate Change (Working Group I) states that human inﬂuence on the climate system
is clear [2]. Among the many human activities that produce greenhouse gases, the use of energy
represents by far the largest source of emissions. Smaller shares correspond to agriculture,
producing mainly CH4 and N2O from domestic livestock and rice cultivation, and to industrial
processes not related to energy, producing mainly ﬂuorinated gases and N2O as shown in
Figure 1.1 [3].

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
With regard to the European Union, heating and cooling for the buildings and industry
accounts for half of the energy consumption. In EU households, heating and hot water alone
account for 79% of total ﬁnal energy use (192.5 Mtoe). Cooling is a fairly small share of total
ﬁnal energy use, but demand from households and businesses such as the food industry rises
during the summer months. This trend is also linked to climate change and temperature rises.
In industry, 70.6% of energy consumption (193.6 Mtoe) was used for space and industrial
process heating, 26.7% (73.3 Mtoe) for lighting and electrical processes such as machine motors,
and 2.7% (7.2 Mtoe) for cooling [4]. About 84% of heating and cooling is still generated from
fossil fuels while only 16% is generated from renewable energy. In order to fulﬁll the EU’s
climate and energy goals [5], the heating and cooling sector must sharply reduce its energy
consumption and cut its use of fossil fuels [4].
Currently, the demand for heating both in the households and industry outweighs the demand
for cooling. However, in future the demand for cooling will rise due to the increasing necessity
for air-conditioning/refrigeration of food and medical supplies. For example, according to
the EU, by 2030 the energy used to cool buildings across Europe is likely to increase by 72%,
while the energy used for heating buildings will fall by 30% [6]. Globally, energy demand for
heating is projected to increase until 2030 and then stabilize. It is estimated that by about 2060
the amount of energy used worldwide in cooling will overtake that used in heating [7].
The need for cooling in Denmark is inherently smaller when compared to other countries with
warmer and humid weather conditions. Though, the new building regulations are being a
hurdle for the growth of refrigeration industry, the requirements for indoor climate comfort
and growing machine parks in ofﬁces might increase the demand for cooling because the
energy utilized for cooling also simultaneously increases. As far as the future construction is
concerned, the building regulation requirements for the total energy supply encourages the
builder to seek alternative solutions for reducing the cooling demand. However, the theoretical
cooling requirement may grow due to the conditions which include [8],
• Economic growth which allows greater leeway to spend money on comfort in terms of
improved indoor climate.
• Behavioural changes: "When the neighbour has the air-conditioner we will also have it".
• Increased internal heat dissipation from computers, electrical and electronic devices, etc.
Although, this is partly counteracted by energy savings.
A new report investigating the potential for district cooling in Denmark concludes that it could
save the society 10 billion Danish Kroner (1.5 billion Euros) to implement district cooling of
4200 GWh cooling energy and 2.4 GW cooling capacity [9].

1.1. MOTIVATION
The Cooling Plan shows signiﬁcant large-scale beneﬁts of district cooling in typical industry
areas which reduce the overall investment needs in cooling capacity. Storage of both heating
and cooling can optimize the co-production of cooling and heating to the ﬂuctuating electricity
prices and the value of the excess heat. This way, district cooling gives a signiﬁcant contribution
to energy efﬁciency in buildings and in district heating, said Anders Dyrelund, Senior Market
Manager at Ramboll Energy and main author of the ”Køleplan Danmark 2016” (Cooling Plan
Denmark 2016) [10].
Adding to this, an enormous amount of surplus heat from power production is commonly
wasted. This energy resource is increasingly being integrated into District heating systems and
used for heating and cooling purposes in buildings [11]. Moreover, the cost of heat in the sum-
mer is cheap, thereby increasing the potential for district cooling market during the summer.
The waste heat energy during these summer days can also be used for cooling through the
process known as absorption cooling and whose utilization CO2 emissions as well.
In the case of District cooling, the demand can be met by using the following combinations of
the technology,
• Free cooling and traditional cooling compressors where the excess heat is discarded.
• Heat pump driven by excess heat.
• Absorption chillers driven by excess heat.
This project is based on the analysis and feasibility of the absorption chillers within the chosen
region, Copenhagen. The absorption technology is driven by heat instead of electricity as in
the case of vapour compression cycle. This means the power consumption is only limited
to the circulation pumps and therefore the electrical energy invested is minimal. In turn the
absorption technology requires large amount of low-cost heat at a high temperature to be
economically feasible and also it requires additional resources for cooling such as sea water,
ports, etc., The COP of the absorption chillers typically varies between 0.7 and 1.2 [10].
In an energy-efﬁciency competition, motor driven vapour compression chillers will always
stands better than absorption chiller. However, there are other speciﬁc applications where
absorption chillers will have a substantial advantage over vapour compression as listed below
[12],
• For a facility that has a co-generation power plant or other process generating excess
thermal energy, absorption chillers can utilize it to produce chilled water instead of all
the excess thermal energy being wasted.
3

• For a facility that has inadequate electrical infrastructure or bringing electrical infras-
tructure to the facility is cost prohibitive, absorption chillers have a substantially lower
electrical power requirement than motor driven vapor compression chillers.
• For a facility with high electrical power cost and low fuel cost, absorption chillers may
have a lower operating cost than motor driven vapor compression chillers.
• For a facility that requires substantial system reliability, the lower electrical requirements
for absorption chillers will reduce emergency generator load requirements.
• For a facility that has high electrical demand charges, absorption chillers can be used as
part of a peak shaving strategy.
• For a facility that has very low acoustical and/or vibration requirements, absorption
chillers have lower noise and vibration generation than motor driven vapor compression
chillers.
1.1.1
Problem Statement
The overall problem statement of the thesis is correlated as follows:
How well the District heating network can serve as a good source for District cooling during the
summer period (June, July and August) in Copenhagen, when there is a availability of heat at cheap
price and requirement for cooling ? Also, is it technically and economically feasible when compared to
the traditional way of cooling, Vapour Compression cycle at stand alone condition?
The above summary of problem statement will be answered based on the following sub-
questions,
• Would the chosen technology for district cooling, absorption chiller based on Water/Li-Br techni-
cally feasible to sufﬁce the cooling demand with limitations in the input variables?
• What would be the overall cost of the feasible absorption chiller system and can it be cheaper than
the traditional way of cooling, Vapour compression cycle?
• Which optimized conﬁguration of the absorption chiller would be more beneﬁcial in terms of
investment?
• Can the absorption chiller conﬁguration with the lowest investment be a replacement for the
vapour compression cycle?
• If so, can the system sustain in the future, keeping the price of heat for upcoming years into
account?

1.2. APPROACH AND THESIS STRUCTURE
1.2
Approach and Thesis structure
This project is based on the Top-Down approach to determine the optimal and feasible design
of the absorption chillers (ABC). So, it starts from the overall cooling demand followed by the
design of each components. As result, the respective UAk value of components, the mass ﬂows
within the cycle, mass ﬂow at cold and hot sink (district heating network) are determined.
Then the cost analysis based on the obtained UAk for the system is calculated to compare with
the traditional method, Vapour compression cycle. Then optimization of the base case and
other system conﬁguration (by changing input variables) are held to ﬁnd the best combination
on the grounds of investment. Both the technical and economical validation is based on the
peak load design condition.
The evaluation of the system only considers the system operation during the summer period
when there is availability of cheaper waste heat and it also makes more sense when a region
like Copenhagen is taken into consideration. Since the demand for heating outweighs the

1.3.2
Absorption chiller - single stage Water/Li-Br cycle
The single stage Water/Li-Br Absorption chiller uses water as a refrigerant and Li-Br as an
absorbent. The basic components which are used in the case of single stage Water/Li-Br cycle
are Condenser, Expansion valve, Evaporator, Absorber, Solution Heat exchanger and Desorber.
Evaporator: The evaporator is where the liquid refrigerant removes the heat energy from the
circulated water to meet the cooling demand. The refrigerant evaporates by removing the heat
and leaves the evaporator towards the Absorber. In general, the evaporation temperature for
the Water/Li-Br cycle will be around 5 to 10 °C [19].
Absorber: In the absorption cycle, compressing refrigerant vapour is achieved by the absorber,
desorber and solution pump, in combination. The vaporized refrigerant coming out of the
evaporator enters into the absorber and get absorbed into a strong Li-Br solution and the
absorption process needs to dissipate the heat to the ambient through the circulating water
in the absorber. After absorbing the refrigerant (water), the Li-Br solution becomes weak and
pumped to the desorber through the solution heat exchanger.
Solution heat exchanger: The solution heat exchanger is a heat recovery heat exchanger which
is utilized to transfer the heat from the strong Li-Br (high concentration) to the weak Li-Br
9
==PAGE:24==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
(low concentration). The COP of the cycle is inﬂuenced through the effect on the solution heat
exchanger heat transfer rate.
Desorber: The weak Li-Br solution entering into the desorber gets heated by steam or hot
water from the heat source and the liquid refrigerant vaporizes and travels to the condenser.
Then the concentrated Li-Br solution (strong solution) ﬂows down to the absorber via solution
heat exchanger.
Condenser: It is where the cold water from outside source is circulated in order to condense
the vapour state refrigerant (water) to liquid state and the heat absorbed is rejected to the
ambient. The condensed refrigerant (water) ﬂows through an expansion valve, where the
pressure and temperature of the refrigerant is reduced and fed into the evaporator thereby
completing the absorption cycle. Usually, the circulating water source in the condenser will be
from the sea depends on the availability with respect to the location of installation.
The conventional chillers (Vapour compression chillers) use mechanical energy (from electrical
energy) in a compression process of refrigeration, while the absorption chiller primarily uses
thermal energy and limited mechanical energy for pumping. Hence, the advantage of the
absorption chiller over the conventional Vapour compression chiller are that they consume
very little electricity and they can be used for waste heat recovery applications having few
moving parts which leads to low noise and vibration levels. Also, they do not emit ozone
depleting substances. [19]
In general the Li-Br absorption chiller can be classiﬁed into single-effect, double-effect and
triple-effect cycle and each has its own capacity characteristics whereas, the components which
are used are same for all but, the number of individual components in a cycle differ. The overall

The pump work is quite small when compared to the heat transfer associated to the other
components in the system. Thus, it can be assumed that the pump work is negligible for the
single-effect water/lithium bromide cycle. Though from the thermodynamic point of view the
work of the pump can be calculated using the following energy balance equation,
W = m[2] ∗[h2] −m[1] ∗[h1]
(2.67)
The nature of the salt solutions like Li-Br is that the salt component precipitates when the
mass fraction exceeds the solubility limit. The solubility limit is a strong function of mass
fraction and temperature and a weak function of pressure. Once crystals begin to form, the
crystals themselves provide favourable nucleation sites and the crystals grow on themselves.
The phenomenon of the precipitation can be readily observed when the mass fraction of the
solution is at 0.70 of Li-Br. The solid precipitate tends to cling to piping components and if,
conditions are allowed to persist, the precipitate can completely clog the ﬂowing system and
stop the ﬂow [13].
Crystallization being a critical phenomenon to make the system run and so, a function to
denote a warning message is used in the EES model as,
Tcr = LiBrSSCTCryst(xhc)
(2.68)
The above function deﬁnes the crystallization temperature and it limits the mass fraction of
the Li-Br solution (xhc) in the range of 48.47% to 71.91%. The critical temperature varies from
-50 °C to 100 °C [35].

DISCUSSION
The base case (100/90 - without optimization) conditions for the absorption is made to under-
stand how the system works with the input variables and so a better foundation is derived,
upon which a cost effective system can be built on the required and available conditions of the
chosen region, Copenhagen. The results show that the nominal working conditions satisfy the
demand and obey the thermodynamic law at any state of point. The VCR technology at stand
alone condition is majorly modelled to have an economic comparison with ABC.
Starting with the performance of each chiller type, the VCR has a higher COP of 3.77 whereas
for ABC (base case scenario) the COP is 0.724, though it is higher for a single-effect water/Li-Br
absorption chiller. In both the cases the cooling demand has been always satisﬁed (100% load
fulﬁllment). This proves that both the models can be installed to meet the cooling demand on
the grounds of technical feasibility.
With regard to the cost incurred for the cooling technologies, the TAC for the ABC is higher
than that of the VCR. The ACC and AOC which together contributes to the TAC of the system
are 63% and 37% respectively for both ABC and VCR chillers. Only the internal split-up for
the AOC varies in each case. In ABC chillers the cost of heat, Cheat contributes to 29% of TAC
and for the VCR, it is the maintenance cost which contributes to 19% of VCR’s TAC followed
closely by the cost for electricity, Celec at 16%.
It can be observed that the VCR has a better performance and economical output when com-
pared to the base case VCR system for the same cooling requirement, though it has to be
realized that the model of base case ABC is not optimized for its best output conditions. Hence,
the input variables which affect the heat exchanger area, Ak are optimized for the best econom-
ical output of the system since the area, Ak is directly related to the cost function of the system
64
==PAGE:79==
and also the return temperature of the heat source from the desorber is reduced to undertake
the sensitivity analysis and see its corresponding impact on the performance, COP and majorly
focused on the economic beneﬁt from the new conﬁguration. The input variables for each do
not behave the same way for an optimized result as the operating temperatures inside the ABC
cycle are highly dependent on the input variables and should strictly obey the thermodynamic
laws at any state of point.
In the optimized condition for the ABC case of 100/90, the COP of cycle has been increased
from 0.724 to 0.729 and there is greater impact in the TAC of the newly optimized base case,
directly cutting the cost up to 7.2% from the unoptimized case. The other case scenarios:
100/80, 100/70 and 100/60 have increased performance of the cycle to 0.740, 0.756 and 0.790
respectively whereas they become economically deﬁcit as the overall TAC of the case scenarios
increase than that of the optimized ABC base case. Even upon optimization of ABC case
scenarios, the VCR out performs all the ABC cases with a lower TAC. This is majorly due to
the lower electricity consumption by the VCR and higher heat price for the ABC case. Both of
these cost variables are volatile to the ambient temperature/weather conditions.
The ABC technology can be advantageous over VCR on a condition that the average heat price
of each case scenario should be less than the price depicted in Figure 3.32. Then these prices
can compared to the actual trend for heat price in upcoming years until 2040 as stated in Table
1.1 and so the feasible conﬁguration for the ABC technology can be determined. The optimized
base case scenario: 100/90 is the most promising and viable system in future as the minimum
heat price is 30 DKK/GJ which is higher than the heat prices in future. The Case 1 scenario:
100/80 is feasible only in 2025 or from 2040. Whereas, the other case scenarios, Case 2 and Case
3 are not feasible as they require very less heat prices when compared to the actual future heat
prices in the market.
There is a chance where Case 2 and Case 3 can come into prevalence if there is increase in the
efﬁciency of the power plant or new technological improvement in future that brings down
the price of heat less than the margin price of both the cases. Thus, it will have a direct impact
on the Cheat which is the only volatile price of the system as the ACC and others cost variables
are non-volatile as they are equipment and construction costs. Another non-reliant factor that
can make the optimized Case 2 and 3 feasible in future is the electricity cost, Celec incurred for
the VCR cycle. The Celec will always be ﬂuctuating with the ambient temperature and in the
case of Copenhagen, the peak ambient temperature from the VCR cycle is just 17 to 18 °C as
depicted in Figure 3.8 modelled in the Pack Pro [27]. But in real case/in future the ambient
temperature might be even higher and also the price of electricity has a direct impact on Celec
which imposes more strain in the cost function of VCR system.

C H A P T E R
5
CONCLUSION
The initial modelling of base case single-effect Li-Br absorption chiller is technically feasible
using district heating as a heat source to sufﬁce the required cooling demand of 5910 kW for
the chosen region, Copenhagen with a COP of 0.724 and the TAC for the system is estimated
as 319098 EUR. On comparing the same with the stand alone vapour compression chiller, it
out performs both in performance and TAC with 3.77 and 271776.65 EUR respectively. Thus,
the vapour compression is the better option over absorption in district cooling application for
Copenhagen.
The optimization of the base case scenario and other sensitivity cases: 100/80, 100/70 and
100/60 for the absorption chiller yields COPs of 0.729, 0.740, 0.756 and 0.790 respectively.
Whereas, with regard to the TAC of the system the optimized base case has the best economical
value of 295886 EUR which is 7.2% less than that one not optimized, where the other sensitivity
cases: 100/80, 100/70 and 100/70 yields the TAC of 305717 EUR, 322652 EUR and 358242 EUR
respectively. The 100/80 case scenario has still better economical beneﬁt than the base case
scenario. Even after the optimization of the case scenarios, the vapour compression chiller is
still better and economically feasible than all cases of absorption chillers. With the average
price of 41 DKK/GJ none of the absorption chiller conﬁguration is beneﬁcial over the vapour
compression chiller.
Only with the heat prices in the future years from 2020 the optimized base conﬁguration
of 100/90 becomes completely feasible and has better economical outcome than the vapour
compression chiller. The case conﬁguration of 100/80 will be feasible only from 2040 and the
other case scenarios (Case 2 and Case 3) are completely out of the game in competition with
66
==PAGE:81==
the vapour compression chiller. In practicality, the newer resources or technology which lead
to decline of the heat prices in future can pave way for the feasibility and convenience for the
use of Case 2 and Case 3 scenarios.
Thus, it can be concluded that the optimized case scenarios for all the absorption chiller type
are not economically feasible in comparison to the vapour compression system for the present
market price of heat. Keeping the time for planning and installation of a refrigeration system
into consideration, the optimized base scenario of 100/90 absorption cycle will be a promising
cooling technique and 100/80 can serve after a certain extent of time. With the short time
planning the vapour compression system is preferred to be a feasible solution to meet the
cooling demand in Copenhagen.

C H A P T E R
6
FUTURE WORK
From the above analysis on the feasibility of absorption cooling requirement using the district
heating as source in Copenhagen, it is understood that the cheap heat price will promote the
economical feasibility of absorption chiller over the vapour compression chiller. Moreover,
higher the ambient temperature the larger will be the electric consumption by the vapour com-
pression chiller. Thus, the regions with higher ambient/air temperature have more potential
for installation of absorption chillers.
The countries which are in the tropical belt require more cooling demand to be satisﬁed than
that of the heating demand because of the higher ambient temperature and it can be clearly
seen from Figure 6.1. Most of them are developing countries such as India, China, Brazil, etc.,
and currently, they use the traditional way of cooling, the vapour compression chiller and the
demand for cooling in these places are increasing every year [57]. Especially, countries like
India and China are the big players in the air-conditioning market with high person cooling
degree days [58].
It should be taken into consideration that most of these countries don’t have the facility for
the district heating network to serve as a source for both heating and cooling. But there is a
high probability of utilizing entire waste heat energy from the incineration plants or power
plants co-generated with the electricity and this can be an eye-opening chance to develop
economically and environmentally beneﬁcial methods of cooling through absorption chillers.
Modelling absorption chillers for these areas will be a good future research work to do and
this may eventually open the gate for big players in the market.
