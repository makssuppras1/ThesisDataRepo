Abstract
This is a feasibility study of a small satellite solution to monitor arctic maritime trafﬁc. Two payloads
are used for this. One is a radio receiver for the Automatic Identiﬁcation System (AIS) to identify any
ships detected. The other is a Navigation Radar Detection (NRD) system, using phase differences
between antennas in a planar array to determine the angle of attack of radar pulses from maritime
navigation radars. Combined with knowledge of the spacecraft attitude and position, this angle of
attack can be used to determine the location of the radar transmitter on the surface.
A ﬁrst iteration baseline design is presented for a 6U cubesat that appears feasible for carrying out the
mission as described. This includes initial estimates of power, mass and pointing budgets. The most
difﬁcult primary design requirement to meet is the target detection accuracy of 40 km, ambitiously
stretched out to elevation angles of 5◦.
Combining a small satellite solution with primarily Commercial Off-the-Shelf (COTS) components is
aimed to reduce costs, targeting a mission in the 10 million DKK class for a mission duration of about
1 year.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Recent years have seen an increase in trafﬁc in the arctic - a trend which is expected to continue [5].
Reduced sea ice coverage is a part of this, allowing more ships to traverse the area that would not
normally be able to do so safely.
Denmark has a strategic interest in keeping an eye on ships moving through the large areas of sea
surrounding Greenland and the Faeroe Islands. Creating infrastructure to do this can be an important
part of maintaining sovereignty in these areas.
An ideal way to cover these large areas regularly is from orbit, as a spacecraft can provide far greater
coverage in the arctic than other options.
Norway is working on their NorSat-3 spacecraft [6], with similar objectives focused on the waters
surrounding them. If Denmark wants to remain signiﬁcant in the arctic, falling behind on this kind
of infrastructure and technological development is the wrong way to go.
As the Danish budget for space operations is not enormous, this project explores the feasibility of
fulﬁlling such a mission while keeping costs low. This is done with a small satellite using primarily
Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) components. A small satellite is here deﬁned as being less than
100 kg.
1.1
Customer Objectives
A set of customer objectives for the mission have been estimated or assumed. The customer is as-
sumed to be the Danish military. The primary objective is to monitor maritime trafﬁc, particularly
in the waters surrounding Greenland and the Faeroe Islands. Monitoring the remainder of the arctic
is of secondary interest, but practically automatic from a polar orbit, so there is little reason not to
include it.

With Automatic Identiﬁcation System (AIS) tracking most trafﬁc already, the focus is on dark targets,
deﬁned as vessels operating either without AIS or having tampered with their transmitter in a way

Should future, more detailed analyses or customer contact prove some of these assumptions inaccu-

Chapter 2
Theory
The basic principles behind the technologies used for the mission design as well as some methods for
evaluating performance are presented in this chapter.
2.1
Image Recognition
One way of observing maritime trafﬁc from orbit is through imaging. Images can be produced in a
number of different ways, but they are assumed to be reduced to a grid of one brightness value for
each pixel (or multiple values in the case of color/multispectral images).
Recognizing patterns and objects in images is a complex process that can be done in many different
ways with software. The details of these algorithms and other methods will not be covered here,
except a look at some general requirements to the images for an object to be recognizable. The primary
statistic under investigation here is the image resolution compared to the size of the smallest object
recognized within the image. Multiple different methods have been compared in table 2.1, numbers
obtained from [4]. Other factors than resolution likely also play a role in this, such as contrast between
objects and background. Resolution is the one parameter that is commonly designed around and easy
to ﬁnd numbers for from different examples, so it has been chosen as the point of comparison.
Based on the comparison in table 2.1, a minimum number of pixels to detect a ship can be estimated.
It is plausible that several of the higher end estimates are over estimated - the smallest ship detected
is not necessarily the smallest possible, just the smallest one present in the data and detected. It
should be noted that the data in table 2.1 is from a literature review and the individual examples are
unlikely to be based on the same or even similar datasets. Based on this and the numbers presented,
a conservative estimate is set at 5 pixels necessary to detect a ship, to be used for estimating and
comparing the performance of imaging sensors.

2.2
Sensor Comparisons
Several different sensor technologies are compared as payloads to observe dark maritime targets.
A general selection of advantages and disadvantages of each technology will be presented, before
comparing their expected performance for three different scenarios, described below.
Civilian Dark Targets
The primary objective is monitoring targets operating either without an active transponder (e.g. AIS)
or having tampered with it. This can be the case for all kinds of shady behavior, a few examples could
be illegal ﬁshery operations or smugglers.
Military Vessels
A secondary point of interest could be to monitor foreign military vessels, which are likely to be
equipped with more advanced systems to avoid detection than a civilian vessel.
Search & Rescue
While not a part of the primary objective, search & rescue has been selected as a relevant secondary
one due to the limited infrastructure in the arctic. For the sake of detection through image recognition,
life boats are assumed to be roughly 5 m targets. Based on the conclusions from section 2.1, this means
that approximately 1 m resolution is required to reliably detect them.
2.2.1
Passive Navigation Radar Detection (NRD)
As navigation radars are common for maritime vessels - being mandated for all ships over 300 gross
tons and passenger ships of all sizes [7] - detecting the radio transmissions of such systems is one
way to monitor maritime trafﬁc. Navigation radars are particularly useful in the arctic for tracking
icebergs and avoiding collisions, making it unlikely that anyone would operate without one in this
region.
This technology has several advantages. Using passive detection of radio signals emitted by the
targets, payload power consumption can be kept lower than an active system. Being able to provide
quite high FOV allows these systems to achieve a lot of coverage, NorSat-3 promises swath widths of
roughly 1400 km with a payload of this type [8]. The general lack of this type of sensors in existence
(publicly) makes countermeasures less likely.
A drawback to Navigation Radar Detection (NRD) payload technology is that it will be designed
to receive certain radio frequencies, so any ship without a radar operating in that band will not be
detectable.

Civilian Dark Targets
Monitoring abilities for this category of targets depends heavily on the radars of the vessels in ques-
tion. Even criminal targets are assumed to be equipped with legally mandated radars, as they likely
still want to appear legitimate if inspected.
Military Vessels
Monitoring foreign military vessels with a payload like this depends on whether they operate radars
within the same frequency bands as common civilian radars. Common hardware would seem to
make this likely. Correspondence with Jesper Holm, FMI, has provided speciﬁcations of the radar
on a Danish military vessel, table 2.4, as an example. Assuming this to be common, monitoring such
military vessels is entirely possible with a payload like this.
Search & Rescue
This payload offers limited performance in this regard, as life boats are generally not equipped with
radars. The exception to this is in any case where the ship radar remains operational. In these less
desperate cases (the ship has not sunk) the wide swath of the payload makes detecting the target
more likely, regardless of how well known their position is already.
2.2.2
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) provides active illumination and is able to detect a wide range of dif-
ferent targets. Detection is done in image recognition following observations, adding a delay between
observing and identifying a target.
The active nature of SAR sensors results in a signiﬁcant power consumption and limits use of very
small satellites for this type of payloads. The smallest SAR equipped satellites found are Finnish and
Polish ICEYE, at around 85 kg, with resolution of 1 m in ”spotlight” mode and 3 m in ”stripmap”
mode. ”Spotlight” steers the antenna during a pass to focus on a predetermined area of 5x5 km,
making it useful for detailed observations of known target locations. ”Stripmap” mode simply keeps
the antenna attitude ﬁxed and scans as the satellite moves, imaging a swath 30 km wide [9]. Both of
these modes provide quite low coverage. Another example of a larger SAR satellite is NovaSAR-1 at
450 kg, with a maritime imaging mode: over 400 km swath width, 6 m across track resolution and
13.7 m along track [10]. This is speciﬁcally intended for ship detection. These SAR payloads show an
inverse relation between ground coverage and imaging resolution. This is a clear drawback for this
type of technology in the applications discussed.
Beyond simply being able to detect an object in the ocean, systems used in the arctic also need to
be able to distinguish between ships and icebergs. SAR technology provides additional polarization

the distinction between icebergs and ships in some cases [11].
SAR operates by integrating a lot of observations to make a single image, limiting the number of
images that can be made of any target in a pass.
Civilian Dark Targets
With sufﬁcient image recognition technology, all relevant vessels will likely be detectable with the

Military Vessels
For this type of payload there is little difference between civilian and military targets, so the same

SAR would be quite useful in some search and rescue situations where the search radius is limited.
Already having a general idea of an area to search for targets in would mitigate the issues of coverage.
Not all such payloads have sufﬁcient resolution to detect small objects like lifeboats, and those that
do have even less coverage. However, an extra sensor scanning just some of the area is likely to help
even if it can’t solve the problem entirely on its own.

antennas are needed to ﬁnd an angle. They give the angle in the plane made up by the line between
them and the boresight axis - φ in ﬁgure 2.1. Two such measurements, by orthogonal antenna pairs,

2.4.1
Impact of Frequency Modulation
This process has assumed the signals to be at the same constant frequency. The frequency modulated
radars also included as a subject for this payload do not live up to this assumption. As the conversion
from phase difference to a difference in distance, ∆d, depends on the wavelength of the signal, a pulse
varying in frequency will show a different phase difference for the same angle of attack, depending on
when during the pulse this phase measurement is taken. Assuming a linear chirp down in frequency,
the wavelength increases linearly over the pulse duration. This means a measurement at the end of
the pulse will show a lower phase shift than at the beginning, because ∆d corresponds to a smaller
fraction of a wavelength.

2.4.7
Alternative Locating Strategies
During this project, a few alternative ways to locate targets through their radar emissions have come
up, these are brieﬂy mentioned here along with a few reasons why they have not been selected.
Retrodirective phased array scanning is an approach using phase shifting in an array of antennas.
The example used here is a planar patch antenna array. Through changing phase shifts on the array
components, the directivity pattern is changed and the central beam can be scanned across the FOV to
determine the direction of incoming signals, by ﬁnding the direction of maximum signal amplitude.
A prototype of such a system for 2.2 GHz achieved 1.5◦-5.5◦detection accuracy [23]. While it is an
interesting technology, this is orders of magnitude worse than the 0.098◦requirement from section
3.3.
Another simple alternative could be rotating the spacecraft and using rotation modulation to deter-
mine the direction to the target. This simple option has some serious drawbacks, including potential
communications issues from the ground link antennas rotating with the rest of the spacecraft. In ad-
dition it only provides information on one axis of the target’s position, likely necessitating the along
track direction to be determined from signal modulation due to the satellite’s movement while the
rotating antenna(s) scan the across track direction. Without payload antennas on multiple sides of the
spacecraft, this would also limit the payload to not actively observing the Earth all the time, with the
antenna facing into space a signiﬁcant portion of the time. These concerns are considered sufﬁcient
to drop this option without further examination at this stage.

Mission Requirements
The technical system requirements of the satellite mission are formed on the basis of the customer
objectives from section 1.1. These are the initial requirements for the ﬁrst iteration of the mission

3.1
Response Time
To monitor maritime trafﬁc, a limited response time is necessary. For example it serves little purpose
to know that a ship was detected last week, compared to a ship that was detected two hours ago.
For example, a delay of 5 hours would allow a ship moving at 20 knots to be anywhere in a 160 km
radius.
Response time is a combination of time spent on data processing, downlink and distribution as well
as wait times before ground station access. Drivers for this include orbit selection, ground station
location, link data rates compared to the amount of data produced plus data processing power and
architecture.
Assuming on-board processing, transmitting a list of coordinates and timestamps down requires very
little downlink capacity. If the full data set needs to be transmitted through the downlink, required
data rates depend on the amount of data produced by the payload. Basic on-board detection of
observed activity should enable ﬁltering out empty parts of the data set to help meet these limits if
necessary.
As the data is intended for military use, the downlink may have a limited number of available ground
stations, depending on how tight limitations are on the secrecy of this data. Ideally only Danish
ground stations should be used, but if necessary other NATO allies likely have some in the region to
plug some gaps. For this reason, commercial ground stations have been excluded from the design
considerations.

3.3
Detection Accuracy
Detection accuracy is very payload speciﬁc, and some requirements will be set for the NRD pay-
load below. Accuracy is affected by a number of factors, including errors in attitude and position
determination and inherent payload errors from noise.
Similar to the response time above, the accuracy of each detection needs to be within a given dis-
tance on the surface, to allow the customer to follow up the detection. Estimates of a naval patrol
vessel’s radar range are around 40 km, so a detection within that range is considered a reasonable
requirement.
Assuming a horizon looking payload the FOV will cover a lot more ground across track than along
it, meaning a ground distance accuracy requirement translates to a stricter requirement of angular
accuracy across track. From 500 km LEO looking near the horizon, where the satellite is at an elevation
angle of 5◦, a 40 km ground track covers 0.098◦or 353 arcsec seen from the satellite. Appendix 9.7
shows the calculations for these estimates.
From this, the technical requirement is clear: the total error on the angular accuracy of the system
shall be no more than 350 arcsec. It may be ambitious to stretch this accuracy requirement out to
elevation angles of 5◦, but it can always be relaxed in later iterations if it proves infeasible.
3.4
Multiple targets
Detecting a given target within the sensor’s FOV has a lot of sensor speciﬁc factors inﬂuencing it. A
universal factor is the signal to noise ratio of the target compared to the background. Additionally, if
two similar signals arrive simultaneously it is unlikely they can be distinguished from one another.
Detection, for an NRD payload, requires the target’s radar pulse to arrive at the antennas from within
the FOV while the receiver is sampling and within the correct frequency band. In the assumption that
the sensor cannot distinguish two or more simultaneous radar pulses from different targets, a clut-
tered FOV reduces the probability of detection as some pulses are lost to garbling. Two pulses from
different radars arriving simultaneously should be a limited occurrence, as the signals are modulated
by pulse repetition frequency, pulse width and the rotation rate of the radar antenna. This broad set
of factors are unlikely to match perfectly between two targets. It is still possible that areas of sufﬁcient
trafﬁc will cause issues with garbling, especially in coastal areas, assuming coastal radar installations
overlap the observed frequency band. Based on ITU numbers for normal navigation radars, table 2.3,
accounting for beamwidth, pulse width and pulse repetition frequency a conservative estimate is that
any single given radar is actively transmitting towards the satellite for no more than 1/15000th of the
time. Calculations for this are shown in appendix 9.6. Given this it seems realistic to set a requirement
of handling up to 5 simultaneous targets in the footprint as a technical requirement.

4.1
Design Overview
The NRD payload consists of four parallel radio receivers, with the antennas arranged in a rectangu-
lar pattern. To determine the angle of attack for an incoming signal, two orthogonal pairs of antennas
need to be used. One antenna can be a part of both pairs, making it doable with three antennas,
arranged in an L-shape. Adding a fourth antenna allows redundancy if any of the antennas, or cor-
responding receivers, fail. This leads to a rectangular conﬁguration with an antenna in each corner,
likely patch antenna arrays. The fourth antenna serves to have a spectrum analyzer monitoring the
received signals and targeting the measurements at the right frequencies, while the other three are
used for phase measurements.
The antennas connect to 4 parallel and identical receiver front-ends receiving the 9.2-9.5 GHz band,
where a shared Local Oscillator (LO) is used to downconvert them all to a ﬁrst Intermediate Fre-
quency (IF) centered on 1.5 GHz. Following this front-end is a switching block, which will not be
designed in detail here except for a set of requirements. It needs 4 inputs that can connect to any of
the 4 outputs, controllable digitally. The design needs to maintain high isolation between the signal
lines. Regardless of how it is conﬁgured, it should have very similar signal path lengths to not intro-
duce phase shifts when switching. This last requirement is relevant to the entire receiver design, as
the ﬁnal phase comparison cannot tell any difference between the phase shifts this payload is meant
to measure and any additional ones introduced by components in the receiver chain. Whenever these
phase shifts cannot be avoided entirely, they should be characterized and modeled so they can be
compensated for in data processing. For design parameters, these should be kept well below one
wavelength of the signal, an initial design limit is set at a quarter wavelength total, to allow for solid
margins.
Three of the four outputs of the switching block connect to the Phase Comparator System (PCS) as the
reference, x and y lines. The fourth connects to a Software Deﬁned Radio (SDR) running as a spectrum
analyzer. The PCS ﬁrst does an additional downconversion to a 10 MHz band centered around 350
MHz on all three lines, using a shared tunable LO. It then splits the reference line to 4 mixers, where
it is mixed with the x and y lines, in-phase and quadrature. This theoretically produces two outputs
from each mixer, one at DC and one at twice the frequency, 700 MHz. Using a low-pass ﬁlter, the
second output is removed and the four DC outputs from the mixers can be fed into an Analog-to-
Digital Converter (ADC) before digital processing.

4.3
Front-end
Following the antennas, each of the four front-ends are identical, starting with an LNA amplifying
the weak radar signal (roughly -73 to -43 dBm) before ﬁltering it through a 9.2-9.5 GHz bandpass
ﬁlter. In the relatively quiet environment of orbit it is assumed safe to place the ﬁlter after the LNA
and this enables a slightly better SNR by avoiding losses from the ﬁlter before the LNA. The expected
radio environment and the maximum input power of the LNA should be compared to verify this
assumption, for this project it has only been done for the expected signal strengths from maritime
targets.
The signals are then downconverted to the IF by mixing with a shared LO running at 9.35 −1.5 = 7.85
GHz. Signals leaking through the LO (crosstalk) is a concern, as they could be going down the wrong
lines and adding a self-correlation component in the PCS leading to additional errors in the ﬁnal
measurements. To prevent this, isolators are installed between the power splitter, connecting the LO
to the lines, and each mixer. The combined isolation provided by the mixers, power splitter and
isolators should reduce this issue.
The mixed signals are ampliﬁed and ﬁltered again, this time through a 1.35-1.65 GHz bandpass ﬁlter.
The IF ampliﬁer needs to have a sufﬁciently high output 1 dB compression point to maintain linear

4.4
SDR Spectrum Analyzer
One output of the switching block connects to an SDR which operates as a spectrum analyzer. In
this role it scans any received signals, looking for signatures of a radar pulse. If such a signature is
detected, it conﬁgures the tunable LO in the PCS to the right frequency to pass the next pulses into
the PCS. It may be possible to estimate the pulse repetition frequency, if enough pulses are detected,
to optimize the payload sampling to only sample when there is something to see.
Two important parameters of this subsystem are how wide a band the SDR can observe at a time and
how quickly it can switch between them, combining to limit how long it takes to scan the entire 300
MHz band.
4.5
Phase Comparator System (PCS)
The ﬁrst step in the PCS is downconverting a selected 10 MHz channel to 350 MHz. This downcon-
version involves a shared LO like the IF downconversion in the front-end, this one being tunable
between 1-1.3 GHz and digitally controlled by the SDR.
With another shared LO, similar precautions to the front-end are taken to minimize crosstalk - isola-
tors are placed between each mixer and the LO power splitter.
Following downconversion another ampliﬁer compensates for losses in the lines up to this point to
keep the signal power high for the phase comparison. The signals are then ﬁltered through a narrow
345-355 MHz bandpass ﬁlter.
A 4-way power splitter then divides the reference signal to 4 mixers that perform the phase compar-
ison with the x and y lines. These are both split in 2-way 90◦quadrature hybrid splitters, producing
an in-phase and quadrature output. The mixers produce I and Q phase comparisons between the
reference signal and both of the x and y lines. Running each output through a lowpass ﬁlter removes
the double frequency component at 700 MHz and leaves a DC signal to be converted to a digital value
in an ADC.
Assuming gain and loss symmetry between the receiver chains, the reference signal will be 3 dB lower
in amplitude at the mixer due to splitting 4 ways instead of 2. Given the symmetry of phase com-
parisons, which signal is operating as input and which as LO in the mixer is theoretically irrelevant.
From a practical perspective, the x and y signals should be used as LO, given the 3 dB extra power
for driving the mixers. Compensating for this in the data processing is as simple as changing sign on
the phase shift measured.

4.6
Data Processing
With sufﬁciently high sampling rates on the ADC, multiple samples can be taken from each radar
pulse to ﬁnd an average value and even out random errors from noise. A minimum sampling rate
determines the length of the shortest pulses the payload can reliably detect. 10 MHz is chosen to
allow detection of pulses down to 0.1 µs long. This also has to stay far enough below the 700 MHz
double frequency output to allow a ﬁlter between the mixer and the ADC to cut that out without
dampening the pulse outputs from how short the pulses are and their resulting frequency, despite
being DC signals.
Each data sample from the PCS produces 4 numbers: Ix, Qx, Iy and Qy. Finding the total length of
the (I,Q) vector for x and y respectively allows the angle of attack to be determined in both planes -
the (x,z) and (y,z) planes, with the z-axis being along boresight - by comparing the I and Q numbers.
The method behind this is described in sections 2.4 and 6.
As the payload is expected to receive multiple pulses from a target in succession, the results can be
combined to ﬁnd the average and get a more accurate target position.

5.1.4
Attitude Control
Technologies for attitude control can be divided into the two categories of external and internal tor-
quers. Due to limitations of the systems available in each category, some combination is common.
External Torquers
In order to compensate for external forces on the spacecraft, some way of acting against an external
medium is necessary - this includes technologies such as thrusters, magnetorquers working against
the geomagnetic ﬁeld (or theoretically any other external magnetic ﬁeld), light pressure from the sun
(e.g. solar sails) and using a gravity gradient to apply a torque on the system.
The latter two are of limited interest due to their performance. Thrusters can provide high torque but
have a limited lifespan due to requiring fuel. Fuel tanks and plumbing add signiﬁcant complexity
to the design and are often expensive in the mass budget. Magnetorquers are useful within the geo-
magnetic ﬁeld, but limited by the local ﬁeld direction as they produce a torque perpendicular to the
local ﬁeld [25]. In a polar orbit this vector direction changes as the spacecraft travels along its orbit,
allowing 3-axis control with sufﬁcient planning and timing.
Internal Torquers
Through angular momentum transfer within the spacecraft, internal torquers like reaction wheels,
momentum wheels and control moment gyroscopes can spin up or down to apply a torque on the
rest of the system. Due to conservation of angular momentum this eventually leads to saturation
of these systems when compensating for external forces and disturbance torques, as they reach the
highest rotation speeds they can reliably handle. The external torquers mentioned above are required
for desaturation and slowing the wheels back down.

5.1.5
Orbit
To achieve coverage of the relevant areas in the arctic, some form of polar orbit is necessary. This
can be boiled down to polar LEO or a Molniya orbit - going any further out provides limited beneﬁts
and many added difﬁculties. While a Molniya orbit is quite well deﬁned, polar LEO covers a broader
range of orbits and altitudes. The primary differences between these two orbit types are coverage
and cost (from distance inﬂuencing other performance requirements combined with launch costs).
The added path loss in a Molniya orbit puts additional strain on all link budgets (payload and com-
munications), in return for excellent coverage of the entire arctic region. The added distance also
tightens the angular attitude and payload accuracy requirements substantially. This is likely to drive
up costs in several ways including the cost of launching to a higher orbit with signiﬁcantly less trafﬁc,
limiting options for ride-shares.
Assuming the reduced coverage of a polar LEO is acceptable, orbits in this category are very common
and so acquiring a cheap ride-share launch should be possible. Sun synchronous orbits fall under the
polar category. Their consistent nature relative to the sun makes them ideal for simplifying solar
power estimates.
Another factor places a limit on orbit selection, the expected satellite lifetime. With a 25 year limit on
the life of a satellite, it needs to either deorbit naturally (normally from atmospheric drag) before then
or include a way to do it manually after the mission is over (which will require thrusters that might
otherwise not be necessary).
5.1.6
Data Processing
Processing the payload data can be done either in orbit or on the ground. As processing tends to
lower the data volume this is largely a trade between processing power and downlink budget.
Estimating the total raw volume of data produced by the payload and the total amount of calculations
needed to process it can give a basis for selecting an initial architecture. These estimates have not been
done in detail at this stage of the mission design. The NorSat-3 mission, using similar payloads, has
been assumed to be an accurate example and available data about that mission can be used to give an
initial suggestion for how data processing can be done on this one.
5.1.7
OBC
Based on the processing necessary and the architecture decided upon, the On-Board Computer (OBC)
will need a given performance. Two approaches exist for high performance computers in space: very
expensive space qualiﬁed hardware or COTS components. These can achieve similar performance,
but generally have a trade off between lifetime and cost. If an architecture which requires little pro-
cessing on orbit is selected, the cost and risks can be reduced by lowering the performance necessary.
Essentially it is generally only possible to get 2 of the 3: high performance, low cost and low risk.

Next is the switching block, which is not detailed in this project beyond the requirements listed in
section 4.1.
Following the switching block, there is an SDR. One option is the TOTEM nanosatellite SDR platform,
from Alen Space [32]. It uses less than 2 W in receiving mode. This will be conﬁgured to run as a
spectrum analyzer monitoring the received signal from one of the four receivers. This particular
SDR solution is packaged with embedded Linux, and the viability of it depends on how quickly
this control system can scan through the frequencies. It may be necessary to use a faster solution,
e.g. FPGA-based control. As of the time of writing, an inquiry into more information on the system
has not received a reply, so the bandwidth available is unknown. More detailed investigation of the
timing for scanning the frequency band has been left to future design iterations. Future design of this
part of the payload is likely to be the primary determining factor in living up to the requirement for
handling multiple simultaneous targets in the footprint, by how quickly it can switch between targets
detected.

5.3.5
Operational Modes
The payload operates in three different modes: low-power, scanning and measuring.
41
==PAGE:50==
Mission Analysis of Satellite for Arctic Monitoring
DTU, August 10, 2020
Low-power mode is the payload powering down and turning off as many components as possible,
for when the satellite is not above an area of interest or power constraints prevent full operation.
When active, the payload starts in scanning mode where the PCS is in low-power mode, while the
SDR is analyzing the spectrum of the received signal. This assumes that the PCS can power up quickly
enough to react, which may not be possible, necessitating it being powered up in this mode as well.
When the signature of a radar pulse is detected by the spectrum analyzer, the PCS is set to observe at
that frequency and the system switches to measuring mode.
In measuring mode the PCS is set to the frequency of the signal observed by the SDR, and the SDR
stops scanning and stays at that frequency as well. It outputs the pulse repetition frequency to the
data processing computer after observing several pulses, if possible, so it can avoid sampling the PCS
output when nothing is happening. The SDR will also be responsible for monitoring when a pulse is
received, to avoid having to ﬁgure out when a pulse is coming in just from the sampled output data.
After a set number of pulse delays without a pulse observed (probably at least 2, to avoid doing it too
early), it is assumed that the target is out of FOV or the radar transmitter has rotated away from the
satellite. The payload then switches back to scanning mode, looking for a new target. In the mean
time, the data processing computer ﬁnds the angle of attack for each pulse measured, allowing the
position of the source to be determined.
Some limits on the timing of switching between these modes can be estimated with numbers from
section 2.3.4. If a radar beam can potentially be visible for as little as 2 ms at a time, that naturally
leaves less time than that for the SDR to detect a pulse and conﬁgure the PCS LO. Depending on
future analysis of the capabilities of the ﬁnal choice for SDR a minimum required visible time for
the radar beam may be found higher than that, limiting what radar systems the payload is able to
reliably detect. Timing this within 1 ms does not seem implausible, but it should be investigated in
future design iterations.
The payload design discussed will also be unable to reliably locate radar sources if they do not trans-
mit at least two pulses in the time their beam is visible. More would naturally help provide additional
data points for averaging out random errors and potentially ﬁnding the pulse repetition rate for better
sample timing.

5.5
OBC and Data Handling
The computer system on the satellite needs to be able to store at least two orbits worth of data. In
case of a communications issue during one orbit, data can be stored for downlink at the next ground
station access. NorSat-3’s similar payload produces about 25 MB of data in an average orbit [8], so
this storage should not require anything groundbreaking, and more long term storage is plausible.
With a separate computer handling payload data processing, the primary OBC is tasked with teleme-
try, C&C and attitude determination/control. While important, these tasks are not generally very
resource intensive. To make a conservative estimate, a second computer like the one used for pay-
load data processing should have plenty of processing power for these tasks. This adds another 4 W
to the power budget.
5.6
Orbit
A 500 km sun synchronous orbit allows full arctic coverage in less than a day with 16◦FOV and
access every orbit to ground stations in northern Greenland. STK simulations of the coverage have
been used to produce ﬁgure 5.4, showing full coverage of everything north of Jutland in just over 12
hours - the approximate southern limit of this full coverage is indicated by the red line. A dusk-dawn
orbit will be ideal, for optimal access to solar power with almost no eclipses. The resulting uniform
thermal environment might also be an advantage, if future thermal design allows for constant sun-
light without overheating. A dusk-dawn orbit should, according to STK simulations, only experience
eclipses directly over Antarctica in the middle of summer. A potential drawback, although likely very
limited in scope, is the fact that a sun synchronous orbit is limited to passing over a given area at two
set local times every day. For a surveillance system, this degree of predictability may not be desirable,
as it allows it to be avoided more easily.
In case the desired dusk-dawn orbit is not achievable, e.g. due to a ride-share launch resulting in
a different orbit, another common sun synchronous orbit has been investigated. Earth observation
satellites make frequent use of orbits around the Local Solar Time (LST) 10:30. These orbits make little
difference for the payloads directly, except by how much power is available from solar panels. This
is discussed further in section 5.10.
Fulﬁlling the requirement to deorbit within 25 years sets an upper limit on the orbit altitude. Simula-
tions have been made for this by Flemming Hansen, RadioLab Consulting, and used with permission
in this report. They assume a 6U cubesat moving with the smallest face in the ram direction, and are
detailed in appendix 9.1. These simulations show a 550 km orbit having an expected lifetime of 22.5
years. This is very close to the 25 year limit - too close with the high uncertainty of atmospheric mod-
els and solar activity. A 500 km orbit, using the same method, shows a lifetime of 9.9 years, providing
a solid margin of error.

5.7
Communications
Assuming the ground station uses a parabolic antenna, the choice of carrier frequency should have no
direct impact on the link budget [1]. That decision will then come down to other factors. Ideally then,
the frequency should be chosen to match existing ground station equipment, to keep down cost. To
keep antenna sizes down S-, C- or X-bands are likely best suited. For X-band there is the added caveat
that the frequency should not be too close to the operational band of the NRD payload (9.2-9.5 GHz).
To lighten the pointing budget and simplify operations, redundant communications antennas may
be an option. Installing high FOV patch antennas on multiple faces of the spacecraft would allow
downlink from most attitudes with little correction required.
The same SDR as for the payload receivers will be used as a baseline for the communications system.
Based on NorSat-3 estimates of 25 MB of payload data per orbit [8], a single comms link can handle
data downlink, telemetry and commands. The radio draws about 5 W when transmitting to produce
30 dBm. This is assumed to be transmitted power, but it is not speciﬁed on the site [32].
A rough link budget for a system like this (assuming maximum distance to the ground station at
5◦elevation angle), shows 80 dB Eb/N0 for a 2 Mbit/s downlink assuming a 30 dB ground station
receiving antenna gain, 5 dB transmitter antenna gain and 30 dBm transmitted power. This link
budget estimate is calculated in appendix 9.8.
5.8
Ground Stations
A ground station located in the arctic can allow access every orbit for sun synchronous orbits down to
at least 500 km altitude. A good candidate is the existing research/military outpost at Station Nord.
STK simulations of 500 km sun synchronous orbits show access every orbit to this location. Cost
can be minimized by choosing communications frequencies that are already supported by existing
hardware, or still lowered signiﬁcantly if this is not possible, by using a preexisting installation like
this to host new antenna hardware.
5.9
Attitude Determination and Control
For 3-axis attitude control, three reaction wheels, CubeSpace CubeWheel Large, provide 2.3 mNm of
maximum torque and 30 mNms momentum storage [42]. Each consumes less than 180 mW power
on average.
Magnetorquers are required to desaturate the wheels. EXA MT01 Compact Magnetorquer seems to
be a viable solution [43]. It can be powered with 250-1750 mW, depending on the necessary torque.
Three of these orthogonally placed will result in a peak power consumption of 5.25 W. A situation
45
==PAGE:54==
Mission Analysis of Satellite for Arctic Monitoring

requiring all three providing peak torque simultaneously does not seem very plausible, but this is for
a conservative power budget.
For the primary attitude determination sensor, the Sagitta Star Tracker, found on CubeSatShop is a
promising candidate [44]. It provides 2 arcsec accuracy cross boresight (pitch and yaw), and 10 arcsec
around boresight (roll), using <1 W nominal power. This is a single sensor that allows 3-axis attitude
measurements. This presents a few options, either installing one and saving on power and mass
budgets or several for slightly increased performance and redundancy. For the baseline, just one is
included.
A separate, simpler attitude sensor can be included for redundancy - in sun synchronous orbit having
a sun sensor on a sun-ward face or a set of Earth horizon sensors are both good options. There appears
to be multiple options with <0.5◦attitude accuracy and low power consumption. One of them is
to use a pair of MAI-SES IR Earth Sensors which can provide better than 0.25◦accuracy and draw
roughly 130 mW each [45]. With a payload designed to point in a speciﬁc conﬁguration pointing near
the horizon (and a circular orbit), it should be possible to install these sensors in a conﬁguration so
they have a constant view of the horizon as the spacecraft maintains a constant attitude relative to
nadir. The performance of such sensors is not good enough for optimal mission performance, but
their low cost (both directly and in mass/power budgets) make them good candidates for backup
sensors should the primary system fail.
While a gyroscope for high frequency attitude updates is also included in the design, details on this
have not been a focus of this project. It will need low drift, but quite frequent reference updates
should be expected, especially if a dusk-dawn orbit is used, as it will be feasible to install the star
tracker pointing in a direction that never gets blinded by the Sun.
Attitude control is assumed to be handled by the OBC, so no additional computers should be re-
quired.
5.9.1
Pointing Budget
The total pointing budget for the NRD payload depends on several contributing factors. The most rel-
evant factors are attitude determination, antenna misalignment from differential thermal expansion,
the accuracy of the payload determining angle of attack, timestamp errors and spacecraft position
errors.
From section 3.3, the ﬁnal angular accuracy of the vector to the radar source can be no worse than
350 arcsec. A simple ﬁrst approximation would be to evenly divide the accuracy requirement be-
tween the contributing factors, giving each a margin of just over one arc minute. This is not realistic
however, as achieving better than 1◦accuracy in the phase difference measurement is very challeng-
ing, according to personal communication with Sten Schmidl Søbjærg, DTU Space, likely limiting the
payload angle-of-attack determination to 16/360 = 0.044◦, or 160 arcsec. This calculation assumes
the payload to be scaled to have exactly 360◦phase shift from one extreme of the antenna FOV to

the other. This also tightens the limits on remaining factors. The star tracker mentioned can provide
10 arcsec accuracy, leaving 180 arcsec (or the equivalent ground distance accuracy) in the budget for
position, timestamp and structural errors. Adding an additional star tracker to the spacecraft, or-
thogonal to the ﬁrst, can lower the attitude determination error to 2 arcsec if necessary, from 10. Time
invariant errors such as inaccurate mounting of payload antennas, attitude sensors etc. should be
possible to calibrate out. This calibration work is assumed, so those factors are not included in this
budget.
Position errors can be reduced by introducing a Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) receiver.
NewSpace Systems GPS Receiver is available on CubeSatShop and has ﬂight heritage [46]. It draws
1 W, and has better accuracy than 10 m for position and 25 cm/s for velocity. Simply getting a few
measurements right before starting a payload measurement will correct orbit propagator drift and
provide more accurate data for subsequent orbit propagation. This means it will help signiﬁcantly
with these errors, even at a very low duty cycle. Reducing the total position error to the order of 10 m
effectively eliminates it, being negligible in comparison to the 40 km accuracy requirement.
Timestamp errors should also be fairly negligible for this. Assuming a large error of 1 s and approx-
imate LEO velocity of 7500 m/s, this will result in 7.5 km along track error. Lowering timestamp
accuracies to millisecond level is assumed plausible, resulting in errors on an order of 10 m from
timestamps as well, also negligible.
Accurate attitude control is less important than accurate attitude measurements. The attitude mea-
surements have to fall within the stated accuracy requirement, while pointing control is acceptable
within 1◦.

Launch
A satellite of this scale imposes practically no limits on possible launch vehicles. With a low cost

5.13
Cost Estimates
Detailed estimates of the ﬁnal cost of this mission are not realistic at this early iteration. It includes a
number of factors that it is only possible to guess about at this stage, including launch, work hours for
design and testing as well as ﬁnal production of the spacecraft. The components listed for this design
are not expected to make up a large part of such a budget, particularly when using COTS parts. As
component selection is far from ﬁnal and with some producers not providing easy access to a price,
summing up the component costs has not been deemed important for this iteration of the design.
5.14
Thermal design
While thermal considerations are important for any satellite mission, this design has not yet reached
a level of detail where it seems relevant to apply a lot of effort to this aspect. None of the components
that have been found for the baseline require any particular thermal control outside of standard in-
dustrial operating temperature ranges for electrical components.
With the choice of components being far from ﬁnal, there is not yet a design in place for how they
will be arranged within the satellite structure, making accurate thermal simulations impossible.
5.15
Radiation Design
As most of the platform components are at minimum designed for space use, or has some ﬂight
heritage, they are assumed to be fairly robust in regard to radiation. In the low orbit of 500 km, the
radiation environment is assumed to be relatively benign.
In later stages of the mission development, the payload design and critical components should be
tested for radiation sensitivity. Control systems should also be designed with the ability to handle
radiation errors (e.g. single event upsets). It is especially important for the ZU9EG computer intended
for OBC and data processing, but these considerations should be considered in design of all systems.

6.1
Scalability
Looking at the scalability of the mission, a few important parameters and their effect on performance
are discussed here.
Increasing the physical size of the satellite can improve surface area available for power and NRD
payload antennas. More power available would allow the mission a higher total dutycycle on all
systems or to increase certain power limited performance metrics. Many payload components were
rejected solely for having very high power consumption. Scaling up to provide more power would
lessen this restriction and likely allow better performance in the receiver chain to improve SNR. The
viability of this for future design depends heavily on a solution to detection ambiguity for the NRD
payload antennas, currently handled with an upper limit on antenna baseline.
This project has generally assumed that antenna phase centers are at their geometrical centers. De-
pending on the ﬂexibility of positioning these antenna array phase centers, it may be possible to scale
each individual antenna array up signiﬁcantly without increasing the baseline distance between their
phase centers, and thus staying under the limit to avoid ambiguous detections. The idea is illustrated
in ﬁgure 6.1, showing that placing the phase centers near the central corners of the patches, the base-

Increasing the internal volume of the satellite, i.e. providing room for larger subsystem components

mass available for everything and less power. It seems unlikely at this stage that a mission smaller
than 6U is feasible with the payload design presented, especially within the cubesat standard.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The design presented in this report shows that it is feasible to perform a mission such as the one de-
scribed. Depending on some assumptions of hardware performance, the 40 km accuracy requirement
for the NRD payload may need to be drawn back to 10◦elevation angle (from 5◦). The arctic is fully
covered by the NRD payload within 14 hours. Installations exist (e.g. Station Nord), whose location
allows access to the satellite every orbit for communications and downlink if used as ground stations.
Expected trafﬁc in the arctic should be well within the system’s capability to handle multiple targets
on the NRD payload, while the AIS receiver may face issues with garbling, but not to a critical degree.
Target identiﬁcation appears entirely feasible by cross referencing NRD detections with AIS messages
and matching similar positions.
NRD accuracy is affected by a number of error sources. Static errors are assumed to be calibrated for.
Variable errors in the phase stability of receiver components as well as unknown attitude errors from
variable thermal expansion of the spacecraft frame are the two primary factors contributing to it.
The design philosophy of using COTS components when possible appears feasible for mission per-
formance. Although no ﬁnal cost estimates have been made at this stage in the design, nothing has
been found to suggest the target cost of roughly 10 million DKK and lifetime of 1 year are not feasible.
The cubesat design has also excluded some technologies like thrusters from the design, due to their
high cost in mass and volume budgets.
Another beneﬁt of a small satellite is practically removing all limitations on viable launch vehicles,
opening up the option to cut costs signiﬁcantly with a ride-share launch. Due to not having very strict
orbit requirements, such a solution has limited drawbacks.
Scaling the mission up to provide increased surface area for power and NRD payload antennas is an
option, with the natural increase in cost from a larger system. Increasing the internal volume of the
spacecraft does not appear to be as important for mission performance. Scaling the mission down to
a 3U cubesat does not appear feasible, as it would reduce available power without lowering payload
power requirements and cut the internal volume available in half.

Chapter 8
Future Work
As has been emphasized throughout this report, the project is a ﬁrst iteration design and many hours
of engineering work are still needed to make it into a complete space mission. A few of these next
steps have been highlighted in this section, along with ways their results might impact the design
presented in this report.
Following a more detailed customer analysis and contact as mentioned in section 1.1, an important
step to realizing this mission is a subject study of the maritime radars in use. A number of assump-
tions have been made in this report and several design decisions are based on numbers from a doc-
ument no longer supported by the ITU. If a number of ships are using radars with signiﬁcantly less
transmitter power, these may be difﬁcult to detect with the existing design. It is possible to just accept
that and only track some ships, or a redesign with higher sensitivity may be necessary. It is also pos-
sible that such a study may reveal a large portion of 9.2-9.5 GHz radars to fall in a narrow part of the
band, providing the option to focus on that band and speed up the scanning process for the payload
spectrum analyzer.
This report makes many assumptions about the antennas needed for the NRD payload. These anten-
nas need to be designed and the payload receiver may need to be changed, potentially quite drasti-
cally, to suit any ways the ﬁnal antenna design does not align with these assumptions. For example,
if the ﬁnal antenna array design does not have the 4 antennas assumed, the NRD payload receiver
design presented in this report is not going to work.
An important factor for NRD detection accuracy mentioned several times in this report, but not esti-
mated, is the payload attitude error from variable thermal expansion on the spacecraft. How low this
can get on a low cost cubesat mission is important for mission performance, and should be investi-
gated further. Reducing it below 100 arcsec should allow the payload performance requirements to be
met (assuming the other assumptions about detection accuracy hold), but this may involve expensive
methods that are not realistic for a mission like this.

Ray Tracer Example
The Matlab script presented on the following pages calculates the position of a target on the surface
of the Earth, based on the spacecraft position and the direction of the signal. Finding the distance
between the target coordinates for 500 and 500.3 km altitudes has been used to estimate the error con-
tribution from altitude inaccuracy. The coordinates are sc for the spacecraft and det for the detection.
==PAGE:87==
% Position from detection vector
R_E = 6371; % km
h = 500+0.3; % km
% name = [r, phi, theta] % [km, rad, rad], [r, azi, polar]
sc = [h+R_E, 70*pi/180, 5*pi/180]; % [km, rad, rad]
det = [1, 250*pi/180, 109.5*pi/180]; % [km, rad, rad]
nabla = ( sin(det(3))*cos(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*cos(sc(2))  + ...
     sin(det(3))*sin(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*sin(sc(2))  + ...
     cos(det(3)) * sc(1)*cos(sc(3)) )^2 - (h^2 + 2*h*R_E);
if(nabla < 0)
        fprintf('Detection vector does not intersect Earth surface.
\n');
else
    if(nabla == 0)
        d = -sin(det(3))*cos(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*cos(sc(2)) 
 + ...
     sin(det(3))*sin(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*sin(sc(2))  + ...
     cos(det(3)) * sc(1)*cos(sc(3));
        fprintf('Detection vector is tangent to Earth surface.\n');
    else
        dplus = -sin(det(3))*cos(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*cos(sc(2))
  + ...
     sin(det(3))*sin(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*sin(sc(2))  + ...
     cos(det(3)) * sc(1)*cos(sc(3)) + sqrt(nabla);
        dminus = -sin(det(3))*cos(det(2)) *
 sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*cos(sc(2))  + ...
     sin(det(3))*sin(det(2)) * sc(1)*sin(sc(3))*sin(sc(2))  + ...
     cos(det(3)) * sc(1)*cos(sc(3)) - sqrt(nabla);
        if(dminus < 0 && dplus < 0)
            d = dplus;
        else
            if(dminus >= 0 && dplus >= 0)
            d = dminus;
            end
        end
        fprintf(['Detection vector intersects Earth surface, at
 distance of ' num2str(d) ' km.\n']);
    end
end
