Abstract
Within the field of energy production by the means of onshore and offshore wind turbines, the
drive for longer and faster spinning turbine blades leads to the need for better blade tip coatings
to prevent leading edge erosion and subsequent annual energy production losses. In the current
state of the art, accelerated testing of coating solutions is performed at rain erosion test facilities,
which collect information about coating performance based on pictures captured in situ. These
images are then annotated by a user with their subjective understanding of erosion damage.
Influence of annotator’s subjectivity on the coating evaluation is set out to be decreased by
introducing changes to the annotation workflow. The resulting erosion tracking methodology
provided the means of reducing the possibility of erosion omission while simultaneously reducing
the time requirement by ∼33%, compared against a conventional manual annotation procedure.
A novel technique of tracking erosion area progression highlighted the connection between user’s
subjectivity and tested specimen’s erosion performance, but further development is necessary
to fully remove user’s subjectivity from manual annotation. Both methodologies are publicly
available at [22].

Introduction
In the field of wind turbines, the demand for increased energy production is a driver of increasing
rotor sizes and power capacity for both onshore and offshore installations [9, 50]. Renewable
power production by means of wind turbines is expected to cover up to 50 percent of the
electricity market in the European Union by 2050 [20, 29]. This in turn introduces a new set of
challenges to be overcome with respect to various wind turbine components [26]. One of these
challenges is the resistance of the blade leading edges to erosion caused by presence of particles
in the air, predominately water droplets, hitting the leading edge. The high relative velocity
component Vrel reaches up to 100 m/s towards the blade tip [29] which causes blade tips to
erode the quickest. On Figure 1.1 an eroded blade tip is visible where the observed damage
has approximately been accumulating for a year.
Figure 1.1: Eroded leading edge of a wind turbine blade towards the blade tip [34]
The issue of blade erosion, all similar to the one presented on Figure 1.1, has been identified
in recent years as installed wind turbine blades started ageing and a drop in power production
was noticed. This was based on lower lift produced by eroded blades in addition to the increase
of required leading edge maintenance decreasing the Annual Energy Production (AEP) [18, 8].
Various studies performed by Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEM), or otherwise relevant
stakeholders have concluded the effects of rain erosion making up a significant portion of the
Operation & Maintenance (O&M) budget over a wind turbine lifetime [50, 31, 29].
With this problem identified at the wind turbine leading edge, various parties search for a
cost-effective mitigation or prevention solution for rain erosion and its effects on the aerody-
namic performance. The most common solution is manifested in a form of a paint coating, with
shells and tapes being less prevalent, where the majority of these systems are applied towards
the blade tip [15]. For the purposes of testing and validating such solutions, distinct methods
of quantifying and categorizing rain erosion and its effects have been developed [31, 29] where
the lifetime of a proposed protection system is the most crucial parameter [18] for evaluating
performance. The knowledge of the lifetime parameter for Wind Turbine Blades (WTBs) then
allows for a more bespoke and detailed maintenance plan, which is of utmost importance es-
pecially for offshore wind farms where access is heavily limited resulting in high O&M costs
[8, 18]. To estimate and quantify the solutions lifetime, various methods are currently being
employed and developed. For leading edge coatings specifically, accelerated rain erosion tests
are performed using water jet erosion facilities [43, 44], such as Single Point Impact Fatigue
Testers [24, 17] or Whirling Arm Rain Erosion Rigs [11, 7, 13, 14]. These methodologies which

allow for estimating coating solution lifetimes with accelerated testing take form in a variety of
configurations with varying experimental parameters. Currently, those differences in forms of
testing and experimental conditions lead to issues in comparing lifetime results obtained from
separate research campaigns [24], as even the most minuscule uncertainties in testing carry
over into final estimates. Additionally due to the nature of deterministic erosion tests that
only provide lifetime estimates of tested coating solutions, a comparison to realistic rainfall
events proves diﬀicult [32]. For a wind turbine in operation, the occurrence and accumulation
of erosion is dependent on site-specific parameters which are stochastic in nature, and highly
dependent on the local microclimate [7]. This in turn identifies the issue of correlating exper-
imentally obtained results against real world erosion as well as quantifying datasets resolved
with varying testing procedures.
For the purposes of closely studying rain-to-blade surface interactions, computationally in-
tensive numerical solutions are currently being employed. Methods such as Computational
Fluid Dynamics (CFD) or Finite Element Methods (FEM) [46, 4, 28, 10] provide insight into
the small scale erosion process and material interactions. Results obtained by such method-
ologies however prove diﬀicult to relate into a lifetime estimate of a coating, as scaling single
impact simulations into a realistic rainfall occurrences on a sample area representative of a
physical WTB would require CPU times unrealistic for even the shortest of timescales [5]. Cur-
rently, OEMs are known to use empirical models of extrapolating damage already accumulated
on the blades at the time of inspection to dictate maintenance schedules. Additionally, forms
of correlation between facility testing and real-life erosions are being employed [27] for the same
purpose. For the viable numerical testing, focus on single impact dynamics implies the use of a
flat coating surface which disregards the effect of curvature and roughness accumulated during
manufacturing in the rain-to-blade interaction [47]. As such, the use of numerical solutions
for resolution of coating lifetime or WTBs resistance to realistic occurrences of atmospheric
effects is out of reach. This in turn accentuates the importance of real-time erosion monitor-
ing on operational wind turbines as well as the need for reliable quantification methodologies
with respect to erosion results obtained by experimental means. Currently, efforts are being
made to correlate the aerodynamic changes induced by erosion patterns observed on real world
WTBs with a combination of CFD and wind tunnel testing. For this endeavor the focus lies
on constructing a database of leading-edge erosion patterns, where the aerodynamic losses are
attributed and validated to a specific erosion pattern. One of the goals of the discussed project
dubbed Leading Edge Roughness Categorization [37] is to make the obtained results public
to all OEMs. This will be done in order to establish a common terminology with respect to
erosion patterns and their expected losses during turbine operation. Overall, such a classifica-
tion database could in turn serve to help with correlating the various erosion patterns obtained
from various rain erosion facilities. Knowledge of the incurred aerodynamic losses would pro-
vide additional information for a more precise lifetime estimate. Since blade lifetime is a very
important parameter in O&M endeavors, all interested parties are motivated to decrease esti-
mate uncertainties as this ends up having a direct economic impact on turbine operation.
The introduction aims to outline the issue of rain erosion detection and quantification
obtained results from operating wind turbines and comment on the presence of these issues in
experimentally acquired datasets. The state of the art in the field of rain erosion testing is
then presented to provide the reader with necessary background with relation to the present
diﬀiculties of obtaining results with low uncertainty. All identified issues are lastly commented
upon, from where the thesis scope is defined.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.1. Erosion detection and quantification on real world turbines
Leading Edge Erosion (LEE) is one of the main causes for a decrease in AEP for individual
wind turbines in large wind turbine farms, as driven by the yearly occurrence of meteorological
effects such as rainfall, or other particulate aerosols in the atmosphere [9, 50]. The erosion
physically experienced by the blade in the form of increased surface roughness has in turn
adverse effects on the aerodynamic performance of the wind turbine resulting in lower power
production [16]. Specifically, the effect of erosion on the leading edge is considered to shorten
the lifespan of the affected turbine both from the budgetary and structural perspective, as the
blade’s structural cohesion is directly at risk when an eroded blade is left without maintenance
[36].
As such, the turbine operators responsible for the O&M of the wind turbine farm are
motivated to track and understand the progression of LEE on WTBs. By determining losses
incurred by erosion, a more accurate and eﬀicient maintenance plan can be developed to save on
expenses related to blade failures and AEP losses down the line. Currently, various non-invasive
methods are in use for the purposes of inspecting eroded blades. Both internal defects such as
layer delamination as well as surface damage can be tracked with drone equipment, which can
visually detect damages by the means of flying inside or outside of the inspected wing [3, 27].
With visual methods based on pictures or remote sensing various turbine manufacturers take
it upon themselves to track and quantify the damage present on the blades. This has an effect
on the resulting erosion quantification, where the appraisal of damage tends to vary due to
subjectivity of user’s evaluation and quality of obtained datasets containing the blade erosion
information. Such an issue has been investigated and documented in the IEA Wind Task 46
report [34], where anonymous participants with a varying degree of experience in the field of
grading blade erosion damages have been tasked with grading damages visible on given images.
Presented on Figure 1.2 is the result of such survey on a damage classification grading scale for
LEE.
Figure 1.2: Results of the draft erosion classification system test, taken from the IEA Wind
Task 46 report [34] figure 3-2 page 20
Based on Figure 1.2, personnel with varying technical backgrounds were tasked with grading
the LEE damage based on provided photographs. A guideline damage classification method-

ology was provided to them, but the inherent uncertainty of erosion markdown leads into a
presence of subjectivity. For the purposes of statistical analysis, the median and variance of
the scores assigned by each participant was calculated. With this, conclusions were made with
regards to the provided classification system, where high variance statistics meant subjectivity
is indeed a large factor thus dictating a need for a more robust grading system. Such differences
in user evaluation and grading of erosions could induce differences in maintenance schedules
and O&M cost estimates as well as AEP estimations based on the estimated remaining life-
time of a leading edge. As such this issue remains an unknown factor, as there is currently
no objective way of providing a quantitative overview with respect to the costs experienced as
a direct effect of an incorrect lifetime estimate. With regards to grading erosion, the issue of
subjectivity comes up again in the context of manual detection of erosion on a coating specimen
tested experimentally in Rain Erosion Testing (RET) facilities.
More robust quantification guidelines for damages present on real wind turbine blades have
been proposed by various stakeholders concerned with solving the rain erosion quandary [39,
38, 34, 23, 9] in order to reduce the subjectivity concern. To achieve such a goal the proposed
methodologies, focus on following a rigid guideline with either visual examples for guidance,
or a mathematical model translating erosions into volumetric deficits which aims to decrease
ambiguity.
With regards to accurate reconstruction of surface level defects for the purpose of estimating
remaining lifetime of a physical WTB, the eroded topology can be extracted using a drone
solution in the field [27], or more commonly after the blade has been taken off the turbine [16,
37]. Multiple methodologies such as laser inline scanners [2, 30] translate the physical blade
into a 3D point cloud dataset, where the erosion information can then be directly compared to
the original shape of the WTB as provided by the manufacturer. With this direct comparison
a more accurate lifetime estimate is facilitated, provided the site-specific climate is available.
Ground based erosion inspection on WTBs leads to a dataset which provides a higher level of
detail than a methodology used in field erosion quantification [12], as logically a closer inspection
of the incurred damages lends itself well to the later stages of analysis. For field-based solutions
such as ground based observations or drone assisted data collection, the information gathering
process is influenced by the camera and lighting present at the site [12, 42]. These factors,
introduced into the collected dataset might initiate uncertainties with regards to the damage
present on the leading edge, which can influence the remaining lifetime parameter just as easily
as a user’s subjective erosion classification. Such uncertainties pertain to small initiations of
erosion being lost due to insuﬀicient camera quality. Irregardless of the applied methodology
the obtained information about the erosion patterns present on the WTBs requires further
investigation, in order to correlate the extracted erosion patterns into AEP losses experienced
by the wind turbine and a remaining lifetime estimate. This correlation is undertaken through
numerical methodologies and simulations [16, 21, 48] or predictive and autonomous workflows
[1, 49, 33]. The order of magnitude with respect to the accuracy of the resulting loss estimates
is heavily dependent on the complexity of the applied collection and verification methodology.
This relates back to the issue where due to the scope of the erosion issue and the inherent
differences in data procurement and analysis as performed by various interested parties, no
consistent monitoring and AEP loss scheme has been established although currently progress
is being made [34].
As such, this variety in approaches used for tackling the rain erosion
issue make it diﬀicult to directly compare results obtained from different sources, where the
subjectivity of each observed damage introduces additional uncertainty to the quantification.
These issues directly translate into the field of RET, both in terms of the results comparison
between facilities and user’s personal understanding of what constitutes an erosion damage.

1.2. State of the art in the field of rain erosion testing
With regards to development of protective coatings in service of extending wind turbine life-
times, the estimation of a specific solutions lifetime is conventionally performed experimentally
which necessitates a physical testing facility incorporating the most important aspects of the
rain erosion mechanisms. In the field of RET, the complexity and controllable parameters of a
device are related to the end goal purpose of testing. A machine for testing erosion on aircraft
applications will have a different set of requirements when compared to a machine designed
for eroding WTB coatings [19]. As such, the majority of experimental testing methodologies
for inducing erosion are outside of the scope where this section chooses to outline the most
pertinent testing methods related to the RET facilities. In the field of WTB coatings, the
complexity of an experimental device is directly related to the decision of keeping the sample
stationary or in motion.
For purposes of testing rain particle-to-material interactions stationary sample setups such
as interrupted water jet or solid projectile testers are often applied [43, 44, 24, 17]. Experimen-
tal configurations such as these rely on energy transfer from the accelerated medium with a
controlled speed. In its current state of the art, such way of material erosion provides reliable
results which are quantified with dimensionless number of impacts N0 [51, 6]. This leads to a
straightforward quantification methodology, where an automatic system can be responsible for
performing and collecting a dataset of pictures and experimental parameters. All the collected
information is then processed manually, and the coating lifetime can be extracted. Due to its
simplicity and controllability of testing these methodologies benefit from very low user subjec-
tivity, as a single velocity sample in testing can be closely monitored. The central downside
present in sequential impact machines is the time required in order to produce a large enough
dataset, in addition to droplet dynamics and heat dissipation issues which cause additional
delays before a lifetime for a specific material is estimated [45]. Since only one particle speed
can be used on the tested sample and accurate mass loss notation is required for each observed
erosion stage, data accumulation for a reliable lifetime estimate is very cumbersome to acquire
[45]. For coating materials which are in use currently, one would expect the first sight of erosion
to occur after approximately 1500 separate impacts at a given velocity and a specific particle
size. Even in the most conservative estimate, if one is to provide a viable Stress and Number of
impacts curve from which a coating lifetime is evaluated, one would expect to test a minimum of
20 velocities. By taking the aforementioned considerations with respect to recording mass loss,
droplet dynamics and heat dissipation one can expect to collect 10 measurements per minute.
Thus, one coating solution test would end up taking over 40 hours to collect. This estimate does
not include any errors or data analysis further prolonging the testing period, and the total time
cost is why single impact machines are not used for large scale testing campaigns. Overall, the
stationary sample setups are good for investigating small scale interactions as their results can
be compared to numerical methodologies and analyzed. However, due to the aforementioned
time investment required in order to equate a coatings performance to a real-world scenario
these methods are only applied by the industry for purposes of developing coating solutions
without estimating their lifetime. This is because a single impact testing rig will always be
cheaper to construct, run and maintain than a full-scale distributed impact facility, if only
simple material interactions are desired to be investigated.
In order to increase the number of water particle impacts on the sample in turn greatly de-
creasing the time needed for testing, the Whirling Arm technique is universally applied. Such
devices take form as a wheel and jet or distributed impact whirling arms [11, 7, 13, 14, 44].
The impact energy is provided by the sample moving at a constant speed while the particle
relies on gravitational forces in order to intercept the sample path. A whirling arm wheel and

The composition of a RET facility testing specimen resembles closely a configuration of a full

and after mounting a reference image of the un-eroded surface is captured.
Parameters such as the rotational speed of the mounted samples, water flow rate from the
needles and their gauge and the duration of the test run directly impact the erosion rate of the
tested specimen. Usually, a coating solution is expected to be tested at a range of rotational
speeds, requiring multiple coating solution specimens. This practice is performed in order to
cover a wider range of velocities in turn serving to provide a better coverage of velocities and
increase robustness of the lifetime estimate for the tested coating. For some coatings, testing at
multiple RPM is the only way to receive any viable results as high impact resilience or unusual
erosion progression might get into the way of accurate data acquisition.
Throughout the runtime of a test of a coating solution at the specified experimental param-
eters, the distributed rain field is turned off and the test is interrupted at predetermined time
intervals in order to make use of an in-line camera present within the testing chamber. During
this testing intermission the rotating disc and the specimens are slowed down from operational
RPM to 3 RPM, which facilitates image capture. These pictures are how the RET facility
documents the progression of erosion on the tested samples, meaning that each image capture
becomes the only source of information with respect to the sample’s erosion resilience. The
in-line camera captures the entire specimen at once during the blade slowdown at a predeter-
mined resolution and with the help of diffused LED lighting. Due to the camera being 1.5m
away from the captured specimen, optical magnification is used to frame the image subject
correctly. Figure 1.5 showcases the image capturing system installed at the DTU Risø RET

loss and the criteria at which a sample is considered damaged are the same. For the proper
lifetime evaluation, the established conventions related to what is classified as erosion damage
are explored in the next subsection.
1.2.2
Wind turbine rain erosion test practices
For the relevant testing inside of facilities designed to erode and quantify the lifetime of a
coating solution, the importance of providing replicable and comparable results is paramount.
In order to aid in the process of erosion testing, various recommended practices have been
established throughout the years. An important distinction is made here, where the presented
erosion quantification implementations are not considered as standards but rather as guidelines
to be followed. This speaks to the level of maturity in the field of rain erosion quantification
and its uncertainties, where since no concrete standards are available the workflows have room

to improve and change. Even with this fact, it is in the best interest of every facility owner to
adhere to the recommended practices, as it makes direct comparisons with results obtained in
other facilities easier. As the subject matter of this thesis is focused on improving the quan-
tification methodologies for LEE, the relevant practices are presented since they will provide
insight into the widely accepted methodology concerning erosion detection.
The most recent recommended practice, which is built upon the practices presented in the
ASTM G73 - 10 [6] is the DNVGL-RP-0171 [13] for experimental testing of blade erosion pro-
tection systems. This document explicitly covers result processing obtained by a whirling arm
distributed impact testing facilities. Additionally, DNVGL-RP-0573 [14] provides insight into
the evaluation of LEE and delamination of blade protection systems. The practices presented
in DNVGL-RP-0171 take into account the Whirling Arm rain erosion facilities as the one de-
veloped by R&D [40]. Such facilities vary from stationary sample testers by introducing a rain
field, allowing for testing using varying impact velocities during one test run. As opposed to
the conventional way of quantifying sample lifetime based on mass loss and impact number
N(t), an impacts per square meter N/m2 definition is used. This change in the quantification
methodology was motivated by the increase of sample lengths and coating solutions resilience
to erosion. With those parameters increasing, the move to a numerical estimate of damage is
based on the time each individual erosion test would end up taking. For long resistant samples
one would risk spending more time measuring mass loss as well as removing and securing the
specimens to a test rig rather than having the erosion test running, which would be unsustain-
able as a way of testing coating solutions.
More importantly for the subject of this thesis, the recommended testing practice goes over
the definitions of failure modes as well as clarifying what constitutes a valid point which goes
on to determining a coatings lifetime performance. These guidelines and how they can be inter-
preted impact the manual markdowns done by the industry, leaning into the aforementioned
subjectivity problem when estimating solution lifetimes. As such the next subsection focuses on
outlining the recommended erosion damage definitions and how these feed into possible biases.
1.2.3
Sources of subjectivity in manual markdown procedures
The DNVGL-RP-0171 recommended practice specifies that two distinct occurrences during a
progression of erosion are of note. Either of these definitions can be used to determine the
solutions lifetime, as marking down how many impacts need to occur before the damage occurs
is what dictates coating resilience. Because of this dependence on a precise markdown to spec-
ified failure, any subjectivity in accurate notation can lead to a knock-on effect when providing
a lifetime estimate. The resulting effect of deviating on even a single occurrence of damage is
further discussed in section 5.1.
The first important definition of a stage in the erosion progression is the end of incubation
period. An incubation period is defined as the time in which the coating is operating up to
specification. This is the most common definition used by the industry. The focus therefore lies
on defining an exposure time until damage on the outer surface of a test specimen is observ-
able. Since this damage is determined to happen at a specific time instance by means of visual
acquisition, there are many vectors which can introduce false positives or subjectivity into the
performed markdown. For example, any manufacturing defects or damage in the undercoating
could cause the topmost layer to appear on the surface which could be falsely identified as an
end of incubation at the specified velocity. Moreover, failure modes such as material cracks
or abrasion do not directly fulfil the end of incubation definition, but their occurrence on a
specimen can very easily be subjectively misconstrued as a positive when in fact it is a false
9

positive. This is why picture quality of the captured datasets is of utmost importance, in order
to keep all uncertainties as low as possible. As such, the primary objective of analyzing all
collected photographs is to determine the locations of damage initiations where all other pixel
information does not directly influence the evaluation of lifetime.
The second definition as defined by the recommended practice is the time instance until
breakthrough to the specimen substrate is achieved. For some coatings, the progression from
end of incubation into breakthrough might happen at the same time instance, due to all dam-
age occurring on the specimen underlayer until the top coating is suddenly removed in a larger
chunk. This type of erosion damage has very little in terms of subjectivity, as a large topcoat
removal is always easy to observe no matter the picture quality. In the collected test datasets,
the final photograph always denotes the specimen at its most eroded state which usually depicts
multiple erosions progressing past the breakthrough point. This information is currently not
used to any benefit, as defining end of incubation is the industry standard.
No matter the medium used to damage a test object’s surface or its total area, the expected
progression of a coating specimen ends up being split into three distinct regions. The total size
of these regions is heavily dependent on the experimental conditions induced during testing,
with parameters such as droplet size and impact velocity having the biggest influence. In order
to deal with this variability on a case-by-case basis, the DNVGL practice specifies that the
used definitions of damage are to be clearly specified in order to allow for easier reproducibil-
ity against results from other facilities. As an example of a specimen’s erosion progression,

velocity component, in turn decreasing the speed at which erosion progresses. This means that
points towards specimen root tend to only have initiated into the linear progression before the
test run was finished. Since only the end of incubation occurrences are deemed important for
coating evaluation, the rest of the information contained within the dataset is disregarded.

1.3. Identified issues within rain erosion quantification
Various issues are present within the field of rain erosion and its effects of wind turbine op-
eration, where this thesis will leverage access to the state-of-the art RET facility to procure
datasets representing the best obtainable results. Each issue is discussed below.
• Currently, the quantification of erosion on blade coatings as obtained by means of ac-
celerated rain tests in a RET facility is performed manually with visual confirmation of
impingement. This introduces subjectivity into the extracted information and data anal-
ysis, in addition to being cumbersome and time consuming. As such a development of a
more streamlined manual solution is desired, whereby improving the erosion quantifica-
tion methodology the above-mentioned issues can be minimized.
• Various erosion quantification methods can be applied in order to produce lifetime esti-
mates. Some of these methodologies are available for the purposes of this thesis, where
each of them ends up approaching the identification of point erosions differently. These
markdown techniques therefore need to be evaluated in order to provide a more compre-
hensive overview with respect to their best quantities which could then be incorporated
into the new solution for erosion quantification.
• With the current state of the art methodology focusing purely on determining the time
taken until the end of incubation, the rest of the information contained within the datasets
signifying the progression of erosion is left unused. With the goal of further reducing
perceived subjectivity in manual markdowns this information could be applied to the
benefit of the entire erosion markdown methodology.

Research of current erosion detection

The goal of improving the manual methodology used on datasets collected at various RET
facilities begins with an exploratory analysis of the currently developed LEE quantification
methodologies. As outlined in chapter 1 many avenues of damage detection are viable for iden-
tification of erosion damage on real turbine blades. Within the field of rain erosion testing, the
viable methodology pool is much smaller. This section’s focus lies on presenting and discussing
the accessible workflows for the purposes of drawing concrete conclusions to be applied in latter
sections during development and testing of the improved methodology. Written below is a brief
outline of each investigated method.
1. The most common and robust way of obtaining information about a coating’s performance
is by manual means. For this, a methodology called ”RET Point Erosion image analyser”
has been provided developed in the MATLAB programming language by Nicolai Frost-
Jensen Johansen [24]. The purpose of this methodology is to facilitate extraction of point
erosion information in the form of point erosion locations and corresponding velocities
from photographs captured within a Whirling Arm testing facility.
2. A semi-automatic Machine Learning (ML) assisted methodology called ”RandomForrest
annotation” is being developed in the Python programming language by Jamie E. Si-
mon. This methodology facilitates use of ML training and deployment of the subsequent
model for detection of point erosions. In its current form the method does not facilitate
extraction of point erosion locations and velocities. As such this methodology will be
investigated with respect to the viability of less complex ML deployments.
3. A fully developed ML code base was made available for inspection by the IFAM Fraunhofer
company employee Jannes Adam. The purpose of this investigation is to scope out the
full capabilities of low uncertainty machine vision, as the tool in its current form does
not directly specialize on erosion detection, having been developed as a flexible ML for a
number of applications. This tool does not directly facilitate extraction of point erosion
locations and velocities and as such a Python surrogate script has been written to bridge
the gap towards erosion markdown as part of this thesis.
4. An online tool used for manual markdown was made available by Wind Power Labs
company. This methodology has been co-developed by Nicolai Frost-Jensen Johansen and
is expected to provide very similar results to the aforementioned local manual method.
The similarity continues with this method being able to ingest images from RET facilities
as an input and output point erosion locations and velocities.

5.1. Manual erosion identification
Currently the quantification of a tested coating’s resilience to erosion caused by rain impinge-
ment, and the evaluation of a coating lifetime is performed manually on the datasets collected
through experimental means. This entails keeping track of the relevant parameters related to
the rain field and testing conditions and individually going through the collected photographs
which depict a progression of erosion damage on a tested coating specimen.
The available manual method has been used for the purposes of evaluating the obtained
results in [7, 24, 41], which qualifies it as a viable method of working with experimentally
obtained information. This methodology has been defined as tool 1. The point erosion identi-
fication procedure begins by collecting the picture progression of erosion with set experimental
parameters. With the picture information consisting of three image series corresponding to
each blade put into the RET facility, the dataset is processed manually by way of visual as-
sessment. If desired, light preprocessing of the individual images can be performed to enhance
image quality and allow for easier detection of point erosion. This is done with software such
as Adobe Lightroom in which one can change the image contrast, sharpness or color balance
in order to bring out more information out of the photographs. Essentially, the developed
manual marking methodology only relies on the users input to provide the coordinate locations
of the accumulated points of damage on the testing specimen. In order to discern between
actual damage and image errors such as noise or blur, the manual marking is performed by
comparative means as in the currently investigated picture is compared to the previous one in
quick succession which greatly assists in assessing the desired information. With the ability to
always compare to the previous picture, the crux of the manual marking lies on the subjective
ability of the user to detect the end of incubation and consequently providing the [x,y] position
of erosion. To aid in this process, high definition screens with accurate color gamut’s are often
used to facilitate the visual process of detecting pixel changes. The workflow used for manual
markdown of test specimen is visually presented on Figure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Manual markdown procedure workflow flowchart
In its current form this methodology facilitates the extraction of erosion positions and times,
meaning that further work is required to fit a power curve to the VN information as well as to
provide a lifetime estimate. Since the methodology itself lacks functionality of these steps, this

adds additional time to the processing of picture information output from RET facilities. With
an average test run containing 25 images, the user is expected to spend at the minimum 2.5
hours total to produce a VN curve for one test run. Total time taken varies heavily based on
the number of erosion points which need to be tracked as well as the performed preprocessing
of images and data analysis.
Irregardles of the time taken and the diligence of the user, the obtained information is
recommended to adhere to the practices set forth by DNVGL-RP-0171 [13]. This requires the
conversion of point erosion positions and time intervals into velocities in [m/s] for specific ero-
sion positions against the number of impacts in [N/mm2] to which a power fit is then applied,
as outlined in section 3.2. For testing of coating solutions, a performance curve such as the one
presented on Figure 3.1, is the intermediate step of defining the leading-edge protection perfor-
mance leading into lifetime estimate. On the presented example figure, the progression of the
erosion front is well behaved which is the best-case scenario for manual marking. Otherwise,
the evaluation of a coating solution can be made harder by surface imperfections and problems
related to the quality of captured pictures. These issues are dealt with on a per-dataset basis,
especially when dealing with datasets from different testing facilities or of different coating
solutions.
Beyond the time and data quality requirement for accurate notation of erosion points, the
issue in focus of this thesis is the subjective perspective of the user which affects the accuracy
of markdown positions in manual erosion identification.
This issue, which could introduce
errors into the resulting solution analysis has been identified to have two sources. The first
source of error is due to the subjective nature in human decision marking, meaning what a
person might consider damage. As presented on Figure 1.2, Figure 1.7 and discussed in [34,
25] various people might evaluate the same point erosions differently which down the line can
lead to errors in markdown. As one gains experience in marking and evaluating damages on
photographs, this error source is mitigated and thus the pursuit of decreasing its influence
is not in focus of this thesis. For the second source of errors, is the user’s failure to notice
the minuscule changes between two photographs or a false positive markdown of a damage
non-representative of an incubation end. Currently this issue is mitigated by comparing two
neighboring pictures in quick succession, as such a process allows for a better recognition of the
changes on photographs which could indicate a positive onset of a point erosion. However, due
to the scale of the features desired to be detected which present with the slightest of pixel value
changes, the user could find themselves in a situation of either overlooking a forming erosion
point or mislabeling a forming crack. In fact, the desired incubation end points are usually so
unnoticeable when being sought after, no visual example of such a tiny feature can be presented
in this thesis. To illustrate this issue, point erosion onset images are presented on Figure 7.8
in the Appendix. What can be visualized however is the negative effect a user oversight or
mislabeling on the resulting VN curve and subsequently a lifetime estimate. This is presented
on Figure 5.2, where the lifetime was estimated based on parameters from Table 3.1.

5.2. Semi-automatic erosion identification methodologies
Compared to the straightforward nature of the manual marking methodology, the semi-automatic
workflows can vary from simple improvements such as on the spot picture preprocessing with
algorithmic functions, all the way to complex feature recognition assisted by machine learning.
5.2.1
Simple algorithmic functions
For the picture processing, the manual method mostly relied on enhancing image exposure and
using conventionally available methods of picture processing such as adjusting image contrast
in Adobe Lightroom.
There exist however a variety of filters which can be applied to the
photographs in order to exasperate the features present on an otherwise uniform photograph.
Sharpness filtering can be used to more clearly define feature edges, the use of a Canny Edge
method allows for quick extraction of adverse gradient changes and a Sobel Edge method
introduces directional feature detection. The utility of such filters is showcased against the
original image in Figure 5.3.
All mentioned filters do not use any ML models, depending
instead on algorithmic operations such as feature convolution or gradient exasperation.
Figure 5.3: Example filters applied to a photograph with visible erosion damage
For easier visualization of the filter’s effectiveness, the features were already visible in the
original image and could be detected visually by a user. In all, the applied filters serve to
highlight features in different ways.
Where sharpness focuses the image removing blurring
around feature edges, and Canny / Sobel Edge filters focus highlighting gradient changes on
the specimen surface. As such the utility of preprocessing is not immediately apparent, since
a qualified user would be able to discern erosion features on the image without any aids. This
means that the application of filters on captured photographs would be the most useful for
detecting the earliest onset of erosion damages. There isn’t a filter method which reveals the
desired end of incubation however, as the minimal material loss and the slight change in pixel
values elude even the strongest of filters. Therefore, the assistance provided by the filtering
would come down to applying a combination of mild filters in order to resolve the wanted
point erosions, as an example combining a Sharpness filter with Canny Edge might make
erosion features stand out more. A caveat of such a solution is the issue of variability between
datasets, where depending on the lighting conditions or pixel density on the photographs could
have an impact on the resulting filtered image. Since the discussed processing functions produce
instantaneous results, their implementation would not delay a markdown workflow and as such
there are no time related downsides for their implementation. Overall, by including the user
in the image processing workflow qualified decisions can be made with regards to the desired
filter strengths thus ensuring maximum usefulness during the markdown process.
Such an
implementation of algorithmic treatment will provide the best results, as the algorithmic filters
currently do not provide results with low enough uncertainty.

5.2.2
Semi-automatic erosion markdown
The usage of algorithmic filters is also the cornerstone of erosion markdown methodology which
employs a degree of ML such as the investigated ”RandomForest annotator”. This methodology
has been defined as tool 2. Filters are applied in order to extract distinct features out of an
image which are used to train a ML model optimized for detection of point erosions based on
perceived gradient changes on an image. The crux of the method relies on the user manually
marking the areas of interest on a sequence of images depicting point erosions of varying sever-
ities, from which relevant feature information is extracted for the purpose of training a decision
tree of the ML method. For this semi-automatic methodology, a random forest decision tree
is applied made possible by the sklearn package in the Python programming language. The
Python shell written around the ML functionality facilitates markdown of regions containing
point erosions on pictures which are used to then train the random forrest classifier model. As
such the user goes through the collected pictures one by one, marking the areas of interest on
each photograph and training the model. Depending on the number of features and their size
the decision tree model can end up being computationally heavy, especially when features from
varying datasets are introduced which can extend the time needed for each training cycle or
feature prediction. The model file which is the output product of the methodology can then be
applied for feature prediction in other datasets, where the end goal is to leverage the prediction
to detect features indicative of an incubation end, which could’ve been otherwise omitted by a
manual markdown.
The whole process of annotating a dataset is about as time consuming as the manual mark-
down method when one dataset is considered. Such a set usually constitutes of an average 25
pictures per blade with a varying number of areas indicative of erosions to be marked dependent
on the material and experimental properties. The expectation of a ML assisted methodology
would be that the time invested in training a robust model would then pay dividends, as feature
prediction on other photographs or dataset would not require user interaction. However, in its
current state this methodology does not output any information which could be used for anal-
ysis of a coatings performance such as VN curve data. Currently it only contains the means of
training and predicting of features meaning additional analysis would have to be performed in
order to translate the predictions into positions and velocities. As such, results obtained from
this semi-automatic method are discussed with respect to the accuracy of feature prediction
and its usefulness for markdown of point erosions.
Detection accuracy
The obtainable accuracy of predictions is very dependent on the extensiveness of the train-
ing performed for the model. At a minimum level of model training which includes manually
”painting” regions of interest on a set of eroded pictures from one test run, the subsequent
feature prediction only works for the initial image dataset the model was training on. This
problem is a product of issues present with intensity-based feature detection methods, as those
are not robust enough when applied on a different sample set. The uncertainty of possible pre-
dictions is high as well, making it unsuitable for the resulting erosion positions to be considered
accurate for the purposes of determining blade coordinates and velocities. The requirement for
high precision is motivated by the markdowns ability to influence the lifetime estimate as show-
cased on Figure 5.2. In order to increase the certainty of feature prediction the model training
would have to be very extensive, where a large number of features have been characterized
with the aforementioned algorithmic filters. As an example of results which are possible with
a moderate training dataset containing 25 images, constituting a 1/3 coverage of blades for a
dataset, the output of the semi-automatic methodology is presented on Figure 5.4.

with regards to the usefulness of determining a coatings performance. This similarity extends
to the time investment needed for a singular dataset, that being approximately 2.5 hours. The

5.5. Research summary
The main takeaways for each method are outlined below.
1. The manual methodology facilitates the markdown of each point erosion where the user’s
subjectivity has the largest influence on the obtainable VN curves and thus lifetime
estimates. It does provide good insight into how to better keep track of all point erosions,
and being the most straightforward methodology its principles of tracking erosions on
images are the best way of going about manually marking the damages.
2. The semi-automatic ML methodology fails to provide any meaningful results, as a large
portion of time needs to be spent on training a model ,which currently would still provide
results with unsatisfactory uncertainties all which would have the user’s biases carried
over. This leads to the possible results having lower accuracy than the ones obtainable
with straightforward manual markdown. The preprocessing applied to each image to
extract all the features is helpful for aiding the user during manual markdown, and the
use of these processing methods for this benefit needs to be investigated further.
3. The advanced ML methodology requires additional time investment and knowledge, which
in the end serves to provide results comparable to ones obtained with manual mark-
downs. With proper development and model training a ML methodology could stand to
outperform a user’s manual markdown, however for the time frame of this thesis such
an investigation is not feasible. Focus therefore lies on improving the quality of results
obtainable manually by a user, and an ML implementation is disregarded.
4. The online markdown tool has been developed to provide the same results as the manual
methodology, thus users subjectivity has the highest influence. This method keeps track
of different damage types and provides a better way of inspecting each damage up close.
Tracking each damage type would only add time and uncertainty to the manual markdown
process, but having a good way of inspecting each damaged area up close could stand to
increase user’s confidence in accurately marking down each point erosion. This feature is
to be kept in mind during the development of the improved methodology.

Through working with the locally available methods, the most time consuming issue to be

Based on the performed analysis in section 6.2, all the points of erosion across datasets were
considered to fall within the end of incubation definition as outlined in [13] with a degree of
subjectivity. To pinpoint the positions and velocities of those points thus establishing the VN
curves, information present on the collected images has been used. Irregardless of the method
or the user, after the location of an erosion point has been established its precise position and
a small area around it was considered to have provided the maximum amount of value for the
purposes of specimen evaluation. This is false however, since the picture sequence also defines
a progression of each damage point as an increase to its area over time. As such this is related
back to Figure 1.6 where for each marked down erosion point all images end up depicting that
points progression from incubation into linear damage and non-linear damage for points at high
velocity radial positions.

tracks each subjectively marked down erosion location by the increase to its area progression

have mass loss progression similar to the one presented in Figure 1.6. For tracked points which

6.5. Discussion
Based on the results provided in section 6.2, the improved manual markdown methodology
outlined on Figure 6.2 manages to leverage the methodologies scoped in chapter 5 to facilitate
a faster markdown of erosion points while providing results comparable to a different manual
method. This can be substantiated by discussing the coeﬀicient of variance of each obtained

methodology form Figure 6.6 as a ’forwards’ methodology since for each point erosion the area

with a goal of subjectivity removal. The effectiveness of the novel methodology is tied to the
erosion progression of the tested specimen and in turn the experimental parameters used for
testing. In cases where the subject’s erosion progression is clearly observable by the human
eye, meaning little ambiguity is present for tracking each point, the resulting power fits and
lifetime estimates fall closely within each other. When each erosion has its area subsequently
tracked, a degree of subjectivity is removed which ends up providing a power fit which could be
considered to be an objective interpretation of a coating’s erosion resistance. This was the case
for datasets two and three. For datasets one, four and five in which each found point erosion
had a higher degree of ambiguity as to its definition of being classified as an end of incubation.
Such an issue can stem from the blade material having unclear erosion progression or having
a high uncertainty with respect to the exact time at which an incubation end occurs. This
meant that the resulting power fitting and lifetime evaluations lied further apart. As such,
these datasets would have benefited from having their subjectivity removed in order to obtain
an objective lifetime estimate. However for these sets, although tracking the area progression
of each point did end up shifting each individual erosion point with a higher value of ∆tlinear
the resulting power fittings could not have been called objective as the area tracking drove
the lifetime estimates away from each other. Overall, this highlights a connection between
the individual specimen’s erosion progression and how its interpretation affects the obtained
lifetime estimate. This connection is substantiated by the derived outcomes from section 6.2
and section 6.4 both.

Conclusion
The performed literature study provided insight into the field of rain erosion quantification
for both field measurements and concerning the use of testing facilities. From there focus was
placed on outlining the specifics of testing blade coating specimen at rain erosion testing facil-
ities, and how user’s subjectivity carries over as an uncertainty into the final lifetime estimate
of the tested coating solution. Based on this information, key areas were identified in which
improvements could be made to decrease the amount of bias a user introduces. This in turn
dictated the thesis scope, which set out to investigate how to address the named issues through
research of currently available methodologies and techniques.
Through the investigation, most beneficial elements of each methodology were pointed out
on the basis of which the improved manual markdown methodology was developed. By com-
paring the results obtainable with the new manual method to results obtained by an older
methodology, it was determined that the new methodology provides similar results in terms of
lifetime estimates and the amount of subjectivity introduced by the user. This is achieved while
keeping better track of each erosion occurrence, decreasing the likelihood of omitting a point
erosion. The new methodology provides these results with an ∼33% reduction to the time
needed from importing test run images to obtaining a VN curve and a lifetime estimate, when
compared against the conventional manual markdown method. Statistically, no correlation was
found between point distributions of the new and old methodology.
Based on the thorough analysis of point erosion markdown techniques, a new novel method
of tracking erosion progression on test specimen was proposed and developed. This methodol-
ogy leveraged the previously unused information within the collected images in order to improve
point erosion times of occurrence based on an assumption of linear mass loss progression. By
applying this novel technique, the goal was to provide a markdown result fully in line with
the definitions outlined in the relevant DNVGL recommended practice. As such this workflow,
when applied to the analyzed datasets, served to highlight a connection between user’s sub-
jectivity and erosion progression of test specimen. For datasets depicting erosion which have
visible damage progression, the novel method reduced the subjectivity further which benefited
the final lifetime estimate. For more uncertain datasets in which user’s subjectivity is higher the
novel method provided no benefit. Statistically the novel technique decreased the coeﬀicient of
covariance, however this decrease was not attributed to the decrease of subjectivity.
Overall, the identified areas of improvement have been addressed, where the new improved
methodology produces adequate results at a lower uncertainty with respect to erosion omission
in addition to requiring less time. Leveraging the information contained within the pictures
for area tracking has been introduced, but its effectiveness was not fully realized. To further
investigate the novel way of damage area tracking, a bigger study is required involving mul-
tiple users tracking erosion points on the same datasets in order to reduce the uncertainties
encountered in this thesis.

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
7.1. Future work
During the research and development of both methodologies in chapter 6 additional techniques
which would serve to improve the final markdown were discovered, but not included in the
thesis due to being outside of the scope or time constraints.
For the novel damage area tracking methodology, its usefulness was limited due to the
distinct decision to structure the decision tree as outlined on Figure 6.7. Most investigated
points ended up taking the top branch of that tree, since erosion damage with clear mass loss
was visible. For these points, no ∆tlinear time was either added or removed. This assumes
that the observed damage occurred exactly when observed, where the true occurrence might
have happened just after the previous photograph was taken, thus being in the gap between
the two images. As such, this can have an impact on all tested coatings where time between
images varies between 10 to 20 minutes and the calibration test runs with take a picture every
40 minutes. For further work therefore, a new decision tree would be considered which does
not dismiss a point if mass loss damage is visible but instead continues to track the linear
area progression. By doing so it is expected that the uncertainty related to the precise time of
occurrence would be further decreased, benefiting all the points.
During literature research, a paper concerning the impact of blade tip geometry on the
energy transfer between a rain droplet and coating solution was read [28], which inspires an
addition to the developed manual markdown methodology. As a tendency, rain droplet impacts
away from the leading-edge line transfer less energy which is attributed to lower erosion rates.
For further work therefore, the position of each erosion can be not only tracked along the blade
on the x-axis but along the specimen y-axis height as well. By doing so a distribution can be
applied to each point which prioritizes points closer to the center line which would have an
impact on how the coating solution performance is evaluated. One would be able to produce
a VN curve which only takes points within a certain threshold away from the center of the
investigated specimen.
The full benefit of such a methodology cannot be predicted, as no
trends have been identified with respect to the height importance of each point erosion during
this thesis.
