Abstract 
Thanks to innovations in the fields of computer science and telecommunication, 
automation in train control and operation has become a global trend within railway 
industry. Success of automated train operation (ATO) in railway systems has proven 
that it is feasible in technical and safety perspective.  
Conversion from conventional lines to driverless railway systems is however a new 
trial in railway history. This study addresses on the feasibility of such conversion on 
different type of railway systems. In the first part, mandatory and optional 
equipment needed based on the operational requirement and safety concerns are 
investigated. Organizational impacts of the conversion on the railway enterprises are 
researched. Guides for railway operators on staffing model design, redeployment 
plan and the strategies on the negotiation with unions are proposed in this study.  
Finally, a thorough feasibility analysis entails the impact mentioned above is 
performed for three railway systems – Kaohsiung Mass Rapid Transit, Copenhagen 
S-tog, and a hypothetical mainline railway. Potential uncertainties of a driverless 
train conversion project are evaluated within the sensitivity analysis and risk 
assessment and the approach for mitigating the risks are suggested for each case.

Chapter 1. 
Introduction 
1.1.  Motivation and research objectives 
Automation in railway industry had been developed for decades and applied in 
various fields such as revenue collection, signaling, and train operation. Among these 
fields, automated train operation is one of the amazing breakthroughs ever in the 
railway history. Behind the realization of driverless train, innovation of computer 
science and telecommunication has been major driving forces of it.   
To date, driverless railway has become a growing trend in worldwide railway 
industry. As of July 2016, there are 55 fully automated metro lines in 37 cities around 
the world, operating in total 803 km, a 14% increase in km over 2014 figures. The 
projection is that by 2025 there will be 2,300 km of automated metro lines in 
operation (UITP Observatory of Automated Metros, 2016).  
It is therefore interesting to investigate the reasons behind the “fashion” spreading 
over the railway industry and the real impact of driverless train both to the railway 
operators and the users, which is the society1. By understanding these factors, the 
consequence of driverless train is better understand and could be provided as a 
foundation for future driverless train projects.  
The research target of this study is selected as the railway systems switching from 
driver-controlled to fully automated operation. As such conversion is a relatively new 
trial in railway industry (the first case in the world, Nuremberg U-Bahn, was launched 
in 2008), related researches and practices are wanted. Compared to railway projects 
built with the design as driverless systems, these cases are more complex ones that 
require careful timing to ensure the technical and financial viability of the project

Chapter 2. 
       Automatic train operation 
2.1.  Driverless train 
Driverless train, or so-called automated operating train, is a train operating without 
on-board staffs’ control. The International Association of Public Transport (UITP) 
defines a metro system adopting driverless train as “In metro systems, automation 
refers to the process by which responsibility for operation management of the trains 
is transferred from the driver to the train control system.”2.  
 
Railway systems adopting automatic train operation could be categorized as 
“Automated people mover (APM)” or “Automated Urban Guided Transport system 
(AUGT)”. It can be observed from the definition of these systems stated within 
standards for driverless transit systems. American society of Civil Engineers (ASCE, 
2013) defines an automated people mover as a guided transit mode with fully 
automated operation, featuring vehicles that operate on guideways with exclusive 
right-of-way. In the safety requirement for automated train system developed by 
International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), the definition of a railway system 
adopting driverless train is “a system featuring driverless or unattended train 
operation with self-propelled, guided vehicles, operating on an exclusive guideway.” 
(IEC, 2008). 
 
Based on responsibility of man and machine on the functions of train operation, the 
grade of automation of the railway system could be categorized as five levels as 
table 1 (IEC, 2008). From GOA0 to GOA4, the degree of automation is ascending and 
the responsibility of man is reducing. In this study, GOA4 (UTO) is the degree of 
automation being investigated. Railway operators with various background and 
different needs could select their own suitable mode for driverless train operation.

 Advantages 
Potential benefits could be gained by railway systems through adopting UTO are 
summarized as table 2.  
Through controlled speed profile, uniform running time could be realized and 
therefore delay is expected to be mitigated. Safety could be improved by the 
collaboration of ATO and ATP system with the result of speeding-prevented 
operating. With optimized acceleration and deceleration, energy consumption and 
the wear of wheel and track are possible to be reduced. 
 
Realization of uniform dwell time could enhance punctuality due to higher priority at 
punctual departure than service to late travellers. Punctuality could also be 
improved by the shorter and uniform reverse time at terminal for turnaround as a 
consequence of the removal of train driver. In addition, quicker turnaround allowed 
reductions in total journey time as well as the time to recover from incident, which 
benefit the capacity and safety of a railway system. 
 
Another important characteristic of UTO is the staff independence form train 
operation. The connection between frequency and staff availability (which also 
represents labour costs) is broken and therefore regular departure and 
demand-driving strategy could be realized. Studies revealed that thanks to the 
adoption of UTO, one European metro now needs one hour’s notice rather than 
three months to schedule service for unusual events (Cohen, Judith M., et al., 2015).

This is especially beneficial for railway systems frequently dealing with event 
ridership. As a driverless metropolitan rail system, Vancouver SkyTrain successfully 
handles crowds after football games or other event, brining additional revenue two 
to three times marginal costs (Railway Gazette, 2000).  
 
Realization of shorten service interval means that the amount of cars in train sets 
could be reduced without affecting the capacity and consequently saves the 
infrastructure costs derived from platform length. Halving the length of a station cuts 
the capital cost by about one-third, particularly significant for underground routes 
(Railway Gazette, 2000). Punctuality could be improved thanks to elimination of 
driver-caused delay and quicker recovery from incident. Results of an investigation 
on delay incidents across 42 metro lines of 15 different metro systems shows that, 
switching from manual to automatic operation (from GoA1 to GoA2 or above) is 
associated with a 33% reduction in 5 minute delay incidents (Melo, P.C., et al., 2011). 
Removal of driver cabin has positive impact on capacity as well. In Paris Metro Line 1, 
6% additions on capacity were brought by UTO thanks to more physical space within 
the train (Ossent T., 2010).  
 
 Disadvantages 
However, there are possible negative influences of UTO on railway systems.  
 
The real saving on infrastructure costs is controversial. Thanks to strict safety 
requirement for UTO systems, intrusion protection equipment such as platform door 
and elevated/underground guideway will be needed to shape an 
exclusive-right-of-way environment. In addition, total system reliability might be 
affected by higher complexity and significant amount of technology components, 
which increase the probability of failures.  
 
Improvement of the operational performance might not be as optimistic as stated. 
As a driverless train operates with the support of various sub-systems, collaboration 
between sub-systems could determine the operational performance such as 
punctuality and capacity of the railway system. 
==PAGE:23==
8 
 
Availability of system is considered as having a potential impact on safety of 
driverless railway system. Thanks to the absence of on-board staff, recovery from 
failure might take longer and can potentially worsen hazard consequence (IEC, 
2008).  
 
Public concern about safety and security due to the absence of drivers on board is 
another challenge that the UTO railway operators have to confront with. However, it 
could be mitigated by deploying staffs straying around the train and the platform 
providing services.  
 
2.2. History of automated train 
As automation technologies and digitalization stormed over all kinds of fields 
including railway industry, the idea of driverless train has become realistic. The 
innovation in computer science has brought opportunities for significant upgrade on 
train protection and motion control. Evolution of Information and Communication 
Technology (ICT) had made the remote control of train possible. New signaling 
system using efficient communication devices had been a strong driving force for the 
tendency of UTO adoption. It could be observed in the increasing amount of CBTC 
UTO lines as figure 2 (Georgescu, M. P., 2006). Besides, the development of 
detection equipment and automatic platform doors allowed the driverless train 
operation feasible.

With the revolution of technology, the dream of automated trains running on the 
track had been realized. Railway operators’ interests on UTO could be observed from

Since 1960s, railway systems, especially but not limited to metro systems began to 
conduct trials on driverless train. This trend had since then spread over the world, as 
cases are across Europe, North America, and Asia. In between many systems 
adopted ATO, including most of the new US metros. People movers such as 
inter-terminal transit systems within airport are one of the fans of unattended train 
operation as well.     
 
After success of most of the UTO systems, conversion of manual operation to 
driverless train became the advanced challenge for railway industry. Not until 2008 
the idea of converting existing conventional line to automatic train operation had 
been realized. Encouraged by the impressive result of automated train launched in 
other lines, Paris Metro Line 1 became the first high-capacity metro system switching 
from conventional line to UTO system (Mancone P., 2011).

Chapter 3. 
Technical Requirement of UTO 
 
As railway system operates under highly integration between subsystems, 
equipment and devices with different functions are mandatory or optional for 
running UTO.   
 
3.1. Systems required for train operation 
Here the least requirements of the system are stated in this section. However, a 
railway system might equip other optional sub-systems based on specific purposes. 
3.1.1. ATO system linked with ATP/ATC system 
In modern railway operation, UTO could be realized through the installation of 
Automatic Train Operation (ATO) system and the support from Automatic Train 
Protection (ATP) / Automatic Train Control (ATC).  
ATO is the system to execute the train movement by giving commands of start, 
acceleration, braking, and stop to train. It also controls opening and closing of train 
doors while trains stop within station areas. 
Whilst ATO is the “executor” of motion on the train, ATP/ATC serves as a “supervisor” 
to ATO to assure that the train operates safely, effectively, and efficiently. The main 
function of ATP/ATC is to generate commands about train movement to ATO. As 
figure 5 (Di Claudio, Mariano, et al., 2014), with the information of track topology, 
point positions, speed restriction, track occupancy, and characteristic of train 
(braking capability, train weight, maximum speed, etc.), the ATP/ATC could generate 
==PAGE:27==
12 
 
movement authorities and braking curve to define the speed for train to run in 
different sections along the route. 
Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC) is the most common train command 
and control system in existing railway systems adopting UTO4. It has been proven as 
the best choice for driverless systems due to inherent high levels of safety and 
reliability with a low life cycle cost. (Georgescu, 2006) The configuration of a typical 
CBTC system is shown as figure 4 (Hitachi, Ltd, 2014).

On-board system 
Compared to staff attended train, the train without a driver needs on-board 
equipment carrying various functions to help itself moving appropriately. Functions 
needed for an automatic train should be aligned with the contents of tasks 
performed by a human driver while driving the train. However, with more concerns 
about accuracy of motion and safety level of automated train operation, the 
functions of the on-board system shall be at least but not limited to the function of 
human drivers. 
For a train running under an automated train operation system, one of the 
mandatory functions it needs is to make itself receiving and transmitting information 
relevant to movement such as receiving movement authorities and transmitting 
current speed and position report of train toward the control center. Therefore, 
equipment for receiving and transmitting such as radio, antenna and balise 
transmission module (BTM), are necessarily equipped on a train. The information 
caught – whether directly from control center or indirectly through trackside 
transmission modules – would be the input of the “brain” of the train, that is, the 
train computer (which is represented in figure 5 as “command and control unit”). 
The main purpose of the train computer is to execute the motion of the train and 
brake according to movement authorities issued.  
To avoid train positioning bias which affects the safety distance between trains, 
optional distance measurement equipment such as odometry and Doppler radar 
could be equipped on trains. The role of this kind of equipment is to provide extra 
support to train positioning while running between reference points such as balises 
or transmission loops. It is especially important in routes applying moving block due 
to its influence on moving block lengths. 
In-cab signalling, which is one of the important on-board equipment within ATC 
system, replacing the role of wayside signalling system by displaying information 
such as speed limit, target speed, and distance to the target for driving. Although 
within railway systems operating with ATO, in-cab signalling is not used in most of 
time, it is a mandatory device on board for safety reasons.

3.1.2. ATO over ETCS 
As a train control and protection system, ETCS could serve as an underlying 
functionality of ATO system. To adopt UTO on railway systems under the protection 
of ETCS, approaches are needed to combine these two subsystems.  
Bienfait B., Patrick Z., and B. Barnard (2012) provided an incremental approach for 
collaborating functions of ATO and ETCS within one railway system. As shown in 
figure 9, the minimum required function of a system with combination of ATO and 
ETCS is speed control. It could be realized by the provision of dynamic information of 
track and train from the wayside equipment of ETCS, generation of speed profile on 
the European Vital Computer (EVC) of ETCS, and the speed execution performed by 
the command and control unit of ATO on train borne. 
As ATO’s role in a UTO railway system might not be limited to the speed execution 
but also door opening/closing of the train, an advanced function of the combined 
system is door operation control. To perform this task, information about platform

equipment to EVC as an input for station entry management.  
The global throughput of the railway network and the capability of system could be 
enhanced by the injection of Traffic Management System (TMS). The role of TMS is 
to provide operational data on the basis of theoretical timetable and traffic 
regulation algorithms, offering “strategic” speed profiles for ATO system to execute 
on the routes. Railway operators could adjust the parameters and algorithms applied 
in TMS according to respective operation policy. For example, with TMS, a railway 
company with an emphasis on energy efficiency can expect trains operating under 
the timetable generated by considering the optimal energy consumption.  
 
3.2. Safety features for UTO railway systems 
In UTO railway systems, at most of the time the trains operation completely relies on 
computer and machines without the presence of staffs. Safety requirement for 
driverless railway system therefore has been seriously emphasized by authorities, 
operators, and public.

on avoiding unsafe actions and issues, railway operators with intentions to adopt 
driverless train operation could evaluate their extra investments for a safe UTO 
railway system.

Chapter 4. 
Organizational impact 
 
Driverless operation is an enabling technology for alternative models of train and 
station staff deployment requiring both technological and organizational changes 
(Thomas P., 2006). The most significant organizational change within a railway 
company caused by the adoption of UTO is on employment structure. Staffing plans 
and strategies regarding to redeployment and redundancy for existing staffs are 
investigated in this section.   
4.1.  Redesign of employment structure 
4.1.1. New staff model 
Despite the removal of the train drivers would be the main change to the staff model 
of a railway enterprise converting conventional lines to driverless operation, it might 
be however not the only adjustment needed. Thomas P. (2006) mentioned that, 
driverless operation will change the working functions of operational staff, leading to 
more varied job roles and increased flexibility in staff deployment to fulfill these 
roles and benefits. Therefore, job profile change regarding to task redefining would 
be the one of the organizational consequence of switching to UTO.    
One of the differences in employment structures between conventional and 
driverless railway system is the presence of roaming multi-skilled staff or so-called 
mobile staff. The task of the roaming multi-skilled staff much depends on the staffing 
model designed by each UTO railway system. In metro lines in Barcelona, Spain, 
roaming multi-skilled staffs are served as Customer Service Agents. These staffs 
spend part of their time on each shift driving the ATO trains, and the rest patrolling 
stations and assisting customers, and in some case need to take care of minor 
equipment failures (Serrallonga, C., 2006). In the study of automation of 
==PAGE:43==

Copenhagen S-tog, Parsons Transportation Group (2011) assumed that the mobile 
staffs would spend majority of their time on customer facing services and sometimes 
on managing technically failure issues. In addition, the responsible area of roaming 
staffs could be vary, either on trains only or also on stations (Cohen, Judith M., et al., 
2015).

operator need strategies for redeploying the staffs. The strategies particularly aims 
at former train driver due to this is the job role with most dramatic change in the 
organizational redesign process.

train drivers through mechanisms as following (Mancone P., 2011): 
 
Provide attractive new positions 
 
Promotion 
 
Offering early retirement 
 
Dismissal

The first strategy is to encourage the former drivers to take up a new position. New 
position for drivers could be relevant to their previous jobs (e.g. mobile staffs which 
has part of tasks similar to train drivers’) or within quite different roles. The 
employer could attract the drivers by redesigning the job contents or offering 
incentive transfer pay.  
 
The employer could also provide managerial positions to some of the former drivers. 
In the case of Paris line 1, the automated line is run with a middle management that 
mostly comes from the driving category (Mancone P., 2011).This type of 
re-deployment is part of strategic human resource management which utilize 
experiences and skills of former drivers.  
 
A railway company could also encourage former drivers to retire prior to normal 
retirement age by offering attractive buyout program. However, consequent 
expense on pension, severance payments, and related incentive benefit such as 
extended health care insurance and job search assistance would add extra financial 
burden to the railway company.

packages for re-deploying train drivers. Strategic priority of these mechanisms

In the process of communicating and selling the proposals of reorganizing plans, a 
railway operator would need strategies and approaches to achieve the desired

outcome. As the negotiation between railway employer and employee is usually an 
interaction process undertaken by two parties with divergent interests and 
intentions toward specific policy or plan, it could be categorized as one of the social 
conflicts occurred in society. Therefore, strategies used for dealing with social 
conflicts could be adopted by UTO railway operator during the negotiation process.  
Pruitt, D. G., & Carnevale, P. J. (1993) developed approaches for the resolution of 
social conflict, as presented in figure 12. Three classes of strategies with methods 
included could be applied under different situations depending on power and 
resources possessed.  
The first class, joint decision making, most likely occurs when both parties are willing 
to participate the conversations to each other with a common goal as achieving an 
agreement. It could be realized by negotiation and mediation. Whilst negotiation is 
processing among the participation of the parties who would like to achieve an 
Figure 12  Methods for resolving social conflict 
==PAGE:49==

agreement, mediation is a third-party assisted joint decision making. Due to the 
intervention of the mediator, resolution of social conflicts by adopting the mediation 
method could lead to less control of the process such as prolonged schedule and 
duration for the involving parties.   
To achieve an agreement, decision making involved with third parties is another 
option. These third parties are intermediaries that are not directly involved in the 
conflict. The intermediary institute could be a court providing a lawful result for the 
dispute while the involving parties were seeking court-based adjudication. The 
disadvantages of third-party decision making over joint decision making are the cost 
spent on adjudication and arbitration services. In addition, as the court or other 
intermediaries might not completely understand the interests of each party, 
decisions without regarding to either or both parties’ aims are possible to be made 
(Kiesler, S., 2014). 
Separate actions made by opposing parties could be one of the strategies for 
resolving conflicts. The most common strategy of separate actions is struggle with 
several ways. For railway industry, physical combat could be strikes called by unions. 
Wars of words usually occur while railway operator and unions express their opinion 
or accusation against the opposing one through press. Southern rail management, 
for example, emphasizes the aim of their policy on media several times in order to 
mitigate the misunderstandings of public on this issue. The price of this kind of 
strategy is resource consuming (e.g. loss of customer in a strike) and deteriorated 
relationship between the involving parties.

example, during the restructuring of the Swedish state-owned railway (SJ), unions 
were brought into the decision making process for the developing the redeployment 
plan (Kopicki, R., Thompson, L. S., & King, M. A., 1995). There are also possibilities 
that different negotiation stages are recurring due to the feasibility of the 
agreement.

Chapter 5. 
UTO for specific types of 
railway systems 
 
Being one of the operation options for railway systems, UTO is literally applicable for 
each kind of railway system. However, in reality, the characteristics of railway 
systems do affect their suitability for driverless train operation.  
In this chapter, particular attractiveness and difficulties of UTO to specific types of 
railway will be researched.  
5.1.  Metro 
Metro has been the major UTO player within railway industry. Per UITP (2013), 
automated metros accounted for 6% of the world’s metro line kilometers. To date, 
there are 55 fully automated metro lines in 37 cities around the world, operating in 
total 803 km, a 14.2% increase in km over 2014 figures7. Reasons behind the trend 
among metro systems and the challenges the metro operators need to face will be 
elaborated in this section. 
 
5.1.1. Attractiveness of UTO for metro systems 
Being a rapid transit generally providing services within urban areas, metro systems 
are expected to handle peak-hour commuting and demand surge generated from 
events. The high operational flexibility of UTO derived from the disconnection 
between train schedule and crew plan is the major attractiveness to metro systems. 
This brings revenue to metro operators by allowing the implementation of 
                                                          
7 Extract from the website of UITP Observatory of Automated Metros. 
==PAGE:53==
38 
 
demand-generative strategy without additional driver costs (Cohen, Judith M., et al, 
2015). 
Metro systems with strong intermodality are expected to be particularly benefited 
from UTO adoption. To achieve seamless transfer between transport modes, 
provision of on-time service is vital to metro systems with high request for 
adaptability of the transport offer. This attractiveness was a major element of the 
automation choice made by Paris Metro Line 1, which is a major metro line in Paris 
serves 5 out of 15 major multimodal nodes of the network (Yelloz, Gerard, and 
Patricia Charon, 2008). 
Another reason behind the decisions of metro systems to adopt UTO might be the 
suitableness of it on 24-hour-a-day operation. For cities needs night metro service, 
the adoption of UTO makes it more feasible and economical.  
 
5.1.2. Suitableness and difficulties 
Suitableness 
Bienfait B., Patrick Z., and B. Barnard (2012) listed the possible reasons for the fact 
that the introduction of ATO on metros is simplified thanks to the characteristics of 
most metro applications: 
 
Lines are predominantly in protected areas (tunnel, or elevated structures) 
As the developed standards of AUGT/APM, the definition of ATO railway system 
is for automatically operated train running on guideway with exclusive 
right-of-way. Most of the metro systems are either elevated or underground 
and therefore satisfy the basic safety requirement of UTO system. 
 
 
Each line is sufficiently self-contained to be re-equipped to a uniform standard 
at each upgrade during its life 
Most of the metro systems are closed system without sharing routes, 
equipment, or fields with other railway systems. Complexity of signaling system 
==PAGE:54==
39 
 
and diversity of rolling stock are lower and therefore make it easier to equip 
new systems. 
 
There is little inter-working between different lines 
This characteristic is applied for most of the metro system. However, there are 
some exceptions. For example, lines within the network of Paris metro system 
were upgraded to UTO in different stages and therefore faced interface issues 
such as mixed-mode operation between lines.  
 
 
The train fleet is homogeneous and dedicated to passenger traffic 
Compared to mainline railways, normally the train fleet is more homogeneous 
in metro systems and therefore the operator can gain cost saving from 
economies of scale on on-board equipment procurement and maintenance. In 
addition, thanks to the fixed door layout for each train, it is suitable for Platform 
Screen Door to be equipped within the metro system. 
 
 
Due to the intensive services, all on-track engineering work must take place 
outside service periods. 
This characteristic is both the suitableness and difficulty for metro system to 
implement UTO. Whilst the migration could be performed without causing any 
disruption to passenger service and more safely, the switching period might be 
extended due to very limited time slot for construction and test.  
 
Difficulties 
For a metro system adopting CBTC as the train command and control system for 
driverless train operation, one of the risks it needs to suffer is the interventions to 
the communication networks.  
Used by CBTC as radio technology, Wi-Fi has advantages such as low costs due to it is 
unlicensed and available worldwide. However, the high availability of it might cause 
high risk of interference to railway networks. The risk is particularly high for metro 
systems due to the level of interference are more severe within networks located in 
tunnel and city center, where are the areas metro trains mostly running through. 
==PAGE:55==
40 
 
Effected quality of communication not only influences operational efficiency of the 
metro system on train capacity but also threats the safety of the railway system.   
 
5.2. Main line railway 
Main line railway, or so-called conventional rail is railway system that providing 
long-distance or regional train services. So far main line railways are just the 
beginners for UTO and the suitability of driverless train to this kind of system is still 
in controversial. 
5.2.1. Attractiveness of UTO to main line railway 
Compared to metro systems, there are no particular and obvious attractiveness of 
UTO to main line railway. Although the advantages of driverless train are generally 
applicable to main line railway systems, benefits brought by UTO are weakened by 
part of the inherent characteristics of main line railway. More investigations about 
the inherent characteristics of main line railway could be found in the following 
sections.  
5.2.2. Suitableness and difficulties 
Suitableness 
There is no obvious characteristic of main line railway make it particularly suitable 
for adopting UTO. 
Difficulties 
The features of main line railway that might cause difficulties for implementing UTO 
are:  
 
Complex composition of train fleet 
==PAGE:56==
41 
 
The difficulties caused by this characteristic would be on cost efficiency of the 
implementation. On-board system might need to be customized for specific train 
type and therefore the total cost of the equipment is expected to be higher and the 
installations and testing time would be longer. Another issue is the installation of 
platform screen doors. Different train type might mean diverse train door layouts 
and therefore makes it hard to set up a platform screen door system that fits all type 
of train doors. Furthermore, high diversity of train fleet would weaken the benefit of 
operational efficiency brought by UTO. Difficulties on integrating the braking 
capability of different kind of trains might lead to unbalanced safety margins for 
trains and subsequently cut down the effectiveness of moving block (figure 14).  
 
 Trains are not dedicated to passenger traffic 
Whilst passenger train service is mostly provided during day time, freight trains are 
normally operated in the night time. This means that there are very limited time slot 
for installing equipment or upgrading the infrastructure during the migration 
process. 
 
 
Partially exclusive right-of-way  
Thanks to the difficulties for trains without presence of on-board staffs to detect and 
react to road vehicle incursion or collision to obstacles on track, absolutely exclusive 
Figure 14  Different safety margins caused by train types in a moving block system 
==PAGE:57==
42 
 
guideway is one of the basic safety requirement UTO railway systems. Since some of 
the main line railways are without fences or barrier along part of the routes, the 
safety level of driverless trains running within these systems are worrying. It is also 
dangerous for driverless trains running through sections equipped with level crossing 
due to the possibility of intrusion into the guideway from the crossing.   
 
Threat of weather condition to equipment  
Since most of the main line railways are above ground, extreme weather condition 
might be harmful to critical UTO equipment along the routes. One of the examples is 
that the balises as a part of ATP systems equipped on the track in Taiwan’s main line 
railway were once unable to work properly under hot and humid weather in the 
beginning of its launch, which caused up to 1300 minutes delay within one day.     
 
Interoperability 
Interoperability is another issue to main line railway which provides inter-city or 
inter-nation train services. If the train running through both UTO and manual 
operation sections along the route, more than one package of system and operation 
rules are needed to support mixed-mode operation. However, this issue could be 
partly solved by adopting ATO over ETCS, which may bring about greater 
interoperability of ATO systems and ease the potential signaling compatibility issues 
when automated metro services run over main line infrastructure (Powell, Peter, 
Jonathan et al., 2016). 
 
5.3. Freight railroad 
Within the history of automatic train, UTO application on freight railroad is even 
rarer than the existence of driverless train running on main line railway system. The 
difficulties to adopt UTO on freight railroad mainly arise from non-exclusive route 
and threat of weather condition to equipment. 
However, there are motivations for freight railroads operators to adopt UTO. Similar 
to the demand-generative strategy that could be applied to metro systems adopting 
==PAGE:58==
43 
 
UTO, automated freight railroads is expected to be able to provide just-in-time 
deliveries of small consignments at short notice (Huges M, 1996). It would make the 
freight train more competitive against the road freight transport.

To understand the quantitative value for converting manual-controlled to automatic 
trains, three cases will be analyzed in perspectives of technical design, organizational 
changes, and financial and economic feasibility.  
 
6.1. Methodology 
The feasibility analysis of each project consists of four parts: 
 
Motivations of UTO conversion 
 
Technical solutions 
 
Organizational impact 
 
Financial and socio-economic profitability 
Based on the result of the investigation of technical requirement in chapter 3 as 
table 7, a checklist of equipment needed for individual railway system will be 
conducted and served as a basis for cost evaluation. For the part of organization 
impact, specific staffing plan with customized redeployment strategies will be 
developed for each case. Finally, an analysis combined profitability and 
socio-economic effects of each case regarding to monetary impact of infrastructure 
investment, organizational changes, and derived benefits will be performed to 
examine the feasibility of the UTO projects.    
The monetary feasibility analysis consists of two parts, which respectively evaluates 
the financial and economic profitability of a project. The assessment in the 
private-economic perspective takes expected costs and revenue of the company 
gained by the railway operator into accounts. The socio-economic evaluation

includes the elements of the financial analysis and expected benefits for society. 
These additional costs, revenue and benefits are estimated by comparing the 
monetized value generated by the conversion with the base case, which is the 
existing railway system without the UTO project.

Sensitivity analysis 
As the business model in the feasibility analysis is built under assumption of 
parameters and estimated value of costs, revenue, and benefits, a sensitivity analysis 
will be conducted to test the robustness of the result of the feasibility assessment. 
Selection of the critical variables to be investigated for the sensitivity analysis is 
based on two criteria: 
 
Have great impact upon the result 
 
Most likely to occur 
As the business model is built based on the technical requirement, result of 
organizational redesign, and the assumption of economic development, the 
uncertainties could be related to technical, political and economic conditions.  
The outcome of the sensitivity analysis will give an indication of the risks, and 
illustrate how likely each project will remain feasible in the case of changes in the 
parameters. 
 
For transport infrastructure projects, optimism bias is one of the critical risks of 
feasibility analysis. According to UK Department for Transport (2017), optimism bias 
is the tendency of individuals to expect better than average outcomes from their 
actions. In the context of rail infrastructure projects, optimism bias can lead to 
underestimation of project duration, underestimation of its total cost, and 
overestimation of its benefits  
Observed from the consequence of optimism bias, two variables could be used as 
the parameter for sensitivity analysis: 
 
Construction costs 
 
Transport demand benefits 
For cases applicable for conducting sensitivity analysis with optimism bias, the 
average cost escalation would be set as urban rail with 45% and conventional rail 
with 30%, which is according to the results of Flyvberg et al. (2003)’s investigation on 
the cost overruns in global railway infrastructure projects. To test the impact of 
overestimated transport demand benefits to the projects, the value of time or the 
demand rate used for calculating total socio benefits would be adjusted.

Depending on each project’s background, motivation and assumptions, other 
parameters which match the criteria of variable selection mentioned above will be 
chosen for further sensitivity analysis.   
 
The impact of individual critical variables on the financial feasibility could be 
evaluated through computing switching values, which is the value for which the 
FNPV becomes zero. By calculating the switching values, the maximum allowed 
variation range of the parameter for a financially break-even project could be 
observed.  
 
Risk assessment 
Risk assessment of each project will be performed by conducting a risk matrix. 
Factors which is excluded in the business model and uncertainties occurred within 
project life would be considered as potential risks. Risk level is evaluated in terms of 
the likelihood of the risk and the severity of its consequences on the profitability of 
the UTO project.  
 
 
6.2. 
Case 1: Kaohsiung Mass Rapid Transit system 
Kaohsiung City is the second largest city with a population of 2.78 million. The 
railway network within this populated city includes high-speed rail, regional trains, 
metro, and light rail. Operated by Kaohsiung Rapid Transit Corporation (KRTC), 
Kaohsiung’s metro system, Kaohsiung Mass Rapid Transit system (KMRT) consists of 
two metro lines: Red Line with 28 kilometers and 23 stations and the Orange Line 
with 14 kilometers, 14 stations and 3 depots. The passenger train service of the 
completed existing metro network commenced in 2008.  
The existing metro service is provided by a fleet of 126 3-carriage trains with around 
180 drivers in total. In the mainline, a driver’s task is to monitor the operation of 
doors, visually monitor the track ahead, and resolve emergency issues.  
==PAGE:64==

6.2.1. Motivation 
There are two main motivations for KRTC to adopt UTO in its metro system.  
The possibility of saving in staffing cost brought by adopting driverless train 
operation is believed to financially benefit this metro company. Since the operation 
began in 2008, this company had been suffered eight consecutive years of financial 
losses including annual losses reached to NTD 2.5 billion (DKK 0.55 million) in 2014 
(Taiwan News, 2012). As employment contributes to 20% of the annual total 
expense of the company, reduction of employee number caused by efficient staff 
redeployment resulted from automatic train operation is expected to heal the 
debt-ridden company. Besides, in a relatively indirect way, the higher attractiveness 
of the metro system to potential users brought by the possible results of automated 
operation such as better punctuality and more frequent train service during peak 
hours, could boost the ridership and ticket sales.  
As a metro network with high event ridership, this system could be benefited from 
the improved capacity brought by UTO. Events such as concerts and festivals usually 
bring significant higher passenger demand than normal days to this metro system. 
The ridership increment during Lunar New Year, for example, could even climb up to 
75% higher than normal as shown in figure 15. Possibility of shorten headway, would 
allow the metro system to satisfy extreme demand in short time. In addition, as UTO 
decouples train availability from staff availability, it would enhance the flexibility of 
fleet arrangement, enabling adding service at short notice and low marginal cost.

6.3. 
Case 2: Copenhagen S-tog 
Operated by Danske Statsbaner (DSB), Copenhagen S-tog is a suburban railway 
system providing train services for city centre and greater Copenhagen regions. The 
system length is 170km with 87 stations along 7 lines. It has a fleet of around 120 
train set running in a maximum speed up to 120km/hr.  
6.3.1. Motivation 
The attempt to satisfy the roaring passenger demand is one of the motivations for 
switching to UTO (Transport-, Bygnings- og Boligministeriet, 2013). As the population 
of greater Copenhagen area is expected to be growing, an urban transit system with 
more frequent and reliable service would be vital to this city. Adoption of UTO is 
believed to favor the railway system with higher train capacity and improved 
punctuality, which will make S-tog service more attractive to the growing population.  
Possible improvement on punctuality makes this option more desirable to DSB, an 
operator who has a 10-year contract signed with The Ministry of Transport on the 
condition to meet the operator punctuality (DSB, 2017). 
 
6.3.2. Technical and organizational solutions 
Required equipment 
By 2021, the signaling system of entire Copenhagen S-tog network will be replaced 
with CBTC system and therefore the ATP/ATC system is relatively complete. The 
main equipment for UTO’s operational need is the ATO system. In addition, for 
safety reasons, intrusion prevention or detection for platform area is a critical 
system required.

6.4. Case 3: Mainline railway 
The third case is a 100km state-owned mainline railway with all routes and 26 
stations at ground level. This railway line offers both regional and intercity train 
services at speed up to 130km/hr. Thanks to the multiple kinds of train service it 
provides, there are 10 types of vehicles running within the system.  
Along the line there are 60 intersections with roads and therefore have the same 
amounts of level crossing installed on the route. Except the sections with level 
crossing, the rests of the line are exclusive with fences.  
6.4.1. Motivation 
There are two reasons for this railway line to consider UTO as an option for upgrade. 
One of the motivations is to improve the punctuality of this system. In recent years 
this railway line has having low performance in punctuality, with average punctuality 
as 90% and average delay in 5 minutes, which is far below its target value at 95% and 
3 minute. The increasing proportion of delays due to passengers has bothered this 
railway system for long time and contributed to the low punctuality. The realization 
of uniform dwelling time brought by UTO is expected to mitigate the delayed caused 
by waiting for late travellers.  
The heavy load of labour cost and labour relatiom issues is another driving force for 
this railway line to convert from manual operation to UTO. As a company been 
suffering from significant loss in recent years, any chance cost saving could relieve its 
worsen financial burden. Besides, drivers of this railway line have been taken several 
times of strike, affecting the service provided by the railway system. Therefore, 
elimination of the need of drivers might be one of the solutions to the labor issue.

Chapter 7. 
Conclusion and Future works 
 
7.1.  Conclusion 
From the results of case studies, it could be observed that conversion of railways 
from manual to driverless trains is not a panacea for every railway operator. 
Thorough feasibility analysis and risk assessment have to be conducted as an 
important foundation for decision making.   
 
Essential system required for operation and extra equipment for safety purpose are 
the main components of the costs for UTO conversion projects. Technology gap 
between the UTO system and the existing conventional systems determine the 
amount of investment on the infrastructures. However, depends on the inherent 
feature of each railway system and the budget constraints, customized system 
packages will be chosen for upgrade. Threat from risks in construction and migration 
phases shall be concerned and mitigated by adoption of measures and strategies.  
 
With well-designed staffing model and appropriate redeployment strategies, 
employment restructuring subsequent to the decision of UTO conversion is possibly 
beneficial to the railway enterprise in financial and organizational aspect. 
Nevertheless, non-monetize effects on employee morale, labor relation, and 
goodwill are implicit costs for the railway enterprises.  
 
Profitability in both financial and economic perspective is suggested to be assessed 
for UTO conversion projects. As benefits of UTO are distributed to the railway 
operator as revenue and the society as consumer surplus, interests and concerns of 
both private business and public sectors shall be involved in the evaluation of the 
feasibility of UTO conversion projects.     
==PAGE:103==
88 
 
7.2. Future works 
To have a clearer picture of the feasibility of UTO conversion projects, simulation of 
operation pattern and consequence of potential risks could be performed under 
customized scenarios. Local safety regulation and organizational culture are worth to 
be investigated for the UTO conversion appraisal. The change of labour productivity 
caused by UTO conversion is another interesting topic for the railway enterprise to 
gain deeper understandings about the connections between organizational 
restructuring and profits.  
As UTO on ETCS system is a relatively new concept arise in railway industry, 
feasibility of this type of trial is another interesting research. Along with the 
appearance of self-driving car, autonomous trains might emerge in the future as well. 
The impact of this advanced technology to railway systems in aspects of operation, 
organization, and finance might be quite different from automatic trains.

 Assumptions of parameters 
General assumptions for the business model are listed in table 39. 
 
 Calculation of costs and benefits 
CAPEX – ATO system cost 
Benchmarking case:   
Paris line 1 automation spent Euro 30 million (220 million DKK) on design and supply 
of equipment necessary for driverless train operation, including wayside equipment 
for 17 km route and onboard equipment installed on 49 cars. 
 
Consider the scale of the ATO system installed on mainline railway is around 5 times 
to Paris line 1 ( Mainline railway: 80 trains with 10 types of fleet running on 100km 
track; Paris Line 1: 49 trains running on 17 km route), the price of ATO is calculated 
as: 
DKK 220 million * 5 = DKK 1.1 billion 
 
CAPEX – Stations cost 
Benchmarking case: 
For Copenhagen S-tog which also plans to install Platform Intruder Detection System, 
the Parson’s study (Parsons Transportation Group, 2011) estimated that across the 
whole network of approximately 200 platforms the station costs are an estimated 
DKK 1.08 billion. 
 
Consider the ratio of the scale between the mainline railway and Copenhagen S-tog 
is around 5 times to Paris line 1 ( Mainline railway: 60 platform; Copenhagen S-tog: 
200 platform), the price of station cost is calculated as: 
DKK 1.08 billion * (65/200) = DKK 585 million 
 
CAPEX – Trackside protection cost 
Assumption: the average price of an elevated railway/road bridge is DKK 17 million   
The cost of elevating railway or road for existing 60 level crossing along the mainline 
railway is:  
DKK 17 million *60 = DKK 1.02 billion  
 
 
==PAGE:125==
110 
 
OPEX – Maintenance cost 
Based on the assumption of the maintenance costs, the annual maintenance cost is 
calculated as: 
DKK 585 million (Station costs) * 10% + DKK 2.12 billion (sum of the costs of ATO 
system and trackside protection) * 5% = DKK 165 million 
  
OPEX – Energy saving 
The formula used for calculating annual energy saving is as following: 
Energy saving = Total energy consumption of the metro system * Percentage of 
energy consumption of train to total energy consumption *Energy saving rate * Cost 
of energy 
Where  
Total energy consumption of the metro system = Passenger-Kilometer * Energy 
consumption per Passenger-Kilometer 
 
The cost in this case is calculated by using the parameters as following: 
Annual Passenger-Kilometer: 1.8 billion n  
Energy consumption per Passenger-Kilometer: 0.09 kilowatt hour   
Percentage of energy consumption of train to total energy consumption: 50%  
Energy saving rate: 12% 
(Reference stated in table 39) 
Cost of energy: DKK 0.88 per kilowatt hour 
 
As a result, the energy saving per annum is calculated as:  
1.8 billion * 0.09 kilowatt hour * 50% * 12% * DKK 0.88 per kilowatt hour = DKK 5 
million
