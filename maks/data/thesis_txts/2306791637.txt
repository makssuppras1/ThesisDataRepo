Abstract
This study proposes a numerical framework for evaluating the perceptual aspect of the diffuse
ﬁeld conditions with intended ﬁnal use in music auditoria. Impulse responses are simulated based
on the time domain Poisson process with increasing reﬂection density. Geometrical divergence
and room characteristics such as the volume, absorption of surfaces, air absorption are accounted
for in the model. Different diffuseness conditions are realized by altering the directions of arrival
of the reﬂected waves and presented to the listener in a series of listening tests in an anechoic
64-channel Environment to examine how sensitive the human auditory system is to changes in the
diffuseness condition. Under uniform incidence conditions, this time domain model is compared to
the stochastic pure-tone diffuse-ﬁeld inference model originally developed by Waterhouse, in order
to validate its selection for the diffuseness simulations.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this study different diffuseness scenarios are simulated based on a stochastic process and the
conditions and propagation characteristics typical of a rectangular music hall. The simulations are
presented to a group of listeners in a surround playback system, for which multiple Room Impulse
Responses are generated through a process known as Ambisonics. Listeners are asked in a series of
tests to quantify the perceptual experience of diffuseness and other attributes by means of these
simulated environments to establish qualitative characteristics of the sound ﬁelds.
1.1
Motivation
Diffusion in music halls is emphasized as an important acoustical parameter in concert hall design,
but still there is no substantial description of the relation of diffusion and subjective criteria, nor
a deﬁnitive quantitative measure of the term itself. There is no widely accepted deﬁnition of
diffuseness yet, which places it in a nebulous territory.
Diffuseness however, can be deﬁned as the characteristic quality of a sound ﬁeld in a diffuse state.
The diffuse state is a concept widely explored in acoustics with varying deﬁnitions, some of which
are presented here for context:
Sabine (from [Bassett (2012)]) refering to the diffuse state mentions that "if the duration of audibil-
ity of the residual sound is nearly the same in all parts of the auditorium [...] and the efﬁciency
of an absorber in reducing the residual sound is independent of its position" the sound will be
approximately diffuse.
[Beranek (1996)] deﬁnes it as "any moment sound is travelling in a room in all directions equally."
[Kuttruff (2009)] refers to "the amplitudes of incident waves [...] distributed uniformly over all
possible directions of incidence in a way that from each element of solid angle the same amount of
energy arrives per second and per unit area element perpendicular to the respective direction [...]
the phases of the elementary waves are distributed at random so that interference effects can be
1
==PAGE:20==
2
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
neglected and we can simply add the energies of the waves impinging simultaneously."
[Schultz (1971)] refers to a diffuse ﬁeld where "there is uniform total energy density at all points in
the room", "there is equal probability of energy ﬂow in all directions and random angle of incidence
upon the boundaries of a room" and "the phase relation of the waves are random at any given point
in space."
[Jacobsen (1979)] refers to two deﬁnitions: "In a sound ﬁeld there is equal probability of energy
ﬂow in all directions" and "a diffuse ﬁeld comprises an inﬁnite number of plane propagating waves
with random phase relations, arriving from uniformly distributed directions."
All deﬁnitions encompass the properties that can be drawn to assess the diffuseness of a sound
ﬁeld, that is:
1. That the amplitude of incident waves is distributed uniformly over all angles of incidence.
2. That equal energy arrives from each direction over a designated period of time.
3. That the phase relation of the waves is random.
In principle these are all measurable properties, but in practice there is still no way of measuring
the diffuseness of a sound ﬁeld, furthermore of quantiﬁying the degree of diffuseness in a room
objectively or subjectively. In spite of this, diffuseness is considered to be an important quality of
music auditoria and is commonly acknowledged in literature [Wang (2003)].
Most of the literarure on diffuseness focuses on how to achieve such a condition in reverberation
chambers and measuring facilities, which ideally is not the desired state of diffuseness expected
in a performance space. It is clear that a music auditorium posseses certain characteristic traits
in relation to the sound propagation, such as prominence of sound coming from the front, which
entails separation of the direct sound from the diffuse, and the need for strong lateral reﬂections in
the early stages of sound propagation, which limits the uniformity of amplitude in all directions.
These characteristics distance the sound ﬁeld in auditoria from a true diffuse sound ﬁeld, and as
a consequence diffuseness should not be looked upon in the same way as that of a reverberation
chamber.
The criterion for establishing a desirable diffuseness in auditoria is varied, with several authors
proposing different aspects to be of importance in terms of the spatial distribution of the sound ﬁeld,
[Barron (1993)] for instance emphasizes the importance of receiving sound from the sides and
behind the listener), and of the temporal aspects of the sound ﬁeld (Beranek for instance separates
the diffuse ﬁeld into early and late stages [Bassett (2012))]. But aside from the differences of
concept it is clear that controlling the intensity, direction and delay times of reﬂections is crucial in
auditorium design to obtain diffuseness.
==PAGE:21==
1.2. OBJECTIVES
3
The above mentioned aspects served as motivation for testing different scenarios of diffuseness in a
music hall via simulations, with the aim of subjectively quantifying the degree of diffuseness and
other attributes of the sound ﬁeld that might be perceived differently as a byproduct. The acoustical
quality of an auditorium is in the end judged by the spectator, so a subjective criterion of the sound
ﬁeld is paramount for proper design principles.
1.2
Objectives
As indicated in [(Barron 1971)], the aim of any work on subjective hall acoustics is to discover
subjectively audible qualities to derive design criteria for halls in order to produce optimum degrees
of these subjective qualities. Although this might seem too optimistic or far-fetched, an attempt is
made here to quantify and understand the differences in perception of these simulated scenarios
and their relation to diffuseness. Under this scope the objectives of this study are:
• To propose a numerical framework for evaluating the perceptual aspect of the diffuse sound
ﬁeld for music auditoria.
• To quantify the perceived level of diffuseness and other subjective attributes of the sound
ﬁeld based on different simulated reﬂection patterns.
• To analyze if the concept of diffuseness is interrelated to other subjective attributes and to
determine if the listener is able to conceptually discriminate the term from other deﬁnitions.
• To analyze the relationship between perceptual diffuseness and currently valid objective
parameters that are used to describe the acoustical quality of a music auditorium.
• To analyze if there are any signiﬁcant differences in subjective diffuseness rating based on
the type of stimulus used for the listening tests (Music material or a Shot Noise) and the type
of listener (specialized or non-specialized).

2.1
Simulation of a Room Impulse Response
2.1.1
Sound generation - Abstraction
Consider a fast impulsive burst of sound, like a shot noise, which can be represented theoretically
as a Dirac Delta pulse emitted by an omnidirectional source. This process can be modelled as an
inﬁnite number of rays uniformly distributed over a solid angle of 4π that propagate as a spherical
wavefront, with each ray representing a plane wave section. In the free ﬁeld the rays would
propagate inﬁnitely in all directions, but within an enclosure, or a room, part of the ray’s energy
will be reﬂected when arriving at the boundaries, and the rest will be absorbed or dissipated.
A listener in the room will ﬁrst hear the direct sound, which corresponds to the ray that propagates
in the direction of the line that connects him or her and the source. A reﬂection may reach the
listener inmediately after bouncing off a nearby surface, or it could reach the listener later in time
after bouncing off multiple surfaces.

2.5
Listening tests
In order to obtain ratings for the subjective parameters, psychoacoustic experiments were done in
the form of listening tests. The listening tests were desgined as MUSHRA tests, performed in the
AVIL Lab at the Technical University of Denmark DTU. The MUSHRA test concept and the room
setup at the AVIL lab will be explained brieﬂy in this section.
2.5.1
MUSHRA Test
The MUSHRA test (Multi Stimulus Hidden Reference and Anchor) [BS1534-3 (2015)] is a tool
to compare multiple signals and systems. Listeners have access to each of the test items and the
reference signal and rate their perception on a continous quality scale. The subjects have random
access to the test items, one of which is a copy of the reference signal.
2.5.2
AVIL Lab
The Audio Visual Immersion Laboratory (AVIL) at the Technical University of Denmark is an
anechoic room with a built-in spherical speaker array for spatial sound reproduction. It contains 64
speakers surrounding the listener in concentric circles at, below and above the horizontal plane that
passes through the listeners ears.
The top hemisphere is comprised of a horizontal plane ring at the listeners height, made of 24
speakers evenly spaced 15° apart around a circumference centred around the listener’s head. A
second 12 speaker rings follows placed above the listener at an elevation angle of 28°; each speaker
is spaced 30° apart from one another in the horizontal plane. A third ring follows at an elevation of
56°, comprised of 6 speakers spaced 60° apart, and ﬁnally a top ring of two speakers at an elevation
of 80°, with one of the speakers in front (θ=0°) and the other behind (θ=180°) the listener.
The bottom hemisphere is a reﬂection of the top hemisphere.
2.6
Statistical analysis
A brief description of the statistical methods and assumptions used for analyzing the data from the
experiments in this study is presented in this section. The methodologies are explained without
the mathematical formulations for implementation. In this study, [Field (2009)], [Meyers et al.
(2013)], [Laerd (2015)] and [Pelegrin (2011)] were used as reference for details on formulations
and implementations. All statistical analysis was done on SPSS, a statistical analysis software.
2.6.1
One way repeated measures ANOVA
One way repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) is used to determine if there are any
statistically signiﬁcant differences between the means of different levels of a within subjects factor
==PAGE:36==
18
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
(independent variable) when the levels are related [Laerd (2015)], such in the case of having the
same participants for different experiments.
An ANOVA test assumes the null hypothesis (H0) that the means (ν) of the different levels are
equal, against the alternate hypothesis that at least one of the means is different, that is:
Ho : µ1 = µ2 = ... = µk
(2.38)
where k is the number of levels of the within levels factor. The one way repeated measures ANOVA
calculates an F ratio to evaluate the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis at a deﬁned signiﬁ-
cance level (p-level). The ANOVA test highlights systemic differences in the means of the different
levels, but it does not give information on the differences between means, for which a series of
post hoc tests need to be evaluated. Post hoc test for pairwise comparisons used are Bonferroni
post hoc tests, which consist of multiple paired samples t-tests, applying a correction to adjust the
conﬁdence intervals and p-values as a result of taking account multiple comparisons.
Additionally, the effect size η2 is reported, which states the proportion of variance in the the
dependent variable explained by the within subjects factor.
The ANOVA test assumes the following conditions to be true:
1. No signﬁcant outliers in any level of the within subjects factor.
2. Normally distributed residuals (errors).
3. Variances of the differences between all combinations of levels of the within subjects factor
must be equal (also known as sphericity).
Condition 1 can be checked by visual inspection of box plots, or by a more robust test, called test
of studentized residuals which is the quotient of a division of a residual (error) by an estimate of its
standard deviation. A data point with a standardized residual greater than 3 is considered an outlier.
Condition 2 is checked by the Shapiro Wilk test, which is a powerful normality test for small
samples (n<50) [Razali and Wha (2011)]. the condition is violated if the p-value is less than 0.05.
The ANOVA test is robust against violations of normality, and its results are deemed admissible
even if the data is not exactly normally distributed. An alternate procedure to compensate for
violations of normality is to run a non-parametric test, called a Friedman test, where the medians of
the levels of the within subjects factor are tested under the assumption of a null hypothesis where
the medians are equal for all levels. Paired comparisons can be run by a series of post hoc tests
based on the Wilcoxon Signed Rank test [Laerd (2015)].
Condition 3 is checked by Mauchley’s test of sphericity. Under this test, Sphericity assumptions
are violated when the signiﬁcance (p-value) of the test is greater than 0.05. Violations of sphericity

increase the type I error [Field (2009)], unless a correction of the degrees of freedom for the
within subjects factor and the error effect is done. This correction is called the Greenhouse-Geisser
correction, it reduces the degrees of freedom based on the degree of departure of sphericity.
2.6.2
Two way repeated measures ANOVA
A two way repeated Measures ANOVA is used to determine if there is a statistically signiﬁcant
interaction effect between two within subject factors (independent variables). A repeated measures
two way ANOVA test calculates three F ratios: one for each independent variable and one for an
interaction term (independent variable 1 × independent variable 2).
If there are statistically signiﬁcant interactions the p-value that results from the interaction term F
test must be lower than the predeﬁned signiﬁcance level. Post hoc tests are required to determine if
there are statistically signiﬁcant differences between the levels of one of the dependent variables vs.
the levels of the other dependent variable, this involves running a one way ANOVA and Bonferroni
corrected pairwise comparisons.
The same assumptions of the repeated measures one way ANOVA apply, with the added assumption
that the sphericity condition must hold for the interaction term. If the condition is violated, then the
Greenhouse-Geisser correction is applied to the degrees of freedom of the interaction term and the
error effect.
2.6.3
Two way mixed ANOVA
The two way mixed ANOVA compares the mean differences between groups that have been sepa-
rated into two independent variables. A two way mixed anova has two independent variables or
factors. A between subjects factor and a within subjects factor. The between subject factors has
independent groups (e.g. type of listeners), whereas the within subject factor (repeated measures)
has related groups such as the different cases tested. The purpose of a two way mixed ANOVAis
to determine if there is a two way interaction between the between subjects factor and the within
subjects factor [Laerd (2015)].
The two way mixed ANOVA calculates two F ratios, one for the within subjects factor and the
error effects, and one for the interaction term (within subjects factor × between subjects factor)
and the error effects. If there is a statistically signiﬁcant interaction the p-value that results from
the interaction term F test must be lower than the predeﬁned signiﬁcance level and Post hoc tests
are done like described in Section 2.6.2 to determine statistically signiﬁcant differences between
the means of the groups for a speciﬁc level.
A two way mixed ANOVA has a set of assumptions that need to be fulﬁlled, aside from the ones
mentioned in Section 2.6.1:
1. There must be homogeneity of variances.
==PAGE:38==
20
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
2. There must be homogeniety of covariances.
3. The variance of the differences between groups must be equal.
Condition 1 requires the variance of the dependent variable to be equal between the groups of the
between subjects factor, which can be veriﬁed if the variance of the residual between the groups are
the same. It is an important assumption when sample sizes between groups are not equal [Laerd
(2015)]. Levene’s test for equality of variances is used to test the veracity of the assumption. For
p < 0.05 there is a violation of the assumption.
Condition 2 requires that the degree in which two variables change together in the same direction is
the same. Homogeneity of variance is the bivariate equivalent of homoscedasticity [Meyers et al.
(2013)]. This assumption can be tested with Box’s test of equality of covariance matrices. For
p < 0.001 there is a violation of the assumption.
Condition 3 is the same assumption of sphericity, except that the variances are concerned with the
differences between the groups, rather than the variances within each group [Laerd (2015)]. It can
be checked by Mauchley’s test of sphericity.
2.6.4
Pearson Correlation
Pearson product correlation determines the strength and direction of a linear relation between two
continous variables via a coefﬁcient ρ. The value of ρ varies from -1, denoting a perfect linear
negative relationship to +1 for a perfect positive linear relationship. A value of zero indicates no
relation between the variables [Laerd (2015)].
As for the previous statistical analysis methods some assumptions are required for the analysis to
be valid:
1. There needs to be a linear relation between both variables.
2. There should be no signiﬁcant outliers.
3. The variables are assumed to be normally distributed.
2.6.5
Linear Regression
A simple linear regression determines the linear relationship between two variables to predict the
outcome of a dependent variable based on an independent variable. A simple regression model can
be described by:
Y = β0 + β1X + ϵ
(2.39)
where β0 is the intercept or constant, β1 is the slope parameter and ϵ represents the residuals or
sample errors. A way to get the line of best ﬁt regression is through the method of least squares,

The quantity R2 is called the coefﬁcient of determination, which is a measure of the proportion of

Once the pressure amplitude vectors are calculated, the directional information of the reﬂections
must be included in the model. For this, a procedure was established based on the Spatial Decom-

∆t = 1/fs, an average direction is estimated for all reﬂections arriving during the time window.

lie within the ﬁrst 50 ms of the event, and the number of reﬂections that occur within this time
window do not exceed one per time step. The time at which the average number of reﬂections
exceed one happens close to the 200 ms mark.
Second, the time window between steps is equal to 2.0833 ×10−5 s (1/fs), and the maximum
audible reﬂected densities reported in literature range between 2000 and 10000 reﬂections per
second [Jeong et al. (2010)]. The simulation is considering in average 23,000 reﬂections per second
by using the SDM based process which is well above the reported ﬁgure. For the most extreme
case the Poisson process outcomes a maximum of 9 reﬂections per time bin, but this happens close
to the end of the process where the energy of the reﬂections has diminished considerably and the
sound ﬁeld is isotropic, therefore it is highly unlikely that the SDM based approach will create any
audible differences.
An advantage of opting for an SDM based approach is that the process is less demanding computa-
tionally since the model must assign directional information to 46,767 image sources, instead of
85,953 sources, which would be the case if all of the reﬂections were assigned a random direction.
3.1.5
Reﬂection pattern of a rectangular music hall
A directional information vector that includes azimuth (θ) and elevation (ϕ) angles for each of
the reﬂections must be included in the simutaion. In order to create a credible impulse response
the directional pattern of a shoebox hall auditorium was simulated. For this several literature
references [Yamasaki and Itow (1989)], [Pätynen et al. (2008)], [Tachibana et al. (1989)] were
consulted and two ODEON simulations were performed: The ﬁrst of a rectangular concert hall
model (Concertgebouw, Amsterdam) which is included as one of the standard models in ODEON
12, and the second, of a rectangular prism with the dimensions of the simulated hall and the surface
absorption coefﬁcients from table 3.3.
Before analyzing the previous ﬁndings, some assumptions taken in the present study are mentioned.
First, a single receiver is simulated seated 10 m away from the source strategically located in the
center of width of the hall. In this position, the most important lateral reﬂections are assumed to be
symetrical in the horizontal plane. Second, a single point source plays the role of the orchestra,
meaning that the sound event (or shot noise) comes from a unique point in space. This is a limitation
as the interaction pehenomena between the different players (ideally point sources) that happens
==PAGE:47==

in an actual auditorium cannot be fully taken into account in the model. Since the simulation for
the present study is based on a stochastical process happening in an arbritary imaginary scenario,
there is no way of distributing sources along the space, and accounting for the interaction processes
would generate a much more complex problem. Some of the spatial information regarding the
location of the instruments is retained, as the sound ﬁles used to generate the Impulse responses
were mixed using a stereo perspective of a symphony orchestra, but this will be explained in detail
in Section 3.2.2. Finally, the simulation only considers specular reﬂections, and effects of scattering
are omitted, to include this a radiosity component could be added to the simulation as the one
included in the Ambisonics algorithm by Mabjerg (2015).

3.1.6
Simulating the directional pattern of reﬂections.
Based on the reviewed information the directional pattern of the simulated hall is deﬁned. The
azimuth angles (θ) are referenced as 0° being directly in front of the listener. A positive angle
implies a counterclockwise direction, and a negative angle a clockwise direction. The elevation
angle (ϕ) is referenced to the horizontal plane that passes through the listener ears, parallel to the
==PAGE:51==
3.1. SIMULATIONS
33
ground, meaning that a positive elevation angle implies an angle opening above the listener’s head
and a negative angle opening is below the listener’s head, as seen in ﬁgure 2.3.
The spatial pattern is divided into the direct sound, the ﬁrst reﬂection, and then into 50 ms time
intervals. t0 = 0 represents the time when the direct sound reaches the listener and it is ﬁxed to be
directly in front, (θ, ϕ) = (0, 0). The ﬁrst reﬂection at t1 = 16.9 ms, is set in front of the listener in
the horizontal plane, coming from the ﬂoor area at a randomized angle from -15° to -40°.
For the interval t1 < t < 50 the incoming rays are assigned random directions in the horizontal
plane (θ) in a range of 45° to 70° and -45° to 70°. A ﬁxed condition is imposed so that the ﬁrst ray
within the time range must be followed by a mirror imaged reﬂection about the x axis to maintain
symmetry caused by the listener seating in the centre of the width, after that the rays can arrive
randomly within the speciﬁed range. For the elevation plane (ϕ), based on the analysis of the time
series of the ﬁrst 50 ms in Figure 3.7, the rays are assigned to come from the ﬂoor area, in any
random direction in the range of -15° to -40 °.
For the interval 50 < t < 100 the rays are assigned to come from any random direction from
0° ≤θ ≤140° and −140° ≤θ ≤0°. Again the condition of mirroring the ﬁrst ray from the time
window is imposed to maintain symmetry. After that the directions are randomized within the
angle range. In the elevation plane the rays are assigned random directions from −30° ≤ϕ ≤65°
to simulate reﬂections from the ﬂoor area and ceiling, similar to the one reported by Pätynen and
the ODEON simulations.
After t > 100 the sound ﬁeld is assumed to be isotropic, and the directions for incoming rays are
randomized in the 4π solid angle, so that −180° < θ < 180° and −90° < ϕ < 90°.

3.2.1
Simulating different degrees of diffuseness
In order to rate the condition of diffuseness different scenarios need to be presented to the listener.
To do so, the same methodology from Section 3.1.6 is used. All scenarios are simulated with a
frontally incoming direct sound, after that the transition period for (0 < t < 200ms) is varied
according to predeﬁned directional patterns that vary over time and ﬁnally for t > 200 a condition
of isotropy is simulated.
The direct sound (t = 0 ms) is always presented arriving from the front, at the same height at which
the listener is seated so that θ = 0 and ϕ = 0. The ﬁrst 50 ms of the event consist of the direct
sound and an early ﬁrst reﬂection coming from the ground, followed by random lateral reﬂections,
as seen in the time series plots from Section 3.1.5, this early directional cues provide the listener

until 200 ms have elapsed, when an isotropic state is presented and reﬂections arrive randomly
from a solid angle of 4π.

3.2.2
Selection of sound stimulus
One of the objectives of the listening test was to evaluate the differences in preference ratings due to
the use of different sound stimulus. A music sample and a shot noise were selected for conducting
the tests.
Music
For the music stimulus, anechoic music recordings of a symphony orchestra were used. A set of
recordings in high quality format (48000 Hz WAV ﬁles) were obtained from Jukka Pätynen of the
Aalto University in Finland. The recordings included individual instrument recordings of different
symphony orchestra musical compositions (for a detailed process on the recording process the
reader is refered to Pätynen et al. (2013a)).
The piece chosen for this study was an excerpt from Dona Elvira, an Aria from Mozart’s opera
Don Giovanni. Although the piece is meant for a small music orchestra, it was selected because it
was found to be the most neutral of the music works in terms of dynamic range and compositional
complexity and could therefore reduce bias caused by preference for the piece. The other 2 pieces
were a recording by Mahler, (Symphony I, movement IV, Bars 1-85) and Beethoven, (Symphony 7,
movement 1, Bars 1-53) which are more modern works with a stronger character.
The instrument recordings included ﬁrst violin, second violin, viola, cello, double bass, oboe,
clarinet, basson, french horn and a soprano. After some consideration it was opted to remove
the soprano from the set, which was a continous source of sound from beginning to end of the
recording and a major focus of attention when listening to the music. Removing the soloist provided
the listener with discernible directional cues coming from staccatos, silences and fast transients
produced by the rest of the orchestra that would be masked otherwise.
File Preparation
The provided recordings contained a substantial amount of high frequency noise (hiss) that in-
creased considerably when playing all instruments simultaneously. For this a noise removal
software (IZOTOPE RX) was used to clean up the ﬁles, with special attention not to remove too
much high frequency content from the instruments. This process was done for each of the individual
tracks.
Once noise was reduced the ﬁles were mixed as a as a multitrack session using PROTOOLS audio
editing software. Copies of the individual instruments were made to create a virtual orchestra to
match the actual number instruments required for the piece. The string section was formed by 4
double basses (1 original + 3 duplicates), 5 chellos (1 original + 4 duplicates), 5 violas (1 original +
4 duplicates), 8 ﬁrst violins (1 original + 7 duplicates), 7 second violins (1 original + 6 duplicates),
2 individually recorded basoons, 1 clarinet, and 4 french horns (2 original + 2 duplicates). Finally,

Shot noise
Prior to using a shot noise, the recorded impulse response of the simulated room was used as a
sound stimulus. The impulse had a light synthetic sound quality to it, almost metallic sounding,
with some ringing in the high frequencies.
The cause for this is can be that that in reality the direct sound, considered as a dirac delta pulse,
becomes broader after it is reﬂected. A dirac delta pulse has a uniform frequency spectrum, but
once reﬂected it becomes a low pass frequency version of itself since some of the energy, specially
in the high frequencies, is absorbed by the reﬂective medium. In the temporal domain this translates
as a widening of the pulse for increasing reﬂection orders [Jeong et al. (2010)]. This pulse widening
is not considered in the simulation, where all of the reﬂections are treated as delta pulses that
decrease in amplitude over time, and the realness of the sound becomes compromised.
So to compensate for this, a shot noise recorded in an anechoic chamber was used as a stimulus
which resulted in a more realistic sound, with less ringing and more noticeable low frequency
content. This type of fast transient sounds are known method of testing the acoustic qualities of a
music hall.

The sound stimuli are convolved with each of the 64 impulse response signals that resut from the
Ambisonics process (See Equation 3.9). The simultaneous playback of all 64 convolved signals
consitute the auralizations of the different cases. Previous studies [(Barron 1971)], [Pätynen et al.

(2008)] reported bias in the results due to loudness differences between tests, so to avoid this the
ﬁles were normalized with regards to the loudest impulse response of all of the different cases.
The convolved impulse responses of each case played back over the 64 channels represent the

3.2.4
Deﬁnition of attributes
A Direct ellicitation method was used for the test, where the verbal descriptors of the rated at-
tributes are presented to the listener prior to commencing the test. The vocabulary used is of
great importance as the deﬁnition of the question and the quantiﬁcation are assumed to have a
direct relationship. The answers provided by the listeners constitute the dependent variables of the
experiment and the questions represent the dependent variables. The importance of the vocabulary
lies in that the subject will be quantiﬁying a speciﬁc task based solely on the choice of words and
his or her understanding of them. Unfortunately in the ﬁed of auditorium acoustics the verbal
descriptors used to describe the sound quality of a music auditorium are numerous and vary in
general from study to study. There is no concensus of a set of words to describe different attributes
of a sound ﬁeld yet, and one must thread carefully when using verbal descriptors in subjective tests.
Recommendations when selecting a verbal descriptor emphasize that the terms discriminate be-
tween the stimuli, have little overlap with other terms and relate to physical measures describing
the stimuli [Bech and Zacharov (2006)].
In this study the vocabulary used was selected from a pool of descriptors used to describe the
acoustic of concert halls from previous studies [Beranek (1996)], [Hidaka and Beranek (2000)]
[Lokki (2013)], [Lokki et al. (2011)], [Lokki et al. (2012)] and [Giménez et al. (2012)]. All of these
have in common that the descriptors revolve around the categories of level, spatial extent, deﬁnition,
reverberance, timbre and openness of sound. Oddly not many of the revised references refer to the
concept of diffuseness. Giménez et al. (2012) make mention of the directionality of sound with
the coined term iso-directionality, asking acousticians and concert goers to rate on a ﬁve point
scale the observation of sound being equally perceived from all directions. Hidaka and Beranek
(2000) mention the diffussion of the auditorium in terms of the reverberant ﬁeld, and how a good
level of diffusion can be obtained by means of projecting surfaces on the walls and ceiling, but dis-
mmiss the evaluation of the concept due to lacking measurements for the evaluating the quality of it.
After review, the terms selected to refer to the qualities of sound are:
1. Diffuseness: to refer to the directionality of sound and the intensity of the sound ﬁeld from
such directions.
2. Surround: to refer to the degree of feeling inmmersed in the sound.
3. Source Width: to refer to the apparent width of the source of sound in the horizontal direction.
==PAGE:63==
3.2. LISTENING TESTS
45
4. Clarity: to refer to the degree to which the instruments stand apart from one another.
5. Pleasantness: To refer to the degree of enjoyment produced by the sound.
6. Naturalness: To refer to the degree of realism in the sound.
7. Timbre: To refer to the degree of low frequency content of the sound.
For this study the attribute of reverberance was not analyzed given that all of the different scenarios
tested have the same reverberation time, nor any level related attributes since the sounds were
normalized and had the same perceived loudness.
Attributes 1, 2 and 3, can be categorized within the spatial extent category. Previous studies have
linked source width with measures of lateral reﬂections of the early part of the impulse response
(0<t<80 ms) such as LF and envelopment to late lateral reﬂections (t>80 ms) and objective measures
such as LG ?. For the experiments in this study directional information is varied conjunctly in the
horizontal and vertical plane over both early and late time segments and this is the concept that
is being tried to quantify as diffuseness, hence there is a differentiation between attribute 1 and 2,
and 1 and 3. Whether there is a subjective difference between them will try to be proven in the
following chapters.
Attribute 4 belongs to the deﬁnition category. Attributes 5 and 6 are affective terms and quantify
the overall impression of the sound stimulus and stand on a category of their own. For Timbre
the name of the category was chosen to deﬁne the attribute, this was to separate it from mea-
surements such as warmth which is commomly used to describe the low frequency perception,
but the idea was to use words that had no relation to any other senses that coud cause misconception.
3.2.5
MUSHRA Test
With the deﬁnition of the attributes, and the auralizations of the different cases the MUSHRA test
is implemented.
The MUSHRA test dictates that a single global attribute is used to judge any and all detected
differences between the reference and the object [Bech and Zacharov (2006)] therefore the listener
must only rate one attribute per comparison of cases. The same stimuli can be used to rate other
attributes but it must be done in different tests. This helps to limit problems with auditory memory
that could arise if judging more than one attribute per case at the same time.
The rating scale is deﬁned as a continous scale ranging from 0 to 100 % for all of the experiments,
where 0 is the absence of the attribute, corresponding to the reference signal, and 100 indicates the
maximum perceived amount of the attribute.
==PAGE:64==
46
CHAPTER 3. METHODS
7 tests are designed, one for every attribute. In each the user is presented with a set of 7 cases. One
of the cases is the reference signal, that corresponds to the direct sound and all reﬂections presented
coming from the front speaker, and the other six correspond to the rectangular hall simulation and
the ﬁve diffuseness scenarios from Table 3.7.
The different cases are randomized in every test to avoid context effects and bias from presenting
the stimulus in a sequential order. In every test the user is asked to rate the perceived amount of
the attribute per case from 0 to 100% by means of adjusting the position of a slider. Vertical lines
denoting same interval lengths are positioned every 25% of the length of the slider with a numeral
indicator to aid the listener in the quantiﬁcation of the attribute.
7 buttons labelled A to G are mapped randomly to the different cases and the reference signal.
Upon pressing any of them playback of the selected case starts. The playback is continuous and the
user can switch between cases instantaneously by pressing any of the buttons. When switching
cases the playback continues uninterrupted, which is convenient for A/B comparisons.
The user can switch to the reference signal at any time, by pressing the reference button in the
bottom of the screen. Once the user is satisﬁed with the responses, they can move on to the next
test by pressing the conﬁrm buttom in the bottom right corner of the screen. The graphical user
interface is presented in Figure 3.14. The tests were done on a touch screen resting over the subjects
lap.

Instructions to the listener
Prior to the tests, the listeners were handed a sheet of paper with the printed instructions for the
experiment. They were asked to read them carefully and ask any questions if anything was unclear.
The instructions asked for the listener to imagine being seated in the stalls of a large concert hall,
and a picture of the Boston Symphony Hall was presented, taken from the front stalls in the center

for context reference.
For simplicity the deﬁnitions of the attributes were presented in the form of a question so that all of
the participants, regardless if they had knowledge of sound verbal descriptors, could understand the
what it was they were going to rate about the quality of the sound. The questions presented were:
1. Diffuseness: Do you feel sound coming from all directions?
2. Surround: How surrounded do you feel by the orchestra? Do you feel immersed in the
sound?
3. Source Width: How wide does the orchestra seem to be?
4. Clarity: How clear does the orchestra seem? Can you pick up individual instruments more
clearly in one case than another?
5. Pleasantness: How pleasant would it be to listen to a concert under such a condition?
6. Naturalness: How realistic does the orchestra sound?
7. Timbre: How much bass do you feel? Can you feel more low frequency content from a
speciﬁc case?
On the reverse side of the sheet of paper a small description of the graphical user interface was
given and they were explained how to manipulate the sliders in order to rate the attributes. The
rating scale was explained and they were indicated how 0 % corresponded to the rating of the
reference signal and 100% to the one they perceived as having the largest amount of the quality
being rated.
The participants were encouraged to move their head slightly to the sides as they got used to the
different scenarios, since this could aid in the differentiation in the front and back directional cues
and resolve localization issues in the vertical plane specially.
The listeners were asked to take a 5 minute break after completing the ﬁrst 7 tests relating to the
music stimulus to avoid listening fatigue.

4.3
Statistical analysis
In this section the statistical analysis of the data is done to determine any signiﬁcant effects of
the different cases on the rated attributes, effects of the stimulus being played on the ratings of
the subjective parameters and differences in the ratings caused by the type of listener. Possible
relationships between parameters are examined and equations for expected responses based on
objective parameters are suggested. All statistical analysis was done using SPSS software.
4.3.1
The effect of the different cases
A one way repeated measuresANOVA is done to reject, or fail to reject the null hypothesis that the
means of the different cases tested within the same attribute are the same. The within subject factor
is deﬁned as the different cases (A, B, C, D, E, F) and the dependent variable is deﬁned as the mean
rating of the attribute (Diffuseness, Surround, Source Width, Clarity, Pleasantness, Naturalness and
Timbre). Whenever the difference of means are statistically signiﬁcant, Post hoc tests are done in
order to establish which of the cases are different.
For every attribute outliers are veriﬁed through the method of studentized residuals. Assumption
of normality is tested based on the Shapiro-Wilk test. If the sphericity assumption is violated, a
Greenhouse-Geisser correction is applied to the degrees of freedom of the repeated measures one
way ANOVA. Pairwise comparison are done through post hoc analysis with bonferroni adjusted
paired samples t-tests.
If deviations from normality are high, a non parametric Friedman test is performed to evaluate
the differences in the median of the different scenarios and a pairwise comparison is done by a
Wilcoxon Signed Rank test.
Only the violated assumptions and corrections are mentioned. Omitted remarks imply that the
assumptions are met.
The results are presented for each of the stimulus: Music and Shot Noise.

4.3.3
The effect of the type of listener
A two way mixed anova was done to test for the effect of the type of listeners on the rating of the
different attributes. The within subjects factors is deﬁned as the repeated measures: cases (A, B, C,
D, E, F) and the between subjects factor is deﬁned as the type of listener (trained or regular). The
F ratio and signiﬁcance values reported are for the interaction term (Case × Type of listeners).
The regular listeners included 14 participants and the trained listeners 7. The unequal sample size
should not represent a problem unless there is no homogeneity of variances. For cases when this
condition was violated, a separate comparison was run with equal sample sizes by selecting 7
random participants from the regular listeners group.
In the absence of statistically signiﬁcant interaction effects no post hoc tests are performed.

Discussion
In this section the results are summarized per attribute and discussed.
5.1
Diffuseness
5.1.1
Music
Due to the violations of normality the Friedman test was used to compare the differences between
the medians of the cases. The test shows there are no signiﬁcant subjective differences between
cases C,D and E, (rated at 75%, 75.17% and 74.82%) (see Figure 4.1 for reference).
Cases C and D present horizontal reﬂections and vertical reﬂections coming from ± 60°, ±90°
respectively, yet they were rated similar to the diffuse case E, this is an indication of difﬁculty to
audibly discern differences in localization in the horizontal and vertical plane at angles greater than
60° in the early time stages. This idea is in line with studies of localization in the vertical plane
[Perret and Noble (1997)], which showed that listeners could not discern between sources above
60° and just perceived sound as coming from the upper hemisphere at angle below 60°.
The case of the rectangular hall (case F) proved to be signiﬁcantly different from case E (diffuse
case) which was expected, but it comes as a surprise that there are no signiﬁcant differences
between case F and C, D. While case F and C are very similar with regards to the horizontal plane,
the direction of reﬂections in the elevation plane is substantially different, even more so for the
cases D and F (see Tables 3.7 and 3.4 for reference).
Interestingly, the case of the rectangular hall (case F) proved to be signiﬁcantly different from
case E (diffuse case). In case F isotropy is achieved after the ﬁrst 100 ms, while in cases C and D
isotropy is achieved at 150 ms and 200 ms respectively, yet they were statistically no different than
case E. This can be an indication that the perception of diffuseness for a complex signal such as a
music stimulus, might be linked more to the direction of horizontal and vertical reﬂections of the
early time stages rather than to the time when isotropy is achieved. Clearly the amplitude of the
reﬂections in the inital times stages (0<t<100) is higher than for the late stages, and the localization

Relationship to objective acoustic parameters
Diffuseness rated with a music stimulus presented a high direct correlation to late lateral strength

sensitive to lateral reﬂections and interestingly, more to the late stages of the impulse response

the sound sources in the ﬁrst 50 ms of the impulse response.
A linear regression to the highest correlated parameter LGx1k (See Table 4.16), proves the parame-
ter to be a strong predictor of diffuseness. The regression, seen in Figure 4.6a, shows the cases split
into two clusters, A, B, F on the lower values of the predictor variable, and C,D,E on the higher
values. Eventhough LGx is directly related to the lateral direction of reﬂections, the clusters seem
to be grouped in accordance to the direction of the vertical reﬂections in the ﬁrst 50 ms of the event,
with cases A, B and F spanning from 0° to 40° on the top hemisphere and 0 ° to -30° on the bottom
hemisphere, and cases C,D from 60° to 90° on the top and -60° to -90° on the bottom. Case E
spans throughout the entire vertical plane (0° to 90° and 0° to -90°).
5.1.2
Shot Noise
For the stimulus of shot noise, cases A, B and C show no statistically signiﬁcant difference, as
well as cases D, E and F. (See Figure4.3a - dashed line) The mean rating is very similar for all
three cases A, B and C (45.47%, 45.71% and 49.42%) but increases notably once the reﬂections
in the horizontal and vertical plane go past the ±60° threshold as in case D and E (72.65% and
68.86%). Surprisingly case F is rated similarly (69.60%) eventhough the reﬂections in the vertical
plane are limited to 40° in the top hemisphere in the ﬁrst 50 ms. This can be an indication that the
perception of diffuseness is more sensitve to the horizontal direction of strong reﬂections rather
than the vertical direction.This idea goes in line with the ﬁndings of previous studies [Coussins
et al. (2015)], where diffuseness is rated based on a set of concentric speakers surrounding the
listener in the azimuthal plane at different heights using uncorrelated noise signals. The study ﬁnds
that diffuseness is rated higher whenever sound is coming from the horizontal plane at the height of
the listener’s head, compared to above or below.

negative correlation with the Directivity index calculated using the speaker as sources in the late
time stages DI(t)sp(100−150) (ρ = −0.883), reinforcing again the idea that diffuseness rating is

5.1.3
Stimulus effect
Only cases A, C and F were found to have statistically signiﬁcant different ratings due to the type
of stimulus used (see Figure 4.3a - dashed line). The shot noise stimulus is rated higher for the
conditions A and F (16.5% and 14% higher respectively) and case C is rated 17% lower than the
music stimulus. But it is not possible to make any general statements about the effects of the
stimulus based on the information collected.
5.1.4
Type of listener
No statistically signiﬁcant differences where found for the interaction effects of case and type of
listener. Although upon inspection of Figures 4.4a and 4.5a it can be seen that the trained listeners
rated diffuseness higher for cases C, D and E for both Music and Shot Noise. In this cases strong
lateral and vertical reﬂections arrive from angles beyond ±60°.
Trained listeners come mostly from a sound engineering background and are used to working with
sound separation and panning in a stereo ﬁeld (azimuth plane) so they might be more sensitive to
lateral cues.

5.2.1

Because of violations of normality the Friedman test is used to compare differences between the

differences between the cases, but found it challenging to rate them numerically, reason being
that they found the variation in lateral and vertical directions of reﬂections between the cases too

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
The use of a statistical method based on a Poisson process is seen to be a valid model to simulate
the diffuse ﬁeld in the temporal domain. The numerical framework proposed in this study resulted
in a realistic auralization of a rectangular concert hall under various scenarios of diffuseness. The
proposed method is useful for the evaluation of subjective aspects of sound ﬁelds. The method
is replicable and the simulation conditions can be tailored to speciﬁc needs to acomodate for
geometric and absorptive characteristics of particular music auditoria. The use of Ambisonics
provided a convenient method of playback that can easily accomodate to various types of surround
systems where the user can deﬁne the number of channels for reproduction.
Several limitations such as not including the effects of scattered energy, simulating sound sources
as point sources, and not considering the broadening of the pulses due to reﬂections limit the
degree of realism of the simulations. But overall the method represented a simple and effective way
of producing trustful auralizations for multiple channel playback. More information is required
regarding the directional pattern of Music halls to improve the realism of the sound ﬁelds.
The perceptual study determined that the various simulated cases ellicited differences in the quan-
tiﬁcation of the selected attributes, although evidence suggests that there is difﬁculty in separating
the concepts of Diffuseness and Surround, and that audibly the differences in perception are not
very notorius.
The effect of using different stimulus did not have produce a large difference in the results, but it
showed that listeners can pick up more differences between the cases and feel more at ease when
rating using a music stimulus, compared to an impulsive sound such as a shot noise. The responses
when using a shot noise stimulus showed a clear grouping of results, one where the sound ﬁelds
were limited to a range of ±60° in the horizontal and vertical plane, and another when the angles
of the incident reﬂections were larger. The music stimulus did not show such a strong separation,
but a much gradual increase depending on the range of the sound ﬁeld.
The differences in the responses of a group of specialized listeners compared to a group of regular
listeners did not show any signiﬁcant differences. There is an indication that trained listener are

6.1
Future work
The following points could be contemplated for further work using this same methodology:
• The diffuseness scenarios can be designed in a way that theres is more control in the
dependent variables, possibly ﬁxing one of the directions of the incident reﬂections (azimuth
or elevation), or varying the directionality within a speciﬁc time frame only, in order to
perform a more detailed analysis .
• Objective parameters not included in this study, such as IACC can and a front to back energy
ratio can be measured in the anechoic room in order to assess any relation between the
subjective and objective parameters.
• Directional patterns of other types of auditoria can be investigated to be included in the
model.
• A radiosity component can be added to the model to include the effects of scattering.
• Listening tests should be done more than once with the listeners, to account for reliabilty in
the answers of the test subjects.
• An investigation into how the results can relate to the wavenumber analysis suggested by
Nolan et al. (2016) for evaluating the diffuse ﬁeld conditions in non-anechoic enclosures can
be done.
