Abstract
In this thesis, we research on the electronic and the transport properties of the helical edge state in
Quantum Spin Hall insulator coupled to an environment localized spin: spin bath. We calculate the
density of states and the current of this system using equilibrium and nonequilibrium Green’s function
formalism, respectively. Our result of the current agrees with the one derived from the Fermi Golden
rule. Furthermore, the calculation is also performed for other two systems: a system where spin species
in the bath have ﬁxed direction, and a system of the isotropic spin bath with an additional external
magnetic ﬁeld.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In 1879, Edwin H. Hall discovered that when an electric current ﬂows in conducting material with
an external magnetic ﬁeld applied perpendicular direction to the current, the propagation direction of
electrons would be pushed to the lateral direction (see Figure 1.1 (a)), which is observed as the transverse
voltage [1], and this eﬀect is called the Hall eﬀect. The mechanism of this eﬀect could not be understood
at that time since the electron is discovered 18 years later, and then, the eﬀect was ﬁnally explained
using Lorenz force, which is felt by moving electrons under the perpendicular magnetic induction.
He also found that this transverse voltage can be larger in ferromagnetic (FM) material than the
non-magnetic conductors when he performed similar measurements on the FM materials nickel (Ni) and
cobalt (Co) [2] (see Figure 1.1 (b)), and this eﬀect can be observed even when the external magnetic
ﬁeld is absent. This eﬀect is named the Anomalous Hall eﬀect (AHE) to distinguish from Hall eﬀect.
Although the Hall eﬀect and the AHE are similar, the mechanism behind is diﬀerent since no external
magnetic ﬁeld ,i.e. Lorenz force is needed to observe the transverse voltage in the case of the AHE.
In 1980, the quantization of the Hall eﬀect, so-called the Quantum Hall eﬀect (QHE) is observed by
von Klitzing [3]. The two dimensional electron gas creates Landau levels when a strong magnetic ﬁeld
is induced to it, and if the Fermi level lies in between the neighboring Landau levels, the carriers in the
bulk can not contribute to the current ﬂow, but the electron on the edge can propagate along the edge
of sample (see Figure 1.1 (c)). This eﬀect can also be understood as a cyclotron motion of the electrons
in the sample. If the magnetic ﬁeld is strong enough, the electrons in the bulk make full circles, which
make them to be localized and the bulk becomes insulator. However, the electrons on the edge cannot
make full circles because of the presence of the edge. They make half circles instead, and they propagate
along the edge. Therefore, conducting property arises only on the edge.
Spin version of the Hall eﬀect, the Spin Hall eﬀect (SHE) is predicted in 1971 by Dyakonov et al.
[4], and experimentally observed in 2004 [5]. Unlike the normal Hall eﬀect where positive and negative
charge career are pushed to opposite edges of a sample, in the case of SHE, the spin-up and -down
electron can be separated to opposite edges (see Figure 1.1 (d)).
Quantum Anomalous Hall eﬀect (QAHE), quantized version of AHE is predicted by Haldane in
1988 [6]. In his model, two-dimentional (2D) honeycomb model like graphene can have QAH eﬀect by
introducing a periodic magnetic ﬁeld with zero net magnetic ﬂux. He proposed the possibility of QHE
without an external magnetic ﬁeld (see Figure 1.1 (e)). But, in his paper, he did not provide concrete
strategy to obtain such a system, and 25 years later, the QAHE is ﬁnally observed by Chang et al [7].
Quantum Spin Hall eﬀect (QSHE) is a quantized version of SHE, and ﬁrstly predicted in graphene
by Kane and Mele in 2005 [8]. Their model is understood as two copies of the Haldane model, and the
spin-up electron propagates along the edge of sample in, say clock-wise direction while spin-down also
propagates along the edge in anti-clock wise direction (see Figure 1.1 (e)). This eﬀect is also predicted
later in semiconductors [9, 10]. In addition, Bernevig, Hughes, and Zhang proposed the existence of the
QSH state in a HgTe quantum well at the condition of a certain critical well thickness in 2006 [11], and
one year later it was observed experimentally [12].
1.1
Quantum Spin Hall Insulator
In Quantum Spin Hall insulator (QSHI), there are two conducting up- and down-electron on the edge of a
sample, and they are counter-propagating (see Figure 1.1). In other words, there is a correlation between
each spin and its wave vector (propagation direction). This is called the spin-helicity. Thus, we called

these states the gap-less helical edge states (HESs). The existence for this pair of HESs is protected by
so-called the time reversal symmetry (TRS), which means that the Hamiltonian describing the systems
of any QSHIs is invariant under the time reversal operation (TRO). Thus, if a wave function is the
solution of a Hamiltonian, the wave function with TRO has to be also the solution of that Hamiltonian
(Mathematical argument of TRS for HESs is found later in chapter 4). Therefore, it is often said that
HESs are mathematically protected. Thanks to the fact that HESs are protected by TRS, the back-
scattering from one HES to the other is absent. Thus, the scattering induced by impurities is strongly
suppressed unless TRS is broken. Therefore, ballistic transport and integer conductance, e2/h per pair of
HESs can be observed [12, 14] (the schematic of Ballistic transport of HESs in the case of HgTe quantum
well is shown in Figure 1.2). However, the scattering between HESs is possible in the presence of a
potential, breaking TRS, such as the coupling to magnetic impurities [15, 16], interactions with nuclear
spin [17], and an external magnetic ﬁeld.

ballistic integer conductance, e2/h. Figure is taken from [12]
In this thesis, We consider the system consisting of a single pair of HES coupling to an environment

of a localized spin, so-called spin bath. The spin bath could be magnetic impurities or nuclear spins. The
HESs are located at each boundary of a sample. Assuming the boundaries are far enough each other,
we can focus on a single pair of HESs without loss of generality. In order to simplify the discussion, we
employ spin-1/2 bath, and this choice does not aﬀect the essence of physical discussion. As a concrete
system modeled in this thesis, HgTe quantum well doped by magnetic atoms such as Mn is considered [18].
In this case, in order not to hinder the existence of HESs, low concentration of magnetic impurities are
required.
To discuss electric current through HESs with spin bath perturbation, we deﬁne our system as Figure
1.3 (a), where the QSHI is connected to left(right) lead with the chemical potential µL(µR). Thus, the
occupation of the HESs are determined by the contact where they originate. Approximately, the up-spin
(right-moving) HES is occupied up to the chemical potential of the left lead, µL while the down-spin
(left-moving) HES’s occupation is determined by the chemical potential of the right lead, µR. Thus,
ﬁnite bias voltage between left and right leads can control the scattering mechanism between a pair of
HESs as shown in Figure 1.3 (b) and (c). If the chemical potential of the left lead is greater than the
one of the right lead, the scattering from up-spin HES to down-spin is favored as in Figure 1.3 (b), and
vice versa as in Figure 1.3 (c). This scattering mechanism coincides with the opposite spin-ﬂip in the
bath, such as ↓→↑in the case of Figure 1.3 (b), and ↑→↓for (c).

1.2
Project
The initial motivation and basis of this master’s project lies in the reference [15], where the mechanism of
the magnetization of isotropic spin bath induced by the current through HESs is proposed. The current
calculation has been done using the Fermi Golden rule in [15], and the calculation is fairly easy. Thus,
it is worth trying to discuss electronic and transport properties in the similar system utilizing both the
equilibrium and non-equilibrium Green’s function formalism, which is new trial for this speciﬁc system.
Also, as an extra challenge, the same calculations are tried for 2 systems: a system where the spins in
the bath have favored direction, and a system with an additional external magnetic ﬁeld (these three
cases are explained in later section again).
Since in the case of transport property, e.g., current, the system is not only perturbed by the spin
bath but also by a bias voltage induced from left and right leads, We started the project with obtaining
the tools to calculate a current in such a system, e.g., non-equilibrium Green’s function formalism.

1.3
Objectives
Under above circumstances, the aim of my master’s project is to investigate the eﬀect on the electronic
and transport property of spin bath perturbation to HES using equilibrium and non-equilibrium Green’s
function formalism.
1.4
The three systems
In this thesis, We considered three diﬀerent systems. The ﬁrst is the case that the spin bath is completely
isotropic, which is considered in reference [15, 19, 20, 21, 22].
The second system is the case that the spin in the bath has magnetic ordering of the impurities
moment.
The itinerant carriers can mediate magnetic ordering [23].
The early attempts to induce
magnetic ordering of the impurities moment have been done, and resulting magnetization was out of
plane [24, 25, 7]. Recently, it is proposed that strained HgMnTe system with in-plane magnetization can
induce QAHE [26, 27]. Thus, it is reasonable to research on the case of in-plane magnetized spin bath.
We called it anisotropic spin bath in this thesis. Reference [16] deals with such a system.
Finally, the third system consists of the isotropic spin bath with an additional out of plane external
magnetic ﬁeld.

In this chapter, We summarize general and basic Non-equilibrium Green’s function (NEGF) formalism
discussed in [28, 29] to get some basic idea to deal with complicated systems such as interacting electron
with bias voltage. We assume that the readers are familiar with the equilibrium Green’s function (EGF)
formalism (Those who do not know EGF formalism may consult [30]).
2.1
Contour ordered Green functions
All non-equilibrium problem can be described by following Hamiltonian:
H = H0 + Hi + H′(t)
(2.1)
where H0 is "simple" part of Hamiltonian, which means that it can be diagonalized, and Wick theorem is
applicable. Hi is "complicated" part, meaning that it contains many-body aspects of the problem. H′(t)

contacts which have diﬀerent chemical potentials. Normally H′(t) is switched on at t = t0 and vanishes

this procedure lets us exclude the discussion of transient phenomena. To describe transient phenomena,
diﬀerent treatment is needed. Since the central object to discuss in this thesis is not transient phenomena,
We skip the discussion of needed treatment for transient phenomena: an interested reader may consult

Chapter 3
Nonequilibrium Techniques in
Mesoscopic Tunneling Structures
In this chapter, one very well-known formalism using NEGB theorem is summarized. This is well more
explained in Chapter 12 in [28]. Even though this formalism is not used in this thesis to calculate current,
since this is the orthodox set-up and end-result can be a starting point of many calculations, we include
this chapter as a basis of the NEGF formalism.
In nonequilibrium perturbation scheme, it cannot be assumed that a target system goes back to
thermodynamic equilibrium state. In other words, there is no symmetry between t = −∞and t = +∞,
which is utilized for the derivation of equilibrium perturbation scheme.
To avoid this problem, the
time evolution is taken to be from t = −∞to t = t0, which is the moment of interest, and goes back
to t = −∞. Thus, all states can be deﬁned to be prepared in the remote past. In this case, unlike
equilibrium scheme, one must to take two time branches on an equal footing.
Here, tunneling problems is considered using nonequilibrium formalism. The system under consider-
ation is that there is the central region coupled to left/right contacts (leads) from left/right, and each
region is in thermal equilibrium in the remote past. Their equilibrium distribution is characterized by
their respective chemical potentials. Then, the coupling starts and treated as perturbations.

Part IV
Conclusion
52
==PAGE:55==
As a summary, we have investigated the eﬀect of the spin bath perturbation on the electronic and
transport properties of HESs using both the equilibrium and nonequilibrium Green’s function formalism.
The three systems are considered in this thesis: isotropic, anisotropic (spin in the bath is align to the
one speciﬁc direction), and isotropic with an external magnetic ﬁeld. The real system of our model is
considered to be HESs in the HgTe quantum well with Mn magnetic impurities.
Using the Matsubara formalism, we performed the perturbation expansion with respect to the spin
bath potential and calculated DOS. The perturbation of the isotropic spin bath does not have a huge
eﬀect on the DOS as can be seen in Fig. 5.7 when up to the second order Feynman diagrams are included
in the calculation. Two HESs have conducting property, and no gap opening has been seen as shown in
[16]. In the case of the transport property, we employed the formulation used in [34, 35], i.e. deﬁning
the density matrix as in Eq. (5.76) since in our system, right-(left) moving electron is injected from
left (right) leads which have diﬀerent chemical potential and temperature. Using this formulation, we
evaluated the lesser Green’s function deﬁned in Keldysh contour in order to calculate the electric current
in the system.
Ballistic conductance, i.e.
e2/h is obtained in the unperturbed system as it should.
The second order current correction became the same as the one obtained from the Fermi Golden rule.
The result tells us that even though the order of magnitude is almost negligible as shown in 5.138, the
correction has a linear relation with the number of impurities, not the density of impurities.
In the case of the anisotropic spin bath, the diﬀerence from the isotropic case is just factor 3 for the
self energies and the 2 for the current. It tells that the magnetization align to the z direction in the bath
does not contribute to the current correction while the x and y equally contributes the correction. This
is because sz does not mediate the scattering between two spins in HESs.
Additional external magnetic ﬁeld to the isotropic bath allows the ﬁrst order self energy to have
non-zero value. Since the ﬁrst order self energy does not have the imaginary part, it gives the additional
shift in the HESs energy dispersion on top of the Zeeman shift. Thus, the corresponding DOS remains
constant as in clean system. Because of the energy shift, the result of DOS is separated to three cases:
DOS corresponding to energy dispersion of up-spin, the one for down spin, and the one for both up and
down-spin as shown in Eq. (7.14) and Fig 7.2. In the case of the current, depending on the sign of
the magnetic ﬁeld along the z direction, the bias voltage diﬀerence, i.e. the diﬀerence of the chemical
potential of the left and right leads, is increased or decreased with the amount of Zeeman energy. The
ﬁrst order correction just vanishes because z-component of the spin only remains. As we mentioned
above, the z-component does not contribute to the scattering between two spins in HESs.

In this thesis, we have not taken an eﬀect of the time evolution for the spin bath into account. When
the inter HES scattering occurs (say up-spin to down-spin), the opposite spin ﬂip has to occur for the
spins in the bath as well (down to up). In short, the current corrections from the spin bath perturbation
are dependent on the number of up(down)-spin in the bath, N↑(N↓). However, we assumed that the
numbers of up and down-spin in the bath are equivalent all the time, i.e. N↑= N↓, and this equivalence
does not break as the bath have inﬁnite number of both spins. If we deﬁne the scattering rate of inter
HES as Γ↑←↓for the down-spin to up-spin and Γ↓←↑for the up-spin to down-spin as in [15], the current
correction, the time-evolution of up-spin in the bath, and the one of down-spin are given as

(7.25)
∂tN↑= Γ↑←↓−Γ↓←↑
(7.26)
∂tN↓= Γ↓←↑−Γ↑←↓.
(7.27)
Note that the spin indices in the HESs are opposite to the spin ﬂip in the bath. The increase of the
number of up-spin in the bath occurs together with the inter HESs scattering of up-spin to down-spin.
Thus, evidently, in steady state, the both rates, Γ↑←↓/Γ↓←↑are equivalent and the current correction
becomes 0. In this case, the ﬁnite bias voltage induces the diﬀerence of the number of up and down-spin
in the bath, i.e. magnetization. The detail of the discussion above is explained in [15].
To get the same physics above, what we have to do is to deﬁne the spin-spin correlation function for
