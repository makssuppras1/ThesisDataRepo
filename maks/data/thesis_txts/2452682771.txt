Abstract
The dissertation reviews the renewable energy trends that the Vietnamese power sector is
experiencing.
In past decades, the sustainable power generation was high thanks to hydro
resources. However, with the increasing demand Vietnam is undergoing, the share has been
diluted due to coal power installation.
In the second chapter of the text, the author describes the energy context in which Vietnam
is. In there, pieces of information build the knowledge to wonder why the renewable energies
are suﬀering a delay in Vietnam while a massive deployment is happening worldwide. The
method used is to develop a discounted cash-ﬂow analysis in order to investigate in economic,
ﬁnancing and investment feasibility for three technologies: wind, solar and coal. Then, the
policies implemented in the power sector are analysed and recommendations are formulated
from there.
The results show that the solar technology is the best and only feasible option with 11.57%
internal rate of return (IRR). However, the sensitivity analysis show that the coal project is
strongly aﬀected by fuel costs and electricity price and could be feasible for public entities as
Electricity of VietNam (EVN) (7.74% IRR). Indeed, in 2016, 77.3% of the installed capacity
was state-owned.
Nearshore wind output is bad, 6.39% IRR. The lack of a wind industry
and know-how makes this technology have this result. However, the policies analysis section
identiﬁes a gap to improve. With the same net support that the feed-in-tariﬀgives but applied
as investment support, the project would be feasible (12.01%). Other institutional barriers as
the non bankability of the power purchase agreements or the number of approvals needed makes
the deployment of renewable energies be shifted.
Nonetheless, the F-i-T have recently boosted renewable energy deployment. Meanwhile in 2017
there was 189.2 MW of wind capacity and 8 MW of solar capacity installed, now there is 304.85
MW and 5051.69 MW.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Before the industrial period, carbon levels followed a natural cycle [1]. The ﬁrst power plants
and grids were installed in the end of the 19th century, which led to the most prosperous
period in human history [2].
At that time, energy accessibility informed technological and
social progress. Thus, a trend tying economic growth to energy consumption was established.
By then, the most common use of electricity production was coal. This framework established
a tendency to consume fossil fuels that still persists.
Thanks to scientiﬁc investigation and research, today it is known how pollution negatively
aﬀects the environment and results in the warming of the earth [3]. In addition, air contamination
has a major impact on public health, causing respiratory deceases. This provokes monetary
losses, decreases social welfare and results in a market failure.
However, ﬁrst with the Kyoto protocol in 1997 and then with the Paris agreement in 2015,
society took the ﬁrst steps towards a more sustainable future. This last agreement was signed
by 195 countries on April 22nd 2016. The aim of the Paris agreement is to reduce global carbon
emissions to stay below a 1.5 oC temperature increase with regards to pre-industrial levels. As
a global problem, countries have set national commitments, the so-called nationally determined
contributions or NDCs.
The business as usual (BaU) scenario in Vietnam projects: 474.1
million tons of CO2 equivalent for 2020 and 787.4 million ton of CO2 equivalent for 2030. Their
NDC is to reduce their projected emissions by 8% by 2030 [4].
The power industry is a key sector in order to stay under the permitted temperature level.
In fact, the International Energy Agency (IEA) sets the coal-ﬁred electricity generation plants
as responsible for 30% of total CO2 emissions [5]. Hence, renewable energy is considered as a
central piece for the energy transition. Yet, Vietnam has not taken the necessary steps towards
a greener future; in 2005 the share of renewable generation (excluding hydro) was 0.093% and
in 2015 0.118% 1 of the total energy mix.
Nonetheless, Vietnam faces other challenges that, with coordination and preparation, can be
turned into opportunities. The electricity consumption is projected to grow signiﬁcantly in

A variety of recent studies have shown the feasibility of high shares of sustainable power
generation in Vietnam [6][7][8][9].
In these, mathematical models are used to ﬁnd optimal
least-cost solutions.
Even though Vietnam is now taking advantage of its economic boost,

Studies found RE generation feasible and foreign investors hesitate to enter the Vietnamese

Coal generation is forecast to increase while renewable energy generation faces diﬃculties
increasing capacity in the Vietnamese market.
This begs the question: why is fossil fuel
generation delaying renewable technology deployment in the Vietnamese power sector?
1.2
Objective and scope
This dissertation will try to clarify why in Vietnam the deployment of renewable energies (RE)
is still delayed. According to the Vietnam energy outlook report (EOR) 2017, the ﬁrst results
of the Vietnam EOR 2019 are consistent with historical trends: power generation coming from
coal technologies will experience a steep increase.
This project investigates the feasibility of diﬀerent, relevant RE projects as well as a coal project
and considers the respective externalities. These steps allow for a critical investigation on tax
and support levels in Vietnam. However, precursory stages are required in order to do a proper
study.
These stages are: understanding the Vietnamese power system, gather policies and regulations
governing it, describe the energy structure of the country and analyse the most relevant local
renewable sources. At a more generic level, understand the energy economic theory necessary to
perform a discounted cash ﬂow (DCF) analysis of each considered project. The input parameters
in the tool are crucial; therefore, a critical review is fundamental to have the most reliable data.
The method used will determine the environmental and regulatory feasibility of a sustainable
energy project, perform a ﬁnancial analysis and assess the risks and uncertainties related to
investment and bankability results. Diﬀerent sensitivity analyses will be performed to ﬁnally
formulate recommendations to possible decision-makers.
The aim of the dissertation is to investigate in the renewable development delay. Two sustainable
technologies will be chosen for further studies. By comparing diﬀerent green project outcomes,
it is possible to extract conclusions on the strengths and weaknesses of each one.
Health externalities will be calculated empirically for a coal power plant and will be linked
to the tax level. This relation is important in order to assess if the pollution impact is well
internalised. The energy economic theory section will show that supports have to be settled
in accordance to technology and market development. An assessment of Vietnamese supports
and market conditions ﬁt will be done.

In this essay, barriers and challenges from a project investor and institutional point of view
will be reviewed. It will not cover the Transmission System Operator side of integrating and
managing the intermittent generation. The social acceptance or awareness of climate pollution
is not included. The discussion makes recommendations to policymakers but does not calculate
optimal taxes or support.
1.3
Methodology and outline
After this introductory part, a chapter covering the background and context of the thesis follows.
At the end of that part, a section presents prior art. This connects with the following chapter
3. It covers the energy economic theory, which gives the necessary policies knowledge needed
to do a consistent analysis on that.
While in technical and economic feasibility studies the authors work directly with a given
project, in this case there are not speciﬁc requirements.
In order to identify the relevant
sustainable technologies, a ﬁrst step will be to narrow the scope by doing a technology screening
as it is done in multi-criteria decision analysis [10] .
Chapter 4, sustainable technology one by one, collect the most important information. Including:
regulation, energy potential in the country, Feed-in-Tariﬀ(F-i-T), levelised cost of energy
(LCoE), governmental targets, Danish Energy Agency (DEA) Balmorel results and other supports.
Having gathered the necessary data, a discussion on which power generators are more relevant
is done. Based on that, two technologies are chosen for further analysis. The majority of the
relevant data applied is public, facilitating the process
The ﬁrst phase for the project consists of ﬁnding a speciﬁc location and size of the power plant.
This will be carried out in chapter 5. There, the key values of the three technologies will be
presented. Chapter 6 gives more details on the methodology used in the DCF analysis and the
results calculation for the feasibility projects.
After ﬁnalizing the above steps, it is possible to compare and analyse the results of each case.
The results are presented in chapter 7. This procedure helps to understand the weaknesses and
advantages of every project. The conclusions extracted from each result analysis are expected
to be more consistent thanks to the comparison methodology.
Finally, chapter 8, will discuss the health cost externalities, tax level and support level in the
Vietnamese power sector and how modiﬁcations can aﬀect its system.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter’s objective is to give the reader an understanding of the energety sector in Vietnam.
As well, to give a clear framework in where this investigation is situated in academia. Moreover,
the political aspect is also covered as it plays a key role in Vietnam’s power system structure.
2.1
Danish Energy Partnership Programme
This master’s thesis project is in the framework of the Danish Energy Partnership Programme
(DEPP) lead by the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) and the Ministry Of Industry and Trade
(MOIT). It is acknowledge introduce this collaboration as it has an important contribution to
this work.
The DEPP started in 2008 in China with the aim of sharing and exchanging technological
knowledge within the Energy industry with other countries. Nowadays, the DEA works together
with 12 countries; Vietnam being one of them [11]. The knowledge exchange vary from long
term energy planning to provide funding and technical assistance depending on the focus of the
program.
The Vietnamese government hired the DEA services for the ﬁrst time in 2013. The Vietnam
Energy Outlook Report (EOR) 2017 was one of their main contributions in the ﬁrst phase.
This document will be presented in more detail in section 2.4.1. The second phase of this
collaboration started in 2017, which is currently running until mid 2020. The Danish institution
works together with public and private entities in three main capacities:
• Capacity development for long-range energy sector planning with the Electricity and
Renewable Energy Authority (EREA)
• Capacity development for Renewable Energy Integration into the Power System with the
Electricity Regulatory Authority of Vietnam (ERAV)
• Low carbon development in the industrial sector with the Energy Eﬃciency and Sustainable
Development Department (EESD)
The collaboration is within the ﬁrst line, ”Capacity development for long-range energy sector
planning”. Many stakeholders are involved with it and will give relevant data and information

2.2
Vietnamese power system
In 1975, after the war against USA, the North and the South of Vietnam were united and formed
a communist country, which it still is nowadays [12]. However, in 1986, Vietnam launched a
number of political and economic reforms known as ”Doi Moi”. Those policies were the ﬁrst
step for Vietnam towards an open market and a rapid growth in their economy [13].
For the next 30 years, Vietnam GDP grew at an average rate of 6.7% per year (World Bank).
In Vietnam, GDP and energy consumption are correlated [14]; during the last two decades the
electricity consumption grew at an average 12.79% per year (Institute of Energy data, EOR
2019). Figure 2.2.1 indicates the evolution of GDP and power consumption over the years in
Vietnam.
This quick development has not been followed by a similar trend in the generation installed
capacity and self-suﬃciency of domestic fossil fuels. Due to that, Vietnam became from being
a net exporter of energy to a net importer in the year 2015 [6]. This is a major problem from
the government viewpoint.

2.2.3
Electricity market
This part covers how the electricity market works in the South-East Asian country. This is
vital as it introduces the market mechanisms for investors and players in the energy industry.
Electricity of Vietnam (EVN) is the local and only utility. It is state owned, so ruled by the
government. It is also in charge of operating the grid (SO).
EVN is responsible of smaller entities which are in charge of speciﬁc tasks. The power plants
owned by the public utility are gathered together forming ”Gencos”, generation companies.
7 of 91
==PAGE:15==

The Transmission and Distribution System Operator form the National Power Transmission
(NPT). In the other hand, the Power Corporations (PCs) work as electricity retailers. Finally,
the National Load Dispatch Center (NLDC) is under the management of EVN. It is responsible
of the electricity market to supply enough energy to the grid and keep it balanced 1.
Apart from EVN there are many other producers known as Independent Power Producers
(IPP). These producers are able to monetize the energy they produce in three ways.
• Feed-In-Tarif. This support is available for some technologies. This mechanism works as
in Europe and will be presented in detail in Chapter 4.
• Power Purchase Agreements. It is possible to do it with EVN. However, private PPAs
are not in place nowadays.
• Electricity Market. Which will be explained in detail below.
The market ruled in Vietnam is diﬀerent compared to European standards. In November 2013,
the Primer Minister (PM) approved a road map to liberalize the electricity market (Decision
No 63/2013/QD-TTg). Until then, the generator revenues were stated by the government with
PPAs.
This plan started in 2016 by introducing a pilot Electricity Wholesale Electricity Market
(VWEM) for suppliers. Nowadays, it is in full operation; every producer can bid in the spot
market, who works under the same principles as in the European Union. However, the retail
market is not coupled with the supplier one. The price for consumers is settled by EVN, MOIT
and the PM. The liberalization is planned to be completed in 2023, when the Electricity Retail
Market will enter in full operation.
Even though there are great eﬀorts towards an optimal and liberalised market, the current
situation entails big risks. EVN is a public entity but has its own budget and is self-ﬁnanced.
Therefore, when suppliers bid in the market at high prices, resulting from high coal prices or
during the dry seasons, the retail price they receive does not reﬂect the real cost of electricity
as retail prices are ﬁxed. This can entail monetary losses for EVN. Moreover, the feed-in-tariﬀ,
is a support paid by them. All this, place the public utility in complicated situations when
confronting challenges and issues; they have no power to confront them.
Finally, in the case of payment problems, the government does not take responsibility in paying
the IPPs and ”gencos”. This creates uncertainty in case EVN goes bankrupt.

3.1
Optimal regulation theory
3.1.1
Introduction
To understand optimal regulations it is necessary to go through the concept of market failure
and market theory. The ﬁgure 3.1.1 is a simpliﬁcation of how demand and supply work in
markets and the main actors involved.
Figure 3.1.1: Demand-Supply chart
16
==PAGE:24==
Analysis of the renewable trends in the Vietnamese power sector with feasibility studies
The demand and supply curves set a price (P1) and quantity (Q1) at which the market would
be optimal. The green area corresponds to the consumer surplus (CS) and the red area to
the producer surplus (PS). The social optimal allocation pass by maximizing CS and PS as
the social welfare (SW) is deﬁned as: SW=CS+PS. An optimal market outcome is a Pareto
optimal which is where improving an agent’s position is done at the cost of another agent. A
market failure occurs when the optimal values are not reached.
The failure happens when, among others, there are not well deﬁned property rights and there
is not proper information available which means there is no appropriate allocation of resources.
Climate change is considered as a market failure due to the non-excludability and non-rivalry
condition of the environment which is considered a public good.
The deﬁnition of excludability is: ”A good is excludable if it is feasible and practical to
selectively allow or prevent consumers from consuming the good”.
For example, it is not
possible to exclude any consumer from using clean water. On the other hand, rivalry means:
”A good is rival if one person’s consumption of the good diminishes the amount of the same
good available to others for consumption”. Logically, breathing fresh air does not lower the
available air for others.
Non-rivalry means a marginal cost of 0, entailing no market development. In addition, non-
excludability permits free-riders; which utilize goods without contributing their fair share.
These two combined lead to a market failure.
Externalities are related to the non-optimal allocation of resources. An externality is when
agents take non-responsibility for the costs they entail or have total beneﬁt from their actions.
Therefore, all kind of contamination are considered as externalities. In the power sector, mainly
related to polluting contaminants as CO2, NOX or SOX among others.
When a company maximizes its beneﬁts, it looks into the marginal cost (MC) it has for
producing goods. However, externalities entail other types of costs to the society. Indeed,
the true MC value for the society is calculated as: MSC=MDC+MC. Being, MSC the marginal
society cost and MDC the marginal damage cost from externalities. The additional cost would
change the demand-supply curve and lead to a new optimal price (P1) and quantity (Q1).
3.1.2
Externalities
These cost are associated in the power sector, as already mentioned, with the environment. It
is possible to diﬀerentiate between two kinds of pollution. (1) A global contamination, more
related to global warming, ozone depletion or acid rain and (2) a local contamination linked to
health issues within the pollutant region.
The ﬁrst type of impact is harder to tackle as it requires global cooperation between countries
and internalisation of externalities cannot be done. However, local pollution is easier to handle
as countries are motivated to reduce these ineﬃciencies as they are aﬀected themselves. They
can internalise them through diﬀerent methods: taxes, supports for cleaner technologies or force
ﬁrms to abate by legislating a maximum pollution level.
In this dissertation, the externalities assessed will be the local ones. It is more accessible to
investigate on that as many studies link the pollution level to the health costs it provokes. The
method used to assess the costs will be explained in detail in section 8.2.1.

3.2
Regulation mechanisms
In Vietnam there is a polluter pay principle (PPP). The agent responsible for the emissions
has to pay their cost. In theory, by internalising them, the optimal pollution level is reached.
However, the marginal externality costs (MEC) are not simple to assess. Leading to lack of
information and optimal levels not being reached.
Regulation is done in order to improve social welfare. There are two types of regulations:
• Direct regulation, known as command and control (C&C). Legislation in this case sets
mandatory standards as: ﬁlters, mandatory fuel choice or quotas (limiting the pollution
level).
• Market based incentive approach. External cost are internalised through: taxes, tradable
quotas and supports to greener technologies.
However, C&C regulation has defaults. When regulators aims for a global optimal abatement
level (a*), C&C forces emitters to abate to the same level (1/2a*), even if polluters have diﬀerent
abatement costs (MAC1 and MAC2). In an eﬃcient market, the ﬁrm with a lower abatement
cost would abate more in beneﬁt for the ﬁrm with a higher abatement cost. Hence, C&C is an
ineﬃcient regulation. In Vietnam, C&C mechanism are placed, ﬁlters are mandatory through
legislation among others.
But this kind of regulation are also in place in many developed
countries as they set maximum pollution levels. Figure 3.2.1 is a visual explanation of C&C
eﬀects on the market.
Figure 3.2.1: Command and control regulation impact on abatement levels

diﬀerent abatement costs (MAC1 and MAC2), ﬁrms abate diﬀerent amounts until they reach
the same marginal abatement cost (t*). In this case, the ﬁrm with a cheaper MAC would
abate more. Hence, market based regulation is eﬃcient. Again, in the Vietnamese case, this
regulation method is also used. As it will be presented later, there is an environmental tax in
order to compensate for pollution externalities. However, in the analysis section it will be seen

3.3
Market based regulation
This section explains the main mechanisms of market based regulation for externalities. The
dissertation focus on this type of regulation as, when it is well implemented, optimal pollution
levels are reached. Moreover, polluters are responsible to ﬁnd solutions.
This part is divided in supports and taxes. Market based regulation can be done by penalizing
polluters or supporting alternatives solutions with no externalities. Theoretically, in a perfect
world, taxes and supports would make ﬁrms be indiﬀerent to invest in greener or fossil fuel
technologies. The additional MAC would make their MSC equal. In fact, tradable quotas
and green certiﬁcates are respectively a penalty and a support. However, they have the same
economic impact and reach the same results, in theory.
3.3.1
Penalties
In the polluter side, market based regulation relies on taxes and tradable quotas.
Optimal taxes are settled by ﬁnding the total MAC of the industry. This is calculated by
summing individual MACs horizontally. Consumers or polluters are more aﬀected by the tax
depending on the demand elasticity. A higher elasticity implies a higher cost for producers.

Unlike for the solar and wind projects, coal power plants are subjects to the wholesale electricity
market price. As explained previously, this market is still under a testing phase. Indeed, just
the generation side of the market is liberalised, meanwhile the consumer prices are ﬁxed by
EVN, MOIT and the PM. The coal power plant is considered as price taker; thus, not changing
the power price.
In spot markets, perfect market conditions are met when operator and bidding generators have
no information ﬂow between them. This way, optimal prices reﬂecting the true cost of electricity
are reached. In Vietnam, most of the coal power plants are state owned by EVN. In addition,
EVN is the owner of the market regulator NLDC. Vietnamese electricity market by its nature
cannot reach perfect market conditions entailing wrong market signals.

6.1.2
Discount rate
In corporate ﬁnance, a discount rate is the rate of return used to discount future cash-ﬂows
back to their present value. This rate is often a company’s Weighted Average Cost of Capital

of the investment. [59]
Discount rate is usually done with the balance of a company’s account. In this case, the projects
are evaluated as generic not from the viewpoint of a speciﬁc investor; therefore the valuation
cannot be done.
Discount rate is related to values as inﬂation and security of the investment which are themselves
correlated to the economical and political situation of a country. Even though Vietnam has a
great economic evolution, developing countries frameworks lead to risky investments.

the equity amount given by the investor, there is debt repayment and interest repayment. The

Discussion
The total health costs for the 2023 year is 64 millions USD2014. Including the externalities
would mean an increase of 10.11 $2019/MWhe in costs. Consequently, the LCOE would rise
from 67.42 $/MWh to 81.35 $/MWh making coal more expensive than solar.
Vietnam has a polluter pays principle. This implies that polluters should pay for their emissions
and yet, the environmental tax applied is far from reaching the right marginal society cost. The
calculations reveals that the tax is 25 times less than the true cost. With the same methodology,
it has been calculated that under a the Balmorel business as usual scenario, the health costs
would be 273,488.54 M.USD just in coal externalities from 2020 until 2050 in nominal values;
more than Vietnam’s annual GDP. Vietnam would beneﬁt from a proper coal taxation.
In the support side, feed-in-tariﬀsupports properly solar technology. In all the tested cases,
the IRR increases. Neighbouring countries have a more generous tariﬀ, in the Philippines case
maybe driven by its insular power system complexity. In the EU in the other hand, it can be due
to a higher power price. In any case, Vietnam has signals to have reached a technology maturity
and has overcome technological risks. As a consequence, applying an investment support policy
would make projects very proﬁtable (29.6% IRR), even at the same real monetary aid level.
This could entail in a saturation of the solar market.
However, when applying a feed-in-premium support, the economic feasibility gives an unfeasible
solution. The solar farm is still at a regulatory risk level, mainly due to the wholesale market
complexity in Vietnam.
In the other hand, Ben Tre wind farm has good results with generous feed-in-tariﬀs. The Asian
countries have less wind resources than Vietnam, explaining the high supports to develop this
technology [20]. But for Ireland and Portugal it has even a lower IRR than for the unfeasible
studied case. In the french case, a power system ran by nuclear, RE are being promoted to
meet EU targets. The F-i-T does not work Vietnam, this can be low resource. In fact, oﬀshore
wind has a capacity factor around 37%, meanwhile in the studied case it was 28%. In addition,
it can be due to an immature market, when an investment support policy is applied in the Ben
Tre case, the porject becomes feasible with an interesting IRR, 12.01%. As mentioned, this
support is the same in real values, which would not make any diﬀerence to the aid provider.
Thus, it is reasonable to recommend Vietnam decision-makers to use this type of support.
Solar is in a next deployment stage due to a simpler technology implementation. This ease to
import the know-how. Wind oﬀshore, entails more complexity. In fact, sea ﬂoor data is unique
to the area and wind resources are less predictable. This makes the wind nearshore OPEX and
CAPEX be higher than for the danish case. To move forward in the technology maturity, it
is necessary to implement investment supports. If so, in the long-term a wind industry will be
build by acquiring know-how and a greener power system will be reached.

Chapter 9
Conclusions
Vietnam is facing a growth in its coal power plants capacity and thus, in CO2 emissions and
health costs. In the upcoming years there are 16.13 GW of commissioned coal power plants.
Why is there an increasing raise of coal generation in Vietnam? First, coal LCOE is still lower
than renewable sources; at same conditions it is more attractive. However, the feasibility study
showed that the project is unfeasible as a private investor, 7.74% IRR. Yet, the coal price
used could be overpriced. Indeed, the sensitivity showed that a little change in the price would
entail in feasible outputs. By using imported coal instead of domestic, the IRR would change to
8.15%. In fact, 83% of the commissioned plants will use imported coal. Private companies and
mainly EVN could assume lower discount rates, making their projects feasible. On top of that,
EVN is state owned, meaning that it could beneﬁt from others power plant revenues to balance
their overall budget. Being Vietnam communist, to meet the energy demand is compulsory,
leading to non-economic reasons to build coal projects. In fact, in 2016, 61.2% of total installed
capacity was owned by EVN. PetroVietnam and Vinacomin, two others state-owned entities,
had 11.5% and 4.6%.
The new coal installation will lead to higher emissions in particles harmful for health. As seen
in the literature review, switching to more expensive technologies could lead in savings in the
externality side. In this dissertation it has been presented that the SO2, NOx and PM emissions
associated to a coal power plant have an external impact of 10.11 USD/MWh, which is not
internalised. The environmental tax has been demonstrated to be ineﬃcient as it is 25 times
lower than its impact. There is a natural discouragement to develop stricter policies as the
main agent aﬀected would be also governmental.
Paris Agreement aim is to lower CO2 emissions and world health organisation links 60,000
diseases to air contamination in 2016.
There is a path towards sustainable development as Vietnam has good potential. Solar feasibility
has shown good economic and investment results with a 11.57% IRR and 18.53 % IRRi. The
feed-in-tariﬀapplied in Vietnam is correct for the technology maturity. In fact, the number of
installed MW in 2019 supports this. Solar technology will play a key role in the future power
sector, probably reaching the projected scenarios seen in the literature and maybe surpassing
them.
On the other hand, oﬀshore wind seems to have a capacity factor problem. The feed-in-tariﬀ

open wind speed oﬀshore data.
A challenge Vietnam is facing is the distribution RE resources. These are located in the same
area making. It could entail in grid congestion if the network is not prepared. In fact, some
solar projects have suﬀered delays due to this. A recommendation is to support and develop
RE industry in the north part as well as the hydro resources located there could balance their
intermittence and save grid losses.
Regarding the regulatory framework, renewable projects face problems when attracting international
developers. (1) PPA are not bankable, (2) Vietnam’s organisation is complex and (3) renewable
projects are obliged to comply with regulations on power development plans, the power purchasers
and power purchase agreement (PPA) execution process, and approvals and consents from the
authorities.
In the case of the wholesale market, good eﬀorts are done towards an open and liberalised
market, which is good. The supplier side is already in a developed stage and the retail market
is estimated to be coupled in 2023. It is important that for then, the NLDC is an independent
body from EVN in order to ensure fair competition and have optimal results.
The research question has been posed in a crucial moment. Vietnam is experiencing a steep
increasing demand combined with a sustainable movement worldwide. Vietnamese actors are
making a great eﬀorts to change carbon trends and promote RE. Still, there can be relevant
improvement with adjustments.
