Abstract
Quantum processes consisting of a sequence of experimental operations on an open
quantum system, are complicated by the presence of temporal correlations. Recently
the process tensor was proposed as a natural framework in which to model such
processes [16]. The explicit construction of this process tensor for a relevant open
systems model, has been an open problem.
This master‚Äôs thesis present two new developments along these lines. The Ô¨Ārst is a
novel new numerical algorithm for eÔ¨Éciently simulating the process tensor numerically
exact, for open systems with Gaussian environments. The algorithm makes use of
tensor network methods originally developed for problems within condensed matter
physics. We apply the algorithm to spin-boson type models, and it is found that
the computational resources required are signiÔ¨Ācantly smaller than what is the case
for earlier numerically exact methods. The second development is a generalization
of the discretised Nakajima-Zwanzig master equation. Recently it was shown that
the conventional Nakajima-Zwanzig equation describing the reduced density operator
can be seen as the result of a tomographic reconstruction of an arbitrary open system
dynamics [2], [4]. Here we generalize this idea to the case of multi-step processes,
and show that this generalization makes it possible to perform long-time simulations
eÔ¨Éciently.
Originally the process tensor framework was developed within the quantum infor-
mation community to provide an operationally meaningful notion of what constitutes
a Markovian quantum process [13]. Given such a notion it becomes possible to in-
troduce meaningful measures of the degree of non-Markovianity. Both the developed
methods are applied to study various controlled processes of the spin-boson model,
and memory e‚ÜĶects are investigated using these new non-Markovianity measures.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Memory e‚ÜĶects in open quantum dynamics have been the focus of active research
for decades, as they play a critical role in a wide span of physical and chemical pro-
cesses, ranging from the chemical activity of complex organic molecules [37] to the
optical emission of semiconductor quantum dots [36], the latter being of consider-
able importance to the development of single photon sources [6]. An illustration of
an open quantum system is shown in Ô¨Āgure 1.1. When the interaction between a
system and its environment is weak and the environment unstructured, it can of-
ten be assumed that the dynamics is memoryless, or Markovian, leading to relatively
simple time-local equations of motion for the system [19]. However, whenever system-
environment interactions become stronger, or if the environment is structured, the
dynamical description of a quantum process could in general be vastly more complex,
with non-Markovian memory of the system‚Äôs past inÔ¨āuencing its future evolution.
A host of techniques have been developed to account for non-Markovian e‚ÜĶects
in quantum dynamics, with greater or lesser breadth of applicability. Conventional
approaches usually fall into two classes; the Ô¨Ārst is analytic approaches based on
the formally exact Nakajima-Zwanzig formalism [19].
The method works well for
a wide range of problems but is restricted to regimes with weak damping, high-
temperatures, short memory time or short simulation time [4]. The second class of
approaches is to adopt the harmonic bath approximation where the environment is
treated as a collection of harmonic oscillators, in this case it is possible to design
powerful numerical algorithms making use of the Gaussian inÔ¨āuence functional [7],
[20], [21], [22]. However these methods su‚ÜĶer from poor convergence with system
size, memory length and the strength of the dissipation. This limits the ability to
apply them to important processes involving large complex systems or where long
time dynamics is important.
Most of the methods developed to model non-Markovian quantum systems, have
focused on the reduced density operator encoding the state of the system at a given
time. In instances where one seeks to model multiple measurement procedures, or
any other control operations that an experimenter can perform, on an open quantum
system, the standard reduced quantum state provides an incomplete description [3].
The characterization of temporal correlations and the development of tools provid-
ing complete descriptions of multi-time correlations, has gained renewed interest in
recent years. The process tensor [16], and the idea of a super-density operator encod-

In this thesis we investigate how simulation methods developed for studying open

and look at the behaviour of the non-Markovianity measures derived from the new
notion of what constitutes a quantum Markov process [13].
Outline of thesis
The thesis consist broadly of two background chapters, and two results chapters. The
main results of this thesis are the development of two new methods for the eÔ¨Écient
simulation of multi-time quantum processes, these are presented in chapters 4 and 5.
All the computer code used throughout has been developed and implemented by the
author.
Chapter 2 : The basic structure of quantum mechanics, which will be used through-
out the thesis, is outlined. The process tensor is then introduced, as a framework in
which multi-time controlled quantum processes can be characterized. The key con-
cept of the chapter is the e‚ÜĶective state which encodes the temporal correlations of

a quantum process as spatial correlations in a many-body state. The introduction of
such an e‚ÜĶective state enables one to specify a joint probability distribution of tempo-

Chapter 3 : Here we discuss the quantiÔ¨Ācation of memory e‚ÜĶects and the problem
of constructing the e‚ÜĶective state. We describe the operational Markov condition
[13], and outline how meaningful measures of non-Markovianity can be deÔ¨Āned. An-
alytical approaches to simulation are commonly structured around the use of the
Nakajima-Zwanzig formalism [19]. We also show that such techniques are applicable
to the e‚ÜĶective density operator, in the process we point out the connection between
the quantum regression theorem and the operational deÔ¨Ānition of Markovianity. The
chapter ends with a discussion of how many-body quantum states can be represented
eÔ¨Éciently by using tensor networks, and why it is reasonable to assume that the ef-
fective state should have en eÔ¨Écient matrix product state representation.
Chapter 4 : In this chapter we develop a tensor network algorithm to simulate the
Gaussian inÔ¨āuence functional. Recently, methods have been proposed utilizing ten-
sor network tools, originally developed to study quantum many-body systems [26].
Most prominently, the proposed time-evolving matrix product operator (TEMPO)
algorithm [7], succeeded in eÔ¨Éciently simulating the Gaussian inÔ¨āuence functional
with a computational complexity growing as a small polynomial. Here we generalize
the TEMPO algorithm to describe multi-time correlations. It is found that the gen-
eralization leads naturally to a modiÔ¨Āed algorithm, which signiÔ¨Ācantly outperforms
previous numerical methods.
Chapter 5 : Numerically exact methods often have to balance an error minimization
with a tolerable complexity scaling. One way to approach this diÔ¨Éculty is with the
transfer tensor method [4], originally developed to study reduced system dynamics.
In this chapter the transfer tensor method is generalized to describe multi-time corre-
lation functions, making it possible to generate two-point functions, and higher, very
eÔ¨Éciently, and with an approximately constant error. The developed method is used
to study memory e‚ÜĶects in a simple controlled process.

In this chapter we will introduce the background quantum theory, that will be used
throughout the thesis. Having outlined the basics, we will turn to a discussion of the
recently developed process tensor framework [16]. This framework provides a useful
conceptual and practical tool for discussing the temporally correlated measurement
outcomes of experiments involving multiple temporally separated measurement pro-
cedures on an open quantum system. The Peres quote above is included to motivate
what is called the operational approach to quantum physics. We seek to model the
things that experimenters do, and to this end we look for a description where the
experimental choices can be seen as probing a underlying probability distribution
independent of the speciÔ¨Āc operation that the experimenter carries out. When the
processes considered include multiple experimental operations at di‚ÜĶerent points in
time, it seems to become impossible to identify a probability distribution which can
be studied independently of the speciÔ¨Āc experimental operations implemented. The
major utility of the process tensor framework is that it achieves such a separation of
controlled and uncontrolled aspects of the process. We will see that this separation
comes at the price of having to consider an extended state space.
2.1
Basic structure of quantum mechanics
In this Ô¨Ārst section we will discuss the basic structure of quantum mechanics, and
introduce some of the notation and the concepts which will be used throughout.
Quantum mechanics is a probabilistic theory and we will discuss how probability
distributions are represented in quantum mechanics. The material presented here is
completely standard, and can be found in most books on quantum theory such as
[10], [11] and [12].

The mechanical postulate
A physical system is characterized by a collection of variables i.e. spins, positions,
momenta etc.
Each of these variables can take a range of values, and the set of
these values form the phase space of the system. Classical physics builds on the as-
sumption that it is, at least in principle, possible to specify the state of the system
arbitrarily precisely. This means that if the state of the system is speciÔ¨Āed to occupy
a Ô¨Ānite region R of phase space, containing the physical variable values characterizing
the system, then in principle this region could be made arbitrarily small. Quantum
physics is di‚ÜĶerent in that the minimal phase space volume that the state of a system
can occupy can be no smaller than 2‚á°~ per degree of freedom, where ~ is the Planck
constant. A consequence of this constraint is that the physical variables describing
a system can take on only discrete values [35], this fundamental discreteness in the
description is what gives quantum theory its name. Of course if we are interested in
physics on the scales of electrons, photons and atoms, then many variables can take
on e‚ÜĶectively continuous spectra. Technically the physical fact that physical vari-
ables take on discrete values is implemented into a mathematical framework through
the following postulate, that to each physical variable there corresponds a linear op-
erator, and the possible values of the variable are the eigenvalues of the operator.
The justiÔ¨Ācation for this postulate is that there are operators which possess both
discrete and continuous eigenvalue spectra, and thus all possibilities are accounted
for. In representing physical variables by operators, we also introduce the possibility
of non-commuting algebras of these, this non-commutativity is responsible for most
intrinsically quantum phenomenon [11].
The statistical postulate
The next question is how to assign probabilities to the various allowed values of phys-
ical variables. Quantum mechanics is at heart a statistical theory, and has no means
by which to predict the speciÔ¨Āc outcome of a single experiment [11]. It is useful to
divide a quantum mechanical experiment into two phases, there is a preparation pro-
cedure followed by a measurement. A speciÔ¨Āc preparation procedure determines the
probabilities of the various possible outcomes of the measurement. The preparation
procedure is independent of the subsequent measurement, and therefore determines
the probabilities of all conceivable measurements.
This leads us to introduce the
idea of a quantum state, which is the probability distribution associated with a spe-
ciÔ¨Āc preparation procedure. A state preparation procedure may be a sequence of
control operations performed by an experimenter, or it might be a natural process.
Importantly any two preparation procedures which give rise to the same probability
distribution, are said to prepare the same state. In quantum mechanics the state is
represented mathematically by a density operator, also called a state operator, ‚áĘ, and

3.3
Correlation structure and tensor networks
The e‚ÜĶective state will in general inhabit an exponentially large state space. Intu-
itively we would suspect that certain classes of states in this full available space are
more relevant than others. For example, it is in most cases physically sensible to
suspect that memory e‚ÜĶects which are more short ranged are more important than
memory e‚ÜĶects which are longer ranged. Thus it would be useful to Ô¨Ānd a way of
parameterizing the state space such that these more local states are treated prefer-
entially. We know that a Markovian quantum process is represented by a product
state, which can be seen as the most local class of states. One would expect that
the introduction of memory e‚ÜĶects into the process would produce an e‚ÜĶective state
which is close to the class of product states. Luckily there is a class of states which
can be seen as expansions around the manifold of product states, these are the matrix
product states [26]. In this section we introduce the matrix product states, and in
the next chapter they are applied extensively.

a diagram as in Ô¨Āgure 3.1 where dangling legs of the diagram represent the indicies.

3.4
Concluding remarks
In this chapter we have deÔ¨Āned what is meant by a Markovian quantum process,
and discussed the relative entropy measure of non-Markovianity. We have outlined
how master equation methods applies to modelling the e‚ÜĶective density operator, and
pointed out the connection between the operational notion of Markovianity and the
regression theorem. Master equation methods are often diÔ¨Écult to apply for non-
Markovian processes, and instead one has to turn to numerical simulation methods.
We have introduced some of the basic notions of tensor network theory, and argued
why representing quantum process as matrix product states would be physically sen-
sible.

propagator network should be signiÔ¨Ācantly faster than the TEMPO network.

5.4
Concluding remarks
In this chapter we have introduced and generalized the transfer tensor method. The
original method provided a powerful tool for propagating the reduced density operator
eÔ¨Éciently, and with an approximately constant error. The generalization presented
here makes it possible to do the same for multi-time statistics. We have used this
new method to study memory e‚ÜĶects in the steady state of the spin-boson model,
and found quantitative di‚ÜĶerent behaviours in the case of subsequent measurements
of the spin localization.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and outlook
In this thesis we have developed methods to simulate the joint probability distribution
of multiple temporally correlated measurements, or control operations, on an open
quantum system. It was recently shown that these probability distribution can be
conveniently expressed as an e‚ÜĶective state ([16]), and the unifying theme has been
the development of simulation tools for this object.
In chapter 2 we argued that the joint probability distributions describing multi-
time controlled quantum processes can be encoded in an e‚ÜĶective state operator.
The introduction of such an operator makes it possible to study multi-step processes
independently of the speciÔ¨Āc controls implemented. Chapter 3 introduced the oper-
ational deÔ¨Ānition of a quantum Markov process, and investigated the structure of a
Markovian e‚ÜĶective state. It was illustrated formally that standard master equation
techniques are also applicable to the e‚ÜĶective state, and that there is a clear connec-
tion between the operational notion of Markovianity and a process described by the
regression theorem. Lastly we discussed tensor network representations of quantum
states, and argued that one could expect the e‚ÜĶective state to have an eÔ¨Écient ma-
trix product state structure. In chapter 4 we introduced and generalized the tensor
network approach to simulating the spin-boson model inÔ¨āuence functional. It was
found that the generalized algorithm could be modiÔ¨Āed, and a signiÔ¨Ācant improve-
ment in the eÔ¨Éciency of the algorithm was achieved. To our knowledge this is the
Ô¨Ārst algorithm achieving a constant scaling for modelling harmonic oscillator envi-
ronments, and the computer code used has been implemented and developed from
scratch. Chapter 5 discussed the use of tomographic reconstruction techniques for
modelling the behaviour of open quantum systems. The successful transfer tensor
approach has been generalized to describe multi-time processes, which makes it pos-
sible to construct few-point statistics highly eÔ¨Éciently and with an approximately
constant error. The developed methods have been used to study memory e‚ÜĶects in
the steady state of the spin-boson model. To our knowledge this is the Ô¨Ārst results
which successfully establishes a generalization of the transfer tensor method, and the
applications of the method in open system dynamics and condensed matter physics
are potentially vast.

Outlook
A natural next step is to gain access to a high-performance computing facility. This
would make it possible to investigate how the CaMPP algorithm performs on com-
putationally diÔ¨Écult problems. These problems could include carrying out a detailed
examination of the Ohmic localization transition for the spin-boson model, and the
sub-Ohmic localization transition. For the sub-Ohmic case in particular, the simu-
lation of the localization transition is an open problem. Another direction of future
work would be to include multiple baths at di‚ÜĶerent temperatures into the model,
and apply the CaMPP algorithm to study energy transport in such networks. As the
algorithm simulates the e‚ÜĶective state, it would also be possible to study a range of
speciÔ¨Āc controlled processes.
The generalized transfer tensor method has been developed and implemented,
but due to time constraints it has not been applied widely. Perhaps the major area
of application of the method is in the simulation of Greens functions in condensed
matter physics. Greens functions are essentially two-point correlation functions. In
many cases powerful numerical methods exist for simulating Greens functions over a
restricted time interval, however the error accumulation often becomes problematic.
The transfer tensor method would make it possible to generate an extended set of
Greens functions, from an initially constructed set, with an approximately constant
error.
This would make it possible to study for example steady state behaviour
numerically. On a di‚ÜĶerent note, the transfer tensor method makes it possible to
study the validity of a regression theorem description for a wide class of interesting
open systems models. In the near future the plan is to apply the method to study
memory e‚ÜĶects in pump-probe spectroscopy.

Appendix A
Notes on developed code
The tensor network simulation and the transfer tensor simulation has been imple-
mented using the python programming language. The design of both the algorithms
have been explained in the main text. Technically the main diÔ¨Éculty is to develop
the set of basic tensor operations which are required. For the code developed in this
work, all tensors have been represented as matrices. In that case the only operations
required are the ability to contract indicies, the ability to raise and lower indicies,
and the ability to shift indicies around.
Having developed the tools necessary to manipulate matrices, the tensor network
simulation can be implemented in a conceptually simple way. The compression of
a matrix product state resulting in a given step is the most challenging part. Here
we use a standard SVD compression algorithm with multiple left and right sweeps
as outlined in [34], [25]. The SVD compression approach is usually fast, however it
is not always the best method to Ô¨Ānd the optimal representation of the full matrix
product state. Alternatives include variational compression schemes [25].
The transfer tensor algorithm is technically much simpler to implement.
One
complication in the case of the general set of tensors, that is the set not restricted to
a speciÔ¨Āc class of instruments, is that it is necessary to add together matrix product
states. How this is done is outlined in [25].
