Abstract 
 
The bleeding disorder hemophilia A is caused by deficiencies in coagulation Factor 
VIII (FVIII). Treatment of the disease consist of replacement therapy with 
recombinant or plasma-derived FVIII. A high affinity binding between FVIII and von 
Willebrand Factor (VWF) protects the coagulation factor from rapid clearance, but 
poor plasma stability is still an issue and frequent intravenous injections are required 
for prophylaxis. 
The a3 region (E1649-R1689) of FVIII has been shown to play a crucial role in the 
binding between VWF and FVIII. The region is highly acidic and has been post-
translationally modified with two sulfotyrosines (see Figure I). A study of the binding 
interaction between a3 and analogues hereof with VWF could provide knowledge for 
potential prolongation of the half-life of recombinant FVIII. 
 
 
Figure I: The sequence of the a3 acidic region in FVIII. 
 
 
In this thesis the a3 peptide was synthesized by automated solid phase peptide 
synthesis by incorporation of neopentyl (nP) protected sulfotyrosines at position 
sY1664 and sY1680. After cleavage from the resin the nP groups were removed by 
solvolysis with 2 M CH3COONH4 at 37 ⁰C. Finally, neutral purification yielded the 
a3 peptide in excellent purity. In addition, mono-desulfated, desulfated, and truncated 
a3 analogues were synthesized in high purity by the same protocol. 
 
Structural analysis of the a3 peptide confirmed the target sequence, however, no 
secondary structure was found and the peptide was determined to be disordered. In 
the pH range from 5 to 8 the a3 peptide showed high stability. 
 
An NMR study elucidated the nP cleavage mechanism. The reaction was found to 
proceed through an SN1 type reaction with methyl migration followed by hydrolysis 
of the formed cation.

1 Introduction  
 
Hemophilia A is a genetic disease caused by deficiencies in coagulation Factor VIII 
(FVIII). The disease is linked to the X chromosome and affects 1 in 5000 male births. 
Patients suffering from hemophilia experience uncontrolled bleedings. The severity 
of the bleedings vary but can in some cases be fatal if untreated.1  
 
The process of hemostasis is responsible for controlling bleedings and making the 
blood coagulate. The process involves a series of events ultimately leading to the 
formation of a stable fibrin clot.2 Fibrin is the final product of the coagulation 
cascade. The coagulation cascade consists of an advanced network of proteolytic 
reactions. It involves multiple coagulation factors that are activated in a highly 
organized order upon vascular injury. Coagulation FVIII serves as a cofactor in one 
of these activation reactions. The cascade reactions result in the generation of 
thrombin, which plays a crucial role in the final clot formation.3 Hemophilia A 
patients miss effective and functional FVIII, which cause an incomplete coagulation 
cascade. 
 
Nowadays hemophilia A is treated with recombinant or plasma-derived FVIII, but 
the infused protein is rapidly cleared from the bloodstream and therefore there is a 
need for improved treatment and prolonged half-life of FVIII.4  
 
The FVIII protein is to some extend protected from clearance by a high affinity 
binding to the protein Von Willebrand Factor (VWF). A small region of FVIII called 
a3 plays a crucial role in this interaction.5,6 How this region interacts with VWF 
remains unknown, but may provide important knowledge to the prolongation of the 
half-life of FVIII. 
 
In this project the synthesis of the a3 peptide in high purity will be explored. The a3 
region is a complicated peptide to synthesize presenting several difficulties including 
the incorporation of sulfotyrosine residues (see Figure 1.1). Subsequently, the 
structure of the a3 peptide is to be investigated for any secondary structure. 
Furthermore, the aim of the project is to develop a procedure for the synthesis of a3 
analogues. This should allow for future interaction studies between VWF and the a3 
region.

2 Hemostasis 
 
Hemostasis is the system that maintains the blood in a fluid and clot-free state under 
normal conditions. During vessel injuries the hemostatic system is responsible for the 
rapid formation of a localized clot. A blood clot is necessary to prevent blood loss 
and to allow healing at the site of vascular injury. In addition, it is important for the 
clot to remain localized to avoid widespread clotting within intact vessels. The 
process of localized clot formation is obtained in four overlapping stages: 
Vasoconstriction, primary hemostasis, secondary hemostasis, and clot removal 
(Figure 2.1).2,7  
 
 
Figure 2.1: The four stages of hemostasis.8 
 
2.1 Vasoconstriction 
 
The first and immediate response to a vascular injury is vasoconstriction also referred 
to as vascular spasm. Local contractions in the smooth muscles cause the blood vessel 
to narrow and thereby decrease the blood flow. This process is mediated by a reflex 
neurogenic mechanism and the endothelial secretion of the vasoconstrictor 
endothelin. The vasoconstriction is transient and is therefore not sufficient in stopping 
the bleeding, consequently primary hemostasis is needed.2 
 
2.2 Primary hemostasis 
 
The purpose of primary hemostasis is the formation of a platelet plug, which rapidly 
stabilizes the injury. Platelets are anuclear cellular fragments that arise by budding 
==PAGE:24==
 
 4 
 
 
from megakaryocytes in the bone marrow.9 The transformation of platelets into a 
hemostatic plug consists of three reactions: Adhesion, granule release, and 
aggregation and consolidation.2 
 
Platelets adhere to the subendothelial collagen of the damaged vascular surface by 
two molecular interactions. VWF binds to surface receptors on the platelet 
membranes and to the exposed collagen mediating a “bridge” between the two. VWF 
is protein secreted by injured endothelium and later by activated platelets. In the other 
interaction glycoprotein (GP) VI on platelets interact directly with exposed collagen.2 
 
When the platelets are adhered to the collagen they undergo activation by changing 
shape and releasing granule contents. Large amounts of ADP, Ca2+, ATP, serotonin, 
VWF, and platelet factor 4 are secreted from platelet granules. The activated platelets 
also generate thromboxane A2 (TxA2).2 
 
TxA2 and ADP are important for platelet aggregation as they both lead to the 
expression of functional GPIIb-IIIa, which is the membrane integrin that mediates 
platelet aggregation. Fibrinogen functions as a bridging molecule between platelets 
and it has multiple binding sites for GPIIb-IIIa, which results in crosslinking of the 
platelets. This interaction is extremely important for the platelet aggregation, which 
ultimately leads to a reversible clot. 
 
2.3 The coagulation cascade (secondary hemostasis) 
 
In order to form a stable clot at the site of the vascular injury secondary hemostasis 
is required. Secondary hemostasis is also referred to as the coagulation cascade.2 The 
model of coagulation was constructed by two independent groups in 1964, which 
described the process as a waterfall or cascade.10,11  
Originally the model was divided into two cascades that converged at the activation 
of Factor X (see Figure 2.2). The two cascades are called the extrinsic pathway, 
requiring an external factor, and the intrinsic pathway with all components present in 
the blood. The two pathways unite to follow the “common” pathway leading to the 
production of a stable fibrin clot.7 
 
==PAGE:25==
 
 5 
 
 
 
Figure 2.2: Simple overview of the coagulation cascade.12  
Blood coagulation is a complex process involving a networked series of protein 
reactions and interactions. These proteins are called plasma coagulation factors. They 
are synthesized by the liver and circulate in the blood as inactive proenzymes. To 
become activated the proenzymes are proteolytically cleaved by the activated factors 
that precede them in the cascade. Calcium ions play a critical role in a number of 
these reactions since they contribute to the right conformation of the proteins and are 
required for the interaction of the proteins with the membrane surface. The reactions 
are catalytic, which ensure a rapid generation of large amounts of fibrin at the site of 
injury.2,3 
 
The traditional cascade model does not include the role of different cell types and a 
cell-based model has therefore been proposed. This model describes secondary 
hemostasis in three distinct but overlapping phases and involves the two cell types: 
Platelets and tissue factor (TF) bearing cells (Figure 2.3).7,13 
 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
 6 
 
 
 
Figure 2.3: The three phases of secondary hemostasis.14 
2.3.1 Initiation phase 
 
The first phase is the initiation phase, which resembles the beginning of the extrinsic 
pathway. Upon vascular injury the subendothelial cells are exposed to the 
bloodstream and they start expressing membrane bound TF. TF is an external factor 
that binds to Factor VII (FVII) and promotes proteolysis thereby activating FVII 
(FVIIa). Together TF and FVIIa form the extrinsic tenase complex, which activates 
small amounts of FIX and FX converting them into FIXa and FXa, respectively. FXa 
is then allowed to associate with its cofactor FVa and forms a prothrombinase 
complex on TF-bearing cells. This complex serves to convert prothrombin (II) into 
thrombin (IIa) generating trace amounts of thrombin.7 
 
 
2.3.2 Amplification phase 
 
The slow accumulation of thrombin is responsible for the amplification phase. 
Thrombin is a key enzyme in the coagulation cascade and serves multiple important 
functions. First of all, thrombin further activates platelets that have already adhered 
to the site of injury. This contributes to a higher level of procoagulant activity. 
Secondly, thrombin activates the cofactors FV and FVIII on the platelet surface. 
FVIII circulates in the plasma bound to VWF and when activated FVIIIa circulates 
unbound. FVIIIa is a cofactor to FIXa and together they support the generation of 
FXa. Third, thrombin converts FXI into FXIa. At the end of the amplification phase 
preparations have been made for large scale thrombin generation.2,7,13 
 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 7 
 
 
2.3.3 Propagation phase 
 
The propagation phase takes place on phospholipid containing surfaces provided by 
activated platelets, activated endothelial cells, and activated leukocytes. The activated 
coagulation factors generated in the amplification phase are used in this phase. FXI 
is either activated by FXIIa or by thrombin. The formed FXIa catalyzes the 
conversion of FIX into FIXa. As mentioned, the pathways and phases are overlapping 
and the TF-FVIIa complex also contributes to the activation of FIX. FIXa associates 
with the cofactor FVIIIa to form the intrinsic tenase complex, which mediates the 
cleavage of FX into FXa. Rapidly, the prothrombinase (FVa/FXa) complex is formed 
on the platelet surfaces and sufficient amounts of thrombin is produced (see Figure 
2.3).7,13  
2.3.4 Clot formation 
 
The final step in secondary hemostasis is the formation of a stable clot and here the 
formed thrombin is used. In the clot formation process thrombin serves two even 
more essential functions than the three described in the amplification phase. 
Thrombin catalyzes the conversion of the plasma protein fibrinogen into fibrin, which 
forms long insoluble polymer fibers. In order for the fibrin chains to form a highly 
stable meshwork, adjacent chains need to be covalently crosslinked. Thrombin 
catalyzes the activation of FXIII and FXIIIa then catalyzes the crosslinking process 
yielding a stable, elastic, polymerized fibrin clot (see Figure 2.4).2,7 
 
 
 
Figure 2.4: Illustration of the clot formation process. II represents prothrombin and IIa is used for 
thrombin. Modifications to the original image has been performed.14 
 
2.4 Clot regulation and removal 
 
Hemostasis is highly regulated in order to maintain localized hemostasis at the site of 
vascular injury. Furthermore, the size of the platelet and fibrin clot must be restricted 
to sustain a continuous blood flow through the vascular lumen. 
Besides procoagulant factors, involved in the coagulation, activated endothelial cells 
also produce anticoagulant factors. These factors ensure that the propagation of the 
==PAGE:28==
 
 8 
 
 
clot does not expand beyond the site of injury. The procoagulant factors are bound to 
the membrane of endothelial cells while the anticoagulant factors are secreted by the 
endothelial cells and are soluble in the blood. This contributes to the localization and 
limitation of hemostasis.2 
 
The endothelium surrounding the injured area is involved in five mechanisms that 
regulate hemostasis. The first mechanism involves prostacyclin, which increases the 
level of cAMP leading to inhibition of platelet aggregation and granule release. 
Secondly, antithrombin III inactivates thrombin and other coagulation factors. 
Protein C and S slow the coagulation cascade by inactivation of the cofactors FVa 
and FVIIIa. This is a feedback mechanism since protein C is activated under thrombin 
excess. The fourth mechanism involves tissue factor pathway inhibitor, which limits 
the action of TF and thereby preventing excessive activation of FIX and FX.2 
 
The last mechanism not only facilitates regulation of hemostasis, but also removal of 
the fibrin clot by the fibrinolytic system upon wound healing. Plasmin is the major 
fibrinolytic protease and plasmin exerts its effect by cleaving fibrin into fibrin 
degradation products. Plasminogen is converted into plasmin in a proteolytic 
cleavage catalyzed by tissue plasminogen activator.2,15 Recent studies have suggested 
that fibrinolysis can occur independently of plasmin with plasmin being substituted 
by other proteases.16

3 Hemophilia 
 
The hemophilias are inherited bleeding disorders caused by genetic defects in the 
blood coagulation proteins. In hemophilia, bleeding occurs due to the failure of 
secondary hemostasis. The most common types of these lifelong bleeding disorders 
are caused by deficiencies in FVIII (hemophilia A) or FIX (hemophilia B). The 
defects lead to low concentrations or even absence of the coagulation factor.1,3,17,18 
  
The most common bleeding disorder is von Willebrand disease, which affects 1 in 
1000 or even more. The incidence of hemophilia is lower and hemophilia A affects 1 
in 5000 male births while hemophilia B affects even fewer with 1 in 30000. It is 
estimated that half a million people worldwide are suffering from hemophilia and the 
disease occurs in all racial groups and has no geographical limitation.1,17,18 
 
There are three forms of hemophilia A: Mild, moderate, and severe. The bleeding 
tendency is related to plasma coagulation factor level or the concentration of FVIII 
compared to normal conditions. An overview of the intervals can be seen in Table 
3.1.1,17 Children with severe hemophilia most often experience their first bleeding 
into joints by the age of four years, but they may also bleed from other sites before 
this age. In most cases of moderate hemophilia, it is diagnosed by the age of five 
years while mild hemophilia may first be diagnosed later in life after a trauma or a 
surgery.17

The gene for coagulation factor VIII is found on the X chromosome and when 
mutated it causes the X-linked recessive trait hemophilia A. Thus, hemophilia usually 
occurs in males. Normally, the affected boy has inherited the mutant gene from his 
carrier mother, but hemophilia can also arise from spontaneous mutations. This 
happens in approximately 30% of hemophilia cases.1 
 
The most common gene mutation in hemophilia A is a large inversion and 
translocation of exon 1-22 away from exon 23-26. This mutation affects 45% of the 
severe cases of the disease. Besides the inversion mutation, point mutations are also 
observed. Missense mutation is mostly seen, but nonsenses mutations also occur and 
about 5% of the mutations are large or small deletions or insertions.17 
 
3.1 Treatment of hemophilia A 
 
Hemophilia A and hemophilia B are clinically indistinguishable, but the treatments 
of the diseases are completely different. The hemophilic conditions are caused by 
deficiency in different coagulation factors and diagnosis is therefore established by 
coagulation factor assays.18 
 
Severe hemophilia A is fatal if untreated. The disease is treated with replacement 
therapy where the deficient coagulation factor is replaced by well-functioning FVIII. 
In the 1970s, plasma concentrates of coagulation factors became available. These 
plasma concentrates were, however, manufactured from pooled plasma collected 
from thousands of donors. This resulted in concentrates contaminated with hepatitis 
B and C that were post-transfused to all hemophilia patients who received the plasma 
concentrate treatment. At that time chronic hepatitis was considered relatively mild 
and non-progressive and the benefits therefore outweighed the risks. But this view 
changed enormously in the beginning of the 1980s. Plasma concentrates had been 
contaminated with HIV and 60-70% of patients with severe hemophilia in western 
Europe and the United States became infected.1 
 
In 1984 the gene for FVIII was cloned20 and this cloning made the production of 
recombinant FVIII possible.17 The currently available recombinant FVIII molecules 
have a number of different structures and they are produced differently e.g. cell types 
and purification methods.4 
 
The recombinant FVIII products are injected intravenously. There are two ways of 
administering FVIII replacements: On demand or by given regular injections 
(prophylaxis). On demand treatment means that only when a patient is exposed to an 
injury FVIII is administered. Prophylaxis has the aim of abolishing spontaneous 
bleedings into joints. Patients with severe hemophilia A are given FVIII three times 
==PAGE:31==
 
 11 
 
 
a week or alternate days. This type of treatment is recommended for children with 
severe hemophilia. Prophylaxis prevents the long-term consequences of joint bleeds 
so even though prophylaxis is expensive in the short term, it might be cost saving in 
a life-time, assuming the adult patient has normal joints and can live a normal life.17 
 
Modifications have been made to the recombinant FVIII molecule in order to enhance 
some of the aspects of its biology in particular half-life, functional activity and 
immunogenicity. The half-life can be prolonged by fusing FVIII with another 
molecule, which either shields the cofactor from its own clearance mechanisms or 
the fused molecule itself has a longer half-life.4 Existing FVIII products have short 
half-lives of approximately 12 hours. However, it has been found that by fusing the 
recombinant FVIII molecule to the Fc domain of the immunoglobulin G (IgG) 
antibody the half-life can be increased to 19 hours21  and polyethylene glycol (PEG) 
conjugations have resulted in half-lives of up to 18.4 hours.22 
 
One of the most serious complications in hemophilia treatment is the development of 
antibodies (inhibitors) to infused FVIII. This occurs in approximately 30% of patients 
with hemophilia A. The antibodies can inhibit FVIII’s interaction with VWF, 
phospholipids, FIX or the antibodies can decrease the half-life of FVIII in plasma.17,23   
 
Current research is looking into prolonging the half-life of recombinant FVIII to 
avoid the frequent injections e.g. in small children. In addition, focus is put on 
reduction or bypassing inhibitory antibodies.4

4 Factor VIII and von Willebrand Factor 
 
FVIII and VWF are important proteins involved in bleeding disorders like hemophilia

described in this section.  
4.1 Structure of Factor VIII 
 
As described, FVIII is a cofactor to FIXa in the coagulation cascade. Together, FVIIIa 
and FIXa on a phospholipid surface in the presence of calcium ions activate FX. The 
role of FVIIIa is to markedly increase the catalytic efficiency of FIXa.24 The sequence 
of FVIII has a length of 2351 residues, where the first part is a 19 residue signal 
peptide and the remaining 2332 amino acid residues is the mature unprocessed 
FVIII.25 In the blood FVIII circulates as a heterodimer composed of two polypeptide 
chains. These chains are derived from a single peptide chain and are divided into a 
light chain and a heavy chain by processing in the Golgi apparatus.26,27 FVIII is 
arranged in six domains with the following structure: A1-A2-B-A3-C1-C2. The first 
three domains are located on the heavy chain and the last three domains on the light 
chain (see Figure 4.1). FVIII share approximately 40% sequence homology with 
FV.26

The three A domains have similar lengths of approximately 330 amino acid residues 
and are 40% identical to each other. Each A domain consists of two connected β-
barrels, which resemble the fold of a typical cupredoxin-type domain.26,28 As 
illustrated in Figure 4.1, the A domains are bordered by short acidic spacer regions 
(a1, a2, and a3), which contain clusters of aspartic acid and glutamic acid.29 The A1 
and A2 domains are followed by a1 and a2 respectively, while the a3 region precedes 
the A3 domain. These regions are post-translationally modified by sulfation of 
specific tyrosine residues.24 The C domains are shorter than the A domains with a 
length of approximately 160 residues. C1 and C2 are distantly related to the discoidin

4.1.1 Activation of Factor VIII 
 
FVIII is proteolytically activated either by thrombin or FXa. The activation involves

is at Arg1689, which releases the a3 acidic peptide from the light chain and generates 
the 73 kDa A3-C1-C2 fragment (Figure 4.3). These actions convert the inactive FVIII 
into the active cofactor (FVIIIa).24,31

4.1.2 Activation of Factor X 
 
FVIIIa is involved as a cofactor in the activation of FX while FIXa functions as the 
enzyme and FX as the substrate. FX and FIX are both glycoproteins and have similar 
structures. They both consist of a γ-carboxyglutamic acid (Gla) domain, two 
epidermal growth factor-like domains (EGF1 and EGF2), and a serine protease (SP) 
domain. The Gla domain, EGF1, and EGF2 are located on the light chain while the 
SP domain is located on the heavy chain (see Figure 4.4). The light and the heavy 
chain are held together by a disulfide bond. The Gla domains of FIXa and FX interact 
with the phospholipid surface on activated platelets in the presence of Ca2+ ions. The 
active site of FIXa is located in the SP domain and FX is activated by the cleavage of 
an internal specific peptide bond (Arg52-Ile53) in the N-terminal region of the heavy 
chain.32,33

complex also called the Xase complex. FVIIIa has the role of increasing the rate of 
the activation reaction. The A1 domain of FVIIIa is the domain interacting with FX. 
The residues of the acidic C-terminus of the A1 domain interact with the SP domain 
of FX.34 The C domains are involved in the interaction with the phospholipid surface

4.3 The interaction between Factor VIII and von Willebrand Factor

protease cleavage and dissociation of non-covalently bonded domains e.g. A2.39 The

cofactor from rapid clearance. The association is critical for maintaining normal half-

clearance of FVIII and plasma protease-mediated degradation. In addition, the

5 Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis 
 
In this section, the synthesis of peptides will be described. All relevant mechanisms 
for the reactions can be found in Appendix A. 
 
Peptide synthesis has traditionally been performed in solution,42 but in 1963 R.B. 
Merrifield published a novel approach for the synthesis of peptides.43 The technique 
was called “Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis” (SPPS) and was based on the construction 
of a peptide with heterogeneous reactions between soluble reagents in the liquid 
phase and a peptide attached to an insoluble solid support.44 The invention of this 
technique was just the beginning of a new way of synthesizing peptides and other 
organic molecules. The contemporary solution phase methods were not suited for the 
synthesis of long chain polypeptides due to difficulties in purification and solubility 
when the number of amino acid residues increased. In the new SPPS technique, the 
only purification of the products after each reaction consisted of a washing step. This 
gave SPPS its speed and simplicity.45  
 
An overview of the concept of SPPS is illustrated in Figure 5.1. The peptide chain is 
assembled from the C-terminal. The first amino acid is connected through its carboxy 
group to an insoluble support and the following amino acids are added stepwise to 
obtain the desired sequence. The final step involves the release of the peptide from 
the solid support.42 
 
In Figure 5.1, the first step is the introduction of a linker that is covalently bonded to 
the solid support. The solid support is typically a solid polymer, also called a resin, 
that is completely insoluble, and thus allows for the separation of either reagents or 
dissolved byproducts by filtration. In step 2, the C-terminal of an Nα-protected amino 
acid is attached by a covalent bond to the functional group of the linker. The coupling 
is performed either by pre-activating the carboxy group for amide bond formation 
with the generation of an activated ester or in situ in the presence of a coupling 
reagent. An excess of the Nα-protected amino acid is used to drive the reaction to 
completion.  After the coupling reaction the resin is again washed with solvent to 
remove excess reagents. The Nα-protecting group is a temporary protecting group, 
which is removed in step 3. Any functional groups present on the side chain of the 
Nα-protected amino acids must be protected with semi-permanent protecting groups. 
These protecting groups must not be affected by the reaction conditions during the 
synthesis. After deprotection of the N-terminal, the resin is washed thoroughly with 
solvent to remove byproducts and unreacted reagents. In step 4, the next Nα-protected 
amino acid is coupled. Step 3 and 4 are repeated until the desired peptide sequence is 
assembled (step 5). The washing steps allow for the intermediate peptides to be 
purified by dissolving away the impurities and excess reagents. Finally, the peptide 
==PAGE:44==
 
 24 
 
 
is cleaved from the linker-support to give the peptide as a free acid or amide 
depending on the linker (step 6). Generally, the side chain protecting groups are 
chosen so that they will be removed under the same conditions as the release of the 
peptide from the resin.42,45  
 
 
Figure 5.1: The basic principle of solid phase peptide synthesis 
SPPS offers several advantages. The reactions can all be carried out in a single 
reaction vessel, thus, the manipulations and associated losses of product can thereby 
be avoided at the same time as the multistep synthesis of peptides is simplified and 
accelerated. Furthermore, the loss of product that can be related to isolation and 
==PAGE:45==
 
 25 
 
 
purification can be avoided due to the simple washing steps. In SPPS, high yields are 
often obtained due to the use of excess of reagents.44 
 
5.1 Boc/Bzl Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis 
 
The SPPS chemistry developed by R. B. Merrifield has changed slightly since its 
discovery in 1963. The strategy of the method is based on graduated acidolysis to 
obtain selectivity in the removal of temporary and semi-permanent protecting groups 
(Figure 5.2). In the originally method, benzyloxycarbonyl (Cbz) was used as 
temporary protecting group. Today, the Merrifield technique commonly referred to 
as Boc SPPS, involves linking the C-terminal to the resin via formation of a benzyl 
ester with hydroxyl-methylphenylacetamido-methyl polystyrene (PAM resin). The 
tert-butoxycarbonyl (Boc) is used as temporary protecting group of the N-terminal 
and the Boc group is removed either by neat trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) or TFA in 
CH2Cl2 (Figure 5.2). The resulting free N-terminal and the trifluoroacetate are 
neutralized with diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA) in CH2Cl2 prior to coupling, or they 
can be neutralized in situ during the coupling reaction. When new amino acids are 
coupled to the peptide, pre-formed activated amino acids are used. These are either 
symmetrical anhydrides or benzotriazolyl esters and the preferred solvents are N,N-
dimethylformamide (DMF) or N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP). Different benzyl 
(Bzl) based protecting groups are used as semi-permanent protecting group for the 
functional groups in the side chains. The final step of releasing the peptide from the 
resin and removal of the semi-permanent side chain protecting groups are achieved 
with anhydrous hydrogen fluoride. A special polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)-lined 
equipment is needed to handle the highly toxic and corrosive liquid HF. The final 
step is therefore inconvenient, impractical, and extremely dangerous especially for 
newcomers to the field of peptide synthesis. A new method that is more convenient 
and safe has therefore been developed. This new method is called Fmoc/tBu SPPS.45 
 
 
Figure 5.2: Illustration of the Merrifield strategy.

5.2 Fmoc/tBu Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis 
 
In this method, 9-fluorenylmethoxycarbonyl (Fmoc) is used as the temporary 
protecting group and acid labile protecting groups such as tert-butyl as semi-
permanent side chain protecting groups (Figure 5.3). This method is based on an 
orthogonal protecting group strategy since the Fmoc group is base labile and the side 
chain protecting groups as well as the resin linkage are acid labile. In addition to tert-
butyl protecting groups trityl, Boc, and pentamethyl-2,3-dihydrobenzofuran-5-
sulfonyl (Pbf) protecting groups are used in Fmoc/tBu SPPS. Alkoxybenzyl-based 
linkers are widely used. In the final step, the semi-permanent protecting groups and 
the linker are removed with TFA, which is a considerable milder condition than HF. 
Therefore, standard glassware can be used. Furthermore, TFA is volatile and is 
readily removed by evaporation. The convenience of the final cleavage reaction 
makes the Fmoc/tBu strategy the most commonly used method for peptide 
synthesis.42,45,46  
 
 
Figure 5.3: Illustration of the Fmoc/tBu strategy. 
 
 
5.2.1 Solid Supports 
 
The growing peptide is covalently anchored to an insoluble support and consequently 
the peptide is also insoluble during all stages of the synthesis.44 There are several 
requirements for the polymeric support. It must be chemically and physically 
resistant, mechanically stable, and completely insoluble in the solvents used in order 
to allow separation by filtration. Furthermore, a sufficient number of reactive sites 
must be present while at the same time keeping the interactions between the growing 
peptide chains at a minimum.42,47 The most commonly used solid polymer consists of 
a copolymer of aminomethylated styrene, which is crosslinked by 1% divinyl 
benzene. This results in spherical beads that swell to five or six times their dry volume 
in organic solvents such as CH2Cl2 and DMF.43,44 The number of reactive functional 
groups located on one bead determines the loading of the resin. During the synthesis 
==PAGE:47==
 
 27 
 
 
of the peptides the resin and the peptides are highly solvated. The reactions do not 
only occur on the surface of the beads, but also in the interior of the solid matrix. The 
peptides are therefore uniformly distributed within the polymeric matrix.42,44 Thus, 
the resin must have appropriate solvation and good swelling properties in order to 
facilitate diffusion and accessibility of the reagents to all the reaction sites. The 
solvation and swelling of the resin also contribute to the speed of the reactions and 
thereby the efficiency of the synthesis.42,44,47  
In addition to the traditional polystyrene solid support, PEG-functionalized 
polystyrene supports have been developed together with pure PEG-based resins.47  
 
5.2.2 Linkers 
 
The linkers serve to provide a reversible linkage between the resin and the peptide 
chain. Therefore, the bond between the resin and the linker must be stable whereas 
the bond to the peptide must be labile to the cleavage conditions. The linker 
determines the loading of the resin and the distance between resin and peptide. Most 
importantly, the linker protects the C-terminal α-carboxyl group during the synthesis 
and determines the C-terminal functional group. Most linkers release the peptides as 
acids or amides, but the C-terminal can also be modified e.g. as hydrazide, alcohol, 
aldehyde, or thioester. Some of the most common linkers used in Fmoc/tBu SPPS are 
shown in Figure 5.4.42,45,47

Hydroxide ions give acids, alcohols give esters, ammonia or amines give amides,

5.2.3 Fmoc deprotection 
 
The Fmoc group was introduced to peptide chemistry in 1970 as the first base labile 
amino protecting group.48 Its application in solid phase peptide synthesis was 
reported in 197949 and today the Fmoc strategy is one of the most preferred synthetic 
concepts in SPPS.42 
A variety of alkoxycarbonyl-type (urethane-type) amino protecting groups have been 
developed with Fmoc being the only one cleaved under mildly basic conditions. The 
Fmoc group is sensitive towards secondary amines, thus, deblocking the amino group 
can be performed with dilute solutions of piperidine or diethylamine in DMF. 
Standardly, 20% piperidine in DMF is used for the deprotection and the reaction 
occurs in seconds at room temperature. The reaction follows an E1cB mechanism 
with initial deprotonation proceeded by formation of dibenzofulvene, which is 
trapped by piperidine (see Appendix A).42

groups for trifunctional amino acids have been developed. The most commonly used 
protecting groups in Fmoc/tBu SPPS are shown in Figure 5.5. They are all acid labile

are applied without side chain protection: Ala, Gly, Ile, Leu, Met, Phe, Pro and Val. Trt: trityl, tBu: tert-
butyl, Boc: tert-butoxycarbonyl, Pbf: pentamethyl-2,3-dihydrobenzofuran-5-sulfonyl. 
 
The guanidine group of arginine has a strongly basic character thus it is protected by 
protonation under normal reaction conditions. However, protonation can give rise to 
low solubility in organic solvents, which is a disadvantage in peptide synthesis. 
Therefore, a side chain protecting group is used for arginine. The protection strategies 
rely on Nω- or Nω, Nδ-protection but ideally all three side chain nitrogen atoms should 
be protected. Arene sulfonyl moieties offers complete protection of the guanidine 
group and can be modified with respect to their acid lability by introducing electron-
donating substituents on the aryl residue as seen in Pbf. Lactam formation upon 
activation of the carboxy group cannot be excluded completely by Nω-
monoprotection but it may be reduced by the application of additives. Among the 
arene sulfonyl protecting groups Pbf is the most acid labile and during cleavage of 
Pbf scavengers should be used to minimize the risk of serine, threonine or tryptophan 
sulfonation.42 
 
Lysine contains two amino groups, which must be orthogonally protected in peptide 
synthesis. As the Nα-amino group is protected by the base labile Fmoc group the acid 
labile Boc group is suitable for protection of the side chain amino group. The aspartic 
and glutamic acid residues contain two carboxylic acids and the acid labile tBu group 
is commonly used. However, tBu cannot fully prevent the side reaction forming 
isoaspartyl through a succinimide intermediate (see Section 5.3). The hydroxy groups 
of serine, threonine and tyrosine are also protected by tBu which prevents the 
generation of potent nucleophiles under basic Fmoc deprotection reaction 
conditions.42,50  
 
Asparagine and glutamine are protected in order to avoid side reactions like 
dehydration and subsequent nitrile formation of carboxy activated asparagine and 
==PAGE:50==
 
 30 
 
 
glutamine residues. Furthermore, asparagine can undergo a cyclization reaction in the 
same manner as aspartic acid with the release of ammonia. Blocking the asparagine 
and glutamine residues also contribute to better solubility in organic solvents since 
unprotected amides tend to form intra- and intermolecular hydrogen bonds. The N-
triphenylmethyl (Trt)  protecting group is stable towards piperidine and the trityl 
cation formed during the cleavage does usually not alkylate sensitive side chains 
which makes Trt a suitable protecting group in Fmoc/tBu SPPS.42,51  
 
Histidine contains an imidazole ring with two nucleophilic nitrogen atoms (Nπ and 
Nτ). When unprotected, histidine is prone to partial acylation and racemization 
(involving Nπ) during coupling reactions. The bulky Trt group is regioselective 
towards Nτ and reduces the basicity of Nπ. Racemization and acylation can therefore 
be avoided with the Trt protecting group on Nτ or by direct protection of Nπ.42,52 
 
The indole ring of tryptophan normally does not require any protection during peptide 
synthesis, but in the final cleavage step problems may arise. tert-Butylation of the 
indole ring can usually be suppressed with scavengers but sulfonation as a result of 
Pbf cleavage may occur. This can be avoided by using the Boc protecting group on 
the indole ring, however, the decarboxylation tends to be slow for tryptophan and a 
transient impurity of CO2 is often seen in peptides containing tryptophan.42 
 
The thiol of cysteine is highly nucleophilic, easily oxidized, and has an acidic 
character thus it requires semi-permanent blocking during peptide synthesis. Trt 
serves to protect cysteine and when cleaved a thiol scavenger is needed to release 
cysteine as a free thiol.42,53 The thioether of methionine usually does not cause any 
complication during peptide synthesis thus it is not protected in Fmoc/tBu SPPS.42  
 
In addition to the commonly used protecting groups a repertoire of different 
protecting groups exists for each amino acid. Various deprotection conditions 
including nucleophiles and Pd catalysis are used which facilitates selective cleavage 
of protecting groups.54  
 
The amino acids that do not contain any side chain functional groups include alanine, 
glycine, isoleucine, leucine, phenylalanine, proline, and valine. These residues do not 
require any protecting groups during peptide synthesis. 
 
5.2.5 Coupling reactions 
 
Peptide bond formation is a nucleophilic substitution reaction of an amino group at a 
carboxy group resulting in the formation of an amide bond. For this reaction to take 
place the carboxy component must be activated prior to amide bond formation. The

activation increases the electrophilicity of the carboxy component by changing the 
hydroxy-group into a better leaving group. This is usually obtained by the formation 
of an active ester either pre-formed or generated in situ with the use of a coupling 
reagent (Figure 5.6). Alternatively, activation can also be achieved with acyl halides, 
anhydrides and acyl azides.42,45

The ideal coupling reagent provides peptide bond formation in high yields and avoids 
byproducts and racemization of the amino acid. The carbodiimide DCC was the first 
reagent used for carboxyl activation and resulted in the unreactive byproduct 
dicyclohexylurea, which was insoluble in most organic solvents. DCC was therefore 
useful in solution phase but not in solid phase peptide synthesis. Another 
carbodiimide, DIC, was therefore used instead since the urea byproduct generated 
from DIC remained in solution (see Scheme 5.1). The O-acylisourea formed during 
the activation is very reactive leading to racemization of the amino acid and 
suppressants such as HOBt are therefore needed.  HOBt and the analogue HOAt have 
shown to minimize the racemization since the benzotriazolyl esters are less reactive 
than the O-acylisourea and at the same time minimizing formation of the unreactive 
urea byproduct (see Figure 5.6 and Scheme 5.1).47,54,55 In 2005, a safety study 
revealed HOBt and its analogues to be labeled as Class 1 explosive. An alternative 
and more safe suppressant was therefore found in the compound called Oxyma.56 
Oxyma  is used in DIC-mediated peptide bond formation in automated SPPS (see

5.2.6 Cleavage reaction 
 
Final cleavage of the peptide from the solid support normally represents the last step 
in SPPS. As described in section 5.2.2 it is the linker that determines, which 
functional group is present on the C-terminal of the final peptide. The linker is usually 
chosen so that deprotection of side chain functional groups and cleavage from the 
resin are obtained under the same conditions.42,45,46 In Fmoc/tBu SPPS release of 
peptides is generally achieved by treating the peptidyl-resin with TFA for 1-3 hours. 
During the deprotection process highly reactive cationic species like tert-butyl and 
trityl cations are generated. Furthermore, resin-linker cations are formed. The cations 
must be trapped in order to avoid modification of amino acids containing electron 
rich functional groups like tyrosine, tryptophan, cysteine, and methionine. 
Scavengers are various nucleophilic reagents added to the TFA solution to quench 
the cations.45,46 
 
Water is a commonly used scavenger, which reacts with the tert-butyl cations and the 
products of Pbf cleavage. The best scavenger for tert-butyl is 1,2-ethandithiol (EDT). 
Both water and EDT offer protection against sulfonation of unprotected tryptophan. 
Effective, non-odorous substitutes for EDT have been found in the form of 
triisopropylsilane (TIPS) and triethylsilane (TES). These reagents have shown to be 
==PAGE:54==
 
 34 
 
 
very efficient at quenching cations generated from Trt and the Rink amide linker. In 
addition, they are effective against peptides containing Pbf. Hence TFA:water:TIPS 
(95:2.5:2.5) is a standard cleavage cocktail for peptides containing all amino acids 
with commonly used protecting groups except Cys(Trt) and methionine.45,46 
5.3 Challenges in the synthesis of the a3 acidic region 
 
The FVIII a3 region is a large peptide containing 41 residues which can lead to 
complications during peptide synthesis (see Figure 5.8). As the peptide grows, poor 
solvation of the immobilized peptide can arise and result in aggregation.58 The 
peptide chains self-associate and interact with each other through intra- and 
intermolecular hydrogen bonding. This can minimize the interaction with the medium 
and the amino termini thereby limiting the accessibility of the reagents.59 Eventually, 
this can cause defective chain growth due to incomplete couplings, deletions, 
truncations, etc. The purity of long peptides can therefore be reduced. The impurities 
formed during the synthesis are peptides extremely similar to the desired peptide and 
purification may therefore pose impossible challenges.58 Ultimately these difficulties 
result in lower yields for the synthesis of large peptides. In addition, it should be 
emphasized that standard coupling reactions are not 100% efficient and neither are 
deprotections. Therefore, for 82 steps including deprotections of e.g. 99.5% 
efficiency the theoretic yield is 66%.  
 
 
 
Figure 5.8: Sequence of the a3 region. The red letters indicate acidic residues, the blue letters are basic 
residues and the orange letters represent sulfotyrosines (-OSO3-). 
Several solutions for problems associated with long peptides have been proposed. 
These include more effective coupling reagents, change in solvents, improved solid 
supports and the use of backbone protection with Hmb (2-hydroxy-4-methoxybenzyl) 
protected amino acids or pseudoproline dipeptide units.58 
 
The FVIII a3 region is highly acidic containing eight aspartic acid and seven glutamic 
acid residues. The most prominent side reaction of aspartic acid is the formation of 
isoaspartyl via the corresponding succinimide intermediate. The backbone nitrogen 
located C-terminally next to aspartic acid attacks the carboxy group to form the 
succinimide intermediate also called aspartimide. The ring opening with a 
nucleophile (e.g. water or piperidine) yields a mixture of α-aspartyl peptide and β-
aspartyl peptide (isoaspartyl) (see Scheme 5.3).42 Furthermore, racemization can 
occur with deprotonation of the succinimide moiety resulting in D-aspartyl.60 
==PAGE:55==

Scheme 5.3: The formation of aspartimide and the following side reactions. 
 
Usually these side reactions can be avoided with the tert-butyl protecting group but 
aspartimide formation has been reported to occur in Fmoc protocols even with the 
tert-butyl protection.42 Studies have shown that aspartimide formation appears to be 
sequence-dependent and thereby highly influenced by the residue located C-
terminally next to the aspartic acid residue. Sequences found in the FVIII a3 region 
that could be problematic are Asp(OtBu)-Thr(tBu) and Asp(OtBu)-Asp(OtBu).61,62 If 
suppressants such as Oxyma or HOBt are not effective in minimizing these side 
reactions, protection of the amide nitrogen in the backbone with Hmb can be 
incorporated.42,63  
 
The a3 peptide also contains a methionine residue. The thioether group of methionine 
usually does not cause complications during peptide synthesis. But the oxidation 
sensitivity of the group represents a handling problem and oxidizing agents such as 
oxygen can lead to the formation of methionine S-oxide (see Figure 5.9). The 
sulfoxide formation may be reduced by the use of special cleavage cocktails

sometimes cannot be avoided completely even with the use of scavengers.42

Finally, the a3 region has been post-translationally modified by sulfation of the two 
tyrosine residues. Tyrosine is the only amino acid known to undergo sulfation which 
is the most common post-translation modification for tyrosine.66  
 
The synthesis of peptides containing sulfotyrosines (sTyr/sY) gives rise to 
complications due to the tendency of sTyr to undergo rapid desulfation/hydrolysis 
under acidic conditions. However, the stability of sulfotyrosines increases when they 
are paired with cations other than protons e.g. intramolecular ion pairing with basic 
residues within the peptide. The sulfotyrosine stability is therefore dependent on the 
sequence of the peptide.66 As described the a3 peptide is highly acidic by only 
containing four basic residues and the stability of sTyr might therefore pose a 
problem. 
 
There are two approaches to the synthesis of sTyr containing peptides: Chemical 
sulfation of tyrosine after peptide chain assembly or incorporation of protected sTyr 
during the peptide synthesis.66 
 
In the first approach orthogonal protection of tyrosine is needed to allow subsequent 
on-resin sulfation with sulphur trioxide·N,N-dimethylformamide (SO3·DMF) or 
sulphur trioxide·pyridine (SO3·Pyr). Furthermore, other side chain protecting groups 
must be stable towards sulfation chemistry. Special protecting groups for serine and 
threonine are introduced since their hydroxy groups are more readily sulfated than 
the phenolic hydroxy group of tyrosine. Lysine is also protected by a special 
protecting group in order to avoid the formation of sulfamic acid derivatives. 
Unfortunately, this approach is associated with incomplete reactions, especially when 
multiple tyrosine residues are to be sulphated in large peptides. In addition, the 
method can be time consuming for peptides with many trifunctional amino acids that 
need protection.66,67 The approach has been reported to work best for small peptides 
and is therefore not ideal for the synthesis of the FVIII a3 region. 
 
The second approach avoids on-resin sulfation procedures and enables site-selective 
incorporation of sTyr residues thereby making automated synthesis possible. The two 
most successful methods have employed either the neopentyl (nP) or trichloroethyl 
(TCE) groups for protection of sulfate esters.66–68 
 
The TCE group is stable towards strong acids and therefore allows for the use of Rink 
amide resin and standard Fmoc/tBu SPPS conditions with the exception of the 
replacement of piperidine with 2-methylpiperidine (2-MP) in Fmoc deprotection 
steps. Piperidine cause the TCE group to undergo rapid elimination of HCl resulting 
in the formation of the more stable dichlorovinyl (DCV) sulfate ester. This is 
followed by a slower attack by piperidine on the sulfur atom forming a stable sulfon 
amide (Scheme 5.4).66,68

Scheme 5.4: Elimination of the TCE protecting group resulting in the DCV group and further

When using 2-MP, elimination is still observed but it occurs more slowly and 2-MP 
does not react with the sulfur atom and the protecting group is thereby preserved. The 
HCl salt of 2-MP formed during the Fmoc removal, produces a precipitate which can 
be removed by treatment with CH2Cl2. Both the DCV and TCE group can be cleaved 
by catalytic hydrogenolysis upon treatment with Pd/C, H2 gas and ammonium 
formate resulting in sulfotyrosines.66,68 
 
The nP group is highly stable towards strong acids and bases including piperidine 
and TFA.67,69 TFA-mediated resin cleavage and deprotection of completed peptides 
can therefore be performed with minimal degradation of the nP protected 
sulfotyrosines.67 The nP group is extremely stable and cleavage usually requires high 
temperatures and long reaction times. The removal of nP can be achieved with small 
nucleophiles such as azide or cyanide in a hot polar aprotic solvent such as DMF (60-
70 ⁰C). Due to the steric hindrance of the nP group the nucleophilic substitution on 
nP is considerably slower than other alkyl groups such as methyl and ethyl.69  
However, milder and more convenient deblocking conditions have recently been 
discovered. The nP group can be removed by dissolving the peptide in aqueous 
ammonium acetate (1-2 M) and heat it to 37 ⁰C for 6-12 hours (Scheme 5.5).67

6 Project Outline 
 
Novo Nordisk is a global healthcare company developing products for the treatment 
of hemophilia. Their research is looking into finding a more convenient and effective 
treatment of hemophilia. The company is therefore interested in the interaction 
between FVIII and VWF in the search for prolonging the half-life of infused FVIII 
products. Since the a3 region of FVIII is known to play a crucial role for the 
interaction between the two proteins, it is of great interest to study the interaction 
between a3 and VWF. In order to do so, isolated a3 is needed. An external CRO 
company has previously made an attempt to synthesize the peptide. Unfortunately, 
the synthesis of a3 presented several complications and resulted in extremely low 
yields and purities even before incorporation of the sulfotyrosines into the peptide.  
 
The scope of the present project was to first of all identify the complications in the 
synthesis of the a3 peptide. The difficult couplings in the sequence of FVIII a3 were 
to be detected and improved. Furthermore, a method for incorporation of 
sulfotyrosines was to be found. Ultimately a protocol for the synthesis of full-length 
a3 containing sulfotyrosines was to be developed. Ideally the protocol should enable 
the synthesis of FVIII a3 analogues.  
 
 
6.1 Synthesis Strategy 
 
The synthesis of the a3 peptide (1.1) was planned to be carried out using automated 
Fmoc/tBu SPPS. Couplings performed with DIC and Oxyma should enable the 
synthesis of even long peptides. A strategy of starting out with the synthesis of simple 
a3 analogues was chosen. The simplifications were selected to eliminate the expected 
complicated residues described in Section 5.3. One modification to 1.1 was therefore 
replacement of methionine with norleucine. In addition, sulfotyrosines should in the 
start-up phase be replaced with tyrosine. 
 
Complications could in this way be identified and solved before proceeding to more 
advanced analogues. For incorporation of the sulfotyrosines, the described nP 
approach should be tested and if needed modified and optimized to enable the 
synthesis of 1.1.

7 Results and Discussion 
 
The objective of the experimental work was to synthesize the a3 peptide and 
analogues in high purity to ensure reliable results in the future VWF binding studies. 
The experimental work has been divided into five parts: manual peptide synthesis, 
synthesis of simple Factor VIII a3 analogues, synthesis of FVIII a3 peptide, synthesis 
of FVIII a3 analogues, and finally an NMR study of the nP cleavage mechanism. 
Most of the reactions could be followed by RP-UPLC and RP-UPLC-MS (from this 
point forward referred to as UPLC and LC-MS respectively) due to the absorption of 
the peptide bonds at 214 nm. In MS the [M+4H]4+ and [M+3H]3+ ions were the most 
abundant and has therefore been used in the assignment of compounds. 
 
 
7.1 Manual peptide synthesis 
 
In order to investigate the suitability of the nP approach for incorporation of sTyr in 
the a3 peptide, a smaller test peptide was synthesized. The peptide was designed with 
an aspartic acid residue next to the nP-protected sTyr to resemble the environment 
where the sTyr residues are located within the a3 peptide. The sequence H-
SDY(SO3nP)GGFL-NH2 (7.2) was chosen and the peptide was synthesized by 
manual SPPS, where cycles of Fmoc deprotection and COMU-mediated couplings 
were performed on a Tentagel RAM resin (see Scheme 7.1).

The synthesis of large peptides is associated with several complications as described 
in Section 5.3. A strategy of starting out with the synthesis of simple a3 analogues 
was therefore chosen to enable identification of the complications arising, and 
thereby making it possible to determine and amend the cause of the complications.

7.3.4 Characterization of Factor VIII a3 peptide 
 
The final a3 peptide (1.1) was analyzed by UPLC, LC-MS, circular dichroism (CD) 
spectroscopy and NMR. 
 
The purity was determined to >95% by UPLC, but an impurity of 4 % was observed. 
The impurity was analyzed by LC-MS and identified to be M-18. As seen in the first 
synthesis of 7.5, the a3 peptide had lost a water molecule, which was assumed to be 
caused by the formation of aspartimide. The previous syntheses had shown that the 
peptide sequence was susceptible to aspartimide formation upon heating. The loss of 
water could therefore be caused by the heating during the nP cleavage, or on the 
column during preparative HPLC where the column was heated to 40 ºC. In contrast 
to the stability test of 7.9, where two peaks with the same intensity and mass of the 
nP deproctected peptide 7.12 was observed, the a3 peptide did not lead to any 
splitting. 
 
CD analysis was used to determine if the peptide had any secondary structure. The 
a3 peptide appeared to be primarily or fully disordered (see Figure 7.16). The 
classical secondary structures β-sheets and α-helices have characteristic CD spectra 
both with positive bands at 193 nm and 195 nm respectively. Negative bands near 
195 nm is typical for disordered structures.72

performed by an internal NMR department at Novo Nordisk A/S. COSY, TOCSY, 
NOESY, and HSQC was recorded in order to fully assign the peptide. The NMR 
characterization confirmed the amino acid sequence of a3 in the synthesized peptide 
supporting the results obtained by LC-MS. Only sequential NOEs were observed and

7.4 Synthesis of Factor VIII a3 analogues 
 
The synthesis of the acidic a3 peptide had shown to be successful, and a procedure 
for the synthesis of large acidic peptides containing sulfotyrosine had been 
developed. The procedure allowed for the synthesis of a3 analogues that could be 
used in the investigation of the peptides’ interaction with VWF.  
 
Following the synthesis described for 1.1, monosulfated a3 analogues (7.19 and 7.20) 
containing methionine were synthesized. These analogues were chosen in order to 
obtain information about the impact of each sulfonyltyrosine on the VWF binding. 
The peptide containing sTyr at position 1680 (7.19) was synthesized in 8% yield with 
a purity of 88%, and the 1664 sTyr containing peptide (7.20) was synthesized in a

7.5 The mechanism of neopentyl cleavage 
 
The syntheses of the a3 peptide and analogues hereof showed that the nP approach 
for incorporation of sTyr was successful. The nP group was chosen as protection 
group for the sulfate ester due to its stability towards the different reaction conditions 
encountered during synthesis and purification as well as its relative ease of removal. 
The nP group is stable during basic Fmoc deprotection and acidic TFA cleavage 
reaction and shows excellent stability in the organic solvents applied during 
automated synthesis. 
 
In early literature the nP group was removed using a small, unhindered nucleophile 
such as azide in polar aprotic solvents. The mechanism was described as a 
nucleophilic substitution following a SN2 mechanism (Scheme 7.2).69,75  
 
 
Scheme 7.2: Mechanism for nP cleavage with small powerful nucleophile. 
 
The milder conditions involving aqueous ammonium acetate67 was described as a 
solvolysis where the solvent reacts as the nucleophile. The acetate group is a poor 
nucleophile, but is present in great excess. The mechanism was therefore in 1976 
proposed to be an acetolysis of the nP sulfonates proceeding via an assisted SN1 type 
reaction with methyl migration and rearrangement. Furthermore, the reaction was 
described to be concerted forming the t-amyl cation (7.25).76 
In this project two alternatives to the SN1 type reaction with acetate as the nucleophile 
were proposed. The mechanism could also be an elimination reaction resulting in 2-
methylbut-2-ene (7.27), and since the acetate group is a poor nucleophile, hydrolysis 
of the t-amyl cation was also a possibility (see Scheme 7.3).

In order to elucidate how the mechanism of nP cleavage proceeds, the reaction was 
followed by NMR. For simplification reasons, the nP protected sulfotyrosine was 
used as starting material. Modifications was done to the commercially available

removed, the N-terminal was capped with acetyl, and the carboxylic acid was 
transformed to the primary amide (Scheme 7.4). These modifications were done to 
avoid charge interactions and to make the model substrate resemble a peptide 
backbone more closely.

8 Conclusion 
 
In order to examine the expected complications in the synthesis of the a3 peptide, a 
series of test reactions and syntheses were performed. Under the tested conditions it 
was found that none of couplings in the a3 sequence caused insurmountable 
difficulties. Furthermore, nP protection of the sulfotyrosines followed by cleavage 
with 2 M CH3COONH4 at 37 ⁰C was found to be a highly efficient method for the 
incorporation of sulfotyrosines into the acidic peptides. 
 
A protocol for the synthesis of the a3 peptide and analogues hereof were developed. 
The peptides were synthesized by automated SPPS with all reactions performed at 
room temperature. Cycles of Fmoc deprotections with 20% piperidine in DMF and 
DIC/Oxyma mediated couplings followed by TFA mediated cleavage from the resins 
afforded the nP protected crude peptides. nP deprotection was performed upon 
treatment with 2 M CH3COONH4 at pH 8.5 and purification was performed by 
preparative HPLC under neutral conditions. An overview of the synthesized a3 
peptides is given in Table 8.1

The mass of the target a3 peptide was confirmed by MS and the amino acid sequence

Finally, an NMR study elucidated the mechanism of nP cleavage. The cleavage was 
performed on a simple nP protected sulfotyrosine model substrate (7.32) using 
deuterated ammonium acetate in D2O. 1H NMR indicated that deprotection proceeds

9 Future Perspective 
 
With a3 peptide and analogues synthesized in high purity binding interaction studies 
are to be conducted. The aim is to map the interaction between the a3 peptide and 
VWF. As binding between FVIII and VWF is known to primarily occur in the D’D3 
domain of VWF focus will initially be on the interaction between a3 and the D’D3 
domain. 
  
If binding can be detected in the interaction study, additional a3 analogues are to be 
synthesized to further explore binding interactions. These analogues could include 
modifications to the acidic residues by replacement with neutral or basic residues in 
order to understand the purpose of the highly acidic region. Ultimately, the goal is to 
increase binding affinity through modifications of the a3 peptide. 
 
In the case a3 or analogues hereof are needed in higher quantity or purity, 
optimization of their syntheses should be performed. Attempts to reduce the 
aspartimide formation in the final products will be a priority. To avoid the loss of a 
water molecule, the nP cleavage could be performed at 23 ⁰C for two days instead of 
at 37 °C for 7 hours. Furthermore, the purification could be attempted using a column 
temperature of 23 ⁰C.

General methods 
 
Commercially available reagents and solvents supplied from Sigma Aldrich or 
Bachem were used without further purification, and all employed solvents were of 
HPLC quality. Solid phase peptide synthesis was carried out using plastic syringes or 
reactors fitted with Teflon/polypropylene filters, which allow suction to be applied 
from below for removal of reagents and solvents. Rink amide (RAM) functionalized 
polystyrene or Tentagel resin with a loading of either 1.1 mmol/g (manual synthesis

Novabiochem) was used for all reactions on solid support.

in the following sections.

Manual solid phase peptide synthesis procedure 
 
Prior to use the resin was swelled in NMP and washed with CH2Cl2 (3x) and NMP 
(3x). For all solid phase reactions, a volume of solvent just enough to fully swell and 
cover the resin was used to keep the concentration of reagents as high as possible. 
 
Fmoc deprotection was performed by treating the resin with 20% (v/v) piperidine in 
NMP for 2 min followed by a second treatment for 20 min. The reaction mixtures 
were removed by suction. The resin was then washed with NMP (3x), CH2Cl2 (3x), 
and NMP (3x). 
 
Coupling reactions was carried out by dissolving 4 equiv. of Nα-protected amino acid 
and 3.75 equiv. COMU in NMP and 8 equiv. DIPEA was added. The solution was 
allowed to preactivate for 5-10 min under shaking before adding the solution to the 
resin. The couplings were allowed to react for 30 min followed by wash with NMP 
(3x), CH2Cl2 (3x), and NMP (3x). 
 
Peptide synthesis was accomplished by repeating cycles of Fmoc deprotection and 
COMU-mediated couplings of Nα-protected amino acids.

Automated solid phase peptide synthesis procedure 
 
The automated peptide syntheses were performed on a 150 µmol scale using Fmoc 
amino acids preloaded to the synthesizer. The resin was added to a reactor and placed 
in a chamber on a SymphonyX peptide synthesizer. All reactions were run under a 
nitrogen atmosphere at room temperature unless otherwise stated. First a swelling 
program was run wherein the resin was swelled in DMF and washed with DMF (3x).  
 
Stock solutions of Fmoc protected amino acid solutions were prepared by dissolving 
20 mmol amino acid in 58 mL 0.34 M Oxyma Pure® in DMF. All amino acids were 
side chain protected by standard protecting groups used in Fmoc SPPS (see section 
5.2.4). Norleucine were prepared in the same way. When special amino acids (Fmoc-
Tyr(SO3nP)-OH) were prepared, 600 µmol amino acid was dissolved in 2 mL 0.34 
M Oxyma Pure® in DMF and placed on the synthesizer in a special container suited 
for single shot delivery of the entire volume. 
 
A coupling cycle on the synthesizer was initiated by an Fmoc deprotection with 20% 
(v/v) piperidine in DMF (2x 15 min) followed by wash with DMF (9x). Four-fold 
excess of the amino acid/Oxyma Pure® solution was mixed with four-fold excess of 
DIC relative to resin loading, giving a coupling concentration of 0.15 M. The solution 
was allowed to pre-activate for 10 minutes and was then added to the resin. The 
coupling proceeded for 1.5 h and was agitated by sparging with nitrogen. The resin 
was then washed with DMF (4x) and the coupling cycle was repeated. 
 
At the end of the synthesis a final wash program was run and the resin was washed 
with DMF (1x) and CH2Cl2 (6x) followed by drying for 30 min. 
 
Cleavage of the peptide from the resin 
 
The resin was swelled in CH2Cl2 and washed with CH2Cl2 (3x). Then the resin was 
treated twice with a solution of TFA:H2O:TES or TIPS (95:2.5:2.5) for 1-2 h. The 
combined solutions were concentrated under a N2 flow and diethyl ether was added 
to precipitate the peptide. The precipitate was washed twice with diethyl ether and 
centrifugation was used to remove the supernatant. The resulting crude peptide was 
allowed to air dry.  
 
Test cleavages were performed on a small fraction of the resin (10-20 beads) 
following the above procedure but without precipitation with diethyl ether. The 
concentrated TFA solution was diluted with H2O:CH3CN (1:1) and LC-MS was 
recorded.
