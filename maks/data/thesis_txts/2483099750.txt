Introduction
1.1
Project motivation
In this project I will use virtual reality to create an experience of what it is like
to experience extraterrestrial gravitational ﬁelds, and speciﬁcally the moon.
In recent years virtual reality technology has improved immensely, and the
growth is continuing. With new virtual reality technology we are able to expe-
rience and interact with immersive worlds unlike anything we have been able
to do before. But creating immersive experiences has its diﬃculties with the
currently commercially available technologies. The vast majority of virtual re-
ality users do not have any leg tracking, and only a rather small play area to
move around, so creating immersive movement is one of the biggest issues with
virtual reality. This is even more ampliﬁed with a moon experience, which has
a diﬀerent gravitational ﬁeld than what we are experiencing on Earth. Another
important aspect of an immersive experience is the audio. We as human beings
expect audio feedback from many things we do, but on the moon the atmo-
sphere does not allow much sound. In this project I have tested how to create
an immersive experience with limited audio options due the the lack of sound
in the atmosphere of the moon.
To create the experience I will use Unity and SteamVR with an Oculus Rift
headset and touch controllers. One of the biggest challenges is how to use the
limited tracking of the headset and controllers, and convert that into believable
movement in the virtual environment, which I will go in depth with in the
movement section of the report.
My goal with this project is to ﬁnd ways to create an immersive experience
of the moon, and explore diﬀerent solutions to the immersion breaking issues,
using the Oculus Rift and a pair of touch controllers.
1.2
Visualization of the moon environment
In the experience the user will explore a moon environment, as such it is im-
portant that the virtual area properly represents a moon environment, and in
this section I will deﬁne some elements that this entails.
1.2.1
Moon-ﬂoor and craters
An obvious requirement is a moon ﬂoor that the user can move around on. The
moon ﬂoor should have craters the user can explore by jumping into and out
of.
Another important aspect of the moon ﬂoor is the horizon. Due to the moon
being smaller in size than the Earth, the horizon is slightly diﬀerent.
A horizon on an interstellar object is the limit of our vision due to the
curvature of the interstellar object. This limit is dependant on how high up the
viewer is. Due to the Earths oceans being large ﬂuid bodies, the ocean surface
keeps an even distance to the center of the Earth. This makes the oceans follow

1.2.2
Sky
The visible sky of the moon is another important visual element for the demo.
In the universe there are tons of stars that are theoretically visibly to our naked
eye, but due to light pollution on Earth we are rarely able to see many stars,
especially in cities. Due to this fact, most people I interviewed assumed a starry
sky (myself included).
But looking at pictures from the moon, the sky was
almost always black (see ﬁgure 3). This is due to multiple factors. First of all
Figure 3: Image of the Earth taken by NASA during the Apollo 11 mission in
1969
is the lack of atmosphere on the moon. On Earth, our atmosphere refracts the
light of the sun, which causes us to perceive the sky as blue during the day,
and red in the evening when the light is refracted at another angle. Due to the
moon having virtually no atmosphere, the light of the sun lights up the moon
directly, which is extremely bright, which causes the starry sky to be invisible
during the ”day” of the moon. What we know as moon cycles, where the moon
transitions from e.g. crescent moon to full-moon is actually the day night cycle
of the moon. A full ”day” cycle of the moon is roughly 28 days. Another reason
for the lack of stars in the pictures from the moon is the fact that the Earth is
extremely bright compared to the stars. This causes the cameras to focus on
the Earth instead of the much less bright stars. Compared to our view of the
moon on Earth, the Earth seems roughly 4 times larger and 16 times brighter
than a full moon [Bo16].
This does not mean the astronauts cannot see the stars, especially during
the ”night” period of the moon cycle as can be seen in ﬁgure 4.

1.2.3
Importance of perspective
When I began this project, I quickly realised the importance of perspective.
Even though the moon is smaller than the Earth, it is still a huge open area,
and even with the craters on the moon, it can easily feel similar to being in
an endless ocean with vast emptiness as far as the eye can see. So to give the
user some perspective, it is necessary to add known elements to give the user
perspective on the scale of the demo. The objects to give perspective could be
the renowned USA ﬂag planted on the moon. It could be a vehicle like a moon
rover or space ship. Alternatively it could be a model of another astronaut. In
”Scaled Jump in Gravity-Reduced Virtual Environments” section 3.2[ea17] they
also note how giving the user perspective is important to give them ”a sense of
presence”
1.3
Movement
1.3.1
Motion sickness
In VR applications where the user views the game through a ﬁrst person cam-
era, motion sickness is a common issue, especially with conventional movement
controllers, such as using a joystick to simulate walking or running. To quote
Eric Van de Kerckhove [dK19] ”the human body doesn’t like it when there’s a
mismatch between what it sees and what it feels”, and if we see the environ-
ment move as if we are walking in virtual reality through for example a joystick,
this mismatch is created. This is a huge issue for virtual reality, and a lot of
6
==PAGE:8==
research has gone into ﬁnding good movement options that which minimizes
motion sickness in users when trying virtual reality experiences. In my demo,
the user is not supposed to spend hours in the demo, so the issue with motion
sickness is somewhat less prevalent compared to videogames where the user can
often play for hours at a time. But even still I am taking motion sickness into
account and I have explored diﬀerent movement options for the demo.
1.3.2
Common Virtual Reality movement solutions
For starters I will mention some of the most common movement options in ﬁrst
person virtual reality experiences/games.
Teleportation
A common type of movement in ﬁrst person VR experiences/games
is teleportation. The way it usually works is that the user uses their controllers
to select an area, then the user and camera is instantly transported to the area,
often with a transition animation that blocks the view momentarily, before ”ar-
riving” at the new location.
Vehicles
Another very common type of movement is vehicles. Vehicle move-
ment is extremely suited for VR, since real world vehicle usually requires the
user to sit or stand still. If a person is prone to getting motion sickness in cars,
they are probably still likely to get it in virtual reality, but people who do not
suﬀer from motion sickness from real life vehicles are likely to be ﬁne in a vehicle
simulation.
Interesting types of vehicle movement for my project could be a type of moon
rover, or moon-boarding which NASA has also theorized about1.
Hand based movement
Some games or experiences have created some unique
hand based movement options, due to virtual realities great hand tracking with
controllers such as the Oculus Touch and the vive controllers.
The climb by ”Crytek” is an example of a VR game that has used hand based
movement as the core of their game. The idea is to simply use the controllers
as your hands, and use them to climb a mountain. By moving the controllers
downward you will pull the character up, and by doing it fast and releasing
your grip, the user is able to jump to the next grip location. The concept is
simple, but it clearly showcases a good use of hand based movement to create
an immersive experience.
Another example of hand based movement is Lone Echo. The basic controls
of Lone Echo is similar to The climb, but the simulated environment is in zero
gravity. An issue with moving around in zero gravity, is that the user can get
stuck without any objects in reach, thus being unable to move the character. To
circumvent this, the developers ”Ready at Dawn” have implemented a ”thumb
booster” which works similarly to a jet-pack, but using the thumbs instead. This
is a great solution, since it allows for immersive fully hand based movement.

Joystick and button movement
A more old school approach is the classic
joystick and button movement. As mentioned earlier, this method is prone to
cause motion sickness to users after prolonged use. This method is very intuitive
for people who are used to video games, but unintuitive for people with no video
game experience.
One of the advantages of joystick and button movement is that it can easily
be implemented, and it can often be implemented as a secondary movement
option.
Positional tracking
Another type of movement is positional tracking. The
basis of this movement option is to track the position of the user through the
controllers and headset, and convert real world position changes to movement
in the virtual world. This allows a close relation to real world movement and
virtual movement, which minimizes the chance of motionsickness. The main
drawback of this movement method is the limited play area of most virtual
reality setups. The most common virtual reality setups are usually limited to a
small area, and the headset itself is connected to a PC with a cable of limited
length.
The new Oculus Quest headset would probably be ideal with this movement
option, since it allows free movement, without being limited to a small play
area.
Virtual reality setups with feet tracking
Some less common options are
virtual reality set-ups with feet tracking. Simple feet tracking set-ups simply
requires the user to strap a compatible controller to their legs, and use the
positional tracking of the controllers to detect leg motions. This option was not
realistically available to oculus set-ups until 2018 where they allowed multiple
controllers to be paired through ”VR Objects, which was implemented with
their mixed reality system.
Another type of feet tracking is the Omni treadmill by Virtuix (see ﬁg 6).
The Omni allows 360 degree movement for the user, and even allows them to
jump and crouch.

Other VR experiences
In Japan they have something called ”VR Zone”, which has created diﬀerent
VR experiences people can try. They have some dedicated places where people
can buy a day-pass that allows them to try most of the experiences as many
times as they want for the day. It is also possible to purchase tickets instead
to try individual experiences. Some arcades in Japan also has smaller VR Zone
sections, but they can usually only be played with tickets. The VR Zone can
have up to roughly 20 diﬀerent VR experiences for users to try. I myself have
been to one of the VR Zones where I tried most of the experiences available.
A major diﬀerence from my project and the experiences at the VR Zone is
that all the VR zone experiences are games, and has speciﬁc goals the player
has to complete (or fail) after which the game ends.
This design has been
implemented for the VR Zone to make it easier to cycle people similar to an
amusement park, but many of their games feels more like experiences with a
goal attached to it, instead of an actual game, which is why I feel it is relevant
to my project.

1.5
Other papers about exploration through virtual real-
ity
The book Virtual Reality Applications and Explorations([W.93]) have a chap-
ter dedicated to planetary exploration (Chapter 8). The book was published
in 1993, and explains how NASA has had a huge eﬀect on the development
of virtual reality. It is extremely interesting to read how the author Michael
W.McGreevy predicts VR to be a huge commercial factor, and be used for plan-
etary exploration and exploration of other things such as art and architecture
roughly 20 years ahead of his time. It is only in recent years that virtual reality
has become more mainstream and a lot of the things he predicted is happening
today. Nowadays The Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts has for multiple
years now used virtual reality to showcase the architecture of their designs, as
predicted by McGreevy. Of course at the book was published, there were some
virtual reality headsets, and they were used in the same way as modern VR
headsets, but the technological diﬀerence, and the availability is like night and
day.
An article by Michael Gross called ”Exploring virtual worlds”[Gr17] de-
scribes a lot of the diﬀerent uses Virtual Reality can have in our society. In the
section called ”Armchair travel” he explains the use of virtual reality to explore
diﬀerent environments from the comfort of our own home. He explains how
astrobiologists might be able to use VR space exploration to understand the
opportunities and limitations of potential extraterrestrial life on other planets.
Or how it can be used by environmentalists to show people the beauty of nature
to motivate them to preserve it on Earth.

Hardware
Oculus rift:
The Oculus Rift3 is a virtual reality headset that started as
a kickstarter. It is now owned by Facebook, and is one of the 2 major Virtual
reality headsets available for the PC. The Oculus Rift is connected to the PC
with an HDMI cable. To allow tracking of the headset and controllers it also
needs to connect trackers that should be set-up in the room and connected to
the PC through USB. Since the Oculus Rift is wired to the PC, mobility is
limited and there is no leg tracking, only hand tracking through the controllers
and head-tracking through the headset. Additional equipment can of course
be purchased at connected to allow additional tracking, but it is not a part of
common Oculus Rift set-ups.
Facebook has recently released a new type of Oculus called the Oculus Quest.
The Oculus quest is designed to be a stand-alone device, meaning it is not
connected to a PC. This means there is no cable limitation and allows for much
more mobility.
In this project I will only work with the Oculus Rift, but allowing additional
mobility available through the Oculus Quest is something I will keep in mind
for my project.

Oculus touch controllers:
Oculus touch are the correlated controllers
to the Oculus Rift headset.
The touch controllers are designed to simulate
a persons hand in virtual reality by tracking the controller motions, and by
having a button layout and grip form that naturally that is designed to be
natural to the grip. The touch controllers has thump-rest, index-triggers and
primary hand triggers, each with sensor in them. Theses sensors are in addition
to the trigger/button presses also available to the controllers. These buttons
and sensors are used to detect the hand pose of the user. For example if the
primary hand trigger detects the presence of the users hand, without any trigger
input, and the other sensors do not detect any user presence, then they are most
likely in a ”ﬁnger gun” pose. If all the sensors detect the presence of the users
hand, but without any trigger/button inputs, then the hand is most likely in a
relaxed semi-closed pose.

1.7
Software
Unity:
Unity4 is a game engine designed to eﬃciently create games. In
this project I will use unity to create the moon experience in combination with
other tools. Unity has its own build-in physics engine that has many settings
to customize the experience.
Unity is an object based game engine, so components and code are all placed
on objects that are instantiated in the virtual world. These objects are called
game objects. A common method to apply code to the world without a visible
object is to simply make invisible game objects that run code. Each game object
is an instance of an object but they can be stored un-instantiated as a prefab.
The word Prefab is shorthand for Prefabrication. In the context of unity it is
a prefabrication of a game object. Essentially this means that gameobject are
instantiated objects, where as prefabs are a ”recipe” for the gameobject, similar
to classes. For example if I want to create many rocks to populate the moon, I
will instantiate a lot of rocks based on the ”rock prefab”.
SteamVR:
SteamVR5 is a package that can help developers create Virtual
Reality applications. SteamVR is supported in Unity and can be added through
Unity’s build in Assets Store. It is created by the company Valve, which also
created the video game digital distribution platform Steam (online game-shop),
which steamVR is named after.
SteamVR 2.0:
SteamVR 2.0 is a new version of steamVR that is mas-
sively diﬀerent from the original steamVR. In my project I initially used steamVR,
but changed over to SteamVR 2.0. Since steamVR 2.0 there has been multiple
new updates, but a lot of sources and guides simply refers to it as steamVR 2.0,
since the diﬀerence between original steamVR and steamVR 2.0 is much larger
than the diﬀerence between steamVR 2.0 and newer updates.
Blender:
Blender6 is a popular free and open 3D creation software. Due
to its popularity it has a lot of community support, guides and tutorials to

Analysis
In this section I will describe the requirements and challenges imposed by creat-
ing a virtual reality moon experience. I will introduce a lot of keywords that are
relevant to my project, and I will try to explain them all properly as they are
introduced, but additionally there is also a glossary included in the appendix
to help clarify certain technical expressions or tools. Finally I will explain my
decision making for the project based on the analysis.
2.1
Virtual reality and technical limitations
For virtual reality, the number of frames per second is incredibly important for
the experience. The human eye does not view the world in frames per seconds,
but to get an immersive experience we want to view as many frames per second
as possible, otherwise it will instantly break the immersion. The Oculus Rift
can at most show 90 frames per second. So to give the best experience I aim
for a performance that allows me to render at least 90 frames per second with
my computer set-up (see appendix for computer speciﬁcations).
The Oculus Rift renders everything for each eye, which doubles performance
load, which makes it more of a challenge to reliable render 90 frames per second
during the experience.
2.2
User-interaction requirements
To help deﬁne the expected requirements of the moon demo from a user per-
spective, I interviewed a dozen of people from diﬀerent social segments to gauge
their expectations and gain inspiration for my project. I compiled the answers
into a set of reasonable usecases that combined allows them to do most of the
interactions they described:
Use cases
• Walk on the moon
• Run on the moon
• Jump on the moon
• Pick up a virtual item
• Throw a virtual item
• Crouch in the virtual world
• Climb on the moon

A more formal version of the usecases with a description can be found in the
appendix (section 9.2). But in short, I aim to let the user do as they expect
intuitively. As mentioned earlier in the section about other VR experiences;
letting the user assume they can do certain actions, without actually allowing
the actions is extremely frustrating and immersion breaking. This is why it is
a focus to letting the users do as they please as much as possible.
The most important movement actions are walking, running and jumping. I
decided to allow 2 diﬀerent types of movement due to restrictions with my set-

Additional movement is hand motions. The Oculus Touch controllers have
excellent tracking I use to allow the user to climb up cliﬀs, or throw items in
the moon environment.
One of the challenges with jumping is how easy it is to break immersion with

the ﬁrst things people want to try out is jumping. Therefor it is important that
I allow their real world jump to be applied in the virtual world. But when the
user lands in the real world, they would still be ﬂying in the moon environment.
The landing in real life can easily break immersion and is something I will test
through simple methods, such as having the user jump on a mattress instead of
a hard ﬂoor.
Others have explored something similar ([ea17]), where they used a cable

Environmental requirements of the moon
An important aspect of the demo is of course the visual graphics shown to the
user through virtual reality. In this section I will describe necessary requirements
for the moon environment to give an immersive experience to the user.
I can simulate the horizon by creating a sphere or sphere segment and use
that as the playable area.
An alternative to creating a horizon would be to use the height diﬀerence of
the moon ﬂoor to create a limit to the visible distance (e.g. creating mountains
to avoid the horizon issue). This would allow me to use a plane as the playable

As a rule of thumb, the unity physics engine prefers to have each of their
measurement units to correspond to 1 meter, but the engine has certain limita-
tions.
The main reason to match the unity units to equal 1 meter is mainly because
of the default unity gravity, but the default unity gravity has another issue for
representing the moon; it expects the ”ground” surface to be ﬂat. To properly
give a moon experience, the surface could be a sphere or a spherical cap segment
to properly simulate a horizon, which means the ground is non-ﬂat, and I have
to create my own physics engine which I called my faux gravity system to handle
this type of terrain.
If I am to use a spherical cap segment, I have the choice to either create a
limited sized playable area to demonstrate a section on the moon, which I can
edit to allow in depth exploration of the segment, including for example craters
of the moon. Another choice is to create a spherical cap segment to simulate
the moons horizon, but simulate movement of the user by moving the texture.
This would allow the user to move around an inﬁnite space, but they would
not be able to properly explore a diﬀerence in heights such as craters. Since
exploration of heights is essential to my project I decided to only test with the
limited size spherical object.
Another important aspect of the moon environment is interactable moon-
rocks. As mentioned earlier in the usecases, being able to pickup and throw
rocks is expected in the demo, and is also a fun way of playing with gravity.
Since the gravitational pull of the moon is much weaker than on the Earth, a
human is able to realistically carry and throw much heavier objects than on
Earth. Exploring interactions with diﬀerent size rocks, being able to juggle and
throw them is a great way of feeling the diﬀerence in the moon environment,
and can be implemented very true-to-life through the Oculus Touch controllers.
Since rock objects can be picked up, I will have to create them as game
objects and populate it on the moon through a script.

We as humans hear a lot of sounds, and expect audio feedback from many

Spacesuit

normally. This also allows for sound to move inside the suit, so the wearer is able
to hear the internal sounds of the suit, and their own breathing. Additionally

but the sound of a space suit is something I will implement for immersion.

noise can be used to simulate a diﬀerence in pressure in the environment, and

Haptic feedback

major VR headsets, and has been implemented in most modern controllers for
this reason. Haptic feed back will be a key component in my project.

4.2
Movement controller
4.2.1
Running
Running is the movement applied through the joystick of the controller. To
simulate the movement on the moon, a percentage of the force applied is also
applied vertically to simulate the running motion in a weak gravity environment.
Force is only applied to the avatar while the avatar is grounded, but due to
the low gravity and lack of air resistance the avatar can move long distances
without ever touching the ground. Force is applied constantly when the avatar
is grounded and there is a movement input, but obviously there is a limit to the
speed, so after reaching a certain amount of speed, force will only be applied
in the Y-axis through a movement input. I speciﬁcally do not put a hard-cap
on movement speed, meaning forcing the user to move at a speciﬁc speed when
attempting to move as fast as possible, I simply stop applying force. This means
that the user can break my soft speed-cap e.g. by going down slopes, and the
movement controller will attempt to conserve their momentum as long as they
give input that shows they want to continue moving. If no movement input is
given while the user is grounded, they will start decelerating.
4.2.2
Walking
Making the avatar walk according to real life movement was much more diﬃcult
than I had ﬁrst imagined. I had assumed that I could simply use the movement
of the headset and convert it into virtual movement, but in a lot of cases this
felt extremely unnatural. The most obvious issue was whenever the user leaned
forward, maybe to pick up a rock, the system would register that as movement
and make the user walk forward. I attempted a solution where I checked the
vertical movement of the head to check if the user was moving, but this still gave
issues, since some movements barely change the vertical height of the user when
leaning. This caused issues where if the user actually moved forward, but maybe
moved their body slightly downwards, it would not detect it as movement, and
inversely sometimes it would register movement forward, even though the user
just shifted their body.
To solve this issue I decided to use the movement of the controllers as well.
So I polled the latest movement of both of the controllers, and the headset, to
attempt to read the movements of the user.
4.2.3
Jumping
Jumping is implemented in 2 diﬀerent ways, by a button press, and by a gesture.
Button press
is the most simple form of jumping.
It is simply done by
pressing a button on the controller. This is intuitive for most people comfortable
with a controller, but is not at all immersive. This was still a requested feature
by some of my younger testers, and I used it for testing, so I decided to keep it
in.
33
==PAGE:35==
Gesture jump
is the main type of movement. Gesture jumping also allows
the system to track when users are running, so if the demo is played on a more
mobile platform such as the Oculus Quest, it should be functional.
The way gesture jump works is by tracking the height of the user, and apply
force to the avatar when the user exceeds their height with a lot of speed. And
issue I had with the gesture jump was when I tested the demo with multiple peo-
ple, and a tall person handed the headset to a shorter person without restarting
the demo. This caused the program to still have the previous persons height
stored. To avoid this I recalculate the height whenever the headset is removed
from the users head.
To trigger the gesture jump, both height and speed is necessary to trigger it.
This is to avoid edge cases where the user stands up quickly, which otherwise
could register a jump without the height requirement, even though the user
breaks the speed so they do not actually leave the ground. On the other hand,
the user can easily move around a lot where they might break the height by
tiptoeing, and it would be weird to jump in that case.
Estimating the height of the user
To estimate the height of the user I poll
the Y coordinates of the main camera from the last 60 frames. Since everything
in the demo is designed in SI units, 1 unit in the virtual world is the same as 1
meter in the real world, so the change in Y-coordinate corresponds directly to
real world movement. I use the local Y-coordinate because then the location of
the CameraRig is irrelevant. Since the user is moving constantly which changes
the height it can be diﬃcult to estimate the height perfectly, there for I am
doing it through a polling system. When a person is moving on a ﬂat surface
in the real world, it is impossible for them to continuously being in the air for
a prolonged period of time, and when jumping the height value will change a
lot. So to estimate the height I continuously poll until there is a period of a
second where the user is at a roughly constant height. Of course a user can
stay at a constant height for prolonged periods of time while being below their
actual height, but when the user straightens up, the algorithm will recalculate
the height since the new height is above the previous estimate. As mentioned
this is not necessary for tall heights, since that is only possible when the user
jumps, which the system will recognize and not use to estimate the height. One
exception is of course tiptoeing, but the only issue with tiptoeing is that it
disallows the system from registering very small jumps.

Oculus touch controller

button was pressed. I thought a simple solutions was to simply add another
smaller sphere collider, and set the ”isTrigger” ﬂag to True on the larger sphere
collider, and False on the smaller sphere collider.
When the isTrigger ﬂag is set to False, the object will collide with other
objects with the isTrigger ﬂag set to False, as if they are both solid objects.
When isTrigger is set to True the collider only triggers events, but does not act
as a solid object. By setting the isTrigger ﬂag of the outer collider to True, it
allows solid objects to pass through it, but it can still trigger events, so when
the user presses the grab button while an object is inside the trigger, it will
activate the grab event. And by setting the isTrigger ﬂag to False for the inner
collider, it will allow the user to touch objects without grabbing them.
This solution had one glaring issue, unity cannot handle 2 colliders of the
same type on the same object. To circumvent this limitation I attempted to play
around with children objects of the hand objects, and give the children colliders,
until I found a very simple solution, just using another type of collider. The

4.3.1
Rocks
The rocks are objects with a rigidbody to apply physics, and a box collider
to allow for collision with the ground, and with the hands of the user. Rocks
are created in diﬀerent size, and are randomly distributed across the moon
environment.
As mentioned grabbing rocks is done by pressing the hand button while the
outer collider is colliding with the collider of the rock object. Additionally the
user is able to punch the rocks with the inner collider. This allows the user to
play around with the rocks similarly to how we would play around with balloons
due to the low gravity. When a user makes a throwing motion and releases the
hand trigger, the force of the controller is applied to the rock, and thus the rock
will be thrown.
4.3.2
Climb
Similarly to the rocks, climbing is done by the outer collider touching a terrain
collider and pressing the hand trigger. The gravity of the rigidbody of the user
is then turned oﬀto allow them to hang and climb on the terrain. If the user
moves the controller around while holding on to the terrain, the avatar will
be moved around accordingly, and by quickly using the controllers to ”throw
themselves” the force of the controllers are applied to the avatar, similarly to
a rock toss, and this allows the user to almost ”jump with their hands” when
they are holding on to terrain. This is of course easier to do on the moon due
to the low gravity, so it is fun for the user to feel much stronger, even though it
is simply because of the low gravity.

SteamVR and SteamVR 2.0
Initially in my project I used SteamVR 1 due to my introduction to VR with
unity used the original SteamVR [Mu17]. What I failed to realize was that my
system had automatically updated to SteamVR 2.0, so I had a weird mixture
of the two packages. I ﬁrst noticed this when my computer broke and I used
my new computer, but I had to do this from a recovered backup of my project,
which did not contain all the additional assets. It took me a long time to try and
recover my project, and I found one of the main issues was this change between
the original SteamVR and SteamVR 2.0. When I discovered this I decided to
swap to SteamVR 2.0 for the rest of the project.
The original SteamVR was designed much as normal assets, where most of
the methods were scripts that you applied to your game objects, or referenced
through scripts. In SteamVR 2.0 things were a lot diﬀerent. The biggest change
for me was how the controllers were handled. In the original SteamVR I could
simply access the controllers directly from scripts and apply haptics / inputs
from the scripts.
In SteamVR 2.0 they added an abstraction level to the controllers. Instead
of the controller input being handled in the scripts, I have to create an Action
Set, which is a .JSON ﬁle. Each action in the actionlist contains a name, a type
(boolean, vector, etc), if it is required to be deﬁned, and a localized string.
These actions are then deﬁned in controller bindings, which is an external
program from unity. In controller bindings I can select what application I want
to edit controls for, afterwards I selected the Oculus Touch controllers, and there
I could edit my bindings.
The ”In” actions are the actions inputted by the controller that the applica-
tion has to handle, such as button presses. The ”Out” actions are the actions
that the applications asks the controllers to handle, in my case the outgoing
action is only haptic feedback.
They have decided to add this abstraction level so developers can say ”when
you do this action do this” and then the controller binding will handle what e.g.
button triggers the action. They do this since steam wants VR development

5.3.2
Running
Running is the movement applied through the joystick of the controller. To
simulate the movement on the moon, a percentage of the force applied is also
applied vertically to simulate the running motion in a weak gravity environment.
Force is only applied to the avatar while the avatar is grounded, but due to
the low gravity and lack of air resistance the avatar can move long distances
without ever touching the ground. Force is applied constantly when the avatar
is grounded and there is a movement input, but obviously there is a limit to the
speed, so after reaching a certain amount of speed, force will only be applied
in the Y-axis through a movement input. I speciﬁcally do not put a hard-cap
on movement speed, meaning forcing the user to move at a speciﬁc speed when
attempting to move as fast as possible, I simply stop applying force. This means
that the user can break my soft speed-cap e.g. by going down slopes, and the
movement controller will attempt to conserve their momentum as long as they
give input that shows they want to continue moving. If no movement input is
given while the user is grounded, they will start decelerating.

Climbing terrain is a bit more diﬃcult than the rock throw, since it involves
the player game object, and is also aﬀected by the movement controller. The

constantly moved slightly away from the original position. To circumvent this

6
Results
In this section I will describe some of the results I had based on testing the demo.
I only tested with a small sample size consisting of friends and family who tried
it at my set-up at home. The test persons varied in background from young
people in their early twenties who is used to technology, to people in their mid
sixties who have limited experience with technology outside of e.g. cellphones.
The testing was done blindly for the most parts where no instructions were
given, and only prior information was that it was a ”moon demo in virtual
reality”.
6.1
Movement
People accustomed to video games and controllers used the controllers intuitively
to move around and jump. This allowed the to explore the landscape of the
moon and try climbing and throwing rocks.
People unaccustomed to videogames had a hard time using the controllers
for movement, but could easily move around through real world movement (al-
though it is limited movement due to cables).
I had expected from the beginning of the project that the ﬁrst thing people
would do in the demo was to try and jump, but unless they had prior knowledge
that real world jumping allowed for jumping in the virtual world, no body did
it. It seemed like the people used to videogames focused more on exploration
with the controller, which of course also allows for jumping, where as the people
unfamiliar with games and controller spend most of the time just looking around.
When questioned about they usually did not even think about it, or they were
to scared to make big motions while connected with cables to the computer.
Some people unaccustomed to controllers were able to use the controllers to
grab rocks intuitively, and some struggled with it.
6.2
Environment
When test subjects with no video game knowledge attempted the demo, their
ﬁrst reaction was always to look around and comment on the environment.
They seemed to have a positive reaction to the Earth and to the night sky, and
with a view to big moon craters and a lot of smaller ones in the texture, they
immediately felt they were experiencing the moon, though they did not play
around with the physics unless prompted to. As mentioned earlier, the younger
people with game knowledge were more to the point, they focused on the rocks
on the ﬂoor at immediately attempted to pick it up (but by moving the avatar
with the controller). They were more interested in the game aspects than the
environment.

6.3
Sound
People who only tried the demo for a short while did not have any noticeable
opinion of the astronaut breathing sound when trying the demo, but they seemed

People who played the demo for more than 15

6.4
Gesture Jumping
I experimented a bit with the gesture jump mechanic. When the user is jump-
ing in real life, they land much quicker in the real world than in the virtual
world. The landing obviously had a high impact in the real world, which broke
immersion. In the actual testing, this was not really an issue, since most people
enjoyed seeing how high they would jump on the moon, and it gave them an-
other type of satisfaction, but the landing impact in the real world was deﬁnitely
noticeable. When testing with other people I always had haptic feedback enable
on landing in the virtual world, and I can say from personal testing that it has
a huge impact. The testers knew exactly when they landed due to the haptics,
and speaking from personal experience the lack of haptics upon landing is really
uncomfortable, especially when falling from a major height. It seemed like my
body expected some type of feedback, and it felt wrong if I did not get it.
I attempted to test jumping on a mattress to address the issue of impact
landing, but the diﬀerence in force applied when jumping on the mattress made
the jumps much weaker and less interesting, and standing on a soft surface
simply broke the immersion in another way. When testing with the mattress
the users also generally felt it was hilarious and saw it as more of a joke, which
lead to even less immersion in the actual demo.

7.1
Working with cutting edge technology
During the course of my project I learned a lot of things about developing in

One of the major things I learned was how quickly sources gets outdated when
working with cutting-edge technology like Virtual Reality. The book I initially
used to learn about virtual reality was published the year before I started my
project, and a lot of the material was already outdated. When I followed the
tutorials of the book and used the exact same code but in a newer unity envi-
ronment and (as I found a later when my computer broke) a weird mixture of
the original SteamVR from the books online material, and SteamVR 2.0 I had
downloaded myself, a lot of code did not work as it should. Most tutorials I
found online were still using the old SteamVR as well, so before I found out that
I had a new version where a lot of things were removed and implemented diﬀer-
ently, I got a massive problem very often with things not working as expected,
because the less than a year old tutorials already were outdated.

everything I had, and when it ﬁnally did die, I managed to recover my project,
but I was unable to run it properly. I assume this had something to do with
some packages in unity, since I believe that to be the only diﬀerence, but I had
to extract the resources from my project at create the scene again, and set up
all the variables in unity as well. Looking back at it I should have bought a new
computer before starting the project since I knew there was a chance for it to
become an issue, but I was greedy and hoped it would last longer until some
new computer parts was released.
7.3
Experimenting with the terrain
I enjoyed experimenting with diﬀerent methods of creating the terrain, and in
addition to the methods described in the report, but reﬂecting on it, my end
product would have more features if I had just settled on the unity terrain, which
I knew from the start would be the most likely solution. When calculating the
actual detail level of the diﬀerent height maps I found during my research, all
of the had a detail level that was to small to properly create a moon terrain,
and to create the terrain, from a 3D model in blender, the model needed to be

extremely detailed (have a lot of polygons) which would impact performance.
Additionally it required me to disable the unity physics engine, and the physics

7.4
Limitations of my solution
When I started the project, I wanted the user to be able to explore diﬀerent
parts of the moon, and I might even have wanted to explore diﬀerent planets
in our solar system, and not only the moon. For the most parts, my current
solution is designed to be easily customize-able.
Obviously the environment
needs to be re-designed with a new terrain and other interesting views than
the Earth, but the movement controller it self and the physics engine is easily
changed to suit another environment. Additionally I would have loved to try
testing it out with the Oculus Quest. Theoretically the gesture jump should
allow for running in real world to be applied to the virtual world, be it has been
diﬃcult to test with my current setup.

8
Conclusion
I feel like I explored the gravitational ﬁeld of the moon, but there were additional
things I would have like to experiment with. The immersion was not as high as I
had hoped in test subjects, but it might be due to them being familiar with me,
and wanting to share their enjoyment when playing in a virtual environment.
The visuals of seeing the moon environment in virtual reality deﬁantly had
an impact on people not used to these types of experiences, but they seemed
uncomfortable moving unless prompted to initially, meaning they seemed scared
to move until they got conﬁrmation that it was alright to move in real life.
The audio of a space suit seemed to have impact on some people initially,
but mostly they wanted to talk about their experience, which ruined some of the
immersion that was supposed to come from the audio. Other people who played
the demo for longer periods of time started to ﬁnd the breathing annoying,
which indicates that maybe a white noise alternative could be a better solution
for experiences with an expectation of longer sessions.
I created a terrain where the user can climb in and out of craters, play
around with moon rocks, and physically climb the terrain with their hands.
I created a movement controller where the user was able to convert real
world movement into virtual movement using only the Oculus Rift and the
controllers, but of course it was not as good as complete body tracking, and
only an estimation of the users movements.

9.1
Glossary
User/player:
The user or player is the person who is currently experienc-
ing the moon demo.
Avatar:
The avatar is the virtual avatar of the user. The user will expe-
rience the demo through the in game avatar.
Play area:
The play area is the real world open area the user has available
to move around in. The play area is often limited either by walls or furniture’s,
or by the length of the cables connected to the VR-headset. High-end virtual
reality headsets, such as the HTC Vive and the Oculus rift gives the user the
option to mark their available play area, and when the user gets close to the
edge of the play area a visual warning will be displayed to protect the user from
self harm.
Unity:
Unity8 is a game engine designed to eﬃciently create games. In
this project I will use unity to create the moon experience in combination with
other tools. Unity has its own build-in physics engine that has many settings
to customize the experience.
GameObject:
A gameobject is the diﬀerent objects created in the unity
game engine. An example of a gameobject is the avatar. Each gameobject will
usually have a lot of diﬀerent components attached to them, such as scripts.
Prefabs
The word Prefab is shorthand for Prefabrication.
In the con-
text of unity it is prefabrication of a gameobject. Essentially this means that
gameobject are instantiated objects, where as prefabs are a ”recipe” for the
gameobject, similar to classes. For example if I want to create many rocks to
populate the moon, I will instantiate a lot of rocks based on the ”rock prefab”.
Transform:
The transform is a component of the gameobject that deﬁnes
its position scale and rotation in the virtual world. Objects often has a local and
global position, global position being their actual position in the world, and the
local position is usually their position in relation to their parent gameobject.
Collider:
Collider is a component that can be attached to a gameobject.
When a collider is attached to an object, it allows it to detect collision with
other objects that also have a collider attached to them. When two colliders
interact they call functions such as ”OnTriggerEnter”, ”OnTriggerExit”, ”On-
CollisionEnter”, ”OnCollisionExit”. There are many diﬀerent colliders, such as
8[Te18]
55
==PAGE:57==
speciﬁc shape collides: capsule collider, sphere collider, box collider. There are
also mesh colliders, which applies a collider to the entire mesh of an object, but
these are often quite computationally heavy. Terrain collider is similar to mesh
collider, but is optimized for unity created terrain, so they usually requires less
computation. In the unity engine, when two colliders collider, they will usually
be aﬀected by the build in physics engine, and transfer forces between them.
Colliders have a boolean value: isTRigger.
When isTrigger is true, the
collider will only trigger functions, but will not be aﬀected by the physics engine.
Rigidbody component:
The rigidbody is a component that can be at-
tached to a unity gameobject. The rigidbody is usually what allows the object
to be aﬀected by most of unity’s physics engine. In the rigidbody, the developer
can set how the object will be aﬀected by the physics engine. Notable settings
are the mass, drag, angular drag, and useGravity settings.
Unity Terrain:
Unity has build in tools for creating a terrain. The terrain
creating from these tool are optimized, especially with the terrain collider.
Oculus rift:

Oculus touch controllers:
Oculus touch are the correlated controllers
to the Oculus Rift headset. The touch controllers are designed to simulate a
persons hand in virtual reality, by tracking the controller motions, and with the
button layout, and grip form (see ﬁgure 30). As seen in ﬁgure 30, the touch
controllers has thump-rest, index-triggers and primary hand triggers, each with
sensor in them. Theses sensors are in addition to the trigger/button presses
also available to the controllers. These buttons and sensors are used to detect
the hand pose of the user. For example if the primary hand trigger detects the
presence of the users hand, without any trigger input, and the other sensors
do not detect any user presence, then they are most likely in a ”ﬁnger gun”
pose. If all the sensors detect the presence of the users hand, but without any
trigger/button inputs, then the hand is most likely in a relaxed semi-closed pose.

Use cases
When creating my moon experience, it was important to deﬁne some prevalent
usec ases that was necessary for the experience. I personally thought of certain
actions I would do if I was on the moon, and additionally I conversed with mul-
tiple people in diﬀerent age and social demography’s. Through the combination
of their points of view, and my own thougts, I compiled this list of use cases.
9.2.1
Use case 1: Walk on the moon
Name: Walk on the moon
Description: The avatar walks around on the virtual moon
Actor: User
Preconditions:
• The user has equipped the oculus headset and they are holding both
touch controllers.
• The avatar is grounded.
Main scenario:
1. The user walks around in the real world
2. The program detects the real world movement
3. The player avatar is moved according to real world movement
Alternate scenario:
1a. The user tilts the analog stick of one of the controllers slightly
2a.
The analog input is read by the system and converted to slow
movement of the avatar
This use case describes how the user will trigger simulated walking motions.
When walking, at least one foot will always touch the ground which causes
gravity to have less of an eﬀect on movement compared to running. This means
real world walking movement can be applied in the virtual world without break-
ing immersion.
Additionally a secondary way of applying a walking motion to the virtual
avatar should be available through the controller, to make up for real world
limitations such as a limited play area.

Use case 2: Run on the moon
Name: Run on the moon
Description: The avatar runs on the virtual moon
Actor: User

• The user has equipped the oculus headset and they are holding both

• The avatar is grounded.

4. The avatar is running in big steps
Alternate scenario:
1a. The user tilts the analog stick of one of the controllers a lot
2a. The analog input is read by the system and converted to the avatar
running in big steps
Translating real world running motion to virtual movement on the moon is
naturally less immersive than walking due to the eﬀects of a diﬀerent gravity.
When the user is making a running motion in real life, the force should be
measured and applied in the virtual world, but due to the weaker gravitational
pull on the moon, the avatar will fall much slower than in the real world, and due
to the diﬀerence of atmosphere, the avatar will retain its speed for much longer
due to the lack of wind resistance. This diﬀerence will lessen the immersion of
the user, and in this project I will explore options to mitigate this issue.
As with walking, an alternate way of applying running movement should be
available through the controllers, due to possible real world limitations.

When the user is exploring the moon, they may ﬁnd some loose rocks or other
