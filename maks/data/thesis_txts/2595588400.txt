Abstract
Structures subjected to sufficient operational or environmental changes can obtain changes
in modal properties.
These changes are interesting for Structural Health Monitoring
(SHM) as they can indicate a damage of the structure. One example of change in modal
properties are mode crossings, which means that modes and the corresponding mode shape
are changing order in terms of frequency. When plotting estimated modes in a time vari-
ant system, they are sorted based on the order of the natural frequency. Therefore, modal
changes like mode crossings will not be discovered as the information about the mode
shape is not connected to the estimated natural frequency. This can be solved by sorting
the modes based on the physical behavior, and by doing so, connecting the estimated
mode with a corresponding mode shape.
The scope of this thesis is therefore to investigate the possibilities to track modal changes
in a time variant system. This project is done numerically, so time variant systems are
simulated by variant structural systems, where structural properties are changed over a
number of increments. These changes are implemented to the FE Model made to represent
a platform structure. For all the increments of structural variant systems, the correspond-
ing response is simulated. Based on the response, the modal properties of the structure are
estimated using identification methods known from Operational Modal Analysis (OMA).
The identification methods estimates the natural frequencies and the corresponding mode
shapes for all the increments of the variant systems.
Using two different modal tracking approaches, possibilities of modal tracking is investi-
gated. The first modal tracking approach is utilising the Effective Modal Mass (EMM)
to sort the modes, and the second one utilises the Relative Motion (RM). Both the ap-
proaches uses sorting criteria to sort the estimated mode based on their physical behavior.
By plotting the sorted estimated modes, the mode crossings are detected. Based on this
it can be said that the modes are successfully tracked for the variant structural systems.
Even though this is found to be a rather simple approach compared to other modal track-
ing strategies, some aspects of the modal tracking approach could be enhanced and should
therefore be further investigated. Also, some deficiencies with more complex mode shapes
are discussed. Regardless of the deficiencies, it can be stated that the modal tracking
approaches are successful of tracking the modal changes for the variant systems, and the
corresponding mode crossings are detected.

1
Introduction
Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) aims to give a diagnosis of the state of parts of the
structure or the structure as a whole [1]. This is done through the process of monitoring
the condition of a structure over time, to see if there has been any damage to the structure.
Damage can be identified by the change in the structural behavior caused by change in
the geometrical or the material properties. The monitoring is done by placing a set of
sensors on the structure, to capture the dynamic responses. Based on the response, the
dynamic properties are estimated. By analysing the estimated dynamic properties over
time, the health condition of the structure can be determined. To be able to estimate the
modal properties from the ambient responses from operational conditions, the procedures
from Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) are applied. OMA estimates dynamic proper-
ties of a structure from measurements of the vibration response only [2]. The properties
are estimated based on the correlation between dynamic characteristic of a mathemati-
cal model and the physical properties of a system based on experimental measurements [3].
1.1
State of the Art
The modal properties for a structure can vary due to changes in the operational or envi-
ronmental conditions. The operational conditions are related to the structure itself, and
the environmental are the surroundings that affects the structure. In some cases, these
variations of the characteristic can be so significant, that the modes and the corresponding
natural frequencies can change order. This phenomenon is called a mode crossing and can
be a permanent or temporally change. These mode crossings occurs when the modes are
sufficiently closely spaced in terms of frequency. One example of mode crossing due to
environmental changes is presented in a project of automated modal tracking of structrual
changes performed on a suspension roof [4]. In this article, the temperature decreases the
elasticity modulus of the concrete, which leads to crossing of modes. An example of mode
crossing due to operational conditions is presented in an article about the evolution of
the dynamic properties, where the mode crossing were produced by reduced stiffness from
concrete cracking [5]. Therefore, the tracking of modes can be considered as high relevance
for SHM.
However, mode crossings will not be detected by using the classical identification methods.
This can be explained by the different sorting strategies presented by Galliana et al. [6].
The different classification strategies are presented as
• The frequency scale strategy classifies the modal parameter based on the natural
frequency.
• The mode shape strategy classifies the modal parameter based on the mode shape.
• The physical mode strategy classifies the modal parameter based on the physical
mode evolution.
By using the frequency scale, the information about the mode is lost, and is why it will
not be able to detect if there is a mode crossing. Tracking of modes using the mode shape
strategy is investigated by Marrongelli et al. [7]. Here, they are able to track the mode
crossings that occurs due to changes in temperature using an automated modal tracking

method. This method keeps track of the frequencies and the corresponding mode shapes by
comparing the reference mode shapes and the estimated singular values at each frequency
line using the Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC) [8]. If the MAC value is above a given
threshold for a certain number of singular values, they are linked to a reference mode.
In terms of the physical mode strategy, one approach is investigated by Eldred et al. [9].
Here, mode crossing is detected by checking the orthogonality of the mode shapes between
the previous and current iteration mode shapes. Even though it is related to structural
optimisation, it shows that it is possible to detect mode crossing based on the physical
mode strategy. This project will look further into tracking in terms of the physical mode
strategy, where the order of the modes will be sorted based on their physical behavior.
1.2
Scope of the Project
Based on the remarks above related to mode crossing and mode tracking, this project will
answer the following question:
What are the possibilities of tracking modes based on their physical behavior?
It is the scope of this project to answer this by using the characteristics of the different
modes to sort them. To simulate a time variant system, structural changes are applied
to a structure. Based on this, the increments of structural changes are mimicking change
in a time variant system. As this is investigated numerical, a Finite Element (FE) Model
is made of a simple platform structure consisting of a plate placed on a column. Based
on this FE Model, two variant structural systems are made. The first structural system
has point mass located at the top of the plate with increasing magnitude. The second one
has a reduced stiffness in the column by decreasing the thickness of one side in the cross
section. The system with point mass is simulating operational change of the structure,
while the decreasing stiffness is simulating damage on the structure. For these variant
systems, each increment will have simulated responses and the modal properties will be
estimated using robust techniques from OMA. After the modal properties are estimated,
two different approaches of mode tracking is applied.
Based on this, it is possible to
determine some of the possibilities of modal tracking based on the physical behavior.
1.3
Structure of the Report
The structure of the project is constructed so that the necessary theory is presented first,
then the process of the experiment is presented and finally the results. First, an introduc-
tion to OMA is presented in order to make the reader familiar with the relevant theory
related to this project. Then, the theory and the method of the two approaches of modal
tracking are presented. As these approaches will be tested numerically, the process of mod-
elling the FE Model is presented. The process of simulating responses for the FE Model
and estimating the corresponding modal properties is then presented. Once the process
of simulating the response and identification is described, the variant structural systems
are introduced. Here, the simulation and identification process is applied for all the in-
crements of the variant systems. In order to test the two approaches of mode tracking,
both the variant structural systems will experience at least crossing of modes. Finally, the
two modal tracking approaches are applied to the variant structural systems to obtain the
results. Based on this, the project is finalised by a discussion of the result and a conclusion.
Only the relevant information related to the project is presented in the following sections.
If the reader is interested, additional information can be found in the Appendix.

2
Operational Modal Analysis
The scope of this project is to investigate tracking of modes in a variant system, where the
modal properties related to the mode is estimated by the process from OMA. Therefore,
an introduction of the subject and a set of identification techniques is presented.
2.1
Basic Concept
The basic concept of OMA is to obtain information about the modal properties from a
structure based on operational responses. The responses can be excited by different op-
erational or environmental loads. The fundamental idea of OMA is that these loads has
approximately white noise characteristic, which means that it has its energy distributed
over a wide energy range that covers the frequency range of the modal characteristic of the
structure [3]. The modal properties that can be obtained from the responses is the natural
frequency, fn, damping ratio, ζn, and mode shape bn which corresponds to a certain mode,
n, for the structure. These are obtained by finding the correlation between the response
of the structure and a mathematical model.
The response of the structure can be represented either in the time domain or the frequency
domain [2]. Expressing the dynamical behavior of the structure using equations of motions
are usually done in the time domain as
M ￿y(t) + C ˙y(t) + K¨y(t) = x(t)
(2.1)
where M, C and K is the mass, damping and stiffness matrix, respectively, and y(t) is the
response based on the load x(t). This expression describes the dynamical behavior of the
Degrees of Freedom (DOFs) of the structure. Since the load on the structure is unknown,
and can be considered as white noise, a imaginary filter is introduced. This is a loading
filter added together with the structural system, to a combined system. The loading filter
is assumed to have the dynamic characteristic, so that the white noise in-put results in
out-put as the response of the structure [10].
Based on the response of the structure, the dynamic behavior of the structure can be
decoupled into SDOF systems where each of the DOFs describes the random response of
one mode [3]. This is achieved by considering the correlation function (CF) as free decay
in the time domain, and the Spectral Density (SD) as the corresponding function in the
frequency domain.

2.2
Identification methods
There are several ways of estimating the modal parameters of a structure based on the SD
and CF, and these can be divided into time domain identification and frequency domain
identification. In the time domain, free decays, which is usually estimated as a correlation
function, is used to extract the parameters. In the frequency domain, the frequency bands
are investigated, so the spectral densities are used to extract the parameters.
In this
project, one identification method from each domain will be utilised to identify the modal
parameters of the structure. These are the Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD), in
the frequency domain, and the Time Domain Poly Reference (TDPR), in the time domain.

3
Modal Tracking Approach
The main scope of this project is to research the possibilities of tracking modal changes,
specifically mode crossings, based on physical behavior. Mode crossing occurs when the
changes in characteristic of the structure is so large that modes changes in frequency
order, which is possible when the modes are closely spaced. In this section, the theory

6
Variant Structural Systems
To be able to determine to which extent it is possible to track the modes in a time variant
system, two sets of variant structural systems are made based on the initial FE Model.
This is to simulate time variant systems by changing a parameter in the structure over
several increments. For this project, the goal is to have variant structural systems that
experiences mode crossings. The first variant system has a point mass which is located on
the top plate, where the weight of the point mass is increased. For the second system, the
stiffness of the column is decreased.
As mentioned earlier, white noise is introduced to the response in lack of noise from mea-
surements. To further verify the application of the modal tracking, another case of noise
is introduced. Due to the fact that this project is dealing with a numerical case, addi-
tional noise with the standard deviation of 2% is added to the material densities for all
the increments of the variant structural system. This means that there are two cases of
noise introduced to the structural variant systems, where one only contains the white noise
introduced to the response and one with additional noise added to the mass matrix of the
structure through the material densities.
For each of the increments in the variant structural systems, the response is simulated and
the corresponding modal properties are estimated with the TDPR identification method.
All the structural changes for the structural variant systems are implemented to the FE
Model in Ansys APDL and then exported to MATLAB by using the toolbox FEAutoUp-
date provided by supervisor, Sandro D. R. Amador.
6.1

Considering the amount of increments and the changes in natural frequencies for the
modes, an automated simulation and identification algorithm is implemented in the project.
Because part of the scope of this project is to research ability of the modal tracking meth-

Chapter 5 regarding the signal processing and band pass filtering are determined based
on the known frequencies from the FE Model. By using the determined frequencies from
the FE Model to estimate the right input parameters, all the natural frequencies for the
increments of the structural variant systems is estimated with little or no user interaction.

As seen in Figure 7.2 and 7.6, the mode crossings for 2-BX and 2-BY at 0.5 kg is detected,
as well as the mode crossing for 1-BX and 1-BY at 2 kg for the respective methods. The
modal tracking approaches are also able to sort the modes based on their physical behav-
ior for the system with the decreasing stiffness in the column. As seen in Figure 7.4 and
Figure 7.8, the mode crossing for 1-BX and 1-BY when t1 is at around 70% and 60% of
its initial thickness is detected. These modes are plotted relative to the increments of the
structure, which is implemented as a simulation of a time variant system. This simulation
of the time variant system is considered to be a fair approach, and is used to answer the
question regarding the object of this project. However, since this is a numerical approach
with a simulation of a time variant system, it would be interesting to implement this modal
tracking approach to a experimental model for further verification. Some initiatives are
introduced to the project to get closer to a real case of OMA with a experimental model.
Two cases of noise is introduced: one with the white noise introduced to the response with
a standard deviation of 1% and one with the white noise in the response in addition to
the noise added to the material density with a standard deviation of 2%. Based on the
results of the modal tracking, the noise does not affect the sorting of the modes for neither
of the cases as the mode crossings are detected at the same increments. As mentioned
in Chapter 1 regarding the frequency scale strategy and the physical mode strategy, the
main difference is that the frequency scale strategy looses the ability to detect the mode
crossing, as the estimated modes are only sorted after the increasing order of the natural
frequencies. Therefore, it is fair to say that the modal tracking approaches are successful
in terms of sorting the global modes based on the physical behavior, as they are able to
detect the mode crossings.
The mode shapes of the global modes for the platform structure is easy to relate to a
certain behavior in terms of the EMM and RM. All the bending modes are moving in
either the x- or y-direction, while the torsional mode is rotating around the z-axis. If more
complex mode shapes were introduced, the EMM or the RM would not necessarily be
able to distinguish the behaviors of the mode shapes. It is therefore not certain how well
implemented this tracking method could be for another type of structure, with different
mode shapes. As seen in the automated tracking approach for structural change applied
to the suspension roof, none of the modes seem to have a mode shape moving in a certain
direction but rather a combination of bending and torsional modes [4]. In this case, it
could be hard for the modal tracking approach to sort the modes by comparing the EMM
or RM in different directions. One idea for a new approach could be to make an index
of the initial values of the EMM and RM for the x-, y- and z-direction for the modes,
and continuously compare them with the new estimated modes to sort them based on
the physical behavior. This would however require further testing in order to verify, but
44

==PAGE:55==
would be very interesting to investigate. When it comes to local modes, it is not certain
if it would be sufficient using the EMM and RM to determine the physical behavior of
the local modes. One concern is the fact that the EMM is harder to define for the local
modes [16]. To be able to track modes other than the global modes, it would seem that
the modal tracking based on the mode shape strategy presented by Marrongelli et al. is
more suited, as it compares the mode shapes directly [7]. On the other hand, the modal
tracking approaches in this project is fairly easy to implement, and only requires a rel-
ative little time of preparation. This is compared to the more advanced modal tracking
approach using the mode shape, which uses machine learning and needs several days of
preparations [7]. In terms of SHM and detecting a potential damage in the structure due
to mode crossings of the global modes, the modal tracking approach in this project is well
suited.
Some improvements that could be introduced to the modal tracking approach is to opti-
mise it in a way that less user interaction is needed. For the current state of the modal
tracking approach, the mode shapes are investigated thoroughly for the different incre-
ments of the variant structural systems to make sorting criteria. In addition to studying
the mode shapes, the plots for the EMM and RM relative to the increments of the variant
structural systems are also studied carefully. This can be a time consuming process, and
requires a lot of user interaction, if implemented to several variant structural systems.
There are also more room for improvements when it comes to the automated simulated
response and identification. In this project, simplified methods for simulating the response
and identification of the modes utilises the known frequencies of the FE Model. This is
implemented as identification of the modal properties of the structure is not part of the
main objective for this project. If there were more time available, it would be interesting
to take a closer look at this, and also the application of the modal tracking approaches on
an experimental model.

9
Conclusion
In this project, two different approaches for modal tracking using the EMM and RM is
implemented. This is to be able investigate the possibilities of modal tracking based on the
physical behavior of the modes in time variant systems. Time variant systems are simu-
lated by introducing variant structural systems, where structural properties are changed in
a FE Model of a platform structure consisting of a column and a top plate. The first vari-
ant structural system consists of an increasing point mass at the top plate, while the other
has decreasing stiffness of the column. These changes are simulating operational change
in the structure and damage to the column, respectively. The simulated response and
identification of the modal properties are determined through the FRF and the load vec-
tor, and the modal properties are estimated using the OMA identification method TDPR
with the condensation of the correlation function. Once the modes are estimated, both
the modal tracking approaches are applied to the variant structural systems. The tracking
approach consists of sorting criteria, which are able to classify the estimated mode based
on their physical behavior. The results of the modal tracking approaches are presented
and investigated to determine their possibilities of tracking the modal changes. Based on
the investigation of the result from modal tracking the conclusions are summarised as
• The OMA identification method TDPR can successfully be implemented in a auto-
matic identification process, and is able to estimate modes even if they are closely
spaced. The EFDD on the other hand has some trouble to separate the singular
values for the closely spaced modes.
• The modal tracking approaches, both the one using the EMM and the one using
RM, are able to track the modes based on their physical behavior. This means that
occurring mode crossings are detected for both variant structural systems.
• The modal tracking is successfully utilising the sorting criteria where the EMM
and RM in the x- and y-direction are compared for each mode. This describes the
physical behavior of the modes.
• The modal tracking is successful for both cases of noise. This means that the modal
tracking approach is not influenced by a introduction of noise to a certain degree.
However, it is uncertain how well this modal tracking would be on an experimental
model without any further testing.
• The modal tracking is verified for the global modes for the platform structure, but
it is not certain how well the tracking is capable of tracking local modes.
9.1
Further Perspectives
As mentioned earlier, there are some aspects of this project that could be investigated
further. They are summed up as
• Considering the automated simulated response and identification is using a simplified
approach, an investigation of a more automated approach with less user interaction
and simplifications could be done.
• Since the modal tracking is applied to a numerical case, it would be interesting to
have a look at how well the method would do in an experimental case.
46

• It would also be interesting to see how robust the method is by testing it on other
types of structures with different mode shapes.
• New tracking approaches with an index of the initial values for the EMM and RM in
the x-, y- and z-direction and compare them with the continuously estimated modes
for sorting would be very interesting to investigate.
