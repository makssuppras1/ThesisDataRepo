Abstract 
The marination of herrings is a popular tradition in northern Europe and motivating the populations 
here to eat more fish is one of the main ambitions of the Health Protection Agency. Today the 
consumers make their choice of food depending on a variety of factors such as price, quality, 
product information and marketing. The aim of this thesis is to investigate the effect of different 
pre-salting brine concentrations on different quality measures after the acid marinating process on 
herrings. 
There are seven batches in this trial with different salt concentrations and time in the pre-salt brine:  
1) direct into the acid/salt brine. 
2) 13.5% salt in 15 hours. 
3) 13.5% salt in 4 days. 
4) 16% salt in 15 hours. 
5) 16% salt in 4 days. 
6) 26% salt in 15 hours 
7) 26% salt in 4 days.  
All seven batches receive the same acid/salt marinating concentration after the pre-salt brine, for 35 
days, therefore the only variables are found in the pre-salting. Salt concentration, acid 
concentration, water content, oil and texture of the herrings have been measured at different times 
during the 35 days.  
The results show that the two batches with 13.5% salt rank highest in most of the quality 
parameters. It can be concluded that all seven batches have a high enough salt and acid content to 
be safe to eat and avoid unwanted anisakis larvae and bacteria. The texture of all the batches, 
except batch (direct), is has a firm structure even though there is a significant (p<0.05) difference 
between the batches. From a financial viewpoint, thus, the thesis advices Skagerak pelagic to pursue 
the 13.5% pre-salt brine for the herrings, as this brine produces the lowest water and weight 
decrease, which means that these herring will keep the highest weight, and therefore comparatively 
less herring is needed to sell 100g of product.

1. Introduction 
The consumers today make their choice of food depending on a variety of factors such as price, 
quality,  product information, marketing, packaging, durability, ecology, consumer values, attitudes, 
financial capacity and health (Lise Justesen, 2010).   
To consume a healthy diet, consumers are advised to follow the ten official dietary guidelines from 
the danish Health Protection Agency, they are as follows: 
 Eat a varied diet, not too much and be 
physically active 
 Eat fruits and many vegetables 
 Eat more fish 
 Choose whole grain 
 Choose lean meats and deli meats 
 Choose low fat dairy products 
 Eat less saturated fat 
 Eat food with less salt 
 Eat less sugar 
 Drink water 
 
These ten points describe the best way to eat for the consumers to get all the nutrients they need in 
the appropriate amounts. That way consumers can best stay fresh and healthy and best prevent 
lifestyle diseases (Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2016). 
 
The Health Protection Agency has made consumers aware of the benefits of eating more fish during 
the past several years, due to the nutritional qualities like polyunsaturated fatty acids (n-3 fatty 
acids)(Lise Justesen, 2010), iodine and vitamin D (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet & 
Fødevarestyrelsen, 2017), and  the effort has paid off. In 2008, The National Food Institute (DTU)  
published the average weekly intake of fish among adult Danes to be 154 grams (DTU 
fødevareinstituttet, 2010), and in 2013 it was 259 grams (DTU fødevareinstituttet, 2015). This 
means that the weekly intake of fish has increased significantly (p<0.05) by 68% in five years. 
 
Therefore it is important that if the increase in intake continues, it is possible to produce enough fish 
of all kinds such as, e.g. fresh, dried, salted and marinated, to satisfy and comply with the consumer 
wishes. Denmark's Statistics (Natur Erhvervstyrelsen, 2017) also support the increase in intake, 
with data showing that for the last six years more and more herring are landed in Denmark, see

1.1 Marinating and preparation in the industry 
Preparation 
Upon arrival to the factory the herrings are washed. If they are too soft after the washing, they are 
put in a pre-salt brine for one hour to harden them. After the washing the herrings are be headed, 
gutted in to the two fillets. The fish or portions of fish are then rinsed in a five % salt solution to 
remove traces of blood from the flesh (Torry Research Station, 2001).  
 
Marinating 
The herring fillets are first immersed in a relatively strong solution of acetic acid and salt in tanks or 
barrels in a cool room. The ratio of herring to liquid in barrels usually ranges from 1:1 to 1.5:1 
(Torry Research Station, 2001).  
There is a small amount of liquid in the tanks and the herrings are put into the tanks a few at the 
time and the liquid is again added in small amounts to get rapid dilution. When the tank is full the 
herrings are stirred to ensure complete mixing and to prevent the fish sticking together. The tanks 
are inspected regularly, and if there is any loss of liquid, more brine of the right strength are added 
to ensure the fish are completely covered at all times. The herrings can be removed from the tank 
for packing throughout the storage period. The fish need to be in the tank until equilibrium is 
reached. The time needed to obtain equilibrium depends on where the tank is stored. If the tank is in 
a warm room it will only take one week, if it is in a cool room it will take 3 weeks and if it I stored 
at 3°C it can be stored for up to six months (Torry Research Station, 2001), but the procedure at 
Skagerak Pelagic, do not store the herrings for six months.  
==PAGE:11==
Side 10 af 73 
 
Packing 
When the marinating process is finished the herrings’ flesh is white and firm. All the herrings are 
inspected, and any with brown or red discolorations are discarded. They are then cut into the desired 
size, put into containers and covered with a pickle with the desired spices (Torry Research Station, 
2001). The pickle that cover the herrings  should contain 1-2 per cent acetic acid and 2-4 per cent 
salt and must not have a pH above 4.5 (Torry Research Station, 2001).

2 Project description 
2.1  Aim 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the effect of the brines salt concentration on different quality 
measures after the marinating process, specially, whether the time in the pre-salting of the herring 
fillets has an influence on the yield and other quality parameters in the end of the marinating 
process. The aim is furthermore to see if the yield has reached its equilibrium before the herring has 
been in the acid brine for 35 days.  
 
2.2  Hypothesis 
The salt concentration in the pre-salting will affect the different quality parameters at the end of the 
marinating procedure in herring. The different parameters measured in this trial are the yield, 
texture, salt concentration, acid concentration, oil and water content. 
 
2.3  Description 
This project is developed from knowledge of the 24-hour trial described further down (Helle 
Hougaard Hein, Pia Fiil Christensen and, & Sara Aaslet, 2016). This trial is built up with some of 
the same concentrations as the 24-hour trial, of salt brine (16% and 25%) used for the pre-salting 
trial, again using a 13.5% salt concentration. This project will only look at the pre-salting of the 
herrings. The 24-hour trial showed that the final weight for some of the batches is not in 
equilibrium. Therefore, it was decided to investigate what happens to the weight if the pre-salting is 
stopped after 15 hours, after four days, or if one batch is put directly in the marinating acid brine.   
It is desired to study if there is any difference in the yield and quality between the three different 
pre-salting periods when the herrings have been in the acid brine.  
 
When are all the qualities of the herring in equilibrium and has this happened before the end of the 
35 days the herrings need to be in acid? This is analyzed, as it might be possible to optimize the 
process.

3.1 Herring fish 
The fish is a vertebrate with over 20,000 different species (Lise Justesen, 2010). Fish are 
characterized by their gills that obtain the oxygen in the water and are also equipped with fins. The 
marination of herrings is a popular tradition in northern Europe. Different countries and different 
companies have their own recipes, but all include an acid and salt process (Horst Karl, Allan 
Roepstorff, 1995). On Picture 1 an illustration of a herring can be seen.

3.2 Structure of raw herring

Characteristics 
The herring is in the systematic classification a bone fish, and within the bone fish classification it is 
grouped as a pelagic fish, which means that the herring is a fatty fish with a fat depot in the 
muscles. Although it swims continuously, it has a large share of dark muscle which contains a high 
amount of myoglobin and it finds its food in the water (Lise Justesen, 2010). The herring rarely

3.2.2 
Muscle structure 
The functional properties of fish can be divided by two fundamental characteristics: the meat's 
ability to bind water and the ability of the meat proteins to dissolve and afterwards act as 
emulsifiers. 
The muscle of fish is made up of three major components: proteins, lipids and connective tissue. 
The functionality and properties of the muscle vary by species and by the environment in which the 
fish live. 
Fish muscles consist of individual cells called muscle fibres. The muscle fibres are placed at a sharp 
angle to the skeleton, and the content of connective tissue in the muscle is only 2-3%. (Lars 
==PAGE:16==
Side 15 af 73 
 
Christensen, 2006). The fibres contain a varying number of long cylindrical myofibrillar (50-200 
centimetres in length) which is composed of W-shaped segments called myotomes. These 
myotomes are separated into dorsal myotomes and ventral myotomes by the horizontal septum. The 
myofibrillar are built up of proteins collected in myofilaments which will be further described 
below in section 3.2.5. protein. The myofibrillar lies parallel to the skeleton and are bound together 
with the skeleton with connective tissue (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010) se Figure 2 for the muscle structure.  
 
Figure 2 – muscle structure  
The fish muscles contain approximately 75% water (Lars Christensen, 2006), and most of the water 
is bound to the myofibrillar due to the capillary forces. The pH and the ionic strength determine the 
distance between the individual filaments, therefore if the pH or ionic strength is changed, it is 
possible change the volume of the myofibrillar and thereby the water binding capacity (Lars 
Christensen, 2006) and (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
 
The volume of the Myofibrillar changes when the pH changes. If the pH is either close to or far 
from the myosin and actin's isoelectric pH (pI) the Myofibrillar volume change, the pI is around 5. 
The change in volume is due to repulsion of ions with the same charge. At pI the charge on the 
proteins of the myofilaments is zero and this causes the volume of the Myofibrillar to be as small as 
possible because there is no rejection between the Myofibrillar. If the pH changes and becomes 
different from pI there are either negative or positive changes in the bonds in the myofilaments and 
the myofilaments will therefore repel each other and the volume will rise (Lars Christensen, 2006) 
and (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). By adding NaCl to fish it is possible to improve the water binding 
ability, which is described below in section 3.4.2. salt (Lars Christensen, 2006).

3.2.3 
Water in the fish 
The amount of water is a relevant factor with regard to different quality characteristics of the 
salting, such as appearance, texture and juiciness.  
The water in the muscle of a living fish mainly consists of free water, found mainly within the 
Myofibrillar. This water is held between thick and thin filaments and among the myosin molecules. 
The total water in the muscle is tightly bound to the muscle proteins and not influenced by changes 
in the structure and charges of proteins (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). After the fish is dead the 
distribution of water in the muscle is influenced by the biological conditions, but can also be 
influenced by how the fish is handled in terms of catching- and storage methods. The rigor mortis 
change the distribution of water within the muscle because of the swelling and shrinking of the 
myofibril.  
In pre-rigor muscle, the actin and myosin filaments crosslinking is prevented by high ATP 
concentration within the muscle. After death, this non-existing crosslinking between actin and 
myosin leads to a shrinking of the muscle. The rigor state of the muscle affects the mass transfer 
and weight changes during salting (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). The ATP driven ions that pumps in pre-
rigor will work against salt uptake and in rigor the contraction in the muscle is believed to reduce 
brine uptake (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
3.2.4 
Conditions of water 
There are three different terms used for the conditions of water in the fish muscle. The water can be 
divided into groups according to its mobility and how tight a bound the water molecules have with 
the muscle structure (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010).  The three different types are the constitutional water 
which represent less than 1%, the interfacial water that represent between 5-15% and the 
bulk/immobilized water which represent between 75-95%.  
The constitutional water is bound directly to protein molecules by hydrogen bonds. It is only a 
small amount there is constitutional water and that water is not available for chemical reaction.  
The interfacial water is bound to the surface of the protein by either hydrogen bonds or dipole 
interaction and hydration shells are formed around the protein molecules.  
The bulk/immobilized water is bound in the spatial arrangement formed by the myofibril proteins. It 
can only be removed by force and it is available for chemical reactions and acts as a solvent. The 
bulk water is of great importance as it is strongly affected by processing and storage conditions 
(Thorarinsdóttir, 2010).

3.2.5 
Protein in fish  
There are to main types of protein; water soluble proteins and membrane proteins. The two types of 
proteins have different properties due to the very different environments in which they are found.  
Water-soluble proteins are found throughout the cells and in the extracellular fluid. This type of 
protein is made up of amino acids with hydrophilic side chains. This protein does not denature when 
the fish is in salt and acid brine, because the hydrophobic amino acids are often 'hidden away' inside 
the protein's spatial structure. These hydrophobic interactions contribute to stabilizing the spatial 
structure of the proteins (Duenn & Dyrn, 1952), (Snow, 2011) and (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010).  
 
There are two kinds of membrane proteins; the peripheral membrane proteins and the integrated 
membrane proteins. The integrated membrane proteins is located inside the membrane's lipid 
bilayer, a group of naturally occurring molecules that include fats, waxes, sterols, and fat-soluble 
vitamins. Peripheral membrane proteins are found in close contact with the membrane and are often 
bound to this via, for example, hydrophobic or electrostatic interactions. Many peripheral 
membrane proteins can be loosened from the membrane by changing e.g. pH, temperature or salt 
concentration, and therefore this kind of protein denature in the salt and acid brine, changing the 
structure of the fish (Duenn & Dyrn, 1952), (Snow, 2011) and (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
 
The protein in the fish muscle is divided into three different groups. The three divisions are based 
on the proteins solubility in aqueous solution. The fish muscle proteins are not very stable, and are 
therefore sensitive to denaturation by heating and freezing. The main influence on the muscle 
structure are the Myofibrillar proteins, the cytoskeletal system and the connective tissue (Sikorski, 
2007).  The changes in the Myofibrillar influence volumetric space for the liquid phase in the fish 
muscle and the structural modifications influence the liquid retention (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010).   
 
Myofibrillar proteins are the structural and functional components of muscle and make up around 
50-55% of the total muscle protein (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010).  The Myofibrillar proteins are 
responsible for the stabilisation of emulsions and of binding of water and lipids. As described in the 
section 3.2.2. on muscle structure, Myofibrillar proteins can change the water-holding capacity of 
the muscle both due to the water that is bound tightly by proteins through the hydration shell and 
due to the water that is bound loosely within the muscle structure. The Myofibrillar proteins 
constitute most of the fish microstructure (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
 
==PAGE:19==
Side 18 af 73 
 
It is the amino acid composition that decides the amount of water that is bound by protein, while the 
carboxyl and amino groups are responsible for the actual binding of the water. There is need for  
quite a high salt concentration (2-3% NaCl) to extract the proteins, if the myofibril proteins shall 
display the desired properties (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
 
The Myofibrillar protein in the muscle fibres consist primarily of myosin filaments (50-60%) and 
actin filaments (15-30%). In addition to myosin and actin, the myofilaments consist of a variety of 
other structural and regulatory proteins (Lars Christensen, 2006). Myosin is the main compound in 
the thick filaments and supports the overall structure of the muscle. Other proteins which support 
the overall structure are the C-protein and creatine kinase. Actin is the main compound in the thin 
filaments and troponin and tropomyosin are other major components of the thin filaments. The actin 
protein separates each thin filament (~35 nm), allowing the thick filaments to fit in between and 
interact, se Figure 2 (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
 
Myosin is the protein that leads to contraction of muscles. Myosin has been found to be the easiest 
protein fractions, present in fish muscle, to break down(Snow, 2011).  
To reach a faster penetration of NaCl into the fish muscle and simultaneous removal of water, the 
temperature needs to be as high as possible. Therefore, the critical concentration of sodium chloride 
has been studied. It has been found that at around 9% sodium chloride, the initial process of uptake 
of both salt and water changes to a process of water removal supplemented by continuation of salt 
uptake (Duenn & Dyrn, 1952). 
According to  (Duenn & Dyrn, 1952)  the intake of salt in the fish between 5-20% salt will reach an 
equilibrium in around 45 hours, except at 25%, which will reach equilibrium in around 36 hours, as

3.3 Marination of Herrings 
3.3.1 
Marinating Principles  
In the industry the marination of the herring is often divided in two parts. In the first part, the 
herrings are in a relatively strong acid and salt brine, for example 7% acid and 14% salt (Pirie, 
1971), while in the second part, the herrings are moved to an brine with a low concentration of acid 
and salt;1.5-2% acid and 2.4% salt concentration, and it is to this brine that flavor is added. The 
second part will not be considered in this project. This project have a pre-salt concentration that is 
not used in all productions (26%). 
Marinated herrings are in this trial herring fillets preserved first in a pre-salt brine and thereafter in a 
brine with both acid and salt for 35 days, see Figure 4. This type of cold marinade is called a semi-
preservation, which means that the fish are not sterile and they have a limited shelf life (Pirie, 
1971). The acid and the salt are added in a sufficient concentration for the brine to end up with a pH 
around 4-5 or below, this reduce or prevent growing of all food poisoning bacteria and retard the 
action with enzymes (Pirie, 1971). If the pH is around 4-5 and the herrings are kept at 4°C, the shelf 
life will be several months (Torry Research Station, 2001). But even with the pH at 4-5, some 
bacteria and enzymes will remain in the storage even though there acid and salt is present and  if the 
herrings are left in the brine for too long, the flesh will begin to break down. The marination makes 
==PAGE:21==

the flesh of the herring white and the texture firmer. The firmness is dependent on how much salt 
has been added in the brine - the more salt that is added to the brine, the more firm the flesh will 
become (Torry Research Station, 2001). The biggest wish for the marinating companies is to make 
a product that is in demand and treasured by the consumers, therefore it is important to make a 
product that has the desired taste and structure, without being too tough, and that the product is safe 
to eat.

3.3.2 
Why marinate 
The pre-salt brine is used to alter the chemical structure of the proteins by changing their shape, by 
breaking some of the protein bonds, removing water and add salt to the herring. The salt denatures 
the fish proteins, causing the proteins to unwind and form a matrix that traps the water (J.M. Barat, 
S. Rodriguez-Barona, 1922a). These bonds are sensitive to changes, causing the proteins themselves 
to break down a bit in brines and allowing the salt or acid to permeate the flesh of the fish. The acid 
breaks down the texture of the fish and gives it a more firm and hard structure (J.M. Barat, S. 
Rodriguez-Barona, 1922a).  
3.3.3 
Food safety  
Due to problems with anisakis larvae in herring, German fish regulation requires a concentration in 
the water phase of the herring to be at least 6 % salt and 2.4 % acid and the herring to be in the 
brine for at least 35 days (Gesundheit, 1988).  
In Denmark this regulation is also followed, but there is a difference in how to reach it. In the 
traditional Danish procedure the herring are put in a pre-salt brine of 10-18% of salt for about 24 
hours, before being put in an acid/salt brine (Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995). This acid/salt 
brine has a lower salt concentration (5%) than they have in Germany (14%), which is why Danish 
producers put the herring in a pre-salt brine (Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995).   
 
3.4 The effect of pre-salt brine on Herrings 
The salt and acid are transported in and out of the fish by mass transfer. The mass transfer can be 
categorised in three different types: diffusion, convention and osmosis, and all of these are 
described further down in section 3.4.1 Water. The mechanism is dependent on the driving forces of 
the chemical fluxes. The type of transfer mechanism is influenced by different factors like the 
salting method, condition of the muscle, type of proteins, and the muscle structure. There are a lot 
of different compounds that are transferred during salting, including water, salt, and proteins. The 
salt denaturation and aggregation of the proteins will influence the structural changes and this 
change happens in the intra- and extracellular matrix and will lead to a change in the chemical 
fluxes in the fish muscle. The transfer of different components in the muscle are therefore 
interdependent of each other (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010).

3.4.2 
Salt 
It is generally accepted that salt migration by diffusion plays an important role in salting of fish 
(Sveinung et al., 2005). This is because the salt concentration in the pre-brine is higher than in the 
fish the salt and will diffuse into the fish which is why the fish gain in weight. This is because when 
the salt is dissolved in the water, the water/salt solution increases in density (weight per volume). 
This occurs as there is space between the water molecules to interact with the dissolved salt. The 
amount of space depends on the solute interaction with water molecules – the  more compact, the 
heavier the solution will be (NPL, 2017). The diffusion of salt is dependent on different factors like 
fish species, muscle orientation, lipid content etc. Protein precipitation and solubilisation are time 
==PAGE:25==
Side 24 af 73 
 
and NaCl-concentration dependent and can be related to weight and texture changes in the product 
throughout the salting process (J.M. Barat, S. Rodriguez-Barona, 1922a). 
 
When the fish are put into the acid brine, diffusion will occur and the weight of the fish will drop as 
the water and salt concentration is lower in the brine than in the fish, but the acid is lower in the fish 
than in the brine and will therefore diffuse into the fish. The weight drops because the acid solution 
is not as compact as the salt solution. The fish needs to be in a pre-salt brine before they are put in 
an acid and salt brine to cook, as when the water flows out of the cells and salt flows into the cell, 
the salt dissolves some of the fibre proteins. The fish cell fluids become more concentrated and 
when the fish is cooked in the acid brine and water leaves the cells again, there is still water bound 
in the cells from the water that was added before the acid brine (J.M. Barat, S. Rodriguez-Barona, 
1922a) and (Thorarinsdóttir, 2010). 
 
The salt changes the character and the quality of the fish, but a little salt (1-5%) is not enough to 
change the structure of the muscle. Water is the major component of the muscle, and the 
interactions between water and molecules (like salt) determine the water-holding capacity (Offer et 
al., 1989). As long as the stationary state of the fish is thermodynamically stable, the fish does not 
change in structure. The ability of the salt to change the structure of the fish depends on the 
structure of the specific fish and the parametric structure of the enzymes which vary due to genetics. 
These enzymes have differential flexible responses towards their particular cell, and can therefore  
have quite different influences on the cell and the tissue depending on the current salt concentration 
(Behnke, 1995). 
 
NaCl can increase the water's binding ability as the chloride ions in NaCl bind to the positive ions 
in the myofilaments and thereby lower the pI and increases the rejection of the filaments. By adding 
NaCl the improvement in water binding ability can be increased with increasing concentrations of 
NaCl. The proteins in the Myofibrillar are insoluble in water and by adding NaCl, the ionic strength 
is increased and it becomes possible to dissolve the proteins (Lars Christensen, 2006).  
 
There are several effects of NaCl addition, as the intracellular proteins represent the salt and water 
soluble proteins. NaCl is added for the purpose of increasing solubility and to give extra viability to 
the salt-soluble proteins. The more salt is added, the lower is the pH required for the fish to lose 
==PAGE:26==
Side 25 af 73 
 
water, and also Na+ plays a role in solubility and extraction depending on the pH.  The lowest 
extraction is seen around the isoelectric point (pH 5.0-5.5). Protein extraction is time-dependent and 
freezing and frost storage reduces the availability of salt soluble proteins. A small mnemonic is that 
the fish can bind 0.3 times its own weight of water when salt is added and adding additional 
phosphate can bind 0.5 times its own weight (Koch, 2015). 
3.4.3 
Acid 
The acid in the brine produces the tenderness due to the proteolysis induced by tissue enzymes. 
Proteolysis is the breakdown of proteins into smaller polypeptides or amino acids (Borgstrom, 
1965). The acid is acting as a hydrolytic, molecule or an ion which can deliver one or more 
hydrogen ions to reactions. Acid anhydrides are converted to the corresponding acids, from protein 
to amino acid by hydrolysis (Borgstrom, 1965). 
 
Acid as a preservation method for fish is one of the earliest food-preservation techniques known 
(Idaho, 2007). This method is known all over the world, and fish that are preserved in an acid brine 
may not need to be cooked. If the fish is not properly prepared, the product could potentially be 
dangerous for the consumers to consume. To ensure a high level of food safety, acid is used because 
it is extremely inhospitable to spoilage, unwanted bacteria, and pathogenic organisms.  A marinated 
product above all needs to be safe for consumption, and therefore enough acid is used to prevent the 
growth of the food pathogen Clostridum Botulinum (Idaho, 2007) (for more information about 
Clostridum botulinum see section 3.7Bacteria in herrings). It is important to never have less acid 
than water to insure safety, as the acid gives the fish flavour, develops the desired texture, and 
softens the bones, because the acid helps to partially denature the fish proteins, it is opening up 
"tunnels" in the fish structure so the flavour can get in the fish meat. The acid brine works well on 
fish because the muscle structure is not as dense as beef ore pork (The accidental scientist, 2017). 
The acid concentration must be so high that C. Botulinum and other bacteria will be prevented or 
inhibited, and therefore the pH is desired to be below 3.5, as then food-poisoning bacteria will be 
prevented. This acid level is reached when the brine contains 5 %  or more parts of acid to one part 
water (Idaho, 2007). 
The amount of acid used determines the degree of preservation, when the proportion of fish in 
relation to liquid is increased, the amount of acid absorbed by the fish is reduced. A high ratio of 
fish to liquid is desired to get the highest preservation (Torry Research Station, 2001). 
 
==PAGE:27==
Side 26 af 73 
 
The diffusion of acid occurs more rapidly than NaCl (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984). If there is only 
acid in the brine the pH of the muscle will be lower than if there is salt in the brine. The low pH is 
on the acid side of the isoelectric point, and this causes an open/soft texture which allows the water 
holding to increase and the firmness to decrease. 
3.4.4 
Salt and Acid 
The best way to obtain a accepted and uniform quality is to use a pre-salt brine before an acid and 
salt brine (Idaho, 2007). If the fish is put in a pre-salt brine first, unwanted bacteria will be slowed 
down and the growth of other bacteria will be inhibited. The pre-salt brine reduces the water content 
of the fish, as described above in section 3.4.1. Water. It firms the fish muscle protein for a good 
texture of the final product, and it reduces the activity of some of the enzymes in the fish which can 
cause the proteins to break down during storage. The acid and salt brine do not preserve the fish 
indefinitely, they only slow down the spoilage and softening caused by enzymatic action (Idaho, 
2007).  Acid and salt are necessary for a firm texture because of the anions and cations on the 
muscle texture. As mentioned, if only acid is added the firmness will decrease and when salt is 
added the water holding decreases and the firmness increases (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984).

3.6 Oil 
The oil effect on the salt absorption, is in the case of fatty fish species, the fat content influences the 
rate of salting, associated with the fat content which represents a barrier to transport of water and 
NaCl (Gallart-Jornet et al., 2006). It can be seen in different studies like Gallart-Jornet et al., 2006 
that it needs to be taken into account if it is a lean or a fatty fish, because there is a difference in 
water, salt and total weight change depending on the fish. The fat in fish has been investigated and 
has been found to be a limiting factor for salt uptake during salting. The fat has an impact as it 
replaces the aqueous phase that serves as a vector for transfers or acts as a physical barrier (G. 
Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984). 
Fat imposes a finite series of resistance to the flow of water, fat displaces water but not non-aqueous 
material. Diffusion of a composite membrane is equal to the diffusion of the component layers (A. 
C. Jason, 1965).

3.7 Bacteria in herrings 
Fish can contain parasites that can survive even if they are consumed by humans. Parasites can 
penetrate the human gut and the abdominal cavity causing peritonitis. 
Fish may also contain pathogens such as Listeria monocytogenes and Salmonella spp. Both 
salmonella and L. monocytogenes are common bacterias causing food poison. Humans can become 
infected through food contaminated with the bacteria (Fødevarestyrelsen, 2005), but salmonella, 
Listeria and parasites are not a problem due to the salt and acid content in the brine. 
 
Botulinum coming from infection by Clostridum botulinum is a rare bacteria, but it causes is a 
serious disease and is an important concern in the preservation processes. By some simple rules, 
like more acid than water in the brine, it can be ensured that the fish is safe for human consumption 
(Idaho, 2007). 
Fish may also contain spoilage bacteria, these bacteria are not harmful to umans, but may mean that 
a food is unfit to eat and will smell and taste undesirable.  
Some microorganisms and enzymes are still active despite the high salt content and chilled storage 
Conditions, and can spoil the product  after some time (Pedro, 2004).

A short introduction and overview for the trial and the method are described first.   
6.1 Method and materials introduction 
52 double-sided herrings are prepared for pre-salting for each of the seven batches. Before pre-
salting all the double-sided herrings are cut through the middle to one-sided herrings. The filet is 
then tagged, weighed and placed in the pre-salting brine. From the other side of the herring, a cut is 
taken to test for water content and salt concentration. 
After the pre-salting and every time there is a sampling in the acid brine, 10 herrings are weighed 
and put back in a bucket with acid brine, and these are the same 10 herrings that are weighed every 
time a sample is taken out. Simultaneously with the 10 herrings, 3 herrings are taken out and 
weighed. A piece of them is cut off for testing of texture, water content, acid concentration and salt

8 Discussion  
To start the discussion, it is important to remember that every fish is unique, the variation in the 
initial water content, oil content and biological variation can influence the uptake of acid and salt. 
 
8.1 Weight change  
Sveinung et al., 2005 says that there was a big difference seen in weight gain of the herring between 
the brines that had 16.5% and 25.5% and similar results have been published in Baines, 1969. 
Sveinung et al., 2005 concluded that if high weight gain of herrings is the aim, the brine 
concentration needs to be below 25%, and the theory supports as well as the findings in this report. 
The protein denaturation increases in a high salt brine compared with a low salt brine, which causes 
less weight gain and can promote water loss in the herrings. This matches this trial’s pre-salting 
where values in Table 12 shows that there is less weight gain in the herrings that have been in the 
26% pre-salt brine compared with the 13.5% and 16% salt. The 13.5% and 16% pre-salt brines look 
a lot like each other, and it is therefore assumed that there is no considerable difference between 
these two. The difference in the weight gain during brining is most likely due to the oil content 
which affects the diffusion of salt and loss of water.  
The weight quickly reaches a study phase in this trial, which is due to the brine-to-fish ratio (1:1). 
This means that because there is not more brine than fish, the herring has a limit of how much the it 
can gain in weight.  
Thorarinsdóttir, 2010 established that it is important to keep in mind that the weight changes that 
are obtained in different parts of the process. That means that higher weight yields after the pre-
salting brine often leads to a lower increase in the weight during the next step of the process.  This 
can be seen in this trial in Figure 9. Except (direct), all the other batches have changed space, the 
ones that have gained the most weight in the pre-salting have also lost more during the acid brine 
and thereby and up with a lower total increase in  weight. Thorarinsdóttir, 2010 has also seen the 
same results; the batches that have gained the most also are the ones with the lower increase in the 
final weight. The herrings that had the highest start weight also end up with the highest final weight, 
see Figure 8, so the final weight depends of the start weight, but the weight loss depends on the salt 
concentration the herrings are in.

8.2 Salt 
G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984 investigated how much time it will take for the salt and acid to 
penetrate the herring, by testing the herring at different temperatures and different distances from 
the skin surface. How fast the salt penetrates cannot be compared with this trial because of the pre-
salting. The salt equilibrium has already been reached in the pre-salting, and therefor is not 
investigated in this trial, but to see the result, see (Helle Hougaard Hein et al., 2016). The (G. 
Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984) study found that if there is either is no salt or acid only is present in the 
brine, then the herring does not harden. This is not the same as seen in this trial, where it can be 
seen in the (direct) that if there is only a 5% salt in the brine the herring will still not harden. The 
herring needs to be in a high pre-salt brine before being put in the acid and salt brine or the salt 
percentage needs to be much higher than 5% in the acid and salt brine. This supports the theory that 
if only acid (or low salt) is present, the muscles’ pH is on the acidic side of the isoelectric point and 
causes an “open” structure, which increases the water holding and decreases the firmness. If salt is 
put into the brine these repulsive charges are shielded from one another, and this gives a tighter 
structure, causing decreased water holding and increased firmness (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984).  
 
(Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995) have tested the salt concentration in the fish and the brine in a 
concentration with 10% pre-salt brine and 10% salt in the acid brine. These results cannot be 
compared directly with these trial salt concentrations. (Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995) display 
that the salt in the brine ends up after 35 days to be lower in concentration in the fish, around 1.5%, 
and this can be compared with this trial. All batches in Figure 13 to Figure 19 shows that even 
though they have been in a higher pre-salt brine than used in the (Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 
1995), the equilibrium ends up with the fish salt concentration being a bit higher than the brine; 
around 1%. Even though the article has the herrings in a lower pre-salt concentration and a higher 
salt concentration in the acid and salt brine than in this trial, the article ends up with a salt 
concentration after 35 days of 7.2%. If (direct) is not taken into account all the batches in this trial 
have a 7.0% salt concentration. Therefore if the herrings are in high pre-salt brine and low salt in 
the acid brine, they will end with almost the same salt content in the water phase. 
(Pirie, 1971) has a different result in how the salt concentration is in the brine and in the fish 
compared to this trial and the previous article. In the (Pirie, 1971) the salt concentration in the brine 
is lower in the fish, which is opposite to this trial result. (Pirie, 1971) has an end salt concentration 
of 5.05% in the herring flesh and not in the water phase. The flesh in this trial, except for (direct),

8.3 Acid 
The acid concentration in (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984) is the same as in this trial and can be 
therefore be directly compared. (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984) finds that at a temperature of 5 °C 
the acid equilibrium is reached in 50.8 hours. In this trial, most of the batches had reached an 
equilibrium in around 24 hours after they were put in the acid brine and (16%, 4d) and (direct) after 
72 hours, as seen in Figure 20 to Figure 22. This trial’s batches thus lie on either side of the results 
from (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984), which can be explained due to the variations in the herrings, 
as told earlier, and this result can be seen as comparable. 
 
(Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995) have tested the salt concentration in the fish and the brine in a 
concentration with a 5% acid in the brine and therefore these results cannot be compared directly 
with this trial’s acid concentrations. (Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995) find that the acid in the 
brine ends up, after 35 days, to be lower in concentration than in the fish, around 0.5% lower, and 
the article also states that the acid in the brine does not decrease over time, but stays constant and 
that can be compared with this trial. The acid in this trial is lower in the fish’s water phase than in 
the brine, which is the opposite of the article and there is a much higher acid difference between the 
brine and the fish, see Figure 20 to Figure 22. All the batches illustrate the same as the article; that 
the acid in the brine does not decrease much in concentration. (Horst Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995) 
has an acid concentration of 2.2% and this trial has 2.7% acid concentration, which are very similar 
concentrations.

8.4 Water content 
The water content in (G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984) at 7 % acid and 5 % salt, tells that after 5 days

35 days, the water content is between 66-75%, see Figure 26, which is almost the same as in the

(Batista ’, Pires, Bandarra, & Goncalves, 2001) have tested the water content for different salt 
percentages, example 13%, 17% and 25%, which can be compared to this trial, even though it has 
been tested on bigeye grunt and longfin bonefish. The article states that the water content is 13% 
salt to 72.1% water, 17% salt to 70.1% water and 20% salt to 71.2% water respectively in the 
bigeye grunt, which  is a lean fish. In this trial, 13.5% salt has a 69.89% water content, 16% salt has 
a 69.52% water content and 26% salt has a 67.12% water content. These results are very close to 
each other and it could be the genetics that makes the difference, as the bigeye grunt has a high 
protein content (Batista ’ et al., 2001).

(G. Rodger, R. Hastings, 1984) has also tested texture and moisture (water) content in herrings in

toughness and in this trial the texture is measured by the springiness, in Figure 28, so these two 
cannot be compared directly because the toughness is the peak force during the first compression. 
The springiness is, as mentioned in method and materials, how well a product physically springs 
back after it has been deformed.

8.6 Oil 
In none of the batches a connection between the oil in the herring and the salt and acid uptake is

they all have been caught during the winter and therefore do not vary very much. (Horst Karl, Allan

summer. In this article it is easy to see that there is a difference in the herring in the summer, where 
they have more oil, compared to the winter where herrings that have less oil. The summer herring 
have a higher salt and acid content than the herrings from the winter. In this article, it is clear to see 
that the oil content has an influence on the salt and acid uptake.

9 Conclusion 
This study aimed to investigate whether the salt concentration in the pre-salting would affect the 
different quality parameters at the end of the marinating procedure in herring.  
 
It can be concluded that the acid concentration is the only parameter that does not express any 
significant (p<0.05) difference. For the parameters salt concentration, texture (springiness), water 
content and weight there are more than one batch that is significantly (p<0.05) different.  The 
differences in these parameters is considered to be the oil content in the herrings, but in all the 
batches it was easy to see that there is no connection between salt concentration, texture, water 
content and weight. 
 
As it can be seen in the salt comparison between the brine and the herring water phase, that the salt 
almost ends up in the same salt concentration as in the beginning. The salt in the water phase in the 
herring is only slightly higher than in the brine in all the batches. Only in (direct) the brine and the 
fish do not reach equilibrium, here the herring takes up a lot of the salt from the brine and the salt in 
the herrings’ water phase ends with more than 0.5 % difference. All the batches illustrate that the 
acid in the brine slowly decreases when the acid in the herring increases. It can be seen in the 
results, that the concentration in acid and salt for both brine and fish quickly decreases to the 
equilibrium and stable concentration.  
The batch in 26 % pre-salt brine has the highest average of the ranking, the batches in 16% pre-salt 
brine have the middle average and the batches in the 13.5% pre-salt brine have the lowest average if  
all parameters are compared. This means that 13.5% has the highest values in most parameters. 
(direct) has the lowest values of the different parameters, except water. This means that 13.5% has 
the highest values in the most of the parameters. 
 
It can be concluded that all the batches have a high enough salt and acid content to be safe to eat 
and avoid unwanted anisakis larvae and bacteria. The texture for all the batches, except (direct), has 
a firm structure even though there is a significant difference between the batches.  
From a financial viewpoint, thus, this thesis advices Skagerak pelagic to pursue the 13.5% pre-salt 
brine for the herrings. This brine produces the lowest water and weight change, which means that 
these herring will keep the highest weight, and therefore comparatively less herring are needed to 
produce 100g product.

10  Further perspectives 
There are several different possible further studies that could be done related to this project, such as 
an investigation of the effects of different salt concentrations on the acid brine, whether the pre-salt 
brine can be completely avoided if there is enough salt in the acid brine, and whether the same 
quality can be achieved by doing this. The connective tissue is of great interest with regard to the 
salt and should be tested in a new study in relation to different salt concentrations. 
The next question to be asked is how fast the salt penetrates the herring. This trial tested the whole 
fish, but how fast does the salt reach the middle of the herring, see Picture 7. Further studies could 
also be done to determine when the herring will be safe to eat all the way through. 
 
Picture 7 – herring after 6 hours 
Another area to be tested could be to see how much difference there is on the salt and acid uptake in 
the herring in the winter compared to the summer. The theory states that there is a difference (Horst 
Karl, Allan Roepstorff, 1995), but how much and are the salt and acid uptake sped up when there is 
more oil or when there is less oil? 
How much protein penetrates out of the herring into the brine and how will different treatments 
affect the herrings’ muscle and the protein?
