Abstract
The building sector accounts for a substantial portion of global energy consumption and
greenhouse gas emissions. In response to this situation, the European Union has placed
an emphasis on building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) as a means of decarbonizing
the building stock. However, accurately predicting the power output of BIPV systems is
challenging due to their unique operating conditions. This thesis evaluates the applica-
bility of conventional photovoltaic performance models for BIPV curtain wall systems by
combining outdoor and laboratory measurements to develop and validate these models.
The study begins with an analysis of transposition models used to estimate plane-of-array
irradiance for different orientations. It reveals the significance of accurately determining
building azimuth for precise irradiance estimation. The impact of albedo on BIPV perfor-
mance is also investigated. Among the transposition models examined, the Perez and
Hay models perform well, with the Hay model being selected for subsequent simulations.
The study further evaluates point value models for predicting maximum power point. The
optical losses due to coloration and their impact on module temperature are explored.
It is observed that while coloration introduces additional angular losses, the effect on
overall power output is minimal. Additionally, the temporal evolution of the solar radiation
spectrum throughout the day affects the estimation of effective irradiance, particularly for
colored modules.
The power models evaluated in this study exhibit consistent biases, but these biases tend
to compensate for each other, resulting in near-zero overall bias. However, the models
consistently overestimate power on clear sky days, which can be addressed by refining
the temperature models.
The findings of this thesis provide valuable insights for future BIPV developers and re-
searchers, serving as a reference for model selection and inspiring improvements in the
accuracy of performance models, resulting in reduced financing costs and a higher suc-
cess rate in project implementation.

Introduction
The operation of buildings has a significant impact on global energy consumption and con-
tributes to a substantial portion of greenhouse gas emissions worldwide. According to the
International Energy Agency (IEA), buildings account for approximately 30% of the world’s
final energy consumption and contribute to 27% of global energy-related CO2 emissions
due to their reliance on fossil fuels and inefficient energy usage [1]. The building sector is
not on track to reach net zero emissions by 2050. Specifically, significant advancements
in building envelope performance are crucial to driving the necessary change.
In the European context, the Commission already published a directive in 2010 [2] requir-
ing Member States to develop long-term renovation strategies aiming to facilitate the cost-
effective transformation of existing buildings into Nearly zero-emission buildings (NZEBs).
These are buildings with very high energy performance, and the minimal amount of en-
ergy needed is covered by renewables produced on-site or nearby. Recognizing the need
for faster action, the EU has recently proposed to revise the directive calling for all new
buildings to be zero-emission buildings (ZEBs) from 2030 onwards [3].
As part of the solution, the EU Solar Energy Strategy [4] which is part of the REPowerEU
plan [5], highlights the value of solar energy to decarbonize the building stock. Among
the measures proposed under this initiative, it is stated that Member States will work
together to support an innovative form of solar deployment for both new buildings and
renovations: building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV). The novelty of BIPV products is that
they are designed to be a component of the building [6], which extends the opportunity to
install solar energy beyond rooftops and parking spaces. Additionally, not only it replaces
conventional building materials, but can help regulate the inside temperature, and is more
visually appealing than standard building-applied photovoltaics (BAPV).
The integration of photovoltaics into buildings offers a range of possibilities. The IEA has
identified 12 different types of BIPV systems, which can be categorized into three main
families: roof, façade, and external integrated devices [7]. Figure 1.1 showcases real
building examples that demonstrate the application of BIPV systems.

1.1
Project presentation
Anticipating the significant expansion of the BIPV market, there is a necessity for accurate
power and energy predictions to ensure the financial viability of projects. The challenge
lies in the fact that BIPV systems operate under different conditions compared to traditional
photovoltaic (PV) systems, which can make standard photovoltaic models unreliable.
This thesis investigates the power modeling of BIPV using a prototype located at DTU Risø
Campus (coordinates: 55.6958◦N and 12.1052◦E), which was established in April 2022
as part of the EUDP project UnitSun – PV integrated in prefabricated façade systems [8].
To model the power output of any photovoltaic system, it is crucial to consider various
factors that influence the system’s performance, such as solar irradiance, temperature,
and optical losses. Therefore, these submodels are also explored within the research.
By focusing on the performance modeling of BIPV systems, this project responds to the
United Nations’ urgent call for action and contributes to the achievement of four Sustain-
able Development Goals (SDGs), as illustrated in Figure 1.2.
Improved accuracy in BIPV performance models will have significant implications, lower-
ing financing costs and facilitating the construction of more BIPV projects. This, in turn,
helps to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by promoting clean energy generation and
reducing reliance on fossil fuels, directly relating to SDGs 7 and 13. Additionally, BIPV
enhances the energy efficiency of buildings which directly supports SDG 11. Finally, the
project aligns with SDG 9 by contributing to fostering technological innovation.

Thesis structure
The thesis is structured into five chapters and organized as follows.
After a preliminary introduction about building-integrated photovoltaics, chapter 1 ad-

In chapter 2, previous work and background in performance modeling are reviewed. This

Moving forward, chapter 4 describes the experimental setups, the data-filtering process,

wall systems, along with a thorough analysis, are presented in chapter 5.
Finally, the main conclusions of the work are summarized in chapter 6.

2
Background and Objective
To optimize energy performance in buildings with the use of BIPV, it is crucial to consider
multiple energy-related features. As a component of the building, a BIPV module’s thermal
insulation behavior or light transmittance, among other factors, can contribute significantly
to achieving the highest energy performance. A recent comprehensive review covering all
relevant aspects has been published [10]. However, the scope of this thesis specifically
focuses on one single aspect: the electrical performance of BIPV.
Performance models are used to analyze and forecast the energy or power output of pho-
tovoltaic systems. Extensive efforts have been dedicated to advancing the state-of-the-art
in PV performance prediction, aiming to enhance model accuracy and, subsequently, re-
duce financing costs while increasing the number of viable projects. A notable initiative
in this field is the PV Performance Modeling Collaborative (PVPMC), established by the
Sandia National Laboratories in 2010 [11]. The PVPMC has gained popularity by offer-
ing detailed online resources [12] and providing free collections of pre-built functions and
classes known as PVLIB [13]. Nevertheless, the available resources lack specific infor-
mation tailored to the unique characteristics of BIPV.
The electrical performance of BIPV can deviate from that of traditional PV systems due
to their operation under different conditions. One notable difference is the reduced back
ventilation in BIPV systems, leading to higher operating temperatures. Considerable re-
search and literature have been dedicated to the cell and module temperature modeling,
including specific studies focused on BIPV, such as the work by Lin et al. [14], Gonçalves
et al. [15] and Martin et al. [16]. Another study by the last mentioned author [10] provides
a concise overview of temperature models for BIPV and suggests that simplified analytical
models, such as the Sandia model [17], can provide acceptable results.
Apart from dealing with higher operating temperatures, the design of building-integrated
photovoltaic systems encounters a significant challenge due to their placement in com-
plex urban environments rather than open spaces. This urban setting adds complexity
to the modeling, as these systems are more susceptible to partial shading and receive
irradiance affected by nearby object reflections. Notably, it is important to mention that
shading modeling is yet not covered on the PVPMC website. To tackle these challenges,
various 3D software packages incorporating advanced light transposition models have
been developed, as discussed in a report by the IEA [18]. Additionally, alternative ap-
proaches, such as the one presented by Polo et al. [19], involve the combination of digital
surface models with artificial neural networks for accurate prediction of energy output.
Another key difference is that BIPV systems, particularly façade systems, are mounted
on extreme tilts and non-optimal orientations. This particular arrangement can result in
higher angular reflection losses, which are typically described using the Incidence Angle
Modifier (IAM) value. Martin has made significant contributions to the understanding
of IAM [20] [21] [22]. Several approaches exist for modeling and measuring IAM. A
study by Ebert [23] highlights the importance of acquiring a profound understanding and
conducting detailed characterization of modules with regard to their angular behavior, par-
ticularly when dealing with special orientations like vertically mounted BIPV. Additionally,
the study evaluates three distinct methods, two conducted outdoors and one indoors, and
the findings indicate that the choice of measurement method has minimal impact on the

IAM. However, it should be noted that measurements at extreme angles, such as 85◦,
have been reported as unreliable in another study [24].
Related to the previous point, there is a specific issue concerning vertically mounted BIPV
systems. It has been reported in the literature that many transposition models face difficul-
ties when predicting solar irradiance in vertical planes [25]. Thus, validating transposition
models in different locations becomes even more crucial to assess their performance un-
der diverse climate conditions and verify their accuracy for vertical systems. However,
a recent study reported a lack of validation studies specifically focused on transposition
modeling in the Nordics [26].
In addition to the differences mentioned earlier between BIPV and standard PV systems,
it is worth noting that the incorporation of color in some BIPV installations is gaining mo-
mentum as a means to enhance aesthetic appeal. Several coloration techniques have
been developed by private companies and research institutions, as reviewed in another
report from the IEA [27]. Although the introduction of color in BIPV presents challenges
such as higher transmission and reflection losses, it is promising to see that some proto-
types integrating coloration are slowly making their way into the market. As an example,
the Copenhagen International School in Nordhavn showcases a notable installation of col-
ored BIPV façades. This project has an installed power capacity of 720 kWp and covers
an area of 6,000 square meters with around 12,000 solar panels. Despite this progress,
there is a scarcity of studies investigating the combined effect of IAM and coloration,
with only a few investigations conducted at DTU focusing on coloration applied to the
front glass covers [28] [29] [30].
Since BIPV systems are still in the early stages of development with limited commercial
installations, the study by Pillai et al. [31] highlights the importance of conducting applied
evaluation of small or medium-scale outdoor test-beds. It is mentioned that such evalu-
ations can help future BIPV designers gain a deeper understanding of the performance,
reliability, and operational challenges that arise with BIPV systems.
Considering all mentioned above, this thesis aims to investigate the existing gaps in the
literature by utilizing a combination of laboratory characterization data with outdoor mea-
surements. The objective is to assess the suitability of typical PV performance models
obtained from PVPMC for BIPV (curtain walls) systems. To achieve this, the research
employs an initial indoor IAM characterization of colored coupon-sized samples. The
results from this characterization are subsequently combined with outdoor data acquired
from the weather station, albedometer, and BIPV curtain wall setup, all located at DTU
Risø Campus. The motivation behind this study is to identify any limitations or inaccura-
cies in existing models, which can aid in their improvement or the development of new
models specifically for BIPV (curtain walls) systems.

3
Theoretical framework
The PVPMC approach focuses on tracking the journey of solar energy from the Sun to a
PV array and its conversion into electrical energy. The standard sequence of PV perfor-
mance modeling steps is the following [11]:
1. Define PV system design parameters
2. Choose irradiance and weather data
3. Translate irradiance data to the plane of the array
4. Estimate optical losses from shading, soiling, and reflections on the surface of the
array or module
5. Estimate effective irradiance
6. Estimate the cell temperature
7. Estimate the current and voltage characteristics of the PV module
8. Estimate the DC wiring and mismatch losses
9. Estimate the DC to AC conversion losses
10. Estimate the AC wiring and transformer losses.
The aim is to determine the amount of energy that is effectively converted into usable
electrical energy. In this thesis, the analysis of the power was conducted before the in-
verter, at a module level. Therefore, steps 8, 9, and 10 were left out of the scope and not
further developed.
Several models exist to conduct the above-mentioned estimations. After a brief introduc-
tion to the irradiance data in section 3.1, the models used in this thesis will be developed:
section 3.2 provides models to translate irradiance to the plane of the array, section 3.3
to estimate optical losses from reflections, section 3.4 to estimate effective irradiance,
section 3.5 to estimate the cell temperature, and section 3.6 to estimate the current and
voltage characteristics of the PV module.
In order to ensure coherence and consistency, the notation of certain models has been
adjusted. The purpose is to use the same notation for all, and the notation tries to be the
most similar possible as on the PVPMC website. This standardization of notation aims to
make it easier to understand and compare the different models discussed.
3.1
Irradiance data
The Sun continuously emits photons through nuclear fusion, and a portion of these pho-
tons reaches the Earth. Some photons directly reach the Earth’s surface, referred to as
direct normal irradiance or DNI. However, another portion of the sunlight is either ab-
sorbed or scattered in the atmosphere.
When photons are absorbed by the atmosphere, their energy is converted into thermal
energy, leading to an energy loss from a PV designer’s perspective. These absorbed
photons do not reach the surface for utilization by photovoltaic systems, resulting in a
reduced amount of available energy for conversion into electricity.

On the other hand, scattering occurs when photons change their direction due to collisions
with atmospheric particles. During daylight hours, a significant amount of scattering takes
place across the entire hemispherical sky, giving it a blue or white hue. This scattering
phenomenon contributes to the presence of diffuse light coming from all directions in the
sky.
In addition to general scattering, two other scattering events can be observed, particularly
on clear sky days. The first is circumsolar brightening, which refers to intense scattering
around the direct path of sunlight. The second is horizon brightening, where enhanced
scattering occurs near the horizon. These effects create distinct lighting patterns and
contribute to the overall distribution of light in the atmosphere.
The scattered light that reaches a surface horizontal to the surface of the Earth is known
as diffuse horizontal irradiance or DHI. And the total irradiance falling on a horizontal
surface, known as global horizontal irradiance or GHI, can be calculated using Equa-
tion 3.1,
GHI = DNI × cos(θz) + DHI
(3.1)
where θz is the solar zenith angle, which is the one between the Sun’s rays and the normal
to the Earth’s surface at a specific location and time.
All three, GHI, DNI, and DHI, can be directly measured, derived from measured data,
or simulated using a stochastic model.
3.2
Plane of array irradiance
The GHI, DNI, and DHI can be utilized to estimate the amount of solar radiation reach-
ing any given tilted surface. In this case, the surface refers to a photovoltaic (PV) module
or array, which is essentially a linked collection of modules. This is why the term ”plane
of array” (POA) irradiance is used to describe it.
When the surface is not horizontally mounted but tilted, an extra component needs to
be considered apart from the direct and diffuse irradiance previously mentioned in sec-
tion 3.1. This component is the light that will be reflected from the ground back to the tilted
surface. Although it falls under the category of diffuse radiation, it is distinguished from
the scattering caused by atmospheric particles. All mentioned contributions are illustrated
in Figure 3.1.
The irradiance reaching the plane of array (EPOA) can be divided into three main com-
ponents: the beam component (Eb), the ground-reflected (Eg), and the diffuse (Ed), as
shown in Equation 3.2.
EPOA = Eb + Eg + Ed
(3.2)
The following sections explore how to estimate each component in Equation 3.2. The
estimation of the diffuse contribution is particularly challenging and subject to various
models and approaches.

Beam component

Eb = DNI × cos(AOI)
(3.3)
The angle of incidence is the one between the Sun’s rays and the normal to the PV array.
It can be determined as:
AOI = cos−1[cos (θz) cos (θT ) + sin (θz) sin (θT ) cos (θA −θA,array)]

measured with respect to true north, where the convention in this context is clockwise
from due north. Specifically, east corresponds to 90◦, south to 180◦, and west to 270◦.
3.2.2
Ground-reflected component
The ground-reflected POA irradiance can be calculated as a function of the irradiance on
the ground, the fraction of sunlight that is diffusely reflected by the ground surface, known
as albedo, and the tilt angle of the array:
Eg = GHI × albedo × 1 −cos(θT )

When the solar array is positioned horizontally, it does not receive any reflected light,
resulting in a zero contribution from this component. Conversely, the equation suggests
that mounting the array vertically allows for the maximum collection of reflected light.

3.3
Optical losses from reflections
When the solar radiation does not fall on the PV surface at a perpendicular angle, indicated
by an AOI greater than zero, an increased portion of the incident light is reflected.
The PV modules are typically flashed or characterized by the manufacturers, and this
process involves measuring the current-voltage (IV) curve. This flashing process involves
subjecting the modules to controlled light and temperature conditions to determine key pa-
rameters such as maximum power output, open-circuit voltage, short-circuit current, and
temperature coefficients. Typically, this flashing is performed with perpendicular light,
and the reflected light is inherently accounted for and considered in the extracted param-
eters. The problem is that in reality the sunlight rarely strikes the modules perpendicularly.
Therefore, the increased reflections need to be quantified.

3.4
Effective irradiance
The effective irradiance (Ee) corresponds to the portion of irradiance that successfully
reaches the solar cell, taking into account multiple sources of loss. These losses encom-
pass not only reflections, discussed in section 3.3, but also shading, soiling, and other
effects.
The current approaches widely employed to model effective irradiance tend to overlook
the impact of solar spectral composition and the angle of incidence on the solar radia-
tion [17]. Two reported models that address this particular issue are discussed below.
3.4.1
Sandia: Detailed Laboratory Approach
The Sandia model, proposed by King et al. [17], requires the quantification of various
parameters and coefficients through laboratory measurements. It can be expressed using
the following formulation:
Ee = f1(AMa) × [IAM(AOI)Eb + fd(Eg + Ed)] × SF

The diffuse component and the ground-reflected component of sunlight reach the solar

direction. Consequently, only a small portion of the diffuse and ground-reflected compo-

due to angular reflections, which can be challenging to accurately quantify. This model
ignores these losses for the Eg and Ed components. However, it is worth noting that these
losses are more pronounced for the Eb component.

3.5
Cell temperature
The calculation of the IV curve or power output of a module requires the measurement of
the cell temperature inside the module. This is because solar cells, being semiconductor
devices, are sensitive to temperature. As the temperature increases, the bandgap nar-
rows, allowing a slightly larger number of photons to generate electron-hole pairs. The
open-circuit voltage is significantly influenced by temperature, decreasing as the temper-
ature rises, while the short-circuit current experiences only a slight increase.
Although the cell temperature itself is not directly measured, it can be estimated using
other temperature measurements, such as the ambient temperature or the temperature
at the back of the module. In this context, two models are presented below to estimate
the cell temperature.
3.5.1
Sandia
This model by Sandia [17] provides an estimation of the cell temperature using the back-
surface module temperature (Tm), the plane of array irradiance (EPOA), and a temper-
ature difference parameter (∆T). This parameter represents the temperature difference
between the module and cell temperature. The model is formulated as:
Tc = Tm + EPOA

where E0 is the reference solar irradiance, typically 1000 W m−2. For flat-plate modules
with a thermally insulated back surface, ∆T is typically set to zero.
The model simplifies the heat conduction process within the module materials behind
the cell, such as encapsulant and polymer layers, by assuming one-dimensional thermal
conduction.
Sandia also provides an additional model to estimate the module temperature using the
ambient temperature. However, in this particular study, the module temperature is di-
rectly measured, so there is no need to use the Sandia model for module temperature
estimation.

sections, which directly influence these points on the IV curve.

the short-circuit current shows only slight increases.

The BIPV setup used in this thesis is a curtain wall system, specifically a façade system
falling under category D based on the standards IEC 63092-1 [41] and EN 50583-1 [42].
In this context, a curtain wall consists of panels with integrated photovoltaic modules sup-

stability of the building. Instead, the structural support comes from the primary structure of
the building. Therefore, the BIPV module is integrated into the building envelope, serving
as a protective and aesthetic layer that encloses the building’s exterior.
The system is a container with three PV arrays, integrated into the east, south, and west
sides (see Figure 4.6). The north façade only contains the entrance. Each façade of the

inverter to convert the DC power generated by the corresponding modules into usable AC
power. The system is situated in an isolated location, away from any significant nearby
objects that could cause shading. However, it is worth noting the minor self-shading de-
picted in Figure 4.6c.

before being installed. The techniques used were flash-test and electroluminescence (EL)
imaging.

4.5
Modelling and comparison
After preprocessing the data and storing it in four CSV files, as described in section 4.4,
the modeling and analysis were performed using Spyder (Python).
First, the plane of array irradiance was modeled for each façade: east (θA,array = 90◦),
south (θA,array = 180◦), and west (θA,array = 270◦). The following steps were executed for
each timestamp:
1. The beam component of the POA irradiance was modeled using Equation 3.3 and
Equation 3.4. For all cases, θT = 90◦was used since all modules are mounted
vertically. The DNI measured in the weather station was used as an input, and the
θz was calculated using pvlib/pvlib-python: v0.9.1 library [47].
2. The ground-reflected component of the POA irradiance was modeled using Equa-
tion 3.5 and the measured GHI. Regarding the albedo, both measured data (GHI↓
and GHI↑) and constant values were considered in the analysis.
3. The diffuse component of the POA irradiance was modeled using the four different
models discussed in subsection 3.2.3. The Isotropic, Sandia, and Hay models were
individually implemented by coding them from scratch, while the Perez was imple-
mented using pvlib to save time. Measured GHI, DHI, DNI were used in this
step, while the Ea was calculated using pvlib.
4. The total irradiance reaching the plane of the array was calculated by summing the
three components (beam, ground-reflected, and diffuse) that were modeled in the
previous steps.
5. The modeled POA irradiance was compared to the corresponding measured EPOA
using statistical indicators, as explained in subsection 4.5.1.
Once the above code was ready, a sensitivity analysis was performed to assess the impact
of albedo and container azimuth deviation on the modeled EPOA. Next, a comparison
among different transposition models was conducted.
Second, the power at the maximum point was modeled for each of the six modules facing
west (see Figure 4.6c). The following steps were followed:
1. The POA irradiance was estimated using the Hay model. This decision was based
on two factors: it was identified as the best model during the previous analysis,
and it is the model utilized by Martin and Ruiz in their proposed model for effective
irradiance (Equation 3.24).
2. The effective irradiance was estimated using two approaches while disregarding
any potential minor shading. The first approach was using the Martin and Ruiz
model (Equation 3.24), in which the ar parameter resulting from the fitting on the
lab-collected data was incorporated. The second approach employed the Sandia
model adapted with Brandemuehl and Beckman (subsection 3.4.3), incorporating
the five b parameters acquired as a result of fitting the data in the laboratory.

3. The cell temperature was modeled using two methods. The first used the measured

This chapter examines the analysis of the previously described models within the context

Section 5.1 investigates the transposition models and compares them with measured

Moving on, section 5.2 explores the IAM models and their comparison with laboratory

Section 5.3 delves into the discussion of colored modules’ temperature to understand their

5.1
Plane of array irradiance
5.1.1
Azimuth deviation
The BIPV system in this study was originally designed to have a rectangular shape with
façades facing south, north, east, and west. However, during the installation process,
there can be slight deviations from the initial plan. These deviations can result in the
system not being perfectly rectangular, and the azimuth of the individual façades may
deviate by a few degrees. While the diagonals were measured from the roof and indicated
that the container had a rectangular shape, accurately assessing the azimuth proved
challenging.
These deviations are not uncommon in real building construction, especially in the case
of small-scale projects or buildings with less stringent requirements. Deviations in az-
imuth can occur due to factors such as measurement errors, construction tolerances, site
conditions, or human error.
A sensitivity analysis conducted as part of this study revealed the importance of azimuth
in accurately estimating the plane of array irradiance using transposition models. Hence,
it becomes crucial to precisely determine the azimuth of buildings with integrated photo-
voltaics once they are constructed.
Figure 5.1 illustrates a comparison between experimental data and simulations consider-
ing various azimuth deviations. When a deviation of ± 5◦in azimuth is considered, it not
only shifts the curves in time by ± 15-20 minutes but also impacts the cumulative energy
or insolation received. Calculating the area under the curves, the insolation received in
the east and west varies by ± 7%, and by ± 0.3% for the south.
The figure suggests that there is a 5◦shift in the container under study. This shift implies
that, for instance, the azimuth of the west-facing façade would be 275◦instead of 270◦.
This is an uncertain input that can affect the results significantly.

5.1.2
Albedo
In the context of BIPV curtain walls, the contribution of the ground-reflected component
is heightened due to the vertical mounting orientation of the modules. As a result, the
accurate estimation of albedo becomes more important in comparison to standard PV
systems.
Traditionally, it is assumed that the albedo remains constant when modeling the ground-
reflected component, as in Equation 3.5. However, analysis of the data presented in
Figure 5.2a indicates that the albedo actually varies throughout the day. While this result
is not new [50], it does provide reassurance that the measured albedo data near the
container is reasonable. This variation occurs because the reflectance of a surface differs
under direct sunlight compared to diffuse illumination, and the ratio of direct to diffuse
radiation changes throughout the day. Additionally, on various time scales ranging from
monthly to daily and even hourly, changes in surface moisture, grass orientation, and
other influencing parameters contribute to the observed variation [51].
Snow could also increase the albedo, but it is uncommon for snowfall to occur at the
study location. Figure 5.2a shows that between 8:00 and 11:00 (UTC), the albedo seems
to stabilize for all studied months, which allows to compare albedo between months. By
averaging the data from these specific hours per month, as shown in Figure 5.2b, it be-
comes evident that the albedo does not vary significantly over the year at the precise
location under investigation. It is important to consider that the uncertainty in the mea-

5.3
Temperature
The results presented in section 5.2 confirm that using different colored interlayers gives
the modules unique reflective properties, which vary depending on the chosen color. Con-
sequently, colored modules can exhibit different temperatures. Analyzing the tempera-
tures on the back surface of the modules reveals some differences, although they are not
excessively pronounced. One clear difference is seen in Figure 5.12 when comparing the
reference module to the modules with beige and gray interlayers, respectively. Over a pe-
riod of 276 days, the beige module maintains an average temperature that is roughly 1 ◦C
lower than the reference module, and its maximum temperature remains approximately
10 ◦C below that of the reference module.
Although the impact appears to be minimal, for those seeking high accuracy in predicting
the performance of a colored BIPV system, it is advisable to consider the distinct optical
losses associated with each individual color when modeling the cell temperature.

5.4.1
Modelling of colored modules
When modeling the power output of colored modules, several factors need to be taken
into account. However, certain factors are more relevant than others. To illustrate this,
three scenarios are presented below, gradually increasing the level of complexity and
incorporating additional factors. These scenarios are compared against measured data
to assess their accuracy and effectiveness.
5.4.1.1
Scenario 1: “Reference Model”
In this scenario, all modules are modeled as the reference module, completely ignoring
that the modules are colored.

5.4.1.4
Intra-daily spectral variation
It is well known that the solar radiation spectrum varies throughout the day. This temporal
evolution of the spectrum is relevant because PV modules respond to the incident spectral
distribution. It is even more important if the modules are colored, as they are in some BIPV
systems.
During module characterization, the modules are flashed with solar radiation that closely
approximates the spectrum of AM1.5. However, when these modules are installed in
buildings, the actual light they receive rarely matches the AM1.5 spectrum. This discrep-
ancy is highlighted in Figure 5.19a, comparing AM1.5 spectral irradiance to simulated
incident spectral irradiance on west-facing modules at the specific location of the study.
The plot represents three different hours on April 2nd (in UTC). As the day progresses,
the simulated spectrum shifts toward the IR region. For more details on the simulation
process used to generate these results, please refer to section 4.7.
The measured response of each module to different wavelengths is shown in Figure 5.19b.
For additional details regarding these measurements, consult section 4.6. This plot illus-
trates the impact of introducing a colored interlayer by comparing colored modules to the
reference module. For instance, the red module displays a reduced response to blue pho-
tons. This decrease in response can be attributed to the fact that the red layer primarily
allows red photons to pass through, resulting in fewer blue photons reaching the solar cell.
Consequently, the generation of collectible carriers in that spectral region is diminished.
The same principle applies to the other colored modules as well.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to assess the suitability of typical PV performance models for
BIPV curtain walls systems.
The validation of transposing measured DNI, DHI, and GHI to vertical east, south,
and west orientations was performed using a 5-minute dataset with 276 days from the
BIPV setup. Four different models (Isotropic, Sandia, Perez, and Hay) were utilized to
estimate the diffuse component, and the validation was based on visual comparison and
the statistical indicators nRMSD, nMBD, and r2.
The investigation of the BIPV system revealed a rotation of 5 degrees towards the north
compared to the originally planned design. As a result, deviations in azimuth were ob-
served for each façade. For instance, the west-facing façade had an azimuth of 275
degrees instead of 270, and the same for other orientations. A sensitivity analysis high-
lighted the significance of accurately determining the azimuth of buildings with integrated
photovoltaics for precise estimation of plane-of-array irradiance using transposition mod-
els.
The albedo at the study location was found to vary throughout the day but remained rela-
tively stable over the course of the year at the study location. A constant albedo value of
0.22 was chosen for subsequent simulations, aligning with the suggested range for grass
in PV modeling software. Although accurately estimating albedo is crucial for BIPV cur-
tain walls due to the vertical module mounting orientation, a constant albedo was used,
as commonly done for standard PV simulations.
Among the transposition models, Perez and Hay performed the best and exhibited similar
performance. However, the Hay model was selected for subsequent power simulations
because the Perez model slightly overestimated POA compared to the Hay model, with
nMBD values approximately 2.5-3.6% higher. The Hay model demonstrated nRMSD of
17%, nMBD of 0.75%, and r2 of 0.97 for the east orientation; nRMSD of 16%, nMBD of
0.60%, and r2 of 0.98 for the south orientation; and nRMSD of 16%, nMBD of 1.1%, and
r2 of 0.97 for the west orientation. These results indicated that using a constant albedo
led to satisfactory outcomes in this case.
For predicting the maximum power point, point value models were validated in a similar
manner as the transposition models. However, only the six west-facing modules, five
of which were colored, were considered. Eight combinations of two models for effec-
tive irradiance, two for cell temperature, and two for power were evaluated. A detailed
investigation of the angular losses was conducted to incorporate them into the effective
irradiance calculation.
The evaluation of angular losses from reflections in coupon-sized samples using two em-
pirical models, the Martin and Ruiz model and the Sandia model, demonstrated that the
colored samples exhibited different angular responses compared to the reference sam-
ple. This finding indicated distinct reflective properties for each color. Although the Martin
and Ruiz model captured the general behavior, it struggled to accurately fit the data for
colored samples at incidence angles between 30 and 60 degrees. On the other hand,
the Sandia model provided a good fit with a polynomial equation but lacked a physical
interpretation and required more parameters.

The distinct optical losses associated with each color also result in slight temperature vari-
ations. For example, the beige module exhibits an average temperature of approximately
1 ◦C lower than the non-colored module and a maximum temperature of around 10 ◦C
lower.
Overall, when modeling the power output, the inclusion of added losses associated with
coloration had minimal impact on the overall results. However, it is advisable to consider
these effects if high accuracy is required, as the additional angular losses due to coloration
could lead to an additional energy loss of up to 3%.
Furthermore, it was observed that the power models underestimated differently depending
on the color during the late afternoon of clear sky days. This discrepancy was associated
with the temporal evolution of the solar radiation spectrum throughout the day. The sim-
ulated incident spectral irradiance on west-facing modules shifted towards the infrared
region as the day progressed. This temporal evolution of the spectrum played a signifi-
cant role in modeling effective irradiance, particularly for colored modules with a primary
response in the red range (around 600-1000 nm).
After conducting a more extensive analysis on the non-colored module, it was found that
the eight combinations exhibited a similar pattern, with predictions showing an average
error of approximately 40% compared to the measured values. However, the normalized
mean bias deviation was close to 0, indicating that the model’s predictions were unbiased
on average. The errors in opposite directions compensated for each other, resulting in an
overall near-zero bias. The analysis also indicated that a significant portion of the error
in the model’s prediction was attributable to non-clear sky days, as the performance on
clear sky days individually demonstrated an nRMSD of approximately 7% and an nMBD
of around 3%. Nevertheless, the models consistently overestimated power on clear sky
days, and this overestimation was likely due to an underestimation of cell temperature.
Increasing the estimated cell temperature by 10 °C brought the modeled power closer to
the measured values on clear sky days. This adjustment led to a significant improvement,
reducing the nRMSD to 3% and achieving an nMBD of 0%. These observations suggest
that the considered models could potentially yield satisfactory results if the temperature
models are refined and enhanced.
The results of this study offer valuable guidance for future BIPV developers and researchers.
They can use these findings as a reference to determine which models to employ and
draw inspiration for enhancing the reliability and accuracy of feasibility studies. By apply-
ing these insights, the transition from fossil fuels to a more sustainable energy production
can be facilitated in the future.

ISO 9060 & IEC 61724 Class A
Models CMP10, CMP11, SMP10 and SMP11 are the high 
quality pyranometers that are most commonly used in 
meteorological networks and solar energy applications 
around the world and all comply with Class A of ISO 9060 
and IEC 61724.
 
Analog or digital outputs 
CMP10 and CMP11 do not require any power. Incoming 
solar radiation generates a continuous millivolt output, 
which is converted in a data logger to irradiance in W/m2 
using the calibrated sensitivity. For easy integration into 
SCADA systems SMP10 and SMP11 have Modbus® RTU 
RS-485 serial communication, plus an amplified analog 
output. The sensitivity is stored inside for standardized 
outputs and they feature improved response time and 
better temperature compensation.
With or without drying cartridge
To prevent internal condensation, pyranometers are fitted 
with a desiccant to keep the internal humidity low and the 
accuracy high. CMP11 and SMP11 have an external drying 
cartridge with a desiccant that needs regular inspection and 
replacement every 3 to 6 months, depending on the local

Applications
Albedometers
Albedometer
Two similar pyranometers are mounted horizontally, to 
measure incoming and reflected irradiances in W/m2. The 
ratio of the two is the albedo of the surface below. 
Large area measurement
For meteorology, climate research and agriculture, the 
mounting is usually high up to measure albedo over a large 
area and to validate satellite data. A glare screen prevents 
direct radiation reaching the lower detector when the sun 
is low.
Bifacial PV 
Bifacial PV modules generate extra power from light on the 
rear side that is primarily reflected from the surface below. 
However, this varies with the type of surface, weather 
conditions, angle of the sun and shading effects. These 
variables make it is necessary to measure how the albedo 
of the surface changes. The albedometer is mounted low, 
around 1.5 m, and does not need a lower glare screen.
Select your preferred pyranometers 
Choose pyranometers depending upon the ISO 9060 
classification and signal output that you require. SP Lite2 
and CMP3/SMP3 bolt together back-to-back and have a 
screw-in mounting rod.
Double-dome CMP/SMP pyranometers can be fixed to 
a CMF1 mounting plate and rod, or the larger CMF4 if 
you need CVF4 heating and ventilation units. For both 
configurations a glare screen for the lower pyranometer is 
available if required.

ISO 9060:2018 Overview
The performance characteristics of Class A, B, and C sensors 
established by the International Standards Authority in ‘ISO 
9060:2018’ are the primary method of comparing different 
sensor classes and offer a helpful baseline when comparing 
sensors from different vendors.
ISO 9060:2018 was introduced in 2018, with major changes 
from the previous classification standard launched in 1990. 
The new class definitions and criteria were designed to be 
more consistent and compliant with available sensors, offering 
a clear overview of classes and specifications to help define 
selection criteria and support product comparison.
With many products, including the MS-80 Class A pyranometer 
launched in 2016, EKO led the way, implementing design and 
performance changes that would influence the 2018 standard 
and today help us produce the highest quality, most accurate, 
and reliable sensors in every class.
