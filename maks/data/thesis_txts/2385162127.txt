3 Abstract 
Interest in environmental protection has increased significantly over the course of the 20th cen-
tury. This report analyzes the beneficial effect of catalysis on the environment and examines 
technological techniques to improve catalysis. The report explores the most common uses of 
catalysis. Additionally, this paper explains the history and technological development of catalysis 
in the industrialized world, its usage today and its future potential.  As a result, the report is focus-
ing on how technological development can improve environmental problems. Finally, a common 
understanding of how ecological development is understood will be discussed. 
Experiments are very expensive and very time-consuming. Furthermore, many experiments 
have already been conducted. For this reason, it is very important to conduct proper research 
before the experiment is started. This avoids unnecessary expenses for evaluation. This report 
explains the reasoning behind the improvement of catalysts and what should be evaluated in the 
decision-making process, in order to assure the desired output. Lastly, the report explains the 
theory of catalysts, describing analytical techniques for the assessment of catalysts. 
 
The report connects the theory of reaction chemistry to the theory of catalysis and together 
with H NMR (Hydrogen Nuclear Magnetic Resonance relaxometry) relaxometry, investigates a 
method for the characterization of catalysts. The report uses a fundamental understanding of 
chemistry to explain new opportunities for improvement and how this can be translated into an 
environmental improvement. The report is highly interdisciplinary using economics, chemistry, 
behavioral science and environmental technology to explain the connection between technologi-
cal development and environmental improvements. The report evaluates the way in which tech-
nological development can help in terms of environmental improvements; hence the analysis 
uses technological experiments. The technological experiment being assed is H NMR-
relaxometry.  
 
The report verifies that H NMR relaxometry is a very promising method for characterizing of 
catalysts and explains how this technological improvement is utilized properly. The report exam-
ines why this is potentially a very effective way of reducing environmental impact and why it is 
most likely to play a major role in solving many of the environmental issues society holds today.

Background 
 
Chemistry is an essential part of modern society. Chemistry dates back millennia; even in the 
times of the Ancient Egypt chemistry was used. Since the beginning of the 18th century Chemistry 
has been used more widely in modern society as a broad science. Chemistry today is used in 
almost all industries and plays an essential role in understanding underlying processes and me-
chanics of modern technologies. Chemical reaction processes and chemical mechanics, in par-
ticular, play a central role. Chemistry can be used for the purpose of mass destruction, but also 
rapid development. Chemistry has been subject to much blame due to technological abuse, such 
as creation of the atomic bomb, industrialized killing and chemical weapons during World War II. 
Chemistry arises from the fundamental description of the universe with interaction between mole-
cules. Chemistry is a very broad science able to solve many different matters and is a very strong 
tool in solving environmental issues as well. Chemistry today solves matters such as flue gas 
cleaning, wastewater treatment, energy savings, eutrophication, acidification etc. As the im-
portance of solving environmental matters increases, so too does the need for chemistry.

4 Introduction 
 
Catalysis is one effective solution to control pollution of the environment. It is said that relating 
chemical activity, chemical catalysis, chemistry of life processes, chemistry of the environment 
and chemical behavior under extreme conditions should be of increased interest of USA priority  
1985 [1]. Due to the possibility to see or measure the molecules on the surfaces of the catalysts, 
research in chemistry deserves high priority [1]. This recommendation is based on new powerful 
instruments.  
 
Instrumentations since that time have only improved and are potentially able to evaluate sur-
faces of catalysts with higher precision using Low field NMR-relaxation, compared to the conclu-
sion coming from the aforementioned report [1]. This paper will clarify this relationship later, but 
what should be noticed is that new methods gives better possibilities for improvements and 
thereby reasons for investigation. 
 
This report uses the analytical characterization technique H NMR-relaxometry. Today common 
methods include x-ray diffraction, surface area porosity, analytical electron microscopy, x-ray 
spectroscopy, ion scattering spectroscopy etc. Lab experiments of this sort can easily take a 
significant amount of time. Furthermore, as the use and durability of a catalyst varies from a few 
seconds to multiple hours and even years depending on the nature of the catalyst, it is challeng-
ing and expensive to evaluate all kinds of catalysts without an efficient characterization method. 
For this reason, H NMR-relaxometry is investigated.  
 
H NMR-relaxometry is a measurement, able to measure surface chemistry of different materi-
als. The measurement output is relaxation times namely T1 and T2 times, which describes the 
time of the magnetization caused by the spin of the molecules, either fully relaxed or in phase. 
This type of measurement has been used for analysis of porous material, such as sandstone and 
geographical purposes for identification of soil etc. [2] [3]. The method can also identify hydrocar-
bons in petroleum, also within the food industry H NMR-relaxometry is used to analyze fat content 
and viscosity [4] [5]. The method measures the relaxation of hydrogen, either at a water molecule 
organic compound or at the surface of a solid material. What is interesting is measuring the re-
laxation on the surface of the catalysts. The reason for this is catalysis involves attraction of reac-
tants and desorption of products. Attraction and desorption is mainly caused by magnetic interac-
tion between the catalyst reactants and products. It is for this reason that it is possible to indicate 
the activity of the catalyst, by measuring the NMR-relaxation of the catalyst. The measurement 
can be carried out in any state of the catalyst durability and is depending on the surface chemistry 
and geometries of the catalysts ranging from a few minutes up to 1-2 hours. Changes in the ma-
terial during the measurement, can be evaluated, however materials in a surface-chemical stable 
state may also to be assessed.

4.1 Catalysis for environmental protection 
Catalysis is fundamentally understood as an increasing reaction rate of chemical reactions. 
Catalysis of a chemical reaction is a tool used for lowering the residence time, increasing the 
conversion rate or lowering the amount of energy used for the reaction to take place. In terms of 
chemical processing and industry this has a variety of uses, such as giving higher quality, quanti-
ty and lowering energy consumption of production of products etc. The same is true with flue gas 
cleaning or wastewater treatment. Within environmental catalysis, the quality of the air or the 
water can be improved. Alternatively, the amount of energy used to clean the polluted water or 
gas can be reduced depending on the priority. In this way, improvement of the catalyst can be 
used to improve environmental purposes.  
4.2 Flue gas cleaning 
 
Catalysis for cleaning of air plays an important role in terms of improvement of air quality. Ca-
talysis to remove NOx, SOx, CFCs, VOCs and carbon monoxide are some of the important fea-
tures of catalysis. Removal of these emissions is also possible using other approaches, such as 
sorption and thermal methods. Sorption and thermal methods are usually not ideal, as they re-
quire high capacities and must be disposed of properly. Catalysis is different, as the catalyst is 
able to decompose the pollutant directly at the source.  
4.3 NOx removal 
NOx emissions come from oxidation of nitrogen at high temperatures; usually combustion at 
higher temperatures. High-temperature combustion gives better combustion with lower levels of 
particulate matter and carbon monoxide emissions, as most of the particulate matter and CO 
transforms into CO2. For this reason, engine designers are having a challenge as high tempera-
ture design would create a more efficient engine, but would at the same time create higher NOx 
emissions [6]. 
 
NOx gasses are problematic for the environment, as they causes eutrophication and is forming 
tropospheric ozone which is causing greenhouse effect and thereby creating global warming 
issues. NOx gasses are in contact with water forming nitric acid which causes acidification. NOx 
is also a problem, as it is potentially able to cause the photochemical smog issue, which has been 
a problem in many larger cities. Today this smog still results in, loss of sight, and causes lung 
problems for both humans and other animals. 
 
NOx removal is a classic, when speaking about catalysis. Most people are familiar with NOx 
removal from the catalytic converter in automobiles. Catalytic converters first became widespread 
in United States automobile market, as a result of stricter regulations according to clean air act 
1990. Since that time, motor vehicles and Catalytic converters have been increasingly wide-
spread around the world.  The catalytic approach for motor vehicles includes a dehydration reac-
tion of NOx ammonia and oxygen forming Nitrogen and water. Oxides such as alumina oxide, 
vanadium oxide, titanium oxide, cerium oxide or magnesium oxide are ideal for the purposes of 
dehydration [7] [8], as the Lewis acid pair on the surface of these materials helps protonating 
hydroxyl radicals in the flue gas. Also, minerals such as zeolites oxide are good for the purpose of 
dehydration [8]. This way of controlling NOx emission is known as selective catalytic reduction. 
This way is the best available control technology and it is called “selective”, due to the selective 
usage of ammonia as a reduction agent to acquire the least amount of NOx and ammonia emis-
sions [9].  
 
Control of NOx can also be achieved by reducing the amount of NOx, with platinum, palladium 
or rhodium as a catalyst on an alumina oxide carrier, but these methods are not ideal for the 
purpose of NOx removal. These catalytic converters have mainly been used in cars. Three-way 
catalytic converters are an advantage, since they are able to reduce SO2, as well as CO and 
VOCs. SCR is only able to cope with NOx gasses. Even though SCR cannot solve problems 
related to other emissions, SCR converters are still considered as the most promising method 
[10].

NOx today is very efficient with high conversion rates. As combustion processes work better at 
higher temperatures, the ability to reduce NOx-emissions in the flue gas is essential. Being able 
to efficiently remove the NOx-gasses can even thought the NOx process does not remove other 
emissions by itself, help reduction of other types of emissions. This is because high temperature 
combustion engines have a higher conversion of carbon emissions such as particulate matter or 
CO forming CO2, indirectly solving these types of problems. 
4.4 SOx removal 
 
SOx represent the removal of SO2 and SO3. SOx occur in combustion processes with a high 
content of sulfur. It is expensive to remove sulfur from fuel prior to combustion, for this reason 
fuels with a high content of sulfur are occasionally used.  
 
SOx is a large issue for the environment as it comes into contact with water forming sulfuric 
acid, which is a strong acid. SOx do not cause as many different problems as NOx, but nonethe-
less the magnitude of the problem is high. Many biological microorganisms are dependent on the 
pH value of the soil, water and sediment. Changes in the pH value will remove the living require-
ments for these microorganisms. As microorganisms are in symbiosis with trees, plants and more 
developed species, extinction of some microorganisms will worsen the living conditions for these 
species. A common example of this is lichen. Trees and lichen are in symbiosis with each other. 
Lichen is in symbiosis with trees as their bark provides a good pH buffer for sufficient growth and 
provides an attractive high surface-area place for photosynthesis. Trees are in symbiosis with the 
lichen, as trees require nutrients such as nitrogen [11]. Lichens are nitrogen rich and are able to 
leach nitrogen. Lichens are injured by sulfur dioxide. The effect of pollution upon lichen depends 
on the pH of the substrate on which the lichen groves. At low pollution levels the lichen will simply 
be replaced by more tolerant lichen. However, in areas with high pollution, lichen may only be 
found on places with high basic pH, such as sandstone, walls etc.  
 
This effect will worsen the living condition for lichen as well as trees. As trees are a carbon 
sink removing greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, fewer trees will 
be present resulting in a negative global effect. Hence, although SOx are not causing as many 
different problems as NOx, the overall effect of the singular problem acidification has a high im-
pact; causing problems to the bio system as a whole and thereby much more long term implica-
tions than would be the case at first-glance.   
 
Furthermore, emission of SOx is a problem in relation to human health. SOx can cause chest 
pains, breathing problems, eye irritation and lowered resistance to lung and heart diseases [12]. 
Also, SOx cause infrastructural degradation due to the corrosion properties of sulfuric acid, creat-
ing increased needs for rebuilding of infrastructure and thereby economic issues [13]. Finally, as 
a result of the high cost of sulfur removal, fuels with a high sulfur-content are very cheap. Alt-
hough, fuels with high sulfur content are cheap, they are not necessarily the economic optimal 
solution. To assure durable machinery able to combust sulfuric fuels, expensive alloys and proper 
lubrication, preventing corrosion or more frequent maintenance and exchange of spare parts are 
needed [14]. 
 
SOx removal is possible with the help of various non-catalytic approaches. The most common-
ly used method is limestone injection. This is a non-catalytic approach, but it is feasible due to the 
byproduct of gypsum, which can be used for production of wallboard. SOx removal can also oc-
cur by catalytic processes such as Haldor topsoe’s SNOx process [15]. Haldor topsoe SNOx 
process is achieved by combining SCR with oxidation of sulfur dioxide to produce sulfuric acid, 
removing SOx as well as NOx. In the Haldor topsoe’s SNOx process, the flue gas is preheated to 
approximately 380 degrees and NOx is removed followed by a heated fluegas to 420ºC degrees, 
where SO2 is oxidized with devanadiumpentaoxide as a catalyst. SOx can also be removed cata-
lytically by the cobber oxide process, where cobber oxide is impregnated into γ-alumina, where 
both materials act as a sorbent and catalyst for sulfuric oxidation and NOx reduction process. 
Other processes use Cerium dioxide to catalyze the oxidation of SOx.

4.5 CFC removal 
 
Chloroflourocarbons (CFCs) are an inert gas, mostly known for their uses in refrigerators. Re-
frigerators work by using of cooling circuits, absorbing energy thermodynamically from vaporiza-
tion.  
 
Overheated cooling liquid is (ideally) adiabatically compressed, followed by a condensation 
through a heat exchanger leading heat away from the system. The liquid has less enthalpy in this 
state. The cooling circuit then decompresses the liquid resulting in liquid able to cool and absorb 
energy from the surroundings by vaporization. The liquid is then at the initial overheated state and 
the process is repeated. Cooling circuits are an effective way of removing heat from a system, but 
high efficiency and durability requires liquids that are chemically stable. CFCs are highly chemi-
cally stable, as the covalent bond between fluorocarbon and chlorocarbon is very strong. CFCs 
are also very flexible in the way that the length of the carbon chain can be varied, thereby control-
ling polarity resulting in control of thermodynamic properties, such as boiling point. CFCs are for 
this reason an industrially intelligent way to build a desired inert molecule for the desired purpose 
[16].  
 
From an environmental point of view CFCs are not very intelligent. CFCs are a problem for the 
environment, as they interfere with the upper ozone layer. CFCs are due to the chemical proper-
ties not causing chemical reactions with any natural occurring chemicals in the troposphere and 
are due to their volatile properties interacting with the stratospheric ozone layer. CFCs were not 
seen as a potential threat to the environment, when they were invented. In the 1960s CFSs were 
heavily used in industry, but in the beginning of the 1980s concerns related to the ozone layer 
began to arise. In 1987, the Montreal protocol was signed calling for a 50% reduction in CFS 
production.  
 
The upper ozone protects planet earth from radiation from outer space and for this reason; it is 
problematic to human health. It increases the risk of skin cancer. Moreover it is a problem in rela-
tion to global warming as the ozone layer is letting through more light, which is transformed into 
heat through the atmosphere, sea and ground. CFCs have been reduced in a very effective man-
ner since the Montreal protocol was made, but there is still a reason to take problems relating 
CFCs seriously. 
 
CFCs are today replaced with HCFCs (Hydrofluorocarbons). HCFC is still a problem for the 
ozone layer, but hydrogen bonding leads to higher environmental degradation. The chemicals 
degrade to a concentration not considered toxic to the ozone layer and with a chemical stability 
high enough to assure good cooling circuits etc. HCFCs are, for this reason, much less problem-
atic for the environment [17]. HCFCs are catalytically produced using metal halides, chromium 
oxides in a reaction with hydrogen fluoride. Another way to transform CFCs into HCFCs is by 
hydrogenation and dechlorination processes using palladium and carbon catalysts. Being able to 
catalytically produce alternatives to CFCs has resulted in an effective reduction of emission of 
CFCs to the atmosphere. Catalysis has played a main role in this transformation [18]. 
4.6 VOC removal 
VOC (volatile organic compound) are chemicals originating from combustion processes such 
as cars etc., but VOCs are also occur in all sort of products, such as paint, pesticides, solvents, 
household chemicals and are found in various industries, like paper production, asphalt blowing 
etc.  
 
VOCs vaporize at low temperatures and are problematic to the environment due to their highly 
radioactive forcing properties, which lead to global warming. Also health issues are a concern, 
some of the VOCs cause cancer, but most cause irritation and excessive inhalation leads to a 
shorter life [19] [20].  
 
As mentioned, VOC’s are a byproduct of combustion processes. Most VOCs are due to the 
carbon and hydrogen content able to burn. For this reason, an effective way of reducing VOCs 
effects on the environment is improvement of combustion processes. This can be in terms of 
improved physical conditions, such as optimal temperature and pressure relationships, but also

and perhaps more importantly catalysis of the combustion process. Catalysis of the combustion 
process gives better reaction kinetics in the combustion process, which leads to less emissions of 
VOC, in addition to less consumption of resources, lower global effects and better usage, more

combustion is AlO3 coated with platinum or gold oxidizing the VOCs forming CO2 and water [20]. 
4.7 Industrial Production 
 
Since the beginning of the industrial revolution, catalysis has played a central role in industrial 
production. The first industrial catalytic process was discovered in 1746 by J. Roebuck in manu-
facturing of sulfuric acid using lead as catalyst, since then catalysis for industrial purposes have 
been widely used. In the beginning of the 19th century catalysis for production of fertilizers for 
food production was invented and patented by Wilhelm Ostwald. The Ostwald process uses am-
monia and oxygen to produce nitric acid, which is the main raw material for the production of 
fertilizers. A few years later Fritz Haber invented a process that was able to produce ammonia 
from air. A combination of these two processes made it possible to increase food production 
drastically by the chemical processing of air. This invention has to many, been seen as the 
world’s greatest invention, as the process has resulted in increased wealth and increased popula-
tion.  50 years later, chemical reaction engineering was made into a discipline, quantifying the 
interaction and kinetics in relation to varying inputs. Furthermore, reactor designs were investi-
gated to improve the efficiency of the reactors. Analysis of the surface chemistry of the catalyst 
was also carried out, mostly on microscale and later on much lower scale. Today, highly sophisti-
cated methods are used and one of the potentially promising of these is H NMR-relaxometry. 
 
The environmental aspect of catalysis in industry, aside from pollution control by the cleaning 
of exhaust gasses and wastewater, is mostly a reduction of resource consumption in the produc-
tion. This means lower use of energy, materials, less waste for production of the same unit and 
thereby less emissions.  
 
Many studies show that wealth and population will increase towards 2050.For this reason, in-
dustrial production will not improve the environment in these years, but instead support increased 
population and wealth. After 2050 studies show that wealth and population will stagnate or over-
shoot and collapse [21]. Conversion of energy from hydrocarbons will most likely be a major part 
of the energy production [21]. Conversion of hydrocarbons involves hydrogenation and dehydro-
genation processes. Catalytic conversion with aluminum oxide and other oxides is essential to 
reduction of operational pressures and temperatures, hence saving energy. As industrial produc-
tion is mostly used to support wealth and population growth towards 2050 and since environmen-
tal improvement has been used to clean up pollution not preservation, the environmental aspect 
in terms of improved catalytically activity is not likely to do a difference in terms of the environ-
ment toward 2050 [21]. That said, improving industrialized production in terms of faster reaction 
kinetics by improving the catalysts is still able to improve the environmental aspect, as long as 
wealth and growth remains the same. This means improvement of the catalytic activity in terms of 
more efficient industrial production is most likely to make a difference after 2050. 
 
Today, industrial catalysis is improving plastic production, transportation issues, detergents, 
food supply and energy conversion.  
4.8 Plastic Production 
 
The production of polymers involves catalysts such as Chromium, titanium trichloride, perox-
ides etc. Catalysis of polymer production have made major improvements in terms of product 
development, such as catalyzing reactions for production of polymers of different characteristics 
can create a product with the desired mechanical properties etc. Polymer development by cataly-
sis can also be used in development of new plastic material that is biodegradable not causing 
accumulation in nature [18].

4.9 Transportation 
 
In addition to plastic production, catalysis for transportation purposes has played an important 
role. Improved plastic materials have made it possible to produce lighter materials and better 
rubber for tires. Catalysis has also made it possible to convert a high amount of crude oil content

4.10 
Detergents 
 
Improved quality of detergents is also a major factor in catalyst development. Catalysts have 
made it possible to produce detergent able to clean equally well at lower temperatures. Waste 
products coming from catalyzed detergents are likewise CFCs more biodegradable, causing less 
accumulation in the environment. Improvement of catalysts for detergent can both improve life by 
making cleaning easier, but also reducing the energy needed to clean, as well as lowering the 
amount of detergent necessary. This helps to reducing VOCs and other solvents to the environ-
ment, as well as other chemicals such as sulfate primarily used in surfactants. 
4.11 
Food Supply 
 
Catalysis has been a key factor in food production. Production of fertilizers, pesticides and 
herbicides has increased the yields of agriculture, which have made it possible to meet increased 
food demands in the world. Catalytic production has made it possible to grow more crops with the 
use of less land and less energy. Plastics and CFCS pesticides and herbicides are able to be 
produced with higher biodegradability using catalysis and thereby leading to less environmental 
issues. Today 80% of the ammonia produced worldwide is used as fertilizer, which has trans-
formed wasteland into farmland, thereby transforming land without any usage into the production 
of food. 
4.12 
Energy Production 
 
The future may bring a shortage of energy resources. Catalysts are able to improve combus-
tion processes, hence burning CO and particular formation forming CO2 and water. This will limit 
the emissions of CO and particles, which are essential in terms of health issues, global warming 
and smog. Improved combustion processes of hydrocarbons are also resulting in more energy, 
which will then preserve resources. 
4.13 
Wastewater Treatment 
 
Catalysis for wastewater treatment has been possible since the end of the 19th century. Alt-
hough the method for catalytically cleaning of wastewater has been known since the end of the 
19th century, it was not until the 1960s that cleaning with catalysis was first introduced. Mid-19th 
century had large cholera outbreaks which lead to deaths of tens of thousands people in London. 
This combined with the great stink of 1858 lead to construction of underground sewage systems 
for safe removal of waste. In the beginning, waste was not used as fertilizer on farmland or treat-
ed, but was simply pumped away from the larger cities onto landfills, taking up space? . At the 
end of the 19th century, the increasing size of cities, made the challenge of wastewater treatment 
of increasing interest. Landfills were not able to take up the volume of sewage the cities could 
produce. Sir Edward Frankland was able to demonstrate filtration of sewage, which lead to bio-
logical treatment by oxidizing sewage. At the beginning of the 1960s, sewage cleaning using 
Fentons reagent with hydrogen peroxide as a reactant and ferrous ions as catalysts, were intro-
duced [22]. 10 years later, investigation of photo catalysis using TiO2 as catalyst was of increas-
ing interest. This was mainly due to the 1970s oil crisis’s and TiO2’s ability to form hydrogen as

scribing destruction of pollutants using TiO2 as a catalyst was discovered by Frank and Bard in 
1977, in which they described the decomposition of cyanide in the presence of aqueous TiO2 
suspensions [23].  
 
Over the past 30 years, fast evolution of research activities devoted to environmental protec-
tion have been carried out. Today, advanced catalytic oxidation processes vary from Fenton 
processes, photo assisted Fenton processes, Photo catalysis, Ozone water systems, hydrogen 
peroxide photolysis and Ozone photolysis. 
4.14 
Fenton Processes 
 
Fenton’s reagent is able to destroy toxic compounds, such as phenols and herbicides. Hydro-
gen peroxides are by catalysis with ferrous ions creating OH-radicals. This result in oxidization of 
the toxic compound mineralizing the chemical. The Fenton process is very attractive for use in 
wastewater treatment, as iron is a very abundant material. Other oxidizing agents such as copper 
and gold can be used, but they are scarcer and it is for this reason, the Fenton process is being 
used for wastewater cleaning [24]. 
4.15 
Photo Assisted Fenton Process 
 
The rate of degradation of organic pollutants using Fenton degradation is strongly accelerated 
by light irradiation. The presence of light allows ferrous ions to be reduced allowing higher con-
centration of OH-radicals and longer durability for the catalyst, which results in better oxidation of 
the pollutants [24]. 
4.16 
Photo Catalysis 
 
Photocatalytic process involves semiconductor material, such as TiO2 as catalyst. TiO2 is 
used as it has good performance high stability and low costs. Photocatalytic processes happen 
by radiation and formation of electron hole pairs able to reduce water forming OH-radicals, de-
mineralizing the pollutant [23]. 
4.17 
Ozone Water Systems 
 
Ozone water systems occur in alkaline systems. Ozone water systems are in aqueous media 
forming OH and HO2 radicals with catalysts, such as manganese or molybdenum. It is clear that 
the addition of hydrogen peroxides accelerates the formation of OH-radicals, enhancing formation 
of OH-radicals that can attack the pollutant and thereby demineralize it. Ozone water systems are 
very pH dependent, due to ozone decomposition; the mechanism is very dependent on pH and is 
best in alkaline media [25].  
4.18 
Hydrogen Peroxide Photolysis 
 
Light irradiation of hydrogen peroxides result in homolytic cleavage forming OH-radicals. This 
process is similar to the Fenton process except this process uses metal oxides as catalysts. UV 
light smaller than 280 nm result in formation of OH-radicals. After that point, hydrogen peroxides 
will then be attacked by OH-radicals forming HO2 radicals. The process is pH dependent, which 
is mainly due to the formation of HO2 radicals in alkaline media [25].

4.19 
Ozone Photolysis 
 
Similar to hydrogen peroxide photolysis, the formation of OH-radicals with usage of metal ox-
ide catalyst are occurs as a part of ozone photolysis process, hence degradation of pollutant with 
ozone and light. Ozone light photolysis includes different reaction pathways than hydrogen perox-
ide photolysis. Ozone photolysis is, like hydrogen peroxide photolysis, pH dependent. Increased 
levels of pH are causing increasing formation of HO2 radicals. The advantage with Ozone UV 
light process is that ozone has an adsorption band of 254 nm, which causes a reaction with water 
and hydrogen peroxides. This is through hemolytic cleavage, creating OH radicals for the demin-
eralization of the pollutant [25]. 
4.20 
Catalysis for Other Purposes 
 
Catalysis is able to lower the marginal costs of producing a product.  For this reason, catalysis 
is used to increase profit. In economic theory about supply and demand, lowering the marginal 
cost will change the market equilibrium to a point with higher production. Aside from creating 
environmental improvement, this can also create economic benefits or higher productivity and 
thereby improve access to the produced product. Additionally, catalysis can be used to save time 
being able to solve other problems with a low capital investment. For instance, a reactor with 
improved reaction kinetics can increase the speed of the process of production of a desired prod-
uct. Therefore, the reactor can be used to serve other purposes without the additional investment 
of an additional reactor. Catalysis can also solve problems related to automation. Improved reac-
tion kinetics will result in reactions able to take place at lower temperatures. For this reason, low-
er control of temperature needed and as temperature control require caution processes taking 
place at lower temperatures are less likely to go wrong and thereby creates needs such as sur-
veillance and extra ordinary safety cautions etc.

5.2 Catalysis of Reactions 
Catalysis is used to accelerate chemical reactions. Catalysis is important both in terms of re-
ducing the costs of production, but also and more importantly to reduce the environmental impact 
both in terms of higher chemical transformation and lower use of energy.  
 
Catalysis works simply by adding a component that is interfering with the reaction indirectly 
without any net chemical change. For instance, catalysis only affects the rate of reaction, but 
does not interfere with the equilibrium of the reaction. The purpose is lowering the activation 
energy of the reaction. A simple example of catalysis is corrosion of iron. Iron forms rust by an 
oxidation process. Solid iron has no charge, but polar interference such as water causes iron as 
per oxidation to release electrons to a positive charge, which makes the iron molecule vulnerable 
to negatively charged molecules, such as oxide ions or hydroxide ions. If iron were put in a con-
tainer solely consisting of hydroxides and oxides ions at ambient pressure and temperature with-
out the presents of water, the activation energy of the reaction would be higher, tending to create 
a stagnant state of reaction with a reaction rate of 1/1000 of the catalyzed reaction. In the case of 
interaction with water, the transfer of electrons away from the iron molecule will occur causing 
exchange in electrons with protons and causing a reaction to take place. The transfer of electrons 
and protons is essential for the reaction to take place. When water is added the electrons can 
transport away from the iron molecule through the water and reduce the activation energy of the 
reaction. So in this case water is a catalyst catalyzing the oxidation of iron. Another example of 
catalysis, is the formation of hydrogen(g) and oxygen(g) from hydrogen peroxide by addition of 
iodide. This is a homogeneous catalysis, since the catalyst is the same phase as the reaction 
mixture, where the example with corrosion of iron is a heterogeneous catalyst, since water is at a 
different phase state than iron.

5.5 Polar and Ionic Surface 
Polar surfaces involve surfaces that are able to hold an electronic charge. Polarity often occurs 
when bonding with highly electronegative atoms such as fluorine or oxygen. Polar molecules are 
bonded covalently, but high electronegativity can also result in ionic bonding. Ionic bonds do not 
bond covalently, but are simply attracted to each other due to the electronegativity. Polar and 
ionic surfaces attract reactants, but the reactants are bonded differently to the surface of the 
catalyst. Ionic surfaces are better for hydrogenation reactions and polar surfaces, such as oxides, 
are better at hydration and dehydration processes. 
 
Aluminum oxides are an example of a catalyst that attracts polar reactants. Aluminum oxide is 
used for sulfur production, dehydration of alcohols, the formation of propylene from methanol and 
alkylation of phenols etc. The key features of alumina oxides are their ability to attract polar sites 
of organic chemicals or changing oxidation level of inorganic reaction such as sulfur production. 
Also, alumina has a very high surface area, especially gamma and eta alumina has a high sur-
face area.

5.6 Nonpolar Surface

nonpolar is fitted for biological systems as the polarity is often much lower in these systems.  
5.7 Semiconductors 
Semiconductors are adsorbing reactant similar to polar surfaces. The difference between sem-
iconductors as a catalyst and a polar catalyst is that semiconductors as a catalyst works by hav-
ing an electronic charge. This is where physics plays a role in chemistry. The electronic charge 
can be applied by electronics, but also photons can create an electronic charge on the surface of 
semiconductors. Both negative and positive charges exist; having a negative charge is strength-
ening electron sharing and a positive acceptance of electron. The advantage with semiconductors 
as a catalyst is the strength of the catalyst can be strengthened by applying or removing electron-
ic charge either by electronic current or by light exposure. This can either be used to desorb 
products or adsorb reactants. This information can be used together with the volcano plot to cre-
ate arbitrary catalysts with the help of light or electronics [30]. 
5.8 Addition of Transition Metals  
The addition of transition metals to the surface of the catalyst can be used to regulate the ad-
sorption and desorption strength of the catalyst. Transition metals can be used in combination 
with polar sorption materials such as alumina oxide. In combination with platinum AlO3 creates 
ideal conditions for chemical reactions. This catalyst has been used as a catalytic converter for 
cars, converting carbon monoxides, NOxs and VOCs into CO2, nitrogen and water. This is also 
known as the three-way catalysts. These reaction mechanisms have different natures and there-
by various adsorption and desorption strength among the reactants and products. For this reason 
having a flexible catalyst as Pt/AlO3 is ideal. Although it must be said that Pt is not a very abun-
dant material, which emphasizes the need for recycling, which can be a disadvantage in terms of 
usage in a widespread technology such as cars.

6.1 Analysis of the Geometry 
The geometry, such as surface area analysis, is important to improving the reaction kinetics. A 
simple explanation for this is, if a reaction is going to take place, the plausibility of the reactants 
colliding with the catalyst is greater if the surface area of the catalyst is large. Increased area of a 
catalyst can happen in different ways. One way is simply grinding the catalyst to a smaller size, 
resulting in overall increased surface area due to the geometrical relationship between the parti-
cle(spherical) diameter and the surface area. Another way to increase the surface area of a cata-
lyst, is by manufacturing the catalyst with multiple pores. Building a catalyst with a porous struc-
ture makes reactant transport through the catalyst like a sieve, possibly making reactants more

H NMR is a technique to measure the paramagnetism of molecules. Paramagnetism is defined 
by the magnetism whereby certain materials are attracted. Paramagnetism occurs due to the 
presence of unpaired electrons in the material, which means the atomic orbitals are incomplete 
and thereby paramagnetic. The link between electrons and magnetism was first discovered by 
Michael Faraday in 1831 by wrapping two wires around an iron ring running electrons through the 
wire resulting in magnetization also known as electromagnetism. This knowledge was later con-
nected with Albert Einstein’s theory of relativity, stating that anything with mass has an equivalent 
amount of energy. Quantum was then developed, using the theory of relativity to explain the 
relationship between electromagnetic radiation photons and quantum.  
 
This invention later made it possible to communicate by sending electromagnetic signals, 
which have led to the invention of the radio, television, mobile phone and internet. Other uses 
have later been invented such as LED-light, medical imaging, solar cells (photovoltaic), micro-
wave ovens and much more.  
 
Applying electromagnetic radiation to cause a magnetic spin of Nucleon was first formulated 
by Austrian physicist, Wolfgang Pauli, in 1925. The principle known as parulis extension principle 
was later used investigate molecules using NMR. In the 1950s, the first NMR-studies were pub-
lished and have now evolved to solve a variety of issues, such as quantitative and qualitative 
chemical composition analysis, reaction kinetics examinations, examination of structure, porosity, 
surface area, fat content of food, viscosity evaluations etc.

7.4 Temperature Dependency 
Thermodynamic entropy describes the order of a system. The third law of thermodynamics 
states that: 
 “The entropy of a perfect crystal of any pure substance approaches zero as the temperature 
approaches absolute zero”. 
 
The order of a system can be explained by how the molecules interact with each other. High 
temperatures results in disorder and the formation of gas and low temperatures results in order, 
thus formation of solids see figure 12. 
 
Figure 12: Temperature change result in entropy changes, phase change radically changes 
entropy. 
A chemical system can undergo several molecular motions, including translation, rotation, and 
vibration (see figure 13). The higher the entropy, the higher the possible molecular motions. A 
perfect crystal has no molecular motions. A gas moves more chaotically, when the temperature is 
higher, resulting in increasing volume with temperature. This effect can be explained by the 
Boltzmann effect, explaining the relationship between kinetic energy of a particle in a gas with the 
temperature. Dividing the ideal gas constant with the Boltzmann constant equals the Avogadro 
constant, explaining the volumetric behavior between increasing temperature and the expansion 
of a gas. Evaluating the volumetric behavior of a crystal with changing temperature only very little 
volumetric expansion occurs, hence strong bonding mechanisms results in low molecular motion. 
This verifies the third thermodynamic law about perfect crystals.

7.5 Surface Acidity of Catalyst 
Fundamental acid base theory suggests Lewis acid and bases are able to accept and donate 
electron pair. Any species able to donate an unshared electron pair are called a Lewis base and 
any species able to accept unshared electron pairs are called Lewis acids. Some species are 
able to both accept and donate electron pairs, these species are amphoteric. Water is a classic 
example of an amphoteric molecule (se figure 14). Similarly, to water alumina oxide is an ampho-
teric molecule, able to donate and accept lone pairs. The lone pair on the oxygen atom are donat-
ing electron pairs to the Lewis acid and the alumina is accepting electron pairs from the Lewis 
base. For example, the oxygen lone pairs will be donated to a Lewis acid, such as H3O+ having 
deficit of electrons in relation to the octet rule. Alumina will accept electron pairs from a donating 
Lewis base, such as hydroxide being in surplus of electrons in relation to the octet rule. To evalu-
ate the activity and thereby catalytically properties of the alumina oxides. The strength of the 
Lewis acid sites on the alumina oxides are important.

Figure 14 Example of amphoteric molecule 
Being able to characterize the lewis acidity on the surface of alumina oxide is important in or-
der to acquire full knowledge in relating chemisorption and physical sorption properties of the 
surface chemistry of alumina oxide. Lewis acid represents a polar bond able to adsorb polar mol-
ecules primarily water, but also organic polar sites or acid or bases. Attracting water, acid or ba-
ses to the surface of the catalyst is an important factor in improving hydration and dehydration 
reactions. Furthermore, organic chemistry involving alkylation and isomerization is important as 
attracting the reactant and aligning them properly on the surface require sorption forcing allowing 
the reactants to react in the desired way. 
 
Strength of lewis acid pairs on the surface of the aluminum oxide depends on the amount of 
lewis sites and total amount of alumina molecule. A strong alumina acidity would require many 
lewis acid sites distributed evenly throughout the surface. For this reason, a strong surface Lewis 
acidity would require the strength and distribution of strength on the alumina. Aluminum oxide is 
adsorbing polar molecules to assure high adsorption properties the strength of the Lewis acid on 
the surface of the alumina is important. By looking at different surface pH changes and thereby 
different H-+ and OH- ion-bonding to the surface. The ability of exchanging H+ and OH- ions can 
be evaluated [41]. 
 
Alumina oxide is an amphoteric, which means it is able to react with both acid and bases. 
Alumina oxide is most stable at around 7-9 pH. Surface behavior of the alumina oxide at pH be-
low and above these levels can be interesting to evaluate and can potentially tell the specific 
strength of the oxide and thereby lewis acid. 
 
Applying Hydrogen chloride to the surface of alumina oxide will create a reaction with the oxide 
ions, resulting in alumina chloride binding [40]. Lewis acid at the surface of alumina oxides have 
been characterized in the literature, by the use of techniques such as P NMR (Phosphorous Nu-
clear Magnetic Resonance) spectroscopy and EPR(electron paramagnetic resonance) measure-
ments [42] [41]. P NMR is a spectral approach measuring chemical compounds that contain 
phosphor. Trimethylphosphine is a strong base and because of its high basic strength and small 
size it is able to be sorped to acidic surfaces. Using P NMR to characterize the amount of phos-
phor adsorbed to the catalyst can thereby characterize the lewis acids on the surface of the alu-
mina [42]. EPR measurements to evaluate lewis acid on the surface of alumina oxides is involv-
ing TEMPO(2,2,6,6-Tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl, which is a radical that is able to sorb to the 
acidic sides on the surface on the alumina [41]. TEMPO is an aromatic organic compound and is 
forming paramagnetic complexes on the surface of alumina oxide. Using TEMPO as probe mole-
cule on alumina oxides have and is for this reason possible to be registered using EPR-spectra.  
 
The theory used in the experimental part explains the surface chemistry by using NMR-
relaxometry and is involving addition of chemisorption components such as nitric acid and guani-
dine. The theory is similar to the method described using trimethylphosphine, but instead uses 
nitrogenous compounds and evaluating whether a response in terms of change in the relaxation 
occurs and thereby quantify the Lewis sites on the catalyst.

7.7 pH Measurement 
 
9 samples were carried out. 3 samples at pH 4, 3 samples at pH 7 and 3 samples at pH 11. 
The samples were measured the same day, hence 24 hours after being soaked in either of the 3 
liquids. The samples were then measured 1 time every 24 hours for 1 week. The measurement 
was carried out on Bruker minispec mq20. Using inversion recovery technique combined with T1 
and T2 correlation techniques involving time decay factors in T1 and T2 area forming a 150 by 
1000 matrix. A model suggested by article relating exponential modeling in 3 dimension was used 
[36]. The model suggested using the sum of squared error as objective function minimizing mar-
gin of error.  
 
The upper and lower confidence levels of the noise were determined by usage of a z-score. 
Another fit was made using sum of squared error constricting the upper and lower confidence 
interval of the noise with variation of T1 and T2 relaxation resulting in an estimate of the T1 and 
T2 value with 95% certainty. The experiment was carried out for 3 different samples of each dif-
ferent pH. As the 95% confidence interval is only representing the machine uncertainty and not 
sample preparation the result of each of the different samples of each pH level was evaluated. A 
trimodal distribution was found when including sample preparation. T-score was then used to 
calculate the overall upper and lower confidence interval with inclusion of uncertainty due to sam-
ple preparation. Comparison using t-test was then conducted to determine whether changing in 
pH had a significant influence on the measurement (Se figure 10 and 11).

were due to assurance of stable surface conditions rested for 1 week. The samples were cooled

was repeated at 40 C and 63 C. Additionally, 1 measurement for bulk water was done at the 3 
different temperatures. The uncertainty relating sample preparation was not included for the bulk 
water measurement. The preparation of a bulk of demineralized water was due to simplicity of the 
preparation, evaluated not to cause any uncertainty. T-score was then concluding whether there 
was a significant difference between the samples.

10 Discussion 
Achieving improved sustainability and creating sustainable development, multiple factors are 
discussed. This includes surface chemistry and how this is translated into environmental im-
provements. This involves the environmental paradigm resulting in a discussion about the rela-
tionship between improved surface chemistry and better catalysts and how that influences the 
environment.   
10.1 
Surface Chemistry 
Looking at the relationship between temperature dependency on a crystal and in a bulk, it can 
be discussed whether this takes place or not. In theory everything is temperature dependent, 
nothing can be perfect. According to the third law of thermodynamics, relaxation on a perfect 
crystal should be infinitely high, hence no spin should occur. The question is: Is this relationship 
relevant and is it even possible to measure with modern technology?  
Looking at the device and the method evaluated in this report, no relationship was found. 
Preparation of the sample had a surprisingly high influence. This influence was assumed to be 
due to grinding the alumina oxide. Grinding the alumina oxide resulted in a coarse mix of alumina 
particles. Applying them to the measuring glass resulted in different sizing of the coarse mix in the 
sample. As a coarse particle has different geometries compared to fine particles the result had 
deviations higher than what is possible to look at the temperature relationship on a crystal. Is it 
relevant? Perhaps it is. Slow relaxation times measured on a crystal signals paramagnetic behav-
ior. Change in the paramagnetism, can result in better catalytic activity or change in the crystal 
structure, resulting on changing in pore size and geometries of the catalyst. In theory there should 
be a small relationship between temperatures, but as the change in the entropy is mostly due to 
absorption of heat causing very little volumetric changes, which signals no chemical change and 
rarely change in the crystal structure, such small relationships are statistically thought to evaluate 
and rely heavily on the device and preparation of sample uncertainty.  
 
Looking at applied analytical technique, it can be discussed whether or not it is necessary to 
use guanidine acid as base media to the measure the surface acidity. Alumina oxide is catalyzing 
hydration reaction such as formation of nitrogen and water with reaction of ammonia and oxygen 
neutralization the solution. This was observed at the preparation of the samples, bauble most 
==PAGE:34==
 
Page 34 of 51 
 
likely formation of N2 was observed. For preparation of samples at neutral and acid conditions 
not as many baubles was observed, properly due to less formation of N2. Formation of N2 can 
change the acidity on the surface of the catalyst and thereby creating less pH relaxation depend-
ency. 
 
The disadvantage with this method is that if the pores of the alumina oxide exceed the size for 
assumption of no bulk liquid influence, the calculation of the surface chemistry becomes more 
complex. To avoid to high complexity and assure low inaccuracy in the measurement, the sample 
can be compressed to a pore size where bulk liquid can be neglected. Doing it this way the 
measurement is not measuring directly on the end product catalyst, but is instead interpreting the 
surface chemistry based on a catalyst at different conditions, but assumingly with the same sur-
face conditions. 
 
Increasing the size of the sample in order to get better magnetization and thereby lowering the 
signal to noise ratio, could be a solution to get better certainty on the relationship between differ-
ent factors and measurements. 
 
Overall, understanding the surface chemistry of heterogeneous catalysts is essential to improve 
and measure the catalyst. Measuring the catalysts and knowing how efficient it is not enough. 
Other factors that have no relation to chemistry can play a major role in relation to environmental 
improvements. As the overall purpose of this report is to improve the environment, these relation-
ships must be discussed. These relationships can be explained by looking at the understanding 
of environmental improvement, also known as the ecological paradigm.  
10.2 
Intensive and Extensive Indicators 
When evaluating environmental matters, proper usage of intensive and extensive indicators 
are important. An intensive indicator is a factor describing the system independently on size and 
overall magnitude. The extensive indicator evaluates the overall result of the system. A simple 
example of an intensive indicator is efficient energy saving electronics. Many electronics have 
been due to improved technology relating semiconductors been more energy efficient. But is 
exchanging electronics saving energy overall? A light source using 5 watts 3 hours a week for a 
year, compared to a source using 2.5 watts 3 hours a week for a year would save 390 watts a 
year, but if the overall electricity consumption in terms of production etc. exceeds 390 watts a 
year no actual improvement is occurring. On the other hand, if the impact is lower than the sav-
ings it would make sense in environmental matter. The same is true for catalytically improve-
ments. If the overall savings or improvements do not exceed the total amount of work put in to the 
improvement, the net environmental benefit will be negative. This is common sense, but is often 
forgotten in the decision making process. 
 
When using catalysis for environmental improvement, it is important to evaluate both intensive 
and extensive indicators, solely looking at the surface chemistry or chemical efficiency is not 
enough. The relationships with the surface chemistry and the improved efficiencies need to be 
assessed in a broader sense, evaluating extensive indicators and creating possible scenarios of 
the potential improvements caused by this. 
10.3 
Ecological Paradigm 
Catalytic processes are able to solve many environmental matters, but how are these techno-
logical improvements translated into real environmental impacts? The ecological paradigm is a 
way to translate technological improvement into environmental improvements.  
 
The ecological paradigm is a description of basic science theories and assumptions about how 
the world is understood, explaining the relationship between different scenarios and is based on 
methods such as backcasting and forecasting techniques evaluating the impact. 
 
The ecological paradigm is based on factors such as increasing population, increased con-
sumption, historical and technological development and many other factors, using quantitative 
and qualitative modelling to estimate the environmental impact of a decision, technology, system 
or process.

10.4 
IPAT Equation 
The IPAT equation is a way to evaluate the environmental impact(I). I=PAT describes the im-
pact by looking at the relationship between population(P), affluence(A) and technological level(T). 
The IPAT equation can be used to describe the environmental impact or improvement in relation 
to technological development.  
 
Improvement of catalysts is in relation to the IPAT equation influencing the technological level 
of the IPAT equation. Technological development can indirectly affect affluence and population. 
An example to this is looking at technological development in relation to population growth. If P 
increases as a result of technological development, the environmental impact will not improve. 
This is also true if affluence is increasing as a result of technological development. 
 
Today, there is a tendency toward technological development result in high affluence and 
overpopulation. Compared to a number of Asian countries, the USA does not have a high popula-
tion, but is highly affluent. On the other hand, China has a high population, but low affluence, 
resulting in an overall high impact. The relationship of technological development and increased 
accessibility for the products is for this reason not improving the environmental impact directly 
from improved technology.  
 
When evaluating the environmental improvement of a technology such as improved catalyst it 
is important to analyze affluence as well as population growth and isolate the actual impact relat-
ed to the technology, instead of including trends that is related to other sources. Solely looking at 
the change in the technological improvements can be misleading. IPAT equation says that afflu-
ence and population growth is directly caused by the technological improvement; these factors 
should be included when evaluating the environmental impact [43]. 
10.5 
Three Dimensional Sustainability 
The 19th and 20th centuries focused on the utility of nature instead of the importance of preser-
vation. This has been done as it was believed that nature is self-adjusting. The consequences in 
relation to environmental problems have for this reason not had a high priority. This attitude dom-
inated leadership and decision-making. Increasing realization of relationship between human 
activities and environmental problems have been discussed towards the end of 19th-century. The 
UN introduced the Brundtland commission in 1987 [44]. Later, in 2005, reports relating 3 dimen-
sional sustainable development in terms of economic, social and environmental aspects was 
reported [45]. This approach of evaluating environmental, social and economic issues have later 
been developed in many different directions. The core principles look at factors across different 
section comparing social aspects with economy, environment with social aspects and social as-
pects with the environment, grading each factor and deciding what to do.  
10.6 
Economic Factors 
Economic factors include cost benefit analysis and are based on capital costs, labor costs, 
benefits, such as economical savings of estimated increased sales etc. Economical cost-benefit 
analysis has the goal of estimating payback times and return on investment to calculate economi-
cal management factors.  
 
Catalysts will need capital investments, such as reactor buildings etc. Furthermore, the pro-
cess includes variable costs such as maintenance, input materials, electricity and labor. The 
process will benefit by providing energy savings and better processing, resulting in more product 
etc. 
 
The economic analysis also includes market factors, such as changing markets etc. An exam-
ple of this is food production. Food production is and has for many years been subject to the 
effects of competition, which has led to declining food prices. The effect of competition happens 
due to increasing number of actors and stocks in the market. Even though more effective meth-
ods have been invented to produce food, farmers’ benefits have not necessarily increased. In-
stead, there has been an increase of the quantity of food being produced, causing falling prices, 
resulting in farmers needing to produce even more, creating an even higher food stock and we 
are back where we started. This economic cycle is potentially causing overproduction and excess 
==PAGE:36==
 
Page 36 of 51 
 
waste [46]. Another marked dynamic is monopoly. In a monopoly one single actor have control of 
prizing of the product. Monopoly is commonly used when talking about medicine industry and 
emerging technologies. Monopoly can lead to too-high pricing, limiting the accessibility for the 
product and causing poverty. Both of these market dynamics are taking place within catalysis 
business. Within 3 pillars of sustainability not evaluating these dynamic will result in sub-optimal 
societal and environmental situations. For this reason, not only simple economical models can be 
used, but also more sophisticated taking into account of market dynamics. 
10.7 
Social Factors 
Social factors can be explained by psychologist Maslow hierarchy of needs, where the basic 
needs such as food, water and rest is the first that motivates people. Secondly, is security and 
safety which involve simple issues such as do people have a place to live? and are people feeling 
safe? The third step of Maslow’s hierarchy of needs are relationship and friends. Lastly, is feeling 
of accomplishment and self-actualization. There are multiple environmental and economic ways 
to influence human stage on the hierarchy of needs and catalysis can influence these.  
 
Economic relationships can cause one not to be able to supply food or effort a place to life, 
which then would lead to a low social indication of the social aspect. Environmental matters can 
influence the social indication of sustainability. Pollutions are able to cause disease, which can 
destroy food, but also cause sickness, which gives problems to human in relation to the second 
step in Maslow’s hierarchy of needs.  
 
A situation with high economic wealth can threat where people are on the pyramid of needs, 
even though goods are accessible etc. Changes in weather phenomena caused by changing 
environment can create natural disasters such as tsunamis, hurricanes, floods destroying cites 
etc.  
 
Air pollution is causing illness to people and is making people sick no matter economical in-
come. The environment does not care about social status or economic status. The environment 
acts as caused by the forces of nature.  
 
For this reason, social factors are an important part of 3 pillars of sustainability and catalysis 
can influence and take part of solving these kinds of problems. 
10.8 
Environmental Factors 
Catalysis have varied processes cleaning the environment. For this reason improving the ac-
tivity of the catalysts is potentially good for the environment. 
 
Environmental factors include evaluation of nature, in relation to how the environment is 
changing depending on different actions. The analysis of the environment is often including as-
sessment of biodiversity, human health, resource depletion and climate change. Analysis of the 
environment can use knowledge regarding land use, water use, greenhouse gases, eutrophica-
tion, acidification, toxicity etc. to conclude 1 or all of the first 4 mentioned categories. One method 
to evaluate the environmental factors is LCA (Life cycle assessment).  
 
LCA is a numerical method for evaluating the environmental impact of a system, product or 
decision. LCA is based on inventory flows from water energy and raw material calculating the 
impact to the land, air and water. LCA is using knowledge relating industrial processing connect-
ing the impacts. LCA is having multiple Life Cycle Impact Assessment methodologies, including 
CML-IA, ILCD, IMPACT 2002+, BEES+, TRACI, USEtox and others. The different methodologies 
weigh the impact differently and are based on different assumptions and factors. To give the best 
impact assessment possible for the desired purpose, choosing the right method for the right as-
sessment is important. Some of the methods have better understanding of some specific impact 
categories than others. Some of the methodologies are better for comparison, and others are 
better for geographical or local impact assessments.  
 
LCA is a very flexible tool using an iterative approach, being able to give overviews of the envi-
ronmental impact in a very short matter of time using only a few iterations, but are also able to 
==PAGE:37==
 
Page 37 of 51 
 
analyze environmental impacts in a very detailed manner going into depth, using many iterations 
and possibly varying from a couple of days to multiple years, depending on the goal and scope. 
  
LCA is estimating the environmental impact. It is not creating facts. To find the actual impact 
climatology, toxicology, fields test and analytical chemistry are needed. The disadvantage with 
these methods is that the damage has already happened, whereas with LCA, it is estimating 
“what would happen if?” This means LCA is a proactive decision-making tool. Another disad-
vantage with back casting techniques is that having knowledge about a field test with pollution, 
does not explain the cause. LCA can connect the cause directly to the impact, but does not tell 
the actual impact. Using LCA in connection with other methods can be a good way to analyze the 
environmental aspect [47]. It must be emphasized that environmental assessment is an ongoing 
process and can take very long time, but also very short time depending on the amount of fea-
tures and uncertainty. 
 
High uncertainty relating environmental impacts create much disagreement. Multiple models 
have for this reason been developed describing the environmental factors. One challenge with 
the description of environmental indicators has the level of precision needed to evaluate the envi-
ronmental impact. The biggest challenges are to create a model that is easy to use and are accu-
rate, but are not slowing the development and thereby the environmental improvements [48].

10.11 
Rebound Effect 
Macro economical influences within technological improvement can lead to more excessive 
consumption, rather than prior to the development as described in relation to the IPAT equation. 
This effect is known as the rebound effect or Jevon’s paradox.  
 
Jevon’s paradox was first observed by William Stanley Jevon observing technological im-
provements. Jevon found that the introduction of the watt steam engine with great improved effi-
ciency did not reduce the overall consumption of coal, but due to increased energy yield per unit 
used coal, instead increased the demand for coal, resulting in an even higher total consumption. 
The important point in relation to this is that improved energy efficiency will not help to reduce 
demand. Improved productivity has instead of reducing the energy consumption on a macro level 
moved the constraint, solving other issues not possible to solve prior to the new technological 
development. Higher energy efficiency will simply increase attractiveness and people will do more 
with it. Energy saving based on technological development is only possible if the energy is con-
strained to certain activities and not used for new purposes as a result of increased supply. As-
suming future economic reactions in parallel to those in the past is not a good assumption. What 
should be noted is efficiencies of improved reaction kinetics within chemical processing does not 
necessarily reduce the environmental impact [52].

11 Conclusion 
When looking at alumina oxide, it can be seen that almost no interaction with temperature oc-
curs and thereby the measurement is related to the surface chemistry of the catalyst. This infor-
mation is important, as information about the surface chemistry can give information about the 
activity of the catalyst or at least make the optimal catalyst for the desired purpose more likely to 
happen. As catalysis plays such a large role in a variety of different processes this information is 
crucial to solve and optimize these processes. As chemistry and chemical reactions are so fun-
damental, this explanation is very broad and can be used in a variety of areas. 
 
It was not possible to use the explained method to evaluate the lewis acids and bases on the 
catalyst, but it was plausible that the surface was degrading and causing hydration while creating 
the samples. This was interesting in terms of measuring a sample, which has acquired some 
amount of chemical change, which to some extent can explain aspects of the durability properties 
of the catalyst. 
 
A characterization method has been evaluated and found to be able to measure the surface 
chemistry of catalysts. The ability to evaluate the surface chemistry of the catalyst is essential in 
order to characterize catalysts and thereby document catalysts, measure improvements, use as 
comparison, and usage for further modeling estimating technological improvements. 
 
Usage of NMR is concluded to be a promising method for evaluating the surface chemistry of 
catalyst. Catalysts are used for many different purposes. It is for this reason a very effective way 
to improve. Depending on how the improvement and how it is being utilized this technology will 
be able to solve many environmental issues modern society stands with today. 
 
Catalysis is a tool for solving many different matters of different characters. Looking at modern 
societal problems such as climate change, loss of biodiversity, increasing problems related to 
human health and resource depletion are a large issue and possibly one of modern societies’ 
biggest issues. Industry has found a way to solve problems such as famine and poverty, but 
these have come as a cost. Today it is solving social problem, but have not been used in a broad 
way in solving environmental problems, which is why solutions for this must be carried out. Catal-
ysis is a very fundamental approach and it is possible to increase reaction kinetics and conver-
sion, which solves many of the environmental issues.  
 
The overall point is catalysts can be used to improve environmental, economical as well as so-
cietal problems. What matters is how the improvements are being utilized. If the improvement is 
being utilized to increase the production, the environment might not get any benefit from the im-
provements. On the other hand, if the improvement either stagnates or limits the production, a 
social improvement does not occur, but an environmental improvement takes place instead. 
 
To improve the environment, technological development is important. Although technological 
development can suffer from the Jevons paradox, proper decision-making can lead to a lowering 
of the environmental impact. Proper decision-making involves environmental, societal and eco-
nomical evaluations. Using this decision-making process, development can be a major factor in 
solving the global environmental problems modern society stand with today. Not doing so can 
lead to the opposite. Looking at other developed countries and comparing developed countries to 
developing countries, the tendency on how decisions are being done is trending towards impro-
vising the environment in terms of the 3 pillars of sustainability. For this reason, investigating 
catalysts using interdisciplinary methods is important in order to control the improvement and to 
improve the desired area. This also means that much more work needs to be done. Some of the 
tasks suggested are of those explained in future investigations.
