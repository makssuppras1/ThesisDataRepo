Summary
Ammonia is one of the most important chemicals for humanity, being widely used as a
source of nitrogen. However, currently used production methods requires very large plants
to handle high pressures. Improving the activity of catalysts could help reduce temperature
and thus pressure, reducing the need for large plants.
Production of ammonia is typically performed using iron catalysts. Ruthenium based cata-
lyst have also been employed. Both of these are highly sensitive to oxidation. Additionally,
they are signicantly improved by promoting with potassium.
This project investigates
whether additional activity can be reached by dosing potassium in a metallic form during
synthesis.
To investigate the possible activity increase, a setup is established to test heterogeneous
catalysts, which is capable of providing a oxygen-free, dry gas ow. Additionally, the setup
is able to dose potassium while simultaneously tracking catalyst activity. This allows testing
and dosing at pressures up to 10 bar, with puried gases, using plug ow reactors and at a
wide range of temperatures. The outlet gas can be analyzed using two mass spectrometers.
The setup has been used to test doubly promoted iron catalysts, and catalysts consisting of
ruthenium supported on alumina. Tests have been performed at 2 bar and temperatures up
to 500 ◦C.
Using the setup, the iron and ruthenium based catalyst have been reduced, their activity
characterized, and the eect of doing potassium during synthesis has been investigated.
Both catalysts appear to be reduced, but also show signs of subsequent oxidation in the
seemingly oxygen-free gas. The source of oxidation is unknown, but may be linked to two
unknown peaks in mass spectrometers.
The iron catalyst has activity similar to those reported in the literature. Dosing potassium
does not appear to increase the activity.
This maybe due to the iron catalyst already
containing potassium. Alternatively, the dosed potassium may be not reach, or settle on,
the catalyst.
The ruthenium catalyst showed surprisingly low activity. The exact cause of this is unknown.
Dosing potassium, which cleans the gas of oxidation sources as well, did not appear to
increase activity either.
To further investigate the eect of potassium dosage, the source of apparent oxidation should
be identied. Additionally, deposition of potassium on the catalyst should be veried. Im-
provements to the setup, which can be used to test other catalysts, include increasing signal
response rate.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Ammonia
Ammonia, NH3 is a chemical of extreme importance for humanity. For centuries, ammonia
has found use in explosives, cleaning, and dyeing. Today it is also widely used in metal
hardening, polymer production and articial fertilizers. Ammonia in large quantities are
required to feed the worlds growing population. It is estimated that world wide food pro-
duction could feed only 40% of the worlds current population without articial fertilizers
based on ammonia [1]. Ammonia has also been proposed as fuel for fuel cells, due to its high
content of hydrogen [2].
The wide applicability of ammonia is due to it being a source of easily accesible N, being
bound to 3 H. Although Earths atmosphere contains vast amounts of nitrogen in the form
of N2, this is not suitable for many applications requiring a supply of nitrogen. This is due
to the strong binding between the two N atoms, making the molecular N2 inert for many
purposes.
Luckily, mixing N2 and H2 in the stochiometric 3:1 ratio at ambient conditions gives a
thermodynamically equilibrium, in which all of nearly all gas is converted to ammonia.
Unfortunately, the process is terribly slow, again, due to N2's bond strength. If any ammonia
is to be formed within a reasonable timeframe, the process has to be accelerated. This can
be achieved by using a combination of higher temperatures and catalysts.
Synthesis using a catalyst
A catalyst is a material that is able to speed up a reaction process without itself being
consumed. The rst experiment demonstrating ammonia synthesis by use of a catalyst was
developed by the chemists Haber and Le Rossignol. Further work to scale the process to
industrial scale was performed by Bosch, with his assistant Mittasch performing 2,000 ex-
periments in search of a better catalyst. Mittasch found that iron from Sweden in particular
was a good, and cheap, catalyst [3].
The improved process, taking advantage of an eective catalyst, was named the Haber-Bosch
process. Initially, it was especially used to supply the German forces with explosives during
World War I, as the allied forces controlled supply routes for nitrate. Today, the process
supplies ammonia for a wide variety of applications, leading to a consumption of roughly
1% of the worlds total energy consumption. It is also known today that Swedish iron was
superior due to its potassium content. Catalysts based on iron with added potassium are
still the catalyst of choice for industry today.
Even when using a catalyst, industrially viable production of ammonia requires temperatures

Possible improvements and this work
Extensive work is being performed to nd a substitute for the large ammonia plants. Elec-
trochemical production, production using enzymes as well as improvements to the involved
catalyst are all approaches that are being investigated. This project aims at contributing to
the latter - improving a heterogeneous catalyst. Better, more active catalysts would allow
a reduction of synthesis temperature, and consequently also a lower working pressure.
Apart from iron-based catalysts, supported ruthenium has also been used on industrial
scale[4]. Common for the two, is that both exhibit signicantly more activity when promoted
with potassium [58]. It may be possible to increase activity further, by using potassium in
another way.
In iron catalysts, the potassium is present in an oxidized state. For ruthenium, it is well
established that metallic potassium is a better promoter than oxidized potassium.
This
work seeks to investigate whether the activity of iron can similarly be increased by using
metallic potassium rather than oxidized potassium. Additionally, investigations on potas-
siums eect as a promoter for ruthenium has added potassium rst, then tested activity
afterwards. In the time between addition and testing, the potassium may have partially
oxidized in the presence of trace oxygen or water.
The project also aims to investigate
whether activity may be higher before potassium oxidizes by measuring activity and adding
potassium simultaneously.
1.2
Approach
To investigate these hypotheses, a deeper understanding of ammonia catalysts are needed.
In order to obtain this, a literature study will be performed. The aim of this study is to
describe ammonia catalysts, and how promoters aect their activity. Additionally, the eect
of catalyst poisons will be investigated, to gauge the requirement for system purity.
To perform activity tests, and investigate the eect of metallic potassium, a testing setup is
required, which is capable of dosing potassium while measuring catalyst activity. To achieve
an active catalyst, and to sustain metallic potassium, a high testing system is required.
Verication of system purity is performed to ensure no oxidation sources exist for the potas-
sium. Activity of the catalyst is mapped out as a function of temperature, and compared to
literature. To test the eect of adding potassium, potassium is dosed while the temperature
is xed, which would readily reveal activity improvements.

CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND
A catalyst is a material that speeds up the reaction without itself being consumed. This is
achieved by allowing dierent bindings to occur on the catalyst, changing the mechanism
of the reaction. In ammonia synthesis without a catalyst, one has to break the N2 bond
directly, leading to a signicant activation energy, Ea for N2+3H2 →2NH3. Using a catalyst
allows dierent reaction mechanisms, and thus dierent activation energies. This principle
is illustrated in gure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: The basic principle of a catalyst. Reactants X, Y react to give product Z. With-
out a catalyst, a high energy barrier slows down the reaction rate. Using a catalyst enables
the reaction to occur over multiple subreaction with smaller energy barriers, increasing reac-
tion speed. Illustration by Wikimedia Commons, https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/
File:CatalysisScheme.png.
In the case of the Haber-Bosch process, catalysts help break the strong triple bond of nitrogen
by adsorbing the atomic nitrogen on the surface. In the atomic form, hydrogen atoms that
have also been adsorbed can bond with the nitrogen, and build up the ammonia molecule
step by step. This changes the reaction to a series of steps, which for iron-based catalysts
are illustrated in gure 2.3. The highest energy jump between two consecutive reaction steps
strongly determines the total reaction rate. It is therefore referred to as the rate-limiting
step. The rate-limiting step for ammonia synthesis is almost always the breaking of nitrogens
triple bond. Compared to the rate-limiting step, other steps happen quickly, and abundance
of species on two neighboring steps can be assumed to be in equilibrium.
To speed up a reaction, a catalyst needs to be able to adsorb reactant species well, but also
bind the product species weakly to allow new reactions to occur. An ideal catalyst bind the
reactants very strongly, while barely binding products. Binding energies for adsorbates and
catalysts vary. Often transition metals make great catalysts. This can be explained by the
properties of partially lled d-bands [12]. It has been found, however, that the adsorption
energy of reactants and product scale linearly [13]. Pure metals inherently bind a specic
species strongly or weakly, while a good catalyst has to balance these two. As a result, metal
will be scattered around an optimum, leading to a volcano plot, see gure 2.4. For ammonia
synthesis, an optimum is seen when plotting turn-over-frequency against the adsorption
energy of atomic nitrogen. In particular, the best ammonia catalysts, Fe, Ru, and Os are
the ones closest to this optimum.

2.2
Iron-based catalysts
Iron has proven to be a good ammonia catalyst, and combined with a low price, has become
the go-to choice for industry. As such, it has been studied extensively. This chapter is based
on the information gathered by Nielsen et. al [5] on industrial iron-catalysts.
Commercial iron catalyst is produced by melting iron and adding promoters directly in
the melt. The most important promoters is Al, Ca, and K. Since these have two dierent
purposes, the catalyst is called doubly promoted. Eects of these dierent promoters are
discussed in section 2.2.1. After cooling, the melt is ground to particles of a suitable size.
The catalyst is then reduced at temperatures between 400 ◦C and 800 ◦C, with maximal
activity reached by reduction between ∼400 ◦C and ∼500 ◦C. Reduction causes the iron
to become metallic, while the promoters largely stay oxidized. Due to the loss of oxygen
when going from Fe2O3 to Fe, a porous structure is formed. In addition to the activation
from becoming metallic, the porosity greatly increases the surface area, further increasing
activity.
The rate-limiting step for doubly promoted iron is the breaking of N2 bonds. During am-
monia synthesis, the iron surface is generally covered in N-species. A collection of activity
measurements for promoted iron is given in gure 2.5.

2.2. IRON-BASED CATALYSTS
2.2.1
Promoters
Addition of promoters to the iron catalyst greatly improves the performance. Promoters
are split into two categories: Structural promoters and electronic promoters. Iron catalysts
usually contain both kinds, and is in that case called doubly promoted.
In doubly promoted iron catalysts, Al and Ca are structural promoters, whose main purpose
is to increase the mechanical strength of the catalyst. This conserves porosity and grain size
during use. As such, they are not directly aecting reaction mechanisms, but are import for
high activity regardless.
K act as an electronic promoter, which alters the activity of the catalyst.
As an alkali
metal, K easily donates electrons.
Studies show that potassium migrates to the surface
during reduction and synthesis, and stays near oxygen when at the surface.
Multiple reasons for the promoting structure of K has been given. These include
 Electron donation alters the electronic structure locally
 Potassium aects grain size, helping particles grow to a critical size for catalytic ac-
tivity
 Donating electrons leads to an electrostatic repulsion/attraction for some intermedi-
ates, changing their adsorption strength.
The last listed theory is the most well-established. Density Function Theory (DFT) cal-
culations predict the energy levels during adsorption and dissociation of N2 on Fe[11]. An
illustration of the reaction path for adsorption and dissociation of N2 on promoted and un-
promoted iron is given in gure 2.6. Potassium evidently causes N2 to adsorb more strongly.
As the rate-limiting step for iron is the breaking of N2, an increased number of adsorbed N2
species would increase the number of broken bonds, and thus activity.
2.2.2
Poisoning
Iron catalysts can be poisoned by a variety of species. In general, poisons are split into two
categories: Permanent poisons and temporary poisons. Permanent poisons include S, Cl
and P, which cannot be baked o of the catalyst easily. Concentrations as low as 20 ppm
S or ppb levels of Cl aects catalyst activity. Temporary poisons are mainly O-containing
species, e.g. H2O, O2, CO2. The poisoning eect of these have been shown to depend only
on the amount of O, as the catalyst will facilitate reactions to release the oxygen. Even very
small amounts of O-species will aect the iron catalyst, see gure 2.7. Although these data
are for high pressures, the eect is also severe at lower pressures.

2.3
Ruthenium-based catalysts
Ruthenium has proven to be an even better catalyst than iron [7]. Since Ru is not very
abundant, activity per mass is optimized by using supported nanoparticles to increase surface
area per metal mass.
The rate-limiting step in the reaction pathway is, like for iron catalysts, the dissociation of
nitrogen[7,17]. Activation of N2 has been shown to occur almost exclusively on a particular
kind of edge sites, while terrace sites are largely inactive [18]. The edge sites are also the
most reactive, causing them to be easily oxidized [19].
By using a support material, structural promoters are not required for Ru catalysts. Drastic
activity increases are observed by promoting with K [68]. The increase in activity depends
on the method used for adding K. Using salts to donate K+/KOH leads to a lower activity
than using metallic K, see gure 2.8.
Figure 2.8 shows how K greatly increases activity of Ru catalysts. However, the trend is not
completely trivial. Metallic K shows a signicantly higher activity boost, while KOH is less
eective, in one instance even counterproductive. The decrease in activity is likely due to
covering of nanoparticles, see section 2.3.3.
The cause of the increased activity is likely the same as for Fe - stabilization of adsorbed
N2. Adding K, or other alkali metals, also has other inuences on the catalyst. These will
be described briey below.
2.3.1
Other alkali metals as promoters
Other alkali metals show properties similar to K as promoter. Cs, Rb, and Na has been tested
to work as promoters, with relative eectiveness increasing with increasing electronegativ-
ity[20]. Extending this relation explains why oxidized versions of alkalis are less eective, in
agreement with gure 2.8. This further supports the idea of K stabilizing adsorbed nitrogen.

Experimental Setup
This project aims to test activity of iron and ruthenium catalysts before and during dosing
of potassium, leading to some strict requirements on purity and analysis resolution. Testing
under these conditions is performed on the Very High Pressure Setup, VHP. The setup is
build to test heterogeneous catalysts at pressure of several bar, which is very high compared
to the surrounding Ultra-High Vacuum setups.
The strict demands required an altering of the available setup, which lead to the design
illustrated in gure 3.1. The catalysts to be tested are extremely sensitive to water and
oxygen, as discussed in chapter 2. While performing activity measurements, the setup has
to dose metallic potassium, which oxidizes very rapidly. As a consequence, a completely dry,
oxygen-free gas ow is required. To ensure this, high purity gas is used in the experiments.
The gas is further puried using oxygen traps and zeolites, as described below.
After cleansing, the gas has the option of being passed through a potassium doser, before
being carried to a plug ow reactor in a furnace for synthesis. The resulting gas is analyzed
using a Quadrupole Mass Spectrometer (QMS) and a Time of Flight Mass Spectrometer
(TOF). Further details on the individual components are presented in this chapter.

3.1
Piping and pressure control
The setup is built using Swagelok VCR tubing, using 1/8 inch tubing wherever possible.
Approximately half of the tubing is 1/4 inch. This is required in order to mount the setup on
the supporting rack, adding pressure gauges, and to create the doser system. Due to shortage
of some components, adapters and plugs has been applied liberally. This unavoidably leads
to dead volumes, which together with the increased cross sections leads to transient delays.
A look on delay times in the system is given in chapter 5.
Multiple pressure levels are present in the setup. The setup is designed such that catalysts
can be tested at pressures up to 10 bar. This pressure is regulated using a pressure regulator,
behind which a dry pump create rough vacuum (∼1 mbar). From this rough vacuum, gas is
let to mass spectrometers using leak valves, see sections 3.6 and 3.7. The parts of the setup
before the pressure regulator is denoted as the frontside, while the part after is called the
backside.
Pressures are measured using a series of pressure gauges, summarized in table 3.1. Baratrons
gauge the pressure by measuring the force on a small area, thus giving an absolute pressure
reading at the cost of accuracy. Thermal conductivity (TC) gauges are more precise, relies
on measurements of thermal conductivity, thus being gas dependent. In particular, H2 has
a very high thermal conductivity, making the TC gauges unusable on the setups frontside.
Ion gauges are used in the mass spectrometers to measure high vacuum, but are also gas
dependent.
In order to avoid sticking of water, ammonia, and potassium throughout the system, all
tubing after the gas inlet is heat traced. The backside is heated to 100 ◦C - 120 ◦C. Valves
on the front side can't handle temperatures above 80 ◦C, so this tubing is kept at 90 ◦C -
100 ◦C, with valves untraced. The pressure regulator can handle up to 70 ◦C, so it is kept
near 60 ◦C.
Gases are let into the mass spectrometers from the backside of the setup. Pressure reduction
from ∼1 mbar to ∼10−6 mbar is achieved by using leak valves, allowing adjustable pressure
in the mass spectrometers.

3.6
Quadrupole Mass Spectrometer
One way to analyze the gas composition after reaction is by using a Quadrupole Mass
Spectrometer (QMS). An ionization source is placed inside a vacuum chamber. Here, a ray
of electrons ionize some of the inlet gas. Four parallel metal rods are are placed after the
ionization region, see gure 3.4. These rods all have a voltage applied to guide ions. This
voltage has a high positive DC component U0, which will repel the created cations, and a
smaller AC signal of amplitude ∆U and frequency ω, resulting in a time-dependent voltage:
U = U0 + ∆U cos(ωt).
(3.2)
Opposing rods will have the same potential. Ions aected by the arising electric elds will
spiral between the four rods, with a trajectory depending on ∆U, ω, and the ions mass
to charge ratio m/q. Heavy ions will be aected less by the AC signal, and will tend to
drift out of the rods, see gure 3.4.
Conversely, light ions will be greatly aected, and
will tend to spiral out of the rods. Ions with just the right mass will be led through the
rods, where a detector is placed. This current of ions is amplied, and translates into a
signal that correlates with the concentrations of ions with that specic mass. Since the ion
current follows the concentration of species, the signal indicates the concentration of species
in the inlet gas. Scanning over dierent values of U allows an overview of present species,
and tracking consecutive masses provides information on gas composition changes as time
progresses.
Figure 3.4: Left: Schematic view of the inside of a QMS. Right: Working principle of the
Quadrupole Rods. An AC signal is applied pairwise on the rods (white circles). Heavy ions
(green) and light ions (blue) will drift out of the rods, and not reach the detector on the
end. Ions with a specic mass charge ratio will spiral in a stable trajectory to the end of
the rods.
The mass resolution of a QMS depends on the voltage settings, vacuum quality, and can be
enhanced by using bend poles to avoid false counts. The setup used in this project can be
tuned slightly, but mass resolutions cannot reach much below 1 amu.

Since the signal does not depend directly on the mass m but on m/q, one should be careful
when analyzing mass spectra from a QMS. Double ionized molecules 2q with mass 2m will
have signals overlapping with 1q, 1m species. Additionally, isotope eects and fractured
molecules will lead to signals apart from a primary peak. For instance, NH3 with mass 17
u will also show smaller peaks at 16 u and 15 u, with only small amounts of double ionized
species. Water, which is always present in a vacuum chamber, overlaps with peaks on 18 u,
17 u and 16 u, while 15N overlaps with 15 u. To understand the composition, then, one must
account for all observed peaks simultaneously. Wanting to detect small concentrations of
ammonia, which overlaps with water signal, the QMS could not be used for any quantitative
measurements. It has the advantage of a higher time resolution than the Time of Flight
Mass Spectrometer, however, which has proven useful in this project.

3.7
Time of Flight Mass Spectrometer
The other way to analyze gas composition on the VHP setup is by a Time of Flight (TOF)
Mass Spectrometer. The quite large apparatus is shown in gure 3.5, with a schematic of
components in gure 3.6. A more realistic, but less illustrative, drawing from the manufac-
turer is given in appendix A.
Like for the QMS, gas is let in and ionized, and is guided using applied voltages. Unlike the
QMS, and as implied by the name, a TOF relies on diering ight times of dierent ions
to analyze gas composition. After ionization, ions are focused into a beam using a series of
lenses, see gure 3.7. This beam travels between two grids. One of the grids is grounded,
while the other is exposed to square pulses of high voltage. This deects the beam, and
allows ions deected at 7° to enter a long ight tube, where ions with dierent velocities will
separate in space. This tube is exposed to a high voltage (∼2 kV) to keep ions from the
walls, and is surrounded by a larger, grounded tube to protect users. Just before the end of
the ∼1 m tube, a series of grids make out a reectron. By applying voltages to this grid,
ions will reect and be guided onto a Multi Channel Plate (MCP), creating an electrical
impulse.
Figure 3.5: Photo of the Time of Flight Mass Spectrometer. Picture by Robert Jensen.
Using a reectron has two advantages compared to placing the Multi Channel Plate at the
end of the ight tube: It increases the ions ight distance, thus increasing separation between
dierent species, thereby improving resolution. Secondly, it helps correct for dierences in
ion speeds introduced by the push zone. As some ions are closer/further away from the
pulsed grid, they will travel slightly faster/slower than those in the middle, see gure 3.7.
In the reectron, the ions moving faster will reach further into the reection zone and vice
versa. Ideally, this will cause them to catch up to the centered ions. Slower ions will likewise
turn earlier, also catching up. This is illustrated in gure 3.8.

Mass resolution is determined by quality of, and tuning voltages at, the lenses, grid and
reectron. Tuning of voltages are performed as to optimize signal strength around ammonia,
without loosing too much peak sharpness or signal of other species. Tuning the energy of
electrons used for ion generation is also possible. The choice of ionization energy is discussed
in section 4.3. Detection limits are largely determined by the amount of dead counts (counts
from imperfect vacuum in the ight zone).
A spectrum is obtained by pulsing ions and observing whether an ion generates an electrical
pulse within a given time interval. These intervals are of 40 ns length. After all the pulsed
ions have hit the plate, a new pulse is generated, and detections begin anew. This is then
repeated many times, in this case 3000 times per second.
Since a detection is discrete, two ions hitting simultaneously will appear as one count, thus
miscounting the concentration. The ratio of pulses to counts is called the normalized count
number.
In order to avoid large mistakes to missing double (or triple, etc) counts, the
ion current is tuned so no normalized count number reaches above 0.2. By lowering the
normalized count number, the probability of missing counts is lowered. This is achieved by
adjusting the pressure inside the gas-tight ion source using leak valves, and by adjusting the
lament current.
Peak shapes and locations in the TOF are aected by multiple factors, such as:
 Ions exiting the ion source will have a nite beam width, leading to dierent velocities
due to pulse, as well as travel distances
 Ion optics are tunable, and their eect on each ion depends on the ions position
 The reectron reects ions dierently depending on their velocity
 The ionization potential seems to inuence the peak locations, indicating ion velocities
are aected as well.
These make it dicult to give a theoretical prediction of peak shape. The voltage of ion
optics, reectron and liner has been chosen as to optimize sharpness of peaks without losing
too much signal strength.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This project has investigated ammonia catalysts, and potassium eect on their activity.
Established literature has given an insight in promoters for these catalysts. It clearly shows
the importance of potassium to increase activity. To maximize the eect of K in Ru, metallic
phase K should be used. This project has aimed to establish whether metallic potassium
also improves iron catalysts, and if ruthenium is additionally active before possible oxidation
of added potassium.
In order to test this hypothesis, a setup has been established that is able to dose metallic
potassium and measure catalytic activity simultaneously. The inlet gas stream is puried,
and the outlet is analyzed primarily using a time of ight mass spectrometer. The setup has
proven useful in characterizing catalysts, although signals experience signicant time delays.
Analysis of obtained spectra has been formalized and implemented. Studying spectra to
gauge purity show an oxygen-free environment, and indicates dry gas stream. Two uniden-
tied peaks are observed, which may inuence catalyst activity, but their exact inuence is
unknown.
Ruthenium catalysts were tested from two suppliers and with dierent loadings. Both used
an alumina support. The catalysts did not seem to have any signicant activity, and showed
signs of oxidation after being cooled. Dosing metallic potassium did not appear to alter the
activity. The potassium may not have reached the catalyst to promote activity. However,
it should remove any oxidation source, allowing activity of the catalyst, but this was not
observed. The unidentied peaks may poison the Ru catalyst.
Finally a doubly promoted iron catalyst was tested. The activity of this agreed with those
reported in the literature, although this catalyst also showed sign of oxidation after cooling.
Dosing potassium on the iron catalyst did not appear to increase activity either.
It is
possible the potassium has not reached or settled on the catalyst. Since doubly promoted
iron already contains potassium, although oxidized, additional potassium may not provide
additional activity increase.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
46
==PAGE:53==
Chapter 7
Outlook
The investigation of activity increase by use of metallic potassium is largely inconclusive.
Partly because it is uncertain whether potassium has settled on the catalysts - this is cur-
rently being investigated by XPS analysis on the iron catalyst. Partly because both catalysts
appear to oxidize when it is crucial that they do not. In order to get a more conclusive an-
swer, the cause of oxidation (or apparent oxidation) would have to be identied and xed.
A rst step towards this is determining and removing the species appearing at mass 26 amu
and 27 amu.
As for the setup itself, some very long signal transients are present. To reduce these tran-
sients, and get better analyses, these should be reduced. That could be accomplished by
installing a snier setup instead of leaking gas in from the setup backside. An additional
advantage of a snier system is a shorter path for gas travel, helping reduce eect of sticking.
Sticking could further be prevented by implementing valves that can sustain higher temper-
ature, such that the tubing itself can be heated more. Improving signal response speed, and
reducing sticking, would make the setup more usable regardless of which catalysts are being
tested.
If no potassium has reached the catalyst, it would need to be dosed another way. A start
would be to reduce the distance from doser to reactor. A more reliable way to control dosing
may also be of interest, especially if an eect is observed, and the amount of deposited K
is of relevance. This could be achieved by better heat control for the doser, or by a volume
being dosed with potassium before releasing it into the gas stream.
