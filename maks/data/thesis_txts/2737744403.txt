Abstract
AV Miljø is a modern disposal site, representing the majority of the Danish landﬁlls, as it contains waste
with low organic content. A full-scale in-situ aeration project was initiated in May 2023 at one of the
waste disposal cells (Cell 1.3) at the AV Miljø. The objective of the Master’s Thesis was to assess the
stability of the system, as well as a potential optimization of the system and thereby evaluate how eﬃcient
landﬁll aeration is to shorten the aftercare period and limit the associated costs. The evaluation was based
on measurements of temperature, water level, pressure, and gas composition in the stationary monitoring
wells across the cell. During a campaign with four measurement days in a row, the results showed a stable
system in the unsaturated areas of the landﬁll. The results indicated that the gas composition in the
waste was primarily a product of aerobic degradation. The system was highly a↵ected by precipitation, as
the only external factor, creating anaerobic niches within the waste mass. These areas showed oscillation
in the results and cannot be concluded as stable. A large variation across the disposal cell was observed,
due to the heterogeneous of waste mass. Temperatures ranged from 12-66°C, whereas the water level
ranged between 0-3 m in the monitoring wells with only a few meters apart. The pressure within the
cell was observed to be equalized across the cell. By reducing the injected airﬂow in selected aeration
wells, no to little e↵ect on the temperature and gas composition was observed. The adjusted airﬂow could
therefore be a potential optimization of the system, resulting in cost savings without a↵ecting the system.
An insuﬃcient supply of oxygen was observed in the middle part of the waste mass, risen concerns of
an accumulation of methane beneath the top cover. A further investigation of this showed high levels
of oxygen at the top of the waste mass, potentially caused by an induction of atmospheric air through
the top cover. It is important to state that this study is based on very few and local data measurements
which limits the study. Active aeration at AV Miljø has resulted in an enhanced aerobic degradation
based on the higher temperatures and lower CH4/CO2 ratios observed. It suggests an acceleration of the
bio-stability of the landﬁll, but further investigations and a longer duration of aeration are necessary to
evaluate the potential reduction of the aftercare period. Other case studies across Europe have shown
promising results with active aeration of landﬁlls. Despite this, landﬁll aeration still lacks suﬃcient data
and established guidelines.

Sammenfatning
AV Miljø er et moderne deponeringsanlæg, der repræsenterer størstedelen af de danske deponeringsanlæg,
da det best˚ar af a↵ald med lavt organisk indhold. Et beluftningsprojekt blev igangsat i maj 2023 i en
af a↵aldsdeponeringscellerne (Celle 1.3) p˚a AV Miljø. Form˚alet med kandidatopgaven var at vurdere
systemets stabilitet samt en potentiel optimering af systemet og dermed evaluere, hvor e↵ektiv depon-
ibeluftning er til at forkorte efterbehandlingsperioden og begrænse de tilknyttede omkostninger. Eval-
ueringen var baseret p˚a m˚alinger af temperatur, vandstand, tryk og gaskomposition i de stationære
overv˚agningsbrønde p˚a tværs af cellen. Under en kampagne med ﬁre m˚aledage i træk viste resultaterne
et stabilt system i de umættede omr˚ader af deponiet. Resultaterne indikerede, at gaskompositionen i
a↵aldet primært var et produkt af aerob nedbrydning. Systemet var stærkt p˚avirket af nedbør, som den
eneste eksterne faktor, der skabte anaerobe nicher i a↵aldsmassen. Disse omr˚ader viste svingninger i
resultaterne og kan ikke konkluderes som stabile. En stor variation p˚a tværs af deponeringscellen blev
observeret p˚a grund af a↵aldsmassens heterogenitet. Temperaturerne varierede fra 12-66°C, mens vand-
standen i overv˚agningsbrøndene varierede mellem 0-3 m i overv˚agningsbrøndene, trods en afstand p˚a kun
f˚a meter fra hinanden. Trykket inden i cellen blev observeret at være udlignet p˚a tværs af cellen. Ved at
reducere den tilførte luft i udvalgte beluftningsbrønde blev der observeret minimial e↵ekt p˚a temperat-
uren og gaskompositionen. Den justerede luftstrøm tilførelse kunne derfor være en potentiel optimering
af systemet, hvilket kan resultere i omkostningsbesparelser uden at p˚avirke systemet. En utilstrækkelig
iltforsyning blev dogobserveret i den midterste del af a↵aldsmassen, hvilket rejste bekymringer om en
ophobning af metan under topdækket. En nærmere undersøgelse af dette viste høje iltniveauer i toppen
af a↵aldsmassen, potentielt for˚arsaget af induktion af atmosfærisk luft gennem topdækket. Det er vigtigt
at p˚apege, at denne undersøgelse er baseret p˚a meget f˚a og lokale datam˚alinger, hvilket begrænser un-
dersøgelsen. Aktiv beluftning p˚a AV Miljø har resulteret i en forbedret aerob nedbrydning baseret p˚a de
højere temperaturer og lavere CH4/CO2-forhold. Det antyder en acceleration af deponiets biostabilitet,
men yderligere undersøgelser og en længere varighed af beluftning er nødvendig for at evaluere den po-
tentielle reduktion af efterbehandlingsperioden. Andre case-studier i Europa har vist lovende resultater
med aktiv beluftning af deponier. P˚a trods af dette mangler landﬁll aeration stadig tilstrækkelige data
og etablerede retningslinjer.

1
Introduction
Environmental pollution is largely driven by human activities, with signiﬁcant emissions originating from
waste management practices such as landﬁlling, waste incineration, and biological treatments [Cossu and
Stegmann, 2019a]. Global waste management inﬂuences several of the Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs). SDG 12 - Responsible Consumption and Production aims to reduce consumption and waste
generation, as well as promote responsible waste management. Additionally, poor waste management
impacts; SDG 6 - Clean Water and Sanitation, SDG 13 - Climate Action, SDG 14 - Life Below Water,
and SDG 15 - Life on Land [United Nations, 2023].
The global municipal solid waste (MSW) generation rates vary across regions and are inﬂuenced by
economic development, industrialization, urbanization, and climate conditions. The annual generation
of MSW is expected to increase to 3.4 billion tonnes by 2050. Most of the waste generated globally is
either dumped or disposed at a landﬁll, with the latter accounting for 37% of the waste. The globally
solid waste treatment and disposal emitted 1.6 billion tonnes of carbon dioxide in 2016, which accounts
for approximately 5% of the world’s emissions [Kaza et al., 2018].
Landﬁlling has several negative impacts on the environment, mainly through leachate and gaseous emis-
sions. The production rate of LFG and the quality of the leachate depends on the construction and
operation of the landﬁll, and the composition of the waste. It also relies on the age of the landﬁll and
the environmental factors such as the temperature and bacteria present in the waste [Wang et al., 2021].
When water penetrates waste, forming leachate, it carries numerous pollutants that contaminate the
surrounding environment, including water basins, soil, and sediments [Wang et al., 2021]. Greenhouse
gases (GHGs) are formed by the anaerobic degradation of organic content. Landﬁll gases (LFG) mainly
consist of methane (55-60% vol) and carbon dioxide (40-45% vol) for an old landﬁll [Scheutz et al., 2009].
The production of LFG will continue as long as organic matter is present in the waste mass, which can
last for decades at an anaerobic landﬁll. Methane and carbon dioxide are both GHGs contributing to
the global warming [Scheutz et al., 2009]. The global warming potential for methane is 28 times higher
than carbon dioxide over 100 years, as methane has a stronger molar absorption coeﬃcient [IPCC and
Edenhofer, 2014, Solomon et al., 2007]. Natural atmospheric methane levels have by far been exceeded,
underpinning the urgent need for actions to reduce and limit emissions. More than 60% of the total
methane budget is anthropogenic, whereas 18% of it can be attributed to the global landﬁlls and waste
management [Masson-Delmotte et al., 2021].
The LFG and contaminated leachate generated from landﬁlls results in a long aftercare period. Accord-
ing to the current European landﬁll legislation, the aftercare period lasts more than 30 years, where
the maintenance and monitoring need to be ﬁnancially covered [Cossu and Pivato, 2019].
Numerous
investigations on landﬁlls indicate that the duration of aftercare may extend well beyond the initially
projected time frame. The current landﬁlls that are ﬁnalizing the aftercare period are still emitting LFG
1
==PAGE:16==
and contaminated leachate and yet not in a biological stabilized state [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2013].
Therefore, urgent action is needed to establish more sustainable landﬁlls to reduce both current and
long-term emissions and thereby shorten the aftercare period. In-situ aeration is a potential method to
achieve this. By injecting atmospheric air into the waste mass, aerobic conditions are facilitated which
accelerate the degradation of organic matter, resulting in the landﬁll reaching a state of biological sta-
bility earlier [Hrad et al., 2013]. Landﬁll aeration creates several potential beneﬁts including improving
leachate quality and minimizing the production of methane [Bonilla Prado et al., 2023]. The theoretical
CO2 equivalent emissions caused by methane production from a landﬁll are estimated to be reduced by
77% under aerobic conditions [Ritzkowski et al., 2006]. Landﬁll aeration has been recognized by the
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) as a viable approach for reducing greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions within the waste sector [IPCC and Edenhofer, 2014]. Additionally, the United Nations
Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) has approved this technology for generating certi-
ﬁed emission reductions (CERs) through clean development mechanism (CDM) projects [Ritzkowski and
Stegmann, 2010, UNFCCC, 2009].
Several in-situ aeration concepts have been used in Europe, Asia, and North America, which have shown
promising results [Hrad et al., 2013]. In 2023, Denmark initiated the ﬁrst in-situ aeration project to invest-
igate its performance and explore the potential of application across the Danish landﬁlls [Bonilla Prado
et al., 2023]. The pilot project is operated through a collaboration between AV Miljø, Niras, and The
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The aeration concept is continuously monitored.
1.1
The Objectives
The study aim is to evaluate how eﬃcient landﬁll aeration in Danish landﬁlls is to shorten the aftercare
period and thereby limit the associated costs. The project covers the two objectives:
1. The stability of the system will be investigated to understand the inﬂuence of external factors.
2. A potential optimization of the system will be assessed by reducing the ﬂow rate of the injected air.

important landﬁll theory used to understand the dynamics of the results.

2
State of the Art
In this section, the general aspects of a landﬁll and the degradation of waste will be presented. Further-
more, this section will dive into the concept of in-situ aeration of landﬁlls as well as provide an overview
of existing cases of full-scale landﬁll aeration worldwide.
2.1
Landﬁlls in General
Landﬁlls are one of the least preferred options in the waste management hierarchy and should only be
utilized when prevention, reuse, recycling, and recovery are not feasible [Scheutz et al., 2011]. The life
cycle of a landﬁll undergoes three life stages; the construction stage, the operation stage, and the aftercare
stage, which are shown in Figure 1. The construction stage consists of excavating and backﬁlling soil as
well as the establishment of the landﬁll, including groundwater drainage system, barrier layer such as
gravel, bottom liner, leachate, and LFG collection system. The design of a landﬁll can vary based on
the construction of the waste. The waste can either be placed below ground level, above ground level, or
both, depending on the existing terrain. Accordingly, leachate ﬂows out naturally by gravity or is being
pumped out [Cossu and Stegmann, 2019b]. During the operational stage, the primary activities involve the
distribution and compaction of waste into layers, as well as the management of leachate and LFG [Cossu
and Pivato, 2019]. When a landﬁll cell receives waste, it is shredded into smaller pieces, pushed around
for mixing, and distributed in a thin layer before being compacted to minimize the volume. When the
landﬁll is ﬁlled up at the end of the operational stage, a natural layer which can vary in soil composition,
density, and permeability is added as a top cover to prevent possible impact from the waste [Cossu and
Raga, 2019].
Figure 1: The three life stages of a landﬁll, [Cossu and Pivato, 2019].
After the ﬁnal top cover is placed, the aftercare stage starts [Cossu and Pivato, 2019]. The aftercare stage
includes monitoring and maintenance of the leachate and LFG collection systems as well as monitoring
and maintenance of the top cover to prevent LFG emissions. If the landﬁll is not constructed with a
leachate collection system and/or a bottom liner, the groundwater is monitored and controlled as well.
The duration of the aftercare stage is a critical decision, as landﬁlls can emit pollutants into the air or
groundwater for several decades. Although the decision is somewhat subjective, it can signiﬁcantly impact
the assessment of the landﬁll [Cossu and Pivato, 2019].
4
==PAGE:19==
[Miljøministeriet, 2009] sets the duration of the aftercare period to 30 years, unless the approval authority
considers that the characteristics of the waste mass justify a di↵erent duration. In most countries, no
speciﬁc limit values are set to determine when the aftercare stage can stop [Miljøministeriet, 2009].
Germany was one of the ﬁrst countries to deﬁne a proposal for a monitoring plan and target values for
closing the landﬁll after the aftercare period. The German stop criterion is deﬁned by the requirement
of a maximum methane load for the top cover. Austria has progressed even further by setting a speciﬁc
limit value of the emitted methane through the top cover to be less than 0.77 gCH4/(m2 ˙day) [Kjeldsen
and Scheultz, 2015]. England is the most ambitious country, as they in 2015 introduced legislation with
a limit value of methane emission 0.09 gCH4/(m2 ˙day), which had to be met before the landﬁll could be
considered ﬁnalized [Kjeldsen and Scheultz, 2015].
2.1.1
Degradation and Gas Production of Landﬁlls
Landﬁlls undergo di↵erent phases during the operational and aftercare stage, where di↵erent biological
processes occur in the waste mass according to the circumstances [Rich et al., 2008]. The gas composition
and production are determined by aerobic or anaerobic degradation, depending on the presence of oxygen.
The total gas production and the concentration of the main compounds (Nitrogen N2, Methane CH4,
Carbon Dioxide CO2 and Oxygen O2) in the landﬁll changes accordingly over time. This is illustrated in
Figure 2, showing gas concentration (% vol) and annual LFG production (% of the maximum value) over
time. The landﬁll undergoes nine phases in its lifetime, which also are included in the graph. Some of
the phases occur simultaneously and the dominance of each phase will di↵er depending on the time and
the di↵erent parts of the landﬁll [Rich et al., 2008].
Figure 2: The nine production phases of the main LFG compounds (N2, CH4, CO2 and O2) and the total gas
production over time. The gas concentration (% vol) is shown on the left axis and the annual landﬁll gas
production (as a % of the maximum value) on the right [Ritzkowski et al., 2006].
5
==PAGE:20==
Phase I-III
The gas production begins as soon as the waste is dumped on the site, where oxygen and nitrogen are
still present. Thus the initial phases occur under aerobic conditions and will continue until depletion of
the available oxygen [Joshi and Pant, 2018]. In the initial phases, hydrolysis dominates as the substrate is
readily accessible, and aerobic bacteria are present [Rich et al., 2008, Joshi and Pant, 2018]. Through the
enzymatic hydrolysis, complex lipids, carbohydrates, and proteins are degraded into smaller compounds.
The by-products produced are carbon dioxide and water, which are discharged through leachate and LFG
[Cossu et al., 2019].
Phase IV and V
After applying the ﬁnal top cover the anaerobic degradation phase is initiated as the oxygen supply
becomes limited. Anaerobic degradation is divided into four di↵erent processes; hydrolysis, acid ferment-
ation, acetogenesis, and methanogenesis. Each process depends on the di↵erent microorganisms that are
present [Cossu et al., 2019]. The anaerobic degradation is a considerably slower kinetic of metabolism,
compared to the aerobic degradation, which accounts for the long duration of this phase. Short-chain fatty
acids, hydrogen (H2), and the highest production of carbon dioxide are produced in this phase resulting
in the landﬁll being very acidic [Joshi and Pant, 2018]. The optimal conditions for methanogenesis are a
pH between 6-8 and a moisture content of 60% by weight or above [Joshi and Pant, 2018]. Furthermore,
the growth of methanogenic microbes is enhanced with temperatures in the range of 35-45°C [Joshi and
Pant, 2018]. Methane and carbon dioxide are the main products during these phases. As seen in Figure 2
the volume percentage of the gas composition at these stages, is approximately CH4: 60-70% vol, CO2:
35-45% vol, and the remaining 2-10% vol are other gases such as water vapor and sulﬁdes [Joshi and
Pant, 2018]. During phase V the share of methane and carbon dioxide remains close to stable as seen in
Figure 2, but the total LFG production decreases as the amount of easily degradable waste will be reduced
[Ritzkowski et al., 2006]. Depending on the waste composition and surrounding conditions, phase IV and
V will remain constant for approximately 20 years. If the magnitude of organic matter is suﬃcient in the
waste mass, the LFG can continuously be emitted for more than 50 years [Joshi and Pant, 2018].
Phase VI-IX
After most of the organic matter is degraded during phases IV and V, the structure of the waste mass
will change and facilitate new pathways for the gas transport and allow penetration of atmospheric air
through the top cover. The organic matter in the waste mass will continuously be degraded in phases VII
and VIII, before the landﬁll can be considered ﬁnalized [Ritzkowski et al., 2006]. Due to the waste mass
being heterogeneous, oxygen will not reach all parts of the landﬁll, which can cause a local production of
methane. As methane oxidation is a slow process, methane is likely to be emitted before being oxidized
even though oxygen is present. Methane can therefore be present in the o↵-gas during phase IX [Ritzkowski
et al., 2006]. The di↵erent phases can describe the state of a landﬁll and are determined by the ratio
between the LFG concentration.
6
==PAGE:21==
Temperature
The biological degradation processes under both anaerobic and aerobic conditions are exothermic. The
generation of heat is much higher during aerobic degradation compared to anaerobic degradation, with
temperatures ranging between 50-70°C, compared to 35-45°C, respectively [Cossu et al., 2019].
The
energy production for anaerobic degradation corresponds to 10% of the energy production during aerobic
degradation. The generated heat in the waste mass can also increase due to other exothermic degradation
processes depending on the waste material. For example, iron corrosion which is an oxidation process that
occurs in the presence of water, and is a highly exothermic reaction [Nazaro↵and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001]
[Grande and Didriksen, 2021]. Under aerobic conditions growth of bacteria increases and enhances the
biological processes. The range of temperatures may activate di↵erent microbial groups, thus accelerating
degradation [Cossu et al., 2019]. For instance, methanogens can be psychrophilic (15-20 °C), mesophilic
(33-40 °C), or thermophilic (55-60 °C). By temperatures higher than these ranges, methane production
may decrease or be inhibited, which may result in local varying temperatures inside landﬁll [Cossu et al.,
2019]
Moisture
Water ﬂux and the moisture content of a landﬁll are essential for the bacteria and the processes occurring
within the waste mass.
The water supports the hydrolytic process, carries nutrients, dilutes toxins,
and enhances the movement of the microorganisms [Cossu et al., 2019]. Raw MSW can have a water
content ranging between 30-60% by weight, with some areas reaching ﬁeld capacity at 50-60% of total
solids. To support biological processes, a minimum moisture content over 20-30% by weight is necessary
[Cossu et al., 2019]. However, an excessive amount of water may accumulate in pockets within the waste
mass and thereby reduce or even inhibit some of the biological processes and the gas transport due to
leachate-saturated areas [Cossu and Raga, 2019].
2.1.2
Landﬁll Gas Transport
The landﬁll gas can move freely in all three dimensions towards unsaturated zones [Nilausen et al., 2001].
The waste mass is considered a porous material as it contains solids with distributed void spaces [Nazaro↵
and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001]. The particle size and the density of the waste are strongly inﬂuenced by the
deposition, degradation, and compaction of the waste mass which results in a complex transport path
for gases and ﬂuids [Cossu et al., 2019], [Nazaro↵and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001]. Gas migration through the
waste is a↵ected by the two main mechanisms advection and di↵usion [Nilausen et al., 2001]. Advective
transport is driven by a pressure gradient, which can be induced by the barometric pressure, wind, and
increased pressure in the waste mass, due to LFG production [Scheutz et al., 2009].
The advective
transport in porous media can be described by Darcy’s Law in Equation 1.

increases the methane concentrations, as the volume of the bubbles increases due to higher microbial
activity. A temperature di↵erence may furthermore lead to the movement or mixing of leachate in the
waste mass, this transfer of heat is an advective transport process called convection [Nazaro↵and Alvarez-
Cohen, 2001].
2.1.3
Leachate in Landﬁlls
Leachate is the liquid that percolates through a landﬁll and is typically formed by the water that seeps
through the waste or the moisture generated by degradation processes [Bhatt et al., 2024]. Landﬁlls
contain solid waste, resulting in leachates consisting of high-strength wastewater containing several pol-
lutants. The pollutants are dissolved organic matter (DOM), chemical and biochemical oxygen demand
(COD, BOD), chlorinated salts both organic and inorganic, ammonia and heavy metals [Bhatt et al.,
2024]. Leachate composition is impacted by a variety of factors such as waste composition, depth of
waste mass, and age of the landﬁll. Leachate is also highly impacted by environmental factors such as
temperature, moisture content, and oxygen level [Bhatt et al., 2024]. Microbial activity under anaerobic
conditions controls the strength of the leachate under a slow ﬂow rate [Bhatt et al., 2024].

2.2
Aeration of Landﬁlls
Current and long-term emissions of LFG and leachate from old landﬁlls can be mitigated by the short-
term intervention of in-situ aeration. This is done by actively injecting atmospheric air into the waste
mass. The primary goal of the active aeration is to achieve biological stabilization state of the landﬁll by
creating aerobic conditions, increasing carbon turnover rates, and mineralization processes [Prantl et al.,
2006]. The in-situ aeration technology is the preferred option to shorten the aftercare period for old
landﬁlls, especially when gas recovery is no longer economically feasible [Heyer et al., 2005, Rich et al.,
2008, Ritzkowski et al., 2006]. Landﬁll aeration can also be used for the pre-treatment of deposit waste
before landﬁll mining [Raga and Cossu, 2014].
Aeration will reduce the production of methane, accelerate the settlement of the waste mass and improve
the leachate quality. Methane concentration is reduced due to potential methane oxidation and aerobic
degradation [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2019b].
The carbon discharge will be increased by aeration
and be released as carbon dioxide, which has a lower GWP than methane [Heyer et al., 2005, Prantl
et al., 2006, Ritzkowski et al., 2006].
Additionally, aeration reduces the total organic carbon (TOC)
due to the increase in microbial metabolic processes. The heat produced during biological degradation
causes water to evaporate, resulting in reduced moisture content. The nitrogen content in solid waste
is also reduced, as increasing temperatures enhance ammoniﬁcation [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2019b].
Aeration impacts organic pollutants in leachate, reducing ammonium-nitrogen (NH4 – N), BOD5, and
COD. With the presence of oxygen, nitriﬁcation processes can furthermore occur, resulting in reduced
NH4 – N concentrations [Hrad et al., 2013]. As most of the organic carbon is discharged in the o↵-gas, a
decrease in dissolved carbon is observed in the leachate [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2019b].

2.2.1
Landﬁll Aeration Concepts
Landﬁll aeration is done by actively adding air into a landﬁll. The injected air will reach the waste mass
through either a horizontal piping system or vertical gas wells [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2019b]. The
design and spacing are important aspects of the technology, to determine an appropriate air volume and
pressure, as well as control of the air distribution, temperature, and moisture in the waste mass. Addi-
tionally, the age of the landﬁll and the organic content are important for determining the termination of
the aeration process [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. Local climate conditions, particularly temperature
and precipitation, should also be taken into account. The operational eﬃciency may be reduced by the
leachate accumulation in the waste, and the formation of condensate in the pipe system. A high di↵erence
in temperature between the ambient air and the waste, may at the same time promote the penetration
of air into the waste [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. Landﬁll aeration can be divided into high- and
low-pressure aeration, each having individual beneﬁts and are used for di↵erent purposes.
High-pressure aeration
High-pressure aeration is a landﬁll aeration concept where the air is injected with shock pressure up to
6 bars [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. The high-pressure air can penetrate both through highly and
weakly compacted waste mass [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. High-pressure aeration concepts are
often utilized at landﬁll mining projects, due to the fast and consistent aeration, e↵ectively reducing
odor and methane concentrations, and not prioritizing the costs and long-term aspects [Ritzkowski and
Stegmann, 2012].
Low-pressure aeration
According to [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2019b], low-pressure aeration is the most common concept and
is used for the majority of landﬁll aeration projects to accelerate the biological stabilization [Ritzkowski
and Stegmann, 2012]. Low-pressure aeration can further be subdivided into low-pressure aeration with
and without o↵-gas extraction, passive aeration, and energy-self-suﬃcient long-term aeration as well as
semi-aerobic concepts. The air pressure applied for low-pressure often ranges between 20-80 mbar but
can reach up to 0.3 bars [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. Through di↵usion and advection processes,
the injected air will be distributed in the waste mass.
Passive aeration
Passive aeration is an approach where atmospheric air penetrates into the waste due to the negative
pressure inside the landﬁll waste.
The ambient air can be induced into the ground through the top
cover or the implementation of open gas wells. This concept is used to prevent long-term hazards such as
methane accumulation around buildings. However, this technology can be challenged by the accumulation
of water in the wells, as no pressure is applied to remove the water [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012].
10
==PAGE:25==
Energy self-suﬃcient aeration
The energy-self-suﬃcient aeration is driven by wind wheels that are connected to the existing gas wells
and function as aspirators. The air is compressed into the gas wells which results in a continuous oxygen
supply in well-stabilized landﬁll [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. This concept can be applied for long-
term aeration, as it can be introduced in the transition period after active aeration has been ﬁnalized and
before a subsequent surface cover is installed [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012].
Semi-aerobic concept
The ﬁrst landﬁll aeration concept developed is the semi-aerobic concept [Matsuto et al., 2015]. For this,
the leachate collection system is used where the perforated pipes are designed only to be ﬁlled one-third
with leachate.
Vertical gas wells are installed in the pipe system, to inject atmospheric air into the
landﬁll waste, when the leachate level is low. The is done by the chimney e↵ect, induced by convection
[Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. This technology is seen in most of the landﬁlls in Japan, over half of
the landﬁlls in Korea, and some places in Malaysia [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2013].
Active aeration with and without o↵-gas extraction
Aeration concepts with high-pressure and low-pressure are often combined with o↵-gas extraction as the
aeration can lead to an uncontrolled release of gaseous emissions [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2012]. O↵-
gas is then collected and will subsequently be discharged to the atmosphere after puriﬁcation.

The moisturization approach
The Teuftal landﬁll, located in Switzerland, combined in situ aeration and the moisturization approach
to achieve better results [Ritzkowski et al., 2016]. If the moisture content in the waste mass gets too low,
the biological degradation is limited or is inhibited completely. Moisture can be added in two di↵erent
ways. Firstly, through the leachate system in the upper part of the waste mass. Secondly, through the air
injection, where the ambient air is moisturized by small nozzles of demineralized water. Water addition
is often unnecessary in the case of low-pressure in situ aeration without a top cover, as natural ebullition
is happening through percolating rainwater [Ritzkowski et al., 2016].
Cost and savings of landﬁll aeration
The cost of in-situ aeration varies a lot depending on the site-speciﬁcs, aeration capacity, and the aeration
setup. However, it is impossible to give a general indication of the cost, as it depends on parameters such
as volume, oxygen demand, thickness, number of gas wells, etc. According to [Heyer et al., 2005], the
estimated basic costs are around €650,000 for investment and operation, equal to 1-3 €/m3 for the whole
landﬁll to be stabilized. However, the price is continuously reduced as the technology and operations are
optimized based on various aeration projects. There are several ﬁnancial beneﬁts by implementing in-situ
aeration. The top cover is aimed for low emissions, which has lower expenses, than a long-life surface
sealing. There are less groundwater expenditure and operating costs for leachate puriﬁcation. Finally,
the aftercare period can be reduced by several decades, allowing earlier recultivation and after-use. It has
been shown that a cost saving of 10-25% is possible with the aeration concept [Heyer et al., 2005].

Proposed Stability Criteria and Target Values
The European Union has set directive regulations for both leachate and landﬁll gas but no speciﬁc limit
values [Brennan et al., 2016, The Council of the European Union, 1999]. Previously, the assessment of
the e↵ectiveness of landﬁll aeration mainly relied on the evaluation of leachate impact. Thus, due to
a growing emphasis on monitoring greenhouse gas emissions from landﬁlls, the gas composition in the
o↵-gas has become another critical indicator [Kjeldsen et al., 2023]. However, it is crucial to consider both
factors in combination for a comprehensive evaluation. [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2013] has proposed
six stability criteria and target values to determine the ﬁnal closure of a landﬁll. These criteria are based
on the carbon balance, the solid waste characteristics, and the monitored emission data of leachate and

o↵-gas to determine the completeness of an aeration project. The six criteria are listed in Table 3. Due
to the lack of data, guidelines, and benchmarks, it is important to keep in mind the major constraint
in comparing di↵erent aeration projected.

conversion rate, LFG generation, LFG generation potential (GP21), respiration index (RI4), as well as
temperature and settlement development. BOC is determined by the carbon balance and used to state

laboratory tests, using waste material from the landﬁll.

Site Description
This section begins with a brief introduction to landﬁlls in Denmark, followed by a description of the
initiated pilot project at AV Miljø, including details about the site and its properties. Additionally, the
section dives into the construction of the investigated area.
The type and quality of waste going to landﬁlls are generally similar worldwide, however, there are still
signiﬁcant di↵erences according to the communities, cities, regions, and countries. The type and quality of
waste are therefore essential for developing concepts for the facilities and treatment of the waste. Landﬁlls
receive four di↵erent kinds of waste; inert, nonhazardous waste, MSW, and hazardous waste [Kaza et al.,
2018]. In Denmark the waste fraction going to landﬁlls has remained almost constant from 2014 to 2021
ranging between 3% and 4% [Miljøstyrelsen, 2023]. In 2021, the fractions of waste management were 47%
recycling, 27% other material utilization, 23% combustion and 4% landﬁll [Miljøstyrelsen, 2023]. In 1999
the EU issued a Landﬁll Directive that imposed several restrictions. Some of the restrictions includes the
control of migration and accumulation of landﬁll gas (LFG) and leachate. Additionally, it requires the
establishment of a deﬁnition of when and how the aftercare period is ﬁnalized. Finally, it requires that
the economic costs of maintenance, monitoring, and control shall be covered for at least 30 years during
the aftercare period [The Council of the European Union, 1999]. Since the implementation of the EU’s
Landﬁll Directive in 2009, there has been a large reduction in the number of landﬁlls in Denmark, as
many could not continue operating according to the new rules. This results in 40 landﬁlls in Denmark
still operating [Dansk A↵aldsforening, 2016].

previous monitoring of the pilot project is included to indicate the current progress in the project.
4.1
The Aeration Concept and Airﬂow System
A full-scale in-situ aeration project was initiated in May 2023 at the AV Miljø landﬁll by Niras and DTU.
The pilot project aims to assess the e↵ect of in-situ aeration performed by air injection via 15 aeration
wells over a two year period [Bonilla Prado et al., 2023]. A vertical low-pressure aeration concept was
chosen for AV Miljø. No o↵-gas extraction was installed due to a low organic content, as the emissions are
expected to be too low for energy recovery [Bonilla Prado et al., 2023]. Before the full-scale application, a
test set-up of ten wells was installed, mainly to determine the aeration rate. The test setup was installed
across the whole cell and was afterward used in the full-scale aeration concept together with 50 additional
wells. An overview of the aeration setup at Cell 1.3 can be seen in Figure 9 where the dotted line frames
the underlying waste at Figure 9a. There are a total of 15 aeration wells and 44 monitoring wells, with
A3 no longer functioning. They have been evenly distributed across the cell to cover the entire area by
the inﬂuence radius regulated by the ﬂow rate. Additionally, eight monitoring wells are placed on the
edges of the cell [Bonilla Prado et al., 2023]. Figure 9b shows a close-up of the monitoring wells centered
around aeration well A4.

Methane Screening
A screening was performed on the cell to measure potential emissions of methane from the top cover.
The methane concentration was measured in ppm. The methane screening was performed by the use of a
Laser One, which is a handheld ﬁeld gas analyzer. Laser One logs the methane concentrations just above
the surface together with GPS coordinates. The software program Originpro was used to extract the data,
whereas the software program QGIS was used to create heat map of the concentrations. There are some
uncertainties associated with methane screening, as the terrain and vegetation across the area vary, which
results in measurements at di↵erent heights above the ground. The inﬂuence of atmospheric air increases
with distance from the ground, which can lead to inaccurate results as the methane concentration can be
diluted.
Pressure
The pressure di↵erence in the monitoring wells was assessed by the use of a barometer. The barometer was
connected by a small plastic tube, which by its bent, sealed the monitoring well. The pressure di↵erence in
the monitoring well measured directly after opening the bent tube in the plug. This method could involve
some uncertainties if the plug in the monitoring well, for some reason, had been o↵. The barometer
additionally needs to be calibrated before use, to adjust to the atmospheric pressure.
The pressure
di↵erence was measured in mbar.
During the previous campaigns performed by Niras, a barometer
measuring the pressure di↵erence in Pascal was used, which prevents a direct comparison, as the ﬁgures
are not as precise as the ones in mbar.
Air Cleaning
To obtain the most precise gas composition from the waste mass, the pipes were emptied for residual air
prior to the measurements. This was done by the use of a prenart pump which has a pumping rate of 10
L/min. Based on the maximum volume of the well being 13.7 L, the pump needs to run for approximately
1.5 minutes per well to remove the residual gas. Air cleaning was particularly crucial for the wells with no
or negative pressure di↵erence which may create a slight vacuum inducing atmospheric air into the wells.
During some of the measurement days, the prenart pump ran out of power with only a few remaining
measuring points. As a result, the built-in pump in the Biogas 5000 was used until the gas composition
stabilized, though this introduces some uncertainties.
Gas Composition
The Biogas 5000 was used to measure the gas composition the the monitoring wells. Prior to gas meas-
urement, the biogas undergoes calibration using a speciﬁc gas. Subsequently, the biogas is connected to
the probe with a ﬁlter in between to prevent any water from entering the machine. The suction of the
gas continues until the gas stabilizes, at which point it can be read. Biogas 5000 measures the volume
percentage (% vol) of CH4, CO2, O2, and BAL which are the remaining gases primarily consisting of N2.
Between the adjustment and changing of the tubes atmospheric air could enter the well, which can a↵ect
the gas composition and thereby a↵ect the results.

Temperature
The temperature was measured at three di↵erent depths the bottom, the middle, and the top of the
waste mass. Some of the monitoring wells were equipped with three stationary sensors, a red sensor at
the top of the waste mass, a green one in the middle, and a black one at the bottom. As most of these
stationary sensors were no longer functioning, the temperature was instead measured manually using the
thermometer at the bottom, middle and top of all the wells. The manual measurements involve a number
of uncertainties. It is unknown which approach Niras used during their measurements, whether they used
the stationary sensors or manually measured the temperature at di↵erent depths. Since the temperature
was only measured from the inside of the well, it primarily reﬂects the temperature at the bottom of the
waste mass where the gas enters the gas wells due to the location of the ﬁlter. As pressure from the waste
mass pushes the gas from the bottom up through the well.

The variation in gas composition and pressure di↵erence across the monitoring wells can be attributed to
the heterogeneity of the waste mass together with high leachate saturation. The di↵erences in permeability
of the waste mass can result in the formation of leachate pockets in periods of heavy precipitation creating
anarobic niches. Vertical gas transport can, as earlier mentioned, be inhibited by the leachate and the
layer-structured waste mass, promoting horizontal gas transport instead. Additionally, the cell primarily
received old construction waste, there will be areas that are completely isolated from the air supply,
potentially due to larger items that prevent air distribution to these areas, such as large plastic bags
and plastic covers found in previous waste samplings. The gas will gravitate towards areas free of water
and areas with a lower permeability. The same pressure di↵erence was, however, seen in the short GP-
monitoring wells without any water compared to the long wells, which indicates that the vertical gas
transport was suﬃcient in these areas.
The high resistance of the gas transport causes an inhomogeneous distribution of the injected air, creating
smaller anaerobic areas, which also have been found in the case studies of; the Austrian Landﬁll [Hrad
et al., 2013], an aeration project in Florida, USA [Ko, 2013], at Kuhstedt Landﬁll in Germany [Ritzkowski
et al., 2006] and at the landﬁll in Northern Italy [Raga and Cossu, 2014]. Furthermore, the high variations
in the waste composition also result in di↵erent degradation behaviors and thus di↵erent gas by-products.
Similar results were seen at the Heferlbach Landﬁll in Austria[Brandst¨atter et al., 2020] and at the landﬁll
in Northern Italy [Raga and Cossu, 2014].

Unexpectedly, oxygen was detected in all shallow layers. This suggests a penetration of atmospheric air

It was expected that the injected air would create a pressure di↵erence inside the cell, preventing air from
entering the waste mass and instead emitting excess gas out through the top cover. However, the pressure
di↵erence measured in the shallow probes ranged from 0.2-3.0 mbar, indicating a poor vertical transport of
the injected air. This suggests that the gases from inside the waste mass were emitted through alternative
routes, but will require additional measurements to be conﬁrmed.
The waste proﬁle for GP2, as seen in Figure 47a, shows low carbon dioxide and high oxygen levels at
the bottom of the well, indicating no degradation activity. In the middle layer of GP2, the oxygen level
was zero whereas the carbon dioxide and methane were high. The oxygen has either been consumed
immediately or the vertical transport has been prevented. The top layer of GP2 contained some oxygen,
potentially induced from the atmosphere. There was, however, no indication of oxidation as the CH4/CO2
fraction increased from 0.9 to 2.4, going from the middle of the waste mass to the top layer. Thus, methane
may accumulate on the way out without being oxidized, followed by a dilution of the incoming air. This
was also the case for GP4, as seen in Figure 47b.
Looking at the top layer at GP9, in Figure 47c, high levels of oxygen were also found, which again in-
dicates atmospheric air induction through the top cover. This causes a dilution of the landﬁll gases, as
the CH4/CO2 fraction decreases and oxygen levels were higher compared to GP2. The lower CH4/CO2
fraction indicates more aerobic degradation as well as more methane oxidation. The higher methane con-

centrations found in the middle of GP9 could be attributed to leachate-saturated areas creating an anoxic
environment. Water was, however, not measured in the well, but was found in the nearby monitoring

Overall, the measurements conducted with the short mobile probes at the top of the waste mass showed
no trend of accumulated methane beneath the top cover, which ﬁrst was anticipated. However, based on
the increased CH4/CO2 fractions observed from the middle to the top of the GP2- GP4-monitoring wells,
a vertical methane accumulation is suggested in some areas. These were also the areas that experienced
the highest saturation of leachate in the middle of the waste mass in Campaign 2, creating an anaerobic
environment resulting in the production of methane. In most of the monitoring wells high oxygen concen-

through to the top cover. Cracks were observed in the top cover, which can be found in Appendix J. The
cracks may facilitate an easier penetration path. A migration of atmospheric air through the top cover
was also seen in the case study in Northern Italy [Raga and Cossu, 2014]. The presence of oxygen could
additionally be due to the heterogeneity of the waste mass creating alternative routes for the injected
air. Previous methane screenings indicate little to no emissions of methane at the surface. The top cover
might act as a biocover, where methane oxidation occurs before reaching the surface. As the waste mass is
a dynamic system, the gas transport changes constantly and is highly inﬂuenced by the leachate content,
the degradation of the material, and the settlement. It is important to state, that these ﬁndings are based
on very few and local measurements.

After the implementation of aeration, the waste mass has moved into the ﬁnal stages including methane
oxidation- (VII), carbon dioxide- (VIII), and ambient air phase (IX). As earlier discussed, the air supply
was limited in A14, A17, A20, A28, and A30, due to the waste mas being saturated by leachate, which
kept the waste in the methane oxidation phase. The aeration in these areas was almost suﬃcient, however,
methane was still observed. A15, A19, A21, and A24, received just enough oxygen to perform an aerobic
degradation mainly producing carbon dioxide. Finally, the monitoring wells A13, A16, A23, and A26,
have reached the ambient air phase where little or no methane is present. The gas composition in this ﬁnal
stage approaches the composition of the soil air [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017]. The same observation
was made after the ﬁrst few months in Northern Italy, where methane decreased from 60% to 2%, due to
the gradual decrease of waste biodegradability, formation of new ﬂow paths and the drying e↵ect [Raga
and Cossu, 2014].
This demonstrates that introducing aeration to an old landﬁll accelerates the processes that transition
the landﬁll to its ﬁnal gas phases. Thus, the required aftercare period can be shortened from over 30
years to just 3-6 years [Ritzkowski et al., 2006]. The case study of the Austrian Landﬁll, showed that
the monitoring wells a↵ected by aeration remained stable after completion of aeration, which conﬁrms
success in sustained aeration in some areas of the landﬁll [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017]. However, the
deepest layers of the landﬁll (4-5 m) showed an increasing CH4/CO2 ratio after the aeration was stopped,
conﬁrming a limited vertical gas transport [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017]. Thus, not all the organic
matter had been completely degraded during aeration in the Austrian landﬁll. The increase in methane
seen after the power out at AV Miljø indicates that the cell still contains considerable amounts of organic
matter after almost a year of aeration. This suggests that the period in practice may be longer, or that
the design of the oxygen supply needs to be optimized to achieve a more uniform air distribution during
aeration.

Development in the Terrain
The height above sea level during the aeration has been measured in several of Niras campaigns to
investigate possible sedimentation and degradation of organic matter within the waste mass. This landﬁll
settlement can be used as another indicator for the progress towards biological stabilization [Ritzkowski
and Stegmann, 2013]. The results can be seen in Figure 56 showing the settlement from May 2023 to
November 2023.
Figure 56: The settlement (m) from the baseline in May 2023 to November 2023.
The settlement was seen as inconsistent across the site, explained by the heterogeneous waste composition,
with varying organic content. At the western side of the cell by points 3, 18, 25, 50, 51, and 53 settlement
above 16 cm was observed, where the area around 25, has the largest settlement up to 29 cm. However, the
absence of monitoring wells in these areas prevents observation of the temperature, water level, pressure
di↵erence, and gas composition development. Furthermore, settlements around 15 cm can be observed
points 13, 33, and 48. Additionally, ﬂuctuations have been detected at the southern part of the cell. The
di↵erence in the settlement strongly depends on the operation technique due to the compaction rate and
material.
The settlement in this case study falls within the lower range compared to previous studies across Europe.
For instance, landﬁlls in Northern Italy and the landﬁll Konstanz-Dorfweiher in Germany recorded settle-
ment values of 30-55 cm/a and 5-80 cm/a, respectively [Laux, 2015, Raga and Cossu, 2014]. In the study
case of Konstanz-Dorfweiher, the settlement was reduced to 5-25 cm/a in the second year of aeration.
The reduction could be attributed to the higher stability of the deposited material the following year.
Similar ﬁndings were reported in an Austrian landﬁll, where the older section recorded a settlement of
65
==PAGE:80==
1-5 cm/a, while the younger section measured 10-17 cm/a [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017]. This might
also be the scenario for this case study, considering the lower organic content of AV Miljø and the age of
the landﬁll.
By monitoring the systems development from before the aeration application until now, the results show
that in-situ aeration is accelerating the bio-stability of the landﬁll. The ratio of CH4/CO2 implies that
the landﬁll has transitioned into the ﬁnal stages of its lifetime when air is supplied. However, the state
of the landﬁll without aeration is unknown, as the project only has been running for one year. Since the
values for RA4, GP21, and BOC are currently unknown, it is not possible to determine the duration of
aeration. Additionally, there are no explicit guidelines or termination criteria established. Cell 1.3 at AV
Miljø will therefore be compared to several other projects based on the six proposed criteria.

The results from active aeration for the six case studies were within the proposed stability criteria for RA4
and GP21, except for the landﬁll in Northern Italy which had a GP21-value of 20.9 l/kg DM and a RA4-
value of 2.8 O2/g DM. As RA4, GP21, biodegradable organic carbon (BOC) and methane production rate
primarily are determined through the o↵-gas extraction, and in lab-scale aeration tests, these values are
not available for the landﬁll at AV Miljø. These parameters are fundamental for assessing the biological
stabilization of the landﬁll as well as predicting the aeration volumes, and operation periods. However,
parameters such as the landﬁll settlement and temperatures can be used as indirect indicators for the
progress towards biological stabilization and thereby the completeness of landﬁll aeration. Despite no
speciﬁc values are set for a target, a decreasing trend is proposed [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2013].
The temperature for the case studies ranges between 23 and 55 °C, with a decreasing tendency in three
of the cases towards the ﬁnal phase [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017, Heyer et al., 2010, Laux, 2015].
Kuhstedt landﬁll showed the highest temperature at around 50 °C after 1.5 years of aeration started.
After the temperature peaked a continuous decrease was seen during the next ﬁve years of aeration and
after the completion of active aeration [Ritzkowski and Stegmann, 2013]. The aeration at AV Miljø has
been operating for almost a year, and the high temperatures measured during the campaigns suggest
that the temperature may be close to its peak. A following decrease could be expected. However, it is
important to emphasize that Kuhstedt landﬁll is much bigger than the aerated area at AV Miljø.
The highest settlement was seen for the aeration project in Italy and Germany ranging from 30-55 cm/a
and 20-80 cm/a respectively [Laux, 2015, Raga and Cossu, 2014], where only 1-5 cm/a (old section) and
10-17 cm/a (younger section) were observed for the Austrian Landﬁll [Hrad et al., 2013]. The di↵erences
can be attributed to the fact that the Austrian waste is more stable and not as deep. The settlement at
AV Miljø was 0-29 cm after an aeration period of six months, which is in the lower end compared to the
others. This could be attributed to the short operation time and the low organic content of the waste
mass.
It is important to keep in mind that this comparison is only a sense check, as the criteria mainly should be
used as a tool for individual cases. Landﬁlls are very heterogeneous and show crucial di↵erences according
to several factors such as age, waste composition, organic content, and oxygen utilization rate over time.
The characteristics of a landﬁll are therefore considered unique and making comparisons diﬃcult.
By accelerating the bio-stabilization of landﬁlls, the aftercare period could be completed earlier. Cost
savings are expected due to the shorter aftercare period, based on the speciﬁc measurements such as the
treatment of leachate as the quality signiﬁcantly improves [Ritzkowski et al., 2006]. An estimate of 10-25%
reduction of the total cost during landﬁll closure and aftercare has been calculated in [Ritzkowski and
Stegmann, 2012], which may even be higher, when considering a lower ﬂow rate that uses less energy.
Based on other study cases and the current progress of this study case, in-situ aeration could be the method
used to reduce the pollution of methane and leachate, and thereby reduce the aftercare period. The landﬁll
68

will, however, still emit GHG in the form of carbon dioxide, which has a lower global warming potential
than methane. As global landﬁlls and waste management contribute to a large part of anthropogenic-
caused methane emissions, the reduction could have a large impact. It is, however, crucial to introduce
target values for when a landﬁll is considered biological stabilization, which only very few countries have
done. Setting target values would increase the interest in creating more sustainable landﬁlls.

Limitations
The case study is followed by several limitations. First of all, the results are based on a few measurement
points, which are widely dispersed and only represent a local area of the waste mass. With large distances
between the monitoring wells, greater areas have not been investigated. Furthermore, most of the monit-
oring wells only represented the deepest part of the waste mass, and the short AK- and GP2-monitoring
wells only represented the center of Cell 1.3 around aeration well A4.
The investigated stability of the system was based on four days of measurements where the ambient
weather did not change considerably. This challenges the assessment of the extent to which short-term
weather a↵ects the system. Additionally, this study is also limited by only considering the short-term
weather inﬂuence. The impact of the water content in the waste mass is crucial for the functioning of the
aeration system, which could show di↵erent behavior of the system according to the long-term weather.
During the di↵erent seasons of the year, the frequency and the amount of precipitation will vary as well as
the ambient temperature. Dry weather could impact the moisture content which is crucial for biological
degradation. A dry top cover could also create cracks resulting in emissions of LFG. The aeration system
has, however, not yet been operating for a full year, hindering an investigation of the long-term weather’s
impact. The processes are still in the initial phase and due to their intensity, dominate any changes caused
by the weather.
Limitations were also seen due to the setup of the aeration system. The air supply in the aeration pipes was
a↵ected by condensation. Water was accumulated in the aeration pipes, which could have been avoided
if more straight pipes were used, such as iron pipes. However, this could also increase the economic costs
of the aeration system. Water was a constraining factor throughout the study case, which also was the
case in the set-up. The monitoring wells were equipped with or without a ﬁlter. If water was present,
it would block the ﬁlter and prevent exchange of LFG. Consequently, gas measurements in these wells
were not representative. Furthermore, the results obtained during Campaign 1, 2, and 3, showed that the
vertical gas transport was not very eﬃcient. The vertical distribution of the injected air was observed as
the injected air was only supplied in the deepest parts of the waste mass. It could be suggested that the
injecting of air should be at di↵erent depths, to facilitate a better air distribution in the entire cell.
Finally, the case study is also limited due to the lack of carbon discharge observations. Leachate samples
have been conducted both before and during the active aeration of Cell 1.3, however, no recent enough
69
==PAGE:84==
samples could be used as a comparison with the measurements conducted in these campaigns, as the
pumps were ﬂooded during the case period. The leachate data could have contributed to carbon discharge
assessment.
However, according to [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017] the e↵ect of aeration on leachate
quality is expected to improve over a longer time, and as this pilot project only has been operating for
almost a year, it could be too soon to see any impact on the leachate. The carbon discharge has in
other case studies been observed and calculated based on the o↵-gas extraction and the leachate [Hrad
and Huber-Humer, 2017]. As o↵-gas extraction is not implemented in this pilot project, it has not been
possible to calculate a comparable carbon discharge.
The lack of data is a general limitation of the
aeration technology. Landﬁll aeration projects are increasing worldwide, making it crucial to introduce
protocols and controls to enhance the technology. It will promote comparable and comprehensive data
sets. Documentation and uniform monitoring processes are therefore needed to ensure better comparative
performance assessments in the future [Hrad and Huber-Humer, 2017].

8
Conclusion
Three measurement campaigns were carried out to investigate the e↵ect of aeration in cell 1.3 which has
been operating for a year. During the ﬁrst campaign. the stability of the system was investigated over
four days of measurements. The variations over time and space were assessed based on the temperature,
water level, pressure di↵erence, and gas composition. External factors were taken into consideration for
the possibility of interference. The deepest part of the waste mass, where the atmospheric air was injected,
showed stable results for all four parameters. The short-term weather conditions were relatively stable and
the system showed no oscillations in relation to the weather, except minor oscillations at the temperature
measured at the upper part of the waste mass. Large amounts of leachate were observed in the middle
part causing areas of anaerobic niches. Due to less precipitation during the measurement period, the waste
mass became less saturated. The areas in the middle of the waste mass, which were unsaturated, showed
stable results. Compared to the deepest part, lower temperatures and higher methane concentrations due
to less oxygen supply were observed in the middle part. The pressure di↵erence was consistent in the
deepest and middle part of the waste mass that was not leachate-saturated. All four parameters varied
across the cell, no trends or signs of homogeneity were observed. The high variations in depth and across
the cell can be attributed to the inﬂuence of gas transport and the biological processes. The gas transport
was highly inﬂuenced by the heterogenous waste composition, the compaction of waste layers, and the
accumulation of leachate in the waste mass. The large amount of leachate in the waste mass resulted
in the creation of anaerobic niches and ebullition of methane, causing higher methane concentrations in
these areas. Furthermore, the di↵erences in waste composition inﬂuenced the biological degradation of
the organic material, causing variations in degradation activity and by-products.
For the second campaign, the injected air ﬂow was decreased by 50%, in four of the aeration wells, to
assess the sensitivity of the system and the possibility of reducing the economic costs. A decrease in the
pressure di↵erence to the atmosphere was seen across the entire cell. Despite the lower oxygen supply,
a small increase was observed in the bottom temperatures. This could be attributed to higher aerobic
degradation together with less cooling e↵ect from the injected air. Most of the monitoring wells showed
no change in the gas composition. However, an increase in methane was seen in the middle part of the
waste mass.
This indicates that the supplied oxygen was utilized in the deepest part or the vertical
transport of injected air was prevented. During the third campaign, measurements of gas compositions
just underneath the top cover showed a surplus of oxygen, indicating a penetration of atmospheric air
through the top cover into the waste mass. Reducing the airﬂow in some of the aeration wells did not
cause major changes in the investigated areas, which could indicate a better utilization of the injected air
and thereby not extending the time period for reaching bio-stability. This potential optimization of the
system could lower the economic costs of operation.
71
==PAGE:86==
This case study shows that the aeration system facilitates aerobic degradation across the cell and thereby
accelerating the bio-stability of the waste mass. The methane screenings performed after the implement-
ation of aeration showed a signiﬁcant decrease in methane concentrations at the surface of the top cover.
Other global in-situ aeration projects have successfully obtained bio-stability, as mentioned in subsec-
tion 6.6. In comparison with them, the pilot project at AV Miljø shows similar results at its early stages
of the aeration period. The challenges of heterogeneous waste mass and accumulation of leachate were
also seen in other study cases. In this case study it was not possible to measure the state of bio-stability
and further investigations are needed.
9
Recommendations
Climate change is seen as a higher frequency of extreme weather events such as heavy rain events as
well as higher temperatures [Dessler, 2016]. It could have severe consequences for the desired outcome
of landﬁll aeration. This should be taken into consideration when implementing an aeration system on
a landﬁll in Denmark, as today’s rain events already have shown a huge impact on the gas transport
seen in Campaign 1, 2, and 3. It is recommended to investigate possible solutions for better drainage of
the middle of the waste mass both for better air distribution and more accurate monitoring of the waste
mass’ middle. In this study a much better horizontal than vertical gas transport was observed, which
leads to the recommendation of aerating at di↵erent depths of the waste mass. Based on the results from
Campaign 2, it is recommended to reduce the ﬂow of the injected air to decrease the operational costs
and potentially increase aerobic degradation. As the reduction a↵ected the entire waste mass, the airﬂow
could be reduced in selected aeration wells or all of them. In areas showing high degradation activity, the
airﬂow could remain high to ensure suﬃcient oxygen supply.
Due to the complexity of the gas and leachate transport within a waste mass, it is diﬃcult to optimize
the in-situ aeration system. [van Turnhout et al., 2020] got promising results by using a 3-D multiphase
model to compare di↵erent aeration strategies with the air distribution in a full-scale aeration project.
The model was kept as simple as possible and excluded the biochemical processes. Further development
is recommended within gas distribution models to provide a tool to determine the best aeration strategy
for a speciﬁc landﬁll. A similar model could be implemented at AV Miljø to gain a more comprehensive
understanding of the air distribution using the data collected from the campaigns.
