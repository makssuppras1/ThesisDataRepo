Abstract 
The multidrug resistant Mycobacterium abscessus complex (MABSC) has been shown to have the 
most severe impact on lung functions in patients with cystic fibrosis (CF) compared to 
Mycobacterium avium and other non-fermentative Gram-negative bacteria. Currently there are no 
reliable antibiotic treatments known to cure MABSC chronic lung infection, although the evidence 
points towards long and intensive, multidrug combinations. Evidence suggests that the efficiency of 
several bactericidal antibiotics is enhanced by stimulation of aerobic respiration of pathogens and 
that lack of O2 increases the tolerance of the bacteria. Based on this theory the aim of this project 
was to study the killing effect of bactericidal amikacin on M. abscessus subsp. abscessus reference 
strain NCTC13031 and MABSC isolates from patients with CF during aerobic and anaerobic 
conditions and to investigate the biofilm lifestyle of the bacterium. An experiment with the 
disaggregating detergent Tween 80 was performed testing the effect of Tween 80 concentrations 
demonstrated through confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM), O2 consumption measurements 
and Etest. The ability of NCTC13031 and clinical isolates was shown to form big non-attached 
aggregates with a markedly slow O2 consumption compared to separated planktonic MABSC. MIC 
was higher when M. abscessus subsp. abscessus was cultured in Müller Hinton media containing 
0% Tween 80 compared to 5% Tween 80. The aggregated form might be a strategy for MABSC to 
obtain antibiotic tolerance facilitated by a protective biofilm layer. Amikacin killing assays showed 
a significant increased killing effect of treatment during aerobic conditions compared to anaerobic 
conditions after 1 and 3 days treatment on four planktonic cultured MABSC clinical isolates from 
patients with CF and on reference strain NCTC13031. Amikacin has clearly potential to improve 
the treatment of MABSC in the combination with hyperbaric oxygen treatment and could ultimately 
lead to a novel therapy method for patients with CF suffering from chronic MABSC infection.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Cystic fibrosis 
Cystic fibrosis (CF) is a lethal genetic autosomal recessive disorder (Riordan et al. 1989). 
Approximately 500 people in Denmark have CF, with 12-15 new cases diagnosed each year 
(www.cff.dk). According to the US Cystic Fibrosis Registry more than 70,000 individuals live with 
the genetic disorder worldwide (www.cff.org). The disease is caused by mutations in the gene 
named cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR), which is a cyclic adenosine 
monophosphate (cAMP) regulated chloride selective channel located at chromosome 7 (Rommens, 
Riordan, and Kerem 1989). The gene may also function as an regulator of epithelial sodium channel 
(ENaC); a regulator of sodium (Na+) reabsorption (Donaldson et al. 2007). The CFTR gene is a 
channel protein in the ATP-binding cassette family of transporter proteins, containing 5 domains: 
two nucleotide-binding domains that interact with ATP, a regulatory domain, and two-membrane-
spanning domains (Riordan et al. 1989). CFTR is located apically on the epithelial cells most seen 
in the respiratory and gastrointestinal system where it is involved in transporting chloride (Cl-) out 
of the epithelial cell to the covering mucus whereby a passively transport of Na+ follows, increasing 
the total electrolyte concentration in the mucus (Knowles et al.  2002; Matsui et al. 1998).  
Mutations in the CFTR gene can lead to an abnormal CFTR protein causing defective electrolyte 
transport in the apical membrane of epithelial cells leading to a disturbance in the balance of salt 
and water (Knowles et al. 2002; Rosenstein et al. 1998) (Figure 1A). This leads to a dehydration of 
the epithelium resulting in very thick mucus in the airways disturbing the function of cilia 
movement (Figure 1B). Mucociliary clearance is seen as the most important nonspecific defense 
mechanism of the respiratory tract (Riordan et al. 1989; Stolz et al Welsh 2015). For an optimal 
clearance function of the epithelial-attached cilia normal airway epithelia regulates Na+ and Cl- by 
CFTR resulting in a periciliary liquid (PCL) with an optimal height. The CFTR mutation results in 
abnormally high rates of airway surface liquid absorption in CF airway epithelia, which depletes the 
PCL and leads to a concentrated, viscous mucus adhering to airway surfaces (Matsui et al. 1998). 
This leads to no transport of mucus by the cilia (Hoegger et al. 2014). Since the identification of the 
CFTR gene, more than 1,800 mutations have been identified. Only a relative small number of these 
mutations accounts for the majority of CF alleles. The most seen mutation is DF508, which is a 3-
base pair deletion seen in 70% of the chromosomes of patients with CF worldwide, thus

The clinical course of CF varies from patient to patient, but the clinical characteristics of “classical” 
CF are dominated by pulmonary disease. Respiratory failure, often with recurrent respiratory 
infections, is the major cause of morbidity and mortality. Approximately 90% of people suffering 
from CF die from pulmonary complications with an overall median survival into the late 40s 
(www.cff.org). Approximately 85% of patients have exocrine pancreatic insufficiency, which 
require oral substitution with pancreatic enzymes, and many adults develop a special CF diabetes 
mellitus which require insulin treatment (Kelly et al. 2013). This is a result of hindrance of the 
pancreatic ducts and following scarring and damage of endocrine and exocrine functions. Other 
disorders caused by the CF disease include malabsorption (Siddiqui et al. 2011), male infertility 
(Boyd et al. 2004) and chronic rhinosinusitis (Robertson et al. 2008) and hepatic disease (Colombo, 
C. et al. 2002). All taken together this disturbance leads to a complex multisystem disease affecting 
several organs.

The treatment for patients with CF is palliative and comprises lung physiotherapy together with 
usage of inhaled drugs or hypotonic saline to clear the secretions of the lungs. Common treatment 
also involves antibiotic treatment, improvement of nutritional status by dietary managements and 
replacement of pancreatic enzymes (according to CF Foundation Clinical Care Guidelines) 
(Mogayzel et al. 2013). 
 
1.1.1 Bacterial colonization of CF lungs  
The upper and lower airways of patients with CF are a perfect niche for opportunistic bacteria that 
rarely cause disease in healthy people. The CF airways are uninfected at birth, but during the first 
month of life, bacteria from the upper airway or from environment including other patients enter the 
lower airways (Stolz et al. 2015). Since exposure from pharynx and environmental pathogens is 
high, colonization of pathogen bacteria will turn into an established infection, which can become 
chronic if not treated. The presence of bacteria in the airways results in a state of chronic 
inflammation primary generated by polymorphonuclear leukocytes (PMN) which are key effector 
cells of the innate immune system. The constant inflammation state leads to damage of the tissue, 
the main reason for failure in lung function in patients with CF (Döring et al. 2012). Staphylococcus 
aureus and Hemophilus influenza are common opportunistic bacteria residing during time in 
children suffering from CF (Razvi et al. 2009). However, the risk of Gram-negative colonization 
increases as well as the risk of being chronic infected in adult patients. The most feared Gram-
negative bacteria according to CF include Pseudomonas aeruginosa in addition with 
Achromobacter xylosoxidans, Burkholderia cepacia complex and Stenotrophomonas maltophilia 
(De Baets et al. 2007; Goss et al. 2004; Hansen 2012; Kerem et al. 2014; Lipuma 2005). Recently 
nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM) such as Mycobacterium abscessus complex (MABSC) and 
Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) have received attention since prevalence rates are 
increasing and particularly MABSC seems to negatively impact morbidity and mortality. MABSC 
has been correlated with the most direct impact on lung function in patients with CF compared to 
the slow-growing M. avium (Qvist et al. 2016). Figure 2 demonstrates a lung transplanted from 
patient with CF with MABSC.

1.2 Mycobacteria 
The Mycobacterium genus includes a group of more than 140 species, of which only a limited 
number are pathogens in humans and animals (Van Ingen 2013). It is a resilient group of bacteria 
characterized by a lack of an outer cell membrane and the presence of a thick waxy cell wall rich in 
mycolic acids (Hett et al. 2008).  
 
1.2.1 Mycobacterial cell wall 
Mycobacterial cell walls have unique characteristics that are impermeable to several compounds, 
resulting in an inherent resistance to many drugs. The cell wall consists of an inner layer and an 
outer layer that surround the plasma membrane (Brennan 2003). The outer layer consists of both 
proteins and lipids. The lipids are most often freely linked with the cell wall, with some long- and 
short-chain fatty acids which complement the short and long chains seen in the inner layer. The 
lipid-connected polysaccharides linked with the outer cell wall consist of lipoarabinomannan 
(LAM), lipomannan, phthiocerol-containing lipids such as phthiocerol dimycocerosate, trehalose 
==PAGE:12==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
11 
dimycolat and other lipids dependent on the specific mycobacterium. Fast-growing mycobacteria 
such as Mycobacteria smegmatis, Mycobacteria fortuitum and M. abscessus have phosphoinositol-
capped LAMs known as PILAMs (Nigou et al. 2003). The inner compartment consists of 
peptidoglycan (PG), arabinan (A), galactan (G) and mycolic acids (MA), which is covalently bound 
together to form a complex known as the MA-A-G-PG complex that extends from the plasma 
membrane outward in the layers, starting with PG and ending with MAs (Figure 3). This complex is 
insoluble and is the essential core of the mycobacterial cell wall (Brennan 2003). Many of the 
antibiotics used against mycobacteria target this unique MA-A-G-PG complex (Yajko et al. 1990).

1.2.2 Metabolism 
Not much is known about the metabolism of mycobacteria and most studies have looked at M. 
tuberculosis and related species. Oxygen limitation during granuloma development (inflammation 
during disease) has been suggested to be one of the main signals, which result in a change in 
metabolism of mycobacteria to a state of dormancy (Lim et al. 1999). An in vitro model for changes 
correlated with oxygen depletion has shown that replication is dependent on the presence of oxygen 
(Wayne et al. 1996a; Wayne et al. 2001). A popular in vitro model used sealed tubes with cultures 
==PAGE:13==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
12 
of M. tuberculosis that during growth were gradually depleted of all available oxygen. In this 
model, the bacteria progress through two stages of non-replicating persistence; stage 1, which is 
characterized by a stop of cell division, and stage 2, which is characterized by a more widespread 
shutdown of metabolism. The transition into stage 1 occurs when the exponentially growing 
bacteria have consumed oxygen to a level of 1% saturation, where stage 2 is reached when oxygen 
levels are reduced to less than 0.06% saturation. The transition of M. tuberculosis to anaerobic 
conditions is associated with upregulation of specific enzymes such as nitrate reductase (Wayne et 
al 1998b). Stage 1 has also been associated with increased transcription of the cvdABDC genes 
during stage 1 (Boshoff et al. 2004), which might indicate an efficient utilization of oxygen by the 
encoded cytochrome bd-type menaquiol oxidase with high oxygen affinity, however with lower 
bioenergically efficiency (Kana et al. 2001). Analysis of the expression pattern of respiratory 
components during stage 1 (Boshoff et al. 2004; Sherman et al. 2001) showed that components of 
the proton-pumping type I NADH dehydrogenase was downregulated whereas the transcription of 
the gene ndh, which is coding for one of the type II non-proton pumping NADH dehydrogenases is 
upregulated. Combined with the upregulation of cytochrome bd oxidase, this indicates a decrease in 
energy requirements under these circumstances, which is supported by the decreased expression of 
most subunits used for the ATP synthase. Metabolic adaptions are necessary during stage 1 since 
survival of the bacteria is dependent on the length of transition through this microaerophilic stage 
(Wayne et al. 2001). It has been shown that M. tuberculosis is able to persist in “stage 2-cultures” 
which are not supplemented with external terminal electron acceptors for respiration such as O2, 
nitrate (NO3
-), nitrite (NO2
-) and fumarate indicating that an energized membrane is maintained 
without respiration (Wayne et al. 2001). Figure 4 proposes a route for regeneration of reducing 
equivalents dependent on the availability of terminal electron acceptors. The metabolism required 
for M. tuberculosis to maintain an energized membrane during anaerobic persistence is poorly 
understood. While M. tuberculosis and MABSC are related, there might still be considerable 
differences in metabolism and oxygen consumption, which makes extrapolation from one species to 
the other difficult. Most significantly the two mycobacteria have significant differences in growth 
rate, with MABSC belonging to the rapidly growing mycobacteria and M. tuberculosis to the slow 
growers. However, the M. tuberculosis studies still represent the best available model to understand 
the dormant stages of growth seen in MABSC during oxygen limitation.

1.2.3 Species of mycobacteria 
The most studied mycobacteria are M. tuberculosis. Roughly one third of the world’s populations is 
infected with this mycobacteria (Wanget et al 2014), which make M. tuberculosis a top cause of 
mortality worldwide. A lot of attention has been paid to this mycobacterium which has caused a 
global health emergency. However, the burden of infection from NTM is also noteworthy. NTM are 
increasingly being isolated from sputum samples taken from adults and children with CF (Hjelte et 
al. 1990).  The most common NTM seen in Danish CF patients is MABSC (Qvist  et al. 2015), 
which belongs to the group of rapid growing mycobacteria (RGM) also including mycobacteria 
such as Mycobacterium chelonae and M. fortuitum. M. abscessus is ubiquitous in the environment, 
particularly seen in water distribution systems and soil (Ordway et al. 2008). The term “rapid 
growing mycobacteria” is paradoxically since these bacteria compared to most other bacteria grow 
extremely slow but if compared to the remaining NTM, classified as slow growers, they are truly 
rapid. RGM appear in a culture media within seven days whereas slow growing NTM such as M. 
avium complex (MAC) species appears within three to eight weeks. MABSC have been recognized 
==PAGE:15==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
14 
as the major pulmonary pathogen within the fast-growing mycobacteria with CF patients being 
especially exposed to infection with this mycobacterium (Roux et al. 2016). NTM has previously 
been thought of as a group of rather benign environmental bacteria associated with random 
colonization and only rarely associated with actual infection in the airway (Hill et al 2012), but 
these perceptions have now changed significantly. MABSC and MAC are now well-known as 
opportunists that highly affect morbidity and mortality in patients with CF (Catherinot et al. 2013). 
The prevalence of NTM at 11 % among all patients with CF in Scandinavia has been demonstrated 
(Qvist et al. 2015). In this study, the NTM species showed geographical differences. MABSC and 
MAC targeted different CF patient subpopulations, with younger, pancreatic insufficient patients 
being more inclined to MABSC. Despite treatment, less than a third of MABSC patients with 
repeated positive NTM cultures were able to clear their infection and one-fourth were eventually 
lung transplanted or died (Qvist et al. 2015). While great progress has been made in understanding 
the scope and impact of NTM infections, approaches in diagnostics and antibiotic management 
have been less remarkable.  
 
1.3 Mycobacterium abscessus 
M. abscessus is a RGM and was first isolated from a chronic suppurative leg infection and based on 
this named on its association with deep abscesses in human tissue (Moore et al 1953). The bacteria 
are seen inside skin and soft tissue infections following trauma, needle sticks, or surgical 
procedures (Rodríguez et al. 1997; Wallace et al. 1983) and is also isolated from the respiratory 
tract of patients suffering from CF (Girón et al. 2008), which is of interest in this context.  
 
1.3.1 Morphotypes 
Similar to other mycobacteria such as M. avium or M. smegmatis, M. abscessus shows two distinct 
morphotypes on solid agar media: a smooth (S) variant associated with non-cording, motile and 
biofilm-forming, and a rough (R) variant associated with cording, aggregate forming and non-
motile (Howard et al. 2006). Figure 5 illustrates smooth and rough morphotype of MABSC isolates. 
The main difference between these two morphotypes resides in the completely loss of surface-
associated glycopeptidolipids (GPL) in the R form. The R morphotype appears to arise only during 
infection in the host organism and in addition to this. Switching from smooth to rough phenotype of 
MABSC has been associated with a more chronic infection (Kreutzfeldt et al. 2013)  and increased 
==PAGE:16==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
15 
virulence (Catherinot et al. 2007), while the genetic determinants of cord formation remain 
unknown.

1.3.2 Survival strategies  
One of the main differences between pathogenic and non-pathogenic mycobacteria is the ability of 
pathogenic mycobacteria to survive and replicate within macrophages and dendritic cells by 
arresting phagosome maturation and consequently prevent fusion with lysosomes, which has been 
shown for M. tuberculosis (Clemens and Horwitz 1995; Tailleux et al. 2003; Vergne et al. 2004). 
M. fortuitum and M. smegmatis, which are both saprophytic RGM, are both unable to multiply 
inside macrophages and are rapidly cleared from the infected cells by the immune system (Anes et 
al. 2006; Bohsali et al. 2010). In contrast, MABSC survives inside the macrophages and also have 
the ability to replicate inside the macrophages (Byrd et al. 1999; Nessar et al. 2011; Oberley-
==PAGE:17==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
16 
Deegan et al. 2009). The mechanism and source responsible for pulmonary contamination of 
patients with CF with MABSC are still not known (Ferro et al. 2016). However, it is most likely 
that the S form, which is capable to form biofilm-like structures, is the infecting form. GPLs, which 
are present at the surface of M. abscessus S variant, have been shown to prevent Toll-like receptor 
(TLR) 2 signaling in respiratory epithelial cells (Davidson et al. 2011), possibly allowing lung 
colonization and following survival in a silent and free niche as shown for Mycobacterium marinum 
and M. tuberculosis (Russell 2001; Stamm et al. 2003). After phagocytosis by macrophages, the S 
variant is found as single, separated bacteria in phagosomes, whose membrane is closely apposed 
all around the outer surface of the mycobacterial cell wall. Infection with the R variant leads to 
phagosomes often containing more than two bacteria, surrounded by a loose phagosomal membrane 
and lack of the electron translucent zone, which might allow the bacterium to enter the extracellular 
space and eventually form cords. In contrast to the R variant, the S variant is able to restrict intra-
phagosomal acidification and induces less apoptosis and autophagy. The membrane of phagosomes 
surrounding the S form bacteria has shown alteration in the form of breaks or partial lysis. The 
ability of M. abscessus to transition from an macrophage-resistant S form to an R extracellular form 
might increase the bacterium’s capacity to adapt and survive in the changing environments  (Roux 
et al. 2016).  
The humoral immune response to MABSC does not clear the disease, but can be exploited to 
diagnose lung infection as a rise in specific anti-MABSC IgG in serum correlates to disease severity 
(Høiby et al. 2017).  
     
1.3.3 Subspecies of Mycobacterium abscessus  
M. abscessus has been divided into three subspecies; M. abscessus abscessus (also named M. 
abscessus sensu stricto), M. abscessus bolletii and M. abscessus massiliense, which have been 
differentiated based on their genomes and are also referred to as MABSC. They differ from one 
another in their genes rpoB, hsp65 and other housekeeping genes but also in their susceptibility to 
antibiotics (Heydari et al. 2013). The subspecies are differentiated based on the activity, or lack of 
activity, of an inducible macrolide resistance gene named erythromycin methylase (erm). Macrolide 
antimicrobial agents act by binding to the 50S ribosomal subunit which consequently inhibits the 
peptide synthesis. Erm genes are a diverse collection of methylases that impair binding of 
macrolides to ribosomes and thereby reduce the inhibitory activity of these agents. The main reason 
for innate clinically significant macrolide resistance for fast-growing mycobacteria is the presence 
==PAGE:18==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
17 
of an inducible erm gene (erm41)  (Nash et al. 2012; Nash, Brown-Elliott et al 2009). The 
subspecies M. abscessus massiliense have a smaller, inactive erm gene compared with the 
subspecies M. abscessus bolletii, which has an active gene. M. abscessus subsp. abscessus also 
usually has an active erm gene (Tettelin et al. 2014). Due to the extensive innate drug resistance, 
the treatment of MABSC lung disease remains difficult (Nessar et al. 2012). Data concerning 
efficiency of antibiotic treatment of MABSC disease are inconsistent and shows both high and low 
efficiency depending on subspecies (van Ingen et al. 2013). An explanation for these inconsistent 
results is the presence of a high percentage of isolates in the study population that were really M. 
abscessus massiliense, without an active erm gene and therefor more susceptible to antibiotic 
treatment. Subsequent work has shown that patients with M. abscessus subsp. massiliense infection 
have a highly better response to macrolide-based treatment, which is assumed to be due to inactive 
erm gene, than patients infected with M. abscessus subsp. abscessus (Harada et al. 2012; Koh et al. 
2011). 
 
1.3.4 Resistance mechanisms against aminoglycosides  
MABSC  are intrinsically resistant to most of the antibiotics that are currently available (Ballarino 
et al. 2009; Brown-elliott et al. 2002; Jeon et al. 2007). Some strains appear more susceptible to 
some antibiotics, which may relate to the difference in the subspecies within the MABSC subgroup. 
The 2-deoxystreptamine aminoglycosides kanamycin, amikacin, gentamicin and tobramycin are 
essential drugs for the treatment of the multidrug-resistant NTM infections (Sander et al 1999). This 
class of antibiotics target the 16S rRNA thereby inhibiting protein synthesis by interacting with the 
proof-reading process, leading to errors in the protein synthesis with premature termination (Shakil 
et al. 2008). MABSC hold one copy of the rRNA operon, which make the likelihood of a 
phenotypic expression of a single spontaneous mutation more likely (Nessar et al. 2012). One study 
showed that adenine substituted by guanine at position 1408 (A1408G) within the 16S rRNA was 
responsible for the high level of resistance to kanamycin, amikacin and tobramycin seen in MABSC 
clinical isolates (Prammananan et al. 1998).

1.5 O2 consuming PMNs 
Recurrent infections attacking the respiratory tract eventually lead to persistent inflammation, 
which is mainly driven by neutrophils, that constitute the majority of PMNs, which is the first 
defense line of the innate immune system (Segal 2005). PMNs express different recognition 
receptors at their surface including the TLR 4 and CD14 for lipopolysaccharides (LPS) recognition 
(Sabroe, Dower, and Whyte 2005; Wright et al. 1991). Internalization of invading pathogens are 
recognized and bound by PMNs and the pathogens are embedded in membrane-bounded vesicles, 
the so-called phagosome, by the phagocytic plasma membrane. Phagocytosis leads to a significant 
increase in O2 consumption (Babior et al. 1973; Kolpen et al. 2010) during what is termed the 
respiratory burst. During this O2 is consumed for the reduction of O2 to superoxide (O2
-) (Babior et 
al. 1973) by NADPH oxidase (Rossi et al 1964) working as an electron acceptor.  This internal cell 
killing of pathogens is initiated by fusion of the phagosome with lysosomes containing 
antimicrobial agents such as defensins, elastase, cathepsin and proteinsases (Witko-Sarsat et al. 
2000). PMNs plays a central role in lungs of patients with CF both in the development and outcome 
of chronic bacterial infection as they have advantageous properties by eliminating non-mucoid (Mai 
et al. 1993) and planktonic bacteria  (Jensen et al. 1990) as well as quorum sensing-deficient 
biofilms (Bjarnsholt et al. 2005; Bjarnsholt et al. 2009) . However PMNs are also causing severe 
lung damage to the patients; the continuous influx of PMNs leads to an enormous amount of cells in  
sputum (Witko-Sarsat et al. 2000), which contributes to airway obstruction caused by DNA release 
during necrosis leading to increased viscosity of sputum (Lethem et al. 1990; Shah et al. 1996). 
PMNs are able to produce damaging molecules such as elastase (Owen et al. 1999), proteases  
(Suter et al. 2017) and reactive oxygen radicals (ROS) by the respiratory burst (Babior et al. 1973), 
which are all antimicrobial agents damaging the lung tissue if produced in higher concentrations.

1.5.1 Oxygen depleted zones  
The defective CFTR gene results in an accelerated Na+ absorption, leading to an increased turnover 
rate of Na+-K+-ATPase pumps  (Lung et al. 1997; Stutts et al. 1986)  which further results in a two- 
to threefold increase in CF airway epithelial O2 consumption (Stutts et al. 1985). This together with 
the oxygen consuming PMNs and respiration from the bacteria result in oxygen depleted mucus in 
the airway lumens, where P. aeruginosa has been shown to be inherent (Worlitzsch et al. 2002). 
MABSC is frequently cultured from CF patients (Sermet-Gaudelus et al. 2003) indicating that 
MABSC might adapt to these anaerobic conditions in a fashion similar to P. aeruginosa.  
 
1.5.2 MABSC induce neutrophil accumulation 
It has been demonstrated that M. abscessus subsp. abscessus induces neutrophil accumulation of the 
site of infection by controlling transcription and translation of selective chemokines by the 
neutrophils and the host cells (Lee et al. 2012; Oberley-Deegan et al. 2010). Neutrophils respond to 
M. abscessus subsp. abscessus via released O2
- that may contribute to local inflammation and tissue 
damage, which may enhance the survival changes of the bacterium (Oberley-Deegan et al. 2010). 
Figure 7 shows a stepwise illustration of neutrophil recruitment induced by bacteria.  In the 
presence of neutrophils, M. abscessus subsp. abscessus rapidly form biofilm in conditions 
mimicking the nutrient rich CF airway or inflamed tissue, suggesting that establishing infections 
may exploit the neutrophil-rich environment (Malcolm et al. 2013). Biofilm formation provokes 
greater resistance to antimicrobials and the immune system, which contributes to the generally low 
success rate of  MABSC treatment in patients with CF (Høiby et al. 2011; Høiby et al. 2011).

1.5.3 Damaging of CF lungs by ROS  
Activated PMNs also consume O2 for NO production (Kolpen et al. 2014). NO produced by the

leading to O2 depletion and thereby production of these two alternative electron acceptors.

1.6 MABSC treatment  
MABSC is one of the most drug-resistant bacteria encountered in the clinical setting. Even worse 
than the totally drug-resistant M. tuberculosis (Nessar et al. 2012). The current treatment for 
MABSC is not very effective (Qvist et al. 2014). Most used is intravenous antibiotic treatment 
including the combination of amikacin with clarithromycin and either cefoxitin or imipenem as an 
initial treatment in the therapy (van Ingen et al. 2013). This is often followed by use of oral 
antibiotics, which is similar to the early and continuation phases of M. tuberculosis treatment 
(Griffith et al. 2007). Nevertheless, at least 50 % of the patients either fail this therapy, setback or 
die. Currently there is no reliable antibiotic treatment that cures MABSC lung disease (Griffith et 
al. 2007; Jarand et al. 2011).  
  
1.6.1 Cellular death induced by bactericidal antibiotics  
The major classes of bactericidal antibiotics are quinolones which target DNA replication and 
repair, beta-lactams which inhibit cell-wall turnover, and aminoglycosides which inhibit protein 
synthesis. These major classes have been shown to stimulate the formation of hydroxyl radicals 
(OH·) by reduction of H2O2 via ferrous iron also named the Fenton reaction; 
 
H2O2+Fe2+ à Fe3++ OH· + OH- 
 
The bactericidal drug classes stimulate oxidation of NADH via the electron transport chain, which 
is dependent on tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle. This hyper activation of the electron transport chain 
using O2 as an electron acceptor during aerobic conditions leads to O2
- formation. This O2
- 
formation damages iron-sulfur clusters liberating ferrous iron, which is used by the Fenton reaction.  
==PAGE:24==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
23 
This results in formation of OH· and ultimately leads to cell death by damaging DNA, proteins, and 
lipids of the bacteria (Kohanski et al. 2007) (Figure 8), however this killing mechanism requires the 
target bacteria to be metabolic active.         
Amikacin is a bactericidal antibiotic among the aminoglycoside family and works by blocking the 
function of the bacteria’s 30S ribosomal subunit, making it unable to produce essential proteins. 
This mechanism allows amikacin to work only at metabolic active bacteria since it must interact 
with the metabolism of the bacteria to have a damaging effect. Therefore, a lack of O2 will most 
likely increase the tolerance of the bacteria to antibiotics which is the conditions seen in CF lungs, 
which could be some of the explanation why there is no reliable antibiotic treatment that cure 
MABSC lung disease (Griffith et al. 2007; Jarand et al. 2011).

2 Clinical impact 
NTM are increasingly being isolated from sputum samples taken from adults and children with CF 
(Hjelte et al. 1990). Of the rapidly growing NTM species, the multidrug resistant MABSC has 
emerged as a major respiratory pathogen in patients with CF leading to accelerated decline in lung 
function (Esther et al. 2010). Recently it has been shown that MABSC has the most direct impact 
on lung functions in patients with CF compared to the slow-growing mycobacterium M. avium, and 
non-fermentative Gram-negative bacteria (Qvist et al. 2016). Due to the bacterium’s natural 
resistance towards most clinically available antimicrobials it is challenging to treat MABSC 
infections (Nessar et al. 2012). Chronic bacterial lung infection is the most severe complication in 
patients with CF. It is characterized by antibiotic-tolerant biofilms in the endobronchial mucus with 
zones of oxygen depletion (Worlitzsch et al. 2002) mainly due to PMN activity (Kolpen et al. 
2010). While the exact mechanisms affecting antibiotic effectiveness on biofilms remain unclear, 
evidence suggests that the efficiency of several bactericidal antibiotics, such as ciprofloxacin, is 
enhanced by stimulation of aerobic respiration of pathogens (Kohanski et al. 2007) and that lack of 
O2 increases the tolerance of the bacteria. Several antibiotics have been shown to require bacterial 
metabolic activity, and depends on the formation of ROS (Brochmann et al. 2014; Dwyer et al. 
2015; Walters et al. 2003).  
It has been shown that replication of mycobacteria are dependent on O2 (Wayne et al. 1996; Wayne 
et al. 2001), which may explain MABSC’s tolerance/resistance to the bactericidal aminoglycoside 
amikacin during lung infection since the bacteria have been observed in sputum samples from 
patients with CF associated with O2 depleted zones (Worlitzsch et al. 2002).  
 
3 Aim of study 
The aim of this project is to study the bactericidal killing effect of amikacin on M. abscessus subsp. 
abscessus reference strain NCTC13031 and MABSC isolates from patients with CF during aerobic 
and anaerobic conditions and to investigate the biofilm mode of growth of MABSC.

4 Materials and methods 
4.1 Bacterial strains and growth media 
 
Isolates of MABSC were obtained from sputum samples from patients with CF. As defined by 
“Danish Act of Research Ethics Review of Health research Projects” section 2 this project does not 
constitute a health research project and thus initiated without approval from The Committees on 
Health Research Ethics in the Capital Region Denmark. The study was carried out on a total of 3 
anonymized patients (Table 1).  
Reference strain NCTC13031 belonging to M. abscessus subsp. abscessus was bought from Statens 
Serum Institut (SSI). Patient isolates have not yet been through genome sequencing and are 
therefore referred to as MABSC. All MABSC isolates and reference strain were stored at -800C in 
serum bouillon with glycerol. 
Various amounts of Tween 80 (Tween® 80, Croda International PLC, United Kingdom) were added 
to Müller Hinton II Bouillon media (17.5 g/L of acid hydrolysate of casein, 3 g/L of beef extract: 
and 1.5 g/L starch) (MH) (SSI) to reach final concentrations of 0.1, 1.25, 2.5, 5, 7.5, 10 and 20%. 
MH media containing 5% of Tween 80 were used in larger quantities than other concentrations and 
therefore 1L portions was typically prepared at once, whereas other concentrations were prepared in 
200 mL portions in glass bottles with blue caps. The portions were afterwards autoclaved and 
placed in a 370C incubator until Tween 80 was dissolved.  
MH media containing various Tween 80 concentrations will henceforth be referred to as: 
(X%)MH+Tween, where X is the concentration of Tween 80 added to MH media.  
 
Unless otherwise stated, all experiments were initiated by streaking the reference strain or the 
isolate of interest on chocolate plates (36.0 g/L GC Agar Base (15 g/L Peptone, 1.0 g/L Corn starch, 
4 g/L Dipotassium phosphate, 1 g/L Monopotassium phosphate, 5 g/L Sodium chloride, 10 g/L 
agar), 10.0 g/L IsoVitalex Enrichment, 10.0 g/L Hemoglobin) (SSI) followed by incubation at 370C 
until visible growth (5-8 days dependent on isolate). Each start-culture was prepared by inoculating 
one single colony from chocolate plates in 20 mL 5%MH+Tween in culture flasks (50 mL Tissue 
culture flask TC-25+, TPP®, Switzerland) and incubated at 370C, 155 rpm for 5 days.

4.3 Preparation of cultures with MABSC isolates in selected Tween 80

4.6 Preparation of media for amikacin killing assay 
800 mL of 5%MH+Tween were sterile filtrated through 0.22 µm syringe filters (TPP®) and divided

4.12 Oxygen measurements

proteins (Nuegebauer 1987). Tween is found in four versions; Tween 20, Tween 40, Tween 60 and

Tween 80. The number refers to the type of fatty acid which is associated with the polyoxyethylene 
sorbate part of the molecule; 20 refers to monoleurate, 40 refers to monopalmitate, 60 refers to 
monostearate and 80 refers to monooleate. Tween have been shown to enhance the growth of 
several mycobacteria (Siddiqi et al. 1988), and especially Tween 80 is commonly used as detergent 
for mycobacteria prone to aggregation. To restrict cell aggregation Tween 80 was chosen and used 
for separating bacteria in order to get a more homogeneous culture, which leads to more consistent 
results when counting CFU. In this study, it was of interest to investigate MABSC as planktonic 
cells and therefore it was prioritized to create cultures with separated cells. When starting up the 
experiment a Tween 80 concentration of 0.1% (according to the study Malcolm et al., 2013) and 
later 1.0% (Van Boxtel et al. 1990) was added to MH media when culturing the M. abscessus 
reference strain NCTC13031. Due to extremely inconsistent CFU counting when using 0.1% and 
1.0% of Tween 80 for experiments carried out in triplicates, it was decided to perform a Tween 80 
pre-experiment testing various Tween 80 concentrations to find a concentration separating the 
bacteria sufficiently before continuing with other experiments. The experiment was performed with 
seven various Tween 80 concentrations ranging from 0% up to 20% of Tween 80 added to MH 
media and inoculated with M. abscessus NCTC13031. The cultures grew for 5 days and OD600 was 
measured on all samples. Culture samples were plated on chocolate plates for CFU counting and 
CLSM was performed on selected concentrations.  
The triplicate OD600 measurements showed that the higher the Tween 80 concentration the higher 
biomass was measured, however when comparing sample 0%MH+Tween to the remaining 
concentrations statistical significance was only observed for 2.5% (p=0.028), 7.5% (p=0.0006) and 
20.0% (p=0.0021) (Figure 11A). This indicates a higher concentration of bacteria mL-1 when 
increasing the Tween 80 concentration, however not all concentrations were consistent based on the 
lack of statistical significance. It should be noticed that the cultures containing 5.0%, 7.5%, 10.0% 
and 20.0% Tween 80 were very viscous, which made it difficult in the sample-taking process. This 
can be the reason for the inconsistency. The results from triplicate CFU counting are seen in figure 
11B. In addition, concentrations of 1.25% or higher gave rise to a higher CFU count compared to 
the sample 0%MH+Tween, however when comparing 0%MH+Tween with the remaining 
concentrations significance was only observed for 2.5%MH+Tween. 2.5%MH+Tween were 
compared to the remaining concentrations and significant difference was only observed for 
0%MH+Tween and 0.1%MH+Tween (not shown at graph B).

5.7 Amikacin killing assay of MABSC during aerobic and anaerobic conditions 
To investigate the effect of aerobic amikacin treatment compared to anaerobic treatment an 
amikacin killing assay was carried out on M. abscessus reference strain NCTC13031 and MABSC 
isolates 1S13, 1R13, 2S13, 2R13, 3S14 and 3R14 during aerobic and anaerobic conditions. The 
assay was performed with various amikacin concentrations going from 0-512 µg mL-1 and samples 
were taken out at day 0, day 1 and day 3. Log10 killing of CFU mL-1 were plotted as a function of 
amikacin concentrations. Inoculums of all samples were standardized by inoculating with a 
calculated volume of start culture corresponding to OD600 at 0.005 (for calculations, see Appendix 
A1, for verifying standardized inoculation, see Appendix B). Based on results from CLSM and O2 
consumption measurements, it was found reasonable to use 5.0% Tween 80 when culturing M. 
abscessus. This concentration was used for all performed amikacin killing assays.  
Survival curves for all isolates and NCTC13031 can be seen in Appendix B (used for later 
discussion).

6 Discussion 
New treatment methods for MABSC are needed as the pulmonary infections are very difficult to 
treat and lead to death or lung transplantation within a few years of diagnosis. Based on studies 
reporting that efficiency of several bactericidal antibiotics is enhanced by stimulation of aerobic 
respiration of pathogens (Brochmann et al. 2014; Dwyer et al. 2015; Kohanski et al. 2007; Walters 
et al. 2003) it was of interest to investigate the bactericidal killing effect of amikacin treatment 
during aerobic and anaerobic conditions as well as the biofilm lifestyle of MABSC, which could 
possibly facilitate the development of novel therapies and may improve responses to currently 
available therapies. 
  
6.1 Aggregation ability of MABSC 
The Tween 80 pre-experiment showed that a concentration of Tween 80 at minimum 2.5% 
separated the bacteria sufficiently, which is surprising as the most commonly used Tween 80 
concentrations are in general much lower in other studies, for example; 0.01% (M. abscessus)  
(Rhoades et al. 2017), 0.05% (M. abscessus) (Malcolm et al. 2013) and 1.0% (M. avium subsp. 
paratuberculosis) (Van Boxtel et al. 1990). A report investigating the effect of Tween 80 on clinical 
isolates of M. avium subsp. paratuberculosis showed a gradual increase in cells when growing the 
mycobacterium in 0.001, 0.01, 0,1 and 1.0%, respectively (Van Boxtel et al. 1990a). According to 
this they cultured the mycobacterium in a concentration of 3.0% Tween 80 which resulted in failure 
to grow. These results are not in accordance with findings from this study, which showed that 
Tween 80 concentrations ≥2.5% had the highest CFU count, however they used M. avium subsp. 
Paratuberculosis which differs mainly by the fact that it is a slow-growing mycobacterium. An 
explanation for this inconsistence could be that the tolerance for Tween 80 is dependent on the 
specific mycobacterial isolate. Van Boxtel et al. cultured two clinical isolates from M. avium subsp. 
Paratuberculosis which both failed to grow in 1.0% of Tween 80.  
The aggregation ability of MABSC and the effect of Tween 80 were investigated by culturing 
MABSC isolates and the reference strain in selected Tween 80 concentrations, which were then 
analyzed by CLSM, by O2 oxygen consumption measurements and Etest. Results clearly showed 
that MABSC has a natural preference for living in big aggregates, which were showed by images 
from CLSM and supported by the extremely slow oxygen consumption of MABSC in 
0%MH+Tween samples. In addition to this a higher MIC was observed for NCTC13031 cultured in 
0%MH+Tween compared to when cultured in 5%MH+Tween indicating that separated cells are 
==PAGE:55==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
54 
more susceptible to amikacin than the aggregated form. These results indicate a beneficial biofilm-
mode of growth for MABSC as non-attached aggregates. Even at 2.5%MH+Tween and 
5.0%MH+Tween the images from CLSM showed a tendency for MABSC to clump, which 
underlines the big preference and willingness of MABSC to grow as aggregates. Other studies 
support this aggregated mode of growth for MABSC (Howard et al. 2006; Jönsson et al. 2013). A 
study examining lungs of a CF patient found MABSC in biofilm aggregations, which were 
embedded in the interstitial space of the respiratory zones of the lungs surrounded by PMNs and 
also found MABSC in mucus of the conductive zones indicating that this is a mode of growth for 
MABSC in CF lungs (Qvist et al. 2015). Evidence has shown the smooth morphotype of M. 
abscessus to be non-invasive and biofilm-forming vs. the rough shown to be invasive and non-
biofilm-forming (Howard et al. 2006; Roux et al. 2016). The findings that rough type was non-
biofilm forming were based on the failure to adhere to a surface (Howard et al. 2006). Results from 
this study showed the ability of both smooth and rough morphotype to aggregate in big clumps. The 
ability to adhere to a surface was not tested in our study, however it can still be argued that the 
clumping non-attached form of growing is a biofilm-mode of growth. A report showed that clinical 
P. aeruginosa biofilms rarely are grown as biofilm attached to a surface but are found as non-
attached aggregates (Jensen et al. 2010; Roberts et al. 2015) and it has been showed that non-
attached aggregates of P. aeruginosa in in vitro cultures reach the criteria associated with biofilm 
mode of growth (Alhede et al. 2011). 
From the CLSM images it was not possible to see if the aggregates of the rough morphotype were 
in cord-like structures, which has been reported by several studies (Bernut et al. 2014; Sa et al. 
2011) however it is assumed that this is the case for the rough MABSC isolates in this study. The 
cording form of growth is known for linking to each other and forming giant colonies (Sa et al. 
2011). GPL expression seen in the smooth morphotype has been suggested to play a role in 
environmental colonization, since they are shown to be involved in sliding ability and biofilm 
formation (Recht and Kolter 2001), whereas cord formation is required for mycobacterial 
persistence in the infected host (Glickman et al. 2000). Based on these studies the ability to switch 
morphotype from smooth to rough may allow M. abscessus to transform between a colonizing form 
and an invasive human pathogen. Switching from smooth to rough phenotype of M. abscessus has 
been associated with a more severe chronic infection (Kreutzfeldt et al. 2013)  and increased 
virulence by inducing strong tumor necrosis factor-alpha response by macrophages (Catherinot et 
al. 2007). One report of a severe MABSC pulmonary infection in a cystic fibrosis patient who 
==PAGE:56==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
55 
received a lung transplantation documented that the infecting organism had a rough colony 
phenotype (Sanguinetti et al. 2001), however another study reported the infecting MABSC was of 
the smooth morphotype (Qvist, Eickhardt, et al. 2015). In this study, it was not possible to see a 
consistent difference between rough and smooth isolates on CLSM images and O2 measurements.  
In patients with CF MABSC aggregates are shown to be surrounded by PMNs (Qvist et al. 2015) 
creating a local O2 depletion in sputum as shown for P. aeruginosa in CF sputum samples from the 
respiratory tract (Kolpen et al. 2010). In accordance to this it has been shown that M. tuberculosis is 
able to persist in oxygen levels less than 0.06% saturation which were not supplemented with 
external terminal electron acceptors for respiration indicating that an energized membrane is 
maintained without respiration (Wayne et al. 2001) - this might also be the case for MABSC. 
Oxygen limitation during granuloma development (inflammation during disease) has been 
suggested to be one of the main signals, which result in a change in metabolism of Mycobacterium 
bovis to a state of dormancy (Lim et al. 1999). These studies are in accordance with results from 
this study that the aggregated MABSC can adapt to a state where it uses a very limited amount or 
no O2, which is the conditions seen in sputum of patients with CF (Worlitzsch et al. 2002). This 
might be another strategy for MABSC to obtain antibiotic tolerance facilitated by a protective 
biofilm layer as observed for P. aeruginosa (Alhede et al. 2011). For further studies, it could be of 
interest to elucidate the mechanisms associated with higher tolerance to amikacin for the aggregated 
form of MABSC.    
 
6.2 The killing effect during aerobic amikacin treatment  
In this study, the killing effect of amikacin treatment during aerobic and anaerobic conditions was 
investigated through an amikacin killing assay. A significant increased killing effect was found on 
M. abscessus strain NCTC13031 together with the four MABSC isolates 1R13, 2S13, 2R13 and 
3R14 during aerobic amikacin treatment after 1 and 3 days of treatment. Killing was observed for 
several of the isolates during anaerobic treatment, with a maximal killing of ∼3 log10, however none 
of the killings at different concentrations were significant when comparing to control except from 
3R14, where a significant killing was seen for 32 µg mL-1 at day 3, which indicate that the amikacin 
has diffused into the cells somehow. This might be explained by a leaky lid allowing O2 to diffuse 
into the culture. When looking at the survival graphs for NCTC13031 and isolates (see Appendix B) 
a drop in CFU mL-1 is seen from the anaerobic curves when comparing day 0 to day 1 and day 3, 
which might be explained by that a ratio of the bacteria population added to the anaerobic media 
==PAGE:57==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
56 
will not survive the fast transition going from aerobic to anaerobic conditions. This is supported by 
a study showing that metabolic adaption of  M. tuberculosis is necessary since the survival of the 
bacteria is dependent on the length of transition through a microaerophilic stage (Wayne et al. 
2001).  
The two isolates 1S13 and 3S14 seemed resistant since the amikacin had absolutely no effect on 
these isolates during aerobic treatment. Patients corresponding to 1S13 and 3S14 had been infected 
with MABSC for 10 and 5 years (Table 1), respectively and the development of resistance is not 
surprising as this phenomena is well described in the clinical setting (Ballarino et al. 2009; Brown-
elliott et al. 2002; Jeon et al. 2007). A reasonable explanation might be spontaneous single mutation 
affecting the 16S rRNA which is associated with resistance to 2-deoxystrptamine aminoglycosides 
such as amikacin. These mutations are often seen for aminoglycosides antibiotics. Prammananan et 
al. showed that adenine substituted by guanine at position 1408 (A1408G) within the 16S rRNA 
was responsible for the high level of resistance to kanamycin, amikacin and tobramycin seen in 
MABSC clinical isolates (Prammananan et al. 1998).  
Previous studies have shown that the bactericidal effect of several antibiotics such as 
aminoglycosides is enhanced by stimulation of aerobic respiration of bacteria. Suggested 
explanation is that bactericidal antibiotics are interacting with TCA cycle for producing ROS 
damaging the bacteria (Kohanski et al. 2007). Another study supporting this theory have reported 
that the bactericidal effect of several antibiotics requires metabolic activity of the target bacteria and 
depends on the formation of ROS (Brochmann et al. 2014). In addition to this a study showed that a 
lack of´O2 increases the tolerance of the bacteria to antibiotics (Walters et al. 2003). These studies 
have only been performed on Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, however they are 
consistent with the findings of this study that amikacin has an increased killing effect during aerobic 
treatment compared to anaerobic which based on the mentioned studies can be explained by that 
amikacin interacts with the metabolic active MABSC and thereby generate ROS which kill the 
bacteria - a mechanism that is not possible during anaerobic conditions. This is supported by the 
results from anaerobic treatment showing no killing effect of amikacin, however it is not possible 
from this study to see if ROS generation is the killing mechanism during aerobic conditions.     
In addition to these finding, gene expression analyses have identified NADH-coupled electron 
transport as the key upregulated pathway among the bactericidal antibiotic treatments (Kohanski et 
al. 2007). A study by Wang and Zhao provided compelling evidence that ROS contribute to killing 
==PAGE:58==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
57 
effect of β-lactams, fluoroquinolones, and aminoglycosides. This was shown by making a double 
mutant of superoxide dismutase (sodA/sodB) which is responsible for the conversion of superoxide 
to hydrogen peroxide (substrate for Fenton reaction). The mutation was shown to be less sensitive 
to killing by the three main classes of antibiotics (Wang et al. 2009). Together these studies also 
support the hypothesis of Kohanski et al.  Few studies are investigating mycobacteria within this 
area, however studies have been carried out on M. smegmatis and M. tuberculosis, where especially 
M. smegmatis is of interest due to its comparability to M. abscessus since they are both belong to 
the class of RGM (Grant et al. 2012; Kim et al. 2012). The ability of antibiotics to generate ROS 
production seems to be especially relevant to the treatment of mycobacterial infections. One study 
presented evidence that the activation of host cell autophagy by antibiotics is caused by M. 
tuberculosis ROS production which is induced by antibiotics (Kim et al. 2012). Within the study of 
mycobacteria evidence has shown that as little as a 20% drop in dissolved O2 saturation allows the 
so-called persisters of M. tuberculosis and M. smegmatis to survive in the presence of the 
bactericidal antibiotics ciprofloxacin and isoniazid (Grant et al. 2012) which is comparable to the 
O2 depleted sputum inhering MABSC in patients with CF. In the mentioned study, it was also tested 
if ROS contributed to antibiotic killing against persisters. The authors demonstrated that the 
hydroxyl-radical scavenger thiourea could reduce persister cell killing by clofazimine, 
ciprofloxacin, or isoniazid during aerobic conditions supporting the killing mechanism of hydroxyl 
radicals also applies for mycobacteria (Grant et al. 2012). This suggests that by stimulating ROS 
production mycobacterial persisters can be eradicated.  
When bacteria are threatened, they are skillful at evolving defense mechanisms, which have also 
been observed for the killing effect of ROS production. Evidence has shown that bacterial synthesis 
of nitric oxide (NO) and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) protect bacteria against killing by antibiotics, 
which also provide independent evidence that the production of reactive oxidants including ROS 
contributes to the killing effect of antibiotics. A study showed that endogenously produced NO 
protects Bacillus subtilis and S. aureus against killing by H2O2, which was carried out by two 
mechanisms involving reactivation of catalase and a transient suppression of the Fenton reaction by 
preventing the reduction of Fe+3 to Fe+2 (Gusarov et al. 2005). Another study demonstrated that 
endogenously produced NO could reduce the killing ability of several antibiotics including 
quinolones, aminoglycosides, β-lactams, and cephalosporins to gram-positive bacteria (Gusarov et 
al. 2010). Shatalin et al. provided evidence that endogenously synthesized H2S serves as a universal 
defense system against antibiotics by diminishing oxidative stress (Shatalin et al. 1999). It has been 
==PAGE:59==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
58 
shown that M. abscessus NCTC13031 has a pathway for H2S (http://www.uniprot.org) and it is 
therefore very likely that MABSC can evolve the same defense mechanisms.  
Kohanski et al. suggested a possible model explaining the ROS generations induced by bactericidal 
antibiotic treatment, which this study is based on, however molecular events leading to ROS 
generation in response to an antibiotic are not identical in all bacteria. In addition to this the degree 
to which a ROS component contributes to the overall antibiotic killing can vary significant between 
antibiotics within the same class (Wang et al. 2009). That said, several independent lines of 
evidence show that bactericidal antibiotics stimulate the production of reactive oxidants including 
ROS that contribute to the bacterial killing, however the exact mechanism of the observed increased 
killing of MABSC induced by aerobic amikacin treatment remain unknown and should be 
elucidated by additional experiments.   
This study is an in vitro study testing the killing effect of amikacin during aerobic and anaerobic 
conditions on planktonic MABSC cultures. This does not represent the natural aggregated form of 
MABSC which evidence indicates is its preferred form of growth. This is supported by results from 
our Tween 80 experiments. The effect of amikacin on MABSC during aerobic and anaerobic 
conditions have to our orientation not been studied before. Therefore, the first obvious step was to 
perform a study investigating MABSC in planktonic conditions to get more insight into the 
bacterium’s way of living.  
This in vitro study need further investigations within the area of ROS production - first at all to 
elucidate the killing mechanism of amikacin during aerobic treatment. Another interesting aspect 
which should be investigated is the full immune response, which properly interacts with this ROS 
generating mechanism as seen for M. tuberculosis (Kim et al. 2012) or in other unpredicted ways.  
In a more overall perspective the bactericidal activity of amikacin might be increased by restoring 
aerobic conditions in sputum through hyperbaric treatment, which has been shown to have an effect 
on P. aeruginosa. In an in vitro biofilm-model with reoxygenation of anaerobic P. aeruginosa 
biofilm by hyperbaric oxygen treatment it was demonstrated to enhance the bactericidal activity of 
ciprofloxacin presumably by engaging aerobic metabolic pathways needed for the activity of 
bactericidal antibiotics (Kolpen et al. 2016). Furthermore, in an animal S. aureus endocarditis 
model, another aminoglycoside, tobramycin, was shown to increase the bacterial killing when 
treating with hyperbaric oxygen therapy (Lerche et al. 2017). Next step for investigating the O2 
==PAGE:60==
Cecilie Ravnholt  
S152161 
59 
effect on MABSC might therefore be in vitro amikacin treatment during hyperbaric conditions on 
aggregated MABSC to see if same effect is seen on its more natural way of growing. The goal for 
these findings is to find a novel MABSC treatment tailored for patients with CF suffering from 
MABSC chronic infection. Hyperbaric oxygen treatment is an established treatment method for 
chronic wounds, which has shown significant healing effects (Kranke et al. 2015). Since a 
generation time differing between 7-13 hours for MABSC isolates has been observed in this study 
(other study reported 4-5 hours - very depended on the specific isolate (Pierre et al. 2010)) a 
hyperbaric treatment has to be for long time-intervals (several hours) most likely every day for a 
period. For future perspectives, these findings could lead to a novel therapy method for patients 
with CF suffering from MABSC chronic infections via hyperbaric oxygen treatment combined with 
daily amikacin dosages.

7 Conclusion and perspectives 
The multidrug resistant MABSC has been shown to have the most direct impact on lung function in 
patients with CF compared to M. avium and other non-fermentative Gram-negative bacteria. 
Currently there are no reliable antibiotic treatments known to cure MABSC lung disease, although 
the evidence points towards long and intensive, multidrug combinations. Evidence suggests that the 
efficiency of several bactericidal antibiotics is enhanced by stimulation of aerobic respiration of 
pathogens and that lack of O2 increases the tolerance of the bacteria. Based on this theory the aim of 
this project was to study the killing effect of bactericidal amikacin on M. abscessus reference strain 
NCTC13031 and MABSC isolates from patients with CF during aerobic and anaerobic conditions  
and to investigate the biofilm lifestyle of MABSC.  
 
The effect of various Tween 80 concentrations showed that a minimum of 2.5% Tween 80 should 
be added to MH media when culturing MABSC to reach a high level of separated planktonic cells, 
which most likely also applies to other mycobacteria species. This is a notion for future studies 
which are going to work with planktonic MABSC which has not been addressed previously. 
 
Tween 80 experiment testing selected Tween 80 concentrations demonstrated through CLSM, O2 
consumption measurements and Etest the ability of NCTC13031 and MABSC clinical isolates to 
form big non-attached aggregates with a markedly slow O2 consumption compared to separated 
planktonic MABSC. MIC showed higher susceptibility when cultured in 0%MH+Tween compared 
to when cultured in 5%MH+Tween. This might be a strategy for MABSC to obtain antibiotic 
tolerance facilitated by a protective biofilm layer as seen for other bacteria in chronic infections. 
 
Results from the amikacin killing assay showed a significant increased killing effect of amikacin 
during aerobic conditions compared to anaerobic conditions after 1 and 3 days of treatment on four 
MABSC clinical isolates and reference strain NCTC13031 - all grown as planktonic cultures. These 
findings are the first step in finding a therapy method for MABSC chronic infections. Amikacin has 
clearly potential to improve the treatment of MABSC in the combination with hyperbaric oxygen 
treatment, however further investigations are still needed before this become a reality. Most 
importantly studies elucidating the exact mechanism of MABSC killing during aerobic amikacin 
treatment are needed. Such studies could give rise to further in vivo investigations and ultimately 
lead to a novel therapy method for patients with CF suffering from chronic MABSC infection.
