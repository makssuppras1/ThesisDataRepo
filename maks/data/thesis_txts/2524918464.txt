Abstract
One of the common consequences of surviving a stroke is paralysis in the upper part of
the body. Rehabilitation is necessary for stroke patients to regain motor skills. A promising
rehabilitation technique consists of a brain-computer interface (BCI) that controls a functional
electric stimulation (FES) device.
The ﬁeld of BCI has expanded drastically in the past 10 years. This expansion has resulted
in many variations to the common spatial patterns (CSP) approaches, as well as deep learning,
tensor learning, and Riemannian geometry models. For smaller data sets, currently the most
common models are CSP variations and Riemannian geometry based classiﬁers. The current
state-of-the-art in Riemannian geometry uses unbiased spatial covariance matrices as features,
and is therefore comparable to the CSP approaches that were introduced two decades ago. This
project explores the concepts of the advances in CSP, namely, regularization, spectro-spatial
features, and adaptive approaches. These concepts were applied to Riemannian geometry
and led to novel BCI models that performed on par and even better than some of the current
state-of-the-art models.
An experiment application was implemented to record an inhouse dataset. The application
could both record EEG data and provide neurofeedback using a real time BCI decoder based
on Riemannian geometry. The inhouse data was recorded for 5 subjects as a 3-class single
limb motor imagery task, to resemble a neurorehabilitation setting.
The proposed models are a Spectro-Spatial Mimimum Distance to Riemannian Mean (SS-
MDRM) that build on the Common Spectro-Spatial Patterns concept; a Filter Bank Riemannian
Distance, built on the Filter Bank Common Spatial Patterns concept; and an adaptive MDRM
(aMDRM) classiﬁer. It was found that the best performing classiﬁer was a combination of the
SS-MDRM classiﬁer and the aMDRM classiﬁer. This classiﬁer scored an accuracy of 86.42% in
the benchmark dataset BCI Competition IV 2a in the 2-class setting for right-hand vs left-hand.
This is 3.12% higher than the previous highest scoring state-of-the-art classiﬁer.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Strokes are the most common cause of handicaps in adults. A stroke can happen to anyone
at anytime, but it is more likely to affect elder people. In Denmark, the overall age of the
population is increasing, and it follows that the amount of strokes will increase as well [9].
As any area of the brain can be affected by a stroke, the consequences can vary greatly from
patient to patient. Common consequences of a stroke include paralysis in one side of the body,
speech impairment, or personality change.
Treatments for patients with the side-effect of some degree of paralysis is based around
physio- or occupational therapy in which the patient excercises his or her paralyzed limbs. This
can be time-consuming and even impossible for patients with severe paralysis. A proposed
tool for neurorehabilitation is a Brain Computer Interface (BCI) controlled Functional Electric
Stimulation (FES) System that stimulates the nerves in the paralyzed limb when the patient
performs speciﬁc mental tasks, such as imagining the movement of the limb. A BCI-Controlled
FES System consists of a BCI that decodes the brain waves into actions and an FES system that
stimulates the nerves in the patient’s limb based on the actions sent from the BCI System. This
is hypothesised to make the neurorehabilitiation period shorter and therefore less expensive
and better for the patient.
The ﬁeld of BCIs has expanded massively in the past decade. This has been mainly due to
recent advancements in machine learning, digital signal processing, and neuroscience. One of
the recent advancements is Riemannian geometry, which has shown very promising results for
Motor Imagery (MI) classiﬁcation. However, Riemannian geometry based classiﬁers have not
yet been explored to the same extent as the many variations of the Common Spatial Patterns
(CSP) approaches. This thesis aims to explore how certain CSP concepts can be used in a
Riemannian Geometry setting.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Historical Background
In 1931, the ﬁrst article about electroencephalography was published, authored by Hans
Berger [10] about his discovery in 1929 of the recording of brain signals. He showed that the
brain signals could be recorded using electrodes on the scalp, and he hypothesized that the
corresponding recordings could be used as a diagnostic tool and a tool for studying the brain.
Years later in 1968, Kamiya, in [11], studied alpha waves, which are the neural oscillations
in the range of 8-12 Hz. Kamiya found that with some training, a person could control the
alpha waves at will, as they are present during relaxation and suppressed by focus. Kamiya
used a reward signal to train the subjects to control the alpha-waves at will and therefore
created the ﬁrst concept of neurofeedback[11].
A few years later in 1973, Jacques J. Vidal coined the term Brain-Computer Interface and
proposed potential uses for BCI systems in clinical and control settings [12]. At this point in
time, it was already known that the EEG signals contained information mostly below 30 Hz and
that signals captured by multiple electrodes could have a degree of correlation based on the
shared neural activity in different cortical sites. It was also known that speciﬁc waveforms could
be evoked by sensory stimuli. In Vidal’s study, Toward Direct Brain-Computer Communication
[12], he proposed how to preprocess the EEG data to remove muscular activity and how to set
up an actual interface to provide stimulus and feedback.
Today, the term BCI is used for systems that control external devices using an individual’s
brain signals, where the brain does not control the common output peripheral nerves or
muscles [13].
Recent advancements in signal processing and machine learning have lead to new meth-
ods of decoding brain signals. Previously, this decoding has consisted of ﬁnding spectral or
temporal information in speciﬁc brain regions by applying spectral and spatial ﬁlters. The
state-of-the-art methods consist of modifying these ﬁlters and by applying some of the tech-
niques found in other areas of machine learning, such as tensor decomposition, deep learning,
and Riemannian geometry.
1.3
Aim
The aim of this project is to extend the current knowledge in Riemannian geometry for BCI.
This will be done by exploring methods that has been used in past BCI systems and including
them into a Riemannian geometry setting.
• Exploring suitable modiﬁcations to Riemannian geometry based classiﬁers to increase
accuracy. These modiﬁcations include, regularization, subject speciﬁc spectro-spatial
features, ﬁlter banks and adaptive approaches.
• Implementation of a BCI development environment in Python.
• Design and implementation of a real-time BCI experiment for recording and evaluating
EEG data.
• Evaluation of BCI system in both an ofﬂine and an online setting.

Physiological Background
The central object in BCIs is naturally the human brain. The brain is the most complex organ
in the human body, and possibly one of the most complex systems discovered in the universe.
Non-invasive BCI systems use only a few electrodes. It is therefore important to understand
some of the basic principles of the brain in order to design a BCI system that captures the
essential information for a given BCI task.
The following chapter has two sections. The ﬁrst section covers the basic brain physiology
about the neuron, neuroplasticity, the organization of the brain, and how the connections in
the brain allow MI. This relates to both what happens during MI and why neurorehabiliation
allows post-stroke patients to regain motor control.
The second section covers the cause and effects of strokes. A brief explanation of strokes
and their consequences in terms of both the patient and impact on society is given. This
section also covers some of the current neurorehabilitation techniques and explains how BCI
can be a beneﬁcial resource in this.

CHAPTER 2. PHYSIOLOGICAL BACKGROUND
2.1
Basic Neuroscience
The brain consists of around 100 billion neurons connected in various ways. The neuron
connections makes up all the processing necessary to work with human senses, muscular
control, and emotions. Today, neuroscience is split into four levels of analysis [14, Ch. 1]:
• Molecular Neuroscience: The most elementary level of neuroscience. The analysis of
ﬂow of information from neuron to neuron and the study of which materials can pass
through and interact with neurons.
• Cellular Neuroscience: The study of the neurons, the different kinds of neurons and their
different functions, how they connect, and how they perform the desired computation.
• Systems Neuroscience: The study of the neuron circuits and how they function. This
includes senses and processes related to movement of muscles. This study also works
with decision making and perception.
• Behavioural Neuroscience: The most abstract level, which studies memory formation,
identity behaviour, and dreams.
For BCI systems that work with neurorehabilitation, the most important levels are the
celluar and systems levels. In this case, these are represented by the study of adaptivity in
neurons (for rehabilitation) and the neuronal circuits related to movement tasks.
2.1.1
The Neuron
Also called nerve cell, the neuron is in fact a cell and shares many traits found in regular human
cells. A neuron consists of three main parts: the soma, the dendrites, and the axon, all seen in
Fig. 2.1.
The Soma is the body or center of the neuron. It contains many of the same components
as an ordinary human cell, such as the nucleus, chromosomes, golgi apparatus, and mitochon-
dria, all of which are responsible for building proteins necessary for growth and shaping of the
speciﬁc neuron.
The Axon is unique to the neuron. This structure is specialized in transferring information
throughout the nervous system, including the brain. The initial part of the axon is called the
axon hillock which branches off into axon collaterals. In humans, axons vary in length from
less than a millimeter to over a meter, and the diameter ranges from less then 1 micrometer to
25 micrometers. The diameter of the axon determines the speed limit of information travelling
through the neuron. The axon terminals are positioned at the end of the axon collaterals. The
terminal connects to the dendrites of neurons at a point called the synapse. At the presynaptic
side, the electric signal travelling through the axon is converted into a neurotransmitter, a
chemical signal. The neurotransmitter is captured at the synapse of the other neuron at the
postsynaptic side of the terminal, which then converts the chemical signal into an electric
signal.

2.1.2
Action Potentials and Neural Coding
The neuron has a potential difference of about −70 mV across the inside and outside of the
lipid bi-layer membrane of the cell at rest. Active mechanisms, called ion-channels, maintain
this potential by passing ions into and out of the membrane.
A series of events happens when a strong input from an axon to a dendrite occurs. The ion
channels let a substantial amount of sodium ions (Na+) into the membrane, increasing the
membrane potential above 0 mV. The higher potential triggers the potassium ion channeln(K+)
to pump positive ions out of the membrane, causing the potential to decrease again. This
rapid change of potential is called an action potential or a spike seen in Fig. 2.2. Commonly,
these spikes are modelled as a binary signal (0 or 1).
Information is conveyed through neural coding as the patterns of the spike transmissions.
Neural coding has been studied both for single neurons and a population of neurons (popu-
lation coding). For single neurons, the coding has been studied as the mean ﬁring rate, also
known as frequency coding, where information is coded as the spikes per seconds. Temporal
coding is another type of coding in which information is conveyed by either precise timing of
ﬁring or by modulation to the ﬁring rate. Single neuron coding is not very applicable with EEG
type measurements as a single sensor would cover a large amount of neurons.
Population coding is the joint spike generation of a collection of neurons. A common
example is the work by Georgopulos, where neuron activity was captured as a primate reached

2.1.3
Neuroplasticity
The brain is an adaptive system and can therefore adapt to changes in its environment. This
adaptivity allows a brain to learn and to re-gain otherwise lost skills, as in rehabilitation
settings. On a neuronal level, the adaptation is called synaptic plasticity, which covers the
multiple ways of how neuron connections change based on the presence of stimuli. This is
generalized into long term potentiation (LTP) and long term depression (LTD), as well as short
term potentiation and depression [16]. The short term potentiation and depression only last
a few milliseconds, and they can either amplify (potentiation) or attenuate (depression) a
neuronal signal if spikes occur repeatedly.
LTP and LTD are more related to neuroplasticity, as these are lasting changes to neuronal con-
nections. Put simply, if neurons have correlated ﬁring patterns, their connections strengthen
(LTP). Conversely, their connections weaken if their ﬁrings are uncorrelated (LTD). The LTP
and LTD are the primary underlying reasons for obtaining or regaining skills through external
input [2, ch. 35].
Synaptic plasticity is enforced by repeatedly performing a task. This leads to reorganization

2.1.4
Brain Organization and Cortical Areas of the Neocortex
The brain consists of different regions that each have their own responsibilities. All of the
regions are connected through the brain stem, which conveys signals from the brain to the
rest of the nervous system.
The responsibilities of the brain region can be viewed as hierarchical. For example, in the
case of motor control, the highest layer consisting of the basal ganglia performs the necessary
actions to provide what the goal of the movement is. The middle layer decide on the sequences
of steps necessary to perform the given movement, this layer consists of the motor cortex
(neocortex) and cerebellum. The lowest layer is the brain stem which conveys the movement
information to the nervous system [14, Ch. 14].This is only one example showing how the brain
works. However, this example is relevant in terms of motor imagery BCI application. Note that
the primary motor cortex in the middle layer is a cortical area of the neocortex as seen in Fig.
2.3. Information from this area is accessible using different kinds of BCI acquisition methods,
such as EEG.

2.2
Stroke and Rehabilitation
Strokes occur when regular blood ﬂow to the brain is interrupted. There are two main types of
strokes: ischaemic and hemorrhagic strokes. Ischaemic strokes are caused by a blockage in
the blood supply. The blockage can be, for example, a blood clot that is either formed in the
brain or has moved to the brain through the blood stream. Hemorrhagic strokes occur when a
blood vessel ruptures and bleeds into an area in the brain. The bleeding causes damage to the
affected brain areas because of the extra amount of compression and pressure. 87 percent of
stroke occurrences are ischaemic strokes, and the other 13 percent are hemorrhagic strokes
[17] [18].
Epidemiology: Strokes are the second most common cause of death worldwide and the
leading cause of obtained disabilities [19]. In 2010, there were 16.9 million occurrences of
strokes worldwide, resulting in 5.9 million deaths [20]. From 1990 to 2010, the amount of
strokes increased due to the increase of world population. It is therefore expected that more
strokes will happen in the coming years as the world population increases. From 1990 to
2010 the mortality-to-incidence ratio has lowered. This may be due to better acute stroke
treatment; however, it also means that there are more stroke survivors that are likely to live
with disabilities.
Disabilities: Post-stroke survivors will live with brain-damage, which can have various
effects. These includes paralysis, weakness, or loss of sensation in one side of the body.
Other effects are visual impairment and difﬁculty of speech and swallowing [21]. While
the impairments are a physical disability to the post-stroke survivors, they can also lead
to depression and drastic changes in social role for the patient. The cost of treating stroke
survivors in the US alone is about $73 billion annually [19]. In Denmark, the cost in 2001 was
about $400 million (2.7 billion DKK) or about 4% of the annual healthcare expenses [19].
2.2.1
Current rehabilitation techniques
While post-stroke survivors can experience a multitude of consequences, this section and the
rest of this project will focus on the paralysis of the upper extremities (arm and hand), which
is a very common disability for post-stroke survivors. As previously mentioned, post-stroke
treatment is expensive, and it takes many hours of work with trained professionals.
In an ideal setting, stroke rehabilitation starts within 24 hours of the occurrence of the
stroke. The side-effects of a stroke can vary, but a large number of patients (more than 80%)
suffer from upper limb paralysis. 30-40% of patients that suffered from upper limb paralysis
had regained some motor control within 6 months. [22].
Currently, rehabilitation programs for post-stroke victims vary from 1 to 3 hours a day for 5
days a week, in which 30-45 minutes are usually physical or occupational therapy. The physical
and occupational therapy are commonly task-speciﬁc, meaning they relate to some typical
everyday activities and consist of repeatedly performing a given task [23].

2.2.2
Proposed Method for Neurorehabilitation
FES has been shown to have a positive effect on neurorehabilitation for upper limb paralysis
patients. One study found that intense FES therapy for neurorehabilitation led to regaining
the ability contract muscles in the affected limb, reduction in spasticity, and increased range
of motion [24].
In the proposed system for the overall project, a MI BCI is meant to control the FES system.
Imagining moving a body part is done by actively trying to perform a given movement while
preventing any actual motor output. Doing this activates the same areas of the brain as
that of actual movement, thereby showing that the strategy, goal, and planning of imagined
movement is similar to that of the performed movement [25].
Mukaino et al [26] implemented a BCI system that controlled a neuromuscular electrical
stimulation device. This system was tested on a stroke patient with severe hemiplegia, and
found that there were signs of increased neuroplasticity while using the BCI system as a
neurorehabilitation device. This study concludes by encouraging further research into this
area.

Brain-Computer interfaces
BCI systems provide a means to transfer information from a brain to a computer by decod-
ing patterns that emerge during mental activities. This allows for interacting with external
applications or the environment without the use of the ordinary peripheral neuronal path-
ways, such as hands, arms or the voice. BCI systems give severely handicapped people the
ability to perform common tasks that would otherwise be impossible. An example is giving
patients with locked-in syndrome, in which an aware patient cannot move or speak, a chance
to communicate using a keyboard connected to a BCI system. BCI Systems can also be used
for playing games [27] or controlling a smart home [2] and most importantly for this project:
neurorehabilitation. Each application needs to be tailored to the task. For communication
and control, speed is an important factor. Information has to be conveyed quickly, often at
the cost of accuracy. For neurorehabilitation, accuracy is much more important than speed.
The patient must perform the same task and receive appropriate feedback that validates the
corresponding task.
This chapter gives a description of BCI systems, the elements that make up BCI systems,
an overview of data acquisition tools, conventional techniques for EEG data, and an overview
of the state-of-the-art in BCI.

3.1
Recording the Brain Signals
The goal for data acquisition is to record signals in the brain and transmit them to a device
that can process such data. Data acquisition tools are called neuroimaging devices and can be
implemented in various ways. The different devices can be compared in terms of: invasiveness,
spatial resolution, temporal resolution, hardware complexity and price. The devices can either
perform direct measures, which capture the electrical or magnetic activity of the brain, or
indirect measures, which capture the metabolism and hemodynamics of the brain [2].
A short overview of the different brain signal acquisition methods list listed below:
• EEG: Electroencephalography; noninvasive; electrodes are placed on scalp; captures
electrical activity in the brain.
• MEG: Magnetoencephalography; noninvasive; records the magnetic ﬁeld around the
brain that is produced by the neuronal activity.
• ECoG: Electrocorticography; semi-invasive technique where an array of electrodes are
placed just on the outside of the cortex.
• INR: Intracortical Neuron Recording; invasive; similar to EEG and ECoG, but the elec-
trodes are placed into the gray matter.
• fNIRS: Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy; noninvasive; identiﬁes neural activity by
measuring light absorbance in the brain areas.
• fMRI: Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging; noninvasive; measures the hemody-
namics which is the blood oxygen level-dependent contrast (BOLD).
• PET: Positron Emission Tomography; noninvasive; measures positron emission from a
tracer that is injected into the bloodstream. Identiﬁes neural activity as positron emission
in active areas rises.
• SPECT: Single Photon Emission Computerized Tomography; similar to PET but uses
gamma ray tracer instead of positron emission tracer.
In Fig. 3.1, a comparison by spatial resolution, temporal resolution, and hardware com-
plexity is shown. The chart is meant to be a quick overview of the different systems and their
properties. For this project, the ﬁnalized product should be inexpensive and portable. These
constraints mean that EEG and fNIRS are the only possible candidates [28]. While fNIRS
could be an interesting approach, the lack of test equipment and available data sets makes the
preferred choice EEG.
3.1.1
EEG
EEG is a non-invasive, portable, and relatively cheap neuroimaging method with a low tempo-
ral resolution. The spatial resolution is typically rather high because of the distance towards
the source signal and the spacing between electrodes.

During excitation of a neuron, an electrical impulse is ﬁred from the axon towards the
synapse. The electrical current is made up of ions (mostly Na+, K+, Ca2+, and Cl−). These
ions ﬂow through ion channels at the synapses. A tremendous amount of ions are pumped
through the ion-channels during excitation, which corresponds to a large electrical signal [29].
The excitation of a single neuron does not create enough current for an electrical signal to be
picked up at the scalp. Instead, EEG picks up the signal of a large population of neurons that
are excited simultaneously. The signal has to pass through the brain, the skull, and the skin
before it is picked up by an electrode. This is clearly seen in Fig. 3.2. The EEG electrode has to
pick up signals that are far from the cortex, while ECoG or INR picks up signals close to or at
the cortex. Despite the disadvantages, EEG has become a popular acquisition approach for MI
[30] and has also found applicable for post-stroke rehabilitation [31].
The 10-20 System is an international standard for electrode placement on the scalp for
EEG measurements [32]. This system provides a reliable method for placing electrodes and
communicating which areas of the scalp are being measured. The 10-20 system is named
after the placement of electrodes. When using the 10-20 system, the ﬁrst step is to ﬁnd the
landmarks, which are the nasion (between the eyes), the inion (bony structure in the back of
the skull), and the preaurical points (another bony structure close to the ears). The distances
between these points are then split into percentages. The FPz is positioned 10% of the length
from the nasion to the inion. Moving 20% further is Fz, and 20% further is the center Cz, then
the Pz and in the end, 10% from the inion, is Oz. The odd numbered placements are on the
left side of the head, and the even numbered are on the right, as is illustrated in Fig. 3.3.
Figure 3.3: The international 10-20 system [4].
The 10-20 System allows replicating EEG measurements in a consistent way and for esti-
mation of the location of cortical areas independent of the shape of the subjects head.
3.1.2
Brainwave patterns
Rhythms are always present in a healthy brain. Alterations to these rhythms can happen by
either external stimuli or by activating motor function. External stimuli trigger an event related

potential (ERP), which is a peak or a series of peaks that happen after a stimuli. For example,
the P300 BCI speller paradigm is named after this phenomena. The P300 BCI speller consists
of a grid with letters that light up either sequentially or randomly. When the letter which
the subject focuses on lights up, an ERP is triggered approximately 300 ms after the event [2].
Event-related desynchronization (ERD) and event-related synchronization (ERS) are more
frequent in MI paradigms, in which the EEG rhythms are altered by a change in mental state.
An example of ERD occurs in relation to onset of voluntary motor offset. Studies have
found that the ERD begins up to 2 seconds prior to the onset of the movement[33]. The ERD
for motor function happens in the mu-band (8-13 Hz) and lower beta bands (13-16 Hz). The
same phenomena can be seen in MI tasks, in which an imagined movement induces an ERD
[2].
The ERD happens in both different frequency bands and in different spatial areas depend-
ing on the task [33]. For this reason, different ﬁlter bank and spectro-spatial approaches has
been proposed to BCI systems [34] [35].
3.1.3
Improving the EEG Signal
Issues with EEG signals include problems related to noise, non-stationarity, and the fact that
there are trillions of neurons and only relatively few electrodes to capture this activity.
Noise are all the signal that is unrelated to the current task. Some of this noise is induced
by the subject, such as artifacts from minor movements, which can be movements of the
eyes or jaw, but could also be from other body parts. Even the heartbeat shows in the EEG.
EMC-induced noise from other electronic equipment might also show in the EEG [36].
Non-stationarity is another form of noise that does not show up as artifacts in the EEG
but as a change to the signal over time. This can occur both within a section, where, for
example, an electrode is loose, or the impedance between the scalp and electrode changes.
The non-stationarity can also arise when the mental state of the subject changes, such as if the
subject becomes tired, decides on another mental strategy for a task, or mistakenly performs
the wrong task [36]. The non-stationarities happen between sessions as well, especially due to
electrode placement that might be slightly different between sessions, and the mental state of
the subject has changed.
Robust decoders are therefore necessary in order to suppress the inﬂuence of artifacts and
sources of non-stationarity. Such models have been proposed using regularization that limits
the variance caused by artifacts [37]. Another approach includes stationary subspace analysis,
which is an unsupervised decomposition algorithm similar to Principal Component Analysis
(PCA) and Independent Component Analysis (ICA). In stationary subspace analysis, a signal is
decomposed into stationary and non-stationary parts [38] [36].
The non-stationary part of the signal might however contain discriminant information,
such as if the electrode placement changes. In that case, it might be better to track the changes.
Adaptive classiﬁers aim to track the non-stationary nature of EEG signal. Such methods
track the changes to the EEG signal and update the model parameters accordingly. Adaptive
methods are discussed in depth in Section 5.4

Conventional BCI Approaches
Most BCI systems can be explained in terms of a few processing elements, namely signal ac-

a general scheme for BCI Systems. In a conventional system the data acquisition is most likely
to be performed using an EEG-cap, and the pre-processing is performed in order to remove
noise and artifacts such as eye-blink, muscle movement, powerline, etc. Feature extraction

present, followed by extracting the power of that signal usually based on the logarithm of

discriminant analysis, can be applied to ﬁnd the criteria which separates the classes.

Applications of BCI can be almost anything. The more common applications are based on
controlling a spelling interface [39], the objects in the environment [13], or video games [3]. A
feedback mechanism is usually part of an application. For example, a speller would let the
user know that a letter has been pressed by showing the letter and potentially playing a sound.
The feedback mechanism helps the user know that a given task has been performed with or
without success. This is acts as a reward mechanism and provides training for the user.
3.2.1
Paradigms
BCI paradigms are based on the wide differences in how patterns are emerged. P300, SSVEP,
and motor imagery are the three most common paradigms for EEG based BCI Systems [40].
16
==PAGE:32==
CHAPTER 3. BRAIN-COMPUTER INTERFACES
MI is the paradigm of imagined movement. It is based around the neural activities that
occur related to the intentions of moving a limb without actually performing the movement.
The idea is that the neural activity is spatially similar for imagined and actual movement of
limbs [41], but initially with lower spectral magnitude for imagined movement signifying less
signal. This signal can, however, be improved by training the patients using feedback.
P300 and SSVEP both rely on external stimulation. P300 is based on a peak in the EEG
signal that appears around 300 ms. To detect this peak, the EEG signals have to be processed
by spatial ﬁltering and temporal averaging to create what is called an event related potential
(ERP) [2, Ch. 1.5]. SSVEP is based on detection of spectral features in the evoked potentials.
This is done by having a subject attend to a blinking object (such as an LED). The oscillations
of the blinking object force an oscillation of the same frequency to occur in the neural activity
by the occipital cortex [2, Ch. 1.4]. While both P300 and SSVEP are convenient in terms of low
amount of electrode usage and short calibration time, they both suffer from the necessity of
external stimuli. Though similar, P300 and SSVEP are different paradigms. The brain itself
generates the evoked potential for P300, while SSVEP is a continuation of an external signal.
For neurorehabilitation, MI is preferred since it does not rely on external stimulation.
MI even without EEG has also proven to be an effective technique for rehabilitation and
performance increase [42], [43], but providing a feedback mechanism by utilizing BCI might
be ideal for faster neurorehabilitation.

3.3.1
State-of-the-art
The ﬁeld of BCI has been expanded drastically in the past decade. This section aims to give a
short introduction to recent signal processing and machine learning trends. The current trends
in classiﬁers are discussed in depth by F. Lotte et al in their review article [54]. They summarise
18
==PAGE:34==
CHAPTER 3. BRAIN-COMPUTER INTERFACES
the advances in BCI classiﬁcation since 2007. The following list gives a short overview of the
advances and their pros and cons.
• Adaptive Classiﬁcation: The classiﬁer’s parameters are updated as new EEG data be-
comes available. It enables the classiﬁer to adapt towards a new data distribution.
Adaptive classiﬁers can be constructed in various ways: supervised, where the incoming
class labels are known and the classiﬁer can be retrained upon each new sample [55];
unsupervised, where the global mean and covariance matrix for the classiﬁer (such as
LDA [56]) is being updated as new data becomes available; and semi-supervised, which
predicts class labels and re-trains the classiﬁer based on the predicted labels [47].
• Tensor based Classiﬁcation: EEG data can be expressed as a tensor (multidimensional
array), such as in the shape of (Channel × Time × Frequency). This gives the basis
of using tensor speciﬁc algorithms to extract spectro-spatial features and to perform
classiﬁcation [57]. A downside is that the tensor approaches tend to extract a lot of
features in which heavy regularization is necessary.
• Deep Learning:
Deep neural networks consist of an input layer where data arrives
and an output layer where the classiﬁer resides. In between are the hidden layers that
can be designed and connected in many ways and have shown promise of also being
interpretable[58]. The current state-of-the-art is to use models that ﬁnd spectro-spatial
features, such as using multiple convolutional layers or using convolutional layers that
work on the signal directly such as Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) [52]. A downside
with deep neural networks is that they rely on a lot of data for training and can be too
computational heavy to work in real-time.
• Riemannian Geometry Based Classiﬁcation: The classiﬁers use the underlying geo-
metric space of symmetric positive deﬁnite (SPD) matrices to perform manipulations
and speciﬁc classiﬁcation strategies. This relies on the fact that a mental state can be
expressed as a SPD-matrix, such as a covariance matrix [59]. This thesis explores exactly
this idea.
The mentioned types of classiﬁers all rely on extracting features that contain spatial or
spectro-spatial information. The classiﬁers are also not mutually exclusive; adaptive classi-
ﬁcation, for example, can be implemented in all the other classiﬁcation methods. Likewise,
Riemannian geometry and deep learning models can be combined by introducing hidden
layers that work on SPD-matrices. This has been done for computer vision applications with
great results [60], but has not yet been implemented for BCI purposes.
Based on the current state-of-the-art in BCI, this project has chosen to work on exploring
the possibilities of regularization, subject dependent spectro-spatial features, and adaptive
approaches in Riemannian geometry.

Riemannian Geometry
In mathematics, the ﬁeld of Riemannian geometry is the study of curved spaces that have a
locally ﬂat representation with a smoothly changing inner product.
Riemannian manifolds involve any differentiable geometric space (smooth geometry)
which can be locally explained as a ﬁnite-dimensional Euclidean space [61]. A common
example would be the surface of the Earth, which is a 2 dimensional geometry in a 3 dimen-
sional space, where the shortest distance between two points takes shape as a curve along
the surface. The Earth can be locally represented by a geographic map in which the shortest
distance between two points is the Euclidean distance. This analogy can be explained in
terms of Riemannian geometry, where the Earth’s surface is the manifold and the geographic
map at some point on the manifold is the tangent space. Distortions occur when measuring
Euclidean distances on the tangent space when the point of reference is far from the points of
measurements. Just as a map of the entire world distorts towards the poles.
Riemannian manifolds occur in any place where continuous data are created under some
constraints. Examples of this are unit vectors that take up geometries of n-dimensional spheres.
Seen from far, the manifold is a sphere, but locally the space would seem Euclidean or ﬂat -
similar to the surface of the Earth that can be perceived as spherical from far but locally ﬂat.

4.0.1
Applications of Riemannian Geometry
Riemannian geometry is a new ﬁeld for processing EEG data that has been studied for only
10 years [59]. So far Riemannian geometry has shown promising results and is among the
current state-of-the-art (Tables 3.1 and 3.2). Riemannian geometry is not exclusive to BCI and
has found its uses in a broad spectrum of ﬁelds that work with multidimensional continuous
data, as these data can often be represented as a point on a Riemannian manifold. One such
Riemannian manifold is the manifold of symmetric positive deﬁnite (SPD) matrices, which
includes the geometry where covariance matrices can be considered points.
A common use of this is in computer vision, where an image (or a region of an image) can
be modelled as correlations of low-level features such as RGB values, color gradients, ﬁlter
response, and so on. The covariance matrix of such features is a useful tool, since the size of
the covariance matrix is not determined by the amount of pixels in the picture, but the amount
of low-level features [62]. Also, the covariance matrix is intrinsically robust towards noise as
it is computed with a form of averaging. Finding similar objects in an image could then be
conducted by splitting the image into a set of covariance matrices of low-rank features and
grouping them by distances on the manifold.
For BCI applications, the SPD data descriptor can be constructed as the sample covariance
matrix (SCM) of the recorded time-series data from the electrodes. The covariance matrices
of the EEG trials can then be expressed as points on a Riemannian manifold. Classiﬁcation
can then be performed by computing the distances between a trial and the mean covariance
matrices of each class, and picking the class which has the shortest Riemannian distance to
the trial.
4.0.2
Properties of Riemannian Manifolds
This section will give a short explanation of some of the terms that are used when working
with Riemannian manifolds.
• Manifold: The geometry or space where the data lay; this can be linear or non-linear.
• Tangent Space: The space of tangent vectors of the manifold at a reference point Pµ. The
geometry of this space is Euclidean and can be seen as a local Euclidean approximation
of the manifold.
• Log Map: The transformation that takes a point on the manifold and projects it onto the
tangent space.
• Exp Map: The inverse operation of log map that takes a point on the tangent space and
projects it onto the manifold.
• Geodesics: The shortest path between two points on the manifold. This path follows the
curvature of the manifold.
• Riemannian Distance: The length of the geodesic.
• Fretchet Mean: The mean point of the data, given the constraints of the geometry.

These terms are general and apply to any Riemannian geometry. The methods of comput-
ing the log map, exp map, geodesics, and distances all depend on the underlying manifold. In
this project, the manifold is the geometry of covariance matrices which lay within the space of
SPD-matrices.
4.1
The Space of SPD-Matrices
Symmetric matrices are equal to the transpose of the matrix itself, P = PT . If the matrix is
symmetric and has strictly positive eigenvalues, then the matrix is SPD. These constraints
cause the underlying geometry of SPD matrices to take the shape of a convex cone [2, Ch. 19].
The space of SPD matrices can formally be written as in Eq. (4.1).
Mc = {P ∈Rc×c|P = PT , X ≻0}
(4.1)
Mc is the space of SPD matrices and also a Riemannian manifold. P is a c × c square
matrix, where its transpose is equal to itself and has positive eigenvalues [59]. The constraints
mean that mathematical operations have to be altered in order to respect the underlying
geometry. Having a non-linear shape also means that simple Euclidean operations, such as
averaging or interpolation, might cause the resulting data point to lie outside of the manifold
and therefore no longer be an SPD matrix. Mathematical operations for SPD matrices are
computed using their eigenvalues, such that, log(P) = V log(D)V−1, where P is the SPD-
matrix, V is the eigenvectors, and D is a diagonal matrix of the eigenvalues of P. Other
operations, such as the exponential function exp(P) and power functions Pn are computed
similarly on the eigenvalues of P [61]. The following sections explain common concepts of
Riemannian geometry. These are the tangent space, geodesics, and distances. In the later
sections, it will be shown how these concepts can be combined to compute the Riemannian
mean of a given set of SPD-matrices and how to perform classiﬁcation tasks that have shown
to outperform the standard CSP-LDA classiﬁers.

• Symmetry: G(P1, P2, P3) = G(Pπ(1), Pπ(2), Pπ(3)) for any permutation π {1,2,3}.
• Monotonicity: G(P1, P2, P3) ≤G(P′
1, P2, P3) if P1 ≤P′

5.1.1
Preprocessing
The goal of preprocessing is to increase the signal-to-noise ratio in the raw signals. This can be
done by removing noisy channels, detrending, resampling, band-pass ﬁltering, and even some
spatial ﬁltering like rereferencing [3].
For MI, the most inﬂuential frequency bands lies within the lower bound of mu-waves and
the higher bound of beta-waves. This band is 7-30 Hz. There might also lie some discriminant
information in the higher frequency bands in shape of harmonics of the mu-band. The
proposed preprocessing step is therefore to reduce the sampling frequnecy to be no lower
than 100 Hz to ensure a Nyqvist frequnecy of 50 Hz, followed by a bandpass ﬁlter in the range
of 7-45 Hz to keep some higher frequency information while still removing the induced 50 Hz
element that might be present due to noise from the power line. Another beneﬁt of ﬁltering the
frequencies below 7 Hz away is that detrending is no longer necessary as the trend in unﬁltered
EEG signals can be viewed as a very low frequency.
The artifacts from eye movement show up as the Electrooculogram (EOG) in the EEG
signals. The EOG contains mostly energy in the low frequency range (below 4 Hz), but the
actual frequency contents of EOG may go as high as 54 Hz. The EOG can be attentuated or
removed by performing blind source separation, such as Independent Component Analysis,
followed by removing the component that is correlated the most with a reference EOG [48].

This step was not implemented in this project, but the band pass ﬁlter of 7-45 Hz did remove
the low frequency information that is assumed to belong to the EOG.
Channels should be removed if they do not contain discriminant information for the
classes. This is especially true for Riemannian models, where the channel count should be low
to avoid being inﬂuenced by the Curse of Dimensionality, as distances become very large and
inaccurate as the amount of dimensions increases [6].

5.2.4
Relation Between CSP and Riemannian Distance
The fundamental similarity between CSP and Riemannian distance is that the CSP ﬁlter is
computed using the eigenvectors of C−1
2 C1, while the Riemannian distance is computed as a
function of the eigenvalues.
The eigenvalues are a measure of variance explained by their corresponding eigenvectors.
Therefore, if a trial has a covariance matrix represented as an SPD matrix, P1 that is very close
to the class matrix C1, then the variance of the CSP ﬁltered channels (the diagonal elements of
its covariance matrix) will be similar to the eigenvalues of C−1
2 P1.
However, if the trial is contaminated by noise, such as slight movement to the electrodes or
change in mental state, then the diagonal of the covariance matrix of the CSP ﬁltered signal
might only contain some of the discriminant information.
Riemannian distance is a function of all the eigenvalues before CSP ﬁltering and therefore
does not rely on the diagonal elements of the covariance matrix only. Arguably, this makes
features extracted from Riemannian distance more robust to noise than those extracted by
CSP [5].

5.3
Riemannian Geometry Classiﬁers
Classiﬁers based on Riemannian Geometry are often implemented as either manifold based
or tangent space based [79]. Common for both is that they rely on the underlying geometry of
an SPD matrix and are therefore also known as covariance based classiﬁers. The Minimum
Distance to Riemannian Mean (MDRM) classiﬁer is very popular for manifold classiﬁcation
due to the simplicity and state-of-the-art accuracy for MI tasks. Complex classiﬁers can be
implemented by utilizing the Euclidean approximation of the manifold. These are known as
tangent space classiﬁers, in which the SPD matrix is tangent space projected and vectorized as
a mean of feature extraction for common machine learning classiﬁers. This section covers the
covariance matrix estimation, the MDRM and Tangent Space classifer approach, and explains
how to perform operations on the manifold to increase the accuracy for a MDRM classiﬁer.
5.3.1

matrix estimation is adding a regularization factor that suppresses noise. The major reason for
regularization is to trade off some of the variance for bias, and in that way avoid overﬁtting.
Regularization for covariance matrices can be done by shrinkage, which is done by in-
terpolating the unbiased covariance matrix Cscm with a covariance matrix containing prior
information Cp Eq.(5.15).
Creg = (1 −αc)Cscm + αcCp
(5.15)
This gives two extra parameters to the system. The ﬁrst is αc, which is the shrinkage factor.
Having αc = 0 means no regularization, and αc = 1 turns any matrix into the prior information
covariance matrix. The prior information can in the simplest form be the identity matrix I.
This is known as diagonally loading, where the independence of each channel is emphasized
[71]. Regularization towards the identity matrix has the beneﬁt of an automatic estimation of
the optimal shrinkage factor, as described by Ledoit and Wolf [80]. Other prior information
can be the global mean covariance matrix for the training data or the mean covariance matrix
of multiple selected subjects. Lotte et al [71] compared prior information matrices for use in a
CSP approach. They found that regularization of the covariance matrices for CSP resulted in
better accuracy than no regularization. However, there were no signiﬁcant difference between
the variations of prior information. Shrinkage of the covariance matrix has been well studied
in CSP approaches but is novel in the ﬁeld of Riemannian geometry based classiﬁers.

complexity and some distortion to the underlying SPD-space.
5.3.4

In the previous section, it was described how common machine learning classiﬁers can be
implemented in the tangent space to do complex classiﬁcation. This section show that also
data manipulation can be performed in the tangent space.
A speciﬁc form of ﬁltering using coefﬁcient computed using LDA in tangent space has been
coined Fisher Geodesic Discriminant Analysis (FGDA) [59]. The purpose of FGDA is to ﬁnd the
geodesics that contains discriminant information for class separation, followed by ﬁltering the
data along these geodesics.
This underlying idea is similar to CSP in terms of ﬁnding a set of ﬁlters that minimizes the
within-lass cost function and maximises the between class cost function.

5.4
Adaptive Methods
As previously discussed, BCI systems are prone to being non-stationary due to external noise,
slight changes in electrode placement, or the changes in the mental strategy of the subject.
Creating a system that changes according to the non-stationary parts is called an adaptive
system. BCI systems consist of two systems, the BCI interface and the subject. Implementing
an adaptive strategy for the subject involves creating a feedback loop with a reward strategy for
performing an action correctly. In that way, the non-stationarities from shift in mental strategy
would be suppressed [55] as the subject would be forced to stick with the initial mental strategy
from the training or calibration session.
Adaptivity can also be implemented in the BCI algorithm, which gives the subject more
freedom and adds robustness towards other sources of non-stationarity. The non-stationarity
shows as a covariate shift in the feature space, where both the mean and distribution of
features have changed [51] while the distribution of labels remains the same. A common goal
for adaptive systems is to track the covariate shift and perform compensation. This can be
done in both a supervised, semi-supervised, and unsupervised matter.
The adaptive strategy can be implemented in the signal processing algorithm [7] in which
the spatial ﬁlters of CSP are updated as new samples become available. The update strategy
for the spatial ﬁlters was done in [7] by knowing the label of the trial in which the current
sample belongs to. By having this information, an update strategy was implemented for the
class speciﬁc covariance matrix, C1, as well as the common covariance matrix,Cc. Updating
the eigenvectors of C−1
1 Cc then resulting in updated spatial ﬁlters.
Ang et al [47] proposed a FBCSP algorithm that used online adaptive and semi-supervised
learning. In this study, the initial model was trained using data from a training session. The
supervised adaptive learning strategy consisted of sequentially predicting the evaluation data,
in which the past evaluation data was used to retrain the classiﬁer using their true labels. The
semi-supervised learning strategy used the same approach, but the classiﬁer was trained on
predicted labels instead of true labels. The data with predicted labels would only be used for
retraining if the probability of correct classiﬁcation was above some threshold.
Kumar et al [82] proposed seven different adaptive models using Riemmanian geometry in
a recent study, speciﬁcally the MDRM classiﬁers. The models were grouped into RETRAIN or
REBIAS adaptation. RETRAIN models were initially trained on training data and updated ac-
cording to the evaluation data. In this case the class means were shifted towards the predicted
label or ground truth label of an incoming trial. For REBIAS adaptation, the covariance matrix
of a new trial, Ci, was shifted to adapt to changes. This meant that common mean covariance
matrix, Cc, was recomputed for every new trial, followed by shifting the covariance matrix of

Implementation
In this project, three proposed decoders are implemented and compared. This chapter focuses
on the functionality and reasoning behind the three decoders. Previous chapter discussed
some of the modiﬁcations and considerations that have been implemented in CSP based
approaches and how Riemannian geometry has been used for BCI decoders. This chapter
focuses on how some of the advances in CSP methods can be implemented into a Riemannian
framework.
The development environment was created as a set of Python libraries that could be inte-
grated with various other development libraries and visualization software. The development
framework was not meant to be stand-alone software for BCI, but more of a set of Python
functions that enabled fast prototyping and testing of BCI algorithms.
The ﬁrst decoder is based around the CSSP approach and will be called spectro-spatial
Minimum Riemannian Distance to Mean (SS MRDM). It works by extending the covariance
matrix with lagged channels, which can lead to a very large covariance matrix.
The second decoder also works on spectro-spatial features, but is built around a ﬁlter bank
approach in order to reduce the size of the covariance matrix. However, this comes at a cost of
performing manifold operations on multiple covariance matrices. This decoder will be called
Filter Bank - Riemannian Distance - Linear Discriminant Analysis, as it uses a ﬁlter bank and
Riemannian distance for feature extraction and LDA for classiﬁcaiton.
The last decoder is an exploration in using adaptive approaches in Riemannian geometry,
called adaptive Minimum Riemannian Distance to Mean (aMDRM). It is based on spatial
features only, but can be extended with a ﬁlter bank or a lagged covariance matrix for inclusion
of spectral features as well.
The last section of this chapter describes a method of encapsulation that allows any model
built around the proposed framework to be used in an online setting.

Design Considerations for a BCI System
The overall goal has to be kept in mind when designing a BCI System. A BCI system for neurore-

Implementation Environment
Models in BCI can become rather complex using many steps for preprocessing, signal process-

and compared to each other. For this reason, a uniﬁed framework was developed for model
development, visualization, evaluation and for comparison of different models. The frame-

6.2.1
scikit-learn
Scikit-learn is a library, or so called SciPy Toolkit, for Python. It is developed as a machine
learning toolkit for Python and consists of a whelm of different algorithms for processing data,
both in terms of preprocessing, feature engineering and classiﬁcation. The processing classes
in scikit-learn are called estimators. Estimators can be either Transformers or Predictors.
Predictors, also known as classiﬁers, predicts the target from a set of features. An example
would be to train a SVM by the function SVM.fit(Xtrain,ytrain), and then making it predict
the targets by ypredict = SVM.transform(Xtest).
Transformers are all the processing functions that modiﬁes the data somehow, this can be
preprocessing functions, dimensionality reduction functions or distance measures. An exam-
ple would be a normalization function that subtracts the mean and divides by the standard
deviation. Such function would compute the mean and standard deviation using the funcition
normalize.fit(Xtrain,ytrain) and perform the action using normalize.transform(Xtest)
[84].
A powerful feature of scikit-learn is the possibility of combining estimators into pipelines
and unions. A pipeline is a serial concatenation of estimator classes that each call fit
and transform. A union is a set of estimators that are combined in parallel that concate-
nates their output after running. A CSP-LDA model could be implemented as a pipeline as
[Preprocessing, Spectral Filtering, Common Spatial Patterns, Linear Discriminant
Analysis], if the spectral ﬁltering and CSP were changed with a union of Spectral Filters and
CSP, then this approach would be a FB-CSP-LDA.
Scikit-learn does however lack BCI speciﬁc functions, which is why the processing library
was developed with that in mind, such that the functions in the processing library could be
integrated with the already existing scikit-learn tools.

Model 2: Filter Bank Riemannian Distance Classiﬁcation
The spectro-spatial model from previous section revealed that spectral information in the EEG
signal did contain features that could be used for improving the accuracy. The downside was
that performing the necessary computations for a classiﬁer was to computationally intense and
might not be applicable for a real-time system. Another method for extracting spectral features
is to do work with ﬁlter banks. This has been done with great success for CSP based classiﬁers,
such as Ang et Al[34] that did a FBCSP model as the winning entry for BCI Competition IV 2a
in 2008.
Riemannian classiﬁers has not previously been implemented using a ﬁlter bank. This is
most likely due to the idea from [59] that a Riemannian geometry classiﬁer performs feature
extraction and classiﬁcation in a single step. A ﬁlter bank approach can be implemented as a
preprocessing step for a combined feature extraction and classiﬁcation, however this require
concatenation of of all the signals from every band pass ﬁlter before computing the covariance
matrices. This results in the same issue as with the SSMDRM, where the covariance matrices
becomes too large for efﬁcient computations.
The proposed ﬁlter bank model challenges the combined feature extraction and classiﬁ-
cation method, by using Riemannian distances as features for a classiﬁer. The Riemannian
distances are computed in every sub-band of the ﬁlter bank, and these distances are used
as features for a classiﬁer, such as LDA. The distances as scores might not be interrelated for
each sub-band, it is therefore beneﬁcial to do some normalization and feature selection before
feeding to a classiﬁer. The normalization was computed by a softmax function, which took
the Riemannian distances from a sub-band as input and returned the probability of a trial
belonging to each class. The softmax function also known as the normalized exponential and
generalizes to multiclass settings for computing the probability of trial belonging to a class (Ω)
given a set of features (f) [76]. The softmax function is computed for every feature fk in a set of
features f.

6.6.2
Updating the Riemannian Distance
The Riemannian distance is computed as a function of the eigenvalues of two SPD matri-
ces. The eigenvalues can be estimated by repeatedly computing the Rayleigh quotient and
recursively performing ﬂattening. As described in the Section 4.1.3. This process can be com-
putationally expensive, especially if the covariance matrix is large. The main update strategy
initialize the eigenvectors for the Rayleigh quotient with the previous eigenvectors.
Another optimization is to only compute the most inﬂuential eigenvalues, similar to only
computing the most extreme eigenvectors for CSP ﬁlter. However, this optimization only
provides an approximation for the Riemannian distance. If an approximation seems suitable,
then the Log Euclidean distance might be a better choice, since it is computationally very
efﬁcient to compute.
6.6.3
Real-Time Classiﬁcation and Providing Feedback
The classiﬁcation could be implemented in real-time, simply by the running computation
of the Riemannian distances and selecting the class that was closest to the input covariance
matrix. This method of classiﬁcation might not be ideal for providing feedback as it would the
output of the classiﬁer would be a forced choice, and would not show how certain it was for
being correct. For example, in a trial where the Riemannian distance to Class 1 was 4.99 and
the distance to Class 2 was 5.01, then the classiﬁer would select Class 2. However, in this case
the two classes were so close to each other that the classiﬁcation might be wrong due to some
noise. If the trial was falsely predicted then the subject would be provided false feedback.

To build the real-time classiﬁer which could provide usable online feedback, some post-
processing had to be done to the Riemannian distances. The steps are shown in Fig. 6.13. The
steps are as following:
1. Compute the covariance matrix using Eq. (6.8).
2. Compute the Riemannian distances to the class means using Eq. (4.6).
3. Compute and subtract the mean, such that the Riemannian distances vector has a zero
mean.
4. Perform running average to avoid sudden ﬂuctuations.
5. Gain the output of the running average ﬁlter with a negative constant.
6. Input the feature vector the softmax function 6.3 to compute probabilities.
Figure 6.13: The decoder pipeline for a real-time classiﬁcation scheme for a Riemannian
distance based classiﬁer, The input is EEG data, either in a single sample or as a frame, and the
output is the probabilities of the EEG data belonging to a precomputed class mean.
Step 1 and 2 combined is the MDRM classiﬁer previously discussed, these two steps could
be switched out with either the SS-MDRM or aMDRM. In Step 3 the current offset was removed,
this step does not inﬂuence the softmax function as the difference between values does not
change, however, it was included for readability in the output plots (Fig. 6.14 for example).
Step 4 was a running average ﬁlter implemented as an exponential moving average function
with a factor αrt for averaging over past feature vectors. xn = (1 −αrt)x + αrtxn −1, where x is
the input to the running average ﬁlter. Step 5 multiplies the output of the ﬁlter with a negative
factor. The factor was negative to do a sign reversal because otherwise the softmax function
would give the lowest probability to the class that should have the highest. If the gain was low,
then the softmax function would distribute the probability to each class as being very similar.
If the gain was high then the softmax function would predict one class to be very high (100%)
and the other classes very low (0%).

Experiment Design
EEG data was recorded in-house in order to validate the proposed models on data that would
resemble a neurorehabilitation setting. A data acquisition a tool that could run a motor
imagery experiment and record data through the Lab Streaming Layer (LSL) in addition to
performing real-time classiﬁcation using a sample wise BCI decoder was developed.
The experiment for this project was a 3-class MI setting, with the classes, palmar grasp,
elbow ﬂexion, and pinch using the right arm only. A data recording session consisted of 2
calibration sessions and one evaluation session. A decoder was trained on the calibration
session before the evaluation session was run, such that real-time feedback could be supplied
for the subject.
This chapter contains a description of the chosen paradigm together with the design
choices, and a description of the calibration and evaluation sessions. It also explains the
recording technique in which the acquisition pipeline is explained, as well as an argumentation
for the electrode placement.
The chapter concludes with with a section describing the implementation approach for
the data acquisition and experiment designing tool, as well as how these could be evaluated
through the means of a simulated subject.

7.1
Tasks
MI was the chosen paradigm. This was due to the previous studies that has shown how MI can
be a beneﬁcial factor of neurorehabilitation [31]. A 3-class MI paradigm on a single limb was
set up based on a previous study at DTU [7]. The 3 classes were Grasp, Pinch and Elbow Flexion
as shown in Fig. 7.1. The classes were chosen in collaboration with Helle K. Iversen (M.D.,
DMSc) from the Neurocenter at Rigshospitalet (The National Hospital for Denmark) during
a different Masters Project that focused on an adaptive CSP approach that led to previous
mentioned study [7].
• Palmar Grasp: Using all ﬁngers to hold an object. the subject was instructed to continu-
ously grasp and release an imaginary object.
• Pinch: Collecting the middle ﬁnger, index ﬁnger and thumb. The subejct was instructed
to continously trying to pinch salt.
• Elbow Flexion: Flexing of the elbow while the wrist is aligned with the arm. The subject
was instructed to try an repeatedly hit an imaginary button on the table.
The reasoning for these speciﬁc 3-classes are due to the common consequence of one-
sided upper limb paralysis of stroke, and because the three exercises of grasp, pinch and elbow
ﬂexion is common among current rehabilitation practice.

8.2.1
Channel Selection and Epoch Timing
The ﬁrst pre-processing steps were channel selection and trimming the start and end times
of the epochs. The initial electrode placement was based around capturing information in
the areas around the motor cortex. The channel selection was performed in order to remove
channels that did not contain relevant information. The channel selection algorithm was
backwards selection using a simple MDRM classiﬁer and resulted in the following channels:
[FCz, FC3, C2, C3, C5, C6, CPz, CP3,CP4,Pz], also shown as the green marked channels in Fig.
8.3.
The recorded epochs contained data starting from the beginning of the cue and ended at
the end of the rest period. The following step consisted of ﬁnding the time window in which
the most discriminant information was available. (Fig. 8.4)
Following the best estimated start time, the duration of the epoch was found using a similar
approach which extended the end time(Fig. 8.5).
From this data it was concluded that the epoch window should start at 4 seconds into the
trial, and have a duration of 6.5 seconds. This corresponds to 1 second after the cue ends until
two and a half a second before the the rest period.

Discussion
The major ﬁndings and results, as shown in the previous chapters, are discussed and evaluated
in this chapter. The proposed methods and their respective results are compared to the current
state-of-the-art. The results are evaluated in terms of accuracy and also in regards to their
applicability to an actual neurorehabilitation setting.
9.1
Main ﬁndings
Three approaches, as well as a combination of the two best performing approaches, were
presented in this work. The approaches were all based on Riemannian distance, but with
modiﬁcations previously found in variants of CSP based approaches.
A framework for developing and testing models was implemented using Python. This
framework was built as an extension to the sci-kit learn library, meaning that the signal
processing and Riemannian geometry speciﬁc classes could integrate with the already imple-
mented sci-kit learn machine learning classes. This allowed BCI models to be implemented in
a very quick fashion; any BCI pipeline could be implemented in few lines of code. Furthermore,
a tool for visualizing the pipelines was also implemented to give a quick view of the overall
pipeline and the parameters.
For validating the models, a small in-house dataset was recorded with a 3-class MI task
using 5 subjects. The experiment framework was implemented in Python using the Pyglet
library that recorded LSL data while presenting a subject with a Focus, Task, and Rest image. A
BCI decoder was built into the experiment framework as well, and the decoder could provide
the subject with real-time feedback.
9.1.1
Evaluating the results
The proposed models were compared using the accuracy or Cohens-kappa score of the results
from the evaluation data. These measures give a quick overview of how well the models
perform. What the score does not tell is whether there is a statistical signiﬁcant difference
between the models. This was not done in this study as there are several implications regarding
how such tests should be implemented in BCI tasks with several subjects. The models trained
on different subjects provide results that are arguably in different domains, meaning that the
results and errors from one subject cannot easily be compared to the results from another [90].
A study by Nicolas-Alonso et al [49] compared the proposed model to the current state-of-
the-art models using the Wilcoxon test results (p-values) for evaluating a statistical signiﬁcance.
75
==PAGE:91==
CHAPTER 9. DISCUSSION
The p-values are assumed to be computed using the Cohens-kappa score of the individual
subjects. The issue with this is, ﬁrst of all, that the p-values could not be reproduced, and,
secondly, the statistic was based on the kappa-score of only 9 subjects, which may not be
sufﬁcient for a proper evaluation.
One common way of evaluating models is training and testing the models on multiple sets
of data using cross validation. This way of evaluating models has been done in multiple studies
[34], [91], [92]. The problem with this is the cross validation was performed on the training
session data and is therefore not able to represent how well models perform on the evaluation
session data due to the non-stationarity of EEG data. A possible way to overcome this would be
to record three sessions. A training session, a test session, and an evaluation session. Models
could then be trained, tested and assessed using the training and testing session data. The
evaluation session data would then be used for the ﬁnal evaluation only.
9.2
Ofﬂine evaluation
This section discusses the performance of the proposed models, as well as the preprocessing
steps that differed between the recorded data and the BCI competition IV 2a data set.
9.3
Preprocessing of the In-House Data
The in-house data had some differences compared to the BCI 2a dataset. This was expected as
the in-house data consisted of a single limb motor imagery task, while the BCIC IV 2a dataset
had motor imagery classes in limbs that were far from each other.
The channel selection resulted in the removal of C1, Cz, FC4, and CP4. This meant an
asymmetric electrode layout, which makes sense since the tasks were performed with the
right arm only. The contralateral nature of the brain and muscle movement (the left side of
the brain controls the right side of the body) could explain the removal of FC4 and CP3. This
does not explain why C1 and Cz were removed, though. Their removal might be due to poor
electrode connection or that the information in these channels was already present in the
other electrodes.
Except for the ﬁlter bank approach, the signals were bandpass ﬁltered from 8-20 Hz. The
optimal frequency range for the BCIC IV2a was found to be from 8-50 Hz. This could indicate
that the frequency contents from 20-50 Hz may contain discriminant information when the
mental tasks are very different, while the same frequency range consists of mostly noise when
the tasks are similar.
The epoch was tuned and the optimal time for the trial was found to be from 1 second to
7.5 seconds into the task trial. Removed the ﬁrst second of the task period makes sense, as the
subject has to react to the image change. Removing the data of the last 2.5 seconds before the
task ended was done because it did not contain any additional discriminant information. As
seen in Fig. 8.5, the accuracy does not change much after the 6 seconds duration mark.

9.3.1
Spectro Spatial Approaches
The spectro spatial MDRM models were implemented to be similar to CSSP and CSSSP mod-
els. These models could achieve very high accuracy, with the tradeoff of having a very high
computational cost. With this model, there was a perfect 100% accuracy for decoding subject
3, which has not been observed in anywhere else. However, this required 5 delay taps which
increased the size of the covariance matrix to be 86 × 86 instead of 14 × 14 and and as such
increased the computational cost tremendously for a Riemannain based classiﬁer.
Increasing the amount of lagged channels improved the score for the BCI IV2 dataset. The
increased amount of channels did not, however, increase the score of the in-house dataset. In
the worst case for the Pinch and Grasp comparison, the mean accuracy fell 5% when increasing
the Lag from 3 to 5. This may be due to the curse of dimensionality, as the distances become
increasingly more difﬁcult to correctly compute. It was always the case that introducing
one set of lagged channels increased the score. The spectro spatial approaches had many
entrees in the results tables due to the fact that the accuracy increased at the cost of being very
computationally demanding.
A lag of 1 sample delay increased the accuracy in the BCI competition IV 2a two class
setting by 1.7% and increased the kappa score from 0.53 to 0.56 for the four class setting.
9.3.2
Filter Bank
In this project the ﬁlter bank approach was implemented as well, under the hypothesis that
the Riemannian distances could be used as features for an ordinary classiﬁer. This could be
done, and the results of this approach were better than an approach without a ﬁlter bank.
However, the results were also the lowest of the three proposed models. The implementation
was rather simple, with just computing the Riemannian distances, using them as features, and
following with feature selection and then classiﬁcation. There might have been some value to
be obtained in post-processing the features, such as a normalization method or investigated if
other features were available, such as looking into tangent space approaches.
9.3.3
Adaptive Approach
Adaptive models have been seen previously in BCI context, both in CSP based and recently also
in Riemannian decoders. Most of the models from other studies have either had a supervised
or semi-supervised update strategy in which the models were updated based on ground truth
labels or by predictions. These update strategies was considered for the proposed model,
but were not chosen for implementation, as the ground truth label might not be available. If
the true labels of the evaluation data were used to update the model, then the results would
become invalid for comparison with other models that had not used evaluation data for
training. This fact is fundamental for any machine learning task.
However, in a neurorehabilitation environment, the tasks could be designed such that the
labels would always be known. In that case there would be no issue to update a model based
on that information. For a Riemannian distance model this could simply be implemented by
77
==PAGE:93==
CHAPTER 9. DISCUSSION
moving the corresponding class mean belonging to the current trial along the geodesic using
Eq. (4.4).
Updating the model based on prediction raises the issue of the case where the classiﬁer be-
comes less accurate if a prediction is wrong. In the worst case scenario, this could accumulate
and show as a very biased classiﬁer which would only pick a single class.
For these reasons, an unsupervised model was implemented. This model even had the
highest accuracy and kappa values when combined with the spectro spatial implementation.
Previous studies [82] had an unsupervised model as well, which re-referenced the input
covariance matrix to have the same reference point as the training data. In the proposed model
for this project, the class means were shifted towards the trend of the common covariance
matrix instead of shifting an input sample. In short, the work in [82] forced the input features
to ﬁt a model, while the proposed model in this project changed as the input data changed
instead.
The best performing model was the Spectro-Spatial Adaptive Minimum Distance to Mean
model with a single lag. This model achieved both the highest accuracy for the 2 class setting
of BCI competition IV 2a, and produced a result on par with the current best performing model
on the four class setting. This particular model was also the best performing on the in-house
data.
A physiological meaning is difﬁcult extract from a Riemannian geometry based model.
However, it can be assumed that the SSaMDRM model performs so well due to the fact that the
extracted features consist of spectral as well as spatial elements. In addition, the non-stationary
nature of EEG data is compensated for using an unsupervised adaptive strategy.
9.4
Real-Time Implementation
In this project, a novel real-time implementation for Riemannian distance based classiﬁers
was implemented. Using this implementation, the subject could receive real-time sample wise
feedback on the current task. The feedback consisted of a BCI decoder that was trained on the
calibration sessions and supplied real-time feedback on the evaluation session by a bar that
ﬁlled up next to the task image. This feedback worked perfectly on a simulated subject, but
did not supply the predictions correctly during the recording session with a human subject.
However, the data was recorded, and the same decoder could provide the predictions correctly
when run outside of the experiment program.
The FGDA MDRM clasiﬁers consist of band pass ﬁltering, covariance matrix estimation,
tangent space ﬁltering, computing the Riemannian distance, and probability prediction. All
of this could be done within 12.4% ±0.8 of a clock cycle with a sample frequency 128 Hz,
which corresponds to a processing time of approximately 1 ms. Using more electrodes, a
different system, and another classiﬁer would change the processing time. This was done
for the selected 10 electrodes, on a laptop computer with a 1.60 GHz processor in a Python
environment.
The real-time predictions did have a lot of ﬂuctuations between classes. For example, if
the task was either grasp or pinch, the highest prediction would ﬂuctuate between these two
classes over time. The ﬂuctuations of predictions might be due to the repetitiveness of the task
78
==PAGE:94==
CHAPTER 9. DISCUSSION
as the subject was asked to try to repeatedly do the grasp, pinch, or ﬂex task. For example, if
the covariance matrix updates too quickly then the spatial covariance matrix of the ﬁrst part of
the ﬂexion task might look different from the spatial covariance matrix of the latter part of the
ﬂexion task.
The ﬂexion class was predicted during the Cue and Rest period of the trial. It may be
beneﬁcial to introduce the Rest-period data as another class to prevent this.
9.5
Further Exploration in Riemannian Geometry Classiﬁers
Riemannian geometry is still rather new and unexplored. An interesting approach would be to
look into artiﬁcial neural networks. Previously, some studies have used either convolutional
neural networks for spectro-spatial ﬁltering, but also for LSTM models [52] and regular Hidden
Markov Models [58]. Another approach would be to use neural networks to operate covariance
matrices and, as such, utilize both artiﬁcial neural networks and Riemannian geometry. One
study has already implemented such a library for the pyTorch environment [93], but it has yet
to be used in BCI context.
Operations in tangent space is also a ﬁeld that could be further explored. FGDA was
a ﬁltering process that was implemented in all of the proposed models, which drastically
increased the accuracy [59]. The FGDA was based on shrinkage LDA to ﬁnd coefﬁcients that
could be used to rotate the tangent space representation of the data to be more separable.
Tangent space operations could also be used to explore other methods for ﬁltering data. This
could be done using PCA or LDA for an unsupervised method of determining a subspace in
which information lies. Another possible modiﬁcation to FGDA could be the inclusion of a
penalization term, either in terms of electrode placement, as was done in [37] for CSP, or in
terms of the L1-norm. L1-regularization leads to a method of feature selection and might
therefore be viable as a method for ﬁnding the relevant electrode channels.
Riemannian geometry based classiﬁers lack interpretation. CSP based methods ﬁnd the
optimal spatial patterns which can be visualized topologically.
There is also a challenge in ﬁnding a way to compute the Riemannian distances in a more
efﬁcient matter. That is, ﬁrst of all, to compare AIRM to Log Euclidean, to ﬁnd if the approxi-
mated distance can perform on par with the afﬁne distance. If that is not the case, then effort
must be put into computing the AIRM as efﬁciently as possible. In other words, the appropriate
eigenvalue decomposition methods and update strategies for real-time implementation must
be found.
9.5.1
Improving the adaptive approach
The adaptive method consisted of updating the class means to follow the global mean shift of
the data. This approach did achieve very promising results; however, there are still unexplored
adaptive approaches to be considered.
The FGDA ﬁlter is kept static, but there might be a beneﬁt to updating the FGDA ﬁlter coef-
ﬁcients using an adaptive LDA approach. An adaptive LDA has been proposed by Ghassabeh
79

[94] for an image classiﬁcation setting, but the underlying algorithm should be applicable for
BCI.
9.5.2
Possible Solution for a Portable Device
An envisioned portable device was mentioned in the project description. The future possibility
of using a portable device was kept in mind during implementation of both the decoders and
the data acquisition experiment. All of the code was implemented in Python using libraries
that all run on Mac, Windows, and Linux platforms. The only requirement for the real-time
decoder is that the EEG acquisition system can transmit data over Lab Streaming Layer.
A possible solution for a portable BCI-FES system could be implemented on a small Linux
based platform such as a Raspberry Pi or the variants thereof. These devices run up to 1.5 GHz
quad core [95], and as such have plenty of processing power for the proposed decoder. They
also have plenty of peripherals (USB, GPIO and HDMI) for external equipment such as the
g.Tec EEG system, an FES device and an external display.

Conclusion
A major aim for this project was to explore how some variations to the CSP based BCI systems
could be translated onto a Riemannian setting. This exploration led to three novel classiﬁca-
tion methods that all outperform most of the current state-of-the-art classiﬁers. The three
models were the spectro-spatial MDRM, Filter Bank Riemannian Distance, and Adaptive
MDRM. While all of the novel approaches outperformed the current state-of-the-art, the
model that performed the best was a combination of the spectro-spatial and adaptive MDRM,
the SSaMDRM classiﬁer. This model achieved a kappa score of 0.70 for the BCI competition IV
2a dataset in the 4-class setting, and is therefore among the best performing classiﬁers in MI.
To verify the models, data from 5 subjects was acquired in a 3-class single limb MI experi-
ment. Of all the proposed models, the SSaMDRM classiﬁer proved to be the best performing.
One particular challenge with the in-house data was that the grasp and pinch class were very
similar. When omitting the ﬂexion task, the SSaMDRM classiﬁer achieved an accuracy of 66%
while the CSP-LDA classiﬁer achieved 56%. This indicates that the SSaMDRM classiﬁer is a
better method when tasks are similar.
To develop these models, a Python framework which could be integrated with the popular
machine learning library sci-kit learn was implemented. This meant that an end-to-end
pipeline from EEG data to classiﬁcation could be developed with ease in an environment
familiar to any sci-kit learn user. This framework was implemented using cross platform
libraries and is assumed to be able to work well on a portable platform, which may beneﬁt the
future work of developing a neurorehabilitation device.
A novel real-time decoder was implemented. This decoder could process and perform
predictions on EEG data, sample by sample, using an FGDA MDRM pipeline. The real-time
decoder did not produce as results as good as the ofﬂine models, due to the challenges in
estimating the covariance matrix in real-time.
Riemannian geometry in BCI is a new and very promising ﬁeld. This thesis implemented
and evaluated modiﬁcations that increased the accuracy of previously proposed Riemannian
geometry classiﬁers by applying concepts that, up until now, have been seen in CSP based
classiﬁers only.
