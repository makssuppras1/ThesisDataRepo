Abstract
To accommodate climate change and increase the circular economy, it is important to focus more on
reparation as a strategy. The thesis aims to quantify the impact of reparation from an environmental
and economic perspective for selected household products.
Furthermore, it will highlight conflicts
within the different perspectives and considerations regarding modeling choices.
The economic and environmental models’ methods will be life cycle costing (LCC) and life cycle as-
sessment (LCA) screenings. The reparations were assessed for the products of an average smartphone,
laptop, vacuum cleaner, printer, coffee maker, toaster, kettle, hair dryer, electric razor, washing ma-
chine, dishwasher, and fridge. The benefits of reparation were modeled for specific repairs comparing
it to the replacement of a new similar product.
In general, all the repairs were environmentally found to be beneficial, and relatively more beneficial
than from the economic perspective. From both the economic and environmental perspectives, small
household electronics and large household appliances had the largest benefits due to the high replace-
ment costs/impacts. For kitchen aids and bathroom appliances, the difference between the replacement
and repair was smaller, creating less benefit. For some of these products, the repairs were found to be
more expensive than replacing them with similar products. Due to the economy being a large factor
for the consumer, only economically beneficial repairs are reflected in society.

Introduction
To achieve the 2030 agenda for sustainable development, it is crucial to gain more sustainable consump-
tion accommodating the sustainable development goal of responsible consumption and production. One
way to redeem this goal is to improve the circular economy by expanding products’ lifetime with re-
paration [1]. In 2020 the European Commission made a new circular economy action plan dedicated
to increasing the circular economy to reduce the stress on natural resources and get closer to the
climate-neutral target in 2050. The earlier circular economy action plan primarily targets recycling,
while the new action plan also focuses on prolonging products’ lifetime with reparation [2]. Product
reparation has a smaller loop than recycling, as seen in Figure 1. As a result, it is also necessary to
consider repair and not only recycling.
Figure 1: Circular economy connected to the different life stages.
In the last decade, the demand for reparation services has decreased, and more new products than
ever have been purchased. To increase the circular economy with reparation, it is necessary to enhance
the use of repair services [3]. It is proposed in the circular economy action plan by the commission to
develop legislation on Sustainable Product Policy. The goal is to shift away from planned obsolescence,
where products are intentionally designed to have a limited lifespan. The proposal includes a revision
of the Consumer Rights Directive to incorporate transparency and correct information regarding the
durability of products, reparation manuals, and access to spare parts. It will also ensure the presence of
repair services and access to core spare parts for a minimum of 10 years after the last sale of the product
[2]. All these initiatives will hopefully lead to an increasement of reparations and more successful
repairs. This thesis will investigate the potential of these initiatives by exploring the environmental
benefit of reparation compared to replacement.
From a survey of reparation of household products, it was found that only 10 %- 20 % of all broken
items were repaired [4]. Some of the reasons why reparation services have declined have to be found
within the consumers’ objective. One of the concerns for not repairing was mainly the reparation price.
Other considerations included bad experiences, reparation duration, bad public perceptions, and the
quality of repair services [3]. Another survey by the European Commission found that consumers would
Page 6 of 108
==PAGE:8==
only repair the product if the reparation price were less than 30% compared to a replacement of a new
product [3]. This thesis will consequently also investigate the economic aspect of reparation compared
to replacement. Furthermore, the correlation between the economic and environmental benefits will
be investigated to find potential social implications for the circular economy.
1.1
Objective
This thesis aims to investigate the reparation of selected household products compared to the re-
placement of a new similar product from an environmental and economic perspective. Previous work
within repair modeling is very limited, and the thesis will explore the quantification of reparations from
the two perspectives. Considering both environmental and economic perspectives, the most suitable
combination of methods was a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and Life Cycle Costing (LCC) screening.
This thesis will investigate several household products to address the knowledge gap of reparations
for household products motivated by the new focus of the circular economy action plan. The mod-
eling involves making simple analyses of broken products that are either repaired or replaced. It is
expected that repair is more often beneficial from an environmental than an economic perspective due
to the product producers’ interest in generating more revenue. Further, the expected outcome is to
gain insight into how to quantify reparation and create basic considerations for future modeling of
reparation.
First, the thesis will cover background information on repair initiatives and social implications for
the reparation of household products, together with a summary of earlier literature reviews regarding
methodology use and reparation within the circular economy. Second, it will cover the LCA- and LCC
screenings modeling considerations, data collection, and data treatment. Third, the results will cover
the LCA- and LCC screening results separately and together. Then a sensitivity analysis of the model
was made to determine the robustness. Fourth, it will discuss major findings and correlations with
social implications and recommendations regarding household repair and future modeling.

2.1
Repair background
One of the most current worldwide problems is over-consumption. In 2050 it is estimated that the
world will be consuming materials as if there were three worlds, and Denmark is already consuming
three times its limit. If we don’t do anything, the consumption will be doubled in 40 years for materials
such as fossil fuels, biomass, metals, and minerals. Furthermore, in 2050 waste generation is estimated
to increase by 70%. This will result in great biodiversity loss, water stress, resource scarcity, etc.
To adverse this, we have to increase the circular economy [5]. In the new circular action plan, they
highlight that the consumption has largely been due to the high economic competition. It is crucial
that economic growth is distinguished from material consumption and redesigned to be within the
planetary boundaries. This involved reducing consumption and doubling the circular economy. A new
study estimates that incorporating circular economy thinking can increase the EU’s GDP by 0.5 %
already in 2030. For individual businesses, there is also a huge potential to reduce costs since it is
estimated that they use 40 % on materials [5]. Increasing the circular economy by prolonging the
product’s lifetime has been found to be a really important economic strategy [1].
Right-to-repair movements have pressured the government to enforce laws regarding repair. Right-to-
repair includes the right for consumers to repair the products themselves within and after the guarantee
provided [6]. As mentioned above, the new circular economy action plan suggests revising the legislation
on sustainable product policy. This March 2023, the European parliament and council made a proposal
for common rules promoting reparation. The preferred change proposes to include prioritizing repair
when it is cheaper than replacement, an online platform connecting customers with repair centers,
a standardization form of reparation price, reparability requirements outside the guarantee against a
price, information regarding repairing, and an EU repair standard [7].
France pioneered the Right-to-repair movement and has the most implemented initiatives. They have
enforced a repairability index which includes the producers of electric and electronic equipment, to
provide the consumers with a product score regarding the availability, cost of spare parts, and tools
for reparation [3]. Other common initiatives in the EU include reducing the VAT tax on repair ser-
vices, spare part availability, repair service availability, and information regarding repair. The current
initiatives in the European countries can be found in Table 2.

Many of these initiatives include spare part availability and reduction of VAT tax. This is due to an
analysis of the consumer patterns of repair. As mentioned above, a recent paper highlights that in
the last decade, the demand for reparation services’ have decreased and that more products than ever
are replaced instead of repaired. One of the main reasons is the consumer patterns [3]. The consumer
often evaluated the life extension of the product compared to the cost of reparation to be insignificant.
If the customers choose a product with low cost as opposed to quality, the duration of the product
is assumed to be low, together with a low life extension with reparation. The higher quality of the
product, the more careful the consumer is to maintain the product while using it and repairing it when
needed [1]. From a survey from the European Commission, it was found that only 64% have tried to
get a product repaired and that the consumer would only repair the product if the reparation price
were between 19% and 30% compared to a replacement of a similar new product as mentioned above
[3]. It is crucial to make reparation for the consumer more feasible and gain more awareness regarding
reparation before the consumer pattern changes.
In a survey of reparations of large household appliances, the three most common reasons to repair the
items were the product’s high price, environmental reasons, and it was a high quality product. This
is supported by the assumption that high-priced and quality products are often more repaired. The
reasons for not repairing were that the products were assumed to be too old, broken, or expensive
compared to buying a new product [4]. Therefore, the economics regarding repairs are crucial to get
the consumers to repair instead of replacing. If the reparation is not found economically feasible, it
will often not be repaired.
In a survey of reparation of kitchen and household electronics, the reparation rates were much smaller,
and the most abundant reason to not repair was that it was not feasible to repair the product since a
new product had a similar price to the reparation [9]. This highlights why it is important to investigate
the product from an economic and environmental perspective since the conclusions might be conflicting.
This is problematic for the circular economy and is why all these initiatives are important since the
current competitive economic market often does not favor reparation.
To gain information on whether or how much it is economically and environmentally feasible to repair
a product instead of replacing it, it is crucial to assess the product. Literature available on combining
an environmental and economic assessment will be investigated below.

2.2
Combining environmental and economic assessments
When making a product assessment, it can be for the whole product life cycle or for an isolated product.
One key difference is that a life cycle perspective is often assessed through the stages of raw materials,
production, use, and disposal. This also includes transportation and energy in all of the stages. To
gain the most representative assessment, it is recommended to assess the whole product life cycle [10].
Furthermore, life cycle thinking is crucial when assessing the circular economy. There are different
methods to asses the whole life cycle within an environmental and economic assessment. The methods
chosen for this assignment are a life cycle assessment (LCA) screening and a life cycle costing (LCC)
screening. This is because the methods are most comparable, which will be elaborated below.
Life cycle assessment:
One of the most used and complete environmental analyses is LCA. LCA is
a tool to address a broad range of environmental impacts from a product or service’s whole life cycle
perspective. It is a quantitative method that is widely used and standardized by the ISO 14040/14044
standards. It is assessed from a functional unit that quantifies what, how, when, where, and how well.
One of the main strengths is that it can define different impacts from the life cycle and identify hot
spots of the product or service. Another strength is that it is a very thorough analysis that combines
much information of data on the life stages of raw materials, production, use, and disposal. This can
also be a limitation since it requires a lot of data, and it can be necessary to make many assumptions
and simplifications. An LCA can be very useful since it can compare different products or systems and
find the most environmentally beneficial. This can not conclude if something is sustainable but only if
one thing is more sustainable than the other [10, 11].
Life cycle costing:
One way to assess the life cycle of a product from an economic perspective is
LCC. As the LCA, it is a tool to assess a product or service in the different life stages but from a
value-based perspective. Opposite to an LCA, there is no standardization of the LCC, but it aims to
be aligned with the LCA ISO standers. In an LCC, the most important elements are the activities’
value. This is estimated by the added value, which is the output price subtracted from all input costs
of the activity. The LCC’s main strength is assessing the value distribution in the chain to identify the
most crucial activities. This will highlight where to optimize the process to gain revenue and compete
with the competition [12, 11, 13, 14]. LCC is compatible with the LCA since the method has the same
functional unit and system boundaries but with an inventory with a monetary value [14].
Challenges and possibilities of combining LCA with a LCC:
The economy has always been
an important part from a business perspective, but the environmental impact is getting more and
more attention and is crucial for the green transition. The environmental aspect can, among others,
also contribute economically to the company by promoting the brand reputation and enhancing the
company’s competitiveness [15]. In general, combining the two methods will give a more holistic view
of the product or service, but the different methods have some challenges to combine.

It is suggested to avoid merging all the inputs and outputs when combining an LCA with an LCC
because allocating prices to all the inputs and outputs from an environmental analysis and vice versa
is not considered meaningful. They propose a model where the processes are run in parallel but have
the same functional units and, when possible, the same system boundaries. The LCA has a degree
of detail in each process that is impossible for the LCC. In the LCC, the allocation for added value
can first be implemented when the product or service is sold, and the added value is more roughly
allocated to the different processes. To accommodate this, the systems should be matched as best fits,
creating some assumptions and simplifications [11].
Another complication is the difference in inputs among the methods. In an LCC, the main input is
usually the labor whereas the emission from human activity is often not included in an LCA. On the
contrary, there are emissions in an LCC that does not cost anything for the company and is therefore
not accounted for in the LCA. As mentioned above, this can be accommodated by running the inputs in
parallel. When combining an LCA with an LCC, both methods are very time- and resource consuming
[16]. Even though the two methods share scope, they will still have very different kinds of data that
will make the comparison difficult. When comparing environmental and economic data, there are much
higher fluctuations in the economic data which can make the data outdated quickly [16].
The general combination of an LCA and an LCC can be used to get insight into the product and
system patterns and identify hot spots within the environmental and economic aspects [16]. When
combining the two methods, even though they have different findings they will contribute with im-
portant information within the same scope. Any conflicts between the two methods illustrate a large
difference between the environmental and the economic perspective, which makes the green transition

2.3
Literature review of studies combining environmental and economic assess-
ments
In a special course leading to this thesis, a literature review was conducted to find literature combining
an environmental and an economic assessment. The literature review was made within the area of
circular economy: reuse, recycle, repair, or replace. A screening of the literature combining an en-
vironmental and economic assessment was made from the databases DTU Findit, Sciencedirect, and
Scopus. The search was made to have either reuse, recycle, repair, or replace in the title and then also
have an environmental and economic aspect: (LCA OR "life cycle assessment" OR MFA OR "material
flow analysis") AND (VCA OR "value chain analysis" OR LCC OR "life cycle costing"). Literature
was excluded if it didn’t involve a product or service, the methods were not used, or the paper was
about something else entirely. There were 72 papers found in the literature review that combined an
environmental and economic assessment, as seen in Table A.1 in the appendix. The literature search
showed that the most common combination of the assessments was LCA and LCC. This may be due
to the methods’ compatibility since the LCC is made to fit the framework of an LCA and shares the
same system boundaries and functional unit.
In the literature screening, the contents of the papers were also investigated. The most common area
was within recycling, as seen in Figure 2 and Table A.2 in the appendix.
Figure 2: Content in literature screening combining environmental and economic assessment.

the content reflects that as well. This could be due to the large focus on recycling in the old circular
economy action plan, whereas reparation was only briefly mentioned [17].
One of the most unexploited areas was repair which created a large knowledge gap. Other than the

2.4
Literature review of studies of repair
A screening of the literature on repair from the databases DTU Findit, Sciencedirect, and Scopus was
made as seen in Table A.2 in the appendix. The search was: (Repair and LCA, "life cycle assessment",
MFA, "material flow analysis", VCA, "value chain analysis", LCC, or "life cycle costing"). This led
to 34 papers where the only methodology used was either LCA, LCC, or a combination of the two.
The most used method was LCA with 68 % of the literature. This highlights that reparation regarding
the circular economy is mostly viewed from an environmental aspect and not from an economic.
Approximately 75 % of the papers were published within the last 5 years and it is expected that the
literature within this area will largely increase in the near future. When looking at the content of the
literature 62 % of the studies were performed on repair regarding construction as seen in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Content in the literature screening regarding repair and with either the methodology of LCA or LCC
These papers were mostly about concrete repair, water-damaged houses, roof tiles, bridge decks, and
railways. Due to the high replacement cost, there is historically more reparation within this sector as
the literature reflects. This correlates with consumer patterns mentioned previously regarding higher
quality and expensive products being repaired more often when needed.
On the contrary, in the
household, more products than ever are replaced instead of repaired. This is also reflected in the
literature screening where only 3 papers were found regarding reparation in the household. These
papers were concerning electronics and vehicle repairs. These are often also more expensive household
items, which could explain why the studies have been about these subjects. The increasing focus on
reparation initiatives within household products will hopefully increase the amount of household repairs
instead of replacements. This leaves a large gap in both economic and environmental assessments
regarding the reparations of household products.
From the literature review, it was clear that not only are LCA and LCC the most common methods
for a combined assessment, they are also the methods that are isolated the most used within the
literature regarding repair. Many of the new repair initiatives are within household products where

3.2
LCA- and LCC screening methods
The main methodologies used to evaluate the reparation of household products were an LCA - and an
LCC screening. The reason for choosing these methods was based on the information gathered in the
literature review to best asses repairing from an economic and environmental perspective. The screen-
ings were more simplified and general than a more conventional LCA and LCC and were conducted
to evaluate different household product groups and not only focus on one. This was chosen due to the
very scarce information in this area, as found in the literature review. The analysis was run in parallel
but shared the same functional unit and system boundaries to accommodate the difference in inputs
of the methods. A full LCA- and LCC screening report can be found in section B in the appendix.
The target of the investigation was the citizens of Europe in support of the EU circular economy action
plan. The screening was comparative between reparation and replacement but also comparative to the
other household product groups. The LCA screening was conducted in the software EASETECH.
EASETECH is an environmental assessment software that focuses on material flows where each flow is
built up by different material fractions with different properties. This is especially useful for different
waste treatment scenarios [26].
The LCA methodology used for the impact assessment was the Environmental Footprint version 3.0
(EF 3.0) from 2022. To estimate the environmental impact, the impact category of climate change was
chosen based on the baseline model of 100 years of the IPCC 2013 guidelines [27]. Since the modeling
was very simplified, it focused on having the most important flows for climate change. The methodology
was chosen since it was the newest available version recommended by the EU. For the LCC screening,
an Excel spreadsheet was used to calculate the cost for the consumer, where the price can easily be
changed due to price fluctuations. The Excel sheet and EASETECH database are attached to this
thesis to accommodate changes and for documentation.

reparation. The scenarios of the repair and replacement of one product is illustrated in Figure 4. The
repair scenario is within the blue box and the replacement scenario is within the red box.
The product broke simultaneously in the two scenarios and was either repaired or replaced.

3.2.3
Framework
The decision context was A (micro-level decision support) since it would not directly affect any decision.
Furthermore, it was decision context A due to the simplicity of different products in the study [10].
The LCI modeling framework used was attributional since it looked at an isolated system which was
advantageous when investigating many products. Furthermore, it was also recommended in decision
context A. Almost all processes were from the database Ecoinvent v. 3.8, where allocation at the point
of substitution was chosen. In this method, the allocation model was expanded to include treatment
for byproducts [28]. In the modeling, system expansion via substitution was used for recycled materials
that will either be fully or partly substituted with virgin materials.
The LCC method was a combination of the conventional and environmental LCC. The aspect used
from the conventional LCC was the consumer’s viewpoint and that it only accounted for direct costs.
It followed the environmental LCC by following the LCA frameworks and was calculated based on
the functional unit and system boundaries. It was a very simple LCC screening since it was made to
support the LCA with an economic aspect to create a more holistic investigation. The type of data
used for the modeling will be elaborated below.
3.2.4

current technology even though the reference years are older. The reference year of the process used

The process for manufacturing was based on the reprocessing of the material in

All products and spare parts were assumed to be produced in China and transported

The use was not modeled in the system since it was assumed to be the same for the new and old
product. This was acknowledged to be uncertain for some electronic products, whereas it is possible
that a newer version is less or more energy-efficient for some products.

For the LCA, the reparation would only be modeled as the impact from the spare
parts. The energy use and facility were assumed to be included in the company cost.
The LCC reparation cost was modeled as the spare part cost, labor, company cost, and in-house fee.
The spare part cost was found in the literature or an average price from amazon.com if it was not
possible to find in the literature. The labor costs were determined by the average EU minimum labor,

margin. This variable was very uncertain, and the LCC Excel spreadsheet is attached to make more
customized calculations.

3.2.6
Sensitivity of the model
The sensitivity of the model was assessed to quantify the robustness of the model. The sensitivity
analyses were made on the parameters in the model that have the greatest influence on the result.
There were different types of sensitivity analyses made on the model: A perturbation analysis of
continuous parameters, a relative change analysis of scenario changes, and a break-even analysis of
some important parameters.
The perturbation analysis was made by using the following equation:
RSC = δ output/output
δ input/input
(6)
The relative sensitivity coefficient (RSC) was calculated by the difference in output over the original
output over the difference in the input parameter over the original input parameter. All the perturba-
tion analyses were made with a parameter change of 10%. A parameter was found to have a medium
sensitivity if the numeric value of RSC was above 0.3 and to have a large sensitivity if the value was
above 0.5.
A relative change analysis was made by using the following equation:
RC = ISScenario −ISBaseline
ISBaseline
· 100%
(7)
The relative change (RC) was calculated by the relative difference in the impact score (IS) with the
scenario change. The sensitivity was evaluated based on if the change was more than 10 % and if it
changed the overall conclusion.
A break-even analysis was made to find the point where the impact from a parameter resulted in having
the same impact from the repair and replacement. The break-even analysis would therefore highlight
how much change was needed to reach the tipping point.

4.4.3
Reference flow parameters
The reference flow parameters greatly influenced the LCA and LCC calculated benefit. Parameters in
the reference flow were the product’s lifetime, lifetime after reparation, and when the product breaks.
The sensitivity analysis made for the reference flow was a perturbation analysis with an increase of 10
%. The parameters sensitivities were evaluated by the impact on the repair and replacement scenario
and the impact on the calculated benefits.
The product’s lifetime:
The lifetime of the product contributed in both the repair and replacement
scenarios. The impact of the repair and the replacement share would increase with the life-prolonging.
When making the sensitivity of the benefit, the parameters were very sensitive since they had multiple
changes in the results.
This was the case for both the LCC and LCA. A large sensitivity to the
replacement scenarios were due to the lifetime directly influencing the share of cost/impact of the
replacement share. For the repair scenarios, the lifetime was not sensitive in all of the cases. It was
only sensitive if the repair scenarios had a significant impact compared to the impact from the initial
product.
The detailed relative sensitivity coefficients (RSC) calculated can be found for the LCA and LCC in
Figure A.3 and Figure A.4 in the appendix.
When the product breaks:
The time when the product breaks was prolonged to model a scenario
where the product first breaks down later in the lifetime.
It contributed to both the repairs and
replacement scenarios. The prolonging made the impact of repair smaller but also the share of avoided
replacement smaller. This resulted in the benefits generally being smaller for both the LCA and LCC.
The detailed RSC calculated can be found for the LCA and LCC in Figures A.7 and A.8 in the
appendix.
The lifetime after reparation:
The durability of the product after the reparation was prolonged to
model a scenario where the repair last longer. The prolonging would only impact the repair scenarios
Page 39 of 108
==PAGE:41==
and not the replacement scenarios. It created a larger benefit from reparation for both the LCA and
LCC since the impact from reparation got smaller.
When looking at the RSC, it was not sensitive in all scenarios. For the LCA, the most sensitive scenarios
were the large household electronics, kitchen aids, and bathroom appliances. The sensitivity depended
on the difference in the impact from the initial product compared to the repair.
If the difference
were small, the repair would have more impact and be sensitive. This difference also determines the
sensitivity of the LCC. The differences were generally smaller for the LCC, and more scenarios were
sensitive.
The detailed RSC calculated can be found for the LCA and LCC in Figures A.5 and A.6 in the
appendix.
None of the reference flow sensitivity analyses changed conclusions on whether the repairs were benefi-
cial but only the quantity of the benefit. Furthermore, scenario changes were investigated in the LCA
model to evaluate whether the conclusion changed when contributing factors changed.

5
Discussion
The study’s main objective was to explore the reparation of selected household products benefits from
an environmental and economic perspective. The major findings will be discussed to explore why the
benefit may differ from different perspectives by relating it to social implications and other literature.
Furthermore, the modeling choices will be discussed with a focus on other elements that would be
interesting to consider when modeling repairs.
5.1
Major findings
The results variate greatly between the different product groups and the environmental and economic
assessment perspectives.
Small household electronics:
For small household electronics, the most contributing factor to the
environmental impact was the passenger car’s impact due to the spare part’s relatively low impact.
Nonetheless, the environmental benefit was not that large compared to other products due to the small
size of the products and spare parts. From an economic perspective, repairs were significantly more
beneficial due to the high replacement cost. This difference in the perspective might be due to the
cost of small household electronics often being expensive given the technological aspects. Consequently
making, the high cost less due to the resources used, which is important for the environmental assess-
ment. Since it was very beneficial from an economic perspective, it is realistic that consumers would
repair small household electronics instead of replacing it. This is reflected in society with many repair
centers for smartphones and laptops [2]. A survey found that only half of the broken smartphones and
laptops would be repaired despite the high availability of repair centers. This might highlight that
other aspects have to be considered for this product group. In the survey, the most abundant reason
for not repairing was a want to have the newest model and concern regarding the durability of the
product after the reparation [2].
Large household electronics:
For large household electronics, the most contributing factor was the
spare parts due to the larger size. Within the maintenance scenarios for the vacuum cleaner suction and
printer ink, the largest benefits can be found due to the multiple benefits of avoiding replacements of
new products. The maintenance scenarios represent products where consumers properly maintain their
items, creating long-lasting products. This could indicate a huge enticement environmentally to repair
products multiple times instead of replacing them. From the economic perspective, the maintenance
scenarios were very beneficial since they were within "do it yourself". These scenarios were generally
the most beneficial economically since there was no cost from the company and labor. The economic
benefit of the printer ink was small compared to the environmental benefit due to the printer cost
being close to the ink cost. This is problematic since it encourages the consumer to buy a new product
instead of new ink. This represents a market that creates revenue based on consumers buying cheap
products that are very expensive to maintain. This is problematic for the circular economy since it
is a scenario with a large environmental initiative to repair but almost no economic initiative. Since
the economic attractiveness is so small there is a large possibility that the consumer would replace the
product instead of repairing it.
Page 49 of 108
==PAGE:51==
Kitchen and bathroom appliances:
For kitchen aids and bathroom appliances, the most contrib-
uting factor to the environmental assessment was the passenger car’s impact due to the spare part’s low
environmental impact. Furthermore, there was no large difference between the repair and replacement
due to the replacement’s low impact resulting in a small environmental benefit. From the economic
perspective, the benefit was really small, if not more expensive, to repair instead of replace. This was
due to the low cost of a new product, making it impossible for a repair service to compete with a
replacement of a similar product. The repair centers for kitchen aids and bathroom appliances are
also very limited in society since there is no economic attractiveness. Furthermore, the spare part
availability and the reparation score are very low for these items resulting in consumers almost always
replacing the product instead of repairing it [30]. This was a challenge encountered when modeling
since many of the faults reported was not possible to be repaired from an economic perspective due
to spare part availability/ reparability. The repair scenarios for these product groups were, therefore,
iteratively changed to fit within the scope of the study. It is also the product group that has some of
the lowest lifetimes. This represents a market that creates revenue for consumers to buy cheap short-
lasting products instead of more expensive, long-lasting ones. A survey found that electric kitchen
items were a product group with many early defects and very few repairs (13 %) due to unavailable
spare parts and unrepairable products, in line with the results found in this study [30]. These product
groups are, therefore, found to be quite problematic for the green transition.
Large household appliances:
Large household appliances’ most contributing factor to the envir-
onmental perspectives was the spare parts due to their larger size. The difference between the repair
and replacement was still large due to the high impact of the new product. This created the largest
benefit for one repair. From the economic perspective, the repair was quite expensive due to expensive
spare parts and the addition of the in-house fee.
In general, the benefit was larger from the environmental than the economic perspective due to the
large use of materials and labor for repair. But since the product is very expensive, there is also a large
economic initiative. It is also quite common to get large household appliances repaired when broken
since buying a new one is a large expense. But since the reparation cost is quite expensive, multiple
repairs will quickly be more expensive than replacing the products, as seen in the sensitivity. If multiple
repairs are suspected, it is more likely that the consumer will replace the product. Furthermore, the
reparations costs were found to be above 50 % of the new product making it unlikely for the consumer
to repair the product based on the earlier mentioned survey concluding the reparation price must be
between 19% and 30% of a new product to get repaired [3]. When compared to a low-cost product,
almost all of the repairs got more expensive than a new product, whereas when compared with a
high-cost product, the reparation costs were between 35 % and 45 %. Due to the large price variations
of products, the calculations have to be more case specific to estimate the specific benefit. In another
survey, the largest reason for replacing large household appliances was a concern that the reparation
durability was small and that it would be more expensive. Furthermore, the reparation rate was found
to be as small as for kitchen items (13 %) [30]. To incorporate these considerations in the analysis, the
modeling of the repair needs to be more complicated than in this study.

5.2
Modeling choices
From the uncertain analysis, it is clear that the most important parameters are within the modeling
choices, especially from the economic perspective. As mentioned earlier in the background information,
there is no standardized method when modeling repair and the literature on the reparation of household
products is very limited. This makes it difficult to quantify repair since many different compounds
can be incorporated. The modeling used in this study is very simple and general due to the goal of
the study to incorporate many product groups. To increase the complexity of the model, different
elements will be proposed below that could be relevant when quantifying the impact of repair. One of
the model’s largest assumptions is that the product’s repair depends only on the economy. In reality,
reparation can also be rejected due to the low reparability of the product, repair success rate, and
spare part availability, among others. From a survey of repair of household products, it was found that
only 10 %- 20 % of all broken items were repaired [4].
Obsolescence:
One reason the repair ratio is so low is that some products are designed with planned
obsolescence to make the consumers buy more new products. This could be electronics being unable
to be updated, unremovable parts, or the products breaking faster on purpose. Due to this, many
products will, when getting older, gain exponentially more repairs to make the consumer buy a new
product instead of repairing the old one. At some point, it will be more beneficial to replace the
product instead of repairing it.
Due to this tendency, one of the main reasons not to repair was
concerns regarding the durability of the product after reparation [2]. The break-even analysis showed
that the number of repairs equal to replacement is generally larger from an environmental than an
economic perspective. This means that the point where it will be beneficial to replace the product will
be reached significantly earlier economically than environmentally, which is quite problematic for the
green transition.
In this model, the durability after reparation is assumed to be the same for each repairment independent
of the age of the product.
In reality, the number of repairments would increase with age due to
planned obsolescence and general wear and tear. To accommodate this, an aging efficiency could have
been incorporated into the model by multiplying a time-depending parameter to the durability after
reparation. Since the reparation shares in most scenarios were close to one, adding the aging efficiency
in this model would not make sense. To accommodate this, the modeling should not be from the
viewpoint of the lifetime of the products but modeled from another functional unit, including more
repairs. Other than added complexity for the durability, it is also important to incorporate if the
reparation is even possible.
Reparability and success rate:
Another important aspect of obsolescence is the reparability of
the product.
The reparability can intentionally be made low to get the consumers to buy more
new products, but it can also be a result of cheaper production solutions. It is generally easier and
cheaper to produce a product that cannot be repaired, as seen for many of this study’s cheap kitchen
and bathroom appliances. This is one of the main points in the green transition to thinking more
about the sustainable design of products and creating reparable products. In France, as mentioned
above, it is obligatory to give 11 products repairability scores. The score will include documentation,
disassembly, availability of spare parts, price of spare parts, and product-specific aspects. These are
also the main aspect in the proposal for the new EU circular economy action plan. The reparability
Page 51 of 108
==PAGE:53==
score could have been included in the model to create a more realistic model of the possible repair
of the different products. Since the score is only available for a few products, many assumptions are
needed to incorporate it into the model. Also, the score varies a lot for different types of the same
product, and it might only be relevant for more case-specific studies and not a general model as aimed
for in the study. Another way to take reparability into consideration is by looking at the repair success
rates, where data is much more available. If the repair success rates had been incorporated, the impact
of repair from both the environmental and economic perspectives would probably have been increased
and might have been found to be less beneficial. From a survey of reparation of household products,
the repair success rate variates a lot among the different types of repair. If the repair was made from
where the product was purchased, the repair rate was quite high at 70%, while other repair centers
only had a repair success rate of 30%. Furthermore, the repair success for the "do it yourself" repair
was quite high at 72 % [4]. The high rate might also be due to "do it yourself" repair often being
simpler since the consumer has estimated they can do it themselves. The "do it yourself" repairs also
require that the consumer knows why the product is broken, which often is not the case with electronic
devices. These repairs will be needed to either be diagnosed with help from experts or be repaired by
a repair center. The diagnosis could be made with help from repair cafes, which are becoming more
widespread. In corporation with the "Repair Cafe Denmark" an environmental impact calculator for
repairing household products was currently developed investigating another modeling method [42].
Other modeling methods:
Another more simple way to model the impact of repair is by modeling
it as avoiding the impact of a new product. This method was applied this May 2023, with a Danish
environmental impact calculator for repairing household products in Danish repair cafes in correlation
with the Aalborg University [42]. The calculator was made to increase awareness of the environmental
benefits of repairing instead of replacing it. Furthermore, they wanted to support the development of
increased repairs. The calculator is currently on 10 different household products, and three of them
are comparable with the household product chosen in this study. These products are a toaster, coffee
maker, and vacuum cleaner. The environmental impacts are 5.46 kg CO2, 17.8 kg CO2, and 45.6 kg
CO2 respectively. For the toaster and vacuum cleaners, these benefits are slightly higher than what
was found in this study. The main difference between the calculator and this thesis is that the repair
impact is only from avoided replacement for the calculator, whereas also the impact for the reparation
is included in this study. When looking at the system boundaries for the calculator, it is also an
attributional modeling where the use is excluded. The additional complexity of the calculator is the
inclusion of packaging and more product-specific data. Furthermore, the calculator has a different
functional unit for each product to make it more customized. These are two different methods to
quantify repairs from an environmental perspective. They share the same impact category of climate
change since it is the most common way to quantify environmental impact.
Another very important environmental aspect of reparation is the material used. For example, with
small household electronics, the environmental impact of repairing is quite small compared to other
repairs. If it was viewed from the aspect of resource scarcity, it might be a scenario with a high impact
since the product has more critical raw materials than other types of products. The climate crisis
is only one of many crises, and resource scarcity is also one of the currently important crises. This
aspect could also be addressed from a life cycle point of view. For this study, it would demand a more
detailed material fraction before analyzing it with the impact category "Resource use, minerals, and
Page 52 of 108
==PAGE:54==
metals". Material consumption is a very important aspect of increasing the circular economy. To have
the material consumption minimized, it is important that there is an initiative from the consumer.
5.3
Closing the loop
As mentioned above the repair rate for household products is very low. This is mostly due to economic
considerations. To increase the circular economy, it is important that the benefit is not only envir-
onmental but also economic beneficial. In this study, different scenario changes made the economic
perspective more beneficial which could possibly increase the reparation rate.
Quality of product:
When investigating the scenario in the sensitivity analysis where the new
product did not break, the environmental benefit did change for some kitchen aids and bathroom
appliances.
These scenarios got more beneficial to replace instead of repair.
From the economic
perspective, even more products were not beneficial to repairing as vacuum cleaners, coffee makers,
toasters, hair dryers, washing machines, and dishwashers.
This could indicate that replacing the
product with a more long-lasting product might be more beneficial than repairing it in some scenarios.
It is supported by the background information, where it was found that higher quality products were
more repairable, making them last longer [4]. The benefit-cost of replacing it with a longer-lasting
product is quite large for some products.
Resulting in the new product can be significantly more
expensive and still be economically beneficial if it lasts longer and is repairable.
When looking at the sensitivity of comparing the replacement impact/ cost with a product with lower
or higher impact/ cost, there is a large difference in the results. This highlights that there is a large
variation within each product. To have a correct estimate of reparation, the analysis has to be more
case-specific, especially for the economic aspect. In general, the repair will probably not be economically
beneficial if it is a low-cost/quality product. In contrast, if the product has higher cost/ quality, it is
more likely to be beneficial when repaired. The spare parts also tend to be more expensive when the
product is more expensive. Hence, it is also important that the product repairability score is quite
large before it is beneficial. When comparing it to the environmental impact, the higher cost product
often also has a larger environmental impact, and the benefit from repairing will also get larger.
For these products’ low quality/cost, the most environmental might be to replace them with a new
higher quality product instead of repairing them. In general, when buying a new product, it would
probably be more beneficial to invest in a higher quality/ cost product that is more long-lasting.
Another option to reduce the environmental impact could be to replace the product with a used
instead of a new one.
Used products:
When repairs are not found to be economically beneficial, it might be a consider-
ation to buy a used product instead. When altering the replacement from a new product to a used
product in the sensitivity analysis, the replacement will economically be beneficial instead of repair
for most of the product groups. Furthermore, the environmental impact gets closer for many products
to be more beneficial than repairing. It is also important to consider that a used product might need
repairs sooner than a new product. This will properly not affect the environmental benefit due to the
generally low reparation impact but might be important from the economic perspective.
Page 53 of 108
==PAGE:55==
When looking at the circular economy, reuse is a closer circle than repair, but since the consumer already
has a product, reuse is not better for all products. It really depends on the estimated lifetime remaining
for the used product compared to the remaining lifetime of the broken product when repairing. But in
general, if reparation is not possible, it will be economically and environmentally beneficial to replace it
with a used product instead of a new one. Instead of buying a similar used product, buying a product
with a higher quality might be more beneficial from both an environmental and economic analysis.
Guarantee:
As mentioned earlier, the quality of the products is also very important regarding if
the products are repaired or replaced. The main reason is that with cheaper products, the consumers
find the reparation too expensive compared to buying a new low-cost product. Many products have a
guarantee where it is free to have the product repaired, and within this period, many more products
would get repaired, even cheaper products. The most common guarantee period is two years, but
many household products were found to break within 2- 5 years [30]. If this period was prolonged,
more products would properly be repaired when broken. Furthermore, it would also go against planned
obsolescence and make the producer focus more on making more sustainable and long-lasting products.
Other ways to decrease the economic cost of repairment can either be from the consumers’ initiative
to make more "do it yourself" repairs or governmental initiatives such as a tax reduction.
More "do it yourself":
From this study, an initiative that would reduce the repair cost most is to
propose "do it yourself" repairs when possible. When the repairs costs were modeled only with "do it
yourself" all of the products except the kitchen aids and bathroom appliances had beneficial repairs
below 30 % of the price of a new product. This will decrease the cost substantially. Since this is only
possible when the problem is known, the use of repair cafes could help diagnosticate the problem and
help to repair it. There is an increase in repair cafes, but they are mostly run by volunteers and are
most available in bigger cities. Therefore, the " do it yourself" is very reliable on the consumer and
not on the government.
Tax reduction:
For more complicated repairs, there is still a need to have professional repairs. A
common governmental initiative to decrease reparation costs is the implementation of a reduced VAT
tax, as mentioned above. When looking at the initiatives made in the EU, a common strategy is to
reduce the VAT tax, which will recuse the company cost for the repair that is responsible for a large
part of the cost. This tax reduction implemented is mostly on textiles and not electronics, as focused
on in this study. To decrease the repair cost for these items, the tax reduction would need to be
expanded to contain more common household products.
This will definitely reduce the cost, but there is still a need for a large decrease since the average
consumer would only choose repair if the reparation cost is less than 30 % of a new product. This
is mostly due to the consumer being afraid that the repair would not last long [30].
There are a
lot of considerations when modeling repair and some of the limitations for this specific study will be
described below.

5.4
Limitations of the study
Due to the model being very simple and general, the results may conflict with other literature. When
comparing the replacement impact to other costs/impacts found in the literature, the conclusion
changed regarding whether the reparation was beneficial for many of the product groups. This study’s
main limitation is that it is made to be very general and simple to create a general picture of possible
benefits for repair. Furthermore, it was made to give insight into conflicts regarding reparation from
an environmental and economic perspective. Therefore, this study can not be used to give the specific
cost/environmental impact of a product’s reparation since it will vary a lot regarding the specific con-
ditions. Using the passenger car is one of the largest contributions to the repair scenarios. In reality,
transportation is not the same for all the same repair types due to the availability of repair centers for
different products. Not only the repair centers are properly different there is also a difference in the
reparability and spare part availability for the different products. These aspects are not incorporated
in the model, and it is assumed that it is known why the product is broken and that it is possible to
be repaired.
Another limitation of the study is that there is no standard method regarding modeling reparation,
and many aspects of reparation are not included in this model. Due to the model’s generalization,
much of the used data was based on generalizations and assumptions. For the LCA, the spare parts
and products data were not always available in Ecoinvent, and for some products, it was necessary to
find the data in other literature. Furthermore, there is inconsistency regarding if the spare parts were
found in Ecoinvent or modeled as material use and manufacturing. The latter, in general, created a
smaller impact on the repair. The data that was available in Ecoinvent was limited to only one type
of the product and with quite old data. It was found that there could be a large variation within the
sensitivity of the products. In general, it could be difficult to find average data due to the variation
within each product. Furthermore, the assumption regarding traveling distances is general and might
greatly differ from different scenarios. For the LCC, all of the input prices have great uncertainty due
to the high fluctuation in prices of both products and spare parts. Furthermore, the company cost
might differ for the different products and repairs.
When creating the model, the main problem encountered was the difference in what were the most
common reasons why the product broke and what repairs were realistic. The model products and
reparation was iteratively changed due to unrealistic repairs made clear when researching the economic
perspective since no spare parts were available. This was mostly a problem encountered regarding
kitchen items, as mentioned before, is a product group with very limited repairs. For these products,
the repair ended up being based on the most common repairs and not the common faults as the other
products.
5.5
Recommendations and future work
This thesis is only meant to be used as a superficial screening of modeling of household repair to
highlight certain problematic tendencies regarding repair. So the recommendation regarding what is
most beneficial can change when making a specific repair model. Nonetheless, it was clear that in
most cases, there is a large environmental benefit to repairing instead of replacing household products.
Also, the more model-specific recommendation is to minimize the driving distance with a passenger
car due to the high impact. The impact from the passenger car highlights how different the benefit
can be depending on the scope of the study. From an economic perspective, the largest reduction in

the impact is to "do it yourself" repairs when possible since they are the most economically beneficial.
For more economic/environmental benefits the consumer can focus on buying higher quality products
that are repairable and have a longer lifetime. Initiatives for the EU to reduce the cost could be to
expand the guarantee of smaller and less complicated products to enforce earlier repairs and a more
sustainable design. The initiatives for the right-to-repair movements regarding spare part availability
and reparability would properly also increase the reparation rate, but it is difficult to conclude it from
this modeling. Recommendations for future modeling are to make more specific models to have a
greater knowledge of the impact of repair since the knowledge is very limited. Furthermore, it would
be interesting to view the reparation from a material use perspective to accommodate the resource
scarcity used in more complex electronic household products.

Conclusion
In the new circular action plan for 2020 more initiatives were proposed to increase the circular economy
with reparation. Reparation can substantially increase the lifetime of products and reduce worldwide
over-consumption. The purpose of the thesis is to investigate how reparation of selected household
products is beneficial from an environmental and economic perspective. Furthermore, the perspective
investigated will give insight into problematic systems for the green transition.
In the LCA and LCC, a general pattern was found that more expensive products (small house-
hold electronics and large household appliances) had the largest benefit due to the high replacement
cost/impact. The large cost/impact was either due to the large size of the product or the complexity
of the product. For smaller and less complicated products (kitchen aids and bathroom appliances) the
difference between the replacement and repair was not large, creating less benefit than for the larger
and more complex products. Even some products were more economically costly to repair than replace.
The largest contributions to the environmental assessment repairs were the transport of the passenger
car and the spare part making the transportation a very important parameter for the environmental
impact. The largest contributions to the economic repairs were the spare part and company cost,
making the "do it yourself" repairs significantly more beneficial than the other repair types. In gen-
eral, maintenance for the LCA and LCC had some of the largest benefits due to the multiple avoided
replacements.
In general, the benefit was larger from an environmental perspective than an economic perspective.
Only the much economically beneficial repairs are reflected in society, such as small household electron-
ics, since the consumer often only chooses reparation if it is 30 % of a new product. From the economic
analysis, this is only the case for a few products and the economics for repairing needs to be more
beneficial before the consumer would repair the product instead of replacing it. From the sensitivity
analysis of the model, it is proposed that it would create more economical and environmental benefits
to buying higher quality products, buying used products, prolonging the guarantee, and making more
"do it yourself" repairs.

B
LCA and LCC screening report
The LCA - and LCC screening will include the most important aspects of a full LCA documentation
but will have more simple modeling.
B.1
Goal definition
The reason for carrying out the study:
The main reason for carrying out the study is the
growing focus on reparation regarding the circular economy. The target of the investigation is the
citizens of Europe in support of the EU circular economy action plan. The impact of repair is very
limited, and this study will investigate the impact of many different types of repair and product groups.
The economy regarding repair is very important in increasing the reparation for the consumers, and
the economic aspect is also investigated to see if the reparation is realistic and investigated potential
mismatch regarding the impact of the environment and the economy.
The intended application of the study:
The study aims to investigate many household groups
regarding reparation from an economic and environmental perspective. It is, therefore, not a full LCA
and LCC but a screening. The study will have a lot of comparisons of the different products but
will also be compared to the replacement of the product instead of being repaired. The study’s main
limitation is that the analysis is very general and simplified to gain an overview of many groups and
not only one.
Since the model is very simplified, the only impact category used for the LCA is climate change, and
the model is focused on having the most impotent flows for climate change. The methodology used
is environmental footprint version 3 (EF 3.0) since it is recommended by the EU and is the newest
avalible version. For the LCC, an excel spreadsheet is used to calculate the cost for the consumer,
where the price can easily be changed due to price fluctuations and variations in data.
The target:
The target of the study is the average European consumer. The study’s commissioner
is mainly DTU since it is a master’s thesis. The study will therefore be disclosed to the public since it
will be uploaded on DTU Findit.
The decision context:
The decision context is A (micro-level decision support) since it will not
directly affect any decision. Furthermore, it is decision context A due to the comparability of different
products in the study.
B.2
Scope definition
The deliverable:
The deliverable will be the LCI, LCA, LCC, and comparative results.
Functional unit and reference flow:
The Functional unit and reference flow are thoroughly de-
scribes in the main report in section 3.2.1.

Completeness and Consistency Checks
No detailed completeness and consistency checks are
made on the model due to the simplicity of the screening. In the completed check the general LCI
model is viewed for process and elementary flow coverage. Due to the simplicity of the model, there
is a lot of cut off and only the most important flows for climate change are involved. Regarding the
consistency check in the model, there is a lot of consistency between the scenarios since they share
the same system boundaries. The largest inconsistency is the modeling of the LCA spare part. Some
spare parts were able to be found in Ecoinvent whereas others were modeled as the material use and
manufacturing which in general was a little lower than when finding a spare part in Ecoinvent.
