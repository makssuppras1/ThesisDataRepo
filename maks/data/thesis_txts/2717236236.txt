Abstract 
Industrial fires occur on a regular basis in Denmark as well as all other countries. The risk of fatalities 
in these fires are generally low, but the economic implications can be significant. When including 
agricultural buildings as well as outdoor storage areas to industrial- and storage buildings, roughly 
10% of the total number of fires in Denmark in 2021 were industrial fires.   
In Denmark, a decreasing level of fires have been seen over the last years, but it is unknown if this 
trend also applies to industrial fires explicitly. In general, very little information is available on recent 
trends and generic root causes for industrial fires, both in Denmark and abroad. 
In the Nordic countries data is collected on each individual fire attended by the municipal rescue 
services. Data on individual fires alone are difficult to gain much insight from. The aim of this study is 
thus to transform the data into insightful information using statistical analysis. The primary focus is 
on Denmark but with comparisons to other countries where applicable. 
Data on industrial fires occurring in 2016-2021 were collected by the help of the local authorities 
from Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland, England and USA. Only aggregate data was available from 
the latter two and data from Finland was incomplete.  
Date was presented in different formats for all countries and a clean-up to make comparisons as 
valid as possible was performed. Data on fires in outdoor storage areas were not available from all 
countries, why only industrial building fires were looked at when comparing between countries.  
In general, comparisons between countries were subject to uncertainty as different definitions for 
various parameters were used. Even the number of industrial building fires per year was subject to 
uncertainty, as the definition of an industrial building fire may vary. 
Keeping that in mind, clear indications of a lower proportion of industrial building fires in Norway 
compared to the remaining Nordic countries was observed. A lower incident rare was likewise seen 
in USA and England. It has not been possible to determine if this is caused by fewer industrial fires or 
by the use of different definitions of what an industrial building fire is.  
The number of injuries per fire and the number of casualties per fire is somewhat comparable for all 
included countries. USA has the fewest registered injuries per fire, while Denmark and Sweden have 
the lowest number of casualties per fire. Discrepancies in definitions may again explain differences 
seen.  
Insights with less reservations were obtainable when looking at data for Denmark only. The number 
of industrial fires in Denmark decreased from 2016 to 2017 but remained relatively stable in the 
following years. The decrease from 2016 to 2017 was primarily seen for Industrial- and storage 
buildings.  
It was determined that if the progress of fire at time arrival were kept low (no spread of fire), the 
likelihood of it becoming a Large fire was significantly reduced, emphasizing the importance of fast 
discovery and a short response time.  
The progress of fire at time of arrival was greater for Industrial- and storage buildings with no 
classification than for those classified as flammable businesses, suggesting that the fire preventive 
measures required from flammable businesses are successful. 
In more than half of the specified cases, the assumed source of industrial fires in Denmark from 
2016-2021 is spark, ember or flames. The primary specified contributing factors of the fires are 
welding, grinding and cutting, mechanical error – other and heating source to close to flammable 
material (friction heat).

1 Introduction 
Much effort is put into reducing both the number and the severity of industrial fires, as well as all 
other fires, as the human- and economic cost can be immense. The risk of fatalities in industrial fires 
is much smaller in Denmark compared to the risk in residential fires (Nonnemann, 2022 and 
Beredskabsstyrelsen 2022), but the economic cost as well as the implications can be significantly 
larger.  
A current example of this, is the fire in a silo with wood pellets in Studstrupværket north of Aarhus in 
Denmark, which broke out on September 22nd, 2022, and took almost a month to completely 
extinguish (Aarhus kommune 2022). Not only is the direct damage expected to be substantial, but 
the implications associated with less capacity as well as the nuisance experienced by people living 
close by is significant.  
According to the U.S. National Fire Protection Association (NFPA), an estimated 37,910 fires 
occurred in industrial and manufacturing properties each year between 2011 and 2015 in USA. 
These resulted on average in 16 fatalities, 273 injuries and $ 1,2 billion in direct property damage 
(Campbell 2018).  
In 1980, the corresponding numbers were 114,500 fires, 57 fatalities and 1110 injuries (Campbell 
2018), suggesting a substantial reduction in both the number of fires and the consequences hereof.   
In Denmark, a decreasing number of fires in recent years are also observed, as illustrated in Figure 1 
(beredskabsstyrelsen 2022). The decrease seen is representative of all fires and not explicitly for 
industrial fires.  
Figure 1. Number of emergency responses to fire per 1000 population in Denmark 2016-2021 (taken from 
Beredskabsstyrelsen 2022 p. 28) 
In general, very little overview-information on fires in industrial buildings are publicly available. 
Some data can be found on the national fire safety homepages, but most published literature on 
industrial fires revolves around more specific aspects, like calculation of fire load, risk, prevention or 
evacuation - often with a case specific approach. Not much accessible information on the number or 
types of industrial fires in general is available, just like data on industrial fire losses are difficult to 
obtain (Zalosh, R.G. 2003, p. 11).   
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
On the Nordic Fire Statistics homepage, aggregate data on the number of building fires can be found 
for the Nordic countries, but only from 2010-20211. From here it can be seen that the number of 
building fires in industrial buildings in Denmark has varied between 537 and 675 annually from 2016-
2021. Similar data for the remaining countries are also available (Nordic Fire statistics (n.d.). No 
other information explicitly on industrial fires is available on the homepage.  
In 2021, 589 out of the 10,141 real fires reported by the local Danish rescue services2 took place in 
industrial- and warehouse buildings. When farming-related buildings as well as outdoors storage 
areas are included, the total number of industrial fires in 2021 was just below 1000 (Nonnemann, 
2022 and Beredskabsstyrelsen 2022) – or roughly 10% of the total number of fires.   
To reduce the number and severity of industrial fires, an important aspect is to better understand 
why and when the fires occur as well as the extend of them. Statistics can be a useful tool in 
enabling this understanding as it transforms single pieces of data to useful information and provides 
insights into fire problems (U.S. Fire Administration 2021, p.2). 
The aim of this paper is to do just that. To transform single pieces of data into useful information on 
industrial fires so that a better overview is created and so that more insights are obtained into the 
nature of the industrial fires.  
The focus of the report will be on industrial fires in Denmark, but to gain perspective comparison to 
other countries will be made whenever possible and appropriate.   
1.1 Problem statement 
Can industrial fires in Denmark be characterized using statistical tools on data already collected by 
the municipal rescue services and are industrial fires in Denmark comparable in number and severity 
to industrial fires in a range of other countries?

2 Data collection 
Data has been collected by different sources for each individual country included in the study. To 
perform a comparison between countries several assumptions have been made just as a subsequent 
cleanup of the received data has been carried out.   
In the following, general remarks to how data was requested and treated will be given, followed by 
more specific information on country level.  
Detailed information on necessary assumptions will be given in the relevant sections as appropriate.  
2.1 General 
Data on industrial fires were initially only requested from Denmark and in combination with The 
Danish Emergency Management Agency (DEMA) relevant data was specified.  
Based on the data obtained from Denmark, corresponding data was requested from the following 
countries: Norway, Finland, Sweden, Germany, England and USA. Within the deadline of this report, 
no answer were received from Germany.  
Representatives from the remaining countries provided either datasets or links to publicly available 
data.   
All datasets were standardized to the extent possible, and all relevant terms were translated to 
English. Data not considered relevant were removed from the datasets.  
Clean-up of data was done in Microsoft® Excel® for Microsoft 365 MSO and all statistical analysis 
was carried out in Minitab® version 21.1. 
Even though the sources of the data are considered reliable, and the quality generally is high, some 
reservation to the validity needs to be kept in mind. The data is in all cases reported by the 
municipal rescue services and no overall control of the data is made by the data collectors. 
Further, when comparing data between countries, differences in how the data is collected and 
differences in the definitions used is very likely to influence the results. 
To make a comparison of industrial fires between and within the countries, it is necessary to first 
define what the term industrial fire covers.  In the current study, industrial fire is defined rather 
broadly to include industrial- and storage buildings, farming buildings as well as outdoor storage 
areas. 
Data for outdoor storage areas are not available for many other countries than Denmark, and in 
cases where data is available, it is unclear if the data is related to storage areas only. As a result, it is 
chosen to introduce the definition Industrial building fire, which only includes industrial- and storage 
buildings as well as farming buildings. 
When comparing between countries, only Industrial building fires will be included while all industrial 
fires can be included when looking specifically at one country. 
Data falling within the definition of industrial fire was requested from the authorities in all countries 
included in the study.  Received data, that does not fall under above definitions has been cleaned 
from the datasets to the extent possible.

2.2.2 Norway 
In similarity to Denmark, the data from the fires are reported by the municipal rescue services.  
A file including all building fires, exclusive residential fires, were received from Norway. No fires in 
outdoor storage areas were included.  
The placement of the fire was given in two columns; Building type of the incident4 and NACE codes5, 
the lather containing Standard Industrial Classification 2007 codes obtained from Statistisk 
sentralbyrå Statistics Norway (2022). 
Based on data from the column Building type of the incident, all data not falling within the definition 
of industrial fires were removed. In cases where no data was available in the column Building type of 
the incident relevance was determined based on the column NACE codes.  
In cases where no data on placement was available in both columns, the entry was excluded (664 
entries in total). It is likely that some of these entries were relevant and that this will lead to an 
underrepresentation of the actual number of industrial building fires.  
Raw data contained 4858 entries of which 1342 entries remain for further analysis.    
2.2.3 Finland 
Data from the local rescue services are gathered in an online statistics database called Pronto 
(Ketola, J & Kokki, E 2019).  
Data from 2016 was missing in the dataset provided, hence only data from 2017-2021 is included. 
For warehouses only data from 2017-2019 is included. Based on codes, the data was given as being 
related to either farming or to industrial production buildings. A place of ignition column described 
whether the fire was outside- or inside the building, and this column was used to determine the 
placement of the fire as given in Table 1.  
In cases where the place of ignition was not determined, it was assumed that the fire was inside of 
the building. This assumption is likely to lead to an overrepresentation of fires inside the buildings.  
English translations of the Finnish terms were kindly provided by the Finnish authorities and these 
have been used wherever possible.  
Upon analysis it was discovered that the number of fires included in the provided dataset was 
significantly less than the number of industrial fires included in a pocket statistics pamphlet created 
by the Finnish authorities (Ketola, J and Kokki, E 2019). It was confirmed by the Finnish authorities 
that the dataset provided were missing some entries.  
As a result, all results and values from Finland should be treated with extreme caution and at most 
as indicative.

2.2.4 Sweden 
In Sweden it is also the responsibility of the municipal rescue services to provide data on fires. Only 
incidents attended by the municipal rescue services are included, and data is as assessed by them at

The data from Sweden was received in two datasets – one for building fires and one for non-building

2.2.5 USA 
Data from the National Fire Incident Reporting System (NFIRS) from the U.S. Fire Administration 
(USFA) is divided into 11 modules, with only the basic module being required for all fires. The 
remaining modules are filled out when appropriate. It is a large and complex set up, which require 
much database understanding and knowledge when using (FEMA 2011). Further, NFIRS is a 
voluntary system, where only roughly two-thirds of the U.S. fire departments participate (Campbell 
2018).  
To account for this, an annual survey of the U.S. fire departments conducted by the National Fire 
Protection Association (NFPA) is used to estimate scaling ratios. According to Campbell, R. (2018, p. 
101), the scaling ratios are obtained by “comparing NFPA’s projected totals of residential structure 
fires, nonresidential structure fires, vehicle fires, and outside and other fires, and associated civilian 
deaths, civilian injuries, and direct property damage with comparable totals in NFIRS”.  
Estimates of specific fire problems are thus obtained by multiplying the NFIRS data with the scaling 
ratios.  
As this setup is very different than the one seen in the Nordic countries, it is chosen not to use any 
raw data when looking at industrial fires in the US.  
The aggregate data for Residential and nonresidential building fire and fire loss estimates by property 
use and cause (2003-2019) is publicly available (USFA 2022) and has been used in the following, c.f. 
Table 1.

3 Legislation 
Much effort is put into reducing both the number and the severity of fires in industrial- and 
residential buildings. For the industrial settings, one of the main preventive measures utilized are the 
fire-preventive legislation. Fire-preventive legislation is generally targeted towards preventing fires 
and explosions as well as on limiting the damage on persons, values and environment in case a fire 
does take place. 
Even though the aim of the fire-preventive legislation is similar, differences in the legislation 
between the individual countries is expected. It is outside the scope of this report to dig deep into 
the differences and similarities between the national legislations, so in the following, only the Danish 
legislation will be summarized.  
3.1 Denmark 
The overall requirements, incl. fire-preventive measures, for all new or modified industrial buildings7 
in Denmark can be found in “Bekendtgørelse om bygningsreglement 2018 (BR18)” with amendments 
(Act No. 1399 from 12.12.2019), hereafter only called BR18. Chapter 5 in BR18 contains the fire 
related requirements.  
With BR18, that came into force on January 1st, 2020, a major change was introduced. As a new 
requirement, a certified fire advisor needs to be connected to the build when applying for a building 
permit, ensuring that the requirements in chapter 5 are met. This applies to all builds in fire class 2-4 
(BR 18, §20). The certification of the fire advisor needs to be in accordance with the fire class. The 
highest requirements are found for fire class 4.  
The fire class is determined from a usage category (based on sleeping accommodations, knowledge 
of emergency exits, number of people present at once etc.) combined with the risk (based on 
number of floors, fire load, risk of fire spread etc.) (BR18 §86).   
According to §82 of BR 18, Chapter 5 all builds are to be made in a manner that ensures that a 
satisfying level of safety for people and animal husbandry are kept throughout the lifespan of a 
building. This entails specific requirements towards (§§ 87-146): 
• 
Materials and construction 
• 
Fire technical installations and fire-fighting equipment 
• 
Emergency exits and escape routes 
• 
Fire resistance in bearing constructions 
• 
Prevention/reduction of spread of fire and smoke 
• 
Placement of high-risk installations, furnaces etc. 
• 
Accessibility for the rescue services  
• 
Operation- control and maintenance of the building once in operation 
The requirements are in general applicable to all buildings, but with some specific requirements 
towards for instance industrial- and storage buildings based on size and fire load.   
Further to the overall general requirements, some buildings are also subject to recurring fire 
inspections. According to Bekendgørelse om brandsyn”8 (Act No. 2341 of 09.12.2021, §1) regular fire 
inspections need to be carried out to reduce the risk of fires, spread of fires and harm to people,

Regular fire inspections are a requirement for buildings mentioned in appendix 1 and 2, hereunder

• 
Bekendtgørelse om brandfarlige og brændbare væsker (Act No. 1639 of 06/12/2016), which

as well as number of storage units.  
• 
Bekendtgørelse om tekniske forskrifter for gasser (Act No. 1444 of 15/12/2010) which covers

•

products that can self-ignite or where the quantity is very large.  
• 
Bekendtgørelse om klassifikation af eksplosionsfarlige områder (Act No. 590 og 26/06/2003), 
which deals with the identification, classification and marking of areas with a risk of 
explosions - the so-called ATEX-zones.

quantity. These businesses are also called Seveso-businesses and approx. 150 is categorized

4 Statistical description and comparison 
The countries included in this report varies much in size. To ease comparisons between countries, 
values in the following sections are primarily given per million inhabitants or per fire as appropriate. 
On the Nordic Fire Statistics homepage (Nordic Fire Statistics Nd), an overview of the development

4.2 Spread of fire 
Data for the progress of fire at time of arrival of the rescue service is only available for the Nordic 
countries. It is described somewhat differently in the different countries. In Denmark, data for 
spread of fire is directly given as either yes or no, dependent on whether the fire has spread from 
the room of origin. In Sweden and Finland, the fire development upon arrival of the rescue services 
and at the end is given. When these are identical, it is assumed that there is no spread of fire, and it 
is defined as a no, while all other cases are defined as a yes. In the data from Norway, it is described 
how the fire has spread. Cases where there is no spread of fire from the object of the fire is defined 
as a no. All other entries are defined as a yes. Only data from industrial building fires are included in 
the comparison shown in Figure 6.

Denmark. The proportion is relatively stable while the proportion of fires defined as not spreading is 
increasing. This is likely caused by better reporting from the municipal rescue services as the level of 
unspecified entries are decreasing.  
Norway has the highest proportion of fires that spread. The definition in Norway differs from the 
rest as it includes all fires that spread from the object first ignited. The proportion is, therefore, also 
expected to be higher than for the other countries. The proportion of fires that spread are increasing 
in the period for Norway.

spread is significantly lower in Finland. The proportion of fires that have not spread are, however,

Flammable businesses 
In Denmark, businesses are categorized as flammable businesses12 if they store, use or produce 
items that are flammable or explosive. In the Danish dataset, it is noted if the fire took place in a 
flammable business. Table 5 shows the progress of the fire at time of arrival in percentage based on 
whether the industrial building was categorized as a flammable business or not. There are clearly 
large differences between the two.  
Fires were extinguished at time of arrival in more than 30% of the cases for flammable businesses 
while the corresponding percentage for the non-classified businesses is below 15%. On contrast, the 
overheated (fully developed room fire) is experienced at time of arrival in less than 3% of the cases 
for flammable businesses and in more than 15% of the cases for the non-classified.  
Flammable businesses are subject to additional fire preventive measures, cf. § 33 in 
Beredskabsloven (Bekendtgørelse af beredskabsloven, LBK nr 314 of 03/04/2017). The exact 
measures will differ dependent on the type and size of the business, but a common denominator is 
that the measures are put in place to minimize the risk of fire, increase the rate of discovery of the 
fire as well as ease the extinguishing of the fire – either automatically (e.g. sprinklers), by local 
personnel (using e.g. fire-fighting equipment) or by the municipal rescue services. This combined is 
expected to be a strong contributing factor to the much higher percentage of fires extinguished at 
time of arrival and the much lower percentage of overheated (fully developed room fires) for the 
flammable businesses. 
It should be noted that small fires extinguished locally (e.g. a fire in a trash can) might not be 
registered in cases where there is no automatic notification system. This could lead to a higher 
proportion of fires classified as Extinguished at arrival for flammable businesses with AFA.

4.4 Injuries and casualties 
To be able to compare the number of injuries and casualties that have occurred in industrial building 
fires between countries some assumptions and adjustments to the data have been needed. The 
adjustments outlined below have been used: 
• 
Denmark: Number of injured or killed per fire is given and are used directly 
• 
USA: Total quantity per year is given and are in most cases used directly. Casualties below 3 
per year is stated as “<3“ in the dataset. For these, the quantity is assumed to be 0 with a 
risk of under-estimation. 
• 
England: Total quantity per year is given and are used directly 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
• 
Finland: Injuries are divided between mild and severe. The sum of the two are used in this 
study. Number of casualties are used directly. 
• 
Norway: Injuries and casualties are not quantified in the Norwegian data, but instead 
described qualitatively with a yes or a no. To enable comparison all entries where a yes is 
given will be assumed to relate to only one person  
It should be noted that this will lead to an under-estimation of the actual number of injuries 
and casualties in Norway. 
• 
Sweden: Casualties only include those, who died at the scene of the incident. It does not 
include those that may have died later of their injuries. It is not clear whether this is the 
case for all countries.   
Injuries are divided between those that were treated at the scene of the incident by 
emergency personnel and those, that were transported to a hospital for treatment. The sum 
will be used in this study. 
4.4.1 Injuries 
To ease the comparison between the countries it has been decided to compare the number of 
injuries per industrial building fire rather than the actual number of injuries or injuries per 
population size, see Figure 14.  
Most of the countries exhibit a relatively stable number of injuries – both per year and compared to 
each other. USA differs from the rest with a markedly lower level of injuries per fire and Finland tend 
to be somewhat higher, but with large differences from year to year.  
It was expected that Norway would be at the lower end due to the assumed underrepresentation of 
the actual number of injuries. This is, however, not the case. In Norway, a clear decreasing tendency 
is seen since 2018. 
In Denmark a slight decreasing tendency over the period is also seen, although with an increase in 
the proportion of injuries from 2020 - 2021.  
England exhibits a relatively stable level of injuries while number of injuries per industrial building 
fire has been increasing in Sweden from 2018 to 2021.  
With the high fluctuation from year to year, it is difficult to conclude anything about the differences 
seen between the countries. The low level in USA could very likely be caused by a different definition 
of how severe an injury needs to be to be registered.

A Chi-square test for association was carried out using data from Industrial- and storage buildings 
only to see if the Material first ignited is associated with the Fire object first ignited. Only the 80% 
most frequent categories of Material first ignited and Fire object first ignited was directly included, 
while the remaining categories was combined and included as other.  
The Pearson chi-square statistic was 5094.634 (p-value = 0.000) and the likelihood ratio chi-square 
statistic was 3750.021 (p-value = 0.000). Both p-values are less than the significance level of 0.05 and 
it is concluded that there is association between the Material first ignited and Fire object first ignited 
for Industrial- and storage buildings.  
As much information is lost due to the high frequency of unknown/unspecified/other categories, the 
main learning is that wood is often the first object ignited in roof- and wall constructions as well as in 
fires in wood stock. See appendix 8.5 for more detailed information. 
A similar Chi-square test for association was carried out for breeding- and operating buildings 
combined with barns. Here, the Pearson chi-square statistic was 3257 (p-value = 0.000) and the 
likelihood ratio chi-square statistic was 2377 (p-value = 0.000). Both p-values are less than the 
significance level of 0.05 and it is concluded that there is association between the Material first 
ignited and Fire object first ignited for barns and breeding- and operating buildings.  
Not surprisingly, the category hay and straw is heavily associated with fires in stock of hay. Hay and 
straw is in some cases also the material first ignited for fires in roof- and wall constructions as well as 
chimneys but at a frequency lower than expected if there was no association between the two 
parameters. In similarity with Industrial- and storage buildings wood is given as the material first 
ignited more frequently than expected for both roof- and wall constructions, see appendix 8.5.

5 Conclusion 
Clear and consistent definitions is of utmost importance when transforming data to information by 
the help of statistics. This is even more evident when comparing data obtained from different 
sources. The definitions and categories used when describing a fire varies between the countries 
included in this study – even between the Nordic countries. As a result, much caution should be used 
when drawing conclusions from the comparisons made. This is especially true for comparisons 
including Finland, as the dataset is known to be incomplete.  
With that in mind, the comparisons still strongly indicate that the level of industrial building fires is 
much smaller in Norway than in the remaining Nordic countries. The reasons for this are yet 
unknown. The incidence rate is likewise much lower in England and USA than in Sweden and 
Denmark. With the lack of homogeneity in definitions it is not possible to determine if this is caused 
by an actual lower rate of industrial building fires or if it is caused by a difference in the type of fires 
included in the datasets.  
It is seen that the level of injuries per fire is relatively comparable between most countries, although 
some fluctuation is seen. Finland appears to have a higher proportion of injured per fire while USA 
appear to have less injured. Differences in definitions is likely a major contributor to the lower 
proportion of injured in USA. The number of casualties per fire from 2016-2021 is between 0,04% 
and 0,15% for all countries included. Sweden and Denmark have the lowest proportion while 
Norway has the highest. In general, the number of casualties in industrial fires are very low 
compared to the number in residential fires.  
In Denmark, fires were categorized as small, medium or large by the type and number of nozzles 
used as the water consumption was too unreliable to be used for classification.  
The progress of the fire at time of arrival of the rescue services were markedly different based on 
size of the fire. More than 85% of Large fires were either overheated (fully developed room fire) or 
with visible flames from 1 or more rooms at time of arrival, while the same proportion for Medium 
and Small fires were, respectively, 50 % and 15%. This emphasizes the importance of both fast 
detection and short response time, as the final size of the fire is very dependent on the progress of 
the fire at time of arrival.  
Difference in progress of the fire at time of arrival was likewise seen dependent on the placement of 
the fire in Denmark. A much higher proportion of the fires were extinguished at time of arrival in 
industrial- and storage buildings than in both barns and Breeding- and operating buildings. In 
contrast, the proportion of fires that were overheated at arrival, was much higher in barns and 
Breeding- and operating buildings compared to industrial- and storage buildings.  
In industrial- and storage buildings, overheated (fully developed room fire) or fires with visible flames 
from 1 or more rooms were seen for 28,4% of the fires in businesses categorized as flammable 
businesses while the corresponding proportion for business with no classification was 51,7%, 
suggesting that the fire preventive measures required from flammable businesses are successful. 
The source, material and object initially involved in the development of the fire is in a large 
proportion of the cases either unknown or unspecified.  
Disregarding these cases, the assumed primary source of the industrial fires in Denmark from 2016-
2021 is in more than 50% of the cases spark, ember or flames. The contributing factors of the fires 
are more diverse, having welding, grinding and cutting, mechanical error – other and heating source 
to close to flammable material (friction heat) as responsible for, respectively, 14%, 13% and 13% of 
the specified cases.  
Neither of these categories change markedly based on placement of the fire.  
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
The material and object first ignited are, on the other hand, very much dependent on the placement 
of the fire. For all placements related to farming (Barns, Breeding- and operating buildings and 
storage area – farming) Hay and straw are the most frequently ignited material specified and stock 
of hay is the most frequently specified object. For Industrial- and storage buildings as well as Storage 
area – industrial, wood and plastic are the most frequent materials to be ignited first. In Industrial- 
and storage buildings roof- and wall constructions as well as waste stock are the objects most 
frequently specified to be ignited first. 
In general, much insight into industrial fires can be gained from the data already available, especially 
if looking at one country only -  but a statistical analysis can never be more valid or accurate than the 
data on which it is build. 
If better and more accurate comparisons between countries were to be made, more homogeneous 
definitions are needed just as a better insight into the background for all data would be preferrable.

6 Future work  
It could be of great interest to dive deeper into a comparison between different countries – 
preferably even more countries than included in this study.  
In order for the comparisons to be of greater value a more homogenous collection of data in the 
individual countries, combined with more identical definitions of the parameters examined, is 
needed. 
If more homogenous data is not available, much could be gained from a closer collaboration 
between the owners of the data, when analyzing it, so that a deeper understanding is present and 
less assumptions is needed.  
Further, it could be of great interest to examine the low incident rate of industrial fires in Norway 
better and see if any suggestions for improvements in the remaining countries could be found. It is 
still not established if the difference seen is caused by a reduced risk of industrial fires in Norway or 
if it is caused by less industrial buildings per inhabitant or other causes.
