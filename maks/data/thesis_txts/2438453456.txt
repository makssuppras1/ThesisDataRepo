Abstract
Sustainability is an ambiguous term, but its definition is notable often built on three core
elements, the three dimensions of sustainability, i.e., social, economic and environmental sustain-
ability. A study of the Lund and Copenhagen municipality plans, along with an analysis of
the redevelopment plans of two areas within two large Scandinavian cities was performed. The
redevelopment of Västerbro, formerly known as Öresundsvägen in Lund and Carlsberg City in
Copenhagen. The aim was to determine how well sustainability was incorporated into the cities
municipality plans and in turn the redevelopment plans of the areas within the cities.
The incorporation of sustainability in municipality goals and their manifestation in the municipal
and redevelopment strategies was done by mapping the them on a sustainability scale, i.e.,
mapping how well the plans incorporate the three dimensions of sustainability and how well
the redevelopment strategy enforces the municipal goals and strategies.
The analysis of the redevelopment project plans was done using a eight-step process based on
Maclarens (1996) process for urban sustainability reporting. Indicator sets for a combination
of a sectoral, goal- and domain based framework was used to evaluate the sustainability of
the plans.
From the result the strengths and weaknesses of the redevelopment plans are
identified and used for lessons learned and recommendations for improvements to the Västerbro
development project.
Keywords: Sustainable Development, Indicator Assessment, Casual Framework, Sustainability
Mapping, Västerbro City District, Carlsberg City Districts.

Preface
This Master thesis has been prepared at the department of Management Engineering - System
analysis group (DTU SYS) at Technical University of Denmark. It has been done in fulfillment
of the requirements for acquiring a M.Sc. degree in Civil Engineering. The thesis was conducted
during the spring semester of 2018 and it corresponds to 30 ECTS points. It has been carried
out under the supervision of Jay Sterling Gregg.
The main purpose of the study has been to investigate and assess how well the aspect of
sustainability is incorporated into different cities municipality plans and how that is translated
into redevelopment projects’ strategies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter the background and motivation for the study is introduces, the scope of the
problem where the specific focus area of the study described and the research the study will
entail defined. Additionally, the overall thesis structure is outlined.
1.1
Background
Since the end of the 18th century, the process of urbanization has led to a rapid increase in
population in urban settlement which is continuing today [1].
In 2007, half of the world’s
population was living in urban settlements [2] and with this continuance, it will increase to
about 70% by 2050 [3]. The movement is mostly driven by a prospect of a higher quality of life
with economic prosperity and educational opportunities [2, 4]. However, these advancements
are not always the result as this urbanization trend has both positive and negative effects.
The increase in population concentration results in economic growth due to higher potential in
production of goods and services, more effective transportation systems, higher employment,
better education, health care, culture, etc. [3, 4]. As a consequence of rapid urban growth, sub
optimal management of the urban environment can enhance negative social environmental and
economic impacts [5]. An increased strain on resources and ecosystems, increased consumption,
emission, energy demand and waste generation [6], poverty and pressure on public services,
sanitation and water systems [7] are consequences of poor management and rapid growth.
How can we reduce the negative effects of urbanization and utilize the benefits from it? Rapid
urbanization has forced a change in how urban spaces should be planned and managed, and
with that, a growing concern for sustainable development has emerged.
In order to accommodate the growing population in cities, without expanding its boarders,
municipalities are opting for more efficient land-use within the city limits. Therefore, municipalities
aim to densify the city in “empty” spaces, or gaps within the city. These are often low density
industrial areas with high potential of becoming more concentrated.
1.2
Scope of the Problem
The scope is to determine how well sustainability is incorporated in cities municipality plans and
how that translates into redevelopment projects’, strategies and consequently the sustainability
of the final product of the development. One development project in Lund and one project in
Copenhagen municipality were chosen for this study. The scope is divided into two sections.
The first explaining the problem area of the study, where the two study areas Västerbro and
Carlsberg city district are covered. Then the research questions are stated, which is done to
limit and improve the scope of the thesis.

Problem Area
Copenhagen and Lund municipality, along with other municipalities in Denmark’s capital

Lund’s municipal plan focuses on transforming Lund into a sustainable urban environment

By developing an indicator framework with a set of relevant sustainable indicators to assess the

is estimated to be finished in 2024, but some of the operations in the area have already started

2.1.1
Sustainable Urban development
Sustainable development and sustainability, although being different terminologies, are closely
related terms. Sustainable development is a process by which the state of being sustainable can
be achieved [16]. The definition of sustainable development is ambiguous, as it has been defined
in various manners by different organizations.
The ambiguity of sustainable development
definitions is problematic for achieving actual sustainability [17]. This lack of consensus stems
from the geographic location, climate, social norms and decries, culture, values, economic
environment, environmental priorities, etc. which differ between countries and even within
countries [18]. However, one of the most famous and often cited definition comes from the
1987 Brundtland report Our Common Future from the World Commission on Environment
and Development [5] where sustainable development is defined as “development that meets the
needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own
needs” [5]. It refers to an urban development without a negative effect on natural resources,
which would cause ecological imbalance – compromise the future generation’s ability to meet
their needs. Definitions of sustainability often comprise three core elements. These are referred
to as the three dimensions of sustainability: environmental, economic and social sustainability
[19].

2.1.2
Challenges of Sustainable Urban Developments
Various strategies for urban development have been devised to create a sustainable urban
environment. However, these strategies can have both positive and negative impacts and are
often highly correlated – making it a complicated process with interconnected challenges to
achieve sustainability.
Densification is a common goal for achieving sustainability in urban environments, which results
in an increase in number of residents per square kilometer within a city. The aim of densifying
a city is often accompanied by the goal of creating a mixed-use urban environment [23]. Various
environmental and social benefits are accompanied by densification and mixed-use of an urban
environment.
Creating a high-density, mixed-use urban area can shorten daily commuting leaving more time
for other activities, it provides a greater variety of activities. Moreover, it may provide easier
access to services like shops, schools, supermarkets, post office, etc. [24]. With densification,
natural areas can be preserved by reducing urban sprawl and the accompanying transportation
infrastructure, and provides opportunities to create a more walkable, transit-oriented community.
A relationship between densification and transport patterns has been found, where, with
increased densification, the use of private car decreases, resulting in lowered emissions from
the transport sector [4, 23]. However, a study by Heinonen, et.al. (2013) showed that with
increased densification, a growth in consumption and consequently, a rise in emissions and
energy usage which exceeded the reduction from transportation [25].
Despite the benefits of building a dense mixed-used urban area, it may not always be desired
by its residents. Thus, the design and layout of a dense urban environment is important as
it is possible to create a dense form without the perception of high density.
This involves
creating high density areas without high-rises, with high quality space which provides privacy
and visible greenery [24].
An inevitable result of urbanization is the change in land-use. Human expansion and development
has led to the depletion and fragmentation of natural areas (forests, vegetation, fields, parks,
etc.), which is important for a balanced ecosystem [26, 27]. Natural areas such as forest and
agricultural fields are being replaced by impervious surfaces, e.g., roads, parking lots, building
roofs, etc., or are divided into smaller patches of green spaces, separated by heavy traffic roads.
This results in environmental, social and economic impacts [26, 27].
The loss of habitat results in a decrease in biodiversity, which causes an ecological imbalance
and reduction of resources. Moreover, the depletion of natural green spaces within the urban
environment effects climate change reduction and mitigation, storm water management and
urban climate resources [26].
The emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2) and other greenhouse gases are the causes of climate

change. Trees and other vegetation remove CO2 from the atmosphere through photosynthesis,
thus acting as mitigating measures against climate change, and even improving air quality
within cities [28]. Furthermore, the replacement of green areas with impervious surfaces, reduce
the infiltration and evapotranspiration from these vegetations, amplifying the effects of climate
change [26, 28].
With infiltration, green spaces act as mitigating measures against surface water runoff, decreasing
the intensity and frequency of flooding in urban areas [29]. The increased surface runoff and
highly polluted runoff, due to pollution collected in the impervious surfaces, can result in
pollution of the natural environment and harmful health impacts from the polluted runoff, e.g.,
human discomfort, sentimental and economic losses due to damages from large volume runoffs
[26, 28].
Compared to its rural surroundings, urban areas have different climate characteristics. Impervious
surfaces absorbed the solar radiation and transformed it into heat energy, this process is then
magnified by the lack of vegetation, which by evapotranspiration utilizes the solar energy and
cools the surrounding urban environment. Consequently, the urban climate to have higher
temperature and more pollution, causing human discomfort, stress and even death due to
extreme heats. Additionally, the higher temperature leads to increased energy demand and
therefore increased emissions [28].
In addition to being replaced, natural habitats are being divided and fragmented by excessive
roads and heavy traffic in urban areas. This is causing habitat patches, continuously decreasing
in size, to form, resulting in loss of biodiversity. Small size patches are unable to sustain species
and heavy traffic roads increase species mortality rate, by acting as a resource and gene flow
barrier, resulting in inbreeding and inability to prevail, i.e., loss of biodiversity [27].
Humans are also affected by the reduction and fragmentation of green spaces. Green natural
areas within the urban environment create an attractive space where residents can observe
seasonal changes and wildlife, and enjoy outdoors activities, which support human wellbeing
and good physical and mental health. Natural spaces are a place of serenity for people which
can help reduce stress. Residents of neighborhoods with high density green areas are general
in better health [27].
Although the presence of green spaces is important, the accessibility should also be considered.
Since most people visiting green areas go by foot, it is important for the usability of these areas
that their placement puts them within a 15-minute walking distance.
The areal extent of the green areas also influences human health. Traffic noise can be a factor
which hinders outdoor activities, however, large enough green spaces can provide a sheltered
space where these outdoor activities can be enjoyed [27].
Natural green spaces within urban areas support human wellbeing and are effective to reduce
urban sprawl, as residents become more inclined to live in denser urban areas rather than the
rural outskirts of cities [28].
In short, human development is substantially impacted by the natural environment, therefore,
the preservation of green spaces is an important aspect of sustainable development to create a
viable and livable environment.
With growing cities and population, the demand for transportation increases [4]. Transportation
is vital to create a connected city. Mobility is highly valued and essential for social and economic
activities [23].

Transportation habits are closely related to the residents’ place of living, working, school and
social activities [4]. In their current state, transportation systems are one of the three largest
consumption domains, responsible for negative impacts on the environment [30].
Moreover, the transportation sector has a high influence on human health and economic costs
[23]. These impacts include depletion of natural resources, e.g. oil, a high demand of space,
distortion of landscape and climate change, noise pollutions, social and environmental cost [23].
The use of fossil fuels to drive motorized transportation releases CO2. The high concentration
of CO2 in the atmosphere has led to a global impact known as climate change. The effects
of it is extreme weather, expansion of deserts, rising sea levels and the destabilization and
destruction of ecosystems. Vehicles disperse oil, heavy metals and other hazardous chemicals
onto the road where it flows to surface- and groundwater sources and contaminate them [23].
As discussed earlier, the transportation sector and the development of its infrastructure is a
very inefficient use of land as it covers large areas in impervious surfaces. Eventually, it causes
the depletion and fragmentation of natural spaces, increased flooding and pollution of surface
and underground water, creating microclimates within urban areas - heat islands, reducing the
livability of the areas [23, 26, 27].
Increased land-use for motorized, infrastructure is the consequence of urban sprawl but also the
main aspect which enables it [23]. The road infrastructure both reduces the aesthetics of the
environment and the usability of other modes of transportation, such as walking a bicycling.
Furthermore, the construction of transportation infrastructure and maintaining it is a costly
process, for the government, which resources could be spent on other community activities.
Moreover, accidents are not only financially costly, but also socially as it can result in chronic
pain, disabilities and death. Another quality of life and human health impact of transportation
stems from air and noise pollution. Nitrogen oxides, carbon monoxides, particulate matter,
etc. emanating from transportation (especially motorized) are a serious cause for concern for
human health.
Transportation is a noisy sector, and high levels can disturb sleep and communication, contributing
to stress related diseases. This can then be magnified by congestion as they tend to increase
stress and noise from traffic. Furthermore, congestion reduce productivity, are time consuming
and costly, as delivery of goods become more expensive, commuting become costlier and time
availability for important social activities is reduced [23].
A sustainable transportation system must comprise and integrate the three dimensions of
sustainability. It should be environmentally friendly, i.e., function without a negative effect
on the environment. These systems must meet transportation needs without the emissions of
pollutions that threaten health and climate, minimizing waste footprint, and limiting consumption
of renewable and nonrenewable resources, the use of land and fragmentation of the natural
environment [23]. There should be a variety of transportation modes available and the transport-
ation system should ensure access to basic needs, other people, places, goods and services.
Transportation systems should ensure equity, be affordable, operate efficiently and safely without
an adverse effect on human health. [23, 31]. Finally, the system should be cost effective, and
support a vibrant economy [31].
Within the urban environment, the main modes of transport available for people are private
vehicle, public transportation, e.g. buses, trains, metros, subways, light rails, etc., bicycling and
walking. The passenger car is highly desired, but the least sustainable mode of transport [23].

The desirability of private transportation stems for the mobility, freedom, comfort, flexibility
and reliability it entails [4]. However, the associated infrastructure needed, pollution and cost
results in an unsustainable mode of transport [23]. Public transportation such as buses require
the same infrastructure as passenger cars, however, the passenger capacity of buses compared
to passenger cars provides the possibility of more efficient per capita energy consumption and a
reduction in car volume on roads. This requires buses to operate at high capacity, which is not
often the reality as they are subjected to the same congested road conditions as private cars,
discouraging their use. Moreover, they are often a large source of noise pollution and aesthetics
displeasure [23]. Public transportation by railway is a much more attractive system. They
have a higher passenger capacity than buses and as they operate on a separate infrastructure
system, they are much more reliable. Walking and bicycling are the most sustainable modes
of transportation. Bicycle and pedestrian infrastructure requires less costly infrastructure and
uses a considerably less space, they promote physical good health and are without any emissions
or other negative environmental impacts [23].
There are various strategies to develop a sustainable transportation system to enhance the
overall performance and viability of public transit systems [23]. Technological changes aim to
implement new technologies to reduce the negative impact of the transportation sector on the
environment and health [32].
Examples of technological changes include increasing the user-efficiency and quality of fossil
fuels to reduce their consumption and impact, switching to alternative fuel and developing new
tires and road surfaces which reduces the noise levels from traffic [23, 32]. However, these
measures are expensive, and often trivial as the mitigating effects are often canceled out by the
continuous growth of car use. Therefore, it is believed that there is a need for behavioral changes
in the transportation sector [32]. Behavioral changes aim to change the transportation habits,
resulting in a reduction in transportation activities [32]. An emphasis on making sustainable
transportation more available, attractive and desirable and limiting developments which favor
the private transportation sector is vital to encourage change in transportation activities [4].
Providing more transportation options improves the accessibility for residents as it increases
the possibility to adapt to each individual’s circumstance [23].
Switching from the use of
private automobile traffic to public transportation, bicycling and pedestrian provide benefits
beyond the environmental impact. Reducing automobile traffic in city centers creates a more
attractive space and even has a positive impact on retail sales [23]. Furthermore, by reducing
motorized transportation, more sustainable modes become more usable and safer [33]. However,
achieving sustainable transportation by behavioral change measures are conflicting for some
aspects of quality of life, as they are considered to force additional effort and reduced comfort,
freedom and flexibility of its user [32]. Finally, transportation systems play a large role in
commuting to and from work and a development should not result in reduced mobility, causing
unemployment and other hardships [23]. Therefore, it is important to consider and include the
users of transportation systems in the development design, as it increases the acceptability and
support of the plans and subsequently, the effectiveness [32].

gaps in cities.
At the time of their construction, they were primarily placed on the cities’
boundaries, however, with time the cities have expanded and enclosed these areas with denser
neighborhoods.

2.1.3
Planning for Sustainability in the Urban Environment
With the rapid expansion and growth of the urban environment, promoting urban sustainability
becomes increasingly difficult.
Following the introduction of sustainability and sustainable
development at the end of the 1900s, a global focus has been on planning for sustainable
development [34].
The challenges accompanied by the rapid growth – are rooted in local
activities and are more recognizable and manageable at local scale. Therefore, the concept
of sustainably has been adopted by municipalities along with some strategizes to achieve
sustainability [17].
A development should improve land-use efficiency and create a dense, mixed-use urban area with
widespread, high-quality public realm and protected green natural spaces [4, 22]. Diversity in
housing is important and promoting attractive, eco-friendly businesses, which provide economic
growth, job creation and reduce unemployment is important to meet the locals needs. Preservation
of culture and history is an essential aspect of maintaining community identity and a city’s
characteristic.
To ensure equity, the built environment should ensure easy accessibility to
services for all and equal opportunity for education and employment [22]. The development
should promote the use of sustainable transportation by de-emphasizing road infrastructure,
which facilitate private vehicle traffic, and advance public transportation, walking and bicycle
traffic [4].
Integrating commercial, business and residential areas, creates a more attractive, productive
and lively area with shorter distances. Subsequently, it enables the use of more sustainable
modes of transport of the residents for their daily activities’ [21]. The built environment should
also promote safety by building safe transportation infrastructure and provide sufficient lighting
of streets. An efficient and clean energy and waste disposal system should be implemented but
the main goal is to reduce overall consumption of natural resources and reduce waste production
[19].

2.1.4
Urban Sustainability Assessment
Developing sustainable development indicators (SDI), is the most commonly used tool for
sustainability assessment.
These indicators can serve various functions.
They enable the
assessment of social, economic and environmental impacts of the implementation of an urban
design and policies, measuring and evaluating and monitoring progress or result of a municipality
plan [16, 35]. Moreover, they are used to describe the current state of an urban environment,
identify areas’ strengths and opportunities, or areas and aspects in need of improvement [16].
By utilizing this tool in urban sustainability reporting, i.e., sustainability assessment, local
governments can use them as a basis for planning strategies. It can be used to better implement
new, target oriented management plans, assess the impacts of policies and investments, determine
how to tackle existing issues, and to support overall decision making [16, 19, 35]. Furthermore,
organizations, businesses and individuals can use these urban sustainability reports to pressure
local government for change and educate themselves on how they can make a difference [16].
It’s important to keep in mind that sustainable development indicators are simplifications
of real circumstances and can only provide an indication of the situation. Therefore, a set of
multiple indicators with a wide range must be applied to portray the different aspects of a
situation. The indicators must also fulfill some criterion [16].
Maclaren (1996), defines some criterion for a SDIs. To be considered as a SDI, the indicator
must be developed with input from the multiple community stakeholders.
Moreover, the
indicators must be at least integrating, i.e., correlate the dimensions of sustainability, forward-
looking, i.e., measuring progress over time, or distributional, i.e., consider population and
geographic conditions, as well as distinguishing between local and global sources and effects [16].
Despite their common use, SDIs have their limitations when it comes to assessing sustainability.
Due to the ambiguousness of sustainability and sustainable development definitions, universal
SDI sets are difficult to construct and are often incomplete. Since the development of SDIs are
usually dependent on the objective for the use, the different possibilities to constrict SDI sets
are as numerous as there are definitions of sustainability in development [17]. Consequently,
this often leads to exclusion of some aspects of sustainability and an inadequate selection of
indicators [17, 36]. Data accessibility also causes a constrain to the reliability of using SDI
for sustainable development assessment, e.g., lack of data or inadequate data results in an
incomplete indicator set or a skewed assessment of the system [17, 18].

Maclaren (1996), proposes a structured process for urban sustainability reporting, using sets of
indicators for evaluating sustainability development of the urban environment. It is a nine-step
process for sustainability assessment, producing a report where the indicators and their selection
are described and the progress toward sustainability is evaluated. Furthermore, it includes
suggestions for indicator improvement and even recommendations for action.
The process is iterative, where the indicators are regularly updated. These nine steps can be
seen in figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Maclaren’s (1999) [16] nine steps for urban sustainability reporting
The first step is used to define the sustainability goals, that the indicators are meant to
measure. These are goals already defined in planning documents or specifically defined for
the assessment. When developing the sustainability goals inputs from the community, i.e., the
multiple stakeholder within the community, should be used to reflect the local conditions as
well as possible.
The second step is scoping, where the scope of the area is defined. It involves identifying the
temporal and spatial boundaries of the study area, i.e., the measure time-frame, geographical
context and the intended audience. Additionally, scoping also entails defining the purpose of
the indicators and the preliminary number of indicators needed.
This task is highly dependent on the target audience, i.e., which stakeholder the assessment
is intended for. Diverse audiences have different needs for the number and level of depth of
the indicators. For instance, scientist may be more interested in highly detailed and complex

indicators, relevant specifically for them.
Finding an adequate number of indicators has been proven a difficult task.
Moreover, as
indicators are dependent on communities’ sustainability goals, which are locally influenced is it
possible to develop a “core” set of indicators, adaptable to different communities at all scales?

studies of SDIs in developed western countries where the number 29 was defined as an optimal

can be modified, or additional criteria added.
Identifying a set of potential indicators is the fifth step of the process.
Reviewing already
produced sustainability reports can help – identifying potential indicators.
Brainstorming
sessions, interviews and or questioners are tools to identify indicators and should include both
experts in this field and non-expert community stakeholders. The experts can provide support
for the non-experts, who can offer a different perspective and encourage the selection of easier
understandable indicators.
The sixth step involves the evaluation of the indicators against the selected criteria and require-
ments of the chosen framework and developing a final set of indicators.
There are several
methods of evaluating the indicators against the selection criteria, each varying in complexity.
A simple evaluation is to give all criteria equal weight and measure indicators compliance
on a two-point scale, “criterion met” and “criterion not met”. Giving the criteria different
weighing based on relative importance and measuring indicator compliance on a three-point
or five-point scale results in a more complex evaluation system. These two approaches are
referred to as a one-step approach – where the indicators evaluated against all the selection
criteria simultaneously.
However, it is not always possible to meet all the criteria simultaneously due to conflicts
among them. Therefore, a more sequenced approach may be beneficial. A sequential procedure
evaluates the indicators against the selection criteria in steps. The most critical or objective
criteria are used in the early stage evaluation, these are internal – availability, validity, represent-
ativeness accuracy, etc. Evaluation against the other criteria follow and at the final stages the
external, user-related criteria are used. This step-wise process reduces the number of indicators
which need to be evaluated at each step, concluding with a final indicator set. This also has
the benefit of stakeholders only reviewing criteria relevant to them.
A three-phase procedure allows the addition of new indicators, if critical criterion is not being
met by the initially chosen indicators.
Initially, the indicators are evaluated against some
general, but critical, criteria. Thereafter, the indicators are rated based on compliance, and
additional indicators introduced if not all criteria were covered. Finally, the indicators are

16
2 Literature Review
evaluated against the goals and vision defined.
Step seven is to collect relevant data, analyze the results from the indicator sets and determining
the progress toward sustainability.
Measuring the progress toward sustainability is considered the most difficult step of the process
as the indicators often correlate different aspects of sustainability and the result are often open
to interpretation. For example, rapid economic growth may indicate a healthy economy but
often leads to increased waste footprint.
Thus, it measures progress toward sustainability
from an economic perspective but away from sustainability for an environmental perspective.
Therefore, the interpretation of results should be made clear in the sustainability report.
Step eight, preparing the report and presenting it. The report should include a description
of the selection of indicators, their meaning, their importance, how trends have been in the
past, or how they are expected to be in the future and whether they show progress toward
sustainability. Additionally, it is useful to include a description of the linkage between the
indicators and how it integrates the different dimensions of sustainability. Finally, suggestions
for indicator and data collection improvements and even recommendations for action may also
be included.
Step nine, the final step of the process, is assessing the indicator performance adequacy and
whether they are measuring correctly. It is possible to carry out this assessment periodically,
dependent on data availability. The assessment questions the data collection, scientific and
technological advancements which might affect the quality and validity of the indicator measure-
ments, the comprehensiveness, adaptability and comparability of the indicators.
Moreover,
it questions whether some changes to the defined sustainability goals affect the choices of
indicators and should result in a new process of sustainability reporting.

The development should ensure that environmentally friendly transports, which provide good
mobility and access in the developed area, are promoted. Moreover, these modes should be safe,

accessible, especially by public transport and should be primarily on ground floors and refrain

them and ensure their protection. However, when transforming a historically significant area,

5.4.3.5
Energy, Waste and Climate
Carlsberg has a focus on becoming a CO2 neutral city district with regards to electricity and
heating of buildings, with the ability to withstand the effects of climate change. Roofs of new
buildings have been prepared with the capability of adding green roofs and/or solar cells to the
roofs surfaces. Moreover, green façades can be constructed to delay rainwater as well as the
ability to carry solar cells are made possible. Open spaces are laid with permeable coatings to
drain rainwater locally. Furthermore, green links and elements are added, and green courtyards
built in suitable areas. To lower CO2 emissions, new buildings should be low-energy buildings
and street lighting solutions with the lowest power consumption and lowest environmental
impact should be considered. Carlsberg will join the Copenhagen Climate Plan and connect
all new buildings to the district heating. Moreover, the area is connected to the district cooling
grid and Frederiksberg Forsyning provides the network.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
This chapter contains the discussion of the results found in chapter 6, covering the results and a
general discussion about the stakeholders, analysis methods and limitations of the study. Firstly,
the sustainability in the municipal goals and strategies are discussed, which covers aspects such
as the integration of the sustainability relating to literature etc. Thereafter, the Västerbro and
Carlsberg development strategies are discussed and the manifestation of the municipal goals and
strategies within them. Then the lessons learned from the thesis assessment of sustainability in
the development plans is covered. Next, the responsibilities and challenges in the sustainable
development and the process of incorporating sustainability into the development processes is
explained. Finally, limitations of the analysis and scope of the study are analyzed.
7.1
Sustainability in Municipal Goals and Strategies
7.1.1
Lund Municipal Goals Compared to Strategies
When reviewing the Lund municipality goals, it is clear from the analysis in section 6.1.1 that
they have a high emphasis on social sustainability (see figure 6.2(b)). Comparing this to the
analysis of the strategies in figure 6.5(b) both the goals and the strategies are emphasizing social
sustainability. However, when looking at the analysis of the goals, there is a higher emphasis
on economic sustainability compared to environmental sustainability. This is reversed when
looking at the analysis of the strategies. This is due to the fact that the municipal goals do
not limit the strategies defined, this means that any strategy may be set forth without the
objective of reaching a specific goal. The higher emphasis on the environment in the strategies
might be due to the high focus on the climate change in recent years. There is better general
understanding of our effect on the environment and the obvious solutions which are easily
implemented, e.g., adding green spaces, low carbon energy production, etc. The high emphasis
on social sustainability in both the goals and strategies is due to the desire to create a human
centered city with high quality of life which is easily implemented with simple, understandable
solutions such as adding more green spaces and cleaner energy production.
7.1.2
Copenhagen Goals Compared to Strategies
When reviewing the Copenhagen municipality goals, it is clear from the analysis in section
6.1.2 that just as in Lund, they have a high emphasis on social sustainability (see figure 6.7(b)).
Moreover, Copenhagen goals have a equal emphasis on integrating economic sustainability as
social sustainability. Comparing this to the analysis of the strategies in figure 6.10(b) both
the goals and the strategies have a higher emphasis on social sustainability. However, when
looking at the analysis of the goals, there is a much higher emphasis on economic sustainability
compared to environmental sustainability.
When looking at the strategies, the same trend
emerges as for the strategies in Lund. The economic sustainability inclusion is lowered and

7.1.3
Integration of Sustainability
Sustainability is well incorporated in the goals defined for the municipalities with around a third
of them integrating all the sustainability dimensions (see figure 6.2(a) and 6.7(a)). However,
the municipal strategies suggested to reach these goals incorporate sustainability to a much
lower degree (see figure 6.5(a) and 6.10(a)). Furthermore, when looking at the strategies for
specific development projects, such as Västerbro and Carlsberg, the incorporation is further
reduced (see figure 6.14(a) and 6.18(a)). This is not surprising as the strategies are measures
to reach these goals. The consequences of each goal can be wide ranged and therefore, one goal
can influence more than one dimension. The strategies are generally more specifically defined
and less open to interpretation. Due to their specific focus point, they are more inclined to
only incorporate one or two dimensions of sustainability. An example of this can be seen when
looking at goal 1.2 of Lund’s municipality from table 6.1, which referees to the densification of
the city. This goal promotes economic development limited by the natural capacity where social
equality, inclusion and access is promoted and also the ability to sustain biodiversity. Therefore,
it is a sustainable goal. However, one of the strategies aimed as a measure to achieve this goal,
strategy 1 from table C.2 in Appendix C.1, i.e., increased housing, only promotes social equality
and inclusions. Therefore, it is only a social sustainable development strategy.

7.4
Responsibilities and Challenges in Sustainable
Development
The municipal plans gather the municipality’s objectives and strategies which are used as the
basis for the development frameworks for a specific area.
These frameworks are the local
plans in Denmark, and the detailed development plans in Sweden. These are legally binding
documentations for the development of a specific area, and are used to ensure that the municipal
visions and objectives are actualized.
Urban developments are not only affected on a municipal level, but also a national and global
level. The municipal and development plans are derived based on national visions, goals and
guidelines.
Moreover, these national directives are influenced by international policies and
objectives, such as the UN’s Sustainability Development Goals (SDG’s).

The figure also shows the stakeholder relationship for the developments in this study. Municipalities

Municipalities generally do not follow a specific sustainability program. As other actors, such
as consultants, join the project, they can bring more detailed approaches to sustainability in

These actors are however limited by the objectives, visions and strategies of the municipality.
As municipalities often have restricted funds and land ownership, developers create an opportunity

ensure that the developers maintain the vision and goals set by the municipality.
This is not the only complication as projects of this scale involve many different actors from
various sectors. These are the stakeholders of the project, which interest and effects need to be
considered.

Mapping of Sustainability
The mapping of the municipalities’ and redevelopments’ strategies involved reviewing numerous
reports, plans and documentations and listing the development strategies found. Due to the
time constraints of this study the number of strategies defined had to be limited. Therefore, one
strategy may combine two or more within it. This might distort the results of the manifestation
of the municipal goals and strategies in the redevelopment plans.
Using as an example strategy number 3 from the Västerbro redevelopment strategies in table C.2
in Appendix C.1, as can be seen in figure 7.2. The strategy involves strengthening the bicycle
network, which is a livable strategy within the transportation sector. However, analyzing in
more detail of how the bicycle network is strengthened, five strategies can be defined, which
can be seen in figure 7.2. Notably, these subdivided strategies integrate primarily livability and
social sustainability, within the transportation sector. Undeniably, when combined some of the
dimensions are lost. In this case it is the social which is combined into livability. These would
if included into the Venn-diagrams, move the distribution a bit, however, even so the largest
weight is found on the livable for this example. Therefore, the overall picture would still be the
same, and still give in most cases the same outcome, as when combined.
Figure 7.2: The process of incorporating sustainability from global to local levels
To summarize, although combining these strategies results in some loss of information, these
can be considered minor details. As the strategies would chiefly incorporate the same output
on the total area – the strategies would not diverge considerably.

indicators.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Rapid urbanization and population growth in cities is cause for concern as to how cities’
developments can be planned in a sustainable manner. In recent years, many municipalities
have set the objective to expand in a more land-use efficient manner, within their city limits by
redeveloping industrial areas. However, the process of bringing sustainable aspects into urban
redevelopment involves coordination and refining of sustainability goals between municipality,
national and global goals for sustainable development.
Therefore, the aim of this study was to determine how municipalities integrate sustainability
into their development goals and strategies, and how development projects incorporate these
municipal goals and strategies in their redevelopment plans.
The integration of sustainability in municipality goals and their manifestation in the redevelopment
was done by mapping the municipality goals and strategies for urban development on a sustainability
scale, i.e., mapping how well the goals and strategies incorporate the three dimensions of
sustainability and how well the development strategy enforces the municipal goals and strategies.
Municipality goals have a good integration of sustainability and together incorporate a sustainable
development. However, this level of sustainability does not translate into the municipal strategies
nor into the redevelopment plans of the new city districts, as they incorporate sustainability
much less. Overall, the highest focus is to create a human centered city, i.e., a fair and livable
city where social sustainability is emphasized in 80 to 90% of the municipal goals and strategies.
The municipal plans provide a basis for detailed development plans and local plans for the
redevelopment of city districts and are therefore highly influential of the strategies incorporated
in them.
Moreover, the municipal plans also influence the integration of the sustainability
dimensions. However, they do not limit the integration of the other dimensions of sustainability
into the Västerbro and Carlsberg development plans, as environmental sustainability becomes
an increasingly higher focus area. However, it must be recognized that the different actors
involved in these projects affect the shape of the projects’ outcome.
Additionally, the study aimed to compare the sustainability performance of the two redevelopment
projects, Carlsberg and Västerbro, in order to investigate the perspective on the planning of
new urban districts. The sustainability of the development projects was determined by using
a modified, interactive framework based on Maclaren (1996) process for urban sustainability
reporting. A combination framework where goal-based indicators, which are categorized into
the municipal sectors and their domain identified. These were used to measure the developments’
performances.
A total of eight indicators and twenty-two sub-indicators were produced to
evaluate the performance of these development plans.
Based on the indicator evaluation, the Carlsberg development plans performed very well as
they scored high in almost all sectors and the Västerbro development plans fairly. This was
primarily due to the different stages the two development projects are at – Carlsberg has
started construction and Västerbro has yet to commence their detailed planning. Carlsberg
==PAGE:100==
82
8 Conclusion
has therefore managed to incorporate various sustainable solutions which Västerbro has the
opportunity to learn from and optimize their redevelopment plan with regards to sustainability.
A focus should be on maintaining their strategies for housing provisions, transportation, public
and green spaces throughout the detailed planning process as well as incorporating more green
infrastructure, local energy production and historical protections.
Additionally, historical
structures should be protected at an early stage of the planning process.
Moreover, the
architectural style should be identified to commence planning for how new and old buildings
should be integrated.
The main barriers in a large-scale redevelopment project is the complexity of it, as its various
actors and stakeholders have different interest in and effects on the project. Therefore, good
communication and coordination is required in order to reach high levels of satisfactory between
the different actors. The municipalities land ownerships and funds in redevelopment project
are limited. Therefore, the districts’ developers provide an opportunity to create a high-quality
city district. However, to ensure that the municipalities’ visions are implemented, the detailed
development plans and local plans are created.
Although sustainability has become an increasingly large part of urban development, primarily
derived from the global Sustainable Development Goals, it is a difficult term to define and
incorporate into city development planning. For years, sustainability has been incorporated
in municipal plans, which are an influential force on urban redevelopment plans. Nevertheless,
sustainability does not always translate from one into the other. The method of using sustainable
development indicators is a favorable tool, which provides a basis for evaluating the progress of
sustainability of development in the planning process. They can be used to define the strengths,
which should be maintained, and weaknesses, which should be improved, in the redevelopment
plans. Finally, even though redevelopment project’s stakeholders are often one of the main
barriers, they also provide immense value in the form of opportunities, finance and knowledge.

CHAPTER 9
Future Work
The study touches on a lot of different aspects, which was not able to analyze in depth.
The investigation into the business models of the different actors, involved in redevelopment
projects, was beyond the scope of this study. Therefore, it would have been interesting to get
more insight into the effects the actors have on these types of projects.
Additionally, there is a focus on local energy production in Lund municipality.
Moreover,
there is an increased interest for utilizing smart renewable technologies, such as PV cells on
buildings to achieve this. Then Västerbro and other new urban areas could act as a testbed,
for implementation of these new technologies. Therefore, it would be intriguing to study a
business case for incorporating smart renewable technologies within development project. Here
the focus is on determining how this could be done effectively, the feasibility of it, incentives
for developers and how it is received by users.
