Preface
This is a Master of Science thesis in Civil Engineering completed by Karoline Bråten at The Technical University
of Denmark, DTU. The thesis is a collaboration between the author Karoline Bråten, The Technical University
of Denmark and RISE, Research Institutes of Sweden. The leading supervisor of the project was Associate
professor Anne Simone Dederichs from the Technical University of Denmark, with external supervisor Raúl
Ochoterena at RISE. The thesis is credited 35 ECTS points and was carried out from 22. January 2018 to 22.
July 2018.
The thesis contains a literature study of dust explosions.
Moreover, a case study of previous dust explo-
sions was conducted. Furthermore, statistics of dust explosions in Norway and Sweden was collected and
analyzed. In total were 56 material properties and dust explosion sensitivity and explosibility tests completed
at RISE’s Research Institute of Sweden lab in Borås.

Abstract
Dust explosions occur every year, worldwide. Dust explosions may have devastating outcomes with deaths,
injuries, and property damages. A dust explosion can occur in every industry where dust is handled or stored.
Almost all combustible materials are susceptible to develop the combustible process of a dust explosion when
achieving a small enough particle size. Essential material properties of combustible dust are particle distribution
and the moisture content. How these properties eﬀects the ignition sensitivity and explosibility are investigated
in this thesis.
Based on a statistical analysis and case study, are the industries which are prone to dust explosions, con-
tacted and dust samples collected. 14 dust samples from various industries which handles or stores dust are
tested to ﬁnd the particle distribution, moisture content, ignition sensitivity, and explosibility.
In order to ﬁnd the particle distribution are all the samples sieved, and a dust sample of the materials are
dried to ﬁnd the moisture content. To verify that the dust samples could cause an explosive dust atmosphere
were all dust samples tested in the Modiﬁed Hartmann apparatus. To ﬁnd the ignition sensitivity were the
following tests conducted: minimum ignition energy, minimum ignition temperature, and minimum explosive
concentration. To ﬁnd the explosibility of the samples the maximum explosion pressure and maximum rate of
explosion pressure rise were found.
All the dust samples could cause an explosive atmosphere, except for one. However, by going beyond the
standard procedure would the sample which did not ignite, create an explosive dust atmosphere. In the ignition
sensitivity tests, it was found that combustible dust with a median particle distribution of small particles would
require a lower amount of dust to become explosive, compared to dust samples with larger median particle
distribution. However, the particle distribution did not aﬀect the minimum ignition energy or the minimum
ignition temperature.
The moisture content was higher for the sample with the highest minimum ignition energy. However, the
dust sample with the lowest ignition energy may have paint residuals, which can aﬀect the results. Moreover,
was the minimum ignition temperature not aﬀected by the moisture content, which coincides with literature.
There was also no eﬀects on the moisture content for the minimum explosive concentration.
The explosibility test showed that combustible dust with a particle distribution with smaller particles would have
a more severe explosion, compared to combustible dust with a larger particle distribution. The tests also showed
that the combustible dust samples with the highest moisture content also had the highest value of explosibility.
From the ignition sensitivity and explosibility test, it was found that the particle distribution only played a
role in minimum explosive concentration. Furthermore, it was found that the moisture content of combustible
dust slightly lowers the ignition sensitive. The explosibility of combustible dust is aﬀected by the number
of small particles. Combustible dust with larger amounts of smaller particles has a more severe explosion,
compared to combustible dust with larger particles. Moreover, it was found that the moisture content does not
aﬀect the explosibility of combustible dust.

Introduction
This chapter contains information and theory about dust explosions.
The characteristics of a combustible
material to be considered explosive is described. Moreover, is the diﬀerent ignitions sources and properties
which may aﬀect the explosibility of combustible dust described. Furthermore, previously dust explosions
described with cause and consequence of the explosion to emphasize the possible severity of a dust explosion.
Moreover, is a statistical analysis of dust explosions in Norway and Sweden described, with procedure and
result. The chapter also contains a description of previous work and the problem deﬁnition.
1.1
Dust Explosions
Occupational Safety & Health Administration deﬁnes combustible dust as followed: Combustible dust is deﬁned
as a solid material composed of distinct particles or pieces, regardless of size, shape, or chemical composition,
which presents a ﬁre or deﬂagration hazard when suspended in air or some other oxidizing medium over a range
of concentrations [1].
A dust explosion can occur in every industry where dust is handled or stored. Almost all combustible ma-
terials are susceptible to develop the combustible process of a dust explosion when achieving a small enough
particle size. Even a material, which does not burn in larger pieces can cause a dust explosion if the particles are
small enough, such as aluminum and iron. A traditional rule of thumb limiting particle size for a combustible
material also to be explosive is 0.500 mm. However, the actual value depends on the material reactivity and
particle shape [2].
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the combustion rate by increasing the surface.
Combustible solid materials will have increased burning violence and speed with the degree of sub-division
of the material. A simple and recognizable example is to ignite a large piece of wood. A log of wood burns
slowly and generates heat for an extended period. If the log is cut into smaller pieces, the combustion rate will
increase because of the increase in surface area which is in contact with air. The ignition of the wood has also
become easier. If the wood is divided into small particles with an order of 0.1 mm or less, and the particles are

suspended in a large volume of air that will give each particle the room to burn unhindered, the combustion rate
will accelerate and become very fast. In addition, the energy required to ignite the material would be minimal.
When such a cloud of dust is burning, it is a dust explosion. If the explosive combustion occurs in a limited
space, such as inside equipment or factories, the temperature will rise rapidly and simultaneously increasing the
pressure. The pressure may cause equipment or buildings to collapse [3].
Even though the hazard of dust explosions has been known for centuries, the knowledge is limited. Espe-
cially compared to the knowledge of gas explosions. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence between dust explosions and gas
explosions is that a gas explosion often occurs when there is a leak into the air, and an explosive atmosphere is
generated. A dust explosion mostly occurs in a piece of equipment, such as transportation bands, air ducts or a
funnel.
The pressure from the primary explosion can be so signiﬁcant that a vessel ruptures and cause a secondary
explosion. Primary explosions are the explosions initiated by an ignition source. The primary explosion can
cause settled dust to disperse, forming a new cloud which may ignite by the heat released by the primary
explosion. The secondary explosion may initiate a new explosion, and the procedure may go around in circles as
illustrated in Figure 1.2. Even though it is essential to limit the primary explosion, it is perhaps more important
to limit the possibility of the primary explosion to ignite a series of other explosions.

Three factors have to be present simultaneously for a ﬁre to be able to burn: Fuel, ignition source, and oxidant. In
ﬁre safety, this is known as the "Fire Triangle", and is commonly used to avoid ﬁres in industries. To understand
dust explosions and how they occur the "Dust Explosion Pentagon" may be used. The pentagon contains the
same sides as the ﬁre triangle, in addition to conﬁnement and dispersion. These elements are created when a
fuel, in this case, the combustible dust, creates a dust cloud within a closed area [4]. The closed area may be
equipment or a warehouse. However, it is possible to have an explosion in the open air if the reaction is so fast
that the pressure builds up in the dust cloud is faster than it can be released at the edge of the cloud [5]. Much
like with the ﬁre triangle, removing one of the elements in the pentagon will prevent a dust explosion from
occurring. Even though it is unlikely to eliminate the dust explosion hazard entirely, there are means to reduce
the risk.
Figure 1.3: Dust explosion pentagon and ﬁre triangle
Oxidant
The most common oxidant is oxygen [6]. Oxygen is present essentially everywhere and is the hardest element
to remove from the dust explosion pentagon.
Ignition Source
The ignition source is the element which causes the other elements to combust and creates a dust explosion [4].
The ignition source may be as small as an electrostatic spark. This element can be removed to an extent by
removing open ﬂames and any potential sparks to be created. It is also important to prevent any machinery from
generating heat. However, it is virtually impossible to remove all potential ignition sources, since there are so
may sources. More information about the ignition sources is in section 1.1.1.
Fuel
For a dust explosion, the fuel is the combustible dust. It is virtually impossible to remove the formation of dust.
However, the amount of dust can be controlled with good housekeeping. Removing the combustible dust is one
of the most eﬀective ways to prevent a dust explosion [4].

Dispersion
Dispersion of dust may occur during daily working tasks, such as sweeping, emptying a bag of combustible
material or cleaning. Dispersion may also occur after a primary explosion, as mention previously. As with the
element fuel, dispersion may be prevented by good housekeeping. Cleaning of accumulated dust will prevent it
from becoming dispersed.
Conﬁnement
The conﬁnement can, as mentioned, be as small as a cavity in equipment or as large as a warehouse. If the
combustible material ignites, the conﬁnement will cause a high pressure to build up, and the equipment may
rupture or cause a wall to collapse.

1.1.1
Ignition
As mentioned previously, an ignition source is necessary for a dust explosion to develop. The ignition sources
vary in size from an electrostatic spark, to heat from a primary explosion. The diﬀerent ignition sources are
divided into eight groups, which all may occur within an industrial plant. [6]
• Flames and direct heat
• Self-heating
• Smoldering particles
• Hot surface
• Electrical sparks
• Friction sparks
• Impact sparks
• Static electricity
According to Yuana, Khakzada, Khana, and Amyotte’s paper "Dust explosions: A threat to the process industry"
the most common ignition source is ﬂame and direct heat, followed by impact spark [6]. The deviation between
each category is seen in Figure 1.4.
Figure 1.4: Ignition sources of dust explosions [6]
As intuitively expected, combustible dust dispersed in an atmosphere containing more oxygen than "normal"
air is more ignition sensitive, and the explosive violence increases. Because of the relationship between oxygen
and ignition, the cutting burner ﬂame are particularly dangerous because it applies excess oxygen to the working
zone. It is therefore essential to clean and remove all dust when doing maintenance in areas where there is
generated dust. A burning wood match develops approximately 100 J of thermal energy per second, which is
more than suﬃcient to initiate an explosion in the most combustible dust clouds [3].

1.1.2
Material Properties
The explosive combustion of a dust cloud cannot take place unless there is a certain amount of dust, i.e., the
mass of dust per unit volume of a dust cloud. The concentration has to be between the lower explosive limit
and the upper explosive limit. The upper explosive limit is not used that much when considering dust since the
lower limit is an important parameter when considering the explosibility and sensitivity of combustible dust.
Furthermore, moisture content, turbulence, and particle size are key factors of a combustible dust cloud.
Moisture
Increasing moisture in the dust reduces both the ignition sensitivity and the explosive violence of the dust
cloud [3]. Moreover, a materials dispersibility is also related to its moisture content [7].
Yuan, Wei, Huang, Du, Liu, and Zhu’s paper "Experimental investigation on the role of moisture in coal
dust explosion" [8] states that in dust samples where the moisture concentration is low, the moisture would
mainly consume the heat from the explosion by temperature rise and phase change. In the dust samples with
higher moisture content the moisture, besides consuming the heat, would also cause agglomeration of dust
particles. The agglomeration, which is caused by strong interparticle cohesion forces [7], would increase the
particle size of the dust and the explosion severity will reduce. However, the agglomeration phenomenon does
not occur in coarser dust samples. When the dust sample is coarse, the moisture will mainly work as a heat sink
and consume the heat of the dust explosion by temperature rise and phase change.
Turbulence
There are two types of turbulence. The ﬁrst is generated by the industrial process in which a dust cloud is
formed. This may occur by a mixer, air jet mill or buckets in a bucket elevator [3]. This kind of turbulence is also
called intentional turbulence [7]. The explosion itself generates the second kind of turbulence. The explosion
causes an expansion-induced ﬂow of an unburned dust cloud ahead of the propagating ﬂame [3]. This kind of
turbulence is also called unintentional turbulence [7].
The turbulence generated by obstacles in the ﬂame path results in enhanced burning velocities, which in
turn lead to a rapid ﬂame acceleration in premixed gases. This result is probably due to the increase in turbulent
burning velocity, which has been shown to play a major role in secondary dust explosions [9].
Particle size
The hazard of dust explosions lies at the ﬁner dust particles, because of their large surface area per mass,
resulting in a fast reaction. A ﬁner dust particle is likely to react faster than a larger particle size of the same
dust material. The particle shape and porosity can also aﬀect the particle surface, thus aﬀecting the reaction
rate. Furthermore, the ﬁner particles are easier to disperse and will remain airborne longer [10].
If the dust particles are in the nm-range, it is predicted that the sensitivity and explosion rate would be ex-
treme. However, this is not the case as there are two physical processes that tend to limit the dust explosion
hazard of nm particles. The ﬁrs are the limiting dispersibility of the nm dust. The second is the high coagulation
rate of the ﬁne particles. One of the outstanding characteristics of dust clouds is continuous and spontaneous
coagulation. Particles in a dust cloud will independent of its material, unite or stick together if they get in

1.2.1
Flour Dust Explosion - Italy 1785
The ﬁrst documented dust explosion was a ﬂour dust explosion in Turin, Italy in 1785. The description of the
accident is from Eckhoﬀ’s book, however, the description has details from Count Morozzo’s accounts of the
explosion [3].
An explosion occurred in the evening of 14th of December in 1785 in Turin in Italy.
The explosion oc-
curred in a bakery, and the pressure from the explosion blew out the window frames and windows from the
building. The sound of the explosion was heard from a considerable distance. The explosion occurred in the
ﬂour-warehouse where a small boy was hired to stir the ﬂour. The boy got burning injuries. In addition, another
boy broke his leg when jumping down a scaﬀold when he thought the building was on ﬁre.
The ﬁrst explosion occurred when the boy stirring the ﬂour was digging in the ﬂour to move it from one
chamber to another. A sudden fall of ﬂour from one of the chambers generated a cloud and immediately caught
ﬁre. The ignition source was a lamp hanging from the wall. The ﬂames from the ﬁre moved down one of the
chambers and down a staircase where it caused a secondary explosion in the bakery shop.
The baker stated that the ﬂour was very dry, as there had been no rain for the past ﬁve or six months. At
the time of the explosion, there were approximately 300 sacks of ﬂour in the warehouse.
1.2.2
Cereal Silo - France 1997
All information about the accident is taken from the French Ministry of Environment Report No. 11657 [11].
A massive dust explosion occurred in Blaye in August 1997, in a cereal silo. The company had a capac-
ity of 130 000 tons of cereal in 44 circular reinforced concrete silos. Prior to the explosion, a dumper truck was
unloading corn into one of the delivery pits.
The ﬁrst explosion occurred at the northern end of the plant. The ﬂames propagating from the ﬁrst explo-
sion penetrated the storage unit, which contained airborne dust from the loading operations. The ﬂames ignited
the dust, and an explosion occurred. The ﬁrst explosion occurred either in the dust removal circuit or within a
structural volume of the silo itself. The explosion sent large projectiles up to a distance of 100 meters from the
silo. There were only 16 of 44 silos that were still, for the most part, intact after the accident.

1.2.3
Phenolic Resin Dust Explosion - USA 2003
All the information is taken from U.S. Chemical and Hazard Investigation Board, Investigation Report, Com-
bustible Dust Fire and Explosions, February 2005 [12].
An explosion occurred at CTA Acoustics, Inc. facility in Kentucky, USA in Fabruary 2003. The accident
occurred after the daily cleaning of a production line. During the cleaning, one of the workers had discovered
that one of the transitions leading to baghouse was plugged. To unplug it, compressed air was used and the
material had landed in the production area. The compressed air caused a cloud of combustible dust around the
production line.
The baghouse was turned back on after the cleaning, and moments after, ﬂames were observed from the
inlet ducks, into and out of the baghouse. The ﬂames generated an explosion and witnesses observed ﬁreballs
emerging from one of the ovens in the production area. The pressure wave from the explosion knocked down a
ﬁrewall, and several employees were injured.
The emerging ﬁreballs ignited dispersed combustible dust, which caused a secondary explosion. The sec-
ondary explosion knocked down a second ﬁrewall. In addition, blowing ceiling panels down inside the room.
The accident caused 37 injuries and seven deaths.
1.2.4
Sugar Dust Explosion - USA 2008
All information about the sugar dust explosion in Georgia 2008 is taken from the U.S. Chemical Safety and
Hazard Investigation Board, Investigation Report, Sugar Dust Explosion and Fire, September 2009 [13].
The Imperial Sugar Company located in Georgia, USA, had a dust explosion in February 2008. The ini-
tial explosion occurred in an enclosed steel conveyor belt under a granulated sugar storage silo. The enclosure
was not equipped with any dust removal system, and there were no explosion vents.
The belt was recently enclosed by panels to protect the granulated sugar. The encasement limited the vol-
ume at which the dust could be suspended, and the minimum explosive concentration was quickly reached.
During the investigation, several potential ignition sources were found within the conﬁnement. However, it was
not possible to determine the ignition source of the initial explosion because of the severe damages caused by
the explosion.

1.2.5
Food Powder Explosion - Norway 2014
The 17th of March in 2014, there was a dust explosion in Orkla’s production area in Arna Norway. The
explosion occurred when one of the operators was emptying a big-bag of cornﬂour into a 12 m3 steel silo. One
big-bag of cornﬂour had already been emptied into the silo. The unloading of the powder caused the ﬁne dust
to accumulate at the opening of the silo.
The cause of the explosion was an electrostatic discharge from the operator who was emptying the bag.
The explosion went from the funnel above the silo and into the silo, where it got pushed out through the opening
again where the big-bag and the operator were located. During the loading of the silo, the silo may become
electrostatic charged, and therefore should the silo be grounded. The silo where the explosion occurred were
grounded. However, it was noted in an internal report that the silo was probably not grounded suﬃciently. It
was also noted that there were beautiful weather and a high-pressure outside, which may aﬀect the electrostatic
charge [14]. As a result of the explosion, the cornﬂour was tested at Gexcon AS. A similar test had been
conducted in 2004. The new test showed that the cornﬂour was dryer than the previous cornﬂour [15].
The explosion burned the operator, who was transported to the hospital after the accident. A second em-
ployer got minor injuries caused by the explosion.
1.2.6
Colored Powder Explosion – Taiwan 2015
A dust explosion occurred during a color festival in Taiwan in June 2015. The event in Taiwan was a “Color
Play Asia”, inspired by the Hindu religious festival Holi, also called “festival of colors”. The powder used was
made of cornstarch added color.
The explosion occurred after the colored powder was suspended from the stage onto the crowd, located at
a dried pool. In videos from the explosion is it seen that there is allot of dust suspended in the air. In addition to
the suspension from the stage, was the participants’ given individual bags of the colored powder. The ignition
source was believed to be the lights on the stage, which generates heat. The ignition caused a series of explo-
sions, and witnesses describe a ﬁreball of ﬂames moving through the crowd. The initial explosion dispersed
dust laying in the pool area. The accident injured approximately 500 people and killed 15 people [16] [17].

Fish Feed Explosion - Norway 2017
A dust explosion occurred on ﬁsh feed producer located in Florø, Norway, 7th of March 2017. The explosion
occurred in a silo and caused a ﬁre in an adjacent silo. There were no personal injuries, only material damages.
The pressure from the explosion blew out the roof of the silo, which was spot welded, to reduce some of the

The silo where the explosion occurred contained guar meal. Prior to the explosion, the guar meal had been

The cause of the ﬁre was most likely smoldering material from a bucket elevator with a misaligned belt. The

no signs of a signiﬁcant secondary dust explosion. The investigation of the explosion was carried out by

1.2.8
Conclusion
The described dust explosions are just a small part of the dust explosion that has occurred over the last 25
years. A common denominator of the selected dust explosions is silos and transportation belts. Furthermore,
secondary explosions occur in several of the cases, and in all the cases where the dust in motion before the
explosion occurred.
In the dust explosion in Norway in 2014, it was found that the combustible material involved were dryer
compared to previous tests. The environment of materials are continuously changing, and the industries which
handle or stores dust should conduct regular tests of the dust they process.
The industries which have gone through a dust explosion, are more aware of the hazard of handling and
storing dust. However, generally, the knowledge of dust explosion hazard is not well known.

1.3
Previous work
An essential book regarding dust explosions is "Dust Explosions in the Process Industries", by Rolf K. Eckhoﬀ.
The book was fundamental at the beginning of the thesis to achieve knowledge and understanding of dust explo-
sions. The book also introduces dust explosions that have occurred, with cause and damages of the explosion.
Furthermore discusses the book prevention, design, and testing methods [3].
Eckhoﬀhas written several papers regarding dust explosions. Among these is the investigation of the dust
explosion risk increasing when moving from µm-particle dust to nm-particle. It would be expected that dust
with primary dust particles down to nm-size, would have extreme ignition sensitivity. However, Eckhoﬀfound
that it is extremely diﬃcult to achieve a dust cloud dispersion consisting of nm-particles in normal operations
in process industries because of the strong interparticle cohesion forces [7].
"Dust explosions: A threat to the process industry" by Yuan, Khakzad, Kahn, and Amyotte is a paper discussing
and illustrating statistical features of worldwide dust explosions. According to the paper, dust explosions are
closely related to industrial activities, and the number of dust explosions is decreasing with time. Moreover, the
lack of reporting dust explosions are mentioned, and the investigation and knowledge about dust explosions are
speciﬁc to each countries industrial structure [22].
"Dust explosions - Cases, causes, consequences, and control" by Tasneem Abbasi and S.A. Abbasi discuss
several aspects of dust explosions. The paper illustrates previous accidents and the damages they have caused.
Moreover, the diﬀerent methods of determining the explosion sensitivity and violence are discussed, and how
the combustible materials properties aﬀect the explosibility of the material. Furthermore, diﬀerent methods of
prevention are reviewed [6].
Even though there have been several ignition sensitivity tests conducted, there are few databases where the
results are noted. The Institute for Occupational Safety and Health of the German Social Accident Insurance
has created a database with several materials and their explosibility category and minimum ignition energy.
These results are useful to use as references. Link to the database: http://staubex.ifa.dguv.de/?lang=e

As expected, the number of dust explosions varies from year to year, with no apparent pattern of a reduction
or increase in the number of explosions each year. However, from conversations with researchers working with
dust explosions, it is apparent that there are dust explosions which do not appear in the research. As Lees
mentions [20], there is a gap between the reported and the actual numbers of accidents. The gap is increasing,
implying that the accident with less damage is neglected by the agencies, as opposed to the accidents with
severe damages. Another contributor to the gap are the accidents that cause minor damages and/or are not
reported to any agency. Because of the lacking knowledge about dust explosions, there is also a chance that
a minor explosion occurred, however, the operator might not have known that there was, in fact, a dust explosion.
Furthermore, all investigation reports are highly reliant on the investigators’ opinion and experience. After
a ﬁre or explosion, the accident scenes are usually severely damaged and leads to the cause of the ﬁre may be
lost. A dust explosion may, therefore, be categorized as something else in a report.

1.5
Problem Deﬁnition
1.5
Problem Deﬁnition
The majority of industrial plants have dust processing equipment which is susceptible to dust explosions. Al-
most all materials are inclined to develop the combustible process of a dust explosion if the particles are small
enough. Sugar, wood, ﬂour, feed, paper, and grain are just some of the materials which are susceptible to
explode when divided into small particles. There is an occupational hazard within the industries which handle
or store these materials, and the knowledge is limited. There are several dust explosions worldwide each year,
causing extensive property damages, injuries, and deaths.
The explosibility of combustible material is aﬀected by its material properties. Particle size and moisture
content are some of the essential features that will aﬀect the explosibility of combustible materials. How
these factors aﬀect ignition sensitivity and explosibility are investigated in this thesis. Within the term ignition
sensitivity, is the Minimum Ignition Energy (MIE), Minimum Ignition Temperature (MIT), and Minimum
Explosive Concentration (MEC). The lower these numbers are, the more sensitive is the studied samples. To
investigate the explosibility are the Maximum explosion pressure (pmax), Maximum rate of explosion pressure
rise ((dp/dt)max), and the dust explosion class (Kst) investigated. The higher these values are, the more severe is
the explosion. The tests are carried out on biomass collected at relevant industries found by statistical analysis,
and case study. The collected samples are tested as received to investigate the actual conditions at the industrial
sites.

The industries contacted about providing dust samples are build upon the statistics of dust explosions in Sweden,
and the knowledge of materials involved in previous dust explosion accidents. The statistical analysis showed
that the wood industry, food industry, and power/heating plant are the industries which are prone to a dust
explosion. These industries were contacted and asked if either the author or the industries could collect dust
samples at their production site. From the case study of dust explosions, it would be interesting to investigate a
similar colored corn-starch dust as from the dust explosion in Taiwan.
The objective of sampling is to gain knowledge of the characteristics of the whole.
The quality of the
measurement depends on how representative the sample is, compared to the bulk from which it is drawn. A
small sample from a large bulk of the material, will not be presentative of the actual material conditions [24].
2.2
Particle Distribution
A sieve analysis is one of the oldest and most used methods to determine the characteristic particle size dis-
tribution of materials. The process separates the ﬁne particles from the more coarse particles by passing the
material through diﬀerent sieves of diﬀerent mesh size. Sieving analysis can either be dry or wet. The chosen
analysis was dry, as the dust was going to be reused to determine ignition sensitivity and explosibility.
The dust is vibrated through a series of decreasing sieving meshes using a combination of horizontal, ver-
tical and rotational motion. The ﬁner particles will eventually pass through the ﬁner mesh opening. However,
to make sure that all the particles have passed through the smallest possible mesh size, it is essential to sieve the
sample a certain amount of time.
All samples are sieved through the same number of sieves, and with the same amplitude, time and inter-
val. The same settings were chosen for all the samples in order to have consistency throughout all the samples.
The sieves used had a mesh of 0.500 mm, 0.250 mm, 0.125 mm and 0.075 mm. The amplitude used was 200
mm/”g” over 10 minutes with intervals of 10 seconds.

The procedure with inductance simulates a longer duration dis-

and control equipment. Without inductance has a shorter spark duration and simulates a discharge from a pure

The method to ﬁnd the minimum ignition time is an iterative process, where the energy is set to a level
where an ignition most likely will occur. The energy is lowered to a level where the dust concentration does

concentration ignites, the ﬁrst is step repeated. The last step is to lower the turbulence by delaying the ignition
time, with the found energy and dust concentration [28].

dispersed, but the spark should not try to ignite as the dust is dispersed.
Between each test, the glass cylinder and the cup in which the sample is placed are cleaned to avoid con-
tamination between each test.

MEC - Minimum Explosive Concentration
The minimum explosive concentration also called the lower explosion limit, determines the minimum concen-
tration at which combustible dust can explode. The two chemical ignition sources each have an energy of 1 kJ.
The highest concentration of dust which does not ignite in three consecutive tests is the lower explosion limit.
An explosion takes place if pex ≥0.5 bar.
Between each test, the vessel is cleaned. For dust concentrations, lower than 60 g/m3, the vessel is cleaned with
a single "blank shot" to burn out the residuals in the vessel. The vessel is then vacuumed and wiped down. The
cleaning between each test is to avoid contaminations.
The combustible dust can only be ignited when within its explosive range deﬁned by the lower explosive
limit, also deﬁned as minimum explosive concentration and upper explosive limit. In general, the distribution
are: [34]
• 20-60 g/m3: Lowest concentration
• 2-6 kg/m3: Maximum concentration

3.3.1
Errors
Only 50-100 grams of each sample is required to ﬁnd the particle distribution. However, some of the explosi-
bility tests require approximately 1 kg to complete the test series. To achieve the required dust concentration for
all the tests, additional sieving was necessary. The second sieving was done by hand to sieve more eﬃciently
and on site. The same sieves were used, and the particles above 0.500 mm were removed. Since the procedures
are not identical, are the particle distribution of the dust tested not identical to the particle distribution shown in
Figure 3.2, 3.3, 3.4, and 3.5. However, if the sample initially sieved is representative of the rest of the dust, the
diﬀerence should not be signiﬁcant.
The sieving process does not account for the particle shape. Furthermore, eﬀects the shape of the opening
and the particle the probability of passage. The ﬁbrous particles only permit passage of the sieve mesh in one
direction. Moreover, can blockage of the mesh opening occur by particles of the same or similar diameter as the
mesh. This reduces the available openings for the remaining particles to pass through the sieve. Therefore, can
blockage result in particles that are smaller than the mesh size being trapped and aﬀect the mass distribution of
the sample [35]. This behavior was observed with the samples with ﬁbrous particles.
During the sieving process, it was clear that the process of pre-weighing the sieve and then re-weighing
after the vibrations would lead to errors in the results. Some of the dust would stick underneath the sieve, and
the noted weight of the sieve be incorrect, as the dust which had passed through the sieve would, therefore, be
noted on the wrong mesh size. An alternative method by sieving is to collect the dust in each sieve and weigh
each sample which has passed through each sieve. The process is a more time-consuming, and the sieving-loss
would be more signiﬁcant. It was decided not to redo the sieving, because of the limited time to complete the
ignition sensitivity and explosibility tests.

MIE - Minimum Ignition Energy
3.6
MIE - Minimum Ignition Energy
The minimum ignition energy is one of the factors which indicates the ignition sensitivity of the dust samples.
To ﬁnd the minimum ignition energy is a time-consuming process, and each sample takes approximately one
day to test. Because of limited time to complete the sensitivity and explosibility tests of the dust samples, only
four samples were tested to ﬁnd the minimum ignition energy. Table 3.8 shows the results of the testing. The
dust samples chosen have a variety in moisture content, from 3.5 % in the cocoa powder and 10.8 % in the
corn-starch and vegetable fat. The median particle distribution is also varied between the samples.
Table 3.8: Minimum Ignition Energy results
To do a complete minimum ignition energy test of combustible materials, should both with and without induc-
tance be tested. However, due to limited time, one of the two was chosen. Without inductance is in some cases
considered more conservative. To achieve a variety of results were four samples tested, and two of the samples
were tested with inductance.
Throughout the testing, it was clear that there could be diﬃculties to complete a test for some combustible
materials. Elaboration of the errors and complications that occurred is discussed in section 3.6.1. Because of
the errors, cocoa powder and grinded painted plates were the only two dust samples which were completed.
Grinded painted plates have lower minimum ignition energy than the cocoa powder, even though the grinded
painted plates were tested with inductance. The low value may be aﬀected by the paint residuals which the
dust may contain. Grinded painted plates has a low moisture content and over 50 % distribution of particles
less than 0.075 mm. The cocoa powder has a slightly higher moisture content and has over 60 % distribution
of particles less than 0.075 mm. However, the minimum ignition energy is less for the cocoa powder than for
the grinded painted plates. This indicates that a higher amount of smaller particles will increase the minimum
ignition energy. The moisture content is lower for grinded painted plates. The results suggest that less moisture
in combustible dust can reduce the minimum ignition energy. However, it might be trivial to compare the two
results because the materials are independent of each other.
The procedure document used during the testing is found in the Appendix section 6.5.

a process equipment or in a working area during processes that releases dust or other processes where dust is
dispersed.

3.11
Additional Discussion
As previously stated, in a dust sample with high moisture content and small particles will the particle coagulate
and form larger particles when dispersed and ignited. The larger particles would then reduce the explosion
severity. This phenomenon is present during all the ignition sensitivity tests and explosibility tests. Especially
for the dust samples with a larger amount of smaller particles and higher moisture content; by-product of grain,
active carbon, and ventilation box.
Even though coagulation is one of the outstanding characteristics of dust clouds, will the coagulations oc-
cur at an early stage of tests. The ignition occurs within 20-60 ms after the beginning of the dispersion process.
This means that the ignition occurs while the coagulation of the particles in the cloud is at an early stage.
However, if the ignition delay time is signiﬁcantly longer in an accidental dust explosion scenario, may the data
from the laboratory test overestimate the ignition sensitivity and explosion severity of a real industrial cloud [7].

CONCLUSION
4
Conclusion
The risk of a dust explosion is present in all industries handling or storing combustible dust. The statistical
analysis carried out found that the industries at which is prone to dust explosions in Sweden are the wood and
power/heating plant industries. Moreover, it was found that the food industry, worldwide was involved in over
one-fourth of the dust explosions in over a 100 cases. Moreover, the analysis showed that more dust explosions
are occurring during the winter months because of the low moisture in the air.
Because of the limited schedule to complete the ignition sensitivity and explosibility test, not all 14 dust
samples were tested in all the ignition sensitivity and explosibility tests. The ignition sensitivity test implied
that combustible biomass material dust with a particle distribution of smaller particles would require a smaller
amount of dust to become explosive, i.e., the minimum explosive concentration. However, a particle distribu-
tion of smaller particles would not aﬀect the minimum ignition temperature, and the minimum ignition energy
would become higher. Making the dust less sensitive. Regarding the minimum ignition energy, where only
two samples tested, in which one of the dust samples may have paint residual which can aﬀects the results.
Moreover, only two test is too limited to give a reliable conclusion.
The sensitivity test demonstrated that lower moisture content of the combustible dust samples would reduce the
minimum ignition energy. However, one of the two dust samples may contain paint residuals which can aﬀect
the results. The moisture content did not aﬀect the minimum explosive concentration or the minimum ignition
temperature. The moisture did not make the dust more or less sensitive.
The explosibility test implied that a combustible dust sample with a particle distribution consisting of small
particles would have a higher maximum explosion pressure and a higher maximum rate of explosion pressure
rise. The explosibility test also showed that higher moisture content of the combustible dust sample would give
higher values of both maximum explosion pressure and maximum rate of explosion pressure rise.
The ﬁndings contradict some of the statements in some of the available literature of dust explosion.
Ac-
cording to Eckhoﬀin "Dust explosions - In the Process Industries" [3], increased moisture will reduce the
ignition sensitivity and explosibility of a dust cloud. However, according to the ﬁndings, the moisture content
is irrelevant for the minimum explosive concentration and the minimum ignition temperature. However, it does
lower the sensitivity regarding minimum ignition energy. For the explosibility were the ﬁndings utterly diﬀerent
from Eckhoﬀ’s statement. The test implied that an increased moisture content would increase the violence of
the explosion. However, it is essential to mention that the conclusion is only based on two samples, which is
too low to support the statement.
Furthermore, the current procedure and apparatus to determine if a combustible dust sample can cause an
explosive dust atmosphere and to determine minimum ignition temperature may give inaccurate results. The
current Godbert-Greenwald apparatus has ﬂaws and weaknesses which lowers the dust concentration. The error
may in some cases label a dust as not to cause an explosive dust atmosphere, even though it is explosive. This
incorrect conclusion would be given to the color run dust, had the procedure not been performed beyond the
standards procedure and apparatus.
