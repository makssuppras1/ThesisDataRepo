Summary
The increasing production of organic waste produced all over the world, can cause
severe environmental issues. On the other hand, the search of low cost raw material
for production of high added value products can solve the problem above.
The
optimization of municipal organic waste fermentation was investigated and Forward
Osmosis (FO) as a downstream process was proposed.
The project included fermentation batch tests and bioaugmentation with two dif-
ferent bacteria strains,Lactobacillus delbrueckii DSM 20074 andPediococcus acidi-
lactici NCIMB and under diﬀerent operational conditions. The addition of each
bacterial strain was selected to examine the increase of lactic acid (LA) production
and compare their performance to the fermentation of the substrate without inocu-
lation. Fermentation of food waste with no inoculum addition, L. delbruekii and P.
acidilactici inoculation achieved maximum titers and yields of 14.61±0.02) g/L and
0.55 ± 0.00 g/g-sugars, 14.61 ± 0.02)g/L and 0.59 ± 0.01 g/g-sugars, 19.2 ± 0.42 g/L
and 0.9 ± 0.01 g/g-sugars, respectively. P. acidilactici inoculation seems to be the
most promising bacterial strain, provided the highest titer and maximum yield un-
der the same operational conditions. A statistical analysis, based on Box-Behnken
model analysis, applied to optimize the system operation. The most favorable results
found, were pH 8, substrate concentration of 120 gTS/kg and optimum tempera-
ture at 32.4◦C, and P. acidilactici strain used as inoculum. The validation of the
predicted optimum conditions performed in up-scaled batch tests, using 3-L biore-
actors. Titer and maximum yield achieved were 31.9 ± 0.28 g/L and 0.78 ± 0.00
g/g-sugars, respectively.
Moreover, batch tests were conducted under optimum conditions, P. acidilactici
inoculation and thermal pretreated substrate. The purpose was to assess the per-
formance of the inoculum during fermentation and its contribution to by-products
formation. The maximum yield achieved of autoclaved and pasteurized substrate
was signiﬁcantly low (0.32±0.02 and 0.35±0.01 g/g-sugars, respectively) compared

Introduction
1.1
Background
The industrial and economic evolution the last decades has led to resource depletion
and an excessive production of waste worldwide. Thus, a lot of research has been
focused on the utilization of waste as resources. One outstanding example is the bio-
waste that are generated and collected separately of garden waste and mixed kitchen
household waste in Europe. This stream of organic waste has a high energetic and
nutritional potential (Probst et al., 2013). It can be used for biogas production,
compost production, animal feed and biotechnological valorization for added value
chemicals production such as succinic acid and lactic acid (Vandermeersch et al.,
2014; Dreschke et al., 2015; Pleissner et al., 2016).
The most common way to utilize organic waste is anaerobic digestion, as it is
a suitable substrate to produce biogas. However, there are a lot of challenges in
anaerobic digestion when food waste is the only substrate, according to Gu et al.
(2018). The food waste is characterized from high solid contend, which may lead to
overloading and excess acidiﬁcation. As a result, the pH drops (under 6.8) and the
methanogenic archae activity is inhibited, leading to anaerobic digestion and biogas
production impediment. An alternative solution could be the separation of anaerobic
treatment in two stages, the acidogenic stage and the acetogenic and methanogenic
stage, and targeting in more than one ﬁnal products (Kim et al., 2016; Tang et al.,
2017).
The intermediate products of anaerobic digestion after the ﬁrst-stage fermenter
are carboxylic acids that can be used as chemical platforms to produce high added
value products. One of these carboxylic acids is the lactic acid (LA) (Pleissner et
al., 2016; Li et al., 2018). It is a product with a high interest and wide application
in food, pharmaceutical, cosmetics, textile and leather industry, and speciﬁcally in
the production of biodegradable polylactic acid (PLA) plastic and polyhydroxyalka-

recovery from the fermentation broth produced there is still an amount of biomass
untreated. Based on research from to Dreschke et al. (2015) and Kim et al. (2016),
the fermented substrate after LA extraction had a high biomethane potential, giving
more space for improvement and utilization of the remained biomass. A techno-
economic analysis from Bastidas-Oyanedel Schmidt (2018), revealed that combined
fermentation for LA production and anaerobic digestion of the residue biomass for
biogas formation create an extra proﬁt of 75 USD/t−VS of the food waste valoriza-
tion. This is due to high diﬀerence between the market prices of LA and methane
(biogas), which are 1000-1600 USD/t and 400 USD/t, respectively.
Nowadays, the majority of the LA is produced by microbial fermentation of corn-
starch derived sugars in commercial sector. Many industries focus on the decrease of
cost of raw materials and replace the pure sugars and food crops substrate used so
far with agricultural residues and food industry by-products (Singhvi et al., 2018).
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are the group of bacteria that are applied in industry
for LA production. Food waste is characterized by a highly organic content which is
easily degradable and has a high nutritional potential (Probst et al., 2013). Studies
have shown that LAB are present in fresh food waste, representing the 90% of its
microbial community (Probst et al., 2013; Partanen et al., 2010). In addition, LA
concentration in fresh food waste was measured at 15.9 gLA/L (Probst et al., 2013),
rendering bio-waste a suitable substrate for LA production.
Important parameters that can aﬀect LA fermentation by LAB is pH decrease,
temperature, bioaugmentation, nutrients content and sugar availability of the sub-
strate (Wang et al., 2015). The LA accumulation in fermentation broth reduces the
pH under 4 and leads to microbial growth inhibition, as optimum pH during fermen-
tation ranged from 5 to 7. Moreover, LAB can grow at temperatures from 5◦C to
45◦C, though low fermentation temperatures required by most of the LAB strains
can lead to contamination. The nutritional requirements of LAB are complex, as
apart from carbon sources (hexoses and pentoses) they also require nitrogen sources,

2015). In addition, biogmentation with metabolic engineered strains contributes to
reduce of lag phase and enhancement of targeted LA production.
An other important factor that contributes at a high degree on the production
cost and quality of LA is the downstream processing. The recovery and puriﬁcation
process that traditionally is used leads to high amount of chemicals consumed and
by-products formation. The chemicals used for the recovery stage account for up to
50% of the production cost, without including the management cost of by-products
generated (Jambunathan & Zhang, 2016).
Alternative recovery and puriﬁcation
technologies have also been studied either for single-unit operation or combined
units (Phanthumchinda et al.,2018), avoiding insoluble salts formation and being
more cost and energy eﬃcient.
In the present study, a membrane technology as a single-unit operation for the
recovery of the LA from the fermentation broth is recommended. The application
of forward osmosis (FO) as a ﬁrst step to up-concentrate the LA is evaluated. FO
is a promising membrane technology based on the principal of natural occurring
osmosis, where the solvent, usually is water, is diﬀused through a semi-permeable
membrane barrier from a low to a high concentration solution (Awad et al., 2019).
The role of high concentrated solution (draw solution (DS)) is to transfer the water
(permeate) from the low concentration solution (feed solution (FS)). Compared to
similar membrane technologies has already been used in LA separation process, such
as reverse osmosis, ultraﬁltration and nanoﬁltration, FO operation is characterized
by lower energy consumption, lower fouling tendency, easier fouling removal and
higher water removal (Zhao et al., 2012).
The application of FO ﬁrst reported 80 years ago for juice concentration, and
the last decade has also been used researched for seawater desalination, wastewa-
ter reclamation, energy generation and food processing (Awad et al., 2019; Zhao
et al., 2012). Many studies have investigated speciﬁcally the employment of FO
to recover/up-concentrate organic liquids using diﬀerent DS with positive results

1.2
Objectives
The aim of the present project is the optimization of lactic acid production from
municipal organic waste and the investigation of up-concentration of the downstream
by Forward Osmosis.
Fermentation batch tests were performed for examination
of lactic acid production under diﬀerent operational conditions (pH, temperature,

bacteria. In addition, forward osmosis was applied where the pH of feed solution
(FS), the type and concentration of draw solution (DS) were explored.

Lactic acid
LA (2-hydroxypropanoic acid) is a natural organic acid, with the chemical form:
CH3–CH(OH)–COOH. First was discovered by Carl Scheele and isolated from sour
milk in 1780 (Singhvi et al., 2018).
LA exists in two optical isomers, D(-) and
L(+) LA, as shown in Figure ﬁg:DL. The chemical behaviour of LA is expressed
by its a) acidic character in aqueous solution, b) bifunctional reactivity associated
wih the presence of a carboxyl and a hydroxyl group, and c) asymetric optical
activity (Castillo Martinez et al., 2013). LA could be present in its undissociated or
dissociated form, based on the pH value of the solution/fermentation broth (López-
Garzón & Straathof, 2014). The pKa value of LA is 3.86 at 25oC. Figure ﬁg:pKa
shows the abundance of LA and lactate salt at diﬀerent pH values.

2.1
Economic importance and industrial application
The last years LA is gaining more and more interest due to its wide range of appli-
cations in pharmaceutical, cosmetic, chemical and food industry. Additionally, its
potential to produce biodegradable and biocompatible poly-lactic acid (PLA) poly-
mers that are used as raw material in plastics and thermoplastics (e.g. packaging,
ﬁbers and foams) production increased its global demand (Eş et al., 2018). The
demand of LA was estimated worldwide at 1220 kt in 2016, and is predicted to grow
about 16.2% between 2017 and 2025 (Singhvi et al., 2018). The market price for
PLA formatted from pure LA is estimated at 1900-200 USD/t increasing the value
of LA production. Moreover, each optical isomer of the LA has diﬀerent industrial
applications, thus the production of optical pure D(-) and L(+) LA isomers liquids
is desirable (Abdel-Rahman et al., 2011). L(+)-isomer is naturally present in hu-
mans and other mammals and can be assimilated by the human body, and is used
in food and drug industries (Ghaﬀar et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2015). Also, it can be
used for poly L-lactic acid (PLLA) synthesis, a semi-crystalline biodegradable ther-
mostable polymer used for goods packaging and medical products (Abdel-Rahman
et al., 2011). D(-)-isomer is not suitable for use in the food, drink and pharmaceuti-
cal industries because it can cause metabolic problems and is toxic to human body,
but it can be used for poly D-lactic acid (PDLA) production (Marques et al., 2017).
2.2
Lactic acid production
LA can be produced by two diﬀerent ways: chemical synthesis and fermentation.
The chemical route includes acetaldehyde reaction with hydrogen cyanide in liquid
phase and under pressure, in presence of a base to produce lactonitrile. Afterwards,
crude lactonitrile is recovered and puriﬁed by distillation, and hydrolyzed to LA by
concentrated HCl or H2SO4 addition. Then, the liquid is esteriﬁed with methanol
to produce methyl lactate, which is recovered and puriﬁed by distillation. Finally,
methyl lactate is hydrolyzed in a acidic aqueous solution to LA and methanol.

Methanol is recycled in order to be reused for LA production.
Other chemical
routes for LA synthesis include base-catalyzed degradation of sugars, oxidation of
propylene glycol, carbon monoxide and water at high temperature and pressure,
hydrolysis of chloropropionic acid, and nitric acid oxidation of propylene, among
others (Castillo Martinez et al., 2013). A racemic mixture of D(-) and L(+) isomers
results from the above methods, which is the major disadvantage of this approach
(Abdel-Rahman et al., 2011).
Conversely, fermentative production of LA has an advantage as inexpensive and
renewable resources are utilized and, low production temperatures are applied, less
energy is consumed and optical pure D(-) or L(+) isomers can be obtained, by se-
lecting the appropriate microorganisms (Eş et al., 2018). There are diﬀerent types of
fermentation and novel techniques that have been researched to improve the LA pro-
duction and meet the market needs. Open fermentation with non-sterile substrate,
is one of those techniques, which decreases the energy consumption as well as the
cost of the whole process (Gao et al., 2011; Abdel-Rahman et al., 2013). Simultane-

2.3.1
pH, temperature, substrate, inhibitors
The common bottleneck that can be met in fermentation for LA production is the
pH drop form LA accumulation in fermentation broth. The low pH aﬀect negatively
the microbial activity which can be decreased with the increase of LA production
in the fermentation broth. LAB can grow under acidic conditions 4.5-5.5, but the
accumulation of LA decreases the pH essentially inhibiting the microbial activity.

cost and leads to LA salts (carboxylate form) formation. To achieve high yields pH
should be in a range of 5-7 (Abdel-Rahman et al., 2013). The pH decrease is also
aﬀected by the fermentation by-products
Temperature aﬀects the progress of the fermentation at high level. Most of the
LA producers are mesophilic bacteria with a maximum growth temperature of 50◦C.

2.3.2
Microorganisms
LA fermentation can be conducted using either fungi, bacteria or yeast with pH
tolerance characteristics (Jambunathan & Zhang, 2016).
The group of bacteria
that can be fermented for LA production are named Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB).
According to literature (Abdel-Rahman et al., 2013), LAB are a diverse group of
Gram-positive microorganisms that exist within plants, meat, and dairy products
and can produce LA under anaerobic conditions. These bacteria are acid tolerant
as they can grow in the pH range of 3.5–10.0 and temperature range of 5–45 ◦C.
LAB are grouped as either heterofermntative or homofermentative depended on
the fermentation end product. Obligate species, such as the Leuconostoc, Oenococ-
cus and Lactobacillus are utilizing solely the phosphoketolase pathway in order to
degrade sugars and thus produce lactate, CO2 and acetate or ethanol along with LA.
The theoretical yield on pentoze for the bacteria who utilize this pathway is 1.0 g/g.
On the other side facultative heterofermenters and homofermeters such as Lactococ-
cus, Streptococcus, Pediococcus, Enterococcus, and some Lactobacillus are using the
the Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) pathway to degrade speciﬁc sugars producing
only LA.Wang and others reports that the theoretical yield for LA production cat-
alyzing glucose via EMP pathway is 1.0 g/g. Moreover, facultative heterofermenters
are able to make use pentose sugars via the phosphoketolase pathway (Holzapfel
Wood, 2014).
Lactobacillus strains is one type of LAB that are commercially important as they
are acid tolerant and able to be engineered for selective production of D-or L-lactic

salts in terms of reverse diﬀusion and osmotic pressure, but their high cost has to
be further investigated (Cai & Hu, 2016; Johnson et al., 2018).

The invention of synthetic membranes for pressure-driven membrane processes
application started its history 50 years ago. The ﬁrst cellulose acetate membrane
was created for reverse osmosis desalination (Tang et al., 2013). All these years more
technology membranes have been proposed improving their water ﬂux and solute re-

as a result, membranes suitable for FO application have also been manufactured.
Cellulose Triacetate (CTA) and Thin ﬁlm Composite (TFC) are most common mem-

and trace organics removal such as pesticides, endocrine disruptors and pharmaceu-
ticals from water (Madsen et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2017; Camilleri-Rumbau et al.,
2019).
The last decade though new technologically advanced type of membranes have
been introduced in FO and RO, the biomimetic membranes. The theory behind this
type of membranes is the mimicking of biological membranes functional mechanism.

3.3.1
Synthetic broth trials
Preliminary tests were conducted to evaluate the pH eﬀect of FS on the membrane
performance. Based on the obtained results (Table tab:4), a synthetic solution called
"synthetic broth" was prepared to conduct the testing experiments for the diﬀerent
pH values.
According to literature (Abousnina
Nghiem, 2014; Zhang et al., 2017; Law
Mohammad, 2018; Kedwell et al., 2019), the pH of FS can aﬀect the FO process
and a range of 2-11 pH values has been examined. In the present study, FS pH was
measured at 4.98, thus the pH values selected to evaluate were from 3.5, close to FS
pH up to 7, to cover a wide range (3.5, 4, 4.5, 5, 5.5, 6, 7). The pH adjusted using
10.0 molar NaOH and 3.0 molar HCl in all tests. The DS selected for all the tests was
a NaCl solution. NaCl is characterized by low, cost, non-toxicity, low replenishment
cost and generation of high osmotic pressure (Achilli et al., 2010; Bowden et al.,
2012).The concentration selected was 1.5 M to provide a ∆π of 53.8 ± 4.1 bar. The
volume of FS was 1 L and DS was higher, 2 L, to avoid the quick dilution of DS
and decrease in ∆π on all operations. The DSs were continuously being stirred to
preserve homogeneity during operation and the weight of FSs was monitored and
recorded by the software. The membrane was operating only with distilled water
for one hour at the beginning and at the end of the experiments.

the DS’s concentration eﬀect on the of LA up-concentration was performed. The

4.1
SAB experiments
The experiments conducted to deﬁne the optimum conditions for production of
fermentative LA from bio-waste. Bioaugmentation of two strains was compared with
indigenous microbial performance. Initially, the 13 combinations of the operational
conditions were tested and the results of run 8, which represents the average values
of all parameters, were analyzed. Moreover, a carbon mass balance was conducted
to analyze the sugar degradation during fermentation.
The production of LA obtained under 37◦C with initial pH: 6.2, and substrate
concentration: 80 gTS/kg of all the set of tests are illustrated in Figure ﬁg:g1. It
is observed that the production of lactic acid at the end of the fermentation (Day
5) using no inoculated biopulp achieved a titer of 14.61 ± 0.02). The maximum
yield (Yf) of LA production over the sugars consumed was 0.55 ± 0.00 g/g-sugars
and observed on Day 4. The theoretical yield of lactic acid production from glucose
degradation is 1 g/g-sugars, almost double from the yield achieved at the present
test.
Tang and co workers (2017) used sterilized food waste for lactic acid produc-
tion with fresh food waste as inocula, under uncontrolled pH fermentation. They
reported that a titer of 12.5 g/L was achieved after 5 days of fermentation. They
also pointed out that the low pH (4) of the substrate negatively aﬀected the soluble
carbohydrates degradation. This can justify the relatively low sugar degradation
in the fermentation broth, as only 66 ± 0.03% of the initial sugar content was con-
sumed and the ﬁnal pH of the broth was 3.83 ± 0.01. Hence, a rapid increase of
lactic acid concentration was noticed from Day 0 to Day 1, due to initial pH of the

4.5.3
DS type and concentration parameters analysis
The DS selected to evaluate apart from NaCl is MgCl2, Both have been widely
applied in several FO applications (Abousnina Nghiem, 2014; Zhang et al., 2017;
Schneider et al., 2019), and MgCl2 has provided better results than NaCl , especially
in solute rejection performance (Achilli et al., 2010; Bowden et al., 2012). Mg2+ has
a higher molecular size than Na+ and can achieve higher osmotic pressure at lower
concentrations. Figure ﬁg:E7a illustrates the water ﬂux (Jw) of diﬀerent DS and
their concentration. DS with higher osmotic pressure resulted in higher water ﬂux
from FS to DS due to increased driving force. However, the water ﬂux follows a
sharp decrease, due to ICP eﬀect, as high amount of water permeates quickly and DS
concentration decreases at the area close to the membrane surface. At low osmotic
pressure operational conditions, the water ﬂux during the experiment where MgCl2
DS used decreases suddenly compared to NaCl. CP is the explanation again, as
after stabilization of water ﬂux the ∆π was still high at 42.2 bar. The diﬀusion
coeﬃcient of each organic salt plays an important role on this issue as NaCl has
higher diﬀusion coeﬃcient, thus can easily be diﬀused to the bulk of DS against
MgCl2 (Achilli et al., 2010).
Comparing the performance of the two draw solution at the same concentrations
based on the up-concentration factors achieved, it can be outlined that the MgCl2
achieved highest lactic acid up-concentration. The WCF calculated for MgCl2, 1 M
and 2 M concentrations, was 6 and 7.5 , respectively. Compared to NaCl 1.5 M and
3 M concentrations, which was 2.5 and 3.5, respectively, MgCl2 provided the best
results. Finally, Mg2+ cations reached really high rejection rates (>99%), as long as
Cl−anions (>98%) and Na+ noted slightly lower rejection rate (>97%).
As a conclusion, the performance of MgCl2 resulted in higher LA up-concentration
compared to NaCl, especially at concentrations with the osmotic diﬀerence was the
highest.

5
Conclusion
The production of lactic acid using an organic waste as substrate is based on the
valorization of waste to produce high added value products. This approach is an
environmental friendly way to manage huge amounts of waste that are generating
worldwide and pose severe environmental issues, making a proﬁt out of it. In the
present study, an investigation of optimization organic bio-waste fermentation and
bioaugmentation for production lactic acid was conducted. Two bacterial strains
were used as as inoculum, Lactobacillus delbrueckii DSM 20074 and Pediococcus
acidilactici NCIMB 702925 for substrate fermentation, and their performance com-
pared to fermentation of bio-waste with no inoculation, under diﬀerent operational
conditions. It was concluded that P. acidilactici resulted in the highest lactic acid
production and maximum production yield of 19.2 ± 0.42 g/L and 0.9 ± 0.01 g/g-
sugars, respectively. The optimum operational conditions deﬁned by a Box-Behnken
model analysis of the experimental results. The pH deﬁned at 8, concentration of
substrate at 120 gTS/kg, optimum temperature at 32.4◦C, and predicted concen-
tration and maximum yield were 31.2 g/L and 0.74 g/g-sugars accordingly. The
proposed optimum conditions were validated in 3-L reactors, and the obtained con-
centration and maximum were yield 31.9 ± 0.28 g/L and 0.78 ± 0.00 g/g-sugars,
respectively.
As a result, the validation was successful and the predicted values
were obtained. In addition, the performance of the P. acidilactici strain under the
absence of indigenous microorganisms was assessed. The outcome was the decreased
production of lactic acid using only P. acidilactici as fermentation LAB strain.P.
acidilactici was not able to degrade the carbohydrates and produce lactic acid, but
in synergy with the indigenous microbes resulted in high lactic acid production.
The downstream process resulted in high up-concentration of lactic acid under feed
solution pH 4, and MgCl2 DS with a concentration of 2 M. The ﬁnal organic liquid
concentration noted at 160 g/L and the up-concentration factor was 6.3. The reverse
solute ﬂux was recorded at 99% for both Cl−and Mg2+.
To conclude, food waste cnstitutes a good candidate as substrate for fermentative

Recommendations
For future work, it is recommended the examination of bio-waste fermentation under
controlled pH during operation. As it was discussed, pH is the major inhibitor of
the fermentation process for LA production. Even though low acidic tolerant LAB
are used as inocula, still there are limitations that need to be overcome. An other
approach that could be investigated for fermentation of food waste improvement is to
perform repeated batch tests and target to microbial adaptation at low pH values.
Considering the downstream process, alternative DSs could be tested in terms of
low cost sources utilization, such as seawater and industrial waste water streams
with high salinity. Seawater has osmotic pressure close to 0.7 NaCl solution. This
concentration is very low to operate and up-concentrate organic liquids. Hence, the
use of seawater with NaCl addition might be a good candidate for DS. Moreover,
waste streams from cheese industry or sugar industry could be explored for their
osmotic potential and application on forward osmosis.
