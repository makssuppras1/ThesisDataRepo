Abstract 
 
Temperature rise associated with climate change has been identified to lead to changes in fish 
community compositions worldwide. In this study, we investigate the effects of climate change on 
demersal fish communities in East Greenland from 1982 to 2022. This is important because it 
affects the important local commercial fishery in the area. With the increase in ocean temperatures, 
species are expected to migrate to new suitable regions. This was investigated by the mean 
temperature of the catch (MTC), which analyses the fish community regarding both colder and 
warmer adapted species. Additionally, generalized additive models (GAM) were used to discover 
abiotic drivers of the calculated MTC in the region.  Species composition was analyzed by catch 
occurrence frequency, catch weights and calculation of catch probability for the species.  
this study, revealed an increase in the MTC thought the years and found that a borealization had 
occurred in the region. Surprisingly, no clear negative impacts on arctic species were observed 
despite the changes. Yet, species richness was detected to decline in the region throughout the study 
period.  A limitation of Cheung et al. (2013) was detected regarding its calculation of temperature 
preferences for the species, by only utilizing sea surface data and not considering bottom 
temperature data. This was the most prevalent for deep-water species with high habitat ranges that 
resulted in extravagant MTC values in the analysis.  Consequently, an update of the Cheung et al. 
(2013) study is recommended.

1. Introduction 
 
With the increasing temperature of the oceans due to climate change, fish composition in the oceans 
has been shown to change over time. These changes are important to observe to gain important 
knowledge for managemental implications and economic opportunities for coastal communities. 
Other processes that also affect local temperatures in the oceans are frontal region by ocean current 
changes, wind directions, atmospheric aerosols, and sub-gyre variations (Amato et al., 2009; Yang 
et al., 2020; Post et al., 2021; Emblemsvåg et al., 2022). 
With the temperature rise associated with climate change, it is evident that fish will either migrate 
or perish depending on their temperature tolerance.  A study by Cheung et al. (2013), found that 
tropical oceans can experience a decline in tropical species, whereas temperate regions will 
experience a rise in subtropical species. Based on this logic, polar waters should experience an 
increase in the abundance of more warm-adapted species due to immigration and colonization 
processes (Cheung et al., 2013). 
The effects of temperature rise from climate change can impact whole ecosystems through changes 
in species compositions and possible losses of ecosystem services that native species serve. This 
can lead to imbalances in food webs and force changes in habitats (Brander 2007). The outcome can 
be a negative influence on biodiversity in some areas. However, the reversed outcome can also be 
the case for other areas. Furthermore, the outcomes of the temperature change can be challenging to 
predict and directly observe because nature is very complex and vast. 
Moreover, in this report, the analysis of fish communities will be conducted due to their significant 
economic importance for the Greenlandic society. East Greenland is a common offshore fishing 
area for commercial trawlers and is a relatively highly productive area, because of the warm North 
Atlantic Current and the cold East Greenland Current. The area will likely be the first to experience 
the changes and movement of new visitors from warmer regions. This has already been experienced 
before when the Atlantic mackerel and the Atlantic Bluefin tuna came into the area in 2012 for a 
while, before disappearing again (Jansen et al., 2016; Jansen et al., 2021).  
In East Greenland, annual fish surveys are conducted on the shelf region inside the Greenland 
exclusive economic zone and are mainly off the continental shelf water type. The oceanographic 
conditions consist of shallower waters with the shelf closer to the mainland (<500m), but depth

increases rapidly after longer eastward and southward areas, where it can reach + 2000 meters. Here 
the east Greenland current is at play in this area and contains a northern and southern part. The 
northern part of this current contains Polar waters which have a temperature of <0°C, and less saline 
in content. On the other hand, the southern part comes from Icelandic waters to east Greenland and 
continues down south to the tip of south Greenland (Figure 1). As visualized in Figure 1 by its 
colour it contains warmer waters, and more saline conditions and is a part of the Irminger Current 
from the South Atlantic region (WGIEAGS Report 2020).

This study aims to explore the impact of the rise in temperature and its effects on the fish 
community composition regarding both colder and warmer-adapted species in the region, this 
analysis was accomplished by examining the MTC (mean temperature of the catch), a tool 
==PAGE:8==
3 
 
 
employed to identify changes in fish communities. It has been used to detect that tropical species 
are moving to subtropical oceans (Cheung et al.,2013).  This thesis hypothesises that with global 
warming East Greenland will experience an increase of warmer adapted species, that have higher 
temperature preferences than the native colder adapted fish. With this movement of fish species 
from warmer waters to colder waters it will increase the calculated MTC value for the region. Here 
we look back to the year 1982 until 2022, to investigate if this trend can be observed for Southeast 
Greenlandic demersal fish communities. Furthermore, this was investigated by species analysis 
from survey data to detect species composition changes or abundance level changes, MTC will be 
analyzed with GAM models to dissect the possible drivers of it and its relationships with abiotic 
factors. 
 
2. Materials & Methods  
2.1 Trawl Survey Data 
 
The Greenland Institute of Natural Resources and the Thünen Institute of sea fisheries from 
Germany both made a systematic demersal trawl survey.  
The survey data were retrieved from these institutes.  
The German data covers the years from 1982 – 2020, whereas the Greenlandic data covers the years 
2007 – 2022. 
The Greenlandic data consisted of two surveys, the first the fish and shrimp survey (SF) and the 
second, the Greenland Halibut (GHL) survey. The GHL data was eventually filtered out by the 
300m criteria implemented and will therefore not be used further on.  
Station allocations of the different surveys were selected using a combination of random 
stratification and fixed station method. 
All three surveys are designed to study groundfish stocks, whereas the SF survey also included 
shrimp. 
The survey data contains gaps when the surveys were not possible for reasons like bad weather, 
pandemics, financial reasons, equipment failures or from data cleansing work.

on trawl doors (Subsea standalone temperature logger, 2017).

The survey data contained information on catch position, species, numbers, weight by species 
caught, depth, swept area for the haul, and surface and bottom temperature. In a few cases, catch

individual weight from the other trawl hauls.

Only demersal fish were to be inspected in the analysis and therefore the pelagic species, such as 
Capelin (Mallotus villosus), Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) and Atlantic mackerel (Scomber 
scombrus) were excluded. To avoid including misidentified and rare species, a filter of 0.5% on 
species occurrence in surveys was added. Initial inspection of individual MTC values deduced from 
Cheung et al. (2013) showed that values were unrealistic and large for deep water species. 
Therefore, to remove odd values from deep water species that would bias the results, it was decided 
to use a maximum trawl depth of 300 meters. After the filtering processes, a total of 33 different 
fish species were included in the analysis (table 3). Analized data consisted of 17201 individual 
hauls from the German (GERM) data, and 13783 from the Greenland fish and shrimp survey (SF) 
(Figure 3).

2.3 Data Analysis  
 
The mean temperature of the catch (MTC) (described in the introduction and previous chapter) was 
investigated for the survey data by using the Generalized Additive Model (GAM), to explore 
spatiotemporal trends. This was done because of nonlinear relationships between variables, 
discovered by first applying Generalized Linear Model (GLM) for the variables.  
A backwards selection was used for model selection where the start model is the most complicated, 
and the end model is the simplest one. This was done by using the Akaike Information criteria 
(AIC) where low AIC values indicate the best model (Wood 2017).

2.4  Model Validation 
 
To assess the models an R package called “gam.check” was used. The package does multiple things 
at once (Wood, 2017).  
Among others, it was used to investigate if the k-value (related to smoothness/degrees of freedom of 
the smooth function) was adequate for the model. This k-value tells how well the smoother fit data 
for each variable for the model. If the k-values were close to the edf (effective degrees of freedom) 
value and the p-value was significant, one should increase the k-value for the variable in the GAM 
model (Wood, 2017). 
A Q-Q plot shows if the residuals are normally distributed in the data (Figure 5, top left). Normality 
was then assessed from visual inspection of whether the point followed the red line. A histogram of 
the residuals was also used to check if the variance was normally distributed (Figure 5, bottom left). 
The top right is the residuals against the linear predictor plot, and this should show values 
distributed around the zero value on the y-axis to indicate there were no systematic errors in the 
model. The last plot on the bottom right is the response against the fitted values, this should show a 
somewhere linear distribution for the cluster of data (Ross, 2023). 
During model selection, it was discovered that the “REML” method from R “mgcv” package was 
used for fitting smoother implemented for all models made, the REML method is preferred for 
mixed models (Larsen, 2015).

2.5 Sensitivity Test 
 
The sensitivity of the models was detected when specific species with particularly high MTC values 
were in the data. These values were given by Cheung et al. (2013) for the species. All surveys were 
inspected and showed sensitive behaviour to the removal of the extremely valued MTC species, 
these changed the smoother patterns for the GAM models significantly. One of these changes was 
for the depth variables and showed a difference in smoother patterns in the GAM models.  This was 
also a reason for the removal of the deep-water Greenland survey (GHL).  
==PAGE:16==
11 
 
 
Data fit was investigated with the ‘fitdistrplus’ package and then a log-normal transformation was 
tested but no major improvements to model fit was observed, so no transformation was 
implemented to the data (Delignette-Muller et al., 2015). 
 
2.6 Data Cleaning 
Besides the already mention cleaning of the trawl survey data mentioned in the trawl survey data 
paragraph, extra cleaning was done.  
A Pelagic species criterion was also implemented, and the following species were removed from the 
data: Mallotus villosus, Clupea harengus and Scomber scombrus.  
The surveys were then examined for extremes and too-low values in the explanatory variables in 
Table 2, these were removed, and with all the mentioned filters it resulted in the loss of the 
following years: 1981,1991 and 2000.  
 
2.7 300m Survey Haul Filter  
 
After the initial data cleaning procedures, the results were peculiar and showed sensitivity to data 
input in the models, so it was decided to implement a depth filter for all the survey data. 
The depth criteria of a maximum 300-meter filter for the surveys led to a full discarding of an entire 
survey, the Greenland Halibut Survey (GHL).  
The number of hauls removed with this filter for the surveys was in total 72352 hauls, where the 
German survey lost 16038 hauls and the two Greenland surveys lost 56314 hauls.

2.9 Environmental Temperature 
 
Temperature data collected from the surveys contain missing data (Figure 6). For the SST there is 
no data in 2018 and only the German data had available SST (Table 1). There was no SBT data in 
1991, 2000, 2018 and 2021. 
Figure 6 shows the yearly average temperatures for both surface and bottom temperatures from 
available survey data, as GAM models with smoother on.

4 Discussion 
 
==PAGE:37==

The above mentioned results show that MTC has increased throughout the years for both surveys. 
For the other variables that show significant effects on the MTC values, there are no clear 
similarities between the two separate survey models.  
There are several similarities between the survey combined model and the German survey model, 
which indicates that the German model drives most of the trends. A reason for this could be due to 
the longer time series.  
There is no obvious big increment of species with high-temperature preferences in the 10 – 20 °C 
range for the German survey data (Figure 20). Although it seems like the species with more 
moderate temperature preferences in the range of 5-8°C are becoming more common in the data.  
Based on the mentioned results of the increment of MTC for the surveys, it could be presumed that 
species with this moderate temperature range are the drivers for the detected increasing MTC values 
for the surveys through the years (Figure 24 in appendix). 
Cheung et al. (2013) shows the East Greenland area has a peak in MTC in 2006 (Figure 2 on page 
366), which is in accordance with our results (Figure 24 in appendix). However, a direct 
comparison between their study and the present is difficult because we account for extreme MTC 
values generated by deep water species that give unrealistic high-temperature preferences. It is also 
important to clarify that in Cheung et al. (2013), use commercial catch data, which is very limited 
and restricted to a few species in the region. Therefore, the present study likely gives a more 
realistic description of the trend. 
 
MTC was also investigated in another study in west Greenland waters done by Lekanda et al. 
(2021). Their calculations were done for demersal fish for the depths ranges 400 – 1500 m and 
analyzed in three separate ranges described as shallow zone (400 – 800 m), middle zone (801 – 
1200 m) and deep zone (1201 – 1500 m).  In this study, they observed stable to no change for all 
species models for both the middle and deep zones, whereas their shallow zone experienced an 
increase from 3.23°C to 3.61°C (+0.38°C) from the year 2006 to 2007, but then it decreased back to 
the 3.24°C in 2019 (Figure 6 on page 2901, Lekanda et al., 2021). Our findings reflect more a 
reverse of these results because our data experienced a very small decrease in 2010 (Figure 24 in 
appendix), and the MTC also increases from 2010 – 2019, from 6.1°C to 6.3°C (+0.2°C). 
All the investigated depths by Lekanda et al. (2021) are too excessive compared to our study with 
the depth range of 97 - 300 m, so caution is needed to compare the results with this information and 
==PAGE:38==
33 
 
 
with the area difference in mind. It it’s interesting that our results suggest MTC decreases with 
depth, which could show similar values at the same depths (Figure 9). 
 
4.1 Species Richness 
The decline in species richness we observe (Figure 15), has also been detected by Emblemsvåg et 
al. (2020), who focused on deep-water fish species in east Greenland for the period 1998 – 2016. 
They analyzed species in South Greenland. For a northern and southern area (latitude ~62°N and 
65°N) at depths from 400 – 1500 meters, which covers greater depths than in this study.  In their 
analysis, species richness, was not significantly different across the years, though it declined with 3 
- 5 species over the years. Compared to our study the loss was pronounced, as we only observed a 
mean number loss of 1.1 species from 1981 to 2022, observed via the smoother on Figure 15. The 
lower decline in our study could be expected as we included 28 fish species, whereas Emblemsvåg 
et al. (2020) included 61. 
Another study also detected declining species richness in east Greenland, when analyzing depth 
ranges 150 – 350 m, 350 – 1000 m and 1000 m - 1500 m (Emblemsvåg et al., 2022). Here they 
detected a decrease for the two first-mentioned ranges between 1998 – 2016 and then a more stable 
behaviour for the deep range with an almost flat linear trend. They argue this decline can cause a 
loss in functional diversity and indirectly cause ecosystem changes in the area and is driven by the 
increment of boreal species.  
4.2 Trends of Individual Species 
Emblemsvåg et al. (2022), detected an increase in the abundance of demersal boreal fish species in 
east Greenland, and no sign of new warm-adapted species. They attributed this trend to improved 
feeding conditions from the increased pelagic production in the area (Frainer et al., 2017; Frainer et 
al., 2021), which could be the reason why we also observed an increase of boreal species. The 
boreal species that our study observed more frequently were Atlantic wolffish (Anarhichas lupus), 
Spotted wolffish (Anarhichas minor), Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua), Beaked redfish (Sebastes 
mentella) and American plaice (Hippoglossoides platessoides), but a decrease for Atlantic halibut 
(Hippoglossus hippoglossus) and Golden redfish (Sebastes norvegicus) (Figure 16). The same 
species in the increase was also observed in a study done in the Barents Sea (Frainer et al., 2021). In 
==PAGE:39==
34 
 
 
our study arctic species such as Polar cod (Boreogadus saida) and Greenland halibut (Reinhardtius 
hippoglossoides), increased in occurrence over the study period (Figure 16), which contradicts the 
statement that arctic species will experience a decline because of predation and competition 
(Renaud et al., 2012). However, UiT Munin, (2022) found that other arctic species like Arctic 
rockling (Gaidropsarus argentatus) and Threadfin rockling (Gaidropsarus ensis) declined. 
Therefore, it seems that not all arctic classified species react the same way to the changing 
environment in east Greenland. 
Another contradicting observation from our study and Emblemsvåg et al. (2022) is that they did not 
observe borealization in the depth ranges our study analyzed. They only observed borealization for 
depths 350 – 1500 m and not in the 150 – 350 m, they used the same data regarding the German 
survey data, but not the Greenlandic Fish and Shrimp survey. Our study observed borealization in 
East Greenland and this was also done by Post et al. (2021), which makes sense because of the use 
of almost the same survey data, our data have additional years and did not include the GHL data.  
One of the clear abundance changes of a species was the Atlantic wolffish. In our analysis, it 
primarily increased, while Post et al. (2021) found it to decrease from 2006 – 2016. In Post et al. 
(2021), tusk (Brosme brosme) increased until 2016 whereas in our study, it declined slightly (Figure 
16). These differences might be because of different trends at different depths investigated.   
In Figure 27 in the appendix, Blue ling (Molva dypterygia) now only has data from 2007 where it 
decreased and increased, decreased again, and increased again till 2022. For Roundnose grenadier 
(Coryphaenoides rupestris) few catches were registered after the 300 m depth filter and it is a deep-
water fish species (Coad and Reist, 2004). For Saithe (Pollachius virens) it left off from the small 
increase in 2016 detected by Post et al. (2021) and increased till 2020 in our study (Figure 27). The 
only species not in our data is Round ray (Rajella fyllae) to compare the figure with Post et al., 
2021, but the rest of the species not mentioned still experience the same trends verifying that 
borealization is still occurring in the Arctic Ocean (Campana et al., 2020; Csapó et al., 2021). 
Furthermore, there seems to still be an abundant Polar cod presence and an increment of boreal 
species, this makes it difficult to say if there is increased predation and competition on the Polar cod 
from the boreal species (Renaud et al., 2012). Renaud et al. (2008), stated it could be a reason for 
the likelihood of a decrease of Polar cod in abundance in the Arctic soon. Our results show that this 
has likely not occurred in recent years in East Greenland.

4.3 300m Depth & Pelagic Species Filter 
 
The reason for applying a ceiling for a depth of a maximum of 300 meters for the data, was because 
we detected peculiar high values of species temperature preferences values acquired from the 
Cheung et al. (2013) study, which lead to extremely high results in the MTC calculations, highest 
MTC of 24.8°C (Figure 25 in appendix). 
When these deep-water species with high values of temperature preferences were removed, there 
was detected sensitivity in the results of the GAM model smoother trends and significant variables 
in the models changed. This detection leads to the discovery of signs of a shortcoming of their 
study, which indicates that they used only sea surface temperature (SST) data and did not consider 
sea bottom temperature (SBT) data for calculating temperature preferences for the species (Cheung 
et al., 2013). This resulted in deep-water species that have known wide habitat coverage getting 
high-temperature preference values, this was most prevalent for the species Blue antimora 
(Antimora rostrata), with a 50% quantile value of 25°C temperature preference (Figure 21 In 
appendix). Blue antimora affected the values for the MTC calculations because it was quite 
abundant in the deep-water survey (GHL) that was eventually removed by the 300m filter 
implication. The issue of the deep-water species could also stem from the lack of biological 
knowledge in the current science of the species, as it has been shown that deep-water species are 
particularly difficult to study (Orlov, 2022). 
The pelagic species were discarded from the analysis because they are mainly caught at the 
beginning or end of the hauls when the trawl net is in the free water column where the pelagic fish 
reside. The main part of the haul period is at the bottom where pelagic fish reside to a lesser degree. 
This phenomenon will result in skewed representation presumptions for the species because they 
will be caught far less frequently and not because of not being less present in the area.

The year variable is discussed in section 4.7 Comparing plots for the Year variables below. 
The dome-shaped SST relationship with MTC (Figure 9), could be explained because some areas 
with high surface temperatures have lower bottom temperatures. This apparent contradicting 
relationship is difficult to argue for because then one could include the bottom temperature variable 
in the model even though it is not the best model (Table 5).

Or it could be argued that areas with high SST are located inshore to the Greenlandic coast that is 
closer to glacier meltwater runoff that is located in the bottom water column, in figure 1 it is also 
visible that the colder East Greenland current is located closer to the coast.  
For the latitude (LAT) variable, its effects indicate that with more movement to higher latitudes 
MTC increases, and this could be explained by more northward movement the closer to the warm 
water Irminger Current one gets, and boreal species are probably in this warm water current which 
fits well with the signal from SST in the model (Figure 1) (Figure 9). 
The SWEPTAREA_KM2 variable, effect was quite small (insignificant in terms of p-value = 
0.13095) and here the explanation could be that species with higher temperature preference values 
are better swimmers and will get caught later in the haul i.e., after longer hauls (Csapó et al., 2021).  
In regards to the DEPTH variable, with increasing depth the MTC trend decreases which could be 
explained by the higher the depth the colder the water gets, and the less favourable the water 
temperature is for the species with higher temperature preferences (Figure 30 In appendix). It can be 
reversed for areas where the warmer current is in the deeper water column, i.e., a thermocline but 
this is not possible to detect with a 300 m maximum depth in our study.

MTC value (figure 18). The species that drive this value higher in this period, were: Blue whiting, 
Atlantic wolffish and the American plaice.  
For the years 2000 - 2020, the MTC value around 6°C (figure 28) looks to be driven by the same 
fish species in 2000 regarding frequency of occurrence (figure 18).  
In addition, there also seem to be a few species increasing in probability of capture in this period, 
the species are Silvery lightfish (Maurolicus muelleri), Ling (Molva molva), Haddock 
(Melanogrammus aeglefinus), Saithe and Atlantic wolffish and American plaice (figure 20).  
The main reason for these species to experience an increase in frequency, catch probability and 
catch weight in both survey data is because of the observed increase in SST in East Greenland 
(Figure 6). 
 
4.8 Future Work 
For future work the study could be improved with more complete data, these are SST and as well as 
salinity data for the Greenlandic survey data. If the mentioned update of the Cheung study could be 
done it would also improve our study, so we don’t need a depth filter of 300 meters that leads to the 
loss of a whole survey (GHL).  Another possibility is not relying on other studies’ calculations of 
the temperature preferences for the relevant species and doing this ourselves. A deeper investigation 
can also be done into areas with trends showing higher MTC values, and what causes this pattern.  
The species that drive the MTC smoother higher and lower could be identified for each year, to 
detect their weight in the results of this study.  
 
5 Conclusion 
 
==PAGE:44==
39 
 
 
This study shows that since 1982, Southeast Greenland has gradually experienced an increasing 
amount of demersal boreal fish species. The increasing MTC throughout the years in east Greenland 
is caused by an increasing amount of boreal species in the area. Species with relatively high-
temperature preferences have a significant effect on the calculated MTC values, with small 
increments of these species resulting in a significant change in MTC values. This indicates MTC is 
sensitive to detect fish community changes when species with relatively warmer temperature 
preference enters the area.  
It was shown that species richness has declined in east Greenland, which can indicate with a 
warming climate the arctic species are more in a less favourable climate and known boreal species 
are becoming more abundant, yet there is no evidence of new warmer adapted species now residing 
in Southeast Greenland. The study of the thermal preferences of the East Greenlandic demersal fish 
community and changes in species composition since the late 90s are important for fisheries 
management purposes and to understand the changes imposed by climate change on ecosystems. 
With the increase in temperature associated with climate change, there will be effects on the 
demersal fish communities in the Arctic oceans. The expected effect is a shift or at least an increase 
of more warm water-adapted species in East Greenlandic waters. 
In this study, unintentionally there was discovered a shortcoming of the Cheung et al. (2013), study 
where they looked at the mean temperature of the catches throughout the world. In our study we use 
the temperature preferences calculated by them, to investigate the MTC patterns for the east 
Greenland area and found that deep water species cause problematic heightened estimates of MTC 
estimates.  
The unravelling of the shortcomings of the associated deep-water species on the MTC calculation, 
calls for an update of the calculation of the temperature preferences for these species, to avoid over-
estimation of MTC.
