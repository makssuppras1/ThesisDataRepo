Abstract
This collaborative thesis, conducted in partnership with the ReHyb project, focuses on
post-stroke rehabilitation by leveraging assistive technologies. The primary objective is
to design and implement a NoSQL database for a Human Digital Twin-based Stroke Re-
habilitation System. The research explores existing healthcare and digital twin databases
to identify the most suitable solution that aligns with the project’s requirements.
The thesis begins by gathering requirements through workshops involving therapists, en-
suring their valuable input and expertise are incorporated into the design process. Em-
ploying an evolutionary development model, the database design undergoes iterative re-
finement to meet the specific needs of the rehabilitation system. The schema is meticu-
lously tailored to harmonize with the dashboard design, optimizing query speed and over-
all efficiency. To provide seamless data access and operations, a user-friendly REST API
is developed, facilitating intuitive interaction with the database.
The backend system is implemented using the Rust programming language, renowned for
its performance and reliability. Continuous development feedback and improvement are
facilitated through the use of GitHub, enabling efficient collaboration and version control.
Rigorous testing demonstrates the stability of the backend system, showcasing effective
concurrency management as well as robust data protection and privacy measures.
To enhance interaction testing between the Database and ReHyb components, an inno-
vative approach is employed, extending sample personas with realistic mock data. By
incorporating insights from literature and expert feedback, the personas are refined to
offer a comprehensive representation of physical state variables and their temporal pro-
gression. Furthermore, the impact of patient mood on daily performance is considered.
These refined personas serve as invaluable tools for testing and evaluating the intricate
interactions between the Database and ReHyb components.
This thesis makes a significant contribution to the overall integration and success of the
ReHyb project by providing a crucial component for storing patient data and facilitating
seamless connectivity between specific system modules. The designed and implemented
NoSQL database enhances the system’s capabilities, ensuring efficient management and
retrieval of patient information. By effectively storing and connecting data, the thesis sup-
ports the project’s overarching goal of enabling personalized and effective post-stroke
rehabilitation through the utilization of assistive technologies and the concepts of Human
Digital Twins.

1.1
Motivation
Stroke has a serious effect on mortality and disability rates, making it a major public health
concern worldwide. People of all ages and socioeconomic backgrounds are affected by
it, which is a major cause of long-term disability and a leading cause of death. Despite
improvements in stroke prevention and treatment, there is still a high global burden of
stroke, underscoring the urgent need for more research, awareness-raising, and inter-
vention efforts to address this important health issue[1].
The increasing importance of digitalization presents significant potential in healthcare and
therapy, particularly in the personalization and individualization of treatment based on
data-driven virtual representations of the human body, referred to as Human Digital-twins
(HDTs). HDTs are currently defined as “computer models of humans tailored to any patient
to allow researchers and clinicians to monitor the patient’s health, for providing and test
treatment protocols”[2]. In more detail, this means that information from the physical,
biological, and historical realms is used to track the health and behavior of the patient
and to aid in quick and accurate therapeutic decision-making [3]. The EU Horizon 2020
ReHyb project aims to create a novel stroke rehabilitation system integrating cutting-edge
technologies such as exoskeletons, virtual and augmented reality, and patient HDTs [4].
There has been already a substantial amount of work done in terms of the specific modules
of the project. However, in order for them to work together as a coherent system, a
database is needed to store the patient data and thus connect the specific component of
the system with each other.
Due to the complexity and diversity of the data linked to each patient, such as sensor
data, patient records, and rehabilitation progress records, a NoSQL database is preferred
over traditional relational databases[5]. Because conventional relational databases are
designed to store structured data in tables with predefined schemas, where each row in
the table represents an instance of the data, and each column corresponds to a specific
attribute of the data[5]. The relational database system enforces constraints on the data,
ensuring that each piece of data has a specific format and type[5]. NoSQL databases
offer a more adaptable and efficient method of handling these data types, which can be
accessed and analyzed in real-time [6]. A NoSQL database will overcome the limitations
of traditional relational databases by ensuring data accessibility and query performance,
thereby supporting the development of an effective stroke rehabilitation system based on
HDTs.
Therefore, the primary objective of this thesis is to develop a robust NoSQL database
capable of effectively storing crucial data pertaining to the patient’s therapy process. Ad-
ditionally, an accompanying Application Programming Interface (API) will be created to
facilitate seamless access to this stored information by the various components of the
specific ReHyb system. Through the creation of this database and API, a significant ad-
vancement will be made in the field of patient rehabilitation, as it will enable the establish-
ment of the ReHyb system’s Human Digital Twin. This digital representation will serve as

2.1
Stroke Rehabilitation System
2.1.1
Stroke
Stroke is a medical condition that occurs when the blood supply to the brain is interrupted
or reduced, resulting in the deprivation of oxygen and nutrients to brain tissue. This can
lead to brain cell death and potentially cause various neurological impairments, including
speech difficulties, paralysis, and memory loss [7]. Strokes can be classified into two main
types: ischemic and hemorrhagic, depending on the underlying cause of the disruption in
blood supply. Ischemic strokes are caused by a blockage in a blood vessel, while hemor-
rhagic strokes are the result of bleeding in the brain. Both types of strokes require prompt
medical attention and appropriate management to prevent further damage and improve
outcomes [7, 8]. To optimize outcomes and promote functional recovery, a combination
of in-clinic and home-based rehabilitation approaches are typically employed.
In-clinic rehabilitation [9] refers to the traditional model of stroke rehabilitation, where indi-
viduals receive intensive therapy within specialized rehabilitation centers or clinics. This
approach involves a multidisciplinary team of healthcare professionals, including phys-
iotherapists, occupational therapists, speech and language therapists, among others.
The team collaboratively develops personalized treatment plans tailored to the individ-
ual’s specific impairments and goals. In-clinic rehabilitation provides structured therapy
sessions conducted in a controlled environment, offering access to advanced equipment
and resources that facilitate targeted interventions.
Alternatively, home-based rehabilitation [9] has emerged as a complementary or alterna-
tive approach to in-clinic rehabilitation. Stroke survivors are provided with tools, technolo-
gies, and training programs that enable them to engage in rehabilitation exercises and
activities within their own homes. Home-based rehabilitation offers advantages such as
increased convenience, flexibility in scheduling, and extended therapy duration. Through
telemedicine and remote monitoring, healthcare professionals can remotely guide, mon-
itor, and adjust the rehabilitation process, providing ongoing support and intervention as
needed.
The scarcity of qualified therapists in many healthcare systems poses challenges to the
timely initiation and continuity of stroke rehabilitation services. Stroke rehabilitation sys-
tems, particularly those designed for home-based use, bridge this gap by providing remote
access to guided and monitored therapy. These systems extend the reach of rehabilita-
tion services, enabling stroke survivors to receive necessary care and support regardless
of geographical location or therapist availability.
Moreover, stroke rehabilitation systems empower stroke survivors to actively participate
in their recovery process. By providing accessible and user-friendly tools, these systems
promote independence and self-management. Stroke survivors can engage in rehabil-
itation exercises and activities in the comfort of their homes, fostering confidence and
self-efficacy. Integrating rehabilitation practices into daily routines through these systems
contributes to long-term functional gains and improved quality of life.

2.1.2
Human Digital Twin (HDT)
Providing a high degree of personalization is important to make new rehabilitation systems
engaging and increase their effectiveness. Thus, exercises as well as their parameteriza-
tion need to be determined according to the patient’s specific condition and needs. One
way to achieve this is by incorporating a Human Digital Twin of the patient.
This digital model serves as a personalized simulation tool that enables the monitoring,
diagnosis, and prediction of health outcomes for a specific person [10, 11]. The develop-
ment of HDTs has been made feasible by technological advancements such as big data
analytics, machine learning, and artificial intelligence, which enable the integration and
analysis of vast amounts of data from diverse sources [10].
The field of HDT is an emerging area of research with considerable potential to revolu-
tionize healthcare [11]. By creating a comprehensive digital representation of a patient,
healthcare providers can adopt a more precise and individualized approach to treatment
and prevention. HDTs can be employed to evaluate interventions and treatment strate-
gies before their implementation, thereby reducing the likelihood of adverse events and
improving patient outcomes. Furthermore, digital twins facilitate the exploration of un-
derlying disease mechanisms and the identification of novel therapeutic targets, thereby
supporting the advancement of precision medicine [10].
Nevertheless, the creation of HDTs poses significant challenges that must be addressed.
These challenges encompass the acquisition of large and diverse datasets and the de-
velopment of sophisticated algorithms for data integration and analysis [11]. Additionally,
ethical considerations pertaining to data privacy and security must be meticulously ad-
dressed to ensure the responsible utilization of personal information [10]. Despite these
challenges, the potential advantages offered by HDTs make this field an exciting area
of research, holding promise for transformative advancements in healthcare and the en-
hancement of patient outcomes.

Database (DB) and Database Management System
(DBMS)
A database (DB) is a fundamental component of modern computing systems that enables
the organized and efficient storage and management of vast amounts of data. Specifically,
it constitutes an organized collection of data that is stored and managed by a computer
system, typically controlled by a database management system (DBMS) [12]. The DBMS
serves as a software system that allows for the definition, creation, manipulation, and
querying of databases, enabling users to efficiently interact with the data in a structured
and secure manner [13].
The combination of the data and the DBMS, along with the associated applications, form
what is commonly referred to as a database system, or simply a database [12]. These
systems provide a systematic way of accessing, managing, and updating vast amounts of
information, ensuring consistency, accuracy, and security. By organizing data into tables,

2.2.1
Type of DBMS
One common type of DBMS is the relational DBMS (RDBMS), which is based on the
relational model of data management. RDBMSs use SQL (Structured Query Language)
to manipulate and query data organized in tables, which consist of rows and columns [14].
This type of DBMS is widely used in business and enterprise applications, as it provides
a high level of data consistency, integrity, and security.
Another type of DBMS is the NoSQL DBMS, which stands for ”not only SQL”. NoSQL
databases are non-relational and do not follow the strict schema structure of RDBMSs.
Instead, they use a variety of data models, such as document, graph, or key-value, to
manage unstructured or semi-structured data [15]. NoSQL databases are often used in
applications that require high scalability, availability, and flexibility, such as web applica-
tions, social media, and IoT (Internet of Things) systems [15, 16].
Object-oriented DBMS (OODBMS) are another type of DBMS that organizes data as ob-
jects, which encapsulate data and behavior [17]. This type of DBMS is used in applications
that require complex data structures and relationships, such as CAD/CAM (computer-
aided design/computer-aided manufacturing) systems, engineering, and scientific simu-
lations.
Other types of DBMS include hierarchical DBMS [18], and columnar DBMS [19], among
others. Each of these types has specific characteristics and use cases, depending on the
requirements of the application.
In summary, the choice of DBMS depends on the specific needs of the application, such
as data volume, complexity, scalability, and performance. Selecting the appropriate type
of DBMS is a crucial decision for any organization that aims to effectively manage and
utilize its data assets.

On the other hand, NoSQL databases utilize a range of data models that are optimized for
handling unstructured or semi-structured data. For instance, document-oriented databases

Another significant difference between SQL and NoSQL databases is their scalability [20].

2.2.3
Database in healthcare area
In the healthcare domain, the utilization of databases plays a crucial role in managing
and organizing vast amounts of medical data. Traditionally, relational database systems,
exemplified by SEER [21] and MIMIC [22], have been the preferred choice for constructing
medical databases due to their ability to handle structured data effectively and the regular
updates they receive. Relational databases provide a stable and reliable foundation for
storing structured medical information.
However, in the context of healthcare application development, NoSQL databases offer a

compelling alternative. The initial stages of database construction often involve iterative
refinement of data structures, requiring flexibility and adaptability [23]. Simultaneously,
when application development and database construction occur concurrently, enforcing
strict structure constraints in a relational database becomes challenging. Hence, it is

heterogeneous data by utilizing a document-based model [24, 29]. With MongoDB, med-

enabling seamless integration of different data types and facilitating agile data model-

data schema, enabling the incorporation of new data elements or attributes without ne-
cessitating extensive schema changes throughout the entire database. This flexibility is
particularly advantageous in healthcare applications, where data requirements can evolve
rapidly as new medical discoveries are made and clinical practices evolve.
Furthermore, the scalability of NoSQL databases is paramount in healthcare applications
that handle large volumes of patient records, medical imaging data, genomic information,
and real-time sensor data [28]. As healthcare organizations and research institutions
generate an ever-increasing amount of data, the ability to scale horizontally becomes es-
sential. NoSQL databases distribute data across multiple servers or clusters, enabling
efficient parallel processing and improved performance. By adding more servers to the
database cluster, healthcare applications can easily accommodate increased data stor-
age requirements and handle high query loads, supporting real-time analytics, decision-
making, and personalized healthcare interventions.

2.3.1
Secure Communication Protocol
HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol) and HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure)
are two communication protocols commonly used for transmitting data over the Internet.
These protocols facilitate the exchange of information between clients and servers in a
networked environment [30]. However, they differ significantly in terms of security mech-
anisms and data protection.
HTTP operates as an application-layer protocol, designed to enable efficient retrieval and
presentation of resources, such as HTML documents, from web servers [31]. It functions
on the basis of a client-server model, where the client initiates a request to the server,
which then responds with the requested data. However, HTTP lacks inherent security

To address the security vulnerabilities inherent in HTTP, HTTPS was developed as an
extension of HTTP. HTTPS incorporates additional encryption and authentication mech-
anisms to ensure secure communication between clients and servers. This is achieved
through the utilization of SSL (Secure Sockets Layer) or its successor TLS (Transport
Layer Security) protocols, which establish an encrypted connection between the client
and server [30].
When a client establishes a connection with an HTTPS-enabled server, a process called
“SSL/TLS handshake” takes place [30] in Figure 2.3. During this handshake, the client
and server negotiate encryption algorithms and exchange digital certificates to verify the
authenticity of the server. Once the handshake is successfully completed, the subsequent
communication between the client and server is encrypted, ensuring the confidentiality
and integrity of the transmitted data.

encrypted channels for data transmission, ensuring confidentiality, integrity, and authen-
ticity.
2.3.2
REST API
Representational State Transfer Application Programming Interface (REST API) is a soft-
ware architectural style that facilitates communication between different computer sys-
tems over the Internet. In Figure 2.4, REST API is based on a client-server model where
clients initiate requests to the server, and servers respond with the requested data. REST
API has gained widespread adoption as a flexible and efficient way to implement web ser-
vices and exchange data between different applications [33].
The REST API architecture is characterized by a set of constraints, which are commonly
known as the Richardson Constraints [34]. These constraints include the use of uniform
resource identifiers (URI) to identify resources, a stateless client-server communication
model, and the use of HTTP methods, such as GET, POST, PUT, DELETE, and PATCH,

The benefits of using REST API in software development include its simplicity, scalability,

scalable, and compatible approach to implementing web services.
2.3.3
Rust
This project has chosen Rust as the development language for this project. Rust is a sys-
tems programming language initially introduced by Mozilla Research in 2010. It is highly
regarded for its safety and efficiency, enabling developers to create high-performance ap-
plications while ensuring robust memory safety and thread safety [36, 37]. The language
has gained significant popularity in recent years, particularly for the development of REST
APIs, thanks to its distinctive features that make it an appealing choice for building secure
and scalable web applications.
One of Rust’s primary strengths is its ability to provide strong memory safety guarantees,
effectively eliminating entire classes of vulnerabilities commonly found in languages like
C and C++ [37]. Buffer overflows, null pointer dereferences, and use-after-free errors,
among others, are mitigated through Rust’s ownership and borrowing system. By lever-
aging these features, developers can prevent many of the pitfalls that lead to security
vulnerabilities, thus facilitating the creation of secure code [36, 38].
Concurrency support is another significant advantage of Rust, crucial for the develop-
ment of scalable RESTful APIs. The ownership and borrowing system in Rust ensure
thread safety by enforcing strict rules regarding mutable access to shared data [39]. This
approach allows for safe and efficient concurrent programming, ensuring the integrity of
data and preventing race conditions [36].
Furthermore, Rust offers high-performance capabilities, which are essential for develop-
ing fast and responsive APIs. The language’s zero-cost abstractions and fine-grained
control over memory allocation empower developers to write code that is both efficient
and expressive, without sacrificing performance [36].

2.4
Regulation on data security and privacy
Despite the absence of a specific legal framework exclusively dedicated to digital twins
[40], existing regulations concerning data privacy and protection are applicable to digital
twin implementations. One notable regulation in this context is the General Data Protec-
tion Regulation (GDPR).
The GDPR is an extensive data protection framework established by the European Union
(EU) to govern the processing and handling of personal data. Its primary objective is to
reinforce the privacy rights of individuals within the EU and regulate the transfer of per-
sonal data outside the EU [41, 42]. When digital twins involve the processing of personal
data, the obligations and requirements outlined in the GDPR become applicable. Entities
engaged in digital twin implementation are obligated to adhere to the GDPR’s principles,
which include ensuring lawful and transparent data processing, obtaining explicit consent
for data collection and usage, implementing appropriate security measures, and providing
individuals with the rights to access, rectify, and erase their personal data.
The applicability of the GDPR to digital twins is not contingent upon any specific char-
acteristics of the technology itself [40]. Instead, once personal data is processed, the
obligations outlined in the GDPR come into effect. While a comprehensive review of all
GDPR provisions is beyond the scope of this discussion, it will focus on the most rele-
vant aspects pertaining to our subject matter. Article 5 (Chapter 2) establishes a general
framework that encompasses several obligations, such as the obligation of transparency,
loyalty, storing data for a reasonable duration, and keeping data up to date. Articles 6 and
7 establish the fundamental requirement of obtaining the data subject’s consent for data
processing and, consequently, provide individuals with the right to withdraw their consent
at any time. Consent is particularly crucial, as Article 9 emphasizes, for the processing
of health data, which is generally prohibited unless the patient provides consent. Further-
more, Article 17 of the GDPR introduces the right to erasure (also known as the right to
be forgotten), enabling individuals to file complaints or seek legal remedies if they believe
their rights have been violated (in accordance with Chapter 8).
A significant challenge arises when considering how to reconcile the rights of an individual
regarding their data with the rights of another person associated with them. For exam-
ple, in the context of medical matters, if a digital twin captures information related to a
third party that has an impact on the human twin, such as a spouse with a chronic con-
tagious disease, the question arises as to whether such information can be incorporated.
Additionally, other regulations may supplement the GDPR, particularly in the domain of
healthcare. If the digital twin is deemed a “medical device,” the legal framework may
become even more stringent [40].

5.3.1
Data Management
Identifier Management
In the context of the present research, the generation of multiple unique identifiers is a
necessary requirement, with a primary focus on ensuring their safety and uniqueness. To
address this requirement, the application of the sha256 algorithm [52] has been chosen as
the preferred approach due to its well-established cryptographic properties. By employing
this algorithm, a unique string is hashed to produce the desired identifiers. Specifically, in
the case of generating user identifiers, email addresses are utilized as the input to derive
the unique userID.
The use of email addresses as input for generating unique userIDs capitalizes on a fun-
damental characteristic of email addresses: their inherent uniqueness within a given do-
main. Email addresses commonly serve as globally distinctive identifiers for individuals,
offering a dependable and widely recognized means of identification. This strategic se-
lection ensures that each generated userID is linked to a distinct email address, thereby
augmenting the overall uniqueness of the generated identifiers.
Regarding the generation of sessionID, it is produced based on the userID, timestamp,
and protocolID. Since the protocolID is limited in scope and not intended to be kept secret,
it can be sequentially incremented.
DateTime Data
Timestamps are not stored using built-in DateTime data structures in our system. Instead,
timestamps are stored in accordance with the rfc3339 [53] format. This format ensures
compatibility and standardization across different systems when storing and retrieving
timestamp information. By adhering to the rfc3339 format, it maintains consistency and
facilitates seamless integration with other systems or applications that also adhere to this
widely recognized timestamp format.
Handle Large Array Data
In order to ensure efficient query processing and minimize the time spent on queries, the
system has implemented a strategy to maintain suitable tuple sizes for large arrays. As il-
lustrated in Section 5.2.5, it employs an array-based approach to track the complete track-
ing information. However, appending new data to a large array can be time-consuming.

To ensure secure communication and data protection, the server strictly accepts requests

mandatory conversion of the HTTP requests into HTTPS requests. This stringent policy
guarantees that all data transmission occurs over encrypted channels, minimizing the risk
of unauthorized access and preserving the confidentiality and integrity of the exchanged
information.

Discussion
This chapter presents a discussion of the requirements for HDT (Human Digital Twin)
databases in future stroke rehabilitation systems and explores the implications of the
database design and implementation on security, safety, and system performance. Ad-
ditionally, the limitations of the study are acknowledged, highlighting areas for further
research and improvement.
6.1
Requirements for HDT Databases in Future Stroke
Rehabilitation System
The identified requirements for Human Digital Twin (HDT) databases in future stroke re-
habilitation systems encompass four key aspects: performance and scalability, integra-
tion, data security and privacy, and maintenance and upgrades. These requirements
provide valuable insights into the necessary considerations for developing effective HDT
databases in the context of stroke rehabilitation.
The requirement for performance and scalability emphasizes the need for a database that
can handle the increasing volume of data generated by stroke rehabilitation systems. It
is crucial to ensure that the database can efficiently process and retrieve data in real-time
to support timely decision-making and analysis. Furthermore, the scalability requirement
implies the ability of the database to accommodate future growth and expansion, as the
demands on the system may increase over time.
Integration is another vital requirement, as the HDT database should seamlessly integrate
with other components and systems involved in stroke rehabilitation. This integration fa-
cilitates the exchange of data and information, enabling a holistic view of the patient’s
condition and progress. The database should support interoperability standards and pro-
tocols to ensure smooth communication with various devices and applications within the
rehabilitation ecosystem.
Data security and privacy are paramount considerations in HDT databases. The sensitive
nature of patient data necessitates robust security measures to protect against unautho-
rized access, breaches, and data leaks. Encryption, access controls, and authentication
mechanisms should be implemented to safeguard the confidentiality and integrity of the
data. Privacy regulations and compliance requirements must also be adhered to, ensuring
that patients’ rights and privacy are respected.
Maintenance and upgrades are essential requirements to ensure the long-term usability
and effectiveness of the HDT database. Regular maintenance activities, such as data
backups, system monitoring, and performance optimization, are necessary to prevent
data loss and maintain optimal system performance. Additionally, the database should
be designed to accommodate future upgrades and enhancements, allowing for the incor-
poration of new features, technologies, and research findings.
These four requirements should be fulfilled when the project is iterated in the future. By
considering these requirements, developers and researchers can guide the design and
development of HDT databases to meet the specific needs and challenges of stroke re-
habilitation, while also paving the way for potential advancements in the broader field of
rehabilitation systems.

HDT Database and Implementation
Based on the results presented in Chapter 5, the database demonstrated the ability to
handle the current throughput. However, it is important to note that the database has
a connection limitation of 300. Consequently, when the number of data manipulation
operations exceeds this threshold, the database may encounter errors. To ensure unin-
terrupted system operation, it is crucial to monitor and manage the number of concurrent
connections to the database.
The evaluation involved testing the system’s performance with 15 users in the read-heavy
scenario and 30 users in the write-heavy scenario. The results demonstrated that the
system performed well under concurrency in the test environment, with the average re-
sponse time consistently about approximately 4 seconds. This suggests that the system
is capable of handling a moderate level of user load without significant degradation in
performance.
The current database design implemented in the ReHyb system successfully fulfills the
requirements set forth. It allows for easy manipulation of data, with developers from other
components able to create, update, delete, and retrieve data using the provided REST
API. Furthermore, the backend of the system can respond to requests with limited con-
currency in approximately four seconds, demonstrating satisfactory performance.
However, the analysis of response times revealed some challenges related to certain
types of requests, particularly in query operations such as the ‘GET /overview’ request.
The response time for these queries was found to be excessively long, resulting in ex-
tended waiting periods for clients until they receive the response body. This issue can be
attributed to a mismatch between the schema design and the latest application design.
For instance, when generating the overview page, the server needs to perform queries
across multiple tables, leading to inefficient data retrieval and slower response times.
Additionally, the experiments conducted, as indicated in Table 5.30 and ??, highlighted
the impact of authorization on the system’s performance. As the number of connections
increased, the authorization process resulted in a significant number of failures. This
suggests that improvements are required to address the authorization mechanism’s scal-
ability and ensure smoother operation under high loads.

The implications of the HDT database design and implementation on security and safety

rights and privacy.
Moreover, the safety of the system relies on the accuracy and reliability of the data stored
in the database. The HDT database should ensure data consistency and integrity to pro-
vide a reliable foundation for decision-making and analysis in stroke rehabilitation. Regu-

6.4
Limitations
Several limitations should be acknowledged regarding the findings and measurements
presented in this study. First, the evaluation of the HDT database and its performance
characteristics was conducted in a controlled test environment. While efforts were made
to simulate realistic scenarios, the results may not fully represent the database’s perfor-
mance under real-world conditions. Real-world factors such as network latency, varying
user behavior, and system load fluctuations could impact the database’s response times
and overall performance.
Additionally, the evaluation focused on specific aspects of the database, such as response
times and concurrency handling. Other important factors, such as data backup strategies,
system maintenance procedures, and long-term scalability, were not extensively exam-
ined. Future studies could explore these areas in more depth to provide a comprehensive
understanding of the HDT database’s capabilities and limitations.
Furthermore, the evaluation of the database design and implementation was based on
the ReHyb system, which may have specific requirements and constraints. While efforts
were made to discuss the broader implications of the findings, it is important to consider
the specific context of stroke rehabilitation when interpreting the results. Different reha-
bilitation systems and use cases may have unique requirements and considerations that
could impact the applicability and generalizability of the findings.
To overcome these limitations, further research and experimentation are necessary. Con-
ducting real-world deployments and long-term evaluations of the HDT database in diverse
rehabilitation settings would provide valuable insights into its performance, scalability, and
usability. Additionally, conducting comparative studies with alternative database solutions
and architectures could help identify trade-offs and optimal design choices for future stroke
rehabilitation systems.
Overall, while the current study provides valuable insights into the requirements, design,
and implementation of HDT databases in stroke rehabilitation systems, further research
is required to address the limitations and enhance the understanding of their full potential
in real-world applications.

Conclusion and Future Work
In this thesis, we have successfully implemented a robust backend system for a Human
Digital Twin-based stroke rehabilitation system, enabling developers to access and modify
data through a REST API. Through comprehensive testing and evaluation, our system
has demonstrated satisfactory performance in terms of concurrency management and
response time. Additionally, stringent data protection measures have been implemented
to ensure the security and privacy of user data.
However, several areas require further attention and refinement to enhance the system’s
capabilities. Firstly, as the user base expands, the current backend implementation may
encounter performance limitations. To address this concern, future work should involve
devising and implementing a scale-up or scale-out strategy. Scaling up the server’s re-
sources, such as augmenting memory capacity, or adopting a scale-out approach by dis-
tributing the workload across multiple servers, will improve performance and accommo-
date a larger user population. It is also worth considering parallel processing of workload-
intensive tasks or implementing asynchronous background processing to optimize system
responsiveness.
Furthermore, optimizing the processing time when handling requests from Unity is cru-
cial for an enhanced user experience and improved developer convenience in the ReHyb
system. Identifying the underlying causes of extended processing time and devising ap-
propriate solutions will streamline data exchange and communication between Unity and
the backend. Optimization efforts should focus on minimizing latency and reducing com-
putational overhead, resulting in a more efficient and seamless interaction between Unity
and the backend.
In addition to performance considerations, the database schema design requires regular
review and adjustment to align with the evolving design and requirements of the ReHyb
project. A dynamic iterative approach to schema refinement ensures that the database
remains in sync with the evolving needs of the application, facilitating efficient data storage
and retrieval. Regular schema reviews and updates will enable the system to adapt and
accommodate future enhancements and modifications.
Further improvements can be made to the current REST API implementation. Exploring
optimization techniques such as caching mechanisms, request/response compression,
and adopting efficient data transfer formats like Protocol Buffers can significantly reduce
network overhead and enhance the overall performance of the REST API. Continual eval-
uation and refinement of the REST API will lead to improved scalability and responsive-
ness, ultimately enhancing the user experience.
Moreover, as a future prospect, the database can be populated with data derived from
these sample personas to conduct enhanced experiments, simulating a complete therapy
process. This approach will provide valuable insights into the system’s effectiveness and
assist in further refinement and optimization.
In conclusion, this thesis has laid a solid foundation for the backend system of the Re-
Hyb application, demonstrating satisfactory performance and robust data protection mea-
sures. However, future work should focus on addressing scalability challenges, optimizing
processing time for Unity requests, regularly refining the database schema, and enhanc-
ing the REST API. By addressing these areas, the ReHyb application can evolve into
