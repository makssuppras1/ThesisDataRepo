Abstract 
Nowadays, heating and cooling account for around 20-40% of the total energy consumption in a 
typical European home. With new strict low energy class requirements for the future Danish building code, 
it is important to make sure that the energy consumption in the building can be lowered  while providing a 
better atmospheric indoor climate to the occupants. Previous studies have shown that energy savings are 
achieved by using thermo active building system (TABS) in the buildings. By implementing a control strategy 
for the TABS that takes into account the future weather, the overall energy consumption of the building can 
further be decreased. The control strategy used for this thesis is model predictive control (MPC). However, 
MPC takes several things into account, such as a cost function, constraints and disturbances. This thesis will 
focus on the saving potential of the system only by using looking at constraints and future disturbances 
which are, weather, occupant behaviour and internal gains.  
The vision of this study was to achieve an acceptable indoor environment while simultaneously 
reducing the energy consumption of the building. To find the best solution, four different control strategies 
were examined. But before that the time constant of the TABS and the surface temperature were found 
using HEAT-2. The first simulation was carried out on a building with TABS that is controlled by a 
conventional controller to find the annual energy consumption and the thermal indoor environment of the 
building. After that, another simulation was carried out by using TABS controlled by MPC. The system were 
then further upgraded by using MPC in the window openings as well. Finally, both the system was activated 
together in order to find the energy consumption and improvements in the thermal indoor environment by 
increasing the predictive systems in the building. The system was tested in two geographical locations, 
Copenhagen and Milan.  
The results from these studies confirmed that MPC implemented in TABS and windows together are 
capable of maintaining the building consumption below the proposed 2015 regulations from the Danish 
building code while being able to provides a thermal indoor environment equivalent to a category II 
building.  
The comparison between the different control strategies showed that all MPC controlled strategies 
improved the energy consumption of the building up to 20 - 30% compared to conventional control system 
with manual window openings. The indoor climate was however best when MPC in TABS was combined 
with the MPC in windows.  
Due to the warmer climate of Milan in the cooling period, the systems closed the window for most of 
the time. While in Copenhagen, the window was forced to shut down for only few hours in the whole 
cooling period.

1.1.1 Introduction and literature review 
A literature study was conducted to get a wider knowledge about the previous researches made on 
TABS control in general and the use of Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Thermo Active Building Systems 
(TABS). Furthermore, the literature study has been used to find the allowable limit with which the TABS can 
operate according to the standards. Moreover, the advantages and limitations, operational characteristics 
and different types of TABS are also investigated and described.  
1.1.2 Case study 
The building envelope was described and the internal gains calculated according to the standards. 
Furthermore, time constant of TABS and the surface temperature of the ceiling and the floor were 
investigated using transient HEAT-2 simulations. Finally a building section with integrated TABS controlled 
by different control strategies was investigated for energy consumption and acceptable indoor 
environment. The building section was created in Revit and imported to IDA ICE.  
1.1.3 Simulation performed with different control strategies and locations  
Different MPC strategies were introduced in the project in order to find the maximum energy savings 
possible. The performance of the MPC control strategies were also examined separately to get an 
understanding of how much each system was contributing to the energy savings and the differences in 
thermal indoor environment.  
1.1.4 Result analyses 
The results were evaluated by analysing the thermal indoor environment according to the four 
indoor climate categories for the heating and cooling load (DS/EN 15251, 2007) while the energy 
consumption of the building was evaluated by the future energy frame by the Danish building code, 
according to which offices, schools, institutions etc. are in building class 2015, if the total building 
consumption is lower than 41 kWh pr. year + 1000/area kWh/m2  pr. year (Strange, 2015).  
The performance of the different controllers were compared and analysed for their importance in 
the system.

 Background  
1.2
People spend up to 90% of their time indoors, either in the office or at home (Fornebu, 2001). It is 
therefore important to have an acceptable indoor environment in the buildings while decreasing their 
energy consumption. Nowadays, heating and cooling account for around 20-40% of the total energy 
consumption in a typical European home (Solomon et al., 2007). It is therefore important to design low 
energy building that will fulfil the 2015 low energy class requirements for the future Danish building code 
while having an thermal indoor climate that corresponds to at least category II building  (DS/EN 15251, 
2007). 
There are several ways to minimize the energy consumption within the building. Among the 
countless possibilities, Building Automation System (BAS) is a notable solution to manage the indoor 
environment while optimizing the energy needs of the building. BAS can be used to automatically control 
the Heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) system, blind control and window openings.  With 
these integrated controls, the system can offer a potential in optimizing the building energy consumption. 
A market survey done by Siemens(2014) shows that a considerable amount of energy can be saved by using 
BAS. 
Model Predictive Control (MPC) is one of the control concepts of BAS (Gyalistras, 2010). The peak 
loads during the working hours in offices can be minimized and controlled by using MPC, and at the same 
ensuring a better indoor environment at all times (Gyalistras, 2010). The system works by sampling the 
current state of the building along with the current weather condition, occupancy and internal gains. The 
information is sent to the MPC optimizer. The MPC controller uses the building dynamics alongside the 
predicted disturbances such as predictive weather, occupancy period and internal gain to give an optimal 
solution to the optimization problem. This signal is then applied in the building and the circle goes on.  
This study was conducted to improve the thermal indoor environment while reducing the energy 
consumption of the building by using MPC in Thermally Active Building System (TABS) and windows. Due to 
the predictive nature of the MPC, it was considered an ideal system to control slow response systems such 
as TABS. According to Opticontrol1, 10-15% can be saved on the energy consumption by using MPC in the 
TABS (Gyalistras, 2010). This is important in order to reduce energy consumption of the buildings so as to 
make them more energy efficient and ready for the future energy consumption demands (Strange, 2015). 
The goal of this study was to compare the energy consumption and the thermal indoor environment by 
using difference control strategies and determine the potential of MPC in TABS and in windows.

==PAGE:15==
In this project, initially TABS were simulated using HEAT-2 in order to find the time it takes for the 
system to reach steady state. In the second part of the project, IDA ICE was used to carry out the dynamic 
simulation of a building with integrated TABS.   
TABS and windows openings were controlled by using predictive weather data. In TABS, the supply 
water temperature was controlled by a control strategy that utilized predictive weather data to control the 
water mixing. While in windows, a system was designed to open the window for some hours during the 
night before the outside temperature exceeded a certain value the upcoming day in order to utilize the free 
night cooling. It is important to mention that the system took into account the thermal indoor 
requirements of the occupants at all times during the occupancy period. 
Two offices with a corridor in the middle have been used for the simulations.  One room facing north 
and the other is facing south with a corridor in the middle. The simulations were initially made in 
Copenhagen, whereafter the geographical position of the building was changed to find out whether the 
designed MPC system can provide acceptable thermal indoor environment in Milan. The energy saving by 
using MPC in different environment will also be investigated.  
Some BAS systems are also introduced in the southern orientation of building that uses internal 
temperature sensors. These systems include window shadings and window openings in the day time.

 Literature review 
1.3
 
HVAC systems consume roughly half of the total energy used in buildings (Solomon et al., 2007). This 
results in 20% of the total worldwide energy consumption, only for operating these systems (Gyalistras, 
2010). For the European Union (EU), these numbers are slightly lower making it to around 19 % (Eurostat, 
2015), while in the United States of America, the energy consumption for these systems is 21 % of the 
overall energy consumption (Solomon et al., 2007). Due to the overall construction boom in the EU, it is 
very important to reduce the energy consumption of HVAC systems and make them more energy efficient 
(Gyalistras, 2010). These changes are only realistic if changes in the HVAC systems are implemented. These 
changes include sustainable heating and cooling devises with advanced control strategies.  
Radiators are the most common heating devise used in buildings but within the last decade floor 
heating systems are getting more common among the modern buildings. In Denmark about 30 – 50% of all 
the new residential buildings are equipped with floor heating (Olesen, 2002). These systems give a more 
uniform heating throughout the room, making it more suitable for a better indoor comfort with big saving 
potential (Jin, Zhang, & Luo, 2010). Plastic pipes (PEX-pipes) and metal pipes are mainly used in the floor 
heating systems (Babiak, Olesen, & Petras, 2009). 
There are several types of radiant systems developed over the years (Park, et al., 2014). Some of 
these types are Thermo Active Building Systems (TABS), the Embedded Surface Cooling System (ESCS) and 
the Radiant Cooling Panels (RCP) (Babiak et al., 2009).  
TABS are defined by small water pipes embedded in the structural layer of the building usually with a 
big thermal mass like concrete. The concept of the TABS is, to have an active building structure, which can 
store heat in its thermal mass and exploit it when needed, even when the system is turned off; giving an 
acceptable indoor environment continuously (Park, et al., 2014). Due to the big thermal mass, such a 
system can operate with a higher water temperature for cooling and lower water temperature for heating 
making it an ideal solution for the future 4th Generation District Heating regulations (Lund, 2015). The low 
temperature heating will also make it possible to use renewable energy as a heat source (Lund, 2015).

A typical TABS system can be seen in Figure 1-2. As mentioned earlier the pipes are embedded in the 
concrete layer providing heat through the floor, ceiling or the wall. An important aspect of TABS is that due 
to the thermal mass of the construction and the slow response of the system, the temperature may not 
remain the same but fluctuate within a range (Babiak et al., 2009).  
 
In order to fully exploit the potential of TABS system, it is important to have a control system that 
will regulate the temperature of the water circulating through the pipes namely increase or decrease it 
according to the required temperature (Babiak et al., 2009). It is also important to take into account the 
intended use of the building, the schedule, outdoor air temperature and the solar radiation. Therefore this 
control system has to ensure that the system is working according to the demand of the building and not to 
its maximum (Babiak et al., 2009). This need to be done in order to reduce the operation and maintenance 
cost of the system and decrease the CO2 emissions. In well insulated building the internal heat gains covers 
the cooling demands of the building. In that case only the cooling demands needs to be considered (Babiak 
et al., 2009). 
Model Predictive Control (MPC) is one of the control concept that can minimized peak loads during 
the working hours in offices and can also ensure a better indoor environment at all times (Gyalistras, 2010).  
Studies have been carried out where MPC have been used to control heating, cooling, lighting, 
shadings and ventilation systems more efficiently. The OptiControl 1 project (Gyalistras, 2010) is probably 
one of the largest researches that have been conducted on the MPC in building simulation. The research 
has been carried out by Eidgenössische Technische Hochschule Zürich (ETH Zürich) in association with Swiss 
Federal Laboratories for Materials Science and Technology (EMPA), Siemens, Swiss electric research among

==PAGE:18==
others. The study was conducted to assess the theoretical saving potentials of predictive control system 
compared with conventional control strategies (Gyalistras, 2010). These new control approaches were then 
implemented in a demonstration building to assess the actual energy savings (Gwerder & Gyalistras, 2013). 
The results showed that occupants’ comfort has also been improved and the peak energy demand 
decreased. By using MPC to control the TABS in the building, 10-15% was saved on the energy use 
(Gyalistras, 2010).

In the OptiControl project, the lowest control cost possible was found for a given building by 
assuming that all the disturbances (weather, internal gains occupant behaviour) are known as well as the 
dynamic behaviour of the building (performance bound). It is not possible to achieve performance bound 
because all the parameters cannot be known perfectly. The aim here is to find the transition from the 
perfect model to the real world model, and by that finding the realistic potential by using the MPC.  
In another project, MPC had been used to control the air conditioning, windows shading and window 
opening to ensure an acceptable thermal indoor quality while reducing the energy cost to a minimum 
(Kolokotsa et al., 2009). In this project, a cost function had been used to make a system that minimized the 
operational cost of the system by combining the two parameters mentioned above in a complex function. 
The results were evaluated by analysing the temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration. 
However in this study, the indoor comfort requirements were not considered and the predictive control 
algorithm was not improved using different prediction techniques.

Finally, a study was carried out by implementing MPC controlled radiant heating system on Czech 
Technical University in Prague. Energy savings of 15% to 28% were achieved by using a MPC compared to a 
conventional control system. All requirements from the standards regarding indoor climate in the building 
were met while the peak loads were reduced (Široký, 2011). The approach was limited to the heating 
systems and the indoor comfort requirements were not considered. 
This research focuses on making use of the current energy sources to minimize the energy 
consumption while insuring a good thermal indoor environment in cooling and heating period. By applying 
advanced control strategies such as MPC to control TABS and window in the building.  
The criteria for design and operation of the building need to be met in order to have an acceptable 
thermal indoor environment. Different building categories are made in order to assess the thermal indoor 
environment (DS/EN 15251, 2007). These categories can be selected for the building design and used as 
design criteria for the building. Even though one category has been selected, it may still operate in a higher

2.1.1 Introduction 
 
Building elements where water or air is used as a heat carrier and is circulated inside the cavities of 
the slabs or by circuits attached outside of the structure are called radiant systems. Although most of the 
heat transfer that occurs is through thermal radiation, radiant systems are also dependent on convection.  
There are different types of radiant systems in the market, depending on the need these can be used 
in a particular construction. In this project a TABS has been used to control the thermal environment of the 
building.  TABS can be defined as water based system, used to heat or cool the building using embedded 
pipes incorporated in the thermal mass of the building.

2.1.2 Advantages and disadvantages of TABS 
There are several advantages and disadvantages of TABS, some of them are as follows. 
One of the advantages that a TABS has over a conventional ventilation system is space consumption. 
When cooling/heating is delivered by ventilation air, the system needs to deliver higher air volumes to 
maintain a comfortable thermal indoor environment. Higher air volume require bigger ventilation ducts 
through the office or smaller ducts with a big flow rate which will result into pressure loss and draft. 
Therefore in office buildings where saving space is very important, a lot of area can be saved by having 
smaller ventilation ducts and mechanical rooms. Smaller ventilation ducts will also lower the overall height 
of each floor, saving building material or insuring more office floors by using the same amount of materials 
(Babiak et al., 2009). 
Compared to radiators, a TABS has a much bigger surface area. Therefore the surface temperature 
only needs to be a few degrees above or below the room temperature to heat or cool the building 
respectively. Therefore renewable sources can be used to heat or cool the water. It also increases the 
energy efficiency of heat pumps, condensation boiler, solar collectors, ground heat exchanges etc. (Babiak 
et al., 2009).  
The large thermal mass of a TABS also ensures that the cooling is distributed over a longer period 
resulting to lower peak load and energy savings. It is also possible to decrease the maintenance cost as 
these pipes are well protected in the concrete slab. Furthermore, a TABS gives uniform heating and cooling 
distribution resulting in a better comfort for the occupants.  
Every system has advantages as well as disadvantages. Therefore in order to make the right decision, 
the advantages and disadvantages needs to be compared to make the right decision.  
13 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:25==
TABS are more efficient without suspended ceiling; therefore suspended ceiling is not recommended 
for TABS. But among other advantages, suspended ceilings also help dealing with the acoustical problems 
between the floors. Without the suspended ceiling the problem could be solved by having insulation layer 
above the concrete in every deck. Unfortunately this blocks some of the heating/cooling potential, since 
the insulation layer also has a bigger thermal resistance.  
TABS alone cannot offer a good indoor environment. For example, TABS can provide an acceptable 
thermal indoor environment but cannot provide the occupants with fresh air while removing pollutants, 
odours and CO2 from the indoor air. Therefore a mechanical ventilation system (with a smaller flow rate) is 
still needed to provide a good indoor environment. Therefore a well-engineered solution where the 
ventilation system is providing fresh air and the TABS is dealing with thermal indoor environment is 
required.  
Fresh air can also be delivered in the system through natural ventilation, but in colder environment, 
this may not be the most energy efficient solution in the heating period unless it is controlled by an 
automatic system.   
As mentioned earlier a TABS integrates the thermal mass of the construction to regulate the thermal 
indoor temperature of the building, making it a system with a big thermal capacity. Due to the building 
mass, the system has a slow dynamic response and needs to be controlled by automated systems in order 
to utilize it efficiently

2.1.3 Conventional TABS control and MPC in TABS  
In order to fully utilize the potential and minimize the disadvantages of TABS, MPC has been used in 
different scales. MPC is used to predict weather, occupant behaviour and internal gains in order to 
minimize the thermal discomfort due to the slow response of the TABS. By optimizing the irregularities, the 
system will react many hours before the changes occur.  Furthermore, the storage capacity of the TABS can 
be utilize in a much better way by using MPC, because the system will be ready for peak hours. Therefore 
the idea of using MPC is to optimize the building by making a system that accounts for the current and the 
future building disturbances as mentioned above.  
The power of the system can be calculated by the temperature difference and the specific heat of 
the liquid.

As mentioned above, the two regulating parameters are temperature and flow rate of the water

The temperature difference ∆T is influenced by the inlet temperature and the return temperature of

the water and the concrete slab and the thermal balance of the concrete slab and the air temperature of 
the room.  
The flow rate was kept constant, while the inlet water temperature of the TABS had been controlled 
by different control strategies which are also commonly used in the literature. According to studies carried 
out by (Olesen, 1997), the best control strategy for TABS is to control the water temperature as a function 
of outdoor temperature. This strategy ensures the best energy performance. Another study done by 
(Karlsson and Hagentoft, 2011) suggests that controlling the supply fluid temperature is a robust enough 
strategy.

Figure 2-3: Hydronic diagram of the TABS 
As can be seen in the Figure 2-3, there were two loops in the system (blue rectangle). The first loop 
was for hot water while the second loop was for cold water. These loops were connected to two water 
tanks, one for cold water storage and one for hot water storage that maintains the a constant water 
temperature. The system could be switch from heating to cooling mode by switching the direction of the 
valves.  
The other loop (red rectangle) recirculated the flow coming from the TABS. The circulation pump was 
set to run at a constant speed in order to have a constant flow. The water from the second loop was mixed 
with the cold/hot water depending on the control system settings, which increases or decreases the water 
temperature that circulated through the TABS. The hot water pump and the cold water pump were 
activated when there was a need for heating or cooling respectively. The valves which controlled the water 
mixing were controlled by PI-controllers that took into account the room temperature and the setpoint to 
give a value between 1-0, where 1 is complete opening of the 3-way valve while 0 was closed, which meant 
that there was no need for heating and cooling (see section 3.3.4.1).  
MPC in TABS will work in the following way (basic concept) 
16 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:28==
 
Figure 2-4: MPC in building construction, the basic concept 
Initially the detailed building model sends current states measurements with actual weathers, 
occupant behaviour and internal gains to the optimization problem. Secondly, the predicted disturbances 
and the constraints (given by physical or logical limitations) are sent to the building model alongside time 
varying parameters and afterwards to the optimization problem. The current state measurement are then 
optimized with the parameters and then applied to the detailed building model. The loop continues where 
current state measurements are again sent the optimization problem over an optimization horizon.    
In this study, the focus was on the disturbances which are weather, occupant behaviour and internal 
gain and the constraints. These disturbances were analysed and calculated to design the MPC system for 
the building section. The MPC system controlled the water temperature running through the TABS and the 
window openings. 
Time varying parameters such as cost function are not analysed and calculated. Therefore a simpler 
version of MPC has been investigated where the predicted disturbances are sent to the optimization 
problem and the control actions such as water temperature in the TABS and window opening are 
controlled.

3.3
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (ICE) is a program that can be used to study indoor environment of 
different rooms within the building as well as determining the total energy consumption of the building. 
The program is unique in a sense that it can operate on three different interfaces, wizard, standard level 
and advanced level. In standard level, the user can design the building according to its needs. All the 
parameters like geometry, materials, controller settings, loads, etc. are easily accessible and should be easy 
for any engineer to handle. On the other hand, in the advanced level, the building is not defined physically 
anymore. Now the building is in the form of connected component models, whose meaning is defined by 
equations. Only these two interfaces are used in this project, the standard level and advanced level. 
Unfortunately, it was not possible to switch from advance interface to standard interface. Therefore a 
“save as version” was used where the initial file was the building model in the standard interface and the 
updated file was the advance level of the model. All the changes made throughout the simulation were 
automatically projected to the updated model.

3.3.2 Window opening in the building 
The automatic window opening system was evaluated in order to determine the amount of savings 
while improving the thermal indoor conditions of the building section. The automatic window opening 
system was controlled by control strategies that could adjust the temperature by opening the window to 
decrease the indoor temperature in the peak hours. This system was only operating when the outdoor 
temperature was lower than the indoor temperature. This was done by having thermostats that monitored 
the indoor temperature of the office rooms. When the temperature exceeded the maximum temperature 
for building category I (see section 1.1) in the cooling period and the heating period, the system opened the 
window.  
Furthermore MPC was also implemented in the system. The system opened the window for some 
hours during night time when the temperature for the upcoming day exceeded a certain value (see section 
3.3.4.2). By doing so, the temperature of the building was kept at the minimum allowable temperature 
possible when the occupants enter the building.  
The system also had to ensure that the windows automatically shut down when the outdoor 
temperature exceeded the indoor temperature by 1oC.  This was very important in order to prevent the 
heat coming from the ambient air.

cooling period was defined to be from the 1st of May, until the 30th of September 
In Copenhagen heating period was decided to be from 1st of October, until the 30th of April, while the 
cooling period was from the 1st of June, until the 30th of August. May and September were not considered 
in these two periods. In these months, recommended design values of the indoor temperature for design of

3.3.4 Control algorithms for MPC systems 
As explained earlier, three types of control algorithms had been used in this project. The first one 
was already explained in section 3.3.2. The other two used data for the upcoming day to make changes in 
the system that would improve the thermal indoor environment and decrease the energy consumption of 
the building. The first control system was MPC for water temperature control in the TABS. In this control 
system the temperature varied between the maximum and the minimum allowable temperature 
depending on the needs of the building. The second MPC system controlled the opening of the window at 
night hours to utilize the free cooling in the cooling period. The system only opened the windows for night 
cooling when the system estimated that the temperature was going to be higher than 15 oC the upcoming 
day.

6 Result analysis and discussion 
 CPH/MILAN TOT 
6.1
6.1.1 South 
MPC in TABS and windows combined (CPH/MILAN TOT) gave the best result amongst the control 
strategies tested. For Copenhagen, the thermal indoor environment in the south oriented room was within 
category I and category II for more than 99% of the time and for Milan the thermal indoor environment in

indoor temperature deviated more than 5% of the occupied hours from category I.

proposed 2015 regulations from the Danish building code, according to which the total building 
consumption in office buildings has to be lower than 41 kWh pr. year + 1000/area kWh/m2  pr. year 
(Strange, 2015).

 Comparison between CPH/MILAN TOT and CPH/MILAN  MANUAL 
6.5
Compared to CPH/MILAN MANUAL, CPH/MILAN TOT was more energy efficient. Energy saving of up 
to 20-30 % was achieved by using CPH/MILAN TOT over CPH/MILAN MANUAL. Figure 4-16 and Figure 4-21 
show the energy consumption of the building section in Copenhagen and Milan. The lowest energy 
consumption was achieved by using CPH/MILAN_TOT therefore this control strategy will be used as a 
reference to compare the energy consumption of other systems. The lower consumption of CPH_TOT could 
be influenced by these factors. 
• 
MPC in window opening  
By predicting/using data from the upcoming day, the system made sure that the indoor 
temperature was around 230C before the occupants entered the building (lowest temperature for 
category II) 
• 
MPC in TABS 
By predicting/using data from the upcoming day, the system made sure to limit the heating or 
cooling to the TABS system.   
• 
Automatic window opening and closing (BAS) 
The automatic window opening was much more controlled compared to manual window openings. 
As mentioned earlier, it is difficult to predict human behaviour when it comes to window opening. 
Therefore the extra heating or cooling coming from the window had to be removed by the system 
using electric heating or cooling.  The purple lines on Figure 4-20 also suggests that heating and 
cooling was activated in irregular periods. In Milan, the ambient day time temperature in the 
cooling period months can be higher than the indoor temperature. The automatic window opening 
also made sure that the window was closed automatically, when the outdoor temperature was 1 0C 
higher than the indoor temperature to avoid the wastage of cooling. 
For CPH/MILAN MANUAL, the thermal indoor environment depended highly on the location of the 
building and the orientation of the office rooms

for the case CPH/MILAN MANUAL (see Figure 4-1 and Figure 4-7), since the manual control of the windows 
in the building operates according to a schedule set by the designer which runs regardless the weather 
compared to CPH/MILAN TOT where all the window openings were controlled.

WINDOW TABS was controlled by a conventional control system while in CPH/MILAN TOT, TABS was 
controlled by MPC. As seen in Figure 3-24, the signals coming from the PI-controller were limited by the 
system that was controlled by predicted weather. This was limiting the signal without compromising the 
thermal indoor environment since the predictive weather control would make sure that, only if the 
upcoming day was cloudy, the signals from the PI-controller were turned to zero. 
Furthermore, there was no window shading in CPH/MILAN WINDOW that would have helped 
CPH/MILAN TOT to limit the internal heat gain more efficiently. Figure 4-3 and Figure 4-5 gives a good 
illustration of how much the shading control contributes to lower the operative temperature. The window 
opening does have an effect but not in the same magnitude as can be seen by comparing Figure 4-4 and

7 Conclusion 
The vision of this study was to achieve an acceptable indoor environment while simultaneously 
reducing the energy consumption of the building. To find the best solution, four different control strategies 
were examined. But before that the time constant of the TABS and the surface temperature were found 
using HEAT 2. The first simulation was carried out on a building with TABS that is controlled by a 
conventional controller to find the annual energy consumption and the thermal indoor environment of the 
building. After that, another simulation was carried out by using TABS controlled by MPC. The system were 
then further upgraded by using MPC in the window openings as well. Finally, both the system was activated 
together in order to find the energy consumption and improvements in the thermal indoor environment by 
increasing the predictive systems in the building. The system was tested in two geographical locations, 
Copenhagen and Milan.  
MPC in TABS and windows combined (CPH/MILAN TOT) gave the best result amongst the control 
strategies tested. This control strategy offered the best thermal indoor environment corresponding to a 
category II building in both geographical locations while being capable of maintaining the building 
consumption well below the proposed 2015 regulations from the Danish building code. Energy savings of 
20-30% compared to a conventional control with manual window openings were achieved. 
The energy consumption of the building section by using control strategies CPH/MILAN TABS and 
CPH/MILAN WINDOWS were close to the energy consumption achieved by CPH/MILAN TOT, but they could 
not maintain the same thermal indoor environment corresponding to a category II building. The thermal 
indoor environment for control strategies, CPH/MILAN WINDOWS and CPH/MILAN TABS highly depended 
on the location and orientation of the building. 
The energy consumption of the building section by using control strategy CPH/MILAN MANUAL was 
higher than by using other control strategies and at the same time they could not maintain a thermal 
indoor environment corresponding to a category II building. The thermal indoor environment by using this 
strategy was very much influence by the geographical location and the orientation of the office. 
It remains to future investigations to incorporate the missing cost function in order to improve the 
predictions techniques used in this study. Furthermore, a comparison between the theory and the real 
controller in a real building is still required to be investigated. The study does not analyse the effects of 
thermal mass on the performance of MPC. A future study where the effects of different thermal mass of 
the buildings are compared is still to be investigated.
