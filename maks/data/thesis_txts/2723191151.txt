Abstract 
This project presents the feasibility of estimating the release of large reservoirs in Thailand using 
satellite altimetry and satellite imagery data. The release is calculated using the water balance of 
the reservoirs, which considers precipitation and evaporation data from publicly available 
sources. Observed data of reservoir water level, release and inflow, obtained from the Hydro-
Informatics Institute in Thailand, are used for validation. Other components of the water balance 
are derived using water level and extent data from satellites, specifically Sentinel-3 for altimetry 
data and Sentinel-1 and Sentinel 2 for water surface extent data. The water volume change is 
estimated by integrating the extent-level relationships of each reservoir. The inflow to the 
reservoirs is estimated using the NAM model in combination with precipitation and evaporation 
data. Thirteen reservoirs were studied, and for nine of them, satellite altimetry data were used 
to simulate the outflow, as some of the data from several reservoirs were found to be unreliable. 
The water level obtained from satellite altimetry was found to be consistent with the measured 
water level (average RMSE = 0.53 m; R = 0.94). The water surface extent obtained from satellite 
imagery had some errors, but the trend was generally consistent with the measured value 
(average NRMSE = 0.72 km2; R = 0.94), although the peak value was difficult to obtain accurately. 
The NAM model was used to estimate the inflow to the reservoirs, and the results were 
moderately accurate (average KGE = 0.58; R = 0.74). The simulation of the outflow was found to 
be reasonable, with some fluctuations and errors compared to the measured data, but the overall 
trend was consistent (average KGE = 0.52; R = 0.68). The accuracy of the calculated flow was 
considered acceptable for typical reservoir operations. The results also indicate that the 
estimation of the release is highly sensitive to errors in the storage volume change and inflow. 
Measured data may also have errors, so it cannot be considered as true values. Therefore, 
satellite altimetry and imagery data can be used as sources of information to monitor the 
operation of reservoirs with relatively low uncertainty. The combination of satellite data and 
hydrological models shows better overall performance and has great potential to provide an 
effective method for understanding the operation of reservoirs and better management of water 
resources. 
Keywords 
Reservoir release; Satellite altimetry; Satellite imagery; SAR; Hydrological models; Climate change; 
Water balance

1. Introduction 
1.1 Research background and significance 
Water is the most fundamental natural resource in the ecological environment system, representing 
a basic condition for the survival of all living organisms and an indispensable economic resource in 
social and economic development. It serves various essential purposes, such as drinking, irrigation, 
energy production, and industrial processes. The availability and quality of water resources have 
significant impacts on human health, food security, and economic growth, while also playing a 
crucial role in supporting ecosystems and biodiversity. Although water covers 71% of the Earth's 
surface, only a very small proportion of it is usable freshwater resources. Moreover, the majority of 
these resources are located in remote areas such as the poles and Greenland, which are difficult to 
exploit for human use(Hoekstra and Chapagain 2011, Postel and Richter 2012, Connor 2015). 
Climate change induced changes in hydrological cycle, including precipitation, evaporation, runoff, 
soil moisture, and other aspects, resulting in a redistribution of water resources over time and 
space, as well as changes in the overall water resource quantity. This will increase the frequency 
and intensity of extreme disasters such as floods and droughts, exacerbating the problem of regional 
water scarcity. These changes will have a serious impact on various aspects of human society, 
including the development, utilization, planning, and management of water resources, and further 
impact the sustainable development of the ecological environment and social economy 
(Organization 1987). As awareness of the impact of climate change on various components of the 
water cycle grows, regions across the world are increasingly recognizing the strategic importance of 
understanding these effects in managing this critical resource. The key research areas are 
elaborated regarding the impact of climate change on hydrology and water resources, as well as the 
current issues and weak points in the research (Li, Zhang et al. 2013). It also proposed future 
research trends and key problems that need to be addressed. Another article focused on using 
hydrological models to assess the impact of climate change on water resources in Iran (Abbaspour, 
Faramarzi et al. 2009). 
Although reservoirs and lakes only occupy a small fraction of the Earth's land surface around 
3.7%(Verpoorter, Kutser et al. 2014),  they provide significant social and economic benefits in the 
form of hydropower generation, flood control, and water resource distribution (Intralawan, Wood 
et al. 2018). They are a critical element in the hydrological and biogeochemical cycles and an 
important tool for the management of water resources. Over the past few decades, nearly 50,000 
large dams have been constructed worldwide, with a total reservoir surface area exceeding 6,500 
square kilometers(Nilsson 2009). Many new reservoirs are still under construction or planned 
(Lehner, Liermann et al. 2011). 
The construction and management of reservoirs have significantly altered natural hydrological 
regimes and played an important role in regional socioeconomic development. Many research 
works have pointed out that water resources will face mounting pressure due to population and 
economic expansion, as well as the impact of climate change and growing demands for energy and 
food(Haddeland, Heinke et al. 2014). Releases from reservoirs are a critical variable because they

affect downstream hydrological processes and available water supply (Liu, Song et al. 2020) and 
significantly impact downstream ecosystems(Shin, Pokhrel et al. 2019). For example, Dams can also 
damage aquatic habitats, obstruct species movement, and obstruct downstream nutrient and 
sediment transport, resulting in severe damage to natural riverine production systems, particularly 
fisheries, flood recession agriculture, and dry season grazing (Dams 2000, Nilsson, Reidy et al. 2005, 
Richter, Postel et al. 2010).Therefore, monitoring of reservoirs is particularly important. 
Traditionally, water levels in lakes and reservoirs have been measured through onsite measurement 
stations installed near river mouths, bridges, weirs, and sluice gates. However, in recent years, the 
number of onsite measurement stations worldwide has decreased (Alsdorf, Rodríguez et al. 2007, 
Duan and Bastiaanssen 2013).Many remote lakes and reservoirs have never been monitored, 
especially in developing countries (Zhang, Xu et al. 2006, Medina, Gomez-Enri et al. 2008). Even if 
water levels or flows are monitored, such information is seldom accessible free of charge due to 
strategic, political, commercial, or national legislative reasons (Lehner and Döll 2004, Downing, 
Prairie et al. 2006). Therefore, exploring an alternative method for discharge estimation is crucial. 
Currently, the combination of hydrological modeling techniques and remote sensing data collection 
will be a valuable approach. 
Satellite remote sensing altimetry is a new type of space-based observation technology that has 
emerged with the development of computer technology and spatial information technology. It can 
rapidly cover the entire globe and can detect various natural phenomena and their changes on the 
earth's oceans and land areas over large regions, with high precision, long periods, and periodicity 
(Fu and Cazenave 2000). In recent years, with the continuous improvement of satellite altimetry 
technology, its wide application in ocean studies has provided a new technical means for monitoring 
inland lakes, reservoir, rivers, and other hydrological aspects (Troitskaya, Rybushkina et al. 2012). 
Compared with traditional methods of measuring lake water levels, radar altimetry satellites (such 
as Poseidon, Envisat, Jason-1, Jason-2, Sentinel 3, etc.) have the advantages of real-time and all-
weather monitoring. Especially for rivers and lakes in high mountains, plateaus or remote areas with 
a lack of ground-based hydrological observation stations, satellite remote sensing can provide real-
time monitoring of water level changes, filling in the gaps of missing lake water level observation 
data, and providing great convenience for the establishment of multi-year time series of lake water 
levels (Le Gac, Boy et al. 2021). Using satellite remote sensing imagery, such as Landsat TM/ETM+ 
and the new Sentinel series from Copernicus, can monitor changes in the water area of lakes over 
long time series and large spatial scales (Schmitt 2020). 
Thailand, as a tropical country with abundant water resources, has a long history of managing its 
water resources for multiple purposes, including irrigation, hydropower, and domestic water supply 
(Apipattanavis, Ketpratoom et al. 2018). Reservoirs play a critical role in this process (Tebakari, 
Yoshitani et al. 2012). 
Fortunately, detailed measurement records of large reservoirs within Thailand are publicly available 
as open data, allowing us to obtain them for free. Therefore, in this project, we conducted hydraulic 
analysis of large reservoirs within Thailand by simulating storage change and reservoir release based 
on remote sensing data, while also validating our findings with the measurement data.

1.2 Research status 
Water surface elevation  
Water surface elevation changes are an important indicator for evaluating the water balance of 
reservoirs. Early monitoring of water level changes mainly relied on on-site observation data from 
hydrological stations. However, lakes or reservoirs located in remote areas are difficult for humans 
to access, it is impossible to obtain their water level information. Satellite altimetry technology has 
significant advantages compared to traditional observation methods. For example, satellite 
altimetry operates in near-real time and covers most area in the planet. Different satellite altimetry 
missions provides measurements of global lakes, reservoirs, rivers and wetlands (Birkett 1998). 
The concept of satellite altimetry was first proposed by the renowned American geodesist W.M. 
Kaula at the Solid Earth and Ocean Physics Conference in Williams Town in 1969 (Technology, Center 
et al. 1970). In 1973, the US National Aeronautics and Space Administration launched the first active 
microwave remote sensing satellite, Skylab, which carried the S-193 sensor that served as both an 
altimeter and scatterometer, and could observe environmental parameters of sea surface 
measurements to increase people's understanding of the ocean (Center 1978). Although the initial 
observation results were not ideal due to the precision of satellite data, radar altimetry technology 
quickly developed, and various types of radar altimeters were successively carried on professional 
altimetry satellites such as ERS-1, TOPEX/Poseidon, ERS-2, GFO, Jason-1, Jason-2, Envisat, Sentinel, 
and so on. Figure 1 shows Timeline of modern radar altimetry missions. Satellite altimetry has the 
unparalleled advantage of providing accurate observation values of surface heights for lakes, rivers, 
and other features, through large-scale, all-weather, and repeatable observations from space, which 
cannot be matched by other monitoring methods. The use of altimeter data to study changes in 
inland water is a new development in the application field of altimetry technology in recent years 
(Le Gac, Boy et al. 2021). 
TOPEX/POSEIDON is the first space mission designed and executed specifically to study the 
circulation of the world's oceans. The satellite was launched in 1992 (Fu, Christensen et al. 1994). In 
1991 and 1995, ERS-1 and ERS-2 satellites were launched, respectively, (Attema, Duchossois et al. 
1998) and high-resolution global ocean circulation maps were drawn using T/P and ERS-1 and ERS-
2 data by the joint efforts of Ducet and others (Ducet, Le Traon et al. 2000). In 2001 and 2008, Jason-
1 and Jason-2 were launched as successors to the T/P satellite, maintaining high precision altimeter 
measurements provided by TOPEX/Poseidon (T/P) since 1992 and continuing the mission of 
monitoring global sea surface topography (Ménard, Fu et al. 2003, Lambin, Morrow et al. 2010). The 
paper conducted an absolute calibration test of the Jason-1 and Jason-2 height measurement data 
using tide gauge stations and GPS buoys, estimating the accuracy of satellite altimetry, and 
obtaining results for monitoring water level changes in the  the Marblehead lake of North America  
(Cheng, Kuo et al. 2010). Envisat, a successor to ERS-1 and ERS-2, was launched by the European 
Space Agency in 2002, carrying the RA-2 next-generation radar altimeter, which achieved a relative 
measurement accuracy of centimeters compared to the original meter level (Da Silva, Calmant et al. 
2010). The dynamic water level changes were monitored in Lake Izabal, Guatemala using Envisat 
RA-2 data and actual measurements, and analyzed their relationship with climate change (Medina,

Gomez-Enri et al. 2008). The Envisat radar altimeter measurements are also used from 2002 to 2009 
to generate time series of water level changes for Qinghai Lake and the northeastern Tibetan 
Plateau's Ngoring Lake and studied their relationships with precipitation and temperature changes 
(Lee, Shum et al. 2011). CryoSat-2, a high-precision radar satellite launched by the European Space

thickness of sea ice and changes in ice cover at the North and South Poles (Laxon, Giles et al. 2013). 
High-precision new satellites such as CryoSat-2 satellites can continuously monitor the water level 
of plateau lakes. In the paper(Song, Ye et al. 2015), Nam Co is selected as the research object, and 
based on the measured water level data, the accuracy of the altimetry data of CryoSat-2 satellite 
(moderate ground feet) is evaluated. The Sentinel-3 mission, as part of the European Commission's 
Copernicus program, is designed as a two-satellite constellation in polar orbit, consisting of Sentinel-
3A (S3A) and Sentinel-3B (S3B), to provide high-resolution operational ocean and land monitoring 
services. It is the first satellite altimeter to provide global coverage in SAR mode, enabling finer 
along-track resolution observations (Jiang, Nielsen et al. 2020). Kittel, Jiang et al. (2021) extracted 
Sentinel-3 observations of key wetlands in the Zambezi River Basin, and validated them with in-situ 
measurements, demonstrating the clear seasonal patterns captured in Sentinel-3 Water Surface 
Elevation (WSE) and highlighting the benefits of the dual-satellite constellation's high 
spatiotemporal resolution. 
Satellite altimetry observations are not only susceptible to external interference in reservoir 
observations in narrow valleys but are also limited by sparse spatial coverage and long revisit times 
(Zhang, Jiang et al. 2020). Therefore, the data from previous studies using Jason series or CryoSat-2 
are very limited for altimetry observations (Han, Long et al. 2020). The Sentinel-3 satellite altimetry 
is a very promising and excellent option (Jiang, Nielsen et al. 2020, Kittel, Jiang et al. 2021).

Water surface extent 
In recent years, the development of high-resolution remote sensing satellites has provided effective 
data for the monitoring and research of large-scale lake area changes. The data sources used for 
studying lake area changes have evolved from topographic maps, map data, and aerial photographs 
to high-resolution satellite remote sensing images. Commonly used data sources include optical 
remote sensing images such as NOAA/AVHRR, SPOT/VGT, EOS/MODIS, Landsat TM/ETM+, CBERS, 
as well as microwave radar images such as ERS-1, ERS-2, JERS-1, Radarsat, Envisat, ALOS PALSAR, 
Sentinel, etc. Optical remote sensing images contain a large amount of spectral information, and 
the information is relatively easy to extract, which has been widely used in the field of water body 
monitoring. However, due to the susceptibility of optical remote sensing sensors to nighttime and 
cloudy/rainy weather, it is difficult to obtain high-quality remote sensing images, which to some 
extent limits the acquisition of water body information. Microwave radar sensors, on the other 
hand, can work all day and all-weather. Among them, the spaceborne Synthetic Aperture Radar 
(SAR) has great advantages in surface water body monitoring (Li, Dang et al. 2022). 
As early as the 1980s, scholars proposed using remote sensing images and remote sensing methods 
to systematically monitor the changes in lakes and wetlands (Bennett 1987) and studied the 
relationship between these changes and regional or global climate change and human activities. For 
example,Schneider, McGINNIS Jr et al. (1985) analyzed the Chad Basin in Africa using data from 
LANDSAT and NOAA polar-orbiting satellites, tracking changes in the boundaries of lakes and basins. 
They recorded the relationship between the lake conditions and recent climate records. Changed 
detection algorithms are used  in Landsat MSS data to monitor and analyze Egypt's Brullus Lake, and 
the results showed that the shrinkage of the lake area was severe due to land reclamation and other 
reasons (Guirguis, Hassan et al. 1996). Using Landsat TM/ETM+ data, a new water index was used 
to monitor the changes in the areas of five East African Great Rift Valley lakes (Ouma and Tateishi 
2006). Multi-temporal Landsat remote sensing images are also used to analyze that inland lakes in 
Alaska were decreasing in area between 1979 to 2019 (Rover, Ji et al. 2012). Sentinel-2 data with 
high revisit frequency (about every five days) and high spatial resolution (up to 10 m) is used to 
estimate monthly surface water extent in France (Yang, Qin et al. 2020) and is also used to provide 
a spatially and temporally consistent view of the 2018 hydrological drought in Germany as observed 
from Sentinel-2 satellite images (Wieland and Martinis 2020). 
With the development of microwave remote sensing technology, using SAR data to extract water 
information has become an important research direction. Bwangoy, Hansen et al. (2010) used 
TM/ETM+ optical images, JERS-1 SAR data, and SRTM data to map wetlands in the Congo River Basin 
using a decision tree algorithm. Water bodies in the Peace-Athabasca Delta are identified and 
mapped using Radarsat and SPOT images, and proposed that the fusion of SAR data and optical 
remote sensing images could complement each other's strengths and be more advantageous for 
water information extraction (Töyrä, Pietroniro et al. 2001). Druce, Tong et al. (2021) explained a 
method called GRAS-Surface Water Dynamics (GRAS-SWD) which using a fusion approach 
combining optical and SAR data is used to process the SWE data in detail.

Reservoir storage change and release 
Reservoir water storage changes can be esimtated by satellite altimetry and reservoir surface area. 
Many studies have focused on the changes in reservoir storage by combining satellite altimetry and 
surface water extent derived from satellite imagery. This approach is used to quantify the reservoir 
storage in the Yarmouk Basin in southern Syria, where monitoring has been hindered by the ongoing 
civil war (Avisse, Tilmant et al. 2017). The volume changes of Lake Mead (U.S.A.), Lake Tana 
(Ethiopia) and Lake IJssel (The Netherlands) are estimated by combining satellite altimetry data from 
four different datasets (GRLM, RLH, HydroWeb, ICESat-GLAS) with satellite remote sensing images 
(Duan and Bastiaanssen 2013). Gao, Birkett et al. (2012) estimated the storage values of 34 global 
reservoirs from 1992 to 2010 based on satellite altimetry and the estimated water surface areas are 
highly correlated with observed storage estimates.  
Reservoir release can be estimated using water balance equations, where the outflow is equal to 
inflow ducted by storage changes. To monitor the water balance of Lake Victoria in East Africa, 
providing a deeper understanding of the water balance of lakes in East Africa Satellite, remote 
sensing data is used to monitor changes in lake level and water surface area, and combined with 
meteorological data and hydrological models to calculate changes in the water volume of the lake 
(Swenson and Wahr 2009). When utilizing satellite data in conjunction with hydrological models to 
calculate water balance of reservoirs, it is necessary to have a reliable inflow simulation model 
unless upstream observed inflow data is available (Muala, Mohamed et al. 2014). For ungauged 
basins, the combination of satellite observations and a hydrological model can provide accurate 
estimation of reservoir outflow, which is of great importance for watershed water resources 
management and planning (Han, Long et al. 2020). Recently, Zheng et al. explored the method of 
using multi-source satellite data and hydrological modeling techniques to estimate reservoir release. 
The study showed that the combination of multi-source satellite data and hydrological modeling 
can provide accurate estimation of reservoir release, even in data-scarce regions, highlighting the 
potential of utilizing these techniques to support water resources management and planning (Duan 
and Bastiaanssen 2013). In Southeast Asia, previous research has focused on utilizing multi-source 
satellite data and hydrological modeling techniques to estimate flow changes in the Mekong River 
Basin. In a recent study, Zhang et al. investigated the changes in flow regime in the Lancang River, 
which is a crucial river in the Mekong River Basin. The study employed an integrated modeling 
approach by combining a hydrological model with multiple Earth observation datasets to analyze 
the variations in the flow pattern and water resource supply in the Lancang River Basin (Zhang, Jiang 
et al. 2023). In another article, satellite data was used to examine the changes in water level and 
volume of reservoirs in the region, and machine learning algorithms were employed to identify and 
analyze their operational patterns, exploring the potential of using space observations to monitor 
reservoirs in the Mekong Basin (Bonnema and Hossain 2017). In the context of Thai reservoirs, there 
is currently a scarcity of research on the use of satellite data and hydraulic models to estimate 
reservoir releases. Therefore, exploring this method could be a valuable direction for further 
investigation and could potentially yield important insights for water resource management in the 
region. he research on estimating reservoir releases by combining satellite data and hydraulic 
models for Thai reservoirs is still very limited, making it a promising direction for future exploration.

1.3 Objective 
The aim of this study is to reconstruct reservoir discharge using a combination of multi-satellite data 
and hydrological modeling techniques. To monitor the operation of a large reservoir in Thailand, we 
employ WSE observations from Sentinel-3 and SWE observations generated using a novel algorithm 
that merges optical and SAR data. Our approach leverages advanced WSE and SWE observations to 
enhance the precision of reservoir storage monitoring. We integrate satellite observations with 
historical monitoring of ganged stations to restrict model parameters and boost model performance 
in regions with limited data. This project intends to address the following key research questions: 
1. How accurate and reliable are the simulated reservoir releases based on hydrological        
modeling and multi-source satellite datasets? 
2. Is it possible to enhance model performance and constrain model parameters in basins with 
limited data by utilizing satellite observations? 
3. How do the simulated inflows and simulated Reservoir Water Storage Capacity (RWSC) affect 
the accuracy of discharge estimates? 
4. What is the impact of reservoir regulation on the flow regimes of the reservoir? 
The research in this paper mainly includes five parts: 
The first part is the water level changes of major reservoirs in Thailand based on satellite altimetry 
data. This chapter uses the height data from Sentinel-3 A/B radar satellites to monitor the water 
level changes of Thai reservoirs from 2016 to 2022. The relative water levels of each reservoir are 
calculated based on the principle of satellite altimetry and the water level formulas provided by 
various satellite data, and a long-term lake water level sequence based on the altimetry satellite is 
constructed. Different geoid models are used to eliminate differences with measured data, and 
comparisons are made. 
The second part of this study focuses on monitoring the area changes of large reservoirs in Thailand 
using optical satellite imagery. The study makes use of Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 remote sensing 
imagery as the primary data sources to monitor the area changes of reservoirs over a period of time, 
specifically from 2017 to 2022. The purpose of this part of the study is to determine how well the 
satellite imagery can be used to accurately monitor the area changes of large reservoirs in Thailand. 
To validate the accuracy of the satellite imagery, the results obtained from the imagery are 
compared with measured data from other sources. 
The third part of the process involves establishing the NAM Model which is used to simulate the 
inflow of the reservoir. This is achieved by taking into consideration the rainfall and evaporation 
data that has been recorded at the observation stations of the reservoir. To ensure the accuracy of 
the model, it is important to calibrate the model parameters using the measured inflow. This 
calibration helps to refine the model by adjusting its parameters to better reflect the actual 
conditions of the reservoir. 
==PAGE:14==
 
8 
The fourth part of this study involves obtaining the L-A-V (water Level - Area - Volume) regression 
curve and equation based on the relationship between the water level and area of the reservoir. 
This is achieved by analyzing the data obtained from the previous stages of the study and plotting 
the water level and area data on a graph. Once the L-A-V regression curve and equation have been 
obtained, it is possible to calculate the volume changes of the reservoir during a certain period.  
The fifth and final part of this study involves calculating the release of the reservoir based on the 
water balance equation, simulated inflow, and volume changes. This is important for verifying the 
reliability of the data obtained from the previous stages of the study. The calculated release is then 
compared with the actual recorded release to ensure reliability of the data, this part of the study 
also focuses on analyzing the regulation of river water levels by reservoirs and their downstream 
impacts.  
The results obtained from this part of the study can be used to improve the management of water 
resources and ensure that water is used in a sustainable manner. By understanding the regulation

Sentinel-3 is an Earth observation satellite mission specifically designed for Copernicus to ensure 
the long-term collection and operational delivery of high-quality measurements to GMES ocean,

The mission foresees a series of satellites, each having a 7-year lifetime, over a 20-year period

with a return period of 27 d (Donlon, Berruti et al. 2012). Sentinel-3 is equipped with a SAR altimeter 
and has an advanced open-loop tracking mode to measure the water surface elevation of inland 
water bodies (Jiang, Nielsen et al. 2020). 
In this project, the level 2 data of Sentinel-3 altimetry can be downloaded from the Scihub website

generated by the Sentinel missions, which are part of the European Union's Copernicus program, an 
Earth Observation initiative aimed at providing accurate and up-to-date environmental information 
(Tona, Bua et al. 2018).

3.2 Satellite altimetry data 
Altimetry is a technique for determining height from space using a radar altimeter. It works by sending high-
frequency signals from the satellite to the Earth's surface and measuring the time it takes for the 
echo to return. This measurement, called a "range R measurement," is proportional to the satellite's 
altitude above the ground. The range measurement is then corrected for various environmental and 
instrumental factors to calculate the surface elevation relative to a reference surface. The final 
elevation is obtained by subtracting the range from the satellite's altitude, which is precisely known. 
When the radar altimeter transmits a pulse, it travels towards the Earth's surface and the beam 
begins to widen, resulting in a "footprint size" on the surface. The footprint size refers to the area 
illuminated by the beam and can be several kilometers wide. Within this footprint, there will be 
numerous echoes from different heights reflecting off the surface, each with a different travel time 
(Benveniste and Picot 2011). 
The main operation process of satellite radar altimetry is divided into tracking and re-tracking. In 
the tracking stage, the altimeter generally uses two different tracking modes, one is "Closed-loop" 
(CL) mode, also known as autonomous mode, which requires the acquisition stage to set the echo 
window for reception. This is a real-time self-correction method, in which the altimeter sends radar 
pulses to the earth, measures the return time of the echo to determine the distance between the 
satellite and the earth's surface. If the measurement result is different from the expected height, 
the satellite's onboard computer adjusts the satellite's position and velocity in real-time to improve 
the accuracy of the next measurement (Desjonquères, Carayon et al. 2010). However, it has been 
gradually noticed that the quality of altimetry data in inland water bodies is often poor or even 
missing, and the altimeter often fails to obtain the correct signal from the water surface 
(Biancamaria, Frappart et al. 2017). 
Therefore, for recent missions, the DORIS navigation system and DEM are used for repositioning the 
analysis window (open-loop tracking). The open-loop mode involves coupling the orbit position 
information provided by the onboard real-time navigator with the terrain elevation data stored on 
the SRAL instrument along the track in a one-dimensional digital elevation model (DEM). In this 
configuration, the Ho and HPR parameters that define the range window can be directly calculated 
based on the satellite ephemeris (position and velocity) contained in the navigation message and 
the height values from the DEM. This feature is very helpful for inland water bodies (Le Roy, 
Deschaux-Beaume et al. 2007). 
The waveform undergoes processing on the ground to determine the duration of travel between 
the satellite and the surface, and back again, which is designated as "retracking". This is 
accomplished through fitting the waveform's shape to a model function. The divergence between 
the leading edge of the waveform and the altimeter tracking gate, known as the "epoch," is 
computed in reference to the predicted travel time, and utilized to adjust the satellite's range 
measurement. The tracker range is then combined with the retrieved epoch to obtain the final range 
measurement. The retracking algorithm can be either physically grounded or purely empirical in 
nature, and a variety of algorithms are accessible depending on the objectives of the study 
(Benveniste and Picot 2011, Gommenginger, Thibaut et al. 2011).

Based on different Retracking Algorithms, we obtain different water surface elevations (WSEs). These can 
mainly be divided into two categories. One is Narrow Primary Peak Threshold Retracking algorithm 
(NPPTR). This retracking algorithm eliminates the noise in the waveform, extracts more accurate

Another one is SAMOSA (SAR Altimetry Mode Studies and Applications), which using SAR (Synthetic 
Aperture Radar) altimetry to measure the height of the water surface. The basic principle behind 
SAR altimetry is the measurement of the time delay (or "two-way travel time") between when a 
radar signal is transmitted from a satellite or aircraft and when it is reflected back to the receiver. 
When the radar signal is transmitted towards the water surface, a portion of the signal will be 
reflected back to the receiver by the surface. The time delay between when the signal is transmitted 
and when it is received is directly related to the distance between the transmitter and the surface. 
By measuring this time delay, it is possible to determine the height of the surface (Ray, Martin-Puig 
et al. 2014). 
In this case the geoid model is the EGM 2008. EGM08 (Earth Gravitational Model 2008) is a global 
geoid model that represents the Earth's gravity field and is used to determine the precise height of 
a location on the Earth's surface above or below sea level. The geoid is the surface of equal 
gravitational potential that is defined by the gravity field of the Earth. 
The theory behind EGM08 is based on the gravity field of the Earth, which is a representation of the 
distribution of mass within the Earth. The gravity field is affected by both the internal structure of 
the Earth and external factors such as the presence of oceans and mountains. EGM08 uses a 
combination of satellite and ground-based measurements of the gravity field to create a highly 
accurate model of the geoid (Pavlis, Holmes et al. 2012). 
EGM08 is widely used in various applications such as geodesy, surveying, mapping, navigation, and 
oceanography. It is considered to be one of the most accurate models of the geoid currently 
available and is continuously updated with new measurements and improvements in technology. 
However, in Thailand, the in-situ data obtained is based on Thailand's own geoid model. The 
Thailand Geoid Model 2017 (TGM2017) is a hybrid geoid model with one arc-second spatial 
resolution. The model includes the latest gravity dataset from ground and airborne gravity 
measurements activities between 2015 and 2016. The combination of global geopotential models 
EGM2008/GOCE and DTU13 contribute to the TGM2017's long-wavelength information, including 
the sea areas. TGM2017 was tested using 100 GNSS heights with respect to the national vertical 
reference (Kolak-1915) established in 1915 (Dumrongchai and Duangdee 2019).

the regional variations in the gravity field, which can lead to improved accuracy in height

3.3 Satellite surface water extent data 
The SWE of reservoirs can be estimated using satellite imagery. A method called GRAS-Surface

optical and SAR data is used to process the SWE data. 
In the pre-processing of Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 data, the Sentinel-1 dataset on GEE has 
undergone standard pre-processing procedures, including orthorectification, radiometric 
calibration and atmospheric correction. However, since the GEE Sentinel-1 dataset does not apply 
terrain flattening, we use the Digital Elevation Model (DEM) to mask areas affected by radar shadow 
and layover effects (Druce, Tong et al. 2021). In the case of Sentinel-2 images, a machine learning 
algorithm called s2cloudless is employed to classify and remove clouds, and the Sen2Cor scene 
classification (SCL) method is used to remove snow (Main-Knorn, Pflug et al. 2017). Additionally, 
information regarding solar azimuth angles and known cloud positions, as well as cloud shadow 
algorithms are used to mask cloud shadows (Hollstein, Segl et al. 2016). Furthermore, solar azimuth 
angles and the GLO-30 digital elevation model (DEM) are used to remove topographic projection 
shadows.  
Then the surface water is extracted from the optical and SAR data using various image processing 
techniques such as thresholding, edge detection, and region growing. Finally using monthly data as 
inputs to a logistic regression model. a maximum surface water extent mask was created from the 
Sentinel-2 imagery, covering the entire time-series. This final mask is used to identify the maximum 
extent of surface water across the entire period of analysis. Detailed data processing was described 
by (Druce, Tong et al. 2021).

3.5 Rainfall-runoff model 
Rainfall-runoff model is a mathematical model used to simulate the movement of water through a 
watershed. It is used to estimate the amount of water that flows into a stream or river as a result of 
precipitation. The model takes into account various factors such as precipitation, evapotranspiration, 
infiltration, and storage in order to estimate the amount of runoff(Sitterson, Knightes et al. 2018). 
The NAM model (Nielsen and Hansen 1973) is one of the most basic rainfall-runoff models in MIKE 
Hydro, it is widely used for simulating streamflow in catchments. The NAM model is a simplified, 
conceptual model that simulates the runoff generated by rainfall. It divides the flow into three 
components: overland flow (surface flow), interflow (subsurface flow), and base flow. The model 
uses a set of mathematical equations to describe the behavior of the land phase of the hydrological 
cycle. The structure of NAM model is in Figure 6. The simulation in the NAM model is based on four 
interconnected storage compartments: surface storage, groundwater storage, root zone storage, 
and snow storage. The model takes into account the different types of storage and how they interact, 
in order to estimate the amount of water that enters and leaves the catchment (Agrawal and 
Desmukh 2016). The model's simulation of runoff is based on the observations of in-situ runoff and 
the model's representation of the hydrological processes in the catchment. 
The NAM model uses a number of parameters to simulate the hydrological processes in the 
catchment, however, by default it considers only 9 parameters automatically. These parameters are 
==PAGE:28==
 
22 
adjusted during the calibration process to achieve the best possible relationship between the 
simulated and observed discharge. The parameters range are shown in Table 2. The explanation of 
the parameters are showing below: 
Umax: Maximum water content in surface Storage. 
Lmax : Maximum water content in root zone storage. 
CQOF: Overland flow runoff coefficient. 
CKIF: Time constant for routing interflow. 
TG : Root zone threshold value for groundwater recharge. 
CK1, CK2: Time constants for routing overland flow. 
TOF: Root zone threshold value for overland flow. 
TIF: Root zone threshold value for interflow. 
CKBF: Time constant for routing base flow. 
 
In this project, we aim to simulate the water flow in a reservoir system by using only precipitation 
and evaporation as inputs. Adjust parameters according to in-situ inflow to get simulated inflow. 
This approach simplifies the modelling process by only requiring two inputs, rather than 
incorporating multiple inputs from various sources. 
To accurately estimate the final parameters of a NAM model, the model must be calibrated using a 
series of hydrological observations. The automatic calibration, also known as auto-calibration, is 
used to optimize two objective functions: (1) minimizing the water balance error (%WBL) to ensure 
agreement between the average simulated and observed runoff, and (2) minimizing the overall Root 
Mean Square Error (RMSE) to ensure overall agreement in the shape of the hydrograph. The goal of 
auto-calibration is to improve the accuracy of the NAM model's predictions by fine-tuning its 
parameters (Hafezparast, Araghinejad et al. 2013). 
The calibration is implemented by giving all objectives equal weight and using the shuffled complex 
evolution algorithm to search for the optimal solution. This helps to avoid getting stuck in local 
optimums. The calibration scheme includes the overall volume error and the overall root mean 
square error (RMSE) performance statistics (Madsen 2000, Madsen 2003) .

4.1 Evaluation and analysis of WSE datasets 
Using in-situ data from water level gauges to validate satellite altimetry measurements can be 
challenging due to the relative accuracy of the gauge measurements. However, it's important to 
keep in mind that a direct comparison between the heights measured by gauges and those 
measured by satellite altimetry can be difficult, as the location, reference height, and vertical datum 
of the gauges may not always be precisely known or may even be unknown. This can result in offsets 
between water level time series from gauges and altimetry, which must be taken into account 
during the validation process. Therefore, we convert satellite data to TGM07 and compare it with 
the actual recorded data in Thailand. 
The performance of the Sentinel-3 has been validated over 13 reservoirs (9 in Figure 8 b) series, 
another 4 are shown in Appendix). Altimetric data show good agreement with gauge records. The 
validation results and metrics are listed in Table 3. The results of a comparison between the Samosa 
and NPPT algorithms indicate that while only four RMSE values were below 0.5 in the results 
obtained through Samosa, three of the reservoir data had RMSE values greater than 1. On the other 
hand, the results obtained through the NPPT algorithm were superior to those obtained through 
Samosa, even when three different thresholds were considered.  
In regard to the three thresholds in the NPPT algorithm, the differences in the RMSE results were 
minimal and the results were largely confined within a small range, generally between 0.1 and 1. 
The threshold affects the calculation of Narrow Primary Peak Threshold (NPPT) Retracking by 
determining the minimum peak intensity required to be considered as a primary peak in the 
retracking process. A lower threshold will result in more peaks being detected as primary peaks, 
while a higher threshold will result in fewer peaks being detected.(Davis 1997) This is why in some 
reservoirs, a lower threshold may result in slightly better results compared to a higher threshold, as 
a higher threshold may cause the real primary peak to be missed. In particular, for the Pasak 
reservoir, a threshold of 25 resulted in an RMSE of only 0.46 meters, while a threshold of 80 resulted 
in an RMSE of 2.23 meters. However, at the same time, a lower threshold may lead to the detection 
of more noise or false peaks. For most of the reservoirs studied in this research, results with a higher 
threshold tend to have slightly better RMSE compared to those with a lower threshold. After 
balancing the need to detect as many real primary peaks as possible while minimizing the impact of 
noise or false peaks, we chose data with a threshold of 80 for further analysis.  
Among the 13 reservoirs, except for the Pasak reservoirs, 9 of them showed relatively good RMSE 
results with values less than 0.5. The satellite elevation data and station data for Srinakharin, 
Chulaphon, and Bang Lang reservoirs show a high degree of consistency with small RMSE values, all 
less than 0.2 meters. From the Wachiralongkornin Figure 8 b) series and  Nong Pha Lai, Nam Un, Mae 
Ngat Sombun Chon in Appendix, it can be clearly seen from the graph that the satellite water level 
data of these four reservoirs have high consistency and low RMSE with the station data when water 
elevation at a high level. However, the satellite water level data is not able to accurately measure 
the actual water level when it is at its lowest point, due to the satellite orbit not passing directly 
over the main body of the reservoirs but rather towards the edges or narrow water surfaces with 
==PAGE:33==
 
27 
complex terrain in tributaries. As the dry season arrives and the water level drops to extremely low 
positions, the areas where the satellite orbit passes become land, and the satellite altimetry detects 
the ground signal instead of the actual water surface, causing the satellite height data to always be 
higher than the actual station data at low water levels and unable to accurately measure the actual 
water surface elevation.  
For other reservoirs with moderate RMSE (>0.2), the error mainly comes from the complex shape 
of these reservoirs, with many tributaries, and the satellite height measurement signals are affected 
by noise formed by non-surface signals, resulting in abnormal observation values and causing 
fluctuations in the time series. For the Sirikit reservoir, there was a large time gap from 2017 to the 
end of 2018, which may have been caused by the arrival of Sentinel-3B on its final orbit and 
activation of OLTC in the end of 2018, and the system update and OLTC version upgrade of Sentinel-
3A in early 2019. 
The dry season in Thailand is from December to April, and the wet season is from June to October. 
From the figure, we can see that the water levels of all reservoirs show pronounced seasonal

and then enters the dry season, and the water level will start to drop. We can notice that in almost

4.2 Evaluation and analysis of surface water extent datasets 
In order to validate the satellite data pertaining to water surfaces, it is necessary to compare it 
against measured data. As direct measurements of water surface area are unavailable, the use of 
==PAGE:34==
 
28 
on-site water level area curves is employed. The water surface area is derived by incorporating the 
actual water level readings into the equation of the curves, serving as a proxy for on-site data. This 
derived on-site data is then compared against the satellite data. Figure 8 e) series and Table 4 show 
the results of comparison. 
According to the comparison of the results shown in the image and table, the agreement between 
the satellite measurement of water surface area and the in-situ data is not entirely satisfactory. 
With the exception of the Nam Un reservoir, where the satellite measurement data matches the 
measured data relatively well with a minimal NRMSE value 0.33, there is a significant deviation in 
the results for all the other reservoirs. In the context of a study, the NRMSE of only three reservoirs, 
Wachiralongkorn, Lampao, and Ubonrat, can be considered to be of medium quality, as they exhibit 
NRMSE values lower than 0.6. Conversely, the data for the remaining reservoirs can be considered 
to have subpar quality, as their NRMSE values fall within the range of 0.6 to 1. The situation is worst 
in the case of the Ratchaprapa reservoir, with NRMSE 1.82, where there is no overlap between the 
measured data and the satellite data. The most common problem is that the satellite data fails to 
accurately capture the peak values, especially the high peaks, when compared to the measured 
data.  
However, it can also be noted that despite the considerable error, the trend of the satellite 
measurement results and the in-situ results is consistent and they both exhibit the same seasonal 
patterns, which is a positive aspect. This indicates that although the accuracy of the satellite 
measurement may not always be sufficient to capture the exact minimum area, it can still provide 
valuable information about the general trend and seasonal changes in the water surface area. 
Satellite measurement of water surface area accuracy is influenced by multiple factors, such as the 
unevenness of the water surface. Even with the most advanced satellites and measurement 
technology, it is not possible to measure the actual area of each small lake, river, riverbank, etc., 
because the water surface is uneven, causing a certain degree of error in the measurement results. 
In addition, factors such as weather, clouds, light, etc. may also affect the accuracy of satellite 
measurement. Therefore, the accuracy of satellite measurement of water surface area is limited 
and cannot measure the accurate area. The main reasons for the inability to accurately capture peak 
values in satellite measurement of water surface area include: The presence of vegetation, 
particularly along the edges of reservoirs, significantly affects the satellite data processing. In 
environments with high soil moisture content, the signal decay from the vegetation can impact the 
satellite data processing chain, resulting in misclassification of water bodies as land, thereby 
underestimating the total water area. Additionally, the use of shapefiles to represent the reservoirs 
in the data processing process is limited, as we only provide a rough estimate of the reservoir's 
shape, rather than the maximum area of peak water storage. This also results in an inability to obtain 
accurate peak water surface area measurements. As a result, it is imperative to incorporate robust 
techniques to address these limitations in order to enhance the accuracy of satellite data in 
detecting peak water surface area. 
In Figure 7, we have selected two satellite photos of two reservoirs in December as examples. The 
yellow shape is the shape file used in the processing of water surface data, it is obvious that in Nong 
==PAGE:35==
 
29 
Pha Lai reservoir, the shape file did not cover all the water areas, whereas in Mae Ngat Nombun 
Chon reservoir, there is a large area within the yellow shape file that has already formed vegetation 
instead of water body, especially in the peripheral region of the reservoir. 
However, it is important to acknowledge that the use of in-situ data as a sole representation of 
reality may not be fully accurate. While the reservoirs have been constructed for several years and 
the published water level-area curves have been in use for an extended period, without updates, 
the actual conditions of the reservoir, such as the water surface and volume, have undergone 
changes over time due to various factors, rendering the original data inaccurate. Furthermore, the 
relationship between in-situ water level and area is based on a 1:50000 topographic map of Thailand 
and some local re-measurements, which are not derived from precise actual measurements and 
thus may introduce substantial inaccuracies. Therefore, it is advisable to treat in-situ data as a 
reference rather than relying on it entirely, as it may not provide a complete and accurate 
representation of the reality.

4.6 Impact of reservoirs  
Figure 11 a) seires shows the impact of the reservoir on the downstream flow, with the blue line 
representing the natural flow without the reservoir, the red and green lines representing the 
simulated and in-situ outflow after the construction of the reservoir, respectively. The figure shows 
that the seasonal fluctuations of the reconstructed flow are consistent with the measured data. 
Moreover, there is a time lag between the reconstructed outflow and the measured natural flow. 
Additionally, the peak reconstructed flow during the flood season is slightly lower than the natural 
flow, however, the opposite is observed during the dry season, indicating that the reservoir plays a 
role in flood control during the flood season and water release during the dry season. 
As seen from the figure, due to its very small volume of less than 1000 million cubic meters, the 
regulation capacity of the Pranburi and Pasak reservoirs is very limited. The regulated flow during 
the dry season is approximately 50 million cubic meters, while during the rainy season it is around 
150 million cubic meters. The effective regulation capacity of other reservoirs can be observed to 
be much better.

During the dry season, the regulation volume of the various Thai reservoirs is higher than the 
natural flow. The largest differences are seen in the regulation of Wachiralongkorn, Sirikit and 
Srinakharin Reservoirs, which during the dry season, have regulation volumes that are triple, 
double, and 6 times higher, respectively, than the natural flow. During the rainy season, the

operated normally, reservoirs can effectively reduce peak flow through controlling water levels, 
thereby mitigating flood damage. At the same time, during drought seasons, the reservoirs can 
supplement insufficient flow, ensuring normal water demands in the region. Through effective 
management of the reservoirs, their flood prevention and drought resistance functions can be 
realized, bringing practical benefits to the region.

5. Discussion 
5.1 Advantages and limitations 
This study proposes a method of using satellite remote sensing data combined with hydrodynamic 
models to estimate the volume changes and releases of a reservoir to analyze its impact on 
downstream rivers. This method has many advantages, first and foremost, satellite data can provide 
real-time and long-term monitoring, such as Sentinel-1, 2, and 3 satellites, which have a mission life 
of 15-20 years, a cycle of 27 days for Sentinel-3 with a spatial resolution of 300 meters (Donlon, 
Berruti et al. 2012), a two-week repeat coverage time for Sentinel-1, and near real-time data is 
expected to be made available to users within 3 hours of observation (Torres, Snoeij et al. 2012), 
while Sentinel-2 has a global revisit time of less than 5 days with a resolution ranging from 10 to 60 
meters (Drusch, Del Bello et al. 2012). Furthermore, the automation of the data collection process 
through satellite measurement reduces the potential for human error, leading to increased accuracy 
in the data and having global coverage, especially in areas that are difficult for human activities to 
reach.. Lastly, the use of satellite measurement is more cost-effective compared to traditional, 
manual monitoring methods, reducing the costs (Rogan and Chen 2004). 
In addition, there are several limitations to the method used in this research. These mainly stem 
from the limitations of input data and the deviation of volume calculation methods. The limitations 
of this method primarily stem from the restrictions of the input data and the volume calculation 
method. Firstly, the error in satellite altimetry data arises from various factors. On one hand, errors 
can be introduced during the geophysical correction of the Earth's surface when computing the 
water surface elevation. On the other hand, the height measurement footprint can be elevated by 
up to 16 meters above the land surface, and the impact of the surrounding land within the footprint 
can alter the accuracy of water level measurement by disrupting the waveform of the height 
measurement instrument (Fu and Cazenave 2000). As a result, the presence of reservoirs can also 
impact the satellite altimetry data, with smaller lakes or reservoirs potentially yielding larger RMSE 
for the water level estimates (Schwatke, Dettmering et al. 2015). In addition to these factors, the 
accuracy of the measurement is also determined by many other factors, such as the surrounding 
topography, surface waves, winter ice coverage, water body shape, and the position of the altimeter 
orbit (Busker, de Roo et al. 2019). This is why some large reservoirs in the present project may have 
higher RMSE values than smaller reservoirs. Additionally, the error of the reservoir surface area data 
is also a significant part, as we have already analyzed the error of the surface area data in section 
4.3.  
Volume change and inflow are two crucial components that impact the calculation of release, with 
WSE and SWE affecting the volume change of the reservoir and inflow being derived from the 
simulated results of the NAM model. In terms of the errors of the NAM model, there are several 
aspects to consider. Firstly, meteorological data input. The accuracy of the NAM model results is 
heavily dependent on the quality and accuracy of the meteorological data input. The meteorological 
data we used in this project is mainly from precipitation records of the observation stations near 
the reservoir and was integrated based on the proportion of the area covered by the observation 
station. Therefore, the accuracy of the meteorological data cannot be guaranteed. Secondly, the 
==PAGE:63==
 
57 
complexity of the model is also crucial. In this project, we only input meteorological data, and did 
not consider factors such as ground storage and snow zones, which simplifies the model and may 
have an impact. Finally, the calibration of parameters. We only used observed inflow to 
automatically calibrate the parameters of the model, and these parameters are not always well 
understood, and their values may have a significant impact on the model's results. 
In addition to the limitations of the data, the display of the method for calculating release is also a 
critical aspect, as water level and area observation values may involve different dates, with one 
being the data for the beginning of the month and the other being the data for the end of the month. 
This would result in increased uncertainty in regression, which has different impacts for different 
reservoirs. For a lake where the water level changes significantly within a month, this uncertainty 
increases. In the Eq.6, we only consider the inflow, volume change, precipitation, and evaporation 
and ignore the unknown leakage, which also affects the results. In this project, Thailand is a humid 
area, and the evaporation volume is very small compared to the inflow and can be ignored. 
However, in other areas, such as arid regions, the evaporation volume would be a potential key 
variable, so if the same method is used for research in different regions, a comprehensive analysis 
should be conducted based on actual conditions. 
5.2 Future application 
The use of remote sensing data to calculate reservoir release is a highly effective method that has 
numerous future applications. This method can provide accurate, timely, and comprehensive data, 
which is crucial for making informed decisions in various fields. The most important application is 
the advancement of remote sensing technology, which creates more opportunities for water bodies 
that are either unmonitored or have inaccurate or unusable ground-measured data. For example, 
the Chulaphon Reservoir, whose inflow, and release data recorded in observed datasets are 
inaccurate and unavailable. The Figure 12 shows the simulation of the reservoir's release volume and 
its role in regulating the river using satellite data. The figure shows the correlation between the 
volume changes of the Chulaphon Reservoir and the precipitation, which follows a seasonal pattern. 
The release rate is observed to have a sustained low from 2019 to 2020, which is in line with the 
changes in SWE and WSE. Despite its small capacity of only over 200 MCM, the reservoir can mitigate 
peak flows as demonstrated in graphs C and D, however, its ability to regulate flows is limited. 
Typically, models use ground-based measurements for model calibration, however, these data 
points may not always be accurate, thus, satellite data provides a wider range of data sets to 
calibrate the parameters of hydrological models. Such as using water elevation and river width data 
from satellite datasets to calibration the 1D hydrodynamic river models (Jiang, Westphal 
Christensen et al. 2021). 
Another of the key areas where this method can be applied is in water resource management. With 
the help of satellite measurement data, water resource management departments can better assess 
the release of reservoirs, thereby allowing them to better manage water resources. This can be 
particularly useful in regions where water resources are scarce or where water management 
practices are crucial for maintaining a sustainable water supply. Sheffield, Wood et al. (2018)  describe 
==PAGE:64==
 
58 
the current and potential future role of satellite remote sensing in improving water resource 
management in data-scarce regions. 
In addition, satellite measurement data can also be used for disaster monitoring (Schumann, 
Brakenridge et al. 2018). In the event of a disaster, such as a flood, satellite measurement data can 
help monitor the flood conditions of reservoirs, thereby enabling better prevention of disasters. This 
can be especially useful in regions where natural disasters are frequent or where rapid response is 
crucial to minimize damage and loss. 
In conclusion, the method of using remote sensing data to calculate reservoir release has numerous 
future applications, providing accurate, timely, and comprehensive data that can be applied in 
multiple fields. By leveraging this method, decision-makers can make better decisions, ensuring a 
sustainable water supply and reducing the negative impact of reservoir releases on the surrounding 
environment.

6. Conclusion 
This study evaluates the ability to reconstruct reservoir releases using various satellite dataset and 
hydrological modeling techniques to address poorly monitored reservoirs. The study focused on 
large reservoirs in Thailand and simulated releases for 9 reservoirs based on volume change 
calculated from WSE and SWE and inflow simulated by NAM model. The main findings are as 
follows: 
Satellite-based methods have achieved satisfactory results in estimating reservoir volume change 
and releases, thereby supporting further study of unmonitored reservoirs. The performance of 
discharge estimation is variable and greatly influenced by the accuracy of inflow and volume 
change estimates. The volume change was calculated from Sentinel3 WSE and Sentinel1 and 
Sentinel2 SWE datasets. The results indicate that the accuracy of volume change estimation is 
greatly influenced by the satellite data SWE. Both volume change and inflow have a significant 
impact on the accuracy of release calculation. 
Accurate estimation of discharge during reservoir water storage overloading is of significant 
importance to hydrologists and local management officials. Satellite-based reservoir state 
estimation (e.g., releases and volume change) is expected to advance further research plans to 
understand the impact of reservoirs on river systems.
