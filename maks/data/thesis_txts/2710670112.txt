Abstract
This thesis investigates and compares the performance of Plug-and-Play (PnP) Priors
to that of total variation (TV) regularization in tomographic image reconstruction. The
performance of the two methods is assessed on simulated tomographic data with varying
relative noise levels and projections, respectively.
An implementation of TV regularized least-squares was considered using the Alternat-
ing Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). The implementation was verified against
software to establish the correctness of the implementation. The PnP framework was
then implemented, also using ADMM. The PnP implementation uses two denoisers,
Block-Matching and 3D Filtering (BM3D) [16], and DnCNN [57], as implicit priors, re-
spectively.
Preliminary experiments were conducted on simulated tomographic data to gain insight
into the behavior of the PnP implementation. Effects of the denoising strength were first
investigated to get a feeling for what would be considered a suitable choice of denoising
strengths when using the PnP method for tomographic image reconstruction. This was
mainly suited for the BM3D denoiser, as it has an adjustable parameter that accounts
for the denoising strength, whereas the DnCNN denoiser only has pre-trained denoising
strengths.
The effects the number of iterations has on the reconstruction quality of PnP were
then investigated. It was generally observed that the RMSE of the PnP reconstructions
tended to stabilize rather quickly.
However, some of the considered reconstructions
seemed to depend on the number of iterations used since a minimum RMSE was at-
tained before the maximum number of iterations was reached.
Randomly initialized input on different scales was considered for the PnP implementa-
tion to see how it affected the reconstruction quality. It was observed that the recon-
struction quality in terms of RMSE did not show any dependence on the initializations.
The performance of PnP and TV regularization was compared on simulated tomographic
data with relative noise levels of 1%, 13%, and 25% while using 256 projections. It was
found that TV regularization performed better than PnP in terms of RMSE at the 1%
and 13% relative noise levels. The PnP reconstruction using DnCNN as the denoiser
performed better than the TV regularized reconstruction at the 25% relative noise level

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
X-ray computed tomography (CT) is a non-invasive scanning technique used to recon-
struct the interior of an object without causing any physical damage to it. X-ray CT is
an essential tool in medicine, where it is used for its diagnostic possibilities [7]. The uses
of X-ray CT also extend beyond that of medicine and has many applications in other
fields, such as airport security, where it is used to detect contraband. [34].
Optimization-based image reconstruction methods are widely used for tomographic im-
age reconstruction. One such method is total variation (TV) regularization. TV reg-
ularization is known for its ability to retain sharp edges in images, something other
optimization-based image reconstruction methods struggle with. TV regularization is
adept at partially or fully removing noise and handling data with limited projections,
which analytical methods like the filtered back projection struggle with.
In 2013, Venkatakrishnan et al. [53] proposed the Plug-and-Play (PnP) priors method
that extended optimization-based image reconstruction methods by incorporating state-
of-the-art denoisers as implicit priors. The PnP method exhibited great results within
many different fields and because of that it has gained increasing popularity ever since it
was introduced. However, the introduction of state-of-the-art denoisers as implicit priors
in the PnP framework has resulted in a loss of interpretation regarding the initially con-
sidered optimization problem, which may no longer exist, as this relies on the denoiser
being related to a proximal map of a closed, proper, and convex function. Similarly,
convergence also becomes more involved due to this, and as a result, the convergence of
PnP is still considered an open question.
Due to the great success and versatility of PnP, it seems like a good choice for to-
mographic image reconstruction. In fact, PnP has already been used for tomographic
image reconstruction [53, 49], but how does it compare to a robust method like TV
regularization?
1.1
Structure of this Thesis
This thesis aims to investigate the use of state-of-the-art denoisers as implicit priors for
tomographic image reconstruction using the PnP framework. The performance of PnP
will be analyzed and assessed based on numerical comparisons with a robust reconstruc-

The main contributions of this thesis are:
• Insight into the behavior of PnP when considering various factors such as denoising
strength, the number of iterations used, and random initializations of the input.
• Comparison of the performance of PnP and TV regularization when considering
simulated tomographic data with varying relative noise levels.
• Comparison of the performance of PnP and TV regularization when considering

This thesis is structured in the following way:
In Chapter 2, we introduce the setting for 2D X-ray CT and Lambert-Beer’s law of at-
tenuation. We then introduce the Radon transform that can be used as a forward model
in tomographic image reconstruction. An inverse of the Radon transform, the filtered
back projection, is then introduced as an analytical reconstruction method. Lastly, we
show how to discretize Lambert-Beer’s law, which gives rise to a discrete linear model
that can be used in practice to obtain reconstructions.
In Chapter 3, we introduce regularization and why it is necessary for most inverse prob-
lems.
Hereafter, two popular choices of regularization methods, Tikhonov- and TV
regularization, are presented.
In Chapter 4, we cover the optimization-based theory needed to introduce the PnP
method. First, an in-depth introduction to the Alternating Direction Method of Mul-
tipliers (ADMM) algorithm is made, which is the engine that runs the PnP method.
Afterward, we display how to formulate total variation regularized least-squares so that
ADMM can be used to solve it. This will be used for comparisons with PnP later in this
thesis.
In Chapter 5, we briefly introduce the field of image denoising and the denoisers that will
be of main interest in this thesis. The denoisers covered in this chapter are the famous
BM3D, long known as the state-of-the-art within image denoising, and a deep learning-
based denoiser, the convolutional neural network DnCNN, which itself has shown re-
markable denoising capabilities.
In Chapter 6, we introduce the PnP method. We start by introducing the method, and
where it originates. We then cover some of the theoretical challenges we are faced with
when using this method. In the end of the chapter, we set the stage for how PnP will
be used for tomographic image reconstruction.

CHAPTER 2
Computed Tomography
This chapter focuses on describing the settings for 2D X-ray computed tomography (CT)
and Lambert-Beer’s law of attenuation, which relates the intensities of X-rays in terms
of the matter they are interacting with. Then we introduce the Radon transform, which
acts as a forward model in tomographic reconstruction for the 2D parallel-beam setup.
We then present (and derive) the filtered back projection as a tool for inverting the
Radon transform and thus a way to obtain reconstructions from measured projections.
Lastly, we show a method for discretizing Lambert-Beer’s law to arrive at a discrete
linear model that can be used in practice for obtaining tomographic reconstructions.
2.1
X-Ray Computed Tomography
Tomography is derived from Greek with tomos meaning to slice and graphite meaning
to write. Tomography is the science of reconstructing an object using cross-sectional
slices. In an applied sense, the acquisition of the cross-sectional slices is obtained in
a non-destructive manner, i.e., without cutting or destroying the object. This is often
called computed tomography (CT) since the reconstructions are obtained using a com-
puter. The cross-sectional slices are typically obtained using penetrating waves such as
X-rays.
In X-ray CT, a source emits an X-ray with an initial intensity that penetrates an object.
As the X-ray penetrates the object, its intensity is reduced. This is known as attenu-
ation. The X-ray then reaches a detector that measures the reduced intensity of the
X-ray. This is known as a projection. The source and detector are then rotated around
the object, giving multiple projections. The process of acquiring these projections can
be done with various setups. The most common setups in 2D X-ray CT are the parallel-
beam and fan-beam setup, which are shown in Figure 2.1. In the parallel-beam setup,
the source sends parallel X-rays fixed at some angle that attenuate as they pass through
the object and reach the detector. The source and detector are then rotated around the
object. From Figure 2.1, it can be seen that there is an inherent symmetry to this setup,
which only requires projections to be made within an angular range of 180 degrees. The
fan-beam differs from the parallel-beam since the source emits diverging X-rays, which
allows for simultaneously obtaining all projections at each projection angle, which is
less time-consuming than the parallel-beam setup. While the symmetry of the parallel-

An object can be described in terms of how X-rays interact with it. X-rays traversing
through materials of large density, such as bone, will be heavily absorbed compared to
material of lower density, such as tissue. We quantify this interaction using the spatially
dependent attenuation coeﬀicient µ(x1, x2). Thus, if we can estimate the attenuation
coeﬀicients of an object, then we can gain information about what material this object
consists of. To obtain the attenuation coeﬀicients, we need a link between the attenua-
tion coeﬀicients and the object the X-rays traverses. Such a relationship was discovered
by Johann Lambert and August Beer and is known as Lambert-Beer’s law of attenuation.

CHAPTER 3
Regularization
In this chapter, we introduce the notion of regularization as a tool to solve inverse
problems that violate Hadamard’s conditions in some form. We will introduce two well-
known regularization methods, namely Tikhonov and TV regularization with the latter
method being of main interest in the thesis.
3.1
The Importance of Regularization
Mathematical problems come in many shapes and sizes, but typically they can all be
divided into two groups of problems, namely direct problems and inverse problems. These
two problems are linked in the sense that they are opposites of one another. Loosely
speaking, in a direct problem, one seeks to find an effect from a cause, whereas, in
an inverse problem, one seeks to find the cause from a given effect. An example of
a direct and inverse problem can be seen in Figure 3.1. When considering a specific
Figure 3.1: An example of a direct and inverse problem in X-ray CT. In the direct prob-
lem, the goal is to determine the X-ray projections of the object (phantom),
which we know precisely. In the corresponding inverse problem, the goal
is to reconstruct the unknown object (phantom) from the measured X-ray
projections.
problem, we typically impose certain conditions to ensure that the problem makes sense
==PAGE:34==
20
3 Regularization
to consider in the first place. Jacques Hadamard popularized such conditions. Hadamard
proposed that every mathematical model describing physical phenomena should satisfy
the following conditions [14]:
1. (Existence) There should be at least one solution.
2. (Uniqueness) The should be at most one solution.
3. (Stability) The solution must depend continuously on the data.
The first two conditions are trivial, whereas the last condition may need some explain-
ing. The reasoning behind requiring a solution to depend continuously on the data is
that data always will be some form of measurable quantity. Moreover, since errors in
measurements are inevitable, it is necessary that minor errors in the data only produce
small errors in the solution. This is ensured from the third condition. If a problem
satisfies the three conditions, it is considered to be a well-posed problem in the sense
of Hadamard. Likewise, if a problem fails to satisfy one or multiple conditions, it is
considered to be an ill-posed problem in the sense of Hadamard.
Although ideal, we can not expect every problem to be well-posed. Many classes of prob-
lems we encounter in the real world are, in fact, ill-posed problems. [33]. An example
of this is tomographic image reconstruction. The problem of tomographic image recon-
struction is ill-posed since the solutions are highly sensitive to errors [24]. Recalling
Figure 2.8, we saw that small perturbations in the sinogram resulted in large pertur-
bations in the corresponding reconstruction when using the FBP, which highlights the
problem of instability.
As shown in Chapter 2, the problem of tomographic image reconstruction could be
expressed as a system of linear equations (2.15). It was then briefly mentioned that
potential issues could arise with attempting to solve the corresponding system. These
issues will now be covered. To do this, we first consider a general linear inverse problem

5.1
What is Image Denoising?
A fundamental challenge in the field of image processing is image denoising. The concept
of image denoising is the process of recovering a clean image from a noisy image while
retaining the features of the clean image as much as possible. This is an essential task,
as acquiring images is an inherently erroneous process.
Mathematically speaking, the problem of image denoising considers the following
y = x + e,
(5.1)
where y ∈Rn is the observed image that consists of a clean image x ∈Rn that has
been degraded by noise e ∈Rn. The noise e is often considered to be additive white
Gaussian noise (AWGN) with zero mean and standard deviation σe. From (5.1), the
goal of image denoising is now to recover x, which essentially amounts to decreasing the
noise in y while also maintaining as much of the original features in x. This process is
illustrated in Figure 5.1. We can essentially picture a denoiser as some machinery that
takes in a noisy image and outputs a less noisy image. The task depicted in Figure 5.1
is, however, not a trivial task. The main challenge is that information has been lost in x
due to noise, which results in image denoising being an ill-posed inverse problem. Often,
prior knowledge is needed in order to account for the information that has been lost in
the process of (5.1).
An example of a simple denoiser is the median filter [6]. As its name suggests, the median
filter consists of translating an m × n filter over the noisy image. The pixel value at the
center of the filter is then replaced with the median of the pixel values within the filter.
This process often results in a smoother image. An illustration of median filtering can
be seen in Figure 5.2. When the filter exceeds the boundary of the image, one extends
the boundary of the image such that it fits (which is also depicted in Figure 5.2). This

is known as padding. There are different ways to pad an image, with the one depicted in
Figure 5.2 being padded with zeros. One could also extend the boundary with the same
pixels as in the boundary of the image, which results in symmetrical padding. In Figure
5.3, we demonstrate the use of the median filter as a denoiser. From Figure 5.3 in the
bottom left, we see that the median filter has smoothed and removed a lot of the noise
from the noisy Shepp-Logan phantom. However, we also notice that a crucial part of the
clean phantom, the white border, has almost completely vanished. The reason for this is
that the white border makes up a very small percentage of the entire pixels. This means
that when the 3×3 median filter is applied, most of the pixels in the vicinity of the white
border will be very close to zero, and thus the filtered pixel around the white border will
be replaced by a much smaller value. Therefore, only the top of the border remains, as
it is denser in that area compared to the rest. We can, however, fix this issue slightly by
using a smaller filter, which is displayed in the bottom right of Figure 5.3. From this,
we see that most of the white border is retained. However, the corresponding image is
also noisier, and it also appears more blurry than that of the 3 × 3 median filter, which
also appears to be much sharper. This highlights an important observation, namely,
that image denoising often becomes a compromise between removing the noise in the
image (Figure 5.3 bottom left) and retaining the features in the clean image (Figure 5.3
bottom right).

5.2.1
BM3D
Block-matching and 3D filtering (BM3D) [16] is a 3D block matching algorithm that
is famous within the field of image denoising. It is known for its excellent denoising
performance and has been considered the state-of-the-art in image denoising for some
time. BM3D essentially tries to exploit the smoothness and self-similarity of natural
images. This is achieved by using block-matching and collaborative filtering, which we
will elaborate on as we go along. The BM3D algorithm is carried out in a somewhat
involved 2-step procedure. We will elaborate on step 1 of the algorithm and briefly ex-
plain step 2, as it is quite similar to that of step 1 with only minor changes. For a more
detailed explanation of BM3D, see e.g. [35].
The first aspect of step 1 in the BM3D algorithm is the use of block-matching, which is
a method used to group similar blocks within a 2D noisy image to that of a given refer-
ence block. An illustration of this procedure can be seen in Figure 5.5. Note that the
Figure 5.5: Illustration of block-matching.
There are 3 different reference blocks
marked with an ”R” in different colors. In the vicinity of these reference
blocks it can be seen that there are other blocks (of the same color) that
look similar to that of the reference block.
blocks displayed in Figure 5.5 are not necessarily identical to the corresponding reference
blocks they are matched with. Discrepancies between the block and the reference block
are allowed, as searching for blocks identical to a reference block will be very restrict-
ing. Instead, given some reference block, other blocks will be matched to it if they are
similar within some tolerance. The similarity of a block compared to a reference block
is calculated using a distance measure. The blocks in the noisy image that have been
==PAGE:61==
5.2 State-of-the-Art Denoisers
47
matched to the given reference block are then stacked together to form a 3-dimensional
group.
After the block-matching process, groups of noisy blocks have been obtained. For each
group, a 3D isometric linear transform is applied, followed by hard thresholding, which
shrinks the coeﬀicients in the transform domain. Then the inverse 3D transform is ap-
plied to return to the image domain. This process aims to transform the noisy group
into another domain, where denoising is simpler, and then omit the smaller coeﬀicients,
which are assumed to be caused by the noise, using the hard thresholding operator. Fi-
nally, the inverse 3D transform is applied to return to the original image domain. This
process is the collaborative filtering aspect of BM3D.
After the collaborative filtering, each block is returned to its initial position in the noisy
image. However, due to the possibility of overlapping blocks in the block-matching pro-
cess, each pixel in the noisy image has most likely been a member of several groups.
Moreover, the corresponding blocks containing these pixels have been modified differ-
ently, depending on their group. This results in different estimates for the same pixels.
However, there should only be one estimate for each pixel in the image. This pixel re-
dundancy is handled in the aggregation step, which essentially combines all the different
estimates at each pixel location into one. This is achieved by a weighted average of the
pixel locations where blocks overlap to obtain one estimate for the pixel location while
still retaining the different structures imposed by the different groups. The result of
the aggregation step is referred to as the basic estimate. The authors found the weights
used in the aggregation in [16] to be satisfactory when they were inversely proportional
to the sample variance of the block estimates. The idea behind this choice of weights is
to give less weight to noisy groups when producing the basic estimate.
Having obtained the basic estimate completes the first of the two steps of the BM3D
algorithm, and we can now proceed to step 2. The main difference in step 2 is that the
block-matching is now done with the basic estimate rather than the noisy image. The
use of the hard thresholding operator in the collaborative filtering step is now replaced
with Wiener filtering. Lastly, aggregation is used in the same manner to obtain the final
estimate, which is the output of BM3D.
BM3D consists of many internal parameters that can be tweaked. Examples of such are
the size of the reference block, the threshold used for the hard thresholding operator,
the 3D transform etc. The authors in [16] investigated the effects of changing many of
these parameters (this was also considered in [35]), and consequently, the authors have
set many of the parameters to values they deem optimal from the investigation. This
also displays that BM3D is a highly engineered algorithm. As for the use of BM3D, we
will keep these preset parameters fixed throughout this thesis. The only parameter that
will be tweaked is the standard deviation of the noise, σe, which controls how aggressive
the denoising from BM3D becomes.

DnCNN
As deep learning has gotten increasingly more popular over the years, the question of
whether a neural network could compete with state-of-the-art denoisers such as BM3D

in one of the data sets. However, the word outperformed should be taken lightly, as we
are talking about a minimal improvement regarding performance measures. However,
this did show the potential of using neural networks within image denoising and has led
to further research in this area.
While an FFN was used for image denoising in [9], another class of neural networks has
been heavily used within image processing, namely the convolutional neural network
(CNN). CNNs are explicitly adapted to handle data with a grid-like structure, such as
images [22]. This makes CNNs a good choice for areas in image processing, e.g. image
denoising. A CNN differs from an FFN by use of convolutional layer(s), to which it
gets its name. Whereas an FFN contains fully connected neurons between each layer, a
convolutional layer in a CNN only connects neurons in a localized region to one hidden
neuron. This localized region is called the local receptive field for the hidden neuron.
Translating the local receptive field around the image will result in a new hidden neuron
connected to that local receptive field in that new spatial location in the image. This
process is illustrated in Figure 5.7. The local receptive field contains weights that are

neurons in the hidden layer detect the same feature but at different spatial locations

A state-of-the-art CNN used for image denoising is the so-called DnCNN, which was
proposed by [57] in 2017. This network uses residual learning, which has become in-

between the noisy image and the clean image, rather than predicting the clean image.
Hence, the difference between the noisy image and the prediction from DnCNN will
result in the predicted denoised image.

addition with a strongly convex forward model f. For small denoising strengths, the
authors in [48] believe it to be a reasonable assumption, however it is still challenging
to verify. More importantly, the requirement of a strongly convex f is not met for all
imaging problems such as super-resolution [38].
In summary, while there exists theoretical results that guarantee convergence for PnP,
in practice, the assumptions imposed on the denoiser Dσd are either very restricting
or hard to verify, which still makes it unclear whether we can expect convergence in
many different scenarios. But as PnP has achieved excellent results in such a variety of
different areas, it remains of interest to use it, despite its theoretical challenges.

7.1.1
Verifying the Implementation
To verify and establish correctness of TVregADMM, comparisons will be made to the
TVReg software [29]. TVReg solves the TV regularization problem (4.15) by means of
smoothing the TV functional (3.10). This is done by replacing the 2-norm ||Dix||2 with
a Huber functional (see e.g. [29] for its definition), which allows for the use of gradient-
based methods. This also means that TVReg does not solve (4.15) exactly due to smooth-
ing. However, for small smoothing parameter values, we should still expect to obtain
similar results for various configurations of the regularization parameter λ. TVReg has
different algorithms available to use for solving (4.15). However, in this section, we have
simply chosen to compare TVregADMM with one of the varieties, e.g. tvreg_gpbb.m
as the main interest is to ensure that TVregADMM produces reliable results and not
necessarily performance aspects. As for comparing TVregADMM and TVReg, we will
consider the 2-norm of the difference of their reconstructions. To construct the system
matrix A mentioned in Chapter 2, the Matlab function paralleltomo.m from the soft-

The Effects of the Number of Iterations Used
As stated in Chapter 6, convergence can be established for PnP under various assump-
tions on the denoiser Dσd, and thus stopping criteria can be used for the mentioned

these assumptions are generally very hard to verify. Therefore, it seems more reasonable
to initially let the PnP algorithm run for a set number of iterations rather than using
stopping criteria based on assumptions that we do not know are met. This may spark
the question of how the reconstructions change throughout the iterations? If the PnP
iterates converge, we should expect that the reconstructions stabilize throughout the
iterations. This experiment might help establish a suitable number of iterations needed
when conducting the later presented experiments.
We try to answer the above in the following way. We consider a noisy Shepp-Logan phan-
tom with a fixed relative noise level (3%). For the two denoisers, BM3D and DnCNN, we
randomly select, say 3, values of their corresponding regularization parameters λ and η.
We then run 1000 iterations for each of the two implementations for these regularization
parameters. Throughout the 1000 iterations, we calculate the RMSE at each iteration
to measure how the reconstructions change over the iterations. We note that randomly
selecting regularization parameters η and λ may not produce the best reconstructions,
but this is not the aim here. Instead, the aim here is to see how the reconstructions
change and if they appear to stabilize. Figure 8.6 shows the RMSE of the PnP recon-

also happens rather quickly throughout the iterations (black and blue). However, for

minimum located early in the iterations. From this, it appears that the choice of the
regularization may impact how the RMSE behaves over the iterations. Moreover, it also
appears that there is some dependence on the number of iterations when considering
the quality of the reconstructions when based on RMSE.
We can also visually inspect the reconstructions throughout the iterations, which is

8.3
Data With Varying Projections
Using CT scanners with many projections and a high exposure time allows for acquiring
a large quantity of good quality data. However, using many projections is not always re-
alistic due to various restricting factors. An example of such is encountered in medicine
when X-ray CT is used for medical examination of humans.
Prolonged exposure to
X-rays can be dangerous for humans, as there are many health risks associated with
it, such as increasing the risk of developing cancer [43]. Therefore, it is a high priority
to minimize this exposure to X-rays as much as possible. This can e.g. be done by
decreasing the number of projection angles. However, doing so will lead to a reduction
in the quality of the data.
The above motivates investigating how the quality of the reconstructions obtained using
PnP and TV regularization change as we vary the number of projections. We know that
TV regularization is capable of producing good reconstructions for a limited number of
projections [24]. However, it is not clear how PnP will handle a limited number of pro-
jections. An apparent hypothesis would be that the limited number of projections will
be problematic for PnP since the denoisers may not be equipped to handle the streak
artifacts that will inevitably appear when we are using a limited number of projections.
However, as stated in Chapter 6, PnP has shown to work well in many areas, which
suggests that this still is worth exploring.
In this experiment, the number of projections are chosen to be 256, 64, and 16, which
results in an overdetermined, square, and underdetermined system, respectively. This
also corresponds to the sinograms in the column of Figure 8.1. In this column, we have
fixed the relative noise level to 1%. Moreover, the decrease in projections are obtained
by sub-sampling the projections from the overdetermined system. What this means is
that in order to obtain the square system, we sample every 4th projection from the large
system, and similarly every 16th projection in order to obtain the underdetermined sys-
tem. Likewise, we sample the corresponding element from the system matrix and the
noisy right hand side to match with the sub-sampled projections.
Like with the first experiment, the setup is as follows.

and TVregADMM, ρ = 1 is fixed and λ is varied. When making visual assessments, we

the quality of the reconstruction obtained by a method different from PnP and TV

Summary
In this chapter, we conducted several experiments regarding the behavior of PnP. It was
first seen that the denoising strength σd could significantly impact the quality of the
denoised phantoms when using the two denoisers, BM3D and DnCNN, respectively. We

saw that phantoms with a relative noise level up to 15% only needed a relatively small
denoising strength for optimal denoising to occur based on RMSE when considering
BM3D as the denoiser. Considering the same experiment regarding DnCNN was more
restrictive due to DnCNN being pre-trained on fixed denoising strengths. The noisy
phantoms were too aggressively denoised for all the considered relative noise levels, as
the minimum RMSE was obtained for each noisy phantom at the smallest pre-trained

PnP reconstructions. It was observed that randomly initializing z(0) and u(0) did not
change the stabilizing behavior, or the value, of the RMSE of the PnP reconstructions.
This indicated that the PnP reconstructions were independent of the initializations z(0)
and u(0).

tained a better reconstruction than TV regularization in terms of RMSE.
Considering data of varying projections, we saw that TV regularization obtained better
reconstructions in terms of RMSE for 256 and 64 projections. It was observed that
a decrease from 256 to 64 projections did not yield much of a visual difference in the

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
In this chapter, we discuss the results displayed in Chapter 8, whereafter ideas for future
work then will be discussed.
From Chapter 8, we saw that TV regularization generally performed better than PnP
based on RMSE in both simulated experiments, except for two cases. Those two cases
consisted of simulated data with a 25% relative noise level and 16 projections, respec-
tively. Here we saw that PnP obtained better reconstructions in terms of RMSE. We
also saw that PnP could obtain good quality reconstructions on simulated data with a
1% relative noise level and 256 projections. The same could also be said for the simu-
lated data using 64 projections. Thus while TV regularization may outperform PnP in
the instances mentioned, PnP is still capable of keeping up with TV regularization.
In the instances where PnP performed better than TV regularization, we may be able
to say that when considering data of low quality, i.e., data that is either very noisy or
containing very few projections, then PnP may be a tool worth considering. With that
being said, one could also ask if the minor improvements in RMSE are worth choosing
PnP over TV regularization, considering that PnP has its flaws. One of the significant
drawbacks of PnP is that it is a more complicated framework than TV regularization.
PnP has been documented to be very sensitive to changes in parameters [54]. The se-
lection of the parameters in PnP is often a result of trial and error, and the parameters
often need to be readjusted when considering new data, making it a cumbersome process.
Moreover, the behavior of PnP is far from simple. As it was seen in the preliminary
experiments, the RMSE of the PnP reconstructions did not consistently decrease and sta-
bilize. For certain configurations of regularization parameters, an increase in the RMSE
was observed in which stabilization occurred afterwards. Also, as it was observed that
the RMSE of some of the considered PnP reconstructions obtained a minimum before
the set number of iterations had been run, which suggests a potential dependence on
the number of iterations in terms of the quality of the PnP reconstructions. Looking
beyond the reconstructions that exhibited slightly peculiar behavior, we also saw that
the RMSE of some of the PnP reconstructions, especially when using DnCNN, decreased
drastically and then stabilized. This could mean that when using PnP and a suitable
configuration of regularization parameters, only a few iterations are needed to obtain a
good reconstruction, which can be a potential advantage for PnP, as it in that case will
be quick and eﬀicient to use.
==PAGE:114==
100
9 Discussion
Essentially, what all the above amounts to is that we have established that PnP can
compete with TV regularization in some contexts and, in other cases, outperform it
based on RMSE. However, the PnP method, as stated above, has its flaws, making it a
less straightforward method to work with compared to TV regularization. Moreover, as
a last remark, the observations we have made from the conducted experiments are based
on simulated data that has been entirely generated from just the Shepp-Logan phantom.
Hence, it is not easy to determine if any of these observations can be generalized from
the conducted experiments.
Regardless of whether one would prefer TV regularization or PnP, the obtained results
using PnP show potential and suggest continuing to develop and improve the PnP
framework.
9.1
Future Work
Many ideas come to mind regarding expanding the work that has been made in this
thesis. We will therefore present a few ideas for possible future work. Most of the ideas
share the same objective of hopefully improving the performance of PnP for tomographic
image reconstruction.
The first idea that comes to mind regards the denoisers used in this thesis, as they play
a significant role in the PnP framework. The DnCNN denoiser is pre-trained, which in
one way is very practical, as it is directly ready for use, and no (possibly exhausting)
training process is needed. However, the images DnCNN has been trained on may not
be suitable for tomographic data, which was considered in the experiments. Therefore,
it could be interesting to retain a denoiser of the same structure as DnCNN but go
through the effort of training it on tomographic data in hopes that it may improve its
performance. We may also seek to incorporate other denoisers than those considered in
this thesis. Since many open-source implementations of state-of-the-art denoisers exist,
it should not be too demanding to incorporate additional denoisers into the PnP frame-
work.
The lack of a tunable denoising strength in DnCNN is a drawback as we are not able
to control the denoising as much as with BM3D. An approach to fix this issue was
presented in [56], where they proposed the addition of a tunable scaling parameter for
DnCNN. This has been shown to boost the performance of CNNs within the use of PnP.
While this has been shown to improve performance, it also increases the complexity by
including an additional parameter that needs to be tuned, which can further complicate
things in terms of both computations and the overall sensitivity to the settings of all the
other parameters.
Recalling the conducted experiments in Figure 8.1, we can think of it as a 3 × 3 matrix,
==PAGE:115==
9.1 Future Work
101
where the rows are the number of projections in the data, and the columns are the rela-
tive noise levels of the data. In that context, a 2 × 2 matrix is left undetermined. Since
we observed that the performance of PnP was surprisingly good for fewer projections,
then perhaps this could still hold if we increase the relative noise levels.
The resolution of the images used in the experiments is rather coarse. Hence it makes
sense to investigate how the performance of PnP may change as we increase the resolu-
tion of the images. This thesis considers phantoms of just 64 × 64 pixels, mainly due
to the computation time. The more we increase the resolution, the more pixels will be
dedicated to smaller features in the image. This may allow for better reconstructions
if the denoisers are better at filtering out the noise from noisy images of increased res-
olution. However, increasing the resolution will also result in a heavier computational
burden. Thus, potential optimization of the implementations may be a good idea to
consider before advancements are to be made in this direction.
While this thesis has focused on PnP as a method to allow state-of-the-art denoisers into
the mix, other regularization methods like this exist and could be worth considering.
Such a method could e.g. be Regularization by Denoising (RED) [46]. RED mainly
differs from PnP in the sense that it aims to solve the optimization problem (6.1) using
the explicit prior g(x) = xT(x−Dσd(x)). Under certain assumptions on the denoiser Dσd,
g(x) is convex and convergence to a global minimizer of (6.1) is guaranteed. However, the
assumptions needed for this to be satisfied are generally not met for most denoisers. In
[45] it was shown that if the denoiser does not have a symmetric Jacobian, then the RED
framework will not solve (6.1) using the explicit prior g. However, great results have still
been achieved using RED, even when the underlying assumptions on the denoiser are
not met [46]. Therefore, this still suggests considering RED as an alternative to PnP.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
An implementation of TV regularized least-squares using ADMM was successfully im-
plemented and verified against the software TVReg. Then, an implementation of the
PnP method was considered using the denoisers BM3D and DnCNN, respectively, as
implicit priors.
Preliminary experiments using simulated tomographic data were conducted to investi-
gate the behavior of PnP. The effects of the denoising strength σd for the two denoisers,
BM3D and DnCNN, were then investigated. We found that for BM3D, relatively small
denoising strengths were suﬀicient to obtain denoised phantoms of optimal quality based
on RMSE. The same could not be said for DnCNN, which was more restricted due to
the fixed denoising strengths it had been pre-trained on. It was found that DnCNN de-
noised too aggressively, as the minimum RMSE was obtained at the smallest pre-trained
denoising strength for all the considered relative noise levels. This indicated that smaller
denoising strengths could be needed for better performance using DnCNN.
We investigated how the number of iterations affected PnP reconstructions and found
that the RMSE of the PnP reconstructions mostly appeared to stabilize quickly for both
denoisers, respectively. However, it was also observed that some examples showed the
RMSE not stabilizing, which occurred for PnP-BM3D. Here a minimum RMSE was ob-
tained early in the iterations, whereafter the RMSE increased throughout the remaining
iterations. This may indicate that the reconstruction quality depends on the number of
iterations. Moreover, it was generally seen for PnP-BM3D that the RMSE of the recon-
structions increased before they stabilized. In contrast, for PnP-DnCNN, the RMSE of
the reconstructions appeared to decrease quickly and stabilize.
Another behavioral aspect that was investigated was how random initializations of z(0)
and u(0) affected the reconstructions of PnP throughout the iterations. It was observed
that the RMSE of the PnP reconstructions all stabilized around the same value for dif-
ferent initializations of z(0) and u(0). This indicated that the PnP reconstructions were
independent of the initializations of z(0) and u(0).
Simulated tomographic data with varying relative noise levels and projections were con-
sidered, respectively, where visual and numerical assessments was used to compare the
performance of PnP and TV regularization. In the simulated data with varying relative
noise levels, it was found that TV regularization obtained better reconstructions than
