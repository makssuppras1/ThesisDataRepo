Abstract
This thesis presents an innovative approach to optimizing train platform assignments at
Copenhagen Central Station. Utilizing a mixed integer programming model, it addresses
the complex challenge of efficient platform allocation, balancing operational constraints
with timetable adherence. The model is implemented in Julia and leverages the Gurobi
solver, demonstrating significant improvements in station functionality and passenger ex-
perience. The results showcase the potential of mathematical optimization in public trans-
portation, offering practical solutions for major train stations. The study sets a new bench-
mark in the field and opens avenues for future research in dynamic, data-driven routing
and train platforming problems.

Introduction
DSB (Danske Statsbaner), is the largest train operating company in Denmark, which was
founded in 1885 following the merger of various private and regional railways. DSB pri-
marily operates passenger train services, connecting major cities and regions across Den-
mark. It provides a range of services, from local trains to intercity and high-speed services.
DSB’s trains are known for their punctuality, comfort, and efficiency, making rail travel a
popular choice for commuters and travelers in Denmark. They also utilize advanced tech-
nology for ticketing, scheduling, and passenger information systems. Like many railway
companies, DSB faces challenges such as maintaining infrastructure, adapting to techno-
logical changes, and meeting increasing passenger demands. However, they continue to
invest in development projects and innovations to enhance service quality and operational
efficiency.
Copenhagen Central Station, a key hub in the DSB railway network, offers a wide range
of services and features, can be seen from the following figure 1.1. It serves as a cen-
tral point for various train services including international trains to Sweden and Germany,
Intercity and Express train services across Denmark, regional train services to and from
Zealand and southern Sweden (also referred to as Øresund trains), and commuter rail
services of the Copenhagen S-train network across the Greater Copenhagen area. Ad-
ditionally, it connects with the Copenhagen Metro network lines M3 (City Circle Line) and
M4.[1]
Copenhagen Central Station currently serves over 100,000 passengers daily. However,
to maintain seamless and efficient operations, planners responsible for assigning trains
to platforms continue to grapple with the challenge of devising a practical schedule. This
schedule must not only function effectively but also provide excellent service to the com-
munity. The project falls within the realm of transportation logistics and optimization, with
a specific focus on platform scheduling and management at Copenhagen Central Station.

1.1
Literary Study
Multiple different research teams have worked on some version of the train platforming
problem, each using unique methods and focusing on distinct aspects. The following will
be shown in detail:
The paper [2] tackles the intricate challenge of guiding trains within a station, a critical
component of devising optimal timetables for the rail service in the Netherlands. This
challenge is compounded by the dynamic and complex nature of railway stations, where
a diverse array of trains must navigate through a restricted network of tracks and plat-
forms, each adhering to its distinct schedule. The goal of their model is complex, aiming
to increase the throughput of trains within the station during a specific time frame while
reducing unnecessary shunting activities and improving the allocation of platforms and
routes. To achieve this, the model must carefully navigate a myriad of constraints, includ-
ing safety considerations, station capacity, and expectations for service quality.
In their pursuit of a solution, the paper [2] proposes a central model that essentially
views railway stations as complex networks of interconnected nodes and aims to find
the best way to route trains through the network, this innovative method also known as
the weighted node packing problem (WNPP), which offers a sophisticated and structured
approach to addressing the complex issue of train routing in railway stations. At its core,
the WNPP is depicted as an undirected graph, a network consisting of points referred to
as nodes, each connected by lines known as edges.
Here means each node represents a possible route a train might take through the station,
with every potential path for every train represented in this graph-like structure. These
nodes are assigned weights, reflecting the desirability or efficiency of the route they rep-
resent; the higher the weight, the more preferable the route, and the connections between
these nodes, or edges, are crucial as they represent the conflicts or constraints between
routes. For instance, if two routes would cause trains to arrive at the same track simul-
taneously, creating a safety hazard or scheduling conflict, the nodes representing these
routes would be connected by an edge. This connection signals that these two routes
cannot be part of the same solution set, as they are incompatible with each other.
This weighted node packing problem (WNPP) reduces the number of points to be consid-
ered, which is the preprocessed part of the model, and then uses integer programming
to solve the problem. The innovation of this model is its ability to make the problem more
manageable by effectively ”packing” the trains into the station layout, like solving the puz-
zle of each train having to get into the right place at the right time, ensuring smooth runs
without delays. This approach is not only theoretically sound but also works in practice,
making it a valuable tool for optimizing train operations at busy railway stations.
The primary goal of the WNPP in this setting is to select a subset of these nodes, a
collection of routes, that not only avoids any conflicts (ensuring no two selected nodes are
connected by an edge) but also maximizes the total weight. This means the chosen set
of routes not only is conflict-free but also represents the most efficient or desirable routing
solution according to the assigned weights. Essentially, this process involves carefully
balancing various constraints, such as ensuring safety, maintaining station capacity, and

[3] investigates how to schedule trains in stations to avoid conflicts and ensure they con-
nect correctly while meeting schedule requirements, by first describing the station, the
possible train routes, and what they need to consider for a train’s schedule. They then
use a system of symbols to make it easier to discuss these details. The problem is then
formulated as an Integer Linear Program (ILP), where binary decision variables represent
the assignment of trains to specific routes. Constraints are introduced to ensure safety
and operational feasibility, including capacity constraints to prevent track overloading,
routing constraints to ensure feasible paths, and timing constraints for train schedules.
Additionally, [3] also discusses the relationship of the routing problem to the Fixed In-
terval Scheduling Problem (FISP), highlighting its broader implications in scheduling and
optimization. Translating this to train routing, each train has a fixed ”interval” (the time it
needs to pass through the station), and the ”resources” are the tracks and platforms. The
ILP model in the paper would use binary variables to represent the assignment of trains
to specific routes and time slots, with constraints ensuring safety and adherence to the
schedule.
Both papers [2] and [3] focus on the same core problem: Routing Trains Through Railway
Stations. However, they approach issues in different ways and offer different approaches
and perspectives. [2] focuses on the optimization of train routes through railway stations
by proposing a node packing model, which considers stations as a network of nodes
(tracks, platforms) and seeks to optimize the allocation of trains to these nodes. This
focuses more on the efficient use of station infrastructure, minimizing train delays or max-
imizing throughput. [3] does not propose a specific model like [2] but mainly focuses on
exploring theoretical and computational challenges in train routes. This question focuses
more on understanding the factors that make train routes complex, such as multiple tracks,
different train timetables, and platform constraints. It is analytical and aims to deepen the
understanding of the inherently intractable nature of the problem.
[4] mainly focuses on train rescheduling and platforming in large high-speed railway sta-
tions, proposing a multi-objective mixed-integer nonlinear programming (MO-MINLP) op-
timization model to address the complexities of train delays, track occupation, and route
selection.
The model in [4] comprises three main objectives: minimizing deviation from the primary
station plan, reducing track and route deviations, and ensuring operational and safety
constraints are met. These include maintaining proper train sequencing, preventing il-
legal overtaking, ensuring track and route fidelity, setting appropriate dwell times, and
managing train headways. The model is designed to keep operations as close to the
planned schedule as possible while adapting to real-time disruptions. The rolling hori-
zon optimization algorithm is a core component of the solution strategy, which breaks
down the dispatching time horizon into manageable intervals for sequential optimization.
This approach, coupled with a hierarchical sequence algorithm and a strategy to reduce
unnecessary constraints, ensures the model is both efficient and practical for complex,
large-scale railway station operations, balancing the need for prompt, reliable service with
the realities of unexpected delays and infrastructure limitations.
[4] does not propose a specific mathematical model for optimizing train routes, as [2] does,
nor does it adopt a theoretical approach to discuss the complexity of the problem, like [3],
[4] combines optimization models with real-time data processing, focusing on the dynamic

aspects of train dispatching and platform allocation in the specific context of high-speed

The paper [5] shows a kind of set-packing model, which introduce a branch- and price-
based solution for scheduling trains through railway hubs. The method first assumes
that all possible train routes are known. Taking into account the dynamic nature of trains
and signaling systems, it uses dynamic programming to enumerate all potential paths for
each train. Train dynamics are modeled using kinematic equations, focusing on constant
acceleration and deceleration. Each train’s path is marked with a spatiotemporal label,
which tracks the train’s position along the track and respects constraints such as the
maximum time allowed within an intersection.
Then these paths are organized into a tree structure, improving computational efficiency
by promoting efficient pricing of paths within the tree. The linear programming (LP) re-
laxation of the problem is dynamically updated by adding new variables as violated con-
straints, starting from a basic initial solution with no path for each train. Developing ef-
ficient pricing routines for these tree structures is critical because the solution requires
only a subset of possible train paths. The basis for updating the LP relaxation exploits the
dual form of the original feasible solution, thereby reducing the computational burden as-
sociated with large original basis. The method then applies techniques to enforce integer
values on the train path variables, consistent with the branch-and-bound algorithm.
Finally, the solution employs constrained branching to solve the set partitioning problem,
which is particularly useful in scenarios where trains compete for the same resources. This
integrated approach effectively addresses the complex challenges of train routing through
rail hubs, striking a balance between computational efficiency and practical feasibility of
train routing solutions.
Compared with the node packing model in the paper [2], the set packing model tailored
for railway hubs adopts a dynamic approach that emphasizes resource-based constraints
and adaptive train route management through branching and price tree structures. In con-
trast, the node packaging model is station-centric and aims to optimize the internal oper-
ations of the train station, using branch cutting methods to centralize the station layout,
minimize shunting operations, and optimize platform and route preferences. This con-
trast highlights the broader adaptability of collective packaging models to the rail network,
whereas node packaging models are optimized around specific stations.
The document focuses on the real-time train scheduling and routing problem (RTTSRP)
in busy and complex railway networks, a strongly NP-hard issue. [6] propose a mixed-
integer linear programming (MILP) formulation by considering both train routing and schedul-
ing decisions to address this problem, to be more specific, extend the MILP formulation
to include all possible train routing combinations, introduce binary variables to represent
routing decisions. The objective is to minimize the maximum consecutive delay, focusing
on keeping train schedules as close to planned times as possible.
And then [6] presents a straightforward approach to calculating high-quality lower and up-
per limits for real-time train scheduling and routing. Initially, it introduces a unique method
to find a reliable lower limit by simplifying the task to one route per train and solving it fully.
This step involves creating a special graph where nodes are train activities, and arcs are
the rules and links between them. The significance of these arcs is based on the least
time needed and the options for routing. The lower limit is then determined by solving this
graph completely using a specific algorithm.

[6] differs from [2] and [3] in its focus on real-time operations and its algorithmic approach,
particularly in the use of lower- and upper-bound algorithms for scheduling and routing
trains. [6] specifically targets real-time train scheduling and routing, emphasizing the need
for fast and efficient solutions in dynamic environments, focusing primarily on algorithmic
strategies to deal with the uncertainty and variability inherent in real-time operations, par-
ticularly the use of boundaries to guide decision-making, which is particularly important
where rapid response to operational changes is required, such as delays, cancellations,
or unexpected congestion.
Traditionally, the Train Platforming Problem focuses on efficiently assigning platforms to
trains. The extended version, exTPP, goes further by also scheduling locomotives along-
side the trains. This means it not only decides which platform a train should arrive at and
depart from but also how locomotives (the engines that move the trains) are managed
and scheduled within the station yard, which ensures a more efficient and reliable railway
system. [7] introduce a 0-1 integer programming model that schedules both trains and lo-
comotives on a time-space-state network, considering the high correlation between their
operations, focusing on the simultaneous scheduling of trains and locomotives within a
railway station yard.
The objective of this model is to minimize the total deviation from planned arrival and de-
parture times, aiming to enhance punctuality and reliability in train services. To ensure
practicality and efficiency, the model incorporates a set of constraints including the se-
lection of time-space arcs for train movements, maintaining operation consistency, and
adhering to the station yard’s resource capacities like tracks and platforms. To tackle
the complexity of this optimization problem, the study proposes two dual decomposition
methods: Lagrangian relaxation (LR)-based and Alternating Direction Method of Multi-
pliers (ADMM)-based approaches. These methods break down the problem into more
manageable sub-problems, each solved using a label-setting algorithm and two greedy
methods, facilitating a practical and efficient solution to the intricate scheduling challenges
faced in busy railway stations.
Only focus on the train platforming part in [8], mainly determining the specific platform and
station route for each train without conflicts. [8] considers the different running times for in-
bound and outbound routes and integrates practical headway requirements, and a binary
integer programming model underpins this method, aiming to optimize train schedules
and maintenance plans by minimizing the total train weighted running cost and accom-
modating flexible maintenance operation tasks. And [8] choosing a heuristic algorithm,
dynamically adjusts the mathematical model’s size by updating the time window for a
train’s first departure or dwell times, which narrows down the possible solutions to focus
on the most promising ones, making the search for optimal or near-optimal solutions faster
and more efficient.
By putting station topology and train timetables as the main inputs, [9] addresses the Train
Platforming Problem (TPP) by proposing a 0-1 quadratic programming model, which aims
to assign platforms and routes to trains within a station, based on the physical layout of
the station and operational constraints, such as track layout and minimum time intervals
between train movements. However, for the solution methods, the complexity of the TPP

Finally, turn eyes on both strategic and tactical levels of the Train Platforming Prob-

are variable. This flexibility in junction times is essential for capacity studies at a junc-

pairs of trains. The model categorizes train pairs into three types based on the potential
for their occupation time intervals to overlap and sets up constraints accordingly to pre-
vent conflicts. Although the model maintains both platform and route choice variables, it
assumes only one route variant between each incoming/outgoing line and each platform.
This simplification helps in reducing the model’s complexity.
And [10] chosen MILP due to its flexibility and the clarity it provides in indicating how far
results are from the optimal solution. The model uses advanced solvers like Gurobi, etc.
to manage the computations. The goal function of the model aims to minimize penalties
associated with changing a train’s platform, with a higher penalty imposed for moving
a train to a fictive platform. This encourages solutions that keep trains on their current
platforms when possible and only move them when necessary to avoid conflicts or ac-
commodate more trains, thus maximizing the number of platformed trains and optimizing
station capacity and train schedules.

Methodology
The main objective of this study is to develop a mixed integer programming model that
maximizes the robustness of the Copenhagen Central Station platform. This chapter con-
sists of two parts: 1. Overview of the general structure of the 0-1 integer programming
model, including objective functions, constraints, and variables. 2. Design an algorithm
to find the trajectory path using the provided nodes.
2.1
Mixed Integer Linear Programming
2.1.1
Introduction
The mixed integer linear programming (MILP) problem is a major problem in the fields
of production, scheduling, and logistics and is a special form of optimization. In MILP,
some decision variables are restricted to integer values, while other variables can remain
continuous. The core challenge is to determine the optimal set of values for these vari-
ables to best achieve the specified goals, given the constraints. This involves a complex
decision space where a mixture of integer and continuous variables must jointly satisfy all
the linear equations and inequalities that define the problem to find the most efficient and
feasible solution.

2.1.2
Big-M method
The Big-M method is a mathematical technique used in linear programming and inte-
ger programming to handle constraints involving conditions or logical relationships. It is
particularly useful when dealing with mixed integer linear programming (MILP) problems,
where some variables need to be integers (0 or 1) and certain constraints depend on the
values of these integer variables. The Big-M method introduces a large positive constant
(usually denoted as M) to represent the cost or penalty associated with violating these
conditional constraints. The method aims to find the optimal value of the decision variable
(x1, x2, ..., xn) and integer variables (y1, y2, ..., ym) while maximizing (or minimizing) the
objective function Z and ensuring that all constraints are satisfied.

For the constraint 2.11: dij is the coefficient, xi is the decision variable, and yi is the
binary variable (0 or 1) to activate or deactivate Constraints. A Big-M value, denoted by
M, is a number that is large enough, the size of which is explained in the next paragraph.
When yi = 0, the constraint becomes active because M(1 −yi) turns into M, making
the inequality easy to satisfy due to the large value of M. Conversely, when yi = 1, the
inequality is effectively deactivated (or becomes irrelevant) since M(1−yi) equals 0. This
method allows for the inclusion of certain constraints in the solution only under specific
conditions dictated by the binary variables yi.
Choosing an appropriate size for M in a Big-M approach is a balance between making
it large enough to effectively deactivate the constraints when necessary, but not so large
that it introduces other problems, like numerical instability or reduced solver efficiency.
In practice, M should be slightly larger than the maximum value that the expression in
the constraint can achieve within the feasible region of the problem. For example, if you
know that the sum of the constraints will never exceed 1000 in any feasible solution, you
can set M to a value slightly above that, such as 1001 or 1100, to ensure that it is large
enough. However, setting M too high (such as 100000 or higher in this example) may
cause computational difficulties or less accurate results.
The branch-and-bound method shown below explains in detail how to solve the above
mixed integer linear programming (MILP) problem step-by-step:
1. Linear Relaxation:
• Original Approach: Solving the linear relaxation by allowing integer variables
xi to take fractional values.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Here, the binary decision variables (like xt,p or
yt,p in your model) are allowed to take any values between 0 and 1, instead of
being restricted to 0 or 1.
2. Branch:
• Original Approach: If a variable x1 is non-integer, create sub-problems.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: If a binary variable, say xt,p, takes a fractional
value (anything other than 0 or 1), then branch into sub-problems.
3. Branch into Sub-Problems:
• Original Approach: Split into two sub-problems, rounding x1 down (x1 ≤⌊x1⌋)
and up (x1 ≥⌈x1⌉).
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Create two sub-problems: In one, set xt,p = 0,
and in the other, set xt,p = 1.
4. Linear Relaxation of Solving Sub-Problems:
• Original Approach: Solve each sub-problem by relaxing the integer con-
straints.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Each sub-problem is solved by relaxing the bi-
nary constraints, allowing the other binary variables to take values between 0
and 1.

5. Feasibility Check:
• Original Approach: Check if the solution is an integer.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Check if all binary variables in the solution are 0
or 1.
6. Bounding:
• Original Approach: Update the bounds based on the sub-problem solutions.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Same as the original approach; update the bounds
based on the solutions to the binary-variable sub-problems.
7. Pruning:
• Original Approach: Discard sub-problems with bounds worse than the current
best integer solution.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Discard sub-problems if their solutions don’t im-
prove the current best solution where all binary variables are 0 or 1.
8. Terminate:
• Original Approach: Continue until all sub-problems are solved or discarded.
• Binary Variable Adaptation: Same as the original; terminate when the best
solution (where all binary variables are 0 or 1) is found or no sub-problems are
left.
In essence, the adaptation for binary variables involves treating the fractional values of
these variables in the linear relaxation as the branching criteria and ensuring that the final
solution adheres to the binary constraints. This approach is highly effective in models
like yours, where decisions are binary (e.g., whether a train uses a particular track or
platform).

Find Path Algorithm Design
Trains need to travel along a designated track path, which is essentially a series of nodes
connecting various points along the way to and from the station. The primary function
of track paths is to create connections that ensure trains can move seamlessly between
stations. For example, if a train departs from Nørreport Station towards Central Station, a
suitable track path must exist to facilitate the smooth movement of the train along its route.
These track paths are critical infrastructure for the efficient operation of the rail network.
The project created a ”Path Finding Algorithm”, the pseudo-code of which is shown in A.1,
which is based on finding a node and connecting its nearest neighbor nodes to find an
orbital path from one site to another. It can be seen from the figure2.1.
To make it easier to understand, think of the algorithm as a route browser, methodically
plotting all possible routes from various starting points to destinations. It starts from each
starting point (i.e. departure station) and initially considers the orbital path from there. As
it explores, it gradually extends its route, avoiding going in circles by returning to the start-
ing point or previously visited locations. If the route reaches its destination, it is considered
successful. The process continues, systematically adding new extensions to explore until
all routes from all starting points have been thoroughly examined. This meticulous explo-
ration integrates all possible paths from the chosen starting node to the destination node,
ensuring that no stone is left unturned in the full route adventure.

is Platform 26 at Copenhagen Central Station is located approximately 250 meters south

Conclusion
The project successfully solved the complex problem of optimizing train platform allocation
at Copenhagen Central Station. The use of mixed integer programming models aims to
maintain the robustness and efficiency of the station platform system. It combines various
constraints and variables in detail by ensuring maximum headway between two trains on
the same platform, while each train can only be assigned to one train and one track path.
To be more specific, each track path occupies a time scope from 2 minutes before the
train’s arrival time to the platform, and its structure has been carefully designed to reflect
the realistic dynamics of the train’s fixed timetable.
At it core, this research is harnessing the power of mixed integer programming, efficiently
implemented in Julia and solved using the advanced Gurobi solver. This approach takes
into account various operating variables, ensuring a holistic solution. More details:
• Optimized Scheduling: The model cleverly balances three key elements: adhering
to a fixed timetable, optimizing the headway between trains sharing the same plat-
form, and maintaining operational dynamics. This balance contributes to an efficient
and streamlined train dispatching process.
• Prevention of conflicts: An important result is the smooth operation of the station,
which is characterized by the absence of conflicts in the same direction. This can
be seen from the figures 5.2 and 5.3 in Chapter 5, which reflect the train timetable.
It can be seen from the figure that there are no duplicate trains in the same period.
The approach provided of this project within following advantages:
• Safety enhancement: This project includes constraints on the minimum headway,
that is, the time interval between trains on the same track or platform. By implement-
ing these headway restrictions, the model ensures adequate separation between
trains, thereby reducing the risk of accidents. In addition, the model can prevent
overlapping track assignments, further enhancing safety by reducing the likelihood
of track conflicts. Compared to manual dispatch, the model’s algorithmic rigor en-
sures that safety parameters such as headways and track assignments are always
adhered to, allowing systematic enforcement of these safety restrictions to result in
safer rail operations.
• Cost-Effectiveness: By optimizing the utilization of resources (trains, platforms,
tracks), this model ensures that these assets are used in the most effective way
possible. This leads to a reduction in operational costs because resources are not
being wasted and services are being provided most economically.
• Practical Relevance: Focusing on a major transportation hub like Copenhagen
Central Station, the study bridges the gap between theoretical research and practical
application.

timetables allowing the schedule to adjust for delays, changing passenger demands, or
other unforeseen circumstances, into the train scheduling model. This involves:
• Exploring Advanced Optimization Techniques: The approach provided by this
project likely uses deterministic approaches, optimal for stable and predictable sce-
narios but less effective in fluctuating real-world conditions. Developing models that

adjust to patterns in train delays or passenger flow, which enable the model to make
informed decisions even under uncertainty, such as predicting potential delays and
preemptively adjusting schedules.
• Algorithms design capable of efficiently managing platform assignments dur-
ing peak hours: The model provided by this project can be optimized by focus-
ing on train platforms and routes in each direction separately, but may not specifi-

were developed to handle peak hour situations, particularly when trains coming from
opposite directions require the same platform, and platform allocations can be dy-
