Abstract
Material extrusion based additive manufacturing (ME­AM), or 3D­printing is a rapidly growing
field within manufacturing, and the most accessible of all AM technologies due to the influence
of open source initiatives such as the RepRap project. Despite the technologies widespread
adoption, POM, a favored semi­crystalline engineering polymer, has not seen much application
as feedstock in ME­AM, and little literature is available on the subject.
The goal of this thesis is to develop a cost effective, open source ME­AM platform that is able
to utilise POM as feedstock material, and characterise its build quality with respect to warping
and delamination.
An existing machine was modified within predefined constraints in order to facilitate favorable
conditions, identified in prior studies, to process highly crystalline polymers by ME­AM.
Warping was characterised and quantified by straightness tolerances of printed samples
and the effect of print speed and chamber temperatures were analysed. Delamination was
also characterised by measuring crack length of samples and the effect of nozzle temperature,
cooling fan speed and chamber temperature were analysed. The results showed correlation
between print speed, and the combination of print speed and chamber temperature on warping,
while all factors and their interactions were correlated to delamination according to the analysis.
The following work documents which parameters to control in order to improve part quality
in ME­AM parts from POM feedstock material and how to build a cost effective ME­AM plat­
form in order to control these parameters. The results show that with resources available to
most enthusiasts and on a budget of less than 4000 DKK, a platform can be constructed that
significantly improves part quality, when compared to unmodified consumer level 3D printer.
The results present a novel approach to the ME­AM of POM, and serve as a catalyst for
future development in previously unused materials, for both amateur enthusiasts, and academic
or industrial researchers.

1
Introduction
The following work is on the development of material capabilities within the field of material
extrusion­based additive manufacturing (ME­AM) with an emphases on the utilisation of
polyoxymethylene (POM). Furthermore, the project aims to develop a technology platform that
is as cost­effective as possible to allow for the widest possible adoption potential. The goal is
to demonstrate the capabilities of the platform to process possible next­gen polymers, currently
not used in ME­AM.
Manufacturing technology has been improving and developing at an exponential rate since
the start of the 20th century, and with innovations within the field of materials and manufacturing,
humanity has seen radically improved quality of life. The introduction of synthetic polymers
paved the way for countless innovations in product design and novel solutions. Furthermore the
introduction of the injection moulding process drastically reduced the cost of mass production
of thermoplastic parts. The massive improvement to living standards that these material­ and
process developments have made, may never be fully realised. Sadly, the same can be said
about the catastrophic environmental impact that has followed.
In recent years, some of the most novel innovations within the discipline of manufacturing,
have been in the field of additive manufacturing, commonly known as 3D­printing. Metals, poly­
mers, ceramics and even composite materials can be processed by additive manufacturing with
unprecedented freedom in geometry. Hollow parts with a solid outer surface, complicated in­
ternal geometry and even multi­material parts can be fabricated using this novel technology.
The development of additive manufacturing has come a long way, even in the last 10 years and
the machines are getting ever more sophisticated. From the ”self replicating rapid­prototyper”
(RepRap) initiative by Adrian Bowyer, where the idea was to design an open­source, inexpen­
sive extrusion based additive manufacturing machine that would be able to reproduce parts
for another copy of itself [1], [2]. To the vat photopolymerisation printers of today, which can
replicate parts with great precision. Additive manufacturing has, and will continue to drive inno­
vation with its capability in terms of freedom in geometric design, both on its own and coupled
with conventional processes, e.g. lost wax casting or injection moulding [3]–[5].
1.1
Additive manufacturing
Additive manufacturing and 3D­printing are terms that both apply to the same process, accord­
ing to the ISO/ASTM 52900 standard; the process of joining materials to fabricate parts usually
in layered increments [6]. However, the terms represent different approaches to the subject.
3D­printing is a more common term with a connotation to more consumer oriented products and
solutions, whereas additive manufacturing will primarily be associated with scientific literature
or industrial applications [5]. In the following report 3D­printing and additive manufacturing will
be used interchangeably as well as 3D­printer andadditive manufacturing (AM) machine. The
terminology used in the following work is defined in ISO/ASTM 52900.
1.1.1
Extrusion based additive manufacturing
The most common and accessible form of additive manufacturing is arguably material extrusion­
based additive manufacturing (ME­AM). This process has the capability to rapidly produce
physical prototypes at low cost and little effort [7], [8]. The immense popularity of this method
can be attributed to the open source hardware (OSH) RepRap project and the subsequent
development of mass manufactured machines, so called ”clones”. The price of an ME­AM
machine when the RepRap project started was in excess of 100000 US$, however the most
popular consumer level 3D­printer on Amazon.com today, is priced at 165 US$ [9]. Whether
this is a direct result of the RepRap initiative is uncertain, however the 10 most popular choices
for ME­AM machines on Amazon.com are all clones of the Prusa Mendel I3 design [10].

The technology is based on the controlled extrusion and rapid solidifying of molten thermo­
plastic polymer to deposit material in stacked layers to construct a model. The plastic is most
commonly supplied in rolls of filament of which an extrusion mechanism drives the plastic down
through a liquifier and a nozzle. The deposition is controlled by continuously extruding filament
through the nozzle while moving either the build platform or nozzle around the volume. This
is typically done via a Cartesian cnc motion system and usually in layered increments on one
axis. Once the desired layer has been deposited the nozzle or the build platform is moved by
one layer height to deposit the subsequent layer [11]. Figure 1.1 illustrates the main features of
a ME­AM system.
Figure 1.1: This figure illustrates the basic functional elements of a ME­AM machine: (1) A
spool of filament feedstock, (2) thermoplastic filament, (3) printhead with 2 axis mobility1, (4)
an extrusion mechanism, (5) a liquefier, (6) a nozzle, (7) the printed part in layered increments
and, (8) a build platform capable of 1 axis mobility. The figure was acquired from article by M.
Spoerk et al (2019) [11].
The most common materials in ME­AM are poly(lactic acid) (PLA) and acrylonitrile butadi­
ene styrene (ABS). These materials have been utilised for many years and as manufacturers
invested in making filament feedstock for ME­AM, the processability of these materials have
been improved [11]. In recent years, new materials have been introduced to the market and the
possibilities of ME­AM are growing with every new material addition.

1.1.2
Open source development
Through open source initiatives in hardware (OSH), firmware and software (OSS), numerous
solutions and innovations have been developed by professionals and amateurs all over the
world. The RepRap project, directly or indirectly spawned many commercial ME­AM machines
that are on the market today, and the Arduino open source hardware and software initiative
has been utilised to develop and implement motherboards for many of those machines. Prusa
printers were famously developed from the RepRap initiative and spawned a completely new
design of RepRap machines, the Prusa Mendel I3 [12]. Their most recent model, the MK3S
shown in figure 1.2, shares most of the fundamental design as Josef Prusa’s Mendel I3 RepRap.
Furthermore, open source firmware, such as Marlin and RepRap firmware have allowed
amateur hobbyists, and professionals alike, to configure and tweak machine control for the
purpose of research and development in the field of ME­AM. Even the software necessary to
turn 3D models into machine language, or ”G­code”, has been developed as an open source
software (OSS) initiative. Software such as Sli3er and Cura are continuously being developed
and improved, and numerous contributions from the community in the form of ”add­ons” or
”plug­ins” have been made to cater to specific needs of users.
Figure 1.2: The most recent Prusa MK3S machine, based on the Prusa Mendel I3 RepRap
machine. Picture acquired from Prusa3D website [13].
The communities that have developed around these initiatives have also spawned a culture
of ”users as innovators”, where users have every opportunity to design, make and implement
their own improvements or modifications to these machines [14]. Furthermore, the community
is a hub of knowledge and experience where users are able to get advice and guidance on the
subject from other users and developers.
1.2
Polyoxymethylene
Polyoxymethylene POM is a thermoplastic engineering polymer first synthesised at the begin­
ning of the 20th century and patented under that name in 1956 by Robert N. McDonald. There­
after, chemical producer DuPont started production of POM homopolymer (POM­H) with the
trade name Delrin, which is still today the preferred name for the polymer in the US [15].
POM is a semi crystalline polymer which is semi­translucent at low thickness, but becomes
a natural white colour as it increases in thickness. It is produced in a wide range of variants with

additives to affect it’s processability and various other qualities, both as co­ and homopolymer.
However, generally POM is considered to have a great balance between mechanical proper­
ties and chemical resistance. Furthermore, it’s low friction and wear characteristics coupled
with moisture, and hydrocarbon resistance make it one of the best polymer materials for food
processing application e.g. conveyor belts and anything in direct contact with food products.
POM is also used a great deal in the automotive industry due to it’s rigidity and resistance to fuel
and oils. Many of those properties can be attributed to it’s high crystallinity, typically between
70­80%. It is applicable to all conventional manufacturing processes such as injection mould­
ing and extrusion. Furthermore, it can be supplied as machining feedstock such as bars, rods,
and sheets which are further processed in machining tools for part fabrication in low quantities.
POMs excellent machinability allows for part finishing on machine tools, for tighter tolerances
on more critical features, after conventional processing [15].
With all these attractive qualities, POM seems like an obvious choice for a variety of appli­
cations. However, with all its great properties, processing POM in ME­AM is still very limited.
1.3
ME­AM of POM
Currently very little research can be found on the subject of ME­AM of POM, even a very thor­
ough review of semi­crystalline polymers used in ME­AM listed POM as an example of a semi­
crystalline feedstock material. However, it did not discuss it’s application in ME­AM any further
or cite any literature regarding the subject [16]. Primarily, the following work could be found
that address the application of POM in ME­AM: M. Galeja et al. on POM/EVA (ethylene­vinyl
acetate) blends for enhanced processability with respect to ME­AM [17], I. Muro­Fraguas et
al. on plasma surface treatment of build surface for improved initial layer adhesion of POM
in ME­AM [18], and P. Tian with a very thorough thesis on the subject of ME­AM of POM [19].
However, non suggest an extensive guide on how to obtain acceptable print quality with regards
to machine requirements.
There have been a number of studies on ME­AM of crystalline thermoplastic polymer which
have been discussed and reviewed by D. Vaes and P. Van Puyvelde. In their article they iden­
tify four phenomena specifically related to semi­crystalline feedstock for ME­AM. These are:
insufficient melting and self­nucleation, oriantational effects and flow­induced crystallisation, in­
terlayer adhesion, and shrinkage and dimensional stability. Although interlayer adhesion is a
subject of interest in any ME­AM process, and in fact any layer based AM, there are some
properties and factors specific to semi­crystalline polymers that effect interlayer adhesion.
Internet forums dedicated to 3D printing are numerous and can provide substantial insight
into the field of ME­AM, albeit somewhat rudimentary and lacking in scientific methodology and
convention. A few posts have been made on various forums documenting the challenges users
were facing when trying to use POM filament, some also suggested solutions and reported
some success.
From both scientific literature and community feedback, the main challenges identified with
ME­AM with POM as feedstock material are: shrinkage and warping, interlayer adhesion and
build surface adhesion.
1.3.1
Shrinkage and warping
When a thermoplastic polymer solidifies and cools it also contracts, a phenomenon commonly
known as shrinkage. The level of shrinkage is determined by various parameters such as the
coefficient of linear thermal expansion (CLTE) of the material, which is defined by the following
equation:

determined by the original length of the part, and the temperature difference between its hot
and cold state.
Amorphous polymers have a mostly constant CLTE at temperatures above their glass tran­
sition temperature (Tg), where most of their shrinkage occurs, and their volume shrinkage is
linear throughout the temperature range. Semi­crystalline polymers, however, display a much
higher level of contraction due to crystallisation. The reason for this is that as the polymer
starts to solidify and crystal structures begin to form, the polymer chains are packed into a
much denser structure than their amorphous counterparts [16]. According to the datasheet for
DuPont Delrin 500P NC010 POM­H [20] the CLTE is 110 · 10−6/K or 110µm per meter, per
degree Kelvin/Celsius. However, according to data from campus plastics database, the CLTE
of DuPont Delrin 500P NC010 POM­H is non linear and follows a curve from 220 · 10−6/K at
150◦C down to 110 · 10−6/K at ­40◦C [21]. This behaviour can be explained by the crystallisa­
tion of the material. Furthermore, highly crystalline polymers such as polypropylene (PP) and
POM have been identified as having a greater tendency to shrink in ME­AM processes when
compared to amorphous, or less crystalline polymers. M. Spoerk et al. referenced numerous
sources that have studied the warping of PP in ME­AM processes and outlined the correlation
between crystallinity and volumetric shrinkage. In their work they show a comparison of the
specific volume of ABS, and two types of PP with different levels of crystallinity. Figure 1.3
shows specific volume normalised with respect to each material’s level at 40◦C [11]. Using data
from Campus plastics a similar graph can be made for POM which can be seen in figure 1.4
[21]. The figure also has approximate values for the relative specific volume displayed in figure
1.3 plotted in for comparison. The comparison shows even grater tendency to warping in POM
when compared to PP and ABS.

In traditional processes, such as injection moulding, warping occurs when the material cools
non­uniformly, sometimes caused by uneven part geometry where surface to volume ratio dif­
fers. This causes the part to shrink non­uniformly and internal stresses build up in the part
causing it to deform. In ME­AM this non­uniform cooling is caused as the nozzle, depositing
hot extrudate moves higher, the lower layers get colder and contract, while the layers closer to
the nozzle remain at a higher temperature. Moreover, the edges of the printed geometry cool
faster and retain less heat than the center. These factors create a thermal gradient that causes
internal stresses to form in the part which results in deformation. An example of a warped part
can be seen on figure 1.5. Furthermore, part cooling has been shown to have a significant
impact on the dimensional stability during the printing process though sources do not agree on
it’s specific effect, leading to the assumption that it depends on other parameters as well [16].

sequently detaching from the build surface.

1.3.2
Interlayer adhesion
Interlayer adhesion has been a subject of interest in ME­AM since the beginning. Because
of the nature of the process of depositing layers sequentially to fabricate an object, the level
of bonding between said layers is critical to the mechanical properties of the printed part [8],
[16], [22], [23]. The inter­layer bond strength of ME­AM parts has been studied somewhat, e.g.
by C. McIlroy & P.D. Olmsted and by Seppala et al. Their work relied on the theory of polymer
welding, and both recognised weld temperature as one of the main factors in weld strength [22],
[23]. However, it should be noted that both studies were conducted with ABS and PC which are
both amorphous polymers and it is unclear if their conclusions apply for POM, which is a semi
crystalline polymer.
Furthermore, numerous studies were reviewed by D. Vaes and P. Van Puyvelde on interlayer
adhesion identifying crystallisation as an obstacle for polymeric chains to form bonds between
layers. Mainly the forming of crystal structures on the polymer­polymer boundary limits the
mobility of polymer chains to defuse and intertwine through the interface. A consensus among
the various studies was that melt temperature was a significant factor to control in order to
improve inter­layer bonding [16].
Figure 1.6: The picture is of a printed specimen beginning to show delamination failure.
Delamination is a term used primarily on the subject of laminate materials and the word
delamination is derived from lamina, meaning layered, delamination meaning un­layered or
becoming separate. Delamination is defined as a failure mode in which a part fractures into
layers [24]. In this project, the delamination occurs when internal stresses due to non­uniform
cooling (or warping) overcome inter­layer bond strength and fracture permeates through the
part between extruded layers. The result of this can be seen on figure 1.6. The mechanism
that drives delamination is the same as warping while the resisting mechanism is the bond
strength between layers. Therefore, minimising warping and maximising bond strength would
both reduce the risk of delamination.

1.3.3
Build surface adhesion
Due to the excessive shrinkage and warping experienced when printing with POM, the resulting
deformation causes the part to curl up and tear away from the build surface. POMs low coeffi­
cient of friction is also a contributing factor to the materials poor ability to mechanically adhere
to the build surface.
Figure 1.7: Compatibility of polymers acquired from nemould.com [25]. It shows that POM is
very reluctant to bond with other polymers
Furthermore, as shown from the figure 1.7 above POM has a very limited adhesion com­
patibility with other polymers. This means that common solutions for build surface adhesion,
such as a sheet of polycarbonate (PC) or the application of hairspray or ABS slurry to the build
surface, do not work when printing with POM.
M. Spoerk et al. (2018) studied the effects of various process parameters on bond strength
between PP and the build surface in ME­AM. Their results showed that the bond strength could
be improved with increased platform­ and nozzle temperature, increased surface roughness,
and decreased thickness of the initial layer height. Furthermore, they concluded that using a
build surface of the same material as the feedstock material had great effect on the adhesion
properties of the printed parts [26].
1.3.4
Part detail quality
As the melted polymer is extruded, it needs to solidify quickly after exiting the nozzle in order
to replicate detailed features in the part. That is, if the extrudate remains molten and at low
viscosity at higher temperature, it is unable to retain the desired geometry. Control of extrudate
cooling is important to control the quality of detail in the build process.

Material selection for consumer level ME­AM has been slowly increasing in the last few

by introducing another material into their ”toolbox” seems like the best way to advance the
technology.
As testament to the novelty of research into POM as feedstock for ME­AM, D. Vaes and P.
Van Puyveldes review on semi­crystalline feedstock material for polymer ME­AM included 283
sources on the subject, however, none involved POM material [16].
1.5
Constraints
With the motivation of contributing back to the OSS and OSH communities that enabled so much
progress, certain constrains must be made to the project. Primarily limitations to procurement
budget, and access to equipment and facilities. The modifications must not exceed 4000 DKK
or involve using any equipment or machinery not available to the average contributor to OSH
projects. This pertains to the use of machine tools, such as lathes and mills, or any expensive or
specialised equipment that would not be considered commonplace in a home workshop. Fur­
thermore, any purchased components must be commercially available for anyone to procure.
By staying within these constraints, it can be assured that replication of the construction
would be feasible to most enthusiasts with access to the most basic equipment and resources.
1.6
Research questions
• Is it possible to construct an ME­AM machine within the boundaries of the predefined
constraints that can address or overcome the aforementioned challenges?
• Is it possible to identify and control process parameters that would improve part quality
with regards to the level of warping and delamination experienced in the ME­AM of highly
crystalline polymers such as POM?
• To what extent can the build quality of such a machine be improved when compared to a
consumer desktop ME­AM machine when using POM as feedstock material?

2.1
Process parameters
From the discussion in the previous chapter, it is clear that temperature control is very important
and the balance between heat and cooling will be a compromise. As with any ME­AM process
for polymers, melt temperature control is very important [16]. Furthermore, as identified by D.
Vaes and P. Van Puyvelde, adjustable chamber temperature would be optimal to control as
well as any cooling applied on the part while printing in order to potentially reduce warping [16].
Control over build surface temperature could also be beneficial to increase adhesion to the
build surface according to the findings of M. Spoerk et al [26].
2.1.1
Temperature control
Warping occurs when a part cools and contracts unevenly or if the parts geometry introduces
differential stiffness throughout the part making some sections deform as the part shrinks [16].
With figure 2.1 as reference, two methods of temperature control were considered to reduce
warping.
Figure 2.1: Specific volume of two grades of POM normalised to specific volume at 40◦C, rep­
resenting comparative shrinkage. The vertical lines are placed at 100◦C and 150◦C for clarifi­
cation. Values were acquired from Campus plastics database [21].
The first method would be to keep the whole part as hot as possible while printing, while
still being able to support itself. Ideally this would mean keeping the entire part at over 150◦C,
around its crystallisation temperature to keep the material from crystallising fully. After the build
cycle was finished the chamber could be slowly cooled down to ambient temperature and the
Material Extrusion Additive Manufacturing of Polyoxymethylene
11
==PAGE:22==
part could uniformly crystallise and contract. This is very difficult to achieve within the scope of
this project due to the complexity involved in operations at this temperature. A few examples
of which would be: isolating components and equipment from the heated air in the chamber,
implementing a heat­source able to maintain the temperature, and, the risk associated with a
machine operating at these elevated temperatures. Additionally, it is challenging to control the
extrusion and freeze the melt in place to form the desired geometry.
The second method considered would be to cool the melt rapidly below about 100◦C, where
the polymer has experienced most of the shrinkage and crystallisation. This expeditious cooling
needs to happen as rapidly as possible to reduce the effect of the shrinkage on the rest of the
part. This in turn requires immense cooling, concentrated below the tip of the nozzle. The
downside to this method is that if the previous layer is too cold, the melt temperature will have
to be increased to provide enough energy to allow for adequate fusion between the layers [16].
A few other possible methods were considered as described by D. Vaes and P. Van Puyvelde,
e.g. using a laser to concentrate heat on the path in front of the extrudate or using IR emitting
heat lamps to keep the print at elevated temperatures during the cycle [16]. However, these
options fell outside the constraints and scope of the project and were therefore left as additional
options to consider as future addition to the platform.
2.1.2
Build surface
For improved build surface adhesion a few options were considered. The first option would be
mechanical locking by increasing surface area, potentially with extruding fibers enveloped in
the initial layers. This has shown promising results in prior work by using the rough side of a
”Masonite” fiberboard as the build surface [27]. One downside of using fiberboard is it’s low
thermal conductivity, which results in less heat being conducted through the build surface than
expected. Another, more significant downside, would be the material that sticks to the printed
part and removed along with it. This results in the build surface becoming uneven and requiring
replacing after some number of build cycles.
A second option was considered and tested, which was to cover the build platform with a thin
sheet of POM material. The argument for adhesion to a build surface of POM were based on
the principles of polymer welding [22], [23]. To further improve the chances of good adhesion,
the surface of the POM sheet could be abraded to increase surface roughness as suggested
by M. Spoerk et al [26].
Both methods benefit from increased temperature of the surface. In the case of mechanical
adhesion, the increased heat of the surface will slow cooling of the extrudate and keep it at
a lower viscosity for longer, allowing it to permeate further into pores and cover fibers in the
substrate [28], [29]. In the case of the POM substrate, the weld strength is improved by higher
temperature of the surface material [22], [23], [26].
2.2
Parametric control, machine design and construction
2.2.1
General concepts and technology
The constraints mentioned in section 1.5 limit the equipment choices available, to the level of
consumer/hobby 3D­printer technology. Generally a ME­AM machine needs to control move­
ment on 3 axes, control extrusion and temperature of the nozzle, and receive and read instruc­
tions, commonly in the form of G­code.
Motion control has generally utilised some form of mechanical system actuated by a stepper
motor. Stepper motors are capable of controlling their rotation by angular increments, a common
step size of a stepper motor is 1.8◦. Furthermore, they are capable of applying a holding torque,
meaning that applying current to them without changing polarity will cause them to resist rotation.
Temperature control is usually achieved by a pair of heating element in the form of a car­
tridge that employs Joule heating, and a thermistor that changes resistance dependant on the
12
Material Extrusion Additive Manufacturing of Polyoxymethylene
==PAGE:23==
temperature. The heating element heats up when supplied with a certain voltage, usually ei­
ther 12V or 24V. The thermistor is placed close to the element, and its resistance measured
continuously.
Receiving, reading and interpreting G­code requires some level of processing power. There­
fore, the machine must be equipped with a processor that can be programmed to receive and
read G­code, send signals to drive motors and power heaters, and read thermistors. Further­
more, it would be practical if the controller would have a communication interface so the machine
could receive commands and send information to another device such as a PC or a laptop.
In the development of the desktop 3D­printer, from the early days of the RepRap project and
until a few years ago, the Arduino Mega 2560, shown on figure 2.2 has been the controller of
choice for a large majority of these machines. The Arduino Mega 2560 is an open source, pro­
grammable micro­controller based on the ATmega2560 processor, with 54 digital input/output
pins, 16 analog inputs and 4 UARTs (hardware serial ports). It can be programmed by writing
code for it in it’s dedicated integrated development environment (IDE), and uploading it onto
the device using a USB connection [30]. Furthermore, since the Arduino Mega 2560 was so
popular for the use of controlling electronic and mechanical systems, a special attachment or
”shield” was designed for the Mega by the RepRap community, for easy interfacing with modu­
lar components to control both stepper motors and heaters, as well as reading thermistors and
more. This board was called RepRap Arduino Mega Pololu Shield (RAMPS), pictured in figure
2.3, and has been a very popular option for controlling hobby/consumer level ME­AM machines
due to it’s flexible modular design and accessibility.[2]

The Witbox, displayed in figure 2.4 is a 3D­printer made by BQ, first released in 2014. It has

the frame and drives a belt that moves the x­axis gantry, along with the x­axis stepper motor.

screw is connected to the build platform and moves it along vertical rails when the lead screw

2.2.3
Machine modifications
Before any modifications were made on the machine, the external panels, along with any other
components that were seen as unnecessary or hindering, were removed. The sheet metal
frame of the machine proved to be beneficial since adding fixtures and mounts was as easy
as drilling and tapping a hole in the frame. As previously discussed, the main emphasis was
made on controlling the temperature of the nozzle, build surface and chamber, as well as some
way of controlling the amount of cooling that is applied and the print speed. The Witbox comes
equipped to control the nozzle temperature, and the build speed can be controlled in the G­
code. Additionally the machine has a fan that blows air through a duct to cool the extrudate.
The fan speed can be set in G­code and is controlled via pulse width modulation (PWM) from
the printer’s motherboard.

Heated build chamber
Control over the build chamber’s internal temperature, requires the means to increase and
decrease the temperature inside the build chamber while simultaneously monitoring it. Since
it was unclear how high the chamber temperature needed to be for the best build quality, the
aim was set to be able to bring the chamber temperature up to as high as possible without the
risk of damaging any components or the risk of physical harm to the operator. This limit was
set to be 100◦C in the firmware, allowing the set temperature of the machine to be 90◦C but
shutting down if temperature rose above 100◦C. Below 100◦C, physical contact with metallic
parts does not burn the skin before a person with healthy reflexes has the time to react and
in the case of handling hot components it is enough to wear gloves and cover any exposed
skin. As a precaution to avoid complications due to overheating, the extruder was removed
from the x­y carriage inside the build chamber and converted into a ”Bowden” extruder, with the
stepper motor sitting outside the chamber. Furthermore, a panel was designed to isolate the
electronics from the build chamber since the stepper motor drivers need considerable cooling,
along with the PSU and many other electronic components. The stepper motor on the z­axis
lead screw was located beneath the dividing panels and was therefore operating in a colder
environment. However, the stepper motors controlling x and y movement remained inside the
heated chamber because of the complexity of moving them outside or otherwise isolate them.
This did not seem to affect their performance throughout the project but might have long term
effects.

Bowden conversion
A Bowden extrusion configuration consists of the extruder being situated away from the print­
head, e.g. on a x­axis gantry, or on the frame of the machine as illustrated in figure 2.17. This
configuration reduces the amount of mass on the printhead and allows for more rapid acceler­
Material Extrusion Additive Manufacturing of Polyoxymethylene
19
==PAGE:30==
ations and potentially better print quality. The principles of this method is that the filament is
fed into the extruder and pushed through a PFTE tube, called the Bowden tube, to the hotend
on the printhead. Whereas a direct drive extruder, such as the original Witbox utilises, has the
extruder on the printhead directly above the hotend and nozzle. This configuration benefits from
better extrusion control, especially with soft, and flexible filaments like TPU and TPE’s. Since
POM has a relatively high Young’s modulus compared to other polymers that are applicable to
Bowden extrusion, it was considered acceptable to convert the machine to Bowden extrusion.

ABL sensor
Ensuring that the build surface is parallel to the plane of the x­y carriage improves adhesion
to the build surface since the initial layer height and extrusion pressure is even throughout. It
was therefore decided to implement an ABL sensor to allow the machine to compensate for
an uneven build surface. A few different types of sensors are available for this task, the most
common of which are; tactile, capacitive and inductive. Ultimately it was decided to use a
capacitive sensor for this purpose. One advantage of using a capacitive sensor, rather than
an inductive sensor, is that it can be triggered by proximity to non­metallic objects. This was
considered beneficial since the heated aluminium build surface might have to be covered by
some other material to improve adhesion. Furthermore, the capacitive sensor is not as sensitive
to elevated temperatures as a tactile sensor e.g. which is also capable of triggering with any
solid material.

to the microcontroller and damage the system. In the circuit in figure 2.25 there are multiple
resistors between 24V and the Arduino to ensure a 5V supply on the input pin, and even a
resistor between the signal wire and the Ardunio to further protect the system from a short.
Ventilation
If POM is heated over a certain temperature, ranging from 230­260◦C depending on the grade,
or if it is left at elevated temperatures for extended periods it can start to degrade and release
formaldehyde gas [38]. It is therefore necessary to implement a ventilation for the machine, in
case of thermal decomposition. This was done by cutting a hole in the top insulation panel,
modeling and printing a simple flange from PLA (see appendix A.1 for detailed drawings) and
attaching a ducting hose from the preexisting ventilation ducts in the laboratory. The ducting
and printed vent can be seen in figures 2.26 and 2.27 respectively.
For facilities without preexisting ventilation, the flange design can be modified to fit a 100mm
diameter fan to push air through the ducting hose that can be made to exhaust through an open
window.
However, no smell was detected when printing from POM throughout the duration of the
project, and the ventilation is mostly considered a precaution for the user.

3.1
Response evaluation & specimen design
The main challenges with ME­AM of POM have been identified as the shrinkage, and subse­
quent warping of printed parts resulting in unacceptable build quality. Slower part cooling, and
increased ambient temperature of the build volume have been suggested as a solution to this.
However, this strategy would compromise geometric detail in the part due to the reduced control
over material deposition. Therefore, the responses selected to be evaluated during experiments
were the level of warping, and the geometric quality of features.

being able to characterise the quality of detail and the ”fin” was designed to exaggerate warping.

3.2
Screening
The quantity and variability of build parameters is practically unlimited. The various parameters
that can be adjusted and tweaked are in the hundreds. However, many of them are irrelevant to
the planed experiments and can be ignored, while a few have a huge impact on the response.
The very first builds done by the machine were meant to establish feasible print settings and
reliable build parameters. Thereafter, and before making the design of experiments (DOE),
factors and factor levels had to be determined. This was done by process of elimination where
one by one, factors that did not affect warping or build quality were filtered out and set as
constant, and a few factors selected for further experimentation. Unfortunately, at the time of
starting this preliminary screening, the comprehensive review, cited multiple times in previous
chapters, by D. Vaes and P. Van Puyvelde had not been published [16]. However, some of the
other sources mentioned in section 1.3 served as a baseline and guided the screening process,
as well as previous work by the author et al [27].

3.3
Design of experiments
The experiments were conducted as full factorial ANOVA analysis with three factors. ANOVA is
a ”analysis of variance” method that uses statistical methods to determine if a controlled factor
has a statistically significant effect on a response. Each factor had two levels, low and high,
and three repetitions of each run were conducted. Only one specimen could be built at a time
so the number of build cycles becomes
c = n · 2k
(3.1)
where c is the number of cycles, n is number of repetitions, the level of each factor is 2 and k is
the number of factors, so c becomes
c = n · 2k = 3 · 23 = 24.
(3.2)
This means 24 build cycles must be conducted for each specimen type. The runs were built in
sequence and the sequence repeated 3 times, meaning, one specimen from each run was built
after the other and when 8 specimen had been built the sequence was started again from the
top, this was repeated 3 times.

3.4
Process preparation
Process parameters are set in a slicing software which takes a 3D model of the part and converts
it into G­code for the machine to process. The part is split into layers of a chosen thickness
and extrusion paths are constructed in the x­y plane, which the printhead follows and extrudes
material accordingly before moving the build platform down by one layer height and continuing
with the extrusion path of the next layer. The part is usually converted into a shell with a certain
thickness and the solid interior is replaced by sparse infill, with variable density and pattern
design. The software used in this work was Ultimaker Cura, developed as an OSS by Ultimaker,
a machine manufacturer that makes OSH ME­AM machines. Cura allows the user to select and
adjust various parameters. The main parameters to mention that were specified for all runs were
a layer height of 0.2mm, extrusion width of 0.4mm and, for the cubes, an infill of 20% with a
grid pattern was used. Furthermore, a brim function was selected in the slicer which creates a
perimeter of a specified width around the first layer of the part to improve adhesion to the build
surface.
3.5
Build process
The build platform was roughly aligned by hand, by moving the nozzle around the build area
and thumb screws adjusted to level the build platform. Thereafter, a 2mm thick POM sheet
was restrained to the build platform, after being conditioned by abrasion, and the chamber and
platform heated up to operating temperature and a unified bed leveling (UBL) mesh program
started. The UBL mesh was populated by the machine moving the ABL sensor to designated
points across the build area and measuring deviations in the z­ height at locations. The program
then stores the mesh and adjust the z­ height according to the nozzle’s position on the build area.
This was done to compensate for an uneven build surface or warping of the platform due to the
elevated temperature. It is important to note here that the ABL sensor was not triggered by the
2mm POM sheet alone, and the machine did not compensate for any deviations on the surface
of the POM sheet. The nozzle was set to z = 0 at 2mm above the aluminium sheet, i.e. when
the nozzle was starting to touch the surface of the POM sheet. This routine was only conducted
before starting each experiment group. Between build cycles the POM sheet would be replaced,
the machine would heat up, execute a ”home” command and move to the machine origin, and
load the previously acquired mesh before starting the next build cycle. Once the POM sheet
on the build platform has been replaced, the part is removed from the first sheet by slipping a
box­cutter blade between the sheet and the printed part and pushed along to carefully separate
the two. The sheet is then inspected and any residual material is removed and the surface is
re­abraded using sandpaper if necessary. Part and residual removal is demonstrated of figures
3.5 and 3.6.

4.2
Quantification of warping
Before conducting measurements, the specimen were conditioned by cutting and filing away
excess material from the build process to allow the samples to lay flat on their side while being
measured. The process was similar to deburring of a metallic part and caution was taken to
not influence the relevant geometry. The DeMeet 220, an optical/tactile coordinate measuring
machine, was used to characterise the warping of the fin specimen. The fins were positioned
in the measuring area of the machine with the flat top edge against two points and one end
pressed against a third point while using a spring to pin them down. A program was created for
the measurements of these specimen. The program instructed the operator to designate two
points on the straight edge of the fin and aligned a reference axis between those two points. It
then instructed the operator to designate one more point on the side of the specimen that was
also pressed against a point. A perpendicular line was then drawn between the previous axis
and the point and a reference coordinate system was aligned with the x­axis along the first line
and the y­axis aligned with the second line. The origin was then set at the intersection between
the two. After the reference coordinate system had been established, the program instructed
the operator to designate two more points on the curved bottom edge, each close to either end
of the specimen. These points had to be carefully defined outside of any inconsistencies such
as a crack, a broken section or any obvious or exaggerated defect. For this reason, the position
of these two points varied significantly between specimen.

edges. Incidentally, some samples displayed various degrees of delamination which were also

Delamination
Without a standard way to characterise the level of delamination the following improvised method
was used: Each delamination crack was measured lengthwise on all sides of every cube. The
cracks were measured manually, using a caliper and are therefore subject to substantial hu­
man error. Furthermore, the method of measuring the crack length does not follow any specific
characterisation procedure and the value of crack length is an arbitrary value used to quantify
the severity of delamination. In the field of composites any delamination is considered a failure,
however, in this work the level of delamination has been quantified to indicate build quality. This
should be considered while examining the results since it might affect the conclusion.

Build Quality
Unfortunately, the method chosen to characterise the samples was unable to recognise the

data from this analysis will not be published in this thesis since it is of no scientific significance.

5
Discussion
Despite finding only a minor correlation between the factors that were investigated and im­
provements to build quality, there is still evidence to suggest that with further experiments and
modifications or upgrades to the machine, build quality could be improved further. The ANOVA
analysis suggested that print speed, and the interaction between print speed and chamber tem­
perature resulted in less warping. Therefore it is plausible that other factors, e.g. part cooling
fan speed, or nozzle temperature, could effect warping. Furthermore, since the fin specimen
built at the lower nozzle temperature in the experiments failed, the experiments could have been
repeated with different levels for the nozzle temperature or that factor replaced with another to
improve the analysis.
The analysis on delamination response showed a statistically significant correlation between
nozzle temperature, cooling fan speed and chamber temperature, as well as every combination
of those factors and the value of the total crack length of specimen. However, it is important
to note that this value is not a part of any standardised characterisation and therefore might
need further investigation into its validity before drawing definitive conclusions or equating these
findings to model any regression. The previous work of Seppala et al. and, McIlroy and Olmsted
on the mechanics of ME­AM inter­layer bonding as related to polymer extrusion welding theory,
report print temperature as a significant factor to weld strength which matches results from this
work [22], [23].
Unfortunately, quality evaluation could not be performed in a scientifically robust manner due
to limitations in time, experience and characterisation methods. An infinite focus microscope,
which has been used to characterise parts from conventional processing e.g. injection molding
or extrusion quite successfully, proved inapplicable in this case due to the highly irregular geom­
etry and poor tolerances of the printed parts. With parts of such poor detail and resolution, the
variance could be better represented in terms of volume when compared to a reference model.
A 3D scanning approach with a heatmap based on the Hausdorff distance could be considered,
where a histogram of the results could be generated and analysed as well.
As discussed in section 1.3.4, the increased temperature could have reduced part quality by
effecting melt deposition control, which was observed during testing in the form of oozing and
ineffective retraction. However, melt control can also be influenced by the level of temperature
control in the printhead.
5.1
Possible build improvements
5.1.1
IR­heating
Using Ni­Cr wire to radiate heat onto the part during the build cycle might reduce warping caused
by uneven cooling. This was considered at the start of the project but deemed too complicated to
implement in the allotted time. A company called Apium has implemented this solution in their
machines and reported some benefits when compared to heating the whole chamber. They
also claim to be able to reliably print with POM material [41]. Furthermore, Vidya et al. reported
some success using IR­lamps to preheat previous layers on a large scale ME­AM platform [42],
[43].
5.1.2
Improved printhead design
In the limited time of this work, the printhead mount was not improved further after confirming
that the initial design was sufficient to start the experiments. The build quality of the printed parts
suggest that melt control might be lacking, which could be caused by inadequate cooling of the
heat sink on the hotend. By improving the design to allow improved cooling on the heat break,
extrusion control could be improved to reduce over­extrusion, stringing and improve retraction.

==PAGE:58==
5.1.3
Improved part cooling
Improved duct design could help to localise and concentrate cooling on the point of extrusion;
rapidly cooling the melt down below melt temperature, or even below crystallisation temperature.
There is no guaranty that this would improve build quality, however this was a theory that was
not possible to verify during this work.
Furthermore, the chamber temperature thermistor could be implemented into the part cool­
ing duct to ensure the temperature of the cooling medium is known. In its current location the
thermistor is measuring the temperature of the air in the front­left corner of the machine, which
is of no real interest other than as a general indication of the chamber temperature. Since the
parameter of interest is the temperature of air being blown around the part, it would be beneficial
to move the thermistor to monitor this value.
5.1.4
Improved chamber heating
Based on experiences gained during this project, chamber heating could be improved. During
machine warm up the heating was rather slow, however, once the build cycle started, the cham­
ber temperature would continue to rise without input from the heater and would require lifting
the top insulation panel to help cool the chamber.
The Marlin firmware allows control of a ”chamber fan” which is used to cool the chamber
by either pushing colder air into the chamber or vent hot air out of it. This method could be
implemented to help better regulate the chamber temperature, albeit only by venting cold air
in, because of health and safety issues addressed in section 2.2.3, unless there is ventilation
available.
5.1.5
Tactile ABL sensor
A tactile auto bed leveling sensor could be implemented instead of the current one, with the abil­
ity to compensate for any unevenness of the actual build surface, and for differing thicknesses
of the surface sheets. This could allow for thicker surface sheets, with increased stiffness that
could resist the warping stresses of the printed parts, to be tested.
5.1.6
Thicker surface material
As explained in section 3.5.1 the thin sheet would warp when exposed to the differential tem­
peratures of the heated build platform and the build volume. Furthermore, when the fin samples
started warping, the POM surface sheet would be contracted with the contracting layers of the
fin and be lifted away from the hot aluminium, further reducing the heat diffusion through the
build surface. Therefore, it might be of interest to further experiment with a more rigid surface
that would better resist the warping of the printed parts.
5.1.7
Solution for cooling motors and fans
Stepper motors rated for a 80◦C temperature rise and 50◦C maximum ambient working temper­
ature would suggest that the maximum working temperature of these motors would be between
100­130◦C. However, working in a chamber that reaches 90◦C ambient temperature leaves only
10­40◦C in temperature rise. This means that either increased cooling is required to dissipate
enough heat to keep the temperature of the motors within safe working conditions or reducing
power to the motors, reducing their performance. Furthermore, the cooling fans used on the
carriage for both, the hotend heat sink and the part cooling, are generally only rated for operat­
ing in about a maximum of 70◦C ambient temperature. Therefore, the effects on the long­term
operation of the machine are uncertain and a suitable cooling solution would be suggested as
a further development for this machine.

5.2
Future work
5.2.1
Further study of POM and polymer theory
M. Spoerk et al. studied the ME­AM process of polypropylene (PP) in their work and described
similar challenges associated with PP as with POM. High warping, difficulty achieving adhesion
with the build surface etc. [11], [26]. Further study of their work could spark some ideas for
further experiments using POM. Furthermore, the work of C. McIlroy and P.D. Olmsted and, J.
E. Seppala et al. on the interlayer bond strength in ME­AM as related to the theory of polymer
welding, could help to identify some optimal process parameters for POM [22], [23].
5.2.2
Experiments with varying nozzle sizes and extrusion widths
The size of the nozzle inner­diameter (ID), and the layer height along with the extrusion scalar
determine the width and shape of the extrusion. Small layer thickness and wide nozzle ID results
in a more rectangular shaped extrusion. Whereas a larger layer thickness and narrower nozzle
ID results in a more circular shape of the extrusion. The shape of the extrusion determines
how much contact area is between the extrudate and the previous layer, which influences the
amount of heat transfer. Moreover, the surface­area­to­volume ratio also affects heat transfer
and cooling, which, in turn affects warping, interlayer bonding and more.
It could be worth the effort to investigate different nozzle sizes and layer heights, and record
their influence on print quality.
5.2.3
Employing 3D­scanning technology to characterise part quality
As mentioned in chapter 5, the part quality could be characterised by a method of 3D­scanning
and compared to the nominal reference model. The presented work is clearly lacking char­
acterisation of build detail quality to compare and analyse similarly to the other parameters.
Furthermore, the method of 3D­scanning could be considered applicable due to it’s ability to
scan the entire part with acceptable accuracy in a relatively short amount of time. After the part
has been scanned it can be compared to a reference model, and a heatmap could be generated
with deviation from nominal geometry. The heatmap could also be visually analysed in order to
identify any systematic defects or inconsistencies.
5.2.4
Employing IR­imaging to investigate thermal behavior in the process
As successfully demonstrated by R. Dinwiddie et al. and J. Seppala et al, infrared­ imaging or
thermography can be utilised to measure two­dimensional temperature profiles during ME­AM.
The weld temperature could then be estimated according to the average temperature of the
profile. Implementing this method could offer a possibility of deeper analysis of the mechanism
at work, and possibly enhance understanding of the underlying physics.

6
Conclusion
The results from this work show that without the need for advanced equipment or resources,
a consumer level ME­AM machine can be modified to be capable of building parts from POM
material to a level of quality that might be considered sufficient for a less demanding application.
In principle, the major modifications that are necessary to achieve similar results are an insu­
lated build chamber, a chamber heater, a heated build platform and a sheet of POM material to
serve as a build surface. To have the possibility of experimenting with elevated temperatures,
an all metal hotend should also be considered. These modifications were implemented while
operating within the constraints specified in the beginning of this project.
Figure 6.1: The modified Witbox, or the ”POM­Box”.
Furthermore, the results show that part warping can be reduced with the adjustments of
process parameters that are controllable by the machine and further investigation of parameters
could yield better results with respect to warping. Delamination of printed parts is definitively
tied to various process parameters and can be significantly reduced by tuning temperature­ and
cooling settings. Further experiments with regards to print detail quality are required before any
conclusion can be made on that subject, however, the presented results show promise to that
effect.
Lastly, as shown from comparison with results from an unmodified machine, the difference
in build quality has been improved on a fundamental level. The modified machine is capable of
producing parts conceptually similar to nominal models and with optimised parameters, toler­
ances could possibly be identified and measured. However, even the most successful results
Material Extrusion Additive Manufacturing of Polyoxymethylene

==PAGE:62==
from the unmodified machine were not able to finish a single build cycle before failing. More­
over, without a POM build surface the extrudate was unable to adhere to the machines build
surface, even at the highest temperatures that the printer could reach.
In conclusion, the preceding work serves as a guide for researchers and enthusiasts to make
their own cost effective ME­AM platform capable of successfully utilising POM, and possibly
other highly­crystalline polymers, as feedstock material, and hopefully propel the development
of next­gen ME­AM materials.
