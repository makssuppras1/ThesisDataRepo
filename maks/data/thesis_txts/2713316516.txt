Abstract
The environmental microorganism P. putida has become one of the most imporôÙ
tant metabolic engineering chassis. However, a constant development of new
synthetic biology tools is still necessary to completely domesticate this bacterium.
In this study, two different goals are set to improve P. putida as a universal engiôÙ
neering workhorse.
The first aim was to engineer this microorganism as a highôÙthroughput screening
wholeôÙcell biosensor to detect a broad range of chemicals. To this end, a preôÙ
viously developed sensor strain was further developed to utilize PET enzymatic
degradation products. Once terephthalate (TPA) and ethylene glycol (EG) meôÙ
tabolization was enabled and optimized in our strain, usability of the system was
confirmed by detecting concentrations as low as 0.15 mM TPA, showing an almost
linear correlation between fluorescence signal and TPA concentration. FurtherôÙ
more, the strain can detect secretion products in a mutuallyôÙdependent fashion
when cultured with production strainsëƒ thanks to the shutôÙdown of the utilization of
glucose and putative overflow metabolites.
Additionally, it was intended to develop an irreversible toggleôÙswitch system that
allows shifting between desired phenotypes without the constant supplementation
of inducers. In order to accomplish this, a system based on the inversion of reguôÙ
latory motifs by the action of the Bxb1 integrase was designed. Though the develôÙ
oped switching system requires further optimization, functionality was proven by
using reporter proteins and more complex phenotype regulators (diguanylate cyôÙ
clase). Thus, the foundation was laid for the development of control systems that
allow the expression of desired phenotypes in a cheap, feasible and essentially,
flexible manner.

In recent years, the development of the ãôÙomicsã has broadly increased the range

Enabling growth of P. putida SENS on ethylene glycol
Ethylene glycol (EG) is the second component of PET besides TPA, and similarlyëƒ
a product of its enzymatic degradation. P. putida possesses the necessary enzyôÙ

although the shunt permits this conservation when using C2 substrates that enter

Engineering of P. putida as a TPA wholeôÙcell biosensor
Through this project part, several approaches were followed to dote the previously
developed P. putida SENS strain with the capacity to detect PET enzymatic degraôÙ
dation. Transformation with the pBTãT_ tph plasmid (Narancic et al.) allowed the
use of TPA as a sole carbon source, showing tight correlation between growth
and emitted fluorescence.
This TPA utilization was further improved by adaptive laboratory evolutionëƒ after
which the duplication time decreased from approximately 2 h to slightly more than
1 h. This improvement was caused by a mutation in the RBS (T ãC in the ôÙ16
nt) of the regulator (iclR) in charge of the tph operon expression .
When the evolved sensor strain was used to detect differential concentrations of
TPA, the response provided was almost perfectly linear at concentrations between
0.625 and 10 mM, losing linearity at concentrations below 0.313 mM. Furthermore,
it was demonstrated to be able to distinguish between the efficiency of different
enzymatic PET hydrolysis treatments.
As PET enzymatic degradation liberates several degradation products, some strateôÙ
gies were also designed to allow metabolization of these compounds. The deleôÙ
tion of the gclR gene was performed in the genome via baseôÙediting, allowing the
utilization of ethylene glycol (despite showing a long lag phase at a concentration
of 200 mM EG) as described in Li et al.[56]. The insertion of MHETase gene in
the genome was also targeted, but satisfying results were not achieved.
Though our sensor showed usability to differentiate between PET degradation
treatments and operational range, the sensitivity of the strain could be improved
by MHET utilization. Considering that MHET is released in great amounts in enôÙ
zymatic PET hydrolysisëƒ some steps could be taken to further improve the perforôÙ
mance of our sensor strain: homologous recombination for directed integration of
the MHETase gene or completion of conjugative mating for correct transformation
when using a pBAMD 14 derivative vector.
On top of this, the P. putida SENS strain usage of overflow metabolites was shut
down, permitting its use in a mutuallyôÙdependent fashion when grown together
with E. coli production strains. Given that the SEM group is developing E. coli
strains engineered to secrete lactate, the capacity of P. putida SENS öaceA to
detect and titrate lactate was tested. Results showed that the GFP signal emitted
kept correlation with lactate concentration at concentrations higher than 1.25 mM.
This detection range provides our strain with usability at the time of sensing and
differentiating between variable lactate production and secretion rates.

Engineering a cascading toggle switch for P. putida
Along this project part, the functionality of the genomeôÙintegrated Bxb1 integrase

When used to activate directly the expression of reporter genes, the switching
