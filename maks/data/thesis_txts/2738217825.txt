Abstract
Adhesive solutions are widely used in industry, because they are light, cheap and reliable
solutions. Their behavior and failure under shear loading is often studied by applying a
dead load and measuring the time before failure [23], [5]. Dynamic shear measurements are
however needed to understand the adhesives’ behavior and to investigate a failure criterion
at diﬀerent strain rates. The failure under intermediate angles, between pure tension (0°)
and shear (90°), could then be investigated.
In the ﬁrst place, a setup for dynamic shear measurements is developed.
This type of
measurements is not yet done in this labor, so the method will be described and investigated.
The purpose of this method is to study the adhesive’s behavior and failure in shear under
several conditions of velocities and temperature.
Several loading angles, between 0° (pure tension) and 75° are then measured. A failure crite-
ria for the pure tension case has been studied in a previous work [13]. These measurements
at diﬀerent angles are needed to ﬁnd a combined criteria between shear and tension.
These diﬀerent tests are then modelled using Finite Element Method to study the local
behavior of the tape and better understand their response. This will help the investigation
of a failure criterion, that would combine the shear stress and hydrostatic pressure responsible
for failure in Conﬁned Tension [19].

Introduction
This section will give an overview about the motivation for the thesis, and deﬁne the diﬀerent
goals.
1.1
Motivation for the thesis
Adhesive solutions are widely used in our everyday life. They provide a simple solution
to bond many diﬀerent surfaces together.
The research about these materials and their
properties allow the creation of strong and durable bonds. Their lightness and durability
make them a great advantage in automotive and aviation industry. Furthermore, their small
thickness is interesting for electronics industry.
However, the materials used are complex and not always understood. Many experiments,
under several conditions, are needed to picture their behavior, and their material models
are diﬃcult to calibrate. The failure investigation, under several loading conditions is an
important topic for many applications: electronics, automotive or everyday uses. Finding
suitable models and failure criteria for these materials, under diﬀerent conditions would help
saving a lot of time in testing. The many parameters that can inﬂuence the results have to
be considered and make these investigations more diﬃcult.
1.2
Problem statement
In this thesis, a method for shear measurements will be developed. It will allow the study
of failure under shear loading, for several conditions. A similar study has been performed
in pure tension in a precedent master thesis [13]. Here, several angles will also be tested to
link these shear and tension failure investigations. This will help to cover a broader range of
loading conditions, and better understand the material’s behavior. These diﬀerent tests will
then be modelled thanks to Finite Element Method, in order to have a local understanding
of the test.
The ﬁnal purpose is to ﬁnd a failure criterion, which would be relevant in shear testing. A
criteria has been investigated for another loading angle (0°) [13]. Following this work, the
goal is to ﬁnd a criterion suitable for diﬀerent angles in between by trying to combine the
shear and tension criteria. Intermediates goals will be listed below:
• Development of a shear method: description of sample preparation, test of diﬀerent
possible setups
• Creation of an experimental database with measurements under diﬀerent angles, ve-
locities and temperatures
• Deﬁnition of a material model that would ﬁt large deformations, and many diﬀerent
loading conditions
• Investigation of failure under shear loading
• Investigation of a combined failure criteria for angles between 0°and 90°

Theoretical knowledge
In this section, some introduction about adhesive solutions, and some theory needed in this
study will be discussed. The material’s behavior will be introduced, as well as diﬀerent

2.1
Adhesives and applications
An adhesive is deﬁned as any non-metallic substance used to bind two surfaces together
and resist their debonding. It can be used in some cases instead of welding, screwing or
sewing for example. One of their advantages is the ability to bind diﬀerent type of surfaces
together. Furthermore, it doesn’t reduce the surface properties due to high temperature,
melting or holes. However, the surfaces often need a good preparation for the adhesive to
work as intended.
Adhesives can be split in two main types: the reactive and non-reactive adhesives. Reactive
adhesives will work by creating a chemical reaction between the adhesive and the surface,
whereas non-reactive adhesives are in their ﬁnal state when used. The work of this thesis
will focus on Pressure sensitive adhesives, that are non-reactive ones.
The applications of these technologies are broad.
It can be for everyday uses, but also
in automotive or electronic industries. They are indeed cheap, light and thin solutions to
bond many diﬀerent types of surfaces together. Some examples of applications in electronic
industry are showed in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Examples of applications for adhesives in the electronic industry [2]
2.2
Pressure-Sensitive Adhesives (PSA)
This section will focus on PSAs. It will introduce the diﬀerent types of adhesives, and the
speciﬁc properties of PSAs that will be used in this thesis.
2.2.1
Properties of PSAs
A Pressure Sensitive Adhesive is a type of adhesive that only need a small pressure to
adhere to the surface. There should be no physical or chemical change in the adhesive or
the surface for it to stick. Their tackiness and the strength of the bond are mostly due to
their mechanical properties and viscoelasticity. A highly viscous material with a good chain
mobility results in a high tackiness. In the other hand, some elasticity is needed to resist
to debonding and have a higher adhesion energy. The number of crosslinks can be adapted

to increase this elasticity, but it also has to be adjusted. Too many crosslinks would indeed
prevent the adhesive to ﬂow (and the wettability would not be optimal) and would reduce
energy dissipation which is needed for a better adhesion energy [27].
PSAs are typically made of natural rubber, acrylic or styrenic block copolymers. As stated
before, their rheological and mechanical properties must be adjusted. Some of the needed
properties are listed below: ([7], [6])
• Glass transition temperature lower than usage temperature: the polymer chains need
to be mobile
• High degree of viscoelasticity for small and large strains, in order to have a large energy
dissipation
• Elasticity at long times to prevent creep
The PSA used in this thesis is based on acrylic. It also contains some rubber to strengthen
it. Furthermore, it is a foamed material to improve its resistance to choc. This property will
be important, as it makes the adhesive highly compressible.
2.2.2
Viscoelasticity
A viscoelastic material is a material that encounter both viscous and elastic properties. It
is the case of rubbers, and most polymers. Their behavior depends on strain rate, but also
temperature. They have the following properties:
• Hysteresis response to loading-unloading, visible on stress-strain curve
• Relaxation: the stress decreases if a constant strain is applied
• Creep: the strain increases if a constant stress is applied
• Dependance on strain rate
A viscoelastic behavior can be characterized in two steps: an elastic and a viscous one. A
visual representation of these behaviors can be done by using a spring for the elastic part,
and a damper for the viscous one.
Elastic behavior
A material has an elastic behavior if the deformation is proportional to the stress applied
and is reversible. Therefore, as soon as the load is removed, the shape should come back
to its undeformed state again. Therefore, its behavior is not history dependent, and the
response is immediate after the load is applied. It can be modelled by a spring. The stiﬀness
may be described by its elastic modulus E.
σ = E · ε
(2.1)
Viscous behavior
A viscous material is highly dependent on velocity and temperature. Its behavior is described
by the strain rate ˙ε and its viscosity µ. For a perfectly viscous material, the strain increases
inﬁnitely when a load is applied.

2.3.1
Rheology
As stated earlier, the materials used are viscoelastic. Their rheological properties can then
be studied, for instance by a method called DMA (Dynamical Mechanical Analysis). This
method is used to measure the dynamic response of a material as a function of frequency
and temperature. To do that, a sinusoidal load is applied to the sample, and their sinusoidal
response is analyzed. An elastic solid would have an instantaneous response to the solicita-
tion. In the other hand, the response of a purely viscous material (Newtonian ﬂuid) would
occur with a phase angle shift of 90°. For a viscoelastic material, this phase angle shift is
between 0°and 90°. This is pictured Figure 2.4. The variables that this method measures,
and their utility will be described in the following.

Time-Temperature Superposition
This method can give the materials’ behavior on a very broad range of frequencies of many
decades. However, testing at these frequencies would be expensive or even impossible. The
polymer also has a dependency on temperature.
When the temperature decreases, the
polymer usually gets stiﬀer (just as when the velocity increases). In the other hand, the
polymer gets softer when the temperature increases or the velocity decreases. Therefore, by
measuring on a smaller range of frequency, and at diﬀerent temperature, the behavior on a
broader range of frequencies can be discovered.
This shift at other frequencies can be made thanks to a shift factor, that will also depend
on frequency. This shift factor can also be determined experimentally, or calculated thanks
to some models. It will allow the construction of the master curve. One of the main models

a reference temperature Tr, and two empirical constants C1 and C2 that must be ﬁtted. It
obviously depends on the temperature T. This equation will be used to ﬁt the measured
shift factors, in order to create the material model.

3.1
Adhesion mechanism
Adhesion is not yet well understood and can come from several mechanisms.
Its study
involves diﬀerent ﬁelds, including fracture mechanics, physics and chemistry of surfaces,
mechanics of materials and polymer chemistry [4]. Some of the adhesion mechanisms will
be introduced here, before focusing on the most important ones for our study.
3.1.1
Physical properties
Physical properties of adhesives can be split in three main components: tack, adhesion and
cohesion. These three properties are illustrated in Figure 3.1. Adhesion refers to the bonding
strength of the adhesive to the substrate, Cohesion is the internal strength of the adhesive,
and Tack is the ability of the adhesive to wet the surface [22].
Figure 3.1: Triangle of adhesive properties [22]
Tack
Tackiness is the instantaneous capacity of adhesion. PSAs have the property to have per-
manent tack [22]. It refers to the wetting behavior, which can be measured thanks to the
Page 10 of 57
==PAGE:16==
3.1
Adhesion mechanism
contact angle between the polymer and the substrate: the higher the angle, the better the
wetting. Surface wetting is indeed the ability to spread over a surface and maintain contact
with it. It depends on chain mobility of the polymer [18].
Adhesion
Adhesion is the ability of the adhesive to stick to the surface. It depends on Van der Waals
interactions, but several mechanisms can have an inﬂuence. They will be described in the
next section. This mechanism depends on the polymer’s properties, but also on environ-
mental factors such as temperature, humidity or dwell time. The surface roughness of the
substrate also has an inﬂuence. A surface a bit rough, with a soft adhesive could lead to a
better adhesion, whereas it might be the opposite if the adhesive is too stiﬀ[25].
Cohesion
Cohesion refers to the inner strength of the adhesive. It can be improved by crosslinking.
However, it only increases the strength of a bond if the adhesion is high enough [21]. Cohesion
is the property tested in shear measurements, so the main one studied here [23].
3.1.2
Diﬀerent adhesion mechanisms
The ﬁrst mechanism that will be introduced here is mechanical interlocking. According to
this theory, the adhesive penetrates pores, voids or crevices. Depending on the size and
shape of these defects, this creates adhesion force. Therefore, surface roughness plays an
important role, as well as wettability of the adhesive [28].
Adsorption theory, also called thermodynamic theory states that adhesion is due to inter-
molecular forces between adhesive and substrate, when they are in contact.
It involves
several mechanisms, such as surface tension, acid-base interaction and many more or polar
interactions [4].
The adhesive may also form chemical bonds with the substrate. These bonds can be co-
valent, ionic but they are mostly Van der Waals interactions. Therefore, more surfaces in
contact increases these interactions [28].
Finally, the weak boundary layer is a mechanism that lower the adhesion. It can be due to
air bubbles, impurities or even reactions between adhesive and substrate. Most of these can
be avoided by a good sample preparation [4].

3.1.3
Adhesion promoter
An adhesion promoter is like a liquid adhesive technology used to enhance the adhesion
between tape and substrate. It is applied on both sides of the substrate, where the tape will
be bonded. A reactive group made of titanate or silane for instance will allow a chemical
bonding between tape and substrate [29]. This mechanism is 10 to 100 times stronger than
other adhesion mechanisms. In order to work properly, this promoter has to be applied, and
dried before applying the tape.
For this thesis, the same promoter will always be used. The procedure will be described in
further sections.

grow horizontally. It can also be called interfacial growth [7]. The cavities then start to

3.2.3
Failure criteria
Some failure criteria have been studied and are used for these materials. A few of them will
be presented here.
Maximum principal stress
By studying the maximum stress in principal directions, the failure can be studied. If this
stress reaches a critical value, the material fails. The principal stress is used, as the adhesives
could fail due to tensile stresses, that arise even under shear loading [3]. With the same idea,
the maximal principal strain can also be studied. Another possibility is to split shear and
normal stress [5].
Criteria for cavitation
Cavitation happens when the hydrostatic pressure in the material becomes too high. When
it reaches a critical pressure, the air bubbles already present in the material starts to grow,
which is at the origin of cavitation [11]. It is an unstability problem, which has been studied
in rubber and is applicable with the material used here [10]. This critical pressure is found
proportional to Young’s modulus E as follow. The complex shear modulus G* can be used
in our case [11].

4.1
Standard characterization
In order to describe the material’s behavior, its response at diﬀerent loading conditions
need to be known. The purpose here is to cover as much as deformation states as possible.
Therefore, any general geometry would be a combination of these deformation states. Several
characterization measurements will be performed to test the material under diﬀerent loading
conditions. Here, the mechanical behavior is tested, and not the adhesion. Therefore, talcum
will be applied in every sample to remove its stickiness. Thanks to that, they will be easier
to handle, and this property won’t intervene in the measurements.
This standard characterization can be split into three parts:
isochoric, volumetric and
thermo-dynamic behavior. In isochoric measurements, the volume is not constrained, which
means that it remains constant, the samples can contract or expand in the unloaded direc-
tions. For volumetric measurements, this contraction or expansion is constrained. Therefore,
the change of volume can truly be studied.

This method is used to measure the dynamic response of the material as a function of
frequency or temperature.
A shear sinusoidal loading is applied to the sample, and the
stress is measured. This will give the moduli G’ and G”, as well as tan δ, as explained section
2.3.1. The result as a function of frequency is shown Figure 2.4. Two peaks are visible on
the tan δ curve. This corresponds to the two glass transition temperatures of the material.
As the adhesive used is a blend of two diﬀerent polymers, two Tg can be seen here.
The shift factors are also found thanks to DMA measurements. By multiplying the strain rate
by these factors, for measurements at a certain temperature, the results will be horizontally
shifted and a master curve could be created.

4.3.3
Discussion
The repeatability seems good for every setup. Single Lap Shear is easier, as only one tape
is bonded. However, even if the clamps are moved, some bending might occur and could
interfere with the results. The clamps can indeed only be moved by steps of 1 mm, and a
gap exists, which makes them able to move a little bit. Even if this movement is less than
1 mm, it can still have an inﬂuence on the measurement, and it is diﬃcult to garantee pure
shear in these conditions. This bending could also get worse as the displacement increases.
For Double Lap Shear with spacer, the results are even more repeatable, especially for the
stress at failure which is similar for all four tests. However, one tape always failed completely
adhesively, whereas the other one failed cohesively. This might be due to the spacer. The
thickness of the spacer was slightly lower than 2.4 mm, which is theoretically the space in
between the plates (one substrate of 2 mm and two tapes). This can be veriﬁed as it is able
to turn around its hole. Thanks to this gap, it should not prevent a good bonding between
the adhesives and the substrates. However, the edges are slightly higher. Because of this, one
tape can’t adhere properly to the substrate and it fails adhesively. Furthermore, it can’t be
checked if the pressure applied to the tapes during bonding is really applied on the samples

or on the spacer. In the other hand, if the spacer thickness is make lower, bending might
occur during this application of pressure.
Finally, for Double Lap Shear without spacer, the curves also look similar, but the failure
does not occur at the same stress for each sample. However, all failure types are cohesive.
These discrepancies might be explained by the four tapes involved. It is more likely to make
a mistake in the bonding or the preparation with four adhesives than with two. Furthermore,
the alignment of all adhesives is more diﬃcult to do precisely. In the other hand, this setup is
fully symmetrical. There is no bending as the upper and lower plates are on the same plane.
Furthermore, the failure is cohesive. Thus, this setup is chosen and all shear measurements
will be done with it.

7.2.1
Set up of simulation model for shear
Geometry
The ﬁrst assumption is that all four tapes see the same deformation and force, before the
ﬁrst failure as the test is considered symmetric. This can be checked by the immobility of
the middle substrates (the plates without a hole), that is veriﬁed on video. This assumption
allows the modelling of one tape only.
The displacement and stress are also considered homogeneous on the whole surface in contact
with the substrate. Therefore, a 2D model is chosen and correspond to a cut of the sample.
So, the geometry used is a rectangle of 15 mm width and 0.2 mm high. The thickness of 15
mm will be deﬁned with the section, by assuming a plane strain state. For the measurements
at 0°C, the rectangle is adapted to 13 x 0.2 mm2 to correspond to the measured geometry.
Step
To deﬁne the step, the non-linearities are taken into account as the material is highly non-
linear. Furthermore, a viscous step is deﬁned. The step time is deﬁned depending on the
time of the experiment.
Mesh
For the mesh, square elements are used as it makes sense with this geometry. The mesh
type is plane strain, with hybrid formulation. This allows to take better into account the
non-linearities of our model [1]. Furthermore, it can be assumed that there is no deformation
out of plane, due to the geometry and the direction of the load applied.
Boundary conditions
The bottom part is ﬁxed, and the upper part is moved in the x direction. Furthermore,
the y direction is free, as the thickness can change during the test. The amplitude imposed
is the one from an actual test.

7.3
Discussion
As seen earlier, the model ﬁts the experience for Conﬁned Tension experiments. However, it
doesn’t ﬁt for shear, and the diﬀerences increases with the angle. At 75°, the model is already
much stiﬀer than measured. The isochoric part of the material model does not represent the
material’s behavior. As said earlier, the deformations seen by the samples goes up to 1500%
(3 mm of displacement over 0.2 mm of thickness). None of the measurement made went until
this level of deformation, and it would be impossible to reach it in standard measurements
like UXT with the machine available.
During the simulation, the thickness of the adhesive increases, which could not be measured
experimentally. Furthermore, the shear results show some hydrostatic pressure, which should
not be that high for a perfect isochoric material. With these two observations, one can
conclude that the compression modulus of the model is not accurate, and does not represent
the real material’s behavior. This high compressibility of the foamed polymer used in this
thesis make the model more diﬃcult, and this characteristic of its behavior should be studied
further.
Finally, seeing that the samples break after 1500% of deformation, one might think that the
material is damaged before, even if it didn’t break yet. This ﬁrst damage is not visible on
the stress-strain curve, and might start at any moment. For Tensile Adhesion experiments,
this ﬁrst stage of damage was obvious with this ﬁrst peak (except for some 75° experiments).
It is not the case for 90° where this ﬁrst peak does not happen.
8
Deﬁnition of a failure criterion
The failure has been investigated experimentally, under several loading conditions of angle,
velocity and temperature. A failure criterion should now be determined. The behavior in
shear will ﬁrst be studied, before studying the hydrostatic pressure.
Page 53 of 57
==PAGE:59==
8.1
Deﬁnition of a criteria for shear
8.1
Deﬁnition of a criteria for shear
First of all, a criterion can be found for failure under shear loading. To investigate it, the
stress at failure for all angles, as studied in section 5.2.3, can be plotted as a function of strain
rate. However, the shear and normal components of the force will be split. It will be plotted
as a function of the shear component of the strain rate called ”ER13”. This parameter is
taken from the simulation result, at the time of failure and in an element located in a part
considered homogeneous. Thanks to that, the inﬂuence of shear should be isolated. The
plot can be seen in Figure 8.1, for every angle except for 0°, as it only sees normal stress.
For all angles except for pure shear, all master curve are aligned. Only the curves at the
highest strain rate show some deviations (measured at 0°C and 33 mm/min). With these
conditions, the stress-strain curve at 75°angle did not show any clear ﬁrst peak, which can
explain the lowest point. According to this result, the adhesive fails at the same shear stress
for every angle.
For 90°, only the results at the lowest strain rate follow the trendline. It corresponds to
measurements at 45°C for all speeds, and 25°C for the lowest velocity. It also corresponds
to the ”smooth” failures, which was not the case for every other condition where the tape
broke abruptly.
This curve at 90°could be the criteria for shear. The other angles would fail before, because
some other mechanisms are at stake due to hydrostatic pressure for example. Some cavitation
has indeed been observed for every angle except for shear. Furthermore, the tests where it
failed at a lower stress (for all angles) corresponds to the adhesive failures (see Table 2). The
ﬁrst peak was still visible on the result, but the tape failed adhesively. This failure often
occurs at lower stress levels than cohesive levels, and that could explain this result.

8.3
Combination of shear and normal components
Some combinations have been tried, to ﬁnd a failure criterion valid for all angles. The idea
is to use a variable for failure by cavitation (hydrostatic pressure), and one for failure by
shear (strain or stress). These two components are summed together. The goal is to ﬁnd,
thanks to some factors in front of each component, a common value for all angles which
would correspond to one measurement condition. It has been tried ﬁrst for measurements
at room temperature, and 3.3 mm/min. The two sums are written below, where FV means
Failure value.
FV1 = A1 · σmax + B1 · p

FV2 = A2 · LEmax + B2 · p

9
Conclusion and outlook
This thesis focused on the failure investigation of Pressure-Sensitive Adhesive under shear
loading, and the study of a failure criterion. In the ﬁrst place, a test method for shear has
been studied and developed. Several setups were tried and compared. The method chosen
showed good repeatability, and the inﬂuence of velocity or temperature could be studied.
This study included the investigation of the inﬂuence of several parameters, especially during
sample preparation.
The second experimental setup used (Tensile Adhesion) allowed measurements in Conﬁned
Tension (0°) which is important to ﬁt the material model as it is the only nearly volumetric
measurement in tension. It also allows measurements at intermediate angles, which is helpful
to understand the transition between the behavior in tension and in shear.
These two
”extreme” loading conditions have indeed completely diﬀerent failure modes.
Finally, a simulation model has been deﬁned for all these measurements. The results are
diﬀerent from measured values, especially in shear. This model has been used to understand
the material’s behavior, especially for strain rate and hydrostatic pressure. This has been
helpful for the investigation of a failure criterion. For this study, the idea was to combine
the shear and tension failure. Experimental datas has been used, but also simulation ones
where a true combination might be found. However, more time is needed to conduct this
study to an end.
Some further investigations could follow this thesis and could not be made here. Firstly, the
experimental setups can be improved. The Double Lap Shear method should be tested with
diﬀerent materials, and compared to the 90°
measurements made with Tensile Adhesion
setup. Furthermore, an investigation of a ﬁrst failure, similar to the one performed for every
other angle than 90°, could be made. It could involve stopping the measurements at diﬀerent
strains, before the ﬁnal failure for instance.
For Tensile Adhesion, the sample preparation could be improved to avoid the bonding issues.
It could be by adding some weight during bonding, or changing the conditions during dwell
time (time or temperature). Another option for the 0° substrate would be to have steel
plates, as it is the case for 90°. It would make the sample preparation easier.
Then, the material model should be improved. The high deformations could not be modelled
with a good accuracy. The compression modulus could probably be investigated and changed.
Some other models could also be tried for the isochoric part.
For the failure investigation, the link between a criterion at 0° and one in shear could be
studied further. The ﬁrst idea would be to plot the normal stress or the hydrostatic pressure
as a function of a volumetric strain rate. As it has been made for shear (Figure 8.1), where
the strain rate in shear has been used, it could be more relevant to plot this hydrostatic
pressure as a function of the corresponding strain rate. Finally, some other combinations,
maybe more complicated as a sum, could be investigated, as well as other failure criteria.
For instance, the bond strain energy rate could be investigated and has been showed relevant
for shear [20].
