Abstract
A Vestas V52 wind turbine is situated at DTU Risø campus and it is used for research ex­
periments. For the implementation of this research project, data of the wind field around
the turbine were obtained from the meteorological mast and from the WindScanner wind
lidar. The meteorological mast tower is located upwind the wind turbine and it captures the
inlet wind speed and direction, while the WindScanner lidars were located downwind the
turbine, measuring the wind speed in the turbine’s wake. For a more clear identification of
the wake, the horizontal wind speed, measured from WindScanner, is presented graphi­
cally using color density plots. The wind data are arranged in different groups according
the wind direction, to investigate how the wake changes for different wind directions. Fol­
lowing that, to locate the center and the spread of the wake across the horizontal and
vertical axes, the simple 2­D Gaussian wake model was used. To solve this model, a
linear regression fitting tool was used. In order to estimate the wind momentum deficit
in the wake area, the equations of continuity and momentum were used, to calculate the
Drag Force (Thrust), that is applied on the wind turbine’s rotor shaft. Last but not least, the
data were split in different wind speed ranges, to inspect how the wake and the estimated
thrust change in different wind speeds.

1
Introduction
There are many remote sensing techniques that measure the wind speed and direction.
This report focuses on the laser­based remote sensing technique, known as Light Detec­
tion and Ranging (Lidar). In this current thesis, measurements have been used from the
Wind Energy department of DTU. Data were acquired from both the Meteorological Mast
(met mast) and the WindScanner (Lidar), in order to estimate the wind momentum deficit
in a wind turbine wake.
1.1
Motivation
During the last decades, people are trying to minimise CO2 emissions as much as possi­
ble, in order to make Earth a more sustainable and safe place to live on. For this reason,
more things are becoming electrified such as cars, public transport, housing heating sys­
tems etc. This leads to an increased demand of electricity and therefore the need of
Renewable Energy Sources (RES) is increasing drastically.
The development of the wind industry has faced a significant rise, during the last couple of
decades. Turbines have become bigger in terms of size and rated power. Most wind farms
nowadays, are simulated in specific softwares (such as WAsP and WindFarmer Analyst),
before their construction, in order to see if the location is suitable and whether the project
will be profitable or not. The exact wind conditions must be known in order to perform these
simulations. For this reason, the determination of the vertical wind profile (wind speed
and direction), has become very important. The traditional way of measuring the wind
conditions is to mount calibrated cup anemometers on tall meteorological masts. Due
to the fact that wind turbines become bigger and bigger, high meteorological masts and
measuring instruments become more complex and expensive. Knowing the exact wind
conditions will help the design and development of the future even larger wind turbines.
Modern wind turbines reach hub heights above 100 m and rotor plane diameters of 120
m or more. Wind speed distribution from one single measuring point (met mast) is not
enough to cover this whole area. For accurate determination of the wind profile around a
wind turbine, it is necessary to use an instrument that can monitor the wind conditions over
the whole area of the rotor plane. In this context, the use of remote sensing measurement
techniques is very popular these days for wind energy applications. An example of the
wind field from data acquired from two LiDARs, from an experiment performed in June
2012, in Collonges Switzerland, is shown in figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Wind speed field measured over the vertical plane of the wake with scans
from two LiDARs [1].
Until 2008, the remote sensing devices were able to measure the wind speed profile along

the vertical direction, i.e., how wind speed varies with height from the surface. In 2008,
a new ground­based research facility was designed and constructed at Risø DTU’s Na­
tional Laboratory for Sustainable Energy. This ground­based remote sensing instrument
is called ”WindScanner” and it is able to make true real­time measurements of the atmo­
spheric velocity field in all three­dimensions and time, which can cover the whole volume
that surrounds the today’s very big wind turbines [2]. The ”WindScanner” lidar plays a sig­
nificant role to the wind energy community, because it has the potential to accelerate the
design and development of larger wind turbines and provide new knowledge regarding
the turbulent conditions that surround the turbines.
So far the ”WindScanner” lidar has been used in several applications: To characterise
the upstream wind velocities around a wind turbine [3], to characterise the complex and
turbulent wind flow below a helicopter [4], to evaluate the coherence of turbulence on
the Lysefjord bridge [5], to characterise the flow inside a wind tunnel [6], etc. In this
current thesis, measurement data from the WindScanner lidar are used to calculate the
momentum deficit in a wind turbine wake.
The objective of this research project is to investigate how the wind behaves around a wind
turbine, i.e. how the presence of the wind turbine affects the wind momentum. The wake
of nearby wind turbines located in a wind farm, can cause serious reduction to the annual
energy production (AEP) of the wind farm. To avoid this, the location of each turbine in a
wind farm must be carefully chosen. For this reason, it is important to acquire observations
of the wake of a full­scale wind turbine. An important part of the thesis objective, is to
use the wake equations to locate the wake downwind of the turbine, and then use the
momentum equation to calculate the wind momentum at the estimated location of the
wake. Another significant goal of this project, is to investigate how the wake and the
momentum change for different sets of wind speeds.
1.2
Research Scope
For the implementation of this research, measurements acquired from a meteorological
mast were used along with data measurements taken from three short­range synchro­
nized WindScanner lidars. To begin with, an initial investigation of the data quality was
carried out, by calculating the 10­minute average wind speeds and comparing the time
series of the obtained measurements. Next, the scanning plane of the WindScanner is
defined and the acquired data were compared with the met­mast data. Moreover, an in­
vestigation was done on the direction of the wind and the data were sorted according to
different wind directions, in groups of 5◦range. A simple wake model was used, to lo­
cate the center and spread of the wake along the y and z axes, for each group of data.
Once the location of the wake is defined, the momentum deficit in the wind turbine wake
is calculated using the continuity equation and the momentum equation. Furthermore,
to examine how different wind speeds affect the momentum deficit in the wind turbine
wake, the data are grouped in different wind speed bins, with a width of 1m/s, and the
calculations are repeated for each group.

Measurement Principles
Since the 60’s researchers at Risø DTU have been making meteorological investigations
to estimate the spread of radioactivity in case of an emergency event, at the nuclear re­
search reactors at Risø. For this reason, they were investigating ways to make measure­
ments of the wind profile more accurate. This led to the development and improvement
of the instrumentation for remote sensing of wind. Back then, the wind conditions were
necessary for meteorological investigations. Nowadays, the research and experimental
development within meteorology, along with the measurement programs at Risø DTU,
have become more associated with wind energy applications [7].
In the beginning of 2003, a lidar unit was mounted on a wind turbine for the first time
in order to estimate the wind speed in front of the rotor plane. A short time after this,
a ground­based wind profiling was followed [7]. This commercial prototype lidar unit is
called ZephIR and it is a continuous wave (CW) coherent lidar system. The ZephIR lidar
system is a robust and reliable system and it is known for its high sensitivity to observations
relative to wind resource assessment [7].
2.1
Lidar’s Measurement Principle
The regulation that a coherent lidar measures the speed of a target is very simple. In
lidar methodology, light is transmitted from a laser at a specific wavelength, it illuminates
many small aerosols that exist in the air, and then it is back­scattered to the sensor.
The aerosols are particles that exist in the atmosphere, such as dust, ash, raindrops or
snowflakes. A small frequency shift is then detected in the back­scattered light, known as
Doppler shift. If the target is moving towards the lidar, the wave is compressed and the
frequency increases, while if it is moving away from the lidar the wave length increases
and the frequency decreases. The frequency shift is measured as the difference between
frequency of the transmitting beam and the frequency of the back­scattered beam on the
photodetector [7]. A generic lidar system is indicated in figure 2.1.
In order to capture the wind profile, a ground­based wind lidar is used, which is transmitting
a continuous wave or a pulsed radiation in three or more predisposed directions. This
ground­base lidar is called ”wind profiler” and its job is to estimate the radial wind speed
in multiple directions above the ground. The Doppler shifts of the back­scattered radiation
of each beam are also determined. Wind lidars have transmitting and receiving antennas,
which the modern wind profilers have both in a single optical telescope.
They are divided into two categories, depending on their operation principle:
• Pulsed lidars
• Continuous wave (CW) lidars
However, there are some main differences between these two, regarding their spatial
resolution and their ability to measure the mean wind speed and turbulence characteristics
of the atmospheric boundary layer.
A pulsed lidar emits many short pulses and the Doppler shift is detected in the back­
scattered light of each pulse. In contrast to the CW lidar, the pulsed lidar can measure
wind speeds from several heights simultaneously, usually up to 10 range gates at a time
and its spatial resolution is independent of the measurement range.

The CW lidar emits a continuous laser beam at a specified height from the ground. When
measures from multiple heights are required, the CW lidar can alter the focus of its tele­
scope to a different height, one at a time. The wind profile is captured by continuously
scanning the beam at fixed ranges. A circular scan is commonly used for each range.
They are capably of doing 1­second ”snapshots” of the flow across the scan disk, at each
range. It is equipped with a 1 W 1.5 μm eye­safe laser, can capture up to 500 wind speed
samples per second, while a pulsed lidar can only take 2­4 wind speed measurements
per second. The high spatial resolution in the near range and the fast data gathering of
the CW lidars, makes them suitable and preferable for turbulence estimations [7].
CW systems have the advantage to custom­fit their performance close to the wind industry
requirements. However, when it comes to choose between CW or pulsed lidars, several
factors must be taken into account, like their cost, their sensitivity, the velocity spatial
resolution as well as maximum and minimum ranges. The operation principle of a lidar
system is shown in figure 2.1.

2.1.1
Transmitter operation principle
The transmitter’s part is to maintain the beam focused at a desired location. The lidars
used for wind profiling, use a conical scan with vertical aligned axis, as illustrated in figure
2.3. This conical scan pattern is also known as VAD (velocity­azimuth­display) and has
been extensively used. Usually, the cone half angle is 30◦which means that the beam
elevation angle is 60◦. Lower angles can be used in some turbine mounted lidars, but a
few factors must be taken into consideration before choosing the optimum angle, such as
the measurement ranges, the placement position on the turbine and the rotor diameter.
The beam intercepts the wind at different angles as it moves and captures the wind speed.
The highest Doppler shifts are obtained when the azimuth scan angle aligns with the
upwind and downwind directions. When the azimuth angle is perpendicular to the flow,
the Doppler shifts are close to zero.
Figure 2.3: Conical scanning pattern of lidars. Left: ground based vertical scanning.
The cone half­angle (θ) is commonly 30◦. Right: wind turbine mounted lidar, where the
scanning is done around a horizontal axis [7].

However, both CW and pulsed lidar profiling, are relying on the assumption that wind flow
over the lidar is strictly homogeneous. Homogeneous wind means that the air flow is
unaffected by nearby complex terrain, like hills, forests, buildings or other turbine wakes.
Based on this assumption, wind lidars are combining the measured radial wind speeds,
over different locations in space, into a single wind vector. For this reason, wind lidar
measurements of the wind profile are not accurate in sites located in hilly or complex
terrain, where hills, forests or other turbines affect the wind field.
Like all the sensors, so do the wind lidars need calibration. At Risø DTU’s test site at
Høvsøre, the calibration of the wind lidars is done by inter­comparing and correlating
the wind speed measurements obtained from a lidar, to the wind speeds obtained from
calibrated cup anemometers, at the 119­m tall met mast. The captured wind speeds from
lidars and cup anemometers are averaged every 10 minute periods and compared in order
to estimate the correlation between the two sensors. The resulted correlation coefficient
can reach the high value of 0.99 [7]. Images of the CW and pulsed lidars are indicated
in figure 2.4 below.

2.2
WindScanner
In order to make accurate measurements of the inflow wind speeds in today’s huge wind
turbines, it is necessary to take measurements at many grid points within the entire rotor
plane.
Back in 2008, at Risø DTU, the aim was to make multiple real­time 3D measurements of
the wind field. This led to the design of the first WindScanner lidar, which is able to capture
the wind vector at various hundred points per second. The WindScanner consists of three
continuous wave (CW) wind lidars and is able to make 3­D scans of the atmospheric
wind velocity field. A 3­D overview of the wind profile will help to comprehend better the
turbulent atmospheric flow around the very big wind turbines of today. Knowing the exact
wind field and the turbulent conditions of the wind, will be extremely helpful for the wind
energy because it will provide new knowledge that will benefit the design and development
of the future enormous wind turbines.
Two of the most important risks a wind farm faces, are the structure failure and the low
power production, which can lead to an unprofitable investment project. Usually wind
turbines located in complex terrain undergo extreme loads that can cause the structure to
fail, e.g. the blades hit the tower. The source of these problems is the complex flow that
surrounds the turbines, which is often caused naturally or by the wake of neighbouring
turbines. This can lead to a lower power production, by the affected turbine. Therefore,
the determination of the wake of a turbine is very important. Hence, for a more accurate
assessment of the structural loads and the power production, a more precised image of
the turbulent flow is required. For this reason, the direct three­dimensional wind fields at
high spatial and temporal resolution need to be measured for all the directions that the
wind approaches the turbine.
Three continuous wave (CW) ”ZephIR” wind lidars of National Power and one ”wind cube”
pulsed lidar, from the French company ”Leosphere”, were used to make the first 3­D
WindScanner. The three CW lidars were modified in order to have fast control of focus
and fast full bandwidth throughput, up to 500 Doppler spectral captures per second per
device. For fast elevation and azimuth control of the laser beams, a 2­D scan head (”Pi­
scanner”) was designed and manufactured. The idea behind this, is to be able to steer the
beams into specific 2­D scanning patterns [2]. However, the version of the short­range
WindScanner used in this research project is not based on a ”ZephIR” lidars, but on wind
lidars developed in DTU, shown in figure 2.5.

2.3
Summary of lidars
Lidars have become a very necessary tool for the wind energy market, the last couple of
decades. Nowadays, continuous or pulsed wind lidars are considered to be very precise
wind measuring devises, which are able to satisfy the wind industry’s needs. In order to
make precise and repeatable measurements of the wind profile, the lidars must be prop­
erly calibrated, aligned, installed and maintained. The wind lidars of today are capable of
replacing the tall meteorological masts, and provide realistic mobile alternatives for wind
resource assessments, on and offshore.
Furthermore, WindScanner wind lidars play a significant role to the wind industry, due to
the fact that they are suitable to make accurate 3­D scans of the wind profile, which will
help the wind energy society to understand better the turbulent flow that surrounds the
big turbines of today.

3.2
The WindScanner
The three CW lidars of the short­range WindScanner are called R2D1, R2D2 and R2D3,
respectively. Each lidar can steer the beam to any location withing the scanning cone with
an opening angle of ±60◦from zenith of the instrument. The three lidars are steered by a
central control unit, in order to focus on the same point in space [9].
The wind data were gathered by scanning along two orthogonal lines, spreading 120 m
horizontally and 90 m vertically, at approximately 110 m East (downwind) from the turbine.
3.3
Experimental Setup
The three units of the WindScanner were installed approximately 73 meters from the origin
of the measurement coordinate system. The three lidars focus their beam at the center of
the coordinate system, thus the scanning pattern is located at the origin of the coordinate
system, approximately 110 meters East of the turbine. The angle that separates the three
units is around 120◦. Their elevation angles were set to be around 30◦from horizontal,
and their azimuths to point approximately 1.8 meters above terrain. The scanning plane
is a vertical plane, where the x­direction is normal to the plane, y is the horizontal in­plane
component and z is the vertical in­plane component. The azimuth angle between x and
East is ­22.3◦[9]. The orientation of the coordinate system is shown in figure 3.2.

4.3
Calculation of the wind momentum deficit in the wake
As the flow of the upwind approaches the turbine, it starts to deviate around it. This is
happening because pressure is induced to the flow, from the presence of the turbine,
which makes the streamlines to diverge and changes the way that the flow propagates.
In order to do a momentum deficit study, a control volume must be constructed. Control
volume is an area where there is an equilibrium both in the mass rate and in the momentum
rate, which means that the input rate of the flow equals the output rate of the flow. To
estimate that, a wind volume V must be chosen within two areas, upstream (Ainlet) and
downstream (Aoutlet) of the turbine. In addition, to go on with the determination of the
momentum deficit, some assumptions must be taken into account: [10]
• The Ainlet and Aoutlet must be chosen for the case that the inflow mass rate equals
the outflow and the edges of these two areas are connected with streamlines.
• The turbulent fluxes are ignored in the calculations.
• The pressure term can also be neglected.
• The force applied to the turbine by the wind, is equal to the force applied by the
turbine to the wind (Fd=T).

6
Discussion ­ Conclusion
From the obtained results, it can be concluded that the WindScanner gives very accurate
information regarding the wind profile, provides high quality measurements, that can be
used to satisfy the wind industry needs or for further research projects and experiments.
Especially by comparing the data obtained from the met­mast with the data from the Wind­
Scanner (figure 3.11), which shows the high sensitivity of the WindScanner to changes of
the wind speed.
As already known, the presence of the wind turbine affects the wind profile at that specific
location. The wind speed curtails downwind the turbine (wake), compared to the wind
velocity upwind. An investigation was done in the wind direction and it was found that the
majority of the time, the wind blows from the north­west, which is a well­known fact for
Denmark (Westerlies). After the data were grouped in different wind direction ranges, and
presented graphically in color plots, it has been noticed that when the wind blows from the
west, the wake is located at the right side, downwind the turbine. However, when the wind
direction is closer to the north, the wake moves to the left side, downwind the turbine.
As mentioned in section 5.2, the outcome data of the wake detection model (2­D Gaus­
sian) are not satisfactory, when using non­normalised values of wind speed. The 2­D
Gaussian model gives more proper results, when using the normalised wind speed and
the estimated parameters are closer to their initial values. One of the main advantages of
the 2­D Gaussian model is its simple usage, while its drawback is that the initial values of
the parameters must be predetermined, guiding the model to find the right solution.
As for the momentum deficit study, it has been decided to focus on the wind direction 290­
295◦, where the thrust was calculated to be 94.27 kN, for the whole range of wind speeds.
The advantages of using the continuity and the momentum equations to estimate the wind
momentum deficit, is that the theory behind this is easy to comprehend, few simple steps
required for the calculations and the fact that it gives accurate results. However, the main
disadvantage of this study is that although in theory the mass rate difference between inlet
and outlet the turbine must be zero, in practice it is never zero due to the discretization of
the grid.
By grouping the data in different wind speed ranges, it can be detected that in lower wind
speeds, the wake is deeper, which means that in those cases there is higher reduction
of the wind speed in the wake of the wind turbine. The thrust is then estimated for every
wind speed set and the results are well correlated with the thrust values calculated from
the bending moments of the turbine’s tower, in another research project [11].

6.1
Uncertainties ­ what could have been done better
As mentioned earlier in this section, the mass rate difference (∆in) between inlet and outlet
the turbine is never zero, due to the discretization of the grid. Nonetheless, one way to
make ∆in to be closer to zero is to increase the spatial resolution of the grid. Another
idea, could be to calculate the ∆in for more areas of upwind data. This can be done by
decreasing the inlet radius step, from 2.5m to 0.5m, so that the inlet radius would be 40,
39.5, 39, 38.5m etc.
6.2
Future Recommendations
As already mentioned in section 4.3, the size of the area of outlet control volume (Aoutlet)
was kept constant during the momentum deficit study. To investigate how the size of the
Aoutlet affects the thrust, the trust curve for different sizes of the area of outlet control
volume could also be calculated in the future. Instead of 40 m outlet radius, 38 m and
42 m could be chosen. In all cases using the corresponding inlet area that minimize the
mass difference.
One of the further calculations that could be also done, is to perform the momentum study
for the other wind directions as well, to examine how the results are affected by different
wind directions.
