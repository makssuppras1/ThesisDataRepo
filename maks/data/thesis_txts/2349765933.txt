Abstract
The power system is changing from being dominated by traditional centralized power
generation to a system with more decentralized power generation, as variable renewable
energy sources are becoming more dominant. With the increasing penetration of ﬂuctuat-
ing generation, the power system is facing a series of challenges which must be addressed
by the system operators in order to assure security, stability, and reliability.
The electric vehicle is becoming more popular and is seen as a future solution for a more
environmentally friendly method of transportation. As the use of electric vehicles grows
and becomes a larger part of the electrical load, there is a greater possibility of using many
vehicles as a large ﬂexible load to assist with the control of the power system. In this
thesis the possibility of using electric vehicles as a ﬂexible load for frequency control is
investigated. The investigations will be performed in a Pan-European interconnected grid
with varying wind power penetration and different operational scenarios. Within this grid,
the thesis will focus on primary frequency control provision from electric vehicles and
how the system behaves as the vehicles are being controlled within their respective areas.
This thesis presents an investigation into how electric vehicles can be modeled in a
high voltage grid. The electric vehicles are modeled with a controller which can control the
charging level of the vehicles to provide primary frequency control and the performances
are tested on several scenarios. The controller is tested with different time delays, including
different time delays between areas and phases in order to investigate how the system
reacts.
The investigations showed that electric vehicles can be used for primary frequency con-
trol with different wind power penetration. By controlling the vehicles the steady state
frequency is raised and if the vehicles react fast enough to the frequency changes the nadir
is raised and ROCOF lowered. Different time delays were investigated which introduced
phase unbalanced conditions to the system but did not cause any instability.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The European Union is working towards a substantial reduction of CO2 emissions, and
the energy sector is set to play a big role to achieve this. The energy sector in its current
state uses mainly fossil fuels for energy generation. There is a possibility of large CO2
reductions by exploiting renewable energy sources instead of the conventional energy
sources. Denmark has set goals of generating 50% of their electrical energy by wind power
generation in 2020 and to have 100% of the electrical energy generated by renewable
energy sources in 2035 [2] page 13.
The electrical energy generation is changing from centralized generation units to de-
centralized generation units with the introduction of renewable energy sources. The
transition to a decentralized, fossil-free energy system has to a certain degree caused the
system controllers to lose control of the energy generation in the system. The production
from the decentralized units are weather-dependent and require an accurate weather fore-
cast to determine the actual production. In addition, the deployment of a large amount
of renewable energy sources into the power system causes a loss of inertia in the system.
Current renewable energy sources are connected to the grid through power converters,
therefore they do not provide any mechanical inertia contribution to the system. This
means that the system becomes more sensitive to all power imbalances, which can cause
signiﬁcant frequency changes, and in the worst case, a blackout of the system.
The requirement of ensuring system balance is therefore becoming more demanding
and difﬁcult, compared to the previous system which included large centralized generation
units. The large centralized generation units were easy to control for maintaining the
systems power balance and provided a large inertial response in the system due to the
large synchronous rotating mass of the conventional units. These units are now gradually
being shut down and replaced by renewable energy sources. The system operators are

therefore looking for new ways to ensure that the system is in balance at all times and how
to maintain a stable power grid.
Denmark has around 5.7 million [3] inhabitants who are distributed almost equally between
the eastern and the western part of the country. The Danish power system is split into two
decoupled synchronous regions: the western part of Denmark is the electrical area called
DK1 and the eastern part is called DK2. For those 5.7 million inhabitants are there about
2.4 million [4] personal vehicles which on average means 0.42 cars per inhabitant. The
electric vehicle is becoming more popular and in some countries there is even a talk of
banning the sale of new combustion vehicles within the next 15-20 years. That means that
there is a high probability that the electric vehicles will represent substantial share of the
total loading of the electrical power grid in the near future.
With this growth in the number of electric vehicles comes the opportunity of using these
vehicles to maintain the power balance in the system, as the electric vehicles can be
considered as energy storage systems. If the charging of the vehicles could be adequately
controlled, a great control possibility could be achieved. By using the electric vehicles, the
control of the system would be moved by a certain degree from controlling the generation
to controlling the consumption. The electric vehicles could allow controlling the system by
adapting the consumption-side, rather than relying only on traditional control of the gener-
ation. Controlling the generation or the consumption are two different techniques which
technically are supposed to provide the same effects on the power grid, and ultimately
maintain the power balance in the system.

ELECTRA IRP is a joint research program of many research partners with the aim of

The project’s aim of reinforcing the power system for the future is set to make sure
that the future goals of the European Union can come true. The goals are to include as
much renewable energy sources in the power system as possible. If that goal is to become

1.2
Problem deﬁnition
As the power system is being changed from mostly centralized generation to more decen-
tralized generation new problems are arising within the control of the system. With more
renewable energy sources in the system the control is in some ways lost and new ways
are needed to maintain the power balance in the new power system. The electric vehicles
are seen as a likely solution for the future of transportation and will therefore become
a big part of the power system. With enough vehicles it might be possible use them to
assist with the control of the system since the charging level could be adjusted according
to dedicated algorithms.
The electric vehicles are a single-phase load which are located at a residential voltage
level. The vehicles and their charging controllers do not necessary all have the same
characteristics. This can lead to different response time from the vehicles which can cause
an unbalanced response time between areas or the phases.
1.3
The thesis goals
The thesis goals are to:
• implement and model groups of electric vehicles into a high voltage grid.
• examine whether the electric vehicles can provide primary frequency control.
• examine how the electric vehicles react to different operation scenarios within the
grid.
• examine how different time delays within the electric vehicle control can affect the
behavior of the system.
• examine how different distributions of the vehicles on the phases can affect the
behavior of the system.
1.4
Problem boundaries and assumptions
The thesis main focus is on the frequency control and therefore it was decided not to
include any voltage support from the electric vehicles in this thesis. Voltage support from
electric vehicles is a possibility and is currently being investigated by other electric vehicles
related projects. The electric vehicles will therefore not be programmed to produce or
absorb any reactive power. Although the vehicles will not be participating in voltage

control, they still must not cause a voltage instability and therefore the voltage will be
investigated in the simulations scenarios.
For simpliﬁcation the electric vehicles are assumed to charge exactly as the controller
instructs them to. In reality the controller would send out a charging current set-point and
the vehicle would charge at a level close to the set-point but not necessarily the exact level
as assumed.
How the control of the charging vehicles would be exactly implemented in reality and
who would own the controllers is not a part of this thesis. The exact implementation,
ownership, and market aspects of the possible ancillary services from electric vehicles are
not covered in this thesis. Those topics could be projects of their own, and before they can
be answered the possibilities of using the vehicles for ancillary services must be researched.
How the control process affects the expected lifetime of the battery in the electric vehicles
is also not included in this thesis. The control process will probably have some effects on
the battery and the electronics associated with it. What the effects would mean for the
expected lifetime and what causes the effects are seen as too big topics to ﬁt in this thesis.
1.5
The simulation program: DIgSILENT
PowerFactory
The simulation program PowerFactory from DIgSILENT was chosen for this thesis as the
program is a leading power system analysis software for applications in the generation,
transmission, distribution and industrial systems [8].
The program is a windows-based integrated power systems modeling and analysis software
package which uses a combination of graphical and script-based modeling methods. It is
created for analysis of transmission, distribution, and industrial electrical power systems.
The program is designed to be an economical all-in-one solution with a broad coverage of
state of the art power system applications.
PowerFactory uses a hierarchical, object-oriented database [14]. All the data which
represents power system elements, single line graphics, study cases, system operation
scenarios, calculation commands, program settings etc., are stored as objects inside a
hierarchical set of folders. All data which deﬁnes a power system model is stored in the
Projects folder within the database. Inside the Projects folder are the study cases and
==PAGE:24==

operations scenarios, which are used to deﬁne different studies of the system. The folders
are arranged in order to facilitate the deﬁnition of the studies and optimize the use of
the tools provided by the program. Within this tools are there four main categories of
modelling components in PowerFactory:
• The composite frame (*.BlkDef), which is a block diagram which deﬁnes the
connections between the inputs and outputs of various models
• The composite model (*.ElmComp), which is an element built based upon a com-

• The common model (*.ElmDsl), which is an element built based on a block deﬁni-
tion.
The dynamics simulation functions available in PowerFactory are able to analyze the
dynamic behaviour of small systems and large power systems in the time domain. The dy-
namic simulation involves the analysis of the behaviour of power systems under conditions
before and after sudden changes in load or generation, during faults and outages. Those
operational changes causes transients in the system which can be classiﬁed according to
three possible timeframes:
• Short-term, or electromagnetic transients
• Mid-term, or electromechanical transients
• Long-term transients
To perform the simulations carried out in this thesis is a three-phase function used, which
uses a steady-state (RMS) network model for mid-term and long-term transients under
balanced and unbalanced network conditions. This simulation function uses a steady-state,
three-phase representation of the passive electrical network and can therefore compute
unbalanced network conditions, either due to unbalanced network elements or due to
asymmetrical faults.

The power system is a highly nonlinear system which operates in a constantly changing
environment, where the load, generation, and operation parameters are continuously
changing. The systems frequency is dependent on the systems power balance, which
means that a change in the demand or generation is reﬂected in the frequency. This can be
expressed by the equation of motion [31], [26] Chapter 3.9:
Pm −Pe = 2H df

frequency in p.u. and H is the inertia constant in seconds. As more renewable energy
sources are being integrated to the system, ﬂuctuations in power generation become greater
and the inertia becomes smaller. This means that larger frequency swings can occur in
the system. There is therefore a need for a set of services to ensure the proper stability
and security of supply in the power system. Those services are called Ancillary services,
which refers to a range of services that system operators can use to guarantee the systems
stability and security.
Ancillary services represent an important part of the operational costs of networks and are
critical to efﬁcient and secure grid operation. Typical examples are active power balancing
(frequency control and stability), voltage control and stability, transmission system security
and black start capability. The ancillary services are deﬁned by the Continental Europe
Operation Handbook or the UCTE operation handbook as [9]:
"ANCILLARY SERVICES are Interconnected Operations Services identiﬁed
as necessary to effect a transfer of electricity between purchasing and sell-
ing entities (TRANSMISSION) and which a provider of TRANSMISSION
services must include in an open access transmission tariff."

2.1. PRIMARY RESERVE, DK1
9
2.1
Primary reserve, DK1
Primary reserve regulation is automatic and is used to maintain the systems frequency as
close to 50 Hz as possible by responding to grid frequency deviations [13]. In a case of
major operational disturbances will the primary reserves stabilize the frequency at close
to, but deviating from 50 Hz. The frequency of a power grid is dependent on the balance
between the generation and consumption, if the generation is not equal to the consumption
the frequency will deviate from the normal 50 Hz. The primary reserve will therefore react
to these deviations, and whether the regulation is up- or down-regulation depends on the
direction of these frequency deviations.
The western part of Denmark (DK1) is a part of the ENTSO-E RG Continent Europe grid
and is therefore obligated to provide a share of the combined requirement for primary
reserves [13]. The combined requirement is determined once a yea. For example, in 2011
Energinet.dk was required to provide ±27 MW of the total of ±3000 MW in primary
reserves. Neighboring TSOs within the ENTSO-E RG Continent Europe grid are allowed
to exchange primary reserves with the maximum delivery of ±90 MW.
The primary reserve regulation is normally supplied to a frequency deviation up to ±200
mHz which gives the frequency range of 49.8 to 50.2 Hz where a dead-band of ±20 mHz
is permitted. The reserve must be supplying the frequency linearly at the given frequency
range where 50% of the committed reserve must be activated within 15 seconds and fully
activated within 30 seconds at a ±200 mHz frequency deviation. The reserve must be able
to run at full committed power for 15 minutes where then a secondary reserve can take
over. After the reserve has been deployed it must be ready to run again after 15 minutes.
The minimum bid for participation in the market is 0.3 MW.
2.2
Secondary reserves (LFC), DK1
Secondary reserves are automatic and provided by generation or consumption units which
respond to a signal sent from Energinet.dk [13]. The secondary reserves are used in case
of major operational disturbances where the secondary reserves restore the frequency to
50 Hz after the primary reserves have stabilized the frequency. The secondary reserve
therefore plays a big role in maintaining the stability of the overall system. The secondary
reserve releases the primary reserve and restores the frequency back to 50 Hz. By doing so
any imbalances on the interconnections are also corrected and the agreed plan for them
can be kept.

determine their requirements based on the ENTSO-E RG Continent Europe’s recommen-

Each TSO needs to have some reserves in case of a blackout of the power system and the

cooling fans need power to be able to function. Some power stations do have a small diesel
generator which can be used to start the larger generators. When the larger generators have
been energized they can be used to perform a black start.
Black Start is the procedure to recover from a total or partial shutdown of the power
system which has caused an extensive loss of power generation. This entails isolated

Frequency-controlled normal operation reserves in DK2 are automatic and, like the primary

must be supplying the frequency linearly at the given frequency range where the reserve
must be fully activated within 150 seconds. Once the reserve has been activated it must be
possible to maintain the regulation continuously. The minimum bid for participation in the
market is 0.3 MW.
The eastern part of Denmark (DK2) is a part of the ENTSO-E RG Nordic grid and
is therefore obligated to supply a proportionate share of the total frequency-controlled
normal operation reserve. The total reserve is 600 MW and each TSO has to supply reserve
in proportion to their electrical energy generation relative to the entire ENTSO-E RG
Nordic generation. The share of each TSO is determined once a year. Energinet.dk buys
the frequency-controlled normal operation reserve in collaboration with Svenska Kraftnät.
In 2012 Energinet.dk’s share was 23 MW, while Svenska Kraftnät’s share was 230 MW.

reserve (Chapter 2.6) has taken over. The frequency-controlled disturbance reserve must

reserve must be 50% activated within 5 seconds and the remaining 50% must be activated

Manual reserve, DK1 and DK2
Manual reserve is, as the name implies, a manual reserve which is manually activated by the

The reserve is used to relieve the secondary reserve and the frequency-controlled normal

15 minutes. The delivery can be made up of several production or consumption units but
cannot be made up of supplies from a mix of consumption and production units.

3.4
Wind power generation
The grid has three wind power plants which include several wind turbines. The wind
turbines used are a type C wind turbines as that is the most common type of wind turbines
used today.
Figure 3.3: Type C wind turbine construction model [16] page 76.
Type C wind turbines use a wound rotor induction machine with a converter connecting
the rotor to the grid [16]. This type of a wind turbine construction is usually referred to as
a doubly-fed induction generator or DFIG. The stator of the machine is connected directly
to the grid and the rotor is connected to the grid through a converter. The converter is a
pair of two back-to-back pulse width modulated voltage source converters. The converters
are bi-directional and connected together by a common DC-bus. One converter is con-
nected to the grid and the other to the rotor. The converters control the behavior of the
wind turbines generator in all conditions. The rotor side converter controls both the rotor
voltage-magnitude and -phase angle, which is used for both active and reactive power
control. The grid side converter controls the DC link voltage and guarantees unity power
factor in the rotor branch.
The type C wind turbine is a variable speed wind turbine which in most cases uses
pitch control as speed control. In this thesis the wind power generation is set as a constant
and therefore the speed control of the wind turbine is not of interest. The speed control is
built in the PowerFactory model of the DFIG but not used as the wind is set to be constant.
The PowerFactory model includes all the components of the wind turbine and its control
units. This includes all the mechanical components like the shaft and the turbine itself, as
well as all the measurements units needed for the control. Each wind turbine has a step up
transformer which is not included in the model and is therefore placed between the wind
turbines and the grid. The transformer is equipped with a tap changer at the high-voltage
side of the transformer. The tap changer has eight steps, (four up and four down) where
each step will change the voltage by 2.5%.

The electric vehicle and the power

The idea of the electric vehicle is believed to have been ﬁrst explored as early as the early
19th century, when the battery and electric motor were invented [18]. The electric vehicle
became very popular in the US and in the early 20th century the electric vehicle became
much more popular than the combustion engine vehicle, as the electric vehicle was much
easier to drive and did not make as much noise. In this time horses were still the main
form of transportation and could be frightened by the noise. Then in 1908 Henry Ford
came up with the mass-produced Model T vehicle. Shortly after, the road system in the US
was improved greatly and people wanted to be able to drive further. Oil was then found in
Texas which meant that fuel became easier and cheaper to purchase. All of this lead to a
decrease in the sale of the electric vehicle and in 1935 it had fully disappeared from the
US market.
As technology progressed and more people became more aware of the detrimental effects
of combustion engine vehicles to the environment, the electric vehicle again became
interesting. With new battery technologies, the range of the electric vehicles is getting
longer, and electric vehicles can compete against the combustion engine vehicles. However
it is not just the range that is making electric vehicles competitive again in the market CO2
emissions are a very important factor and most countries are trying to cut down on their
CO2 emissions. This gives the electric vehicles a great advantage over combustion engine
vehicles, as they do not pollute as long they are charged from renewable energy sources.
The power grid is therefore facing several new challenges, where it is not only being
changed from the conventional centralized power generation to decentralized generation
with a lot of renewable energy sources, but there is also a signiﬁcant load being added to

will therefore have a ﬂuctuating power generation, less inertia, and a larger load. To meet

ensure the integrity of the power system as a whole.

CHAPTER 5
Simulations scenario deﬁnition
The chosen simulation cases are performed to investigate how the electric vehicles can
affect the behavior of the power system. The thesis focuses on frequency control and
therefore the main focus of the behavior is the frequency, however the voltage will also
be investigated to ensure that the electric vehicles are not causing critical voltage levels
within the system.
It was decided to use the simulation program DIgSILENT PowerFactory for the investi-
gations in this thesis. To perform the simulations carried out in this thesis, a three-phase
function was used, which uses a steady-state (RMS) network model for mid-term and
long-term transients under balanced and unbalanced network conditions. Before running
the simulation the simulated grid needs to be initialized by a valid load ﬂow, and the
PowerFactory functions determine the initial conditions for all power system elements
including all controller units and mechanical components. The simulation calculations step

2% limit at any time of the simulations period and therefore it can be concluded that
the electric vehicles control process does not cause a larger voltage unbalance than the
standards allow.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis has presented simulation results performed in the simulations software DigSi-
lent PowerFactory, where the possibility of using electric vehicles for primary frequency
control was investigated. The grid used for the thesis investigation was the modeled Pan-
European test grid for the European project Electra Task 5.4. where the electric vehicles
were added at a residential voltage level.
The investigation was performed because the power system is being changed from the
centralized generation to decentralized generation which introduces new problems to the
control of the system. With more renewable energy sources in the system the control is in
some ways lost and new ways are needed to maintain the power balance in the new power
system. If the electric vehicle continues its popularity it will become a large part of the elec-
tric power consumption and could therefore be used to assist with the control of the system.
The electric vehicles were successfully implemented into the grid at a residential voltage
level. The vehicles were modeled as single-phase loads where each load modeled in the
grid represented 1111 electric vehicles. Each load had its own controller which controlled
the active power consumption of the load. That means that 1111 electric vehicles are
aggregated together during the simulation. The grid included 45 single-phase loads which
were distributed on three buses, one bus in each of the three AC areas.
The possibility of using the electric vehicles for primary frequency control was investigated
on the Pan-European grid with different levels of wind power penetration. The simulations
scenarios showed that the electric vehicles can contribute to the frequency control by
raising the steady-state frequency and do not have any noticeable negative effects on the
system. The different levels of wind power penetration did not affect the vehicles much
but with 90% wind power penetration a greater reaction was observed from the vehicles
85
==PAGE:104==
86
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
because the frequency fell to a lower level than in the other scenarios. The electric vehicles
controller was ﬁrst simulated with a 4% droop characteristic but it was found that a 1%
droop gave a better result if the logic part was activated. With a 1% droop characteristic for
the controller the logic part is necessary to prevent oscillations within the control process
as the control process becomes more sensitive for any frequency changes.
The control process includes some time delays which represent all the different time
delays of the whole control process. Those time delays were used in the investigations
as the different areas and vehicles might not have the same characteristics. By giving
different time delays to the controllers in the three areas the vehicles had different reactions
times between the areas. This was found to cause no instabilities or signiﬁcant behavior
changes in the grid. In two of the cases the vehicles in one area were given a fast reaction
time and it was noticed that there was a higher value in the nadir but a lower steady-state
frequency. When the grid was tested with different time delays between the phases no
instabilities were found while some imbalances were noticed in the voltage. Again two
cases which included fast reacting vehicles and the same changes were noticed in the
nadir and steady-state frequency. The different time delays between the area and the
phases do not cause any instabilities but different time delays between the phases do cause
imbalances within the grid, which can be seen in the grids currents and voltage levels as
well as the low voltage transformers neutral current.
The last scenario was performed to investigate if the vehicles could change the RO-
COF value by reacting fast to the frequency changes. The controllers were given very low
time delays values for this investigation. The investigation showed that the fast reacting
vehicles affected the ROCOF and nadir value, but as previous investigations had shown,
with a lower steady-state frequency.

7.1. FUTURE WORK
87
7.1
Future work
Through the analysis conducted in this thesis several interesting topics have emerged,
which could be investigated further. This thesis has approached a big research project
in its early phase and is therefore the initial approach to the analysis. Furthermore, to
maintain this approach a number of assumptions were made. These assumptions enforce a
natural limitation to the investigations, if they were to be adopted in a real life scenario.
The following topics were identiﬁed as potential extensions of the completed work:
• Can the electric vehicles provide virtual inertia?
• Investigate how often there is a need in the power system for a downward regulation
reserve in the primary frequency control. For this the controller could be optimized,
where in this thesis the controllers normal charging current level was at 11 A.
• Investigate possible conﬂicts between frequency control and voltage control by
electric vehicles.
• Find the actual time delays for the control process as they have been proven to be
very important for the concept.
