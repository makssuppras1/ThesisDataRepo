Abstract
Autonomous driving is believed to be the next big revolution within private and com-
mercial transportation. This is understandable as the technology has the potential to
eliminate the need for truck drivers thereby reducing costs and also increase safety on
public roads significantly. Despite this the technology has not gained the trust of the
public yet so to build on this trust the technology has to be perfected in controlled
environments with minimal risk.
This thesis builds on an existing student developed autonomous car and addresses
the problem of vision-based perception of a track. The track roughly resembles a simple
road and can thereby be considered as a controlled test environment for developing
solution that also are viable in real-world scenarios.
When analysing the existing capabilities of the car it was discovered that it can
maneuver a track by only using a LiDAR as its sensor for perception but not without
problems and occasional failures. To solve some of these problems a camera and pro-
cessing unit has been added to the autonomous system. The camera adds the color
information of a scene which is crucial when perceiving markings on a road and it can
also add robustness to the detection of the edges of the track.
Several methods of system integration and track perception were considered in the
project. And it was determined that the best solution would be to detect points along
the edges of the track and merge them with the similar data produced by the LiDAR
perception system. To achieve this a combination of image processing, data processing
and clustering was used.
Two implementations using different clustering methods were developed and tested
on prerecorded data.
The tests showed that the implementation that uses K-means
produced a poor clustering of the track. The tests also showed that using DBSCAN
clustering instead significantly increased the quality of the clustering but also the pro-
cessing time.
In conclusion the implementation was found useful for the DTU Roadrunners team
although it will need some improvements to work optimally at the required rate of
the perception system. Another approach could be to circumvent the limitation of the
processing time and use the developed method as a teacher for the active training of a
convolutional neural network.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter describes what has lead to the topic of this master thesis. The description
will cover necessary background information and the motivation for the project. Then
the problem that the project has sought to solve and the limitations of the project is
presented. Lastly some comments are left regarding the time schedule and the structure
of the rest of the report is described.
1.1
Background
This master thesis is possible because of a group at DTU called DTU Roadrunners.
The vehicle that the vision-based track perception will be implemented and tested on is
the car that they are currently in possession of. The goals of the project will therefore
align with those of the Roadrunners team. This section presents the information that is
required to understand what DTU Roadrunners is and why this project has come to be.
1.1.1
DTU Roadrunners
DTU Roadrunners is an ongoing project that is run and managed by students at DTU.
The goal of the project is to push the limits of sustainable transportation by making a
car that is as fuel eﬀicient as possible. Since 2004 the cars that the project has produced
have been entered into the yearly Shell Eco-Marathon urban concept car competition
where they have placed high on the scoreboards on several occasions. This means that
the main purpose of the work that has been carried out on the car has been to get a
good placement in the competition. It has so far resulted in a remarkably fuel eﬀicient
car named Dynamo that can be seen on figure 1.1. The car is constructed around a
carbon fiber monoque, has four wheels and a carbon fiber shell. It has a 50ccm internal
combustion engine as its motor and runs on 2nd generation bio-ethanol. The current
version of the car has achieved a fuel consumption of up to 665km/L. In 2017 the team
decided to further improve the fuel eﬀiciency of the car and at the same time prepare the
car for the future by augmenting it with autonomous capabilities. Autonomous steering
combined with sensors for perception has enabled autonomous driving and navigation
for the car.

1.1.2
Shell Eco-Marathon
Shell Eco-Marathon is a competition where teams consisting of students from educa-
tional institutions compete against each other with fuel eﬀicient vehicles to encourage
innovation within the field. The competition is hosted by shell and is held yearly in
every part of the world. The competition is split into several categories depending on
the type of car and what propulsion method it uses. There are two types of cars that can
be entered into the competition. The first one is a prototype car. This category allows
for a lot of freedom in design and optimization as there are few requirements to the
construction of the car. The second car type is an urban concept car. This category is
aimed at city driving and has more requirements to the construction of the car. The re-
quirements state that the driver has to be comfortable and sitting upright, the car needs
to have a door, a windshield wiper, four wheels and there should be a space for luggage.
In 2018 the competition was expanded with a new format where the teams compete
with autonomous functionality in their urban concept car. This competition challenges
the students in their ability to develop an autonomous car and while simultaneously
optimizing the energy consumption.
1.2
Motivation
For years autonomous driving in cars have been researched and developed as it is believed
to be the next big revolution within transportation. This is understandable since fully
autonomous driving will bring along many improvements to the transportation sector
but also to regular car driving and traﬀic in general. It has the potential to greatly

increase safety by removing human errors like being distracted or speeding. And with
the quick and still decreasing processing times of autonomous systems the problem of a
humans slow reaction speed can be eliminated. Fully autonomous cars can also severely
reduce the amount of time that cars are parked. And if they are deployed like taxis
this could lead to less cars in urban areas. The energy consumption of cars could also
be managed and optimized by an autonomous system to a better degree than what a
human driver is capable of. Even though there are all of these benefits a big challenge
in adopting the technology is the public’s trust in the systems. To build on this trust it
is important to have companies, scientists and researchers develop and test autonomous
systems for cars. And to do it in controlled environments like on closed roads or race
tracks before releasing the technology onto public streets.
With this in mind Shell decided in 2018 to enable student teams to test in controlled
environments by expanding their already existing fuel-eﬀiciency competitions to also
contain an autonomous competition. Here student teams can compete with their au-
tonomous vehicles which allows them to research and test new methods for autonomous
vehicles and thereby bring innovation to the field. DTU Roadrunners have participated
in the autonomous competition since its beginning but due to Covid-19 the competition
was cancelled last year. This did not stop the team from developing the car further
allowing it to drive around a race track autonomously as long as the texture in the area
around the track is distinctly rougher than that of the track. This requirement causes
problems when considering regular road maneuvering where flat and smooth lines on
the road are used to navigate it. Investigating the possibility of using a camera to as-
sist the existing system and solve this problem will further push the car towards fully
autonomous driving.
1.3
Problem description
For many years a team at DTU called the DTU Roadrunners have developed and im-
proved upon a highly fuel-eﬀicient car. Recently they decided expand on the functional-
ity of the car and part of the team has therefore been dedicated towards implementing
autonomous capabilities to the Ecocar platform. Until now the Ecocar has been able
to drive autonomously on the gokart track at Roskilde Racing Center at a slow pace
although not without failures. The failures are due to mistakes in the cars perception
of the track which is currently derived from the data points of a LiDAR scanner. This
project will build upon the existing implementations and aims to enhance the track per-
ception of the autonomous ecocar with a camera and image processing. Two projects
have been done previously that tested some potential methods for track detection with
a camera, so the results of these projects was considered while developing a solution.
The goal of this project is to add computer vision features to the Ecocar that enhances
the cars perception of Roskilde Racing Centers gokart track.
The objectives that need to be completed to reach the goal are:

4
• Develop a method that uses a camera and image processing to detect and perceive
a track.
• Combine the data from the camera perception system with that of the LiDAR

• Integrate the new hardware and code into the Ecocar platform.
• Test the system and evaluate its performance.
1.4
Limitations
Some limitations have been made to narrow down the scope of the project and give it a
suitable workload. A list containing all the limitations are presented underneath.
• Any implementations that are made as part of the project have to be targeted
towards the DTU Roadrunners Ecocar platform and preferably using the compo-
nents and equipment that the team already has available. The main components
being a Stereolabs ZED 2 camera and a Nvidia jetson Nano.
• All functionality regarding image processing will be implemented on the Nvidia
Jetson Nano and the Jetson will then be integrated into the current environment
of the car.
• The implementation will focus completely on enabling autonomous driving on the
gokart track at Roskilde Racing Center. Meaning that no effort has to be put into
generalizing the perception for it to work on other roads and tracks.
• The track will not have any objects or other obstacles on it and there can not be
any objects along the track to assist with the track detection either.
• The code will be built with ROS since this is the framework that is already handling
all other communication within the autonomous system of the car.
• It will be assumed that no alterations of the existing mechanical functionality of
the car has to be made. And any comprehensive alterations of the autonomous
platform will be avoided.
• The autonomous system is assumed to only be operated during the day with
suﬀicient lighting conditions.

1.5
Time schedule
The time period of this master thesis started on the 22nd of March and it ended on the
22nd of August where a report describing the work that has been done throughout the
period has to be handed in. An estimated time schedule was made in the beginning of
the period. It can be found in the Project Plan Report or a simple illustration of it can
be found in appendix A.
It is important to note that only two track days at Roskilde gokart track were sched-
uled for testing the vehicle in this period. This means that the car would have to be
prepared before these days along with any software that needed to be tested. For this
project the on-track tests was mostly relevant for testing the integration of the camera
perception system in the existing system. Since the camera perception system itself can
be tested with prerecorded data from the track.
The first track day on the 2nd of June was meant for testing the integration of the
camera perception system. The second track day on the 16th of June should have been
used to test the combined perception systems. This was not possible as time had to be
put into solving problems with the LiDAR perception system so the camera perception
system could not be finished in time for this day. On both days data was gathered from
the camera which allows for more images to perform off-track tests on.
The shell competitions that the car and team was supposed to attend was cancelled
for 2021 due to Covid-19. To still allow DTU and other nearby universities to test and
drive their cars, DTU Roadrunners decided to host an autonomous competition for a
select group of universities. Although all of the invited universities showed great interest
when receiving the invitations all of them were forced to cancel. DTU Roadrunners
decided to still use the time slot that was booked at the gokart track to test the car on
the 28/06/2021. The competition was instead held in a Zoom meeting on the 29/06/2021
where each university presented their autonomous systems and were rated by a jury.

2.1
Autonomous cars
Autonomous driving for cars have been researched and developed for many years with
the goal of maturing the technology of self-driving cars so that they can operate on
public roads.
According to [SN04] the capabilities that are required to make up an
autonomous vehicle are:
1. Steering - setting and moving the steerable wheels within the available degrees of
freedom.
2. Braking - controlling the brakes of the vehicle.
3. Acceleration - controlling the engine of the vehicle.
4. Perception - recognizing the important features in the environment that the vehicle
operates in.
5. Localization - using sensors to determine the pose and velocity of the vehicle.
6. Navigation - using sensors to navigate through the environment that it operates
in.
7. Planning - calculating a route that allows the vehicle to go from its current position
to a new one.

neers). They have developed a standard that splits autonomous cars into six levels of

2.3
Vision-based perception for autonomous
driving
Cameras are widely used for perception in autonomous vehicles as they are cheap and
can gather a lot of information about the area surrounding a car but it comes at the
cost of having to process all of this information. For this reason many image processing
methods have been researched, developed and optimized.
The data from a camera can be utilized in multiple ways to contribute to the percep-
tion of an autonomous vehicle. If the overall system also uses other sensors to perceive
the environment one possibility is to fuse the data from all of the sensors. This can gen-
erally result in a better perception of the environment. Many methods for data fusion
have been suggested as the data can be fused at different abstraction levels as described
in [Cas13] where some of the common techniques used in the field of data fusion are
reviewed.
When considering road perception based only on image processing of the output of a
camera there are also a wide range of methods that have been developed. The methods
developed can generally be split into two categories depending on how they seek to
perceive the road. Lane detection and drive-able area detection.
Lane detection uses markings on the road in combination with the sides of the road to
identify any lanes in the image. This information can then be used to determine how the
car should move in relation to the identified lanes. [Ass+08] suggests a method of doing
==PAGE:22==
2.3 Vision-based perception for autonomous driving
12
lane detection with traditional image processing in the form of Canny edge detection,
Hough transform and hyperbola fitting much like it was attempted in [Chr20]. Recently
a lot of focus has been on using machine learning for camera perception in autonomous
vehicles and therefore methods that use deep learning like the one in [Nev+18] has been
proposed. This specific proposal uses a neural network, referred to as LaneNet in the
paper, to give an estimated segmentation of the lane lines. Another network, referred to
as H-net, is then used to determine a homography that when used on the output from
LaneNet, straightens the segmented lane lines. This allows for the fitting of a simple
2nd or 3rd order polynomial to each of the lines.
Drive-able area detection on the other hand is focused on detecting the road as an
area in the image. This is arguably a better approach for the detection of a track as the
track in most cases does not have any markings on it. And in the places where there are
markings, like at the start/finish line where there are lines to mark the starting positions
for a race, they do not have any relevance during driving. The output of this approach
should be a segmentation of the drive-able area in the image. There are many methods
that can results in the segmentation of a data set. Manual rules can be designed to
perform the segmentation but due to the nature of the changing information in images,
machine learning is commonly used to solve these problems. This can either be with
unsupervised machine learning or by training a model with supervised machine learning.
In unsupervised machine learning a commonly used method for segmentation is clus-
tering. Throughout the years many clustering methods have been developed. Some of
the common methods are described and tested in [Sax+17] and [Rod+19]. A common
thing for all clustering methods is that they require the determination of hyperparame-
ters to perform optimally with the data that they are clustering. When the hyperparam-
eters have been defined only the data is needed as input for the algorithm. When data is
processed by the clustering algorithm the output will usually be a numeric label for each
observation in the data. A weakness of using clustering methods for road segmentation
is that it can be diﬀicult to tailor to the problem because of the large variation in road
conditions. And many of the complex methods are computationally demanding.
Supervised segmentation methods on the other hand have better possibilities of work-
ing in varying conditions and a great deal of research and work has gone into developing
these methods. The methods are usually built on deep neural networks and require a
large labelled data set for training. In [Fan+20] a deep learning method for semantic
segmentation of a road is proposed. The output of a model that performs semantic
segmentation is similar to that of the clustering methods with a single difference being
that the labels assigned in semantic segmentation hold information about the segmented
area instead of it just being a numeric value. Segmentation can also be carried out in the
form of instance segmentation which uses object detection and then semantic segmen-
tation for each object instance. This creates separate labels for each object in an image
even though there are multiple instances of the same object. The method is described
in [SSD20]. Here panoptic segmentation is also described which is a combination of the
two segmentation methods that results in a semantic segmentation of the entire image

If previous knowledge about the environment is available another way that the data
from the camera can be utilized for navigation is by using SLAM. This is a computa-

localize the car in it. Several slam methods have been developed but an explanation of
the general principle can be found in [DB06].

3.1
Color spaces
When taking pictures with a color camera the image information is stored in multiple
channels to create a representation of the color in each pixel. Traditionally colors are
represented in the RGB (or BGR) color space which is a three channel format where
each pixel is represented by three values indicating Red, Green and Blue. This makes it
possible to indicate at vast amount of colors through combinations of these three colors.
The intensity in each pixel for each channel is typically expressed in 8-bit, meaning
numbers in the range of 0 to 255, where 0 is no light and 255 is saturated by light.
Figure 3.1: Illustration showing the color space and common values for RGB.
One disadvantage of this color representation is that it can be diﬀicult to identify
what color a pixel is from the numbers in the three channels. This is because it is the
relation between the three numbers that creates the color. A color space that more

space H is the hue which indicates what color the pixel has, S is the saturation of the

in only two channels.

3.2
Filter kernels and convolution
A kernel is an object that is used for filtering in image processing. The kernel can be
viewed as a matrix with an arbitrary size and shape but it must have a defined anchor
point which is usually in the center. The kernel contains information about how the
pixels around the pixel in the anchor point influence the outcome of the filtering. An
example of a kernel can be seen on figure 3.3. The number of possible ways to filter an
image with a kernel is not the same for binary images and non-binary images.
Figure 3.3: Illustration showing three common examples of kernels.
When working with binary images a binary kernel is usually used and the operation
that is being performed with the kernel determines the outcome.
When performing
dilation the kernel is placed so that it overlaps the image with its anchor point on a
pixel. Then the maximum value of the pixels in the area under the kernel is determined
and the pixel under the anchor point is set to this value in the filtered image. A full
dilation of an image can then be performed by applying this kernel with its anchor point
on each pixel in the image. The same kernel could also be used to perform an operation
like erosion which is the opposite of dilation meaning that instead of the maximum value
in the area being chosen the minimum value is chosen. An illustration showing dilation
can be seen on figure 3.4.
It is also possible to perform dilation and erosion in combination which is normally
referred to as opening and closing. An opening is an erosion follow by a dilation and it is
commonly used to remove small objects from the foreground. A closing is the opposite
namely a dilation followed by an erosion which is used to remove small holes from the
foreground. It should be noted that in the case of binary images, pixels with a value of
1 is considered foreground and pixels with a value of 0 is considered background. The
four operations of dilation, erosion, opening and closing are described in greater detail
in [SV92].

3.3
Machine learning within computer vision
The methods of data analysis that can improve automatically are categorized as machine
learning which is considered a part of artificial intelligence. Several types of machine
learning exists but they all have in common that some data and hyperparameters for
the method is used as input and then an output is generated without having to specify
exactly how the output should be generated. Machine learning methods can be either
supervised or unsupervised. A supervised method needs to train on labelled data to
create a model that then can be used on data of the same nature in the future. An
unsupervised method does not require any training but usually has some hyperparame-
ters that need to be set for the method to function properly for the problem that it is
attempting to solve. The hyperparameters can often be determined by experimentation
or by using fundamental knowledge about the problem that the method is attempting
to solve. One of the reasons why machine learning has become an important tool for
image processing is that it in many cases is diﬀicult to specify how the data in the image
should be interpreted. The diﬀiculty comes from the big amount of data and the large
variety of ways the same object can be seen in different images depending on lighting,
angles, the camera, etc. An often useful operation within image processing is segment-
ing the components in an image and defining the areas that these components take up.
Segmentation of images can be done in several ways with the use of machine learning.
The two following subsections will describe two of these possible methods.
3.3.1
Clustering
Clustering is a form of unsupervised machine learning which means that it does not
require any labelled data. A clustering function takes a data set and several hyperpa-
rameters as input and based on this it determines how the data set should be fitted into
some amount of clusters. When considering this from an image processing perspective
the clusters can be viewed as different segments in an image. A large amount of cluster-
ing methods have been researched and developed through the years. So when solving a
problem with such a method it is imperative that it is chosen based on knowledge regard-
ing the problem that is being solved. To give insight in to different types of clustering
methods two of them will be described.
3.3.1.1
K-means
K-means was first described in [Mac67] and is one of the most common clustering meth-
ods because of its simplicity and its low computational requirement. The method works
by first initializing the amount clusters that the data should be separated into and the
==PAGE:31==
3.3 Machine learning within computer vision
21
centroid of each cluster is then determined, with the most basic method for centroid ini-
tialization being to choose random data points from the input data. Then the distance
from each data point to all of the cluster centroids are calculated and the data points are
assigned to the cluster which it has the shortest distance to. Now new centroids for the
clusters are determined as the mean of all the data points assigned to the cluster. Then
the data points are assigned to the new centroids and the centroid are updated again.
This is done iteratively until the centroids move a significantly small amount with each
iteration.
A weakness of the K-means method is that it assumes the clusters to be convex and
isotropic so it responds poorly to clusters with an irregular shape. The iterative process
also has the potential to end up in a local minima and not produce a proper result. This
depends on the initialization of the centroids and can therefore sometimes be avoided
by rerunning the algorithm with different initial centroids or by using a better method
for determining them. There are many ways of doing this with one being the kmeans++
method described in [AV07] that initializes the centroids distant from each other. Else
a static initializing of the centroids can be made manually if it makes sense in relation
to the consistency of the data.
3.3.1.2
DBSCAN
Compared to K-means DBSCAN, which was first proposed in [Est+96], is a more com-
plex clustering method. The method clusters based on density meaning that the clusters
will have a high density and they are separated by low density areas. This means that
the method does not assume the clusters to have any specific shape contrary to K-means.
It is part of the DBSCAN method to determine the number of clusters that the data
should be separated into and it will also label data points that it can not properly cluster
as noise. When performing DBSCAN clustering several hyperparameters can be set for
the algorithm to produce a successful clustering although only two are generally required
in most implementations. These hyperparameters are ϵ and minPts. The computations
performed when clustering with DBSCAN can roughly be described with these steps:
1. For each data point find the points that are within the ϵ neighborhood and cate-
gorize it as a core point if it has at least minPts points within this neighborhood.
2. Using the neighbor graph or tree find the connected components of core points,
leaving out all non-core points, and assign each collection of connected components
to a cluster.
3. If a core point is within the ϵ neighborhood of a non-core point assign it to the
cluster of the core point. If no core points are near the non-core point then label
it as noise.

3.3.2
Classification
Another way of segmenting an image is by looking at it as a classification problem. In this
case the term classification does not refer to the common practice of image classification
where an entire image receives a label based on the information in it. It rather refers
to the problem of classifying each pixel to a class where other pixels in the class has
similar properties. A common way to solve such a classification problem is with neural
networks. Training a neural network is a supervised method within machine learning
meaning that it requires labelled data for training. A traditional neural network consists
of an arbitrary amount of layers each with an arbitrary amount of nodes and each node
in a layer is connected to every node in the previous and the next layer. This type of
network can perform poorly when processing images as the network will process each
part of the image the same way every time. This means that such a network will not
be able to identify a feature if it changes positions in the image.
A type of neural
network that solves this issue is a convolutional neural network. The CNN still consists
of layers but these layers are different from the layers in a traditional neural network.
A CNN has two main types of layers a convolutional layer and a fully connected layer.
The convolutional layer consists of a filter kernel of arbitrary size and depth which is
convolved with the input data much like in traditional image filtering. A pooling layer
can be used to reduce the size of the data coming from the previous layer for example by
creating a convolved feature of the average of each 4-neighborhood in the input data. In
CNNs used for segmentation upsampling/unpooling layers are also commonly used. This
layer as the name suggests performs the opposite operation of pooling so it can bring
the final layer to a scale that is similar to the input. So there is an output corresponding
to each pixel in the original image. On figure 3.6 an illustration showing an example
composition of a CNN used for segmentation can be seen.

general purpose of the vehicle. The purpose of the autonomous system of the Ecocar is
to achieve autonomous driving with optimal fuel eﬀiciency and to do this turns need to
be detected and anticipated so that the vehicle can position itself in the ideal position
on the track. To do this it will need to see further than it can currently because with its
current range it is only viable to drive in the middle of the track. A camera is perfect
for this as it has a very high resolution, depending on the amount of pixels in the sensor,
compared to other sensor options including LiDARs. The resolution is important for
distance in perception when looking for data points near the horizon of the ground in
relation to the sensor.
The mentioned master thesis also describes several weaknesses of the current system
that should be addressed in the future work done on the Ecocar. One of these weaknesses
is that the LiDAR cannot detect the places along the track where a white line separates
the track and a paved side road. This is because the marking of the white line is not
significantly higher than the tarmac and it is also smooth like the tarmac.
This is
both critical for the specific race track but also on public roads where the markings are
extremely important when navigating. The reason for using this type of marking on a
road is that the color difference makes them easy to see. This is why a camera is ideal
for adding the functionality of perceiving such markings.
Another weakness that was described is the resolution of the data from the LiDAR.
Only 7 rings of the LiDAR data can be used reliably for track detection and the distance
between the rings increase with the angle of the lasers. This ultimately means that a few
amount of data points with poor quality can dramatically alter the systems perception of
the track. Here the high resolution of a camera once again can be helpful as the camera
is able to acquire data with more data points on the track and also on the surrounding
area. This will in turn make the autonomous driving of the Ecocar more robust.

4.4
Track detection
Now that the method that will be used for data combination has been determined the
methods that can be used for perceiving the track through image processing can be
analysed. As mentioned in related work a project focused on researching possibilities
for track perception with the camera has been completed previously. In this project
the author took an approach where a couple of algorithms that tried to locate the lines
or edges along the sides of the track were tested. Although the project showed decent
results in certain cases the performance was not suﬀicient for successful autonomous
driving.
It might be possible to improve on the used methods but since the initial
results did not look promising a new approach to the problem was chosen. The new
approach focuses on the detection and segmentation of the track itself. Methods using
this approach does not need to fit a curve along the sides of the track meaning that if the
sides of the track cannot be represented by a curve to a satisfactory degree, identifying
what is track and what is not is the most accurate approach. This will also become
relevant if the camera in the future were to assist in navigating between obstacles as
obstacles might obstruct the line of vision between the camera and the sides of the track.
In the future a combination of the two might be relevant if the competition is expanded
with disciplines where the car should stay within a lane. Determining what is track
and what is not track is a complex problem with an infinite number of possible image
positions and scenarios. This suggests that machine learning could be a good solution in
contrast to the previously researched and tested methods which used traditional image
processing algorithms. Due to the complexity of the problem, the wish to make the
system able to drive in different environments and the big body of research suggesting
machine learning is a good solution for autonomous vehicles, this approach is chosen
==PAGE:40==
4.4 Track detection
30
for the project. To segment the images into track and surrounding area a method like
thresholding the image will not be suﬀicient.
More advanced and complex methods
like unsupervised machine learning in the form of clustering or machine learning using
convolutional neural networks should be better solutions. The problem with supervised
learning like using a CNN is that to train the model a lot of labelled data is needed.
Data sets containing labelled images of segmented roads are available online but they
mainly contain images of regular public roads. To assure the best solution with a CNN
the data should be based on the specific track that the car will drive on or at least
a similar one.
Because of the lack of labelled data it is therefore chosen to do the
segmentation by performing clustering on the images.
There exists a wide range of
clustering methods 2. When choosing a method to use several factors come into play.
The method should ideally be transductive even though an inductive method would
require less computations after the initial clustering. This is because the transductive
methods allow for a successful segmentation when there are greater changes in the image.
Changes that are likely to occur since the track and its surroundings vary depending on
where on the track the car is and also which track it is driving on. The method should
be able to handle uneven cluster sizes as the size of the track and the different clusters
in the surroundings will vary with each image. Ideally it should also be able to identify
points that it cannot cluster with high confidence as noise since a wrong clustering could
cause trouble in the navigation of the car. It would also be beneficial for the method to
calculate the clustering based on the distance between data points that are close to each
other. This will take advantage of how the road is assumed to be in front of the car and
the pixels showing the road will all be connected. These criteria suggests DBSCAN to
be a good solution. Since processing time is expected to be one of the main limitations
of the system the simplest solution that still delivers suﬀicient results will be the best
one. Because of this K-means clustering will also be implemented and evaluated as this
is the fastest of the common clustering methods.
Both of the clustering methods take hyperparameters other than the data as input.
These will have to be determined before performing the clustering. The standard K-
means algorithm, as it is implemented in popular programming libraries, takes two
inputs in the form of the data that needs to be clustered and the number of clusters
that it should be separated into. So in this case the hyperparameter that will have to
be determined beforehand is the number of clusters that the algorithm should split the
data into. When considering the scene that will be in the images it might be possible
to determine a set number of clusters by looking at the images and using reasoning. A
more analytical way could be to use the elbow method for K-means described in [YY19].
This method often results in a good estimation of the ideal number of clusters and it
can also give an indication of how well the K-means algorithm will perform on the data.
The indication comes from the shape of the curve.
If the curve is relatively flat or
smooth it is not as clear which amount of clusters will perform the best. But if there
is a big change in the slope of the curve to indicate the ideal amount of clusters this
amount of clusters will differentiate more from those that will not be as ideal. Another

hyperparameter that may be useful to provide when calling the K-means function is the
values of the initial centroids. The centroids are usually determined by the K-means
implementation of the programming libraries by default. Providing the function with
a list of values for the initial centroids might reduce processing time and can in some
cases also increase reliability and the quality of results of the method. DBSCAN is
a clustering method that has a higher complexity than K-means and it also has more
hyperparameters that could be relevant to adjust. The most important of which is the
epsilon hyperparameter as it directly influences how the clusters are formed. Epsilon
might be able to have a constant value in cases where the relative values of the data
does not change significantly. In such a case it would be possible to determine it by
adjusting it manually until the function produces the desired results. For the clustering
problem faced in this project this will not be the case since the surroundings of the track
and the lighting conditions can change drastically when driving around a track. This
means that the value of epsilon will have to be determined automatically depending on
the data that DBSCAN is going to process. A method described in [RS16] proposes
that this can be achieved by calculating the distance to the k-nearest-neighbor for each
data point, where k is the value of MinPts. The distances should then be sorted from
shortest to longest to form a curve. The gradient in each of the points of the curve
then has to be calculated. Now a threshold for the gradient has to be determined and
this threshold value will be used to automatically determine the value of epsilon. This
is done by finding the index at which the gradient curve first exceeds the set threshold
and then the distance to the k-nearest-neighbor at this index will be the value used for
epsilon. Now the gradient threshold can be adjusted until the clustering produces the
desired result.
To allow the clustering step to give the desired results it is important to determine
what data from the images the clustering should be based on. As mentioned earlier
processing time is going to be a critical limiting factor and therefore the amount and size
of data points processed by the clustering algorithm should be minimized. This should
be done while still maintaining good segmentation results and an adequate accuracy. In
image processing the amount of data can be reduced by down-scaling the image to a
lower resolution. This will generally keep most of the data from the full sized image
depending on how big the change in resolution is but it will greatly impact the accuracy.
With this in mind the data that can be extracted from the images and is relevant for
perceiving the track should be determined.
In the Scene and image analysis section it was discovered that converting from RGB
format to HSV format can condense the color information into just two channels, namely
H and S. Since it is the color information that best separates the track from its surround-
ings the data in the H and S channels should be used for clustering. This will also reduce
the amount of data from three channels to two channels. Another property that can be
used is the fact that the track always will be one continuous area. This means that the
positioning of the pixels in relation to one another in the form of the (x, y) coordinates is
relevant information. The (x, y) coordinates of each (H, S) instance should therefore be
included in the data passed to the clustering algorithm. Other information that could

This would be useful when detecting obstacles or when there is a significant spatial dif-

of the data in the message is deconstructed in the beginning and the ids are not used at
all. This means that the data from the Jetson can be combined with the data from the
LiDAR node by just appending it as a group of points with a new unused id.

images, gray-scale images, depth images etc. These topics can then be subscribed to
by any nodes in the network for them to receive the desired image format each time
it is updated. This is advantageous because multiple nodes will be able to receive and
process or store the images separately. Several services are also available with the ROS
implementation like recording the output of the camera to a file. The disadvantage of
this way of getting images from the camera is that the node crashes if the connection
to the master is lost. This can be an issue with the master running on the NUC and
not the Jetson. A second master could be run on the Jetson just to handle the camera
communication but it is recommended to keep the number of masters in a network to a
minimum to avoid complications. Running a second master would also take up some of
the Jetsons limited computing power.

the camera with the python API in the ZED SDK. This suggests that only one node

The method of reading images that was used when processing the images to detect

5.3.3.2
DBSCAN
To perform DBSCAN clustering the implementation from the scikit learn library is used
3. For usage of this function three of the hyperparameters that it takes as input will be
defined while the rest will have the default values of the scikit learn implementation. The
hyperparameters that will be provided as input to the DBSCAN function and therefore
have to be determined are the variables eps also known as epsilon, min_samples also
known as minPts and n_jobs.
The n_jobs input is the number of cores in the processor that should be utilized
when DBSCAN performs the clustering. The clustering should be performed as fast as
possible to limit processing time constrictions and the value is therefore set to -1 which
indicates that all cores of the system should be utilized.
When determining the hyperparameters that have an impact on the clustering itself
the first hyperparameter that should be determined is minPts. The common guideline
for determining minPts says that minPts ≥1 + features which in the case of this
implementation is minPts ≥5. With this in mind minPts is determined by utilizing
knowledge of the data. Since the final result should be a segmentation of the track it is
not considered important what labels the pixels that are not on the track get. Meaning
is does not make a difference if the everything other than the track is labelled as noise
or in a cluster and how many clusters it is separated into.
Several factors were considered when determining minPts. One of the considerations
that has to be made it that minPts should be low enough for the algorithm to cluster
the entire track together when there is a corner at a distance like in the image on figure
5.12. MinPts will also have an impact on how the pixels near the boundary between
the track and surrounding area are clustered. Thereby it will have an impact on how
close to the boundary the clustering of the track can get. These two considerations both
advocate for a low value of minPts.
Another consideration that was made is that a low value of minPts could place
pixels that have H and S values similar to the track and that are right on the other
side of the boundary in the same cluster as the track. MinPts also influences how small
disturbances or objects on the track are clustered as a lower value of minPts will cause
3https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.cluster.DBSCAN.html

the small disturbances to be identified as a cluster separate from the track.
These
considerations both advocate for increasing the value of minPts.

selected for the implementation is 25 as this is deemed fitting for the resolution of the
images and the track in them. The dependency on resolution also means that minPts
would have to be changed if another resolution of the image is passed through the
DBSCAN clustering algorithm.

6.3
Evaluation of results
The results of the test show that the image processing in the K-means implementation
only takes 65 ms. This means that the processing time for this implementation should
not be a problem if it were to be used on the car. The quality of the output rules out the
possibility of using the implementation on the actual car since not a single output was
good enough to be used to determine a drive point for navigation. And since the root
of the problem is some of the functionality of K-means clustering being that it assumes
convex and isotropic clusters. The method is not deemed fit to solve the problem when
performed on the observation set as it is.
The DBSCAN implementation was found to take much longer to process with a total
processing time of 475 ms per image. This equates to a processing frequency of 2.11 Hz
and a perception frequency of about 2 Hz would not be enough to base the navigation
of the car on, especially as the speed of the car increases. But looking at the video it
seems as if it would be suﬀicient for long range track perception and anticipation which
is mentioned as one of the advantages of a camera perception system. At least when the
car moves at the speed at which it does in the video.
Considering the quality of the output of the DBSCAN implementation it performed
significantly better than the K-means implementation. The video shows that the output
gives a good estimation of where the edges of the track is located in the image. But the
test also shows that a single point from the output covers a large area in the original
image. This is due to the massive down-scaling of the image that is done before clustering
it. This means that a small inaccuracy in the pixel determined as an edge point can
cause a big difference in the real world position of this point. Although the accuracy
is not ideal it should still be suﬀicient enough for driving the car on tracks or roads
that are not so narrow that the accuracy becomes an issue. The amount of points along
the track that the method generates with its output is also greater than the amount
of points that the LiDAR generates so the resolution is better than that of the LiDAR
perception.
Another problem that was noticed in the test results is that the side roads were not
always separated from the track correctly. The output that was produced while the car
drove past the first side road should be good enough for the car to use for navigating
in a real world scenario. The output from when the car passed the second side road is
not satisfying and if the car was to navigate with those points it would most likely drive
of the track. Based on the test this seems like the most immediate problem as one of
the main reasons for adding a camera perception system is that it is able to perceive
markings on the road better than the LiDAR.
The final concern that was identified during the test was that sometimes disturbances
in the form of white debris caused some of the edge points to be placed in the middle of
the track. This would obviously cause big problems if the implementation were to run
on the car in its current state. But by tuning the parameters of the clustering algorithm
or adjusting the filtering in post-processing the problem should be easily solvable.
==PAGE:72==
CHAPTER 7
Discussion
After performing the tests and evaluating the results the method that uses DBSCAN
clustering produces the best output to use for drive point generation and navigation.
But since neither of the methods are perfect it does not make sense to choose one to
develop further without first considering the possible improvements and applications for
both of the methods.
One challenge of the camera track perception where an improvement of the results
should be sought after are separating the side roads from the track. Tuning the hyper-
parameters of the clustering or adjusting the pre- and post-processing of the methods
will not be able to improve on the performance when a side road is encountered. This
is because the data that is best suited for detecting a white line on a gray surface is
removed before the clustering is performed. This data is in the V channel of the HSV
color space and is the brightness of a pixel. The brightness is great in this case because
both the gray tarmac and the white line does not have a color and therefore there is not
a great difference between them in the H and S channels. Even though the difference is
amplified slightly by the addition to the blue channel it not suﬀicient to make it robust
enough to cover all cases. It might therefore be beneficial to add the values of the V
channel to the observation set. This would reduce the certainty of the segmentation of
the track from the surrounding grass areas but the V channel could be added with a
weight factor to reduce its impact on the clustering. Such a data addition would also
increase processing time though. But it is likely necessary unless other methods for
improving the data concerning the white lines are discovered.
Another method that could be used to improve the detection and exclusion of side
roads is performing more erosion before calculating the connected components and re-
moving the side road. This will make it more diﬀicult to solve the problem of detecting
turns ahead of the car as they will likely also be separated from the track right in front
of the car. This is not favorable and therefore this method should not be used if it can
be avoided.
The method the uses K-means showed a poor performance with regard to the quality
of the output. As mentioned this is due to the nature of the algorithm and its assump-
tions that the clusters are convex and isotropic. The method might be improved by
omitting the x and y positional data of the pixels from the observation set passed to
the K-means algorithm. This would reduce the processing time although this is not
that important for this method. But a critical effect that such a change would have on
the entire program is that the requirements of the filtering in the post-processing would
==PAGE:73==
7 Discussion
63
increase significantly. This would be necessary as all pixels that would have a color
similar to the color of the track would be put in the same cluster as the track. And all
this noise would have to be removed by the filtering in the post-processing part of the
method. So although this alteration to the K-means method is possible the viability of
it is uncertain.
The method using DBSCAN clustering showed promising results but still has room
for improvement in certain areas. First of all a fine tuning of the hyperparameters of
the algorithm could be beneficial. In this case the hyperparameters being the value of
minPts and the threshold that is used with the gradient curve of the sorted distances
to the nearest neighbor of each observation to determine epsilon. A weakness of the
current method using DBSCAN that became apparent from the test results is that it is
not all of the disturbances on the track that are segmented as part of the track. This
problem should be easy to solve either with the fine tuning of the hyperparameters or
by increasing the kernel that is used for the closing step during post-processing. The
accuracy of the output of the method would also be a good thing to improve on. The
accuracy can be improved in two ways. The first one being by getting the determined
edge points to lie closer to the actual edge in the image and the other one being to
make each produced point cover a smaller area of real world coordinates. The second
one can only be improved by increasing the resolution unless advanced methods like sub
pixel precision is used. The first one could be improved both by using the methods just
described but a simple way to move the determined edge points closer to the actual
ones could be to perform dilation on the track segmentation just before determining the
contour of it.
A challenge that was not addressed in the development and testing of this system
but that will also be present on the track is white markings on the track. The car will
encounter these markings at the start/finish line of the track. An image of the area can
be seen on figure 7.1.
Figure 7.1: Image showing the markings that are at the start/finish
line of the track.
This area posses a great challenge as the white lines separates the track into a grid of
gray areas divided by the white lines. Even if all of the gray areas were to be clustered
into the same cluster the method that has been developed would not be able to combine
==PAGE:74==
7 Discussion
64
them because of the side road removal functionality. So either another method for side
road removal should be found or a bigger part of the method should be altered.
A whole new method that could be used for finding the edge points along the track
is classification. Classification requires labelled data to train on but this data could
potentially be generated by the method developed in this thesis. This would make the
method a teacher to use for active learning. Doing this would require that a single
clustering method is selected and improved on. Since processing speed does not matter
and the most important factor of labelled data is the quality of it the DBSCAN method
is the obvious choice based on the test results and discussed possible improvements.
Using the clustering to label data instead of processing it in real-time also allows for
the resolution of the image being clustered to be significantly higher thereby increasing
the accuracy. And if the full resolution is not used for training a model for classification
the labelled images can just be scaled before they are trained on. An addition to the
observations set that might increase the quality of the output is the depth map that
was deemed unnecessary in the analysis but still holds some information that might be
useful. The classification approach seems promising as it might be able to compute fast
depending on the complexity of the model. It will also be able to learn the complex
challenges that the car encounters on the track like the problem of removing side roads
but also including white markings in the track. This complex problem solving also points
further in the direction of general autonomous driving which will be beneficial when the
car has to drive on other tracks and roads.
Based on the discussion the path that seems most promising would be to improve
on the method that uses DBSCAN clustering. But this should be done while keeping
in mind the possibility of using it as a teacher for active learning to label the images so
that they can be used for training a classification model.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The thesis project has provided DTU Roadrunners with the most crucial parts of a
camera perception system that can be added to the current autonomous system and
improve its track perception. The camera perception system uses the Stereolabs ZED 2
and Nvidia Jetson Nano that were provided by DTU Roadrunners to perceive the track
in front of the car.
The system has been tested on the hardware and shows promising results. The goal
of adding computer vision features to the ecocar to enhance its perception of Roskilde
Racing Centers gokart track have been reached.
All of the objectives that were specified in section 1.3 have been completed to reach this
goal:
• A method that uses computer vision to detect and perceive a track has been
developed and implemented as a node on the Nvidia Jetson Nano.
• A method was developed for combining the output of the camera perception node
with that of the LiDAR perception node.
• Both hardware and software has been integrated into the current autonomous
system.
• Tests were planned and performed on the hardware of the car to evaluate the
performance of the system.
• Improvements that could be made to the developed camera perception solution
has been identified and specified.
The development of the camera perception system was accomplished by completing
a thorough analysis of the system already present in the car, the environment that
it has to operate in and the requirements of the camera system. The camera system
was integrated into the car by developing and implementing software on the existing
autonomous platform that allows for the communication of information between the
two systems.
Testing of the system showed that it is able to enhance the perception of the track
for the car and increase the resolution of the track edges. The system will also allow
the car to perceive the track at a distance that is longer than what it has been able to

previously. Furthermore it can add the ability to detect white lines along the edges of
the track and use them for better navigating of the car. Although the robustness of the
white line detection should be improved by making adjustments to the method.

CHAPTER 9
Future work
This chapter describes examples of future tasks that can be completed to improve the
camera perception system and prepare it for full integration into the car. The tasks are
based on the results of this project and experiences that were gathered while developing
the system.
The tasks also includes the improvements mentioned in the discussion
although explained in more detail and with concrete steps for development.
9.1
Jetson and NUC communication
Although the final system was not tested on the track an attempt was made to test
the communication between the Jetson and NUC while the car was in operation on the
track. Although the connection between the two units was established and they were
communicating correctly trouble shooting the rest of the autonomous system caused
several restarts to be necessary which broke the connection. The units suddenly were
not able to ping each other but due to the limited time slot of testing on the track this
problem could not be investigated further at the time. An attempt should be made to
try and recreate this problem and find a solution for it if it still persists.
The scripts that have been made for the remote launch of the camera perception
system have been made but they might need slight alterations to accommodate for
changes in the file structure on the Jetson. When the alterations have been made the
script on the NUC should be incorporated as one of the scripts that are run when the
button on the steering wheel that starts the autonomous system is pressed.
9.2
LiDAR and camera data combination
An investigation into other methods of combining the LiDAR and camera data and
places in the pipeline where is can be made. It may be possible to combine the data at
an earlier point in the pipeline or to exchange data between the to system so that the
perception of both systems can utilize data from the other system. Although if this was
to be developed it would require close attention to problem that can arise from timing
and processing time issues.
==PAGE:78==
9.3 Camera track perception
68
9.3
Camera track perception
Several improvements of the track perception algorithm itself has been mentioned in the
discussion. The method that showed the most promising results and seems to have the
most promising improvements is the method that uses DBSCAN clustering. Therefore
a list of its issues and possible solutions to them is presented here. The list is ordered
by importance with the first one being most important and the last one being the least
important.
1. Determine the best way to ensure that no disturbances on the track will be present
in the final segmentation of it. This should be achievable by increasing the size of
the kernel that the closing function uses to remove such disturbances.
2. The part of the method that separates side roads from the track should be altered
so that it performs better and becomes more robust. The first thing that should be
attempted is scaling the values in the V channel by a factor less than 1 to reduce
their weight and adding the weighted values as a feature to the observations. If this
does not improve the results the kernel in the erosion step of side road separation
can be increased but it will cause the distance of the camera track perception to
increase.
3. Adjust the method so that the edge points are placed closer to the actual edge in
the images. This can for example be achieved by simply using a dilation filter on
the binary track segmentation just before the contour of it is found.
9.4
Classification
If it is chosen to move in the direction of a track perception solution that uses classi-
fication. A program can be made based on the DBSCAN clustering method and used
to label data as a teacher for performing active learning. When this program has been
developed the next step would be to research possible methods for solving this classifi-
cation problem and implement and train the one that is most suitable. An example of
a method that is commonly used to solve classification problems is a CNN.
