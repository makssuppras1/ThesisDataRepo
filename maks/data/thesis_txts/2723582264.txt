Abstract
With the growing number of TPMs in use today, especially since Microsoft has made the
TPM a system requirement on Windows 11 systems, the integrity of these TPMs is getting
more and more crucial. TPMs are used to ensure trust by storing measurements about
the components in a system and then reporting on these measurements, so the system
can detect abnormalities. Previous research has shown that the communication channels
between the TPM and the CPU are not secure enough and can easily be compromised.
Furthermore, various attacks have been carried out on the communication channels, such
as sniﬀing the data sent on these channels to the CPU from the TPM. These attacks have
successfully compromised the confidentiality of the TPM, by sniﬀing confidential data.
With this project, the communication channel between the TPM and the CPU will be
further exploited. The attacks that will be designed and carried out in this project are
based on previous attacks performed on the communication channels, more specifically
the LPC bus, on which many TPMs are installed on. The attacks in this project will
take it a step further and inject data into the TPM, besides just sniﬀing the data. This
will break both the integrity and confidentiality of the TPM, and thereby compromise the
system as a whole and the various services, such as Secure boot and BitLocker, that make
use of the TPM to protect against malicious attacks, such as malware and rootkits. The
toolsets designed and implemented in this project successfully injected commands to the
TPM and sniffed commands to and from the TPM. Furthermore, secure commands were
also sent to the TPM, which otherwise could not be sent outside certain secure scenarios.
Lastly, it was possible to reset the TPM and, thereby, reset the measurements the TPM
had stored, which gave way to injecting forged measurements. These attacks successfully
compromised both the integrity and confidentiality of the TPM. This will hopefully show
the manufacturers of the TPM to better secure the TPM, especially the communication
channels that the TPM uses.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The TPM is a hardware component that provides security features such as generating
encryption keys, a RNG, RoTs, etc. It plays a crucial role in securing computing devices
and ensuring the integrity of sensitive data. With the increasing dependence on technology
and the increasing amount of sensitive information stored and transmitted online, the
security of TPM is of utmost importance.
In recent years, there has been a significant rise in cyber-attacks and data breaches, with
criminals becoming increasingly sophisticated in their methods. The TPM is a key compo-
nent in preventing these types of attacks and protecting sensitive information. However,
as technology advances and the threat landscape evolves, new security vulnerabilities may
arise, highlighting the need for ongoing research and development of TPM security.
Furthermore, the TPM is increasingly being used in a wide range of industries, such as
the automotive industry and healthcare. In the automotive industry, TPMs are used to
secure the embedded systems used by cars [1], these embedded systems control the fuel
consumption and infotainment systems, and in more modern cars the sensors and systems
that control the self-driving capabilities of the car. In healthcare, the TPM protects the
devices, which contain confidential information about patients, from unauthorized access
[2].
These industries often deal with sensitive and confidential information that must
be protected at all times.
Attacking a self-driving car could give the attacker control
of the car, and stealing servers and PCs from the hospital could give attackers sensitive
information about patients. The importance of TPM security in these industries cannot
be overstated, as a security breach could have serious consequences, both financially and
in terms of public trust.
In addition, the TPM is also used in the deployment of IoT devices [3]. With the growth of
IoT and the increasing number of connected devices, the security which the TPM provides
is becoming even more critical. A security breach in an IoT device could have far-reaching
consequences, potentially affecting not just the device itself, but also the network to which
it is connected and the data it stores and transmits.
In conclusion, security issues with the TPM are essential to be addressed, as TPMs play
a vital role in systems that rely on the TPM, such as Secure boot. Therefore, this project

the systems that make use of TPMs, as if the attacks succeed it would mean that the

1.2
Objectives
The objective of this project is to research whether it is possible to perform MITM attacks
on a TPM device, installed on the LPC bus. To conduct the research, a somewhat deep
understanding and knowledge of TPMs in general, and more specifically the TPMs used
in PCs, and the LPC bus must be obtained, together with a general knowledge of the
equipment used in the attacks. The objectives of this project are listed below, which are
divided into functional and non-functional requirements.
1.2.1
Non-functional objectives
The non-functional objectives are objectives that will support the completion of functional
objectives. They do not have a clear goal, unlike functional objectives. The non-functional
objectives are listed below.
• Obtain knowledge on TPM devices specified for LPC buses on PCs.
• Gain relevant knowledge of TPMs in general; this includes the functionality, such as
commands/responses, architecture and structures of TPM devices, and the commu-
nication used by TPMs.
• Obtain knowledge of the LPC bus.
• Find the most optimal way to attach a device that will perform attacks in the
communication between TPM and CPU.
1.2.2
Functional objectives
The functional objectives have a definitive goal. The success of the project will be deter-
mined by the completion of the functional objectives. The functional objectives are listed
below.
• Design a toolset to attach to the LPC bus between the TPM and the CPU.
• Build the toolset that implements the abovementioned design.
• Analyze the communication between a TPM and the CPU on the LPC bus, using
the toolset.
• Carry out low-privilege command injection to the TPM device using the toolset, to
prove the ability to send commands to the TPM.
• Carry out high-privilege command injection to the TPM device using the toolset,
to prove the ability to send commands, which are reserved for special situations and
circumstances, to the TPM.
• Design and carry out different attacks that will reset the TPM.

1.3
Related Work
The premise for this project, and the attacks that will be developed, are based on previous
attacks on BitLocker and the LPC bus. BitLocker is a security feature in Windows that
encrypts the data on a computer’s hard drive to protect against unauthorized access. It
uses a combination of encryption algorithms and authentication methods to ensure that
only authorized users can access the data. The encryption is done by a key called the
BitLocker key [5]. Bitlocker will be explained in more detail later on. This section will
introduce these attacks and why they are relevant to this project and how they affected
the decision-making for this project in some aspects.
1.3.1
LPC sniffer
The LPC sniffer is a sniffer made for the iCE40 family of Lattice-built FPGAs [6] by
Alexander Couzens [7]. The sniffer is written in Verilog, a HDL [8]. The sniffer works
by programming an iCE40 FPGA, like the iCEstick that comes with an iCE40-H1XK
chip [9], with the provided Verilog code. Then attach the iCEstick onto the LPC bus,
and finally run the provided Python script on a different computer running Linux. The
Python script will then print all the data that are being transmitted on the LPC bus, on a
terminal on the attacking Linux computer. The LPC sniffer by Couzens was not finished
as it lacked functionality for some LPC data types. Furthermore, it was not specifically
made for sniﬀing TPM data on the LPC bus, but rather any kind of data on the LPC bus.
1.3.2
Bitlocker key extraction
When Couzens released his LPC sniffer to the public, it was not long after others repro-
duced the sniffer and improved on it. One of them was Denis Andzakovic, who modified
the sniffer and used it to extract the BitLocker key from the TPM [10]. The TPM will,
if enabled, encrypt the BitLocker key with one of its encryption keys.
Therefore, the
premise of Andzakovic’s attack was to sniff the BitLocker key from the LPC bus after
it has been decrypted by the TPM, as the BitLocker key will be sent to the TPM for
decryption. Andzakovic rewrote the LPC sniffer by Couzens and made it specific for the
TPM, and only for sniﬀing data sent from the TPM, not data sent to the TPM. There-
fore, the Andzakovic LPC sniffer can only partially sniff data. The attack can be found in
Andzakovic’s blog post ”Extracting BitLocker keys from a TPM” [10] and on his GitHub
page, where the Verilog code is also stored [11].
SySS Research also performed an attack on the TPM [12], but it was merely a modification
on Andzakovic’s attack, with extensive Python scripts that automated the key extracting
process. SySS Research also modified the Verilog code a bit and wrote it more cleanly, but
the overall sniﬀing functionality remains the same. However, SySS Research did provide
an extensive guide on how to connect the iCEstick to the LPC bus, together with how to
configure and program the iCEstick. The code and guide can be found on their GitHub
page [12].
The toolset that will be designed in this project will be based on the LPC sniffer and the

Chapter 2
Problem Statement
The TPM is essential to the confidentiality and integrity of the data stored on PCs [4],
which uses TPMs to ensure trust.
The RoT and the chain of trust that the TPM is
an integral part of upholding, cannot be compromised, and still expect that the system
is in a trusted and secure state.
With the release of Windows 11, Microsoft has put
the TPM on its system requirements list. The TPM has been deemed tamper resistant
and secure enough for Microsoft to consider it important enough as a requirement for
Windows 11. In Windows systems, the TPM is used, among other things, as part of its
Secure boot feature that protects the PC from malicious and harmful attacks, therefore
the TPM, and the confidentiality and integrity of the TPM, is of utmost importance in
newer Windows systems, as these systems rely heavily on the TPM for trust and security.
However previous attacks have shown that the communication channels between the TPM
and the CPU are not secure enough and are prone to various attacks, such as sniﬀing the
BitLocker key from the LPC bus. With today’s threat landscape and the number of tools
out there, these sniﬀing attacks are both cheap and easy to reproduce, with little knowledge
required. However, the attacks do not stop there, with the sniﬀing attacks exploiting the
poorly secured communication channels between the TPM and the CPU, other more
devastating attacks are also possible. With this project, the communication channel will
be examined and further exploited, to design and create more extensive attacks. This is
done in hope of showing the TPM manufacturers that some of the current ways the TPMs
are built and installed are not secure enough and that better security, especially regarding
communication is required. These attacks compromise the confidentiality and integrity of
the TPM and break the chain of trust, which the TPM is part of creating. If these attacks
are not mitigated, by enhancing the security of the communication channels among other
things, more and more attacks will be produced and they will be more devastating.

Computer Architecture

3.2
LPC interface
The LPC interface is a computer bus interface used in desktop and mobile implementa-
tions. It is a replacement and improvement of the previous ISA interface [13]. The LPC
bus is used to connect devices using low bandwidth, these devices are BIOS Firmware
Memory, Audio devices, and Super I/O devices, such as mice, keyboards, TPMs, etc.
The devices on the LPC bus communicate synchronously with the southbridge in a mas-
ter/slave manner using a 33MHz clock. The southbridge is the host/master and controls
the LPC bus, while the devices connected to the LPC bus are the peripherals/slaves, the
communication is done by sending frames that contain chunks of data. The LPC interface
only has 7 required signals to function, with several optional signals for extended usage.
For this project only the 7 required signals are relevant. An overview of the 7 signals can
be seen in Table 3.1 [14] [15].

buffers and logic, the LPC interface can rely on only 4 wires for the 4 LAD signals [17].

3.3.1
Roots of Trusts
The RoT, as the name suggests, is the most fundamental trust in a chain of trust. The
RoT must be kept secure, as it is the foundation of trust in a computer; therefore, it is
usually in the form of hardware. The TPM is a hardware RoT. There are 3 fundamental
RoT types, these are described in detail below [18] [19] [20].
The Root of Trust for Measurement
The RTM performs measurements of the software/firmware that is about to be executed. It
performs measurements before the particular software/firmware can perform any changes
to the system, to prevent harmful software/firmware from manipulating the measurements.
It is used during the boot phase, as part of the Secure boot feature used by TPM and
UEFI. The measurements are done by creating a digest using a hash algorithm.
The
measurements are not performed by the TPM itself, therefore the TPM is not a RTM.

The Root of Trust for Storage
When the CPU or the BIOS/UEFI has performed the measurements, they need to be
stored somewhere. Here, the RTS comes in. RTS is responsible for storing and securing
the measurements. In the case of TPM, the measurements are stored in the PCRs of the
TPM. Therefore, the TPM is a RTS [18] [19] [20].
The Root of Trust for Reporting
When the measurements have been stored in the PCR register by the TPM, they can
then be exported from the TPM. This is done by signing the PCR values with an AK. An
AK is used to sign data such as PCR values, to verify the integrity of the PCR values.
However the AK cannot verify if a TPM is a genuine TPM, therefore an EK is needed.
The EK, which is a RSA key, also known as an asymmetric encryption key, is created when
the TPM is manufactured and cannot be deleted or changed, it therefore never leaves the
TPM, and this is the reason why it cannot be used to sign the PCR values. The EK
can, however, be used to verify the AK, since the EK is unique to a TPM and cannot be
changed. The TPM is therefore also a RTR [18] [19] [20].
3.3.2
Chain of trust
The chain of trust is based on the RoT. When the CPU or the BIOS, which are RTMs,
performs measurements and sends those measurements to the PCR registers in the TPM,
which is a RTS, the RTMs relinquish their control and pass it onto the next component.
This is known as the chain of trust since the RoT will check if the next component in line
is trusted or not, and if it is, the RoT will then pass control to that component, which
becomes the new RoT, and this cycle will continue, creating a chain of trusted components,
i.e. a chain of trust. The first RoT is the CRTM, which cannot be measured and is placed
by the manufacturer of the platform; therefore, it is always trusted and will establish the
chain of trust. The CRTM usually consists of simple instructions for the CPU. Figure 3.3
shows a chain of trust, starting from the CRTM, which first measures the next component
in line, which is the BIOS, before passing control to it. The measurements are then sent
to the TPM [21].
The RoT and the chain of trust are essential and fundamental in a trusted system. There-
fore, an attack that compromises the RoT will be detrimental to a PC that relies on the
trustfulness of the TPM and the other RoTs.

3.4.1
TPM architecture
The TPM is a chip that functions as a secure cryptoprocessor, among other things. The
TPM is developed by TCG, however, TCG does not manufacture TPMs, but they do
write the specifications for it. As of now, the newest version of the TPM is TPM 2.0.
The TPM is used in many applications such as storing keys, RTS, RTR, RNG, etc. The
architecture of a TPM is shown in Figure 3.4, which consists of a secure I/O interface
for external communication, a cryptographic processor, a persistent memory, and finally
a versatile memory.
Cryptographic processor
The cryptographic processor implements all the cryptosystems, such as the RNG, the
key generator for the cryptographic algorithms such as RSA and ECC. It also generates
hashes for SHA-1, SHA-256, etc. Finally, encryption and decryption are also handled by
the cryptographic processor [19] [22].
Persistent memory
The Persistent memory, also known as NVRAM, stores the keys that should not be deleted
during a reset or restart of the TPM. These keys are the EK and the SRK, which are created
when the TPM was manufactured and cannot be erased or modified. Furthermore, the
access to NVRAM can be restricted, such as some of the keys cannot be accessed until
certain conditions are met, for example, if the hard drive is decrypted or not [19] [22].

Versatile memory
The data stored in the versatile memory will be erased when the TPM is powered off,
opposite to the NVRAM. The PCRs are stored in versatile memory together with certain
keys such as the AK. The versatile memory is used to quickly load data into and perform
operations based on commands sent by the TPM, such as loading keys [19] [22].
Platform Configuration Registers
PCRs are registers that store measurements done on software, hardware, and firmware,
such as firmware components, OS, or applications. PCRs are RTSs, as described above,
that store RTMs. To detect even the slightest difference in the state of software, the PCRs
store hashed digests of the measurements and cannot be set but are instead extended. In
TPMs the PCRs can use a variety of hash algorithms such as SHA-1, SHA-256, and SHA-
512, etc., each PCR register must have a separate bank for each possible hash algorithm,
so if the TPM can store measurements in 4 different hash algorithms, each PCR must
have 4 banks, one for each algorithm. Even though PCRs can only be extended, and not
set, they can however be reset, this can be done by resetting the TPM, in which case
all the PCR registers will get an initial value, which is usually ’0’h. The size of each
bank in a PCR register is the size of the digest of the corresponding hash algorithm, so
a PCR bank for SHA-256 hash digests will have a size of 32 bytes. PCRs can be used as
authorization, ex. where the CPU requests an object or wants to decrypt an object, the
TPM will then only release or decrypt the object if the current values of one or multiple
PCRs are the same as when the object was created, this is to ensure that the platform is in
a trusted state. PCRs are extended by concatenating the measurement, which is hashed,
with the current value of the PCR, and then hashing it again, using the corresponding
hash algorithm. The extension of a PCR is shown in Equation (3.1) [23].

3.4.4
History of TPM
The first TPM to be released was TPM 1.1b in 2003 by TCG. However, TCG quickly
found issues with TPM 1.1b, with one of them being hardware incompatibility. Therefore,
the next TPM to be released was the TPM 1.2 which was released in 2005. TPM 1.2 were
more standardized both in its hardware and pin layout and also its software interface.
However, the TPM 1.2 was not perfect and TCG began working on a new TPM [23]. The
TPM 2.0 eventually came out in 2014. When TPM 1.2 was released it only supported
SHA-1, which at the time was secure enough, however, shortly after it proved insecure
enough for TCG to start the development of a new TPM, the TPM 2.0. The problem
with TPM 1.2 was that SHA-1 was hard-coded in the TPM and therefore meant that
it was not possible to just add another more secure hash algorithm such as SHA-256.
With this in mind, TCG made TPM 2.0 modular, in such a way that any algorithm could
be implemented in TPM 2.0 and resigned from just hard-coding algorithms. With the
addition of implementing any algorithm, the specification and even the structure of the
TPM were written from scratch, thereby giving TCG the chance to improve many more
things than just the option of implementing any algorithm. TPM 2.0 was, therefore, more
versatile and could be implemented in various platforms with optional features, whereas
TPM 1.2 was hard-coded and didn’t offer flexibility in the same way. Table 3.5 shows the

3.4.6
Secure boot and Bitlocker
A TPM device in combination with UEFI makes up the foundation of Secure boot. Secure
boot, and the related trusted boot, are used to protect the PC from bootkits and rootkits,
by only loading trusted OSs. Rootkits are malware that takes over the OS by loading
before it; this is done by either overwriting the firmware or replacing the OS bootloader.
By loading before or even during the OS, the rootkits can then bypass logins, or record
keystrokes using keyloggers, which in turn will reveal passwords and sensitive information
such as credit card details, etc. Bootkits are like rootkits but more advanced and are
loaded very early in the boot phase. Bootkits can completely take over the target PC by
encrypting the hard drive and demanding a ransom to decrypt it, or bootkits can even
prevent the OS from loading at all, requiring a reinstall of the OS. Because rootkits and
bootkits are loaded so early in the boot phase they can hide from anti-malware services
that are first loaded after the OS is loaded [33].
Protection from rootkits and bootkits requires methods that are used very early in the
boot phase, and this is where Secure boot comes in. Because Secure boot uses both the
TPM and UEFI, the security features are set before the OS bootloader. After a Secure
boot, a trusted boot takes over and verifies the OS and the kernel. The Secure boot and
the trusted boot are part of the measured boot, i.e. where RTMs are performed. An
overview of the boot process on a PC running Windows and with Secure boot enabled is

BitLocker
BitLocker is a security feature included with the Windows operating system that encrypts
the entire hard drive to protect against data theft, loss, or exposure from a lost or stolen
computer. Bitlocker is used in combination with a TPM device, but can also be used
without, however, the protection is not as good.
If BitLocker is enabled and used in
combination with a TPM device, the keys used to decrypt the hard drive are encrypted
and decrypted by the TPM. The TPM will only decrypt the keys if the computer is in a
trusted state, i.e. the PCR registers contain trusted values. BitLocker offers additional
security measures such as multifactor authentication and assurance of a correct PIN or
startup key to preventing unauthorized access. BitLocker has hardware requirements for
use with TPMs, such as TPM 1.2 or higher, TCG-compliant BIOS or UEFI firmware
for TPM-enabled computers, and support for the USB mass storage device class. The
computer will go into BitLocker recovery mode if the values of certain PCR registers
are not trusted, which indicates that the computer has been tampered with. When in
BitLocker recovery mode, the computer asks for a recovery key to progress the boot
process [5] [38].
3.5
TPM Library Specification
The TCG, which created the TPM, provided a series of specifications. One of them was the
TPM Library specification, which is the largest of them all. This specification is divided
into 4 parts [19]:
• Part 1: Architecture
Here the architecture of the TPM is specified, such as the RoTs, the algorithms
used in the TPM, the architecture related to the command/response structure of
the TPM, etc.
• Part 2: Structure
Here the structure of the TPM is specified, this includes all the constants, handles,
parameters, attributes, etc. used in the commands and responses, to divide them
up so that the TPM and the PC know what is what in the long streams of bits sent
back and forth.
• Part 3: Commands
This part describes the actual commands and responses used by TPM. Together
with how the commands and responses are processed. It also includes tables for
each command and response, which contain all the structures used by that particular
command and response.
• Part 4: Supporting Routines
The final part contains C code, which implements the structures used by the com-
mands and responses.
This section covers parts of the TPM Library Specification.

3.5.1
Structures
Communication with the TPM is structured according to part 2 of the TPM Library
Specification [19]. This structure of sending and receiving commands must be upheld by
both the TPM and the platform communicating with the TPM. The definitions are many
and not all of them are relevant to this project; therefore, only parts of the definitions will
be covered.
Structure Tags
Structure tags TPM_ST are 16-bits long and are the most significant 16-bits in a command.
Structure tags are used to, for example, differentiate between commands that have sessions
TPM_ST_SESSIONS or not TPM_ST_NO_SESSIONS. The MSB in a structure must be ’1’b to
not confuse the value with an algorithm id, except for the TPM_ST_RSP_COMMAND tag,
which does not have its MSB set to ’1’b. A list of all possible structure tags together
with a description of each one can be found in table 19, pages 45-46 in part 2 of the
TPM Library Specification. For example, the structure tag for a TPM command without
sessions is TPM_ST_NO_SESSIONS with the corresponding value ’8001’h [19].
Command Codes
Every TPM command follows the same command code structure, the TPM_CC structure,
where CC stands for Command Code. This structure is 32-bit long and has 4 fields in order
from MSB to LSB:
• Reserved, 2-bit long This field is reserved and must be ’00’b.
• Vendor value, 1-bit long The value in this field is chosen by the vendor and must
be the same for all commands.
• Reserved, 13-bit long This field is reserved and must be ’0000000000000’b.
• Command index, 16-bit long This field represents the command index, which is
unique for each command.
A list of all the command codes for each command can be found in Table 12, pages 31-34
in part 2 of the TPM Library Specification.
For example, the command code for the
get random command is TPM_CC_GetRandom, with the corresponding value of ’0000017B’h
[19].
Response Codes
Response codes TPM_RC are 32-bit long structures. The 20 most significant bits are all 0s,
and the least significant 12 bits represent the actual response code. A successful command
execution will result in the 12 least significant bits being 0s as well. However, if a command
execution fails, the response code will not be 0s but a value corresponding to the reason
for failure. All the different response codes can be found in table 16, pages 38-42 in part
2 of the TPM Library Specification.
For example, if the response code is ’00000101’h, then a look at Table 16 part 2 of the
TPM Library Specification will reveal that the error is due to a TPM_RC_FAILURE. The
value for TPM_RC_FAILURE is stated to be RC_VER1 + 0x001, now another look at Table
16 will show that RC_VER1 has a value of 0x100 and adding 0x001 with 0x100 gives 0x101
or ’101’h. As stated earlier the 20 most significant bits are all 0’s; therefore, the response
code ’00000101’h corresponds to a TPM_RC_FAILURE error [19].

3.6.1
Localities in PCs
When the TPM receives a command from the southbridge it does not know where the
command comes from, if it is from the isolated computing environment in a CPU, or if it
is from the OS. To ensure the source of a particular command sent to the TPM, the host
will use localities. Localities are a part of each LPC frame sent to the TPM and depending
on who sent the command, the locality will change accordingly. So, the TPM can simply
read the locality, and then it knows which component sent that particular command.
Localities are important to restrict the use of certain commands from less secure sources,
for example, only some localities are allowed to reset PCRs and not others such as those

3.6.4
D-RTM Execution Sequence
The D-RTM execution sequence is part of the H-CRTM and is used to reset PCR 17-22,
which are the PCRs used by the D-RTM. These PCRs can only be accessed and reset
by locality 4, which is the locality used by the D-RTM to send the CPU microcode.
The D-RTM execution sequence uses three commands, the HASH_START, HASH_DATA, and
HASH_END. The sequence is described in section 5.3.1 of the TPM PC Client specification.
The sequence must be sent after a Startup command for it to be a D-RTM Execution
Sequence [19] [39].
The sequence begins with a HASH_START, which is done by sending a single I/O write cycle,
with an arbitrary value in the frame data field, to address 4028’h. After the HASH_START,

Configuration modules
As mentioned earlier, the configuration modules used in this project are taken from Andza-

pll.v
The iCEstick has a reference clock of only 12 mHz, however, the LPC bus operates at a
frequency of 33 MHz, therefore the iCEstick needs to multiply its frequency to achieve
the same speed as the LPC bus (33 MHz). There are multiple ways to perform frequency
multiplication, but a PLL is used in this project. A PLL will take an input frequency and
multiply it with an integer using a VCO, then the multiplied frequency will be the output
frequency. The output frequency will need to pass through a PD, the PD will generate a
voltage which is the phase difference between the input frequency and the output frequency,
however, the output frequency needs to be the same as the input frequency. Therefore
the output frequency will be divided to match the input frequency before passing through
the PD. The PD’s job is to control the VCO and keep the phase difference between the
input frequency and output frequency constant as to avoid inconsistency in the output
frequency. Lastly, a LPF is used between the PD and the VCO for stability. To generate
frequencies that are not a multiple of the input frequency, counter circuits can be used
either before (prescaler) or after (postscaler) the PLL [49]. The PLL used in this project
will multiply the input frequency of 12 mHz by 88 and then use a postscaler to divide it
TPM Sniﬀing and Injection
35
==PAGE:50==
by 32, to then achieve the desired output frequency of 33 MHz. An overview of the PLL
used in this project can be seen in Figure 4.4.

bufferdomain.v, ringbuffer.v and buffer.v
These three modules control the internal iCEstick buffer. The internal buffer has a limit
of 2048 frames, which means, if the buffer is full, no more frames will be saved in the
buffer until it is emptied again. If the buffer is full a green LED on the iCEstick will
turn on. Data sniffed from the LPC bus will be handled in the lpc.v module, and from
there the relevant data will be saved in the buffer by the bufferdomain.v module. From
the bufferdomain.v module the sniffed data will go to the ringbuffer.v which is the top
module for the buffer.v module, where the actual data is stored. The ringbuffer.v module
handles read and write requests to the buffer.v module, which is the actual internal buffer.
The buffer is designed as a circular buffer, hence the name ringbuffer.v. A circular buffer,
as the name suggests, works as a circle with a read and write position, where data is
circularly written to the buffer until the write position is the same as the read position,
i.e. the buffer is full. It essentially acts as a queue with a FIFO policy.
mem2serial.v and ftdi.v
The mem2serial.v module will take a LPC frame from the internal buffer and prepare it for
the ftdi.v which will then send the LPC frame to the UART port via the FTDI adapter.
The UART port will then transmit the LPC frames via a USB cable to a computer, where
the LPC frames will then be displayed.
power_on_reset.v
The power_on_reset.v module is just a simple PoR module that controls the PoR gen-
erator. The PoR generator makes sure that the iCEstick will start in a known state, i.e.
the same state as when it was last synthesized.
trigger_led.v
The trigger_led.v module will just turn on a red LED every time data is being sniffed
from the LPC bus.
Finally, the top.v module is the top module that connects all the other modules.

Chapter 5
Attacks
The goal of this project is to first understand what a TPM is and what the LPC pro-
tocol is, and the communication between the TPM and the host on the LPC bus. This
understanding is then used to design the sniffer and injector shown earlier. The purpose
of the sniffer and injector is to perform various attacks on the TPM to demonstrate and
highlight security issues with the TPM and its current implementation. In this project,
4 different attacks have been carried out, where the Secure boot attack is a combination
of the command injection attack and the TPM reset attack. The attacks vary in severity
with the Secure boot attack being the most severe as it attacks the most fundamental
security feature of the TPM, namely the RoT. A list of the attacks is shown below:
• Attack 1: Command injection
• Attack 2: PCR reset
• Attack 3: TPM reset
• Attack 4: Secure boot attack
5.1
Attack 1: Command injection
The command injection attack is done by using the injector to inject any command into
the TPM. At first thought, it does not seem to be an attack since the IBM TSS can also
send commands to the TPM, but some commands require administrator access such as the
PCR_Extend command, but the injector does not need any permission or administrator
access. Furthermore, the IBM TSS can only send commands after the OS has been booted,
while the injector can send them at any time, even during the boot phase, which will be
relevant later on.

the attack a TPM2_PCR_Read command is sent to PCR register 0 on the SHA-1 bank, using
the IBM TSS, as shown in Figure 5.1.

5.4.2
Reset Counter and Clock
The TPM has some countermeasures for reset attacks, one of them is the reset counter.
The reset counter is incremented every time a successful TPM reset has been carried out.
The reset attack shown earlier will result in an increment of the reset counter. The reset
counter is used in authorization policies, where certain keys are only decrypted if the reset
count is trusted. Furthermore, it is also used to check if the static state of trust has been
changed, since a higher reset count than expected will indicate that malicious resets have
been performed as explained in section 36, in part 1 of the TPM Library Specification [19].
Another countermeasure is the clock which shows the elapsed time since the last TPM
reset, which is also used to detect malicious TPM resets. To read the reset count and the
clock a TPM2_ReadClock command is sent to the TPM. The ReadClock command and the
response are described in section 29.1 in part 3 of the TPM Library Specification [19]. The
reset attack shown earlier will increment the reset count as can be seen in Figure 5.14,
where two ReadClock commands are sent to the TPM. The first ReadClock command is
sent before the reset attack, which shows a reset count of 258 and a clock of around 10
minutes. The second ReadClock is sent right after the reset attack, which shows a reset
count of 259, an increment of 1, and a clock of only 6 seconds, which is the elapsed time
since the reset attack.
The reset count must be taken into account when performing reset attacks on the TPM, as
prematurely resetting the TPM may trigger countermeasures by the TPM or the computer.

How to perform the attack
As mentioned earlier the attack will happen during the initial PCR_Extend operations
and when the BitLocker key is loaded and unsealed. This is because the BitLocker key
will not be unsealed unless the computer is in a trusted state, which means that when

a PCR policy must be met, which means that the PCR registers must be in the same
state as when the decryption key was created, i.e. a trusted state. This means that if the

discarded and the smaller, but fully working, iCEstick was again used. Therefore some
steps to reduce LC usage needed to be taken.
The first step to reducing the number of LCs used is to reduce the amount of PCR_Extend
commands. In Listing 5.3, the 35 PCR_Extend commands only extend 14 PCR regis-
ters, therefore instead of sending 35 PCR_Extend commands the injector only needs
to send 14 PCR_Extend commands.
However 8 out of the 14 PCR registers receive
PCR_Extend commands which extend two banks at once, the SHA-1 and SHA-256 banks,
and those commands are too long to be stored without using too many LCs. Therefore
those PCR_Extend commands must be split into two separate PCR_Extend commands,
one for each bank. This will then result in a total of 22 PCR_Extend commands. For the
PCR registers that receive multiple extensions, the final value of the PCR register can be

value as explained earlier, and during boot up, all the PCR registers are set to their initial
value, which is all zeros. This step will then greatly reduce the number of LCs used.
The second step would be to split up the commands. Since the commands are stored
as registers, the longer the register is the more LCs it takes up. However many of the
initial bits in all the PCR_Extend commands are the same, so instead of storing full

Chapter 6
Discussion
The results and findings presented earlier, show that various attacks can be made to the
TPM and the trusted state of a computer. The attacks mostly demonstrate issues with
the communications channel between the TPM and the south bridge, mainly the LPC
bus. This means, that the attacks can only be carried out because the LPC bus is not
encrypted.
The attacks varied in severance, but they did demonstrate that any type of command can
be sent to the TPM on any locality, which is severe because the TPM relies on the locality
to make sure that only certain components can perform certain tasks. Furthermore, the
resetting of the TPM and the sending of PCR_Extend commands to the TPM, shows
that the attacks can compromise the RoTs which are an integral part of the TPM and the
trusted platform as a whole. With these attacks, the TPM, and Secure boot as well, can be
deemed useless in computers with their TPMs configured on the LPC bus. Furthermore,
as mentioned earlier, Microsoft made the TPM a system requirement for Windows 11
[50] [51], which means that Microsoft has deemed the TPM device secure enough to be a
requirement. However this thesis, with its attacks, shows that the TPM is not so secure,
especially not TPMs that are installed on the LPC bus.
The attacks presented in this thesis were only performed on modular TPMs, and only
on the LPC bus, where it is easier to connect the wires from the iCEstick FPGA and
the pins on the TPM and the LPC bus.
A discrete TPM on the LPC bus would be
significantly harder to connect the wires to, as it requires good soldering skills, however, it
is not impossible but takes longer and requires soldering expertise. Besides the LPC bus,
TPMs can also be connected to the I2C bus or the SPI bus, which these attacks cannot
be performed on, for now.
However considering the soldering skills, it was intended to create a sort of clip-on device,
that would eliminate the need for soldering, and simply just ”clip” the FPGA onto the
LPC bus.
There are many ways to mitigate the attacks performed in this project and make the TPM
more secure. First of all the communication between the TPM and the south bridge could
be end-to-end encrypted, this will make it almost impossible to perform a MITM attack
and sniff the data on the LPC bus if the encryption algorithm is strong enough. Another
64
TPM Sniﬀing and Injection
==PAGE:79==
security feature could be physical security, where TPMs are by default an integrated part
of the motherboards and secured in a way that will prevent attackers from soldering wires
on the busses, without rendering the TPM or the motherboard useless. Probably the most
secure implementation of the TPM would be if the TPM was moved to the CPU, so it will
in the future be part of the CPU itself. This will make it more secure as MITM attacks can
not be performed on TPMs which are a part of the CPU, as the communication channel
between the TPM and the CPU will be near impossible to get physical access to. These
are just some ways that the TPM could secure better to mitigate the attacks presented in
this thesis.
The final attack, where Secure boot could have been circumvented, was not fully successful.
However, it was shown that the TPM can be reset and that PCR_extend commands can
be sent to the TPM, as well as other commands such as the PCR_reset command on
different localities. This implies that other attacks can be carried out.
Another aspect of these attacks which needs to be addressed is the fact that they are
physical attacks, and they only work on certain TPM that is configured in a certain way,
as mentioned earlier. So to perform these attacks, the attacker has to have physical access
to the device first of all. Furthermore the attacks not only require the attacker to be
physically present, but they also require access to the internal hardware of the computer,
which requires removing the panel of the computer and taking components out of the
computer to get access to the LPC bus and the TPM pins. All this takes time, which can
be limited in some situations due to the attacker’s presence which cannot be known, as
opposed to remote attacks, which don’t need a physical presence, the attacker does not
have to worry about being caught handling another person’s computer. So these attacks
do have their limitations due to them being physical attacks.
Finally, the fact that not all discrete TPMs have the same pin layout on the LPC bus.
As was mentioned earlier, TPMs can have pin layouts ranging from 14-1 to 20-1. This
complicates the attack as the attacker needs to either have 3D printed adapters for each
kind of discrete TPM currently in the market, which is cumbersome, or only target com-
puters that have the exact pin layout as the 3D printed adapter the attacker poses at
the moment, which limits the number of computers that can be attacked. Therefore the
attacker would either need to know the type of TPM before carrying out the attack or use
a cheat sheet with the pin layout of every discrete TPM, which significantly prolongs the
attack, and the risk of getting caught is therefore also higher.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In conclusion, the objectives of the thesis were achieved, which were to implement the
sniﬀing attack and acquire a way to inject commands into the TPM. Furthermore, the
PCR registers and the RTM of the TPM were successfully manipulated, both by extending
them and by resetting them, both individually and collectively by resetting the whole
TPM. The attacks presented in this report were, in part, designed by using the sniffer to
gain knowledge of the communication between the TPM and the southbridge.
Furthermore, the injector was used to inject various commands such as the Startup com-
mand, PCR_Reset command, etc. which were successfully received and handled by the
TPM by receiving a successful response from the TPM. This demonstrates that the in-
jector is indeed working. A range of hypotheses was constructed and tested, in which the
results were validated using the IBM TSS.
Therefore, the nonfunctional and functional objectives such as gaining knowledge of the
TPM and the LPC bus were all achieved, together with gaining enough experience in
using Verilog and FPGAs to design and implement the sniffer and injector. However, the
objective of circumventing Secure boot was not achieved due to countermeasures put up
by Secure boot and BitLocker. Further work is required to circumvent Secure boot or to
conclude that it cannot be realized.
The communication channel of the TPMs installed on LPC buses was compromised to
demonstrate the security risks of certain TPM installations, as illicit access to the TPM and
some of its security features was achieved. This further shows that the system requirements
made by Microsoft, of which one is the presence of a TPM device, should be reconsidered
to at least include that only certain installations of TPM devices should be allowed or
recommended. Furthermore, the findings in this report also show that more extensive
countermeasures to prevent the attacks demonstrated earlier should be implemented.
7.1
Future work
As mentioned earlier, the Secure boot attack was designed based on allowing the PC to
send its PCR_Extend commands to the TPM first, during boot up, then reset the TPM
afterward, and then finally inject the PCR_Extend commands with trusted values via the
iCEstick. The other option was, as mentioned earlier as well, to block the PC from sending
66
TPM Sniﬀing and Injection
==PAGE:81==
its PCR_Extend commands during boot up, and inject the PCR_Extend commands from
the iCEstick instead, thus not needing to reset the TPM at all. However, since the first
option didn’t result in a successful attack, the second option can be explored, as a way
to block commands from the PC while injecting commands from the FPGA at the same
time. This can result in a successful Secure boot attack.
The main attack, which was circumventing Secure boot, could not be carried out due to the
PC detecting the reset of the TPM. However, a series of other attacks can be performed,
since the sniffer can sniff any frame sent to and from the TPM, and the injector can
inject any command on any locality. Therefore attacks such as altering the PCR registers
after boot up can be performed, as the TPM can be used by applications to enforce
anti-cheating in games, DRM, etc. Therefore resetting the TPM post boot up and alter
the PCR registers could trick applications into believing that the computer is in a trusted
state. Furthermore, The remote attestation of the TPM could also be attacked by altering
the PCR register before the values are sent to remote servers for validation, which can
happen post boot up [52].
Another vulnerability that could be exploited is the buffer overflow vulnerability presented
in vulnerability note 782720.
This vulnerability essentially allows read-only access to
otherwise hidden and protected data together with the overwriting of cryptographic keys.
The vulnerability requires an attacker to send certain commands to the TPM, which is
possible with the injector developed in this report. Therefore further work can be done to
exploit those vulnerabilities and see how much they can be exploited [53].
Furthermore, it was mentioned in the book, ”A Practical Guide to TPM 2.0” on page
205 [23], that MITM could not be performed during HMAC sessions, due to the secrecy
of the bind authValue, salt, and entity authValue, which are used to generate the HMAC
key. An attacker can therefore not perform a replay attack due to not knowing these
values. However, as it has been demonstrated, the attacks presented in this report can
sniff data from boot-up, where the HMAC key or the elements used to generate the HMAC
key can potentially be sent on the LPC bus, which means that the sniffer will pick them
up. Therefore an attacker could potentially acquire the HMAC key, and perform MITM
attacks even during HMAC sessions. The possibility of such an attack, and its design and
execution could be looked into.
The clip-on feature discussed earlier could also be designed and created as further work,
this would as mentioned earlier eliminate the need for soldering and thereby ease the use
of the attacking device. It would also make it faster to carry out the attack and reduce
the chance of being caught.
