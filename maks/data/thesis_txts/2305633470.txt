Abstract 
Many engineers have tried different approaches to minimize the passengers waiting time as well 
as total travel time. To accomplish this, the bus bunching effect must be considered since this 
affects directly the passengers waiting time and travel time. Within the bus transit services, bus 
bunching effect is a case where several studies have been carried out and many control strategies 
have been suggested in order to mitigate this effect.  
The objective of this Master Thesis is to explore new techniques based on bidirectional 
communication between passengers and the bus system, through the use of a smartphone app. 
This has the purpose of improving the passengers waiting time and minimizing or eliminating the 
bus bunching effect. In a bidirectional communication setting, not only the passenger can get 
information about the location and schedule of the bus, but also the bus itself can receive 
information directly from passengers such as location and intended bus line. Based on this idea, 
Wait4me, a new bus holding request app is being proposed, to support a new type of bus holding 
strategy. With this new strategy, passengers can request to hold a bus in real time and the bus can 
only hold if it is ahead of its schedule. It differs from previous approaches because the buses will 
only be held for passengers that are about to board. 
It is expected that the Wait4me strategy will help to reduce the passenger waiting time at the 
stops since those passengers requesting for a hold will have zero waiting time. At the same time, I 
believe that this strategy will help buses to keep their headway and thus avoid the bus bunching 
effect.  Finally, it is my hypothesis that the travel time (inside the bus) will increase slightly 
because of the holdings. 
The main software that has been used to develop this project is Arena Simulation which is a 
discrete event simulation software based on the process oriented paradigm.  Based on simulation 
methodology, three different models have been created which each of them is representing one 
different strategy.  
==PAGE:4==
 
 
ii 
 
1) Non-Holding Strategy simulates a fleet of buses that are operating without any kind of system 
where they can be held. Therefore bus bunching effect is more prone to happen. This is a common 
situation in the real world. 
 2) Classical Holding Strategy simulates a fleet of buses that are running under supervision and in 
case one of the buses is getting off schedule, holding is applied at the stops in order to recover.  
3) Request Holding Strategy (Wait4me). The headway of the bus is calculated each time it arrives 
at one of the stops. If it is ahead of schedule and if a passenger has requested a hold, the bus will 
hold until the request passenger have board the bus.  
All simulation models share the same transit network which is composed of a single line where a 
total of five buses are running in one direction. The station one represents the beginning of the 
bus line while station 15 is the last station of the bus line. Passengers arrive at each on the bus 
stops based on a Poisson distribution. The passenger arrival rate is different from station to 
station.  The design of the simulation layout was built as an inspiration of the line 150s stretch 
from Nørreport St to Ryparken St in the city of Copenhagen.  
The finding shows an improvement in waiting time by nearly 7% in comparison with the classical 
holding and 32% when comparing with the Non-Holding strategy. Based on weighted average, the 
total travel time shows an improvement of nearly 6% in comparison with the classical holding and 
1,2% with the Non-Holding strategy.

CHAPTER 1 
Introduction 
One can imagine how the public transportation as we know it cannot operate without schedules 
or without a known frequency, how people cannot organize their trips without schedule 
information, or how transit agencies cannot operate without schedules. Therefore, it is important 
that the transit agencies accurately plan the bus schedules and bus frequencies in order to remain 
reliable and provide a good level of service.  
About 20 years ago, it was a considerable challenge for transit agencies to provide highly reliable 
bus operations. It was not possible to coordinate and monitor buses in real time and there were 
no communication between buses at all. This led to a well-known phenomenon in public bus 
transport: the “Bus bunching effect”.  It happens when a bus is unable to keep its desired headway 
and ends up being ahead or behind schedule.  As a consequence, either the front or rear headway 
of the bus will become shorter. Simultaneously, a longer headway will be created in front or rear 
bus (see picture 1).  This effect is neither beneficial for the bus transit operators nor for the 
passenger.  
Bus bunching increase waiting time for passengers and is ineffective and costly for operators since 
two buses will run as one. At the same time the reliability will decrease.  Nowadays, technology 
such as Automatic Vehicle Location (AVL) and Automatic Passengers Counters (APC) are key 
devices for transport agencies that help make better decisions based on real time information in 
order to use the available resources in a more efficient way.  
Since public transport came to exist, engineers have been looking for ways to improve public 
transport and create suitable schedules, optimize bus operations, reduce operational and 
maintenance costs, improve level of service, attract users, increase bus utilization and decrease 
dwell time. Some engineers have developed bus strategies, technologies and even new ways of 
bus operation such as Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) [1].  In the end, all these parameters are completely 
linked both to passengers waiting time and total travel time and these are directly affected by the 
bus bunching effect. 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
2 
 
 
Taking all the above mentioned elements into account, the objective of this research is to explore 
new techniques based on bidirectional communication between passengers and the bus system to 
decrease the passenger waiting time by minimizing or eliminating the bus bunching effect. In a 
bidirectional communication setting, not only the passenger can have information about the 
location and schedule of the bus, but also the bus itself can receive information directly from 
passengers, such as destination and location (e.g. walking to the bus stop). Would such type of 
information help decrease bunching? What about waiting time?  Could we design a bus operations 
strategy that chooses when and where to hold (to avoid bunching), given the communicated 
demand? This report focuses on these questions.  
Engineers have taken several different approaches to minimize the passengers waiting time, and 
in most of the literature the bus bunching effect is somehow involved. Chen, Adida and Jane Lin 
(2013) made a study which shares many similarities to this study.  They proposed a new holding 
strategy based on real time bus information. The supervisor from the transit agency holds the bus 
at stops if buses are close or separated from their critical point. The result was significant 
improvements in passenger’s waiting time and headway performance compared to similar studies.  
their study takes into account passenger’s boarding the bus when it is holding while previous 
studies did not consider this.  
This thesis proposes a new strategy named Wait4Me. It is a strategy that introduces holding the 
bus based on real time information on the condition that buses will hold only if a passenger has 
requested it and if the back headway is long enough (and/or it recovers the schedule) to make the 
holding.  In this case, holding a bus will have two purposes: 1) reduce the bus bunching effect 2) 
reduce passengers waiting time.  The bus calculates its headway based on real time information by 
using AVL technology. 
In summary, this Master thesis differs from previous approaches because the buses will be held 
for passengers that are about to board, thus will reduce waiting time for those passengers. 
According to Ben-Akiva and Lerman (1985) waiting time inside the bus is much more tolerated by 
the passengers compared to waiting time at the bus stop. Therefore, if the thesis’ approach results 
in an average wait time per passenger that is less or equal to current approaches, it provides a 
valuable contribution to the field.  
==PAGE:15==
 
 
3 
 
 
These approaches have been studied using simulation methods together with stochastic 
specification for passenger arrival rates, bus running time from stop to stop, passenger destination 
and passenger request holding.  The simulation has been run by assuming accurate real time 
information in the network in order to test the new proposed strategy “Wait4Me”.  These 
simulations intended to test, in the “lab”, if and in what conditions this strategy brings 
improvements, and therefore considerable simplifications are made with respect to a real-world 
scenario, as demand patterns are generally constant through time, as well as trip times between 
stops. It is our belief that this is a conservative scenario: if, under very stable and predictable 
conditions, our strategy works well, then in more complicated situations (e.g. disruptions, large 
events, non-recurrent congestion), it should perform even better because the extra knowledge 
about demand could only help the system. 
 
1.1 Causes of bus bunching effect 
Buses that attend low passenger demand are normally running based on headway-schedule (e.g. 
over 15 min).  For this, timetables must be available for the passengers. Another way to operate 
buses is based on headway-frequency (e.g. less than 15 min) without schedule in order to satisfy 
high demand. 
In order to improve passenger waiting time and travel time under headway-frequency, it is 
important that the buses maintain their proper headway. High frequency buses are prone to 
bunch each other due to the uncertainty in passenger’s demand and congestion. Other factors 
that causes this effect are early departures, weather and bus breakdown situations.  
Figure 1 illustrates different scenarios on how a bus bunching effect occurs. For example, scenario 
“b” shows that when a bus departs early according to its schedule it will pick up fewer passengers 
at the coming stop and therefore the bus will stay less time at the stop compared with normal 
situations. This effect will consciously happen until this bus bunching effect occurs. Looking into 
scenario c, it is possible that a high unexpected number of passenger board the bus, this will cause 
the bus to stay longer time at the stop. As a result, this bus will arrive late at the next stop and 
therefore a higher number of passengers are expected at the coming stop. In this way, the bus will 
get behind schedule and it will bunch with the rear bus. Scenario “d” is similar to scenario “c”. In

CHAPTER 2 
Background - ITS  
The deployment and development of bus Automatic Vehicle Location systems (AVL) has been 
carried out since 1970. The first generations of AVL were using a signpost method to track 
vehicles. When Global Position System (GPS) became available in 1995, the bus operators started 
to combine AVL with GPS technology [2]. This was more convenient since the maintenance of the 
wayside infrastructure was no longer needed. In the last 10 years, the capabilities of the AVL 
system have been increased and new components have been integrated to the AVL system making 
it more functional and reliable.  Nowadays, most of the commercial AVL systems include GPS as a 
main feature, and a set of options such as voice communication, next bus station announcements, 
Automatic Passenger Counter (APC), schedule adherence, data in real time between buses and 
real time information to passengers [2].  
 
Migrating from an old technology to the AVL system can be a big challenge for the transit 
agencies.  The implementation produces some changes in the organization, practices as well as, 
roles of the employees. Besides that, external departments need to be considered before start 
operating with AVL systems. An example of possible changes in different areas is listed below 
(Ibid): 
 
• Operations:  New way of communication between drivers and supervisors.  
• Passenger service: New information will be communicated in real time such as arrivals times, 
incidents, etc. 
• Information technology:  introduce software, servers, data bases, store of real time 
information and data analysis.  
• Human resources:  new tasks, practices and roles will be created. 
• Cost, expenses and revenues:  new expenses will be generated.  
==PAGE:20==
 
 
8 
 
 
 
Many transit agencies experience that the operational costs do not decrease through deployment 
of an AVL system because the AVL system requires different resources in planning, information 
technology and maintenance. Nevertheless, the transit agencies agree that this system adds value 
as it improves the customer service and reliability due to accurate data and useful real time 
information to passengers such as arrivals times. In the long term, if the passenger demand 
increases, the operation can be carried out by the same number of staff, which saves operation 
cost.  However, in 2004 studies from the University of Delaware [2] calculated the benefits of an 
AVL system implemented for the Delaware Authority for Regional Transit (DART). The system was 
estimated to cost $7.6 million USD with annuals operations of $870,000 USD. This presented an 
annual benefit of approximately $2.3 USD million (Ibid).  
 
According to the U.S Department of Transportation [3], a capital cost of AVL per vehicle can in 
general vary from $10,000 to $20,000 USD. The department developed a formula to calculate the 
AVL costs based on awards from 34 agencies.   The following formula provides an estimate for 
fleets less than 750 vehicles. 
 
Contract Award = $17,577 X Fleet Size + $2,506,759 
 
2.1 The Wait4me APP – The System Architecture 
As we know, many transit agencies are using AVL technology. Each vehicle will have an AVL system 
installed which uses GPS as a tracking device. The GPS satellites will send signals for at least 3 
satellites to the GPS tracking device. These signals can determine the position and speed of the 
vehicle.  The AVL/GPS tracking device collects this information and sends it in real time via mobile 
phone networks such a GSM to the control operation center. Then, the data becomes the input of 
the AVL software that checks and monitors actual location and time of the bus and this is 
compared with the bus schedule or frequency. After the information is processed, the bus driver 
receives updates telling whether they are ahead or behind schedule.  
==PAGE:21==
 
 
9 
 
 
Passengers can request holding the bus from a smart phone by using the Wait4me App (to be 
implemented). When passengers request a hold, the information such as the passenger´s location 
and current time is sent to the control operation center. This data, together with the location of 
the bus, its capacity, and time schedule, is then processed in order to calculate whether the bus 
can hold at the stop and wait for the passenger.  The system will only alert the bus driver in case 
the bus needs to hold while the passengers will not get any information back (in the simulation 
model). This is further discussed in the chapter of discussion.

CHAPTER 3 
Literature review 
Much literature has been written about how to reduce total travel and waiting time because these 
are important components for both passengers and bus operators. In the following pages, I will 
present a literature review divided into the following themes: Passenger waiting time and travel 
time, categories and strategies, causes and consequences, bus reliability and automatically 
collected data, and strategies with real time information. 
 
3.1 Passenger Waiting Time and Travel Time 
Once a transit agency has designed and implemented the layout of the bus route including bus 
stops, schedules and headways, its main goal is to provide a stop-to-stop service with a high level 
of reliability to its passengers. The passengers should experience the minimum travel and waiting 
time.  Brian, Hiroyuki, Mark and Michael (2007) learned by comparing the onboard time with the 
waiting time at stops, that passengers perceive time as two or three times longer when waiting for 
the bus at the bus stop than when they are on board. Likewise, Liu and Hollander (2008) 
confirmed that passenger’s perceived waiting time can be much higher compared with the actual 
waiting time. Based on a preference survey, they reported that passengers value the service 
reliability four times more than the travel time. They also proved that the unreliability of buses has 
a major impact on the passenger on the waiting time. Mohring, Schroeter, and Wiboonchutikula 
(1987) expanded this research on the “value of waiting time” by conducting surveys on routes 
where passengers had the option to choose between different frequencies and price level.  They 
found that during rush hour passengers value their waiting time between 75% and 130% in 
relation to their household wage rate while in non-rush hour the value of waiting time is between 
60% and 120%. Even though non-wage users do not perceive their value of waiting time as 
valuable compared to employees, it still has a significant value. This strongly supports the idea that 
the transit agencies should focus on minimizing passenger waiting time and total travel time.  
==PAGE:24==
 
 
12 
 
 
 
Before passengers take a ride in the public transport they must have a sense of the expected 
travel and waiting time in order to arrive at their destination on time. The users can reduce their 
waiting time and travel time by knowing in advance the bus arrival time at the origin and the 
arrival time at the destination. Nowadays it is common that transit agencies provide such 
information using different channels such as internet, mobile phones applications, text messages 
and electronic displays at the bus stops (Pilachowski 2009). There are studies showing the benefits 
of using real time information and both travel time and waiting time have been evaluated. 
Hickman and Wilson (1995) evaluated the benefit of the passengers by providing real time 
information data at the bus stop and they also analyzed the passengers’ behavior when deciding 
whether to take the coming bus or another. Their results showed small improvements in 
variability and travel time. Therefore, providing real time information for passengers in this setting 
is questionable. However, in a recent study, Ridho and Sumi (2009) discovered that by providing 
the passengers with real time information of bus arrivals via mobile phones the waiting time could 
be reduced by up to 44%. This was due to the fact that passengers could look up the bus arrival 
time without being physically at the bus stop. Besides this, the total travel time could be 
decreased by up to 19%. 
 
3.2 Bus Bunching - Categories and Strategies  
Within the bus transit services, bus bunching effect is a case where several studies have been 
carried out and many control strategies have been suggested in order to mitigate the negative 
effect.  Basically, the holding strategies can be separated into two different categories:  the first 
one known as “schedule-based control” where buses are running based on schedule. The second 
category is referred to as “Headway-based control” where buses do not have any schedule 
information (Linaza, Giesen, Muñoz, Delgado, 2014). Schedules are normally used when there is a 
long headway between buses (more than 10 minutes) (Ceder A 2001). Schedules are not useful 
when buses are running on short headways (less than 10 minutes) (Hickman, 2001). The literature 
introduces different holding strategies based on headway categories. The way that the holding 
strategy works is very simple; when buses are running ahead of schedule these must hold at the 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
13 
 
 
bus stops until they match their schedule. On the other hand, if a bus is running behind schedule 
the holding strategy is not applied or, in some cases, skipping a bus stop can be considered as part 
of the holding strategies (Oort and Wilson, 2010).    
The passenger arrival rates vary according to the bus frequency. For instance, when buses are 
running on schedule-base, the passengers tend to arrive according to the bus schedule. When 
buses are running with high frequency the passengers are more likely to arrive based on a uniform 
distribution (Chen, Adida and Lin, 2013). In this case the schedule becomes irrelevant since it 
should not take that long for the next bus to arrive. Therefore, in high frequencies the bus 
bunching effect is more prone to happen due to high passenger demand. This effect will lead to an 
increase in both the passenger average waiting time and the variance of the headway (Welding 
1957). Short dispatching headway or high frequency takes place in the main bus lines, crowded 
cities and during peak hours. Thus, “headway-based control” may be more beneficial and have 
better performance than “schedule-based control”. 
 
In 1957 Welding was the first to point out the bus bunching effect, since then, several strategies, 
methodologies and problem formulations from different angles have been analyzed with the aim 
to address the bus bunching problem.  Particularly, several strategies have been studied, for 
example: holding buses at stops, skipping stops, application of speed manage control, restricted 
capacity and a combination of these. In real operations, the most common strategy that is applied 
is holding buses at the stop since it is the simplest and easiest to implement (Felipe Delgado, Juan 
Carlos Muñoz, Ricardo Giesen, 2014). The basic approach is based on adding a buffer time in the 
schedule at each bus stop and hold the buses until they match the schedule, this will allow to keep 
buses on schedule (e.g. Osuna , 1972; Hickman, 2001; Xuan,Argote, Carlos and Daganzo 2011).  
Managing holding in all bus stops can be very difficult. Therefore, most of the studies introduced 
just one holding control on a specific stop along the route.  Hickman (2001) presented an analytic 
holding model based on stochastic inputs such as running time from stop to stop and dwell time.  
His model determines the precise holding time for the bus in a specific stop.  Xu Jun (2001) looked 
into the same problem but in this case the headway of buses was calculated in real time by 
collecting real time information. The model determined that the most suitable places to hold the 
buses are at the beginning and at the end of the route and suggested that a second holding 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
14 
 
 
control is not necessary. Besides that, the model also revealed reduction in passenger travel time. 
However, later on, Daganzo (2009) created a method that considers a time control in several bus 
stops and the model is created in a more realistic approach to real life by including randomness 
into the system such as demand, dwell time and bus running time. Pilachowski and Daganzo 
(2010) argued that control points at the stops are not relevant since these points cannot prevent 
bus bunching when the buses reach the critical distance. This is due to the fact that if there is an 
unexpected higher demand then the buses do not have the option to proceed quickly in order to 
catch up.  Instead, they proposed a real time adaptive headway control based on bus-cooperation. 
This means that a bus is able to communicate with the behind and ahead bus. In case of delays or 
high demand the drivers can adjust the speed in order to keep the desired headway. They 
concluded that this method yields to improve bus travel time and keep a regular headway in 
comparison with time the strategy that uses control points. 
 
3.3 Bus Bunching - Causes and Consequences 
As mentioned before, the bus bunching effect was first pointed out by Welding in 1957.  In 1964, 
the bus bunching problem was further studied by Newell and Potts who mathematically modeled 
the bus bunching effect for a single line. Their model assumed that the distances between the 
stops were the same length. They concluded that if the passenger arrival rate was larger than 50% 
of the bus’ capacity then the bus would spend an undesired amount of time by boarding and 
alighting unexpected passengers.  This would increase the headway distance to the front bus and 
it would reduce the distance to the rear bus. Hereby, the beginning of the bus bunching effect will 
occur and small perturbation in the original schedule would happen (Potts and Newell, 1964). 
Those buses arriving ahead of schedule to the bus stop were prone to pick up fewer passengers, 
hence the service time would decrease and so this would make the bus arrive even earlier to the 
next bus stop. In the same way, it was expected that a bus that arrived at the bus stop behind its 
schedule would bring on a higher number of passengers so that more time would be required to 
serve the passengers. This would cause the bus to arrive even more delayed to the next bus stop. 
This had a negative impact on the time that the passengers had to spend waiting at the stop and 
resulted in longer travel time. 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
15 
 
 
Newell and Potts groundbreaking study opened up a big discussion on how to regulate and avoid 
“the bus bunching effect”.  Since then, bus bunching problems have gained major attention in the 
bus transit operations and new research with different strategies has been added to the list of 
literature on the topic.  
Strathman, Kimpel and Dueker (2001) collaborated with Tri-Met (transit agency located in Oregon, 
USA) and evaluated the variability of the transit operations based on real data which was collected 
from an Automatic Vehicle Location (AVL) and Automatic Passenger Count (APC).  Headway 
variability and passenger loads were analyzed and they concluded that bus bunching is caused by 
failure of headway maintenance. The instability of the headway relies on many factors such as 
payment methods, number of stops, stop infrastructure and accessibility. Even traffic signals and 
traffic congestions can be responsible for the bus bunching effect causing serious discrepancies 
between actual bus running time and their scheduled time. Furthermore, when a bus leaves 
behind schedule it is very unlikely that it will get into schedule again and this effect will keep 
growing. Once the bus arrives at the last stop or depot, the gap between the last bus that has 
been dispatched and the actual bus can be significant. 
 
In the early bus bunching literature, Potts and Newell (1964) examined the effect of a single line in 
a route and the parameters such as time between buses, running time from stop to stop, 
passengers demand and dwell time were assumed to be static. Besides this, in this study bus 
overtaking has not been considered.  However, in real life these circumstances may not be the 
same since time variability is always present. For instance, Nagatani (2001) studied the behavior of 
the passengers and bus delay and he concluded that the bus delay correlates with the number of 
passengers on board. Nagatani also looked into the strategy of skipping a bus stop and its effect by 
considering passengers on the bus and the actual distance of the bus headway along with the idea 
to get the delayed bus into schedule. Fonzone, Schmöcker and Liu (2015) showed that 
randomness in arrival passengers have a significant effect on either improving or aggravating the 
bus bunching effect.  Therefore, they concluded that arrival patterns should be considered when 
bus bunching is discussed. It has been common that randomness in passenger arrival and dwell 
time are considered when dealing with public transport models. McLeod, F.N. (2007) said that 
when buses operate on high frequency, the arrival passenger distribution is assumed to be 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
16 
 
 
random and over a time period the passenger arrival distribution become uniform. McLeod (2007) 
and Chen, Yang, Feng and Zhiya (2012) share the same idea referring that when the transit service 
is running in high frequencies then the bus schedule become irrelevant and therefore passengers 
tend to arrive randomly at the stop. Seddon and Day (1974) also made several observations in 
Greater Manchester and concluded that passenger randomness is accepted for bus headways up 
to 12 minutes. Even if the departure time of the bus become irrelevant for passengers, nowadays 
it is common that transit operators are using Automatic Vehicle Location (AVL) which allows 
managing continuous specifics data such as speed, location, door openings and passenger 
counters from a vehicle. With this, it is also possible to display the predictable arrival time of the 
bus at the stop and passengers can better plan their journeys choosing the most relevant bus at a 
specific time. 
 
3.4 Bus Reliability and Automatically Collected Data 
In the literature there are many techniques to improve the service reliability in terms of passenger 
travel time and waiting time. New York City Transit (NYCT) created a customer-oriented program 
with the aim to measure two bus service indicators that passengers experience; on-time 
performance and service regularity. Nakanishi (1997) investigated a NYCT program which defines 
on-time performance as the percentage of departures that are made up to 5 minutes after the 
departure time and Service regularity is measure as the percentage of headway with an offset of 
50% of the schedule headway for buses running in frequencies of 10 minutes or less. Nakanishi 
concluded that accurate results were obtained and these showed a clear picture of the customer’s 
experiences. This can help customers participate in decision making regarding transit policies.    
 
In order to create better strategies and improve the bus service reliability a survey was applied in 
20 bus agencies throughout Canada and the United States. The aim was to know more about 
applied methods that allow monitoring travel time and counting number of passengers.  The 
results showed that in 1989 none of the transit agencies used any kind of system to collect data 
automatically. In 2001, a total of 19 % of the buses were equipped with Automatic Vehicle 
Location (AVL) and the portion of buses was raised to 64% in year 2011.  According to American 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
17 
 
 
Public Transport Association (APTA) (2012) the use of AVL are important devices that help to 
manage and control the buses operations and increase the efficiency of bus operations in real 
time [4]. In 2013 APTA made another study where it surveyed 75 transit agencies. The results 
showed that 70% of the agencies said that they were able to monitor their fleets in real time. 92% 
of those agencies that did not have any system to track their buses were interested in acquiring an 
AVL technology to monitor buses in real time [5].  
 
3.5 Strategies with Real Time Information 
Before Automatic Vehicle Location (AVL) systems appeared in the market, transit dispatchers did 
not have information of the exact location of each bus unless the drivers communicated via phone 
or radio to report. Now supervisors can easily monitor buses that are equipped with AVL 
technology from the operator center in real time. This technology allows supervisors to respond 
quickly to unexpected disruptions and allows monitoring bus headways in relation with their time 
schedule. It can also display the bus arrival time information on internet and at the bus stops. In 
combination with AVL, Automatic Passenger Counters (APC) is extensively used in the public 
transport sector. Over the past 20 years, these advanced technologies are among the most 
important devices within the transit industry. It can be found in the literature with different 
studies that introduce Global Positioning System (GPS) with the idea to measure service reliability 
by monitoring bus units in real time [6].  Bullock, Jiang, and Stopher (2005) argue that the 
appropriate use of GPS in buses can improve service reliability and identify potential problems. 
They used a software application that collected ongoing GPS data from a bus operator and 
converts it into meaningful information. They showed that GPS is a low cost solution for collecting 
travel data and a way to measure the performance of the operations. Pilachowski (2009) proposed 
the use of GPS and communication between buses in order to counteract the bus bunching effect 
by permitting buses to interact with each other. This would adjust their speed based on their 
specific locations. Pilachowski presented a continuum approximation model to analyze the 
performance of the buses and proposed a control tool that avoids buses to bunch each other by 
speed adjustment. It concluded that the control can determine boundaries in space between 
buses which can prevent bus bunching. Chen Adida and Lin (2013) studied a new approach of bus 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
18 
 
 
holding with real time information by considering boarding passengers while the bus is on hold. 
The implementation of a heuristic method is implemented which is appropriate for real time 
operations. In order to evaluate their model, they made a simulation based on a case study.  The 
case study contained real information data reported from an Automatic Vehicle Location and 
Automatic Passenger Counter. These data had been provided by Chicago Transit Agency (CTA). The 
results showed that, considering boarding passengers in the total waiting time, the level of error 
propagation decreases and the model performance was stable.  
 
Recently, in 2015 Chicago Transit Authority (CTA) has replaced its old track bus system for Bus 
Transit Management System. This system alerts ahead to the supervisors when buses are too close 
to each other or when there is a big gap between them, and it also provides a recovery solution 
for bus bunching.  Since then, major improvements have been observed. Nine of the South busiest 
lines in Chicago have shown to have 40% reduction in big gaps.  The main features of this system 
are: Hold a bus at stop, speed up, take a bus over, and follow another route temporarily. However, 
there is a limit to what this technology can do, and basically it depends on externals factors such 
as accidents, weathers, number of stops, traffic and even a double-parked car can cause big 
disruptions for the operations. [6] Joseph Schwieterman, a transportation researcher, stated “As 
traffic rises, even small, random events van cause buses to loose time. That makes fixing the 
problem more difficult and it will test the limits of the technology”. So, basically, the Transit 
Management System used in Chicago gives access to supervisors to control and maintain the 
buses’ headway. These systems collect a lot of data, and it is important to ask how this can be 
used to make predictions and identify when a bus bunching will occur. That approach was taken 
on by Miami Date Transit (MDT) in cooperation with IBM, which launched a pilot project for two 
months which would consider only main routes. The IBM predictive system was reported to be 
able to give alerts 60 minutes before the bus bunching happened, which provides supervisors time 
to plan and control the buses accordingly and to provide better schedules. Improvements of 
waiting time and travel time were not given [7].

 Methods 
This chapter gives a brief introduction to the simulation systems and presents two different modes 
of simulation: continuous simulation and discrete event simulation. The simulation software used 
in this project is named Arena Simulation and is based on discrete event simulation.  Then the 
methods used to create the strategy Wait4me are introduced in this chapter with a specific focus 
upon the simulation software, Arena. 
4.1 Simulation Systems and Programming  
Simulation programming can often be very complex regarding coding and debugging.  It is a 
challenge because it requires parallel programming in which many calculations (activities in 
progress) have to be done simultaneously. For this, many developers have developed simulation 
software, and they have created different ways of programming to be able to create models by 
the use of simulation codes.  SIMULA is an example of a simulation programming language. 
However, creating simulation languages can take a long time. Therefore, nowadays it is easy to 
work with simulation libraries that can be imported from common languages such as Java, 
VisualBasic, and C++. So, what can we simulate using such libraries or simulation programming 
codes? 
Systems that change over time can be considered to be simulated. There are plenty of such types 
of applications. Simulation systems can be divided into two branches. Firstly, when a simulation 
system has a state that continuously changes over time, then it is called “continuous simulation”. 
For example, the temperature of a liquid changes continuously when it is being heated. If we 
create a graph of the temperature value over time, we can see a continuous curve. Secondly, 
“Discrete event simulation” is used when a line of events happen at specific times. In this case, the 
state of the system is discrete and it changes at a particular time and contain its state for a period 
of time until a new event occurs. If we graph the value of the inventory of a particular product 
against time, we can see a line which breaks at specific times. This break represents the in and out 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
20 
 
 
of the products from a warehouse for example. Moreover, discrete simulation is further divided 
into three different methodologies: events, activity and process.   
 
The Activity Paradigm.  In this paradigm the system checks for all possible occurrences of events 
within a very short period of time (0.001 seconds).  With the activity paradigm, simulation can be 
very slow since most of the increments of time will not change the status of the system. Thus, the 
simulation can take days to complete. 
 
Event Paradigm.  This paradigm stores the events and their consequences are processed according 
to a time corresponding to the list of events. The executing time decreases, however, it is not 
possible for this model to run a continuous simulation. 
 
Process Paradigm. This paradigm is the most used in the simulation software. The key entities of 
the system pass through a sequence of processes which form the basis of the simulation system. 
The primary entities are usually those that we want to study and the others are resources that are 
used by the main entities. 
 
There are plenty of discrete event simulation software on the market like Simio, Analogic, Arena 
and Flexsim. Most of these have the same characteristics, and they are powerful enough to 
simulate a system. They share the same simulation engine, but they are different in the user 
interface and have different programming environment (languages), prices, licenses and even 
software literature.  
 
The main software that has been used to develop this project is Arena Simulation which is a 
discrete event simulation software based on the process-oriented paradigm. It offers a large range 
of distribution functions that can be used for transfer times, arrival times, use of recourses, delays, 
etc. Arena is a powerful simulation software that offers predesigned modules that represent logic 
processes.  Arena simulation models are built upon modules that perform actions on the entities. 
These modules are connected to each other by lines that form the process flow for the entities. 
The modules remain in passive state, and these are activated when an entity enters the module. 
==PAGE:33==

Entities go from module to module and change the state of the system over time.  To represent 
this in real life, it can be said that objects are represented as entities and the modules are 
represented as points that perform a task when an object requires this.  Figure 3 shows an

5.1.7 Bus Dwell Time: Boarding and Alighting 
Traditionally, dwell time has been considered as the time of alighting and boarding passengers, 
including door openings. The dwell time depends on many different factors such as payment 
methods, the design of the bus, peak hours, the number of doors, and height of the floor. 
According to York (2002), the alighting time for buses with two and three doors is 1-1.04 second 
per passenger and boarding time is 1.64-2 seconds (payment time is not considered). In the 
simulation, I have decided that boarding time takes 1.5 seconds per passenger, and it is the same 
for alighting.

5.1.9 Passenger Walking and Holding Time 
As I mentioned in the above section called “Passenger Arrival Rate”, the passengers are created 
based on a Poisson distribution. Once a passenger is created, a value is assigned to the passenger 
and this is given based on a uniform distribution. This value represents the time that it takes for a 
passenger to go from origin to bus stop (walking time). The minimum value of walking time is 0.1 
minutes and the maximum value 3 minutes.  In arena simulation this value is set as UNIF (0.1, 3). 
This walking time is also considered to be the value of request holding time. For example, 
assuming that a bus is at the station, and there is a passenger one minute away by walking from 
the station, then the system will calculate if the bus can hold one minute or not.  
 
5.2 Description of the Scenarios 
In this project, 3 different simulations scenarios have been analyzed. These are the “1-Non holding 
scenario”, “2- classical holding scenario” and “3- passenger request holding scenario”. Each 
scenario was simulated two times with different datasets as inputs. DataSet-1 contains variance 
passenger demand among the stations while DataSet-2 has been configured to have equal in 
passenger demand among the stations.  
 
The “No - holding scenario” simulates a fleet of buses that are operating without any system 
where they can be monitored. Since the location of the buses is unknown, no strategy for keeping 
their headway is applied, and bus bunching is more prone to happen. This, however, is a very 
common approach in practice.  
 
The second scenario “2- classical holding scenario” simulates a fleet of buses that are running 
under supervision and in case that one of the buses is getting off schedule, a holding is applied at 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
34 
 
 
the stop in order to recover its headway. Of course, while the bus is holding at one of the stops, 
arriving passengers can board the bus.  Applying this strategy, bus bunching could be avoided 
most of the times, but it may cause frustration to passengers inside the bus as it is not obvious to 
them why holding is happening. 
 
The third scenario “3- request holding scenario” is the main focus of this project. A fleet of buses 
runs on short headways.  Every time when a bus arrives at the bus stop it calculates its headway. 
The bus will only hold if it is ahead of its schedule and if there is a passenger that has requested a 
hold. The passenger holding time should be less or equal compared with the needed time for the 
bus to match its schedule. In this case, the buses have a purpose to stop (wait for a passenger) and 
at the same time it will partly or wholly recover its headway. This strategy has never been applied, 
but the idea is to decrease passenger waiting time at the bus stop and improve reliability of the 
system by alleviating the bus bunching effect.

Arena simulation represents the half width in the following categories: 
Insufficient 
For Arena to calculate the half-width, a minimum of 320 samples is required. If this condition is 
not met, then Arena will consider the half-width to be “insufficient” indicating that there are not 
enough samples to calculate the half-width. To correct this, a longer period of the simulation 
needs to be considered or run a higher number of replications.  
 
Correlated 
When calculating the half-width, the data samples need to be independent.  If two sets of data are 
compared and these are strongly linked, it means that the data is correlated. Therefore, more 
running time may be applied to solve this problem. Otherwise, the confidence level will display 
“correlated”. In other words, since Arena is based on stochastic processes, it is expected to have a 
different set of data when running more than one replication. If the set of data are pretty much 
the same, this means that data are correlated and more replications are needed. 
 
A value 
When Arena returns a half-width value, it means that in 95% of the repeated trials, the sample 
mean would be reported as within the interval sample mean ± half-width.

CHAPTER 7 
Discussion 
The line 150s that runs in Copenhagen has served as an inspiration to design the layout of the 
simulation as well as the data that feeds the simulation model.  We must note that the results of 
this thesis are based on 60 minutes simulation (+27 minutes warm-up). Besides these, 
assumptions such as dwell time, time for buses returning to the depot, and ensuring that holding 
passengers will arrive at the station are part of the simulation system. On one hand, by taking 
these considerations into account, the credibility of the results can be questionable.  While on the 
other hand, after analyzing the results and the behavior of the systems, the results seem to be 
logical and understandable, a further analysis needs to be done by considering real data, the 
actual layout of bus 150s and eliminate as many assumptions as possible. It would be interesting 
to see the performance of the simulation by feeding the simulation model with real data from bus 
line 150s or any other real one that reveals service quality problems. Moreover, other promising 
case studies could be night operations, rural areas, large events, and inclement weather. For 
example, in night operations or rural areas, buses are running every hour and missing a bus by few 
minutes can be very frustrating. It could be interesting to study the effect of having night buses 
with fix schedules where passengers still have the option to request to hold the bus.  
In this study, several indicators were analyzed such as headway, waiting time and travel time. Even 
if some results were favorable for the Wait4me strategy, other indicators need to be considered 
before this strategy is deployed in real life. For example, evaluating the satisfaction of those 
passengers that hold the bus as well as those that wait inside the bus. It may happen that 
passengers traveling for longer periods will get unhappy since their travel time will increase. 
Therefore, many different business strategies can be applied. For example, depending on how 
many passengers are on the bus an extra fee can be charged for those passengers that request a 
holding. Opposite of this, passengers waiting for a long time in the bus can get some 
compensation. To ensure that holding passengers will show up at the station, a fee can be charged 
in advance so passengers take the situation seriously. 
==PAGE:88==
 
 
76 
 
 
In the simulation, the Wait4me app does not receive notification of whether the bus is holding or 
not. This made it easy to simulate the model and did not affect the simulation results. Even it was 
not considered, this need to be considered in real life in order to have better communication with 
the passengers. 
It is important to mention that there is room for improvement of the holding algorithm of the 
request holding strategy. Currently, the algorithm only checks for bus capacity and the back 
headway, the bus will only hold if this is ahead of schedule and if a passenger has requested a 
hold. The holding algorithm can be extended by checking both headways back and front, allowing 
multiple communications among the stops. By constraining the holding by checking back and front 
headways, it is expected that buses will have a more stable headway, and it would be interesting 
to see the performance of others indicators such as passengers waiting time and travel time. 
Applying communication at the stops, the resources can be used in a better ways and more 
passengers will benefit from it.   
It could be beneficial to generate more scenarios. However, the limited time frame of this thesis 
has not permitted to make more extensive simulation experiments by changing different number 
of buses, different headway times, holding times and passenger demand at the stops.  
 
The Wait4me strategy can easily turn into an optimization problem where a model formulation 
can be applied to minimize the number of buses, the holding time at the stops, the number of 
passengers served or even look for the right size of a bus.

CHAPTER 8 
 Conclusion 
In this Master Thesis I have proposed a new strategy, Wait4me, which aims to improve the 
passengers waiting time and minimizing or eliminating the bus bunching effect based on 
bidirectional communication between passengers and buses. This strategy was compared with the 
non-holding strategy and classical holding strategy.  
A simulation model was created for each strategy and several metrics were evaluated and 
compared between all three strategies.  The headway of the classical holding showed to have 
better headway stability compared with the other two strategies. However, the Wait4me strategy 
showed to have an acceptable headway performance and its frequency is 30 seconds lower than 
the classical holding. The headway of the Non-holding strategy showed to have a poor 
performance.  This poor performance can be reflected on the number of passengers getting on 
board at the stations. The results showed that with the non-holding strategy 12% of the buses did 
not pick up any passengers at the stop because of the bus bunching. While in the other two 
strategies the expected number of passenger were taken at each of the stops with small 
variations.  
The level of the capacity was measured. The non-holding strategy showed to have an extremely 
poor performance by having empty and crowded buses, contrasting to the better performance of 
the other two strategies. These are generally similar to each other, with the difference that 14% of 
the time the classical holding will present crowded buses while for the Wait4me strategy this only 
happened 9% of the time.  
Regarding the passenger waiting time and total travel time, the results show that the Wait4me 
strategy has some improvements compared with the other two strategies. The average waiting 
time has decreased approximately 7% compared with the classical holding, and 32% when 
comparing with the Non-holding strategy. In the total travel time, this strategy is the one that 
showed best performance for all trips (short, medium and long). It showed that the shorter the 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
78 
 
 
trip, the shorter the gap of time will be between the Wait4me and classical holding strategy, and 
for the longer the trips, the longer gap of time will be between the Wait4me and Non-holding 
strategy. Opposite to this, the longer the trip, the longer gap will be to the classical holding and 
the shorter gap will be to the non-holding strategy. In the total travel time for long trips, the 
Wait4me Strategy shows an improvement of nearly 6% in comparison with the classical holding 
and 1,2% compared with the Non-holding strategy. 
In summary, looking at all the metrics that were analyzed we can conclude that he Wait4me 
strategy performs better than the classical holding and the Non-holding strategy. However, for the 
analyzed scenarios, the differences are not considerable between the classical holding and request 
holding.  
Personally, I often experience missing a bus for few seconds, so I believe that holding a bus with a 
smart phone application can be a future possibility and can enhance passenger satisfaction. 
However, for that, this strategy needs to be further studied by considering real data and testing it 
with different scenarios.  This will help to define the circumstances to implement this strategy in 
real life.
