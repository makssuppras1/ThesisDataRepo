Abstract
Magnetic elastomer actuators are a promising tool for the application of mechanical strain
to engineered tissues in vitro. These actuators deform in response to external magnetic
fields. This allows wireless and biocompatible stimulation, even in sterile environments.
In this project, magnetic composites are fabricated using a 3D printing technique, Direct
Ink Writing (DIW). The inks for printing are composed of SEBS, a thermoplastic elas-
tomer matrix and hard magnetic particles, NdFeB (Neodymium-Iron-Boron). To improve
dispersion and matrix compatibility, the surface of the NdFeB particles gets modified with
surface chemistry. First, a silica layer is applied on the particles. Then, they are silanised
with alkyl chains of three different lengths.
During the project each surface treatment is evaluated with X-ray Photoelectron Spec-
troscopy (XPS) to confirm their success.
Then the coated particles are used to pre-
pare composite elastomer inks with a fixed particle loading.
The ink is used to cast
dogbone-shaped samples and test their mechanical properties using uniaxial tensile test-
ing. Then the printability of each ink is evaluated through extrusion measurements on
the 3D printer. Lastly, the inks are used to print actuator beams that get magnetised
and deflected under different magnetic fields to assess how the coatings on the particles
can affect their magnetic abilities.
Overall, this project explores how modifying the surface of magnetic particles can in-
fluence the flow, mechanical behaviour, and actuation response of magnetic elastomer
composites. And subsequently contributes to the design of functional magnetic actuators
used for tissue engineering applications.

1.
Introduction
Three-dimensional (3D) tissue models have improved biomedical engineering by provid-
ing more realistic in vitro platforms for modelling of diseases and drug testing [5]. These
engineered tissues aim to mimic the biochemical, structural, and mechanical conditions
of biological tissues. However, a significant disadvantage in these systems is the lack of
integrated, scalable actuation platforms that apply controlled strain to the tissues [5].
Mechanical stimulation is important for the growth, alignment, and development of the
muscle tissue, especially for skeletal and cardiac muscles.
Cells react to cyclic strain
through mechanotransduction pathways that control gene expression and alignment. In
vitro models, that do not provide such stimulation often do not accurately represent na-
tive tissue behaviour [6].
Most actuator systems created for this need are still rigid, passive, or rely on cleanroom-
based manufacturing techniques. This restricts their ability for customisation and force
measurement capabilities, so they are less suitable for soft tissue environments [5, 7]. To
resolve this, a new type of magnetic elastomer actuator is under development that is soft,
biocompatible, and can be controlled wirelessly with an external magnetic field. These
actuators can be 3D-printed with DIW, embedded into tissue scaffolds and deflect under
magnetic fields, and subsequently apply mechanical strain to the engineered tissues [8, 5].
The method for printing the devices, DIW, is an extrusion-based 3D printing that allows
an accurate placement of soft, functional materials. The ink is a composite made from a
thermoplastic elastomer matrix, SEBS, and hard magnetic particles, NdFeB [9].
The particles are not initially magnetised, making them easy to work with the printer.
After the device is fabricated, the particles are magnetised. Once that happens, they
hold a permanent magnetic moment, and when an external magnetic field is applied,
they respond by producing internal torques that force movement to the printed device.
As seen on Fig. 1.1, one end of the actuator (with black colour) is mounted on the plat-
form (grey), and the other end is attached to the tissue (pink), moving freely according
to the torque that is generated by the interaction between the magnetic field and the
particles embedded in the elastomer [8]. Each platform has two actuators along the tis-
sue’s length. Each actuator is placed strategically in the magnetic field (B), so each one
of them is deflecting the tissue in opposing directions when B is applied externally [5].
This movement applies mechanical strain directly to the surrounding tissue.
1
==PAGE:15==
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the 3D-printed magnetic actuator integrated into a tissue
platform. The actuator (black) is fixed at one end to the platform (grey) and deflects
under an external magnetic field (B). This movement applies controlled mechanical strain
to the engineered tissue (pink) that is attached between the two opposing actuators.
The connection between the polymer matrix and magnetic particles is important for
the overall printability, dispersion, and mechanical performance of the elastomer ink.
Factors like particle quantity, how well they are dispersed in the matrix, and the surface
chemistry of the NdFeB particles affect properties such as viscosity, adhesion, Young’s
modulus, and strain at yield point or failure [10, 5].
To enhance the compatibility between the NdFeB particles and the SEBS matrix, sur-
face modification techniques are used. First, the particles are coated with a silica shell
to improve stability and provide anchoring points for further functionalisation. Then,
silane chemistry is applied to attach carbon chains of different lengths, which adjusts the
hydrophobicity and how the particles behave within the composite [11].
Direct Ink Writing with SEBS allows the integration of these modified magnetic compos-
ites into soft, biocompatible platforms. SEBS is suitable for such an application because
of its elasticity, ability to be processed at room temperature, and compatibility with the
solvents used in the composite formulation [11, 5].
This project explores the idea that, modifying the surface chemistry of magnetic par-
ticles can change their interaction with the polymer matrix and subsequently tailor the
mechanical properties and printability of the composite ink. My specific focus, as shown
in Fig. 1.2 is to surface-modify NdFeB particles, attach to their surface carbon tails with
three different lengths, short (C3), medium (C6), and long (C12) chains. Then the plan
is to formulate ink using the coated particles and test their mechanical, printing and
magnetic abilities.

2.
Background
2.1
Particle–Polymer Interactions in SEBS Compos-
ites
The deflection ability of magnetic elastomers depends critically on the microscopic inter-
actions between magnetic particles and the surrounding polymer. The magnetic particles,
NdFeB [12] and the polymer matrix, SEBS, which is a triblock copolymer composed of
two rigid styrene domains (S), and soft ethylene–butylene midblocks (EB). The following
Fig. 2.1, shows the structure of SEBS. The P regions act as physical cross-links, while
the E and B phase provides elasticity [2].
Figure 2.1: Chemical structure of SEBS
The nature of particle–polymer interaction in these systems is driven by the surface
chemistry of the particles and their compatibility with each one of the phases. The main
hypothesis is that, particles that interract with the P blocks tend to stiffen the composite
by anchoring in the rigid phase, while those that are dispersed in the EB phase, maintain
the softness and deformability of the polymer [2]. By investigating this, the viscoelastic
ink can be tailored and achieve the best performance for DIW. To get there, SEBS is
dissolved in butyl acetate (BA) to form the viscoelastic ink. When a matrix contains
BA, it decerases the viscosity and enables a shear-thinning behaviour that is essential for
extrusion-based printing [2]. However, incorporating hard magnetic particles, like NdFeB
modifies the ink’s rheological behaviour. Particle–polymer interactions can restrict chain
mobility, increasing the viscosity and yield stress. When properly balanced, this can
result in a stackable ink that can flow under shear during 3D printing and solidifies when
the solvent evaporates, allowing high-resolution multilayer structures to be formed [5].
2.1.1
Particle Percentage
The quantity of particles dispersed in the polymer matrix has a great influence on both
mechanical and magnetic properties. As the rigid fillers are introduced, they can rein-
force the composite, raising Young’s modulus while reducing strain at failure due to their
4
==PAGE:18==
limited chain mobility [13]. At the same time, magnetisation increases with volume frac-
tion of magnetic material, as long as particle dispersion remains uniform and interaction
effects with the matrix remain balanced, enhancing the actuation potential [8]. However,
this ends up in a compromise in performance: higher particle content improves magnetic
torque but reduces the ability to deflect [5]. Based on previous findings and experimental
testing, this project uses 30 wt% NdFeB, which provides sufficient actuation strain while
preserving the printability of the polymer [8, 5, ?].
2.1.2
Surface of Particles
Beyond quantity, the surface chemistry of magnetic particles plays a critical role in the
behaviour of the composite. Uncoated NdFeB particles contain oxidised iron that is caus-
ing corrossion, thus they are not biocompatible. Additionally, they have a poor chemical
compatibility with the SEBS matrix, leading to weak bonding and poor stress transfer
[5]. To overcome this, a two-step surface modification process is followed. First, a sil-
ica (SiO2) coating is applied to the NdFeB particles [5]. Silica-coated particles display
improved biocompatibility, avoiding the cytotoxic effects from corrosion [5]. Second, the
silica shell is functionalised with hydrophobic alkyl-silanes—specifically, carbon chains.
This hypothetically will cause a decrease in particle aggregation and promote their uni-
form dispersion in the matrix [2]. Treated particles bind more effectively to the SEBS
matrix and can enable enhanced mechanical reinforcement and subsequently allow the
formation of printable, stable pastes, demonstrating how critical surface chemistry is in
particle–polymer systems [2].
2.2
Silane Chemistry
As discussed, the surface modification of the magnetic particles is an important step to
tailor the material properties and 3D print a functional actuator. Silane chemistry can
improve dispersion and compatibility of the particles within the SEBS matrix and reduce
their surface oxidation with two different coating processes.
Firstly, a uniform silica shell is formed around the NdFeB microparticles to act as a
stable and reactive surface, and then with a second coating, carbon tails with varying
chain lengths (3Carbon, 6Carbon and 12Carbon) are attached to the silica shell. Differ-
ent lengths of the carbon tails are used in order to investigate how each length can affect
particle-matrix compatibility. This approach has been shown to alter both mechanical
properties and magnetic actuation behaviour of soft composites [5, 8]
2.2.1
Silica Coating on NdFeB Particles
To enhance the particle stability and enable further surface functionalisation with the
carbon tails, a thin silica shell is formed around each particle using the St¨ober method.
The silica layer on the particles has two purposes: first, it provides a chemically stable
and compatible surface that prevents corrosion of the reactive NdFeB core during storage
and later processing [8]. Secondly, it can enable better attachment during the function-
alisation with carbon [5].
For the reaction, the particles are scattered in ethanol, TEOS (tetraethyl orthosilicate)
5
==PAGE:19==
and ammonium hydroxide (NH4OH). TEOS acts as the silica precursor, ammonia as the
catalyst and ethanol provides a uniform solvent environment for the reaction and enables
the mixture of TEOS and ammonia [14].
Regarding the chemical process, the St¨ober method consists of two steps [14], an ini-
tial step of hydrolysis, followed by condensation [14].
1. Hydrolysis:
Si(OC2H5)4 + H2O →Si(OH)4 + 4C2H5OH
Here, the ethoxy groups (–OC2H5) from TEOS are replaced with hydroxyl groups
(OH), giving silanol (Si–OH) and ethanol (4C2H5OH) as a byproduct.
2. Condensation:
Si–OH + Si–OH →Si–O–Si + H2O
Silanol groups form a growing silica shell on the particle surface.
The high ammonia concentration used, ensures the rapid hydrolysis and favours shell
growth on the surface of each particle. The resulting silica layer can provide a chemically
active shell that serves as a platform for further functionalisation. [14].
2.2.2
Salinisation of NdFeB Particles
After forming a stable silica shell on the NdFeB particles, the next step is to chemically
modify their surface using silane agents with carbon tails of different lengths. The aim
of this functionalisation is to tailor the interaction between the particles and the SEBS
matrix by adjusting the hydrophobicity and compatibility of the surface.
The three
different silanes used were
• Hexamethyldisilazane (HMDS) – (CH3)3Si−NH−Si(CH3)3 for 3-carbon chains
(3C),
• Trichlorohexylsilane (TCHS) – Cl3Si−(CH2)5CH3 for 6-carbon chains (6C),
• Trichlorododecylsilane (TDCS) – Cl3Si−(CH2)11CH3 for 12-carbon chains
(12C).
Structurally, salines typically contain a reactive silicon-based head group and an or-
ganic tail that allows tailoring the properties of the material for different applications.
Silanes can anchor to oxide surfaces while exposing their organic carbon groups to the
surrounding polymer matrix [15].
The salinisation reaction forms covalent Si–O–Si links between the hydroxylates silica
surface and the silane reagent. In the case of HMDS (3C), the reaction releases ammo-
nia. At the same time, TCHS (6C) and TDCS (12C) which are trichlorosalines, need to
undergo hydrolysis, followed by condensation. This results in the surface being covered
by hydrophobic alkyl groups of varying lengths [15].

The length of each attached carbon tail affects the interaction of the particle’s surface
with the polymer matrix:
• 3C (HMDS): Very short hydrophobic tails, lower compatibility with SEBS.
• 6C (TCHS): Moderate length of hydrophobic tail, balancing particle-polymer
compatibility.
• 12C (TDCS): Enhanced hydrophobic properties with longer carbon tail, stronger
interaction with SEBS, maybe steric interference
Although [15], do not list the byproducts of each reaction, the mechanisms imply
the generation of hydrochloric acid during thrichlorosilane hydrolysis and ammonia from
the HMDS reaction. Any possible byproducts or untreated agents are removed since the

2.3.1
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
X-Ray Photoelrctron Spectroscopy (XPS) is an analytical technique used to determine
the elemental composition of a material’s surface. In an XPS experiment, the sample
is irradiated with monochromatic X-Rays as seen in Fig. 2.2, resulting in the emission
of photoelectrons from the top few nanometres of the particle’s surface [16, 17].
By
measuring the kinetic energy (KE) of the emitted electrons, the binding energy of each
electron can be calculated using the photoelectronic equation:
BE = hν −KE −ϕ
Where: hν is the X-ray photon energy, KE is the measured kinetic energy, and ϕ is the
spectrometer’s work function [18]. Each element produces peaks at characteristic binding
energies, and shifts in these peaks reflect changes in chemical environment, making XPS
ideal for elemental and chemical analysis.
Figure 2.2: XPS principle: X - ray targeting and exciting a photon.
According to [17, 18], for KEs between 10 and 1000 ev, the penetration depth of the
target is close to 5nm, which means that XPS is well suited to evaluate the surface coat-
ings [16].
XPS analysis typically begins with a survey scan, which covers a broad energy range
(i.e. 0-1200 eV), to detect all elements present on the surface. This is followed by one or
more high-resolution scans focused on individual core peaks, in this case, Oxygen (O),
Carbon (C), Si (Silica) [16, 3]. The output graphs from the software used (Avantage,
[19]), with this project’s samples, can be seen next, with Fig. 3.7a showing a sample
graph from a survey scan and Fig. 2.4 showing the graph from a high-resolution scan for
oxygen.

2.3.2
Uniaxial Tensile Testing
Uniaxial Tensile Testing is a method to characterise mechanical properties. Elasticity
can be evaluated, along with ductility and strength of the samples. For the testing, a
sample is under controlled stretch with a known load. And with the help of a software
(Bluehill [20]), typical key parameters like stress and strain can be extracted.
For my study, the tensile tests were performed on casted, dogbone-shaped samples,
fabricated from polymer ink, containing each coated particle group. All the samples were
under strain with a constant strain until rupture, and their recorded stress-strain data
were used to calculate the following mechanical parameters:
• Young’s Modulus (E): stiffness of the material, calculated as the slope of the
elastic-linear region of the stress/strain curve. For the calculations, only the first
3% is used out of the data to ensure that only the elastic region is included. From
Fig. 2.5 the elastic region is the linear area right before Yield [1].
9
==PAGE:23==
• Strain at Yield Point (σY ) : the strain value represents the transition from the
elastic to plastic deformation. Unfortunately, the Yield point is not always easy to
spot on a graph, so the 0.2% Offset Method was applied. A linear offset by 0.002
strain, parallel to the elastic slope, intersects the curve as seen in Fig. 2.6 [1]. This
point is used to define the strain at the yield point in a standard way for all the
samples.
• Strain at Failure: also called strain at rupture at Fig. 2.5 & 2.6. This is the maxi-
mum strain that the sample can withstand before failure. On a typical stress–strain
curve, this point is the endpoint of the graph, and it is far beyond the necking region
and shows the limit of ductility, as seen in Fig. 2.5 [1].
Figure 2.5: A typical stress–strain curve, showing the elastic and yield region at first and

last, the rupture point [1].

2.3.3
Materials for DIW
DIW is a 3D printing technique that relies on the extrusion of viscoelastic inks through
the nozzles. To achieve adequate printing, the ink must have specific rheological proper-
ties, like proper viscoelasticity and shear thinning [5].
A suitable DIW ink must flow under applied shear during the printer extrusion but also
maintain its shape upon placement on the slide. This balance is described as the stor-
age modulus (G′), which represents the elasticity. Additionally, the loss modulus (G′′),
reflects the viscosity. An ideal ink for DIW, has G′ > G′′ at rest, showing a dominant
elastic behaviour that supports shape preservation. Under increased strain, G′′, might be
greater than G′, marking the transition to flow. This crossover can be seen in Fig. 2.8
[2].
Figure 2.8: Oscillatory rheology of a compound polymer versus polymer ink, showing the
crossover of storage modulus (G′) and loss modulus (G′′) during strain increase [2].
Inks that contain thermoplastic elastomers such as SEBS are well-suited for DIW
because of their viscoelasticity. When SEBS is dissolved in a violent solvent like BA, the
behaviour of its flow can be adjusted by changing the ratio. Low concentration (like 1:5
SEBS to BA), results in lower viscosity, which is suitable for thin trace deposition. Higher
concentrations (like 1:2 SEBS to BA), lead to gel-like behaviour, ideal for free-standing
structures [2, 5].
Filler particles, like the NdFeB particles, highly influence the rheology of the ink by
increasing viscosity, modifying yield stress, and changing the shear-thinning dynamics.
Surface-treated particles improve compatibility with the SEBS matrix, enhancing disper-
sion and having a more predictable rheological behaviour. In contrast, poorly dispersed
or clustered particles will increase the yield stress unpredictably or cause clogging in the
nozzle.
For a successful DIW print, it is important to optimise the balance between
particle percentage, solvent ratios and polymer structure.
2.4
Magnetic Actuation
Magnetic actuation can enable remote, non-contact deformation of soft structures by
embedding magnetic particles in an elastomer and applying an external magnetic field.
12
==PAGE:26==
In this project, the magnetic particles are used to transfer permanent magnetisation to
the actuators, which can deflect when exposed to a magnetic field.
2.4.1
Magnetisation
Magnetic materials can be categorised as soft, hard or supermagnetic, with the hard
magnetic materials being the most useful for permanent actuation [8]. Soft magnetic ma-
terials like iron magnetise easily but lose their magnetisation when the external magnetic
field is removed. On the contrary, hard magnetic materials, like the NdFeB particles,
maintain magnetisation after the magnetic field is removed. [8].
The NdFeB particles are dispersed in a polymer matrix to form the ink, but the initial
polymer is non-magnetised, and that is suitable since the ink is used for casting and/or
3D printing the actuating beams. After the fabrication, each device is magnetised by
exposing it to a strong external magnetic field, which aligns the magnetic domains of the
embedded particles along the desired orientation as seen in Fig. 2.9. The magnetisation
process is done with a brief and high-field pulse to ensure that all the particles reach
saturation. This way, the polymer elastomer can become permanenlty magnetised and
the soft actuators can maintain a uniform magnetic moment along their length [21, 8, 22].
Figure 2.9: To the left, the dipoles are misaligned and the material is not magnetised.
At the right, after applying the strong magnetic field, the dipoles are aligned and the
material is magnetised.
2.4.2
Deflection of Cantilever Beams
The focus of the actuator design, is magnetically responsive cantilever beams. The beams
are anchored at one side, and since they are embedded with magnetic particles, they get
uniformly magnetised along their length and bend when exposed to an external mag-
netic field that is perpendicular to their magnetisation. Bending occurs because of the
magnetic torque τ = M × B, where M is the magnetisation vector and B is the applied
magnetic field [8, 5]. The distributed torques along the length of the beam sum up and
create an internal bending moment that deflects the free edge of the beam toward the
direction of the applied magnetic field.

2.4.3
Magnetic Field for Actuation
The magnetic actuation of soft materials depends on the interaction between the dipoles
and an external magnetic field. The interaction is based on the magnetic torque, τ =
M × B, which causes deformation of the free edge of the beam, and so when designing
the actuation setups, it is important to think about the nature of the applied magnetic
field.
Magnetic fields can be generated using permanent magnets or electromagnetic coils.
Although permanent magnets have several limitations and are not well suited for this
application [5]. Also, electromagnets have programmable field control and a larger design
space to generate custom fields. At the same time, systems with coils allow modification
of the field strength and direction since the current is a variable, making them ideal for
this application. To proceed with the application, the Helmholtz coil is used since it can
14
==PAGE:28==
generate almost uniform magnetic fields [8].
A Helmholtz coil consists of two identical circular coils that are separated by a dis-
tance that is equal to their radius (R), with current (I) flowing in the same direction.
This arrangement ensures a homogeneous magnetic field in between, described by:

A Helmholtz coil is ideal for applications that require a small workplace. That is

magnetic object is moved within the workspace of a coil because it is inducing a current
within the coil [13, 5].

Methods
3.1
Particle Surface Coatings
The procedure for the surface coatings on the NdFeB particles has two main steps. Firstly,
NdFeB particles are silica coated, this procedure is explained under section 3.1.2. Then
the glass particles are salinised, the carbon chains are attached to their surfaces with the
procedure under section 3.1.3. When the particles are ready – and their surface evaluated
– they can be dispersed in SEBS to form the polymer ink, but that will be explained in
a later stage under section 3.3.
Figure 3.1: Illustration that shows the coating flow of the NdFeB particles before entering
SEBS polymer, formulating the ink.
3.1.1
Coating Group Dependency
Before moving to the chemical procedures, it is important to explain how the dataset is
structured. To have an adequate number of samples, each surface coating was repeated in
three independent batches. Each batch includes four coating types: a silica group (Glass)
and three carbon-based silane groups (3C, 6C, 12C). All carbon coatings are applied on
silica-coated particles, so the success of the carbon coatings is dependent on the quality
of the underlying silica layer too. In batch 1, separate silica coatings were prepared for
each carbon group, while in Batches 2 and 3, a larger silica batch was prepared and
shared across the carbon coatings. Understanding the relations between each glass and
carbon coating is important since a failed or poor-quality glass coating can compromise
the performance of all dependent carbon-coated samples. The following diagram in Fig.
3.2 is summarising the dependencies across all the batches and groups.

3.1.2
Silica Coating
The NdFeB microparticles were coated with a silica (SiO2) shell using the St¨ober method,
which involves hydrolysis and condensation of TEOS in an ethanol-based solution. Firstly,
40g of untreated particles are dispersed in 1000 mL of absolute ethanol while stirring with
a digital overhead mixer (EUROSTAR 20 digital) at around 1250 rpm to prevent particle
sedimentation at the bottom of the flask. Once the particles are completely scattered into
ethanol, 60ml of 28-30% ammonium hydroxide solution is added slowly, followed by an
addition of 2ml of TEOS. The reaction mixture has to be stirred continuously for around
20 hours to ensure that all the particles are fully covered by the coating.
After the completion of the reaction, the particles are washed as described in A.1.2 and
dried as described in A.1.3 to get ready for further coatings, evaluations or ink formation.
3.1.3
Salinisation of NdFeB Particles
Following the silica coating, the particles were functionalised with silanes of different
chain lengths. To promote silane bonding, the particles are firstly oxidised in a UZ-ozone
oven as described in A.1.1. This step increases the density of hydroxyl groups on the
surface of the particles, improving the reactivity of the silica surface during silane con-
densation.
After oxidation, the particles are transferred into a glass bottle and dispersed in 98ml
of Toluene, then 2ml of each silane agent were added: hexamethyldisilazane (HMDS)
for short-chain (3C), trichlorohexylsilane (TCHS) for medium-chain (6C), and dodecyl-
trichlorosilane (TDCS) for long carbon chain (12C). Then the glass bottle is transferred
to the orbital shaker (PSU-10i) for mixing for around 20 hours to ensure a sufficient
coating around all the particles.
After the completion of the reaction, toluene is immediately removed from the bottle,
and the particles are washed as described in A.1.2 and dried as described in A.1.3 to get
ready for further coatings, evaluations or ink formation.

3.3
Ink Formulation
3.3.1
Ink Formulation for Magnetic Particle Composites
To evaluate the effect of surface treatment on magnetic particles for mechanical per-
formance and printability, composite inks were formulated by dispersing coated NdFeB
particles into a SEBS-based polymer solution. Each group of magnetic particles was used
to produce a corresponding ink formulation, which was later used for DIW or casting of
dogbone samples, used for the uniaxial tensile testing.
All ink formulations were prepared at a constant 30% particle loading, with the remaining
70% consisting of a SEBS solution made by dissolving SEBS (Kraton G1645) in butyl
acetate at a 1:2 weight ratio. The components were weighed on a precision scale and
combined in a 30 cc plastic mixing cup along with a 1 cm diameter glass mixing sphere
to ensure homogeneity.
Each formulation was then mixed using a Hauschild SpeedMixer (DAC 150.1 FVZ) at
2500rpm for 5 minutes. After mixing, the ink was processed in one of two ways, depending
on the application:
• Direct Ink Writing: the ink was transferred in a 3 cc printing cartridge with a
200µm conical plastic nozzle. The cartridges were mounted to the 3D printer using
an adapter and connected to a pressure line for extrusion.
• Dogbone Casting: the same ink was transferred into 10 ml syringes with an
840µm plastic nozzle. The ink was directly cast into the dogbone moulds.

3.6.2
Magnetization of Actuator Beams
Each polystyrene slide with three actuator beams is magnetised using an impulse mag-
netiser. The slide is placed into a custom laser-cut PMMA sample holder, which ensures
the correct positioning for all slides. All the slides are set in the same orientation, with
the actuators aligned towards the coil.
The magnetiser charged an internal capacitor bank to a defined voltage and discharged
it over 300 µs, generating a peak current of 3.6 kA through the coil. Based on a previous
calibration performed in 2016 (Magnet-Physik, Mag Dr Steingroever GmbH), a charging
voltage of 1500 V is producing a peak current of 4.2 kA, corresponding to a magnetic field
of 1.59 T. During the magnetisation, the current reached 3.6 kA, which is estimated to
cause a magnetic field of approximately 1.36 T.

5.
Discussion
5.1
XPS Results
The XPS results confirmed that the coatings were generally successful. The uncoated
particles showed high Fe and O peaks, which is consistent with a surface that has iron
oxide. However, the unexpected presence of phosphorus in this group is raising the ques-
tion of what other contaminants from the manufacturing process or storage are present.
Also, it is important to note that in the particle datasheet in Appendix A.7, there is no
information about the elemental percentage of the NdFeB particles. It may be worth it
to repeat XPS for uncoated particles after washing them with acetone to determine if the
phosphorus signal will be removed.
For the glass-coated particles, the high Si percentage and reduced Fe signals confirm
that the surface is somehow covered by Si. However, some Fe signals still remain, which
may indicate uncoated surface patches or surface damage during sample preparation. For
example, pressing the particles with the sample rod might have cracked the coating layer.
Also, the Glass 3 coated group showed higher carbon content than the others, which
could have an impact on the subsequent carbon coatings for the whole Batch 3. That
could be because the system only contains a limited amount of water coming from am-
monia, hydrolysis may remain incomplete, resulting in residual ethoxy groups. On the
surface of the particles, these could manifest as carbon. Also, that could be a sign that
larger-volume coatings might need more mixing time. It is very important that the sil-
ica reaction is stirred for almost a day to ensure that all the particles have been coated
properly and nothing was sitting at the bottom of the bottle.
The silane-functionalised groups showed the expected behaviour: as tail length increased,
carbon content also increased. The consistent presence of Si and C along the 3C, 6C,
and 12C groups is proof of the successful attachment of the tails to the surface of the
NdFeB particles. However, there are some Fe peaks, showing that some areas of the
particle surface remained uncovered or have coating defects/cracks. The 3C batches were
consistent with each other, while the 6C samples unexpectedly showed a small Fe peak
that was absent in their related glass samples (relations between the samples in Fig. 3.2),
possibly indicating local coating failure – uncoated patches. The 12Carbon groups were
also consistent with each other, with small but present Fe peaks in all batches.

5.2
Uniaxial Tensile Testing
5.2.1
Casting of Dogbones
During the casting and ink preparation procedure, the BA was evaporating quickly, so
the polymer ink has to be prepared right before usage. Also, the ink container must
be sealed with parafilm during the speed mixing for the same reason. Additionally, an
ink formulation using a 1:3 SEBS to BA ratio was tested for the casting. During the
curing stage of the samples, it caused violent reactions with polystyrene slides, leading
to bubbles and damaged dogbones. The damaged mould and samples are shown in Fig.
5.1. This reaction highlights the importance of the interaction of the polymer matrix
with other materials.
Figure 5.1: Dogbones with SEBS 1:3 to BA. The high concentration of BA reacted badly
with the slides. The samples and the mould are destroyed with big bubbles covering the
surfaces. To the left is the mould and to the right is the appearance of the dogbones after
removing them.
Even in ratios like the one used (1:2 SEBS to BA), some pores were observed in
the cast dogbones, proving that there are solvent interactions with the slides. These
pores were minimised by curing the samples for two days in the fume hood to allow full
evaporation before transferring them to the vacuum oven. Placing them in a vacuum
too early can accelerate evaporation, forming larger bubbles and defects on the samples.
Later in the process, glass slides were tested to replace the plastic ones but the samples
showed different mechanical behaviour during the testings and I was not able to compare
them with the rest of the samples.
5.2.2
Mechanical Properties of Polymer Ink
The mechanical behaviour of the composite materials is significantly different between
coating groups. The Young’s modulus is increasing for the 6Carbon and 12Carbon coat-
ings, indicating improved filler–matrix interaction and a denser particle network (Fig.
4.9). The 3Carbon group was closer in behaviour to the Glass coated particles, implying
that the impact of shorter tails is limited.
Strain at yield point was the highest for the 6Carbon group (Fig.4.10), having an ideal
combination of stiffness and elasticity. In contrast, the 3Carbon and 12Carbon groups
have much lower strain at yield, which may limit their applicability to the project. Simi-
larly, strain at failure was significantly lower for the 3Carbon and 12Carbon groups, even
44
==PAGE:58==
lower than the uncoated particles, (Fig.4.11), indicating more brittle behaviour. The 6C
group again had the higher value, making it the most ductile.
5.3
Printability
The required extrusion pressures for each polymer ink highlighted the differences in print-
ability between them. As shown in Table 4.2, the 6Carbon polymer ink had the lowest
pressure requirements, indicating a smooth printing behaviour and good polymer–particle
interactions. In contrast, the 3Carbon ink could not be printed due to the need for even
higher pressure (700 kPa) than the printer’s ability. The ink curled around the nozzle
and failed to stick, showing that there was not enough adhesiveness between the matrix
and the filler particles.
The 12Carbon polymer was printable but inconsistent in behaviour. There was some
clogging and it required relatively high pressure to extrude. During printing, extruded
lines failed to fuse properly, resulting in weak structural integrity. Overall, the coatings
had a clear influence on printability, with the 6Carbon group being the easiest to work
with.
5.4
Deflection of Actuator Beams
Actuator deflection results are summarised in Table 4.3, indicating a different behaviour
between particle groups. According to the deflection equation (2.1), the deflection of
the beam is inversely proportional to Young’s modulus. Thus, it is expected that stiffer
materials like 6Carbon would deflect less, as it is indeed conformed from Table 4.3. Also,
the groups with lower E values, like Glass and 3Carbon showed the highest deflection.
These deflections confirm the relation between stiffness and magnetic actuation.

6.
Conclusion
This project proves that coating the NdFeB particles with silane molecules indeed has
an impact on the mechanical and magnetic behaviour of SEBS-based magnetic elastomer
composites. The tests indicate that the length of the carbon tail affects the bond between
the filler particles and the matrix. Short tails from the 3Carbon group did not provide
enough interaction. Long tails, like the 12Carbon group, seemed to oversaturate the dis-
persion within the matrix. The 6Carbon group has the most balance, offering enough
bonding without disrupting DIW flow or compatibility.
In all the tests, the 6Carbon coated particles had the better performance. They showed
low extrusion pressure, which means they print well, and they achieved a mix of stiffness
and elasticity, shown by their high Young’s modulus and strain at yield point values.
Although the magnetic beam deflection for 6C was not as high as for the more flexible
groups, its mechanical strength and elasticity made it the most promising option for the
actuator development.
The 3Carbon group was difficult to print and required pressures beyond what the printer
could handle. Mechanically, these samples were brittle and had the lowest elasticity. It is
interesting, though, that this group did show the highest deflection in beam tests, likely
because of plastic deformation. The 12Carbon group showed average mechanical strength
and elasticity. However, its ink had high viscosity and inconsistent extrusion, probably
due to excessive interactions of the particles with the matrix because of the long carbon
chains.
Overall, the project confirms that silane coatings have a great influence in the inter-
action between SEBS and magnetic particles. The surface chemistry affects not only
the mechanical properties but also printability and actuator performance. Tailoring the
surface modifications is an important method for optimising the behaviour of magnetic
elastomers in soft actuator applications.

A.
Supplementary Information and

A.1
Silane chemistry additional procedures
A.1.1
Ozone oven procedure
Before each silane treatment, the glass-coated particles were exposed to ozone surface
treatment. As mentioned in section 2.2, this step is important to enhance the attach-
ment of the carbon tails to the surface of the particles.
The particles were evenly spread in a petri dish and placed in the ozone oven for three
cycles of 5 minutes each. Between cycles, the particles were gently stirred with a spatula
to ensure their uniform exposure. After the final cycle, the particles were immediately
used for silane coating to prevent surface deactivation.
A.1.2
Particle washing procedure
After each functionalisation step, either silica silane treatment, the particles were washed
to remove unreacted chemicals and contaminations. The solvent volumes listed below
were adjusted for each batch size of 40 g of particles. The same ratios were maintained
when smaller or larger quantities were processed.
• After silica coating: Acetone 500ml x 2 times
• After silane coating: Toluene 100ml x 3 times, followed by Ethanol (96%) x 3 times
Following each functionalisation, the reaction slurry was filtered using a double layer of
filter paper while gently stirring with a plastic spatula until all. When the slurry appeared
dry, the particles were transferred to a glass bottle.
A.1.3
Vacuum drying procedure
Right after the washing procedure, the dry-looking slurry is transferred to a small glass
bottle. In order to ensure that the residual solvents are completely evaporated, the bottle
is placed in the vacuum oven at 50°C and stays overnight. When the slurry appears fully
dry, the particles are ready for evaluation and ink formation.

slide, which was then spin-coated to get a uniform and thin film.

Mold assembly

Appendix A.3.2, they were assembled together to form the casting setup. The PMMA
mould was placed directly on top of the PVA-coated slide, making sure that the surfaces
are aligned and in full contact. To keep the slides stable during casting, the two parts
were taped together along the edges using standard lab tape.

A.3.4
Curing & unmoulding of dogbones
After casting, the dogbones were left to rest inside the fume hood for two days to allow

the cured dogbones without stretching.
A.4
3D printing additional procedures
A.4.1
Biosurfine slides preparation
To create a non-adhesive printing surface on the polystyrene slides (25mm × 75mm),
they were treated with a biosurfine coating before printing. That is to prevent additional
adhesion between the printed magnetic elastomer structures and the substrate.
Before biosurfine application, all slides were treated with UV-ozone using a NanoBioAn-
alytics UVC-1014 cleaner to promote even coating. The biosurfine solution was prepared
by mixing with MilliQ water and 96% ethanol in a 1:1:1.5 volume ratio. The solution was
transferred to a 1ml syringe using a pipette and mounted onto the volumetric dispenser
of the RegenHU 3D Discovery printer. The nozzle used for printing was Teflon lined of
250µm. The coating was printed in defined square regions on the UV-treated PS slides
with a custom G-code. After printing, the coated slides were left in a fume hood for
about 20 minutes before further use. The procedure was adapted from prior work of the
same group [5, 22].
