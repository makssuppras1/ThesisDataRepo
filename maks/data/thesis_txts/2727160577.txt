Abstract
Denmark has well-working forest areas and wood production but only produces roughly
25% of the wood needed. The remaining 75% are imported from neighboring countries
[1]. The interest in wood keeps growing since the invention of CLT and glulam, more wood
is needed, and producing it in Denmark would be preferable for the construction market
[2]. Grandis grows fast but has not yet been evaluated as a species used for construction.
This means the properties of Grandis are unknown.
Four main areas about the Nordic-grown Grandis as construction wood are investigated:
1) The physical and mechanical properties as well as the connection between properties
and the comparison to similar coniferous species. 2) The conditions of the harvest sites
and the influence this has on choosing the best sites for harvest. 3) How grading with
INSTA 142 [3] works with Grandis and compares to mechanical grading. 4) The potential
of using Grandis for glued products with Douglas as a reference species.
These are all investigated with experiments from DS/EN 408 [4]. The following exper-
iments are executed: four point bending of 320 planks from different harvest sites in
Northern Jutland, compression parallel to the grain, compression perpendicular to the
grain, tension parallel to the grain, and four point bending of finger-jointed Grandis and
Douglas with three different MUF adhesives.
Grandis is showing much scattering in the results. The characteristic density is 303 kg/m3
with a characteristic bending strength of 24.1 MPa. The strengths parallel to the grain is
better than those species with similar density but the perpendicular compressive strength
is much lower. The same is true for the stiffness. The general trend is that a higher
density lead to higher bending, compressive and tensile strengths. A higher strength is
also connected to a higher stiffness. However, there appears to be no connection between
bending strength and compressive or tensile strength.
The conditions of the harvest sites are not the best tool for defining the best site although
they give some implications. The sites produce differences in outcome both regarding the
average values and the standard deviation. One site is deemed better than the rest as it
produces results with greater similarity making it more predictable as a harvest site.
INSTA 142 [3] is not conservative enough and can therefore not be used to estimate
the strength class of Grandis. It overestimates the strength 40% of the time. Mechanical
grading is also not a conservative option but does provide better results than visual grading
as it only overestimates 20% of the time.
All three adhesives tested work with Grandis but applying sufficient pressure during the
production of the finger joints is a problem. It causes the wood to split. Since the pressure
is too low the strength also becomes too low. The same adhesives and low pressure work
for Douglas indicating that Grandis is a poor choice of wood and the execution of gluing
is acceptable.

1
Introduction
The interest in wood as a construction material have been going up since the invention
of cross laminated timber entered the market. CLT eliminates the challenges timber has
in regard to size and directional strength. The material can now be made larger, more
uniform, and stronger. This way of using wood makes it possible to use wood almost
equally to concrete and steel. It is furthermore considered more sustainable and can be
faster to build with [2].
Denmark has well-working forest areas and wood production but only produces roughly
25% of the wood needed. The remaining 75% are imported from neighboring countries.
The forests have a rotation period of 50 to 150 years where softwoods take the shortest
time to grow [1]. Structural wood is primarily made from softwoods. One of the fastest-
growing trees in Denmark is Grandis Fir. Grandis is a softwood tree grown in the Danish
forests as it grows fast and in many different soil types. Danish Grandis is primarily used
for packaging and woodchip boards as it is considered too poor for construction wood [6].
The fast growth of Grandis makes it interesting to the Danish construction market. It
grows faster than Norway spruce and will therefore produce more wood. The improved
wood production is beneficial if the timber can be used for structural usage. Especially
the demand for wood used as glued engineered products is of high interest [1].
The properties of Nordic-grown Grandis are, however, not yet known. The knowledge
of Nordic-grown Grandis’s mechanical properties is unknown and the physical properties
have only been investigated for other purposes by Bennuah [7]. They write about the
physical aspects of how the annual rings and density perform with different site conditions.
Aside from this Grandis is merely mentioned in listings of coniferous species where the
knowledge is regarding other species such as Norway spruce, Douglas fir, and Scots pine
[8].
Nordic-grown Grandis remains untested and the relation between mechanical properties
is unknown, however, it is still a wooden material and is expected to behave as such. It is
known that slow-growing trees tend to have a higher density than fast-growing trees [7],
and density is connected to the mechanical properties [9, 10].
Mechanical properties are properties that can be determined by applying force. In this
project that means bending strength, stiffness, compression, and tensile strength. The
physical properties include density, moisture, and appearance. These can be measured
without applying force causing a reaction [11].
For Nordic-grown Grandis to be accepted on the Danish construction market it must be
possible to grade it and the properties should follow the rules used in Denmark which is
the European Norms - in this case, DS/EN 1995[12] for structural timber.
1.1
Project Description
Grandis is a fast-growing fir species. A larger 3-year-long project is investigating the
material properties of Grandis in relation to existing grading standards, e.g., INSTA 142
and machine gradings, to see if it is possible to use the timber as a structural building
material. There are grading rules for Grandis grown in Canada and North America like
INSTA 142, but no grading rules exist for Nordic-grown Grandis yet. This master thesis is

a part of the project and will investigate the material parameters of the timber as well as
the ability to use Grandis in glulam production.
In the project, Grandis timber is now being graded according to existing standards for

gradings according to existing grading rules could count Grandis as well. The goal is to

1.2
Research Questions
To investigate whether or not Nordic-grown Grandis can become a structural material in
the Danish construction market, the material properties of Grandis will be investigated with
experiments to investigate its structural usage. Along with the properties on their own, the
connection of the properties will make it easier for future use of the wood to estimate be-
longing properties. If the connection between density and various mechanical properties
is possible the users of Grandis will not have to use destructive testing to estimate their
materials’ mechanical properties.
1. What is the physical behavior of Grandis and how do its physical properties compare
with those of similar coniferous species?
2. What is the mechanical behavior of Grandis and how do its mechanical properties
compare with those of similar coniferous species?
3. What are the connections between the physical property, the density, and the me-
chanical properties of Grandis?
4. What is the connection between the mechanical properties of Grandis?
To test the relationship between harvest sites and the wood, data about density and bend-
ing strength will be considered in connection to the conditions of the sites. Knowledge
about the sites will be produced from existing literature and not investigated further as the
main focus is on the material properties of Grandis.
5. What growth factors, such as soil type, precipitation, and sunlight, at the harvest
sites are determining why the mechanical and physical properties of the sites differ
from each other?
6. Which one(s) of the harvest sites can be considered more suitable for the harvest
of Grandis for construction?
Wood grading is an important factor to anticipate how a piece of timber will perform in
a structure. Visual grading rules from INSTA 142 [3] are used in Denmark for Norway
spruce and other species listed in DS/EN 1912 [13]. Visual grading does not require any
equipment and is less expensive than mechanical grading by a handheld grader or larger

2.1
Abies Grandis - the Species
As Grandis is not normally used for construction wood not much literature about the
species’ structural properties exists [6]. The sources that do exist are mainly about forestry,
growth, and density. Bennuah [7] is the only in-depth source found with knowledge about
Grandis. Though, they speak mostly about the influence of the site conditions on physical
properties.
Grandis is a fast-growing species typically with large annual rings. A large proportion of
the annual rings are the weaker early wood [7]. In Denmark, examples, where trees reach
one meter in diameter and a height of 34.5 meters, are seen to happen within 50 years of
growth. The rapid speed caused the annual rings to be up to 16 mm wide [6].
Grandis is known to prefer good drainage and moist soil but thrives on various soils.
However, it is seen that Grandis in coastal areas generally grow much better[7]. Bennuah
[7] also discovered that: ”Faster-growing provenances generally, tended to have lower
wood density than slower-growing provenances.” The strength is not mentioned.

2.5
Physical and Mechanical Properties
Nordic-grown Grandis remains untested and the relation between mechanical properties
is unknown, however, it is still a wooden material and is expected to behave as such. It is
known that slow-growing trees tend to have a higher density than fast-growing trees [7],
and density is connected to the mechanical properties [9, 10].
Mechanical properties are properties that can be determined by applying force. In this
project that means bending strength, stiffness, compression, and tensile strength. The
physical properties include density, moisture, and appearance. These can be measured
without applying force causing a reaction [11].

density of wood is a direct measurement of the concentration of cell walls. The cell wall

2.5.3
Fiber Direction
The fibers in the wood make the material have different properties depending on at what
angle the wood is subjected to force. The anisotropic material also shows different brak-
ing patterns depending on the fiber angle. In principle, all angles from 0°to 90°can be
investigated but the ones relevant for structural use are parallel and perpendicular to the
grain [10, 39].
Hoffmeyer [10] suggests a linear dependency between density and strength tested parallel
to the grain. The same dependency is true for stiffness. The regression is based on
numerous experiments.
The linear dependency is also seen in the tension tests of wood. The strength and stiffness
are known to increase by 1-1.5% for every percent the density increases [10].
In the perpendicular compression tests, wood breaks as a result of the cell walls slowly
collapsing and the regression between density and strength will show a second-order
dependency [10]. Müller et al. [39] describe the failure perpendicular to the grain as
the ”absence of a clear failure”. Furthermore, they point out the influence of density on
mechanical behavior in agreement with Hoffmeyer [10].
Figure 2.6: ”Radial compressive stress deformation curves of beech (dashed line), oak
(dotted line), and spruce (continuous line). All curves show a plateau stress which follows
the initial linear elastic deformation.” [39]
Figure 2.6 shows the plateau zone of the spruce to go slightly up and down due to ear-
lywood consecutively failing [39]. When tested, this behavior is also expected of Grandis
where a large portion of the annual rings are earlywood [7]. Microscopic observations
from Müller et al. [39] show the failure of the samples always happens in the earlywood
for compression perpendicular to the grain.

2.6
Grading - Visual and Mechanical
2.6.1
Visual Grading
DS/EN 1912 [13] provides an overview of the different methods approved to grade timber
in different countries in Europe. The Nordic countries use INSTA 142 [3] for visual grading.
The factors determining the grade are knot size, knot placement, wanes, crookedness in
any direction, damage due to rot, insects, or handling, etc. Perslev’s project [25] describes
in detail how the Grandis planks are visually graded following INSTA 142 [3]. The overall
takeaway is that the larger the defect the more likely the timber will be placed in a lower
grade. The grades then connect to the strength class.
The alteration of the grading rules over time has made it harder to predict the strength
directly from the acquired visual grade [41]. Furthermore, visual grading has an element
of the individual person’s perception of defects when they categorize the timber. This
can make the visual grading unpredictable and a certain safety is necessary. This safety
makes visual grading a more conservative option compared to machine grading [42].
Øvrum et al. [41] are looking into the significance of the length of the timber since the
grading is based on locating the worst part of the timber. When the timber gets longer
14 of 112
June, 2023
==PAGE:29==
there is a higher risk of running into larger knots, potentially giving the timber a lower
grade simply because of its length. Hautamäki et al. [43] describe the same problem:
”The effect of the size of the board is somewhat contradictory; in all grading systems
larger knots are allowed in larger pieces, but the probability that defects appear increases
as the size of the piece is larger”.
Øvrum and Vestøl [44] find the effect of the length to be greatest in butt logs and the
least in top logs. The effect of the length of the timber is less for the planks taken from
the center of the wood compared to those farther from the center. Hautamäki et al. [43]
agree with the first statement but find no connection between the center and edge pieces
tending to end in different grades. A larger variation in knot size is seen in trees from poor
sites. Based on this Øvrum et al. [41] suggest cutting the timber from poorer sites into
shorter pieces to obtain a better grade.
The grading parameter that most often moves timber a grade down tends to be knots.
The probability increases with the size of the knots and particularly dry, rotten, or sound
knots cause down-grading [41, 43].
Both Hautamäki et al. [43], Øvrum et al. [41], Øvrum and Vestøl [44] are looking at
Norway spruce. For Norway spruce, Hautamäki et al. [43] predict how often the visual
grading grades end up being correct. They find that the highest grades are graded more
accurately than the lower grades. Their model discovers that visual grading of Scots pine
and Norway spruce are able to predict the correct grade 40-50% of the time. Working
with only four possible grades instead of all grades they can grade correctly 76-83% of
the time.
2.6.2
Machine Grading
Machine grading follows DS/EN 14081-2 [18] and can be done with different methods
such as longitudinal resonance, and x-rays. The machines available on the market are
both handheld and stationary and test at different speeds [45].
The machine graders test the whole plank and do not focus on the weakest spot. They
identify a global stiffness where the length of the plank becomes insignificant to what
grade the plank gets. The tree size is also not affecting the machine grading [41].
An article by Fortuna et al. [42] writes: ”The results show that machine grading is a
better option since we can grade to higher strength grades and the number of pieces in a
grade is higher. Also, machine grading is faster, easier to implement, and more reliable.”.
Determining machine grading to estimate the grade of timber higher while also doing it
correctly.
2.6.3
Comparison
The knots will often predict a lower grade when visual grading and can potentially get a
higher grade on the machine grader due to the stiffness of the wood that makes up the
knot. Machine grading appears to give the most accurate results independent of plank
length [41, 42]. The reason is that the modulus of elasticity provides higher accuracy
when grading timber compared to visual grading [41].
Visual grading gives conservative results but is a possible solution, especially considering
that it is free compared to the cost of a machine grader. The visual grading relies on the
knot size and the high dependency can explain why the length of the plank is important
to visual grading [41].

2.7
Engineered Wood Materials - Application of Grandis
2.7.1
Glued Timber
Turning timber into an engineered material by gluing opens up the options for using timber
in construction. Figure 2.8 shows the gluing options for structural use. The finger joint is
the base for making a longer plank. This plank can then be used in glulam production to
make larger beam/column elements or in CLT to create whole decks [14]. Gluing is also
used in the timber industry to make different fiber composite boards [46].
Figure 2.8: From DS/EN 14080 [14]. Options for using glue in timber products. Left:
Finger joints, which can then be included in even larger glued timber products. Middle:
Glulam beams and columns. Right: CLT.
Reasons for using engineered timber products compared to structural timber are the po-
tential for larger structures and the properties becoming more uniform. The anisotropic
properties, wood is known for, are minimized and the timber can now compete better with
other construction materials [47].
Vick [46] describes what the adhesives do as such: ”Adhesives can effectively transfer and
distribute stresses, thereby increasing the strength and stiffness of the composite.”. By
choosing suitable joint geometry and adhesive the strength of the glued timber becomes
greater than the structural timber [48].
The compatibility between wood species and adhesive is tested in different ways. DS/EN14080
[14] describes bending, shear, and delamination tests. These are commonly used to test
the performance of how well the wood adhered and the effect of environmental exposure
[46].

2.7.2
Gluing and Glue Type
When timber is being glued there are different important parameters. This can be summed
up to the need for low surface energy to secure a good spread of the glue, a prepared
surface for an easy spread, and good absorption, which comes from having glue with low
viscosity and optimal capillary pressure [10]. When all of this is optimal the glue needs to
cure, sometimes under pressure.
The surface needs to be cut with a tool precise enough to make it smooth, this can be a
planer, jointer, or other tools providing a smooth surface. Saws tend to make the surfaces
too rough [46]. Gluing needs to happen soon after the surface has been prepared. The
freshly cut surface is better at absorbing the adhesive. If the waiting time becomes too
long the wood will have absorbed the moisture from the surroundings and will be too
saturated to absorb the glue[10]. The standards overcome this problem by only allowing
six hours from cutting until the specimen needs to be glued [14].
Glue can be made from one or two components. The former is easier to use since there
is no weighing and mixing of components where errors might occur. The latter is harder to
use as they require particular assembly conditions but they are stronger and more rigid.
This makes them suitable for construction usage, where they also show less deterioration
than one component glue [46]. ”Examples of rigid structural adhesives include phenolic,
resorcinol, melamine, urea, and casein” - Vick [46]. The examples of adhesives Vick [46]
lists will have to be mixed with fitting hardeners to react and form the adhesive abilities of
glue.
The amount of adhesive varies with the previously mentioned parameters along with the
temperature and humidity of the surroundings. The goal is for the adhesive to form a thin
uniform layer when the pressure is applied. The application needs to be as uniform as
possible and can be achieved by applying adhesive on one or both surfaces [46].
What the glue does is form a bond between the two pieces of wood. In the best scenario
the glue not only mechanically interlocks the wood cells but also penetrates the cell walls
of the wood to create contact on a molecular level [46].
The glue is cured under pressure to ensure that the glue can penetrate the surfaces of
both pieces and keep them connected. The curing can either be hot or cold depending
on the glue. Some adhesives are carried by the use of water. This has to be evaporated
to cure the glue completely. Cold pressing can take from 15 minutes up to 24 hours
depending on different factors of the environment like temperature and humidity but also
depending on the properties and size of the timber. Hot-pressing goes faster and varies
from 2 to 30 minutes. An even faster way to cure the glue is by using high frequency. The
pressing time is reduced to below 3 minutes, making it a desirable tool for the industry
[46].
The glued product can technically maintain its strength forever if the temperature stays
below 38°C and the moisture content in the timber is below 15%. The strength of the
glued product is not affected by low temperatures but the high temperatures can cause
deterioration for some adhesives [46].
2.7.3
Geometry of Joints
Figure 2.9 shows four different ways to make end-grain joints. It is not possible to make
butt joints strong enough for structural use with conventional gluing. The reason is the
lack of tensile strength in the joint. This is where the scarf and finger joints are introduced.
These joints connect the timber in a way where the fibers are bonded parallel to the grain,
giving the timber a high tensile strength [46].

Figure 2.9: [46] Four different end-grain joints. A shows a butt joint, B a plain scarf joint,

Patil and Hanamapure [48] and Anshari et al.[49] find that the geometry of the finger joints
is statistically significant on the strength and stiffness of the glued product. Habipi and
Ajdinaj [50] have similar findings for strength but find no significant connection between
geometry and stiffness properties. Finger joints are described by finger length and the
slope of the finger, which naturally is affected by the number of fingers [20]. Finger length
is optimal around 18 mm and strongly influences the bending strength [48, 50].
The number of fingers seems to be optimal in the middle range when tested by Anshari
et al.[49]. When testing joints with 8, 12, and 16 fingers, they find 12 fingers to be opti-
mal. Increasing the number of fingers increases the risk of fracture since the thickness of
each finger is smaller. Decreasing the number of fingers, on the other hand, causes an
increasing angle and the adhesive becomes less effective [49].
2.7.4
Influence of Wood Species
Both hardwood and softwood can be used for glued products but they react differently to
the process. Hardwoods have shorter fibers and this makes their shear strength lower
than that of softwoods. Otherwise, hardwoods are normally stronger than softwoods.
Softwoods tend to be the preferred choice for glued products [46].
Woods with high density have difficulty bonding. They have thicker cell walls, making
the penetration of adhesives difficult. This makes mechanical interlocking difficult and
requires higher pressure when curing to create sufficient contact [46]. The high density is
also affected more by shifting moisture content. Shrinking and swelling lead to increased
delamination and therefore worse bonding quality. The lower-density woods have more
voids and are therefore not affected by a change in moisture content to the same extent
[51].
Density is not the only factor affecting the ability to glue wood. Vick [46] writes: ”Ex-
tensive research indicates that extractives on wood surfaces are the principal physical
and chemical contributors to surface inactivation, hence to poor wettability by adhesives.
This is particularly true for resinous species, such as the southern pines and Douglas-fir.”.
Species with high density normally have a higher concentration of interfering extractives
making the gluing process difficult [46].
The rate of shrinking and swelling as well as the stability of the alkali content are important
factors if two different species are to be joined in a glued piece. The shrinking and swelling
of high-density woods produce high stresses that cause bonding difficulties. It can cause
the bonding not to happen or fracture in the wood [46, 51]. The shrinkage and swelling
are approximately proportional to the density, so the closer two species are to each other
density-wise the less likely they are to experience bonding problems caused by shrinking
and swelling. The alkali content is important because it can cause disturbances in the glue
line when two different materials meet [51]. In a study by Yusoh et al. [51] Douglas fir

was one of the species with the most stable alkali content compared to the more neutral
spruce. When mixing species this can be a problem and should be considered when
choosing species to mix.

Method
To identify the mechanical properties of Grandis, experiments are made. Four-point bend-
ing experiments were executed as part of the larger EU project. These experiments were
continued and the results were used to calculate the expected values for strength and stiff-
ness. The four point bending was made following DS/EN 408 [4] and the characteristic
strength and stiffness were found following DS/EN 14358 [5].
Previous to the four point bending the plank was visually graded and categorized accord-

the weakest side pointing down. The purpose of this was to test the worst-case scenario.
The side was marked worse if it had cracks, larger knots, or larger groups of knots, prior-
itized in this order. Then the four points for supports and loads were marked along with
the position for a measuring bridge. The specific position can be seen in figure 4.1. The
distances were determined by the geometry of the plank’s cross section. The planks were
all 150 x 50 x 3600 mm (height x width x length). The height and width were checked for
each plank and noted to adjust results according to actual measurements.

The graph shows that the visual grading of the full length of the plank generally places

similar results. 20% of the planks are estimated into a class too high with the machine
grader. Visually grading the midsection places 60% of the planks in a lower class or the
correct class. The remaining 40% of planks are estimated too high.
Visually grading the midsection rather than the whole plank is intended to be more correct
since this is the part that gets tested but it appears to be less conservative. It places
the planks into too high classes twice as many times compared to the full-length visual
grading. Yet, the number of times the visual grading places the planks in exactly the

full-length grading obtains. To complete the comparison, the machine grader places the
planks correctly 40% of the time.

lower classes compared to the grading of the midsection.

grading of Scots pine and Norway spruce. They can find the correct grade 76-83% of the

the grading of the midsection are close to these numbers. This is pointing in the direction

compared to mechanical grading and that is not the case here [43, 41, 44]. Another
expectation not fulfilled by the visual grading is that since Grandis has many knots, and

generally get graded into worse categories [41, 43].

4.5.1
Method
Another mechanical property is the compression strength parallel to the grain. This was
tested and determined following DS/EN 408 [4] as well. The test pieces were 45x45x270
mm and produced with plane and parallel end surfaces perpendicular to the grain. The
samples had no knots and were as close to straight-grained as possible.
The measurements of the samples were chosen so they would fit the testing device the
best. The height is always determined by the smallest side. The spherically seated load-
ing head attached it the Instron series 2580-301 100kN static load cell had a diameter of
75mm with a flat surface allowing a square of max 53x53mm to fit under it. The uncer-
tainty of the load was maximum 0.23% relative to the reading. For easier placement of
the sample, it was made smaller than this but also to accommodate the maximum force
the Instron can provide. Based on the mean bending strength of 53.9MPa the estimated
mean force needed was 64kN. It was assumed that some samples would need more
force, coming closer to the limit of 100kN.
Figure 4.17: Test setup for compression parallel to the grain.
The sample was then placed in the Instron with a metal piece at the bottom as seen in fig-
ure 4.17. The sample was placed concentrically under the loading head and compressed
with 0.34 mm/min to obtain the desired breaking time of 300±120s. The loading head
was secured against movement in all directions.
One already damaged sample was sacrificed to determine the testing speed. This sample
was removed from the final data set as it does not fit the requirements of DS/EN 408 [4].

4.8
Finger Joints
4.8.1
Method
Besides investigating typical mechanical properties the ability to glue Grandis is also
tested. Finger joints are the first stepping stone towards a glued product as it is included in
larger structures. Testing the glue in a finger joint will also indicate if Grandis has potential
to be glued in general.
Douglas is tested with finger joints as well. As more wood types enter the marked for
glued products it is favorable to have one glue type at the factory and be able to use
it for several wood types. Some glues are approved for Douglas and some are not yet
approved. Douglas is already known to be able to be glued and acts as a reference for
comparison here.
Unlike the bending strength, the finger joints will be tested in flat-wise bending. The setup
is still with four points following DS/EN 408 [4]. For comparison the flat-wise strength of
the finger joints will be converted to edge-wise strength. The flat-wise testing will ensure
that the strength of all fingers is tested, unlike edge-wise bending where only the fingers
in tension will experience the maximum force.
4.8.1.1
Glueing
The samples were prepared for gluing by having the fingertips cut out on one end. The
production of the finger joints as well as the testing followed DS/EN 15497 [20]. The
number of samples produced and tested of both Grandis and Douglas is seen in table
4.12. Each half of a whole plank measured 34x86x600 mm. Grandis was sized down
to match the received Douglas planks and the height was governed by the size of the
cutter making finger joints. The geometry of the finger joint is seen in figure 2.9. The
finger length was 20mm, the tip gap varied with the applied pressure but was in general
approximately 3mm. The pitch was 6mm.
Figure 4.41: Geometry of the finger joint. p is the pitch, as seen it is the distance between
the fingertips. lt is the tip gap. li is the length of the finger.
The adhesive types used are listed in table 4.12 and are all so called MUF (Melamine
Urea Formaldehyde) adhesives consisting of two components a resin and a hardener.
These are commonly used for finger joints and are usually applied one by one at the

at a factory and the glue was therefore mixed by hand. For an easy process the lowest
possible amount of hardener was added to the mixture. The ratios seen in table 4.12 gave
an opening time of 90-120 minutes at 20°C, good workability, and viscosity for application.

5.4.1
The Potential for Using Grandis in Glulam
The high pressure required during production causes Grandis to split. The splitting is
a concern because it can weaken the wood. Therefore, the pressure is lowered during
experimentation to limit splitting. Results do however point towards that high pressure is
needed for obtaining sufficient strength when gluing Grandis.
This means that Grandis on the one hand appear to have good potential for gluing as
seen when visually inspecting the breaking patterns. It is also confirmed when looking
inside the failed joints. This is seen in appendix C and E as well as figure 4.49 and 4.50.
The breakage appears in the weakest parts of the connection as expected. At the same
time, Grandis show brittle behavior that causes splitting when glued and sudden failures
when testing. This behavior is not suitable for gluing nor in general. The brittleness can
potentially be so bad that it causes unwarranted failures that endanger people.
Along with the splitting and brittleness comes the fact that none of the adhesives tested
provided a high enough characteristic strength. The strength appears to improve with
higher pressure but if this is not considered an option due to splitting then the strength is
not high enough with this finger configuration. A higher surface area of the fingers contact
area can maybe prove beneficial. This is not tested but can be executed by making longer
fingers. The larger contact area is seen to have an effect on one of the batches. Though
it might be because of the contact area or because the higher pressure also limit the
distance between fingertips, creating a stronger bond.
The type of adhesive gives varying results and an optimal type along with an optimal ratio
can be further investigated. Since the breakage appear in the weak parts of the wood and

6
Conclusion
6.1
Material properties
The material properties of Grandis are not as bad as expected or maybe even feared,
but they are not excellent either. Generally, the results show large scattering, especially
around the higher and lower densities. The high variances lead to lower characteristic
properties and the behavior of the wood appears to be more brittle than other softwoods.
All the properties have been displayed in table 5.1.
1. What is the physical behavior of Grandis and how do its physical properties compare
with those of similar coniferous species?
The density of Grandis varies from approximately 300-600 kg/m3 which is a larger vari-
ance than seen for Norway spruce. This also means that when finding the characteristic
and mean density the difference between the two values is higher than expected. The
large variation in Grandis can be caused by the rapid growth as well as the annual ring
being larger in size. There is a connection between smaller annual rings leading to higher
densities.
2. What is the mechanical behavior of Grandis and how do its mechanical properties
compare with those of similar coniferous species?
Several mechanical properties have been tested with different experiments. The charac-
teristic bending strength is 24.1 MPa which is higher than expected from the observations
of the density and general appearance of the wood. The elastic modulus does how-
ever not follow the unexpectedly good results and only qualifies for a class C18. Similar
species with the same density usually perform better strength-wise but the stiffness of
Grandis appears to be similar to expectations of coniferous species.
The compression and tension strength parallel to the fibers performs better than expected
when compared to other softwoods with the same bending strength or density. The per-
pendicular strength on the other hand is much weaker. This is probably due to the fast
growth rate causing large annual rings. This is the one property really differentiating Gran-
dis from for example Norway spruce.
The failure of the experiments follows the expectations from the theory of other species
introduced in chapter 2. Since the properties are not following those of similar species it
is difficult to estimate the correct mechanical properties of Grandis based on the same set
of expectations shown in table 5.2 and 5.3.
3. What are the connections between the physical property, the density, and the me-
chanical properties of Grandis?
For the data gathered from the four point bending the whole spectrum of densities are
present. This is not the case for the compression and tension experiments. Therefore,
the connection between density and bending strength and stiffness have stronger corre-
lations. The stiffness based on the tension experiment is seen to have fewer data points
which make it difficult to conclude how the tension is connected to the density because
the density range is rather small.
That being said, a higher density is in general connected to higher strength and stiffnesses
independent of fiber angle. The connection does come with a large scattering that needs

to be taken into account. This means that density is not the only factor affecting the
mechanical properties. The size and amount of knots also have an influence. For the
construction market this means that the density cannot be used to predict the mechanical
properties with great precision. If the density is used for estimating strength and stiffness
a safety factor has to be incorporated to take the large scattering into account.
It would be beneficial for the users if they could estimate mechanical properties based
on a physical property such as density as it would limit the need for destructive testing.
Destructive testing can be costly and it would save material and money if the material
could be easily implemented without it.
4. What is the connection between the mechanical properties of Grandis?
Using the bending strength as an estimation for other mechanical properties is what
DS/EN 384 [15] does. This does however not appear to work for Grandis based on the
limited data from the experiments. They show no strong connection between bending
strength and compression and tension. The lack of connection implies that destructive
testing with four point bending is not enough to estimate the fiber directional compressive
and tensile strengths.
The strength and stiffness do appear to follow the theory and are connected linearly. A
higher strength comes with a higher stiffness just like observed for other species.
6.2
Harvest Sites
5. What growth factors, such as soil type, precipitation, and sunlight, at the harvest
sites, are determining why the mechanical and physical properties of the sites differ
from each other?
From the 320 planks tested with four point bending it is not obvious what conditions pro-
vide better growth and properties. The results from density, bending strength, and stiff-
ness generally point in the same direction but for each of the three different parameters
are used to estimate properties. The theory can be used as an indicator but cannot be
directly linked to the properties. Along with the properties, Bennuah [7] also found that
provenance can influence the results quite much and this has not been taken into ac-
count here. This means that site conditions in itself cannot solely be used to predict the
properties of Grandis.
6. Which one(s) of the harvest sites can be considered more suitable for the harvest
of Grandis for construction?
Sites F1013, F1261, and L111 generally perform better as they have the best properties
compared to the average of all sites. Site F1261 has a smaller range of results meaning
the site produces trees with similar densities and bending strengths. This causes a smaller
standard deviation and makes the site more predictable. The predictability makes site
F1261 a more favorable choice. The option of changing the forestry strategy to obtain a
more uniform species is discussed in the future work in chapter 7.
6.3
Grading
7. Can INSTA 142 be used for the visual grading of Grandis?
No, because the visual grading of Grandis with INSTA 142 [3] is not conservative. The
results show that 40% of the time the visual grading with INSTA 142 [3] overestimates
the stiffness. visual grading is usually a conservative option for grading wood as it has

==PAGE:99==
to have a buffer for people’s subjectivity when using the rules. Further discussion on the
topic of visual grading is found in the future work in chapter 7.
8. How does visual grading with INSTA 142 compare to mechanical grading and the
actual mechanical properties of Grandis?
Besides estimating 40% of the planks too high the visual grading with INSTA 142 [3]
estimates the exact grade correctly 29% of the time. For comparison, the machine grader
only overestimates the planks 20% of the time and estimates correctly 40% of the time
even though it is testing the whole plank compared to the visual grading only being for the
tested mid-section.
Hautamäki et al. [43] get much better results when using visual grading on the intended
species. They can even obtain correct estimations approximately 80% of the time when
the classes are limited to fewer options. Neither machine grading nor visual grading with
INSTA 142 [3] appears to be good options as they give wrong estimations and do not
come close to the same predictability seen by Hautamäki et al. [43] for Scots pine and
Norway spruce. The addition of safety factors and other changes to look into can be found
in chapter 7 as well.
Since the existing grading methods cannot be used directly it is difficult to implement
Grandis in the construction market. It might also cause confusion if other methods are
used for grading Grandis as the users will have more plates in the air to juggle with and
this can cause confusion and mistakes in the production.
6.4
Gluing
9. What is the potential for gluing finger joints in Grandis with adhesives approved for
Norway spruce?
Grandis can be glued but in the finger joint configuration, the wood is too prone to split-
ting. The standards do not say whether or not splitting is allowed. If the splitting is to
be avoided the strength of the planks does not become high enough. A higher pressure
during production is needed for producing sufficient strength but it causes splitting. The
adhesives work as intended but the species is too weak and brittle. The brittle behavior
is also disturbing. As it causes breakage without much warning and in general can make
it difficult to make connections. When making buildings especially larger ones a more
ductile failure with a longer warning time allows time for people to escape and is therefore
desirable.
10. How does Douglas respond to different adhesives?
Douglas responds quite similarly and quite well to the three tested types of adhesives.
Although the pressure during production was too low the results are still acceptable. To
stretch the obtained knowledge to almost being too far-fetched: Douglas appears to glue
quite well and if other adhesive types that have not yet been approved for Douglas would
work well with Douglas as well it is not surprising. This should of cause be tested with
the individual glue as alkali content and the amount of resin in the wood can have an
influence.
The results from Douglas confirm that the glues tested have been used correctly for Gran-
dis as well. Getting different glues tested and approved for more species can help extend
the construction marked for glued products and in time maybe limit material shortage as
there will be more species to choose from.

7
Future Works
7.1
Finding the best usage for Grandis
Based on the found results, the general properties of Grandis are known. Based on this
the best usage of Grandis can be discussed. The conclusion is that Grandis is too un-
predictable and maybe even too weak to be used as structural timber, especially in glued
structures. The general brittleness of Grandis will also make it difficult to use it in con-
nections. The behavior of Grandis in connections has not been investigated and can be
considered one of the important investigations to be made if Grandis is to enter the con-
struction market. In general, materials that give some kind of warning before complete
failure is desired and Grandis would possibly not be able to do that.
The future usage of Grandis should take into consideration that Grandis is strong parallel
to the fiber direction and weak perpendicular to the fiber direction. A suggestion is using
Grandis for boards but this is already one of its uses and does not bring the material new
value to the construction market [6]. Therefore, the options on how to use Grandis in
glued structures are suggested in the following chapter.
7.2
Gluing
The observation is that the adhesives work well with Grandis but the high pressure needed
causes splitting. The standards do not say whether or not splitting is allowed. If Grandis
is glued sufficiently in a finger joint, and due to this experience some splitting then using it
in e.g. a glulam beam might still be an option. The adhesive applied between the layers
can fill out the gaps from the splitting and make it stronger again. It can also be discussed
how much strength the finger-jointed planks actually lose from the splitting. From these
experiences the splitting does not appear to be a problem and the finger joints might
actually be stronger because they got more pressure.
Having initial splitting can make it difficult to keep an eye on the splitting by visual inspec-
tions of the building afterward e.g. the cracks could be from wetting/drying and be more
severe or it could just be the initial splitting from when pressure was applied.
Specifically with Grandis, the configuration and size of the fingers could have an influence
since the annual rings are so large. If the goal is to make finger joints with Grandis the
configuration could be investigated. Even though Patil and Hanamapure [48] and Anshari
et al.[49] find optimal configurations a different one could be true for Grandis.
An option is also to use Grandis in a glued material with mixed species. An investiga-
tion of what it would do to the strength of a CLT element where the center is made of
weaker wood such as Grandis can be investigated. This way some of the less fortunate
behaviors of Grandis can be helped by other materials and become less prominent. When
mixing species the considerations mentioned by Vick [46] and Yusoh et al. [51] should be
taken into account. They focus on choosing species with similar shrinking and swelling
properties as well as alkali content to get good results.
7.3
Adaptation of Sites
Provenances with the best growth potential can be chosen after six growing seasons [7],
so since weather or soil does not have a large impact on the results, then the provenance
could be used to find the best trees. When a good provenance of the species is found

87 of 112
==PAGE:102==
the best sites can potentially be made even better by adopting the best possible forestry
up-keeping. As shown in the theory if an area is trimmed down at one point in time it
leaves only the best trees and the future trees will become even better as they descend
from the selected optimal provenance. This would make Grandis perform better and more
consistently in the future construction market.
7.4
Connection between Parameters
The connection between parameters has only been investigated with two parameters
compared. Here the conclusion is that density for example is not the only parameter
governing the strength. An investigation taking several parameters into account at the
same time is interesting because it has the potential to provide a clearer understanding of
the influence of knot size, the size of annual rings, the proportion of early wood, etc. This
is an area of investigation for the future.
7.5
Grading
Visual grading is used by smaller factories as it requires less technical equipment. From
here the investigations could either go into how INSTA 142 [3] can be used to become
conservative enough or find another visual grading standard. The problem with inventing
something new is that the construction market is not familiar with it and whatever method
they use should be suitable for all the species they use to avoid confusion during produc-
tion. Other visual standards are an option as they already exist and are based on previous
knowledge. Still it is difficult to be allowed to use these standards in Denmark as they are
not approved for such in DS/EN 1912 [13].
INSTA 142 [3] have been changed since its invention and changes have also been more
political than scientific [41]. This means that the rules in it no longer are directly connected
to the properties of wood. This also makes it challenging to apply the rules to other species
such as Grandis. A suggestion is to look into if small changes can be made to include
Grandis in the grading. Changing the rules so they fit each individual species is not optimal
for the user of INSTA 142 [3] but if one change can make the rules work for Grandis it
might be worth it to implement specifically for the species.
Moving all planks to a class lower than estimated with INSTA 142 [3] still gives 20% planks
that have been overestimated. This means that the result is still not conservative even
when lowering all the grades. It is easier to estimate a change to get more safety for the
machine grader as a safety factor can be added. If the goal is a maximum of 5% over-
estimations to follow the general calculation rules of a 5% percentile commonly used for
wood, then a safety factor of 1.26 can be added. Meaning that the machine grading results
can be divided by 1.26 to obtain values safe to use.
