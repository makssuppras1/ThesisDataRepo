Abstract 
New antimicrobial agents to counteract the increasing emergence of antibiotic resistant 
strains have been in the limelight of drug research. There is a need for innovative ways 
aimed at designing novel antimicrobial compounds and at the same time efficient methods 
of drug targeting. There are a variety of potentially suitable drugs; however, they fail to 
reach their target due to impermeability issues or due to inefficient target selectivity and 
can have other side effects on the host cells. 
 
This master’s thesis aims at addressing the above-mentioned problem by aiming at ena-
bling the antibiotic to cross the plasma membrane and enter the cell via the bacterial trans-
porter, POT. Our approach towards solving this ‘entry problem’ is to bind a peptide group 
to the antibiotic such that the transporter of the pathogen perceives it as a nutrient substrate 
and accumulates it within. We have named this peptide-linked antibiotic as a ‘trobiotic’. 
This method can be used to tailor existing antibiotics, which have been deemed useless due 
to their inability to cross the plasma membrane of a cell.  
 
In particular, this study aims at determining the substrate specificity of the POT in N. men-
ingitidis. The bacterial gene NMB2136 is among the major genes, which get up regulated 
during the infection process and can most likely be a drug target. β-Ala-Lys-AMCA was 
the reporter substrate of choice used in characterizing NMB. A variety of dipeptides and 
tripeptides were tested to replace against the reporter substrate on NMB. The inhibition 
profile of these peptides suggested that the active site of NMB is not well equipped to deal 
with natural bulky positively charged dipeptides at its C-terminal.

1. Aim and Objectives 
The aim of our study was to investigate the substrate specificity of the endogenous nutrient 
transporters of pathogens. Proton coupled oligopeptide transporters (POTs) are expressed 
in a number of pathogenic bacteria. Due to broad substrate specificity, these transporters 
could facilitate active uptake of compounds which we have named Trobiotics (Trojan anti-
biotics) i.e. antibiotic compounds conjugated to a carrier peptide that mimics a natural sub-
strate of the transporter.  
 
The objectives of this project were: 
 
1) Over-expression of POTs from various pathogenic bacteria, focusing particularly on 
Neisseria meningitidis 
 
2) Substrate profiling to establish the substrate specificity of POTs

2. Background and Hypothesis 
Rapid emergence of antibiotic resistant microbes and limited discovery of new antibiotics 
have become a major concern in fighting against infectious diseases. A large number of 
microbes are becoming unresponsive to conventional antibiotics, and the need for develop-
ing new antibiotics is crucial [1]. However, the development of antibiotics is a difficult feat 
to achieve, and the rate of new antibiotic drug is not at par with the emerging resistance 
among pathogens [2]. Therefore, as a resourceful human we constantly need innovative 
ways to recycle our existing stock of antibiotics and look for intelligent ways of drug tar-
geting.  
 
The main priority in drug delivery is to identify suitable targets of actions, however more 
than often it transpires that the drugs are potentially suitable, but unable to reach and block 
the target [3]. Various factors can pose challenge to drug targeting, such as improper target 
selection and so on. In order to reach the site of action, every drug has to cross various bio-
logical barriers, and these obstructions can either inactivate or block the drug [4].  
 
Our focus was towards enabling the antibiotic to cross the plasma membrane of the gram-
negative bacteria; Neisseria meningitidis and enter the microbe. In order to do that, we 
needed to characterize the N. meningitidis POT. Therefore, in this study our objective was 
to determine the substrate specificity of N. meningitidis POT so as to ensure the suitable di 
or tripeptides, which could act as a decent substrate in both binding with the active site of 
POT, and ultimately linking with the antibiotic. We will not be dealing with the latter part 
in this study i.e., the linking of the antibiotic with the peptide scaffold.  
 
Gram negative bacteria are especially more tricky in drug targeting due to the presence of a 
second outer membrane made up of phospholipid bilayer and lipopolysaccharide (LPS) 
which surrounds the inner thin peptidoglycan layer. This outer layer provides an additional 
protection for the cell when compared to gram-positive bacteria. This layer is also believed 
to play a major role in restricting hydrophobic molecules such as antibiotics into the cell 
allowing them to enter the cells only via selective porins. Thus, in turn limiting a wide 
range of antibiotics from entering the cell. [2]

An alternative approach towards tackling this problem could be the possible use of the mi-
crobe’s own transport system, so as to help the antibiotics to permeate the pathogenic 
membrane. Our hypothesis utilizes the fact that microbes express transport proteins in their 
plasma membranes, which allows transfer of molecules in the cell. The ‘Trobiotics’ pro-
cess requires creating certain modifications within the scaffold, such as attaching a peptide 
group to the antibiotic so as to convert it to a substrate, or ‘Trobiotic’. This trobiotic will be 
perceived by the pathogen as a natural substrate and hence will be easily assimilated within 
(Fig. 1). This process can be used to customize both, an existing antibiotic or a new antibi-
otic (Fig. 2).  It should also be noted that these transporters or POTs in our study, occur 
widely in nature and also have broad substrate specificity. Thus making this method appli-
cable on a variety of pathogens at the same time a variety of peptide analogues can be used 
to smuggle the antibiotics within. This method will pave way for several potentially cyto-
toxic compounds to hit their desired target which was previously inaccessible.

3. Introduction  
Membrane transporters are membrane proteins involved in the movement of ions, small 
molecules, or macromolecules, across a biological membrane [9]. Transport proteins are 
membrane-spanning proteins that assist in the movement of substances by facilitated diffu-
sion or active transport. This mode of transport is also known as carrier mediated transport 
[10]. 
3.1. Peptide transporters 
 
Peptide transport across membrane is one of the most important transmembrane activities, 
since peptides can be a major source to satisfy carbon or nitrogenous requirement in many 
living systems, including humans [11]. Peptide transporters occur universally in nature and 
their structure and function remain comparable among various life forms [6]. In bacteria, 
the peptide transport system is involved in reusing cell wall peptides released from pepti-
doglycans during cell growth and can also be heavily involved in chemotaxis and sporula-
tion processes [11]. In animals and humans, peptide transport system found in the intes-
tines is responsible for digestion and absorption of dietary proteins and peptides [11].  
Additionally, the human peptide transporter system plays an essential role in oral absorp-
tions of various drugs such as β-lactam antibiotics, angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) 
inhibitors, renin inhibitors, anti-tumor drugs and a variety of peptide-mimetics [11] [12]. 
Peptide transporters are of major scientific interest due to the pivotal role-played by them 
in mediating drug transfer across membranes [13]. They represent excellent drug targets 
for drug delivery because they have a wide substrate-binding site, which is capable of ac-
commodating a wide range of molecules [14]. 
The peptide transporter function can be divided into two major categories of transporters 
namely; primary transporters and secondary transporters (Fig. 4). The primary transporter 
harnesses energy for active transport directly from ATP hydrolysis whereas the secondary 
transporters use the electrochemical gradient as a driving force for substrate uptake [15].

3.1.1. ABC superfamily 
 
ATP binding cassette (ABC) transporter superfamily is a primitive protein family found 
across all living species [16]. ABC transporters are highly efficient translocation systems 
and they utilize the hydrolysis of ATP to direct the movement of substrates across biologi-
cal membranes [17]. ABC transporters can be further split into three categories; the first 
two categories are involved in transportation; ABC exporters and ABC importers [18]. 
ABC exporters are found in almost all living systems and help in the translocation of com-
pounds outside the cytoplasm of a cell [19] [20]. ABC importers are found in prokaryotes 
and plants, and assist in the movement of nutrients into the cell [21]. The third category is 
involved in DNA repair and mRNA translation [18] [22].  
 
ABC transporters are also involved in intake of peptides in prokaryotes. Peptides are trans-
located by ABC permeases (Dpp) and oligopeptide permeases (Opp) [18]. In addition to 
nutritional uptake, these transporters have also been linked to signaling and virulence in 
microbes. Dpps can accommodate peptides of upto 2 or 3 amino acids in length and the 
Opps can contain up to 5 amino acids length peptides [18].

3.1.2. Major facilitator superfamily (MFS) 
 
The MFS is among the largest group of secondary active transporters that have been con-
served from bacteria to humans. They are ubiquitous and are responsible for selectively 
transporting a wide spectrum of substrates such as; ions, carbohydrates, lipids, peptides etc. 
across biological membranes [16]. They are accountable for various important physiologi-
cal processes in living organisms. On the basis of phylogenetic analysis, substrate speci-
ficity and working mechanism, the MFS superfamily has been divided into 76 subfamilies 
in the Transporter classification database. They have been a major focus of research due to 
their crucial role in physiological processes. However, only 7 MFS proteins from six sub-
families have been structurally explained [23] (Fig. 5).  
 
The majority of MFS members consist of 12 core transmembrane helices (TMs). Proteins 
from the 6 subfamilies show low sequence similarity but they all share a common structur-
al fold - the MFS fold. An MFS fold consists of 12 TMs, which contains 2 folded domains: 
the N and C domains. Each of these domains is composed of 6 consecutive TMs. The N 
and C domain show a twofold pseudo symmetry that is related by an axis perpendicular to 
the membrane plane. The conserved sequences held in between the cytoplasmic TMs may 
be responsible for the major function played by the MFS transporters [23].

Various important families are a part of the MFS superfamily, however we are mentioning 
only OPTs in this study apart from POTs because these are the only 2 types of peptide 
transporters found in our organism of study i.e., N. meningitidis. 
3.1.2.1 Oligopeptide transporters (OPTs) 
 
The OPT family of peptide transporters and iron siderophores transporters are found in 
both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. However, their distribution is more widespread in pro-
karyotes with members in eukaryotes found only in fungi and plants [24]. OPTs play a 
major role in nitrogen storage, quorum sensing, and sexual induction in organisms [24]. 
Members of this family uptake substrates using a cation-solute symport mechanism. OPTs 
help in transfer of specific size of amino acids usually ranging from 3 to 8 amino acids 
[24]. OPT family proteins has not been well characterized yet, however it has been found 
that they have 16 or 17 transmembrane spanning α helical segments (TMs) [24]. 
3.1.2.2 Proton-coupled oligopeptide transporters (POTs/PTRs) 
 
In this study, we will be focusing specifically on proton-coupled oligopeptide transporter 
(POTs). POTs belong to the MFS family, are highly conserved and can be ubiquitously 
found in a wide range of organisms such as bacteria, yeasts, plants and higher animals [11]. 
Members of POT family are driven by proton motive force (PMF), which in turn is gener-
ated by different mechanisms such as proton pumps coupled to electron transport chain in 
bacteria and Na+-H+ exchange channel in animals [14].

Working Mechanism of POTs  
 
Translocation of substrate by POTs involves three major structural changes; outward fac-
ing, occluded and inward facing conformation [13]. In the outward facing conformation the 
active site is made available to the binding of substrate and a proton. When the substrate 
and proton binding occurs, the transporter changes to the occluded form, during which 
both sides of the active site close down [25]. After which, the transporter goes into the in-
ward facing conformation and releases the substrate and proton into the cytoplasm [26] 
[27].  The conformational changes in transporters are the molecular origin for energy cou-
pling between transportation of substrates. This process is known as the alternating access 
cycle (Fig. 5) [28].

GkPOT). The PDB code for each structure is written in parenthesis. Taken from S. Newstead, 2014 
[29].  
Structural characteristic of POT 
 
POTs are secondary active transporters, which belong to the major facilitator superfamily 
(MFS), and therefore share similar structural characteristics.  They have a size of approxi-
mately 400-750 amino acids residues [16]. As can be observed from Fig. 8, they contain a

number of conserved sequence motifs and a conserved framework of 12-14 transmembrane 
α helices, which are arranged into N (H1-H6) and C terminal (H7-H12) bundles. The two

The peptide-binding site located at the apex of a long hydrophilic cavity, which open out-

3.2. Mammalian POTs  
 
PEPT1 and PEPT2 were the first mammalian transporters, which were identified. In hu-
mans, PEPT1 has 708 amino acids and PEPT2 has 729 amino acid residues, they are found 
in the small intestines, mammary glands, lungs and kidneys. They use the proton gradient 
as their driving force [14]. In this process, the substrate uptake depends entirely on the ex-
tracellular pH and membrane potential; usually a pH of 4.5-6.5 is considered apt for uptake 
[14]. Dipeptides and tripeptides taken in by the transporters are hydrolyzed by the cell pep-
tidases and are either utilized by the cell or released. Mammalian transporters are also in-
strumental in transporting a variety of peptidomimetics compounds, such as β-lactam anti-
biotics, peptidases and protease inhibitors, into the cells [14].  
 
However, it should be noted that most of the potent drugs candidates fail to reach their 
target site in significant amount. Therefore, bioavailability is an important aspect in design-
ing appropriate drug candidates. The promiscuity of POTs towards peptides could be ex-
ploited in delivering appropriate amount of drugs to cells [31]. The structural requirements 
which needs to be considered while designing drugs which are possible substrates to POTs 
should include: a) a natural L,L-dipeptide skeleton b) the N-terminal should have a free 
amino group c) uncharged hydrophobic side chains. Various oral drugs such as antivirals, 
anti-cancer are being designed in the given fashion for greater effect [32].

3.3. Bacterial POTs 
 
In this project we initially started working with four bacterial transporters: Neisseria men-
ingitidis (NMB2136), Neisseria gonorrhoeae (NGO195), Listeria monocytogenes 
(LMOf2365) and Enterococcus faecalis (EF044). However, after completing the first few 
experiments concerning finding the appropriate substrate for our POTs, we settled on N. 
meningitidis for our further research and the reporter substrate of choice was β-Ala-
Lys(AMCA).   
 
The Neisseria genus is a large genus of bacteria. They are gram-negative proteobacteria. 
Out of 11 species that colonize humans, only 2 are pathogenic: N. meningitidis, which 
causes bacterial meningitis and meningococcal septicemia, and N. gonorrhoeae, which 
causes the sexually transmitted gonorrhea [33] [34] . Diseases caused by these two patho-
gens are a serious health concern throughout the world (Fig. 9). The development of an 
effective vaccine has not been very successful due to the variability and poor immunogen-
icity of their outer cell components. Considering the nature of being strictly human patho-
gens, these organisms are very well adjusted to the human environment [35]. As a result 
strains resistant to antibiotic treatments are on the rise and novel methods need to be de-
vised in order to deal with these resistant strains.  
 
 
Fig. 9: The history of N. gonorrhoeae antimicrobial resistance. Taken from Goire et al, 2014 [36]. 
 
N. meningitidis is a strict human pathogen and has no successful animal model till date. 
Additionally, it is also among the very rare bacteria that can cross the blood brain barrier 
==PAGE:20==
 
    
 
 20
(BBB) and establish an infection in the cerebrospinal meninges. The ability of this organ-
ism to surpass the most well imposed area of the human body makes it an interesting or-
ganism to study [37].  
 
 N. meningitidis expresses only two genes that encodes for peptide transporters namely; 
NMB 2136 which is a member of the POT family and NMB 0060 which belongs to the 
OPT family [38]. Before mentioning the importance of these two proteins, we will deviate 
from the topic a little and mention that N. meningitidis escapes the polymorphonuclear 
neutrophil leucocytes (PMNs) of the human immune system by uptaking L-glutamate and 
converting it to glutathione which in turn protects it from oxygen radicals produced by 
neutrophils [37]. Reverting back to the topic, it should be noted that the presence of just 
two peptide transporters in this pathogen makes it further more interesting to study, be-
cause the preference of certain peptides by any of these transporters increases our chances 
of drug targeting. Hence suggesting that, the choice of our study NMB 2136 can be a pos-
sible drug target.  
 
N. meningitidis has all the necessary pathways for synthesizing amino acids and most spe-
cies are capable of growth in an amino acid free medium, which explain the presence of 
just two genes encoding for peptide transporters [39] [40]. In comparison E. coli has sever-
al different kind of transporters for peptide delivery. However, it should be noted that dur-
ing infection N. meningitidis encounters a harsh environment within the human host and 
has to adapt accordingly and these peptide transporters definitely play a major role in this 
process.  
 
Currently, little is known about the host-pathogen interaction and the establishment of an 
infection. However, transcriptome analysis of N. meningitidis show that various genes are 
up or down regulated when the organism is within a host and NMB2136 was one of the 
most important up-regulated genes during the infection process. Thus proving that it is 
involved in some important mechanism helping in establishing an infection or ensuring the 
survival of the organism within the host [41]. This behavior of the organism further ce-
ments our belief in the fact that NMB should be explored as a drug target in itself and not 
just a drug delivery medium.

3. Materials and Methods 
The competent E. coli cells were transformed with the plasmids containing the gene for the 
desired POTs. The transformed organisms were grown in LB broth cultures and whole cell 
assays were performed on transformed cells harvested from these cultures. 
 
 
 
Fig 10. : The experimental strategy 
3.1 Transformation  
 
Transformation of competent cell with the desired plasmids pTTQ18-( NMB2136, 
NGO1954, LMOf2365, EF0440, YdgR) or empty pTTQ18 was performed. 1 µl of intact 
plasmid was added to 50 µl to competent BL21(DE3)pLysS cells and allowed to stay on 
ice for 30 minutes. This treatment provided enough time for the plasmid to adhere to the 
cell wall. This mixture was then subjected to a heat shock at 42°C for 45 minutes, which 
altered the physical state of the cell walls enabling the uptake of plasmid into the cell. The-
se cells were placed back into ice for recovery. 200 µl of SOC media was added to the cells 
and incubated at 37°C for 1 hour. 50 µl of this cell suspension was spread over culture 
==PAGE:22==
 
    
 
 22
plates containing 20 ml of LB-Agar media containing antibiotics ampicillin and chloram-
phenicol at a final concentration of 100 µg/ml and 34 µg/ml respectively. The resistance to 
ampicillin was attained from the plasmid and chloramphenicol resistance was the charac-
teristic feature of BL21(DE3)pLysS cells. These plates were allowed to grow overnight at 
37°C.  Colony formation on plates validated the process of transformation. 
3.2. Protein expression 
 
A single isolated colony of BL21(DE3)pLysS cells containing plasmids pTTQ18-
(NMB2136, NGO1954, LMOf2365, EF0440, YdgR) or empty pTTQ18 vector was care-
fully transferred into 3 ml LB media containing ampicillin and chloramphenicol at a final 
concentration of 100 µg/ml and 34 µg/ml respectively. The inoculated LB media was al-
lowed to grow overnight at 37°C with shaking at 250 rpm.  
 
Expression culture containing 10 ml LB media with antibiotics as mentioned above was 
inoculated with overnight culture at a dilution of 1:50. These expression cultures were al-
lowed to grow at 37°C at 250 rpm until it reached an OD600 of 0.6-0.8. 1 mM isopropyl β-

4.2 Competition assays using β-Ala-Lys(AMCA)  
 
The second step in the study was to determine if β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) was the appropriate 
substrate of choice for our POTs. β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) is a fluorescent substrate. The princi-
ple behind this assay is to measure the fluorescence of a fluorescent substrate taken up by 
the cell through the transporter. 
 
A competition assay using a ligand and β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) as a reporter substrate was 
conducted, where the (AMCA) was the fluorescent moiety attached to the di-peptide side 
chain β-Ala-Lys. The cellular uptake of this di-peptide corresponds to the fluorescence 
intensity of AMCA inside the cell. The fluorescence intensity varies according to the con-
centration of ligand and its affinity with the transporter (Fig. 14). 
 
==PAGE:30==
 
    
 
 30
 
Fig. 14: Pictorial representation of the principle behind competition assay. The figures shows the lig-
and competing and blocking out β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) from entering the cell.  
 
 
It should be noted that a possible downside of these assays is that, the amount of ligand 
taken by the cell is not being measured here, rather the amount of  β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) not 
entering the cell. Therefore the ligand being studied might not be a substrate at all and just 
an inhibitor blocking the entry of β-Ala-Lys(AMCA).  
 
Competition assays were performed at different time intervals; 90 sec (Fig. 15), 5 minutes 
(Fig. 16) and 25 minutes (Fig. 17) respectively to check the variance in uptake levels at 
different time intervals.    
 
EV was kept as a control so as to discount the background noise.  Ala-Ala was kept as a 
competitor ligand so as to compete against β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) to enter the cell. The setup 
with the competitor was named minima. The setup containing just β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) was 
subsequently named maxima. The maxima value alone pointed out if β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) 
was a suitable reporter  substrate for the POT being studied in this project. The difference 
between minima and maxima showed whether the ligand was a good competitor against 
the reporter substrate.  The concentration of ligand used was 50 mM and β-Ala-
Lys(AMCA) was 0.5mM.

However, it could be possible that positive charge alone might not be the issue behind the 
bad substrate specificity of Ala-Lys. A probable reason could also be the combined effect 
of the bulky group along with a positive charge on the C-terminus. If this might be the 
case, then Ala-Arg might give a similar IC50 value as Ala-Lys however we still would not 
be able to say for sure if its the positive charge alone or the combined bulky positive 
charge effect. Ala-His might not be a promising substrate enough to provide answers due 
to the presence of the imidazole ring which might be balancing the positive charge effect.  
 
It should be noted that despite low IC50 values of Ala-Phe (0.2 mM), with Phenylalanine 
being a bulky group at C-terminal, we cannot inevitably assume that C-terminal is capable 
enough of handling bulky side chains because Lys is not entirely comparable to Phenylala-
nine in terms of bulk. Also the presence of a benzene ring in phenylalanine might be an 
important factor in binding at the C terminal and the long side chain of lysine might just 
not be getting accommodated due to its sheer length.    
 
Therefore, in order to come with a conclusive answer, a good idea would be to test the in-
hibition profile of a synthetic peptide, which has a positive charge without the bulky effect 
of lysine or arginine. If the IC50 value continued to be high, we can say for sure that the 
positive charge alone is somehow affecting the C terminus. However, if the IC50 appears 
slightly high but not as high as previously observed (>15mM), we can say that it’s the 
combined effect of the bulky side chain with a positive charge behind the high inhibition 
value of Ala-Lys. However, due to lack of time and also because of the fact that all our 
experiments were performed on live cells, it became a little difficult to accomplish more 
ambitious plans.  
 
Nonetheless, based on our current findings we can summarize that NMB POT is specific 
both to the dipeptides and tripeptides. It is capable enough to accommodate bulky dipep-
tides through its active site as in the case of Ala-Phe (IC50 of 0.2 mM) and Phe-Ala (IC50 of 
0.3 mM) and also Trp-Ala (IC50 of 0.2 mM). Additionally, the active site can also manage 
negative charge at both its N terminal and C terminal. However, the scenario shifts when a 
positive charge at the C-terminal comes into account, as in case of AAK, AK (IC50 of  >15 
mM), WK, YK and MK.  It appears that while N-terminal definitely has some underlying 
mechanism such as salt bridge formation or the presence of a negative charge balancing the 
==PAGE:45==
 
    
 
 45
effect of the positively charged lysine, the C-terminal seems to lack this mechanism and 
fails to accommodate the positive charge of lysine thus rendering the substrate inefficient.

5. Conclusion 
Our first major task was to screen for the adequate POT which utilized β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) 
as an efficient reporter substrate. After a brief screening process, we selected NMB as the 
choice of our study. The next step was to determine the appropriate pH needed to ensure 
the proper work environment for NMB. A series of accumulation profiles performed con-
firmed that just like the previously studied prototypical POTs, NMB works best at a pH of 
6.5. At this point we also conducted a saturation curve of β-Ala-Lys(AMCA) for NMB. 
We compared almost all of the data generated against the prototypical YdgR, which had a 
very similar inhibition profile compared to NMB.  
 
The next stage of the study was to determine the ligand specificity of the POT. We initially 
performed a competition assay for mono-, di-, tri-, and tetra alanyl peptides against β-Ala-
Lys(AMCA) so as to determine the suitable peptide length which could be accommodated 
by the active site of the transporter. Just like YdgR,  NMB could accommodate both di and 
tri-peptides.  
 
After performing a series of competition assays on various peptides. We performed a series 
of inhibition profiles for a selected few peptides so as to understand a little more in detail 
the effect of each peptide on NMB.  The results presented can be concluded in a table be-
low when compared to the non-polar aliphatic dipeptide Ala-Ala.

The main conclusion that we can draw from the results displayed in this study if that de-

comes to dealing with a positive charge on the C terminus. This behavior makes it rather 
unique compared to all the POTs that have been studied so far. This selective preference of

gen testing will also be required to affirm the POT behavior in its parent pathogen.    
 
Anomalies found in the newly characterized POTs show that there is still a lot left to ex-
plore and understand about this category of secondary peptide transporters and a more in-
depth research on NMB could perhaps prove that it can be a major drug target in itself and 
not just a drug delivery target.

6. Future Work 
 
Fig. 27: Figurative representation of the part of master thesis in the broad research project.   
 
 
The future work would first include performing a few more experiments to determine the 
inhibition profiles of NMB for a few dipeptides that could not be performed during this 
study.  
 
The next step would be performing characterization studies on the transporter using vari-
ous mutants of NMB in order to understand in more details the various mechanisms in-
volved in transportation. Developing a homology model at this stage would help us under-
stand the composition of the active site especially the C-terminus.  
 
Considering the fact that NMB is very much similar to YdgR in most respects, it would be 
ideal to compare it with the YdgR structure. At a very superficial level, and just based on 
theoretical knowledge the NMB appears very similar to the Peptso POT, considering the 
==PAGE:49==
 
    
 
 49
fact that the crystal structure of YdgR has not been determined yet, it might be a good idea 
to understand the structure of NMB from a Peptso perspective.  
 
More ambitious later plans include, synthesizing a model compound that could be used as 
a substitute to antibiotic initially, to test the CM-conjugate for its size with respect to the 
active site of the transporter. Eventually we could develop trobiotics and test them for 
compatibility with NMB POT, and then possibly carry on to in vivo testing on the pathogen 
(Fig. 27).  
 
It is needless to say that the project is still in its infant stage and a number of tests need to 
be performed in order to establish the POT from N. meningitidis a drug target or a carrier 
mediated delivery system for antibiotics.
