Abstract
This thesis explores the characteristics of methanol blends consisting of a range of ignition
improvers, including FAME bio-diesel, Beraid3555m, Glycerol and DME, emphasizing on
their ignition quality and combustion behavior. The investigation utilizes an ignition qual-
ity tester (IQT) device in order to obtain ignition delays (IDs) and estimated cetane numbers
(ECNs) to assess the the blends, drawing comparison to the CIMAC ECN recommendations
for 4-stroke and 2-stroke CI applications.
Initial testing with FAME reveals challenges in relation to the IQT setup, highlighting the
issue of friction and wear in the solenoid pump. A new pump concept is introduced, however,
its full implementation and testing remains pending. Methanol/FAME and Methanol/Beraid
blends demonstrate successful ignition along with HRR analysis indicating a inherent differ-
ence in combustion between the two blends. Methanol/Glycerol and Methanol/DME showed
no sign of ignition - leaving room for further exploration of the DME blend.
While results suggest promising application performance for the fuel blends, conclusive in-
sights demand real engine testing in 4-stroke and 2-stroke CI applications at full load ranges
to ascertain autoignition quality for each blend comprehensively.

Introduction
1
Introduction
In the fight against climate change, it is crucial to reduce CO2 emissions related to energy
use and production. Transitioning from fossil-based fuels to green zero-carbon fuels is a
great step in the right direction in this fight. Looking specifically at the shipping industry,
utilizing large scale internal combustion engines for powering vessels, it is estimated that the
shipping industry alone is responsible for 2-3% of the global CO2 emissions. This is however
recognized by the industry and regulations have been developed and adopted by organiza-
tions (i.e., the International Maritime Organization), resulting in over a decade long focus
on reducing greenhouse gasses (GHG) - such as CO2. Hence, motivating the need of transi-
tion from fossil fuels to green fuels. Even with the shipping industry’s focus on sustainability
and GHG reductions, residual fuels such as heavy fuel oil (HFO) are still predominant today.
Alternative fuels, such as methanol (CH3OH), can be produced as a carbon neutral fuel on
a well-to-wake (WtW) basis. Either as Bio-methanol derived from biomass feedstocks or
E-methanol derived from green hydrogen and captured CO2 [21]. The Danish shipping and
logistics company Maersk have continued to commit themselves to use methanol by ordering
an additional 6 dual-fuel methanol capable mid-size container vessel in June this year (2023)
expanding their orderbook to 25 methanol able vessels total. This results in the global or-
derbook standing at approximately 100 methanol enabled vessels [12]. Further, they are, in
collaboration with MAN Energy Solutions, looking into the possibilities of retrofitting exist-
ing vessels for methanol dual-fuel operation [13]. Hence, a future commitment to methanol
as a main engine fuel, utilizing pilot fuel injection, has begun.
This is possible due to the often large physical size of the two-stroke main engines, allowing
for implementation of both a main- and pilot fuel injectors. A pilot flame is needed for the
ignition of methanol due to the fuels low CN [6] - therefore, pilot ignition using a fuel with a
higher CN is required. However, for some smaller 4-stroke diesel gensets (diesel generators),
it is not always possible to implement fuel injectors to run a pilot fuel injection setup due
to the limited space on the cylinder head. In such cases dual fuel injectors exist and can be
utilized. However, they can be costly and add more complexity.
One project by the name of the GreenPilot project have already hinted at the feasibility of
methanol engines - among these a CI engine - utilizing methanol and ignition improver fuel
blends [11]. However, a broader look at options for such blends are missing.

Theory
2.1
The cetane number and ignition delay
The cetane number (CN) is a measure of ignitability/ignition quality for diesel fuels. A
high cetane number corresponds to a shorter ignition delay (ID). Vice versa, a lower cetane
number corresponds to a longer ignition delay. Hence, the ID of CI engine fuels and CN
are inversely proportional. The general definition of ID is the time measured in milliseconds
between start of fuel injection to the onset of combustion. This is illustrated in figure 1 in
relation to an igntion quality tester (IQT). The IQT is presented in section 2.2.
Figure 1: Illustrative example of injector nozzle needle movement signal and the relative
pressure increase inside the CVCC of the IQT during a single fuel injection event resulting
in ignition. Here, the ID is shown as the time measured between start of injection and start
of the combustion onset. Source: ASTMD6890 −16ϵ2 standard [17].
2.1.1
Combustion and ignition delay duration
A fuel showing a longer ID can result in the accumulation of unburned fuel injected into
the combustion chamber and when combustion at some point occurs, it burns rapidly. Con-
sequently resulting in a steep relative pressure rise, creating undesirable forces, which can
potentially cause running irregularities, diesel knocking and in extreme cases, severe engine

2.1.2
CN, ECN and CIMAC recommendations
For diesel engines, operating on the CI principles, relaying on the ability of fuel to auto
ignite under compression, a higher cetane number fuel is considered ideal. The opposite of
the cetane number is the octane number, indicating a fuels ability to withstand compression
and auto-ignite in an engine (i,e., a spark ignition (SI) engine running on gasoline). The
relationship between octane an cetane is inversely related, but not directly. A fuel with CN
20 does not directly correspond to an octane number of 80 - assuming a scale from 0–100.
Both are measured on different scales and should be treated separately.
The CN is exclusively related to fuel testing utilizing a cooperative fuel research engine
(CFR), a single cylinder, 4-stroke, indirect diesel injection engine specifically designed with
the intent of testing and quantifying fuel ignitability. The ASTM D613 standard describes
the ’Standard Test Method for Cetane Number of Diesel Fuel Oil’ - utilizing the CFR en-
gine. However, running the CFR engine can be both timely and costly due to the need of
running multiple compression ratios and testing reference fuels along side the tested fuel of
interest, to determine ID and CN of the tested fuel as described by the standard. Hence, an
alternative to determining ID and CN - the IQT - exist.
Knowing the ignitability of a fuel in terms of CN is important for both designing engine
parameters as well as indicating performance for different types of engine applications. The
International Council on Combustion Engines (CIMAC) has made an illustration relating
4-stroke and 2-stroke engine load cases to the Estimated Cetane Number (ECN) - a variant
of the CN. Figure 2 shows the recommended reference ranges for the ECN. The ECN is
derived from fuel ID measurements and can be obtained by following the method described
in Annex F of the IP541 standard [7].
An important takeaway from the CIMAC recommendations is the necessary ECN of a fuel
to achieve normal operating conditions. As an example, normal operating conditions can

Introduction, method and setup
The IQT is a constant volume combustion chamber (CVCC) instrument intended for mea-
suring the ignition quality of diesel and alternative diesel fuels. The current IQT at DTU,
has been subject to numerous modifications. The most recent modifications were made by
R. Knaack and T. Dalsgaard in 2022, designing and implementing a pre-pump injection sys-
tem, helping smooth out inconsistencies in testing gaseous fuels among other modifications.

the system through the reservoir. Additionally, sealing oil levels in the solenoid pump must
be checked and topped off, if necessary, to ensure optimal pumping conditions. With the
added fuel sample, the system must be ’flushed’ (∼20 – 50 ml of sample was added when
conducting circulation). This is done by pressurizing the system with inert gas (∼15 – 20

Fuel sample testing After the circulation is finished, the system is depressurized and emp-
tied. An amount of the fuel sample is now added once more (now ∼50 - 100 ml, enough
so the pump won’t pump air into the high-pressure line between the solenoid pump and
needle fuel injector). This is the amount that will be available for injection into the CVCC.
Circulation is done once more. Afterwards, the fuel circulating circuit must be closed off
by closing the 2 way valve above the fuel injector, creating a high pressure line between
the needle fuel injector and check valve in front of the solenoid pump. Before testing can

The last thing to determine is how many shots a test series should include, and if pre-pumps
are necessary for reaching the fuel injector needle popping pressure required for injection.
Adjusting for pre-pumps is task that requires manual adjusting and varies from type of
fuel tested. The pre-pump feature in the LabVIEW data aqusistion software is created and
implemetned by R. W. Knaack and T. Dalsgaard during their thesis [4] - an in-depth expla-
nation of the pre-pump feature can be found there.
A complete test series for a fuel or fuel blend must consists of 15 preliminary shots and
32 primary shots according to standard. Meaning a minimum of 47 shot total for a series
must be concluded. The 15 preliminary shots are conducted to ’clean out’ any potential left
over fuel of fuel blend from a previous sample. In this thesis, all test presented are from
series consisting of 50 shots total. Hence, 35 primary shots. The parameters mentioned
are describe in the ASTMD6890 −16ϵ2 standard [17]. When a test series is concluded, raw
relative pressure-, fuel pressure- and needle signal- data is logged and saved in the .txt file
format using LabVIEW on a local test PC connected to the IQT setup.
Emptying system After ended testing of a fuel or fuel blend, generally, a decent amount
of still remain in the system. Hence, it must emptied. This is done effectively done by
pressing out the remaining fuel using the system pressure. It is worth noting that is nearly
impossible to ensure every drop of a fuel have left the system doing so. Hence motivating the
need flushing the system with samples and utilizing circulation to flush the system between
testing different fuel blends. Schematic representations of the fuel- circulation circuit, testing
circuit and emptying circuit can be found in appendix B.

Data processing
The raw data acquired from testing is processed using a MATLAB script developed by R.
Knaack and T. Dalsgaard named Solenoid_Data_Compare_Clean. An in-depth explanation
of the script can be found the appendix of their thesis [4]. A few definitions from the data
processing script is highlighted in the list below:
• Script definition of ID start The point in time in which the start of the ID is defined
is at the point in time in which the fuel pressure - measured in the high-pressure line
- is reaches and becomes equal to the popping pressure for the needle fuel injector. In
that exact moment the needle fuel injector moves, allowing for fuel to be injected into
the hot and pressurized CVCC. The popping pressure is defined as 120 bar.
• Script definition of ID end The point in time where the relative pressure reaches
1% of the maximum relative pressure. This is stated as the oﬀicial definition of ID end
point in time by annex F in the IP 541 standard [9].
• Script FFT filtering All shots of each series are filtered using fast fourier transforma-
tion (FFT) to minimize the noise picked up by the sensors (pressure and fuel) during
each shot. All tests have the same filtering parameters. The bandpass is applied for
in the frequencies range 0 – 1000 Hz effectively leaving out frequencies above a 1000
Hz in the pressure- and fuel-injection signals of a test series.

Methanol
Methanol (CH3OH) - also known as methyl alcohol and wood spirit - is a clear, colorless,
volatile liquid with a distinct alcohol odor. It is a simple alcohol that mixes well with many
organic solvents and is considered to be one of the most common chemicals produced glob-
ally. Presently, methanol is considered a multipurpose fuel and makes up part of several
established fuel blends like M85 - consisting of 85% methanol blended with 15% gasoline. In
China, methanol/gasoline blends in ratios ranging from M5 to M100 are widely used as road
transport fuel. Further, methanol is a required component for making FAME-type bio-diesel.
Hence, these methanol-based fuels are established alternatives to conventional gasoline for
SI engines (and methanol-derived bio-diesel for Diesel cycle type engines) in today’s market
[6]. Broadly speaking, three main reasons exist for why methanol is a promising alternative
fuel for marine engine applications: Lower emissions, easy handling, and climate neutrality
potential.

3.1.4
Methanol ignition quality improvement
Having established the status quo of methanol as a future fuel, it is important to note that
methanol has poor auto ignition properties due to having a low CN of 3 [6]. A current
solution for ignition and combustion of methanol is using it in combination with a pilot
fuel with greater auto ignition properties. However, if a pilot fuel could be blended directly
with methanol, promoting (improving) acceptable autoignition quality (depending on the
application) and be injected trough the same injector, it could be possible to run methanol
blends in engines that are physically restricted to operate a pilot fuel system. This is the
case for some smaller 4-stroke gen-sets, normally operating on the same fuel as the main
engine.
Dual fuel injector technology does exist [8] (a single injector capable of injecting sprays of
two different fuels separately) but utilizing these adds a greater level of complexity due to
their design - essentially having two injectors in one, meaning more moving parts and sealing
surfaces. Finding a suitable blend of methanol - improving ignitability - can prove helpful
in reducing both complexity and cost in relation to the fuel injection systems, but also in
simplifying fuel needs for main- and gen-set engines by utilizing blends. Please note the use
of wording ’promoter’ and ’improver’ are used interchangeably.

FAME
Fatty Acid Methyl Ester generally knows as FAME type bio-diesel or just FAME, is a liquid
fuel at ambient temperature, commonly produced from renewable feed stock such as animal
fats and vegetable oils. FAME is derived from triglycerides which are the main components
of fats and oils in plant and animals. This is done by a process called transesterfication
shown in figure 8. The most common feedstocks for producing FAME include canola oil, soy
bean oil, palm oil, rapeseed oil and used cooking oil. The reactants of the transesterfication
process is the triglycerides from a feed stock reacting with methanol. The products of the
reaction are FAME and glycerol. The two products are the separated. Glycerol tends to
easily separate from FAME due to its low solubility with FAME and higher density it settles.
After separation, FAME is washed with water and evaporated to remove residual glycerol
and lower water content. FAME is produced to comply with the ASTM-D6751 and EN14214
standard to ensure the fuel is safe for use in internal combustion engines.

Beraid 3555M is an ignition improver additive for alcohol based fuels produced by chemical

New pump concept
6
New pump concept
A new pump concept originally conceived by DTU Construct Research Assistant, Rune
Wessel Knaack is presented in this section. The concept of the new pump revolves around
the ability to change out at potentially worn out plunger and plunger housing set. The
wear issue reported on current pump can be seen in figure 20. The parts making up the
pump are manufactured in 304 stainless steel (5 parts) and aluminum (1 part). Specific
dimensions, materials and features can be found in the technical drawings in appendix D. A
short description of a few highlighted features for the new pump concept are listed bellow
and illustrated in figure 19.
Figure 19: Section view with highlighted features of the new pump concept.
• Interchangeable plunger piston and piston housing This feature is the heart
of the concepts, allowing for changing piston housing and plunger piston with stan-
dard components if/when wear occurs. The standard components originates from a
YANMAR diesel injector.
• Piston alignment connector The part connects the two standard YANMAR diesel
injector component.

New pump concept
• Funnel feature The feature seeks to improve the release of caught air bubbles dormant
in the fuel line when pumping for circulation or injection.
• Double o-ring sealing The double o-ring sealing ensures a better sealing and is
implemented to mitigate subsequent sealing oil leakages apparent for the current pump.
• Smaller physical footprint A smaller physical size, allows for more available space
on an already crowded IQT setup.
The problem of fluctuations in the STDs for the test series presented in this thesis has been
discussed previously in sections 4 and 5.
It is expected the new configuration of piston
plunger and piston housing would have a positive impact - lowering the the fluctuations due
to less friction and hence a more unform injection event. Nearing the deadline for hand-in of
this thesis, all parts (except the piston alignment connector) has been produced. Figure 21
shows a dry assembly of the new pump concept. However, at the time of hand-in, the pump
has not been implemented onto the IQT setup. Therefore no evaluation of its performance
(and subsequent impact on the STDs fluctuations) - is given in this thesis.

Further work
• Further Methanol/DME investigation No conclusive results were yielded from
testing Methanol70DME30 as mentioned in section 2. Hence, re-doing the test for this

and methanol as liquids under pressure at ambient temperature needs to be further

pump concept is at the time of concluding the thesis almost ready for implementation.

to fully conclude how each fuel blend will perform in real engine operations, actual
4-stroke and/or 2-stroke CI engine testing must be concluded.

8
Conclusion
8
Conclusion
The investigation of methanol and ignition improver blend characteristics for methanol
blends, consisting of methanol and a ignition improver such as either FAME, Beraid3555m,
Glycerol or DME, has yield valuable insights into the ignition quality and combustion behav-
ior of these unconventional fuels. The thesis focused on assessing the ignition characteristics
of said blends by the ECN as the key parameter so as to give an overview of how these
would be applicable in 4-stroke and 2-stroke CI applications by comparison with CIMAC
recommendations.
The initial pretesting with FAME, unveiled critical insights into the challenges associated
with the IQT setup, particularly regarding the wear and friction of the piston plunger
and piston housing believed to be the source of STD fluctuations regarding IDs.
The
Methanol/FAME and Methanol/Beraid tests all proved to ignite.
Both blend types in
each their respective ratios were compared with CIMAC results.
The Methanol/FAME
and Methanol/Beraid blends tested showed an almost equal application range - indicating
normal operation can be expected across all load ranges for the Methanol70FAME30- and
Methanol90Beraid10 blend, while diﬀiculties and even risk of engine failure could be en-
countered for certain loads ranges for the remaining blends depending on application. HRR
analysis revealed a slightly more rapid heat release rate after combustion onset for the Be-
raid blends compared to the FAME blends - underlying the inherent difference of the blend
types. No ignition and hence no ECN were obtained from testing Methanol/Glycerol and
Methanol/DME blends. However, Methanol/DME blends are not entirely ruled out due to
inadequate testing.
A new pump concept has been successfully manufactured in part at the end of this thesis.
However, the implementation and further testing of the pump is still left to be concluded.
The results obtained gives an indication of fuel blend performances for the methanol blends
presented. Testing the fuel blends in 4-stroke and/or 2-stroke CI engine applications is needed
to reach definitive conclusions regarding auto ignition performance for each methanol blend.
