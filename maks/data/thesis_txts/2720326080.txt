Abstract
The non­conventional yeast Debaryomyces hansenii has shown great potential for biotech­
nological application, due to its osmotolerant and halophilic nature, in combination with its
ability to utilize a broad array of substrates. These properties are highly favorable during
the harsh conditions often associated with industrial­scale fermentations.
Nevertheless, its application in these processes have been impeded by a general lack of
tools for genetic engineering in this species. Thus, there is a great demand for developing
efficient methods to introduce heterologous genes in D. hansenii. This has motivated the
development of the CASCADE system for D. hansenii, as it allows for introduction of any
gene of interest at genomic loci constituting high expression, in a defined copy number.
The system relies on the construction of strains harboring gene amplification cassettes,
which supports the amplification of the gene of interest.
To further advance the development of the CASCADE system, a functional gene ampli­
fication cassette, with a suitable selection marker, is required. Therefore, the counters­
electable marker URA3 was evaluated for application in D. hansenii. It was found that
transformation of a plasmid harboring a S. cerevisiae derived URA3 gene, was not ca­
pable of restoring prototrophy in an auxotroph D. hansenii strain. This could have been
caused by the experimental setup, therefore more research is required.
Moreover, the effect of heterologous expression from predefined integration sites was
evaluated. D. hansenii strains expressing the reporter gene mVenus from the predefined
integration sites, were successfully constructed by CRISPR/Cas9 directed homologous
recombination.
Five potential integration sites were characterized in respect to heterologous gene ex­
pression and impact on cell growth. None of the constructed strains showed impaired
growth, but expression levels were found to be highly dependent on the integration site
with differences of up to 46%. Through the construction of strains with varied mVenus
copy number of up to 3, it was established that increasing the copy number of a heterol­
ogous gene resulted in higher expression.

Resumé
Den ukonventionelle gær Debaryomyces hansenii har vist stort potentiale i bioteknologisk
anvendelse, grundet dens osmotolerante og halofile natur, samt dens evne til at benytte
en lang række af substrater. Disse egenskaber yderst favorable under de hårde forhold,
man ofte associerer med fermenteringer på industriel skala.
Dens anvendelse i disse processer er imidlertid blevet hindret af en generel mangel på
genteknologiske værktøjer i denne gær art. Der er derfor et omfattende behov for at ud­
vikle effektive metoder til at introducere heterologe gener i D. hansenii. Dette har motiveret
udviklingen af CASCADE systemet i D. hansenii, da dette system muliggør introduktion af
et vilkårligt gen i genom loci, der understøtter høj ekspression, i et defineret antal kopier.
Systemet bygger på konstruktionen af stammer med genamplifikationskassetter, som un­
derstøtter amplificeringen af det ønskede gen.
For at videreudvikle implementeringen af CASCADE systemet, er det nødvendigt at ud­
vikle en funktionel gen amplifikationskassette, med en velegnet selektionsmarkør. Derfor
blev den selektive markør URA3 evalueret til anvendelse i D. hansenii. Transformation
med et plasmid indeholdende URA3 fra S. cerevisiae kunne ikke gendanne prototrofi hos
en auxotrof D. hansenii stamme. Dette kunne skyldes det eksperimentelle design, derfor
er mere forskning nødvendig.
Derudover, blev effekten af heterolog ekspression fra prædefinerede integrations loci
evalueret. D. hansenii stammer der udtrykte reporter genet mVenus fra de prædefinerede
integrations loci, blev konstrueret med CRISPR/Cas9 induceret homolog rekombination.
Fem potentielle integrations loci blev karakteriseret, i henhold til heterolog genekspres­
sion og effekt på cellens vækst. Ingen af de konstruerede stammer havde nedsat vækst,
men ekspressionsniveauet viste sig at være meget afhængigt af integrations loci, med
forskelle op til 46%. Gennem konstruktionen af stammer med varierende antal mVenus,
af op til 3 kopier, blev det vist at en stigning i genantal medførte højere ekspression.

1
Aim of study
The primary aim of this study is to further develop the implementation of the CASCADE
system in the non­conventional yeast Debaryomyces hansenii, to enable the introduction
and overexpression of heterologous genes of interest.
This involves characterizing predefined integration sites to evaluate their suitability for
implementation of CASCADE, in respect to viability of the cell and gene expression. Fur­
thermore, the effect of heterologous expression using a varying number of integration
sites will be characterized, to establish proof of concept of the CASCADE system in D.
hansenii.
At last, the counterselectable marker URA3, is evaluated as a marker gene in D. hansenii,
for potential use in the CASCADE system.

Introduction
To preserve the current global progress, we have become highly reliant on the production
of different chemical products such as fuels, plastics, small molecules and bioactive com­
pounds. However, the majority of these products have conventionally been produced in
energy­costly processes using non­renewable or unsustainable sources, [1, 2, 3].
Hence, there is a great demand for developing more efficient and sustainable biosynthetic
production of essential products in cell factories. Moreover, the development of cell fac­
tories has also paved the way for production of new natural products such as enzymes,
peptides, and secondary metabolites, [4, 5].
Depending on the product, different microorganisms such as bacteria, filamentous fungi
and yeast in addition to mammalian cells have been adapted for use in these processes.
Each type of organism has specific advantages and are therefore employed in specific
types of production. This work will mainly focus on the application of yeast in biotechno­
logical processes.
Traditionally yeast has been utilized in the food industry for wine and beer production,
distilled alcohol and baking. However, in modern biotechnology they have been utilized
in the production of enzymes, fine chemical and chiral building blocks, [6].
Moreover, yeast are useful in the production of heterologous proteins due to their similarity
to higher eukaryotes, which enables correct folding in addition to posttranscriptional and
posttranslational processing. This facilitates the production of proteins with pharmaceuti­
cal potential. In comparison to mammalian cells, yeast are simpler and more costefficient
due to their low nutritional requirements and adaptive capacities, [6].
Currently, most biosynthetic production processes relies on the use of model­organisms,
like Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Pichia pastoris and Schizosaccharomyces pombe for
yeast, [7, 8]. The model­organisms possess the advantage of being well­studied on a
morphological, metabolic and genomic level, which has eased their implementation into
new processes.
However, in recent years there has been a growing interest in developing new produc­
tion processes using non­conventional yeasts, [8]. It is now recognized that the natural
biodiversity found among non­conventional yeasts, could be utilized in biotechnological
application for development of more stress­tolerant and efficient platform organisms, [9].
By choosing a non­conventional yeast which is phenotypically well­equipped for a specific
production, the need for engineering towards increased tolerance is significantly reduced.
These engineering efforts are often necessary for model­organisms and often come at the
expense of reduced production yields. Alternatively, non­conventional yeasts allows for
a greater focus on engineering towards higher production yields, [8], see figure 2.1.

2.1
Debaryomyces Hansenii
Debaryomyces hansenii is a hemiascomycetous non­conventional yeast, that was origi­
nally isolated from sea­water, which shows great potential for industrial purposes. Some
of the beneficial traits of D. hansenii is its ability to utilize a broad spectrum of substrates
such as mannose, arabinose, xylose and lactose, as well as showing osmotolerant and
halophilic properties, [7, 10].
D. hansenii has shown potential for production of fine chemicals such as xylitol and
flavonoids [8], fat­soluble vitamins [11], therapeutic toxins for prevention of pathogenic
yeast growth [12], enzymes such as thermophilic beta­glucosidases and killer toxins ef­
fective against some pathogenic yeasts, [7]. It can even be applied in the food industry,
for instance in the production of exopeptidases used for the ripening of sausages, [7].
2.1.1
Performance at high salinity
During osmotic stress, cells experience decreased turgor pressure, due to water loss. In
general, there are two main strategies when adapting to osmotic pressure. The cells can
maintain a high amount of salt intracellularly to reduce osmotic pressure, however the
intracellular functions must be adapted to the high concentrations. Another strategy, is to
produce and accumulate compatible solutes, thereby increasing intracellular osmolarity,
[13]. D. hansenii has shown to accumulate Na+ and K+ and produce high amounts of
glycerol which functions as a compatible solute, in both D. hansenii and other yeast,[13,
14, 15].
The characterization of D. hansenii in response to high salinity has been impeded by a
general lack of consensus among different studies, which have utilized a broad array of
abiotic factors, [16]. Therefore some studies have classified the yeast as solely halotol­

erant, while other classified it as halophilic, [17, 18].
A recent study, set out to minimize the inconsistencies in the classification of D. hansenii
by utilizing controlled and monitored environments, in which abiotic factors such as pH,
temperature and salinity was varied. The study investigated the performance of D. hansenii
in harsh conditions, and found that it showed increased growth, respiration and tolerance
towards low pH and high temperature in the presence of 1M NaCl, a concentration consid­
ered toxic for most yeast. Further increment of NaCl, led to lower growth rates, however
growth was not completely inhibited, thereby D. hansenii was classified as halophilic, [19].
Based on these results a new survival strategy of D. hansenii in drying environments was
proposed: While the environment dries out salt concentration increases while pH is de­
creasing. In these conditions, most yeast resolve to dormancy or sporulation when NaCl
concentrations reaches 1M. However, D. hansenii will increase its growth, respiration and
carbon intake. Thereby, most of the available carbon is obtained and it can potentially out­
compete other microorganisms in the area. With further increment of the salinity levels,
the growth of D. hansenii decreases but it still proliferates, thereby overpopulating the
area, see figure 2.2, [19].
The proposed survival strategy is further supported by another study, which found that D.
hansenii showed an increased expression of genes related to oxidative stress in presence
of high salinity, [20].

2.1.2
Cultivation using alternative substrates, waste streams and
non­pure water
Certain properties of D. hansenii have created impetus for assessing its use in alternative
cultivation processes.
Due to its halophilic nature D. hansenii is a great candidate for cultivation in non­pure wa­
ter such as seawater. This is a highly desirable trait since it can lower production cost and
the environmental impact of production especially in arid regions with limited fresh water
supply. An additional benefit would be the reduced risk of contamination when cultivating
in seawater, [9].
The properties of D. hansenii additionally show potential for growth on lignocellulosic
biomass, which is an abundant and renewable raw material that can derive from wood,
agricultural waste, and food waste among others. It can be used in the production of bio­
fuels, organic acids, and other chemicals. However, to release the fermentable sugars,
extensive chemical or biological pretreatment is required. These treatments also release
microbial growth inhibitors such as vanillin, furfural or 5­hydroxymethylfurfural, formic acid
and acetic acid, [16].
D. hansenii exhibits tolerance towards some of these inhibitors such as furfural and 5­
hydroxymethylfurfural, and this tolerance is further increased when grown in 1M NaCl,
[21, 16]. The metabolic capabilities of D. hansenii, further establish it as a suitable candi­
date for industrial processes involving lignocellulosic biomass, since it can grow on sugars
that are often found in this substrate such as mannose, arabinose and xylose, [21].
Furthermore, it has been speculated that D. hansenii could be a candidate for treatment or
even utilization of waste streams from the dairy industry. The effluents derived from these
processes have a high content of nutrients such as proteins, fatty substances and lactose,
but also salts and chemicals. In this environment, the tolerance towards high salinity and
osmotic pressure and the capability to metabolize lactose of D. hansenii could be highly
applicable, [16].
This was further supported by a study which found that D. hansenii could grow directly in
a mixture of delactosed whey permeate and salty waste derived from the dairy industry,
when supplemented with a nitrogen source. Moreover, the study showed that D. hansenii
could outcompete microorganisms already present in the media, in the absence of ster­
ilization or pasteurization. This could potentially be attributed to its mycocin production
and halophilic nature, [22].
2.1.3
The molecular biology of D. hansenii
One of the disadvantages of non­conventional yeasts is the lack of tools to genetically
engineer the organism. Therefore, development of such tools is essential before the or­
ganism can be implemented in new industrial processes, [8].
D. hansenii has several genomic peculiarities, some of which should be considered when
developing the CASCADE system. It belongs to the CTG­clade of yeast, and thus the
CUG­codon is ambiguously translated, and is thereby primarily translated to serine in­
stead of leucine [11].
This entails that all heterologous genes to be expressed, must be optimized to avoid the
use of the codon CUG, as this would result in structural changes of the proteins. This
requirement also include enzymes which are temporarily expressed and used for geneti­
cally engineering the strain such as CRISPR/Cas9 and selective markers.
Moreover, D. hansenii has the highest coding capacity among yeast, which results in a
higher gene density and genetic redundancy compared to other yeasts. There is specif­
ically an over­representation of genes involved in metabolism of cyclic and unusual nu­
cleotides, beta­oxidation of fatty acids, amine metabolism, energy generation, phosphate
utilization and peroxisomal organization. This over­representation may reflect specific

2.2
Genetically engineering of production organisms
When utilizing an organism in industrial processes it is often essential to genetically en­
gineer the genome, for instance by introducing necessary heterologous genes or alter
expression of native pathways to redirect production. Thereby, the organism can be op­
timized for different purposes.
2.2.1
Transformation with yeast shuttle vectors
A common method of genetically engineering yeast is through transformation using yeast
shuttle vectors. There are many advantages of utilizing shuttle vectors for yeast trans­
formation. It is a relatively rapid way of introducing new genetic material. The vectors
allow for construction, genetic manipulation and amplification in Escherichia coli, since
they contain an origin of replication but can also be sustained in yeast, [23].
It is essential that the shuttle vector is selectable in both E. coli and the yeast, thus mak­
ing successful transformations easily visible. This is often also a necessary measure to
prevent loss of the plasmid by making it crucial for the survival of the cell, [23]. In general,
there are three main types of yeast shuttle vectors.
Yeast centromeric plasmids
Yeast centromeric plasmids (YCps) use the endogenous replication and chromosome
segregation machinery of the cell to persist within the yeast cell, and is commonly sus­
tained in low copy numbers. Two sequences are necessary for this function; autonomously
replicating sequences (ARS) and centromeric senquences (CEN). During the S­phase of
the eukaryotic cell cycle, replication will be initiated exactly once from ARS. Therefore its
presence ensures that the plasmid is replicated.
CEN helps directing segregation during mitosis, thereby making YCps genetically sta­
ble. However, even with this form of stability there can still be population heterogeneity,
since there will be differences in copy number throughout the population, and asymmetric
plasmid segregation can occur, resulting in the loss of plasmid in some cells, [23].
Yeast episomal plasmids
Yeast episomal plasmids (YEps) utilizes the native yeast plasmid termed 2µ or parts of
it to persist in the cell. To maintain the plasmids the 2µ replication origin (ORI) and the
plasmid partitioning locus called STB must be present. Similarly to the ARS sequence
replication is initiated once in the S­phase from ORI, thereby the copy number is doubled
during replication, [23].
STB is involved in plasmid partioning during cell division, however the exact mechanism
are not fully understood but involves deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) binding proteins REP1
and REP2 which are encoded in 2µ and host cell factors, [23]. Newer studies suggest that
the partitioning is facilitated by binding of STB to chromatin during cell division, thereby
securing a more equal partitioning, [24].
YEps can comprise of the entire 2µ plasmid, which can then be genomically engineered.
This form of YEp can achieve copy­numbers of 40­80 copies per cell. YEps can also con­
sist of a smaller plasmid with only the ORI and STB of 2µ inserted. These plasmids are
reliant of expression of REP1 and REP2 in trans and only achieves around 10­40 copies
per cell.

In general, it is very rare that the 2µ plasmid is completely lost within the cell, but over­
expression of a gene of interest can affect the copy number negatively, as the production
can burden the cell. Heterogeneity in the population can thus still occur, due to substantial
variations in copy number, [23].
Yeast integrative plasmids
In contrary to the other type of plasmids, the yeast integrative plasmid YIps does not
harbor any mechanisms for replication, and thereby no means for sustaining in the cell.
Instead it utilizes targeting sequences which are homologous to loci of the cells genome,
and can thus be integrated through homologous recombination, described in section 2.2.2.
By choosing an abundant loci in the cell such as ribosomal DNA repeats, multiple inte­
grations can occur, consequently increasing the copy number, [23].
To ensure that the YIps are integrated a selective yeast marker must be included. How­
ever, if the plasmid is integrated using a single crossover event it will be flanked by direct
repeats, which may result in looping out the YIp. In these instances, it will sometimes not
loop out the entire YIp, but keep the selective marker. Thereby heterogeneity can still
occur in the culture, [23].
Transformation by these different types of yeast shuttle vectors have shown to be a fast
method to obtain heterologous gene expression, but as described often results in culture
heterogeneity due to undefined copy numbers and loss of genetic material or even entire
plasmids, [23].
However, yeast shuttle vectors can also be used to gain temporary heterologous expres­
sion with the purpose of constructing stable strains through chromosomal integrations.
The experimental work of this project, utilizes plasmids for D. hansenii transformations
based around the principles of YCps, by including an ARS sequence. However, they
do not include a CEN sequence, thus there is no measure to ensure correct partioning.
Thereby, expression from these plasmid are less stable over time, [25].
USER fusion for plasmid assembly
An essential part of genetic engineering, is the construction of vectors with desired genetic
material. Uracil Specific Excision Reagent (USER) fusion has proven to be a valuable tool
for assembly of plasmids with desired genetic material. This method allows for assembly
of multiple polymerase chain reaction (PCR) fragments and cloning vectors containing a
USER cassette, by the use of complimentary overhangs, [26].
USER fusion relies on the use of specifically designed primers with a uracil­base often
as the eight nucleotide from the 5’­end, which when used in a PCR reaction with a uracil­
insensitive polymerase will incorporate the uracil in the PCR fragment. Subsequently,
the fragments can be treated with USERTM enzyme mix which consists of uracil DNA
glycosidase (UDG) and DNA glycosylase­lyase endo VIII.
Uracil excision is catalysed by UDG followed by cleaving the phosphodiester backbone
by DNA glycosylaselyase endo VIII, thereby leaving the PCR fragment with overhangs,
[27]. This can be combined with the use of a cloning vector with a USER cassette, which
when treated with a combination of a restriction enzyme and nicking enzyme will create
overhangs at the cloning site, [25, 26]. Designing the overhangs of the USERTM cassette
and the primers with directional bases that are complimentary, allows for integration of
the fragments in a uni­directional manner, see figure 2.3, [26].
Some of the advantages of USER fusion is that only small overhangs of minimum 8bp
are required and various concentrations of PCR fragments can be used. Moreover, it can
assemble multiple fragments in a uni­directional manner, in which it is possible to obtain
seamless ligation independent cloning and even create site­directed mutagenesis, [26,

2.2.2
Chromosomal integration
Though transformation using plasmids has many benefits, sometimes it is preferred to
introduce the heterologous genes directly into the chromosome to ensure genetic stability
and heterogeneity. Genes can be integrated by exploiting the cells mechanism for repair­
ing double­stranded breaks (DSBs) in the genome, [29].
The eukaryotic cell has two pathways for repairing DSBs; Non­homologous end­joining
(NHEJ) and homologous recombination (HR). These mechanisms are highly necessary
for the survival of the cell as a DSB is otherwise fatal, [30].
NHEJ is generally a more error­prone pathway, since it often requires DNA­end­processing
when joining two DNA ends. This can lead to smaller insertions and deletions of DNA
called indels, thereby mutating the genome, see figure 2.4, [30], [29].
In HR the DSB is repaired by first processing the ends of the DSB to create 3’­OH over­
hangs. In the presence of a homologous duplex, the overhangs can act as a primer in
DNA synthesis to extend the strand. When the two strands dissociates, the homologous
duplex is integrated, see figure 2.4, [31], [32].
HR can be exploited to genetically engineer yeast, by supplying them with a repair tem­
plate with homology for a specific integration site, resulting in error­free integrations.
Therefore, it is the most widely used method for genome integrations. This method relies
on induction of DSB at the integration site, as this will significantly increase the integration
efficiency [29].

side depicts how Non homologues end­joining often results in either small deletions or
integrations. The right side depicts how homologous recombination enables the complete
repair of a disrupted gene in the presence of a homologous duplex. Figure inspired from
[33].
CRISPR/Cas9 mediated HR
There are several methods to induce DSBs, for instance the meganuclease I­SceI, Zinc
Finger Nucleases (ZFN) and Transcription Activator­Like Effector Nucleases (TALENs),
[29]. In this study clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)
and the CRISPR­associated system 9 (Cas9) will be employed for inducing DSBs.
The CRISPR/Cas9 system is a powerful tool for genome engineering, since it can induce
DSBs in a targeted and precise manner. It is highly customizable and has only few re­
quirements for the target site, which enables its broad application. Moreover, the system
is also suitable for high­throughput and multiplexing gene editing, thereby allowing the
targeting of multiple integration sites at once, [33].

The CRISPR/Cas9 system consists of the Cas9 nuclease and a single guide RNA (sgRNA).
The sgRNA directs Cas9 to a specific site in the genome through a 20 nucleotide se­
quence, called the protospacer. The protospacer is designed to recognize the integration
site through basepairing, and the only requirement is that there must be a protospacer
adjacent motif (PAM) directly upstream of the integration site. The consensus sequence
of the Cas9 PAM is 5’­NGG­3’, where N can be any of the four DNA nucleotide [34].
Thereby, cotransforming a CRISPR/Cas9 expressing plasmid targeting the integration lo­
cus, and a repair template flanked with sequences with homology to the integration locus,
allows for DSB induction and integration of the repair template through HR, see figure 2.5.
After a DSB has occurred the two native repair pathways, NHEJ or HR, compete to re­
pair the genome. In general HR is the preferred mechanism in the conventional yeast S.
cerevisiae, however most non­conventional yeast, including D. hansenii, prefer the NHEJ
pathway, [9, 11, 30]. This can affect the transformation rate substantially. However, dele­
tion of the KU70 gene generates NHEJ­deficient strains, since KU70 is part of the initiating
step of NHEJ. This gene deletion has been shown to increase the HR rate significantly,
[29, 25].
Furthermore, when the NHEJ pathway is deterred, the successful integration of donor
DNA can potentially act as a selection marker in itself, since DSBs will lead to growth
arrest in cells without integrated repair templates, [29].

Experimental theory
3.1
CASCADE ­ a system for stable gene amplification
A considerable challenge in the utilization of D. hansenii in industrial production is the
current lack of genetic engineering tools for this organism, [25].
CASCADE is a gene amplification system, which allows for a simple introduction and sta­
ble expression of any gene of interest (GOI) in a predefined copy number. Thereby, high
expression and a generally more homogenous culture can be achieved, since all cells
have the same copy number, [35].
3.1.1
Implementation of CASCADE
This study focusses on further developing the implementation of the CASCADE system
in D. hansenii, with the prospect of a more rapid construction of cell factories in the future.
The system was first implemented in S. cerevisiae, however some modifications have
been made to the system since then. The modifications serve to simplify the amplification
of the GOI, however the concept of the system is the same, [35]. CASCADE relies on
the construction of Gene Acceptor Starter (GAS) strains, which have defined numbers
of identical Gene Acceptor (GA) cassettes integrated at different integration loci of the
genome.
The GA cassettes are designed to function as a platform for introduction of heterologous
GOIs located on a Donor Amplicon (DA) sequence. The GA cassette consists of a marker
gene, and an I­SceI recognition site for inducing DSBs within the cassette. The cassette
is flanked by two distinct targeting sequences A and B, which are homologous with tar­
geting sequences flanking the GOI within the DA sequence, see figure 3.1.
After constructing the GAS strains, amplification of the GOI is initiated by co­transforming
the DA sequence with an I­SceI expressing plasmid. When I­SceI is expressed, it will
result in DSBs throughout the integrated GA cassettes. For the cell to survive, the DSBs
are repaired by HR, using the DA sequence as a homologous template. Complete am­
plification of the DA can be screened for as a loss of the marker of the GA cassette, see
figure 3.2.

integrated genomically, in characterized integration loci. Modified figure from [35].

3.1.2
Application potential of the CASCADE system
The system was first introduced in S. cerevisiae in 2017, where strains with various copy
numbers of the GOI were generated, thereby allowing for differential expression, [35].
The same study used the CASCADE system for heterologous expression of different
genes, to assess its application.
Expression of cyan fluorescent protein in combination with red fluorescent protein (CFP::RFP)
using the CASCADE system compared to expression of the same genes cotransformed
on two separate 2µ plasmids, showed that the CASCADE system had significantly higher
CFP activity. However, RFP activity was 10­fold lower, when using CASCADE.
Further examination indicated a significant heterogeneity among the cells expressing both
RFP and CFP 2µ plasmids, and only 54% still expressed both genes. Moreover, the CAS­
CADE strains were able to retain their initial fluorescence level while it was reduced to less
than 1% in the 2µ plasmid strains, in long term cultivations with no selective pressure.
Thereby, CASCADE showed to be a superior system in terms of both heterogeneity and
stability [35].
It was found that the CASCADE system can be utilized to elucidate the limiting step of a
pathway, by altering the expression of specific genes of the pathway, using 6­methylsalicylic
acid synthase and its activating enzyme phosphopantetheinyl transferase as a model sys­
tem. When a non­limiting enzyme was expressed at different copy­numbers the titer of
6­methylsalicylic acid remained the same. When the expression of the limiting enzyme
was altered, the titer would increase correlating with increased copy­number, [35].
Besides elucidating bottlenecks of a pathway, the CASCADE system could also be ap­
plied to reduce said bottlenecks. It was discovered that intermediates which accumulated
in the vanillin glucoside pathway could be reduced by overexpressing genes of the path­
way. For instance, the overexpression of aromatic carboxylic acid reductase (ACAR) and
UDP­glycosyltransferase (UGT) resulted in a 6­fold increase of vanillin glycoside, [35].
Some of the methods described exploited the mating system of S. cerevisiae as a fast
method to obtain different combinations of gene expression in diploid strains, [35]. Since,
D. hansenii only has one mating type and is characterized as homothallic with a primarily

haplontic life cycle [36], some of these methods are not necessarily directly applicable
in a D. hansenii­CASCADE system. Nevertheless, the CASCADE system would intrin­
sically be a valuable genetic engineering tool, since once implemented it allows for high
expression of an arbitrary GOI.
3.2
Development of CASCADE in D. hansenii
Several factors must be considered in the current development of the CASCADE system
in D. hansenii. When the system was initially developed for S. cerevisiae, a former study
had previously characterized the majority of the integration loci, [37]. However, when
working with a non­conventional yeast such as D. hansenii, efforts towards characteriza­
tion of integration loci in terms of viability and expression, and designing a functional GA
cassette, are highly necessary.
Moreover, a general issue when working with this species has been a prior lack of con­
sensus among different studies, which can potentially lead to misconceptions, [16]. Thus,
there is an emphasis on evaluating every aspect of the CASCADE system prior to its im­
plementation in D. hansenii.
3.2.1
Choice of integration loci
When working with chromosomal integration, the choice of integration site can affect the
genetic stability and heterogeneity of the culture. Since integration of identical genetic
content can induce direct repeat recombination (DRR), GA cassettes can potentially be
lost in individual cells, [38, 39]. To prevent loss of GA cassettes throughout the culture,
integration loci should be separated by essential genes, thus recombination will eminently
lead to cell death, thereby ensuring heterogeneity, [35].
Moreover, the integration loci have also shown to affect both the viability of the engineered
strain and expression of the integrated heterologous genes, [29]. This creates impetus
for investigating the effect of genetically engineering a specific locus, before constructing
the GAS strain.
A major difference between the implementation of CASCADE in S. cerevisiae and D.
hansenii, is that in S. cerevisiae integration loci were clustered together on the same chro­
mosomes, reducing the risk of losing integration loci as a result of crossing­over events
during mating. This precaution is not necessary in D. hansenii, due to its mostly haplontic
lifestyle. Hence, all the predefined integration sites are dispersed on each of the seven
chromosomes of D. hansenii.
3.2.2
Yeast marker genes
Generally, an efficient system for genetic engineering is highly reliant on being easily
selectable. One of the current issues with implementing the CASCADE system in D.
hansenii, is the lack of known and well­studied counterselectable markers in this species.
Moreover, marker genes also serves the purpose of enabling the initial work, towards
constructing a functional gene amplification system.
Counterselection by URA3
The gene URA3 encodes the enzyme orotidine 5’­phosphate decarboxylase (ODCase)
which catalyzes the decarboxylation of orotidine 5’­monophosphate (OMP) to uridine 5’­
monophosphate (UMP) in the de novo synthesis of pyrimidine in yeast. Loss of the URA3
gene results in cells with auxotrophy for uracil or uridine, [40]. Moreover, ODCase can
convert 5­Fluoroorotic acid (5­FOA) to 5­fluorouracil, a toxic compound resulting in cell
death, [41]. Thereby, the URA3 gene can be used for both positive and negative selection
in molecular microbiology, see figure 3.3.

A yeast resistance marker is a beneficial measure to ensure plasmid transformations. This

organisms are susceptible to NTC, including bacteria, yeast, filamentous fungi and plant
cells. Expression of the resistance marker Nourseothricin N­acetyl transferase (NAT) will
inactivate NTC, thereby allowing cells to grow in its presence, [47].

Construction of plasmids
All plasmids used in this work will be assembled by USER fusion in plasmids harboring

In general the plasmids used can be divided into CRISPR/Cas9­plasmids and a plasmid

dependent on the use.
PCR fragments for the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids will be amplified using primers, which al­

They were assembled in plasmids containing a codon optimized Cas9CUG enzyme, which

constituting half of the URA3­gene. This is necessary, since the URA3­gene of S. cere­

leucine, also within the CTG­clade of yeast, see figure 3.4.1B.

4.6
Generally applied methods
The methods described in this section are commonly applied techniques in molecular
microbiology, and will be referred to in following sections potentially with small variations.
4.6.1
PCR
PCR was performed to amplify different DNA fragments. The PCR reaction was con­
ducted by adding 20µL Milli­Q grade water, 25µL of a suitable polymerase, 1µL of tem­
plate DNA, and 2µL of both upstream and downstream primer to a PCR tube.
The PCR tube was placed in a C1000 Touch Thermal cycler (BioRad) which was set to
30s at 98°C, followed by 35 cycles of 10s at 98°C, 30s at a suitable annealing tempera­
ture, 1 min pr. kbp of the fragment at 72 °C. This was followed by 5 min at 72°C prior to
cooling down to 12 °C.
4.6.2
Gel electrophoresis
Gel electrophoresis was utilized to validate the presence or separate specific DNA bands.
If the PCR reaction was not dyed, it was supplemented with loading dye in the ratio 0.17
µL per 1 µ to reaction volume.
Gel electrophoresis was performed using a 1% w/V agarose gel in TAE buffer supple­
mented with cybrsafe. Samples were loaded in the wells and either a 1kb ladder or a
24
Development of the Cascade gene amplification system in Debaryomyces Hansenii
==PAGE:35==
100bp ladder were applied dependent on expected band sizes. The gel electrophoresis
was performed in a RunOneTM electrophoresis cell (EmbiTec) set to 100 V for 20 min.
To generate pictures of gels Molecular Imager®GelDocTM XR+ imaging system (BioRad)
was used, and the pictures were analysed using the software ImageLab5.2.1­GelDocTM
XR+ imaging system (BioRad).
4.6.3
Validation and purification of PCR fragments
To purify PCR fragments amplified from plasmids, the PCR reaction mix was incubated
with 1 µL of the restriction enzyme DpnI for 1h at 37 °C.
If multiple PCR fragments were produced in the same reaction the entire reaction mix
was separated with gel electrophoresis according to section 4.6.2. Afterwards the correct
PCR band was extracted using a scalpel under UV light and transferred to an Eppendorf
tube. Otherwise, fragments could be purified directly from PCR reaction mix.
Fragments were further purified following the supplied protocol of the GFXTM PCR DNA
and Gel band purification kit (Cytiva). Purified fragments were eluded in 50 µL sterile
Milli­Q grade water and stored at ­20 C.
4.6.4
Plasmid propagation and purification
Plasmids used or generated in the duration of this project were propagated in E. coli DH5α
by inoculation in 10 mL LB­Amp media and incubated O/N at 37 °C with shaking of 150
rpm. Plasmids were purified following the protocol of the GenEluteTM Plasmid miniprep
kit (Sigma­Aldrich).
4.6.5
Colony PCR
Colony PCR was performed to ensure correct integration or assembly in D. hansenii and
E. coli, respectively. To test colonies a small amount of cells from a plate was added to a
PCR tube containing 7.5 µL sterile milli­Q grade water. 1 µL of this culture was transferred
to a suitable plate to retain the colony for future use.
For yeast cells an extra disruption step is required, therefore the tube was transferred to
a C1000 Touch Thermal cycler (BioRad) set to 98 °C for 5 min, 4 °C for 30s, 98 °C for 5
min and 80 °C for 10 min.
Then 7.5 µL DreamTaq polymerase mastermix was added and 0.5 µL of both the upstream
and downstream primer.
The PCR tube was transferred back to the Thermal cycler set to 5 min at 94°C, followed
by 35 cycles of 35s at 94°C, 45s at 51 °C, 3.5 min at 68 °C. This was followed by 5 min
at 68°C prior to cooling down to 10 °C. Afterwards, a gel electrophoresis was performed
to generate gel pictures according to section 4.6.2 by loading the gel with 7 µL of PCR
reaction.
4.6.6
Sanger sequencing
To verify correct integration purified plasmid constructs and PCR amplified samples were
sent to Sanger sequencing at Eurofins Genomics. The DNA samples to be sequenced
were prepared by following the supplied protocol of the Mix2Seq sequencing kit (Eurofins
Genomics) and choosing a suitable primer.
4.6.7
Storage of verified plasmids
E. coli DH5α colonies, harboring plasmids verified by both colony PCR and Sanger se­
quencing, were inoculated in 5 mL LB­Amp media and incubated O/N at 37 °C with shak­
ing of 150 rpm. 500 µL cell culture was stored in 25% glycerol in a cryotube, and stored
at ­80 °C.

Purification of genomic DNA was applied to sequence DNA of transformed D. hansenii
cells. Using a 10 µL inoculation loop a large scoop of D. hansenii cells from a single

4.7.3
Linearization of plasmids
To create USER digested plasmids for USER cloning 3 µL Cutsmart buffer, 15 units of
AsiSI restriction enzyme, 3 µg of plasmid were diluted in milli­Q grade water to a final
volume of 30 µL, in an Eppendorf tube and incubated at 37 °C O/N.
The treated plasmid was subjected to a gel electrophoresis according to section 4.6.2.
Afterwards the digested plasmid was extracted under UV light and transferred to an Ep­
pendorf tube. The plasmid was gel purified according to section 4.6.3 but was eluded in
30 µL sterile Milli­Q grade water. Subsequently, 20 units of Nb.BmsI nicking enzyme, 10
µL NEB3.1 buffer and 58 µL milli­Q grade water were added, followed by incubation at 65
°C for 2 h. Afterwards, additional 10 units of Nb.BmsI nicking enzyme was added and the
plasmid was incubated at 65 °C for 1 h. The treated plasmid was purified as described in
section 4.6.3 and eluded in 50 µL sterile Milli­Q grade water.

4.9.1
Data handling
Initial data handling was conducted using an R­script to generate plots of growth and
fluorescence measured in light scattering units (LSU) over time.
Growth data
To calculate the average growth rate (µ) the data was log­transformed. It was established
that all cells showed exponential growth in the interval [1.65;3.32] LSU with exception of
a single quadruplicate of Multi6 which entered exponential growth at a higher initial LSU,
therefore the interval was adjusted to [2.01;4.06] LSU. A linear regression was performed
on the log­transformed measurements within these intervals. The growth rate was deter­
mined as the slope of this linear regression.
The average growth rate and standard deviation (SD) was calculated for each transfor­
mation. The growth rates were used in an One­Way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) to
establish if there was any statistical significant differences between the groups of trans­
formants and control strains.
Fluorescence data
A superior reading of fluorescence was achieved using gain 10 in LSU compared to 3 and
7, therefore these measurements were used for further characterization. Fluorescence
measurements were normalized to biomass to give relative fluorescence. For each qua­
druplicate the maximum expression value of fluorescence after exponential growth was
found, and average and standard deviation was calculated.
The expression values were used in an One­Way ANOVA in combination with a Post­Hoc
analysis to establish if there was any statistical significant differences between different
groups of transformants. Analyses could be grouped as comparison between transfor­
mant and control strains, comparisons of individual integration sites, and comparison be­
tween number of integrations.
To determine statistical significance a critical value (α) of 0.05 was applied. However, in
cases where multiple analyses are conducted on the same dependent value a Bonferroni
corrected critical value was applied, see equation 4.1.

Results and discussion
5.1
Characterizing integration sites in D. hansenii
An essential part of developing the CASCADE gene amplification system in D. hansenii,
was to find suitable integration sites and characterize their expression of heterologous
proteins. Furthermore, the effects of the heterologous expression from a specific integra­
tion site, in relation to the overall fitness of the cell, are also highly relevant.
As previously mentioned, seven integration sites have been preselected prior to this study.
Therefore attempts were made to construct D. hansenii cells expressing single integration
and up to four copies of mVenus using multiple integration sites.
5.1.1
Plasmid construction and validation
Plasmids harboring CRISPR/Cas9 were constructed using USER fusion to target each
integration site, with the exception of IS1­4, as these plasmids were constructed in previ­
ous work [50]. Plasmids were also constructed to target multiple integration sites at once
by harboring a multitude of sgRNA­fragments. A schematic of the plasmid design can be
found in section 3.3.
All plasmids were successfully validated using colony PCR combined with gel electrophore­
sis. Plasmids used for targeting a single integration site were expected to yield a band
of approximately 1000bp, for each additional target site approximately 200 bp should be
added to the expected length. Based on the colony PCR colonies would be chosen for
Sanger sequencing to ensure that the sgRNA were correctly integrated. Gel pictures can
be found in appendix A.3 (Gel B­D), showing which colonies were ultimately validated by
Sanger sequencing.
Overall, correct plasmids were obtained for all transformations to be performed.
5.1.2
Construction of YFP expressing D. hansenii
D. hansenii cells were transformed with the objective of integrating mVenus in the seven
different integration sites, using the constructed plasmids and repair templates in CRISPR/
Cas9 directed HR.
It was necessary to repeat the transformation of D. hansenii several times, as the initial
transformations yielded no colonies for either the specific transformation or the nega­
tive and positive controls. Different approaches were attempted to troubleshoot the pro­
cess,and it was discovered to be an issue with the preparation of Tris·HCl for the treatment
buffer. Transformations were enabled to proceed, when it was exchanged for the correct
variety (TRIZMA®hydrochloride Sigma­Aldrich).
Successful transformations were achieved for IS2, IS3, IS5, IS6, IS7, Multi2, Multi3, Multi5
and Multi6, figure 5.1 depicts an overview of their respective integration sites. Colony
forming units (CFU) counts for all transformations, including the negative and positive
control, can be found in appendix A.4. The general trend was that the positive control
using the sDIV115 would obtain the most colonies, since it has NHEJ activity. Almost
all negative controls using the NHEJ deficient strain sDIV165 had no colonies, since they
were not supplied with a repair template. The specific transformations yielded a moderate
to low amount of colonies.
Successful transformations for IS1, IS4 and Multi4 were not achieved in the duration of
the experimental work. Successful IS1 integration was included in the transformations
Multi2 and Multi3, and has been obtained as a single integration in a previous study, [50].
Therefore this integration is not thought to affect viability of the cell.
However, the same study did similarly not achieve IS4 integration. This could indicate

Characterizing effects of integration site on growth
The validated strains were subjected to a micro­scale fermentation to evaluate the effect
of integration and heterologous mVenus expression on growth. The growth profiles ob­
tained from the fermentations are depicted in figure 5.9.
D. hansenii cultivations performed using BioLector II are typically only extended for 72h,
[19]. However, cultivations performed in this work was extended to 120h, since the sta­
tionary phase was not reached within the 72h.
From the growth profiles it appears, that the stationary phase was still not reached within
the 120h. However, a general challenge with performing extended fermentations in the
BioLector II, is that the measurements will to some degree become unreliable when ex­
tended for long periods of time. This can be attributed to evaporation reducing the culture
volume. It can therefore not be concluded, whether the cells were still growing, or if cell
density seems to increase due to loss of culture volume.
Moreover, it seems that the cells have two distinct exponential phases. This is often seen

==PAGE:50==
when cells are supplied with multiple carbon sources, which is not the case for the cultiva­
tions performed in this work. This could potentially be caused by the measuring method
applied in BioLector II fermentations, in which cell growth is measured as light scattering
units, using a specific filter. If the cells undergo morphological changes during cultiva­
tion, it could potentially change the way light is scattered, thereby resulting in these two
phases. Generally, there should always be emphasis on the limitations of the equipment
used, to reduce the change of inaccurate conclusions.
The two growth phases does not seem to be prevalent in other studies performing d.
hansenii biolector cultivations, using YNB as media. A common factor in these stud­
ies was the application of the 20.4 g/L potassium phthalate monobasic ≥99.5% (Sigma­
Aldrich) as buffer system, [19, 22]. Thus, the altered growth rate could potentially be
caused by variations in pH during cultivations.
The characterization of growth was based on the average growth rate of the first exponen­
tial phase, since it would more accurately depict the initial growth. All linear regressions
showed R2­values above 0.98, which indicates that growth was genuinely exponential in
the chosen intervals.
The average growth rate and standard deviation is depicted in figure 5.10. It can be seen
that growth appears to be relatively homogeneous.
The performed one­way ANOVA yielded a P­value of 0.1465 which is greater than an α
of 0.05, thus there are no statistic significance between any of the tested growth rates.
Based on these results, it was found that none of the transformed cells had significantly
lower or higher growth rate than the control strain sDIV165.
It was thereby also established, that there was no statistical significance between the

also an important factor to evaluate, since KU70 deficiency is an integral part of establish­
ing D. hansenii GAS strains. Obtained growth rates, the average growth rate, SD, and
the R­script output used for conducting the statistical analysis, can be found in appendix
A.6.
Overall, heterologous gene expression from the evaluated integration sites, do not seem
to affect the overall fitness of the cell significantly.

IS5 which performed similarly.
Based on these results, the importance of integration site in D. hansenii has become
highly apparent. As mentioned in section 3.3, the specific integration site is known to po­
tentially affect gene expression, [29].
Since the majority of the sites showed significantly different expression, it is difficult to
determine which expression level should be considered the standard. For instance, the
expression levels achieved by IS3 could be considered the aim for all integration sites
since it was the highest achieved. However, that would mean that all other integration
sites should be rejected as options, since they were significantly lower.

ally high, since no other integration sites show similar results. In that case, IS2 expression
levels would be a more realistic baseline to use, as it is the median of all obtained expres­
sion levels.
Then expression by IS7 and IS3 would be considered significantly higher and IS5 is con­
sidered insignificantly different and are therefore all suitable integration sites. IS6 would
be considered significantly lower, and could potentially be rejected from future experi­
ments.
Nevertheless, it can still be argued, that there is not enough basis for excluding any of the
evaluated integration sites, since a test size of five integration sites may not be sufficient

band of approximately 1500 bp. Gel pictures can be found in appendix A.3, Gel A and E,
respectively.
Based on the colony PCR, colonies were chosen for Sanger sequencing to ensure that
the URA3 gene was correctly integrated and had obtained the correct mutation, and that
sgRNA was correctly integrated. The sequencing showed that both plasmids were cor­
rect.
5.2.2
Evaluation of URA3 expression through plasmid transformation
For URA3 to be a functional marker gene, it must be possible to restore prototrophy
through heterologous expression. Therefore, sDIV213 was transformed with a plasmid
harboring an S. cerevisiae derived URA3 (pDIV019_URA3), a transformation using only
pDIV019 was included for comparison.
The transformed colonies were replica plated onto ­ura plates and ­ura+uri, see figure
5.15. From the plates it can be seen that both transformations still exhibit uracil auxotro­
phy, therefore it was not possible to restore URA3 expression in sDIV213_pDIV019_URA3.
The lack of prototrophy in sDIV213_URA3 could be caused by numerous reasons. One
of the more plausible is that the S. cerevisiae­derived URA3 gene is not functional in D.
hansenii. Not only should the gene be functional in D. hansenii, but also the promoter and
terminator derived from S. cerevisiae. It can therefore not be concluded if the issue is the
URA3 gene specifically.
As mentioned in section 3.2.2, URA3 derived from S. cerevisiae has previously been used
to restore prototrophy in D. hansenii, [42]. However, the former study used the strain S.
cerevisiae SEY 2108, compared to the strain S288c used in this study, thus the restora­
tion may be strain dependent.
Another difference is that the study obtained their auxotrophic strain through random mu­
tagenesis, while the strain used in this work (sDIV213), was subjected to whole gene
deletion using CRISPR/Cas9. Auxotrophy caused by random mutagenesis is most often
caused by frame­shift mutations or protein inactivation due to small structural changes.
Thereby, these mutations often leaves the majority of the gene intact. The URA3 gene of
D. hansenii lies only 63 bp upstream of another unspecified gene, the whole gene dele­
tion could therefore potentially have removed genetic factors involved in the expression
of this gene.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to further advance the implementation of the CASCADE sys­
tem in D. hansenii, by constructing strains expressing of mVenus from defined integration
sites, and characterize the effect, with respect to growth and achieved expression levels.
Moreover, the use of URA3 as a counterselectable marker was evaluated.
The evaluation of URA3 as a marker gene showed, that it was possible to achieve aux­
otroph D. hansenii strains by both whole gene deletion and URA3 truncation. However,
restoration of prototrophy was not achieved in the mutant with a complete URA3 deletion,
when transformed with a URA3 harboring plasmid. Based on these results it cannot be
concluded whether the lack of prototrophy is caused by the gene variant or the auxotroph
strain itself. Therefore, an auxotroph strain with truncated URA3, was generated for future
studies.
In the prospect of evaluating the potential of CASCADE in D. hansenii, five strains were
obtained, each expressing mVenus from a different defined integration site. Moreover,
strains expressing respectively 2 or 3 copies of mVenus were also successfully gener­
ated. It was elucidated that the unsuccessful transformations of a strain containing 4
copies, was most likely caused by the sgRNA design. A new design was suggested for
future studies.
It was found that heterologous expression of mVenus had no significant impact on the
average growth rate of D. hansenii, independently of integration site and number of inte­
grations. However, the specific integration site was found to have major impact on the
obtained maximum expression level of YFP. The majority of integration sites resulted in
significantly different expression, with the exception of IS2 and IS5. These results em­
phasized the importance in choice of integration site.
At last it was concluded, that the addition of more integration sites expressing mVenus
does result in a higher maximum expression value. This can be considered proof of con­
cept for the principles behind the CASCADE system, as it shows that expression level is
dependent on gene copy number.

7
Future perspectives
This study advanced the progress, towards the future implementation of the CASCADE
system in D. hansenii. The continued development of the system will involve the charac­
terization of more integration sites; both in the aim of defining a more certain baseline for
expression, but also in the construction of strains with higher copy numbers than 3.
Another important aspect of implementation is to further characterize different counterse­
lectable markers in D. hansenii. Proper selection is an important tool in genetic engineer­
ing, and in the construction of a functional GA cassette.
The characterization of more integration sites and the construction of a GA cassette, would
enable the construction of GAS strains. Afterwards the gene amplification efficacy of
these strains could be evaluated using different GOIs, to characterize the full potential of
the CASCADE system in D. hansenii.
The CASCADE system could eventually help mitigate the implementation of D. hansenii in
major industrial processes, by enabling the stable and high expression of relevant genes
of interest.
