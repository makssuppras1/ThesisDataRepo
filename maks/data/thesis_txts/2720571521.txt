Abstract
Produced water (PW) from the extraction of oil and gas is one of the largest waste streams,
discharged directly to the marine environment. The large discharge volumes, and potential
impact on the environment have given rise to increasing concern. Currently the knowl-
edge on the long-term impact and degradation is scarce and the effect may vary greatly
depending on the discharge area. This study aimed to investigate the effect of inoculum
location on the marine biodegradation kinetics of chemicals discharged with produced wa-
ter, using 3 marine water samples as inoculum. The marine water samples were taken to
cover a salinity gradient and represents the seas surrounding Denmark; North Sea (Es-
bjerg, 32h), Kattegat (Gilleleje, 21h) and the Baltic Sea (Klintholm, 9h). A 60days
biodegradation experiment was conducted in three cycles using water samples from the
three locations as inoculum, and PW as stock solution. The experiment was conducted in
20mL closed systems and incubated at 10◦C. PW was added in a 1:200 dilution, to ensure
environmental relevant concentrations. Petroleum hydrocarbons (PHCs) in the PW mix-
ture were analysed and the biodegradation kinetics was determined by substance depletion
in biotic systems relative to abiotic systems. The analytical method was automated solid
phase microextraction coupled with gas chromatography and mass spectrometer as de-
tector (SPME-GC/MS) using a selected ion monitoring (SIM) method. Peak areas were
successfully obtained for 27 PHCs. In the Esbjerg (32h) and Gilleleje (21h) inoculums
all compounds were degraded within 60days, except butyldecalin, which did not degrade.
In the Klintholm (9h) inoculum 5 compounds were fully degraded and the remaining
22 compounds were degraded to 30-70%, except butyldecalin, which showed no degrada-
tion at Klintholm as well. The degradation half time (DegT50) in Esbjerg and Gilleleje
were significant similar for most compounds and there were a factor 2 difference between
the DegT50 of those 2 locations and Klintholm. Additionally, a microbial composition
analysis was conduced and showed that Esbjerg and Gilleleje had more similar microbial
composition as well. No effect of salinity on the biodegradation kinetics was observed,
however this might be due to potential pre-exposure from marine traffic at both Esbjerg
and Gilleleje. Results of this study was compared with PHC DegT50 values from fresh-
water and PW DegT50 values from the North Sea, which showed up to factor 5 difference
between locations. The results showed that compounds might be classified differently in
the persistence assessment depending on inoculum location, and highlights the effects and
importance of sampling location for biodegradation test and persistence assessment.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The Ocean can exist without us, but we cannot live without the ocean. It is the life-support
system of our plant and provides many vital services (NOAA, 2023). Most importantly
over half the oxygen we breath is provided by the ocean (United Nations, 2023). The
ocean regulates the global climate, helps mitigate climate change, by absorbing 25% of the
global anthropogenic CO2 emissions (Watson et al., 2020) and provides the livelihood and
main source of protein to nearly 3 billion people (Food and Agriculture Organization of the
United Nations, 2017). However the ocean is under pressure from numerous anthropogenic
stressors, such as over-exploitation, climate change and pollution (Griffen et al., 2016).
The effects of these stressors highly depends on the location as the different ecosystems
and organisms are acclimatized to specific environmental conditions, such as temperature,
pH and salinity (Smyth and Elliott, 2016; P¨ortner et al., 2014).
Discharge of Produced water (PW) from the oil and gas industry is one of the largest
waste streams, discharged directly to the marine environment (Neff et al., 2011). PW is
a mixture of formation and injection water, to which is added a wide range of process
chemicals in order to increase recovery rates and reduce unwanted conditions in the oil
and gas pipes (Hoek et al., 2022; TotalEnergies EP Danmark, 2020). After extraction
the oil and gas is separated from the PW, using separation chemicals, and PW is treated
to comply with regulations before the PW is discharged out into the ocean. In 2017 the
Danish PW discharge was about 25 million m3 (Danish Environmental Protection Agency
and OSPAR Commission, 2019). The concern of the effects of PW on the environment
has risen due to the large discharge volumes, known ecotoxicity of some PW constituents
and lack of knowledge on the long-term impact and degradation (Bakke et al., 2013).

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Aim and objectives
The aim of this study is to determine the effect of inoculum location on the biodegrada-
tion kinetics of produced Water (PW) at environmentally relevant concentrations. Three
locations in Danish marine water; North Sea, Kattegat, and Baltic Sea, were chosen to
cover a salinity gradient and represent the main seas surrounding Denmark. PW will be
used as stock solution and biodegradation kinetics of petroleum hydrocarbons (PHCs)
will be analysed as PW is a highly complex mixture and not all substances are known.
The biodegradation kinetics will be determined by measuring single substances in biotic
systems against abiotic systems, in order to obtain lag periods and biodegradation rate
constants. Furthermore, the aim is to compare the obtained results with biodegradation
kinetics preciously obtained for the same chemicals near emissions of petroleum chemicals
and in freshwater
Hypotheses
• There is a significant difference in biodegradation kinetics of PW chemicals between
Danish marine locations, across a salinity gradient.
• The degradation order of chemicals is consistent in the three inoculums.
• The biodegradation kinetics of marine water will be slower than that of freshwater
and marine water close to the discharge.

BACKGROUND
2
Background
2.1
Regulation and testing marine biodegradation of chemicals
2.1.1
Regulation
The chemical legislation “Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and restriction of Chem-
icals” (REACH) was implemented in 2007, and aims to ensure high levels of the protec-
tion of human health and the environment (European Commission, 2023; REACH online,
2023). REACH is based on the ”No data, no market” principle, which means that the
responsibility of managing chemical risks and documentation of chemical safety, lies on the
industry (European Commission, 2023). The registration and evaluation of chemicals is
single substance based and required for chemicals manufactured or imported at quantities
of ≥1 ton per year. The detail of information required for registration, depends on the
production and import volume, and for all chemicals produced at ≥10 tons per year, a
chemical safety assessment (CSA) is required (European Chemicals Agency, 2017c). The
CSA is used to assess the hazards and risks to human health and the environment, as well
as how to proper apply risk management measures for the substance (European Chemicals
Agency, 2017c). It includes assessment of persistent, bioaccumulative and toxicity (PBT)
and a very persistent and very bioaccumulative (vPvB) assessment (European Chemi-
cals Agency, 2011). The PBT and vPvB assessment is used to identify if a chemical has
the potential to cause long-term effects in the environment (European Chemicals Agency,
2017b). Especially vPvB chemicals are of concern, because they persist in the environment
and have a high possibility to accumulate in biota (European Chemicals Agency, 2023).
Assessment of persistency is very important, as it is the main driver of long-term exposure
of organisms. Once the chemicals enter the environment, they are very difficult to remove,
even if emissions are stopped. (European Commission, 2003; Cousins et al., 2019; Euro-
pean Chemicals Agency, 2017b). The assessment of persistency is based on degradation
data and can be either abiotic (e.g. hydrolysis and photolysis) or biotic (OECD, 2005a).
Biodegradation is the main removal process for most organic chemicals in the environment
(Birch et al., 2017; Birch et al., 2018). Knowledge and data regarding biodegradation ki-
netics are therefore an important part of water research, environmental risk assessment and
aquatic fate modeling (Birch et al., 2017; European Chemicals Agency, 2017b; Sjøholm
et al., 2021).

BACKGROUND
2.1.2
Testing marine Biodegradation
The assessment of biodegradability for regulatory purposes are conducted by standardised
OECD tests, either as simple screening test or higher tiered simulation tests, which are
divided into three types of test; Ready biodegradability test, inherent biodegradability test
and simulation test (European Chemicals Agency, 2017b; OECD, 2005a). The simulation
test aims to assess biodegradation kinetics at environmental relevant conditions, such
as fresh and marine water (European Chemicals Agency, 2017b; OECD, 2005a; OECD,
2005b).
For simulation tests (OECD 309) the degradation in an specific environment is simulated
by using a inoculum (e.g. fresh- and seawater) and by testing at environmental represen-
tative temperatures. Low concentrations ( 1µg/L to 100µg/L) of test substance should be
used in order to reflect those expected in the environment (European Chemicals Agency,
2017b; OECD, 2005a; OECD, 2005b). By using low concentrations, first order degradation
kinetics can be assumed. (OECD, 2005b).
2.2
Inoculum
Inoculum used for biodegradation tests is an environmental sample of indigenous media
(OECD, 2005a), meaning it will have the same physical and chemical characteristics as
the sampling location, as well as a microbial composition reflecting the sampling loca-
tion. These physical, chemical and biogical properties can potential enhance or limit the
biodegradation. A study by Howard et al. (2005) reviewing more than 200 studies of hy-
drocarbon (HC) biodegradation kinetics, found that biodegradation kinetics varies in the
environment and that this variation is a reflection of different environmental factors. Un-
derstanding the effect of different inoculums and different environmental factors affecting
the biodegradation kinetics is therefore important (Sjøholm et al., 2021; Kristensen et al.,
2015).
2.2.1
Environmental factors effecting biodegradation kinetics
Pre-exposure
It has previously been reported that adaptive microorganism groups which have previously
been exposed to HC pollution, displays much greater potential for degradation of hydro-
carbon, than that of groups which have not been exposed (Abdel-Shafy and Mansour,

2019; Kaur, 2019). The exposure to PHCs can result in a change in microbial composition
and density, as there will be an increase of hydrocarbon degrading microbes (Leahy and
Colwell, 1990). Abdel-Shafy and Mansour (2019) reports that the density of HC degraders
in water with pre-exposure, is around 10% of the microbial communities. Compared to
water without pre-exposure where only 1% of the microbial communities are HC degrades.
Nutrients
The availability of nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus is one of the most important
factors for biodegradation, as all living organisms need nutrients for growth, metabolism
and reproduction (Abdel-Shafy and Mansour, 2019; Kaur, 2019; Patey et al., 2008; Head
et al., 2006). In the ocean nutrient concentrations are typically very low in the surface
water due to high biological uptake, and increases with depth as sinking particulate matter
is remineralized to dissolved nutrients (Patey et al., 2008).
pH
The pH is an important environmental factor, as it affects the growth and functionality
of bacteria, as well as the availability of nutrients. Most bacteria grow at a pH range
between 6-8, with an optimal growth potential around pH 7, where the maximal hydro-
carbon degradation is also observed (Dadrasnia et al., 2019; Kaur, 2019; Abdel-Shafy and
Mansour, 2019; Al-Hawash et al., 2018).
Temperature
Temperature affects the biodegradation of PHC, both on the microbial and chemical side,
where it influences the physical and chemical properties of PHCs (Sjøholm et al., 2021; Al-
Hawash et al., 2018). Oil viscosity, partition coefficients, diffusion coefficients, solubility
in water, and volatilization, all change with temperature (Sjøholm et al., 2021).
For
the microbial side, temperature affects the metabolism rate, and the majority of PHC
degrading microbes have a temperature optimum ranging between 20◦C to 35◦C.
For
marine environments the optimal temperature for degradation ranges between 15◦C and
20◦C (Abdel-Shafy and Mansour, 2019; Kaur, 2019; Al-Hawash et al., 2018). Sjøholm et
al. (2021) have previously demonstrated that the difference in incubation temperature has
a big effect on the biodegradation kinetics. They found that the degradation half-times
(DegT50) of 34 test hydrocarbons, was twice as long for tests conducted at 12◦C compared
to tests conducted at 20◦C, using the same inoculum. Furthermore Sjøholm et al. (2022)
found that the microbial communities between summer and winter inoculum for the same

Salinity
The effect of salinity depends on the environment and organism type, as some groups are
more susceptible to salinity. Thus it have varied effect on biodegradation (Kothari et al.,
2014; Dadrasnia et al., 2019). However high salt concentrations can inhibit degradation,
because of osmosis effects (Kothari et al., 2014; Dadrasnia et al., 2019). Previous studies
have shown that there is a positive relationship between salinity and the degradation of
hydrocarbons, where the degradation increases with lower salinities (Al-Hawash et al.,
2018; Dadrasnia et al., 2019; Leahy and Colwell, 1990). Ward and Brock (1978) found a
great reduction in hydrocarbon metabolism rates, with increasing salinity, from 3.3% to
28.4%.
2.3
Produced water
Produced water (PW) is the largest waste stream from oil and gas production (Neff et al.,
2011; Al-Ghouti et al., 2019), and is a mixture of naturally accruing water trapped in
the oil and gas reservoir (formation water) and injection water, which is water injected to
the reservoir in order to maintain pressure and improve oil and gas recovery (Hoek et al.,
2022). The ratio of PW to oil (WOR) and PW to gas (WGR) during extraction vary
widely depending on well age, reservoir location and recovery method. Globally the WOR
is around 3-4 (Hoek et al., 2022; Al-Ghouti et al., 2019). For nearly depleted reservoirs
the volume of produced water could reach up to 98% of the total volume (Al-Ghouti et al.,
2019).
Before injection, the injection water is added different kind of process chemicals (PCs)
in order to increase recovery rates, reduce levels of hydrogen sulphide, reduce corrosion,
prevent bacteria build up and lowering the risk of leaks (TotalEnergies EP Danmark,
2020). The addition of PCs highly depends on the individual needs and type of operation
on each platform (Røe Utvik, 1999).

2.3.1
Composition of Produced water
PW is an extremely complex and inconsistent mixture, which vary widely from platform
to platform. It consists of both inorganic and organic compounds, in dissolved and partic-
ulate form, which either occur naturally or have been introduced as additives (Neff et al.,
2011; Nielsen et al., 2022; Al-Ghouti et al., 2019). Many factors such as the chemical com-
position of the oil and gas phases in the reservoir and PC added, as well as the geological
location, age and depth of the reservoir, affects the physical and chemical properties of
PW (Neff et al., 2011; Al-Ghouti et al., 2019). Crude oil alone can easily contribute over
100,000 individual compounds (Nielsen et al., 2022). No PW sample is alike because of
the many factors affecting the composition of PW and the composition is therefore very
sample specific. However the major constituents found in produced water are; organics,
primarily petroleum hydrocarbons (PHC), such as benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, and
xylenes (BTEX), polyaromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and phenols. Inorganic metals and
salts, naturally occurring radioactive material (NORM), as well as PCs, such as biocides,
emulsifiers and corrosion inhibitors (Hoek et al., 2022; Al-Ghouti et al., 2019; Alley et al.,
2011; Clark and Veil, 2009). Some of the most common inorganic compounds found in
PW are; chlorides, sodium, iron, lead, zinc, manganese, and barium (Alley et al., 2011;
Clark and Veil, 2009).
2.4
Petroleum Hydrocarbons
Petroleum hydrocarbons (PHCs) range from simple to complex substances and compose
primary of hydrocarbons, but can sometimes contain other impurities such as oxygen,
sulfur, and nitrogen (Ossai et al., 2020; Selley and Sonnenberg, 2015; Aminzadeh and
Dasgupta, 2013). PHC’s can be divided into three main groups of hydrocarbons: paraf-
finic, naphthenic and aromatic hydrocarbons (Selley and Sonnenberg, 2015; Speight, 2013).
The composition of these hydrocarbon groups depends on the different petroleum types,
and can vary greatly from type to type (Ossai et al., 2020; Atlas, 1981).
Paraffinic hydrocarbons are also known as saturated alkanes and are the simplest form of
hydrocarbons, with the general formula CnH2n+2. They are mostly found as straight chain
molecules (normal) in crude oil, but can also be branched chain molecules (isomeric). the
chemical and physical properties gradually change as −CH2 links are added to the chain,
with normal paraffins of the same molecular weight having higher boiling point, than the
boiling point of their isomeric counterpart (Speight, 2017; Selley and Sonnenberg, 2015;
Page 7 of 61
==PAGE:26==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
2
BACKGROUND
Fahim et al., 2010). Low molecular paraffins n < 5 are gases at normal temperatures and
pressures and paraffins with n = 5 −n = 154 are liquid, while high molecular paraffins
n > 15 will range from viscous liquids to solid waxes, as the n increases (Selley and
Sonnenberg, 2015).
Naphthenic hydrocarbons are also known as saturated cycloalkanes, with the general
formula CnH2n, and consist of at least one single bonded ring structure, which can have
one or more paraffinic side chains. The density and boiling points of naphthenes are higher
than those of their corresponding paraffines, even though they have the same carbon atoms.
All naphthenes are liquid at normal temperatures and pressures (Selley and Sonnenberg,
2015). Naphthenes are most commonly found as rings with five or six carbons atoms in
crude oil (Speight, 2017; Fahim et al., 2010).
Aromatic hydrocarbons consist of one or more double bonded benzene rings also called
aromatic rings, which have the formula C6H5. When aromatic hydrocarbons have more
than one aromatic ring, they are called polyaromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) (Speight, 2017;
Fahim et al., 2010). Both aromatic and polyaromatics can have derivatives around the ring
structure, such as toluene (C6H5CH3) and the isomeric dimethyl benzene (CH3C6H4CH3)
(Speight, 2017). Monoarmatics are most commonly found in the light oil fraction, and
some of the most commonly found mono aromatics PHC are the BTEXs. The PAHs are
typically found in the heavy part of petroleum oil (Selley and Sonnenberg, 2015; Fahim
et al., 2010).
Both mono aromatics and PAH are liquid at normal temperatures and
pressures (Selley and Sonnenberg, 2015).
2.4.1
Biodegradation of Petroleum Hydrocarbons
As with most organic compounds biodegradation is the main removal process of PHC
in the environment (Sjøholm et al., 2021), and aerobic oxidation of PHC is the most
common type of degradation (Head et al., 2006).
More than 150 genera of bacteria,
fungi, algae and cyanobacteria have been recognized as hydrocarbon degraders (Deborah
Gnana Selvam and Thatheyus, 2019; Head et al., 2006), with bacteria being the major
degrader in the marine environment (Head et al., 2006; Abdel-Shafy and Mansour, 2019;
Kaur, 2019), and in particular, bacteria of the Gammaproteobacteria class (Head et al.,
2006; Reunamo et al., 2013). Paraffins can be degraded by a wide range of bacteria and
have been recognized as the most easily biodegradable. The ability to degrade the more
complex PHC such as PAHs is less common, and PAHs are among the least biodegradable

exceptions have been observed, such as Kristensen et al. (2015) where different degradation

Biodegrading test
In this study the simulation test OECD 309 with adjustments suggested by Birch et

was avoided and the dilution best represented environmental concentrations.

3.7.2
Solid phase micro-extraction
Solid phase micro-extraction (SPME) is a simple, sensitive and reliable technique used
for extraction of compounds from a sample matrix, without the need for solvents and
is an excellent chose when dealing with low concentrations (Souza-Silva et al., 2015; Ul-
rich, 2000). Doing SPME a polymer-coated extraction fiber is introduced to the sample
matrix either by exposing it to the sample media or headspace. When the fiber is intro-
duced a partitioning equilibrium between the compounds in sample matrix and the fiber
is established and compounds are considered extracted when the equilibrium reaches 95%
(Souza-Silva et al., 2015; Ulrich, 2000; Mills and Walker, 2000). In this experiment the
compounds were extracted from the headspace, where the penetration depth of the fiber
was 20 mm and the extraction time 20 min. After extraction the compounds was injected
to the GC by transferring the fiber to the GC injection port. Because the extraction of
the compounds depends on the partitioning coefficient between the fiber and the sample
matrix for each compound, the relative response between abiotic and biotic test systems
were used, in order to make the degradation results comparable between compounds.
3.7.3
Gas chromatography - mass spectrometry
Gas chromatography (GC) is an analytical technique used to separate constituents in a
mixture and mass spectrometry (MS) is used to detect specific compounds. The com-
bination of GC and MS make it an perfect technique for qualitative and quantitative
determination of low concentration volatile and semi-volatile organic compounds (Sned-
don et al., 2007).
GC is a separation method in which compounds in a mixture is separated by volatilization.
The compounds are heated at the injection port, were they volatilize and the gaseous
compounds are then transported thought a coated column (Stationary phase) by a carrier
gas (mobile phase). As the compounds travel though the column they separate depending
on their individual partitioning between the mobile and stationary phases, which leads to
Page 19 of 61
==PAGE:38==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
3
METHODS
the compounds appearing at the end of the column at different retention times (RT). GC
can only be used for volatile or semi volatile compounds, as it requires the compounds to
be on gaseous form (Sparkman et al., 2011; Harris, 2010; Niessen, 2001).
MS is a detection method which can be used to identify individual compounds. As the
compounds each reaches the end of the GC column they inter the MS detector, where the
compounds are ionized, by blasting them with electrons, causing them to fragment, into
ion fragments unique to each compound. The fragments are then pushed into the detector,
where the signal intensity of the fragments with the same mass to charge ration (M/Z) is
measured. This signal intensity of the fragments can then be represented in a total ion
chromatogram (TIC), where the summed M/Z is a function of the RT (Sneddon et al.,
2007; Harris, 2010). As each compound has a unique fingerprint, the detector can be set
to only detect the signal intensity of specific compound, by giving it RT and a quantifier,
qualifier ion for each compound, this method is called selected ion monitoring (SIM).
In this experiment the sample was introduced to the GC by injection of the loaded SPME
fiber to the GC injection port, for 10 min at 40◦C, in order to desorb all the com-
pounds. The GC cycle were then run for 63 min where the temperature would increase
by 15◦C/min. By the end of the cycle all compound in the SIM method used for this
analysis would have been detected by the MS detector, and the TIC diagrams were used
for further data treatment.

Detection limit
The Detection limit were based on the chromatography. A peak area cutoff was set for each
compound, which represented approximately three times the baseline, and peaks below
this cutoff were integrated to the exact retention time, best as possible. Data points where
the peak area of ≥2/3 of the triplicates were below this cutoff, were considered under the
detection limit. Data points that were consisted below detection limit is represented by
open symbols in the degradation plots.

Discussion
The main aim of this study was to investigate the effect of inoculum location on marine
biodegradation kinetics and the results shows that there is a significant difference in the
DegT50 between Klintholm and Gilleleje and Klintholm and Esbjerg, however Gilleleje and
Esbjerg was observed to be significant similar for most compounds (figure 8). Furthermore
the microbial analysis showed that the microbial composition of Esbjerg and Gilleleje was
more similar as well and possible reasons for these similarities are elaborated further
in this section. Butyldecalin showed no biodegradation in all three inoculums. but had
previously been shown to biodegradate in seawater by Birch et al. (2018) and in freshwater
by Sjøholm et al. (2021) which indicates that it does biodegrade, however information on
biodegrdation kinetic data of butyldecalin is scarce, and more information is needed on this
compound in order to better understand its biodegradation. It was however speculated
that the observed no degradation could be due to the kind of microbes present in the test
systems, as the main type of hydrocarbon found in the PW sample was aromatics, which
could promote higher growth of aromatic degrading microbes than naphtenic degrading
microbes.
Prior to this study it was hyposticed that the degradation order of PHC groups for the
three inoculums would be consistent. The degradation order was interpreted based on the
DegT50 for all compounds within each PHC group (figure 9), and a general degradation
Page 33 of 61
==PAGE:52==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
5
DISCUSSION
order for the three locations could be described as paraffic < naphtenic < aromatic/ poly
aromatic/ naphtenic aromatic, despite of slight difference in the order of aromatic, poly
aromatic and naphtenic aromatic between locations. This indicates that the degradation
order is consistent between the three location, and that the variation in locations in this
study did not affect the order of degradation.
In section 2.4.1 a general degradation order for PHCs was described as n-alkanes (paraffic)
< isoalkanes (iso paraffic) < low-molecular weight aromatics <cyclic alkanes (naphtenic)
< PAHs (Kristensen et al., 2015; Leahy and Colwell, 1990; Birch et al., 2018). When
comparing the degradation order of this study with the general degradation order it does
roughly agree with the exception of the napthenic.
In this study only one napthenic
was found and tested: bicyclohexyl, which previously have been showed to have a lower
DegT50 than other napthenic and deviate from the general degradation order by Birch et
al. (2017). The order of degradation within each PHC group have previously been shown
to vary a lot (Birch et al., 2018), and degradation order for single compounds are therefore
not always enough to conclude anything for the group as a whole. This indicates that the
degradation order of this study might be more adhered with the general degradation than
first assumed.
5.1
Microbial composition
The degradation of organic compounds depends on the microbial composition. The initial
microbial density in the inoculum, might effect the length of the lag phase and efficiency.
Caparello and Larock (1975) have previously found that higher microbial density yields
shorter lag phase and Otiniano et al. (2022) observed a positive linear relationship between
the inoculum concentration and the degradation efficiency of a diesel mixture. The effect
of initial microbial density might explain why the lag phase for most compounds are
longer at Gilleleje than that of Esbjerg, despite the two locations having similar microbial
composition, as Esbjerg has a factor 2 higher density of qPCR (table 3). However this
effect on lag phase of inoculum concentration, seems only to be true when the microbial
composition of the inoculums are similar. As Klintholm has the same amount of qPCR
as Esbjerg, but longer lag phases for most compounds compared to both Esbjerg and
Gilleleje. No relationship between inoculum concentration and biodegradation efficiency
was observed, as Esbjerg and Klintholm have similar qPCR, but different DegT50 values,
and Gilleleje and Esbjerg have similar DegT50 values but different qPCR. This indicates
Page 34 of 61
==PAGE:53==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
5
DISCUSSION
that in this study it is not the amount of initial microbes, but rather the presence of
specific microbes that is most important for the biodegradation. The combination of the
PCA analysis and the biodegradation test could indicated that Esbjerg and Gilleleje have
more of the specific PHC degrading microbes than Klintholm.
5.2
Factors effecting the biodegradation and microbial composition
As described in section 2.2 many factors might affect the biodegradation and microbial
composition of the inoculum, and in this section the effect of the different factors on
biodegradation in the three inoculums is discussed.
Factors such as temperature, nu-
trients and pH can quickly be dismissed.
All three experiments was conduced at the
same temperature and the inoculums were sampled within the same month, which makes
temperature a nonviable parameter. The water quality measurements showed that the
nutrient level and pH was very similar between the three locations (see table 2), which
makes it highly unlikely that theses factors would impact the biodegradation differently
in the three inoculums.
Salinity
It was initially hypothesised that there would be a salinity effect on the biodegradation,
where the compounds would degrade fastest in the least saline water and slowest in the
most saline water. Other studies have previously found that the biodegradation decreases
with increasing salinity (Al-Hawash et al., 2018; Dadrasnia et al., 2019; Leahy and Colwell,
1990). That was not the case for this study, as the highest DegT50 values was obtained in
the low salinity water (Klintholm) and a general trend across all compounds showed that
DegT50 values of the high salinity water (Esbjerg) and medium salinity (Gilleleje) water
was similar (figure 8). This together with the fact that Esbjerg and Gilleleje have highly
similar microbial composition, could indicate that something other that salinity drives the
microbial composition and biodegradation kinetics found in this study.
Pre-exposure
Pre-exposure have previously been reported to increase the biodegradtion rates in both
marine and fresh waters (Abdel-Shafy and Mansour, 2019; Kaur, 2019; Birch et al., 2017)
and Birch et al. (2017) found that the DegT50 of pre-exposed lakes and streams (Urban
and WWTP Impacted) were up to one magnitude lower than that of non-exposed lake and
stream (rural). Both the Esbjerg and Gilleleje inoculum where taken offshore coat areas
where there is a discharge of contaminated water over discharge limits, to the ocean. Due
Page 35 of 61
==PAGE:54==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
5
DISCUSSION
to high dilutions in the ocean and other oceanic factors such as current and turbulence, it
seems unlikely that these discharges would effect the microbial communities found at the
sampling locations, 8 km offshore.
Another pre-exposure which might effect the biodegradation is the marine traffic around
the sampling location, as marine traffic can impact the marine environment in multiple
ways including chemical pollution (Rodr´ıguez-Grim´on et al., 2021). In general there is a
very high density of marine traffic around Denmark (figure 1), and especially the Gilleleje
location, might be affect by the marine traffic. There is a very high density of shipping
routes, as Gilleleje is just outside Øresund, which as a part of the Danish straits, is one
of the only outlet from the Blatic Sea (Biresselioglu et al., 2014). Esbjerg is the center of
the Danish offshore oil and wind operations, and a lot of shipping traffic goes to and from
Esbjerg harbour every year (Esbjerg Kommune, 2023). The Esbjerg sampling location is
just outside the main shipping route to Esbjerg (figure 1). On the contrary the Klintholm
location is a couple of km away from any of the big shipping routes in the area, and
therefore might be less affected by pre-exposure from shipping pollution.
This might
explain why the microbial composition and biodegradation between Gilleleje and Esbjerg
was similar, while the Klintholm location was differed, as both Gilleleje and Esbjerg are
potentially pre-exposed by shipping traffic, where as Klintholm might not be.

well. Furthermore the variation in biodegradation kinetics between studies with different

Howard et al., 2005), and in this study the variation between locations was within a factor
10 as well. The choice of sampling location is therefore important for biodegradation test.

5.4
Which inoculum is best to use for biodegradation test?
In an ideal world the persistincy of compounds should be tested at all possible environ-
mental conditions, however this is impossible, as many factors drives the environmental
conditions and it would be both very costly and time consuming, as well. Instead inoculum
location should be chosen depending on which kind of assessment the biodegradation test
is used for, which is recommended in the OECD 309 guideline as well (OECD, 2005b). It
would therefore be recommended to use the most conservative location for test used for
chemical safety assessment, as it is carried out to asses the general hazards and risks to
human health and the environment from chemicals. However the CSA is carried out by
the chemical producers, who wish to sell their products, and it might therefore be benefi-
cial to find a middle ground, where the conservative location is also representative for the

5.5
Future work
In this study no effect of salinity was found, which might be due to other factors being
more dominant, and further investigation on which factors are the most dominant for
biodegradation, could be conducted, in order to avoid confounding factors, when studying
biodegradation.
As mention previously the findings of this study indicates that pre-exposure could have
a high impact on biodegradation kinetics and further research on this topic is needed,
in order to asses the effects. This could be done by different type of investigations. It
have previously been tested for freshwater (Birch et al., 2017), and a similar approach
could be applied to the marine environment, where known pre-exposed areas for instants
harbours, could be tested against areas with low pre-exposure. Another approach could
be to make 2 identical artificial inoculums, where one of them have been pre-exposed to
the test compounds over a period of time, before the biodegradation test is performed.
The microbial analysis showed that the microbial composition of Esbjerg and Gilleleje is
similar, and further analysis of data on the microbial composition from ‘DNA Sense’, could
be conduced in order to characterize potential PHC degraders, and further understand the
similarities and differences at the different locations.
This study uses PHCs as model substances for PW, however as described in section 2.3.1
PW is a highly complex mixture and other chemicals found in the PW mixture might
be persistent. Further investigation on the biodegradation and persistence of PW chem-
icals are therefore needed in order to asses the effects of discharge of PW to the marine
environment.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
In this study the marine biodegradation kinetics of chemicals, more specific petroleum
hydrocarbons discharged with produced water was determined at three different locations
with varying salinity ( 9h, 21h and 32h). For all three locations biodegradation kinetic
data was successfully obtained by running biodegradation test on produced water in a 1:200
dilution, and fitting a nonlinear degradation model to experimental data. Additionally a
microbial composition analysis was conduced on the initial water samples from all three
locations. All compounds degraded fully within 60 days, except butyldecalin, which did
not degraded for Esbjerg (32h) and Gilleleje (21h). At the Klintholm (9h) location 5
out of 27 compounds were fully degraded. The remaining 22 compounds were degraded
to 30-70%, except butyldecalin, which showed no degradation at Klintholm as well. The
results showed that the degradation half-time (DegT50) of Gilleleje and Esbjerg were
significant similar for most compounds, and that there were a factor 2 difference between
the two locations and the Klintholm location, with Klintholm having the highest DegT50
values. Additionally the microbial composition analysis showed that Esbjerg and Gilleleje
also had more similar microbial composition. This study showed no effect of salinity on
the biodegradation kinetics, as otherwise seen in previous studies, and it was concluded
that the difference and similarities could be caused by potential pre-exposure from marine
traffic in both Esbjerg and Gilleleje. The degradation order of petroleum hydrocarbon
groups were similar between all three locations and aligned with previous studies. The
DegT50 values obtained from this study were compared with values from freshwater and
the North Sea, which showed a general trend of freshwater having higher DegT50 values,
than those of Klintholm, Gilleleje and Esbjerg. Furthermore the comparison showed that
the DegT50 values of North Sea inoculum was a factor 3 higher than those of Esbjerg
and Gilleleje, and a factor 5 higher than Klintholm. The microbial composition of the
North Sea, showed a high abundance of Colwellia which have been shown to degrade
hydrocarbons. The findings showed that 8 out of the 27 compounds would be classified
as persistent at the Klintholm location, but not at the Gilleleje and Esbjerg locations.
Furthermore the results showed that a factor 5 difference would mean that compounds
with a half lives above 12 days at the North Sea location, would potential be classified as
persistent at the Klintholm location. These findings highlights the effects and importance
of the choice of sampling location for biodegradation test and persistence assessment.

Project Plan  
Master Thesis: Biodegradation kinetics of Produced water in Danish marine waters - effect of 
inoculum location 
Tina Kenworth Drist-Jensen (s164287) 
 
Aim of the project  
The aim of the project is to determine biodegradation kinetics of Produced water (PW), at environmentally 
relevant concentrations, at three locations in Danish marine waters: North Sea, Kattegat, and Baltic Sea. 
The three locations have been chosen, to cover a salinity gradient, and because they represent the three 
main seas surrounding Denmark. The biodegradation kinetics will be determined by measuring lag periods 
and biodegradation rate constants for all known chemicals in a Produced water sample. Furthermore, the 
aim is to compare the obtained results with biodegradation kinetics obtained for the same chemicals near 
emissions of petroleum chemicals and in freshwater.     
Hypotheses  
• 
There is a significant difference in biodegradation kinetics of PW between Danish marine locations, 
across a salinity gradient.   
• 
The degradation order of PHC is consistent in the three inoculums.  
• 
The biodegradation kinetics of (nonexposed/clean) marine water will be lower than that of 
freshwater and preexposed marine water. 
Description of experiments  
The experiments will be partition-based, done by spiking of diluted produced water, to ensure low 
environmental relevant concentrations in the three inoculums. Each experiment will run over 28 days, and 
a total of 7 analysis points will be taking for each of the locations. The measurement method will be done 
with fully automated SPME and GCMS.  
 Experiment steps 
1) Collection of inoculums at the location  
2) Set up of experiment (lates 24hours after the inoculum have been collected) 
3) Take out samples for analysis, at the 7-analysis point over a period of 28 days. 
Project timeline  
The project timeline is subject to change, based on possibilities of getting out and sampling inoculums at 
the three locations, as well as availability of the SPME and GCMS
