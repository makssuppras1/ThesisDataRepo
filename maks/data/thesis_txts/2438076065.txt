Abstract
In a world of interconnections, companies’ supply chains are more and more complex. Their
optimization becomes a key priority for businesses. In the same time massive amounts of data are
made available and Big Data appears as a new driver of world economic and industry changes.
Machine Learning algorithms beneﬁt from them, extracting information for better decision making.
This paper deals with Machine Learning applications on Supply Chain. Based on supervised,
unsupervised and association rules algorithms, several problems from diﬀerent parts of the chain
are explored through four case studies: demand forecast, distributors network, warehouses storing
and new product launching.
First, external data (in this case temperature) are integrated to demand forecasts model, leading in
some cases to signiﬁcant improvements thanks to several well-chosen regressions.
Moreover, retailer networks are optimized through a model predicting retailers revenues based
on demographic and geographical data. Diﬀerent supervised classiﬁers (Random Forest, Nearest
Neighbors, Logistic Regression) have been combined for this purpose, predicting with 70% accuracy
the class of revenues a new agency would get in a certain zone.
Furthermore, to deal with current E-Business growth, new warehouse designs are investigated in
this paper. Using association rules (Apriori algorithm) and unsupervised clustering algorithms,
new ways of disposing articles has been suggested, potentially doubling traditional article picking
performance.
Finally, the quantity of new product sold during the introduction phase is predicted thanks to
supervised classiﬁers (Random Forest, Logistic Regression) and key characteristics for product
success are uncovered.
This paper demonstrates the high relevance of Machine Learning for strategical decision support
in Supply Chain.
Keywords: Machine Learning, Big data, Optimization, Supply Chain, Retailer Network, Fore-
casting, Warehouse Design

CHAPTER1
Introduction
This master thesis was conducted as a full-time internship at Diagma, a top French Supply Chain
consulting company.
1.1
Diagma, Supply Chain expert
Supply Chain is the sequence of processes involved in the production and distribution of goods
and services from raw materials manufacturers until retailers and end customers. It connects agents,
transportation and storage activities through sharing of information, planning and processes.
Diagma acts to improve customers process and ﬂow management in order to provide an optimal
Supply Chain organization and reduce costs signiﬁcantly.
1.2
What is Big Data?
Big data analytics examine large amounts of data to uncover hidden patterns, correlations and other
insights. The concept of analyzing data has been used for decades, but often manually. However,
the amount of data available today enables algorithms to understand physical phenomenon behind
the data. This method of feeding algorithms with data is called Machine Learning. The new
beneﬁts brought by Machine Learning are speed and eﬃciency.
Whereas a few years ago a business would have gathered information, run analytics and extract
information that could be used for future decisions, today that business can identify insights for
immediate decisions. The ability to work faster gives companies a new competitive advantage.
1.3
Integration of AI in Supply Chain
The hype around Big Data is unprecedented.
The expectations are really high on Artiﬁcal
Intelligence’s disruption. However, the current level of AI integration in industry is quite low. If
some channels like Finances and Health care have already explored advanced topics, most of the
industrial sectors have not taken the step. AI applications in Supply Chain are really promising
since it involves multiple stakeholders sharing lots of data. There are many new opportunities to
optimize Supply chain through AI. In Figure 1.1 diﬀerent Supply Chain parts and potential Big
Data applications are shown. The Case Studies investigated further are covering most of the parts
of the Chain.

2.1
Literature Review
Most of the literature concerning Machine Learning in Supply Chain focuses on improving demand
forecasts by replacing traditional methods with Machine Learning algorithms.
Carbonneau and al. in [1] explored this topic and found out that neural networks and support
vector machines outperform in accuracy traditional moving average and exponential smoothing
forecasting model. However, the gain is not signiﬁcant compared to the increase in computational
cost.
Shahrabi and al. in [2] and Sun and al. in [3] studied the same topic, applied respectively in car
and fashion industry and found the same results.
Most of innovative industrial companies are probably already using AI techniques for diﬀerent
purposes in Supply Chain but few publications are available on the Internet. It seems like it is a
strategic topic for industries that prefer keeping this knowledge to gain a competitive advantage.
Some of such these AI techniques are presented in the next sections and then used in the following
Case Studies to improve Supply Chain.
2.2
Unsupervised Learning
Unsupervised learning is the learning of behavior similarities in the data in order to group
them accordingly into clusters. This approach is what a new-born baby faces when he learns to
distinguish cats and dogs without knowing what is an animal. The ground truth, or label, is the
variable assessing what the object studied is, the target to predict. In Supervised Learning, the
ground truth is given to train the model, but not in Unsupervised Learning. The ground truth is
unknown, so data are "unlabeled", there is no evaluation of output accuracy, data are compared to
each other, groups are formed without knowing apriori based on what exactly these groups are
diﬀerent. Such methods are often used in fraud detection and market segmentation.
In Supply Chain Management unsupervised learning can be used in numerous cases to ﬁnd
patterns in the data.
Several algorithms exist and are used in the literature. It is hard to know a-priori which algorithm
is going to perform well on your set of data, so several should be tried and compared before
selecting one. Unsupervised Learning is used in case study Chapter 5 to group similar e-Business
orders and in Chapter 6 to group products on similar sales proﬁl
In order to compare data between them, ﬁrst and foremost, an appropriate metric must be used.

2.2.2
Distance for Time Series Clustering
Distance measures can also provide a quantiﬁcation for the similarity between time-series. Applied
to time series, this Mahalanobis distance presents several drawbacks. Indeed it compares only
time series of the same length, it does not handle well outliers and is not able to handle time shift
between series.

Dynamic Time Wrapping is another distance measure, introduced three decades ago for sound
processing. It gives more robustness to the similarity value between two time series. Details about
the algorithm must be found in Zhang’s article [5]. It selects for each point i in time series t the
closest point j in the time serie r (in a window range of ϵ, i.e j cannot be further than i ± ϵ ).
The diﬀerence between Euclidean and DTW distances is shown in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Diﬀerence between DTW distance and Euclidean
Be t and r two time series of size (m, n) and (i, j) ∈Rm×n. D(i, j) is the distance between the two
series t[1 : i], r[1 : j] with the best alignment.
DTW(i, j) can be deﬁned by an iterative method:
D(1, 1) = |t(1) −r(1)|
(2.4)
D(i, j) = |t(i) −r(j)| + min(D(i −1, j −1), D(i −1, j), D(i, j −1))

This distance will be used in Chapter 6 to group sales curves of similar proﬁle.
2.2.3
K-means clustering
K-means algorithm is iterative and assigns each data point to one of K groups based on data
similarity.
Centroids are deﬁned as cluster’s center of gravity. They are ﬁrst placed randomly, and, step by
step their position is updated, which updates as well the data taken in the cluster, until convergence.
These steps are shown in Figure 2.2.
2.2.4
Hierarchical clustering
Hierarchical clustering consists in merging one by one the data according to a criterion. The most
commonly used is Ward’s criterion. It merges the pair of clusters in which, when merged, the sum
of distances between points is minimal, as in Figure 2.3a. It provides the minimum increase in
within-cluster variance. This can be visualized through a dendrogram as in Figure 2.3b, showing
where the merging of two clusters is the most "costly" in variance increase.

Density Based Clustering
DB Scan (density based spatial clustering of applications with noise) is a clustering algorithm, based on
the calculation of density to group data together, found by Ester in 1996 [6]. As shown in Figure 2.4,
based on two given parameters NbPoints (integer) and Eps (ﬂoat), for each data, if at least NbPoints
other data are in a range Eps then these data belong to the same cluster. If the number of points
around is less than NbPoints then this data is considered as an Edge and the cluster stops here. If
there is nothing around, the data is considered as an outlier. This algorithm is autonomous because
intrinsically decides on the number of clusters and it handles well outliers. It relies however on
the strong hypothesis that clusters have the same density. Therefore it does not apply to every
kind of dataset.

2.3
Supervised Learning
2.3.1
Classiﬁcation algorithm
Classiﬁcation is a two step process to predict class label from new data. The ﬁrst step is the training
phase: the model is fed with data having a known label and the classiﬁcation is applied on these
data called "training dataset". Then the model is corrected according to the label of these data,
parameters are chosen to get the best ﬁt. In second step this trained model is tested on a predeﬁned
test dataset to measure the performance. The gap between the real and predicted labels is the
model error.
Supervised learning is used in many sectors, every time a prediction can be made. Some examples
are customer proﬁling, image and speech recognition. It is a very eﬃcient way to build suitable
machine learning models since it does not need to be fed with tons of data. Moreover, it is very
handy for explaining (and quantifying) links between variables.
A lot of diﬀerent classiﬁcation algorithms exist in the literature. The most used are presented in
this section and will be used in the case studies Chapter 3, Chapter 4 and Chapter 6.
Neural Networks algorithms are within the most important topic of current research. They are not
investigated in this paper since they belong to Deep Learning and are not necessary to implement
in the present case studies. It requires several thousands of data. Supply chains are not mature
enough to provide such an amount.
2.3.2
K Nearest Neighbor Algorithm
As presented by Sakshi in [7], the Nearest Neighbor (NN) rule assigns the closest known neighbor’s
class to the data whose label is unknown. This algorithm is the most simple to compute and is
intuitive so the results are easy to interpret. However it does not handle well complex patterns in
the data. It is a good algorithm to start with.
2.3.3
Decision Tree
As presented by Song in [8], a decision tree is a predictive model going from observations about an
item (represented in the branches) to conclusions about the item’s target value (represented in the
leaves). At every step, the algorithm chooses the variable and the value splitting the best the data
into the diﬀerent predeﬁned classes. In Figure 2.5 an example is given. A series of variable values
lead to a ﬁnal choice (target value).
The best variable and its value are chosen to maximize the purity gain. "Gini impurity" is mainly
used: IG = 1 −P p2
i , pi being the probability of belonging to class i in the leaf considered.
Decision trees tend to overﬁt, which means adapt the model too much to the training data, so
predict poorly unknown data (performs really well on the training set but poorly on the test set).
Therefore, this method alone is not robust. Combining several decision trees would give a stronger
robustness. This is the purpose of Random Forest.

2.3.4
Random Forest
As explained by Breiman in [9], Random Forests combine many classiﬁcation trees. To classify a
new object from an input vector, the input vector is put down each tree in the forest. The forest
chooses the class having the most votes over all the classiﬁcation by each trees in the forest. Each
tree is grown with a subset of features and a subset of samples, both being taken randomly with
replacement in the dataset. Selecting diﬀerent subset of features and samples avoid correlation
between the trees, which would increase the subjectivity of the vote and therefore increase the
error.
2.3.5

target. Although in classiﬁcation the target is discrete, in a regression task, the target is continuous.
The goal is here to minimize the error (mean squared error for example) between the target and
the prediction. To be speciﬁc, a regression analysis estimates the value of the dependant variable
given the independent variables.
If regressions are used to predict values of a target, it also gives quantitative insight into the
importance of each variable in the prediction process. Several kind of regression methods exist:

2.3.8
Feature selection
When there is a high dimension (a lot of diﬀerent variables) it is interesting to select only the most
important variables. The feature selection adds and removes predictors as needed for each step.
The two most common algorithms are:
• Forward selection starts with the most signiﬁcant predictor in the model and adds variable
at each step. The variable increasing the performance the most is then kept.
• Backward elimination starts with all predictors in the model and removes the least signiﬁcant
variable at each step.
The model with the best performance is ﬁnally kept.
Otherwise, most of the Machine Learning algorithms are calculating feature importance during
the training phase to classify or evaluate them. In the literature, feature importance calculated
through Logistic Regression and Random Forest is commonly used. Indeed when a Random
Forest is ﬁtted, the algorithm calculates the relevance of each features (purity gain) and output the
variable importance in the ﬁnal decision. By keeping the top values, the most important features
are selected. Logistic Regression uses coeﬃcient attached to every feature, like a Linear Regression.
These coeﬃcients give a nice overview of variable importances.
Principal Component Analysis: Deﬁnition
A Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is usually implemented to reduce the dimension of a system.
PCA is the result of an advanced mathematical demonstration requiring some knowledge. A quick
overview is given in this section since this method is very brieﬂy used in this thesis, in Chapter
4. More detailed explanations can be found in Morup’s book [4]. Principal component analysis
is a method using an orthogonal transformation to convert a set of possibly correlated features
into linearly uncorrelated variables called principal components. The principal components are
deﬁned in order to capture the largest possible variance of the data. The resulting vectors are an
uncorrelated orthogonal basis set. These principal components are in fact the eigenvectors of the
covariance matrix. Using the singular value decomposition, it can be written :
X = USW
where U and W are orthogonal matrices made of singular vectors. S is a rectangular diagonal
matrix of singular values of X. T = US is the transformed matrix of X in the new basis of principal
components. T should contain as many information as X but compacted on the ﬁrst vectors of the
basis.

Introduction
If forecasting is achieving better and better performance, errors remain frequent. It is commonly

In a world with an exponential amount of data available, how could we use all these new

Social media, political changes, social movements, climate changes are examples of exogenous

In this Chapter is explored:
[Q1] To what extent do Big Data analytics improve Demand Forecasts?
Demand Forecast will be improved by the integration of exogenous data ("data from the outside",
other than sales history). A case study has been led on juices and syrup sales in France by
an international company, here named Tyrup1. Some of the sold products are known to be
weather-sensitive. Therefore, the exogenous series chosen in this case study is temperature in

Relative work

In the following case study it is important to evaluate a week to week temperature change impact

stocks full of syrup products and are ready to absorb the demand surplus. The temperature peak is
expected and an addition of several degrees is not going to aﬀect the sales. However, at the end of
the summer, demand is not expected to increase suddenly. So when a peak of temperature occurs
at the end of the summer, our new model must work better.
This can be seen by comparing the summer of the two years on which the test has been made, 2016
on Figure 3.3c and 2017 on Figure 3.3e.

Dynamic Time Warping distance associated with several clustering algorithms.

score of 0.27. Results of clustering are displayed in Figure 3.4.

To what extent do Big Data analytics improve Demand Forecasts?
In this case study a focus have been made on the question: To what extent integration of weather
data can improve Demand Forecasts ?
It has been shown that:
• Product weather sensitivity can be assessed easily if the product is more than 5 years old.
This can be done by the study of correlation between residuals of usual model of sales and
temperature deviation.
• Only some products are weather sensitive
• Only fewer products improve the forecasting results thanks to the model.
• The model is reliable only during summer.
• The study of the type of summer improves the accuracy of forecasts. When there is no peak
of heat at the beginning of the summer, the model is better performing.
• During a summer belonging to the appropriate cluster, the summer forecast of some products
is improved up to a factor 67% and most of the time more than 40%.

4.1
Introduction
Stores are the last link of Supply Chains but not the least important. Choice of appropriate sales
point network is a key business plan topic. The problem is : Which are the most proﬁtable
locations?
A classic Operation Research approach would be to minimize the distance to end customers.
However the multiplicity of customers is often constraining: they can be corporate or private, local
or international. These segments are all very diﬀerent and can make the simple "shortest distance
to customer" model wrong. There is a need to integrate many variables to model a business.
Moreover, most of the today’s successful companies have numerous agencies through several
countries. The use of Big Data seems relevant in such cases: To what extent do Big Data analytics
shape a better retailer network?
MisterCar1 is a rental company owning 345 agencies across Germany and planning to open even
more. The commercial director of MisterCar found interesting to rely on the information gathered
through this existing powerful network shown in Figure 4.1 linked to many accessible extra data
to make the best choices for next agencies creation.
4.2
The problem
Two main problems are raised by the company and are discussed in this project:
• Is it possible to forecast MisterCar agencies revenues based on the characteristics of their location
only?
• Where are the best locations to maximize revenues?
4.3
Related work on the subject
Krause-Traudes and al. in Spatial data mining for retail sales forecasting [16] have also explored the
use of spatial data to forecast the turnover of big food retail company. Relying on demographical
and economical attributes, a Support Vector Regression model on these data outperformed the
Huﬀ’s gravitation model. This classical model calculates the turnover mainly based on distances
ratio between potential customers and agencies.
Our problem is very similar, so the use of similar methods has been investigated in the Machine
Learning methods considered.
Except this one, very few literature is available on the subject even though a lot of companies must
have their own model to take decision.

4.11
Conclusion on case study performance
To what extent do Big Data analytics shape a better retailer network?
First, Diagma’s strategy was to cut the data in small pieces in order to isolate relationships. 36
regression were summed to build a ﬁnal model. This resulted in a high mean standard error
(500Ke) after a six-month long project. The use of Big Data analytics led to a reduction of half the
error after a month-long project. The steps implemented (on train set, before test) were:
• Oversampling of the dataset to balance the classes
• Selection of the relevant variables
• Classiﬁcation combining several classiﬁers: 67% of accuracy
• Regression on each class to get a value estimating the revenues: 378 K emean standard error
The results are better than what human intuition would give if not expert in the ﬁeld. Although it
is a powerful visualization tool, this is not reliable enough to take strategical decisions entirely on
it. Further investigations need to be made after analysis of the results given by this model.
How could this ﬁnal model be improved?
Firsts this study is based on several uncertain hypothesis:
• Hypothesis 1: "the revenues of an agency are only dependant on its geographical attribute." As the
results are not perfectly accurate, this hypothesis is probably not correct. Multiple explaining
variables must not have been registered in the dataset, like the quality of the management,
the visibility of the agency, the local marketing eﬀorts, the local public opinion, etc.
• Hypothesis 2: "MisterCar agencies in Germany are spread enough to map an analysis of the whole
country." There are very few MisterCar agencies in East Germany, so the program is applying
the rules found in other regions (about density of population and industries importance to
get a revenue value) to map East Germany regions where there is no ground truth. To what
extent is this extrapolation correct? Taking into consideration historical diﬀerences between
regions functioning would probably improve the model.

5.2.2
Article picking optimization based on Data Mining
In order to ﬁnd the most frequent combination of articles bought together, data must be analyzed.
Articles related with high conﬁdence are sought (see Section 2.4.1). Such articles could be placed in
same racks and linked to the same packing line. A line consists for example in a conveyor between
two racks where an operator (or a shuttle robot) is picking articles from them. By this way, pickers
can ﬁll orders quickly. In order to organize the line and choose which articles should be placed
together, several data mining algorithms exist:
• Association rules mining can be used to group articles by determining the strength of
relationships between them, that is to say to ﬁnd the set of articles bought most frequently
together (ex:when pencils are bought, they must be ordered often with eyes makeup, which is an
important category)
• Clustering method can be used to ﬁnd groups of orders. Groups of orders are based on
similar type and number of articles ordered.

6.1
Introduction
In a product life cycle, diﬀerent phases are deﬁned, as shown in Appendix Figure 1. The introduction
phase, also called launching phase is crucial for products success. In an eﬀort to reduce risks
associated with new product insertion in the market, the ﬁrst year of sales forecast has become an
important topic in Supply Chain Optimization. However, attempts to forecast the launching phase
are too often oﬀthe mark despite a need of high accuracy. Indeed, the design of new production
lines is costly. To what extent do Big Data analytics help securing new product launching and
manufacturing?
A method commonly used is to ﬁrst identify similar products on the market and use them as key
indicator to forecast the demand. Nevertheless, this approach raises diﬃcult questions:
• How do we deﬁne a "similar product"? Which are the good variables to rely on?
• Based on these "similar products", how do we extract the new product sales proﬁle?
6.2
DecoWalls
DecoWalls 1 is the study case company explored in this section. This company sells thousands of
decoration products like paint, wallpapers, curtains, objects, etc. Because owning a large amount
of references, this company faces a complex supply chain but also oﬀers an extensive database
subject to artiﬁcial intelligence exploitation.
Decoration trends are changing fast and the company desire of responsiveness implies a high
frequency of new products creation. Forecasts, currently relying only on historical data, fail in
forecasting eﬃciently the launching phase of a new product. Therefore, the problematic raised is:
Is it possible to forecast the launching phase sales proﬁle of a new product?
6.3
Method adopted
To answer this problem, Machine Learning techniques have been applied on data extracted from
every existing product: history of sales and their characteristics. To identify new product launching
phase sales proﬁle the methodology followed is:
1. Group products together according to their launching period proﬁle. In other words, create
clusters of sales series. This is the unsupervised learning part of the analysis.

2. Link the clusters to product characteristics (like unit price, product family, supplier, etc.)
and train a supervised learning model which understands how product characteristics are
related to the sold quantity during launching phase.
3. Using this model, forecast new products sold quantity based on its characteristics.
4. Finally, feed continuously the model with external data (like website visits, social media
reactions, current social trends,etc.) must improve the forecast.

The goal of this ﬁrst step is to investigate the existence of diﬀerent proﬁle during launching phase.

6.4.1

As explained in Chapter 2 Section 2.2.1, Dynamic Time Wrapping (DTW) distance is chosen to
create a distance matrix between product sales data.
6.4.2

Very few articles have investigated clustering algorithms performance on time series. Some
measures are given by Liu’s article [21] to evaluate internal quality of clusters. Sarda-Espinosa’s
article [22] gives indications on which methods should be tried in this case.
To get a proof of evidence on which algorithms are reliable to group time series with DTW
distance, a test has been conducted in this Section. Artiﬁcial series of type sinusoidal, exponential,
power, logarithm have been created with added noise as showed in Figure 6.1. The varying
amplitude of the noise allows to evaluate the robustness of algorithms.
The following algorithms, presented in Section 2.2 are tested :
• K-medoids, a version of K-means, is used. Instead of taking centroids as gravity centers of
clusters (ﬁctive point), centroids are here the real data in the middle of the cluster. Then
the centroid, here a time series, is not distorted (as a mean of time series would be) and
represents the best the cluster. K-medoids handle better complex objects like time series.
To choose the value of K, Silhouette index and Calinski Harabatz index (CH index) deﬁned
in Section 2.2.6 are taken into consideration. It appeared that CH index is more reliable.
• Hierarchical Clustering with Ward criterion to merge clusters one per one. The choice of K
cluters is made where the dendrogram has the biggest gap like presented in Section 2.2.4
• DB Scan algorithm with an appropriate value of ϵ

[Q1] To what extent do Big Data analytics improve Demand Forecasts?
Integration of exogenous data improves forecast modelling. Through Tyrup case study
Chapter 3, it has been shown that weather data improved signiﬁcantly the forecasts in some
cases. When cross-correlation is high between temperature deviation and seasonally adjusted
sales, weather-sensitivity is veriﬁed. For each season, a multi-variable linear regression model
has been established for each weather sensitive product. Only a fraction of the products are
showing real improvements in the forecasts. The forecast model is then improved up to 67%
for some products.
[Q2] To what extent do Big Data analytics shape a better retailer network?
Through the analysis of geographical and socio-demographical data around existing agencies
linked to their yearly revenues, it has been shown with MisterCar case study Chapter 4 that a
map of interesting zones to create new agencies can be built. The model is a mix of several
supervised learning classiﬁers (Random Forest, KNN, Logistic Regression) voting to get the
best results. This map is 70% accurate. Although it is a very handy tool for visualization,
further investigations should be conducted to complete this model.
[Q3] To what extent do Big Data analytics design more eﬃcient warehouses?
Through the analysis of historical orders, groups of articles have been created by association
rules and clustering algorithms. This study opened important ways of improving the design
of warehouses. The disposal of articles should be reviewed to maximize the quantity of
orders treated in a minimal length zone. Through the Beautora case study Chapter 5, up to
70% of e-Business orders could be made in an unique zone (of 100m long).
First, association rules between articles have been explored to create groups of articles most
often bought together.
This approach has been compared to a fully automated method where groups are made
based on unsupervised clustering methods, relying on mathematical similarity of orders.
K-Means algorithm provided the most relevant results. The association rules method gives
slightly better and more understandable results. Currently, association rules method would
be smoother and more accepted in a company, while the clustering method could be proposed

[Q4] To what extent do Big Data analytics help securing new product launching and manufacturing?
First by grouping products according to their launching curve proﬁle, then by linking these
groups to the characteristics of the products, a quantity sold at the launching phase of
new products is predicted. In the DecoWalls case study Chapter 6, the quantity sold has
been predicted accurately for 70% of the products only relying on products characteristics
(unit price, supplier, components, etc.). Not only useful for Supply chain, these results are
interesting for R&D and Marketing departments as well. Indeed, by uncovering features
making products successful, it is a good source of inspiration to design new products.
Limits of Machine Learning in Supply Chain
The applications presented in this paper use only classic Machine Learning techniques. Deep
Learning technologies are able to reach better performance. However, Supply Chain is not yet
mature for high Artiﬁcial Intelligence performance. Indeed, data collections are important but
not yet in enough quantity. Many sectors need ﬁrst to step to industry 3.0 and interconnect their
stakeholders before being able to use AI.
Another main obstacle to use AI in Supply Chain is the time lost by gathering, processing and
reshaping data before eﬀectively analyzing it.
The integration of new Block Chain technology will make data completely reliable and boost
immediately the use of Big Data for all industries.
Moreover, problems studied aim to model real life phenomenon, directly linked to human’s
behavior. Even though answers are sought from Big data analytics, the problem should be ﬁrst
well understood to feed the algorithm with appropriate data. Once results are generated, they
must be analyzed, the errors origin well deﬁned, ways to palliate these errors found, and ﬁnally
better data provided.
This focus on data relevance is often discarded by Data Scientists who tend to focus on their
technical skills to improve performance. This paper showed that human reﬂection is crucial in
Machine Learning model success.
Future work
This thesis is an early step in AI applications for supply chains. A lot of future work can be done.
About the projects studied :
1. To improve demand forecasting further, explore Deep Learning potential: use Recurrent
Neural Networks instead of traditional forecasting techniques. The integration of relevant
social media data could be investigated (ex: Instagram inﬂuencer posts).
2. Better optimization of Retailer network could be made by adding management indicators
in the data. Proﬁling agencies’ customers is an interesting topic of research which would
provide more information and lead to model improvements.
3. Simulations of fully robotized warehouse to ﬁnd the optimal design would be interesting.

4. The integration of product characteristics in sales model to predict seasonal and trend
parameters common to a group of products would provide more robust forecasts. Indeed
the prediction won’t anymore rely only on product’s own historical data but also history of
similar products (less overﬁtting).
Some ideas of future work on diﬀerent topics touching this ﬁeld could be :
• Investigate the implementation of Block Chain in Supply Chain.
• Model the correlation between price ﬂuctuations and sales in order to improve the pricing

• Predict the demand location and anticipate deliveries thanks to Machine Learning
