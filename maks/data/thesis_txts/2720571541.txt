Abstract 
Water is one of the most important resources on earth. Though the earth is covered in water, clean water safe 
for consumption is a scarce resource in many geographic areas. Climate change threatens the sources of, and 
access to, safe water. The state of the climate calls for action and innovative solutions. This is stressed in the 
United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 6 where the main target is access to clean water for all by 2030.  
On space missions, the only accessible water is brought, recovered from waste, or produced by the Sabatier 
system. For future space exploration, astronauts will go deeper into space and be away for longer periods. 
This creates a need for a highly efficient water recovery system. Such a water recovery system could be a 
membrane-based hybrid system consisting of forward osmosis and membrane distillation. 
In this thesis, the membrane distillation process used in the proposed water recovery system for the Lunar 
Gateway space station was investigated. This includes an investigation of water flux dependency on draw 
operating temperature and salt concentration, the effect of acids in the draw as well as the impact of high-
level urea contamination in the draw. Furthermore, the system was operated continuously for 34 hours to 
investigate the performance of the system when long operation hours are required for water recovery. 
The initial DTU membranes were discarded due to low water flux or salt retention. The poor performance of 
the membranes was explained by membrane structure, low water contact angles or insufficient membrane 
thicknesses. The Aquastill membrane has been discontinued, why it was only tested in preliminary 
experiments. The Durapore and sixth DTU membranes were tested with varying draw solutions and 
temperatures. A 2 M NaCl forward osmosis draw solution at 60 °C and permeate at 20 °C yielded average 
fluxes of 6.57, 7.54 and 11.08 LMH for the Durapore, DTU #6 and Aquastill membranes respectively. 
Experiments showed that water flux was linear dependant on the vapour pressure difference across the 
membrane. Further experiments showed that the salt concentration had a negative effect on the water flux. 
Both findings were in accordance with the theory. The membranes showed high salt and urea retention. The 
Durapore membrane was tested for 34 hours continuous operation where small amounts of urea were found 
in the permeate. Due to inaccurate temperature measurements from analogue thermometers, no conclusions 
were made on the behaviour of the thermal efficiency of the membranes. 
A comparison of membrane structure, water contact angle and membrane thickness showed that the 
commercial membranes were similar in all parameters except the pore size. The different pore sizes could 
explain the higher fluxes achieved with the Aquastill membrane. The DTU #6 membrane had a similar water 
contact angle compared to the commercial membranes. The structure and thickness could not be compared 
to the commercial membranes as the manufacturing methods produces different membrane morphology. 
Experiments were done with urea-contaminated draw solution to simulate real forward osmosis draw. These 
experiments showed that the degradation of urea may be a bigger challenge than first anticipated. Even 
without urease, urea degraded to ammonium resulting in ammonia transfer through the membrane. Urea 
degradation was more significant at higher temperatures and lower pH. As a result of this, some permeate 
samples measured ammonia concentrations exceeding the limit for drinking water on the international space 
station reaching concentrations above 8 mg/L while the limit is only 1 mg/L.  
Conclusively it was found that a solution to either prevent the accumulation of ammonia, remove ammonia 
from the permeate, or prevent ammonia from passing through the membrane is therefore necessary to meet 
the ISS standards and requirements of product water quality. More research and studies are needed to 
determine the most effective and efficient method for this purpose.

1 Introduction 
Water is one of the most important resources on earth, there would be no life without water. Even though 
the surface of the earth is mostly covered with water, only around 1 % of the water on earth is liquid 
freshwater. Of all the freshwater, 99 % is groundwater where 85-90 % has been under ground for more than 
50 years. As of 2017, 69 % of all groundwater withdrawal was used for agriculture, 22 % was used for domestic 
use and 9 % for industrial purposes [1]. 
Water scarcity, or water stress, arise when the demand for clean, usable water exceeds the supply. Water 
scarcity can be caused by either physical or economic factors. Physical factors include temperature, 
precipitation and general climate conditions, while examples of economic factors are lacking water storage, 
sanitation and transportation [2]. Generally, water scarcity is more severe around equator and in the southern 
hemisphere, as it can be seen in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1. Number of months pr. year in which water scarcity exceeds 100 % (indicating an unsustainable 
withdrawal rate), 1996–2005 [1]. 
 
Global warming is constantly impacting water scarcity globally. It is expected that renewable water resources 
will drop by 20 % for every degree the global temperature increases. Global warming will cause longer lasting 
and more recurrent draughts in already stressed areas. Rising sea-level and more intense rainfalls can also 
contaminate water sources and ruin water treatment facilities, which could increase the transmission of 
diseases [2]. The importance of access to clean water is stressed by the United Nations (UN). In the UN 
Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) six, the UN states the main goal target to be: 
“By 2030, achieve universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water for all.”[3] 
To reach this goal, it is important to protect natural water sources and the ecosystems around them, 
encourage proper sanitation and hygiene and invest in appropriate infrastructure and sanitation amenities [3]. 
In addition to natural freshwater sources, several technologies have been used throughout history to reuse 
water for various purposes. Methods vary from agricultural irrigation with wastewater or fishponds benefitting 
from latrine wastewater to modern high-tech wastewater treatment plants [4]. With the increasing demand 
==PAGE:15==

 2 
and decreasing supply of clean and safe water, reuse and recycling of wastewater is vital. Currently, only about 
20 % of all wastewater is reclaimed. On average, 70 % of wastewater is reclaimed in high-income countries, 
while only 8 % is reclaimed in low-income countries. More than half of the reclaimed water is being used for 
irrigation and only an insignificant amount is being used for potable purposes. The technologies for efficient 
water recycling are already present, but increased use of reclaimed water for potable purposes will require 
policy changes and general rebranding of water recycling. Recovery of water, and thereby reduction of water 
scarcity, is not the only benefit of water recycling. For example, wastewater contains significant amounts of 
thermal and chemical energy that can be utilised through heat exchangers and biogas production respectively. 
Wastewater also contains various nutrients like phosphorus and nitrogen, that can be recovered and used in 
other processes [5]. 
In environments with limited access to water, wastewater recovery can be crucial. One such environment is 
space, where the only accessible water is the water brought from earth. 
1.1 Water & Water Recovery in Space 
Water is required on the International Space Station (ISS) for several purposes. Astronauts require water for 
drinking and hygienic purposes, but water is also used for some equipment functions. Water is normally 
transported to the ISS by both manned and unmanned vessels. However, this transport requires significant 
energy consumption and high costs. The cost of sending water from the earth to ISS is more than 10,000 USD 
pr. kg [6]. Furthermore, the transport of water to the ISS takes up space, that could have been used for 
scientific equipment. The current life support system on ISS produce oxygen through water electrolysis. This 
process creates hydrogen gas, that was previously vented overboard as a waste gas. In 2010, the National 
Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) installed an enhanced Sabatier system to produce water, from 
hydrogen and carbon dioxide. The hydrogen is readily available from the electrolysis and the carbon dioxide 
is also naturally available from the crew’s metabolism. The installed Sabatier system weighs roughly 250 kg 
and is the same size as a big moving box, but as it uses only waste gases to produce water it is an important 
addition to the ISS life support system. The only by-product from the process is methane, which is vented as 
waste [7]. 
However, the water supply from the Sabatier system is not enough to sustain the required amount and a water 
recovery system is therefore also required. The Water Recovery and Management (WRM) system currently 
installed on ISS recovers water from urine, perspiration, hygienic uses and from the Sabatier system. The WRM 
system consists of two sections, the Urine Processor Assembly (UPA) and the Water Processor Assembly (WPA) 
[6]. The schematics of these are shown in Figure 2. 
==PAGE:16==
Introduction 
 3 
 
Figure 2.  Schematics of the Urine Processor Assembly (UPA) (A) and the Water Processor Assembly (WPA) (B) 
installed at the ISS [6]. 
 
Before the UPA, urine is stabilised and stored until a sufficient quantity has been obtained. The urine then 
goes through an evaporation step, followed by a gas separation step where purge gas is removed. 
Subsequently, the urine passes through another evaporation step before it is transferred to the WPA. In the 
WPA, the treated urine is combined with collected condensate and water from the Sabatier system. This is 
first degassed and afterwards passed through a particulate filter and several multi-filtration beds. These steps 
ensure that gas, inorganic compounds, and non-volatile organic compounds are removed. Next, it goes 
through organic catalytic oxidation where low molecular weight organics are oxidised, whereafter the oxygen 
and gaseous by-products are separated from the water. Finally, an ion exchange bed ensures that carbonate 
and bicarbonate ions from the oxidation are removed [6]. 
In 2019, up to 80 % of the water on the ISS was recycled, the rest of the water supply covered by cargo vessels 
amounts to 6-9,000 litres pr. year [8]. The current WRM system at ISS requires frequent supplies of ion-
exchange resins, sorbents, filtration media and hardware for maintenance and replacements. Production of 
one litre of water results in approximately 0.21 kg disposable hardware. The economic burden of the current 
system is not the only concern that illustrates the need for a new and innovative water recovery solution for 
space missions. To stabilise the urine and lower the pH, strong inorganic acids causing corrosion within the 
WRM system are used. The higher concentration of calcium in astronaut-urine also creates CaSO4 precipitation 
in the UPA [6]. 
During the 2020’s the partners of the ISS plan to launch a new space station, the Lunar Gateway. Lunar 
Gateway will act as a staging post, a relay station and a base for scientific research and will, as the name 
implies, orbit our earth’s moon. It is expected that the Lunar Gateway will be of great importance for future 
Mars and moon missions. It will furthermore be used for experiments, where the atmosphere and magnetic 
field of the earth do not interfere [9]. Since the Lunar Gateway will be located even further from the earth 
==PAGE:17==
Introduction 
 4 
than the ISS, it will be even more costly to transport equipment and water to the station. Thus, the Lunar 
Gateway gives an increased demand for efficient and durable water recovery. 
The relevance of water recovery in space led Aquaporin A/S and NASA to start collaborating. With NASA’s 
extensive knowledge of water recovery and Aquaporin’s knowledge in the use of aquaporins, experiments 
have been conducted and new membranes have been developed [10]. The focus on water recovery in space 
also led to a collaboration between Aquaporin A/S and Danish Aerospace Company ApS in the form of a joint 
venture company, Aquaporin Space Alliance (ASA). The purpose of ASA is to create water recovery solutions 
through projects and experiments with space programs such as NASA and the European Space Agency (ESA) 
[11].

2.1 Osmotic Processes 
A simplified definition of osmosis is the spontaneous diffusion of solvent towards a higher concentration of 
solute through a semipermeable membrane. The diffusion occurs to even out the chemical potential of the 
solvent on each side of the semipermeable membrane and reach chemical equilibrium. The osmotic pressure, 
𝜋, defines the required applied pressure to prevent diffusion through the semipermeable membrane. For an 
ideal dilute solution, the osmotic pressure is given by Van’t Hoff’s equation in Equation (1). 
 
𝜋= 𝑖⋅𝐶⋅𝑅⋅𝑇𝑎𝑏𝑠 
(1) 
i is the Van’t Hoff factor and refers to the number of particles a compound dissolves into, C is the molarity of 
the solute, R the gas constant and 𝑇𝑎𝑏𝑠 the absolute temperature [12]. 
In water filtration, the three common osmotic processes are reverse osmosis, RO, forward osmosis, FO and 
pressure retarded osmosis, PRO. RO have many commercial applications both in households for improving 
drinking water quality, in the food industry for concentrating liquids and for water and wastewater 
purification. FO has three general industrial applications: product concentration, wastewater concentration 
and water recovery. PRO is a promising technology in the energy sector where the salinity difference between 
freshwater and sea water is utilised to produce green and cheap electricity [13], [14].  
In RO a pressure greater than the difference in osmotic pressure (Δ𝑝> Δ𝜋) is applied to a saline solution. This 
creates a water flux from the saline solution through the membrane to the freshwater, resulting in an 
increasingly concentrated saline solution.  
In FO no external pressure is applied to the draw solution (Δ𝑝= 0). The osmotic pressure difference is the 
driving force for the process, hence the water permeates from the feed, concentrating the feed and diluting 
the draw solution [13]. 
In PRO a pressure lower than the osmotic pressure (Δ𝑝< Δ𝜋) is applied to the draw solution. Even though an 
external pressure is applied to the draw solution the water still permeates from the feed solution to the draw 
solution. The osmotic pressure of the draw solution is then converted to hydraulic pressure utilizing Gibbs free 
energy of mixing, which is then converted to electrical energy via turbine generators [15].  
2.2 Membrane Distillation 
Membrane distillation is a thermal separation process, where a warm feed and cold permeate are separated 
by a hydrophobic porous membrane. The difference in vapour pressure of the two streams is the primary 
driving force for the separation process. The hydrophobic membrane rejects liquid water but allows gasses to 
pass through the membrane. This allows for excellent rejection of non-volatile components, however, leaves 
the process susceptible to contamination of volatile components with boiling points close to or below that of 
water. The feed stream is in direct contact with the membrane, while the hydrophobic nature of the 
membrane prevents it from entering the pores because of surface tension forces. A transmembrane pressure 
lower than the liquid entry pressure (LEP) must be maintained during operation to prevent wetting of the 
==PAGE:19==
Theory 
 6 
membrane pores. Wetting of the membrane pores is when the liquid begins penetrating the membrane at 
which point the quality of the produced water drastically decreases [16]. 
The most thoroughly investigated membrane distillation configurations are direct contact MD (DCMD), air gap 
MD (AGMD), sweep gas MD (SGMD) and vacuum MD (VMD) [16]. 
 
Figure 3. The most common membrane distillation configurations. (a) direct contact membrane distillation, (b) 
air gap membrane distillation, (c) sweep gas membrane distillation and (d) vacuum membrane distillation [17]. 
 
Figure 3 shows the most common membrane distillation configurations. DCMD is the simplest of the MD 
processes. The driving force consists solely of the difference in water vapour pressure and both the 
vaporisation, and the condensation occurs directly on the membrane. As the permeate and feed stream are 
in direct contact with the membrane, the process is highly susceptible to conductive heat loss. In AGMD the 
driving force is similarly the difference in water vapour pressure, however, a stagnant air gap between the 
membrane interface and the condensate formation surface, reduces the conductive heat loss. The air gap 
does, however, also have the drawback of lowering the mass transfer due to the extra layer of resistance. In 
SGMD and VMD the removal of permeate provides an additional driving force for the separation process. In 
SGMD the permeate is removed by a sweep gas flow across the membrane surface on the permeate side. As 
the condensation occurs separately and not on the membrane interface, the conductive heat loss is lower as 
in AGMD. However, the sweep gas does allow for convective heat loss from the membrane surface. VMD 
removes the permeate by applying a vacuum on the permeate side and thus reduces the conductive heat loss 
without imposing convective heat loss. Both AGMD and VMD generally obtain higher water fluxes due to the 
greater mass transfer driving force. However, the process is more expensive as an external condenser is 
required. It is also more complex as AGMD requires large air flows and VMD a vacuum pump and carefully 
regulated vacuum to not exceed the LEP [16].

2.3 Membranes 
A membrane is a selective surface that controls the flow through it by either physical, chemical or biological 
properties. Membranes are naturally found in all plants and animals where they, as an example, help to control 
liquid balances in the human body. In the industry, synthetic membranes are designed to control the 
permeation of a specific compound that is either wanted or unwanted in the product. Membranes are usually 
used in the downstream processing of chemical or biological products or in waste treatment units. Here the 
membrane can facilitate the permeation of the desired compound(s) and the retention of the undesired 
compound(s) or vice versa. Membrane permeation can be explained by two models, either the pore-flow 
model or the solution-diffusion model. The pore flow model describes the permeation as simply a flow through 
the pores of the membranes, while the solution-diffusion model describes it as the compound(s) dissolving 
into the membrane material and diffusing through to the permeate side. These mechanisms are displayed in 
Figure 4. Examples of membrane processes that are described by the pore flow model are microfiltration and 
ultrafiltration, while examples of membrane processes that are described by the solution-diffusion model are 
reverse osmosis and pervaporation [18]. 
 
Figure 4. The trans-membrane transport is created by either solution-diffusion or through pores in the membrane 
[18]. 
 
Depending on the purpose, membranes can be produced with various functions and designs/configurations. 
In this thesis, two types of membrane configurations are used, that is flat sheet and hollow fiber membranes. 
Flat sheet membranes are, as the name suggests, flat sheets of membranes generally used in a plate-and-
frame configuration. The membranes tested in this thesis all looked like thin sheets of paper and had a texture 
like soft, stretchable plastic. The flat sheet membrane is the most basic configuration of a membrane and can 
be illustrated as the ones seen in Figure 4. Flat sheet membranes have the advantage of being very simple to 
install, replace and operate, while they also generally have a higher flux (flow of permeate through the 
membrane), compared to hollow fiber membranes. However, the flat sheet membranes have a relatively high 
footprint i.e., they take up a lot of space. Generally, it is also assumed, that flat sheet is the most mechanically 
stable configuration [18]. 
 
Hollow fiber membranes consist of small tubes/fibres, where the wall of the tube is the membrane. The tubes 
are fixated on both ends so that one solution can flow through the tubes, while another can flow around the 
tubes on the shell side. An illustration of a hollow fiber membrane can be seen in Figure 5. The feed and the 
draw could be run on either side of the fibres.

Hollow fiber membranes can be made from the same materials and with the same properties as flat sheet

downside of this configuration [18]. 
Several general parameters influence the performance of a membrane including membrane thickness, 
porosity, and liquid entry pressure. 
2.3.1 Membrane Thickness 
The membrane thickness is an important factor to consider when designing an MD process. The membrane 
thickness is inversely proportional to not only the water flux through the membrane, but also the heat transfer 
between the hot and cold sides. For optimal water flux through the membrane, it is thus preferred to use a 
thin membrane, however, this would also result in a very large conductive heat loss from the hot draw to the 
cold permeate stream making the process less thermally efficient. A compromise between the water flux and 
the conductive heat loss through the membrane is necessary and the thickness should be optimised depending 
on what is more valuable for the specific process. However, other considerations related to the membrane 
type are also required when optimising the thickness. This includes among others whether the membrane is 
supported or not, a flat sheet or hollow fiber, the material, the overall mechanical strength of the membrane 
etc. [20]. The mechanical strength requirement of an MD membrane is generally low as pressure is not 
involved in the process. However, it must be able to withstand the liquid flowrates the system is going to be 
operating with and not easily tear. 
Due to the different manufacturing methods, the membrane thickness is not always comparable. As the 
membranes manufactured by DTU are made by electrospinning, they are less dense than the commercial 
membranes. This means that the DTU membranes are generally thicker than the commercial membranes.

2.5 Temperature-, Concentration- & Vapour Polarisation 
Temperature polarisation (TP) is a phenomenon that occurs at the boundary layers of the membrane and 
occurs due to the nature of the mass transfer process in membrane distillation. The hot feed supplies heat for 
the phase change from liquid to vapour. When the vapour condenses on the vapour-liquid interface on the 
cold side of the membrane, the heat is then released to the cold side. Furthermore, as previously mentioned 
conductive heat is also transferred from the hot side to the cold side through the membrane material. For 
these reasons the temperature of the hot side and on the cold side (𝑇𝑓 & 𝑇𝑝) differ from the respective bulk 
temperatures at the boundary layers of the membrane (𝑇𝑚,𝑓 & 𝑇𝑚,𝑝). The boundary layer of the hot feed is 
colder than the bulk temperature, which lowers the vapour pressure of the solution, likewise, the boundary 
layer on the cold side is warmer which increases the vapour pressure on the cold interface of the membrane. 
The lower vapour pressure on the hot side and the higher vapour pressure on the cold side both harm the 
mass transfer as the driving force is described by the vapour pressure difference across the membrane Δ𝑝. 
Temperature polarisation thus provides an additional resistance apart from the membrane resistance and is 
considered the limiting factor in membrane distillation [24].

2.6 Fouling and Wetting 
Membrane fouling is a phenomenon where suspended or dissolved particles deposit on the membrane surface 
or the interior of the membrane pores. Membrane fouling can result in loss of performance, drastically 
reduced water flux as well as affect the quality of the water produced. There are three general kinds of 
membrane fouling: pore narrowing, pore blocking and cake formation.  Pore narrowing occurs when particles 
attach to the membrane around or inside the membrane pores and thus constrict the pore size. Pore blocking 
occurs when particles of a sufficient size get stuck in the membrane pores and completely block the passage 
through the pore. Cake formation is the formation of a foulant layer on the surface of the membrane [17]. 
The fouling types occurring depend on characteristics and concentration of solutes and solvents, membrane 
type and surface characteristics, pore size and pore size distribution, foulant size, operating pressure, 
hydrodynamics of membrane module and flowrates as well as the affinity between foulants and the 
membrane amongst other. Cake formation is external fouling and can often be removed by 
backwashing/physical cleaning or a cleaning solution. Pore narrowing and pore blocking are internal fouling 
and often irreversible resulting in the need for membrane replacement to maintain the desired water flux. 
Due to the hydrophobic nature of membranes used in membrane distillation fouling is more likely to occur if 
the solution contains hydrophobic compounds [17]. 
Fouling can cause wetting of the membrane. However, wetting can also occur because of other conditions. 
When wetting occurs because of fouling it is usually because the fouling deposits reduce the hydrophobicity 
==PAGE:27==
Theory 
 14 
of the membrane. It can also occur because of surfactant deposit on the membrane surface, which like with 
foulants, reduce the hydrophobicity of the membrane. Finally, it occurs when the transmembrane pressure 
exceeds the liquid entry pressure of the membrane if a too-great vacuum is applied on the permeate side of 
the membrane. In either case, the water quality drastically reduces when wetting occurs which immediately 
will be observable in the conductivity, as the membrane no longer can reject the solutes in the draw solution 
[25]. 
There are several methods for preventing, removing, or mitigating fouling. The effectiveness of the methods 
depends largely on the specific system in which they are to be implemented as well as the type of fouling 
occurring on the membrane. Some of the most typical membrane foulant mitigation and control strategies are 
backflushing, physical and chemical cleaning, membrane modification through either surface structure or 
membrane material properties [26]. 
2.7 Forward Osmosis & Membrane Distillation System 
The new water recovery system consists of two sub-systems: an FO unit and the MD unit investigated in this 
report. The FO unit will extract water from the wastewater, comprised primarily of urine and condensate, into 
a salt draw solution. The MD unit then recovers the extracted water from the diluted draw as pure water. The 
wastewater will be pumped through the inside of the fibres in a hollow fibre FO membrane coated with 
Aquaporin Inside® Technology while the draw solution, a high salinity sodium chloride solution, will be 
pumped through the shell side of the membrane in the counter-current direction [27]. The diluted draw is 
then heated to the chosen temperature (40-70 °C) before passing the MD membrane where a cold permeate 
stream (20 °C) is flowing counter-current on the opposite side of the fibres or sheet. 
The system can either be operated as a continuous process or as a batch operation. If the system is operated 
as a continuous process sufficient cooling and heating are required. The hot draw solution leaving the MD unit 
must be cooled before it reaches the FO membrane and re-heated again afterwards for separation to occur in 
the two modules. If operated as a batch process the FO unit and the MD unit could run separately which might 
be a more energy-efficient method of operating the system. The permeate water from the MD unit is expected 
to require remineralisation before being suitable for drinking due to the theoretical complete rejection of non-
volatile components in the draw solution. The consequences of using demineralised water as the sole source 
of drinking water would be ion deficiency, lower blood cell count and inadequate body water distribution. 
Hence the importance of a remineralisation system following the water recovery system [27]. 
An important advantage of the water recovery system’s use in space is that it does not need to operate at high 
temperatures or pressures. High temperatures or pressures are not desired in space due to safety concerns.

3 Objectives 
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate the MD sub-process of a proposed FO-MD hybrid system for 
water recovery from urine. A series of experiments were performed to study the influence of feed 
temperature and feed concentration on the flux through membranes.  The experiments are also used to 
investigate the heat transfer and thermal efficiency of the different membranes for different operating 
conditions. Since the hydrolysation of urea to ammonium can lead to contamination of product water and as 
urease can be destabilised by acid, it was also investigated how acidification of the draw solution will impact 
membrane performance. Furthermore, the experiments will investigate the performance of several 
membranes, commercial and DTU fabricated, with a simulated contaminated draw solution. A 34-hour 
experiment was carried out to investigate the performance of the membrane distillation process as well as the 
product water quality over an extended period of operation. 
The simulated contaminated draw solution, or simulated FO draw, used in the experiments has been the 
“worst-case scenario” draw solution. The worst-case scenario implies a draw solution that has been used for 
an extensive amount of time making the urea concentration in the draw solution the same as in the FO feed 
at 90 % water recovery corresponding to approximately 50 g urea pr. litre [27]. This solution was used in 
experiments to investigate the influence of urea contamination on membrane performance and the possible 
complications caused by it.

The setup was dismantled and the whole FO part of the system was removed. All pipes that were not an

The inlet of the draw pump was unfortunately broken in the process and needed repairing as no similar pump

However, as this did not require as precise and constant temperature (if it is cold enough) the Peltier module 
was deemed sufficient for the purpose. When operating the system for more than 30 minutes it could be 
observed that the system was unable to maintain sufficient cooling resulting in increasing temperature. To 
avoid further delay of the project and time wasted on fixing extensive complications, it was agreed to 
discontinue experiments on the SGMD system. Instead, it was decided to make experiments with the hollow 
fiber modules in the DCMD setup.

As the DCMD was changed during the project period due to broken equipment and to accommodate the

4.2.3.1 Operations Conditions – Initial Setup 
The pumps were set at 300 rpm. The cooling for the permeate was set to 18-19.5 °C depending on the draw

solution was set to 1-3 °C above the chosen draw temperature. The magnetic stirrer in the draw container was

4.2.3.2 Operating Conditions – Final Setup 
The originally used scale was changed by another user of the system and the heater with magnetic stirring 
broke and was replaced by the same person. The significantly larger membrane area of the hollow fiber 
module resulted in a much higher heat transfer. Therefore, the heating plate previously used could not supply 
sufficient heating to maintain the chosen temperatures. The heating plate, initially bought for the SGMD setup, 
was used in series with a heating bath borrowed from Aquaporin, equipped with a homemade copper coil. 
The heating system was very effective, but the copper coil started to oxidise due to the high salt concentration 
and high temperature of the draw. It was also found that the old cooling system could not provide sufficient 
cooling for the permeate stream. The copper coil was replaced with the stainless-steel coil from the cooling 
system. A chiller was borrowed from Aquaporin and brought to DTU, where its coil was installed in a plastic 
bucket alongside several meters of rubber tubing for the permeate. The rubber tubing did not provide the 
necessary heat transfer and other solutions for a quickly delivered heat exchanger were investigated. In the 
end, a tube-bending tool was used to bend a stainless-steel tube into a radiator-shaped coil. The coil was able 
to supply sufficient heat transfer and this variation of the system was used for the remaining experiments. The 
pumps were still set to 300 rpm. Cooling for the permeate was set to 19-19.5 °C depending on the draw 
temperature. The heating bath and plate for the draw solution were set to 0.75-1.5 °C above the chosen draw 
temperature. The magnetic stirrer in the draw container was set to 600-1,100 rpm. Experiments performed 
with the modified system are completely comparable with experiments performed using the initial system as 
the modification had no impact on the operating conditions when using flat sheet membranes. A PI diagram

4.2.4 Operation & Measurements 
Experiments were performed according to the following, self-made, guide. 
The equipment, except the heating plate, is turned on and set to the chosen temperatures. The draw solution 
is prepared, and a piece of membrane is cut and secured in the membrane chamber. 400-700 mL of draw 
solution is added to a container that is put on the heating plate and the “in”-tube is put inside. At this point 
the heating plate can be turned on. The draw-side is then shortly flushed with a small amount of draw solution, 
after which the “out”-tube is put into the draw container, closing the draw loop. A beaker is filled with DI water 
and put on the scale with the “in”-tube equipped, the DI side is then flushed and the “out” tube is put back 
into the DI beaker when the beaker contains approximately 400 mL. When the chosen temperature is reached 
in the heating bath, the pumps are turned on. Once the inlet temperature of the draw solution reaches the 
chosen value, the scale is tared, and a timer is started. To maintain a constant salt concentration in the draw 
solution, DI water can be continuously added to the draw container in amounts equal to the water that has 
passed through the membrane, to maintain constant draw concentration. The scale measurement is read 
manually at chosen intervals and logged in Microsoft Office Excel 2016. Temperature data is read from the 
three manual thermometers as well as the conductivity meter. The conductivity is read from the conductivity 
meter. The flux is measured as the change on the scale measuring the weight of the permeate. If needed, 
samples can be taken from the permeate/DI water. After operation, the system should be flushed thoroughly 
with DI water and drained by flushing with air. 
As readings from the manual thermometers have a certain margin of error, an uncertainty of ±0.5 °C should 
be expected for all readings. The provided accuracies for all measuring equipment are shown in Appendix C.

4.2.5.1 Colorimetric Analysis - Total Organic Carbon (TOC) 
A TOC analysis shows the amount of organic carbon in an organic solution. The analysis is a non-specific 
indicator that cannot specify the organic compounds and is performed using a colourimeter. The TOC analysis 
was performed by Erica Key. 
4.2.5.2 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) – Urea 
High-performance liquid chromatography, also known as high-pressure liquid chromatography, is a technique 
for analysing the composition of a solution. HPLC provides information about the specific compounds as well 
as the amount of each constituent in the solution. A small amount of pressurised solvent containing the 
solution of interest is passed through a column with a solid or liquid adsorbent material. The different 
components in the solution interacts differently with the adsorption material resulting in different retention 
times in the column. Depending on the specific solvent used, commonly denoted the mobile phase, as well as 
the specific adsorbent material used in the column, HPLC can be used to identify a wide variety of compounds 
in a given mixture. For this project, HPLC has been used to measure the concentration of urea in the MD 
permeate as well as the MD draw solution. This has been done to investigate if urea is passing through the 
membrane to the permeate or if urea is decomposing to a volatile compound that permeates the membrane 
[33]. The HPLC analysis was performed by Hans Peter Smits. 
4.2.5.3 Spectrophotometry – Urea/Ammonia 
To determine if samples contain ammonia or urea a Megazyme Urea/Ammonia Assay Kit (Rapid) is used. The 
samples are measured in a Thermo Scientific Genesys 10s UV-VIS spectrophotometer and the absorbance 
indicates the concentrations. The full procedure for the analysis is made by Megazyme and presented in 
Appendix D. 
4.2.5.4 SEM Images & Membrane Parameters 
A scanning electron microscope (SEM) is an instrument that uses a focused beam of electrons to produce a 
variety of information about the sample being investigated. The information that can be derived from SEM 
includes chemical composition, crystalline structure as well as the external structure and morphology of the 
sample [34]. The SEM images were made and the membrane thicknesses and water contact angles were 
measured by Elifnur Gezmis Yavuz.

5.1.4 Failed Hollow Fiber Experiments 
To investigate the difference between the flat sheet membranes and the hollow fiber membranes the Memsift 
hollow fiber membrane was connected in the modified DCMD setup. In the first experiment with a draw 
temperature of 40 °C, the draw outlet decreased to 24-27 °C while the DI temperature increased from a 
temperature of 16-20 °C in the inlet to 29-32 °C in the outlet. Since the streams are run in counterflow this 
gives an average temperature difference of up to 11 °C. This temperature difference was not high enough to 
drive the evaporation and no flux was therefore seen. At 50 °C draw the outlet temperature of the draw was 
29-30 °C while the DI changed from 19-22 °C in the inlet to 37-39 in the outlet. This gives a maximum difference 
of 23 °C which was only enough to yield a flux of 0.1 LMH. At 60 °C where the experiment was only run for 30 
minutes, the draw temperature decreased to 38 °C with a DI inlet of 29 °C and outlet of 47 °C resulting in a 
flux of 0.34 LMH. 
The very low flux observed in these experiments could be accommodated by increasing the flow on both 
pumps. But when this was attempted, the tube-connections and the thermometer in-line glass containers 
burst from the increased pressure. Due to these challenges, the hollow fiber membrane experiments were 
abandoned. If more time was available, the setup could possibly be modified to accommodate the higher 
flowrates necessary for the hollow fiber membranes to function properly. The first thing that should be 
implemented is the removal of the manual thermometers as the glassware cannot be modified to 
accommodate the higher flowrate. However, changes might also be necessary for the connections to the 
heating and cooling coils.

5.2 Operating Temperature 
A series of experiments were performed using the Aquastill, Durapore and DTU #6 membranes with the draw 
(2 M NaCl) at temperatures of 40, 50, 60 and 70 °C (± 1 °C) and the DI water at 20 °C (± 1 °C). This section 
discusses the influence of temperature on the flux of water through the membrane. Since there was little to 
no change in conductivity during any of the experiments, the impact of draw temperature on salt retention is 
considered neglectable. 
 
Figure 13. Average flux values for three membranes at various temperatures. Draw solution: 2M NaCl operated 
with 298 mL/min. Permeate: DI water at 20 °C operated with 293 mL/min. Operation time: 60-90 min pr. 
temperature. 
 
Figure 13 clearly shows that a higher temperature results in a higher water flux which was seen for all 
experiments with all used membrane pieces. This confirms what was expected according to the theory as a 
higher draw temperature would increase the driving force of membrane distillation, i.e., the water vapour 
pressure difference between the hot and cold side. As displayed in Table 6, the water saturation pressure, and 
thus the water vapour pressure difference, increases significantly between 40, 50, 60 and 70 °C. Although an 
increased draw temperature gives an increased driving force, the limiting factor of temperature polarisation 
should also be considered. The resistance from the temperature polarisation is increased with higher draw 
temperatures as it is derived from Equation (16). The impact of temperature polarisation resistance is however 
significantly lower than the increase in water vapour pressure difference. 
The flux observed during experiments with the Aquastill membrane is unexpectedly high compared to other 
membranes. Since this membrane is no longer in production, it was not possible to do a further examination 
of its performance. 
The variations of flux at different temperatures with the same membranes would be expected for most 
experimental studies. While the Aquastill experiments showed noticeable variations, all experiments with the 
Durapore and DTU #6 membranes gave results close to the average of the respective membrane.

5.3 Draw Concentration 
To examine the impact of draw concentration on the flux and on the retention of salt, two kinds of experiments 
were performed. The first type of experiment was a batch experiment where no additional DI water was added 
to the draw and the draw would thereby slowly be concentrated over time. The batch experiments were 
performed with a draw temperature of 60 °C. The second type of experiment was a series of experiments with 
increasing concentrations of NaCl in the draw and a temperature of 50 °C. The series of experiments were run 
with 500 mL of 1 M NaCl for one hour, and then 29.22 g NaCl was added making it 2 M NaCl. This was repeated 
until the experiment had been run with 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5 M NaCl. The experiments were run with up to 5 M NaCl 
since the maximum solubility of NaCl in water is just above 6 M. 
The fluxes measured during the three batch experiments are presented in Figure 15. 
 
Figure 15. Flux results during three batch experiments. Draw solution: 1M NaCl initial concentration at 60 °C 
operated with 298 mL/min. Permeate: DI water at 20 °C operated with 293 mL/min. Operating time, Durapore: 
390 min, DTU: 225 min and Aquastill: 255 min. 
 
Figure 15 shows some varying results as two of the experiments show a decrease in flux with the increasing 
concentration, while the last experiment seems to show the opposite. The decreasing flux is expected as the 
increasing salt concentration also decreases the driving force. Since the change in the mole fraction of water 
is very small from 1 M to 2 M NaCl, only a slight decrease in flux was expected. In this case, a decrease in the 
flux of between 15-27 % is observed. Another observation is that the DTU #6 membrane seems to decrease in 
the flux notably quicker than the Durapore membrane. However, it could appear as if the flux was reaching a 
steady value. The experiment was cut short due to unforeseen circumstances. Unfortunately, neither time or 
the membrane amount provided would allow for a replicate experiment to be performed why the data for the 
DTU #6 membrane is incomplete.  
Considering the experiment showing increasing flux, it is important to note that this experiment was 
performed with the Aquastill membrane. As previously mentioned, this membrane generally showed a much 
higher flux than expected. The factors that might have given this membrane such a high flux might also cause 
the increase in flux seen in Figure 15. But since this trend is opposing the known theory, it might also be caused 
by something else.

enzymatic urea assay did not show any urea in the permeate, it was concluded that at least no detectable 
amounts of urea were passing through the DTU #6 and the Durapore membranes.

Although no detectable amounts of ammonia were found in the other permeate samples, trace amounts

draw solution experiments.

Conclusion 
 54 
6 Conclusion 
In this thesis, the membrane distillation process for the proposed new water recovery system to be 
implemented on the Lunar Gateway space station has been investigated. This included an analysis of the water 
flux and its correlation to draw solution temperature and salt concentration, the effect of acetic and sulphuric 
acid in the draw solution, as well as the impact of high-level urea contamination in the draw solution to 
simulate a solution that has been operated for an extended period. Furthermore, the system was operated 
continuously for 34 hours to investigate the performance of the system in a situation, where long operation 
hours are required for water production. 
After initial experiments, five DTU membranes were discarded due to inadequate flux or salt retention. The 
membranes were produced continuously. When one membrane was discarded, the next was produced until 
the sixth DTU membrane, DTU #6, showed good performance. SEM image analysis and contact angle 
measurements were used to substantiate why the membranes were discarded and to explain the performance 
of the individual membranes. 
The water flux´s dependency on the vapour pressure difference between the cold permeate side and the hot 
draw solution proved to be very well explained by linear regression in accordance with literature. When 
operating the DCMD setup with a 2 M NaCl draw solution at 60 °C draw temperature and 20 °C permeate 
temperature, average fluxes of 6.57, 7.54 and 11.08 LMH were achieved for the Durapore, DTU #6 and 
Aquastill membrane respectively. A decrease in the water flux was observed for increasing draw salt 
concentrations as expected based on theory. However, the Aquastill membrane behaved differently showing 
an increasing water flux with increasing salt concentration. It could not readily be explained why the 
membrane showed this odd behaviour. To further compare the three well-performing membranes, SEM 
images and contact angle measurements were analysed. Very comparable and high contact angles were seen 
for all three membranes. The two commercial membranes showed similar structures in the SEM, images while 
the DTU #6 membrane could not be compared to the commercial membranes as it is manufactured differently 
and therefore has a different membrane structure. 
The three well-performing membranes demonstrated excellent salt retention as the permeate conductivity 
when using 2 M NaCl draw solution did not increase significantly during any of the experiments. As with salt 
retention, the DTU #6 and Durapore membranes demonstrated high urea retention. Urea was only present in 
the product water at a detectable level in the 34-hour experiment. As only a small amount of the DTU #6 
membrane was provided, the 34-hour experiment was only performed for the Durapore membrane, hence, it 
cannot be concluded whether the DTU #6 membrane would perform similarly, better or worse under the same 
conditions. 
Due to the poor quality of the temperature data, it was not possible to conclusively determine whether any of 
the membranes were significantly better than the other, and neither how the thermal efficiency of the 
membrane distillation process would change when operating at different temperatures. Only the data 
collected for the Aquastill membrane revealed a behaviour consistent with theory. 
From the experiments with urea-contaminated draw solution, it could be concluded that the degradation of 
urea to ammonia, without the presence of urease, is not neglectable, especially when operating at higher 
temperatures and at longer operation times. Some of the experiments performed for this thesis measured 
ammonia concentration in the permeate water exceeding the ISS product water quality limit by up to 800 %. 
The experiments were performed with an excessive concentration of urea in the draw solution why these 
results may exaggerate the actual impact on a real-life water recovery system. Further investigation of FO and

the accumulation of urea in the draw solution should be performed to understand the extent and significance 
of urea permeation in the FO unit, its impact on product water quality and the operating lifetime of the draw 
solution in the proposed water recycling system for the Lunar Gateway space station. 
When the challenges with FO-MD water recovery process have been solved, the technology will allow for 
further exploration of space by reducing the supply requirements on space missions while also decreasing the 
time and resources spent on water recovery. In addition to the increased energy and cost efficiency, this will 
also release space for other important equipment to be brought on space missions. 
In summary, the Durapore and DTU #6 membranes were found to have acceptable performance at 
investigated operating temperatures and draw solution concentrations. But the formation and permeation of 
ammonia is a challenge that must be dealt with before implementation of a membrane-based hybrid water 
recovery system. 
6.1 Recommendations 
The concentration of ammonia in the product water was higher than permitted, per the requirements on the 
ISS, no parts of this system can be recommended as is. Only with proper post-treatment removing enough 
ammonia from the product water or by implementing a method to avoid the formation and permeation of 
ammonia, should this system be implemented on the ISS.  Whatever solution is found, it must ensure an 
ammonia level in the water storage on ISS below 1 mg/L. 
As the Aquastill membrane is no longer available, it will not be considered in our recommendations for the 
system. Given the fact that only two sheets of the DTU #6 membrane were provided, it is difficult to 
conclusively determine the performance of the membrane. However, in most of the comparable experiments, 
the DTU #6 membrane seemed to match or even outperform the Durapore membrane regarding both flux 
and retention. If the DTU #6 membrane could be produced with the same performance or improved through 
continuous development, this membrane would be a very reasonable recommendation for the MD process. 
However, if the production of the DTU #6 membrane is not that easily repeated, the Durapore membrane 
would be a reasonable substitute. 
Regarding process parameters, a lot of factors should be considered. Naturally, a larger membrane area than 
the 42.75 cm2 that was used in this project is required to accommodate the requirements of the ISS. An 
assessment of temperature and membrane size is necessary. Higher temperatures and larger membrane areas 
will provide higher water flux, but also result in greater energy consumption or larger footprint respectively.

Future Perspectives 
 57 
7 Future Perspectives 
For further assessment of the performance of the MD system, supplementary experiments should be 
performed to investigate the findings in this thesis. Additional experiments with varying concentrations of 
simulated FO draw, and experiments with real FO draw, should also be performed and would probably provide 
valuable information on the actual performance of the MD process. 
The ammonia formation and permeation create a major challenge, as the limit for ammonia in the water at 
ISS is 1 mg/L. Methods for retention or removal of ammonia or stabilisation of urea should thus be further 
studied. Ammonia removal from the permeate would require an additional process step after MD. This could 
potentially be either chromatography, stripping or adsorption, though some may be more suitable for 
implementation on a space station than others. Methods for stabilising urea are under investigation. Another 
approach to mitigate the ammonia transfer is implementing an ammonia adsorbent directly in the membrane, 
either on the surface or in the membrane material.  No matter the technique, a solution that does not 
negatively impact the membrane performance or result in the permeation of other unwanted compounds is 
needed.  
The concept of a self-sustaining, economically efficient and effective method for recovering water from urine, 
or any contaminated water source, can have a multitude of relevant applications beyond the use on space 
missions. Such a system could help secure safe and easily accessible water for people living in vulnerable, rural 
areas with increased draught where access to safe drinking water is limited. A water recovery system driven 
by solar power could benefit local communities in rural areas by offering cheap, safe and accessible water for 
drinking and sanitation. An additional relevant humanitarian use for a small and efficient water recovery 
system is as assistance in disaster relief. When earthquakes, tsunamis, or tornadoes affects areas where the 
infrastructure is either damaged or generally not sufficient, access to clean and safe water can be crucial for 
the survival of the affected people. Both of these humanitarian applications would not only benefit local 
communities and areas struck by disasters, but also protect the global community in the long run. 
7.1 Improvements of Experimental Setup 
The experimental setups used in the experiments conducted could be improved in many ways. First and 
foremost, the SGMD setup could be rebuilt with durable and drainable equipment so that it does not break 
down during periods of inactivity. 
The DCMD setup has many potential improvements. The most important point of optimisation is the data 
collection. Instead of analogue thermometers and a scale and conductivity meter that also needs manual 
readings, the setup could be equipped with electronic thermometers connected to a computer for continuous 
data collection and storage. This would allow for efficient and accurate data collection, while also allow for 
the setup to be left operating for longer periods. Further optimisation of the setup could be to automate the 
system inputs such as draw temperature, DI temperature and pump speeds. If these values could be controlled 
on a computer, a program could be run to adjust any of the values and thereby analyse the impact of 
adjustments. This type of system would also require heating and cooling vessels to be directly adjustable, 
which is currently only the case for the heating vessel. To fully automate the setup, an automatically operating 
pump could supply the draw solution with the same amount of water that permeates through the membrane. 
This would make the setup completely autonomous. The user would only have to add the draw solution and 
DI water and start the experiment. The system could then operate for any given period and do continuous 
data collection.
