Abstract
This thesis investigates the performance of hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems in
forensic investigation scenarios. Three state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art systems were chosen and evalu┬¡
ated throughout several experiments to test how well these systems can identify a subject
based on hand images. Additionally, three hand image datasets of various complexity
were used to evaluate the biometric recognition accuracy in conditions ranging from ideal
to challenging.
The results presented in this project show that, while the tested systems can reliably
perform biometric recognition on the controlled data, they perform significantly worse in
uncontrolled scenarios.
The experiments conducted in this thesis indicate that the hand position and rotation,
as well as the background, can significantly impact the accuracy of the tested models.
This report concludes with the propositions of further studies that could counteract these
factors and additional future research perspectives.

Introduction
Biometric recognition is a process of identifying individuals based on their physical or
behavioural characteristics. Recognising people based on their fingerprints has been
possible since the beginning of the 20th century. In the 1960ÔÇÖs the automatic fingerprint
identification system (AFIS) was implemented, allowing for automation of identity verifi┬¡
cation [1].
Later, as the camera surveillance technology developed, face and gait recognition [2] be┬¡
came crucial parts of the forensic investigation. These techniques became even more
successful with the recent progress in the field of machine learning. For example, models
like FaceNet [3] or Deep Face Recognition [4] approach human┬¡level recognition accu┬¡
racy.
However, sometimes, especially in cases of serious crimes, face images or fingerprints
are not available. That is why there is a need for other ways to perform biometric recog┬¡
nition. A hand is one of the primary biometric characteristics [5] and can give distinctive
features for biometric recognition.
Early approaches to hand┬¡based recognition used hand┬¡crafted features [5]. Over time,
different hand features have been used for biometric recognition. These features include
palmprints, knuckle patterns or hand shapes.
Nevertheless, as the author of [6] wrote, ÔÇ£...recently, convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
have been largely outperforming hand┬¡engineered features┬¡based methodsÔÇØ. That is why
current state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems employ deep learning
to perform identification based on hand images with very high accuracy [6], [7], [8]. They
employ CNNs to extract features from hand photos and then compare them to find a
match.
However, these systems are primarily evaluated in a closed┬¡set scenario, which means
the unidentified individual is known to be already registered in the system. Additionally, the
datasets used in these evaluations contain controlled hand images like the ones present
in 11k Hands [6] and Hong Kong Polytechnic University Hand Dorsal dataset [9]. This
means the models are tested in ideal conditions for biometric recognition. Unfortunately,
in the case of forensic investigation, the acquired data is very rarely ideal.
The focus of this thesis is to investigate the performance of state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based
biometric recognition systems in conditions more closely resembling real forensic investi┬¡
gations. The aim is to test these systems on uncontrolled data where the background and
hand position are not predefined. These scenarios are much more similar to the actual
applications of such systems and give a good overview of their performance in non┬¡ideal
conditions.

2.1
Biometric Systems
A biometric system is a system that uses physical or behavioural characteristics to iden┬¡
tify or verify a subjectÔÇÖs identity. Physical characteristics refer to the unique physical traits
of a subject that can be used for identification. Examples include fingerprints, facial pat┬¡
terns, hand geometry, vein patterns, iris or retina. Behavioural characteristics refer to the
unique ways an individual behaves. These include gait, signature or keystroke dynamics.
Humans have many different traits and measurements that can be used as biometric
characteristics. As described in [10], several important factors are taken into consideration
when deciding which of them would be useful for biometric recognition. These factors are
ÔÇó Universality: The biometric characteristic should be present in a majority of the
population, or it would exclude a significant number of potential users.
ÔÇó Distinctiveness: The biometric characteristic should be unique to each individual
and not easily replicated.
ÔÇó Permanence: The biometric characteristic should not change over time. Otherwise,
the stored templates would become obsolete and have to be constantly updated.
ÔÇó Collectability: The biometric characteristic should be easily captured using avail┬¡
able technology and can be measured quantitatively.
ÔÇó Performance: The biometric systemÔÇÖs software should be able to accurately and
quickly match the biometric data to the stored templates.
ÔÇó Acceptability: The biometric characteristic should be accepted by the users and
not be culturally or socially unacceptable.
ÔÇó Circumvention: The biometric system should be difficult to bypass or fool, such as
using a fake finger to bypass a fingerprint recognition system.
Real biometric systems are not perfect and often have trade┬¡offs between different re┬¡
quirements. Depending on the use case, specific biometric characteristics may be more
important than others. In addition, multiple biometric characteristics can be combined to
create one more robust system.

2.1.1
Hand as a biometric characteristic
Hand┬¡based biometric recognition is being used in a wide range of applications. Therefore
it is vital to consider the factors mentioned in the previous section when determining the
suitability of the hand as a biometric characteristic.
ÔÇó Universality: Most hand┬¡based biometric properties are present in the vast majority
of the population.
ÔÇó Distinctiveness: Some biometric properties like hand geometry are not highly unique
and are mostly suited for smaller┬¡scale biometric systems [11]. On the other hand,
modalities like vein patterns and palmprints are exceptionally distinctive.
ÔÇó Permanence: Aging can significantly impact many biometric traits by introducing
wrinkles and other deformations. This can reduce the effectiveness of biometric
systems [11]. These properties can also be affected by diseases, like arthritis af┬¡
fecting the hand geometry [12]. However, some of the biometric characteristics, like
palmprints, have been shown to be stable for more than 15 years [13]. Vein patterns
have also been observed to be stable after entering adulthood [14].
ÔÇó Collectability: Most hand┬¡based biometric samples are easy to collect with just a
camera.
ÔÇó Performance: Most state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems achieve
high performance on controlled data (over 90%) [8] [7]. However, the ways of in┬¡
creasing the performance on uncontrolled data is an active field of research [ntu_pi].
ÔÇó Acceptability: Most of the biometric samples collected for hand┬¡based biometric
systems are non┬¡intrusive and easy to capture [15]. This makes the hand a widely
acceptable biometric characteristic.
ÔÇó Circumvention: There are multiple ways a biometric system can be attacked [16].
Though, different biometric characteristics of the hand might have different suscep┬¡
tibility to attacks. The more general the biometric system is, the more vulnerable it
becomes. However, some biometric modalities are much harder to circumvent. For
example, vein patterns have been shown to be difficult to spoof or forge [17] [14].
2.1.2
Operational modes of Biometric System
A biometric system can operate in two modes: verification and identification.
Verification: Also known as ÔÇØone┬¡to┬¡oneÔÇØ or ÔÇØauthenticationÔÇØ mode. In this mode, the
biometric system compares a newly captured biometric sample against a stored template
to confirm the subjectÔÇÖs identity. This mode is commonly used for access control, time and
attendance tracking, and other situations where a person needs to prove their identity.
Identification: Also known as ÔÇØone┬¡to┬¡manyÔÇØ or ÔÇØrecognitionÔÇØ mode. This is when the bio┬¡
metric system matches a newly captured biometric sample against multiple stored tem┬¡
plates to find the best match. In this mode, the system will identify the subject if a match
is found or indicate that no match was found (in an open┬¡set scenario). In addition, iden┬¡
tification mode can be used for negative recognition [18], where the system establishes if
a person is whom they say they are not. This mode is commonly used for forensic inves┬¡
tigations, where an unknown individual should be matched to a pre┬¡existing database of
templates. Therefore this is the mode primarily used in this project.

2.1.3
An architecture of a Biometric System
A biometric system can be divided into subsystems. Each subsystem handles one of the
processes necessary for biometric verification or identification.
Figure 2.1: An overview of the biometric system architecture [19]
Figure 2.1 shows an overview of an architecture of a biometric system presented in [19].
Data Capture Subsystem
The task of the data capture subsystem is to capture a biometric sample from a subject
using one or many biometric capture devices. For hand┬¡based biometric systems, this
sample can be, e.g. an image of a hand.
Signal Processing Subsystem
The signal processing subsystem performs segmentation, feature extraction and quality
control of the captured biometric sample.
The segmentation process involves the separation of the biometric characteristic from the
rest of the collected sample. For example, in the case of hand┬¡based systems, this could
be finding the palm region in the hand image based on landmark points.
The feature extraction aims to extract the relevant biometric feature from the segmented
sample. For a hand┬¡based system, this could be finger lengths for hand geometry features
or the CNN feature vectors for the machine learning features.
Lastly, the extracted features can be accepted or rejected during quality control based
on predefined thresholds. This step is important so that the biometric system does not
store low┬¡quality biometric templates or use them for comparison with already registered
templates.
Data Storage Subsystem
The data storage subsystem stores extracted biometric templates of the enrolled subjects
as biometric references.
Comparison Subsystem
The comparison subsystem compares the newly extracted templates against the biomet┬¡
ric references stored by the system. In case of verification, the extracted template is

==PAGE:16==
compared against the reference associated with the claimed identity.
For identification, the extracted template is compared against many references to find the
best match. For hand┬¡based systems, this could be calculating the cosine distance be┬¡
tween the query templates and the reference templates. After the comparisons are made,
the comparison subsystem outputs the comparison score.
Decision subsystem
The decision subsystem compares the comparison score with a predefined threshold to
accept or reject the query template. In the case of verification, acceptance means verify┬¡
ing the identity of the subject. On the other hand, for identification, the decision module
outputs the best match or a list of candidates that match the given template.
Gallery and query sets
A gallery is a set of data used to evaluate a biometric system. It contains a collection
of biometric templates (in the case of this project, images of hands), and it is used as a
reference for identification. The gallery is considered a collection of subjects ÔÇ£knownÔÇØ to
the system against which the new samples are matched.
A query is a set of biometric data used to test the performance of a biometric system. It
contains a collection of samples that are to be identified. A query simulates a real┬¡world
scenario where the biometric system needs to match new, never before seen samples
against the gallery set.
2.1.4
Identification scenarios
The identification operation can be done in either a closed┬¡set or open┬¡set scenario.
When the query sample is known to be in the reference database, it is referred to as
a closed┬¡set identification. In this case, the system is attempting to match the query
sample against a pre┬¡defined set of templates and only needs to determine which template
the query sample belongs to. This scenario is often used in situations such as access
control, where the system is trying to confirm that the person seeking access is one of the
registered subjects. The authors of [20] state that ÔÇØ... in the literature, most performance
evaluations are conducted in the closed┬¡set modeÔÇØ.
When the query sample contains samples from subjects registered in the reference database
as well as the not registered ones, it is referred to as an open┬¡set identification. In this
case, the system attempts to match the query sample against the entire population of
templates and determine whether or not the subject represented by the probe sample is
in the reference database. As mentioned in [20], ÔÇØ..most biometric identification systems
in real┬¡world applications operate in the open┬¡set modeÔÇØ. For example, this scenario is
often used in forensic investigations, where the system tries to match an unknown subject
to a pre┬¡existing database of templates.
2.1.5
Controlled and uncontrolled scenarios
The scenarios for data capture for biometric recognition can be divided into controlled and
uncontrolled.
A controlled scenario, also known as a cooperative scenario, is one in which the condi┬¡
tions for capturing and processing biometric data are known and carefully controlled. This
typically includes control over the environment, lighting and position of the subjectÔÇÖs face

or body (in the case of this project, hand). Additionally, biometric data is usually captured
using specialised equipment, such as high┬¡resolution cameras or scanners.
Figure 2.2: Examples of hand images in a controlled scenario from 11k Hands database
[6]
The examples of data for a controlled hand┬¡based recognition scenario can be seen in
figure 2.2. It should be noted that, the hands are captured using a high┬¡resolution camera
in previously defined positions. Furthermore, the lighting and the distance from the cam┬¡
era to the hand are constant. These conditions make performing biometric recognition
much easier. Based on this fact, controlled scenarios are often used to establish baseline
performance for biometric systems.
An uncontrolled scenario or uncooperative scenario is one in which the conditions for
capturing and processing biometric data are unknown or subject to significant variations.
This can include outdoor environments with varying lighting conditions and subjects that
may be in motion or not perfectly positioned. In addition, biometric data may be cap┬¡
tured using varying equipment such as smartphones, tablets, or webcams. Uncontrolled
scenarios are generally more challenging for biometric recognition systems because the
variations in lighting and positioning can make it more difficult to capture and process the
biometric data accurately. As a result, the recognition accuracy of biometric systems is
generally lower in uncontrolled scenarios than in controlled scenarios.

2.2
Standardised Metrics for the Evaluation of Biometric
Systems
Various measures could be used to evaluate the performance of a biometric system [22].
In this section, we describe the metrics used in this project.
Accuracy measures how well a biometric system can correctly identify individuals. It is
calculated as the number of correct identifications divided by the total number of identifi┬¡
cations.
For the closed┬¡set scenarios, the systems presented in this project identify the subjects
from the query set by calculating their similarity score for every sample in the gallery set.
After that, the gallery sample with the highest similarity score is then considered a match.
Cumulative Match Characteristic (CMC) is a metric used to evaluate a biometric sys┬¡
temÔÇÖs performance. It is calculated by counting the number of times the correct individual
is ranked within the top n matches returned by the system and then expressing this as
a percentage of the total number of query samples. CMC is presented by plotting the
proportion of correct matches for different values of n (usually from 1 to the number of
individuals in the gallery) against the different ranks.
False Positive Identification Rate (FPIR) measures how often a biometric system incor┬¡
rectly classifies a non┬¡mated subject as a matching one. It is calculated as the number of
false positive matches divided by the total number of non┬¡mated samples.
False Negative Identification Rate (FNIR) measures how often a biometric system in┬¡
correctly classifies a mated sample as not matching. It is calculated as the number of
false negative matches divided by the total number of mated samples.
A Detection Error Trade┬¡off (DET) curve is a graphical representation of the trade┬¡off
between the FPIR and the FNIR for various threshold values.
In an open┬¡set scenario, a biometric system can operate with different threshold values
separating the matching and non┬¡matching samples. If the threshold is too high, the sys┬¡
tem will get many false negative errors as it rejects more legitimate samples. On the other
hand, if the threshold is too low, the system will accept more non┬¡matching samples lead┬¡
ing to more false positive errors.
The DET curve allows visualising the trade┬¡off between the systemÔÇÖs ability to match le┬¡
gitimate users correctly and its ability to reject imposters correctly. It is an effective tool
for comparing the performance of different biometric systems or different configurations
of the same system.
Equal Error Rate (EER) is a measure of the performance of a biometric system. EER is
the point on the DET curve where the FPIR and FNIR are equal. It represents the point
where the number of false positives is equal to the number of false negatives, which is
the point for the system that balances convenience (low FNIR) with security (low FPIR).

Basic Machine Learning overview
Machine learning is a branch of computational algorithms that teaches computers to
learn from data without being explicitly programmed. There are four primary types of
machine learning algorithms: supervised, unsupervised, semi┬¡supervised, and reinforce┬¡
ment learning. The models used in this project are based on the first type.
Supervised learning is a type of machine learning in which the model is ÔÇ£... learning a
mapping between a set of input variables X and an output variable Y and applying this
mapping to predict the outputs for unseen data.ÔÇØ [23]. In other words, the model is given
a dataset of labelled input┬¡output pairs, and its task is to learn a function that would map
the input to the output. The algorithm is trained on this labelled dataset to later make
predictions on new unseen data. The goal is to accurately predict the output based on
the input.
Supervised learning can be separated into two types of problems:
ÔÇó Classification uses a model to assign a label or a category to data.
ÔÇó Regression is used to learn the relationship between dependent and independent
values and predict continuous variables like prices.
Support Vector Machine (SVM) is a type of supervised learning algorithm. The SVM
aims to find a hyperplane or decision boundary in an n┬¡dimensional (n is the number of
data features) space that separates the data points into distinct classes. There can be
many possible hyperplanes to separate data points. The SVM aims to find the optimal
hyperplane. The optimal hyperplane is the one which has the maximum distance between
data points of different classes.
Figure 2.4 presents the example of an optimal hyperplane for the two┬¡dimensional data
from two classes. The data points closest to the hyperplane and affect its position are
called support vectors (filled shapes in the figure). The dimensions of the hyperplane de┬¡
pend on several features in the dataset. For example, if there are two features (as shown
in figure 2.4) and if there are three features, then the hyperplane will be a 2┬¡dimensional
plane. When the data is non┬¡linearly separable (like in an example in figure 2.4), SVM
transforms it into higher dimensional feature space, where the data point becomes linearly
separable. The mathematical function that performs the transformation is known as the
kernel.

The Attentive but Diverse Network [47] is a person re┬¡identification neural network pub┬¡

its initial task is similar. As the authors wrote, ÔÇ£Person Re┬¡Identification (Re┬¡ID) aims to

system can be used for hand┬¡based biometric recognition with just minimal adjustments.
ABD employs a mechanism of attention [48] that allows it to focus on specific regions of
the image.
The main idea behind the ABD model is that the extracted features were often highly cor┬¡

the matching performanceÔÇØ.
The goal of the ABD model was to calculate feature embedding that would be attentive but
diverse. Attentive feature embedding aims to align the image correctly, remove any back┬¡
ground disturbances, and concentrate on the specific, distinguishable parts of a personÔÇÖs
appearance. On the other side, diverse feature embedding aims to reduce the correlation
between features making them more extensive.

Multi┬¡branch with Attention Network (MBA) [8] is a hand┬¡based recognition model pub┬¡
lished in 2021. Similarly to the previously described models, this system is also attention┬¡
based. Additionally, as was the case for the ABD and RGA, the MBA incorporates both
channel and spatial attention modules.
However, the MBA incorporates relative positional encodings along the rows (height) and
columns (width) into the spatial attention module. This technique is used in order to over┬¡
come one of the weaknesses of other attention mechanisms which is not taking into ac┬¡
count the spatial positions of the pixels.
Moreover, the MBA applies the attention modules only to low┬¡resolution feature maps
in later stages of deep network branches which improves computational efficiency and
accuracy of identification.
Figure 4.8: Architecture of MBA
The MBA uses three sets of features as seen in figure 4.8. These feature sets come from
three distinct branches of the network. The Channel Attention Module (CAM) branch (bot┬¡
tom), the Spatial Attention Module with Relative Positional Encodings (SAM┬¡RPE) branch
(top) and the global branch (middle). The features calculated by the branches with atten┬¡
tion modules aim to extract the relevant hand features while suppressing the irrelevant
backgrounds and the global branch focuses on capturing global structural information for
discriminative feature learning. All three sets of vectors are then concatenated and com┬¡
pared between different hands using cosine distance.

Implementation details
The models used in this project were trained and tested using NVIDIA A100 Tensor Core

size of the images was adjusted to fit the default setting of all the models. This means

contrast, the RGA model was trained for 600 (the same number as in the original paper)

The second experiment involves calculating each modelÔÇÖs performance in a controlled en┬¡

Similar to experiment 1 (5.1.1), this experiment aims to establish a baseline performance

7
Summary
This chapter presents the summary of the project as well as future perspectives for re┬¡
search in the field of hand┬¡based biometric recognition for forensic investigation.
This project aimed to investigate the performance of state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based biometric
recognition systems for forensics investigation. While there are multiple recent research
papers investigating hand┬¡based biometric recognition [8], [7], the effectiveness of the
presented systems is not sufficiently tested in conditions close to a real forensic investi┬¡
gation.
Three state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art deep learning models (described in section 4.1) were chosen to test
the performance of the hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems. The tested systems
were: ABD [47], RGA [49], and MBA [8]. Eleven experimental scenarios have been pro┬¡
posed in chapter 5 to give a broad overview of the performance of these models. These
experiments test the accuracy of biometric recognition of the chosen models in several
scenarios, from ideal conditions to ones close to real forensic investigation.
Furthermore, these tests have been conducted using three datasets of varying complexity
described in section 4.2.
The results of the proposed experiments are presented in chapter 6. Based on them, it can
be concluded that while the chosen systems can reliably perform biometric recognition in
ideal conditions, some work is still needed to make them more reliable in uncontrolled
scenarios. Furthermore, factors like hand position and image background, as well as the
amount of training data, can significantly impact the performance of these models.
Hence, further research is needed to improve the accuracy of the hand┬¡based biometric
recognition systems on uncontrolled data to make them more suitable for forensic inves┬¡
tigation applications.

investigation is conducted in an open┬¡set scenario where there is no guarantee that the
suspect is already registered in the database. In this project, an experiment to investigate
the performance of chosen state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems in
an open┬¡set scenario has been planned and conducted (see experiment 5.1.3).
The results of this experiment show that for the controlled data, there is a significant dis┬¡
crepancy between the modelsÔÇÖ performance. MBA, the best┬¡performing system, yielded a
FNIR of 10.18% at a FPIR = 0.1%. This means that one out of 1000 non┬¡mated samples
is accepted while at most around 10 out of 100 mated samples are rejected by the system.
This result shows that MBA can relatively easily distinguish which samples come from the
already registered subjects (present in the gallery set). On the other hand, RGA and ABD
models did not perform well in the open┬¡set test scoring FNRI of 58.8% and 83.04% at
0.1% FPIR, respectively.
Nevertheless, the results of the MBA system prove that a hand┬¡based biometric recogni┬¡
tion model can be used to reliably perform biometric identification in an open┬¡set scenario
on controlled data.
Research question 2:
What is the performance of hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems on uncontrolled
data similar to the real┬¡world scenario?
In the vast majority of cases, state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems
are developed and evaluated using controlled datasets. The performance on controlled
data might be a good indication of how well these systems can extract relevant biomet┬¡
ric features in ideal conditions. However, the conditions during a forensic investigation
are very rarely ideal. Because of that, it is crucial to test how these systems perform in
scenarios closer to actual application.
In this project, several experiments have been proposed to investigate the performance
of state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems on uncontrolled data. These
experiments differ in the amount and complexity of the data.
Based on the results of these experiments, it can be concluded that for the ÔÇ£easierÔÇØ uncon┬¡
trolled dataset, the tested systemsÔÇÖ performance dropped by 33, 54.3 and 42.7 percent┬¡
age points, respectively, for ABD, RGA and MBA (see table 6.5) compared to controlled
dataset experiments. While this is a significant decrease, the ABD and MBA were still
able to achieve a rank┬¡5 accuracy above 74% on the ÔÇ£easierÔÇØ uncontrolled dataset. In
a forensic investigation, it is often more important for the biometric system to output an
accurate list of potential ÔÇ£suspectsÔÇØ instead of just a single identity. Because of that, the
result above 74% for a list of 5 subjects is still promising.
Research question 3:
What is the difference in the performance of hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems
between slightly uncontrolled and fully uncontrolled data?
In this project, there were two uncontrolled datasets used HaGRID (see 4.2.3) and NTU┬¡

PI (see 4.2.3). The most significant difference between them is that HaGRID contains
hand images with predefined gestures, while NTU┬¡PI captures natural hand positions.
Performing and comparing experiments on both datasets can show the hand positionÔÇÖs
impact on the performance of biometric recognition systems.
In the HaGRID dataset experiment, the best┬¡performing model (ABD) achieved the rank┬¡

identification.
Research question 4:
What is the impact of hand tattoos on the identification performance of state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art
hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems?
A good biometric recognition system should be robust in a forensic investigation scenario.
One of the things that impact robustness is the systemÔÇÖs sensitivity to small changes in
the data. For example, in the case of hand┬¡based recognition, a change like that could
be the presence of a hand tattoo.
In this project, an experiment investigating the impact of tattoos on the performance of
state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art biometric recognition systems has been proposed (see 5.1.11). In the
presented experiment, an algorithm to add artificial tattoos has been used to augment the
controlled dorsal data.
The results of this experiment (presented in table 6.11 and figure 6.11) show that on two
of the tested systems, the presence of tattoos had no impact. ABD and RGA accuracy
scores have been the same with and without tattoos. However, the MBA, which in the
experiment without the tattoos was the best performing model, scored significantly lower
during the experiment with tattoos. Its rank┬¡1 accuracy dropped from 96.7% to 88.1%.
This indicates that while the MBA was the best system for the regular controlled data test,
it might not be the most robust.
7.2
Future research perspectives
This section describes the proposed future work related to the topic of this thesis.
7.2.1
Gesture alignment
The differences in the performance of the systems for ÔÇØeasyÔÇØ and complex, uncontrolled
data show the significant impact of the hand position. Because of that, it might be possible
to noticeably increase the accuracy of the hand┬¡based biometric recognition by adding
the gesture alignment step to the data preprocessing. The alignment can be achieved in
multiple ways. One example of possible implementation can be found in [31], where the
authors recognise the edges of the palm and rotate the image so that the hand positions
are aligned.

7.2.2
Region of interest extraction
Similarly to gesture alignment, it might be interesting to investigate if the performance of
the hand┬¡based biometric recognition systems could be improved by extracting a region
of interest from the hand image first. A similar process has been presented in [21] where
Matkowski et al. used a CNN to find and extract the palm region from the uncontrolled
hand data. Extracting just the significant part of the hand image could decrease the back┬¡
groundÔÇÖs impact on the identification accuracy.
7.2.3
Biometric recognition system bias
As biometric systems are more prevalent in todayÔÇÖs world, the questions of ethics are
being brought up more often. One of the vital aspects of biometric systems ethics is
the possible bias that those systems might exhibit, and as such, it is a field of ongoing
research [59] [60]. For biometric systems used for forensic investigation, the problem
of bias is especially important, as a biased system might lead, e.g. to wrongful arrests.
Therefore it might be worth investigating if the features like gender or ethnicity affect hand┬¡
based recognition systems. The data from the 11k Hands dataset used in this project is
already annotated with the gender, skin colour and age of the subjects, which can be used
to research the problem of bias. Additional datasets could also cover a more broad range
of potentially impactful features.
7.2.4
Additional factors impacting hand┬¡based biometric recognition
This project investigated the performance of hand┬¡based biometric systems on uncon┬¡
trolled data close to real forensic investigation scenarios. However, there are several
significant differences between uncontrolled and controlled data. Therefore, studying the
individual impact different factors can have on biometric recognition accuracy might be
interesting. Examples of such factors include hand position and rotation or image blur┬¡
riness. Finding the factors impacting biometric recognition performance could be vital in
counteracting these effects. The data required for such tests might be acquired by finding
new datasets or applying preprocessing techniques to the ones proposed in this project.
