Abstract
Timoshenko beam vibration theory includes rotary interia and shear deformation. It is a
1D beam theory that is suitable to use for short beams or in high-frequency cases where
the wavelength approaches the thickness of the beam. For the speciﬁc case of a free-free
beam Timoshenko theory predicts a critical frequency. The predicted mode shapes change
and the natural frequencies are in pairs beyond the critical frequency. For the case of a
simply supported beam Timoshenko theory gives two distinct sets of frequencies. It has
been a long standing debate whether or not the predicted frequencies and mode shapes
past the critical frequency are physical meaningful, and, if so, how accurate the predictions
are. More recently a modiﬁed version of Timoshenko beam theory that has no critical
frequency has been proposed by Elishakoﬀ.
The project scrutinizes both theories in the free-free beam case, and compares the pre-
dictions to experiments performed with a Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer. The ex-
periments reveal the existence of frequency pairs and very complex mode shapes past the
critical frequency, that show agreement with Timoshenko’s predictions. The deviations
between the natural frequencies of Timoshenko theory and experiment are in the order of
10 % when beyond the critical frequency. A 3D model Finite Element model shows agree-
ment with Timoshenko beam theory. Additionally the project considers the inﬂuence of
boundary conditions through a brief study of the simply supported beam.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Timoshenko beam vibration theory (TBT) is a popular theory in engineering mechan-
ics [1]. Unlike the Bernoulli-Euler model [1], it takes shear deformation and rotary inertia
into account. It is suitable to use for short beams, or in high-frequency cases, where the
wavelength approaches the thickness of the beam. It has however been subject to much
debate over the last decades, as the physical assumptions are not that straight-forward.
The model produces a so-called ’second spectrum’, because of its’ bi-quadratic frequency
equation, that has given rise to many questions [2]. In 2010, Elishakoﬀ[3] proposed, ac-
cording to him, a simpler, and more consistent, diﬀerential equation, than Timoshenko’s
original equation. The last term in Timoshenko’s equation is a higher order term, but
according to Elishakoﬀ, it is inconsistent, as not all higher order terms are included [3].
Elishakoﬀ’s derivation produces an equation without the last higher order term, why the
’second spectrum’ is also avoided.
The aim of this thesis is to examine Elishakoﬀ’s modiﬁed version of the Timoshenko beam
vibration theory (EBT) as well as the original, with an experimental approach. It includes
an examination of both theories’ mode shapes and natural frequencies. The properties of
both theories depend on the boundary conditions. For a simply supported beam, the two
theories do not predict the same ’ﬁrst spectrum’. The modiﬁed theory does not support
a ’second spectrum’. If EBT shows an equal or superior ﬁt to experimental data, it will
render the discussion of the second spectrum redundant. If not, it is looked into, whether
the second spectrum can be found in the laboratory, and how well TBT predicts it.
Timoshenko developed the theory already in 1921 [4], and it has been widely used ever
since [5], despite the recurring debates. It is interesting to review both Timoshenko’s
original theory and Elishakoﬀ’s modiﬁed, because if a simpler diﬀerential equation can
produce as accurate predictions of experimental results as the original, much time can be
saved in mathematical modelling, and the ’second spectrum’ does not have be a cause
of endless worry. The modiﬁed version has been present for some time, and it has been
hovering about without properly being rejected or accepted.
Very recent papers, e.g.
[6], has reviewed the original Timoshenko theory and concluded it to be a good ﬁt to
experimental data, at the same time as Elishakoﬀ[2] was promoting the modiﬁed version.
All in all it is a turmoil of theories, data, and methods, but so far no experiments seem
to have been conducted, where both of the models are set up against experimental data.

2
1 Introduction
It is relevant now to do so, as modern measuring equipment makes it possible to measure
natural frequencies and modes shapes, in a much more precise manner than earlier. By
using a Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer (SLDV), the mode shapes can be recorded in
a ﬁne mesh. This will improve accuracy, and make a comparison between the two models
easier.
In [2] Elishakoﬀreviews the TBT and provides an overview of the current knowledge. It
gives a good idea of how extensive the debate is, as it has more than a hundred references
to articles discussing the existence of the second spectrum, the meaning of it and the
geniality of TBT. This thesis’ background material is build on Elishakoﬀ’s review. It is
deemed a good foundation for a thorough analysis, as references include both sides of the
discussion, as well as both old and new articles. There are several researchers who have
made numerical experiments [7,8], ﬁnding that the second spectrum is physically mean-
ingless, and that Timoshenko’s predictions above a so-called cut-oﬀfrequency should be
disregarded. Others claimed the opposite [9–11]. Most papers are of a theoretical sort,
and papers describing experimental observations are scarce. Recently Diaz-de-Anda et
al. [6,12] made an experimental study of a free-free beam, with a novel set up, consisting
of an electromagnetic acoustic transducer (EMAT). They found that a critical frequency
exists, and that for frequencies above the critical value, certain special eﬀects where found.
They conclude, that the theory is still valid above the critical frequency. It is therefore
of interest, to test if the same can be observed by a Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometry
setup. There are many aspects of TBT, and each researcher has focused on diﬀerent ﬁnd-
ings, depending on the boundary conditions. The thesis will consider the mode shapes
and natural frequencies, for diﬀerent boundary conditions, as well as study terms used in
the research papers, such as cut-oﬀ-frequencies and Timoshenko-doublets.
In this project both versions of the theory are derived. As many researchers have studied
the subject previously, a background study is made to avoid duplications, and to make
out where the issue stands at the moment. The pivot of the project is the use of the
Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer. The main part of the project is the design and im-
plementation of the SLDV experiment, measuring the free vibrations of a free-free beam.
The results of the experiments are compared to both theories. A numerical model of 3D
FEM elements, that does not use neither TBT or EBT assumptions, is made, to have a
reference that is based on a diﬀerent theory.
The thesis is organized as follows; The background of the Timoshenko beam theory as
well as the modiﬁed version is derived in Chapter 2. In Chapter 3 the work of others and
the disagreements between them are outlined in greater extent than in this introduction,
to make the ﬁnal discussion in tune with what other has been concerned with.
The
experimental setups and procedures are presented in Chapter 4. Chapter 5 describes the
setup of a FEM model of a beam and the associated results. Chapter 6 presents the
experimental results. Chapter 7 is a discussion of the experimental results with respect
to the TBT controversy. Conclusive remarks, along with proposals for further work are
presented in Chapter 8.

2.5
Summary
This chapter derived and discussed the equations of motion for both TBT and EBT.
The general eigenfunctions are laid out for both cases. It showed how the solution to
the TBT equations changes as a critical frequency ωc is reached, and how EBT does not
qualitatively change. References to BE-theory show that up until ωc the mode shapes
are the same as for TBT. BE mode shapes are to some extent easier to model. Two
speciﬁc cases are considered. The free-free beam and the simply supported beam are
mathematically inﬂuenced by ωc in two diﬀerent ways. The free-free beam by α’s turn
from real to complex. It will aﬀect the solutions of the frequency equation and the mode
shapes.
In what precise manner this aﬀects the natural frequencies will be shown in
Chapter 6, as it is not obvious from the equations at hand. The simply supported beam
however directly shows two sets of frequencies for TBT, and only one for EBT.

Chapter 3
The TBT Controversy
The previous chapter presented and derived TBT and EBT. This chapter presents some
of the opposing points of view and perspectives to the Timoshenko theory; outlined in
greater detail than in the Introduction.
As stated in the Introduction, there has over time been opposing opinions on the ’second
spectrum’. The chapter is split into sections, each dealing with the diﬀerent issues treated
in the current debate. The following issues are considered:
• The value of the shear coeﬃcient k.
• The existence and accuracy of the ’second spectrum’.
• The inﬂuence from the set of boundary conditions.
• Review of previous experimental results and interpretations.
• The Timoshenko doublets (frequency pairs) and the reason for their occurrence.
• The interpretation of numerical results (3D-theory).
Some of the issues are discussed independently, others in part of the general scope of
either a theoretic or experimental angle.
3.1
The Timoshenko Shear Coeﬃcient
The shear coeﬃcient known as k or κ is a correction factor. It accounts for the variation
in the shear stress across the cross section [17]. It is not the main focus in this project,
and is set to k = 10(1 + ν)/(12 + 11ν) ≈0.8497, which is a tabulation for a rectangular
cross section by Cowper from 1966 [18]. It is nevertheless a parameter which has been
discussed repeatedly [7,17,19]. The assumed value will of course inﬂuence the result, for
both TBT and EBT. Diﬀerent values which have been used are k = 1/1.2 ≈0.833, sug-
gested for a rectangular cross section in [20], also seen is k = 5(1+ν)/(6+5ν) ≈0.8667 [7].
==PAGE:28==
18
3 The TBT Controversy
It is assumed, that the cross-sectional area is planar when obtaining a value for the shear
coeﬃcient, the same assumption is used in TBT. According to [12], this is only true for
frequencies below fc. In [12] it is experimentally determined that the slope ψ along the
cross-sectional area is no longer constant for the ’second spectrum’ in TBT.
A recent article [19] sums up on the work done on the shear coeﬃcient. It compares, for
diﬀerent values of k, the deviation between the TBT natural frequencies to experimental
results from [12]. The error is in the range 0.5-3%, depending on the value of k. It is
therefore important which value is chosen. The article suggests that the value of the
shear coeﬃcient above the critical frequency is diﬀerent from the value below, and that
it is smaller. The main point in highlighting this coeﬃcient is the fact that it is not a
coeﬃcient set in stone. The value of k may be connected to whether one is considering
frequencies below or above fc.
3.2
Theoretic and Numeric Contributions
The Introduction states that Elishakoﬀin his 2015 paper [2] cites more than a hundred
articles. The subject is therefore extensively studied, and over many decades. Each paper
has a diﬀerent approach and it is easy to mix up terms and conclusions. To make matters
more tangible this section tries to consider four main questions that have been asked or
answered in the many scientiﬁc papers. The angle is only theoretic and numeric.
• The simple beam: Can the ’two spectra’ solution be questioned?
• Is the ’second spectrum’ physically meaningful?
• If the ’second spectrum’ is a physical occurrence - How well does TBT predict it?
• How do the boundary conditions come into play?
The two partial diﬀerential equations (2.11) for TBT act in diﬀerent ways depending on
the boundary conditions. Chapter 2 shows that a free-free beam does not split into two
diﬀerent sets of frequencies, but beyond fc a shift is seen in the solutions. The simply
supported beam factorizes and gives two sets of frequencies. From a mathematical point
of view the distinct frequencies are in that case a fact, and it is a ’two spectra’ solution.
This conclusion is backed-up by Elishakoﬀin [2]. He writes: ’Timoshenko’s 1921 theory
predicts two frequency spectra in Eq (11) and (12). This is an undisputed fact.’ (Eq.
(2.44) and (2.45) in this report). Despite this, the acknowledgement of the ’two spectra
interpretation’ is questioned by several authors, e.g. [21, 22].
In this author’s humble
opinion, it is also a matter of perception and what meaning is attached to a ’two-spectra’
interpretation. No matter if the boundary conditions factorises the frequency equation or
not, a critical frequency is present. Whether or not the modes and natural frequencies
found above fc are deemed a distinct new spectra, or a part of the same, the main interest
must be if the predicted modes above that value exist or not.

A paper by Stephen [7], from 2005, is a review of earlier work on TBT along with a numer-
ical study. It considers diﬀerent boundary conditions, and ﬁnds that guided-guided and
guided-hinged boundary conditions also gives factorization of the Timoshenko frequency
equation. With the factorization it concludes that there are mathematically two distinct
spectra. It makes comparisons to a numerical study and ﬁnds a poor correlation for the
’second spectrum’ to plane stress elastodynamic theory. The conclusion is that the second
spectrum of TBT should be seen as non-physical, and disregarded for all these boundary
conditions. Not long after, in 2009, Bhaskar [23] wrote the contradictory statement: The
second spectrum is associated with thickness-shear motion and it must not be disregarded.,
and: Note that the relatively poor accuracy of the second spectrum with respect to detailed
theories cannot be a measure of its physical meaningfulness. The disagreement between
the two authors regarding the physical meaningfulness of the second spectrum emanate
from an analysis of a higher derivative Lagrangian. The bottom line is: Bhaskar [23]
believe the Lagrangian proposed by Stephen [7] to be inadmissible for describing the dy-
namics of a Timoshenko beam. It is beyond this author’s insight to uncover if one or
another is right, but another way to consider the physicality is through experiments. If
experiments give mode shapes corresponding to the ’second spectrum’ predictions, then it
gives reason to believe that [23] has a point when it comes to his analysis of the Lagrangian.
The case of a clamped-clamped beam is considered in [24]. It is a numerical study compar-
ing Finite Element Analysis (FEA) in ANSYS with the TBT predictions. It is concluded
that: For modes with frequencies immediately above cut-on TBT fails to predict modes
clearly present in FEA and produces signiﬁcantly diﬀerent mode shapes than FEA. The
paper further states that: TBT is observed to be invalid above the cut-on frequency. The
cut-on frequency is another word for the critical frequency. In [12] a free-free beam is
analysed by Finite Element Analysis, likewise in ANSYS. Here the respective frequencies
shows great correlation, and the authors support the supercritcal TBT predictions. It
indicates that two factors are in play; the accuracy of TBT depends on the boundary
conditions and/or the conclusions drawn from numeric results varies in between authors.
It is again a good reason to additionally consider an experimental approach to settle the
matter.
Summing up, it can be said that:
• TBT can without doubt been seen as a ’two spectra’ solution for cases where the
frequency equation factorizes.
• The physical meaningfulness is still up for debate.
• The accuracy of the TBT predictions seems to depend on the boundary conditions.

3.3
Experimental Contributions
The experimental contributions to this debate are scarce. Previously the limitations in
the measurement equipment made it diﬃcult to measure many points along a beam and
therefore hard to study higher modes. Another reason could be that the controversy is
concerning high frequencies, and it is diﬃcult to excite structures at such high frequen-
cies. Three papers with experimental test have been found, and they come to opposite
conclusions:
One contribution is made by Chan et.
al [25] in 2002.
It supports TBT predictions
beyond fc for a free-free beam. The experiment only has few measurement points. It
is performed by an accelerometer point by point, with a vibration shaker as excitation
source. They ﬁnd the limiting factor to be the shaker, as it goes up to 20 kHz. For that
reason, the applied aluminium beam is very thick (126 mm) to be able to look at frequen-
cies above fc. The critical frequency depends linearly on the beam height, see (2.24). The
paper concludes that the experiments show an excellent agreement with TBT, also for
frequencies above fc. It ﬁnds the ﬁrst two sets of frequency pairs or so-called Timoshenko
doublets. The mode shapes belonging to these doublets show less conclusive results, as
the experiment only consists of 11 measurement points.
Stephen and Puchegger [8] look at at the valid range of TBT for a free-free aluminium
beam; testing a very small beam of l×b×h = 40.04×10.09×10.04 mm. These dimensions
only give 3 natural frequencies below fc. The paper calculates TBT frequencies up to the
10th mode of approx 280 kHz. It is more than 35% larger than fc of 159 kHz. There is no
description of the measurement method in the paper, and it seems that no other paper,
by the same authors, describes it in further detail. The paper only considers the natural
frequencies and not the mode shapes. It ﬁnds TBT to deviate 12% at the 10th mode (one
of the pairs) and concludes TBT to be invalid above fc. Comparisons to an ANSYS-model
shows a match with the measurements. With no insight in the measurements method it is
diﬃcult to draw parallels to other experiments. As the conclusion is rejection of TBT for
f > fc, there are no comments on the behaviour above fc, but the results in [8] also show
that the frequencies comes in pairs, despite the deviations. It is not a far stretch to make
the opposite conclusion based on these results. The beam in question is barely a beam,
but a small block. Considering that TBT is a beam theory, it could actually be perceived
as incredible that the deviation is still less than 4 % up until the 8th mode, which is 33%
larger than the critical frequency. It could also be argued that the comparison to ANSYS
is not quite a fair way of rejecting TBT: ANSYS uses 3D elements, that are not based
on beam theory. If the block for high frequencies instead has e.g. a plate-like behaviour,
ANSYS will not be aﬀected; it will just ﬁnd solutions for the structure it is given.
A recent study by Diaz. et al. [6,12] conﬁrms the tendency for a free-free beam found by
Chan et al. It also concludes TBT to be an excellent theory, even above fc. The paper
makes a set of interesting observations. Below fc they ﬁnd the shearing over cross-section

plotting them against each other; especially because the frequency pairs come very close
together, and not necessarily in the same order as TBT. Here a MAC plot would have been
a very informative additional feature. It could support the validity of TBT above fc and
give an idea of whether TBT modes represents experiments just as well above fc as below.
It has not been possible to ﬁnd other experimental studies; let alone one that agrees with
the results of [8], that stand in contrast to [25] and [6]. Papers testing a simply supported
beam have not been found. Here are the main extracted points and deduced conclusions
of the three papers:
• Three independent experiments ﬁnd the Timoshenko doublets (frequency pairs) for
the free-free beam when f > fc.
• A plain disagreement in the free-free beam case concerning the validity of TBT for
f > fc.
• The experimental natural frequencies and mode shapes need to show agreement
with TBT to validate TBT.
• For f > fc the order of ’odd-even’ modes seems erratic.
• For f > fc the deformation of the cross section seems to interchange between linear
and sinusoidal [6].
• Numerical computation cannot know it is dealing with a ’beam’. It solves whatever
it is given, ignorant of such assumptions.

4.1.1
Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer
Laser Doppler Vibrometry is a measuring technique that takes advantage of the Doppler
shift. The Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer (SLDV) is an instrument that sends oﬀa
laser beam, and the laser beam is directed at the test surface. This is advantageous, as it
does not have to be mounted on the test specimen. Another advantage is that it does not
add mass to the test specimen, as it is not in direct contact with the test surface, unlike
e.g. an accelerometer. The principle is that as the laser beam hits the test specimen,
the reﬂected light changes in frequency. The change in frequency depends on the motion
(velocity) of the test surface, why the vibration amplitude and frequency can be extracted.
The technique is illustrated in the ﬂow chart on Figure 4.1. The concept is that the
SLDV has an internal reference beam, and it measures the diﬀerence between the refer-
ence and the test beam. A test beam f0 ≈477THz is send oﬀat the test object, and as
the object vibrates it adds a small Doppler shift of ∆f to the frequency f0. The Doppler
shift can be calculated as (2v/λ) cos θ. There v is the velocity of the vibrating object,

laser beam. With a velocity of 1m/s, an angle of zero, and the wavelength of 633nm,
the frequency shift is 3.16 MHz, which is very small compared to 477THz. The reference
laser beam, with the frequency f0 goes through a Bragg cell, that adds a frequency shift

Impact Hammer
An impact hammer works as a single impact. It consists of a force transducer and a line
ampliﬁer. The frequency range an impact can deliver depends on the choice of tip of the
hammer. The frequency range is controlled by the stiﬀness of the surfaces, and the mass
of the impactor head. The hammer has a force transducer at the head. It is assumed
that the force felt by the impactor, is the opposite of that experienced by the structure.
The limit is at the system resonance, given by (contact stiﬀness/impactor mass)(1/2) [15].
There is a direct relationship between the duration of the pulse and the limit. To increase
the range, the pulse should be shorter. [15]. A soft rubber tip will make the range smaller
than a hard metal tip.
The magnitude of the impact is not very important, as it is
accommodated in the frequency analysis, but it should be large enough to provide a good
signal-to-noise ratio, but not large enough to excite signiﬁcant structural non-linearities.
It is the position and orientation of the hits that should be the same. It is important
to avoid double hits. A hammer excitation will cause a transient response. The impact

4.1.3
Test Beams
The choice of beam dimensions is based on several considerations. The wish is to have
a beam, where after the few ﬁrst modes the BE and TBT natural frequencies are visibly
diﬀerent. That is, the higher frequency peaks are clearly seen to be like TBT and not like
BE. It is also the wish to look at frequencies beyond fc. The critical frequency depends
on the material in relation to the ratio of E and ρ, and it depends linearly on h. Another
consideration is the limitations due to measurement equipment. The shaker does not go
beyond 20kHz. Within that limit all the above mentioned has to happen. The shorter and
thicker a beam, the worse BE predictions become compared to TBT. But the shorter and
thicker, the higher are the frequencies, thus not very many modes are found within the
limit of 20kHz. The beam dimensions are therefore a compromise of the two contradictory
requirements. The solution was two beams. One steel beam that is only measured much
below fc, to compare TBT and EBT within the limit of where they should deﬁnitely be
suﬃcient models. The other is a brass beam with a large height h. Brass has a high
density, but half the modulus of elasticity as steel. The brass beam will not ﬁt well with
BE already after the ﬁrst or second mode, but it is possible to look at the behaviour
around the critical frequency. Table 4.1 shows the test beams dimensions, mass, density

The input-output relationship is assumed linear; doubling the input also doubles the out-
put. Why dividing the two makes the frequency function independent of size of the input
force. A commonly used technique is to measure the input force by a force transducer
and the output by an accelerometer.
The time signal is measured for both input and output. Figure 4.7 shows the input signal
from a hammer impact, a single pulse, and the output response measured by the ac-
celerometer; a decaying signal, both depending on the excitation signal and the dynamic
properties of the beam. Transforming the signals by Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) the
outcome is two frequency signals. The impact should have a broad band spectrum suf-
ﬁciently wide for the application.
Dividing the two will give the frequency response
function, that raises above the disturbance from external environment. It is an estimator
of the dynamic properties of the beam. To reduce the inﬂuence of noise in the measure-
ments, an average of the FFT signal can be used. The simple division of output over input
is replaced with cross spectrum calculations, called H1 or H2, depending on whether the
input or output spectrum is ’crossed’ with.
The residue is a third modal parameter. It can be explained as a quantity describing
the strength of the mode. The residue scales the mode shape. R = H(ω0)σ where the
bandwidth of a frequency peak is 2σ, and H(ω0) is the response function value at the
frequency peak. The damping is σ/ω0; thus the steeper the peak the smaller the damping.

it is necessary.

Chapter 5
Numerical Modeling and Analysis
A numerical model is made in the FEM software Comsol [5]. The numerical model is
based on 3D theory. It is therefore used an an independent check of the 1D TBT and
EBT results.
5.1
Model Setup
The model is build in the Comsol ’Structural Mechanics’ module with the sub-module
’Solid Mechanics’. The model is given a geometry, identical to the experimentally tested
beam. Both the brass and steel beam are modelled. The material is a basic material where
the properties; Young’s modulus, Poisson’s ratios and the density are manually typed in.
The values are based on the experimentally determined values that will be presented in
Chapter 6. The chosen mesh is a tetrahedral grid. The free-free beam is now ready for
computation.
In the case of a simply supported beam, the model would need constraints. It is not
straight forward to do so, as the model is parted in elements and solves each element
individually. The pinned-pinned scenario is really a 1D concept, expecting the 1D end
points to be constrained. But as the model has 3 dimensions, the constraints must take
that into consideration and compromise. A prescribed displacement can be added to pre-
clude displacements in x, y and z directions. For the roller end it should not preclude
displacement in z. Diﬀerent version of constraining the beam ends has been tried out:
Two points, two horizontal lines or the end surfaces. None of them correspond completely
to a simply supported beam. The remarks on the simple beam are included to put into
perspective how straight forward the free-free beam is, and how seemingly simple bound-
ary conditions are less simple when the beam is a ’real’ 3D beam.
The build-in ’Eigenfrequency’-study is used to perform the calculations.
The desired
number of frequencies is typed in and the model uses approximately between 2-10 seconds
for calculation for the steel beam geometry and 1-2 minutes for the brass. This is because
the brass has more degrees of freedom. Comsol automatically plots the modes shapes

The form of this section is a repetition of the previous section. The experiments are
the same, but with the brass beam instead. The brass beam has a critical frequency fc
of 17.85kHz, thus within the measurable area of ≈20kHz. The steel beam experiments
showed that it is possible to measure frequencies up to 20kHz, with very little deviation
between methods (e.g.
hammer, laser etc.). The results obtained around the critical
frequency are therefore expected to be reliable.

is only 4mm wide. It is diﬃcult to hit ﬂat on the small surface, why the torsional modes
are more easily excited.

Chapter 7
Discussion
This chapter discusses the results from Chapter 6 in relation to the questions and diﬀerent
perceptions outlined in Chapter 3. All discussions points concern the main experiment of
the free-free beam, unless it is directly stated that the observation concerns the simply
supported beam.
First a note on the experimental results: The experiments performed in the lab are of
course not ideal. The physical properties of the tested beams can only be determined to
a certain level of precision. Factors like temperature, inﬂuence from external vibrations,
Young’s modulus of elasticity, the Possion’s ratio and the shear coeﬃcient k are chosen in
the most robust manner available. All these chosen or naturally occurring factors come
in to play together and form the TBT model. The inﬂuence of each factor and their
interaction is not known.
7.1
The Valid Frequency Range of BE, TBT and EBT
The BE-theory is as expected not a suﬃcient model for high frequency cases. That shows
in Chapter 6 for the steel beam. Nor is it suﬃcient for a short thick beam, where the
wavelength approaches the thickness of the beam, as is the case of the brass beam in
Chapter 6. Timoshenko’s 1921 theory was therefore a much needed extension.
The steel beam measured well below fc shows great correlation with TBT, all the way
up to the 26th mode at 20 kHz. It is simply astonishing how a 1D mathematical model
can predict the dynamic modal properties of a 3D beam with such precision. EBT pre-
dicts nearly the same natural frequencies as TBT, but with a consistently larger deviation
from experimental frequencies, than TBT. Elishakoﬀwrites in [2] Our equation (22) does
not predict two frequency branches. We regard this paper as a consistent realization of
Timoshenko’s desire to provide a correction to the Bernoulli-Euler theory with the shear
deformation incorporated, and further to Bresse’s and Rayleigh’s correction for rotary in-
ertia. Elishakoﬀ’s equations give solutions for the free-free beam that have no critical
point, and therefore no change in the solutions. Bernoulli-Euler and Rayleigh’s solutions
==PAGE:78==
68
7 Discussion
are also like that. In that sense, Elishakoﬀ’s equation can be said to be the ’natural next
step’, and that TBT encompasses an additional feature (with it’s prediction of a critical
point that changes the solution), but there is a problem with that interpretation. In
this author’s humble opinion Chapter 2 shows that EBT does not correctly interpret the
angles in Figure 2.1. It must be the actual rotation of the cross section ψ that associates
to the inertial forces, and not the mathematically constructed angle y′.
Considering the experiment with the brass beam: EBT is a better ﬁt than TBT when ap-
proaching fc, but EBT cannot be used to predict the modal behaviour of a free-free beam
above fc. TBT is the only theory that (at least to some degree) predicts the complicated
modal behaviour and the erratic order of odd and even modes. The critical frequency
could be perceived as a transition point. That a 1D mathematical model can even begin
to predict both the subcritical, the supercritical and the transitional behaviour is impres-
sive. EBT is a better ﬁt approaching fc, but the inertial forces are based on a incorrect
angle interpretation.
The TBT and 3D model deviations of the subcritical frequencies increase rapidly when
approaching fc. From 0.2 % to 13.6 % for TBT. The supercritical frequencies deviate
from the experimental frequencies in the order of 10 %. However, the deviations above fc
seem to stabilize. That could indicate a change in the beam properties. E.g. as suggested
by [19] the coeﬃcient k might assume another value above fc. If it was possible to measure
even higher frequencies it could be established if the deviations increase further or if they
stay on a constant level.
The matter of physical meaningfulness for f > fc, discussed in Chapter 3, can not be set-
tled from the experiments alone. But the results of the brass beam show correlation with
TBT and 3D predictions, thus indirectly supporting Bhasker’s argument [23] concerning
the Lagrangian, and that the frequencies f > fc are physically meaningful.
7.2
The Existence of Timoshenko Doublets
The experiments of the free-free beam shows Timoshenko doublets above fc. Turning
the eye back to Chapter 3 and the controversy of TBT: It is now at least the fourth
time the pairs have been experimentally identiﬁed. With that many independent sets of
experimental results, it is safe to say that the frequency pairs are a fact, no matter if
the TBT predictions are valid or not. If deformation over the cross section is linear or
sinusoidal has not been uncovered in these experiments. It is however a fact that TBT
only predicts a linear shearing over the cross section. If the sinusoidal shape is a reality,
then TBT does not take it into account, why it could be an explanation of the less precise
TBT frequencies above fc.

7.3 1D Theory in Relation to 3D Theory
69
7.3
1D Theory in Relation to 3D Theory
The focus in the report has been on the 1D theoretical models, and the 3D modulation
in Comsol has mainly been used as a reference. The 3D model gives the best prediction
of the frequencies for the free-free steel beam, measured well below fc. The deviations
from experiments are never above 0.2 % for the ﬁrst 26 modes. A natural consideration
is; why bother with 1D theories when 3D models can predict the dynamic behaviour of a
beam with such precision? The 3D model is a speciﬁc model, that is solved for a speciﬁc
number of frequencies and only give insight to that speciﬁc beam. If the dimensions are
changed, everything has to be modelled again. 1D theory is much simpler and general in
that sense. With a minimal of eﬀort all the 1D models can be adapted in dimensions and
properties. The mode shapes are described in general terms, where the numeric 3D model
only gives the speciﬁc modes. Boundary conditions are also an obstacle in 3D theory. The
free-free beam is unique because no constraints have to be applied. As soon as constraints
are introduced the 3D models are more complicated.
Considering the brass beam: As TBT, the 3D model predicts frequency pairs. It also
predicts a change in the mode shapes. The deviations follow the same pattern and mag-
nitude as TBT. Returning to the deformation over the cross section: The 3D colorplots
in Chapter 5 show that the free-free beam ends are not linearly deformed. It supports the
idea of a more complicated shearing eﬀect than TBT can predict. Despite that, the fre-
quencies of the 3D model are not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from TBT, why one must question
the importance of the shearing shape.
7.4
The Not-so-Simple Simply Supported Beam
This project pivots around the free-free beam, but the TBT theory can predict natural
frequencies and mode shapes for all kinds of boundary conditions, including the classical
simply supported beam. Chapter 2 shows, as many papers has done before, that TBT in
that case predicts two distinct sets of frequencies. The predicted mode shapes are however
described by a simple sine, not predicting a change in the shape beyond fc. EBT predicts
the same mode shapes. The performed experiment is not ideal, and cannot be analysed
uncritically. The subcritical frequencies and mode shapes show a correlation to TBT and
EBT. The supercritical experimental mode shapes are not a simple sinusoidal, as TBT
and EBT predicts. As for the free-free beam there is a clear change in the mode shape
behaviour, that the theory does not predict. The experiment is faulty in many ways and
improvement is needed to draw clear conclusions. It is however interesting that TBT only
predicts a change in the mode shapes beyond fc for the free-free beam, and not the simple
beam. The experiment suggest that it is not the true behaviour. A way to explore this
further is to try other boundary conditions that also give two distinct sets of frequencies,
such as guided-guided, and see what it brings.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
This chapter is a brief summery of the essential ﬁndings and points of discussion from all
the previous chapters. It will also give suggestions for future work on the subject.
8.1
Essential Findings
The project’s stepping stone has been the access to the SLDV. It gave the opportunity to
perform experiments with a large number of measurement points along the beam length.
The closely spaced measurement points facilitated the measurement of mode shapes for
very high mode numbers. The simple and novel experimental setups showed that the
SLDV together with the right excitation equipment can measure frequencies up to 20
kHz, with very little deviation from measurements with an accelerometer.
An experiment of a free-free steel beam measured well below fc showed excellent agree-
ment with TBT. EBT predicts nearly the same frequencies, but this report clariﬁes how
the inertial forces should be associated to the rotation of the cross section, and not the
total deﬂection slope, as is the case with EBT. A 3D model made in Comsol showed
remarkable agreement with the experiment and surpasses both TBT and EBT in precision.
The main experiment was that of a free-free brass beam measured around fc. The ex-
periment of the free-free beam revealed frequency pairs in the supercritical domain. At
least three other papers present the same result establishing the frequency pairs as a
physical phenomenon. The MAC-value tool has been used to pair experimental modes
with theoretic. Otherwise, because of the paired frequencies, it is diﬃcult to ensure a
proper match. The experiment also showed how the mode shape order (odd and even)
for f > fc does not interchange in the same systematic way as below fc. For f > fc a
correlation between the TBT predicted mode shapes and the experimental is identiﬁed
and TBT should not disregarded for the free-free beam above fc. Above fc the deviations
between TBT and experimental natural frequencies are in the order of %10 and conse-
quently larger than below fc. It indicates that TBT is ’missing’ a signiﬁcant parameter
or considers it inadequately in some way. The possible sinusoidal shearing over the cross
==PAGE:82==
72
8 Conclusions
section or a change in the shear coeﬃcient k has been considered as potential reasons to
the limitations in TBT above fc.
The composition of the theoretic free-free mode shapes have been unravelled. It has been
shown how the shear angle represent a larger and larger part the total angle of deﬂec-
tion. The shear angle is out of phase with the angle of rotation of the cross section past
the critical frequency, giving a new modal composition. The experiments cannot reveal
if the theoretic composition of the shear and rotation angle is the complete picture of
the reality, but the agreement between theory and experiment is evident, supporting this
shear/rotation relation.
The 3D model for the brass beam predicts frequencies that deviate from the experiment in
the same order as TBT. It does not show a superior accuracy as it did for the steel beam.
It has additionally been uncovered that Elishakoﬀ’s modiﬁed theory (EBT) is limited to
the subcritical domain.
An attempt on a simply supported beam setup put the free-free beam experiments into
perspective. The simply supported beam experiment revealed how constraints are a source
of experimental uncertainties and unwanted inﬂuence from various imperfections in the
boundaries. The constraints are likewise problematic in 3D modelling, as they are deﬁned
by 1D theory as points and that has to be translated to 3D. Chapter 2 showed how the
TBT mathematics change signiﬁcantly with simple boundary conditions instead of free.
TBT predicts two distinct spectra, but no change in the mode shapes. The experimental
mode shapes clearly changed above fc, but further experimental work is needed to uncover
the supercritical behaviour of a simply supported beam.
8.2
Future Work
The results of the project pose just as many questions as they answer. Below are given a
handful of suggestion on future work:
• Study diﬀerent values of the shear coeﬃcient k above fc, along with argumentation
for assuming a diﬀerent value.
• Experimentally uncover the behaviour of shearing over the cross section for a free-
free beam. If it is indeed sinusoidal, how can that be incorporated in a model?
• A further investigation of the simply supported beam: Both in regard to improving
the experimental setup and numerical model, as well as an in-depth analysis of TBT
for that particular case.
• Numerical modelling of beams with various boundary conditions, with comparison
to TBT to check if they are in general agreement for all cases.

function TBT= TBT_omega(no,init_guess,l,E,k,G,c0,r)
syms omega
p=omega*l/c0;
alpha=sqrt(p^2/2*(−(1+E/(k*G))+sqrt((1−E/(k*G))^2+(2*l/(p*r))^2)));
beta=sqrt(p^2/2*((1+E/(k*G))+sqrt((1−E/(k*G))^2+(2*l/(p*r))^2)));
chi_alpha=(G*alpha^2*c0^2*k+E*l^2*omega^2)/(G*c0^2*k*l*alpha);
chi_beta=(−G*beta^2*c0^2*k+E*l^2*omega^2)/(G*c0^2*k*l*beta);
for j=1:2:no
omega_s(j)=vpasolve((tan((1/2)*beta)−beta/alpha*chi_beta/...
chi_alpha*tanh((1/2)*alpha)*(chi_alpha*l−alpha)/(chi_beta*l+beta))...
==0,omega,init_guess(j));
end
for j=2:2:no
omega_s(j)=vpasolve((tan((1/2)*beta)+alpha/beta*chi_alpha/...
chi_beta*tanh((1/2)*alpha)*(chi_beta*l+beta)/(chi_alpha*l−alpha))...
==0,omega,init_guess(j));
end

function mAc=Mac(Phi1,Phi2)
% This function calculates mac between phi1 and phi2

end
