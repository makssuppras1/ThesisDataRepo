Abstract 
Pharmaceuticals present in wastewater effluents are of growing concern, as they threaten environmental 
stability and human health. Conventional treatments are not adequately suited to deal with the removal of 
these organic micropollutants. Methanotrophic bacteria have shown promise in degrading a variety of 
organic micropollutants. By employing a novel bubble-free membrane aerated bioreactor, methanotrophic 
bacteria was successfully cultivated under three different CH4 loading rates. A linear correlation (r = 0.97) 
was observed between biomass productivity and CH4 loading, with a maximum productivity of 372 mg 
VSS∙L-1∙d-1 and a corresponding biomass concentration of 1,117.6 ± 56.4 mg VSS∙L-1. Thus, performing better 
than comparable membrane aerated bioreactors. The highest CH4 loading succeed in removing 71% and 
36% of nitrate and phosphate. Furthermore, the methanotrophic dominated culture showed positive 
correlation between biodegradation of sulfamethoxazole and 1H-benzotriazole, reaching removal rates of 
3.58 L∙g-1∙d-1 and 5.42 L∙g-1∙d-1, respectively. Thereby, outperforming conventional activated sludge and 
moving bed biofilm reactors. The membrane aerated bioreactor showed promising potential as a polishing 
step for secondary effluent. However, consideration should be given to the carbon footprint of the system. 
In addition, the membrane aerated bioreactor was able to recovery nutrient resources as microbial protein. 
The protein content was on average 39%, thus in the low end of methanotrophic bacteria. However, the 
amino acid concentration indicated a potential use as biostimulants. Compared to soymeal, the essential 
amino acids methionine, threonine and histidine were slightly higher and with leucine and valine only 
slightly below. Thus, the microbial protein might be a substitute for soymeal. 
The membrane aerated bioreactor experienced a high gas transfer and was well below 5% (v/v) CH4 in the 
off-gas and therefore safe to operate.

Introduction 
 
In the coming decades, climate change and a global population (expected to reach almost 10 billion by 2050 
(UN, 2019)), are threatening food security (UN, 2021), and fresh water resources (EEA, 2020). To mitigate 
such challenges, the European Commission (EC) has recently passed a regulation that allows for the reuse 
of secondary treated urban wastewaters within agriculture (EC, 2021). Such conventional wastewater 
treatment traditionally focuses on the removal and mineralization of nutrients and organic matter before 
emitting treated water to recipients (Peake, et al., 2016), with little or no focus on the removal of 
pharmaceuticals present in wastewater.  
As such, the EU regulation only mentions pharmaceutical compounds as a factor for consideration, with no 
concentration limits, neither directly nor indirectly by other regulations (EP, 2020). Likewise, Danish 
regulation does not concern about organic micropollutants (OMPs) in the form of pharmaceuticals emitted 
from wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) to receiving surface waters (Miljøministeriet, 2020).  
However, with wastewater effluent concentrations of various pharmaceuticals ranging from 0.5 to >3 µg/L 
(Peake, et al., 2016; DEPA, 2018; Verlicchi, et al., 2012), WWTPs are identified as a major entry point of 
OMPs to the natural environment (Su, et al., 2021). Over the past years, there has been growing concern 
regarding the impact posed by emitted OMPs on ecosystems and human health (Wei, et al., 2012; Zucatto, 
et al., 2010). OMPs can negatively affect the environmental stability and biodiversity in wastewater 
recipients (Torresi, et al., 2016), disrupt reproductive ability in fish (Torresi, 2017; Peake, et al., 2016) and 
risk human health, if drinking water drawn from receiving surfaces waters is not adequately treated (Peake, 
et al., 2016). Furthermore, Christou et al. (2017) and Goldstein et al. (2014) both reported uptake, and even 
accumulation of some pharmaceuticals, in crops irrigated with secondary treated wastewater and thus risk 
to human health if consumed. The concern is only further fuelled by the increased sales experienced by 
pharmacies over recent years (Danmarks Apotekerforening, 2020).  
As pharmaceuticals are used for medical care for humans, their presence in wastewater stems from 
excretion with urine and faeces, either as the parent compound or a transformed product thereof (Torresi, 
2017). Conventional WWTPs can degrade more than 90% of some pharmaceutical compounds, e.g., 
ibuprofen, present in wastewater by applying secondary treatment alone. Conventionally, WWTPs employ 
secondary treatment for biological removal of organic matter and nutrients (Peake, et al., 2016) by utilizing 
aerobic heterotrophic and/or autotrophic bacteria. However, pharmaceuticals are not considered as 
primary substrates and removal must occur by other means than degradation for energy retrieval or carbon 
assimilation. As such, sorption can be a key process in controlling the removal of natural or xenobiotic 
compounds (Min, et al., 2018). However, for most pharmaceuticals biodegradation is considered the main 
driver in OMP removal, with co-metabolism as the main mechanism (Su, et al., 2021). Co-metabolism is 
described as the capability of transforming a non-growth substrate, due to substrate ranges of key enzymes 
expressed by different bacteria (Su, et al., 2021; Tang, et al., 2017). The most significant process 
parameters in biodegradation of OMPs have been established as the solid retention time (SRT), OMP inlet 
concentrations and electron acceptor concentration (Wang, et al., 2020)). Longer SRT have been associated 
with better OMP removal possibly due to slow growing bacteria (Peng, et al., 2015; Wang, et al., 2020). 
However, some compounds such as β-blockers and antibiotics, e.g., metoprolol and sulfamethoxazole, 
respectively, are less prone to biodegradation and thus require further treatment by a tertiary step to 
remove a significant portion from the wastewater (Peake, et al., 2016). In attempts to address OMPs in 
secondary effluent, several physical/chemical treatments have been identified for the removal of OMPs, 
such as ozonation, UV radiation, active carbon or reverse osmosis (Peake, et al., 2016). These are however 
rather costly compared to biological removal (Peake, et al., 2016) and thus purely biological or hybrid 
systems, i.e., biological + physical/chemical, are of interest.

Methane oxidizing bacteria (MOB), a group of slow growing bacteria capable of utilizing CH4 as their sole 
energy and carbon source (Kalyuzhnaya, 2016), have shown promise in the degradation of several OMPs 
(Hedegaard, et al., 2018; Su, et al., 2021; Benner, et al., 2015). They consist of three subgroups, where 
especially type I (gammaproteobacterial) and type II (alphaproteobacterial) are of industrial interest 
currently. The groups differ from each other by the biomass assimilation pathway; ribulose 
monophosphate (type I) and serine cycle (type II) among other traits (Kalyuzhnaya, 2016). MOBs are 
believed to degrade OMPs co-metabolically due to the enzyme methane mono-oxygenase (MMO). This 
enzyme comes in two distinct forms, a soluble (sMMO) and a particulate (pMMO). The two types of MMOs 
differ in their range of substrates which they can co-metabolize (Su, et al., 2021; Hedegaard, et al., 2018; 
Burrows, et al., 1984; Semrau, et al., 2010). The interest in MOBs for bioremediation is further 
strengthened, as the pMMO form shows similarities to the ammonia mono-oxygenase (AMO) enzyme 
expressed by ammonia oxidizing bacteria (AOB), which have shown promising results for in-situ 
degradation of OMPs present in wastewater (Peng, et al., 2015; Su, et al., 2021; Tang, et al., 2017; Tang, et 
al., 2021). Employing either membrane bioreactors (MBRs) or biofilm reactors, such as moving bed biofilm 
reactors (MBBRs) or membrane aerated bioreactors (MABRs), promotes the slow growth rate of AOBs, 
yielding higher removal of OMPs compared to conventional activated sludge (CAS) (Tang, et al., 2021; Tang, 
et al., 2017) and shows better economic potential than adding a purely physical/chemical treatment step 
(Tang, et al., 2021; DEPA, 2018).   
In 2012, 92 Mt CH4 was either flared or vented (World Bank, 2020). CH4 is a greenhouse gas (GHG) with a 
global warming potential of 86 over 20 years compared to CO2. Thus, utilizing CH4 as electron donor for 
bioremediation of wastewater by MOBs pose an appealing use of this GHG. Furthermore, MOBs can be 
used for microbial protein (MP) as protein substitutes in fish and livestock feeds (Tsapekos, et al., 2019; 
Goonesekera, 2020), production of biopolymers, e.g., poly-β-hydroxybutyrate (PHB) (Strong, et al., 2016), 
and catalysts for methanol (MeOH) production (Pen, et al., 2014).  
Especially for WWTPs, utilizing CH4 for OMP degradation in tertiary treatment can pose an interesting 
option, as CH4 availability is present via biogas production, which is an integrated step of many WWTPs. 
This is indicated by Danish subsidies for biogas in 2018, as 20% of the biomass used in biogas production 
originated from wastewater sludge (DEA, 2018). Furthermore, employing MOBs in the removal of nutrients, 
nitrogen and phosphorous, from wastewater, enables the recovery and upcycling of these as MP from an 
otherwise low-grade residual stream (Valverde-Pérez, et al., 2020). 
Leading market players are pushing for legislation allowing for the use of proteins derived from wastewater 
to enhance circular economy (Sadauskas, et al., 2021). However, a significant challenge due to consumer 
attitude toward recycled wastewater still resides (Childers, et al., 2011). As a transition phase, MOB 
cultivated on wastewater could be used as biostimulants within agriculture. A biostimulant is any substance 
or microorganism, except nutrients and pesticides, applied to plants with the aim of improving overall yield 
(Almadi, et al., 2020; Halpern, et al., 2015). They can be as diverse as algae (Ahn, et al., 2020), bacteria 
(Almadi, et al., 2020), seaweed (Bulgari, et al., 2015) or substances derived from organic origin (Bulgari, et 
al., 2015; Halpern, et al., 2015). Within the EU, sewage sludge as biostimulant may not be applied directly 
to arable land without further treatment (EP, 2019). Thus, for MOBs derived from a WWTP, the potential 
would likely be as a resource for amino acids, as they can be high in protein content (Tsapekos, et al., 
2019). Amino acids as biostimulants are reported having positive effects on nutrient uptake, protection 
against abiotic and biotic stressors and increasing biomass production (Halpern, et al., 2015).  
 
However, being a GHG and substrate with low solubility, gas-liquid mass transfer of CH4 is a common 
challenge (Strong, et al., 2016). It cannot simply be supplied to open air reactors at WWTPs, as this would 
negatively impact the carbon footprint due to uncontrolled CH4 emissions. To mitigate the mass transfer 
bottleneck, membrane contactors can be used, as they have large surface areas and the ability to enhance

the gas-liquid mass transfer (Pen, et al., 2014). By applying both CH4 and O2 needed for aerobic MOB 
cultivation through separate membranes, the risk of creating an explosive atmosphere is reduced 
compared to reactors relying on one or both gases being supplied by bubble aeration (Pen, et al., 2014; 
Valverde-Pérez, et al., 2020), and thus closed reactors become more viable.     
In this project a novel bubble-free MABR will be used to determine the ability of MOB to degrade an array 
of 10 different OMPs commonly detected in municipal wastewater. Both CH4 and O2 will be supplied 
through hydrophobic membranes, and it is thus hypothesized that the MOB culture will grow in suspension 
rather than as a biofilm on either of the membranes. As the degradation of OMPs is expected to be 
associated with the presence of MOBs (Semprini, et al., 1990), and that the food-to-microbe (F/M) ratio has 
a high influence on microbial activity (AlSayed, et al., 2018), the impact of different feeding regimes of CH4 
will be tested. Keeping the nitrogen composition of the influent to the MABR constant, the CH4 to nitrogen, 
mg CH4 per mg NO3- -N, ratio (C:N) is bound to change with the different feeding regimes. Thus, it is 
expected that higher protein production at low C:N ratios will occur, as was found by Skadborg (2018) using 
a MABR setup.  
This project aims at evaluating a bubble-free MABR as a polishing step in a WWTP by  
1. determining if the MABR support methanotrophic growth, 
2. investigating if the MABR can be used for nitrate and phosphate removal as polishing step of 
secondary effluent, 
3. studying if it is possible to remove OMPs with the MABR, 
o and if so if the removal can be controlled operationally 
4. if MOB cultivation is possible, determine whether the biomass could be used as a plant 
biostimulant or feed ingredient given the protein content      
 
Materials and method 
 
Pre-test 
Prior to start-up of the MABR, two MOB enrichments were cultivated based on medias of dilute nitrate 
mineral salt (dNMS) with potassium nitrate (Sigma Aldrich, Germany) as nitrogen source. One was made as 
described by (Whittenbury, et al., 1970)(dNMSN) and one on altered dNMS (dNMSA), as to be used in the 
MABR (appendix 1.1). The former was used as a control to distinguish any differences in MOB cultivated on 
dNMSA. The higher nitrate concentration of dNMSA was to ensure adequate ratios between N and CH4, as 
the system pumps had limitations (appendix 1.2). 
Four enrichments were made for each of the dNMS. Two initial enrichments for each dNMS followed by 
additional two for each dNMS. The enrichments were made in 250 mL serum bottles closed with rubber 
stoppers.  
First, the enrichments were activated with dNMSA, adding 97 mL of dNMSA and 3 mL of MOB culture 
provided by Gooneskera (2020). The bottles were then flushed with N2 gas (Alphagaz™ 1 Nitrogen, Air 
Liquide) for 5 minutes. Afterwards 50 mL of headspace were removed and 40 and 60 mL of CH4 and O2 
were injected, respectively. The serum bottles were placed in a dark room at 25◦C for three days on a 
shaking table set at approximately 100 rounds per minute (rpm). After three days the second set of 
enrichments were made.  
The second set of enrichments were made by adding 90 mL of dNMS and 10 mL of MOB from the previous 
enrichments. Headspace concentration of CH4 was continuously monitored and refilled by flushing with N2 
and injecting CH4 and O2 in a 40:60 ratio when CH4 got below 10 mL.

MABR configuration 
The MABR system consisted of three clear acrylic GS casings (Brønnum Plastsnedkeri A/S, Denmark). The 
middle casing had a reactor chamber with a working volume of 406 mL. Two dead-end gas chambers 
enclosed the reactor chamber, from which they were separated by two 2-layered, non-vowen hydrophobic 
flat sheet membranes with gas-permeable coating (Fluence Corporation, USA), each with a membrane 
surface area of 369 cm2. The acrylic casings were kept together with 8mm pins that were tightened 
manually with bolts. To keep the MABR air and liquid tight, the membranes were framed by rubber sheets, 
which were kept in place between the acrylic casings. 
Two peristaltic pumps supplied CH4  (≥99% CH4, Air Liquide, Denmark) and O2 (≥99% O2, Air Liquide, 
Denmark) continuously at a constant CH4:O2 molar ratio of 40:60 under three different feeding regimes as 
shown in  
Table 1. The first 14 days the MABR was operated as batch, as an adaptation period for the inoculum. The 
MABR was run as a continuous flow system, as depicted in Fig. 1, for 89 days. For each feeding regime, the 
MABR was run until steady-state, and then kept at steady-state for approximately one week. The steady-
state conditions were determined based on stable biomass and nutrient concentrations, and if possible 
stable oxygen, CH4 and pH.  
The reactor chamber of the MABR could not visually be inspected during operation. Right before 
dismantlement of the MABR a test was conducted to identify a potential biofilm.

The starvation and feast flow rates were set as half and twice the baseline feeding rate, respectively. 
The MABR was positioned with the membranes in a vertical position to avoid fouling by particles due to 
gravitational settling. Media was fed into the MABR at the bottom using a peristaltic pump at a constant 
feeding rate of 8.1 mL∙h-1 to ensure a SRT and hydraulic retention time (HRT) of three days. This was 
deemed adequate, as pure oxygen was used and the nitrogen to reactor volume ratio was higher than in 
the previous studies (Skadborg, 2018; Valverde-Pérez, et al., 2020). A time schedule for refilling of gas bags 
and media is shown in Table A8 (appendix 1.2). 
Mixing of the MABR liquor was done by recirculation, applied by a second peristaltic pump via tubing 
connected to the bottom and top side of the MABR. The recirculatory flow direction was operated to 
ensure that freshly fed media was distributed in the reactor chamber first. Thus, the setup simulated a 
plug-flow reactor with recirculation rather than a CSTR. The volume of additional tubing raised the total 
working volume of the MABR to 584 mL.

surrounding environment.  
kLa 
The mass transfer rate of gas is dependent on the liquid velocity (Ahmed & Semmens, 1992), as the liquid 
film boundary resistance will decrease with increasing turbulence (Cote, et al., 1989).  
Therefore, a mass transfer coefficient (kLa) test was conducted to estimate at which pumping rate the kLa 
would be decoupled from the recirculation velocity. Based on the findings the recirculation pump was set 
to run at 125 rpm equivalent of 22.8 L∙h-1 (appendix 1.4). 
Media 
The dNMSA had higher nitrate (51.1 mg NO3- -N∙L-1) and copper (7.9 µg Cu∙L-1) concentrations but lower 
phosphor (11.3 mg P∙L-1) concentrations compared to dNMSN (appendix 1.1 & 1.2). The media was spiked 
with OMPs and was contained in a 2 L bottle sealed with film (Parafilm M®, Bemis). The bottle was covered 
in tin foil to minimize risk of any unwanted growth e.g., algae.

OMP degradation 
A batch test was done after each feeding regime. The MABR was set in batch mode by shutting off inflow of 
media and closing the outflow from the MABR. The feeding rate of CH4 and O2 was kept during the test. At 
time zero of each experiment, the MABR was spiked with nutrients and OMP to ensure enough nutrients 
and a starting concentration ≥2 µg∙L-1 for each OMP.  
Analytical methods 
Biomass 
The concentration of biomass was monitored by measuring optical density (OD) at 600 nm (Cary® 50 Bio 
UV-Visible Spectrophotometer, Agilent Technologies, USA), as well as total suspended solids (TSS) using a 
standard APHA method (APHA, 1999) with a 47 mm glass fibre filter (pore size 0.2 µm, ADVANTEC®, Toyo 
Roshi Kaisha, Ltd., Japan). Before OD and TSS measurements, the MABR was shook to ensure re-suspension 
of any settled biomass. This was done the same way each time to make the data consistent (appendix 1.6). 
Correlation between OD and TSS were made as a function of OD based on collected data (appendix 1.8). 
The volatile suspended solids (VSS) were measured to be equal to >99% of TSS, as found by (Zachariae & 
Kjeldgaard, 2018).  
Nitrogen and phosphorous 
To determine NO3- -N and PO43- -P concentrations, a liquor sample was drawn from the MABR, filtered using 
a 0.2 µm filter (Econo filter, nylon, 25 mm, 0.2 µm, Agilent Technologies, USA) and diluted to a factor seven. 
The concentrations were then determined colorimetrically by a continuous-flow auto-analyser (SKALAR 
San++, Netherlands).  
sCOD 
Once per week one sample of 2 mL was collected, filtered using a 0.2 µm filter and then stored at  
-18◦C for later sCOD analysis. The analysis was conducted using a COD test kit (LCK 314, Hach Lange, 
Germany).

CH4, O2, CO2, temperature, and pH 
Measurements were done continuously for dissolved oxygen (DO) (FDO 925®, WTW, Germany), 
temperature and pH (SenTix 940®, WTW, Germany) using a digital multimeter (3420 Multimeter, WTW, 
Germany) with a resolution of 1 minute. 3 mL samples from the MABR were collected in 6 mL vacuum vials 
(Exetainer®, UK) to determine dissolved CH4 and CO2. The vials were injected with 1-2 droplets of 
phosphoric acid (≥85%, Honeywell, USA) to stop any bioactivity and the vials were stored up-side down at 
5◦C for no more than a week.  
Analysis of dissolved CH4 and CO2, was made from the headspace of the collected MABR samples. The CH4 
and the CO2 concentration of the enrichments and off-gas were taken directly from the headspace and gas-
bag, respectively. All CH4 and CO2 analyses were carried out by gas chromatography (Trace 1310 Gas 
Chromatograph, Thermo Scientific) using a HP-Plot/Q column (Length 15 m, diameter 0.32 mm, film 20 µm, 
Agilent Technologies, USA). Prior to analysis the pressure in the vacuum vials were equalized by injecting

Organic micropollutants 
The occurrence of OMP degradation were analysed by, 1) taken three samples for OMP analysis during

different time intervals. Three samples for nutrients as well as two for OD and CH4 were taken during the 
24-hour test. See Table A10 for a timetable (appendix 1.3). 
Each of the 12 samples taken from the MABR was of 5 mL. They were kept in 12 mL glass vials with 1 mL

Prior to analysis the samples were thawed in a refrigerator and 1 mL of each sample were filtered using

Chromatography – Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometry (1290 Infinity II – 6470 LC-QQQ, Agilent

Technologies, USA), with 0.1% formic acid as eluent. For each OMP, the HPLC had a lower detection limit 
(Table A16 appendix 1.5). The data gathered from the HPLC were analysed using the software program 
GraphPad Prism 7.0 (GraphPad Software).

Results & discussion 
 
Pre-test batches  
The enrichments showed growth rates of 0.34 d-1 and 0.42 d-1 for dNMSA and dNMSN, respectively. This 
could indicate different type of MOBs in each of the different dNMS enrichments (Kalyuzhnaya, 2016). The 
growth rate is close to the growth rate, µ, of 0.33 d-1 for the MABR. The YNX for dNMSA and dNMSN were 4.8 
± 0.1 mg VSS∙mg NO3- -N and 17.5 ± 0.1 mg VSS∙mg NO3- -N, respectively. The YCX were 0.36 mg VSS∙mg CH4-
1 and 0.40 mg VSS∙mg CH4-1 for dNMSA and dNMSN, respectively (appendix 2.1). Thus, more nitrogen was 
assimilated per biomass when the nitrate concentration was higher.  
Bioreactor operation 
Baseline feeding regime 
The MABR was operated in the baseline feeding regime from day 0 to day 43. Steady-state conditions were 
reached at day 27. The steady-state was determined based on a stable biomass and nutrient concentration. 
The CH4 loading rate was 4.6 mg CH4∙h-1. Effluent dissolved CH4 was 0.03 ± 0.05 mg⋅L-1 yielding an effluent 
load of 2.3⋅10-4 mg CH4⋅h-1 equal to 99.99% CH4 uptake. Therefore, it was assumed that all CH4 was 
consumed. The DO concentration was 4.3 ± 3 mg⋅L-1. However, the DO concentration was below 1 mg O2∙L-1 
during 33.7% of the steady-state, which could negatively affect nitrate removal (Wang, et al., 2021) (Fig. 
A19 appendix 2.2.1). The temperature during the baseline feeding regime was 22.7 ± 1◦C. Both O2 and CH4 
==PAGE:15==
10 
 
consumption (Yang, et al., 2021) as well as O2 saturation (FEM, 2013) is affected by temperature. However, 
no clear correlation between MABR performance and temperature was made.  
The dissolved CO2 concentration was measured to be 179.0 ± 44.7 mg⋅L-1, which was 410 times higher than 
the solubility of CO2 in contact with atmospheric air (0.44 mg CO2∙L-1 (Arvin, 2002)), consequence of CO2 
production by the MOB. The dissolved CO2 concentration increased with increasing biomass concentration, 
indicating correlation due to bioactivity (Fig. A20 appendix 2.2.1). However, during day 27-33, only 0.1 mg 
CO2 was measured per mg CH4 consumed (CO2:CH4 ratio), which was lower than the expected of 1.32 mg 
CO2∙mg CH4-1 for MOB production (equation 1). Even though the baseline regime had the highest yield on 
CH4 (YCX) of 1.34 mg VSS∙mg CH4-1 (Table A20 appendix 2.2.1), this was only 45% of the theoretical 
maximum (equation 1). This could indicate the presence of by-products, such as internally glycogen which 
is accumulated at low CH4 and high O2 (Kalyuzhnaya, 2016; Semrau, et al., 2010). Furthermore, the baseline 
feeding regime experienced the lowest pH and highest sCOD of all three regimes of 6.23 ± 0.11 and 95.4 ± 
56.7 mg COD∙L-1, respectively. The sCOD from OMPs and CH4 were negligible with a total loading of 0.76 mg 
COD∙L-1 (appendix 2.2.2). Furthermore, the sCOD and pH seemed to be inversely correlated (Fig. A21 
appendix 2.2.1). MOBs can excrete by-products as MeOH, formate and acetate (Valverde-Pérez, et al., 
2020; Gilman, et al., 2017; Strong, et al., 2015). The two latter are organic acids and could explain the pH, 
as CO2 would have a minor impact due to the pKa of H2CO3 and the consumption of protons (equation 1) 
(Eilersen, et al., 2007). However, acetate was regarded as a product of fermentation occurring at DO ≤0.24 
mg∙L-1, which only occurred 2.7% of the time operating the MABR. Based on the oxidation rates of MeOH to 
formate and from formate to formaldehyde, formate would be expected to be more abundant (Gilman, et 
al., 2017; AlSayed, et al., 2018; Strong, et al., 2015).  
However, to fully elucidate which compounds affect the sCOD, and possibly the pH, further research is 
needed. The biomass concentration reached 751.1 ± 23.8 mg VSS∙L-1 at steady-state. At day 8, a ‘pseudo’ 
steady-state was reached, as the biomass concentration seized to increase for some days (Fig. 2). OMPs 
were first introduced in the media at day 8, suggesting an acclimation period for the bacteria.  
Surprisingly, during baseline feeding the removal of nutrients was the lowest of the three feeding regimes, 
with 44% and 23% of nitrate and phosphate removed, respectively (Table A19 appendix 2.2.1). Under the 
operating pH, phosphate was not expected to precipitate, thus phosphate was assumed removed due to 
assimilation for microbial growth (Rittmann & McCarty, 2001; Diamadopoulos & Benedek, 1984). The 
nitrogen and phosphate uptake rates were 7.6 ± 0.4 mg NO3- -N∙L-1∙d-1 and 0.9 ± 0.4 mg PO43- -P∙L-1∙d-1, 
respectively. The biomass productivity (qX) was 250 mg VSS∙L-1∙d-1.   
 
Feast feeding regime 
The MABR was operated in feast feeding regime from day 43 to day 68, with steady-state conditions 
reached at day 60. The loading rate was twice that of the baseline feeding. The effluent dissolved CH4 
concentration and DO concentration was 1.89 ± 0.76 mg⋅L-1 and 5.4 ± 3.8 mg⋅L-1, respectively. After day 58, 
the DO concentration increased (Fig. A19 appendix 2.2.1). Interestingly, the DO did not recede to lower 
concentrations, even though the highest temperature experienced was 28◦C (regime average 25.1 ± 1◦C). 
Thus, neither lower saturation concentration nor bioactivity seemed to have a significant effect on lowering 
DO. The latter could be a result of the type of MOB dominating the MABR, as inherent properties of the 
expressed MMO could have an effect. sMMO expressing cells have an advantage over pMMO expressing, 
at high CH4 concentrations due to higher CH4 turnover rate (Semrau, et al., 2010). However, sMMO has a 
lower affinity for O2 (Shi, et al., 2021), which could lead to slower O2 consumption. Furthermore, Chu & 
Alvarez-Cohen (1999) reported that sMMO could experience aeration damage caused by oxidative stress, 
with less MOB growth at elevated O2 levels (Xu, et al., 2020). However, biomass concentration was rather 
stable at 1,117.6 ± 56.4 mg VSS∙L-1 during steady-state, suggesting growth was maintained. The feast regime 
had the highest measured biomass concentration (Fig. 2).

The dissolved CO2 concentration and pH was 317.1 ± 29.1 mg⋅L-1 and 6.49 ± 0.07, respectively. Thus, both 
CO2 concentration and pH were higher during feast compared to baseline, suggesting that CO2 was not the 
main driver for the pH decline during the baseline feeding regime. The CO2:CH4 ratio was 0.3 mg CO2∙CH4-1, 
which could indicate more CH4 assimilated into biomass and less by-products compared to the baseline 
feeding regime. Furthermore, the sCOD was lower than the baseline regime, though not significantly, at 
39.9 ± 5.5 mgCOD∙L-1. However, the feeding regime experienced the lowest YCX of 0.99 ± 2.9 mgVSS∙mgCH4-1 
but the highest qX of 372 mg VSS∙L-1∙d-1. If the feast regime had more heterotrophs per MOB, this could also 
yield higher CO2:CH4 ratio, lower YCX and sCOD as well as high qx. 
Valverde-Pérez et al. (2020) also found correlation between CH4 loading and biomass concentration, and 
inversely correlation between CH4 loading and YCX. Furthermore, Valverde-Pérez et al. (2020) reported a 
maximum qX for a MOB culture of 59.5 mg VSS∙L-1∙d-1 and Tsapekos et al. (2019) reported a maximum of 238 
mg VSS∙L-1∙d-1. Thus, the MABR employed in this project showed higher qx for MOBs.  
The feast feeding regime had the highest removal of nitrate and phosphate of 71% and 36%, respectively. 
The uptake rates for nitrogen and phosphorous were 12.4 ± 0.2 mg NO3- -N∙L-1∙d-1 and 1.2 ± 0.2 mg PO43- -
P∙L-1∙d-1, respectively, and the YPX was 310.4 mg VSS∙mg PO43- -P-1. Thus, the feast feeding regime had the 
highest uptake rate and biomass yield on phosphate (Table A20 appendix 2.2.1). The required uptake of 
phosphorous varies with the type of MOB, as type I has higher P requirements for the rRNA and 
incorporates stacked membranes with high P demand, whereas type II uses vesicular membranes with 
lower P-demand (Goonesekera, 2020).

Starvation feeding regime 
The starvation regime spanned from day 68 to day 88, with steady-state reached at day 76. The loading 
rate was halved compared to the baseline feeding. The dissolved effluent CH4 concentration was 0.28 ± 
0.41 mg⋅L-1. The DO was more stable with less fluctuations (Fig. A19 appendix 2.2.1) at a concentration of 
5.8 ± 1.1 mg ⋅L-1. The temperature, pH, sCOD and CO2 concentrations were 25.4 ± 1.5◦C, 6.53 ± 0.05, 29.3 ± 
7.9 mgCOD∙L-1 and 271.1 ± 44.6 mgCO2∙L-1, respectively. Thus, the pH was the highest and most stable of 
the feeding regimes. The CO2:CH4 ratio was found to be 1.0 mg CO2∙mg CH4-1 with a YCX of 1.15 mg VSS∙mg 
CH4-1. The regime had a qX of 99 mg VSS∙L-1∙d-1. The nitrogen and phosphorous removal was 52% and 26% 
respectively (Table A19 appendix 2.2.1), yielding uptake rates of 9.2 ± 1.7 mg NO3- -N∙L-1∙d-1 and  
0.9 ± 0.4 mg PO43- -P∙L-1∙d-1 for nitrogen and phosphorous, respectively. The YNX and YPX were the lowest 
among the three feeding regimes yielding 10.7 ± 3.8 mg VSS⋅mg NO3- -N-1 and 114.5 ± 43.7 mg VSS⋅mg PO43- 
-P-1, respectively (Table A20 appendix 2.2.1).  
Biofilm 
A biofilm was detected on the CH4 membrane (Fig. A25 appendix 2.2.3). This classified the MABR as a 
hybrid system. The biofilm density was roughly estimated to be 9.7 mg VSS∙cm-3 (appendix 2.2.3). Little non-
homogeneous biofilm built on the O2 membrane (Fig. A26 appendix 2.2). Biofilm grew more on the straight 
flow paths, i.e., less turbulent paths with less detachment, rather than the turns. MOB tend to grow as 
biofilm for carbon uptake at the gas feeding source (Tsapekos, et al., 2020), which could explain the 
preference for the CH4 membrane. Furthermore, the diffusion coefficient for CH4 in water is lower 
compared to that of O2, which could cause growth conditions to be more favourable closer to the CH4 
membrane than in the middle of the MABR (Strong, et al., 2016; Modin, et al., 2010). Biofilms could be 
beneficial, due to decoupling of SRT from the HRT (Wang, et al., 2021), better OMP removal (Wang, et al., 
2020), higher operating gas pressure due to higher bubbling point (Scott, 1995), as well as better nitrate 
removal per assimilated carbon than suspended biomass (Modin, et al., 2010). The biofilm had uptake rates 
of nitrogen and phosphorous of 7.6 mg NO3- -N∙L-1∙d-1 and 0.6 mg PO43- -P∙L-1∙d-1, respectively. The biofilm 
could only be measured after the final feeding regime and was assumed to have been in steady-state across 
all feeding regimes. Therefore, the mass was assumed equal for all three regimes.  
 
The productivity of the MABR was linear correlated to CH4 loading (r = 0.97), with feast feeding having the 
highest productivity and starvation the lowest. However, the starvation feeding regime had the highest 
CO2:CH4 ratio and lowest sCOD concentration, which could indicate that MOBs during this regime 
assimilated more CH4 and produced less by-products.  
The feast regime had the lowest YCX as well as a low CO2:CH4 ratio. Combined with the sCOD concentration 
this indicated that less CH4 was assimilated into biomass during feast feeding and carbon was stored in by-
products instead. Excreted by-products could be utilized by heterotrophic bacteria (Valverde-Pérez, et al., 
2020; Strong, et al., 2015; Wang, et al., 2021), which could stabilize the community by degrading toxic by-
products and enhance OMP removal (Su, et al., 2021; Tang, et al., 2021; Hesselsoe, et al., 2005). Thus, 
production of by-products could prove beneficial for the OMP removal. Furthermore, the feast feeding 
regime experienced the highest removal of nutrients making it more suitable for nutrient recovery. 
However, the regime had a higher YNX compared to the starvation feeding regime, indicating less protein 
accumulate per biomass (Goonesekera, 2020). The low YNX of dNMSA and starvation regime indicated that 
lower C:N ratios promoted more assimilated nitrogen per biomass, which was also found by Skadborg 
(2018) and Valverde-Pérez et al. (2020). 
Normalizing to biomass concentration, the dNMSN enrichment, baseline and feast feeding all experienced 
an uptake rate of nitrogen of 0.01 mg N∙mg VSS-1∙d-1. The dNMSA enrichment and starvation feeding regime 
had higher normalized uptake rates of 0.05 and 0.03 mg N∙mg VSS-1∙d-1, respectively. Interestingly, the 
==PAGE:18==
13 
 
uptake rates of the baseline and feast feeding regimes resembled that the dNMSN enrichment. Whereas 
the nitrogen uptake rate of the starvation feeding regime was in between those found for the two 
enrichments. The biofilm had the lowest normalized nitrogen uptake of 0.005 mg N∙mg VSS-1∙d-1. The 
difference in uptake rates between the different feeding regimes is likely to indicate a shift in the 
dominating MOB type present (Semrau, et al., 2010; AlSayed, et al., 2018).     
For all feeding regimes, the CH4 gas concentration in the off-gas was below the lower explosion limit of 5% 
(Leis, et al., 2014). And the CH4 and O2 uptake was >99% for all feeding regimes, indicating a highly efficient 
gas transfer in the MABR. 
Organic micropollutants 
The ibuprofen samples were skewed and were therefore not included.  
The biodegradabilities expected by the MOB culture, were assumed to be similar to the biodegradabilities 
found for AOB (Table 2). This assumption was based on the similarities between AMO and pMMO, and the 
overlapping secondary substrate range of AMO compared to both pMMO and sMMO. The AMO enzyme is 
capable of co-metabolising alkanes (≤C8), alkenes (≤C5), aromatic compounds, ethers, sulfides and 
halogenated compounds by reaction pathways such as C-H hydroxylation, O-dealkylation and 
dehydrogenation (Su, et al., 2021). In comparison pMMO can co-metabolise alkanes (≤C5), whereas sMMO 
can co-metabolise alkanes (≤C8), esters, cyclic-alkanes, and aromatic compounds ( (Hedegaard, et al., 2018), 
(Semrau, et al., 2010)). Furthermore, sMMO is only present in some MOBs, whereas pMMO is present in 
most MOBs and can be expressed over sMMO, if Cu2+-concentrations are sufficient (Semrau, et al., 2010; 
Hesselsoe, et al., 2005). 
Steady state performance 
The performance during steady-state was inconclusive for all flow regimes, as many of the OMPs 
experienced negative removal rates (Table A22, appendix 2.3). As the removal rates were calculated based 
on the assumption of initial inlet concentrations of 2 µg∙L-1, deviations from this would impact the results. 
The higher measured OMP concentrations in the MABR was mostly likely due to errors, e.g., imprecise 
pipettes or stock solutions with higher concentrations than expected.

The co-metabolic degradation of compounds by MOBs can mostly be ascribed the activity associated to CH4 
oxidation (Semprini, et al., 1990; Rumayor, et al., 2019), with positive correlation between activity and F/M, 
while C:N was reported to have no effect on activity (AlSayed, et al., 2018). The baseline regime removed 
less OMPs than the starvation, which could be due a more established biofilm in the starvation regime. 
Further research would be needed to determine the impact of the C:N ratio on OMP degradation. The 
highest removal efficiencies for 7 out 9 OMPs were obtained during feast feeding. Thus, OMP removal 
indicated correlation between removal efficiency and biomass concentration and CH4 loading.  
To validate the hypothesis of higher OMP removal at higher CH4 loading, it was tested whether kbio would 
change significantly with CH4 loading by employing one-way ANOVA.  
Out of the 9 OMPs, only 1H-benzotriazole and sulfamethoxazole showed significant differences (p < 0.05) 
between kbio and CH4 loading rate. Thus, indicating that increasing the CH4 loading rate would 
accommodate higher removal of 1H-benzotriazole and sulfamethoxazole. Furthermore, the highest DO 
concentrations were measured during the feast batch test (Fig. A19 appendix 2.2). However, if oxidative 
stress did occur, it did not show notably in the removal efficiencies compared to the other feeding regimes.

Interestingly, the biofilm test experienced higher kbio for atenolol, clofibric acid and diclofenac compared to 
both the feast and starvation regimes (Table A24 appendix 2.3), which could indicate that the different 
OMPs were degraded differently by suspended biomass and biofilm. Better removal of diclofenac in biofilm 
compared to suspended biomass have been reported (Tang, et al., 2017; Falås, et al., 2013). Torresi et al. 
(2016) reported positive correlation between biofilm thickness and OMP degradation, mainly due to 
microbial diversity. However, for diclofenac and sulfonamides, biofilm thickness and OMP degradation 
were negatively correlated (Torresi, et al., 2016).  
The feast regime performed more poorly in the removal of atenolol, trimethoprim, metoprolol, 
carbamazepine, clofibric acid and diclofenac compared to both CAS and MBBR and for venlafaxine 
compared to MBBR (Table A14 appendix 1.5). However, for the OMPs 1H-benzotriazole and 
sulfamethoxazole the feast regime out-performed reported values for both CAS and MBBR. Interestingly, 
the high kbio for sulfamethoxazole was unexpected for an AMO-identical enzyme, due to sulfamethoxazole’s 
amine and sulfonamide groups (Su, et al., 2021), which could indicate MMO being less inhibited by these 
groups. Sulfamethoxazole was likely degraded by dehydrogenation of its alkyl group (Su, et al., 2021). The 
removal pathway of 1H-benzotriazole was unclear, as it does not contain any of the easily biodegradable 
groups for AMO.  
Both 1H-benzotriazole and sulfamethoxazole are aromatic compounds, which suggested the presence of 
sMMO. This was interesting, as the Cu2+-concentration in the media was 0.12 µM and studies report no 
sMMO at concentration 0.1 µM (Chen, et al., 2007; Ho, et al., 2013; Semrau, et al., 2010; Benner, et al., 
2015). Furthermore, the gene expressing pMMO, pmoA, was reported to have higher transcription during

However, the presence of heterotrophic bacteria would be expected. Previous studies have shown that 
MeOH as carbon sources enhances OMP removal compared to Ethanol (C2) (Torresi, 2017). Thus, 
heterotrophic bacteria utilizing C1 substrates, e.g., MeOH and formate, might be able to degrade OMPs co-
metabolically as MOBs. In some degradation tests, >50% of sulfamethoxazole and 1H-benzotriazole 
removal was ascribed heterotrophic bacteria present in MOB cultures (Benner, et al., 2015). Thus, the 
degradation of OMPs cannot entirely be ascribed MOBs alone. 
The low kbio of carbamazepine during feast feeding indicated a recalcitrant OMP (Table A15 appendix 1.5), 
which was in line with literature (Su, et al., 2021; DEPA, 2018). The same was the case with the removal of 
diclofenac and venlafaxine, where literature reported removal efficiencies varying depending on treatment 
type (Peake, et al., 2016; Su, et al., 2021; Tang, et al., 2017; Torresi, et al., 2016). However, the changing 
faith of diclofenac between feeding regimes and biofilm was interesting. It would have been considered 
somewhat recalcitrant during all batch tests, due to the halogenated groups on the aromatic ring (Su, et al., 
2021). 
Surprisingly, during feast feeding atenolol and trimethoprim were found to be slowly biodegradable and 
recalcitrant, respectively. Both OMPs contain alkyl groups, which are considered biodegradable via alkyl 
hydroxylation by AMO (Su, et al., 2021). Furthermore, metoprolol and clofibric acid were found to be 
moderate biodegradable and recalcitrant for the MOB culture during feast, respectively. As both 
compounds contain hydroxyl moieties, they are considered easily biodegradable via dehydrogenation by 
AMO (Su, et al., 2021). Microbial degradation can be hindered by physical-chemical factors of the OMPs, 
e.g., molecular weight, logKOW and polarity (Su, et al., 2021). This could be a reason for the less than 
anticipated removal of some of the OMPs.   
For water re-use purposes, degradation of sulfamethoxazole and 1H-benzotriazole is beneficial, as both are 
associated with negative environmental impacts. As an antibiotic, sulfamethoxazole could promote 
spreading of antibiotic resistance in receiving water bodies (Polesel, et al., 2016). And 1H-benzotriazole 
have shown to be endocrine disruptive and carcinogenic (Shi, et al., 2019).  
The low degradation of diclofenac, clofibric acid and carbamazepine could be problematic for water reuse, 
as these OMPs are considered to accumulate within crops. Especially carbamazepine has shown high 
accumulation when applied to cucumber, tomato, and spinach, due to its hydrophobicity and being 
nonionic (Goldstein, et al., 2014).  
For the majority of the OMPs, the MABR did not outperform MBBRs. However, the MABR poses an 
advantage compared to a MBBR as a polishing step of secondary effluent, as the AOB employed in the 
latter would require additional nitrate removal. Furthermore, the MABR had a highly efficient oxygen 
transfer giving it an economical advantage (Wei, et al., 2012).  
Microbial protein 
The higher CH4 loading during feast feeding gave a protein yield per consumed CH4 comparable to the 
starvation regime but lower than the baseline regime (Table 4). However, the protein content (g protein∙g 
dry cell weight-1 as %) of the feast regime was within the same range as for the baseline. Thus, increasing 
the CH4 loading did not seem to have a significantly negative effect on the protein content. As a result, the 
overall amount of protein would be higher during feast due to the higher biomass concentration. For all 
feeding regimes the protein content was in the low end for MOB cultures (Valverde-Pérez, et al., 2020; 
Tsapekos, et al., 2019).  
==PAGE:22==
17 
 
The protein yield per assimilated nitrogen (N-Protein ratio) had only minor differences between the 
baseline and feast feeding. Interestingly, despite having the highest YNX, the starvation feeding regime had 
the lowest protein content and lowest N-protein ratio. This could indicate nitrogen being stored as nucleic 
acid rather than protein, as MOB exposed to low CH4 loading rates accumulate more gene material 
compared to higher CH4 loading (Wang, et al., 2021). High concentrations of nucleic acid can have adverse 
effects on consumers (Strong, et al., 2015), e.g., humans. However, no negative effects on biostimulants 
correlated to nucleic acid were found. The baseline and feast regimes had higher N-protein ratios than 
found for other protein sources, e.g., for eggs, meat and fish (N-protein ratios of 5.5-6, (Sosulski & 
Imafidon, 1990)). While the starvation regime experienced a N-protein ratio within the range of microalgae 
(2.53-5.77, (Lourenço, et al., 2007)). Goonesekera (2020) reported lower N-protein ratios for MOB 
cultivated on different phosphate-sources, ranging from 1.01 to 4.29. Thus, the MABR exhibits good 
qualities in terms of protein production from the assimilated nitrogen compared to both protein sources 
and MOB cultures. 
Interestingly, across all feeding regimes and enrichments, increasing YNX resulted in increasing N-Protein 
ratio. However, there was no clear correlation with protein content. This was surprising as a low YNX was 
expected to yield more protein per biomass. Furthermore, lower C:N ratio did not influence the protein 
ratio as assumed. Skadborg (2018) showed that protein content was negatively correlated with both C:N 
ratio and SRT. Even though the MABR of this project showed large biofilm growth, the relatively small HRT 
might have influenced the microbial community. This could indicate that nitrogen was used for purposes 
other than protein, as fast-growing bacteria contains more nucleic acid than slow growing, e.g., MOBs 
(Øverland, et al., 2010).

The amino acid concentration profiles are quite similar between the feeding regimes and biofilm (Fig. 5). 
The amino acid concentration from MP is higher than the concentrations found in the biostimulants alfalfa 
hydrosylate (AH) and meat fluor hydrosylate (MFH). AH and MFH were found to promote increased root 
growth and nitrate assimilation in maize leading to increased fertilizer efficiency (Ertani, et al., 2009). 
Furthermore, positive correlation exists between applied concentration of amino acids and positive effects 
of plants (Almadi, et al., 2020; Wilson, et al., 2018). Thus, less bacterial biomass would be needed 
compared to AH and MFH for application. Especially, the relative abundance of glycine, arginine, histidine 
and glutamic acid was of interest, as these can positively affect nitrogen, Fe and Zn in tomato and soybean 
(Ghasemi, et al., 2012; Rodríguez-Lucena, et al., 2010). Proline and glycine were also associated with 
increased oil and fruit yields of olive trees (Almadi, et al., 2020). Based on these values, MP seems suitable 
as biostimulant resource. 
The MP had a lower total amino acid concentration compared to soymeal and fishmeal. Comparing the 
essential amino acid concentration of MP with fishmeal and soymeal, showed that fishmeal had higher 
concentrations essential amino acids (except threonine) than MP (Fig. A33 appendix 2.4). However, the MP

had higher essential acid concentrations for methionine, threonine and histidine compared to soymeal.

extraction (Valverde-Pérez, et al., 2020), however it could be expected to be higher in MP compared to 
fishmeal and soymeal (Øverland, et al., 2010).   
It is noteworthy, that the amino acid concentration derived from the biofilm, resembled the suspended MP 
from the feast regime more than the suspended MP from starvation. This could indicate less proteinaceous 
bacteria in suspension in the latter or that the suspended bacteria during feast was detachment of the 
biofilm. Both scenarios could have implications, as lower amino acids concentration would require more 
biomass to ensure a certain amount of amino acids.

Outlook  
DNA sequencing was not within the scope of the current project. Thus, the project lacked a clear 
identification of the microbial communities during the different feeding regimes. Future research should 
have a focus on mapping both the suspended biomass, as well as the stratification of the biofilm. This could 
determine the microbial communities under each feeding regimes and how it may impact the OMP 
removal. Furthermore, no metabolic by-products, e.g., MeOH, were directly identified, which could further 
help understanding the interaction between heterotrophs, MOBs and OMP removal.    
The temperature impact on MABR performance was not investigated. As temperature affects OMP removal 
(Hesselsoe, et al., 2005), as well as biomass productivity (to a certain threshold) (Yang, et al., 2021), future 
==PAGE:25==
20 
 
research should also have a focus on operating temperatures.  
Other parameters of interest are HRT and O2 loading. The HRT could affect OMP removal by suspended 
biomass (Tang, et al., 2017; Wang, et al., 2020), the protein content of suspended biomass and 
optimization between HRT and O2 loading could increase N removal (Tian, et al., 2015; Peng, et al., 2015). 
Furthermore, knowing the impact of O2 loading on OMP removal was not tested. Too high O2 loading could 
induce oxidative stress, whereas low O2 loadings could promote fermentation (and thus higher production 
of by-products) but might also impact MMOs ability to perform sufficient hydrogenation or dealkylation of 
OMPs (Su, et al., 2021). A last parameter of future interest is the recirculation velocity, as high velocities 
promote better nutrient uptake by the biofilm (Tian, et al., 2015; Wei, et al., 2012) but could have 
detrimental effect on OMP removal by the biofilm (Peng, et al., 2015). 
Future projects should also broaden the range of OMPs to test the removal by MOB dominated cultures 
under more realistic scenarios. Furthermore, the assumption of negligible sorption of OMPs should be 
verified. In the same context, Goonesekera (2020) found that MP derived from MOB cultivated on different 
media were above the EU limit for chromium in fertilizer. Thus, the heavy metal content should be 
evaluated, both for use as biostimulants and feed. 
Furthermore, the current project has neither considered the presence of any OMP derivatives nor their 
degradability. Unknown derivatives could potentially pose a threat to ecosystems and human health. Thus, 
derivatives should be measured and classified. 
The MP should be tested as a biostimulant to gain better knowledge of positive traits. 
To fully elucidate the feasibility of OMP removal by MOB cultures as polishing of secondary effluents, the 
impact of N and P concentrations typical of secondary effluent (i.e., 12 mg NO3- -N and 1.4 mg PO43- -P, 
(Yagecic, et al., 2016)) needs to be studied.  Furthermore, an economical and environmental life cycle 
assessment of a MABR in a WWTP should be conducted. And to determine if any further treatment, e.g., 
physico-chemical, would be needed and if this would accommodate additional carbon footprint.  
Conclusion 
 
Organic micropollutants present in wastewater are of concern, as they pose an environmental and health 
risk if not treated. Methanotrophic bacteria have shown promise in removing a variety of organic 
micropollutants. Furthermore, they present an option for recovering and upcycling of nutrients as microbial 
protein from an otherwise low residual stream. 
A membrane aerated bioreactor was operated under three different CH4 loading rates to evaluate methane 
oxidizing bacteria ability to remove organic micropollutants from wastewater. Furthermore, the removal of 
nitrate and phosphate was evaluated as well as the amino acid content of the bacteria as basis for 
biostimulants or feed ingredients. The membrane aerated bioreactor was capable of removing nutrients 
under the different CH4 loadings, with the high CH4 loading being the most efficient (N 71% and P 36%). The 
membrane aerated bioreactor showed linear correlation (r = 0.97) between CH4 loadings and biomass 
productivity. 
 
The membrane aerated bioreactor was capable of removing organic micropollutants. Of the three 
CH4 loading rates, the highest removal efficiencies were obtained during the highest loading. However, only 
the pharmaceuticals 1H-benzotriazole and sulfamethoxazole showed a strong correlation between 
CH4 loading rate and removal rate. The membrane aerated bioreactor had higher removal rates for 1H-
benzotriazole and sulfamethoxazole compared to known removal rates by conventional activated sludge or 
moving bed biofilm reactors for nitrification.  
More research is needed to determine the microbial community and how this and parameters, e.g., 
temperature, by-products, HRT as well as CH4 and CO2 loadings, affect organic micropollutant removal.

21 
 
Unexpectedly, the microbial protein content was not affected by the changing C:N ratio nor was YNX. With 
an average of 39.4 ± 0.4% the protein content was in the low range for methanotrophs. However, 
surprisingly the N-protein ratio increased with YNX, with the former reaching values comparable to eggs, 
meat and fish for the feast and baseline regimes. The amino acids derived from the microbial protein were 
rather similar across all feeding regimes. The amino acids concentrations were higher compared to two 
known biostimulants, thus posing a viable option for nutrient reuse. For future prospects as feed 
ingredient, the essential amino acid concentrations showed some options as substitution for soymeal. 
Consideration should be given as to whether the better removal efficiency under high CH4 loading rate 
outweighs the trade-off from a higher carbon footprint.  
 
The membrane aerated bioreactor experienced a high mass transfer efficiency and was safe to operate as 
the effluent CH4 concentration was below the lower explosion limit.

1.4 kLa test 
The reactor chamber was flushed with N2 gas for approximately five minutes. Then the effluent and media 
inlet tubes were closed off. The gas chambers were opened one at a time and flushed for approximately 5 
minutes each. After flushing they were closed and the O2 chamber was connected to a pump.   
As a final preparation, a 1 L bottle was filled with one litre of demineralised water. A sparger connected to a 
N2 gas container was submerged and the water was sparged with N2. After 10 minutes the sparger was 
removed, the media inlet tube submerged into the water and the bottle sealed with film (Parafilm M®, 
Bemis). 
The water was then pumped into the MABR and the effluent tube opened to allow gas to be pushed out. 
When water emerged from the effluent tube, the media pump was stopped, and the effluent tube closed 
off.  
Once the DO seemed stabile, the O2 pump was turned on and DO concentrations and temperature were 
measured every minute for an hour.

1.6 Re-suspension of particles 
Before taken OD and TSS samples, the reactor had to be stirred in order to re-suspend any particles that 
might have settled on the horizontal surfaces within the MABR. This was done in a routinely fashion, see 
Fig. A10, in order to be consistent when gathering samples for data collection. The routine was as follows 
(letters in parenthesis refers to Fig. A10). The MABR was situated in a normal vertical position (a) under 
normal operation, i.e., the positioning is with respect to the in- and outlet tubing. The mixing flow was 
perpendicular to in- and outlet flows, i.e., mixing line was situated in a horizontal position under normal 
operation, with the mixing flow entering at the bottom of the MABR and leaving at the top. The MABR was 
released from the anchoring (b). It was then tilted to a more horizontal position (c), thus ensuring that any 
settled particles would be released into the mixing flow field. The mixing flow was then increased to 
maximum flow (d), i.e., 300 rpm equal to approximately 950 mL/min. Holding the MABR it was ensured that 
all tubing was free and not entangled in the tightening pins (e). The MABR was then shaken firmly from side 
to side 20 times (f). The direction of the mixing flow was changed (g). And the MABR was shaken firmly 20 
times again (h). The mixing flow was decreased to normal operation flow of 125 rpm and the flow changed 
back to normal operational direction of flow (i). The MABR was positioned back into vertical stand and 
anchored again (j).

headspace. Thus, all CH4 not consumed, i.e., the concentration in the effluent, is assumed to be dissolved.

1.8 TSS measurement 
The TSS measurement was done using a 47 mm glass fiber filter (ADVANTEC®, Toyo Roshi Kaisha, Ltd.) with 
a pore size of 0.2 µm.  
The filter, with the rough side up, was placed on a Kitasato flask with a holder and funnel, see Fig. A11, and 
vacuum was applied. The filter was then rinsed with DI water before placed in an aluminium dish 
(Aluminum dish w/tab, 57mm, VWR). The dish was then placed in an oven and dried at 105◦C for at least 
two hours. After being dried, the dish, with filter, was removed from the oven and weighed, giving the 
initial mass, m1, of dish plus filter. The filter was then placed on the Kitasato flask setup and the TSS sample 
was poured onto the filter. The container used for the sample was rinsed with DI water, which was then

also poured onto the filter. The funnel was also rinsed with DI water to ensure any biomass that might have 
attached was washed down onto the filter. The filter was then removed and placed in the dish, which was

flow rate of 7.55 mL∙min-1 and 7.66 mL∙min-1 for air and 
water, respectively. The difference is approximately 1.5% 
when using air and thus the assumption was deemed 
reasonable.
