Abstract
The presented thesis focuses on maximizing the aerodynamic performance of a multi-
element airfoil, which consists of a modified FFA-W3-400 main thick airfoil and a leading-
edge slat. To achieve this objective, a computational framework utilizing the adjoint shape
optimization method is set up. Initial CFD model is validated against a study in literature
to ensure reliability and robustness.
The single-point optimization problem is defined to re-design slat geometry for the
maximum value of total lift-to-drag ratio. The study results in a final design that improves
the lift-to-drag ratio by 16.1%. It is observed that the slat size, location of the trailing edge
and leading edge angle have significant impacts on the aerodynamics performance. With
changes in these parameters, the flow separation is delayed significantly, thus the flow is
attached longer on the airfoil. This results in a higher lift and lower drag. The final design of
the slat only improves the aerodynamic performance for high angle of attacks, particularly
above approximately 7.5°.
The improvement on the total aerodynamic performance
is majorly due to the improvements in the performance of the slat airfoil. The stall
characteristic does not change compared to the initial design.
Key findings demonstrate that the inner part of the blade can be re-design to increase
energy production. The tendency in design changes suggest a theoretical explanations
for better flow structure on the multi-element airfoil. Additionally, the adjoint method is
found very powerful technique for shape optimization in aerodynamics.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Research Question
Due to urgent need to address climate change and create a greener world, developments in
wind energy are progressing at a great pace. To increase energy production, wind turbines
have evolved into larger structures with higher towers and longer blades. However, this
growth has led to challenges related to transportation, maintenance, and material costs.
Therefore, the focus has shifted to finding alternative ways to produce more energy while
mitigating some of these issues. One such approach is to run the wind turbines in different
concept and re-design them based on this concept such as offshore or floating offshore
wind turbines. Another approach is to individually optimize elements of the wind turbine
and redesign them for maximum efficiency. As a result, the aerodynamics and design of
the blades have become highly significant areas of research.
Thick airfoils, which are located in the inner part of the blade, are well-known for
their structural advantages but have poor aerodynamic performance compared to thin
airfoils. It has been found that there is an unused potential energy in the inner section
that can be extracted if this section is redesigned. However, such redesigns often result in
longer airfoils, which are not desirable. At this point, multi-element airfoils emerge as an
alternative method.
Multi-elements airfoil are described as a combination of different airfoils, mainly
consisting of a main airfoil and two additional airfoils integrated to that main airfoil, which
are called slat and flap. The advantages of multi-element airfoils in terms of aerodynamic
performance have been proven and are extensively used in aeronautics. However, studies
and applications of multi-element airfoils for wind turbines are very limited.
Research have shown that performance of multi-element airfoils are dependent to many
design parameters. These parameters are generally classified based on the position and
orientation of the slat and flap. Sensitivity analyses of these parameters, or parametric
studies, have been conducted extensively both numerically and experimentally. However,

3
there is a lack of studies focused on the shape design of these multi-element airfoils. This
is because redesigning a shape requires a large number of control parameters, leading to
a complex and expensive study. The present study concentrates a shape optimization
method that addresses the challenges mentioned above.
1.2
Aim of the Project
The thesis aims to develop a numerical framework for cost-effective optimization of slat/flap
airfoils. The first objective is to validate this framework and ensure its robustness, thereby
enhancing our understanding of various aspects of computational fluid dynamics (CFD).
The second objective is to execute this framework to optimize slat/flap elements
for maximum aerodynamic performance of multi-element airfoils. This includes gaining
insights into design behaviors that could potentially enhance aerodynamics. Following
this, the goal is to demonstrate the improvements in flow dynamics.
1.3
Outline
The thesis is divided into four main chapters, aiming to present the content as clearly and
comprehensibly as possible. In Chapter 2, the primary motivations for using multi-element
airfoils, the characteristics of these airfoils, and their studies and applications in both
aeronautics and wind turbines are discussed. Furthermore, the optimization methods
are evaluated, and existing literature on multi-element airfoil optimization is reviewed.
Lastly, the evolution of the adjoint method which is utilized in our optimization problem
is presented.
In Chapter 3, the methodology of this project and its verification are presented. The
CFD setup is explained in terms of geometry, physics, solver, and mesh. This setup is
validated against an established numeric study in the literature. Lastly, several designs
from a previous parametric study are compared to our methodology and decision is made
for the initial design of adjoint shape optimization.
In Chapter 4, the adjoint method is first introduced theoretically, and its working
principle in CFD is given afterwards. Following this, the optimizer setup and the results
of the optimization study are presented.
Finally, In Chapter 5, the brief summary of the project and possible future studies are
presented.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1
Thick Airfoil
A thick airfoil is a type of airfoil characterized by a thickness-to-chord ratio (t/c) greater
than 24% [1]. A blade mainly consists of three parts: root; mid and tip sections. [2] The
root part, or inner part of the blade, plays crucial role in structural integrity. Figure 2.1
illustrates different types of airfoils through out the blade. Thick airfoils are used in the
root section starting from airfoil (A) to approximately airfoil (3).
Figure 2.1 The airfoil types along the blade from root to tip [3].
In both aeronautics and wind energy, thick airfoils have been of great interest since
the they provides structural advantages. In aeronautics, thick airfoils offer an improved
structural depth that can reduce the bending stress acting on the wing significantly.
Additionally, larger fuel volume is achieved [4]. New challenges emerge in wind industry as
the energy demands increase. As the turbines get larger in order to harness more energy,
more stiff turbine blades are needed. The optimal way to achieve higher stiffness while
keeping the weight low is to increase the relative thickness of the airfoils on the blade.
However, the aerodynamic performance of thick airfoils are generally poor compared to
that of thin airfoils. [5]. Flow separation occurs at lower Reynolds numbers (Re) for thick
airfoils. [4].
==PAGE:15==
2.2 Multi-element Airfoils
5
According to the study of Johansen et al, the existing wind turbines actually have a
higher power production potential due to low loading on the inner part of the rotor [6].
However, from structural integrity point of view, a certain thickness (t) is required for the
inner part of the blades that carries the large flapwise loads induced by the thrust force.
Due to limitations on the maximum chord length, airfoils cannot be made longer in order
to harvest more energy in the inner section. Therefore, alternative methods have been
investigated to maximize the power production by means of multi-element airfoils.
2.2
Multi-element Airfoils
Multi-element airfoils (high lift systems) are combination of different airfoils in order to
enhance aerodynamic characteristics. They are intensively utilized in aeronautics and
wind energy industry. The multi-elements airfoils are classified by primarily two additional
features that are integrated to the main airfoil: a slat and a flap [7]. In general, the slat is
located in front of the leading edge of the main airfoil, while the flap is located behind the
trailing edge, as illustrated in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2 Typical multi-element airfoil scheme [7].
Multi-element airfoils have superior properties compared to conventional single element
airfoil. Smith provides a thorough/detailed explanation of high lift characteristics of
multi-element airfoils [8]. The highlights of the study can be summarized as:
The attached flow on a single element airfoil creates a thin boundary layer where the
viscous effects are dominant. Outside the boundary layer, the flow is assumed inviscid.
The Kutta condition states that the flow must leave the trailing edge gently without any
abrupt changes in flow direction. Under this condition, one can achieve very high lift
coefficients by increasing the angle of attack. However, due to adverse pressure gradient
over the airfoil, the flow separates from the surface. This means that thin boundary layer
which is dominated by laminar flow does not last long, leading to a drastic reduction in lift
coefficient. In order to increase the load on the airfoil without influencing the boundary
layer negatively might be placing other blade elements in the vicinity of the main airfoil.
This idea has the following effects:
==PAGE:16==
6
Literature Review
• Slat effect: Circulation on the slat element decreases the pressure peak on the main
airfoil. This way, the stress on the boundary layer decreases and thus, higher stall
angles can be achieved.
• Main airfoil effect: Upcoming flow in the slot, between the slat and main airfoil, is
accelerated. This accelerated flow increases the flow angle at the trailing edge of the
slat resulting in higher circulation over the slat.
• Increased velocity: The slat increases the velocity of the flow inherently. This flow
leaves the slat and follows the main airfoil with higher momentum than it could
possibly had if it was a single airfoil without slat. Achieving a higher momentum
avoids the flow separation. This way, higher angle of attacks can be achieved without
flow separation.
• Adverse pressure gradient at wake: The upcoming flow is accelerated on the slat
surface where, pressure gradient is positive
 ∆p
∆x > 0
!
. However, the deceleration
 ∆p
∆x < 0
!
takes place in the wake of the slat, which results in more efficient
deceleration where the wall is regarded.
• Refreshed boundary layer: Three thin boundary layers on three airfoil elements (slat,
main airfoil, flap) is more effective compared to one thick boundary layer (single
element airfoil) to handle higher pressure gradient.
It is important to mention that the multi-element airfoils might introduce an extra
drag. Therefore, the configuration of the airfoil elements needs to be verified in terms of
drag performance.[1]
2.2.1
Applications in Aeronautics
The multi-element airfoils are quite popular in aircraft industry. Although these components
account for 6-11% of the total production cost of regular jet airplane, their superior lift
performance at higher angle of attacks has a immense importance during the landing
and take-off situations. The study of Meredith points out that an increase in maximum
lift coefficient by 1% converts into an increase in payload of 22 passenger during landing.
Similarly, 1% improvement in lift-to-drag ratio can be interpreted as increase in payload
of 14 passengers during take-off [9]. By actively controlling their configuration around the
main airfoil, the flow characteristics are changed to achieve required lift force. Leishman
has pointed out different lift coefficient curves for three typical airplane positions, namely
landing, takeoff and cruise, as seen in Figure 2.3. It is clear that much higher lift coefficients
with higher stall angles could be achieved. In addition to that, the flaps at the trailing
edge allows better stabilization of the rolling and pitching moment by means of actively
controlled lift coefficient [10].

2.2 Multi-element Airfoils
7
Significant amount of effort in aeronautics has been allocated to the development in
aerodynamic design of multi-element airofils. Dam has reviewed the developments in these
fields for the last half century and found out that the computational tools provided an
enormous improvement compared to the empirical methods [11]. Also, the CFD results
has showed unprecedented similar results to the physical experiments.
Figure 2.3 Typical lift coefficient curves achieved for different airplane positions [10].
Genc et al. has investigated a multi-element airfoil comprised of a NACA2415 (main
airfoil) and a NACA22 (leading-edge slat) both experimentally and computationally at

improvement is explained by elimination of the separation bubble by injection of high
momentum flow from the leading edge-slat.
Furthermore, the utilization of flapped airfoils to delay wake bursting through upper
surface suction has been extensively studied by Veldhuis and van Craenenbroeck [13].
Their study shows significant improvement in the lift performance of the airplanes.
Additionally, Valarezo et al. has conducted a parametric optimization study for an
airfoil configured with a single leading edge slat and two-segment trailing-edge flap [14].
The objective of the study was to investigate the effect of high Reynolds number (i.e.
Re = 16 × 106) on the multi-element airfoils. It was observed that the high Reynolds
number effects which obtained from single-element airfoils may not be applicable to
multi-element airfoils.

2.3.1
Classification
There are two main types of methods: gradient-based and gradient-free algorithms. Both
algorithms have certain advantages and drawbacks due to their mathematical natures.
One must take into account these aspects for their application in order to find optimal
solution effectively. In the next two sections, the algorithms are introduced including the
common methods.
2.3.1.1
Gradient-based Algorithms
Gradient-based algorithm uses gradient information to find direction for optimal solution.
Let f : Rn →R be a scalar function. The gradient at an arbitrary point x0 ∈Rn is a
vector on the steepest ascent direction of f with respect to changes in x. This approach
is utilised in methods such as gradient descent, gradient ascent, conjugate gradient and
Newton’s method [22]:
• Gradient Descent: Gradient descent method aims the minimization of the function by
iteratively searching in the direction of the negative steepest gradient. As mentioned
before, the iteration initiates with an initial guess. The method calculates the next
possible point x(k+1) based on x(k+1) = x(k) −αk∇f(xk), where αk is called learning
rate to limit the impact of the gradient in the estimation. The function is run until
a convergence is achieved.
• Gradient Ascent: Gradient ascent method aims the maximization of the function
instead, but using a similar approach as explained the Gradient Descent method.
Therefore, the algorithm iteratively search for the steepest ascent until the conver-
gence is achieved.
• Conjugate Gradient: Conjugate gradient method is similar to gradient ascent tech-
nique based on the gradient information. The algorithm computes a conjugate
directions as the algorithm continues to iterate. The conjugate direction is a linear
combination of the previous direction and existing gradient. In other words, a mem-
ory is introduced in the search path that can significantly improve the convergence
speed especially for the quadratic functions since the search directions must be
conjugate to each other. With simple gradient descent method, a sudden change in
the search direction could potentially ruin the previous iterations.
• Newton’s method: Compared to the other methods explained above, the Newton’s
method facilitates the second order derivative, also known as Hessian matrix (Hf(x)).
In this method, the next point is estimated as x(k+1) = x(k) −f

Gradient-based algorithms are used in many problems thanks to their superior performance

2.3.1.2
Gradient-free Algorithms
Unlike gradient-based algorithms, gradient-free algorithms do not require any derivation
of the objective function. This gives a great flexibility on implementing the method on
discrete and noisy functions. Due to their robust behavior against non-smooth functions,
the method has been a popular optimization method. Some of the well-known algorithms
are genetic algorithm, particle swarm, simulated annealing and Nelder-Mead simplex
algorithm. An overview of these methods are briefy mentioned below [23]:
• Genetic algorithm: Genetic algorithms is derived from the evolutionary principle in
biology. This method does not offer a single search path to the optimum solution,
but a population of potential solution. Iteratively, new set of solution is proposed
that guarantees an increase in the evaluated objective function with the solution
sets compared to the previous iteration. Algorithm progress further until a certain
stopping criteria is met.
• Particle swarm: Particle swarm also is a similar method to genetic algorithm to form
new set of solutions iteratively. Particle swarm also traces the velocity in addition
to the position of the solution. The position and velocity of the particle are updated
by the best position in the past and best position of the entire particle swarm. This
allows a accelerated convergence towards better solution.
• Simulated annealing: Annealing is commonly used in the material sciences where a
material is heated and then slowly cooled in a controlled manner. When this idea is
implemented in optimization, an initial guess of the variable is expanded around the
value with some randomness. Based on the evaluation of all randomly generated
guesses, an improved guess is selected in the next iteration. This cycle is repeated
until the optima is found.
• Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm: This algorithm uses a simplex depending on the
dimensional space. This simplex updates its shape, position based on a set of rules.

2.4
Evaluation of Adjoint Method
The adjoint method has been a quite popular choice in CFD for gradient-based opti-
mizations. Its success lies in the fact that the adjoint approach requires a single flow
solution and a single adjoint solution regardless of the number of design parameters. This
presents a significant advantage over traditional parametric design optimization, which
typically requires a flow solution for each combination of parameters. Consequently, over
the decades, adjoint-based optimization methods have become quite efficient tools for
optimizing relevant industrial applications [26].

The adjoint method was originally developed by Lions and Pironneau in 1970s and has
since become a very powerful tool in aeronautics, automotive and wind energy industries,
respectively [27]. Initially, the method was applied in control theory to solve optimal

finite element models. However, the use of the method in fluid dynamics has became

The adjoint method has also been extensively used in shape and topology optimization

strategy for a specific problem, one must compromise between efficiency and the latter
attributes. [31].

Throughout the study, the physics model of the simulation described in this section remains

For the physics of the flow, the two dimensional, steady, constant density and fully
turbulent boundary layer models are employed. The Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes
(RANS) Shear Stress Transport (SST) k-ω turbulence model is utilized as the turbulence
model [32]. Additionally, the transition boundary layer solution of Star-CCM + will also
be validated by Zahle’s study in Section 3.4. To achieve this, the γ-Reθ transition model
is employed [33].
Before focusing on k-ω SST model, it is essential to understand how k-ω SST model
differs from the k-ϵ and k-ω turbulence models. Historically, the k-ϵ and k-ω turbulence
models were developed first, and k-ω SST model was developed as an improvement on
these original models. It has been proved that the near wall damping functions which are
necessary in k-ϵ model become unreliable when applied to a variety of flows. Therefore,
the k-ω model was developed in response to this issue in order to provide better near wall
behaviour. The k-ω model is less sensitive to variety in the flow and does not require
near wall damping functions. However, it was observed that the k-ω model were actually
sensitive to the free stream value of turbulence applied at the inlet. This sensitivity can
lead to inaccurate predictions of important parameters such as lift and drag forces for
flows over airfoils, where these parameters are influenced by the turbulence intensity or
length scale applied at the inlet. Also, for both k-ϵ and k-ω models, it was found that
the flow does not separate correctly from the smooth surfaces when compared to the
experimental measurements and this is primarily because the wall shear stress is calculated
too high for these models. Therefore the k-ω SST model was developed by Menter [32]
in 1994 to address some of these problems and predict better separation for external
aerodynamics applications. The standard k-ϵ model has two transport equations for both
turbulence kinetic energy k and turbulent dissipation rate ϵ as given in Equation 3.1 and

3.2.2
Solver Type
There are two main types of solvers in CFD: segregated and coupled flow solvers. In this
project, the segregated flow solver is utilized in Section 3 due to its simplicity compared to
the coupled flow solver. However, it was found that the adjoint method is not compatible
with the segregated flow solver for reasons that will be explained in Section 4. Therefore,
for the adjoint analysis, the flow solver was switched to the coupled flow solver in Section
4.
Both solvers are good enough to predict the flow but they have different approach
to solve system equations. The segregated flow solver solves momentum and pressure
equations independently. The working principle is given in Figure 3.4. As it is shown in
the flow chart, the approach is to create sub-problems and solve them iteratively until
the velocities u, v, w and pressure p converge the set of equations. Semi-Implicit Method
for Pressure-Linked Equations (SIMPLE) is used to solve the pressure-velocity coupling.
The segregated flow solver is quite simple method and the most popular choice for the
CFD simulations. The great advantage of this solver is that it is easy to implement
and computationally cheap compared to the coupled solver. The solver can handle the
various flow conditions. However, the major disadvantage of this solver is that the solution
converges later than other methods due to its iterative nature.
On the other hand, the coupled flow solver solves the system equations simultaneously, the
vector consisting of system parameters (u, v, w, p) is solved by matrix calculation. This
leads to more accurate solution and faster convergence on the solution rather than the
segregated flow solver. However, one iteration takes more time for the coupled flow solver
as the coupled matrix is relatively large. Therefore, the coupled solver is not necessarily

3.3
Meshing
It is essential in any comparative study to show that the numerical results are independent
from the mesh itself. The general errors encountered in the simulations can be traced back
to three main sources:
• Modeling Error: This arises from approximations in the continuous formulation,
including the differential equations, geometry definitions, and boundary conditions.
It describes the difference between the exact continuous solution of the model
formulation and the real flow.
==PAGE:31==
3.3 Meshing
21
• Discretization Error: This error arises from approximations made when discretizing
the continuous formulation into a set of algebraic equations.
It represents the
difference between the exact discrete solution and the continuous solution.
• Iterative (Algebraic) Error: It is defined as the difference between the exact and
approximate solutions to the discrete formulation, resulting from the truncation
errors associated with the iterative solvers for the algebraic systems.
In our case, the first error arises from the difference between the Navier-Stokes solution
and the actual flow. The second source of error comes from the computational grid. The
third source of error is due to the numerical methods and my error tolerances. The most
problematic error is usually the one caused by the mesh.
Therefore, obtaining appropriate mesh density and quality is a significant step in order
to prevent discretization errors. Following this, confirming grid convergence is key in
numerical studies, ensuring that equations are solved accurately and that the solution
remains robust regardless of changes in grid resolution.
Two types of geometries are commonly used in the literature for the fluid domain:
rectangular-type and C-type. The primary distinction arises in defining the angle of attack
of the airfoil. A C-type domain was employed to facilitate easier variation of the angle
of attack by altering the direction of the incoming flow through the semi-circular inlet
boundary. In contrast, for the rectangular-type domain, it is more convenient to rotate
the airfoil to define the angle of attack. The main disadvantage of the rectangular-type
domain is that whenever the geometry of the fluid domain is altered, a new mesh structure
is required each time, leading to inconsistent mesh structures for every angle of attack.
Therefore, the C-domain should offer more reliable or mesh-independent results when
manipulating the angle of attack of the airfoil [37].
The most crucial aspect of airfoil simulations is to ensure an adequate computational
mesh that resolves the boundary layer developed on the airfoil surface. A common practice
is to use prism layers around the airfoil to have high accuracy near the surface. Various
methods exist to control the prism layer structure. The number of prism layers and the
prism layer thickness are defined to control the mesh in the vicinity of the airfoil. The total
thickness is specified as 0.03cmain. When determining the total thickness, an empirical
formula for estimating the boundary layer on the flat surface is utilized [38]. Boundary

Order of convergence
The order of mesh convergence is the trend that the discretization error changes with the
grid size. The relation can be formulated as [39],
ϵ = f(baseSize) −fexact = CbaseSizep
(3.15)
where ϵ is discretization error, f is the flow quantity used as a measure, Cl and Cd values
in this case, baseSize is a measure of grid spacing, C is a fitting constant and p is the
order of convergence. In reality, the expectation of the order of convergence is p = 2 since
the solution is described as "second-order". However, factors such as boundary conditions,
numerical models, and grid often tend to lower this convergence rate.
Indeed, the order of convergence p is determined by the slope of the discretization error
ϵ, which is obtained using a least-square fit. We seek a convergence fit of the form.

Chapter 4
Adjoint Optimization Study
In this chapter, a design optimization study is pursued using the adjoint method. An
in-depth explanation of adjoint method including its mathematical explanation, working
principle in CFD, and limitations is presented. Following the theoretical overview, the
results of the adjoint optimization study are presented. The aim of the study is to maximize
the total lift-to-drag ratio (L/D)total by reshaping the geometry of a given leading-edge
slat.
Gradient-based and gradient-free methods are two main classes of optimization methods.
A review of these is presented in Section 2.3. In this project, a gradient-ascent method is
utilized owing to its efficiency and robustness when dealing with objective functions with
smooth gradients. It is important to remark that even though this method converges fast,
it does not guarantee a global maximum as it might converge to a local maximum.
On top of the optimization method choice, the adjoint approach has been chosen to
calculate the sensitivities of objective function in this project. The main motivations
for this selection are its time efficiency and its greater flexibility for increased design
parameters, due to the fact that computational cost does not increase as a function of
number of design variables. Moreover, the adjoint-based optimization is already available
within the framework of StarCCM+.

Working Principle in CFD
The adjoint optimization study is carried out in Siemens StarCCM+ v2316. The process
consists of several steps that iterate until the local maxima/minima of the cost function,
is found as shown in Figure 4.1. The system can reach the optimum with fewer iterations
by making significant changes to the geometry. However, this approach carries certain
risks, such as crashing simulations, bypassing local minima or maxima, and oscillating
around the optimum, which can result in an infinite loop. This issue can be mitigated by
carefully selecting a damping factor that controls the changes in the geometry and a cost
function that accurately reflects the design requirements.
Figure 4.1 The relation between the cost function and iteration of the process.
The general steps that adjoint optimization method follows are listed as:
• The primary solution is run first as usual regardless of the adjoint method.
• The adjoint solver calculates the sensitivities/gradients based on the chosen cost
function.
• The geometry is morphed, and consequently, the mesh is adjusted based on the
algorithm that maps the calculated sensitivities onto the geometry.
In this project, we use the shape optimization method to solve our optimization
problem.

There are two ways to calculate sensitivities/gradients for a shape optimization in
StarCCM+ environment: either by surface sensitivity of boundaries or by mesh sensitivity
on control points. When morphing is controlled by the point set, the mesh sensitivity
provides more accurate manipulation of geometry based on the cost function compared to
surface sensitivity. However, mesh sensitivity can be costly, as it is highly sensitive to the
mesh domain, any noise within the mesh can lead to optimization failures due to invalid
mesh or non-physical design. If direct manipulation of the surface during optimization is
desired, or an initial assessment of which areas of the geometry should be varied is required,
the surface sensitivity can be a better choice [42]. In this project, the mesh sensitivities
are used to morph the geometry in order to have more accurate geometry manipulation.

Shape sensitivity data is derived by combining the sensitivity of a specific cost function
to mesh vertex position with the sensitivity of these mesh vertex position to control
point positions. This data indicates what degree a change in a control point position will
influence a given cost function.
The calculation of the shape sensitivity relies on the previously obtained adjoint solution.
By incrementally adjusting the displacement boundary vertex positions or control point
positions based on the shape sensitivity, the mesh can be deformed to either increase or
decrease the cost function. This adjustment is achieved by adding or subtracting a small

4.3
Problem Definition and CFD Setup
The optimization problem needs to be implemented into the StarCCM+ framework. No
additional external script is used in this study. First of all, the optimization strategy
we used for the slat geometry is called shape optimization method, as mentioned in the
previous section. The ultimate aim is to re-design the slat geometry to achieve maximum
aerodynamic performance of the multi-element airfoil. The slat configuration 5E has
been chosen as the initial design for adjoint shape optimization, as it already offers the
best aerodynamic performance (see Section 3.5.2.1). The cost function for the problem
is determined as the total lift-to-drag ratio, (L/D)total = (Lmain + Lslat)/(Dmain + Dslat).
The main airfoil boundary remains fixed during the optimization study, only slat boundary
is allowed to change.
In order to shape the slat geometry, 10 control points are assigned around the slat
as shown in Figure 4.3. These control points can be treated as design variables of the
optimization problem. They are associated with the displacement vector and displaced
based on the amount calculated through mesh sensitivities. The control points surrounding
the slat boundary should be positioned far enough from the slat boundary to guarantee
smooth deformations to it, yet close enough to have a significant impact on it. Thus, the
points are located 0.07cslat away from the boundary. If the control points are located
directly on the surface, any displacement can cause to local distortions or irregularities.
This happens because the deformation would be strongly localized, leading abrupt changes
in the surface shape. They are distributed around the surface of the slat with a spacing
of 0.2cslat. StarCCM+ approximates the distance between the control points based on
the target spacing, but the surface of the part often does not match the distance when
the points are equally distributed. Therefore, some points may have larger or smaller
distances than the target spacing to adjacent control points, such as the control point near
the trailing edge. The number of control points is found sufficient for the optimization
process, and they are adequately located. If the number of control points is too high, the
optimization results in many degrees of freedom, making the design impractical due to
very localized changes.
The displacement vector is computed using a first-order steepest descent algorithm
with a factor called "morphing scale factor" or "dampening factor", as in in Equation 4.10.
The morphing scale factor determines how far we want to morph the geometry. Setting
this factor to high value can cause significant changes in the geometry which can lead to a
deteriorated mesh. In practice, this factor is specified between 10−3 and 10−6 [41].

In StarCCM+, the morphing motion transfers the displacement of the set of control

is applied to all mesh vertices in the region, acting like a cloud of points overlaid onto
the mesh domain. For this morphing strategy, a linear fitter is utilized. The linear fitter
employs control point data to establish a least-squares fit for a linear transformation
between the new and original coordinates. During the interpolation process, the motion
resulting from this linear fit is subtracted from the overall motion and subsequently added
back in to determine the final motion. The linear fitter includes 12 modes/degrees of
freedom in total: 3 parameters for translation, 3 parameters for rotation, and 6 parameters
for stretching [42].

4.3.1
Limitations in Setup
One drawback of the current implementation of the adjoint method is that it is only
compatible with specific physical models in the framework of StarCCM+. Before the
adjoint flow analysis, a steady-state solution needs to be obtained using the coupled flow
solver. Hence, the flow solver of the previous CFD setup is changed to the coupled solver.
No other change is required as all of the other physical models (including the turbulence
models) are compatible with the implemented adjoint solver.
The major advantage of use the coupled solver over segregated solver for adjoint flow
analysis is its ability to compute gradients for the systems with many input efficiently. As
discussed earlier, the coupled solver provides a more robust and accurate solution since it
solves the system equations simultaneously in a single system. Conversely, the segregated
flow solver decouples the system equations, causing one variable to be independent of the
others within each iteration. Therefore, segregated flow solvers can encounter convergence
and stability challenges in adjoint shape optimization due to their sequential attitude [43].
The solution using the coupled flow solver needs to be compared to the previous
solution, where the segregated flow solver was used. The solutions of the position 5E at
AoA = 15° are compared. Figure 4.4 shows the convergence of the residuals for both
cases. It shows that the segregated flow solver method converges significantly later than
the coupled flow solver method. The residuals remain under 10−5 after 15,000 iterations
for the segregated flow solver, as seen in Figure 4.4a. On the other hand, the solution
with the coupled solver converges within 1,700 iterations, as shown in Figure 4.4b. The
significant parameters which are measure the design aerodynamically are given for both
solvers in Table 4.1. The total lift coefficient Cl,total is calculated as 1.1% less for the
coupled solver, which is negligibly small. On the other hand, the total drag coefficient
Cd,total is calculated as 3.2% more for the coupled flow solver.Consequently, the total lift
to drag ratio (L/D)total results in 4.2% less compared to the segregated flow solver. The
noticeable difference in the drag coefficient comes from the pressure drag rather than
the skin friction. It indicates that the velocity field are more consistent rather than the
pressure field. It is known that the segregated flow solver use pressure correction whereas,
the coupled flow solver solves the pressure field directly, resulting in a more accurate
solution. Nonetheless, these differences are reasonably acceptable, and both solvers are
good enough to predict the flow. Subsequently, the adjoint method uses the coupled flow
solution as the primary solution.

In the numerical studies, the discretization error is quite well known problem as mentioned
in Section 3.3. Even though, we did a mesh convergence study and prove that the solution
is independent of the grid in Section 3.3.1, deforming the mesh can cause poor mesh, hence
in-accurate results.

Figure 4.17b shows the mesh of the deformed geometry in the second optimization stage.
It is clearly seen that under the trailing edge, shown in a red box, the mesh has deformed
too much and exhibits poor quality in terms of aspect ratio and skewness angle. Briefly,
the aspect ratio is the ratio of the longest length to the shortest length of a cell, where the
ideal value is 1, meaning the element is nearly equilateral. A high aspect ratio indicates
elongated or stretched elements. The skewness angle measures the deviation of the internal
angles of a mesh element from their ideal values, e.g., 90° for quadrilaterals, 60° for
equilateral triangles. It indicates how much a cell is "skewed" or "warped". Low skewness
angle stands for better mesh quality, whereas a high skewness angle means poor mesh
quality. Therefore, in the red box, the high values of aspect ratio and skewness angle are
observed, causing inaccurate calculation of the term ∂q which explains the discrepancy
between the two solution.

However, the study is valuable as it provides insights into how the slat configuration

stages. During the study, the following conclusions were drawn:
• The trailing edge tends to move away from the airfoil until the second optimized
design. In terms of the design variables from Zahle’s study, as shown in Figure 3.15,
the normal distance increases by 20%.
• The gurney flap formation continues until the second optimized design. The Gurney
flap plays an important role in increasing lift by enhancing the flow over the airfoil
through the creation of specific vortex patterns and pressure changes. The vortices
formed by the Gurney flap create a low-pressure region behind the trailing edge,
generating a "pulling" effect on the flow on the suction side. This means that the
pressure of the flow does not need to recover as quickly, resulting in a thinner and
longer-attached boundary layer [44].
• With the combination of increased normal distance and the Gurney flap, the de-
tachment of the flow on the upper surface is delayed. Consequently, a smaller wake
occurs behind the slat, leading to lower drag.
• The results indicate that changes in the slat design do not significantly affect the
performance of the main airfoil. Therefore, most of the improvement in the total
lift-to-drag ratio comes from the enhancements in the slat design.
• The third optimized design is poor in terms of lack of novelty in design parameters
and undesirable characteristics due to very long chord length.

• The relative thickness increased to approximately t/c ≃40% from t/c ≃36% with
the second optimized design and maintains this ratio in subsequent designs. This
suggests that a thicker airfoil yields better results for the slat than a thinner one.
• The chord length of the slat tends to increase at each optimization stage. This

• It is concluded that the aerodynamic performance is highly dependent to the slat

that the specific multi-element configuration is optimized for a single operating condition.
In contrast, multi-point optimization designs investigate thicker airfoils along the inner
part of the blade with different slat profiles. However, such multi-point optimization is

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Future Work
5.1
Conclusion
This thesis aims to maximize the aerodynamic performance of a multi-element airfoil,
which consists of a modified FFA-W3-400 main thick airfoil and a leading-edge slat airfoil
designed by Gaunna and Sorensen [1]. To achieve this, the adjoint shape optimization
is used in a framework of StarCCM+ due to its superior performance for large control
parameters. First, a proper numerical (CFD) setup is developed by examining physics
models and solver types. Subsequently, a mesh convergence study is conducted to obtain
a grid-independent solution, resulting in a mesh domain with 316,821 cells and a near-wall
distance y+ < 0.2. The methodology used in this setup is validated against the study of
Zahle et al. and it is concluded that the methodology of this project aligns with their
numerical setup for both fully turbulent and transitional boundary layer [5].
To define the initial design for the adjoint shape optimization, the parametric study
of Zahle et al. is taken as a reference, and their outputs are also simulated using our
methodology. The study provides an opportunity to validate our methodology once again
with another numerical method, EllipSys 2D, and experimental data. It is found that our
methodology reasonably agrees with EllipSys 2D, showing slightly better predictions for
Cl values but slightly worse predictions for Cd values. Furthermore, our data align with
the experimental results to the same extent as EllipSys 2D, with both numerical methods
failing to predict the flow accurately after stall. The slat configuration 5E is identified as
the best in the reference paper and is chosen as the initial design.
The simulations for adjoint shape optimization are carried out at Re = 2 × 106 for
a fully turbulent boundary layer with TI = 0.3%, and at AoA = 15°. The optimization
study is conducted in three stages due to the necessity for re-meshing the geometry to
prevent inaccuracies from poor mesh quality. Each subsequent stage uses the optimized
shape from the previous stage as the starting geometry for further optimization. The final
optimized design improved the (L/D)total ratio by 16.1% compared to the initial design.
The final optimized design is taken from the last geometry of the second stage. It is

observed that further optimization only stretches the slat, resulting in an excessively high
chord length. Even though it achieves a higher (L/D)total, due to geometrical constraints,
this design is discarded. This signals that the parameters that improves the cost function
are potentially converged.
In the final design, the normal distance between the trailing edge and the main airfoil
increases by 20%. Moreover, a Gurney flap is formed. These changes in the trailing edge
lead to later flow separation and a smaller wake, thereby reducing drag. Additionally, the
relative thickness of the slat increases from t/c ≃36% to t/c ≃40%. This suggests that a
thicker airfoil as a slat yields better results than a thinner one. Moreover, it is evident
that performance is highly dependent on the size of the slat. The chord length tends to
increase at each optimization stage, ultimately reaching 1.3cslat in the final design. In this
context, structural integrity, material cost, and ease of implementation to the main airfoil
should be considered. Lastly, the leading edge angle is increased, resulting in a blunter
leading edge. With all these changes, the mean camber line is also altered.
The final design is simulated across a full span of AoA. It is observed that changes
in the slat design do not significantly affect the performance of the main airfoil. Thus,
most of the improvement in (L/D)total comes from the enhancements in the slat design.
The final design improves aerodynamic performance primarily at higher AoAs up to the
stall (after AoA ≃7.5°). The stall occurs at approximately the same AoA as in the initial
design, indicating that the stall characteristics remain unchanged.
The optimization study shows that the adjoint method is quite powerful tool to change
the geometry of the slat in different aspects. During the study, some problems related to
mesh, convergence, and software are identified. These issues could not be addressed due
to time and software limitations.
In this thesis, it is attempted to expand understanding of aerodynamics of multi-
element airfoils and gain engineering insight into how they can be further improved while
introducing a different perspective in terms of optimization methodology.
5.2
Future Work
During the project, we encountered several problems that could potentially be addressed
with a longer time span. Additionally, this study is quite open to further development
from a multi-point optimization perspective. Some suggestions can be listed as:
• Due to morphing, the mesh deteriorates and produces inaccurate results. Re-meshing
the entry geometry in regular intervals could solve the issue.
• Implementation of additional geometrical constraints could improve convergence of
the optimization.

• Current optimization study can be extended to multi-point study, including the

main airfoil to be morphed as well can lead even further improvement on the aero-
