Abstract
Testing speaker unit for veriﬁcation, needs to be a quick process.
Current
methods are often limited or tedious to conduct. Lumped parameter models
and Thiele-Small parameters are often used as important evaluation parame-
ters. These methods does not incorporate modal breakup in the diaphragm.
Laser scans are available, but this requires more time and is more complicated,
than just measuring impedance. In order to evaluate and improve lumped pa-
rameter modeling, ﬁnite element modeling is incorporated. The analysis uses an
existing loudspeaker as reference. By expanding the lumped parameter model,
it is possible to model the breakup in the diaphragm. This makes it possi-
ble to model the impedance accurately. Deriving the sound pressure from the
lumped parameter model is not eﬀective as detailed information. requires more
advanced circuits.
When comparing the ﬁnite element model to the measurements, the values
were not accurate. The simpliﬁcations made in the model, caused the simu-
lation to deviate from the measurements. Furthermore parameter confusion
increased the complexity. The behavior of the speaker was similar to what is
expected from a speaker unit. The resonances were at the same frequencies as
the measurements, and the overall response exhibited rolloﬀs comparative to
the measurements. This indicates, that by improving the model, a more ac-
curate result can be achieved. Suggested improvements include implementing
an acoustic mesh, to more accurately model the sound pressure. A boundary
element analysis could then be conducted on as well.

lumped parameter modeling of loudspeaker drivers. Most models use a simpliﬁed
model, where it is assumed that the diaphragm moves like a piston. This piston

called the piston mode, as the driver unit moves as a piston in this mode. This
means that the entire loudspeaker moves in phase with the force exerted on the
voice coil. Using a lumped parameter model the piston mode can be modeled using
the circuit seen in Figure 1.2.

The Lumped parameter model is a modeling method, where both electrical and me-
chanical Thiele-Small parameters from the loudspeaker are modeled as an electronic
circuit. In the electrical part, there is no conversion from one domain to another,
as the electrical circuit itself is simply part of the lumped parameter model. The
mechanical part, however, is converted into electrical domain.
This is explained
further in Chapter 2. The model uses the electrical impedance to model the Thiele
small parameters and the sound pressure.
Above fs the diaphragm begins to break up, and subsequent modes have parts of
the diaphragm moving out of phase. This means that the simple lumped parameter
model is not suﬃcient at some point above fs. To improve upon lumped parameter
model, ﬁnite element analysis (FEA) is conducted. In Figure 1.3 an example of
breakup modes can be seen.

When evaluating a ﬁnished product from the assembly line, it is measured us-
ing impedance measurements, and sometimes laser measurements. The measured
impedance is used to ﬁnd the Thiele-Small parameters of the given speaker unit.
These values are subsequently compared to the preliminary speciﬁcations, and if
the parameters are within the given limits, the speaker unit is approved. To enable
testing of several units within a reasonable time frame, the the process needs to
be quick. One problem is that deriving these parameters is not accurate. In many
cases the parameters are derived from a simple lumped parameter model, such as
the investigated in this thesis. As discussed later, there are issues using a simple
lumped parameter model. In [5], several models are evaluated, and each models
uses a diﬀerent ﬁt than the others. Furthermore, the models currently in use have
to be ﬁtted to each type of driver unit. Hence the accuracy of the parameter ﬁts are
uncertain according to Carsten Tinggaard
A few lumped parameter models have looked into incorporating breakup modes
into the model [6]. In [6], most of the modeling and boundary conditions are made
from compromised assumptions and simpliﬁcations, such as constrained movement
in the radial direction at the voice coil. Such simpliﬁcations can be omitted in a
more detailed ﬁnite element model. To limit computation time it must be consid-
ered how detailed the geometry has to be, just as consideration as to which elements
prove to be more eﬃcient has to be taken into account. As ﬁnite element modeling
is often an iterative process, it has to be as eﬃcient as possible.
The coupling between the lumped parameter model and FEM is made to evalu-
ate and improve upon the lumped parameter model. If the ﬁnite element model can

2
Lumped Parameter Models
2.1
Introduction
Lumped parameter models are widely used in acoustical analysis. They are based
on electrical circuits which represent both the electrical and the mechanical parts
of a loudspeaker unit.
They can be further expanded to include the acoustical
domain, such as cavities, cabinets, rooms etc. This thesis only treats the electrical
and mechanical parts of the loudspeaker.
2.2
Tiele-Small parameters
The Thiele small parameters in Table 1.1 are convenient small-signal approximations
of mechanical parameters.
2.2.1
Resonance Frequency
The resonance frequency, fs, is the frequency of the ﬁrst mode in the speaker. This
mode, called the piston mode, can be seen in Figure 1.1b. In the ﬁrst mode, the
diaphragm moves as a piston, and only the outer and inner suspensions move. This
is how a loudspeaker is expected to move, when using low frequency approximations.
2.2.2
Mechanical Q factor
The mechanical Q factor, Qms, is the inverse damping factor of the inner and outer
suspension.
A higher damping factor means more losses.
A metal former often
increases the loss, and thereby decreases Qms, as eddy-currents act as brakes for
the voice coil [5].
2.2.3
Electrical Q factor
The electrical Q factor, Qes, is the inverse of the electrical damping of the loud-
speaker. As current enters the voice coil, a magnetic ﬁeld is imposed around the
coil, and due to the permanent magnets around the coil, the magnetic ﬁeld exerts a
force on the voice, which forces it to move. As the voice coil will always try to reach
equilibrium state, the movement through the magnetic ﬁeld works against the input
current, and creates losses.
2.2.4
Total Q factor
The total Q factor, Qts, is the combined mechanical and electrical Q factors. Qts is
the inverse of the damping ratio.
2.2.5
Force factor
The force factor, Bl, is the magnetic force factor. The force factor determines the
force exerted to the voice coil, at a given current.
The Bl factor is thereby the
relationship between the input current and the voice coil velocity.

2.2.6
Mechanical resistance
The mechanical resistance, Rms, is the mechanical reluctance of movement. Rms is
closely related to Qms.
2.2.7
Moving Mass
The moving mass, Mms, is the moving mass of the speaker unit. This mass includes
the masses of the voice coil, the diaphragm, the former, the dust cap, part of the
suspension and the air load. The air load is the layer of air close to the diaphragm,
that moves in phase with the diaphragm, thus, through air resistance, adding to the
eﬀective mass
2.2.8
Suspension Compliance and equivalent volume
The suspension compliance, Cms, is the inverse stiﬀness of the suspension. The
equivalent volume, Vas, is the volume of air, that the stiﬀness of the suspension
corresponds to, when the speaker is mounted in free air. A stiﬀsuspension increases
fs, but decreases Vas.

Finite Element Modeling
3.1
Introduction
Finite element modeling is used for analysis of various geometries exposed to certain
inﬂuences, such as mechanical force, electromagnetic force, heat, vibration or similar.
The analysis takes any given geometry and creates a mesh consisting of elements.
There are several types of elements, and within each type, further variations exist.
Each element consists of a set number of nodes. The type of element determines
the number of nodes, their constellation towards each other, and lastly the degrees
of freedom in each node. Increasing the number of elements and thus reﬁning the
mesh increases the precision, but also the computation time. The computation time
increases with the number of degrees of freedom, and number of nodes. The quality
of the solution is very dependent on the type of mesh, as well as the element size
and shape. Further more the transition between elements is immediate, meaning
that one node can actually be a part of two diﬀerent materials. This way of describ-
ing structures, causes discontinuities. Hard transitions in the geometry introduces
discontinuities as well, as this introduces a stress singularity in the structure [12].
Finite element analysis relies on numerical solutions. The constellation of nodes in
a mesh, corresponds to degrees of freedom within a matrix,
KD = R
(3.1)
where K is the stiﬀness matrix, D is a vector of unknowns, in this thesis displace-
ments and R is a load vector. The Displacement vector contains the displacements
when solved for. The load vector contains the loads applied to the structure. The
stiﬀness matrix contains the stiﬀness of each element, represented in matrix form
[8]. As the numerical details in the calculations are not within the scope of this
thesis, they will not be shown here.

3.2.1
Damping
Damping is the last parameter used in the material properties. Material damping,
is the dissipation of energy and hence reduction of vibration over time [8]. If no
damping is present, the structure will resonate inﬁnitely, giving rise to velocity peaks
approaching inﬁnity. The simplest damping to model is viscous damping, which is
forces acting to oppose the resonant movements of the structure [8]. Various other
kind of damping models exist, including the previously mentioned internal friction
caused by eddy current. [9] has a suggestion of modeling this using a frequency
dependent resistor, but the approach is a hypothesis backed up by measurements
and simulations. Viscous damping can be implemented using either modal damping
or proportional damping.
Modal damping is damping imposed at the resonance
frequencies, whereas proportional damping damps all frequencies. Both of these are
frequency dependent. Unfortunately there is no simple way of determining damping
of various materials, thus a lot of trial and error is required. Depending on the
usage scenario, diﬀerent implementations of damping can be used. The one used in
this project as an initial guess, is the constant material damping coeﬃcient. As the
name states, this type of damping is material dependent and constant. This rules
out any frequency dependence in the damping. In the material parameters given
from Carsten Tinggaard, the constant material damping coeﬃcient is one of them,
hence the use. One way of determining correct damping is by comparing simulation
results to measurements. The damped resonance frequencies remain close to the
non damped cases, as seen in Equation 3.2.

In ﬁnite element analysis, the mesh is important. If the mesh is too ﬁne, the compu-

Most models use elements of various sizes. According to [8], abrupt size changes may

the shapes regular. The more irregular the structure is, the more diﬃcult it will be
to keep a mesh consisting entirely of regular elements.

3.6
Analysis types
In ﬁnite element, several analysis types are available.
3.6.1
Modal analysis
A modal analysis evaluates natural modes in structures. Boundary conditions needs
to be imposed, to retrieve the modes. A modal analysis can be conducted with-
out imposing boundary conditions, however, as the boundary conditions of the in-
ner and outer suspension aﬀects the modal response when fully constrained, it is
needed. The symmetry condition at the center of the dust cap is needed in the
axisymmetrical model as well. To model the axisymmetry the center point is as-
sumed to be constrained in all translations and rotations, but y-translation. The
modal analysis does not apply a force to the structure, but ﬁnds resonance modes
in the structure. The output of a modal analysis is the modal frequencies as well
as, optionally, mode shapes. The mode shapes is the deformation of the structure
at the modal frequencies.
The mode shapes in ANSYS can be viewed as either
scaled maximum displacements or as animations. The modal analysis can be used
as an initial analysis when conducting a mode-superposition harmonic analysis. The
following harmonic analysis then uses the retrieved modal frequencies as a starting
point for the harmonic analysis, and enables modal damping.
3.6.1.1
Convergence Study
In order to evaluate which of the models and elements are viable, a convergence
study is conducted. As the project concerns modes in the diaphragm, it is the con-
vergence of frequencies in the modal analysis that are taken into account. Other
convergences could be conducted such as stress, displacement or any other param-
eter. The frequencies are converged when increasing the number of elements does
not have signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the modal frequencies. The convergence study is
used to ﬁnd the most viable element size and type. The goal is to have the fewest
elements that still represents the model adequately. This increases eﬃciency and
maintains accuracy.
The convergence study is conducted using the same geometry throughout the in-
vestigation. The geometry is then meshed and a modal analysis is conducted. The
results are saved to a ﬁle for post-processing. Afterwards the mesh is cleared and
a new element size is chosen. The geometry is re-meshed using the new size, the
modal analysis is conducted once more, and the results are saved. This iterative
process is then conducted until convergence is certain. It is important that the test
is started using too large elements, to make sure that the speed is optimized. If
the initial element size is too small, the model is converged, and it is not certain
if the element size can be further increased, to reduced computation time, without
sacriﬁcing accuracy. It is just as important that the test is run for long enough, to
==PAGE:28==
CHAPTER 3. FINITE ELEMENT MODELING
21
make sure that the element size is small enough for the analysis to converge. As
this is done in a APDL script, the process in contained within a loop. The values
are pre-set, thus the margin in both ends need to be large. If the process is either
converged at the beginning, or does not converge at all, the process has to be re-run
using either larger or smaller values. In Figures 3.6 and 3.7 diﬀerent element sizes
can be seen.

computation time is reduced, and modal damping is enabled. The modal damping
damps the structure at the modal frequencies retrieved from the modal analysis.
Each mode can be damped individually if required. The results from the harmonic
analysis depend on the type of load in the model.

5.1
Lumped parameter models
The lumped parameter model is a very simple model that is easy to analyse. Using
basic circuit theory it is possible to analyse advanced models. The analogies from
Section 2.3.1 can be used to model any kind of mechanical system. The combina-
tion of electrical and mechanical systems used in a speaker unit is possible as well.
The model does not depend on any material parameters, but rather Thiele-Small
parameters and mechanical analogies based on the Thiele-Small parameters. This
requires that the parameters are correctly derived, and limits the results to ﬁt the
assumptions made when designing the circuit. The simple circuits such as the one
in Figure 2.2, assume that the diaphragm moves like a piston. As seen in Chapter 4
this is not the case, as already at the second mode at around 61Hz, the diaphragm
begins to break up, see Appendix C for mode shapes. If the model is true to the
real speaker unit, this means that already at the second resonance the two models
begin to deviate. This shows the importance of more advanced modeling of the di-
aphragm. The actual diﬀerence in sound pressure level can be seen in Section 5.3.2.
The issues of using the more advanced model can be seen in Section 5.3.
As the transfer function of the advanced model is much more complex than the
simple model transfer function, it is validated using i circuit simulation program,
ORCAD capture.

5.2
Finite element analysis
The ﬁnite element model has several advantages over the lumped parameter model.
When designing, it is not necessary to determine how many modes need to be in-
cluded, as this is determined in the solution of the model. In theory an unlimited
number of modes can be included as long as the mesh is reﬁned enough, and the
frequency span is large enough.
Increasing the number of modes will of course
increase the number of computations and thus the time required to calculate the
result. When the geometry and material parameters are set, changing the model
parameters such as material parameters, element sizes, type of analysis and more
depending on which investigation is made. If one wishes to change the diaphragm
material from aluminium to paper, plastic or some other material, the only re-
quirement is to know the material parameters. When discussing density, Young’s
modulus and Poisson’s ratio, these are all well deﬁned for pure materials. For com-
posite materials, however the parameters are not straight forward. For instance the
suspension made from rubber is not well deﬁned. Even though it is known that the
material is SBR (Styrene-Butadiene-rubber), the exact parameters vary depending
on how the rubber is made. This means that many materials have a range for the
material elastomer parameters. If a structure consists of ﬁve materials, each having
three material parameters to keep it simple, this gives 15 parameters to change.
When all these have ranges, it is diﬃcult to determine what is right. One way to
determine some of the parameters is to make a known sub analysis. In this thesis
the ﬁrst resonance was used for this analysis. As the ﬁrst resonance is known, this
can be used to determine the stiﬀness of the suspension as explained in Section 3.7.
Unfortunately the rest of the parameters are not that easily found, as they depend
==PAGE:48==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
41
on several parameters instead of just two.
Another diﬃculty regarding ﬁnite element modeling is the geometry. Modeling of-
ten relies on simpliﬁcation of real life scenarios. This holds true for ﬁnite element
modeling. Thus the geometry of the structure is simpliﬁed. The speaker in this
thesis does not have the entire electrical part of the speaker. The mechanical part
of the speaker is simpliﬁed as well. As mentioned in Section 3.3, the transistions
from one material to another is immediate. In the real speaker the materials have
an overlap, as well as a glued part. This increases the complexity of the material
properties. For simpliﬁcation the transitions are left out, except for the dust cap and
voice coil. The reason these are included is the impedance curves seen in Figures
4.9-4.11. The extra peak at 91 Hz is unusually high. Normally this would indicate
a boxed speaker with a vent, or something similar with another impedance. As the
speaker is simulated entirely without box, this is not the case. This is investigated
further in Section 5.3.1.
The boundary conditions are approximated as well. The structure is constrained in
all translations and rotations in both inner and outer suspension. The real speaker
is put in a basket, which is not modeled. As with all materials, the basked is not
inﬁnitely stiﬀ, and has its own resonances. These resonances will have some inﬂu-
ence on the speaker. As the basket is made from thick aluminium, the resonances
are probably well above the resonance frequency. The last great deviation from the
real speaker is the voice coil and former. In real life, the former is a ﬁberglass pipe,
with copper clad aluminium wound around in a coil. For simplicity the former is
modeled as a ﬁberglass pipe coupled with a pipe having a stiﬀness of approximately
ﬁberglass, but using increased thickness and mass, to comply for the coil.
The damping is the last big issue regarding the ﬁnite element modeling. As ex-
plained in Section 3.2.1 there are several types of damping. In reality many types of
damping coexist. An issue using ANSYS is limitations of damping. ANSYS cannot
include both modal and material damping for instance [7]. Where material damping
can actually be measured, modal damping is harder to quantify. Modal damping re-
lies on both material parameters and structural geometry. Thus the modal damping
is hard to determine without experimental modal analyses. The damping is further
investigated in Section 5.3.1
The investigation is conducted using a 2D axisymmetrical model. This set limi-
tations in the results. Axisymmetry is a condition for the modeling technique to bo
valid. As seen in Appendix C, the diaphragm has cooling holes beneath the dust cap.
These holes will create resonances in the diaphragm. This cannot be modeled exact
using an axisymmetrical model. Instead of making a full 3D model, it is possible to
make a 3D slice representing a piece of the diaphragm as seen in Figure 5.3.

5.3.2
Sound pressure level
The sound pressure is one of the most important parameters in a loudspeaker, if
not the most important. If a model correctly is able to predict the sound pressure
a lot of testing can be made easier. As established in Chapter 2, the velocity of
a mass is the same as the voltage over its analogy. If the velocity of the mass of
the speaker is known, the sound pressure can be calculated in the same way as in
Chapter 3. In the simple model, the calculations are easy, as there is only one mass
Cp. Within the limitations mentioned earlier, the piston, represented by Cp, radiates
as a monopole on-axis [2]. In the advanced models, the diaphragm is divided into
cone pieces similar to the ones in the FEM model. In the FEM model, however, the
size of each cone piece is well deﬁned, and thus it is easy to ﬁnd the volume velocity
needed for sound pressure calculations. As the cone pieces are not well deﬁned in
the lumped parameter models, another approach is needed. In the ﬁts above, all
component values are found. If it is assumed that the area of the diaphragm is
directly proportional to the mass, the relative area of each cone piece can be found.
This does not hold entirely true, though, as the moving mass includes the former,
dust cap, and some of the suspension as well. As the model uses the assumption that
the entire moving mass is a piston, it is a fair approximation. The sound pressure
level from the four models is shown in Figure 5.18

5.5
Further Work
In order to make the FEM method viable, the simulations need to comply with
measurements. For this to happen, the model needs improvement. The most simple
improvement of the work is to make sure that the spec sheet and the measured
loudspeaker are the same.
It would be possible to use the parameters from the
measurements instead of a speciﬁcation sheet.
While the model does behave as a speaker unit is expected, some of the assumptions
made does not hold true. The idea of treating each node on the radiating part of
the speaker as a baﬄed monopole, is not valid for high frequencies. Furthermore
the lack of air load appears to have a larger impact than expected. There are sev-
eral ways of solving these problems. One could be to implement an acoustic mesh
around the speaker. This mesh would both simulate the air load as well as the
sound wave propagation in air. This type of model could also include directionality,
which is not included in the model used in this thesis. The results could be used
in the same manner to make a lumped parameter model. This would require some
extra work to include the acoustic mesh, but it could be done without much extra
work. The material parameters, however, would still be hard to ﬁt, as they would
still need several iterations for each material parameter in each material. It is hard
to determine a time frame for determining the correct material parameters, as there
is no easy way of validating the parameters. It would be possible to measure each
material used in the speaker, but this still would not include stiﬀness caused by
geometry or damping. To get the damping correct requires a lot more research.
Another way of improving the model is using a boundary element model on top
of the ﬁninte element model. Boundary element models are a lot like ﬁnite element
models, except for the fact that they only model the boundaries of the geometry.
In [3] an example of boundary element is used. The idea is that from the velocities
of the diaphragm, the complex sound pressure at a given boundary outside the di-
aphragm can be used to estimate the sound pressure anywhere in the far-ﬁeld using
the Rayleigh integral as explained in [3]. This method does not require a mesh in
the far ﬁeld to calculate the pressure, but rather a plane where the complex sound
pressure is known close to the speaker. Using a combination of FEM and BEM,
should results in a more accurate model. It should be noted, though, that for the
diaphragm node velocities to be correct, a mesh representing the air load, as de-
scribed above, is needed. A Matlab program which makes the boundary element
analysis based on diaphragm nodal velocities, ’OpenBEM’ exists. As the lumped
parameter model and FEM analysis rely on the same principles of dividing the di-
aphragm into cone pieces, the boundary element method could be implemented on
top of the lumped parameter model as well. It would be hard to determine the exact
==PAGE:65==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
58
sizes of the diaphragm each link represents, but the approximation, would probably
be better.
Another improvement to the FEM model would be to improve the mesh. Currently
the mesh uses elements of the same type in the entire model. A more intelligent
mesh could probably save some calculations. This would not improve the accuracy
of the model, as it is converged, but rather speed up simulations. As Thorborg
[9] states, it is possible to implement features such as eddy currents into lumped
parameter models. Other improvements could be made as well. The issue is that
by increasing the complexity of the model, ﬁtting becomes harder. If the values of
the parameters are unknown, it is hard to verify if they are correct. even though
the modeled impedance ﬁts nicely, it is not easy to know if all parameters are right.
This is why the SPL simulations are included, to use more than one parameter to
verify the model.
As the impedance measurements only show few modes, it should be possible to
ﬁt a lumped parameter model using fairly few links. When looking at the sound
pressure, the models are not nearly detailed enough. Implementing more advanced
phenomenons such as the eddy currents mentioned above, might improve the sound
radiation as well as seen in [9].
==PAGE:66==
6
Conclusion
In this thesis existing lumped parameter models has been evaluated and improved
upon using ﬁnite element modeling and measurements.
The lumped parameter modeling has been expanded to include diaphragm breakup
modes. The very simplistic approach to modeling enables simulation of mechanical,
acoustic, electric and coupled systems. These systems can be improved upon as
much as desired. A downside of using it for loudspeaker modeling, is that it relies
exclusively on electrical impedance and derived parameters. This means that when
simulating sound pressure, the response is not detailed. When increasing the num-
ber of breakup modes, the sound pressure level detail is increased as well. To get the
parameters for the components used in the lumped parameter model, the complex
impedance is ﬁtted to the output data from a ﬁnite element analysis.
The ﬁnite element modeling proved to be a complicated matter. A great disadvan-
tage is that as a complex modeling strategy it is very dependent on accurate material
parameters. Unfortunately, it is not very simple to estimate all parameters needed
for the model. Composite materials makes it extremely hard to determine many of
the material parameters. The most complex part of the ﬁnite element modeling in
this project is has been determining damping. As there are many types of damping
models and most often several types co-exist in a single structure, it is hard to de-
termine a good way to implement it. It is important to note that though the SPL
and impedance are oﬀcompared to measurements, the behavior of the model is still
realistic, with few exceptions. This means that by improving the model, a more
realistic result could possibly be achieved.
Even though the modeling relied on wrong data for the measurements, both FEM
and lumped parameter modeling had the same starting point.
Introduction of
links or cone pieces into the lumped parameter model, enables the user to simu-
late breakup modes in the diaphragm using simple models. These models can be
ﬁtted to existing data from ﬁnite element analysis as well. In the impedance, the
model is fairly good up to 1000 Hz. The sound pressure, however is not as good and
has much larger deviations already at 100 Hz.
If the FEM model was implemented using BEM and a FEM acoustic mesh, the
model would possibly be able to model measured sound pressure. Fitting the pa-
rameters would still be possible. The sound pressure from the lumped parameter
model, could then be approximated using a simple BEM analysis, by implementing
an external Rayleigh integral, similar to the BEM-FEM combination analysis.

a low-creep rubber surround, and a nomex spider, ensuring long-term reliability under high power conditions. The
