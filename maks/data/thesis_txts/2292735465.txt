ABSTRACT 
 
The rail infrastructure in Denmark is going through great development these years and is being 
continuously upgraded because of the program set by the Ministry of Transport to ensure better 
public transportation in the future.         
In March 2013, the Danish government presented “Togfonden DK”. It is a fund of 28.5 billion 
DKK, which will make it possible to realise the vision of having faster railway connection 
between the largest cities in Denmark. One of the main projects that the fund is financing is the 
One Hour Model.  
Another large infrastructure project, which is currently in process, is the new Fehmarn link 
between Denmark and Germany. With the completion of this link, it is expected that most of the 
freight trains, which today are operating through Jutland will be routed to the new connection. 
Meanwhile, the government has also desired to increase the freight by rail in the future and thus, 
the freight terminal in Hirtshals is currently being upgraded to handle more freight.  
With the Fehmarn link, the number of freight trains that operate through Jutland is not going to 
increase. However, it will still be a reasonable idea to implement a better connection with less 
travel time for the freight trains through Jutland and further to Western Norway from Hirtshals.   
In this report, the Triangle area in East Jutland is analysed, in order to obtain an appropriate 
solution for the rail connection in the area for both passenger and freight trains. The municipalities 
in East Jutland have for many years sought for a better connection to Hamburg, since many good 
opportunities in corporation with the German city is expected. When the One Hour Model is being 
realised, other opportunities can also arise for the Triangle Area. With all the vision and future 
plans that might be realised for the area, a new hub station for passengers near Taulov is proposed. 
Furthermore, a new and shorter alignment with a stop at the station is designed, analysed and 
evaluated in this report.  
The report consists of four main topics: overview, proposal, assessment and evaluation.  
The overview part contains aspects which narrows the focus of the project. Different related 
literature are analysed and discussed to form the prerequisites for the project. Various alternatives 
of alignment and station layout have been roughly analysed and a proposal from each of these are 
selected for further analysis. Figure 1.1 depicts the chosen proposal for the new station layout 
with all the associated lines, which are expected to be in operation.

SAMMENFATNING 
 
Jernbane infrastrukturen i Danmark gennemgår en stor udvikling i disse år og bliver løbende 
opgraderet, idet Transportministeriet har en vision om at forbedre den offentlige transport. 
I marts 2013 præsenterede den danske regering ”Togfonden DK”. Det er en fond på 28,5 mia. 
DKK, som er med til at gøre det muligt at realisere visionen om hurtigere togforbindelser mellem 
de største byer i Danmark. Et af hovedprojekterne som fonden finansierer, er Timemodellen.  
Et andet stort infrastrukturprojekt, som er igangværende, er den nye Femern forbindelse mellem 
Danmark og Tyskland. Med denne nye forbindelse, regnes det med, at en stor del af 
godstogtrafikken, som på nuværende tidspunkt kører gennem Jylland, vil blive overflyttet. 
Derudover, ønsker regeringen også, at der kører flere godstog i fremtiden og derfor er 
godsterminalen i Hirtshals ved at blive udvidet for at kunne håndtere mere gods.  
Med den nye Femern forbindelse, forventes der ikke i fremtiden en øget mængde godstogstrafik 
gennem Jylland. Det vil dog stadig være en fornuftig ide at implementere en forbindelse med 
mindre rejsetid for godstogene gennem Jylland og videre mod det vestlige Norge fra Hirtshals. 
I nærværende rapport vil Trekantområdet i det østlige Jylland analyseres med henblik på at opnå 
en hensigtsmæssig løsning for togforbindelsen i området for både passager- og godstog. 
Kommunerne i Østjylland har i mange år ønsket en bedre forbindelse til Hamborg, eftersom der 
forventes et større samarbejdspotentiale med den tyske by. Derudover kan realiseringen af 
Timemodellen tilmed skabe andre muligheder for Trekantområdet. Med alle de visioner og 
fremtidige planer, der højst sandsynligt vil realiseres for Trekantområdet, vil en ny station med 
knudepunkt nær Taulov blive foreslået. Desuden vil en ny og kortere linjeføring med stop ved 
stationen samtidig blive projekteret, analyseret og evalueret i denne rapport. 
Rapporten består af fire hovedafsnit: Overblik, løsningsforslag, vurdering og evaluering.  
Overbliksafsnittet indeholder aspekter, der indsnævrer fokus i projektet. Forskelligt relateret

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
Following chapter is a description of the objectives for this specific project. In addition a 
delimitation of the project is given, in order to achieve a certain level of quality in the thesis. To 
guide the reader through the report, the structure of the report is defined by use of four main 
topics. 
1.1 Objectives 
The objectives of this project are based on the government’s future plans for passenger and freight 
trains. With the One Hour Model being implemented and the desire for a greater amount of freight 
by rail, the Triangle Area in East Jutland will be scrutinised. Furthermore, proposals for train lines 
will be analysed, since these can improve the overall benefits for the society. 
This project contains following objectives: 
 
Compose and prepare an overall design of a junction in the Triangle Area 
 
Compose a detailed track design of a new line in the Triangle Area to obtain a shorter 
travel time within larger cities in  Jutland and North Germany 
 
Assess the new railway line in a socio-economical context 
 
Evaluate the new infrastructure improvement and recommend a solution for further 
analysis 
In order to complete the objectives, it is necessary to study the relevant standards and regulations 
that the design of the alignment has to fulfil. 
The full learning objectives for this specific project can be viewed in appendix 1.1. 
1.2 Delimitation 
Delimitation of the project is necessary for avoiding an excessive assignment. In general, a lot of 
analyses can be implemented for a project of this size. However, these will not be possible to 
execute within the given time horizon and without compromising the quality of this specific 
project. The focus of the report and the aspects that have not been taken into consideration will 
be described in the following. 
Usually, when implementing an infrastructure project, it is important to examine the area and 
create preliminary studies. Though, this will not be the focus area of the project and will therefore 
only be integrated roughly in the report.  
In relation to the detailed design, the focus will become track design of the new alignment in the 
Triangle Area. This includes the horizontal and vertical alignment for both tracks and creation of 
cross sections, in order to calculate the amount of fill and cut for earth works. Design of cross 
sections depends on drainage conditions, thus an overall investigation of the drainage condition 
for the new alignment is included. Moreover, detailed designs of relocated existing alignments 
are created to ensure interconnection between the existing and new alignment. For the assumed 
alignments and the new station area, it is decided to only create an overall design, as these assist 
defining the location of the new alignment in the station area.

Design of the catenary and signalling systems are not included in the project. The new alignment 
is assumed completed in 2035. Thus, the electrification of the main lines in Denmark, which 
currently are being designed, is assumed completed in 2035. In addition, the ERTMS level 2 is 
assumed installed, when the new alignment is implemented.  
A proposal for the train operation is created. However, as this is not the main focus of the report, 
the level will be limited and delays of trains are not taken into account when doing the

In order to evaluate the project, a cost-benefit analysis will be carried out in an overall level. The 
analysis contains the new alignment and the relocated existing alignment. In general when 
calculating the travel time reduction, the daily variations in relation to disruptions is not taken 
into account. The construction costs can be calculated in different levels, depending on the project 
phase. It is therefore decided to estimate the construction costs that not are part of the focus area 
according to the definition phase. Construction costs which are related to the track design will be 
calculated in both definition phase and program phase. This will form the basis for a short 
discussion about the effect of increasing the detail level by moving from the definition phase to 
the program phase.

1.3.1 Overview 
In the first main topic, an overview of the report will be given. The overview is introduced by a 
problem definition and then followed by a short description of the Triangle Area that is in focus. 
To manage the problem, project assumptions are defined. The assumptions form the basis for 
analysing and comparing various alternative alignments in overall design. One of these proposals 
is chosen for further analysis. The location of the proposed alignment will provide the base layout 
for the station design. Different types of operation in the new station area are examined and one 
is developed to the advised station design. Two scenarios will be defined, one with and one 
without the new suggested alignment. In continuation of this, European and national standards 
and regulations are presented, in order to form the infrastructure.  
1.3.2 Proposal 
This topic is the main focus of the project and consists of descriptions related to the detailed 
design. The first part describes the horizontal design of the proposed alignment and the associated 
alignments, which defines the prerequisites. Vertical alignments, cross sections and drainage 
condition are thereby designed in relation to the new infrastructure proposal. The different parts 
of the detailed design will be introduced by the relevant standards and regulations. In addition, a 
proposal of the future train operation for the two scenarios is given. 
1.3.3 Assessment 
The topics, overview and proposal have formed the basis for the assessment of the project. In 
order to assess the project, a socio-economic analysis is carried out by a cost-benefit analysis. An 
introduction of the analysis will be given by defining methods used in the cost-benefit analysis. 
Aspects as consumer surplus, construction costs, maintenance and renewal costs, operating costs 
and scrap value are estimated in the analysis. The outcome of the cost-benefit analysis will be 
discussed and in addition a sensitivity analysis is conducted in order to estimate the uncertainty 
of the results. 
1.3.4 Evaluation 
Aspects which are not integrated in the cost-benefit analysis will be evaluated in a discussion. 
Moreover, the discussion will contain variations of the proposed alignment for evaluation. The 
discussion leads to a recommendation for further analysis. Finally a conclusion of the entire 
project will be given.  
It is recommended to read the report in the given order. This will ensure the best understanding 
of the project. Lists of all the references can be found in the end of the report and appendixes and 
drawings are placed separately from the report.

2 DEFINITION OF THE PROBLEM 
 
The focus of this railway project is to design a new alignment in the Triangle Area. According to 
the One Hour Model new alignments between Funen and Jutland are planned for passenger 
transport in the future. On the contrary, there are no plans for a new alignment between East 
Jutland and south/west of Kolding.   
For the moment the politicians have a vision to increase freight by rail in Denmark, which has 
resulted in an improved terminal in Hirtshals. This allows a better freight train connection between 
Hamburg and Western Norway. Thus, an improved infrastructure that reduces the overall travel 
time for freight trains can be beneficial. 
This chapter will be introduced by a description of the Triangle Area, and the motives for 
designing a new railway infrastructure here. The reasons for implementing a new alignment for 
passenger trains and freight transport by rail are different; therefore it will be divided into these 
two aspects. The central issues of passenger trains and freight transport by rail will define and 
form the basis for the project. Assumptions for the project will follow as a result of the defined 
key issues. Finally, different conditions related to environmental and geotechnical issues in the 
Triangle Area are described.  
2.1 The Triangle Area 
The Triangle Area consists of six municipalities – Billund, Kolding, Fredericia, Middelfart, Vejle 
and Vejen. The area is according to [Hovedstruktur, 2004] the third most populated area in 
Denmark, which leads to considerable activity. The Triangle Area is located central in Denmark 
and is therefore a junction for traffic.  
The railway infrastructure in the area is quite curvy, which originates from a time when many 
small towns wanted to have a station stop on the line [Jernbanen, 1997].

2.3 Freight by rail 
Freight by rail is not widely used in Denmark yet, but the political intention is to reduce the 
number of trucks and increase the number of freight trains in the coming years [Mere gods, 2012], 
[Gods fra Vej, 2015]. Freight trains can be more efficient and environment friendly for long 
distances than trucks [Bane Gods, 2015]. The reason for this is the great potential quantity that 
can be transported and the small number of employees used compared to trucks. In opposition 
trucks can drive directly from origin to destination, which can be advantageous for short distance 
transportation.  
Figure 2.5 depicts the freight development by rail for transit, import and export in Denmark 
between 2001 and 2013. It is noticeable that during these years, transit freight has increased, while 
import and export have decreased. As it has been stated before this freight development by rail is 
in accordance with the high efficiency for long distance, whereas short distance transportation is 
less advantageous by rail.

2.4 Central issues in the project

3.4 Reduction of the proposals  
When reducing the number of proposals, the junction of the different alignments will have great 
impact. As it has been mentioned, the junction will form the basis for a passenger station. A 
compact station with low costs is preferred, therefore it is required to analyse the location of the 
different alignments in the four proposals. 
Proposal 1 and proposal 2 are quite similar, so it is decided to choose one of them for further 
development. Proposal 1 is chosen because of the location of the suggested alignment between 
Odense and Aarhus. In proposal 1 it is not necessary to design a new alignment when 
implementing a station in the intersection point. The alignment between Odense and Aarhus is 
out of the competences of the project. For this reason, it has been assumed that it is possible to 
select the most appropriate location of the alignment between Odense and Aarhus. 
Proposal 3 is deselected, as the proposed alignment does not intersect with the other alignments 
in a junction that can define the possible station area for passengers. Besides it, this alignment has 
also disadvantages in relation to freight operation. The proposed alignment will result in a new 
costly freight terminal, in case travel time reductions for freight trains are desired. Operating 
freight along the existing alignment will not improve the travel time in comparison with the 
current situation. 
Finally, proposal 4 is chosen, as it is possible to create a junction between the different alignments, 
which defines a shared passenger station. Travel time for passenger trains will be reduced in a 
large scale. Furthermore, freight trains will gain the same travel time reduction as passenger trains 
if it is operates on the new alignment. This will however imply the construction of a new costly 
freight terminal. As in proposal 3 operating freight on the existing line will not improve the travel 
time for freight trains in comparison with the current situation. Main aspects as expected 
expropriation, geotechnical conditions, relics and nature areas can be considerable drawbacks for 
this proposal.  
Further analysis of proposal 1 and proposal 4 are decided.

4 OVERALL DESIGN OF THE STATION  
 
This chapter will contain the overall design of the station, which is placed in the junction of the 
different alignments. Two different types of operation will be analysed in the station area: line 
operation and direction operation. This is decided, because during the process of developing an 
overall design, it appeared that many of the different proposals are a variation of these two types 
of operation. Comparison of these two types will result in a design, which will be developed 
further. 
The station in the intersection area between the different alignments will be named Taulov East 
Station and the terminal for freight located in Taulov will be named Taulov Freight Terminal. It 
is important to consider jointly both stations, when defining an overall design for the station area, 
since the two stations can affect each other.   
In this project conflicts in the station area has been reduced by an out-of-level operation. This 
type of operation is designed with platforms located in different elevations and an amount of 
flyovers. However, the out-of-level operation will result in high construction costs. Determination 
of out-of-level or in-level will therefore become a trade-off between construction costs and 
conflicts.  
Furthermore, following actions have been performed as prerequisites for the two types of 
operation: 
 
The existing tracks are kept as original as possible and the length of the flyovers has been 
shortened in order to reduce the construction costs 
 
The operation of freight trains is avoided on the flyovers in order to decrease the risk of 
stop of heavy and relatively slow freight trains

From January the 1st of 2015 all projects in the Danish railway network have to comply with the 
newest TSIs according to the Danish Transport Authority (DTA). However depending on the size 
and the implementation date of the project, exceptions can be made [TSI, DTA]. 
In addition to the TSIs, national norms and standards must be complied, which are developed in 
Denmark by Rail Net Denmark (RNDK). Some of the most important Danish norms and standards 
are “Sporregler, 1987” from 1987 and “Banenormer” from 1990s. They are both revised 
continuously and it is therefore important to ensure that the project comply with the newest 
version. 
“Sporregler, 1987” contains the technical part of the infrastructure, such as geometry regulations 
and requirements of the alignments, specifications for rail constructions and railway maintenance 
instructions.  
The geometry requirements of the alignments are divided into three levels, which for all levels 
ensure that safety is fulfilled [Sporregler 1987, 2015]:  
 
Requested value: Covers new lines and greater reconstructions. The use of the requested 
value will minimise the wear and ensure outstanding driving comfort. 
 
 
Standard value: Usually covers new lines, reconstructions and maintenance. Using the 
standard value will ensure good driving comfort and less wear. The standard regulation 
compromises comfort and wear, and is the most used regulation. 
 
 
Exceptional value: Only cover projects where the other two levels are impossible to 
implement. The increasingly high level of expenditures by using the standard regulations 
can also be a reason for making use of this value.

7.1.2 Transition curves 
Transition curves are designed to create smooth changes between two curves or between a straight 
line and a curve. Many different types of transition curves can be implemented. Clothoids and 4-
degree parables are examples of the transition curves, which are well applied. 
Clothoid and 4-degree parable both contain change of cant deficiency and change of cant as a 
function of time. However, the difference is that the parameters in a clotoid are constant linear. 
This results in a suddenly change at the endpoints of the clothoid and creates some discomfort for 
the passengers. Discomfort can especially appear for shorter transition curves, since the 
passengers will mostly get affected by the sudden changes of cant deficiency. Compared to the 
clothoid, the 4-degree parable is S-shaped and contributes good comfort for the passengers. 
The clothoid is mostly used as the transition curve, despite the reduced comfort. It is used due to 
the simplicity in control and implementation. The 4-degree parable can be more efficient for very 
short transition curves [Sporregler 1987, 2015].

7.2 Regulations related to turnouts 
The function of a turnout is to assemble or disassemble tracks. Many different types of turnouts 
are available and they often consist of a main track and a diverging track. It is desirable to 
implement the main track as straight track and the diverging track as an out warding curve, due

7.2.1 Speeds above 130 km/h along the diverging track 
Regulation for turnouts with an allowed speed above 130 km/h along the diverging track does not 
exist in Denmark. The well-known diverging tracks in Denmark are mainly constructed with a 
curve and a straight track in each end. Increasing the speed in this type of diverging track will 
create discomfort.  
Turnouts that are designed for a speed above 130 km/h are often larger than the traditional Danish 
turnouts, in order to ensure good comfort for the passengers. Many turnouts from different 
countries with a speed above 130 km/h along the diverging track are constructed with an 
additional transition curve to increase the comfort. 
Figure 7.3 shows an example of a crossover with a speed of 160 km/h along the diverging tracks. 
As seen in the figure, the two turnouts contain a spiral segment and a circle. In addition, another 
spiral is implemented at the rear end of the circles, in order to reduce discomfort for the 
passengers. At the start of the turnout, the spiral is connected with a radius of 10,000 m, but with 
no curve length. The reason for this is to prevent the frog core breaking. If the radius was infinite 
as the other spiral segment instead of 10,000 m, the construction part will be so thin, that the frog 
core will easily break [Schwartz M., 2015].

7.3.1 Types of platforms 
The most commonly used platforms are side platforms and 
island platforms. The two types contribute to different 
purposes e.g. train operation. 
For the side platforms, the tracks are located at one side of the 
platform, as seen in figure 7.5. This kind of platform creates 
less confusion for the passengers, since only one track is 
related to a specific platform. This type is often installed at 
smaller stations or at areas, where the physical space is limited. 
Installation of an island platform requires two tracks. Island 
platforms are located in-between the tracks, as seen in figure 
7.6. The advantage is the possibility of rapid transfers between 
different trains at the same platform.

8.1 Regulations related to vertical alignment 
A vertical alignment of the track is the view on a vertical plan along the alignment. It is important 
to examine the existing terrain, since the design of the vertical alignment in relation to the existing 
terrain can have huge impacts on the costs.  
The vertical alignments consist of straight lines with a fixed constant gradient and curves with a 
constant radius. The vertical curves are mentioned as transition curves, because it refers to the 
transition between the two straight lines with different gradients [BN2-93-1, 2010]. The transition 
curve ensures a smooth transition and therefore improved comfort for the passengers.

8.1.2 Vertical transition curve  
In addition to the advantages of smooth transition and increased comfort, the vertical transition 
curves also contribute to following [Modern Railway Track, 2014]: 
 
Avoiding derailment by decreasing the risk of wheels climbing the rail. The vertical 
acceleration in peak curves are kept low by implementing transition curves, which reduce 
the unevenly distribution from the load on the vehicle springs. 
 
Improving comfort, vision and track alignment by use of long curves. Too long curves 
are very complicated to construct or maintain though, especially on new tracks, because 
of subsidence.

8.2 Regulations related to structure gauge

safely on the tracks without risk of collision with surrounding objects as e.g. bridges, signals, 
platforms etc. The structure gauge defines the minimum distances between the track and the fixed 
object.

electrifications will be explained as they can affect the design of the railway.

9.1 Regulations related to cross sections 
In following chapter the classical cross 
section which is commonly used in 
Denmark will be described. In figure 
9.1 the cross section is depicted. The 
cross section is named “ballasted track” 
and contains rails, which are fastened 
to the sleepers. The sleepers are located 
in the ballast and the sub-ballast is 
placed underneath. The subgrade is 
positioned directly under the sub-
ballast. In order to drain the track, a 
slope is implemented at the surface of 
the subgrade. 
The ballast bed consists of a layer of 
loose, coarse-grained material. The 
material 
can 
absorb 
considerable 
compressive stresses, because of the 
internal friction between the grains, 
which arise from the rolling stock. In 
order to ensure that the subgrade is loaded as uniformly as possible, the thickness of the ballast 
bed will typically be 25 to 30 cm. The thickness is measured from the bottom of the sleeper. 
A contaminated ballast bed can hinder drainage of water from the ballast bed. This can cause 
reduced shear resistance and freezing during frost season. Thus, contaminated ballast beds must 
be avoided by setting requirements for ballast material such as hardness, wear resistance and good 
particle size distribution. Requirements according to the distribution of the particle size are 
important, because the pore holes between the grained materials in the ballast must be a specific 
size to ensure satisfactory drainage. The particles for the ballast must be cubic and contain sharp 
edges to increase the overall volume of the pore holes [Modern Railway Track, 2014].

9.2.3 Piped ditches 
A system, where the water from the ditch leads towards a closed pipe with a drain located parallel 
is called piped ditch. Piped ditch, are as ditches, primarily used for draining of surface water. The 
solution with piped ditches is mainly used in areas, where there is a lack of physical space to 
construct ditches. Piped ditches are often cheaper than drain pipes, but more expensive than open 
ditches. 
9.3 Design of drainage systems 
Design of drainage systems is an important task, when constructing railways. The construction 
and maintenance costs can vary on a large scale depending on the chosen solution.

flow from the drainage system to a rain water basin in the project area. Hereafter, the water will 
be lead to the recipient, which is outside of the project area. When designing a drainage system, 
it is necessary to define the final recipient of the water, since different requirements can occur 
depending on the recipients. Normally in these kinds of projects, the recipients are owned by the 
municipality. In the beginning of the project period, it is therefore necessary to make an agreement 
to ensure the requirements from the municipality will be met.

9.4 Design of cross sections 
In many infrastructure projects, one of the construction costs which can be optimised according 
to the quantity is the earth works. In this project, it has been decided to focus on this area. When 
designing the vertical and horizontal alignments, a balance of soil has been desired. The condition 
of the soil is unknown and therefore excess of soil has been preferred in case of an unbalance. In 
order to define the quantity of the earth works, Bentley Rail Track has been used. In this program, 
a large number of different templates of the cross sections has been designed. In order to achieve 
knowledge in this area, the templates have been created from scratch. Each template belongs to a 
specific interval on the alignments. Different templates can be necessary if variation of following 
occurs: 
 
Number of tracks 
 
Track distances 
 
Surroundings such as platforms 
 
Ballasted track or bridge/flyover 
 
Drainage conditions 
For simplicity the BN2 requirements for cross section have been applied according to new 
constructed railway for all cross sections. In connection to the relocated tracks, this will be on the 
safe side, as the requirements for the reconstruction is less limited according to BN2. All the cross 
sections have been designed according to the requirements for new lines with a speed of 
160<V≤200 km/h. These requirements include an axle load up to 22.5 tons, which include the 
freight trains in this project.

as well as contingency operation will be defined.

11 SOCIO-ECONOMIC ANALYSIS 
 
The socio-economic analysis (SEA) seeks to identify the most feasible alternative/project. 
Essentially, a SEA produces decision support, which ultimately creates a set of means for 
decision-makers and politicians to achieve the best possible information. Thus, the degree of 
political acceptance of a project is higher if the project has been carried out with a SEA.      
A SEA within infrastructure can be carried out in different levels such as regional, national, 
European or global. The politicians decide which projects to construct in the near future by 
support from the SEA, especially for larger levels. Alternatives for a project chosen by e.g. the 
EU commission or the national government will be drafted and compared in order to obtain the 
most socio-economic benefits. However, it is not certain that the most beneficial alternative will 
be selected. Projects with a small or even negative level of socio-economic return can be selected 
if for example other important aspects for the society outweigh the lack of economic advantages. 
Objectivity will be required in this circumstance. It is a requirement in Denmark and Europe to 
make a SEA for infrastructure issues of a certain size.  
A SEA will be created in this main chapter in order to analyse and evaluate if the project will be 
socio-economic beneficial. 
11.1 Cost-benefit analysis  
The preferred tool for carrying out a SEA is the cost-benefit analysis (CBA). The CBA considers 
difference in costs and benefits for a specific project and compare them with each other.  
Transport projects contain impacts, which can be different depending on the purpose and target.

11.1.1 Important factors 
The necessary factors for a CBA contain several different values. These values including their 
meanings and ranges are described here. 
Discount rate 
The discount rate is used to convert the value of money between two different points in time. The 
reason for this is that, theoretically, the money available for a project could be placed into a bank 
account, where it would increase due to the interest rate. In order to be able to compare incomes 
and costs of different points of time, the discount rate is used to make those values comparable. 
In Denmark the discount rates have the following values determined by the Danish Ministry of 
Finance in February 2014: 
 
4 % for year 0-35 
 
3 % for year 36-70 
 
2 % after year 70  
Across Europe the discount rates vary and are also frequently changed. 
Calculation period 
It is important to define the calculation period, price level year and opening year. For larger 
projects within Europe, the calculation period is often 50 years. A scrap value is also defined and 
the value is determined as the discounted cost of the construction at the end of the calculation 
period. This assumes that the infrastructure is maintained properly and the infrastructure is 
thereby expected to be in the similar condition at the end of the calculation period as if it was 
new. 
Scenarios 
A basic scenario, scenario 0, is defined, where the suggested improvement is not integrated, but 
scenario 0 can differ from the current situation. In contrast to scenario 0, the suggested 
improvement is integrated in scenario 1. In CBA, the costs and benefits between the two scenarios 
are calculated and different formulas are applied in order to define the performance level of the 
project.   
Net present value 
The net present value (NPV) determines the level of economic profit of a project. The overall 
costs and benefits during the expected lifetime of the project are combined in the NPV. The NPV 
is calculated for a specific calculation year and is defined as:

11.3 Construction cost  
The finance of railway projects in Denmark are often decided by the Danish parliament and it is 
important to manage the economy, in order to define the quantity of funding. The parliament had 
earlier experienced that the actual construction costs exceeded the planned costs in many projects, 
since unexpected costs and delays occurred. Consequently, excessing construction costs increases 
the quantity for funding, which for the parliament was inappropriate.      
The Danish Transport Authority, DTA has as a response, created a model for the financial 
management of railway projects with a total funding of minimum 50 million DKK [NAB, 2010]. 
The financial model seeks to align the financial status with the economical expectations. Hence, 
it enables the authority to follow the progress in a certain phase of the project and react if 
necessary. According to the model there are five consecutive phases within a railway project and 
these are depicted in figure 11.9 [NAB, 2010].  
In the first phase of the project only the overall infrastructure issue is presented. During 
preliminary studies different alternatives are produced and defined. According to the DTA, 
estimation of the construction costs in this phase implies additional 50 % for the unpredictable 
costs that might occur in this phase. 
The alternatives are weighted and the most suitable alternative is chosen. Phase 2, program phase, 
imply the chosen alternative for further analyses. The detail level for the estimation of the costs 
increases in this phase and therefore the uncertainty is expected to decrease. The additional 
percentage value decreases to 30 %. The 10 % are related to an anchor budget and 20 % for a 
central civil reserve. The anchor budget equals 110 % of the estimated construction cost in the 
program phase and defines the costs that the project owner e.g. RNDK expects. In some cases, an 
additional post calculation factor is applied in the budget for uncertainty reduction of 
underestimation of quantities.   
The last three phases of the model contain the designing, execution and completion of the chosen 
alternative. Projects entering phase 3 will usually be finalized despite exceeding the budget. 
Additional percentages due to uncertainty will not be necessary in these phases, because of the 
high accuracy.

11.6 Social and environmental impacts 
The railway sector has not traditionally dedicated major efforts to calculate the social and 
environmental impacts. One possible explanation could be the complexity of estimating these 
impacts. As many of the social and environmental impacts are difficult to predict, it is decided 
not to implement them in this CBA. Even though, it is expected to have high importance in the 
future to integrate these impacts. An article, [Essen H. v., 2008] have discussed some of the 
aspects related to environmental impacts in freight transportation. The article is used as an 
inspiration for the different impacts of the railway project, which is briefly described in the 
following. 
11.6.1 Noise 
Traffic noise is documented as a source of harmful effects such as sleep disorders, high blood 
pressure and heart attack. Therefore, it is important to examine the noise level related to the 
railways activities. In this project, 20 freight trains and approximately 200 passenger trains per 
direction during a week are relocated from the existing to the new alignment. This will result in 
reduction of noise in the urban area and increase in the rural area, so therefore it will affect less 
people. 
The number of people affected by noise according to the article [Miedema & Oudshoorn, 2001] 
feels more affected by transport on roads than rails. The reason can be due to the systematic in 
the railway operation, where people can get used to the frequently noise. In case of attracting 
some of the car drivers towards the train it would make it possible to decrease the number of 
affected people.  
11.6.2 Accidents 
The number of accidents is in general assumed to have a much lower value for railways in 
comparison with private vehicles. However while an accident appear in relation to railway the 
number of involved people are expected to be higher than for private vehicles. In any case 
accidents can be difficult to predict in this sector, since different aspects can affect the number 
and level of accidents. Various measures can be implemented to reduce the risk of accidents. 
Different suggestions for avoiding accidents risk could be: 
 
Regularly control of devices along the alignment 
 
Regularly control of the equipment related to the trains 
 
Automatic brake control in case of speed exceed 
 
Passenger control at larger stations to avoid terror 
 
Operation stop of trains in bad weather conditions 
11.6.3 Pollution 
Air pollution from transport affects many aspects such as people, air, soil and water. In the project 
electric freight and passenger trains are implemented. Electric trains are not expected to produce 
a large amount of pollution in comparison with diesel-powered trains. Even though, it is important 
to remember the fact that the electricity appears from a source, which produce pollution and

pollution is divided into two parts: local pollution and regional and global pollution. 
Local air pollution 
Urban areas are higher affected by local air pollution than rural areas, due to the density of the

project economically beneficial seems unrealistic. To achieve a beneficial project, it is required 
for the construction costs to be reduced to ¼. If the construction costs are not possible to decrease,

economically beneficial, even if the costs are decreased or/and the consumer surplus is increased, 
since the result will not be able to reach the required minimum.

12 DISCUSSION 
 
The evaluation of the cost-benefit analysis of the new suggested alignment has resulted in a non-
beneficial solution. Furthermore, the sensitivity analysis showed that large effort will be required 
in order to reach a beneficial project. However, with the collected and processed data, this is not 
assumed to be realistic. In the following it will be discussed how to increase the benefits or/and 
decrease the costs in a broader view taking into account additional aspects.    
Benefits increase 
In chapter 2 “Definition of the problem” the share between trips by train and car crossing the 
border was discussed. In the chapter it was stated that upgrading the train connection will result 
in a change in transport mode from cars towards trains. Although this is not estimated in the 
project, a benefit related to this is expected for the society. As an example of benefit, reduction 
of congestion on affected roads, can be mentioned, which results in travel time decrease for the 
remaining part of the car drivers, who are not using the future improved train connection. 
Additional benefits can also arise if the politicians choose to increase the number of freight trains 
operating in 2035, as the improved freight terminal conditions in Hirtshals will support. As 
mentioned in chapter 10 “Train operation” additional room for freight trains at night or at off-
peak hours is expected to be available. This must be analysed further, since capacity restrictions 
along the route can limit the frequency of the trains. Operation of freight trains in Northern 
Germany will also have a great influence on timetabling and frequency in Jutland. In case the 
increased number of operating freight trains along the new suggested alignment is possible, larger 
benefits for the CBA are expected. Nevertheless, the increase in maintenance costs related to the 
possible increase of operating trains could result in a small and limited decrease of benefit. 
In this project, the reduction of noise and local, regional and global pollution is not quantified and 
monetized, but in general these aspects are expected to create small benefits to the overall system. 
When relocating some of the operating trains from urban areas in scenario 0 to rural areas in 
scenario 1, pollution and noise will be moved equally. The reduction of pollution in scenario 1 in 
comparison with scenario 0 will correspond to the difference in energy consumption for trains 
operating according to the scenarios. However, the trains are assumed electric and will therefore 
not have large local pollution effects. In any event in case of estimating the regional and global 
pollution, the energy source of the electric trains must also be considered.    
Given all the benefits described and taken them into account when creating the cost-benefit 
analysis, it is still not expected to be an economically beneficial project. Thus, the chosen solution 
for the alignment is not recommended to be implemented. However some variation of the 
alignment that can reduce the costs will therefore be scrutinised in the following. 
Cost reduction 
The analysis of the project resulted in high construction costs. In relation to this, the capacity on 
the new alignment does not appear as critical. This means that two trains along the tracks per hour 
in both directions can be operating on a shared single track instead of double track.  
One solution can be to continue operating out-of-level, which will keep a high capacity as 
depicted in figure 12.1. The solution requires two additional turnouts and only one track is 
necessary in large areas in contrast to the new alignment in scenario 1. The two flyovers will in

determine the best possible solution. 
In many railway projects actions in one area can affect other areas as it could happen in this 
project outside the Triangle Area. The area north of Vejle Fjord is e.g. important to examine,

14 CONCLUSION 
 
In this specific project a suggestion for a new alignment in the Triangle Area in East Jutland has 
been composed, analysed and evaluated. The project area and the new alignment are shown in 
figure 14.1. 
Figure 14.1, Overview of the project area and the new alignment 
The new alignment contains a double track and is located in the rural area between Pjedsted and 
Taulov in the Triangle Area. The new alignment interconnects with other alignments in a junction 
east of Taulov. Hence, Taulov East Station is constructed in the junction for the different 
alignments and is expected to become a future hub station in 2035.  
The new alignment through Taulov East Station is constructed to achieve a connection with 
reduced travel time in the Triangle Area. The alignment will be constructed apart from Fredericia 
and it will relocate some of the operating trains proceeding through Fredericia today. This can be 
beneficial for the city as it will reduce noise and pollution for the inhabitants of Fredericia. 
However, there is also a drawback for the inhabitants in the city, since the train connections will 
become poorer by implementing the new alignment. In case they prefer to travel with one of the 
new lines along the new alignment, a transfer at Taulov East Station is required. Comparing with 
the existing alignment passing Fredericia, a travel time reduction of approximately five minutes 
is estimated along the new alignment for an ET passenger train and a travel time reduction of 
approximately three minutes for a freight train. 
Construction of the new 6.7 km double track and the necessary adjustments associated with the 
relocation of the existing ones are estimated to 1.30 billion DKK in 2035-values. The high value 
of the construction costs is mainly due to the two flyovers which have been constructed in order 
to achieve operation with less conflict.
