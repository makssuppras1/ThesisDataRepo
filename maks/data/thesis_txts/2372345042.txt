Abstract 
This study was conducted during a research internship at SINTEF Materials and Chemistry, in 
Trondheim, as a part of research project INNO-Drill, which goal is to develop more efficient and cost 
effective tools for deep geothermal drilling in hard rock formations. One of the main problem in 
drilling a well in deep and hard rock formations is the decrease of rate of penetration (ROP) with 
depth. Some well-identified reasons for this phenomenon are (i) a limited depth of cut due to the 
increase in rock strength with the confinement associated to depth, and (ii) poor cleaning of the rock 
debris due to the borehole fluid column pressure and associated "chip hold down effect" (Gohin, 
2013). In addition, hard rock formations are generally more abrasive and prone to a high wear rate of 
the cutters, which leads to more frequent tool replacement and, in turn, decreases the drilling 
efficiency. Therefore, the tool-rock interaction, as the starting point of drilling efficiency decay, is an 
important research topic that can support the improvement of drilling operations. According to 
drilling reports (Wittig et al., 2015; Avila et al., 2013) the ROP in deep wells can be improved by using 
percussive drilling instead of rotary drilling, which is more commonly used in oil and gas wells.  
In this study, finite element analysis of single cutter tests (representative of fixed cutter rotary 
drilling) and indentation of the drill bit (representative of percussive drilling) were carried out with 
two types of rocks: Vosges sandstone and Kuru granite. The results were compared in terms of 
drilling efficiency by means of mechanical specific energy (MSE), defined as the unit of energy per 
removed volume. Additionally, cutting experiment on Kuru granite were performed to validate 
numerical predictions. It was found that the MSE increases (i.e. the efficiency decreases) with 
increasing confining pressure, for both rotary and percussive drilling, and both rock materials 
(sandstone and granite). Besides, the higher MSE were obtained with cutting (i.e. rotary drilling) than 
indentation (i.e. percussive drilling), confirming the field observation of higher ROP with percussive 
drilling. 
Note: a week before the thesis was delivered, the computer used to perform the finite element 
analysis unfortunately crashed, and all the numerical data were lost. It was therefore not possible to 
post-process all the numerical simulations as wished. In the thesis, the unknown data that could not 
be recovered are written in red. It is planned to pursue this study in the project INNO-Drill, so any 
future work on the cutting issue will enable to fulfil these missing data.

1 Introduction 
As can be seen in Fig. 1, the part of energy produced from non-renewables is currently far more 
important than the energy stemming from renewable sources.  However, it can be seen that within 
last 25 years, the amount of energy produced by renewables has significantly increased, and must 
continue to increase its share of the total energy demand in the world for the world to stay below 
the 1.5oC degree scenario agreed upon in Paris (Hulme, 2015).  
With an increasing demand of renewable energy, geothermal energy is recognized as a potentially 
vast and stable energy source - independent of weather conditions (IEA, 2011). The main drawback 
for large geothermal energy use for electricity production is the high capital cost of deep drilling. In 
fact, well cost in this process may reach up to 80% of the CAPEX (Gohin, 2016). The INNO-Drill 
research project (granted from the Research Council of Norway for the period 2016-2019 (grant no. 
NFR254984) aims to address this through building knowledge to support innovation and enable cost-
effective drilling solutions in deep and hard rock formations. 
Many factors affect the drilling process efficiency, among which tool wear, rock breaking and debris 
transport are of primary importance. The drilling efficiency depends on the type of drilling 
technology being used. Two main types of drilling processes are rotary and percussive drilling. Rotary 
drilling is the most common method used in oil and gas exploration, where the formations are more 
generally soft sedimentary rocks. Percussive drilling is more frequently used in the mining industry, 
where the rock has a higher breaking strength and is more brittle. For geothermal applications, and 
in particular for Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS), the formations encountered are generally hard 
and brittle. To bring the cost down and efficiency up on deep drilling in hard rocks, a combined 
percussive and rotary drilling process is foreseen to be needed (Ranneberg et al., 2016). To achieve 
this, percussive drilling must be enabled as a generally applicable technology for deeper wells, and 
the performance of rotary drilling must be improved for harder rocks. The development of innovative 
solutions will require further fundamental understanding of the rock breaking process under impact 
and shear loading. This is the topic of this Master thesis.

1.1 Rotary drilling and cutting 
The most commonly used method to create a well is through rotary drilling. With this method, the 
drill bit is rotated at a certain speed in order to cut and progress through the rock formation (Azar, 
2007).  The rotation of the drill bit is achieved through a rotary table or top drive motor. Rotary 
drilling bits are usually classified according to their design as either drag bits or rolling cutter bits. 
Drag bits, used in petroleum industry since 19th century, have cutter blades fixed to the body of the 
bit and rotate as a unit with the drill string as depicted in Fig. 2a. Rolling cutter bits have at least two 
cones (generally three) that rotate around the axis of the cone, when the drill string rotates (Fig. 2b).  
(Bourgoyne et al., 1985). As presented in Fig. 2, cutting mechanism differs in both cases. Drag bits 
remove rock by shearing on the rock surface, while roller cone bits remove rock by crushing it. 
(a) 
(b) 
Fig. 2 Mechanism of cutting for a) PDC/drag bit and b) roller-cone cutter bit (Farmer, 2016). 
However, there are no fixed rules about when to use which drill bit technology (Bourgoyne et al, 
1985). Usually, roller cone bits are used in shallower and softer formations than drag bits. The rate of 
penetration in rotary drilling decreases with depth, as presented in Fig. 3, where the depth evolution 
for several wells drilled in Bosque field in Venezuela are presented. According to Avila et al. (2013), 
the rate of penetration decreases (e.g. from initial 11 m/h to 1.8 m/h) between surface and 5 km 
depth. It can be seen that that decrease in rate of penetration is not a linear function, it is caused by 
many factors such as different rock formations (initially sandstone and later shale – below around 
12000 ft), different drill bits – initially roller cones and below around 6000 ft fixed cutter bits, drill bit 
wear, different geometries in used drill bits (different cutter sizes), flow outs, etc.

The cutting process applied by a drag bit can be analysed as a horizontal cutting of the rock at 
constant depth. According to a theoretical and experimental study of rock behaviour under 
horizontal cutting done by Nishimatsu (1972), the chipping is done based on a scheme presented in 
Fig. 4. Two crushing zones, ‘a’ primary and ‘b’ secondary, can be distinguished before a rock chip 'c' is 
created and removed. 
 
Fig. 4 Failure process in rock cutting (Nishimatsu, 1972). 
 
According to rock cutting experiments done by Richards (1999), the mechanism of rock removal 
depends also on the depth of cut (parameter d in Fig. 5) – for shallow depths material is crushed and 
model presented by Nishimatsu is not fulfilled, while at higher depths of cut chips are created 
together with the crushed zones. Thus, a transition in fracture mode can be identified: ductile 
fracture - where the material is crushed and the rock debris are the size of grains or smaller, or brittle 
fracture where larger chips are produced. An experimental study on Vosges sandstone (Richards 
1999) showed that this transition between ductile and brittle fracture occurs at 1 mm depth of cut. 
The experimental results of orthogonal cutting of over 200 rock samples presented in Dagarain et al. 
(2004), showed that, with the increasing rock strength, the transition depth is decreasing, as 
presented in Fig. 5, ductile fracture mode is referred to as "grinding" and brittle fracture mode as 
"Primary Chipping". In this study, an additional phase between brittle and ductile fracture mode 
("Secondary Chipping") is proposed to take into account a combined brittle and ductile fracture 
mode.

1.2 Percussive drilling and indentation  
In percussive drilling, the rock is removed by the repeated application of a large impulsive force to a 
drill bit located on a continuously rotating drill string (Depouhon, 2014). The bit penetrates the rock 
by indentation, crushing and chipping of the rock surface. The rotation of the drill string is done in 
order to change the location of the indentation each time so that damage is distributed more evenly 
on the bottom of the well. Outside the crushed zone, cracks are formed as presented in Fig. 6. The 
main mechanism of creation of radial and side cracks is tensile failure, while median cracks are 
created by compressive failure (Tan et al., 1998). 
Fig. 6 Damage zones in rock indentation (Tan et al., 1998). 
Chips are formed when cracks between different indenters, or ones that were formed from the same 
cutting edge are connected what consequently separates element from the base rock. The main 
disadvantage of percussive drilling is that, if applied to non-brittle material (e.g. sandstone), its 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
efficiency (ROP) significantly drops since fewer cracks are formed and consequently less chips are 
produced. Similarly to rotary drilling, the ROP significantly decreases with increasing depth. This can 
be seen in the results of the Deep drilling project (Wittig, 2015) presented in Fig. 7, where a majority 
of the well was drilled in a hard granite formation. However, the ROP was always kept greater than 
10 m/hr even below 5 km depth, which is very efficient compared to classical ROP achieved with 
rotary drilling at these depth as seen in Fig. 3.

1.3 Numerical analysis of rock cutting and indentation 
The analysis of indentation in soft and hard rocks and cutting of soft rocks are well-studied topics, 
but the cutting of hard rocks is to a lesser extent addressed. The PhD thesis of Maria Carolina Jaime 
(Jaime, 2011) and a publication related to the thesis (Jaime et al., 2015), a numerical model for the 
cutting of sandstone is proposed. The model is compared with experimental cutting results of 
Richard (1999). In the work by Jaime (2015), the finite element model (FEM) used validates both the  
ductile and brittle failure mode seen in rock cutting observations of Richard (1999): a length of cut 
equal to 10 times depth of cut is sufficient to calculate the average cutting force. The numerical 
predictions of the rock behaviour at higher depth of cuts were also comparable with experiments, as 
depicted in Fig 8. Further details of this literature study are presented in the following sections of this 
report.

1.4 Scope of the Master thesis 
The main objective of this master thesis is to evaluate and predict the excavation of hard brittle rock 
by rotary drilling, i.e. by cutting. The Kuru granite is subjected to cutting loading conditions both 
experimentally and numerically, using finite element model (FEM). The numerical investigation is first 
compared with existing experimental and numerical studies performed on softer rock materials, and 
an extensive parametrical study is carried out. In particular, the simulated effect of depth of cut and 
confining pressure are quantified and compared with experiments. The numerical analysis of 
dynamic indentation, representative of percussive drilling conditions, was also carried out to 
compare with the cutting efficiency. To summarize, the following activities were done: 
 Literature study on cutting of soft and hard rock: experiments and numerical models. 
 Building the finite elements models for FEM with LS-DYNA for single element calibration and 
cutting tests. 
 Parameter study to achieve comparable results to what found in the literature for the cutting of 
Sandstone. 
 Selection and calibration of material models for Kuru granite: FEM varying depth of cut and 
confining pressure. 
 Single cutting experiments on Kuru granite in laboratory. 
 FEM of impact of percussive drill bit on Kuru granite using a similar material model for the rock 
as used in the cutting model. 
 Presentation of the results during a project meeting at SINTEF. 
In Chapter 2, the experimental study on the single cutter test on Kuru granite is presented and 
compared with data from literature. Chapter 3 introduces the finite element models with the 
material model calibration and a parametrical study.  Chapter 4 presents the numerical results for 
cutting and indentation simulations, as well as a discussion of these results. Chapter 5 gives 
conclusions and perspectives on further work and the relevance of this work to the project. 
2 Experimental study: single cutting test 
2.1 Experimental set-up and procedure 
As a part of this study a series of cutting tests were done on Kuru granite. The test set up consists of 
three main parts: Terratek SN 99008 load cells fixed on the test rig which is plugged into the LabView 
programme that records the data and controls test rig. Both horizontal (or shear) force and vertical 
(or normal) force are measured during the test. In the analysis rock sample is fixed at the bottom of 
the test, while the cutter is fixed with the load cell to the moving part driven by a linear motor. This 
motor allows for applying a constant horizontal velocity of cutting (VOC) from 0 to 10 mm/s during 
the test. The test rig with rock sample is presented in Fig 14.

The load cell maximum force in shear and normal directions is 3 kN, and if exceeded, the system 
automatically stops. The depth of cut (DOC) and length of cut (LOC) are chosen before each cutting 
test. The vertical position of the cutter is measured by two sensors: one linear gauge located above 
the cutter on the top of the load cell, and one next to the control lever.  
The geometry of the rock sample used in these tests is a cylinder with 150 mm length and 50 mm 
diameter. This allows to perform many cutting tests series by rotating the sample. A prismatic cutter 
of 5 mm width was used, manufactured from tungsten carbide material covered with polycrystalline 
diamond layer. Since the rock sample is a cylinder with fine grinded edges, some pre-cuts had to be 
done to achieve a flat surface for cutting. As it can be seen in Fig. 15b, theoretical depth at which

Despite the geometry reasons, pre-cuts are recommended in order to reach depth of cut and steady 
state. Examples of pre-cut shear force and stable shear force obtained at a depth of cut of 0.2mm 
and 0.3mm are presented in Fig. 16. The shear force obtained during the pre-cut tests is increasing 
with the distance, indicating a non-flat cutting surface. The shear force obtained at steady state is 
more constant in average, and the oscillations are caused by brittle chipping of the workpiece 
elements. Besides, the length of cut is slightly shorter than the total length of the specimen to avoid 
sudden drop of force and edge chipping at the end of the specimen. Also, the cutting length is 
decreased by 1mm between each cutting tests to avoid a force peak at the end of the cutting due to 
contact of the rock groove end.  
Three different DOC were investigated in this study: 0.2mm, 0.3mm and 0.5mm. The forces at 0.5mm 
depth of cut remained below the cell limit but the test rig was subjected to visible vibrations and it 
was therefore chosen not to exceed a DOC of 0.5mm. After each cutting test, the sample was 
cleaned and the debris was collected in order to compare their size for different depth of cuts. At 
least three cutting tests were done at each depth of cut, but due to tool wear, not all the tests are 
representative.

2.3 Analytical cutting force models  
Despite of complex mechanism of rock-cutting problems, several analytical solutions for resultant 
cutting force have been proposed in the literature. Three models are investigated here: Evans (1961), 
Nishimatsu (1972) and Detournay and Atkinson (2000), all of them deriving the force from cutter 
geometry, depth of cut and mechanical strength of the rock. The cutting is modelled in two 
dimensions, therefore results must be multiplied by the width of the cut. The models are quickly 
summarized below, but more details are available in Parasie (2009).  
2.3.1 Evans model (1961) 
The original Evans model was evaluated based on observations on coal breakage by wedges. It is 
based on following assumptions: 
 
The rock breaks in tension along a circular path, as presented in Fig. 20, 
 
The failure starts at the tip of the cutter, at the same angle as cutter rake angle, 
 
The force required to break the rock minimize the work along the circular failure line.

3.3 Numerical parameters 
In this study, three different loadings will be analysed: orthogonal cutting, groove cutting and 
indentation. These types of simulations involve high levels of deformation and stresses, erosion and 
complex contacts. Therefore the influence of various numerical parameters (such as element shape, 
size, formulation etc.) was investigated. This parameter investigation was sometimes carried out on 
the orthogonal cutting simulations presented in more details in Section 4. 
3.3.1 Mesh 
In the finite element analysis, the rock domain is divided into finite elements called mesh. Since the 
software calculates the stress state in every element, and the material removal is mostly defined by 
element erosion, the elements shape, size and type will have a substantial influence on the results. In 
theory, when the element size decreases (i.e. the number of elements increases), both the accuracy 
and the computation time of the analysis tends to increase, which makes element size definition an 
important challenge. In the simulations presented in Section 4, it will be explained that the mesh is 
refined only where the deformation and cracks are expected to occur in order to save computational 
time.   
3.3.2 Element  
Element shape 
Two types of elements were analysed – 4 nodes hexahedron element and 4 nodes tetrahedron. As 
mentioned previously, a crack is mostly formed due to localization of strains leading to damage and 
erosion. Therefore it was important to use elements which formulation and shape allowed to 
represent the cracks in a realistic manner. As shown in Fig. 29, the crack formed with hexahedron 
elements is always horizontal. This leads to much higher forces because element separation and 
chipping does not occur and the cutting is achieved by eroding all the elements in front of the cutter.

The force obtained with structured tetrahedron elements is very similar to the hexahedron elements 
for low depth of cuts (below 1mm), where in both cases no chipping occurs. Besides, the use of a

elements. In Fig. 30a, where the elements size is more homogeneous, the cracks are short and

allowing the creation of a chip.  
It is also important to keep an element aspect ratio (i.e. the ratio between highest and smallest edge) 
as close to 1 as possible, to keep accurate and reliable results. Besides, higher aspect ratio elements 
were not capable of producing any crack lines – they eroded more independently.

Element formulation 
When defining elements in ls dyna it is important to remember about correct element definition 
under section option.  Default solid element type is constant stress solid (Hallquist, 2009). It was 
found that for tetrahedron elements it may lead to instabilities and errors in the analysis. After 
checking other available options it was found that 1 point tetrahedron is most suitable element 
definition for this analysis. 
As a conclusion, it is observed that the force results obtained with 1 point tetrahedron mesh and 
element size in a range 0.18 to 0.3 mm are the most accurate. Therefore, this element type and size 
was used further in the FEA. 
3.3.3 Damage Recovery 
The damage recovery RECOV is a user input parameter to *Mat_159 that is responsible for 
recovering stiffness in compression from tensile damage (Murray, 2007). The default value of that 
parameter (RECOV=0) indicates that all stiffness is recovered for compressive pressure. The value 
RECOV=1 leads to no recovery of stiffness, which represent the most brittle case. When RECOV is 
between 0 and 1, partial recovery is modelled. Jaime (2011) stated that this parameter influences the 
fracture in the rock and cutting force significantly. Based on this study, it was decided to use 
RECOV=0.5 to allow partial stiffness recovery and account for both ductile and brittle fracture.  
3.3.4 Erosion 
Erosion was defined in *Mat_159 by maximum element damage and maximum principal strain, 
which are defined by the parameter ERODE (Murray, 2007). When the value of ERODE is 1 (default 
value), erosion only driven by the damage variable (element will be eroded when D reaches 0.99). 
For any ERODE value above 1, ERODE = 1+ε, erosion occurs when the maximum principal strain ε is 
reached. From a sensitivity analysis, it was concluded that with the increase of ERODE parameter, the 
accumulated damage increases and larger deformations are observed in the rock before breaking, as 
illustrated in Fig. 36. This is yet another parameter that has significant influence on the results of 
analysis and has to be calibrated very precisely in order to get realistic results.

3.3.5 Time step 
The time step, as its name indicates, defines the time of computation of each step in the analysis. It is 
defined by the time that acoustic wave requires to travel through the smallest element of all. The 
shortest characteristic distance is checked by the software at each step of the analysis.  By definition, 
an increase of density or element size will increase the time step, and the larger the time step, the 
fastest is the analysis. During each analysis, the initial time step was set to 10-8 s, but due to large 
deformations in the material, the time step decreased by a factor of 100. The computational time 
became unacceptably long so the time step control of Ls-Dyna was used to erode elements that are 
responsible for decreasing the initial time step by a factor above 20.  
3.3.6 Damping 
To decrease instabilities in the model, a global damping model from ls Dyna was used. This option 
applies damping globally to the nodes of deformable body and to the mass centre of the rigid bodies. 
(Hallquist 2009). 
It was found that value of damping coefficient influence significantly the crack formation and 
consequently the resultant cutting force. A higher value of damping coefficient increases the stability 
of the analysis and can avoid segmentation errors. For Vosges sandstone and Kuru granite, the value 
of damping coefficient used in every analysis was the same as used by Jaime (2011): equal to 1x10-8. 
This value gives appropriate results of element damage and cutting force, i.e. more similar to 
experimental results.  
Damping was found to be one of the most important parameters in the study for Vosges sandstone. 
Any difference in its value could significantly affect numerical results for this rock. 
3.3.7 Contact 
Two types of contact were used in this study: tool – rock and rock – rock.  
Tool-rock contact

a rigid wall defined by a set of vectors was used in the same way as in Jaime (2011). All its nodes are 
automatically defined as master nodes. Slave nodes and coulomb friction coefficient are two 
parameters defined by the user. The friction coefficient must be chosen either from literature or by 
trial and error. This parameter is very influential since it may result in different chip formation with,

4.2.3 Results of groove cut simulations for sandstone 
Results of the groove cutting experiments with applied confining pressures are presented in Fig. 50 - 
Fig. 52, where damage contour plots done to the elements after cutting are presented for three 
different depths of cut and different confining pressures. Damage contour plot barien from 0 to 0.5. 
These results can be compared to experimental ones obtains by Kaitikay and Lei( 2005). Among their 
findings is that with the increase of confining pressure cutting force increases as well as shear failure 
- which consequently leads to formation of bigger chips. It was also concluded by Kaitikay and Lei 
(2005) that hydrostatic pressure can transform the rock cutting process from ductile fracture mode 
to an intermediate ductile-brittle mode. It was found in numerical models that with applied pressure, 
chip formation appears. According to the theory presented in (Zhou and Lin, 2012), cutting mode for 
sandstone at depths smaller than 1mm should be ductile, therefore no larger chips were expected to 
be created during the cutting. Also orthogonal cutting simulations in this study seemed to confirm 
that theory, since chips and cracks were formed only for depth of cuts deeper than 1 mm, but 
orthogonal cutting simulation was done without any external pressure. Chip formation observed in 
the output of numerical simulations is affected by change in confining pressure although obtained 
numerical results do not indicate if increasing confining pressure increases the amount of produced 
chips or effecting their size – for all analysis majority of elements from the cutting zone were eroded 
and chips obtained in each model were formed in different locations of the workpiece and no visible 
trend was found related to their size. It can be also seen that damage done to the elements after the 
cut is increasing with the increase in confining pressure. This finding is in contradiction with Jaime 
(2011) in her thesis damage was decreasing with the increasing confining pressure. Although 
knowing that damage level refers to the micro cracks that appear in an element, which, according to 
Kaitkay and Lei (2005), increases with the increase in confining pressure (the more brittle the mode 
of cutting is, the more cracks are expected to appear in the workpiece), it may be expected to obtain 
greater damage level with increasing pressure. On the other hand removed volume of the material is 
decreasing with the increase in pressure, therefore less elements are removed what suggests that 
some remaining elements may have higher damage (for lower confining pressures this damage was 
higher such that elements were removed).

increases. Decrease in specific energy can be explained by the fact that there were no chips at depth 
of cut equal to 0.3mm, some single element chips at 0.6mm and few (still not many) elements chips 
at depth of cut of 1mm. The more elements are removed by chipping the lower is the resultant 
cutting force, therefore it is desired in the drilling process to produce bigger chips.

4.3 Indentation 
In this section, the efficiency of percussive drilling is addressed by comparing the MSE with 
predictions from groove cutting test. The rock sample was subjected by the same confining pressures 
as in the groove test simulations: 0.1 MPa, 10 MPa, 30 MPa, 50 MPa. In this test, the percussive drill 
bit was modelled with solid elements, in the same way as in Fourmeau et al. (2017). The drill bit, 
modelled as rigid with 46 000 solid elements, impacts the rock sample with a velocity equal to 2 m/s 
for the simulations with sandstone and granite. However, the density of the drill bit material was 
taken different for each rock material. For granite, the density was chosen to provide the same 
impact energy to the system as experimental results presented in Fourmeau et al. (2017). For 
sandstone, a lower density was chosen to apply lower impact energy to the rock and insure that the 
indentation depth was not greater than 2mm. The contact between the drill bit and the workpiece is 
defined as surface to surface with penalty method, where the friction coefficient was set to 0.3 for 
sandstone and 0.5 for granite (based on Fourmeau et al. 2017 and Changen et al, 2009 ). Despite the 
fact that the drill bit has 7 edges, only 2 edges were in contact with the workpiece. Fourmeau et al. 
(2017) studied the effect of the shape of the surface impacted (and number of edges impacting the 
rock), but this is not investigated here.  The drill bit and workpiece geometries are presented in Fig.

5 Conclusions 
The main goal of this project was to design reliable finite element model capable of giving insights on 
the excavation of hard brittle rock by rotary drilling (i.e. cutting) and percussive drilling (i.e. 
indentation).  
In this study, cutting experiment on Kuru granite was done with three different depths of cut: 
0.2mm, 0.3mm, 0.5mm. Normal and shear components of the cutting force were compared to 
calculated results from analytical solutions. In agreement with results from the literature (Richard 
1999), we observed that analytical solutions are not applicable for estimating the cutting forces for 
hard rocks such as Kuru granite.  
A finite element model was initially calibrated for Vosges sandstone and a good agreement with the 
results from Jaime (2011) was obtained for cutting. Then, we extended the use of this model to study 
cutting of Kuru granite. Using experimental data from previous projects on Kuru granite at SINTEF, 
two material models were calibrated. These were the Johnson Holmquist concrete model 
(*Mat_111) and the continuous surface cap model (*Mat_159) used by Jaime (2011) for Sandstone. 
*Mat_111  was a model with fewer parameters than *MAT_159 (22 versus 42).  
 
The numerical simulations for cutting of Kuru granite gave the following results: 
 
Simulations using *Mat_111 were not able to predict the chip formation observed in 
experiments,  
 
 
Simulations using *Mat_159 indicated that: 
o Chips formed during granite cutting, and increased in size with the increase in depth 
of cut as observed in the experiments, 
o Good agreement between experimental and numerical cutting force was obtained at 
all DOCs, this means that material model evaluated in this study can be used, in 
designing drill bits for hard rock formations, 
o In line with the literature (Jaime, 2011) simulated cutting force and MSE increase 
with increasing confining pressure,  
o Results indicate that for no confining pressure the MSE of indentation is about 50% 
of MSE of cutting and with the increase in confining pressure MSE for indentation is 
in all cases lower than the one computed in cutting tests.  
 
It has been also noticed that both for indentation and cutting removed volume in numerical models 
was significantly overestimated, leading to much lower values of MSE in unconfined conditions than 
in experimental results. One of the reasons for that might be that in experiments a lot of removed 
elements had smaller diameter than the elements in the numerical study, therefore sensitivity 
analysis on mesh size influence on this phenomena should be carried out (in this study such analysis 
was done only for Vosges sandstone).   
==PAGE:71==
70 
 
In further analysis results obtained in this study could be compared with different numerical 
approach that is discrete element method (DEM). In this approach rock model geometry is divided 
into independent particles – not elements as in FEM and it has been successively used in analysis of 
rock cutting (Huang, 2013, Parasie 2009). This approach may help to solve the problem of too high 
removed volume in analysis.
