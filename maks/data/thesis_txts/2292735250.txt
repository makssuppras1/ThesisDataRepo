Abstract 
Retaining structures are engineering works designed to retain large volumes of soil/rock 
which would otherwise collapse into a more natural shape. They separate the soil/rock volume 
(generally referred to as backfill) from a zone under usage, i.e. another structure (building, 
bridge, road, ports and harbours, etc.) and are continuously used also in seismic areas. During 
an earthquake, permanent deformations of the retaining structures occur, sometimes 
negligibly small, but sometimes large enough to produce significant damages. Therefore, a 
proper earthquake design is mandatory and would be able to mitigate the disasters when an 
earthquake occurs.  
Throughout the years, several methods of estimating the actual force exerted on the retaining 
structures during earthquake have been established. The most commonly used is the 
Mononobe-Okabe method, which implies a pseudostatic analysis. In the pseudostatic analysis, 
the peak ground acceleration is converted into a pseudo-static inertia force and applied as a 
horizontal load. The most accurate, but more time-consuming method is the full-dynamic 
analysis, where the finite element method is used and an actual measured earthquake 
acceleration time history is introduced as load.  
The design of a retaining structure implies the check of stability and strength. Stability wise, 
the structure is verified against overturning, sliding, and bearing capacity. This can be done by 
estimation of the lateral pressures acting on the wall and aiming for equilibrium conditions. 
Under static conditions, the common pressures come from the soil on both sides of the wall, 
the water or any surcharge load from vicinity. When the retaining structures are situated in 
seismic areas, additional to the lateral pressures above-mentioned, a dynamic load due to 
earthquake is applied to each constitutive part of the wall-soil system.  
The objective of the present study is to conclude if, for a specific case study considered, i.e. a 
quay wall, a dynamic analysis would have been a feasible choice and then argue the extent to 
which the dynamic analysis would provide advantages in terms of the ratio between more 
economical design and computational cost in comparison to the pseudostatic analysis 
employed at present. 
In this sense, a simplified model of the quay wall was developed and both a pseudostatic and 
a full-dynamic analysis for the retaining wall structure were performed, using finite element 
method and the commercial software Plaxis. Then, by comparing the behaviour of the model 
in both analyses, the results were assessed in terms of maximum deformations, stresses and 
strains and dynamic response throughout time.

Chapter 1. Aim of Work
 
 
Chapter 1. 
Aim of Work 
Earthquake is a natural phenomenon. Similar to other natural phenomena, it cannot be 
prevented. What can be prevented, though, are the effects in terms of damages, i.e. deaths, 
injuries and property damages. In order to do so, the earthquakes have been studied for 
hundreds of years, in different parts of the world.  Devices that are able to measure the 
magnitude of an earthquake have been built up, seismic design codes have been established, 
such that the effects in terms of destruction can be mitigated. 
During an earthquake, the greatest number of fatalities occurs not because of the earthquake 
itself, but because of the collapse of the buildings/structures and the debris resulted after their 
destruction. A famous saying that comes from seismologists throughout time states that 
"earthquakes don't kill people, buildings do". This is because most deaths from earthquakes 
are caused by buildings or other human construction falling down during an earthquake. [ 1] 
During dynamic loading, permanent deformations occur due to static and dynamic loading 
and, if not estimated accurately, could lead to disastrous effects, especially when retaining a 
large volume of water, as in the case of quay walls situated in ports and harbours. However, 
earthquake response is not at all a simple design assessment and the major interest for the 
design engineers is to find the most efficient cross section of the structure at the lowest 
possible cost, in terms of time and money.  
Several state-of-the-art methods of estimating the actual force exerted on the retaining 
structures during earthquake exist and are extensively used. The so-called Mononobe-Okabe 
method uses the peak ground acceleration given for a specific region and converts it into an 
inertia force acting as a horizontal load. A different approach is performing finite element 
analysis where a real time-history measurement of an earthquake is introduced as a dynamic 
load. As expected, the dynamic analysis predicts results closer to the reality and ensures in 
this sense a more economical design. However, the major disadvantage of this method is the 
computational cost which has proven to be sometimes too high for the amount of economical 
savings. 
A concrete quay wall located in Port of Beirut, Lebanon, which is a seismic area, is 
investigated in this study. The design phase of the quay wall is already completed and after 
both a strength and stability check was performed, the optimum cross section was established. 
Therefore, the purpose is not to redesign the structure, but to conduct two separate analyses, a 
pseudostatic and a dynamic one and compare the behaviour of the models. The simplification 
of the actual geometry of the wall and other assumptions are presented throughout the 
chapters of the study.  
To start with, a sequential numerical modelling consisting of three benchmarks is performed 
by using the two commercial software programs, Abaqus CAE and Plaxis 2D AE. In Abaqus, 
a detailed examination of Westergaard hydrostatic pressure distribution on a submerged 
retaining wall is conducted, firstly by assuming water as an acoustic medium and secondly by 
assuming water as a continuum and introducing infinite elements as boundary absorbers. A

Chapter 2.

When an earthquake occurs, its effect on the retaining wall depends on lateral earth pressures 
acting on the wall. These pressures are the sum of all static gravitational loads acting before 
the earthquake takes place and the dynamic loads induced by the earthquake itself. 
In this chapter, various state-of-the-art methods of designing a retaining wall are presented, 
firstly considering a static state (no earthquake) and then a dynamic one (considering 
earthquake as an additional load). At the end of the chapter, an example of stability check for 
an L-shaped retaining wall is presented. 
2.1 Static Response of Retaining Walls [ 2] 
The static loads come from the retained earth and water and act as pressures on the surface of 
the wall. They can be divided into active earth pressures and passive earth pressures. The 
active earth pressures develop when the wall moves away from the soil, inducing extensional 
lateral strain throughout it. When the movement of the wall is sufficient to mobilize the 
strength of the soil behind the wall, minimum active earth pressures act on the wall. The 
passive earth pressures develop when the wall moves towards the soil, causing compressional 
lateral strain in the soil. When the strength of the soil is fully mobilized, maximum passive 
earth pressures act on the wall. [ 2] The stability of many retaining walls depends therefore on 
the equilibrium between the active and the passive earth pressures.

2.2 Pseudostatic Response of Retaining Walls [ 2] 
The dynamic response of a retaining wall is not a simple problem to be dealt with. There are 
multiple factors that contribute to the final response, such as: the soil layers and bedrock 
underlay, backfill material, the response of the wall itself and the applied motion. Since there 
are very few cases of field measurement of the response of a retaining wall, the knowledge 
existing nowadays comes from model tests and numerical analyses. Some of the conclusions 
driven after performing tests and running numerical models have been gathered from different 
sources in [ 2] and are listed below.  
 Walls can move by translation and/or rotation. Depending on the design of the wall, in 
some cases only translation or only rotation can occur, while some walls can both 
translate and rotate ; 
 The distribution and magnitude of dynamic wall pressures are influenced by the wall 
movement (i.e. translation, rotation about the base, rotation about the top); 
 The maximum soil thrust acting on a wall generally occurs when the wall has 
translated or rotated toward the backfill, meaning that the inertial force on the wall is 
directed toward the backfill. The minimum soil thrust occurs when the wall has 
translated or rotated away from the backfill; 
 The shape of the earth pressure distribution on the back of the wall changes as the wall 
moves with respect to the soil. Therefore, the point of application of the thrust moves 
upwards or downwards along the back of the wall. The position reaches the highest 
point when  the wall moved towards the soil, respectively the lowest point when the 
wall moved outwards the soil; 
 Dynamic wall pressures are influenced by the dynamic response of the wall and 
backfill and can increase significantly near the natural frequency of the wall-backfill 
system.  
In order to properly design the retaining wall, the loads applied during the earthquake are 
estimated and the capacity of the wall to resist them is ensured. Since the exact estimation of 
the loads is extremely complicated, simplified methods can be used instead.

A simplified method has been proposed by Mononobe and Okabe wears the name Mononobe-
Okabe (M-O) method. The method is based on plasticity theory and is in fact an extension of 
Coulomb theory, modified to incorporate a horizontal inertia body force, as well as a

2.3 Dynamic Response of Retaining Walls 
The pseudostatic analysis assumes the application in the model of a constant acceleration 
equal to the peak ground acceleration. On the other hand, in a dynamic analysis, a recorded 
accelerations of an earthquake can be introduced and in such a way, the peak values would 
most likely be smaller than the peak ground acceleration or reached in several time steps and 
not constant throughout time. The analysis could then lead to a more realistic behaviour. But 
running a finite element analysis with an accelelogram as load is time consuming and 
sometimes the results cannot be easily interpreted. Advanced material models are preferred 
for such an analysis and without a good engineering judgement, results can easily be 
misinterpreted.

3.1.1 Effects of Water on Wall Pressures [ 2] [ 8] 
For a complete and detailed presentation of the theory behind, the following section is 
extracted from [ 2] and[ 8].  
The presence of water plays a strong role in determining the loads on waterfront retaining 
walls both during and after earthquakes. Water outbound of a retaining wall can exert 
dynamic pressures on the face of the wall. Water within a backfill can also affect the dynamic 
pressures that act on the back of the wall. Proper consideration of the effects of water is 
essential for the seismic design of retaining structures, particularly in waterfront areas. Since 
few waterfront retaining structures are completely impermeable, the water level in the backfill 
is usually at approximately the same level as the free water outboard of the wall. Backfill 
water levels generally lag behind changes in outboard water level-the difference in water level 
depends on the permeability of the wall and backfill and on the rate at which the outboard 
water level changes. The total water pressures that act on retaining walls in the absence of 
seepage within the backfill can be divided into two components:  
 Hydrostatic pressure, which increases linearly with depth and acts on the wall before, 
during and after earthquake shaking; 
 Hydrodynamic pressure, which results from the dynamic response of the water itself.

3.1.1.2 Water in Backfill 
The presence of water in the backfill behind a retaining wall can influence the seismic loads 
that act on the wall in three ways:  
 by altering the inertial forces within the backfill; 
 by developing hydrodynamic pressures within the backfill; 
 by allowing excess porewater pressure generation doe to cyclic straining of the 
backfill soils. 
The inertial forces in saturated soils depend on the relative movement between the backfill 
soil particles and the porewater that surrounds them. If the permeability of the soil is small 
enough (k<10-3) that the porewater moves with the soil during earthquake shaking (no 
relative, movement of soil and water, or restrained porewater conditions), the inertial forces 
will be proportional to the total unit weight of the soil. If the permeability is very high, the 
porewater may remain essentially stationary while the soil skeleton moves back and forth (the 
soil particles move through the porewater in free porewater conditions). In these cases, 
inertial forces will be proportional to the submerged unit weight of the soil. Hydrodynamic 
water pressures can also develop under free porewater conditions and must be added to the 
computed soil and hydrostatic pressures to obtain the total loading on the wall.  [ 2]

The design of a retaining structure implies the check of stability and strength. Stability wise, 
the structure is verified against overturning, sliding, and bearing capacity. This can be done 
by estimation of the lateral pressures acting on the wall and aiming for equilibrium 
conditions. Under static conditions, the common pressures come from the soil on both sides of 
the wall, the water or any surcharge load from vicinity.  
When the retaining structures are situated in a seismic area, additional to the lateral pressures 
above-mentioned, a dynamic load due to earthquake is applied to each constitutive part of the 
wall-soil system.  
This section is intended to deal with the application of both pseudostatic and dynamic 
analyses on a retaining wall designed by the consultancy company Rambøll , i.e. a concrete 
quay wall, as part of Port of Beirut, Lebanon, situated in a seismic area. The quay wall is an 
extension of an existing quay wall and it is marked with red in Figure 4.4.

4.1 Project Description 
Within Port of Beirut, multiple quay walls have been constructed and serve as retaining 
structures, as shown in Figure 4.4. The quay wall referred to in this paper is the extension of

4.1.1 Geometry 
Since excessive displacements of quay walls can lead to disastrous effects, the optimum cross 
section and geometry that helps in reducing the displacements has been studied over the 
years. The so-called broken-back gravity quay walls have been intensively tested using 
reduced-scale shaking table model experiments. The experimental results indicate that backfill 
settlement is smaller in a region behind a broken-back quay wall in which coastal and port 
facilities are located would undergo less deformation and damage during earthquakes. [25] 
The model considered in [25] is presented in Figure 4.5 (a). For the quay wall in Port of 
Beirut, the final cross section is presented in Figure 4.5 (b). All the dimensions of the cross

4.2 Pseudostatic Analysis 
4.2.1 Seismic conditions 
The design seismic hazard is described by the horizontal peak ground acceleration (PGA), 
determined for a specific return period corresponding to ultimate limit state. PGA describes 
the earth shakes in a specific geographic zone, function of measured data and studies of the 
considered area. 
The easternmost Mediterranean region is characterized by low seismicity, at least during the 
instrumental era. However, this region has suffered from many destructive earthquakes in the 
past which, from all accounts, have originated along the Dead Sea Transform Fault (DSTF). 
[26] DSTF is shown in Figure 4.7 (a), together with the epicentres of earthquakes that took 
place during 1964-1995. Furthermore, the existence of Mount Lebanon Thrust has been 
recently confirmed and this has a strong influence on the seismicity in the area. The thrust is 
shown in Figure 4.7 (b).  
During the design process of the quay wall, numerous sources were consulted and as a 
conclusion, the horizontal PGA was conservatively taken as 0.25g. No vertical acceleration 
has been considered in the design of the quay wall.

Chapter 5. 
Conclusions and Further Research 
As already mentioned, the objective of the study was to bring a new perspective in the 
earthquake design of an existing quay wall constructed in an area with medium seismicity. 
The current design of the actual quay wall includes a pseudostatic analysis and the question 
on how a dynamic analysis would have improved the cost and efficiency of the design arises.   
The study began with three benchmarks that served as an introduction in the subject, before 
going in details on the case study considered. The Westergaard assumption that in the case of 
submerged retaining wall, the hydrodynamic pressure is doubled is investigated first.   
Three models have been created in this sense: one with water defined as an acoustic medium, 
the second with water defined as a continuum and the third, by adding a stiff foundation 
below the entire model. However, only the first two were presented in the study and the third 
is proposed as a further research subject. When water was assumed an acoustic medium, the 
obtained result in terms of pressure showed that there is no caption of the hydrodynamic 
pressure, the magnitude of the pressure registered matching though the hydrostatic pressure. 
Westergaard assumes an infinite rigid wall and this was not implemented in the model. In 
order to obtain comparable results, the assumptions must all be implemented in the modelling. 
The second model includes all the assumptions considered by Westergaard and obtains an 
accurate value of the pressure, but the shape of the distribution is not a convex parabola, but a 
concave one. Due to the fixed vertical boundary conditions assumed at the bottom, shear 
stresses seem to develop, which might alter the result, since water is assumed with no shear 
resistance. Following an example from [ 11] a stiff foundation can be introduced at the bottom 
and an interface with no friction can be assigned for the tangential direction. The results have 
not been assessed from this calculation procedure, therefore could constitute a good further 
research.  
The second benchmark was the development of a dynamic finite element model using Plaxis 
2D AE and assessing the dynamic amplification factor for a single soil layer on which a 
harmonic acceleration time history was applied. 
The final benchmark consisted in the pseudostatic analysis of an L-shaped retaining wall, 
which was also calculated using M-O method. The failure modes are compared and a short 
discussion on the active pressure virtual plane is included as well. 
The first analysis of the quay wall was the pseudostatic analysis with PGA=0.25g, where 
Mohr-Coulomb material model has been assigned to the soil materials. The considered quay 
wall was assumed a linear-elastic concrete block, with interfaces being disregarded. The 
maximum displacements d=60mm were compared to the allowable displacements provided 
by Eurocode            . 
Three models, each of them with a different scaled (to PGA=0.25g) acceleration time history 
implemented were then created. To the fill material the hardening soil model with small

stiffness was assigned, in order to account for the strain dependency of the stiffness. Because 
assessing too many parameters in the same time is a difficult procedure, the stress dependency 
has been deactivated. The maximum displacements (d=45mm) obtained through the full-
dynamic analysis proved to be smaller than the ones obtained through the pseudostatic 
analysis. For the stresses though, a good agreement was observed between the two. The 
computational time for the models has proven to be very large (8h, 14h, 43h for first, second

As a final step, a safety calculation has been performed and the strength reduction factor 
∑          was determined by increasing the total number of steps from the default value

Considering all the results obtained from the two types of analysis, it can be concluded that 
with the assumptions in terms of geometry, material model chosen and parameters considered, 
the results between the two analyses are in good agreement; therefore a pseudostatic analysis 
can predict a good seismic behaviour of the model.   
Plastic points that appeared at the boundary of the model indicate that the size of the model 
should be increased. For a final conclusion on the efficiency of the dynamic analysis, a 
parametric study is suggested as further research and the response re-evaluated with variation 
of different strength and stiffness parameters of the bedrock and fill material. The assumption 
of considering the bedrock as a linear-elastic material could also be argued in further analyses.

6.2 Appendix 3. Material Models [ 28] 
Modelling of soil can be done by making some assumptions with regard to its behaviour in 
terms of stresses and strains. As appropriate the assumptions are to the real behaviour, as 
accurate the results of the analysis are.  These assumptions define a material model and the 
material models that have been used throughout this master thesis are detailed below. 
6.2.1 Linear Elastic Model  
The Linear Elastic model (LE) is based on the simplest available stress-strain relationship, 
namely Hooke‟s Law of isotropic elasticity, formulated in Eq. (6.1) and graphically 
represented in Figure 6.3. In Plaxis, defining a linear elastic material requires only two elastic 
parameters, E (Young‟s modulus of elasticity) and ν (Poisson‟s ratio).  
 
      
(6.1)  
 
Figure 6.3. Hook‟s Law 
In the LE model, the state of stresses is not limited, thus the material in considered perfectly 
elastic with an infinite strength. Since the real behaviour of soil is strongly non-linear and 
irreversible, the only material that is assigned the linear elastic behaviour in this study is the 
concrete.  
6.2.2 Mohr-Coulomb Model  
The Mohr-Coulomb model, unlike LE, assumes the material behaviour as linear elastic for a 
while and the perfectly plastic from that point further, as shown in Figure 6.4. It is assumed 
that the stiffness throughout a soil layer defined has a constant average value or that it 
increases linearly with the depth. While LE model involves only the two parameters E and ν, 
the Mohr-Coulomb model (MC) involves three additional, in order to define the plastic 
behaviour. These are φ (angle of internal friction), c (cohesion) and ψ (angle of dilatancy).
