Abstract 
 
The current thesis deals with the term Level of Development (LOD), how it is addressed within 
various BIM Guidelines and validate on what extent an LOD framework can be checked through 
automated rule - based process.  
In order to understand the term LOD, various approaches, that have been made by different 
organizations world widely, are investigated. Even though similarities are noticed among the 
approaches, each one defines in a unique way the term. However, it is observed that most of 
the approaches use as a base the definition by AIA and the interpretation of it by BIMForum. 
Additionally to the term LOD, the automated rule - based checking, which is lately making a 
headway in BIM and the construction industry, is explored. However, until now the automated 
rule - based checking within building models has to do mostly with regulations, building codes, 
accessibility and fire safety issues.  
In connection to the term LOD, the automated rule - based checking is performed in order to 
ensure that the delivered LOD of model elements is the same with the desired one. In order to 
perform such checking, a simplified framework of LOD, based on existing LOD approaches, is 
created and used. In the current thesis Solibri Model Checker is used as a validation tool and a 
specific model of a DTU building as a study case.  The overall checking process consists of four 
steps: the building model preparation, the translation of the LOD framework to rules, the rule 
execution and the reporting of the results. 
The thesis concludes that the automated rule - based checking can be performed and is more 
efficient than the manual checking but has a lot of limitations. Many graphical requirements 
cannot be checked and the rules should be customized every time depending on the study case 
and cannot be widely implied. Finally, it is suggested as steps of further work to perform the 
checking of LOD requirements through the IDM / MVD process.

Chapter 1 
Introduction, Scope and Motivation 
 
The chapter ‘Introduction, Scope and Motivation’ introduces to the basic terms used in the 
current thesis: Building Information Modeling (BIM), Level of Development (LOD) and 
automated rule – based checking. Moreover, the scope and the objectives of the thesis are 
addressed. A general descriptive methodology, that is followed, is also part of the current 
chapter in order to give the reader a pre – understanding of the thesis. Finally, a brief structure 
guide of the thesis in overall is given. 
 Building Information Modeling (BIM) 
Building Information Modelling (BIM) has become one of the most popular terms in the 
Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry. The concept of BIM first appeared in 
1987 in a program called ‘Virtual Building’ developed by Graphisoft1[1].  
BIM is considered to be the evolution of the earlier digital construction processes, like 3D CAD 
and 3D object based CAD. 3D CAD is a way of modelling in geometrical models, such as solids, 
surfaces or other 3D entities while there are no properties included on the entities. 3D object-
based CAD uses the CAD objects, containing a restricted amount of data and properties. The 
difference between BIM and CAD systems is that BIM may contain any information about the 
construction, including specifications, economy and programs[2].   
                                                          
1 Graphisoft SE develops BIM software products and is a subsidiary of Nemetschek

The term of Building Information Modeling (BIM) has many interpretations and definitions. BIM 
is not just a technological evolution, but also a process change. When saying BIM, we typically 
refer to the process of an integrated workflow, where all the engaged members work together, 
exchange information and collaborate in order to provide a more efficient construction process 
[1]. It is not only a three dimensional model, but an aggregation of information that can be 
exchanged, evolved, shared and managed. The model elements within a BIM are connected to 
each other and contain information about their geometry and properties.  
A BIM process puts a building model all together, starting for the client requirements and the 
regulations, restrictions and limitations to the final product, even after construction. Through 
BIM, the early design stages are developed according to the initial requirements, creating the 
design alternatives showing each time how each solution influences various aspects of the 
building, such as energy, cost and constructability. During the next steps of the more detailed 
design phases, BIM supports multi-disciplinary design, coordinating and combining the work of 
different disciplines into one model, ensuring quality of communication between different 
participants. BIM can prove to be useful not only during the preconstruction phase, but also 
during the construction and even the life - cycle of the building. BIM combines the design and 
information about fabrication, installation and construction.  
In general, BIM is a way to describe and display the necessary information of a building project, 
containing in the design, construction and operation facilities. All the information can be 
gathered in one collaborative environment, where designers, contractors, subcontractors and 
owners work all together, reducing the need for exchange of multiple documents and files.

 Level of Development (LOD) 
Within BIM there is a need to move from approximations to progressively more precise 
information [3]. The precision of the model should be addressed at a given point in each stage 
of the design process. A BIM model can be used for many purposes; starting from a simple 3D 
representation of a building project, the same model can be used for further analysis, such as 
cost estimation, scheduling, energy simulation, code checking, structural analysis etc. For that 
reason, a framework should be created that defines BIM precision for the various reasons for 
usage. 
The term ‘Level of Development’ (LOD) was created in order to address that. LOD addresses the 
specificity required on a model element at the various stages of a building project. The term was 
first introduced by Vico Software© as ‘Level of Detail’ and it was mostly orientated towards the 
graphical requirements of the designed model elements. However, throughout the years and 
the increasing use of BIM tools and processes created the need to evolve the term and create a 
more specific and clear context.  
The term was evolved from ‘Level of Detail’ to ‘Level of Development’ by the American Institute 
of Architects (AIA) and BIMForum, which approached to the term in a more detailed way and 
addressed some specific graphical and non – graphical requirements, that have to be achieved 
in order to classify a model element on a specific LOD. Since then, apart from AIA and BIMForum, 
more approaches of the term have been made by national organizations and companies from 
the construction industry. The aim of these approaches was to create a common language 
between designers, engineers and contractors within a nation or a company respectively.  
In general, Level of Development (LOD) is a common standardized concept in Building 
Information Modeling (BIM) that specifies the clarity and reliability of a BIM at the various stages 
of design and construction process. LOD is a key parameter in BIM that creates a framework of

the digital information exchange. The aim of the Level of Development is to support digital 
deliverables during the project stages [4]. However, each stage does not align with a different 
LOD, as at a given time different elements can be at different points from each other [5].  
 Automated Rule – Based Checking 
BIM is being used in an increasing extent on the construction industry. As BIM has evolved 
throughout the years and more and more BIM platforms support design, fabrication, 
contracting, and other processes throughout all the design phases of a building, more complex 
and detailed building models can be produced[6]. However, since the complexity has evolved 
that much, the users of the BIM tools cannot rely just on the visual inspection of the BIM model 
in order to ensure good quality or whether the design requirements are fulfilled or not. These 
requirements do not have only to do with the geometrical complexity but also with the 
properties attached to each model element [6].  
A way to check the requirements on a BIM model, is the automated rule – based checking, which 
has been adding a huge value to the AEC industry. The evolving efficiency of the technology and 
computer applications used in BIM made possible to perform checking that until recently, were 
made only manually. Rules have been created that can refer to the building throughout its 
lifecycle. Some of the most common rule - based checking, that have been reported until 
nowadays, have to do with the well – formedness of the building model, compliance with 
building codes and regulations, accessibility, constructability and safety during construction [7]. 
In general, automated rule - based checking tends to become an important part of the AEC 
industry, as it is expected to save much time and money throughout the whole lifecycle of a 
building, from the early design phase to the handover and the operation of the building [8].  
 Problem Statement 
In theoretical approach little research has been made about the concept and application of LOD, 
since it is a newly standardized concept [4]. The various guidelines that have been published are 
still on an evolving way and information are added on them year by year and still the content is 
not clear enough. All these BIM standards and specifications that contain the LOD definition, are 
created in order to address how to design and act with a BIM project. However, little progress 
has been made regarding how to evaluate BIM data; to address whether a BIM model is well 
designed, correct and according to the requirements addressed in the various guides and 
specifications or not[7].  
The validation of a BIM model can be ensured in a big extent through automated rule - based 
checking process. However, the research that has been done until todays has to do mostly with 
building regulations, code and accessibility checking. Research has not been found that checks 
the Level of Development (LOD) within a building model though automated rule - based process. 
It has not been addressed if it is possible to create rules in a rule - based software that can check 
the aimed LOD, in terms of required graphical and non – graphical information, within a building 
model.

In overall, this thesis aims to answer to question:  
‘Is it possible through automated rule – based process to check if the designed LOD of model 
elements within an existing building project is the same as the required? If yes, how far can 
that checking go in terms of success and efficiency?’ 
 Objectives 
The overall purpose of this thesis is to validate on what extent is possible to check if the model 
elements within a building model fulfill specific LOD requirements though a rule –based checking 
process. Initially, in order to understand the term LOD, approaches of the term given by various 
guidelines, provided by national organizations and private sector construction companies, are 
explored. For each approach, is explored how is the term LOD is considered, what the graphical 
and non – graphical requirements are, and if there is any relation among the various definitions.  
The research is further focused to validate to possibility of creating an automated rule - based 
system that checks LOD requirements in an existing building model. Using Solibri Model Checker 
as a validation tool and an existing building model as a study case, the checking is performed for 
a specific LOD framework. 
 Delimitation 
This master thesis is delimitated in a specific, simplified framework of LOD that contains 
characteristic graphical and non - graphical requirements, which derive from different 
specifications. The theoretical approach of the LOD definitions is mostly concentrated on the 
Guidelines published in the United States of America (USA) and in Denmark. Some other 
guidelines are included but not in much extent. The created framework for the study case is 
based on the American and Danish definitions, since the American are the base for most of the 
rest guidelines and some requirements are taken from the Danish as they correspond to the 
current Danish construction industry. The automated rule – based checking is concentrated only 
in the HVAC systems of the building model and the selected LODs to be checked are the LOD200, 
LOD300 and LOD350. However, it is supposed that the process is similar also for other disciplines 
and any LOD with any requirements. Finally, it is taken in consideration that the building model 
used as study case was not designed according to specific LOD requirements, so it is expected 
to fail in some checking.  
 Project Approach 
The overall approach of the current thesis is to investigate the term of Level of Development 
(LOD) and by creating a generic framework of LOD, to validate through automated rule – based 
checking various LODs within an existing building model. 
Firstly, in order to understand the term LOD, the chapter 2 ‘Background’ is created and consists 
of two parts. The first part investigates the term Level of Development (LOD). Starting from the 
initial implementation and what led to the creation of the term, the research continues with

how it evolved throughout the years and what is the current state of the term. LOD is a term 
that can be found in various BIM guides world widely. Guidelines from the U.S.A, Australia, 
Denmark, Belgium, Netherlands and U.K are used in order to clarify how each one defines the 
term of LOD. It is investigated what graphical and non – graphical requirements each guide sets 
in order to classify a model element at a specific level of LOD on the corresponding scale used 
each time. The second part of the chapter has to do with the research that has taken place until 
nowadays about the automated rule - based checking. It is investigated how the automated rule 
- based checking has been implemented, since when it exists and what is the most popular 
validation aims. Within the context of these automated checking, one widely used validation 
tool is Solibri Model Checker (SMC). Since it is used in the current thesis, a general overview and 
the capabilities of that tool are being introduced. 
Since the building model, which is used as a study case, is not known if it was designed according 
to specific LOD requirements, an LOD framework had to be created in order to proceed to the 
automated rule - based checking. Having explored the various LOD definitions with their 
characteristics each time, some distinctive graphical and non-graphical requirements are 
selected from various LOD interpretations, mostly from the BIMForum Specification 2015 and 
the Danish BIM Guidelines. The selected requirements shape a generic LOD framework, which 
is used as a basis for rule – based checking process of LOD.  
The first step of the methodology is to translate the requirements of the LOD framework to rules 
that can be used in Solibri Model Checker. The second phase is to prepare the building model 
according to the requirements in order to make a successful implementation of the rules. The 
building model has to be customized in order all the desired model elements to be checked. The 
third phase is to execute the created rules. Finally, the rules are reported mostly graphically but 
written reports can also be obtained that contain the model elements.  
The created framework of LOD, methodology of rule creation and implementation of them are 
performed in the given study case, which is a building model of DTU 211. Moreover, the 
automated checking process is concentrated on the HVAC system of the building model, as it is 
expected that all the model elements can be therefore tested in a similar way. However, that 
building model was not designed or prepared according to the specific requirements of the LOD 
created in the current thesis. For that reason, the second step of the process (building model 
preparation) had to be carefully done and the performed checking is expected not to totally 
succeed.  
A discussion is made according to results obtained on the study case. The level of success of that 
method is being discussed and on what extend the LOD can be ensured; which limitations were 
remarked, which could be overcome and which not. Moreover, the usefulness of the LOD 
framework is discussed. Finally, proposal for further work are set that could make that process 
take place in a more efficient way.

Chapter 2 
Background 
The chapter ‘Background’ consists of two parts. The first one describes the term Level of 
Development (LOD). Starting from the MPS, in which the term was introduced, to the gradually 
evolving development of the term by the AIA. Other BIM guides from various countries and 
organizations, that define the term LOD, are investigated. Moreover, the work that has been 
done until now on the automated checks is described and the tools used for that rule – based 
checking are introduced.  
 Level of Development (LOD) 
According each time to the needed precision, which usually depends on the purpose of the 
modeling (cost estimation, energy simulation, structural analysis, code checking), the model 
elements in a BIM are represented in higher or lower detail. In order to organize and avoid 
confusions within a Building Information Model, a framework was needed that would define the 
precision of the model to the users.  
To address that need, in 2004, Vico Software© introduced the Model Progression Specification 
(MPS). MPS is actually a common language used by all the members involved in a construction 
project, owners, designers, builders, in order to define every element and task in the 
construction process[9]. It defines which elements are modeled, and to what level of detail they 
should be modeled. MPS can be used by the different parties from different companies, or even 
different countries, in order to communicate and work together in a project in a consistent 
way[10]. MPS recognizes and supports the fact that BIM can be developed any time and every 
time a new enriched version of the model can be created. That leads to many model versions, 
even more than the traditional design phases. Moreover, MPS deals with problems of modeling 
progression, as each design part demands more or less time and effort, depending on the pace 
and the demand of detail[11].

One of the most important components of the MPS is the term ‘Level of Detail (LOD)’. The term 
was first pioneered by Vico® and Webcolors® in 2005 as part of their Model Progression 
Specification (MPS) [4]. Level of Detail according to the MPS, is the specificity required for a 
particular element at a particular stage of the project. Later, in late 2008, the term was redefined 
by the American Institute of Architects (AIA) IPD Task Force and finally adopted by the AIA [4]. 
AIA developed a more detailed interpretation of the LOD schema at the document ‘E202-2009 
BIM and Digital Exhibit’ and updated it at the document ‘G202-2013 Building Information 
Modeling Protocol Form’. The last one establishes the Modeling protocols for the project and 
contains the basic definitions together with a standardized responsibility matrix. The latest 
attempt was by BIMForum, which has as a mission to facilitate and accelerate the adaptation of 
BIM in the AEC industry[12]. Within that effort, BIMForum released the document ‘Level of 
Development Specification 2015’. That Specification started being released in 2013 and since 
then is updating in a yearly base, with the latest version of 2015, which contains the term Level 
of Development (LOD) in a more detailed approach compared to the previous documents. 
Within that Specification, an attempt is made to clarify more in detail the design and information 
demands for various building systems.  
Even both Vico Software and AIA use the abbreviation LOD, the first one refers to the term ‘Level 
of Detail’ while the second one to ‘Level of Development’. BIMForum makes a clear distinction 
between the terms ‘Level of Detail’ and ‘Level of Development’. More specific, BIMForum 
Specification defines that Level of Detail (LODt) is ‘how much detail is included in the model 
element’ while Level of Development (LOD) is ‘the degree to which the elements geometry and 
attached information has been thought though’. In other words, Level of Detail can be thought 
as the input, while Level of Development as the output [5]. 
A high Level of Detail does not necessarily mean that it has been sufficiently developed for 
particular uses [13]. A very detailed object, in terms of geometry, is not necessarily in an also 
high Level of Development, considering information and properties attached to it. LOD, 
according to the BIMForum specification, is a combination of equally developed geometry and 
information of each model element. That means that even if a model element is visually highly 
detailed, while the level of development is low, then that object will be classified at a low LOD.  
As Lighthard and Succar mention, LOD is considered to consist of two indices: reliability, which 
is the degree of the information that the users can rely on, and specificity, which is the degree 
of geometrical and information accuracy [4].That means that even the specificity may be high, 
the reliability may be at low levels. In order to have high levels of reliability, the content of an 
LOD framework should be specific and the requirements (both graphical and non- graphical) of 
each level should be connected with a specific use case. The use case (3D coordination, energy 
analysis, cost estimation, fire safety, etc.) defines which properties should be attached in each 
model in order to perform the specific use case with success. The users, only that way, can rely 
on it as context of reliability. Moreover, the aimed LOD of a model element may vary in the 
various phases of a building project, which does not mean that each LOD level is corresponding 
at a specific design phase. In the same design phase, the model may include elements at various 
LODs.

At the same time, a specified LOD helps with avoiding over - modeling. Some studies has shown 
that the LOD has an impact on the modelling effort and required time. Research results have 
shown that the total modeling time from one level to another ranges from doubling to eleven 
times the effort [14]. However, such additional effort leads to better decision making during 
design and construction. However, in these studies, the term LOD refers only to the level of the 
geometrical detail and not the Level of Development, which contains both graphical and non – 
graphical information.  
In general, Level of Development (LOD) can be used as a parameter that makes clear the content 
of a Building Information Model. That means, that depending on the particular purpose of the 
model, LOD address which components, on what geometrically accuracy they should be design 
and which information should be attached to it. The LOD framework allows the participants to 
effectively communicate one another and define for model elements the minimum content 
requirement for each LOD[13].  
 Different Approaches of LOD 
Apart from the LOD definition by the AIA and the BIMForum LOD Specification, among other 
countries has been an effort to address the LOD and create an escalating system of it, which 
corresponds to steps from approximate to highly detailed model elements. Various industry 
organizations and major government organizations have created groups that work on the 
adaptation of BIM within the Architecture, Engineering, Construction, and Facility Management 
industry and developed guidelines. Moreover, companies have addressed their own definition 
of LOD and requirements, depending on the experience of the company and in order to create 
a common language within the company.   
However, the LOD definitions by the AIA and the BIMForum LOD Specification are assumed to 
be the most important, as they have been adapted by most of the BIM standards worldwide or 
at least consist the base of LOD definition of other BIM Guidelines.   
 
AIA – BIMForum  
The first, stand - alone, document published in the United States of America (USA) was the ‘E202-
2008 Building Information Modeling Protocol’ by the American Institute of Architects (AIA). The 
purpose of that document was to: 
‘establish the protocols, expected levels of development, and authorized uses of Building 
Information Models on the project and assign specific responsibility for the development of 
each model element to a defined Level of Development at each Project Phase’[15]. 
The American Institute of Architects through that document defines the Level of Development 
(LOD) as ‘the Level of completeness that the Model Element is developed’ [15]. As Model Element 
is defined a portion of the Building Information Model representing a component, system or 
assembly within a building or building site[15].

Since the ‘E202-2008 Building Information Modeling Protocol’ document, the term of LOD has 
been refined until nowadays with the latest ‘BIMForum LOD specification 2015’, which is an 
interpretation of the definitions of the previous ‘E202-2008’ and ‘G202-2013’ documents. In that 
document, the characteristics for model elements of different buildings systems are addressed 
in different Levels of Development. Through textual description and graphical representation, 
this specification makes clearer the LOD framework and standardize its use.  
The BIMForum LOD specification 2015, same as the ‘G202-2013’ document, identifies five 
progressively detailed levels of completeness. Each level is based on the previous one but every 
time including new characteristics, creating a more and more rich in information model element. 
The basic difference that distinguishes a level from the previous one, is that the previous one 
should not satisfy the requirement of the upcoming one. In total, six levels have been address 
starting from LOD100 to LOD500, with a step of 100 each time and the additional, in - between, 
LOD350. In the following table, the LODs are shown with each specific characteristics according 
to the specification.

Bips 3D Working Methods 
The ‘3D Working Method 2006’ publication is the first Danish instructions as part of the Digital 
construction (Det Digitale Byggeri) [2]. The aim of that document, similarly to the other national 
guidelines, is to support and coordinate the overall exchange requirement within Building 
Information Models, by developing common concepts and workflows.  
Within that publication, seven (7) Information Levels are defined, in order to express how 
concretely specified and precise the construction objects in each disciplines are[2]. The 
Information Levels, according to 3D Working Method 2006, address not only the Level of 
geometrical Details, but also the attributes attached to each element, without addressing 
though the required attributes one by one. The seven levels correspond roughly to the 
traditional construction phases, but the levels can be customized to other phased processes and 
methods[2]. 
It should be mentioned that the current document correlates the information levels with the 
disciple models. However, only one discipline model is show in the document for simplicity. For 
each Information level, a graphical discipline model is accompanied with two tables, one 
containing the type of the construction objects and the other one the construction object 
attributes.

 Automated Rule – Based Checking 
As BIM has evolved throughout the years and more and more BIM platforms support design, 
fabrication, contracting, and other processes throughout all the design phases of a building, 
more complex and detailed building models can be produced. The growing number of 
regulations, codes and standards have increased the scope and the complexity of a building 
project [7].  
Until recently, the only way of dealing with such complexity and increasing knowledge was the 
human cognition and organizational review processes [8]. However, since the complexity has 
evolved that much, the users of the BIM tools cannot rely just on the visual inspection of the 
BIM model in order to ensure good quality or whether the requirements are fulfilled or not. 
These requirement do not have to do only with the geometrical complexity but also with the 
properties attached to each model element. The effort for automated rule - based systems 
began in the late 1980s and it has been adding a huge value to the AEC industry as manual 
checking process has been proved to be time - consuming, expensive, not totally accurate and 
emitting errors.  
Automated checking can be applied either within a building modeling tool (Autodesk Revit, 
ArchiCAD) or within platforms that can read building models (IFC platforms, Navisworks, Solibri). 
BIM design tools do not provide model checking themselves, so plug-ins can be added that 
enable the designers to check during the design process the model. However, the capabilities of 
such extensions are limited. The second and most used approach is the IFC based model viewer 
platforms, where rule - based checking takes place and the platforms do not change or modify 
the building design. It is a BIM based application used in order to validate building designs in 
various stages of a building project. As Eastman mentioned: 
 ‘Automated rule checking is defined as software that does not modify a building design, but 
rather assesses a design on the basis of the configuration of objects, their relations or 
attributes’[8]. 
Since the exchange of BIM data among different applications is so big, a neutral format for 
interoperability was needed. With the development of the Information Foundation Classes (IFC) 
in the 1990s, this building model schema could be used for checking. IFC is a neutral data model 
that is supported by most BIM tools. IFC is developed by BuildingSMART® and represents an 
open specification for BIM data that is exchanged and shared among the various participants in 
a building construction management project. The main purpose of the IFC is support sharing 
information in an effective and standardized way of supporting exchange information. 
Moreover, in financial terms, a study from the 2004 National Institute of Standards and 
technology estimated that the inadequate interoperability costs 15.8 billion dollars annually [7]. 
Rule – based systems apply rules, constrains or conditions, with results such as ‘pass’ , ‘fail’ and 
‘undefined’ depending on the information included [8]. Various rule - based systems for building 
models have been used within the last decades, but still are considered to be at an evolving 
stage. An initial effort was made in Singapore in 1995, with code checking in 2D drawings, which

several years later was switched and name CORENET2. Other efforts that have been made from 
that point, have taken place in Norway with Norwegian Statsbygg, Australia with the Australian 
Building Codes Board and the US with the International Code Council (ICC) [8]. 
Most of the automated checking that have taken place until today have to do with the building 
code and accessibility criteria, as these are the most required in a construction process. In 
general, it could be said that rules may be applied at different levels. At a first, more general, 
level, for general conditions such as building regulations, at a second level, for particular building 
types, and at a third level for specific building project, space requirements, circulations and other 
criteria. However, work has been made also for more specific problem domains [8]. Some 
examples that have been reported are, safety checking during construction by Zhang et al-, 
automated code checking application for water distribution by Martins, HVAC systems by Liu, 
building envelope designs by Tan and many others[6]. Even though there are ruleset that imply 
to the entire model (e.g. complying with regulation), they do not apply to the whole dataset of 
the building model. No matter of the scale of the building and the extent of the ruleset, each 
rule implies on a subset of the data [6].

Rule - Based Platforms 
One of the most popular software platform used for rule – based checking is Solibri Model 
Checker (SMC). SMC is a java-based desktop platform application and applies its rules to IFC 
building models. Solibri enables the user with a variety of checking, such as accessibility, space

Solibri Model Checker is a BIM Quality Assurance (QA) tool that analyzes Building Information 
Models (BIMs) for integrity, quality and physical security. It highlights clashes between 
components, checks if the model complies with building codes, regulations and standards.

Additionally, SMC includes functionalities for Information Takeoff (ITO), analyzing and extracting 
the required information in the BIM models. SMC can federate models and respond quickly to 
any changes in the developing standards for production, distribution and classification.  
One primary capability of that platform is that it enables the user to identify and manage 
practical problems based on regulations and design requirements. SMC enables the users to 
check a BIM file against a set of rules, identify and report potential problems found. Even though 
Solibri MC is widely used, and many checking ruleset can take place, it lacks of an open 
environment of writing rules. Solibri has more than 50 Rulesets that can check a model. 
However, apart from these Rulesets, costume-made Rulesets can be created. These Rulesets can 
be created either from scratch or by modifying existing ones. The rulesets are grouped in Roles. 
A Role is a grouping of resources (Rulesets, classifications, ITO) in order to perform functions to 
support specific BIM cases. After selecting the Role, the required resources associated with the 
use case are loaded. SMC contained several Roles, depending on the way the models are wanted 
to be checked. Each role has some default parameters, but it can always be modified by the 
user. Once the Role is selected and loaded, several Rules that correspond on that Role are loaded 
too. However, rules can be disabled or even removed. Each Role can change its parameters and 
be customized and adjusted to the users’ needs. The parameters of the rule can be modified at 
the checking layout and the new rule can be saved.  
A Rule can check a model from a single aspect (e.g. spaces are aligned with surrounding walls) 
or from specific point of view (e.g. usage of correct construction types). Also, there are rules that 
report key characteristics (e.g. list of window types and size) of a building. Moreover, there are 
rules that check if the model complies with building codes (accessibility). 
SMC assigns a severity level to every result when an issue is found. During the checking process, 
the results of the issues are separated into three different severity levels: critical, moderate and 
low. The aim of the severity levels is to prioritize the issues and determine which issues should 
be fixed first.

Chapter 3 
Framework and Methodology 
The chapter ‘Methodology’ describes the overall process followed in order to perform the 
automated rule – based checking for the LOD using a specific building model as a study case. 
The building model is a multi - disciplinary model of DTU Building 211. The automated rule – 
based checking that takes place is concentrated on the HVAC systems of the building model. The 
methodology describes firstly the created LOD framework used for the automated rule – based 
checking. Therefore, the methodology followed corresponds to the first two steps of the general 
methodology followed in research papers, as mentioned in subsection ‘Automated rule – based 
checking’. In the current chapter, the ‘Building Model preparation’ stage and the translation of 
the LOD framework to rules, that be executed in Solibri, are described. 
 
LOD Framework 
In the chapter ‘Background’ an overall study of various LOD approaches from different countries 
has been made. The definition of LOD according to BIMForum Specification 2015 has been 
selected as a basis of the created LOD framework that will be checked through rule – based 
process in Solibri Model Checker for the building model used as a study case.  
As mentioned in the subsection 2.2.1 ‘AIA – BIMForum’, the BIMForum Specification 2015 
consists of two parts, A and B. The first part addresses the graphical content of specific model 
elements. The graphical content has to do on how much graphical detail the model element is 
designed. Starting from a very rough – outline design at LOD100 the graphical content gradually 
escalates to the highest LOD500, when the model element is designed as - built. The second part 
is a workbook that lists all the possible information attached to each model elements. However, 
according even to the most recent version of the BIMForum Specification (2015), it is not 
addressed which properties should be attached in each model element at each LOD.  
The idea of the current automated rule – based checking is to validate whether the model 
elements are designed according to the graphical and non- graphical requirements set for each 
==PAGE:50==
Chapter 3 
Framework and Methodology 
30 
 
of the created LOD framework or not. That means that the LOD framework and therefore the 
checking, consist of two parts similar to the two parts of the BIMForum specification; firstly on 
a graphical level and secondly on a non – graphical level. The LODs that are investigated in the 
current study case are the LOD200, LOD300 and LOD350. The specific scaling of LOD derives 
from the BIMForum Specification 2015. LOD100 and LOD400 are not being examined, as they 
correspond to too low and too high detail model elements respectively.  
 Part A. Graphical Content Requirements 
In order to address the required graphical content, information from the BIMForum 
Specification 2015 (Part A) is obtained. Additionally to the specification, the document ‘Guides, 
Instructions and Commentary to the 2013 AIA Digital Practice Documents’, which is a guidance 
to the various Documents (E202-2008, G202-2013) published by the AIA concerning the use of 
BIM, is used. The last one proposes which components should be included in the design and 
which can be emitted on various building systems, including the HVAC system, on each LOD. 
Since in the specific study case the LODs examined are the LOD200, LOD300 and LOD350, only 
information concerning those three LODs are taken.  
The idea behind these graphical requirements is to avoid over - modeling when aiming at low 
LODs or to indicate missing components at higher LODs. More specific, some components, often 
those whose size is small and do not influence the space requirements, can be emitted in the 
LOD200 as the detail level is desired to be low and just a rough design of the model is required.

requirements addressed by a specific existing LOD framework, but they are representative. Since 
some requirements can be automatically checked, then it is expected that any other 
requirements can be checked through a similar automatic rule - based checking process. 
Since most of the guidelines do not address at all and some address on too much extent the non 
– graphical requirements on each LOD, in the current study case some specific non - graphical 
information are being checked and derive mostly from the Danish Specifications, Dikon and the

 Model Preparation Stage  
In order to perform the automated rule – based checking for the created LOD framework in 
Solibri MC, as a study case the model of building 211 of DTU Campus is used. The building 211 – 
DTU is used by the department of Chemistry and is located in the center of one of the new DTU 
Lyngby Campus network centers. The building acts like a connector between the two existing 
buildings 206 and 207, as it is connected to their western ends. The overall area of the building 
is more than 3.200 m2 and is used for research and educational reasons [29].  
The building model is designed in Autodesk Revit 2015© and consists of several models, one for 
each design discipline. In order to perform the automated checking, these files are exchanged 
and imported in Solibri Model Checker. 
The specific building model is not clarified on what LOD was aimed to be designed in terms of 
both graphical and non – graphical content. Moreover, the building model might also has been 
designed in other LOD requirements, defined differently than the approach used in the current 
thesis. Since the building model is not designed for the specific kind of checking, it should firstly 
be set up and be suitable to perform the aimed checking.

 Model Exchange 
The first step that has to take place in order to perform all the desired automated rule – based 
checking is to create the appropriate file types that can be imported in the checking platform. 
Solibri Model Checker imports models from an IFC or DWG format. The most preferred import 
is the one with the IFC file, as several problems have been reported when importing a DWG file 
[28]. IFC is a format that provides universal transfer of BIM models between different software 
products.  
In the current study case, the building model consists in total of four Revit files, each one 
corresponding at one of the Architectural, Structural, Electrical and Building Services disciplines. 
Each model is designed in the corresponding template in Autodesk Revit 2015©. The basis for 
the exchange process are these Revit models. In order to import the building model in Solibri 
MC, the Revit files are converted into IFC files. The discipline models are imported in Revit

 Identification of HVAC Components 
Since it is ensured that all the models correspond to the correct discipline, the components 
included in the Building Services discipline are filtered from the rest discipline models. 
Moreover, in the Building Services model, except for the HVAC system, more systems is expected 
to be found, like Plumbing, Sanitary, Lighting and other systems. Since the study is concentrated 
only in the HVAC systems, the components of these systems are distinguished from the rest 
components found in the Building Services discipline model and therefore excluded from the 
further checking. 
In order to make that separation, the model elements found in the Building Services discipline 
are listed according to their ‘Component Name’ attribute. For the components that is not clear 
to which system correspond further identity information are used. Pipes and pipe fittings, for 
example, may be found in both HVAC and plumbing systems. Similarly, more components have 
to be further clarified on which system they belong. All these model elements are identified by 
other attributes, such as ‘Mechanical System Classification’. For the components that cannot be 
identified by their ‘Component Name’ (e.g. Objects) or the ‘Mechanical System Classification’,

 Classification Tasks 
Having prepared the building model and more specifically identified the model elements that 
correspond to the HVAC systems, the automatic checking for the LODs can take place. The 
automated checking consists of two parts. The first part has to do with the graphical content 
and the second part with the non-graphical content of each model element. Firstly, the 
requirement for both graphical and non – graphical content are addressed and therefore, the 
rules that check those two aspects are created.   
The HVAC system consists of smaller systems with various components, such as air distribution 
components, hydronic pipe distribution components and other installation components (water 
tanks, ventilators, radiators and others). All the identified model elements that are part of the 
HVAC systems, they are divided into ‘sub-disciplines’ models in order to create an easier and 
more organized way of checking. That separation of components is also according to the one 
used by BIMForum Specification: Part A. That goal can be achieved by creating in SMC a 
classification system that splits the model elements into three sub – discipline models.  
SMC provides some existing classification systems in order to organize the model elements. 
However, the user can create costume made classification systems for the components found

classification system distinguishes the model elements of the HVAC systems from the rest found 
on the Building Services disciple model. Those found in the HVAC discipline are separated into 
three sub-disciplines. The second classification system distinguishes the model elements among 
the three LODs, according to the graphical content addressed.

Rules Translation Stage 
The created LOD framework, which contains the graphical and non – graphical requirements, is 
translated into rules that are performed in Solibri Model Checker. The two parts that constitute 
the automated checking process is performed by the use of rules and rulesets in Solibri and by 
giving the right structure to them. The created ruleset consists of four steps. The first, parent, 
rule hosts all the rules that check both graphical and non-graphical content of the selected 
model elements (step 1). The next step consists of rules that filter the model elements in sub - 
disciplines, according to classification task 2 (step 2). For each of the sub - disciplines, a sub - 
ruleset is created that filters the model elements according to the graphical requirements of the 
three LODs (step 3). The components that pass the graphical requirements are further examined 
for the non-graphical content (step 4). The model elements that pass all the requirements are 
classified on a specific LOD.

 Part B: Non - Graphical Content 
For each of the rules that check the graphical information, sub – rules are created that check the 
non-graphical information of each model. These rules are hosted by the rules that filter the 
graphical content of each LOD. That way, the components that pass the requirements of each 
LOD in terms of graphical content are being further checked if they also comply with the non-
graphical requirements of each LOD.  
In order to simplify the process, the requirements are checked in two levels. The first one is to 
check each one of the properties independently and on a second level the property rules are 
interconnected. The independent rules are created to check the non-graphical requirements 
one by one and they are executed regardless the LOD. If all the components fail on a rule which 
corresponds to a requirement of a specific LOD, then none components can be classified at that 
LOD, no matter if they fulfil other requirements or not and the interconnected rule is not needed

to be created. According to the results of each rule when performed independently, a second 
more strict checking process derives. The model elements that pass the first rule are further 
examined for the second one. Those that pass both, are checked for the third one and the 
process is the same for as many as set requirements. That way only the components that pass 
all the requirements are classified at the current LOD. If not, they are examined if they comply 
with the requirements of the next LODs. 
That means that in order a model element to be classified at a specific LOD should pass all the 
requirements. That way, the model elements found in the HVAC of the building model are 
classified at various LODs. However, since the building model was not designed according to 
these LOD requirements, it is expected that not all components will succeed in being classified 
on the same LOD and some may also not be able to be classified at any of the LODs.

 Chapter 5 
Discussion  
 
The chapter ‘Discussion’ includes the important points of the performed automated rule - based 
checking of the created LOD framework at the current study case. The barriers and limitations 
figured are mentioned. Moreover, according to the obtained results it is discussed if such 
checking can be successfully performed for any LOD framework and any building model.  
 
The literature review of the definitions of the LOD has shown that the term is flexible. Even 
though a lot of approaches have been made by regulatory bodies and construction companies, 
many interpretations can be made, even within an already defined LOD framework. The way the 
BIM guidelines introduce LOD remains mostly on theoretical state and does not really take 
advantage of BIM tools and little research has been made beyond that theoretical approach on 
how the term can be utilized in practice [4]. That fact has made Level of Development (LOD) a 
controversial term, that many times has been doubted if it is useful or not and big concern has 
been created on how the LOD levels should be used[4].  
The various approaches of the LOD makes the requirements to change from country to country, 
from organization to organization, from company to company and mostly, from use case to use 
case. The last has the most influence in the LOD, as it defines on a huge extent the required 
information attached in order to perform successfully the specific use case. Since it is not 
possible to create a general framework of defining the LOD content, it is also not possible to 
create a general rule - based checking of LOD that can be widely used.  However, standardized 
rulesets can be developed checking the requirements of a specific LOD framework, such as the 
‘BIMForum Specification 2015’. A similar existing idea is the extension ‘Norway Statsbygg BIM 
==PAGE:96==
Chapter 5 
Discussion 
76 
 
Manual’ provided by Solibri that implements rulesets generated according to that manual, which 
contains the generic requirements for BIM in projects[31]. 
As mentioned in the subsection 2.1, LOD consists of two indices: reliability and specificity. Since 
the use case of the provided building model used as a study case was not known, the created 
LOD framework does not check the reliability of the model elements. However, it checks the 
specificity, according to the requirements set at the created LOD framework. The reliability can 
be checked through rule - based checking as soon as the use case and the corresponding LOD 
framework are specific. That way, the delivered LOD of model elements can be validated 
whether it is the same with the desired one or not.  
Beyond the specific needs of the use case, there is one stage (‘Building Model Preparation’) in 
the whole process of the checking process, which does not allow the created rules be applied 
directly to any building model. Similarly here, the building model used as a study case had to be 
prepared properly in order to perform the automated rule - based checking and make sure that 
all the desired model elements are included. More specifically, in the current study, a lot of time 
and effort was spent in order to identify the desired model elements, that would be further 
checked for the LOD. The reason for that is that the building model has not followed specific 
design requirements in order to proceed in such checking. Model elements contain different 
kind of information, which had be explored and find the common attributes in order to identify 
all components. More specific, some model elements were found to be classified according to 
the Omniclass Classification System, some to the CCS Classification System, some to both and 
some to none. The identification process of the components was based on the findings each 
time until all the desired components were identified. In general, that step restricts on a big 
extend the level of automation but not skipped at all. 
That process could be emitted or restricted in a building model designed according to a specific 
design requirements. As mentioned in other research papers, LOD is much more useful when 
used within a MPS that addresses in specific the required information needed to be exchanged 
within a BIM model [4]. A delivery specification should be created in the start, even before 
creating the building model. Having followed the delivery specification, the model is supposed 
to be designed properly and then it can be proceeded into any validation tool.  
In the study case used in the current thesis, the created LOD framework consists of some 
graphical and non - graphical requirements for each level. As shown in the Chapter 4.2.1, in 
terms of graphical content, considering the requirements set at Table 12, the Ventilation and 
Heating distributions system covered the minimum graphical requirements for all three LODs. 
However, following the requirement of the BIMForum Specification 2015, that each LOD should 
not satisfy the requirements of the next one, the ventilation and heating distribution systems 
are classified graphically at the LOD350 [5]. Regarding the installation components, the 
components do not fulfill the same graphical requirements. Nine (9) installation components 
are at the LOD200 and the rest fifty one (51) at LOD300 and above. 
In general, even though the graphical requirements were successfully performed in the study 
case, the Level of Graphical Detail is hard to be checked within a checking tool, like Solibri Model 
Checker. More specific, it is not possible to check whether the components are designed on 
==PAGE:97==
Chapter 5 
Discussion 
77 
 
design – specified or actual size and shape. What can be checked graphically is the existence or 
not of model elements, but it is not possible to check whether all the required components are 
modeled or not. For example, if aiming to design at LOD350, as defined in BIMForum 
Specification Part A, the extra components (hangers, supports, controls, etc.) can be checked 
whether they exist in the model or not, but it is not possible to check whether all the 
components are designed or not. Similarly in the current study case, the graphical requirements 
(e.g. duct accessories, void provisions) to step from the LOD200 to LOD300 and LOD350 was 
possible to be checked. However, it is not possible to check that all desired components exist in 
the model. Moreover, the void provisions found in the ventilation distribution system do not 
assure that the model is also clash free. That had to be confirmed though a clash detection rule 
between the ductwork and the architectural and structural elements.  
In terms of non-graphical content, the model elements are examined and classified at LODs one 
by one. Since each type of components has different non – graphical requirements, it is not 
possible to check a whole building system (Heating / Ventilation), as performed for the graphical 
requirements. The properties attached should be checked for each component of the same type. 
In the current study case, model elements were found at various LODs and some did not fulfill 
the requirements for any of the LOD levels. However, most components were according to the 
LOD300. These results were expected to be obtained since the LOD framework was created 
independently from the study case, which, in its turn, was not designed in order to fulfill these 
requirements. However, all the aimed non - graphical requirements were possible to be 
performed in Solibri. 
The non - graphical requirements are easier to be checked. Since the properties that should be 
attached is defined, the checking process can be performed. For the selected non -graphical 
properties, all rules were successfully created and it is expected that any other information 
requirement can be successfully checked. However, still wrong results may be obtained. For 
example, in the current study case the ductwork checked for a specific insulation type should be 
carefully done, since all insulated ductwork had filled the property set ‘Insulation Type’. 
However, the text was ‘default’. That could lead to misunderstanding as the property set 
contains information, which do not give a specific information content. That is one aspect that 
also restricts the level of automation of the process and makes the coordinator to check 
manually similar cases.  
Even though Solibri allows the user add various parameters in one rule, the execution of the 
rules considering the non – graphical requirements was selected to be performed one by one 
for each parameter. The purpose of that was to create a clearer content of the performed rules 
and understand how each one influences the final results.   
One of the important drawbacks of Solibri was observed during the reporting stage of the 
obtained results. Solibri does not enable the user to create ITOs according to the issues found 
for each model element. Even though when executing multiple rules, the information tab of the 
model elements includes all the issues found according to the rules performed, they cannot be 
reported all together. Finally, it should be taken into consideration that Solibri Model Checker, 
as a tool, does not open the source code and allow user development. That means that the 
==PAGE:98==
Chapter 5 
Discussion 
78 
 
limitations are a lot in terms of extending the validation features. Moreover, Solibri cannot edit 
or add any parameters in the IFC model.  
In general, many limitations are found in the developed checking of the LOD through automated 
rule - based checking and specifically within Solibri. However, most of them were due to the 
undefined use case of the building model. The lack of the preparation of the building model for 
such checking had the biggest influence in terms of limitation. Supposing the building model is 
designed according to specific LOD requirements and within a delivery specification, the 
checking can be performed much faster and more efficiently. Moreover, regular checking of the 
LOD requirements during the design process of the building model before passing the final 
model to the validation BIM tools, can prove to be useful. Such approach can save time and 
effort for the building model preparation stage and also the issues found, regarding the 
compliance or not with the desired LOD, will be less. That way, the rule - based checking can be 
executed faster, as the building model preparation stage within Solibri will require less time and 
the results will be more successful.  
While the graphical content checking is more limited, the non-graphical content of an LOD 
framework can be checked in a much wider extent. In general, the specific study case with the 
created LOD framework can act as an indicator on how much similar checking can succeed and 
it showed that in properly prepared building models may be performed much faster and obtain 
results with less issues.

Chapter 6 
Further Work 
 BCF 
When the validation process of model elements within an LOD framework has not succeeded, 
that can be changed. One way that the building model can be improved is through the BIM 
Collaboration Format (BCF). BCF is the open Standard for Building Issues and its format is based 
on XML schemas and allows the communication between BIM tools. BCF encodes messages that 
inform one BIM tool of issues found by another [32]. The use of BCF eliminates the need for 
transferring large BIM files. The issues found in the IFC file in Solibri MC can be reported and 
fixed. For an issue found in a BIM validation tool (Solibri Model Checker) and a BIM design tool 
(Autodesk Revit), the process is as follows [33]: 
1.  In the validation tool (Solibri Model Checker) examine the model and create a message 
for a specific issue found on component of interest 
2. Send the BCF report to the BIM design tools (e.g. Autodesk Revit which should have the 
equivalent plug-in).  
3. Open message / comments and take action. There the issues can be easily found, as the 
tool drives to user directly to the area of interest. The issue can be easily understood 
and fixed.  
4. Send back the changes and additions. The BCF file can be saved again with all the fixed 
issues and send back to the validation tool. However, the changes can only be displayed 
in the validation tool and not added to the IFC file. For that reason, it is possibly needed 
the creation of a new IFC model within the design platform with all the additional 
information and be checked again in the validation tool.  
Following that steps, a building model can be improved according to any design requirements. 
Similarly, in the case of validating the LOD, the missing or extra information found in any model 
element can be reported and sent to the designers, who with their turn will added or removed 
information according to the requirements. 
==PAGE:100==
Chapter 6 
Further Work 
80 
 
 MVD 
A further step to achieve that aim of automated checking of specific information, is through the 
creation of IDMs. The IDM method usually uses IFC models and the output is a MVD (Model 
View Definitions). MVD IS developed by Building Smart and groups model contents, a subset of 
the IFC, identifying objects and their properties.  
SimpleBIM is an IFC reader tool that offers a new approach to the MVD definition and can be 
used to validate IFC files, edit data within it and also enrich an IFC model. SimpleBIM imports 
the IFC file exported from the design platform, restructures the model in a way that it satisfies 
the requirements and exports the IFC file [34]. SimpleBIM provides the users with templates 
that are Excel files with predefined sets of Worksheets. Through them and more specific the tab 
‘Enrichment’ the users can modify or use as they are in order to enrich the model. Within the 
templates, the enrichment tab lets the user add data from external sources into the model by 
setting property values to objects that match a given criteria [35]. In general, the whole process 
followed in Solibri Model Checker can be executed in SimpleBIM. Starting from the validation of 
the existing to model, to adding or removing any information according to the requirements.

Chapter 7 
Conclusion 
What can be concluded is that the automated rule – based checking of an LOD framework can 
be successfully done on a specific extend since the limitations are a lot. Firstly, the building 
model preparation stage makes the process need adaptation at each study case and does not 
enable the use of the same rules in any building model. That stage was the most time consuming 
in the study case, but it is expected to require less time when the building model is already 
designed according to a specific design requirements.  
In the current thesis, the translation of both graphical and non – graphical requirements of the 
created LOD framework to rules was successfully performed in Solibri Model Checker. However, 
many graphical requirements, even not used in the study case, is hard to be checked through 
rule – based checking in Solibri and that restricts the level of automation. Regarding the non – 
graphical requirements, even though a simplified framework of LOD was used in the study case, 
it is expected that the rule - based checking can be extended in any other requirements. The 
results of the rules of the created LOD framework in the study case showed that not all elements 
were not corresponding to the same LOD requirements, as expected. Some components were 
classified at LOD300, some at LOD350 and some at any at all.  
In general, the automated rule - based checking of LOD, even though with limitations, can 
confirm if the delivered LOD of the model elements is the same with the desired one. According 
to the investigation of the various LOD approaches and the study case, it can be concluded that 
is important to have an addressed use case of the building model. That way, the LOD framework 
can act as a quality control tool and address the reliability of the model. Working in the right and 
agreed LOD can save time and effort and maximize the usefulness of a BIM Model.
