Abstract
Keywords: Sustainability; Wine Industry; Wine Supply Chain; Wine Suppliers; Car-
bon Data Transparency
The wine industry is under increasing pressure to provide transparent and accur-
ate carbon data, driven by regulatory frameworks such as the EU’s CSRD and voluntary
initiatives such as SBTi, leading to increased sustainability demands from B2B customers.
However, SMEs, especially in import-driven markets like Denmark, face significant chal-
lenges in meeting these expectations. These challenges include limited internal resources,
fragmented data flows, and a reliance on generic emission factors from public databases
such as Den Store Klimadatabase. Such generalized values fail to reflect product-specific
differences in production methods, packaging formats, and transportation routes, leading to
non-representative carbon data, undermining both credibility and comparability.
This thesis explores how wine suppliers can overcome these barriers by developing a
practical support framework tailored to their operational realities. Guided by the DRM, the
study follows a structured process of research clarification, descriptive analysis, prescriptive
design, and evaluation.
Systematic literature reviews and stakeholder interviews identi-
fied the stages of the wine supply chain, four key carbon hotspots—bottling, viticulture,
transport, and vinification—along with nine high-impact parameters that drive emissions
variability across different wine products and their availability throughout the supply chain.
Despite the availability of LCA tools, a gap remains for simplified, user-friendly
solutions that are accessible to SMEs. In response, two complementary interventions were
developed: (1) a conceptual readiness framework that maps the wine supply chain, highlights
carbon hotspots, and clarifies data ownership, and (2) a simplified Excel-based tool for carbon
data collection and calculation. Together, these tools enable suppliers to estimate product-
level carbon footprints of wine products more accurately by reducing reliance on generic
emission factors and clustering products with similar characteristics, ultimately enhancing
transparency.
The tool enables suppliers to estimate product-level carbon footprints, encouraging
a clustering approach by grouping products with similar characteristics, thereby reducing
reliance on generic emission factors while improving representativeness and transparency.
Evaluation through a fictional case and qualitative feedback from industry stake-
holders confirmed the framework’s clarity, usability, and alignment with sector needs. While
not a substitute for full LCAs, the interventions offer a scalable and accessible entry point for
carbon accounting, particularly for SMEs with limited technical capacity. The framework
supports internal decision-making, supplier engagement, and external reporting, aligning
with emerging regulatory requirements and market expectations.
Ultimately, this thesis contributes to bridging the gap between sustainability the-
ory and practice in the wine industry. It lays the groundwork for future digital tools and
sector-wide standardization, helping wine suppliers proactively respond to evolving sustain-
ability demands while supporting a transition toward more responsible and resilient business
practices.

Glossary
For improved understanding, short definitions of each of the main terms used through-
out this thesis are organised alphabetically below. While the terms will be further explained
throughout the thesis, this section provides a helpful glossary for the reader to reference.
Term
Definition
Carbon footprint
The total GHG emissions caused directly or indirectly by a product,
process, or organization, usually expressed in CO2e.
CO2e
A unit for expressing the climate impact of different greenhouse
gases in terms of the amount of CO2 that would have the same
global warming potential.
GHG emissions
Emissions of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO2) and
methane (CH4) that contribute to global warming.
Scope 1 emissions
Direct GHG emissions from operations that are owned or controlled
by the reporting company.
Scope 2 emissions
Indirect GHG emissions from the generation of purchased electri-
city, steam, heating, or cooling consumed by the reporting com-
pany.
Scope 3 emissions
All other indirect GHG emissions (not included in Scope 2) that
occur in the value chain of the reporting company, including both
upstream and downstream emissions.
Carbon data collection
The process of gathering relevant data inputs needed to estimate
carbon emissions.
Carbon hotspot
A stage or process in the product life cycle that contributes dispro-
portionately to the total carbon footprint.
Upstream activities
Life cycle stages that include raw material extraction, production,
and transportation to the point of manufacture.
Downstream activities
Life cycle stages that include product distribution, usage, and end-
of-life disposal.
Emission factor
A coefficient that quantifies the emissions associated with a specific
activity, material, or energy source, usually expressed in kg CO2e
per unit (e.g., per km, per kg).
Wine supplier
Any entity that imports, distributes, or supplies wine to other act-
ors in the supply chain, such as retailers, restaurants, or hotels.

Introduction
Over the past decades, environmental awareness has grown significantly, prompting
industries to re-evaluate their sustainability strategies (Merli et al., 2018a; Zambelli et al.,
2023a). As climate challenges intensify, the urgency to reduce environmental impact and
align with global goals such as the Paris Agreement has never been greater (Carrasco et al.,
2021; Ferrer et al., 2023; Wagner et al., 2023). Among the sectors facing this pressure is the
wine industry, a culturally rich and economically significant part of the agri-food domain
(Alessandri et al., 2024; Montalvo-Falcón et al., 2023; Letamendi et al., 2022). With deep
historical roots and substantial international trade value, the global wine industry plays a
vital role in many economies (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; Wagner et al., 2023). However,
despite its often benign image as an environmentally safe product, the wine industry both
contributes to and is affected by climate change (Carrasco et al., 2021; Christ & Burritt,
2013). Rising temperatures, shifting weather patterns, and water scarcity are already im-
pacting grape cultivation and wine production, influencing both yield and quality (Baiano,
2021; Ferrer et al., 2023). At the same time, greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions such as carbon
dioxide (CO2) are generated across multiple stages of the wine supply chain (Arcese et al.,
2012; Bonamente et al., 2016; Marques & Teixeira, 2023; Wagner et al., 2023).
The urgency of addressing climate change has intensified the demand for transpar-
ent and accurate carbon data across industries (Guerra et al., 2024; Marco-Fondevila et al.,
2020). Regulatory frameworks such as the EU’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Direct-
ive (CSRD) and voluntary initiatives like the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) now
require companies to disclose their environmental performance, including Scope 1, 2, and 3
emissions (Pölz & Rosner, 2023). Large business-to-business (B2B) customers setting ambi-
tious climate targets place increasing pressure on suppliers to provide product-level carbon
data to meet their reporting obligations. This dynamic is particularly evident in the wine
sector, where suppliers must now deliver detailed and verifiable emissions data to maintain
market access (Wagner et al., 2023; Alko, 2023; Carrasco et al., 2021).
However, many wine suppliers, especially small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)
in import-driven markets like Denmark, face significant challenges in meeting these demands
(Wagner et al., 2023). The wine supply chain is inherently complex, involving diverse pro-
duction methods, packaging formats, and transport routes (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021).
Conducting full Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) for each product is often too costly and time-
consuming for SMEs, who frequently lack the internal resources and methodological guidance
to do so (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). As a result, many companies rely on generic emission
factors from public databases, which fail to reflect the specific characteristics of individual
wine products (Tsalidis et al., 2022). This undermines the accuracy and transparency of
carbon reporting, limits the identification of emissions hotspots, and weakens the credibil-
ity of sustainability reporting (Guerra et al., 2024; Silva & Silva, 2022). Consequently, the
responsibility for data collection and reporting is increasingly falling on upstream suppliers,
who must now navigate fragmented data, limited resources, and growing expectations for
credible, product-level carbon footprint data.

2.1
Research Clarification
According to (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009), the RC stage is the crucial first phase
of the DRM. Its primary objective is to establish a clear understanding of the research area,
identify a research problem that is both academically and practically worthwhile and realistic,
and develop an overall research plan that provides direction for the subsequent stages of the
project. In essence, the RC stage lays the foundation for the research. It clarifies what is
already known through initial insights gathered from both existing knowledge (literature)
and engagement with industry stakeholders (interviews). This helps plan how to address
what remains to be solved.
In this study, the RC stage involved an exploratory literature review and a series of
semi-structured interviews and discussions with experts and stakeholders to build a broad
understanding of the research area. These insights helped identify key challenges and op-
portunities for improving current practices and informed the initial concept for a support
framework tailored to wine suppliers. They also contributed to identifying research gaps and
developing a comprehensive plan for the research project. Based on the current understand-
ing, the research questions were formulated to guide the investigation. An overall research
plan was then developed and structured into four work packages (WPs), aligned with the
stages of the DRM, to ensure a systematic and organized approach. To support the project’s
learning outcomes, a set of specific objectives was defined (see Appendix A.4), accompanied
by a visual representation of the research structure (see Figure 7 in Section 3.3.4). Addi-
tionally, a detailed project plan and Gantt chart were created to aid task management and
ensure timely completion of each WP.
2.2
Descriptive Study I
The DS-I stage focuses on gaining a deeper understanding of the existing situation
and identifying the key factors that influence the problem and its context, in order to inform
subsequent design activities. According to (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009), this typically
involves an in-depth literature review and empirical investigations.
DS-I is particularly
valuable for identifying the barriers and enablers that shape the development of effective
support.
In essence, it is about deepening the understanding of "what is" (the current
situation) to lay a solid, evidence-based foundation for designing "what should be" (the
desired situation and the support needed to achieve it).
In this study, the DS-I phase followed an exploratory research approach and fo-
cused on two parallel streams: (i) a systematic literature review (SLR), and (ii) qualitative
empirical data collection through semi-structured and informal interviews with various stake-
holders. The SLR was conducted in accordance with the guidelines proposed by (Biolchini
et al., 2005), following a structured and transparent procedure to ensure reliability, transpar-
ency, and robustness in the analysis process and minimize bias in data selection and extrac-
tion (Nightingale, 2009). Both academic and grey literature were included to supplement
academic literature with industry-based reports and commissioned studies. The decision to
adopt an SLR was based on its ability to establish a robust knowledge base in emerging fields
(Martin & Hanington, 2012), which was particularly relevant given the limited availability of

industry-specific guidance. As SLRs aim to identify all relevant research within a domain and
provide a “balanced and unbiased summary of the literature” (Nightingale, 2009), this method

2.3
Prescriptive Study
The PS is the third core stage of the DRM and builds directly on the understanding
developed during DS-I. While descriptive studies aim to analyse the current state (“as-is”)
or assess the impact of interventions, the PS stage focuses on the systematic development
of support to improve the existing situation and help achieve a desired future state. This
support can take many forms, including tools, guidelines, frameworks, or organizational
recommendations.
As outlined by (Biolchini et al., 2005), this phase typically involves
translating empirical insights and theoretical understanding into tangible support concepts.
The development process is grounded in a clear understanding of the problem context and
is strengthened through logical reasoning, iterative refinement, and ongoing alignment with
stakeholder needs.
In this study, the PS stage involved designing two complementary support interven-
tions aimed at guiding Danish wine suppliers toward more accurate and transparent carbon
data reporting:
• Support Intervention I: A conceptual readiness framework that maps the wine sup-
ply chain, identifies carbon hotspots, and clarifies key data inputs and stakeholder
responsibilities. This visual guide helps suppliers build internal capacity for carbon
footprinting and understand where and how to collect relevant data.
• Support Intervention II: A simplified Excel-based carbon data collection and calculator
tool.
This tool enables suppliers to input high-impact parameters—such as bottle
weight, transport mode and distance—and estimate product-level carbon footprints
using emission factors derived from Ecoinvent. Furthermore, it supports a clustering
approach to simplify carbon reporting across diverse wine portfolios.
Together, these interventions aim to bridge the gap between generalized emission
factors and detailed carbon footprint calculations. They offer a scalable and practical entry
point for SMEs to begin structured carbon accounting without requiring full LCA expertise.
The development of these interventions was informed by literature reviews, expert interviews,

2.4
Descriptive Study II
The DS-II stage focuses on evaluating and validating the effectiveness of the solution
developed during the Prescriptive Study. As described by (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009),
this phase investigates whether the proposed support improves the existing situation and
contributes to achieving the desired future state. Evaluation in DS-II typically involves two
complementary components: Application Evaluation, which assesses whether the support
can be effectively used for the intended task (i.e., its usability, applicability, and relevance),
and Success Evaluation, which examines whether the support has the intended impact and
contributes meaningfully to key success factors.
Finally, in this study, the DS-II stage entailed the application and evaluation of the
developed interventions. This was done through a case simulating a typical wine product
scenario, as well as a qualitative interview with a key industry stakeholder from Østjysk

By setting up a narrative scenario, the example facilitates an exploration
of the framework and interactive tool from the user’s point of view (Martin & Hanington,
2012). The aim was to assess the usability, clarity, and practical relevance of the interven-
tions. The evaluation focused on two key aspects: (1) Application Evaluation—whether the
framework and tool could be effectively used by wine suppliers for the intended task, and
(2) Success Evaluation—whether the interventions contributed meaningfully to improving
carbon data transparency and aligning with industry needs. The findings from this stage
provided valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed support and
informed recommendations for future refinement and implementation.

3.2.1
Introduction to Global Climate Change
Global climate change represents one of the most significant and urgent environ-
mental challenges facing humanity today (Ying et al., 2023). The warming of the climate
system is considered unequivocal, meaning it is beyond doubt (Baiano, 2021).
There is
strong evidence that the rising of the mean global temperature over the last 150 years is a
consequence of the rising atmospheric Greenhouse Gas (GHG) concentration. Human activ-
ities, such as the burning of fossil fuels, are increasing the levels of GHGs in the atmosphere,
causing global warming and climate change (Dessler, 2021). The scientific consensus on cli-
mate change is well established. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC),
a scientific and political organization established within the United Nations framework, is
the primary body synthesizing and disseminating the most advanced knowledge on climate
change, especially global warming, its causes, effects, and risks (Masotti et al., 2022).
The GHGs considered most relevant are those defined by the United Nations (UN)
in the Kyoto Protocol, including carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide
(N2O), among others (Pósleman et al., 2015). These gases contribute to the greenhouse
effect, where air pollutants accumulate in the atmosphere, absorbing solar radiation that
would typically escape into space. This process traps heat, leading to a rise in the planet’s
temperature (D’Ammaro et al., 2021; Carrasco et al., 2021). Among GHGs, carbon dioxide
(CO2) is the most important because it remains in the atmosphere for so long and is the
primary cause of modern climate change (Dessler, 2021).
Addressing climate change is considered one of the biggest challenges facing human
society. The Paris Agreement, with its ambition to keep global warming to a maximum
of 1.5°C, is a starting point for climate work (UNFCCC, 2015). To limit global warming
well below 2°C, rapid, deep, and in most cases immediate GHG emission reductions are
necessary across all sectors. Despite nationally determined contributions, a large emissions
gap remains between current efforts and the recommended emission levels for 2030 to meet
the 1.5°C or 2.0°C targets. Corporate climate action is increasingly considered important
in driving the transition towards a low-carbon economy (Siegl et al., 2023). Adapting to
a changing climate requires strategies implemented at different temporal and spatial scales
(Quénol et al., 2016).

3.2.4
Understanding Wine as a Product
To provide a clear foundation for the subsequent analysis of sustainability in the
wine industry, it is essential to first establish a shared understanding of what wine is.
Wine is one of the most widely consumed alcoholic beverages globally, holding deep
cultural, social, and even religious importance across various civilizations (Ferrara & Feo,
2018; Guerra et al., 2024; Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021). The production of wine is one of
the most ancient economic sectors and continues to be a very important agricultural activity
throughout the world (Arcese et al., 2012). Fundamentally, wine is defined as the beverage
resulting from the total or partial alcoholic fermentation of fresh grapes, whether or not
crushed, or of grape must, with production methods varying based on the desired wine type
(Arcese et al., 2012). This definition is established by entities such as the OIV and the
European Commission (Pattara et al., 2012; Notarnicola et al., 2003). The fermentation
process typically involves the conversion of sugars in the grapes into alcohol, primarily eth-
anol, and carbon dioxide, often driven by natural yeast present on the grapes (Silva & Silva,
2022; Zhang & Rosentrater, 2019).
According to OIV, the actual alcoholic strength of wine typically may not be less
than 8.5% vol., although this can be reduced to 7% vol. in specific regions based on climate,
terroir, grape variety, and traditions (Pattara et al., 2012). The quality of wine is influenced
by both intrinsic factors (physical characteristics like age, color, and aroma) and extrinsic
factors (reputation, appellation, and region) (Vázquez-Rowe et al., 2012). Different types of
wine, such as red, white, rosé, and sparkling wines, have distinct production processes and
target consumers (Tsalidis et al., 2022). Unlike many other agricultural products, wine can
be stored for extended periods, and its value may increase with age (Vázquez-Rowe et al.,
2013; Gonen et al., 2024).
While the fundamental definition of wine is straightforward, the product itself is far
from homogeneous. Wine encompasses a wide array of variations as shown in Table 1 (Silva
& Silva, 2022).

3.2.5
Sustainability in the Wine Industry
Interest in sustainability within the wine sector has risen considerably in recent years,
driven by growing interest from consumers, organizations, and academic researchers (Wagner
et al., 2023; Montalvo-Falcón et al., 2023).
However, understanding what sustainability
entails in the wine world first requires identifying key definitions and principles, as well as
reviewing current practices (the latter will be explored in Section 3.2.6).
The concept of “sustainability” was initially introduced in 1987 in the Brundtland
Report published by the World Commission on Environment and Development (WCED)
(Guerra et al., 2024). This report, titled "Our Common Future", provided a widely cited
definition of sustainable development:
"Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the abil-
ity of future generations to meet their own needs." - (WCED, 1987)
The widely accepted framework for sustainability involves three key dimensions:
environmental, social, and economic. This is often referred to as the "triple bottom line"
approach (Golicic et al., 2017). These three dimensions are considered interconnected and
cannot be considered independently of each other (Wagner et al., 2023). Nowadays, it is
mostly represented as three embedded circles as shown in Figure 4, with the inner circle
representing the economic aspects, the middle circle the social aspects, and the outer circle
the environmental aspects (Lozano, 2008). This emphasizes that each circle is constrained i.e.
the development of the economy is constrained by the society in which it is found, which is
constrained by the ecological limitations of natural resources available to the society (Stopper
et al., 2016).

At the same time, research has shown that wine production contributes to environmental
impacts through both direct and indirect impacts (Santini et al., 2015; Flores, 2018; Ferrer
et al., 2023). As a globally distributed industry of great magnitude, wine has raised aware-
ness about its environmental footprint, particularly in terms of GHG emissions (Christ &
Burritt, 2013; Dede et al., 2020; Letamendi et al., 2022; Scrucca et al., 2017). The broader
agricultural sector is a major contributor to global emissions, with the Agriculture, Forestry,
and Other Land Use (AFOLU) sector accounting for approximately 22% of anthropogenic
GHG emissions, equivalent to around 12.5 Gt CO2-eq annually (IPCC, 2023). The wine
industry alone is estimated to contribute about 0.3% of global emissions, stemming from
direct sources (e.g., diesel use, fertilizer application) and indirect impacts associated with
input production across the wine value chain (IPCC, 2023).
As a result, the industry faces growing pressure to adopt more rigorous approaches for
environmental assessment methods and improve GHG reporting practices (Marco-Fondevila
et al., 2020).
Key environmental issues across the wine supply chain include water use
and pollution and pollution, energy consumption, chemical inputs (pesticides, herbicides,
fertilizers), waste generation, land use impacts on ecosystems, and the production of GHG
emissions. These challenges highlight the dual role of the wine industry as both vulnerable
to climate change and a contributor to environmental degradation.
3.2.6
Sustainable Practices in the Wine Industry
While the concept of sustainability can be complex and sometimes misunderstood
(Baiano, 2021; Szolnoki, 2013), there is a clear and growing movement within the wine in-
dustry toward environmentally sustainable practices (Baiano, 2021; Wagner et al., 2023).
Sustainability concerns span the entire wine supply chain, from vineyard to distribution
(Baiano, 2021; Christ & Burritt, 2013), and the industry’s engagement with these issues is
steadily increasing (Baiano, 2021; Guerra et al., 2024). Various practices are being adopted
across the wine value chain to address environmental challenges and enhance overall sustain-
ability (Wagner et al., 2023). However, like much of the agri-food sector, the wine industry
faces significant challenges in achieving sustainability goals (Guerra et al., 2024; Szolnoki,
2013). The broader issue of sustainable food production has gained considerable attention
in recent years, influencing the wine industry as it adapts to evolving business paradigms
(Alessandri et al., 2024).

A wide range of sustain-

impacts (Wagner et al., 2023). Globally, numerous initiatives have emerged to support com-

3.2.6.2
Specific Sustainability Practices Across the Wine Value Chain
Sustain-
able practices are being integrated throughout the wine production process, from vineyard
management to final distribution, reflecting a collective commitment to environmental stew-

inputs such as diesel, nitrogen-based fertilizers, and phytosanitary products (Silva & Silva,
2022). For instance, fuel consumption from field activities and the production and application
of mineral fertilizers and pesticides are identified as significant sources of GHG emissions in
vineyards (Wagner et al., 2023; Jradi et al., 2018). Organic and biodynamic farming methods
are gaining traction due to their low chemical input and potential to enhance soil and ecosys-
tem health. Additional efforts focus on preserving soil quality, promoting biodiversity, and
improving water use efficiency through micro-irrigation and precision agriculture (Borsato
et al., 2020). Some vineyards are also exploring carbon sequestration through soil and vine
management to mitigate GHG emissions (Silva & Silva, 2022).
In the winemaking phase, energy efficiency and the transition to renewable energy
sources such as solar and wind are central priorities (Gonen et al., 2024; Gierling & Blanke,
2021). Wastewater management is also critical, with solutions ranging from advanced treat-

3.2.6.3
Greenhouse Gas Accounting
A key practice within environmental sustainab-
ility in the wine industry (and other industries) is the accounting of GHG emissions (Silva
& Silva, 2022). It involves the quantification of GHG emissions across the entire life cycle
of a product or the operations of a company (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). The primary goal
is to identify and characterize the main sources of GHG emissions in the supply chain, of-
ten referred to as "hotspots," which are critical for identifying areas of improvement and
developing mitigation strategies (Silva & Silva, 2022). This information supports strategic
decision-making to reduce GHG emissions and transition towards more sustainable business
models (Navarro, Puig & i Palmer, 2017).
To do this, companies often use the GHG Protocol, which is the most widely used
international standard (see Figure 5) (Barry, 2011). For example, Comwell Hotels applies the
GHG Protocol in its sustainability reporting (Comwell, 2023). The GHG Protocol divides
emissions into three "scopes":
• Scope 1: These are direct GHG emissions from sources owned or controlled by the
reporting company. Examples include combustion of fossil fuels in owned vehicles or
equipment, and direct field emissions like N2O from fertilizer application in vineyards
(Pölz & Rosner, 2023). For wineries, this includes diesel and petrol use by company
fleets, fossil fuels for heat/steam, and refrigerant leakages (Ponstein et al., 2019).
• Scope 2: These cover indirect GHG emissions from the generation of purchased elec-
tricity, steam, heat, or cooling consumed by the organization (Pölz & Rosner, 2023).
For many organizations, purchased electricity is a significant component of their GHG
emissions (OIV, 2011).
• Scope 3 (Other Indirect Emissions): This is the broadest category, encompassing
all other indirect GHG emissions that occur in the company’s value chain, both up-
stream and downstream, which are not directly owned or controlled by the company
(Pölz & Rosner, 2023). While Scope 1 and 2 reporting is often considered mandatory,
Scope 3 reporting is typically optional, yet it can contribute greatly to a product’s over-
all carbon footprint (Pölz & Rosner, 2023). For the wine industry, Scope 3 typically

cleaning agents, packaging materials), distribution (transportation not owned by the

in corporate climate action is seen as increasingly urgent and essential for achieving overall
reduction targets (Siegl et al., 2023).

(Winsavvy, 2025). As a result, demonstrating strong sustainability performance, especially
through transparent carbon data, is becoming a distinct competitive advantage for suppliers.

3.2.6.4
Sustainability Assessment Frameworks
The growing need to measure and
reduce environmental impacts has led to increased attention on sustainability assessment
frameworks (Silva & Silva, 2022). These frameworks are essential tools for quantifying, eval-
uating, and managing environmental performance across the product life cycle (Frasnetti et
al., 2024). They help identify areas for improvement and support strategic decision-making
(Fusi et al., 2014). Among the most widely used frameworks are Life Cycle Assessment
(LCA) and Carbon Footprint (CF) analysis, both of which have gained prominence due to
rising environmental concerns, consumer demand for transparency, and ambitious corporate
sustainability goals (Frasnetti et al., 2024; Baiano, 2021). These tools enable businesses to
identify environmental "hotspots" (critical points of impact), thereby informing mitigation
strategies and fostering eco-innovation throughout the value chain (Jradi et al., 2018; Nav-
arro, Puig, Kılıç et al., 2017). In the wine industry, environmental assessments often reveal
that significant impacts are concentrated in specific stages of production, such as packaging
and transportation (Bosco et al., 2011).
LCA is a comprehensive analytical method recognized as a leading tool for system-
atically evaluating the potential environmental impacts of a product, process, or activity
throughout its entire life cycle (Arcese et al., 2012; Silva & Silva, 2022).
This "cradle-
to-grave" approach encompasses all stages from raw material extraction and processing,
through manufacturing, distribution, and use, to final disposal or recycling (Masotti et al.,
2022; Barry, 2011). It aims to quantify material and energy inputs as well as environmental
emissions at each stage, offering a holistic view of environmental burdens (Jolliet et al.,
2016). The methodology for conducting LCA is standardized by the International Organiz-
ation for Standardization (ISO) through the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 standards (Guerra
et al., 2024). While LCA is a powerful tool for improving environmental performance and
eco-efficiency, it is often complex and resource-intensive, requiring significant time, expert-
ise, and financial investment (Rugani et al., 2013). In the wine sector, there is a continued
need for greater standardization and transparency in LCA studies to improve comparability
across products, companies, and regions (Tsalidis et al., 2022). Additionally, new tools and
automated platforms are needed to simplifcy LCA processes and make them more accessible,
particularly for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) (Zambelli et al., 2023a).
The Carbon Footprint is a widely adopted and standardized indicator that focuses
specifically on GHG emissions (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). As a subset of LCA, CF quantifies
the climate-related impacts of human activities or products, typically expressed in carbon
dioxide equivalents (CO2-eq) (Bosco et al., 2011). This includes emissions of CO2, methane
(CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and other GHGs covered by the Kyoto Protocol, converted using
Page 19 of 92
==PAGE:32==
3
Chapter 1: Establishing the Research Foundation
their respective Global Warming Potentials (GWP). In recent years, CO2-eq has become the
preferred metric for sustainability reporting (D’Ammaro et al., 2021; Marques & Teixeira,
2023).
CF analysis can be conducted at both the product level (Product Carbon Footprint,
PCF) and the corporate level (Corporate Carbon Footprint, CCF) (Pölz & Rosner, 2023).
PCF assesses the life cycle emissions of a single product, while CCF evaluates total emissions
from a company’s operations, including upstream and downstream activities (Devera AI,
2024; Baiano, 2021). Compared to LCA, CF offers a more focused and accessible approach,
making it particularly suitable for businesses with specific climate targets. Its single-issue fo-
cus also facilitates clearer communication with stakeholders and the public (Vázquez-Rowe et
al., 2013). To support CF assessments in the wine industry, various protocols and calculators
have been developed. These tools aim to streamline the process and provide sector-specific
guidance, helping companies (especially SMEs) navigate the complexities of carbon account-
ing more effectively (Guerra et al., 2024).
3.2.7
Drivers for Adoption of Sustainable Practices
Achieving sustainability has become an objective for all stakeholders throughout the
wine supply chain (Alessandri et al., 2024, 2024). The wine industry has seen a significant
increase in its commitment to sustainable practices in recent years. The adoption of sustain-
able practices in the wine industry is a response to evolving global challenges and increasing
awareness of environmental, social, and economic responsibilities (Barry, 2011). This shift
is driven by a complex interplay of factors, that compel wine companies to reevaluate and
restructure their operations to reduce environmental impact and enhance long-term viab-
ility (Frasnetti et al., 2024). Understanding these drivers is crucial for comprehending the
ongoing sustainability transition within the sector (Alessandri et al., 2024, 2024).
3.2.7.1
Internal Drivers.
Internal drivers are motivations originating from within the
wine company, often reflecting the philosophy, values, and strategic objectives of its man-
agement and employees. They can be motivated by ethical considerations, the desire for
operational efficiency, or market positioning (Steur et al., 2020; Golicic, 2022; Galletto &
Barisan, 2019).
These internal drivers are frequently perceived as more influential than
external pressures in driving the adoption of sustainable practices (Alessandri et al., 2024).
A primary internal driver stems from ethical and philosophical beliefs. Many com-
panies embrace sustainable practices because they see it as "the right thing to do" or aspire
to be a "better" company, driven by a philosophical belief in preserving the environment and
ensuring the business for future generations (Wagner et al., 2023). This commitment often
translates into concrete, strategic decisions. For instance, Østjysk Vinforsyning became Den-
mark’s first B Corp-certified wine and spirits distributor in September 2021, demonstrating
how deeply embedded ethical values can drive organizational decisions (Østjysk Vinforsyn-
ing, 2023). Similarly, Comwell Hotels committed to the SBTi in 2023, an internal strategic
commitment that shapes its supply chain and operational actions (Comwell, 2023). Closely
related to these beliefs is managerial commitment and attitudes, consistently highlighted as

a prevalent internal driver. Managerial responsibility and personal preferences play a sig-
nificant role in implementing sustainability practices. Companies also pursue sustainability
for competitive advantage and economic benefits. This approach includes enhancing brand
reputation and image, improving product quality, achieving greater operational efficiency,
reducing input costs, and optimizing supply chain operations.
Innovation and organiza-
tional capabilities are also critical internal drivers. Companies focus on capabilities such as
product innovation, adoption of advanced technologies, and knowledge exchange. Innovation
management in vineyards and cellars can include new harvest methods, renewable energy
sources, and fermentation software, addressing challenges like scarce resources and enhan-
cing sustainable performance. Lastly, employee engagement and satisfaction can serve as an
internal motivation for adopting sustainable practices (Alessandri et al., 2024, 2024). Im-

for instance, emphasizes involving employees so they can take ownership of the new sustain-
ability strategy (Comwell, 2023). Alko ensures its personnel are competent and have the
courage to discuss responsibility (Alko, 2023).
3.2.7.2
External Drivers.
External drivers originate from outside the firm and exert
pressure on companies to adopt sustainable practices. These external pressures are increas-
ingly influential in shaping industry behavior.
A significant external driver is consumer
demand and preferences. There is a growing consumer interest in environmentally friendly
and socially responsible wines, with consumers increasingly conscious of their purchasing
decisions’ environmental impact. Many consumers are willing to pay a premium for sustain-
ably produced wines. Additionally, evolving legislation, stakeholder demands, and market
competition also push wine companies toward sustainability (Marco-Fondevila et al., 2020).
One of the most transformative drivers is policy and regulatory change (Montalvo-
Falcón et al., 2023). Governments and institutions increasingly legislate sustainability re-
porting to mitigate environmental impacts (Marques & Teixeira, 2023). A key development
is the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which mandates large com-
panies to disclose information on sustainability information, including GHG emissions (Li
et al., 2025; European Commission, 2025a). The first companies subject to the CSRD had
to apply the new rules for the first time in the 2024 financial year, and requires reporting
aligned with the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) (see Table 4 in Ap-
pendix A.1) (European Commission, 2025a). Among these, ESRS E1 ’Climate change’ is
particularly relevant, as climate change affects virtually all companies and sectors, making
it a critical component in sustainability reporting. ESRS E1 requires disclosure of Scope
1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 GHG emissions, encouraging companies to obtain primary data
from suppliers (EU, 2023). Although the CSRD only directly affects large companies, its
effects cascade down the value chain. SMEs must provide emissions data to remain part of
their clients’ supply chains (Dansk Industri, 2024a). A 2024 survey by Dansk Erhverv found
that nearly half of companies now require sustainability disclosures from suppliers (Dansk
Industri, 2024b). As a result, suppliers must adapt to avoid exclusion from procurement
agreements. Notably, suppliers are increasingly willing to provide sustainability data and
adopt greener practices, especially if their customers ask for it (Cozzi, 2025). It is important

Investigating Current Industry Practices
To meet the demand for carbon data, companies in the Danish wine industry cur-
rently voluntarily estimate and report on carbon data of individual wine bottles. To better
understand current practices by Danish wine suppliers, qualitative interviews were conduc-

3.2.8.1
Reliance on Generic Databases
Using sustainability assessment frameworks
like LCAs to calculate and report the CF of individual wine products requires gathering
significant data on material and energy inputs and outputs for all involved processes. How-
ever, obtaining accurate and detailed primary data for every stage, particularly across the
entire value chain, can be a complex, expensive, and a difficult task. This challenge is espe-
cially pronounced for SMEs, which comprise the majority of businesses in the sector, as they
often lack the financial resources, time, and deep sustainability knowledge. Consequently,
wine companies often rely on secondary data sources, such as public databases that compile
average data.

3.3.1
Research Topic
The global wine industry is facing increasing pressure to enhance its environmental
performance, particularly in relation to carbon emissions (Silva & Silva, 2022). This de-
mand is driven by a range of stakeholders (Montalvo-Falcón et al., 2023). These include
B2B customers seeking greater transparency (Silva & Silva, 2022), corporate sustainability
commitments such as the SBTi (Letamendi et al., 2022), and regulatory developments like
the CSRD, which mandates companies to disclose their environmental impacts, as there is
an increasing trend for companies to measure environmental footprints due to legal require-
ments (Karalis & Kanakoudis, 2023). This thesis explores how sustainability in the wine
sector can be enhanced by focusing on a key issue facing Danish wine suppliers: the challenge
of disclosing credible, product-level CO2 emission data. This challenge is significant both
academically and practically, given the global complexity and variability of wine production
systems.
Studies shows that carbon footprints can vary substantially depending on factors
such as production method (Marco-Fondevila et al., 2020), packaging choices (Barry, 2011),
and logistics (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). Furthermore, the wine industry’s engagement with
sustainability is increasing, with various practices being adopted across the value chain (Silva
& Silva, 2022). However, the widespread use of generic emission factors often fails to capture
these nuances, leading to misleading estimates and undermining transparency (Frasnetti et
al., 2024; Vázquez-Rowe et al., 2013).
Suppliers frequently struggle to identify which data points are relevant to their spe-
cific supply chains and how to collect them efficiently and accurately (D’Ammaro et al.,
2021). This is compounded by a lack of consensus on calculation variables and challenges
in obtaining detailed primary data from throughout the supply chain (Guerra et al., 2024;
Meneses et al., 2016). They also face difficulties in collecting and systematizing accurate
data, compounded by a lack of industry-specific guidance on how to initiate and conduct
carbon calculations effectively (Baiano, 2021). While LCAs offer detailed insights and are
considered the best framework for assessing environmental impacts (Fusi et al., 2014), their
application in the wine sector is still developing (Ferrara & Feo, 2018), and they are often too
costly and complex for SMEs to implement across their full product portfolios (D’Ammaro
et al., 2021). This highlights the urgent need for standardization and transparency in data
collection and assessments, and the development of practical frameworks to support wine
companies in navigating these complexities.
To address these challenges, this thesis aims to develop a practical, industry-specific
support framework tailored to the operational realities of Danish wine suppliers. This frame-
work is intended as a preparatory tool that bridges the gap between generalized emission
Page 28 of 92

factors and detailed carbon footprint calculations. It seeks to offer a feasible approach for
wine suppliers to strengthen their sustainability reporting practices and respond proactively
to market and regulatory demands. The framework should serve as a stepping stone toward
more advanced environmental assessments, aiming to help suppliers identify and systematize
the right data, even without extensive LCA expertise. It should aim to be flexible enough to
incorporate specific data points, such as whether a wine is organic, uses lightweight bottles,

The overarching goal is to enable wine suppliers to respond to increasing sustain-

customers, including hotel and restaurant chains such as Comwell Hotels.

study, the aim is to develop insights and support tools relevant to wine suppliers and
the wine industry more broadly. The research seeks to identify generalizable lessons
about carbon data estimation challenges and opportunities within the Danish wine

4.2
Mapping the Wine Supply Chain Stages
To answer RQ2: What are the stages of the wine supply chain?, this section identifies
and maps the key activities that comprise the wine supply chain. This foundational mapping
is a crucial first step, as understanding the basic components and structure of the supply
chain is essential for further assessment. To achieve this, it is necessary to go into a level
of detail that describes not just the broad stages but also the key activities, actors, inputs,
and outputs associated with each stage.
The wine supply chain is a complex network of upstream and downstream processes
involving a range of actors that collectively transform grapes into the final bottled product
(Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; F. A. Garcia et al., 2012; Alessandri et al., 2024). Activities
refer to the specific operations performed at each stage of the supply chain, such as viticul-
ture, harvesting, fermentation, bottling, and distribution, which convert inputs into outputs
(Arcese et al., 2012). Actors are the individuals, organizations, or entities responsible for
Page 34 of 92
==PAGE:48==
4

these activities, including grape growers, winemakers, cooperatives, logistics providers, and
retailers (F. A. Garcia et al., 2012).
These activities and actors can be grouped into distinct stages that reflect the full
life cycle of wine production (Marras et al., 2015; Meneses et al., 2016; Rugani et al., 2013).
In mapping such a complex supply chain, it is beneficial to consider both the operational pro-
cesses and the stakeholders involved. This dual approach provides a comprehensive overview
of “who does what, where, and when,” which is vital for conducting further assessments.
4.2.1
Methodological Approach
To comprehensively address RQ2, a mixed-methods approach was adopted, combin-
ing a SLR with empirical data collection through qualitative interviews. This dual approach
ensured both theoretical grounding and practical validation of the wine supply chain map-
ping.
The SLR served as the foundational step, aiming to establish a conceptual under-
standing of the wine supply chain stages and activities, as commonly presented in academic
literature, particularly within the context of LCA studies. The SLR followed systematic re-
view principles, including structured search strings, inclusion/exclusion criteria, and multi-
phase filtering. A SLR was chosen as the appropriate methodology, as several studies con-
sistently break down the process into phases, and a systematic approach was required to
synthesise these findings and determine the life cycle stages of wine products. The method-
ology for identifying relevant academic journals, keywords, and inclusion/exclusion criteria
is described in Section 4.2.2.
To complement the theoretical insights, qualitative interviews were conducted with
a diverse set of stakeholders across the wine supply chain (e.g., Østjysk Vinforsyning and
Etiquette). These interviews served a dual purpose: validating the stages identified through
the SLR and incorporating practice-based knowledge. This aligns with the mixed-methods
mapping approach advocated by MacCarthy, Ahmed and Demirel, who highlight the value of
integrating primary data to enhance the granularity and contextual relevance of supply chain
maps. The interviews also addressed the challenges of capturing practical understanding that
is often not documented in studies. This methodology ensured a robust understanding of
the wine supply chain, integrating theoretical frameworks with practical industry insights.
The actual mapping was carried out using Miro and combined both visual and
textual elements. There is no universal standard format for completing a supply chain map.
Instead, it should provide a visual tool to help people understand how things fit together.
The dictionary defines the word ‘map’ as both a noun “a diagram to show the positions of
things over an area“ and a verb “to make a map of an area or to discover or give information
about something, especially the way it is arranged or organized” (Oxford University Press,
2025). Similarly, a supply chain map has been defined as a diagrammatic representation,
providing a “likeness and a simplified model” of a supply chain with both visualization and
information about key features (MacCarthy et al., 2022). As noted by MacCarthy et al.,
there is no universal standard for supply chain maps; rather, they should be tailored to
the mapping purpose and audience.
Therefore, a map of a supply chain should present
appropriate and accurate information in a manner that can be easily understood and, at the

4.2.2
Systematic Literature Review Process
The aim of the SLR was to establish a general understanding of the typical sequential
stages of the wine supply chain, as defined in existing research, and structure them into a
map.
The SLR was conducted using the approach proposed by (Biolchini et al., 2005).
This involved three phases: (1) review planning phase, (2) review execution phase, and
(3) results analysis phase. The review included a total of 22 articles that were thoroughly
analysed through full article reading.
4.2.2.1
Review Planning Phase
The primary objective was to identify the typical
sequential stages of the wine supply chain as described in existing research, and to gather
detailed information about the key activities, inputs (e.g., energy, water, materials), and
outputs (e.g., emissions, waste) within each stage.
Search String. To identify relevant literature, a systematic search was performed in
Scopus using the following search string:
( TITLE-ABS-KEY ( "wine" OR "wine sector" OR "wine industry" ) AND (
"supply chain" OR "value chain" OR stage* OR proces* OR steps ) AND ( "Life
Cycle Assessment" OR lca OR "environmental impact*" ) )
This search string was designed to capture literature that discusses the structural
components and stages of the wine supply chain.
The keywords in the first part target
studies focused on the wine industry, the second part aims to find literature that explicitly
discusses the chain or its breakdown into its constituent activities or phases, and the third
part targets studies that specifically assess environmental impacts, as such studies typically
necessitate a detailed understanding of the supply chain steps for analytical purposes. Using
"OR" Boolean operators with variations of the terms ensures a broader capture of studies
within the specific domains. The use of "AND" Boolean operators connecting these three
conceptual groups ensures that retrieved documents must contain terms from all three areas.
This filters out studies that might discuss only wine, or only supply chains, or only envir-
onmental impact generally, thereby focusing the results on research that explicitly examines
the structure of the wine supply chain. The use of the asterisk (*) is a wildcard to capture

variations of the terms like "environmental impacts," "stages," or "processes."
Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria. The inclusion criteria encompassed peer-reviewed
articles published between 2010 and 2025 in English, focusing on the structural components
of the wine supply chain. Exclusion criteria ruled out articles not directly related to wine
production or supply chain stages and non-peer-reviewed sources.
Qualitative Selection Process. Based on the inclusion criteria, a qualitative selec-
tion process was employed involving three filters: filter 1 - reading the title, abstract, and
keywords to determine their relevance to the research question concerning the stages of the
wine supply chain; filter 2 - full-paper skimming focusing on identifying explicit mentions
or discussions of supply chain stages within the text, tables, or figures; and filter 3 - full
paper analysis to extract detailed information on the structuring of the wine supply chain
stages. This process enabled the identification, evaluation, and selection of the most relevant
articles for the research review.

4.2.4.1
Raw Materials Production
This stage encompasses the provision of the vari-
ous inputs necessary for the subsequent processes within the wine supply chain (Anastasiadis
& Alebaki, 2021; Fusi et al., 2014). Specifically, it involves the supply and production of
inputs for viticulture (grape cultivation), vinification (winemaking), bottling, and packaging
(Navarro, Puig, Kılıç et al., 2017; Fusi et al., 2014).
For viticulture, key inputs identified include fertilisers and pesticides, significant
energy in the form of diesel for agricultural machinery, and materials for infrastructure like
trellis systems, as well as vine plants (Fusi et al., 2014; Navarro, Puig, Kılıç et al., 2017;
Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021). Winemaking necessitates inputs such as yeasts, enzymes,
wine additives, oenological products, cleaning chemicals, and energy sources like gas (Fusi et
al., 2014). Water is a crucial input for both viticulture and winemaking, although detailed
data on its use can be challenging to obtain (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; Navarro, Puig,
Kılıç et al., 2017). Bottling and packaging involve the supply and production of components,
primarily the glass bottle, but also closures (like corks), labels, and secondary packaging
materials such as cardboard (Fusi et al., 2014; Navarro, Puig, Kılıç et al., 2017; Ponstein et
al., 2019). The production of these materials involves raw material extraction and energy
consumption, which are sources of emissions (Fusi et al., 2014). Raw materials suppliers
are responsible for providing these diverse supplies to wine producers, fillers, and packers
(Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021).
4.2.4.2
Transport of Materials
This stage broadly encompasses the transport of raw
materials and supplies within the wine supply chain and covers the movement of neces-
sary inputs to the points of production, including viticulture (grape growing), winemaking
(vinification), bottling and packaging (Ponstein et al., 2019; Zambelli et al., 2023b). This
transport can involve varying distances and may utilise different modes or vehicle types, such
as trucks powered by bioethanol or petrol, which can impact economic and environmental
performance (Aivazidou et al., 2022).
For viticulture, this includes materials like fertilisers, pesticides (agrichemicals),
diesel for machinery, and infrastructure components (Ponstein et al., 2019; Barry, 2011;
Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; Silva & Silva, 2022; Tsalidis et al., 2022). For winemaking,
inputs such as yeasts, enzymes, wine additives, cleaning chemicals, and gas are supplied
(Ponstein et al., 2019; F. A. Garcia et al., 2012; Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021). Lastly, for
bottling and packaging, the transport of materials like glass bottles, closures (corks, screw
caps), labels, and secondary packaging (cardboard, plastic wrap) from their production sites
to the winery or bottling facility is included (Ponstein et al., 2019; Anastasiadis & Alebaki,
2021; Iannone et al., 2016; Silva & Silva, 2022).
Actors such as raw materials suppliers are responsible for sending these supplies, and
freight operators or carriers facilitate their movement (J. Garcia et al., 2023; Moccia, 2013).
The environmental burdens associated with the provision of these inputs, encompassing their
production and transport, contribute to the upstream impacts of the wine life cycle (Ponstein
et al., 2019).

4.2.4.3
Vineyard Planting
This stage is an initial and integral part of the wine supply
chain, typically considered within the broader agricultural phase. While some studies treat
it as a distinct stage, others merge it with ongoing viticulture or do not explicitly report it
separately (Zambelli et al., 2023b; Benedetto, 2013; Tsalidis et al., 2022). This stage involves
processes that precede the cultivation of established vines (Tsalidis et al., 2022).
The stage involves preparatory activities like land preparation and the installation
of vineyard infrastructure, such as trellis systems (Fusi et al., 2014; Ponstein et al., 2019).
The inputs required for vineyard planting are notable, including fuel for field operations,
fertilisers, pesticides, water, infrastructure materials, and lubricating oil. The studies indic-
ate that the production and combustion of diesel are particularly significant contributors
to environmental impacts during this phase (Fusi et al., 2014; Benedetto, 2013). Overall,
the vine planting sub-phase is highlighted as having a great influence on the environmental
performance of the whole agricultural phase (Fusi et al., 2014).
Data on this stage can
sometimes be merged with general viticulture data, or not mentioned at all in some studies
(Tsalidis et al., 2022).
Key actors at this point include nurseries, which provide foundational inputs such
as seeds and vine plans (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021).
4.2.4.4
Viticulture
The viticulture stage is identified as an initial and integral com-
ponent of the wine supply chain, typically situated within the broader agricultural phase
(Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; F. A. Garcia et al., 2012; Silva & Silva, 2022; Tsalidis et
al., 2022). This stage occurs at the vineyards and primarily encompasses the cultivation of
grapes, which serve as the fundamental raw material for wine production (Anastasiadis &
Alebaki, 2021). The studies emphasise that the quality of the grapes produced during this
stage is crucial, as it directly influences the final quality of the wine (J. Garcia et al., 2023).
Key activities involved in viticulture include winter and summer vineyard opera-
tions, vine planting, soil preparation, fertilisation, pest and disease control, pruning, and
trellis management (J. Garcia et al., 2023; Silva & Silva, 2022; Neto et al., 2013). From
an environmental standpoint, viticulture is frequently recognised as a stage contributing
significantly to the overall impacts of wine production (Baiano, 2021; Neto et al., 2013)
Primary actors responsible for these processes include vineyard owners, grape grow-
ers (viticulturists), who may be independent farmers or part of a winery and farm workers
(for pruning, etc.) (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; J. Garcia et al., 2023).
4.2.4.5
Harvesting
The harvesting stage is a crucial activity within the wine supply
chain, positioned within the agricultural phase (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; J. Garcia et
al., 2023; Neto et al., 2013; Scrucca et al., 2018; Silva & Silva, 2022; Zambelli et al., 2023a).
It serve as the fundamental raw material for wine production. This stage fundamentally
involves the collection of mature grapes from the vineyards (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021;
J. Garcia et al., 2023). The quality of the grapes obtained during harvesting is considered
one of the most important factors within the wine supply chain, as it is directly related to
the final quality of the wine (J. Garcia et al., 2023).
Conditions or practices during harvest can potentially impact the quality of the

grapes. Grapes have a fixed date for harvesting (Moccia, 2013). Harvesting may be done
manually (common for premium wines or difficult terrains) or by mechanical harvesters
(often used in large-scale operations) (Moccia, 2013; Barry, 2011).
The studies highlight that the grape grower is specifically accountable for the harvest
of grapes (J. Garcia et al., 2023). The grape supply network involved in this process includes
harvesters, growers, carriers, and wineries (Moccia, 2013).
4.2.4.6
Transport of Grapes
Following the harvest, a key step is moving the harvested
grapes from the vineyard to the manufacturing sites or winery where winemaking takes place
(Aivazidou et al., 2022; Benedetto, 2013; F. A. Garcia et al., 2012; Zambelli et al., 2023a;
Barry, 2011; Martins et al., 2018; Neto et al., 2013). This transportation typically occurs
using trucks or trailers, covering distances that can range from being on the same site to
nearby locations (e.g., 22 km away in one case, or average distances of 19 km and 2 km for
different wines) (Barry, 2011; Martins et al., 2018). The studies highlight that grapes need
to be processed by the winery relatively quickly after harvesting, as fermentation starts as
soon as the grapes are harvested, making long distance transportation to a single facility
challenging (Martins et al., 2018). This process contributes to the overall environmental
footprint, mainly through the consumption of fuels like petrol or bioethanol (Aivazidou et
al., 2022), or diesel (Benedetto, 2013), which are associated with transportation costs and
emissions. The choice of transportation mode and fuel type can influence the environmental
performance, such as GHG emissions (Aivazidou et al., 2022).
4.2.4.7
Vinification
The vinification, or winemaking, stage is a core step in the wine
supply chain, where the wine’s character is developed and refined. It is the set of processes
that transforms grapes into wine (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021). It typically begins with the
receiving of grapes at the winery and involves numerous activities. These activities include
crushing, stemming, pressing juice, adding sulfite, decanting, adding yeast, fermentation of
the juice into alcohol, refrigeration, clarification, stabilisation, temperature control, storage
(in tanks or barrels), filtration, preparation for bottling, and potentially maturation (Iannone
et al., 2016; F. A. Garcia et al., 2012; Martins et al., 2018; Neto et al., 2013). The specific
production cycle and the technologies applied can differ based on the type of wine, style,
or brand (Casolani et al., 2022). The vinification process requires various inputs, including
materials such as additives and cleaning chemicals, and energy, and it also generates outputs
like emissions and wastes (Ponstein et al., 2019; Silva & Silva, 2022; Zambelli et al., 2023b).
This stage requires careful monitoring and quality control to ensure optimal conditions,
particularly in relation to temperature, fermentation duration, and hygiene (Anastasiadis
& Alebaki, 2021). The choice of fermentation method, yeast strains, and aging practices
directly influences the final wine’s aroma, sensory profile, and overall quality.
Key actors involved in this stage can include winemakers, wineries, and potentially
unions (J. Garcia et al., 2023; Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; Ponstein et al., 2019; Navarro,
Puig, Kılıç et al., 2017).

4.2.4.8
Bulk Wine Storage
Following the vinification processes (like fermentation),
the wine is transferred to tanks or barrels for storage (J. Garcia et al., 2023; Silva & Silva,
2022; Martins et al., 2018; Moccia, 2013). Key activities during this stage include processes
aimed at stabilising and preparing the wine for final packaging, such as clarification, stabil-
isation, filtration, and temperature control. The purpose of bulk wine storage is to allow
the wine to mature under controlled conditions. Thus, refrigeration and temperature control
are particularly notable activities, often being significant energy consumers (J. Garcia et al.,
2023; Ponstein et al., 2019; Tsalidis et al., 2022). Wineries may also perform blending oper-
ations during storage to achieve the desired wine profile. The storage can take place at the
same winery facility where the wine was produced, or the bulk wine might be transported to
a separate bottling facility for storage before being filled into bottles. The duration of stor-
age varies depending on the wine style, ranging from a few months to several years. During
this stage, winemakers regularly monitor and sample the wine to assess aging progress and
determine readiness for further processing or bottling (J. Garcia et al., 2023).
The primary actors involved are wine producers (wineries) who manage these pro-
cesses (J. Garcia et al., 2023; Moccia, 2013)
4.2.4.9
Bulk Wine Transportation
This stage becomes necessary when the bottling
operation takes place at a different facility than where the wine was produced and stored
(J. Garcia et al., 2023; Martins et al., 2018), or when wine is transported in bulk for bottling
at a destination market (Barry, 2011; Silva & Silva, 2022; Moccia, 2013). This is the case for
Østjysk Vinforsyning’s project “Scout,” where they produce their own wine in California by
sourcing grapes themselves and collaborating with a local winemaker. For the 2024 vintage,
the wine was bottled in California, but the 2025 vintage may be transported in bulk and
bottled by their partner Peter Mertes in Mosel, Germany.
The primary activity is the physical movement of wine in bulk form. This involves
transporting the wine, often by truck or potentially in tanker trucks (Martins et al., 2018) or
flexitanks for longer distances (Barry, 2011), from the storage location (typically managed
by the wine producer) (J. Garcia et al., 2023) to the bottling plant or a separate facility
where aging occurs before bottling (Martins et al., 2018).
Key actors involved include wine producers, who are the source of the bulk wine,
filler, who receive the bulk wine at the bottling facility, and potentially logistics providers
such as freight forwarders and freight operators responsible for the physical transportation
itself (J. Garcia et al., 2023). Maintaining wine quality, including temperature and preventing
oxidation, are important considerations during this transport (Barry, 2011).
While not
always explicitly detailed as a standalone stage in all studies, this transport contributes to
the overall environmental footprint, primarily through fuel consumption, and is sometimes
included within the scope of distribution or industrial phases in various studies.
4.2.4.10
Bottling
The bottling stage is a crucial phase in the wine supply chain, where
the finished wine is transformed into a product ready for consumers (Silva & Silva, 2022).
Key activities in this stage involve receiving bulk wine (J. Garcia et al., 2023), filling it into
various types of packaging, including standard 0.75 L glass bottles, but also bag-in-box or

facility. Important considerations include maintaining wine quality, temperature control,
and preventing oxidation during storage and the bottling process (Martins et al., 2018).
The main actors involved are wine producers (wineries) who manage these processes,

4.2.4.11
Packaging
This stage focuses on preparing the bottled wine into units suitable
for transport and storage. The primary activities include assembling secondary packaging
such as cardboard boxes or cartons to hold multiple bottles, applying materials like shrink
wrap to secure these units, and finally placing the packaged units onto pallets for shipment
or warehousing. This transforms individual packaged bottles into larger logistical units. The
wine may be packed into consumer units like bottles, bag-in-box, or tetra packs, followed
by packing these units into cartons and pallets. The key actors involved are primarily wine
producers (wineries) who oversee and execute these processes, or packers if bottling and
subsequent packaging are outsourced to a separate entity. This stage requires various inputs,
including the bottled wine itself, significant amounts of packaging materials like cardboard,
shrink wrap, and wooden pallets, as well as energy (such as electricity for machinery or
petrol/diesel for forklifts) used in the packing and handling processes.
It results in the
packaged wine ready for dispatch and contributes to associated wastes and emissions from
material production (addressed in Section 4.2.4.1) and energy use (J. Garcia et al., 2023;
Ponstein et al., 2019).
4.2.4.12
Transportation of Finished Wine
The primary activity is the physical
transport of the packaged product, typically bottled wine in cardboard boxes on pallets
(Barry, 2011; F. A. Garcia et al., 2012; Iannone et al., 2016), from the bottling location
(either the winery or a dedicated bottling facility) to warehousing facilities managed by
importers, such as Østjysk Vinforsyning’s warehouse in Hinnerup.
The actors involved begin with the wine producer or packer dispatching the wine.
Logistics providers play a critical role, including freight forwarders who organise the shipment
planning, choose routes, and arrange necessary services, and freight operators who execute
the physical transport using different modes (F. A. Garcia et al., 2012).
These modes
commonly include trucks for domestic or shorter journeys, rail, and significantly, container

4.2.4.13
Warehousing
This stage involves the physical storage of finished, packaged
wine, typically in bottles and secondary packaging like cardboard boxes on pallets (J. Garcia

(Østjysk Vinforsyning, 2023). The core activities include the reception of packaged wine
from the bottling facility, managing the inventory while it is stored, and the subsequent
dispatch of these finished goods for further transport. Specific warehousing activities can
include product reception, picking of orders, and container loading for shipment. For actors
such as importers, re-packing and re-labeling might also occur within this stage (J. Gar-
cia et al., 2023). Maintaining the quality of the wine during storage, potentially including
temperature control, is a significant consideration (J. Garcia et al., 2023; Barry, 2011).
The primary actors managing warehousing are often importers, who are responsible
for receiving, storing, and dispatching wine within their part of the supply chain. This stage
requires inputs such as the packaged wine itself and energy for operating the warehouse,
including handling equipment like forklifts and potentially refrigeration (J. Garcia et al.,
2023). To reduce the carbon footprint of storage, some warehouses are installing solar panels
and transitioning to renewable energy sources for climate control. For instance, Østjysk
Vinforsyning has installed a solar panel system on the roof of their warehouse building,
sufficient to covering 140% of their 2022 energy consumption (Østjysk Vinforsyning, 2021).

bars, and hotels, while retailers serve end consumers through supermarkets and wine shops.
The actors managing and executing this transport include the dispatching parties
(importers), complemented by freight forwarders who organise shipment planning and freight
operators who provide the actual physical transportation of the wine (J. Garcia et al., 2023).
Transport modes commonly employed include trucks for domestic or shorter journeys, rail,
and sea (Aivazidou et al., 2022; Barry, 2011; Martins et al., 2018). Different truck types,

End Consumer Use
At this stage of the wine supply chain, end consumers

stage is the consumer (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; Barry, 2011). The core activity involves

4.2.4.16
Disposal
The disposal stage is the final phase in the wine supply chain, occur-
ring after the wine has been consumed by the end user (Zambelli et al., 2023b). The primary
actor in this stage is the consumer (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021). The core activities involve
the management of the remaining materials, which mainly consist of the packaging, such as
glass bottles, cardboard boxes, corks, capsules, and labels, through disposal and recycling.
Unconsumed wine may also require disposal (Anastasiadis & Alebaki, 2021; Scrucca et al.,
2017; Tsalidis et al., 2022).
Glass bottles are commonly recycled, while packaging materials such as cardboard
and plastic wraps are either recycled, reused, or disposed of through general waste streams,
depending on local infrastructure (Casolani et al., 2022; Barry, 2011; Martins et al., 2018). In
some regions, bottle return or deposit-refund schemes have been implemented. The efficiency
of packaging disposal and recovery varies widely between countries and regions, depending
on regulatory frameworks, consumer behavior, and the availability of sorting and recycling
infrastructure (Casolani et al., 2022; Zambelli et al., 2023b; Ponstein et al., 2019).

supply chain. The wine supply chain was systematically structured into 17 distinct, chro-

identified common stages and activities across 22 academic studies, while the interviews with
wine industry stakeholders validated and enriched the model with practical insights.
The identification of these stages and activities provides a foundational structure
for understanding the wine supply chain. The mapping captured both material flows (e.g.,
transport of grapes, bulk wine, packaging) and transformation processes (e.g., vinification,
bottling), along with key inputs (e.g., fertilizers, bottles, energy) and outputs (e.g., emissions,
waste). Several stages, such as storage, transportation, and warehousing, were underrepres-

4.3
Identifying the Carbon Emissions Hotspots
To answer RQ2a: What are the main carbon emissions hotspots across the wine
supply chain and by how much do they contribute?, this section identifies the most significant
carbon emission hotspots based on a SLR. These findings provide critical input for the design
of the support framework by ensuring that it targets the most impactful stages, enabling
wine suppliers to focus their efforts and resources where they will have the greatest effect.
In line with the overall structure of this study, this section focuses solely on identifying what
the key hotspots are and how much they contribute, without yet exploring why these stages
are emission-intensive. These drivers will be addressed in the following section (RQ2b).
4.3.1
Methodological Approach
While carbon emissions occur across multiple stages of the wine supply chain, certain
stages consistently emerge as critical emission hotspots. LCA and CF analysis are widely
used tools for quantifying the environmental impacts of wine production across its entire
supply chain from "cradle to grave" and for identifying key carbon emission hotspots (Marras
et al., 2015; Dede et al., 2020), as elaborated in Section 3.2.6.3. Therefore, numerous LCA
and CF studies exist that assess the carbon footprint of wine products. Consequently, a
SLR was selected as the appropriate methodology, as a structured and transparent approach
was required to synthesise existing findings and determine which stages contribute most
significantly to the overall carbon footprint of wine.
4.3.2
Systematic Literature Review Process
To collect data on the hotspots in the wine supply chain, an in-depth SLR was
conducted using the approach proposed by (Biolchini et al., 2005).
This involved three
phases: (1) review planning phase, (2) review execution phase, and (3) results analysis
phase. The review included a total of 28 articles that were thoroughly analysed through full
article reading.
4.3.2.1
Review Planning Phase
The primary objective was to identify the main car-
bon emissions hotspots throughout the wine supply chain stages identified in Section 4.2.

Search String. To collect relevant studies, a systematic search was performed in
Scopus using the following search string:
( TITLE-ABS-KEY ( "wine" OR "wine sector" OR "wine industry" AND "car-

This search string ensured that returned articles were explicitly related to both the
wine sector and carbon emission assessment. The first keyword group targeted various terms
commonly used when referring to the wine industry, while the second group focused on rel-
evant emission-related terminology. To further refine the scope, the second part of the search
string specifically focused on carbon, ensuring that this impact category was included in the
study. By combining the groups with the "AND" operator, the search results were narrowed
to studies that addressed carbon emissions specifically within the context of wine.
Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria. The inclusion criteria encompassed peer-reviewed
articles published between 2010 and 2025 in English, focusing on quantitative assessments of
carbon emissions, such as LCA or CF analysis. Studies were excluded if they were not dir-
ectly related to carbon emissions assessment within the wine supply chain or articles lacking
quantitative data in assessing carbon emissions or if they were not peer-reviewed.
Qualitative Selection Process. Based on the inclusion criteria, a qualitative selec-

4.3.4
Product Classification Based on Dominant Life Cycle Stage
According to established typologies that classify products by their dominant life
cycle stage, wine is most appropriately categorized as a mobile product (see Figure 30), wine
is best categorized as a mobile product. Mobile products are those where transportation
is one of the most significant contributor to environmental impact over their life cycle.
This classification aligns well with wine, as numerous studies have emphasized that the
weight of packaging (especially glass bottles), coupled with the globalized nature of wine
distribution, often involving multiple long-distance distribution modes, make transportation
a key environmental hotspot.
Unlike active products, wine does not consume energy during its use phase, and un-
like fixed products, it is rarely produced and consumed in the same location. Consequently,
categorizing wine as a mobile product underscores the importance of transport-focused mit-
igation strategies, including lightweight packaging, bulk shipments, and local sourcing, which
can significantly reduce its overall climate impact (Jolliet et al., 2016).
4.3.5
Key Findings
This section addressed RQ2a by identifying the main carbon emissions hotspots
across the wine supply chain through a SLR of 28 LCA and CF studies. While specific
contributions vary across contexts, four stages consistently emerged as major contributors.
Bottling was the dominant hotspot, with an average contribution of 39% and reported ranges
from 20% to 73%. Viticulture followed, contributing around 24% on average, but with consid-
erable variation, from 13% to over 90%. Transport and distribution also averaged 24%, but
ranged from 10% to 42%, and in some cases became the largest contributor. Vinification was
typically a smaller contributor (average 10%), but in some recent studies reached up to 68%.
Other stages, including end-of-life and consumer use, contributed marginally. These results
confirm that wine’s carbon footprint is highly concentrated in a few life cycle stages and

4.4
Identifying the Key Parameters Affecting Wine Carbon Emis-
sions
Building on the earlier mapping of wine supply chain stages (Section 4.2) and the
identification of carbon emissions hotspots (Section 4.3), this section addresses RQ2b: Which
key product-specific parameters, contribute most significantly to variations in carbon emis-
sions? The aim is to identify parameters to which results are highly sensitive (those which
influence a substantial fraction of the impact in the hotspot analysis) and which are in addi-
tion high uncertainty or variability in carbon emissions across different wine products with
distinct product configurations or attributes (e.g. red wine vs white wine, organic vs conven-
tional). In this case, the dependent variable is carbon emissions, and the independent vari-
ables are possible sources such as wine typology, production method, packaging, geography,
etc. Understanding these parameters is essential to reveal how supply-chain configurations
affect carbon emissions. Identifying their primary drivers ensures the support framework fo-
cuses on variables that both significantly impact emissions and show high variability across
different supply chains.
4.4.1
Methodology
The identification of key parameters influencing wine’s carbon emissions builds on
the SLRs conducted in Sections 4.2.2 and 4.3.2, complemented by practical insights from
industry interviews in Section 4.2.3.
This analysis focused particularly on findings from
sensitivity studies reported within the reviewed LCA literature. Sensitivity analysis is a
critical technique in LCA used to assess how variations in individual input parameters (such
as bottle weight or transport distance) affect the final carbon footprint (Hauschild et al.,
2018). These studies help identify which parameters not only have a substantial impact
on emission results but also exhibit high variability or uncertainty across different product
configurations. By synthesizing results from these sensitivity analyses, the most critical and
variable parameters were systematically identified.
4.4.2
The Key Parameters Affecting Wine Carbon Emissions (RQ2b)
Studies report a considerable range in carbon footprint values for wine products,
typically between 0.6 kg and 3.51 kg CO2 eq. per 0.75 L. While these differences are attrib-
uted to a combination of both methodological choices and actual variations in supply chain
configurations, this section focuses specifically on the product-specific factors that contribute
to actual differences in emissions outcomes. Several such parameters consistently emerge as

key contributors to emissions, primarily within the hotspot stages of bottling, viticulture,
distribution/transport, and vinification. Understanding why these stages contribute so sig-
nificantly is essential to ensure that the proposed support framework is sufficiently granular
and applicable across diverse wine types and production methods. The following sections
explore the underlying causes behind these hotspots and identify the parameters that most
strongly influence variation in wine carbon emissions.
4.4.2.1
Bottling.
The dominant source of emissions in the bottling stage is not the act
of filling bottles, but rather the upstream production and characteristics of wine packaging
(Silva & Silva, 2022). This is particularly true for glass bottles, which require extremely
high temperatures and involve energy-intensive chemical reactions, resulting in substantial
GHG emissions (Silva & Silva, 2022; Maxwell, 2023). Emissions from this stage are primarily
influenced by bottle weight and the type of packaging material, which are not only significant
contributors to total emissions but also major drivers of variation (Ponstein et al., 2019).
• Bottle weight is a critically important factor that significantly influences the final
carbon footprint of wine products (Masotti et al., 2022). It directly correlates with
the amount of raw material and energy required for production (Amienyo et al., 2014).
Sensitivity analyses consistently identify bottle weight as a key parameter that can lead
to substantial differences in greenhouse gas emissions per bottle (Ponstein et al., 2019).
Increasing the bottle weight to a premium category of 750 grams can raise emissions
by 21% compared to a standard 500 gram bottle, while switching to a lightweight
330 gram bottle can reduce emissions by 14% (Hirlam et al., 2023). A 10% weight
reduction can lead to approximately 4% savings in GWP (Amienyo et al., 2014), and
a reduction of 30 to 32% can result in an 11 to 14% decrease in emissions (D’Ammaro
et al., 2021).
Across studies, the weight of a 0.75 liter glass bottle varies considerably, typically
ranging from 202 – 750 grams (Hirlam et al., 2023).
This variability is identified
as a major contributor to the differences observed in carbon footprint results, often
explaining up to 31% of the total variation (Silva & Silva, 2022). In addition, bottle
weight influences transport-related emissions, as heavier bottles increase the total load,
fuel consumption, and associated impacts during distribution, particularly over long
distances (Wagner et al., 2023).
Reducing bottle weight is widely recognized as an effective mitigation strategy (Pölz
& Rosner, 2023). Furthermore, transporting wine in bulk and bottling it closer to the
final market has also proven to be an efficient way to reduce emissions from packaging
and distribution (Hirlam et al., 2023).
• The type of packaging material significantly affects the carbon footprint of wine
products and contributes to variation across LCAs (Silva & Silva, 2022). Sensitivity
analyses confirm that the production of glass bottles has among the highest environ-
mental impacts due to their energy-intensive manufacturing process (Christ & Burritt,
2013). In contrast, alternative packaging formats (such as bag-in-box, PET bottles

primarily because these formats are lighter and require less energy to produce (Alko,
2023). For example, keg wine can result in up to 45% lower emissions than standard
glass bottles (Hirlam et al., 2023). Bag-in-box packaging can reduce environmental
impacts by 30 to 40% (Zambelli et al., 2023a), and in some comparisons, even by as
much as 66 to 90% (Wagner et al., 2023). PET bottles have also been shown to reduce
greenhouse gas emissions by up to 15% compared to traditional single-use glass bottles
(Zambelli et al., 2023a). The choice of packaging type also varies across national mar-

Although glass remains the conventional choice for wine packaging (Alko, 2023), al-

for market acceptance.
4.4.2.2
Transport and Distribution.
Transport is consistently identified as a signi-
ficant hotspot in the wine supply chain (Silva & Silva, 2022). Emissions arise primarily
from the combustion of fossil fuels by vehicles and vessels used to move wine between pro-
duction, bottling, and consumption stages (Christ & Burritt, 2013). Transport emissions
are primarily calculated using tonne-kilometers (tkm), which means that both the weight
of the shipment and the distance it travels, combined with the emission factor of the trans-
port mode, directly influence the calculated emissions (Letamendi et al., 2022). Therefore,
these emissions are driven by three interrelated parameters: the distance the wine travels,
the mode of transportation, and the total shipment weight (Bosco et al., 2011). Together,
these factors influence fuel consumption during distribution and are particularly relevant
for wines transported over long distances or using inefficient modes of transport (Christ &
Burritt, 2013). Although transport is not always the dominant contributor to total emis-
sions (Silva & Silva, 2022), it remains a key source of variation, especially for internationally
distributed wine (Weidema et al., 2016). Sensitivity analyses confirm that transport-related
emissions can vary significantly depending on logistical configurations, making this stage
highly context-dependent and variable (Bosco et al., 2011).
• Transport distance is a central determinant of emissions from transport and distri-
bution. Longer shipping distances result in greater energy use and higher emissions,
especially for export-oriented supply chains (Bosco et al., 2011). For example, in the
case of Australian wine shipped to Europe, ocean freight alone contributes around
32% of total emissions (Amienyo et al., 2014). Sensitivity analyses show that reducing
transport distance can significantly decrease the total carbon footprint of wine (Fusi
et al., 2014).

4.4.2.3
Viticulture.
Viticulture is consistently identified as a major hotspot in the wine
supply chain. Emissions from this stage are largely driven by two interrelated parameters:
the consumption of fossil fuels for operating vineyard machinery and the production and ap-
plication of fertilisers (Amienyo et al., 2014). These inputs are highly connected, as tractors
are typically used to apply fertilisers and pesticides (Ponstein et al., 2019). Together, they
significantly influence the environmental impact of grape production and are consistently
highlighted in sensitivity analyses as key sources of variability (Bosco et al., 2011).
• Fuel consumption is a primary contributor to emissions in vineyard operations. The
consumption of fossil fuels (like diesel) for agricultural machinery and field work is a
major source of emissions in viticulture (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). Agricultural ma-
chinery is used for tillage, spraying, pruning, harvesting, and crop spraying (Gierling &
Blanke, 2021). Fuel consumption varies depending on machinery type, field conditions
(e.g., slope, soil hardness), and management intensity. High mechanisation levels and
frequent machinery passes can drive up emissions, particularly in regions with steep
terrain or challenging soil types (Bosco et al., 2011). Inter-annual climatic variation
also affects fuel demand, especially in years with elevated pest pressure or operational
complexity (Casolani et al., 2022).
• Production and application of fertilisers and pesticides are among the most
important emission sources in viticulture (Jourdaine et al., 2020). Synthetic fertilisers
require energy-intensive manufacturing and emit substantial GHGs (Hefler & Kissinger,
2023). While organic practices avoid synthetic fertilisers, potentially reducing related
carbon emissions, they do not necessarily yield lower carbon footprints due to reduced
grape yield (Baiano, 2021). Grape yield per hectare influences the carbon footprint
per bottle, as higher yields generally mean the emissions from inputs are spread over
more product, reducing the impact per functional unit (Silva & Silva, 2022). This
means that organic wines aren’t guaranteed to have lower CF than efficiently managed
conventional ones (Zambelli et al., 2023a). Therefore, the comparison of organic vs.
conventional is complex and does not always show a clear advantage for organic in
terms of carbon footprint (Weidema et al., 2016).

4.4.2.4
Vinification.
The vinification stage is recognized as a significant, though some-
times variable, contributor to the overall environmental footprint of wine products (Silva &
Silva, 2022). Its contributions differ considerably between studies, sometimes being negli-
gible, and other times quite significant (Silva & Silva, 2022; Abinandan et al., 2024; Scrucca
et al., 2017). This variability can be influenced by factors like the energy consumption for
operations (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). A large share of GHG emissions at this stage stems
from electricity and heat used in winery operations. Sensitivity analyses indicate that elec-
tricity usage and heat usage at the winery stage are key drivers of both overall emissions
and variability across studies (Ponstein et al., 2019).
• Energy consumption from electricity and heat is consistently identified as the
main hotspot in the vinification stage (Silva & Silva, 2022). Key energy-intensive pro-
cesses include refrigeration and temperature control (e.g., cooling during fermentation,
heating barrel rooms), grape pressing, must and wine pumping, cleaning (including
steam cleaning), stirring, and sterilization (Baiano, 2021; Pölz & Rosner, 2023; Pölz
& Rosner, 2023). When electricity and heat are sourced from fossil fuels, they signi-
ficantly increase the carbon footprint of this stage (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). Studies
indicate that high electricity consumption, even with a low emission factor, remains
a concern and an opportunity for improvement (Panizzon et al., 2024). The national
electricity mix can greatly influence emissions. A common mitigation strategy is to
increase the share of electricity from renewable sources, such as solar thermal energy
(Hirlam et al., 2023).
4.4.3
Less Significant Factors
This section outlines factors that, while contributing to some variability in carbon
footprint results, are generally considered less significant than the primary drivers identified
in previous sections.
4.4.3.1
Wine Typology.
Although initially assumed to influence carbon footprint signi-
ficantly, wine typology (i.e., red, white, rosé, sparkling) is not a dominant driver of variation
(Guerra et al., 2024). Several studies and data from the Agribalyse database (see Table 10 in
Appendix A.14) suggest that differences between wine types are often minor when compared
to more impactful parameters such as packaging, energy use, and viticultural practices (Silva
& Silva, 2022). While certain patterns emerge for instance, sparkling wines may show higher
GHG emissions due to additional processing, and white wines may show slightly higher CF
values on average, these differences typically fall within a 3–8% range across wine types
produced by the same wineries (Frasnetti et al., 2024; Rugani et al., 2013). Moreover, any
typology-related differences often reflect underlying production practices (e.g., barrel age-
ing or sugar additions) rather than the wine type per se (Silva & Silva, 2022). Therefore,
typology-specific variations are not considered major determinants of carbon footprint, and
methodological efforts may be better focused on more influential parameters (Guerra et al.,
2024).

4.4.3.2
Geographical origin.
Geographical origin does have an influence on carbon
emissions through factors such as climate, soil characteristics, and regional infrastructure
(Silva & Silva, 2022). These can affect viticultural needs (e.g., irrigation requirements in arid
regions), operational constraints (e.g., machinery use on sloped terrain), and local emission
factors (e.g., energy mix or fertilizer emissions) (Hefler & Kissinger, 2023). However, the
carbon footprint differences attributed to geography are often indirect and mediated through
specific practices or logistical choices (Ponstein et al., 2019). For example, variability in
transport emissions is more strongly linked to distance and transport mode (e.g., road vs.
ship) than to region alone (Wagner et al., 2023). As such, the geographical origin of wine
is not a standalone explanatory factor for carbon footprint variability (Vázquez-Rowe et al.,
2013). Instead, it is the combination of practices, infrastructure, and logistical arrangements
associated with a given region that drives emissions outcomes (Casolani et al., 2022).
4.4.4
Key Findings
This section summarises how RQ2b was addressed through a systematic identific-
ation of the key parameters that drive variation in carbon emissions across wine products.
The analysis builds on insight from the SLR’s conducted in relation to RQ2 and RQ2a,
especially sensitivity analyses from the literature. While the carbon footprint of wine is
influenced by a range of supply chain processes, a smaller set of product-specific parameters
were consistently identified as the most significant contributors to both the total footprint
and its variation across wine products. These parameters were found primarily within the
hotspot stages previously identified, namely bottling, viticulture, transport and distribution,
and vinification. The findings reveal that emissions are not uniformly distributed across all
activities but instead shaped by a few dominant variables. Bottling, for instance, was heav-
ily influenced by packaging characteristics, while transport emissions were closely linked to
distance, mode, and shipment weight. Viticulture emissions varied depending on fertiliser
use and fuel consumption, while vinification was impacted by the use of electricity and heat.
Sensitivity analyses consistently confirm that a limited number of parameters, ac-
count for the majority of the variability and environmental impact in wine’s carbon footprint,
particularly when considering cradle-to-gate emissions. The nine data points recommended
for inclusion in simplified estimation methods. include:
• Glass bottle weight
• Primary packaging material type
• Transport distance
• Transport mode
• Shipment weight
• Production and application of fertilisers and pesticides in viticulture
• Fossil fuels used in the vineyard

• Electricity usage in the winery
• Heat used in the winery
Together, these factors are the main contributors to GHG emissions in wine pro-
duction and are estimated to explain between 80–90% of the observed variation in carbon
footprint results across products. This means that even without conducting full LCAs, wine
suppliers can significantly improve the accuracy of product-level carbon data by focusing on
a few high-impact variables. Importantly, these parameters are not only emission-intensive
but also vary substantially across product configurations and supply chains, making them
central to any strategy aiming to improve emissions data quality and comparability. This
justifies the design of a support framework that targets the most influential and variable
factors, enabling more precise and resource-efficient carbon footprint calculations in the
wine industry.
While additional variables such as wine typology or geographical origin do contribute
to some extent, their influence is more indirect or context-dependent and therefore less
relevant as primary focus areas for support. The wide range in reported emission values for
a 0.75 L bottle of wine (0.15 to 3.2 kg CO2eq) further underscores the need to move beyond
generalized averages and address product-specific variability through better data on the key
parameters identified.
4.5
Investigating the Flow of Carbon Data Across the Wine Supply
Chain
SMEs in the wine sector often struggle to understand what carbon-related data to
collect and how to access it. The industry frequently lacks the quantitative environmental
data required for sustained improvement and instead relies on generic databases such as Den
store klimadatabase. This section addresses RQ2c: How is the current flow and availability
of carbon-related data among stakeholders in the wine supply chain? It examines the data
inputs that are most critical for calculating carbon footprints of wine products, assesses

4.5.2
The Flow and Availability of Carbon Data (RQ2c)
To ensure accurate, transparent, and comparable product-level carbon footprints,
wine suppliers must be able to access and process a range of key emission-related data
from across the supply chain.
However, obtaining this data is often difficult, especially
for small and medium-sized importers who depend on upstream producers, bottlers, and
transporters for information. This section investigates how relevant carbon data flows—or
fails to flow—across different supply chain stages, with a focus on the specific challenges faced
by Danish wine importers such as Østjysk Vinforsyning. Based on literature and empirical
insights, it maps the critical data inputs needed for product-level carbon calculations, assesses
where and how this data is generated, and explores its current availability to downstream
actors.
The section further identifies key bottlenecks and systemic barriers that hinder
data sharing, including methodological inconsistencies, limited digital infrastructure, and
fragmented communication between supply chain partners. These findings are essential for
informing the design of supportive tools and strategies in Chapter 3, aimed at improving
data access and usability for suppliers operating under resource constraints.
4.5.2.1
Critical Data Inputs and their Availability
For a simplified yet robust CF
assessment of wine products, nine critical parameters have been identified. These inputs
reflect key emission drivers across the wine supply chain and vary in terms of data availability,
precision, and the stakeholders responsible for collecting or holding the data.
• Bottle weight (kg/g): Glass bottle weight is typically measured in kilograms (kg) or
grams (g) per functional unit, such as a 0.75 L bottle of wine (D’Ammaro et al., 2021).
For a 0.75 L bottle, weights typically range from 0.4 – 0.774 kg (Ponstein et al., 2019),
with sparkling wine bottles often heavier due to pressure requirements (Frasnetti et al.,
2024). Wine suppliers can usually access this data via invoices or through direct weigh-
ing at the warehouse (Csiba-Herczeg et al., 2023). The widespread availability of this
data enables the use of flexible modeling approaches (e.g., by weight intervals such as
<400g, 400–800g), which can account for product variation and support lightweighting
strategies.
• Primary packaging material type (qualitative): Wine suppliers generally know
the material type (e.g., glass, PET, aluminum, bag-in-box). While qualitative, this
input is essential as material choice significantly impacts emissions (Silva & Silva,
2022). For instance, PET and bag-in-box options often result in much lower CF values
than glass bottles (D’Ammaro et al., 2021).
• Transport distance (km/tkm): Transport distance is typically measured in kilo-
meters (km) or as a combination with weight, such as tonne-kilometers (tkm) (Letamendi
Page 61 of 92
==PAGE:75==
4
Chapter 2: Understanding the Existing Situation
et al., 2022). Detailed data for the entire transportation and distribution phase is fre-
quently difficult to obtain (Martins et al., 2018). From the wine supplier’s perspective,
transport distance is typically known for certain stages, such as from the winery or
bottling facility to their own warehouse. This data may be available through invoices
or coordination with freight forwarders (Iannone et al., 2016). However, detailed in-
formation on upstream transport (e.g., from vineyard to winery or packaging suppliers
to production sites) and downstream distribution (e.g., from retailer or end-customer)
is often inaccessible (Martins et al., 2018). Suppliers frequently lack insight into these
stages, as they fall outside their direct control, resulting in significant information gaps
(D’Ammaro et al., 2021).
• Shipment weight (kg/g): Shipment weight is typically measured in kg/g (Csiba-
Herczeg et al., 2023). From the wine supplier’s perspective, it encompasses the weight
of the wine itself plus all associated packaging, including glass bottles, closures, la-
bels, cartons, and pallets. Freight operators are a primary source for transport-related
weight data. In Denmark, upcoming legal requirements under the new Extended Pro-
ducer Responsibility (EPR) for packaging (emballageforordningen) give suppliers a
strong incentive for Danish wine suppliers to collect accurate weight-based packaging
data. It mandates that all companies placing packaging on the market must register
with the Danish Producer Responsibility Authority and report actual packaging quant-
ities by mid-2025 (KPMG Denmark, 2024).
• Transport mode (qualitative): Transport mode refers to the specific methods used
for shipping wine and its associated materials, such as truck, container ship, rail,
airfreight, or car. Freight operators are a primary source of transport data. They
organize shipments and are responsible for efficient movement of goods (F. A. Garcia
et al., 2012). Suppliers that collaborate with their transporters generally have this
data.
• Production and application of fertilisers and pesticides in viticulture (kg/ha):
Grape growers and vineyard staff typically log data on the quantity and type of fer-
tilisers and pesticides used as part of their agricultural operations and farm registries.
Quantities are commonly recorded in kilograms or litres per hectare (e.g., kg/ha or
L/ha), or as kilograms of active ingredient (kg a.i./ha). For fertilisers, nutrient-specific
data such as kg N/ha, kg P2O5/ha, and kg K2O/ha is often used. When normal-
ized, data may also be expressed in grams per functional unit (e.g., per 0.75 L bottle)
(Gierling & Blanke, 2021). However, data on the upstream production of these inputs
remains limited in existing databases (Amienyo et al., 2014), and wine suppliers typic-
ally do not have access to either the application-level detail or the production-related
emissions (Casolani et al., 2022).
• Fossil fuels used in the vineyard (L/ha): Grape growers and vineyard staff main-
tain records of fuel consumption for agricultural machinery. Data is typically recorded
in liters per hectare (L/ha) or grams per functional unit (Gierling & Blanke, 2021).
Wine suppliers typically do not have access to this information (Amienyo et al., 2014).

4.5.2.2
Challenges in Obtaining Critical Data
Wine suppliers such as Østjysk Vin-
forsyning face significant challenges in obtaining accurate and detailed carbon-related data,
particularly for the upstream stages such as viticulture, vinification and the production of
packaging materials. While data on packaging and transportation can be relatively more
accessible, upstream data is often not directly available, inconsistent, or incomplete.
Limited Visibility into Upstream Processes. A key barrier lies in the reliance on
upstream suppliers, especially small or traditional wineries, who may lack the systems, in-
centives, or capacity to collect and share environmental data (D’Ammaro et al., 2021). The
following options for data collection exist, as data collection procedures or sources: primary
data can include interviews, questionnaires or surveys, bookkeeping or enterprise, resource
planning (ERP) system, data collection tools (online, offline) and on-site measurements;
secondary data can include interviews, statistics and literature; and data generation can
include calculations (e.g., missing emission factors from input data) and estimates (Barry,
2011; Ferrer et al., 2023; Martins et al., 2017). Primary (site-specific) data is essential for
ensuring accuracy in carbon assessments and is rarely available without direct contact with
winery managers and farmers (Benedetto, 2013). However, many wineries fail to measure,
monitor or audit the actual environmental activities undertaken within their facilities (Christ
& Burritt, 2013). They either do not prioritize sustainability reporting or consider it bur-
densome, often due to a lack of clear economic benefits or perceived complexity (Steur et al.,

sustained engagement with multiple actors to gather the necessary information. Østjysk

closest suppliers, indicating that more proactive demand from importers could potentially
drive improved upstream data availability (Guerra et al., 2024). External pressure or cus-
tomer demand may thus serve as a catalyst for more systematic data collection practices
across the wine supply chain (Christ & Burritt, 2013).
Despite upstream challenges, some parameters are already tracked with a high de-
gree of accuracy. For instance, Østjysk Vinforsyning actively collects bottle weight data
by weighing each bottle upon arrival at their main warehouse. This enables them to dif-
ferentiate between lightweight (defined as below 420 g) and regular bottles in their carbon
calculations. Their threshold aligns with industry guidance, such as that from the Sustain-
able Wine Round Table, which considers bottles under 520–525 g as lightweight.
Transport emissions are also a key impact category, yet collecting accurate transport
data can be complex due to multi-modal, international supply chains. Østjysk Vinforsyning
collaborates with their logistics providers to calculate CO2e emissions using the external tool
EcoTransIT. This approach yields data for inbound shipments to their warehouse but is not
yet applied consistently across all wines in stock. Furthermore, while distance and mode
of transport are well-known emission drivers, importers often lack visibility into transport
legs that occur before they assume control of the shipment. For example, detailed data
on intermediate journeys between the winery and the export port is rarely available. Even
for distribution phases, obtaining detailed data can be difficult depending on third-party
logistics involvement (D’Ammaro et al., 2021).

Structural Challenges Across the Wine Sector.
The wine sector faces a range of
systemic and structural barriers that hinder the effective flow, collection, and standardisation
of carbon-related data across the supply chain. These challenges complicate sustainability
assessments and limit the sector’s ability to respond to growing regulatory and customer
demands.
One of the most significant structural barriers is the lack of standardised formats and
protocols for sustainability data reporting. LCA and CF studies in the wine sector require
detailed and extensive data, yet no common framework exists for collecting or disclosing key
indicators. As a result, data is often incomplete, outdated, or presented in incompatible
formats. This absence of standardisation contributes to high variability in carbon footprint
results across studies (from 0.6 to 3.51 kg CO2e per bottle) highlighting the need for a shared
protocol and calculator tool with clearly defined boundaries and assumptions (Silva & Silva,
2022). As discussed in Section 3.2.3, the Danish wine sector lacks a dedicated wine-specific
trade association. While broader organisations such as VSOD represent parts of the alcohol
industry, their agendas are largely shaped by the beer and spirits sectors. As a result, the
specific needs of wine companies (e.g. in relation to carbon data and sustainability reporting)
are often overlooked, further compounding this issue.
These structural limitations are especially pronounced among SMEs. Many SMEs
assign sustainability responsibilities and tasks to staff without formal training or specific
expertise in environmental reporting or LCA methodology.
These individuals are often
given the “sustainability hat” on top of existing roles, without the necessary resources or
knowledge to navigate complex carbon assessments. Consequently, companies struggle to
understand what data to collect, how to access it, or how to interpret the results. This
highlights the need for simplified, user-friendly guidance tailored to SMEs. Furthermore,
LCA outputs are frequently presented in technical formats that are difficult for non-experts
to interpret, highlighting the need for more accessible and user-friendly tools.
There is a strong need for simplified and standardised support tools and guidance
that clarify what data should be collected, how it should be measured, and how it should
be reported. Without this, the administrative burden and complexity of data collection can
discourage companies from engaging in more systematic efforts toward carbon transparency.

Key Findings
This section summarises the key findings from the preceding analysis, which aimed
to answer RQ2c: How is the current flow and availability of carbon-related data among
stakeholders in the wine supply chain?
Through a combination of literature review and
qualitative interviews, the investigation identified critical carbon data inputs, assessed their
availability across the supply chain, and pinpointed key challenges in obtaining critical data,
particularly from the perspective of Danish wine importers and suppliers such as Østjysk
Vinforsyning. This proved to be particularly challenging task. As noted by Anastasiadis and
Alebaki, the complexity of the wine sector, arising from diverse supply chains and numerous
stakeholders, makes it difficult to map how all actors are interrelated through information
exchange.
The findings confirm that, while LCA and CF methodologies clearly define the data
inputs required for accurate carbon footprint calculations, the practical availability of this
data is constrained by structural, cultural, and operational factors.
The wine industry
is characterised by fragmented information flows between producers, importers, and con-
sumers. As López-Santiago et al. notes, this limits transparency and creates difficulties in
obtaining, sharing, and interpreting carbon-related data across different supply chain stages.
López-Santiago et al. further highlights that transparent communication of environmental
information is essential for accountability and informed decision-making, yet in practice,
these information flows remain inconsistent and incomplete across the supply chain.
The most significant data gaps were identified in the upstream stages of viticulture
and vinification. Many producers either lack systems for collecting environmental data (such
as fertiliser use, fuel consumption, and electricity or heat usage), or do not prioritise sustain-
ability reporting at all. Currently, these data are often unavailable, inconsistently recorded,
or held in formats that limit their usability. As a result, upstream data remains largely
inaccessible to importers. Notably, Østjysk Vinforsyning has not yet systematically reques-
ted primary data from their suppliers, reflecting both a cultural barrier and the absence of
established data-sharing practices in the industry. As revealed through stakeholder inter-
views, smaller wineries often perceive carbon data collection as burdensome and of limited
immediate value. This contributes to a continued reliance on secondary databases, such as
Den store klimadatabase, which lack product-specific granularity.
Despite these challenges, certain parameters related to packaging and transport are
more readily accessible. Østjysk Vinforsyning collects bottle weight data upon product ar-
rival in their warehouse and calculates transport-related emissions using EcoTransIT. This
effort by Østjysk Vinforsyning reflects an attempt to bridge existing data gaps.
While
Østjysk Vinforsyning collects and processes some primary data in Excel, Esben Kjærgaard
Sønderby noted that making the data material requires extensive time and resources, and
challenges remains in applying this consistently across all their full product portfolio. There-
fore, the company is looking for ways to systematise and automate this process.
Beyond individual data points, the wine sector faces broader systemic barriers. These
include the absence of standardised data protocols, inconsistent data formats, and a lack of
sector-specific support from industry organisations. For many SMEs, sustainability roles
are often assigned to general staff who might not possess deep sustainability knowledge

5.2.2
Evaluation of Concepts
Following the ideation phase, the potential design support concepts were discussed
and evaluated based on their usability, feasibility, and potential to translate the complex
findings from WP2 into a usable format for SMEs. Key evaluation criteria included: rel-
evance to current industry needs, alignment with sector capabilities, clarity of use, and
ability to support accurate yet manageable carbon data reporting and balance simplicity
with methodological rigor.
Excel tools are widely recognized as valuable for quantifying environmental impacts
and managing data in the wine industry, as they enable users to input activity data and
calculate emissions using emission factors (Hirlam et al., 2023).
While such tools hold
strong potential, particularly more advanced Excel-based calculators, many wine suppliers
and importers, especially SMEs, currently lack the readiness to engage with them effectively.
This stems primarily from widespread data collection gaps, especially in upstream processes
such as vineyard inputs and energy use. As identified in WP2, a significant share of SMEs
lack structured internal systems and have limited access to key data (e.g., on fertilisers,
fuel use, or energy inputs), making it difficult to engage with tools. These challenges arise
from resource constraints (e.g., time, knowledge, or finances) and limited familiarity with
sustainability standards (Christ & Burritt, 2013; Steur et al., 2020). Moreover, confusion
around terminology and the perceived complexity of sustainability reporting contribute to a
broader hesitancy toward adopting new practices (Merli et al., 2018b; Moccia, 2013; Golicic,
2022).
Given these challenges, a guidance-based approach was selected as the most ap-
propriate initial intervention. While Excel tools may be beneficial in the long term, the
current state of sector readiness necessitates a simpler, more foundational solution. The aim
is to provide support that bridges the current knowledge gap, builds internal capacity, and
lays the groundwork for future tools to emerge. The decision to prioritize guidance over a
full Excel calculator also reflected a strategic alignment with the research objectives. To
further bridge the gap between methodological rigor and practical feasibility, a clustering
approach was explored as a key design solution. Instead of requiring a full LCA for each
wine product, which is often unmanageable for SMEs, wine products can be grouped into ar-
chetype clusters based on key emission-driving parameters. These parameters include glass
bottle weight, primary packaging material type, transport distance and mode, shipment
weight, viticulture practices (e.g., fertiliser and pesticide use), vineyard fossil fuel use, and
winery energy consumption. Each cluster represents a typical combination of characterist-
ics, allowing wine suppliers to assign products to the closest matching archetype and apply
a corresponding pre-calculated emission factor. This enables consistent, comparable, and
representative carbon footprinting, while significantly reducing data collection burdens.

5.2.3
Defining Criterias & Components
To ensure a coherent and user-oriented support framework tailored to the needs
of the wine sector, a systematic structure was defined to guide the design process. For this
purpose, the 5W1H method was adopted, which is an established approach used to structure
problem-solving and decision-making. The acronym stands for “Who, Why, What, Where,
When, and How,” and served as a foundational tool for clarifying the purpose, users, and core
design requirements of the framework (see Figure 31 in Appendix A.15). Each dimension of
the 5W1H method helped refine the scope and focus of the framework:
• Who is the framework for?
The framework targets small and medium-sized wine
suppliers and importers in Denmark, particularly those with limited resources (e.g.,
time, knowledge, or finances) and sustainability expertise.
These companies often
rely on generalised emission data and operate without access to standardised tools or
methodologies. Given the fragmented nature of the wine industry and the challenges
faced by SMEs, the framework must prioritise simplicity, usability, and adaptability. It
must offer clear guidance, even to users unfamiliar with sustainability methodologies,
and be flexible enough to support varying levels of technical capacity.
• Why is the framework needed? The wine industry faces several persistent challenges:
a lack of standardized carbon footprint methodologies, inconsistent data practices,
and significant heterogeneity in scale, practices, and product types. At the same time,
buyers and regulators are increasing demands for transparent, product-specific envir-
onmental data. For wine suppliers, failure to meet these rising expectations can result
in a loss of competitive advantage and business opportunities. Thus, the framework
seeks to address these gaps by equipping wine suppliers with the tools and knowledge
to improve the accuracy of wine carbon data.
• What should the framework provide? The framework is envisioned as a guidance-
based tool rather than a full carbon calculator. It should provide clear, prioritised
guidance on the most important carbon-related data inputs, highlighting key emission
hotspots and relevant data sources. By focusing on the most impactful and variable

5.2.4
Expert and Industry Input
To strengthen both the methodological robustness and practical applicability of the
framework, an expert interview was conducted with Professor Olivier Jolliet, from the De-
partment of Environmental and Resource Engineering at DTU SUSTAIN. The purpose of
the interview was threefold:
• To assess the feasibility of focusing on a simplified set of high-impact parameters;
• To explore how variability could be managed in simplified support tools;
• To discuss strategies for balancing usability and scientific rigor in the framework.
The conversation revealed several key insights that directly informed the design
of the support framework.
Professor Jolliet confirmed the value of accounting for more
nuanced product distinctions. He emphasized that databases like Ecoinvent already contain
differentiated inventory data for such parameters, allowing for greater accuracy than applying
generic averages from Den Store Klimadatabase. He advised allowing actual data inputs

5.2.5

Clustering wine products based on high-impact input parameters is methodologically

key variables. Furthermore, LCA literature frequently highlights that focusing on a limited
number of high-impact parameters can capture the majority of emissions variation—as also
emphasized by Professor Jolliet. Simplified LCA tools often rely on such parameter grouping
to balance methodological precision with practical feasibility. Therefore, recommending wine
suppliers and importers like Østjysk Vinforsyning to adopt archetype clustering in emissions

7.1
Methodological Reflections
This thesis employed the DRM, which provided a structured yet flexible framework
for addressing a complex, practice-oriented challenge. The iterative nature of DRM enabled
continuous refinement of both the research focus and the design interventions, ensuring
alignment with real-world needs. Its application in this context also demonstrates the value
of DRM in developing actionable sustainability tools. The user-centered orientation of the
methodology proved particularly effective in translating academic insights into practical
solutions, suggesting its broader applicability in sustainability-driven design research.
The integration of systematic literature reviews and qualitative interviews allowed
for triangulation of insights, thereby enhancing the credibility and depth of the findings. This
mixed-methods approach was particularly valuable in a field where empirical data is often
fragmented or inconsistent, as it enabled the synthesis of both theoretical and practice-based
knowledge. The use of stakeholder interviews not only grounded the research in real-world
challenges but also helped identify practical constraints and opportunities that might not
have emerged through literature alone. While the use of a fictional case in DS-II was appro-
priate for early-stage testing, it limits the generalizability of the results. Future participatory
studies involving real-world companies would help validate the framework and strengthen its
practical relevance. Moreover, longitudinal studies could assess how the interventions per-
form over time and whether they lead to measurable improvements in carbon data quality
or reporting practices.
Another methodological strength lies in the decision to focus on a single envir-
onmental indicator—carbon emissions. This narrowed scope allowed for a more targeted
and manageable research process, particularly within the constraints of a master’s thesis.
However, it also highlights the trade-offs inherent in simplifying complex sustainability as-
sessments. This reflects a broader methodological tension between comprehensiveness and
usability, especially in SME contexts where resources are limited. Although this study fo-
cused exclusively on carbon, the developed support framework can serve as a foundation for
future expansion into multi-indicator assessments. Incorporating additional dimensions such
as water use, biodiversity, and social or economic impacts would offer a more holistic under-
standing of sustainability performance and further enhance the tool’s relevance in broader
environmental reporting contexts.

at the beginning of the project (see Appendix A.4). These objectives guided the research
from initial exploration to final evaluation, ensuring a structured yet adaptive approach. A
detailed overview of how each objective was fulfilled is provided in Appendix A.16, demon-
strating the coherence between academic aims, methodological choices, and practical out-
comes.
7.3
Reflection on the Interventions
7.3.1
Contribution to Research and Practice
The developed interventions, namely (1) a conceptual readiness framework and (2)
a simplified Excel-based data collection and calculation tool, address a critical gap in the
wine industry by providing accessible, sector-specific tools for carbon data collection and
reporting. These tools contribute to both academic and practical domains.
From an academic standpoint, the interventions present a structured, context-sensitive
Page 87 of 92
==PAGE:103==
7
Discussion
approach to assessing environmental performance in the wine sector. They address the cur-
rent lack of standardized methodologies for carbon footprinting and illustrate how simpli-
fied, high-impact parameter models can underpin credible sustainability assessments. On a
practical level, the interventions offer SMEs a scalable, low-barrier entry point into carbon
accounting. By steering users toward more accurate and relevant data collection without
requiring a full-scale LCA from the beginning, the tools help companies respond to in-
creasing regulatory and customer demands. This approach aligns with global trends calling
for simplified environmental assessment methods that prioritize usability, transparency, and
incremental progress. Focusing on a limited set of high-impact parameters enables suppli-
ers to identify carbon hotspots, streamline internal data processes, and communicate emis-
sions data more effectively. The clustering approach further enhances scalability by allowing
companies to group products with similar characteristics and apply representative emission
factors.
Crucially, the interventions go beyond generic emission factors by offering a more
granular, industry-specific methodology. They provide clear guidance on what data to col-
lect, where to find it, and how to interpret it—helping suppliers navigate the complexities
of carbon reporting. The emphasis on high-impact parameters and user-sourced data makes
the framework adaptable to varying levels of data availability and organizational maturity.
It also supports internal decision-making, supplier engagement, and external communica-
tion. There is an inherent tension between achieving full methodological rigor (such as ISO-
compliant LCAs with third-party verification) and the practical feasibility for many wine
suppliers (typically SMEs). Full LCAs are often complex, costly, and data-intensive. The
proposed framework offers a pragmatic alternative: a simplified model based on a reduced
number of key inputs. This allows wineries to draw reliable conclusions with acceptable
uncertainty, while significantly reducing time and resource demands.
Ultimately, the interventions serves as a compass, guiding companies toward more
structured and transparent carbon management. It supports incremental progress that might
otherwise be cost-prohibitive, helping bridge the gap between academic research and industry
practice.
7.3.2
Limitations
While the developed framework and interventions offer valuable contributions, sev-
eral limitations should be acknowledged to ensure transparency and guide future research.
• Data dependency: The accuracy of the tool’s outputs depends heavily on the avail-
ability and quality of upstream data, which is often inconsistent or inaccessible, par-
ticularly in complex, multi-tiered supply chains.
• Simplified scope: The framework focuses exclusively on carbon emissions and does
not account for other environmental impact categories such as water use, land degrad-
ation, or biodiversity loss. A more holistic sustainability assessment would require
integration of these dimensions to avoid unintended trade-offs.
Page 88 of 92
==PAGE:104==
7
Discussion
• Limited testing: The evaluation was based on a fictional case and a single stakeholder
interview.
While this was appropriate for early-stage validation, real-world testing
across diverse supply chain configurations is needed to assess usability, adaptability,
and impact.
• Manual processes: The Excel-based tool, while functional, lacks automation and
real-time feedback. A digital version with features such as automated calculations,
data prefill, or integration with supplier systems could enhance scalability and user
experience.
• Lack of formal standardization: Although the tool supports product-level car-
bon estimation, it does not fully comply with international LCA standards (e.g., ISO
14040/44). This may limit its use in formal reporting or third-party verification con-
texts.
In addition to these technical and methodological limitations, a key concern raised
during stakeholder engagement was the perceived risk of competitive disadvantage. One wine
supplier expressed concern that more detailed and product-specific carbon calculations could
result in higher reported emissions compared to competitors using generic emission factors
such as those from Den Store Klimadatabase. This creates a disincentive for transparency
and methodological rigor, particularly in the absence of standardized reporting protocols.
This insight underscores the urgent need for sector-wide standardization. Without a shared
protocol and harmonized calculator tool, comparisons across suppliers remain inconsistent
and potentially misleading. Establishing clear, comparable, and widely accepted methods
for carbon footprinting would help level the playing field, reduce confusion, and incentivize
accurate reporting.
Finally, several broader limitations should be noted:
• Exploratory and qualitative nature: The study is primarily exploratory and does
not include a full quantitative evaluation of the framework. Findings are based on a
specific case context and may not be generalizable across the entire wine industry.
• Data sourcing and uncertainty: The framework relies on a combination of literature-
based emission factors and stakeholder-provided data. Variability in data quality and
regional practices introduces uncertainty that was not fully quantified in this study.
• Consumer phase exclusions: Due to data limitations, the consumer phase (e.g.,
transport to point of consumption, refrigeration) was excluded. This may underestim-
ate total emissions in certain life cycle stages.
• Geographical specificity: The framework was developed in the context of Danish
wine suppliers and may require adaptation for other regions with different regulatory,
logistical, or production conditions.
Despite these limitations, the framework represents a meaningful step toward making
carbon footprinting more accessible, actionable, and scalable for SMEs in the wine industry.
It provides a foundation for future refinement, standardization, and expansion into broader
sustainability metrics.

7.3.3
Future Work
The limitations discussed above highlight that the proposed framework represents
an initial step rather than a final solution. Future research and development should aim
to refine, expand, and operationalize the framework to enhance its usability, accuracy, and
overall impact. The current Excel-based tool, with its modular structure and parameter-
driven logic, is well-suited for digital transformation. A future version could automate cluster
assignment based on user inputs, allowing users to enter key data and receive a carbon
footprint estimate by matching to a representative cluster. This would operationalize the
framework and increase adoption, particularly among SMEs.
To advance this vision, a critical next step involves piloting the interventions with
SMEs in the wine sector. Usability testing in real-world settings will help identify which
components require simplification or clarification. Structured feedback should be collected to
evaluate the feasibility, clarity, and completeness of the interventions, particularly regarding
the accessibility of user-sourced data and the comprehensibility of default values.
Over time, the simplified carbon calculator could evolve into a web-based platform
or be integrated into enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems. Enhancements such as
automated data input (e.g., from logistics providers), real-time emission factor updates, and
user dashboards would significantly improve usability and scalability. Including parameters
like empty returns in transportation would further support comprehensive emissions account-
ing. To enhance comprehensiveness, future iterations should incorporate Tier 2 and Tier 3
variables (e.g., secondary packaging, warehouse energy use). While not essential for initial
deployment, these variables can improve accuracy and provide a more holistic view of emis-
sions. For instance, secondary packaging data is often readily available due to regulatory
requirements (e.g., emballageforordningen) and could be integrated once core data inputs
are stabilized.
Future work should also address the communication of CO2e data by wine suppliers.
This includes developing guidance on verification and standardization practices to ensure
consistency and credibility. The current lack of consensus on variables, system boundaries,
and emission factors in LCA studies hinders comparability and transparency. While this
framework contributes to bridging that gap, further research is needed to establish sector-
wide standards.
To support informed decision-making, CO2e data should be integrated
into consumer-facing platforms, such as online wine shops.
The EU Directive on Green
Claims provides a valuable reference for ensuring transparency and avoiding greenwashing
(European Commission, 2025b). Future tools should consider how verified emissions data
can be communicated clearly and credibly to consumers, enhancing trust and promoting
sustainable choices.
Although the interventions were developed for the wine sector, parameter-driven
structure makes them adaptable to other industries within the agri-food sector and beyond,
particularly those facing similar challenges such as fragmented supply chains, high product
variability, and increasing pressure to provide transparent environmental data. However,
generalizing the framework requires addressing key methodological questions: How can res-
ults be compared across diverse contexts? How should the quality of outcomes be measured?
How can the impact of the framework be evaluated against non-use scenarios?
Address-

ing these questions is essential to ensure the framework’s robustness, transferability, and
relevance in broader sustainability and design research contexts.
Concluding this discussion, several gaps identified through this project can be trans-
lated into future research opportunities. These include the need for consensus on carbon
footprint variables, improved data quality, and harmonized methodologies. The lack of a
common definition or standardized protocol for sustainability and carbon footprint assess-
ment in the wine sector leads to confusion. Current LCA studies exhibit strong heterogeneity
in system boundaries, assumptions, data quality, and emission factors, hindering comparab-
ility and reliable communication of environmental performance. This thesis contributes to
addressing this gap, but further research is needed to advance both the developed framework
and the broader field of sustainability research.

8
Conclusion
This study has systematically explored the challenges and opportunities for im-
proving carbon data transparency among wine suppliers, with a particular focus on SMEs
operating in import-driven markets such as Denmark.
Motivated by increasing regulat-
ory demands such as the EU’s CSRD and voluntary initiatives like the SBTi, the research
addressed the growing gap between the need for product-level carbon data and the prac-
tical limitations suppliers face in delivering accurate, transparent, and representative carbon
emissions reporting.
Through systematic literature reviews and stakeholder interviews, the research iden-
tified the stages of the wine supply chain, four key carbon hotspots—bottling, viticulture,
transport, and vinification—and nine high-impact parameters that drive emissions variability
across wine products, such as bottle weight, packaging type, and transport distance.
Based on these insights, two complementary interventions were developed: (1) a
conceptual readiness framework to help suppliers map their supply chains, identify carbon
hotspots, and understand data ownership; and (2) a simplified Excel-based carbon data
collection and calculator tool. Together, these tools enable wine suppliers to move beyond
generic emission factors by inputting product-specific data and clustering wines based on
shared characteristics to estimate product-level carbon footprints using a small set of high-
impact variables. This approach enhances transparency and offers a more practical, scalable,
and industry-relevant entry point to carbon accounting.
Evaluation through a fictional case and qualitative feedback from Østjysk Vinforsyn-
ing confirmed the framework’s usability, clarity, and alignment with sector needs. While not
a substitute for full LCAs, the interventions support internal decision-making, supplier en-
gagement, and external reporting, especially for SMEs with constrained technical resources.
However, further research is needed to test and validate the interventions in real-world set-
tings to determine how well the interventions support wine suppliers and address the needs
of the industry. Future iterations should explore the integration of additional environmental
indicators, automation, and sector-wide standardization to enhance usability, comparability,
and impact.
The study also highlighted several challenges and limitations encountered during the
research process. These include structural barriers in the sector, the complexity of designing
a useful and adaptable intervention, the scarcity of upstream data, and technical barriers
such as limited Excel skills. Despite these challenges, the process yielded valuable insights,
and efforts were made to address and overcome these limitations to the greatest extent
possible. This contributed significantly to the overall learning experience of independently
managing and facilitating a thesis project.
Future research should focus on piloting the interventions with SMEs, developing
digital tools, and establishing standardized protocols for carbon data reporting in the wine
industry to stay relevant in the ever-evolving field of sustainability. These efforts will be
essential to ensure that wine suppliers can meet the growing expectations for sustainability
performance and contribute meaningfully to a climate-neutral and resilient wine industry.

A.4
Learning Objectives
This MSc thesis will be conducted with the following learning objectives in mind:
1. Conduct comprehensive literature reviews to establish a foundational understanding
of sustainability challenges and practices within the wine industry.
2. Investigate current carbon data estimation and reporting practices in the wine industry
and their limitations.

A.16
Learning Objectives Fulfilled by this Thesis
The thesis explicitly outlines a set of ten learning objectives that were fulfilled
throughout the research process (see Appendix A.4). These objectives align with the DRM
framework utilized, moving from understanding the problem to developing and evaluating a
solution.
Here are the learning objectives fulfilled by this thesis:
1. Conduct comprehensive literature reviews to establish a foundational un-
derstanding of sustainability challenges and practices within the wine in-
dustry. This objective was fulfilled through the exploratory literature review in the
RC phase and the SLRs conducted in the DS-I phase. These reviews were essential for
mapping the wine supply chain, identifying carbon emission hotspots, and pinpointing
key parameters influencing emissions variability.
2. Investigate current carbon data estimation and reporting practices in the
wine industry and their limitations.
The RC phase included both literature
review and stakeholder interviews, revealing widespread reliance on generic databases
such as Den Store Klimadatabase and highlighting the limitations of current estimation
methods used by Danish wine suppliers.
3. Map and analyze the full life cycle of wine production, identifying key stages
and actors across the supply chain. This was a central focus of Section 4.2, where
the wine supply chain was systematically mapped into 17 distinct stages, forming
the analytical foundation for subsequent hotspot, emissions variability, and data flow
analyses.
4. Identify improvement opportunities in carbon data accuracy and transpar-
ency to inform the design of a targeted support framework. Findings from
DS-I, particularly the identification of high-impact parameters and data availability
gaps, directly informed the design of the support interventions developed in the PS
phase.
5. Develop and apply qualitative research skills, including conducting semi-
structured interviews with diverse industry stakeholders and experts. The
thesis employed qualitative interviews with industry stakeholders such as Østjysk Vin-
forsyning, Nature Preserve, and Professor Olivier Jolliet, providing real-world context,
validating theoretical insights, and informing framework development.
6. Engage with industry stakeholders to ensure practical relevance and create
solutions aligned with real-world needs. This was exemplified by the close collab-
oration with Østjysk Vinforsyning, who served as a key stakeholder, offering primary
data and iterative feedback throughout the research process.
7. Analyze and interpret qualitative data from the interviews to uncover bar-
riers, enablers, and needs. Interview transcripts and stakeholder feedback were
==PAGE:142==
analyzed throughout the thesis to identify practical challenges and inform the design
of the support framework. These insights informed multiple aspects of the research.
8. Design and develop a practical support framework aimed at guiding com-
panies within the wine industry in achieving greater transparency and sus-
tainability objectives. This was the core outcome of the PS phase, resulting in
two complementary interventions: a conceptual readiness framework and a simplified
Excel-based carbon data collection and calculator tool.
9. Evaluate the effectiveness of the support framework and identify strengths,
limitations, and opportunities for further development. The DS-II phase in-
cluded application of the calculator tool to an exemplary case and a qualitative eval-
uation with Østjysk Vinforsyning, assessing usability, clarity, and industry alignment.
10. Present conclusions, acknowledge project limitations, and propose future
research directions to advance sustainability within the wine industry. The
thesis concludes with a dedicated discussion and conclusion section, reflecting on meth-
odological choices, acknowledging limitations such as data dependency and simplified
scope, and proposing future work including digital tool development and sector-wide
standardization.
Overall, the learning objectives are well-fulfilled by the thesis.
