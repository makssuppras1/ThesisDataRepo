Abstract
Quantum thermodynamic is an interdisciplinary field that aims to describe how
energy, entropy and information behave at the quantum scale.
The theory
encompasses a large area of research that either seeks to examine the emergence
of thermodynamic laws from quantum systems or how quantum features, such
as entanglement or coherence, affect processes like heat transfer, work extraction
and thermalization. This dissertation aims to answer the latter issue: How the
presence of quantum entanglement influence thermodynamic processes.
We will consider a system interacting with a thermal reservoir under the
presence of a memory and assume that the entire system comprising the three
subsystems is closed and thus evolves unitarily under an energy-preserving in-
teraction. For the heat-exchange between system and reservoir we construct
an entanglement witness based on non-negative conditional entropy criterion
for separable states. This formalism is extended to include multipartite quan-
tum systems and illustrated for generalized Werner states as well as mixed W
states. The method is experimentally accessible and does not require full state
tomography.

Introduction
The theory of quantum mechanics sparked a revolution of our understanding of
the universe and marked a transition from classical- to quantum physics with
a developed mathematical framework describing experimental particle observa-
tions using, among other subjects, linear algebra. Although quantum physics
exhibit conformity with physical phenomena, the theory is inherently probabilis-
tic and relies on statistical physics to predict particle behaviour. The promising
model predictions have motivated a desire for applying quantum theory to other
scientific areas such as biology, thermodynamics etc., either to achieve theoretic
unity or to obtain new knowledge.
Quantum thermodynamics explores out-of-equilibrium processes and inves-
tigates how the second law of thermodynamics, as first stated by R. Clausius in
1850 [16], emerges from quantum mechanical principles. These processes can be
either ”markovian” or ”non-markovian” depending on whether the dynamical
evolution is memoryless or not [13].
Although mathematical models accurately describe quantum mechanical
behavior, detection of quantum entanglement remains computationally hard.
Apart from violation of Bell’s Theorem, presented by J. S. Bell in 1964 [10],
quantum entanglement can be determined via application of the PPT criterion
[15] given that the quantum state is known. Although we have computational
tools to calculate whether a quantum state is entangled, for some quantum
states, the method is considered NP-hard as proven by Gurvits in 2003 [11].
This dissertation examines heat flow between a system and an environment
in equilibrium with the purpose of certifying quantum entanglement for multi-
partite quantum systems. The purpose of the heat-based witness approach is
to establish an experimentally feasible method to detect quantum entanglement
alternative to standard detection methods, e.g. homodyne detection [3]. The
observations are interpreted in relation to the geometric properties of GHZ and
W states, i.e. the state properties after tracing out one subsystem, and the
Peres-Horodecki criterion serves as a comparable measure to the heat-exchange
entanglement bounds.
The first two theory chapters explain the mathematical framework under-
lying the obtained results where the former explains quantum entanglement
and information theory and the latter explains the quantum thermodynamic
framework used to obtain subsequent results. These results are divided into a
formulation of entanglement bounds of multipartite quantum systems derived
analytically and application of the analytical expressions to examine the gener-
alized Werner states and mixed W-states.

2.3
Mutual Information
The Schmidt decomposition, Eq. (6), allows us to assess the entanglement of
pure bipartite states; the higher the Schmidt rank, the more entangled the state
is.
For such pure states, the von Neumann entropy of either reduced state
can also be used to quantify entanglement. However, for mixed states, these
tools no longer provide reliable information about entanglement. In those cases,
quantities like the quantum mutual information measure total correlations, both
classical and quantum, but cannot distinguish whether a state is entangled.
Detecting entanglement in mixed states requires more sophisticated tools, such
as entanglement witnesses or the PPT criterion.
As previously, we consider a bipartite quantum system AB expressed by
mixed state ρAB. As ρAB is mixed we cannot reconstruct this state from reduced

states ρA = TrB(ρAB) and ρB = TrA(ρAB). This reflects an inherent difference
between the product state ρA ⊗ρB and state ρAB, and we pose the question;
what information is contained within ρAB which is not present within ρA ⊗ρB?
This information is a measure of correlation between the two states and can be
defined as the distance between ρAB and ρA ⊗ρB:
I(A : B)ρAB = S(ρAB||ρA ⊗ρB)

where S(ρAB|ρB) is the conditional entropy of ρAB [6]. The derivation of mutual
information requires no assumption on the type of correlations and therefore
describe a mixture of both classical and quantum correlations.
A more difficult question to address would be: How much of the quantity, Eq.
(10), is due to quantum correlations and how much due to classical correlations?
This is a highly complex question to ask. Assuming that the state ρAB is a pure
state gives some insight into the meaning of the mutual information. In this
case S(ρAB) = 0 according to the definition of von Neumann entropy, Eq. (1),

pure state ρAB is given by:

the entanglement of a pure state. This show a precise connection between the
two measures of quantum correlations.

Theory II: Preliminary Quantum Thermody-
namics
Quantum thermodynamics is a field that explores the interplay between quan-
tum mechanics and thermodynamic principles, aiming to understand how en-
ergy, entropy, and information behave at the quantum scale. It is a broad area
of research that either investigates how classical thermodynamic laws emerge
from quantum systems or examines how unique quantum features, such as co-
herence and entanglement, affect processes like heat transfer, work extraction,
and thermalization. This dissertation focuses on the second question: How the
presence of quantum features influences thermodynamic processes.
Given the breadth of the field, the theoretical tools used to describe the ther-
modynamics of small systems depend on the specific questions being addressed.
For example, one might model an open quantum system to examine whether
quantum correlations can be generated, or study the detailed mechanisms of
thermalization. Alternatively, an information-theoretic approach may be taken,
using tools from information theory to characterize thermodynamic behavior.
In what follows, we adopt the former perspective. Specifically, using minimal
assumptions and the framework of resource-theoretic thermodynamics, we aim
to investigate whether quantum features, more precisely, entanglement, can be
detected by probing thermodynamic quantities

ε(ρS ⊗ωC) = ρF ⊗ωC
where the system ρS is transformed to some final state ρF. A catalyst there-
fore fulfills the key property of being unaffected by the given thermal process,
allowing us to recover the catalyst back to its initial state.
3.2
Optimal Heat-Exchange
The addition of a catalyst ’unlocks’ the non-degenerate energy subspaces and
therefore enhances the energy flow as shown in the article by Lipka-Bartosik [14].
To study the effects of a catalyst in thermal processes, we consider a system S,
a memory M and an environment E. The memory undergoes a cyclic evolution
equivalent to a catalyst, but is not reused in the process; hence we refer to it
as ”memory”. Each subsystem is described by its local Hamiltonian HX with
dimension dX and is prepared in a state ρX with X ∈[S, M, E]. The system
and catalyst are initially in arbitrary states while the environment starts out in
a thermal state, as expressed by the Gibbs state, Eq. (18) [1].
We wish to determine the minimum and maximum heat exchange between
the system and the environment assisted by a catalyst. In order to maintain
that heat is only exchanged between the system S and the environment E we
must state two assumptions. Firstly, we assume that the joint system SME is
closed and evolves via an energy-preserving unitary process U such that
ηSME = U(ρS ⊗ρM ⊗ρE)U †
(22)

with SE. This is achieved by imposing the following condition:
ηM := TrSE[U(ρS ⊗ρM ⊗ρE)U †] = ρM

Discussion
Discussion of results for generalized Werner states
From the Fig. (2) and Fig. (3) we observe that the set of entangled states using
our heat-based witness decreases as the number of partitions is increased for n =
2, ..., 6 assuming that one-qubit marginal is known. This reflects a fundamental
difference in the detection of entanglement from the PPT criterion and our heat-
based witness which relies on the positivity of conditional entropy for separable
states.
Although the PPT criterion detects a larger set of entangled states
than our heat-based witness, this method assumes that we already know the
given quantum state while entanglement detection via our heat-based witnesses
could be applied in experimental processes where the given quantum state is
not known.
Furthermore, in Fig. (3b), we observe that the maximum (hot) heat ex-
change reaches a plateau beyond four partitions. This captures the fact that
the system cannot be cooled below its ground state energy, setting a natural
limit on the extractable heat. It is worth noting, however, that the plateau
depends on the inverse temperature of the environment, which determines how
much thermal energy is available for exchange.
The solutions to the transcendental equations, Eqs. (43) and (42), show the
same convergence of λcrt as the dimension increases. As the dimension grows,
the size of the global Hilbert space increases exponentially, and the structure of
the reduced states becomes increasingly important. In particular, the entangle-
ment detection depends strongly on which marginal subsystems are known.
From our analysis of multipartite generalized Werner states we observed that
the entanglement bound λcrt is highly dependent on the number of subsystems
assumed to be known. Assuming all partitions except one is known, i.e. for
gn(λ), we obtain a larger set of entangled states as the number of partitions in-
creases. Although both entanglement bounds, fn(λ) and gn(λ) are theoretically
valid, the tightest bound, gn(λ), assuming knowledge of only one subsystem, is
most restrictive and yields a bound for which the state is entangled regardless
of which marginal states are known.

Conclusion
Certification of quantum entanglement for multipartite quantum systems via
heat exchange is derived from the conditional entropy condition of Eq. (29).
The method therefore relies on having access to certain marginal states. If we
assume that only one subsystem is known the entanglement bound decreases as
the number of partitions increases for both the Generalized Werner States and
mixed W states. However, assuming knowledge of all subsystems except one,
we obtain increasing entanglement bounds for increasing number of subsystems
when analyzing the generalized Werner states.
The PPT criterion serves as a benchmark for the quantum states presented
in this dissertation. The thermal evolution approach relies only on measuring
the heat exchange during a process which is experimentally accessible and does
not require full-state tomography. Although the PPT criterion detects a larger
set of quantum states in theory, it is not applicable to experimental processes
as it assumes complete knowledge of the quantum state.
Outlook
The approach to thermal evolution presented in this thesis assumes energy-
preserving unitary with a memory system assisting the process. The results
presented in this thesis were derived under the presence of a memory imply-
ing the presence of non-markovian effects. It could therefore be relevant and
interesting to analyze the same quantum states, i.e. generalized Werner- and
W-states, using a mathematical framework that includes markovian dynamics.
An interesting direction for future work would be to explore a potential
connection between the results derived in this dissertation and the concept of
quantum discord. Quantum discord is a measure of quantum correlations that
extends beyond entanglement, capturing non-classical correlations even in sep-
arable states. Introduced by Zurek in 2002 [7], it is defined via the difference
between two expressions for mutual information, one of which involves a mea-
surement on a subsystem. Calculating Quantum Discord is generally challenging
due to the optimisation over measurement strategies, unless one restricts to sim-
ple projective measurements. Despite this complexity, quantum discord could
offer a complementary perspective on the types of correlations probed by the
heat-exchange-based framework developed in this work.
