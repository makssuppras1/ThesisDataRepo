Abstract
This thesis explores the use of deep learning and retrieval-augmented generation tech-
niques to decode natural language from non-invasive brain signals, specifically electroen-
cephalography (EEG). The primary goal is to develop a flexible and modular brain-to-text
decoding pipeline that can support assistive communication systems for individuals with
severe speech impairments. The system leverages sentence-level EEG embeddings ex-
tracted via a custom convolutional neural network, trained to align with contextual em-
beddings from a pre-trained language model. These embeddings are stored in a vector
store to enable semantic retrieval, and the top-matching sentences are refined using a
Large Language Model (LLM) to generate coherent textual output. The approach is eval-
uated on the ZuCo dataset, with both qualitative and quantitative metrics demonstrating
the feasibility of generating fluent sentences from brain activity. The findings highlight the
challenges of working with noisy, single-trial EEG data, but also underline the potential
of combining deep learning with language models for non-invasive brain-to-text transla-
tion. The pipeline lays a foundation for future improvements in decoding accuracy and
real-time application in Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs).

Chapter 1: Introduction
Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) represent a rapidly advancing technology capable of
transforming neural intentions into direct control of external devices. By creating alterna-
tive pathways around damaged neural circuits, BCIs open new opportunities for assisting
individuals with neurological impairments. These systems can rely on invasive or non-
invasive recordings of brain activity and are applied across different domains, including
communication support. In particular, BCIs that aim to translate brain signals into text
offer a compelling path toward restoring communication abilities. Nevertheless, the cur-
rent landscape highlights significant hurdles: much of the existing research focuses on
restricted vocabularies, while attempts to generate complete sentences often suffer from
limited accuracy, restricting their effectiveness for real-world use [1]. This thesis aims
to address these challenges by proposing new approaches to improve the reliability and
expressiveness of BCI-based communication. This chapter first situates the study within
its broader scientific framework, then defines the research objectives, and finally outlines
the organization of the thesis.
1.1
Background and Motivation
1.1.1
Significance of Communication Disabilities in Neurological
Disorders
Locked-in syndrome (LIS) is a rare but devastating neurological condition in which patients
are fully conscious and cognitively intact but experience complete paralysis of nearly all
voluntary muscles, including those responsible for speech, due to damage typically lo-
cated in the ventral pons of the brainstem [2][3]. Despite being fully aware, individuals
with LIS are rendered unable to communicate except through residual movements, most
commonly vertical eye movements or blinking, which often remain as the only means of
expressing needs or thoughts [3]. This minimal capacity for interaction imposes severe
limitations on daily life, making even basic communication highly effortful and dependent
on caregivers trained to interpret subtle cues. Studies indicate that the diagnosis of LIS
is frequently delayed, sometimes by months, as families are often the first to detect signs
of awareness, highlighting the communication barriers these patients face in clinical set-
tings [2]. BCIs have emerged as a transformative solution to this isolation by translat-
ing brain activity into commands for external devices, enabling patients to interact with
their environment without relying on muscular activity [4]. BCIs, especially those based
on electroencephalography (EEG), have enabled LIS patients to write and converse with
caregivers and researchers, significantly improving their perceived quality of life [4]. Thus,
studying and developing BCI technologies for this population is not only feasible but es-
sential to empower those affected by LIS to regain agency and participate meaningfully
in life.
1.1.2
Challenges in Nowadays Solutions for Communication Disabilities
While BCIs have shown promising potential to re-establish communication for individuals
with LIS, current systems still face major limitations that hinder their practicality in every-
day life. Most BCIs rely on decoding systems that operate with constrained vocabularies
and limited accuracy, making real-time, natural communication infeasible. As Murad and
Rahimi (2025) [5] emphasize, current EEG-to-text decoding models struggle to achieve
both accuracy and fluency due to the inherent complexity of EEG signals, which are of-
ten noisy and highly individualized. These limitations often result in text being generated

through slow, character-by-character decoding, which restricts spontaneity and natural
communication. This process can be mentally taxing and inefficient for users, particu-
larly in dynamic, real-world contexts where communication needs to be fast and context-
sensitive [4]. Thus, despite advancements in BCI technology, the gap between experi-
mental success and real-life application remains significant, particularly when it comes to
supporting autonomy and emotional expression.
1.1.3
Towards More Natural Communication Brain Computer Interfaces
With this master’s thesis, I aim to delve into the field of communication BCIs, focusing
on overcoming the current limitations of these systems, such as limited vocabulary, slow
processing times, and reliance on character-by-character decoding. In particular, I will ex-
plore approaches to decoding at the sentence level, which would significantly enhance the
fluidity and naturalness of communication for individuals with severe motor impairments.
A key challenge for BCIs today is their constrained vocabulary, which often requires users
to rely on predefined word lists or dictionaries, limiting the richness of expression. To ad-
dress this, I plan to investigate how large language models (LLMs) can be integrated into
BCIs to provide more versatile, dynamic, and contextually aware communication. LLMs
are well-suited to this task, as they have the capacity to generate coherent and grammat-
ically accurate sentences, thus enabling more meaningful and spontaneous exchanges.
Through this work, I intend to outline a framework that can guide future research in the
development of BCIs that not only bypass current communication barriers but also expand
the potential for deeper interaction, providing individuals with more freedom, autonomy,
and emotional expression. By building upon current technologies and incorporating ad-
vances in natural language processing, I hope to pave the way for a new generation of
communication BCIs that are both efficient and capable of supporting nuanced conversa-
tion.
1.2
Research Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to develop a complete and modular framework that can
translate EEG signals into coherent and semantically meaningful text, with the ultimate
goal of enabling effective communication for individuals who can no longer speak due to
neurological conditions. This project is not only focused on delivering a working system
but also on laying the groundwork for future studies in the field of communication-based
BCIs.
To reach this aim, the project will pursue several intermediate objectives:
• Framework Design – Build a modular structure where each component, such
as preprocessing, embedding, or decoding, can be independently modified or up-
graded without affecting the entire system.
• Language Refinement Using LLMs – Explore how large language models (LLMs)
can reconstruct grammatically correct and syntactically well-structured sentences
from partially decoded inputs, while preserving the original semantic intent.
• Embedding and Semantic Alignment – Investigate whether vector embeddings
and vector stores can effectively capture the relationship between EEG signals and
language, supporting a more accurate decoding process.
Through these objectives, the thesis aims to contribute a flexible and extendable structure
that other researchers can improve upon. The broader vision is to help pave the way
for communication systems that offer freedom of expression to everyone, regardless of
physical ability.

Thesis Outline
This thesis is structured to guide the reader through the motivation, implementation, and
evaluation of a brain-to-text decoding framework based on EEG signals and deep learn-
ing.
Chapter 1 introduces the context and motivation for this work. It outlines the main research
objectives, the significance of decoding language from EEG using deep learning and large
language models (LLMs), and presents an overview of the contributions made throughout
the thesis.
Chapter 2 provides the necessary background to understand the technical and theoretical
foundations of the project. It introduces EEG signal properties, neural networks, and
language embeddings, as well as recent developments in retrieval-augmented generation
(RAG). A review of relevant datasets and previous work in EEG-to-text decoding is also
included, helping situate this thesis within the broader scientific landscape.
Chapter 3 describes the methodology and design of the proposed system. The selected
dataset is introduced, followed by an explanation of the preprocessing pipeline used to
clean and format the EEG data. The full architecture of the modular pipeline is then
presented, including how EEG embeddings are generated, how sentence retrieval is per-
formed using contextual embeddings, and how large language models are used for sen-
tence refinement.
Chapter 4 reports the results of the system through both quantitative and qualitative eval-
uations. Cosine similarity and BLEU scores are used to assess alignment and generation
quality. Additional analyses offer insight into the structure and effectiveness of the EEG
and contextual embedding spaces, and examples of generated text are provided to illus-
trate the model’s behavior.
Chapter 5 discusses the overall performance of the system. It reflects on methodological
choices, highlights the strengths and current limitations of each module, and proposes di-
rections for future work, including potential improvements to EEG representation learning,
retrieval mechanisms, and language model refinement strategies.
Finally, Chapter 6 concludes the thesis by summarizing the main findings and outlining
the broader implications of the work.
The thesis closes with a bibliography of all referenced works and an appendix containing
supplementary materials, including links to the GitHub repository used to develop and test
the proposed system.

Chapter 2: Background and Related Work
2.1
Background
2.1.1
Electroencephalography
EEG is the most commonly used method in BCI applications due to its non-invasive na-
ture, portability, and high temporal resolution [6]. EEG signals are recorded by placing
electrodes on the scalp according to standard layouts such as the international 10-20 sys-
tem [7], which allows for the detection of the brain’s electrical activity in specific cortical
areas [8]. This activity primarily reflects the post-synaptic potentials generated by pyra-
midal neurons in the cerebral cortex. The EEG signal is weak (typically 10–100 µV) and
easily affected by noise and artifacts such as muscle activity, blinking, and environmental
interference, requiring careful preprocessing [8][9].
EEG signals are commonly divided into frequency bands, though the exact boundaries
may vary slightly from study to study. These bands and their typical associations are:
• Delta (0.5–4 Hz): Associated with deep sleep.
• Theta (4–8 Hz): Related to emotional stress, drowsiness, and the early stages of
sleep.
• Alpha (8–13 Hz): Linked to relaxed awareness and calm wakefulness, often ob-
served when the eyes are closed.
• Beta (13–30 Hz): Reflects active thinking, attention, and alertness.
• Gamma (>30 Hz): Associated with higher cognitive functions such as perception,
consciousness, and information processing.
Different brain areas are responsible for different cognitive and sensorimotor functions.
For example, language-related processes are typically localized in the left hemisphere in
Broca’s and Wernicke’s areas, and EEG signals from these regions can capture lexical,
morphological, and phonological encoding during speech production tasks [10]. There-
fore, electrode positioning is crucial when studying specific tasks such as language pro-
duction, and language-related EEG signals can be analyzed in the time, frequency, or
time-frequency domain to extract relevant features for decoding cognitive states [8].
Electrocorticography
Electrocorticography (ECoG) is an invasive neurophysiological technique that records
electrical activity directly from the cortical surface via electrodes placed under the skull
during neurosurgery [11]. By bypassing the scalp and skull, ECoG overcomes some of
the main limitations of EEG, such as signal attenuation and spatial dispersion, resulting in
higher spatial resolution and signal-to-noise ratio. This allows for more precise localization
of cortical activity and improved detection of epileptiform discharges or task-related brain
signals. However, ECoG requires a craniotomy, which involves neurosurgical interven-
tion and therefore carries inherent risks, making it unsuitable for routine or widespread use
outside of clinical or experimental contexts involving patients undergoing brain surgery.
Due to this invasiveness, EEG remains the preferred method for non-invasive BCI appli-
cations despite its lower spatial resolution and higher susceptibility to artifacts.

2.1.2
Neural Network Foundations
This section provides a general overview of the fundamental principles and components of
neural networks. These concepts are essential for understanding the architectural choices
and training strategies employed in modern machine learning systems, including those
applied in EEG-to-text decoding tasks.
Artificial Intelligence
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a broad domain within computer science focused on design-
ing systems capable of performing tasks traditionally associated with human intelligence.
These tasks include reasoning, problem-solving, learning, decision-making, and natural
language understanding. The objective of AI is to model and replicate cognitive functions
in a computational framework, enabling machines to operate autonomously in complex
environments.
Unlike conventional algorithms that rely on explicitly defined rules, AI systems are char-
acterized by their ability to adaptively improve performance through data-driven learning.
In recent years, the field has advanced significantly due to the increasing availability of
large-scale datasets [12], which has enabled the transition from handcrafted rules-based
systems to models that infer knowledge directly from data.
The progress of AI has also been propelled by substantial improvements in computational
power and the development of advanced learning algorithms. These developments have
allowed AI systems to reach state-of-the-art performance in a variety of domains, including
natural language processing (NLP) [13]. Contemporary AI research and applications of-
ten emphasize machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning, which leverages
neural networks to learn complex representations from raw data.
Neural Networks
Neural networks [14] are a class of computational models inspired by the structure and
function of biological neural systems. They are designed to approximate complex func-
tions by learning representations directly from the data. At a high level, a neural network
consists of multiple layers of interconnected nodes, commonly referred to as neurons.
Each neuron receives one or more input values, applies a transformation, and then pro-
cesses the result through a function to produce an output. The network receives input
data at the first layer and produces an output at the final layer by successively applying
these transformations.
Neural networks are particularly effective at learning mappings from inputs to outputs in
cases where the underlying relationship is not easily defined by explicit rules. Their main
advantage lies in the ability to automatically extract and compose features from raw data,
eliminating the need for manual feature engineering. As a result, neural networks have
become fundamental tools in a variety of domains, including natural language processing
and biomedical signal analysis.
Activation Functions
Going more in detail into the operation of a single neuron, each unit in a neural network
computes a weighted sum of its inputs, adds a bias term, and then applies a nonlinear
transformation to the result. The weights and biases are the learnable parameters of the
model and are adjusted during training to minimize a loss function. The nonlinear trans-
formation is defined by an activation function [15], which is a key component in enabling
the network to learn complex, non-linear mappings.
Without activation functions, the entire network would behave as a sequence of linear
operations, regardless of its depth. This would severely limit its representational capacity,
as a composition of linear functions is itself linear. By introducing non-linearity, activation

functions allow neural networks to approximate a wide range of functions and capture
intricate relationships in the data.
Several activation functions have been proposed, each with its own characteristics and
effects on the learning dynamics. Common examples [16] include the sigmoid function,

such as Leaky ReLU, are particularly popular in deep learning architectures due to their

The specific choice of activation function can significantly influence the performance and
stability of a neural network. Therefore, it is typically selected based on empirical evidence
and the nature of the task.
Supervised and Unsupervised Learning
Machine learning encompasses a variety of learning paradigms, each defined by the type
of data and the learning objective. The two most commonly used paradigms are super-
vised learning and unsupervised learning [17].
In supervised learning, the model is trained on a dataset composed of input-output pairs,
where each input is associated with a corresponding target label. The learning process
consists of minimizing the discrepancy between the model’s predictions and the true labels
by adjusting the network parameters. Supervised learning is widely used for tasks such as
classification and regression. Its success depends on the availability of large, high-quality
labeled datasets.
In contrast, unsupervised learning deals with data that lack explicit target labels. The
objective in this case is to identify hidden structures or patterns within the data. This
includes tasks such as clustering and dimensionality reduction. Unsupervised learning is
particularly valuable in situations where labeled data are scarce or expensive to obtain,
as it enables models to extract useful features directly from the raw input.
Training, Validation, Test Sets and Overfitting
In supervised learning, the goal is to train a neural network to learn a mapping from inputs
to outputs using a set of labeled examples. To ensure that the model generalizes well to
new, unseen data, the available dataset is typically divided into three disjoint subsets: the
training set, the validation set, and the test set [18].
• Training Set: This subset is used to fit the model by adjusting its internal param-
eters. During training, the model processes batches of samples and updates the
weights of the neurons in order to minimize the discrepancy between its predictions
and the corresponding target labels.
• Validation Set: This set is used to monitor the model’s performance during train-
ing. It is not used to update the model’s weights, but rather to provide feedback on
how well the model is generalizing. It is commonly used to guide the selection of
hyperparameters.
• Test Set: This subset is strictly held out until the very end of the training process. It is
used to evaluate the final model’s performance on data that it has never encountered
before, providing an unbiased estimate of its real-world generalization ability.
A major challenge in supervised learning is the risk of overfitting, where a model learns to
perform well on the training data but fails to generalize to new inputs. Overfitting typically
occurs when the model becomes too complex relative to the amount or variability of train-
ing data, leading it to memorize patterns and noise rather than learning the underlying
structure.

Symptoms of overfitting include a large discrepancy between training and validation ac-
curacy, as well as improved performance on training data that is not matched by improve-
ments on unseen examples. Several strategies can be employed to mitigate overfitting
and careful monitoring of validation performance is essential to detect and address it dur-

Loss Function, Gradients, and Backpropagation
Training a neural network involves learning the optimal set of parameters (weights and
biases) that minimize the discrepancy between the model’s predictions and the expected
outputs. This discrepancy is quantified by a loss function [19], which assigns a numerical
value to the model’s performance on a given input-output pair. The choice of loss function
depends on the nature of the task: for example, mean squared error [20] is commonly
used in regression tasks, while cross-entropy loss [21] is often used in classification.
Once the loss is computed, the model uses a procedure known as backpropagation [22]
to determine how each parameter contributed to the overall error. Backpropagation is an
application of the chain rule from calculus that allows the efficient computation of the gra-
dient of the loss function with respect to each parameter in the network. These gradients
indicate the direction and magnitude of change needed for each parameter to reduce the
loss.
The gradients are then used by an optimization algorithm, such as stochastic gradient
descent (SGD) [23] or its variants (e.g., Adaptive Moment Estimation (ADAM) [24]), to
update the model parameters. Specifically, each parameter is adjusted in the direction
opposite to its gradient in order to move toward a local minimum of the loss function. This
process is repeated iteratively across many training samples and epochs until conver-
gence is achieved.
The magnitude of each parameter update is governed by the learning rate [25], a hyper-
parameter that controls how quickly the model adapts. If the learning rate is too high, the
model may diverge and fail to converge. If it is too low, training may become excessively
slow or get stuck in suboptimal minima.
One challenge that may arise during training is the vanishing gradient problem [26], which
occurs when gradients become extremely small as they are propagated backward through
many layers. This is especially common in deep networks or when using saturating ac-
tivation functions. As a result, early layers learn very slowly or stop learning entirely,
impairing the model’s ability to capture complex hierarchical representations. Addressing
this issue requires careful architectural design and the use of tailored techniques which
mitigate gradient shrinkage and facilitate stable learning in deep models.
The combination of the loss function, gradient computation, and parameter updates con-
stitutes the core mechanism by which neural networks learn from data. These steps are
repeated across batches of training examples, allowing the network to incrementally refine
its internal representations and improve its predictive accuracy.
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) [27] are a specialized class of neural networks
designed to efficiently process data with grid-like structures, such as time series and im-
ages. Originally developed for image recognition tasks, CNNs have proven effective in a
wide range of applications including speech processing, biomedical signal analysis, and
natural language processing.
The core building block of a CNN is the convolutional layer, which applies a set of learnable
filters (also known as kernels) to the input data. Each filter slides across the input in

a process known as the convolution operation, computing a weighted sum of the input
values within a local receptive field. This produces a new representation, called a feature
map, which captures the presence of specific patterns or features in the input.
Unlike fully connected layers, where every neuron is connected to all input values, convo-
lutional layers exploit local connectivity and weight sharing. This allows CNNs to reduce
the number of parameters, improve computational efficiency, and better capture local
structures and invariances within the data.
Several important parameters define the behavior of a convolutional layer:
• Kernel size: Specifies the dimensions of the filter. Smaller kernels capture fine
details, while larger kernels capture broader patterns.
• Stride: Determines the step size with which the filter moves across the input. Larger
strides reduce the spatial resolution of the output.
• Padding: Refers to the addition of extra values (typically zeros) around the input
borders to control the spatial dimensions of the output.
• Dilation: Introduces gaps between the filter elements, allowing the receptive field to
expand without increasing the number of parameters.
By stacking multiple convolutional layers, CNNs can learn hierarchical feature representa-
tions. Early layers typically capture low-level features such as edges or local activations,
while deeper layers capture more abstract or task-specific patterns. This ability to learn
spatially and temporally coherent features makes CNNs particularly well-suited for pro-
cessing EEG signals, which exhibit both spatial and temporal structure.
Convolutional layers are often followed by additional components, such as activation func-
tions or other kinds of layer, which together form the backbone of modern deep learning
architectures.
Fully Connected Layers
A fully connected layer [28], also known as a dense layer or linear layer, is a fundamental
component of neural networks in which each neuron is connected to every output from the
previous layer. Mathematically, a fully connected layer performs an affine transformation
of the input vector x ∈Rn into an output vector y ∈Rm according to the following equation:
y = Wx + b
where W ∈Rm×n is the weight matrix, and b ∈Rm is the bias vector. Each output
dimension is thus computed as a linear combination of all input dimensions, followed by
the optional application of an activation function.
Fully connected layers are typically used at the output stage of neural networks because
they allow for flexible and compact integration of the learned features into a fixed-size rep-
resentation or prediction. After a series of convolutional or other intermediate layers have
extracted spatial or temporal patterns, the fully connected layer aggregates this informa-
tion and maps it to the desired output space, such as class probabilities or embedding
vectors. In embedding tasks, the output dimensionality of the final fully connected layer
is often matched to the size of a target embedding space, enabling the network to learn
representations that are semantically aligned with another modality, such as natural lan-
guage.

Architectural and Training Enhancements
While the core structure of a neural network defines its capacity to learn, additional tech-
niques are often required to improve performance, stabilize training, and mitigate issues
such as overfitting or vanishing gradients. These methods can be architectural, such as
residual connections or batch normalization, or procedural, such as using regularization,
early stopping, or learning rate scheduling. Each of these tools serves a distinct func-
tion and contributes to making the training process more robust and the final model more
generalizable. The most common and effective of these techniques are described in the
following section.
Residual Connections
Residual connections [29], also known as skip connections, are an architectural enhance-
ment designed to address the degradation and vanishing gradient problems often encoun-
tered in deep neural networks. As the number of layers increases, models may suffer from
a diminishing flow of gradients during backpropagation, making it difficult for earlier layers
to learn effectively. Residual connections provide a direct path for gradients to propagate
by allowing the output of a layer to bypass one or more subsequent layers and be added
directly to the output of a deeper layer.
Mathematically, a residual block can be expressed as:
y = F(x) + x
where x is the input to the block, F(x) is the output of a series of operations (typically
convolution, batch normalization, and activation), and y is the final output of the residual
block. This structure allows the network to learn a residual mapping F(x) = y −x, which
has been shown to be easier to optimize than learning the direct mapping y = H(x) from
scratch.
The use of residual connections has been instrumental in enabling the training of very
deep networks by preserving signal flow across layers. They also encourage feature
reuse and allow the network to adaptively refine representations at different depths with-
out losing earlier information. As a result, residual connections are now a standard com-
ponent in many state-of-the-art architectures, especially in applications involving deep
convolutional stacks.
Batch Normalization
Batch normalization [30] is a technique introduced to improve the training stability and
speed of deep neural networks by normalizing the inputs of each layer. During training,

Dropout
Dropout [31] is a regularization technique designed to counteract overfitting in neural net-
works by randomly deactivating a subset of hidden neurons during training. At each train-
ing iteration, individual neurons are independently removed from the network with proba-
bility 1−p, where p ∈(0, 1] denotes the probability of retention. This process is formalized
by applying a binary mask m ∼Bernoulli(p) to the input vector x, producing the output:

where ⊙denotes element-wise multiplication.
By stochastically altering the network architecture in each forward pass, dropout effec-
tively trains an ensemble of thinned subnetworks. The aggregation of these subnetworks’
predictions mimics a form of model averaging, thereby enhancing the model’s general-
ization capacity [32]. Rather than allowing the network to rely on specific hidden neurons,
this mechanism forces it to develop robust internal representations that are not depen-
dent on any particular pathway. Therefore, dropout encourages the network to distribute
its learned features more broadly and avoid over-specialization.
At test time, no hidden neurons are dropped, but their outputs are typically scaled by
the factor p to compensate for the reduced signal contribution during training. Although
dropout is most commonly applied to fully connected layers, it can also be adapted for
use in convolutional and recurrent architectures.

simpler and more generalizable representations. This is achieved by adding a regular-
ization term to the loss function that increases with the squared magnitude of the model’s
weights.

Large Language Models
A Large Language Model (LLM) [36] is a type of AI system designed to process and gener-
ate text by predicting sequences of words based on patterns learned from vast amounts of
written language data. Built on advanced neural network architectures called transform-
ers, LLMs are trained on extensive corpora containing billions of words, enabling them to
capture complex statistical relationships within language. Rather than truly understand-
ing language or its meaning, these models function by estimating the likelihood of a word

or token following a given sequence, effectively performing next-word prediction at an
impressive scale. This statistical approach allows LLMs to generate coherent and con-
textually appropriate text, making them capable of a wide range of language tasks such
as translation, summarization, and even multi-step reasoning when prompted effectively.
Despite their impressive outputs, LLMs do not possess human-like comprehension, be-
liefs, or intentions; their responses are derived from learned patterns rather than genuine
knowledge or understanding. To be practical in applications like conversational agents
or assistive technologies, LLMs are often integrated within larger systems that provide
context management and dialogue flow, enhancing their ability to interact naturally with
users. This combination of large-scale training, sophisticated architecture, and system
embedding makes LLMs powerful tools for advancing NLP and related fields.
2.1.4
Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) [37] is an innovative technique that enhances
language models by combining their internal knowledge with external information ac-
cessed through retrieval. Unlike traditional models that rely solely on knowledge encoded
in their parameters, RAG introduces a mechanism to consult a large external corpus,
enabling the model to provide more accurate, up-to-date, and verifiable outputs. This is
particularly effective in tasks requiring extensive factual knowledge, such as open-domain
question answering and fact verification.
The architecture consists of two primary components: a retriever that identifies relevant
documents from an external knowledge source based on the input, and a generator, usu-
ally a sequence-to-sequence transformer model, that produces text conditioned on both
the input and the retrieved documents. This retrieval-augmented framework allows for
dynamic and grounded language generation.
Embeddings
At the core of the retrieval process are embeddings, which are dense numerical repre-
sentations of text. Both the query (input text) and candidate documents are mapped into
a continuous vector space by neural encoders. These embeddings capture semantic
meaning, so that semantically related queries and documents have vectors close to each
other in this space.
Typically, a bi-encoder architecture is used, where one encoder generates the query em-
bedding and another generates document embeddings. This design enables efficient
and scalable similarity comparisons. The quality of these embeddings is crucial since
they determine how effectively relevant documents can be identified for a given query.
During training, the system learns to produce embeddings that place related queries and
documents nearer in the vector space, improving retrieval accuracy.
Embeddings thus provide a compact, semantically rich way to represent large text col-
lections and queries, enabling fast and effective retrieval that supports the generator’s
language production.
Vector Stores
To enable rapid retrieval over millions of documents, RAG employs a vector store, which
is a specialized index of document embeddings optimized for similarity search. Instead of
searching through raw text, the system compares embeddings using distance or similarity
metrics like cosine similarity to find the most relevant documents.

The vector store is typically built offline by encoding the entire document corpus, and it can

the parametric model and the external memory store enables flexible, interpretable, and
scalable access to large-scale knowledge during generation.

3.1
Zurich Cognitive Language Processing Corpus Database
Among the datasets discussed in the Related Work section, the Zurich Cognitive Lan-
guage Processing Corpus (ZuCo) was selected for this study due to its alignment with the
objectives and practical constraints of a realistic brain-to-text decoding scenario. First,
ZuCo adopts a silent reading paradigm, which is preferable to reading aloud as it avoids
electromyographic (EMG) artifacts caused by muscle activity [71], and better simulates
the target use case of individuals with LIS, who are unable to vocalize. Second, it employs
EEG rather than ECoG, supporting the development of a non-invasive and accessible as-
sistive solution. Third, the use of English textual stimuli ensures compatibility with widely
available pre-trained language models. Finally, among the datasets analyzed that sat-
isfied all of these criteria, ZuCo provides the longest recording sessions per participant,
offering a more extensive dataset for training and evaluating deep learning models.
The ZuCo is a collection of multimodal physiological recordings that combine EEG and
eye-tracking data during silent reading of English sentences. Two versions of the dataset
are publicly available: ZuCo 1.0 [40] and ZuCo 2.0 [41]. In ZuCo 1.0, 12 healthy native
English speakers read a total of 1,107 sentences (21,629 words), drawn from the Stanford
Sentiment Treebank [72] and the Wikipedia relation extraction corpus [73]. In ZuCo 2.0,
recordings were collected from 18 healthy adult participants who read 739 sentences. In
both datasets, participants read at their own pace in a naturalistic setting, using a control
pad to advance through the stimuli.
The three tasks included across the two datasets are:
1. Sentiment Reading, where participants read movie review sentences and rated their
sentiment;
2. Natural Reading, where participants read encyclopedic content and answered com-
prehension questions;
3. Task-Specific Reading, where participants searched for specific semantic relations
within sentences.
All three tasks are present in ZuCo 1.0, while only the Natural Reading and Task-Specific
Reading tasks are included in ZuCo 2.0. Eye-tracking data were crucial in temporally
aligning brain activity with the specific words being read, providing both sentence and
word-level EEG annotations.
For this project, only one participant from ZuCo 1.0 was selected: Subject ZAB. This
choice was motivated by the fact that recordings in ZuCo 1.0 are longer (i.e., they contain
more data per subject), providing a larger number of trials for training and evaluating deep
learning models. Furthermore, both the Natural Reading and Sentiment Reading tasks
can be used, whereas the Task-Specific Reading task was excluded, as it induces a differ-
ent reading behavior that is less representative of natural, spontaneous reading, making
it less suitable for EEG-to-text alignment models. Subject ZAB was selected among the

==PAGE:30==
ZuCo 1.0 participants as one of the subjects whose recordings were free from technical
or calibration issues.
Regarding the signals, simultaneous recordings were made using a 128-channel
Geodesic Hydrocel EEG system [74], with a sampling rate of 500 Hz and a bandpass
filter of 0.1–100 Hz, with electrode Cz as the recording reference. Participants were in-
structed to minimize movement to reduce motor artifacts. For eye-tracking, a video-based
EyeLink 1000 Plus [75] device sampled eye position and pupil size at 500 Hz. This dual-
recording setup enabled precise synchronization between eye fixations and brain activity,
allowing EEG features to be extracted both at the sentence level and at the single-word
level, using fixation-aligned time windows.
3.1.1
Zurich Cognitive Language Processing Corpus Preprocessing
The EEG signals provided in the ZuCo dataset were recorded with a built-in hardware
bandpass filter set to 0.1–100 Hz. The preprocessing of the EEG data was performed
using the Automagic toolbox [76], a standardized pipeline for artifact removal and data
cleaning. The following steps were applied:
1. Channel Selection: From the 128 electrodes, 105 scalp channels were retained.
Channels primarily located on the neck and face were excluded.
2. Bad Channel Detection and Interpolation: Bad electrodes were identified using
the clean_rawdata EEGLAB plugin [77]. Channels were marked as bad if their cor-
relation with an estimate from surrounding electrodes was below 0.85, if they con-
tained excessive line noise (above 4 standard deviations), or if they showed flatline
periods exceeding 5 seconds. Identified channels were subsequently interpolated
using spherical splines.
3. Filtering: A high-pass filter at 0.5 Hz and a notch filter between 49–51 Hz were
applied using a Hamming windowed sinc finite impulse response (FIR) zero-phase
filter.
4. Eye Artifact Removal: Eye-related artifacts were reduced by regressing out signals
from electrooculography (EOG) channels using linear regression techniques.
5. Independent Component Analysis (ICA): ICA [78] was performed to isolate sta-
tistically independent sources from the EEG signals. Components identified as ar-
tifacts (e.g., eye movements, muscle activity) were automatically detected and re-
jected using MARA (Multiple Artifact Rejection Algorithm) [79], a supervised ma-
chine learning algorithm trained on labeled ICA components.
6. Re-referencing: The signals were re-referenced to the average of all retained chan-
nels to remove common noise.
7. Synchronization: EEG data were synchronized with the eye-tracking signals using
shared trigger events, enabling fixation-locked analysis at both sentence and word
levels.

• icaact: the time series matrix A ∈Rn×T representing the activation of each inde-
pendent component over time, where n is the number of components and T is the

3.2.1
Database Creation
The original ZuCo dataset contains a wide array of information, not all of which are relevant
for the objective of this study. Therefore, the dataset was restructured to form a more task-
specific and efficient database. The resulting dataset consists of entries with the following
fields:
1. sentence_id: a unique identifier assigned to each sentence;
2. content: the natural language string of the sentence;
3. eeg: the reconstructed EEG signal associated with the sentence (as described in
the previous section).
Prior to saving the EEG data, symmetrical zero-padding was applied to ensure that all
samples have the same temporal length. This step is essential because convolutional
neural networks (CNNs), which are typically used for time-series data like EEG, are sen-
sitive to input size [81]. Padding allows the output of convolutional layers to preserve the
original temporal dimensions of the input. Without padding, convolution operations would
progressively shrink the temporal size of the signal with each layer, potentially discarding
relevant features. By centering the EEG signal and padding it with zeros on both sides,
the architecture can maintain alignment and temporal structure throughout the network
depth, facilitating effective feature extraction without loss of information at the sequence
boundaries.
3.2.2
Contextual Embedding
To represent the semantic content of the sentences numerically, each sentence was em-
bedded using a pre-trained language model. The model all-mpnet-base-v2 [82], pub-
licly available through the HuggingFace library [83], was employed for this purpose. This
transformer-based model maps each sentence to a 768-dimensional dense vector. The
resulting embeddings are contextually meaningful: semantically similar sentences are
mapped to nearby points in the vector space. These embeddings were computed and
saved, and they will be used as targets for learning EEG embeddings in subsequent steps.
3.2.3
Electroencephalography Embeddings
The sentence-level EEG encoder used in this project is based on a deep convolutional ar-
chitecture designed to extract meaningful embeddings from multi-channel EEG signals. A
custom DL architecture inspired by EEGChannelNet [84] was adopted. This architecture,
originally introduced in the Thought2Text [59] pipeline for EEG-based image captioning,
was adapted in this study to suit the EEG-to-text decoding task. The model is trained to
align EEG data with contextual sentence embeddings using a cosine similarity loss. The
input to the model consists of preprocessed EEG segments with shape C ×T, where C is
the number of EEG channels after ICA-based reconstruction, and T is the number of time
samples. Symmetrical zero-padding is applied to ensure that all segments have uniform
length equal to the longest sample, preserving the full temporal content of the original
signal. The model outputs a 768-dimensional embedding vector, matching the dimen-
sionality of the sentence embeddings extracted using the all-mpnet-base-v2 model.
The architecture begins with a series of temporal convolutions applied independently to
each channel. These layers use 1D convolutions with kernel size 5, stride 2, and dilations
of 1, 2, and 4, capturing patterns at multiple temporal resolutions. Grouped convolutions
are employed to process each channel separately, reducing the number of learnable pa-
rameters. The use of dilation enables the model to capture both short- and long-range de-
pendencies in the temporal domain without significantly increasing the parameter count.

This design is particularly suited for EEG signals, which exhibit multiscale temporal dy-

The output of the temporal convolutions is concatenated and reshaped before passing
through a set of spatial convolutional layers, implemented as 2D convolutions across the
temporal dimension and stacked feature maps. Three convolutional filters with kernel
sizes 3, 5, and 7 are applied in parallel to allow the model to capture spatial patterns of
varying granularity across channels. This is important in EEG data, where spatial relation-

sources. Inter-channel dependencies and electrode topology are effectively modeled in
this stage.

reuse, the architecture incorporates two residual blocks. Each block contains a pair of
convolutional layers followed by batch normalization and activation, with skip connec-

mitigates gradient degradation as discussed in the background section.

3.2.4
Vector Store Creation
The computed EEG embeddings were stored in a vector database using FAISS (Face-
book AI Similarity Search) [86], a library for efficient similarity search and clustering of
dense vectors. FAISS supports large-scale similarity indexing and retrieval, including
datasets that exceed the available system memory. It offers optimized routines for index-
ing, searching, and evaluating nearest neighbors in high-dimensional spaces.
The vector store enables semantic retrieval based on similarity metrics. In this work,
cosine similarity was employed as the primary distance measure. Given two vectors u
and v, the cosine similarity cos(θ) is defined as [38]:
cosine_sim(u, v) =
u · v
∥u∥· ∥v∥
where · denotes the dot product and ∥·∥represents the Euclidean norm. The cosine sim-
ilarity measures the angle between two vectors, thus focusing on their orientation rather
than magnitude. A value of 1 indicates perfect alignment (i.e., maximal similarity), while
a value of −1 indicates maximal opposition.

3.2.5
Retrieving Chain for the Final Output
A RAG-inspired chain was implemented using the LangChain framework [87]. The pro-
cess is composed of multiple steps and is designed to convert EEG data into natural
language through a combination of neural embedding and LLM refinement. The pipeline
proceeds as follows:
1. Given new, unseen EEG data corresponding to a sentence, an embedding is com-
puted using the trained EEG encoder.
2. The resulting EEG embedding is used to perform a similarity search within the vector
store described in the previous section. The top k most similar embeddings are
retrieved; in this implementation, k = 3. These embeddings correspond to natural
language sentences from the original dataset and are selected based on cosine
similarity.
3. The three retrieved sentences are then passed as input to a LLM with the objective
of generating a single, fluent sentence that captures their shared semantic meaning
while maintaining grammatical and syntactic correctness. The LLM used for this task
is mistralai/Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1 [88], accessed via the HuggingFace
platform.
To guide the LLM in performing this linguistic synthesis task, a carefully crafted prompt is
used:
”You are given three similar sentences. Your task is to generate one single flu-
ent, grammatically correct sentence that captures the same meaning, without
adding or removing any information. Only output the corrected sentence.”
Following this procedure, natural language is generated directly from EEG data. Starting
from the embedding of a new EEG sample, the chain retrieves semantically similar sen-
tences and refines them into a single output, enabling the transformation of brain signals
into coherent text.

4.3.1
Bilingual Evaluation Understudy Score Evaluation
To provide a quantitative benchmark for the system, the BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Un-
derstudy) score [55] is reported. It is a metric originally developed for evaluating machine
translation, but widely adopted in various text generation tasks. BLEU measures the over-
lap between a candidate sentence and one or more reference sentences based on the
matching of contiguous word sequences, called n-grams.
The score is computed by:
• Calculating the precision of n-gram matches between the candidate and reference
Brain-to-Text Translation Using Deep Learning and LLMs
37
==PAGE:46==
(i.e., how many n-grams in the candidate also appear in the reference).
• Applying a brevity penalty to penalize candidate sentences that are too short com-
pared to the reference.
• Averaging the precision across different n-gram levels (e.g., unigram, bigram, etc.),
typically using a geometric mean.
In this context:
• BLEU-1: Measures the precision of unigrams (single-word matches), capturing
word-level accuracy (e.g., how many individual words are shared between the can-
didate and reference).
• BLEU-2: Measures bigram (two-word sequence) precision, capturing short-term
syntactic and contextual coherence (e.g., distinguishing “very good” from “good
very”) [90].
This metric is commonly used in the evaluation of state-of-the-art systems, offering a
standardized point of comparison. However, BLEU has well-known limitations in contexts
where the task emphasizes semantic similarity rather than strict lexical overlap. This is
especially true for paraphrasing or generative tasks like the present one, where the goal
is to produce a sentence that captures the meaning of the original based on EEG input
rather than to reproduce exact words.
In such cases, BLEU may underestimate performance, since it does not reward semanti-
cally equivalent reformulations or concept-level matching. As illustrated in [90], two sen-
tences can have identical meanings but very low BLEU scores if the phrasing differs.
Nevertheless, BLEU is included here to remain consistent with the evaluation protocols
adopted in related literature. The evaluation was conducted over the held-out test set of

Chapter 5: Discussion
5.1
Results Evaluation
The decoding of natural language from EEG signals is a highly complex task, yet the out-
comes of this study are encouraging. The primary objective of this work was to design a
modular and flexible pipeline that could serve as a foundation for future improvements. Al-
though the implementation relied on relatively simple and openly available models for each
component, the system still achieved solid results. This indicates that the pipeline suc-
cessfully captures meaningful patterns in the EEG data, even under resource-constrained
conditions.
A noteworthy aspect of this project is its adherence to a rigorous and transparent evalua-
tion protocol. Unlike many recent state-of-the-art approaches, this study strictly avoided
any use of ground-truth labels during inference. In particular, the work by Jo et al. [91]
highlights a common issue in brain-to-text systems, where teacher forcing is used even
in the test phase. This involves providing the model with correct labels during evaluation,
thereby inflating performance metrics and undermining real-world applicability. By con-
trast, the present work ensures that the EEG encoder, similarity search, and language
model operate without access to the target sentence during testing, making the results
more representative of deployment conditions.
While the reported performance metrics may appear lower than those of some state-of-
the-art systems, they are arguably more reliable and generalizable. Furthermore, the
current pipeline allows for straightforward substitution of improved components, such as
more expressive EEG encoders, richer training datasets, or more advanced retrieval and
generation models. This modularity ensures that performance can scale with technologi-
cal progress.
An additional advantage of the proposed approach lies in its architectural separation be-
tween signal processing and language modeling. Instead of forcing the EEG embeddings
to lie directly in the language model’s embedding space, the system relies on a semantic
similarity search, followed by linguistic refinement through a language model. This decou-
pling reduces the burden on the EEG encoder, which no longer needs to encode highly
detailed semantic features or learn an embedding space aligned with the LLM. As a re-
sult, the system remains both tractable and extensible, offering a promising alternative to
more opaque end-to-end designs.
5.2
Future Directions
As the primary objective of this project has been to construct a modular and extensible
pipeline, every component of the proposed framework remains open to targeted improve-
ments. The design ensures that enhancing individual elements will not disrupt the overall
structure, thereby offering a robust foundation for future research. Several directions are
outlined below for the advancement of this work:
Dataset Improvements.
One of the most significant limitations of the current implemen-
tation is the scarcity and narrow scope of the dataset. Future work would benefit from em-
ploying larger datasets that provide multiple EEG recordings for each sentence, ideally
across different sessions and topics. Such diversity would allow the model to generalize
across contexts, making it more suitable for everyday applications. Additionally, the use
Brain-to-Text Translation Using Deep Learning and LLMs
39
==PAGE:48==
of invasive neural recording techniques such as ECoG could substantially improve sig-
nal quality by reducing the noise inherent in non-invasive EEG data, thus enabling more
reliable detection of subtle semantic features.
Preprocessing Enhancements.
The current study relies on the preprocessing pipeline
provided by the authors of the ZuCo dataset. This includes artifact removal using MARA,
a method that identifies and eliminates independent components likely to be artifacts.
However, MARA is known to be conservative in its filtering when used with default pa-
rameters. Future implementations might consider re-processing the data using ICLabel
[92], a more recent and widely adopted tool for automatic artifact classification, which has
shown improved performance in separating neural signals from noise.
Contextual Embedding Models.
The contextual sentence representations used in this
study were computed using the sentence-transformers all-mpnet-base-v2 model. While
this model offers a strong baseline and is publicly available, there exist more advanced
embedding models that may yield more accurate semantic similarity estimates. Substi-
tuting a more powerful model at this stage could enhance both retrieval and evaluation
phases.
EEG Embedding Optimization.
The EEG encoder is arguably the most critical com-
ponent of the pipeline. Improving its ability to generate meaningful sentence-level em-
beddings could lead to significant gains in overall system performance. Future directions
should explore the use of contrastive learning [93]. In contrastive learning, the model is
trained to minimize the distance between embeddings of EEG samples associated with
semantically similar sentences (positive pairs), while maximizing the distance from un-
related samples (negative pairs). This promotes the formation of a structured embed-
ding space in which semantic relationships are geometrically encoded, making it easier
to retrieve meaningful text from neural signals. Such training can be implemented using
supervised labels or through unsupervised strategies that derive pseudo-labels based
on similarity scores. Incorporating contrastive objectives could substantially enhance the
discriminability and semantic alignment of EEG embeddings, ultimately leading to more
accurate decoding.
Language Model Refinement.
The current system employs the mistralai/Mixtral-8x7B-
Instruct-v0.1 language model, which, despite being freely available and relatively pow-
erful, does not represent the current frontier in LLM performance. Incorporating more
advanced LLMs, potentially with larger parameter counts or improved alignment, would
likely enhance the fluency, coherence, and semantic fidelity of the generated outputs.
Prompt Engineering.
The formulation of the prompt provided to the language model
plays a pivotal role in determining the quality of the refined output. This study relied on
a single instruction format designed to elicit coherent and accurate sentence synthesis.
Future research should explore the impact of prompt variations on the resulting genera-
tion quality, ranging from concise to highly structured instructions (e.g by evaluating the
differences using cosine similarity).

Chapter 6: Conclusions
This thesis investigated the use of deep learning and retrieval-based methods to decode
natural language from non-invasive brain signals, specifically EEG, with the objective of
advancing assistive communication through BCI systems. The research focused on the
development of a modular pipeline capable of semantically aligning EEG representations
with textual embeddings, thereby enabling the generation of fluent natural language sen-
tences from neural activity. The primary aim was to design a flexible framework in which
each component can be independently improved, laying the foundation for future research
and iterative enhancements.
The research was grounded in the use of the publicly available ZuCo dataset, which con-
tains synchronized EEG recordings and corresponding textual stimuli during natural read-
ing tasks. The EEG signals underwent preprocessing and were fed into a custom deep
neural encoder designed to extract sentence-level embeddings from EEG time series.
These embeddings were then compared to contextual embeddings computed with the
pretrained model sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2, by computing vector similar-
ity search among a FAISS vector store.
The system retrieved the top-3 semantically closest sentences from a training corpus and
leveraged an LLM (specifically Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct) for sentence refinement. The LLM
was prompted to interpolate the information from the retrieved sentences and generate
a coherent final output sentence. This pipeline was tested on 50 held-out EEG samples,
each associated with an unseen sentence, to assess its generalization capabilities.
To evaluate the semantic fidelity of the generated sentences, two primary metrics were
used. First, cosine similarity in an embedding space was computed between the original
ground-truth sentence and the LLM-refined output using the sentence-transformers all-
mpnet-base-v2 model. Second, BLEU scores (BLEU-1 and BLEU-2) were calculated to
enable comparison with existing literature, despite their known limitations in capturing
semantic similarity.
The results showed a distribution of cosine similarity values centered around a mean of
0.2169, which, considering the complexity of the task, represents a meaningful degree of
semantic alignment. BLEU-1 and BLEU-2 scores were relatively low (0.1088 and 0.0308,
respectively), which is consistent with expectations given the open-ended nature of the
task and the lack of direct lexical overlap in the dataset.
Several representative examples of system outputs were qualitatively analyzed, highlight-
ing cases in which the pipeline successfully captured the semantic theme and sentiment
of the original sentence, as well as instances where it failed due to noise or ambiguity in
the EEG signal.
Overall, this work demonstrates the feasibility and potential of combining EEG encoding,
vector-based retrieval, and LLM-based refinement for natural language decoding from
brain activity. It also sheds light on the current limitations of the approach and offers a
foundation for future improvements in AI and Neuroscience.
