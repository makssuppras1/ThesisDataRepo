Abstract
This study focused on the mathematical modeling of a parabolic trough collector. The
equations that have been used and the coding is explained. The code is validated by 2 ref­
erences from CAS IEE (Institute of Electrical Engineering, Chinese Academy of Sciences)
and Aallborg CSP. In both cases the error of the provided or measured efficiency was sig­
nificantly close to the calculated one from the model that has been used. In the case of
CAS IEE, the average measured efficiency for the time­span that has been simulated
was 0.593 and the for the same time­span the average calculated efficiency is 0.599.In
the case of Aalborg CSP the results show an error of 0.48% between the calculated and
the provided peak efficiency. The last section of this study shows several simulations for
a better understanding of the system including seasonal analysis and different locations.
The locations were simulated for the places of Nicosia,Cyprus, Copenhagen,Denmark
and Abu Dhanbi,UAE.

1
Introduction
The growing need for energy that the world demands, rises the expectations of energy
sources . That’s why it is needed to manage the energy as much efficient as possible
and innovatively. The abundance of energy that solar has, only makes engineers glob­
ally fascinated to find innovative ways of using this energy for good use. European union
members has high goals of renewable share in the energy sector, that must be achieved in
the following years. They aim for a 32% share of renewable energy in energy consump­
tion by 2030, and 50% by 2050 [24]. These targets will be achieved not only by using
renewable sources but also by using bio­energy technologies to decarbonize industries
that are dependent on fossil fuels. Furthermore, another way to achieve these targets is
the electric mobility with the use of electric vehicles in Europe, where in Norway the 50%
of the car sales in 2018 were electric, looks quite promising for the future[24].
The most abundant source of energy in earth is the sun and is needed to be exploited
to be able to decarbonize from several types of fossil fuels that world is consuming the
last 50 years. There are two main ways of exploiting the sun, first by using its photons
to create electrical energy through photo­voltaic (PV) solar panels, and the second one
is to use the solar heat energy through solar collector technologies. In this paper, the
concentrating solar power (CSP) type is the one that is focused on.
There are four main CSP technologies, parabolic trough collectors (PTC), linear Frensel
reflector (LFR), power towers or central receiver systems(CRS) and dish/engine systems
(DE). These types of solar collectors are using the heat in the form of radiation to heat a
heating fluid for different purposes, such as domestic hot water or to run a steam cycle
and generate electricity. Compare to PV solar panels, CSP technologies are only receiv­
ing the direct normal irradiance (DNI) and they require a high amount of it to be feasible.
CSP plants are operating in the Sun Belt region, where is the range of places between
−40◦south and 40◦north from the equator[9].Another advantage of CSP systems is that
they can be combined with a thermal storage, where the most of the times molten salt is
the favourable solution; and this has many beneficial outcomes such as higher capacity
of a CSP plant, higher availability to elaborate electricity into the grid and minimize the
impact of weather changes (cloudy,foggy, rainy day). In addition, they can easily adapted
into a steam or gas cycle by providing the warm stream into it, which reduce if not neglect
the amount of fossil fuel that they might normally consume.
1.1
PTC characteristics
The type of CSP that will be examined further in the paper is the parabolic trough col­
lectors (PTC). This type uses its parabolic mirror to concentrate the solar radiation to the
absorber where the heating fluid is passing through. The radiation that is concentrated
to the receiver tube is 71 to 80 times the amount of solar radiation energy. The range
of temperatures that they normally operated is between 150 to 400 oC and the heating
fluid could be a synthetic oil, steam or molten salt [29]. But it can be also operated at
temperatures below 100oC for domestic hot water applications. The absorber is typically
a steel pipe which is covered by an outer glass vacuum tube to reduce the heat losses.
The steel pipe has normally high absorption, greater than 90% and high emissivity, higher
than 0.9 as well. The optical characteristics has a key role to the functioning of this type
of systems, because is strongly dependent on the tracking accuracy and the geometry
of the PTC elements (parabola, HCE). Normally, a one­axis tracking system is used so it

1.2
PTC applications
PTC is currently the most mature CSP technology and is used globally in many power
plants. Large PTC power plants can be found in USA, Morocco and Spain. The efficiency
of those power plants in the perspective of solar to electric ranges between 15% to 22%
for thermal power plants with a capacity greater than 30 MWe[11].The largest PTC power
plant worldwide is in Mojave Desert, California, USA with a total capacity of 354 MW.
In Europe many PTC power plants can be found in Spain, where the largest one is in
Logrosan with a capacity of 200 MWe. In Morocco they use a combination of PTC and
solar tower power station which makes it the largest CSP plant with a capacity of 510 MW.
This has been done in 3 phases, the first one they established a PTC plant of 160 MW
with a molten salt storage for 3 hours of low­light producing capacity, the phase 2 where
a 200 MW PTC plant has been added with a 7 hours storage capacity and the 3rd phase
is the molten solar power station where 7400 heliostats with a total aperture area of 1.3
Mm2 are facing the top of the solar tower at the height of 250m. Molten salt maximum
operating temperature is 565 oC and a dry air cooled condenser is used, to reduce the
water consumption that is mainly consumed for the cleaning of these mirrors so they have
the highest reflectance possible.[16].

1.3
History of PTC
The firs step of the PTC systems has been started back in 1870 by a Swedish engineer
in USA called John Ericsson. His first invention was a solar collector with aperture area
of 3.25 m2, that produced steam to drive an engine of 373 W. Followed by his steps,
Abbot took advantage of this to create a PTC that convert solar energy to mechanical
power and run a 0.37 kW steam engine. He also proved that the theoretical efficiency
of this system is 15.5% and the real one is 11.7%, taken into account that the steam
is at the temperature of 225 Co. An inactive period follows until the oil crisis in 1970
which has become the driving force to start focusing on this type of system again. The
U.S. Government’s Sandia National laboratories had designed the first two collectors that
were operated at temperature below 250 oC. After many researches in 2010 at Southeast
university with the corporation of Sanle electronic group has created the first PTC with
a Sanle­3 HCE (heating collecting element) receiver tube which showed promising signs
with decent reliability and performance. The rising of this technology is motivate all around
the world engineers to understand it and optimize it for the best possible solution [18].
1.4
Studies on PTC
Several studies have been made in the passed decade especially regarding this type of
technology.Agagna et al. study [15], which has been done on a 3D optical model in Mat­
lab and on a CFD model on ANSYS, presented the influence of tracking on PTC, and its
results showed that for an incidence angle of 0o (at noon) efficiency is at its maximum
value and as the incidence angle increases to 90o (sunrise and sunset time) optical ef­
ficiency is zero, thus thermal efficiency is zero as well. More outcomes from this study
was the proportional relation between heat losses and operating temperature. Moreover,
it is worthily noted that the temperature of both absorber and fluid increases in the axial
direction of the absorber, from the inlet to the outlet. In the study of A.Mohamad et al.
[10],the impact of temperature variation along the receiver tube has been analysed. It
was concluded that an increase of the length of the tube increases the heat losses, and

a double glazing cover is only recommended above 10 m length by taking into account
the economical perspective as well. Furthermore, an increase of the tube’s diameter is
increasing the heat losses as well. Geometry of the system is critical, to both optical and
thermal efficiencies. In the study of Pandey et al. [26], a CFD simulation based on the
L2S2 PTC from Sandia laboratory (USA in 1994) has been studied and the variation of
HTF temperature and mass flow rate has been analysed. This study showed that the
velocity and heat transfer are correlated and they have significant influence on the outlet
temperature of absorber. For a constant inlet temperature of 375.5K and a solar heat flux
of 933.7 W/m2 the optimum mass flowrate has been calculated equal to 0.6782 kg/s.This
flowrate is the optimum one, as for higher flowrate the heat transfer coefficient and the
velocity of the flow increases. The increase of the heat transfer coefficient has a posi­
tive influence but for higher flowrate the outlet temperature decreases , due to thermal
inertia. Another optimum geometrical characteristic that has been calculated in this study
was the optimum tube length, as for the temperature of the fluid increases as the length
increases too,but there is a crossover point where the huge amount of heat flux that has
been received by the fluid increase the velocity, thus decrease the outlet temperature.
For these specific characteristics that has been mentioned, the optimum length is 4.3m.
A method to increase the outlet temperature is by increasing the diameter of the tube
which allows more heat flux to be received by the HTF. Bakos et al.[4] has done an ex­
perimental study which shows the advantages on using a tracking system instead of a
fixed oriented collector.The results showed that it has an increased received solar energy
by 46.6%. [12] The main objectives was to enhance the heat transfer coefficient, thus
thermal efficiency, by focusing on two critical factors, geometry of absorber and HTF. His
study was done on Solidworks. The fluid that have been simulated were a thermal oil,
thermal oil with nanoparticles of Al2O3 and pressurized water. The alternative geometry
that has been examined was a wavy inner side of the absorber to enhance turbulences
in the flow. Results showed that the use of nanofluid increases the efficiency by 4.25%,
and the geometry changes has a benefit of 4.55%.This outcome can be verified as well
by Kaloudis et al. [13]. , where this study simulate a parabolic trough with a numerical
simulation, and the results proved that a 2­phase HTF increase the heat transfer coeffi­
cient and thus the thermal efficiency. He concluded that even with a 4% concentration of
nanoparticles Al2O3, an increase of efficiency of 10% can be obtained.
The aforementioned studies show the multifarious behavior of PTC systems and there
are many ways to improve its performance, but at the same time many aspects need to
be taken into consideration to design one.
1.5
Purpose of this study
The purpose of this study is to create a simulation tool to theoretically estimate the thermal
performance of a PTC collector under given weather conditions. To achieve this, the study
of the physics that is related to this problem needs to be solved and to create a code
in MATLAB that solves it. The study is separated to three sections, the mathematical
modeling, the validation and the applications. Then this model could be used to predict
the performance of a PTC system at a specific country, taking into account its coordinates
and climate. The computational tools are a helpful and economical­friendly approach
to evaluate a system. Experimental evaluations are quite expensive, and the exponential
growth of computational development is quite satisfying to be able to extract a reasonable
result that can verify the feasibility of a system.
The document presents the methodology of the calculations and the coding, the validation
part of the code and applications for different simulations.

Methods
In this section the methodology of the thesis will be described including the equations that
are required to be solved for the mathematical modeling of the project, the coding meth­
ods that have been followed and assumptions that have been taken. The mathematical
modeling is based on several references.([6],[7],[29],[1].[5]). The heat transfer modelling
of the system is based on the method of Soleff 2.0 ([23],[3]). This model simulates a
flat plate solar collector that solve the heat balance equations with the Newton­Raphson
method for non linear equations, and is discussed further in the section of Computing. The
output of this simulation are several performance parameters, such as efficiency, useful
heat, heat loss etc.An extend theory is shown also in chapter 4 [ Applications], which is
related to the simulations of the code for different cases.

2.4
Thermal analysis
Thermal efficiency relates the amount of useful heat that the heating fluid received and the
amount of solar irradiance that parabola received. To calculate this, several terms that are
related to heat transfers needs to be calculated at first. In a PTC system all three types
of heat transfers are contributing; conduction, convection and radiation. Conduction heat

2.5.1
Implementation of the Newton­Raphson method to the code
The aforementioned method of solving non­linear equations is the main method of the
code that solves the energy balance equations. The structure of it is more complex due to
the non linearity relation between the 4 equations of heat balances. There are 4 unknowns
in the 4 equations of heat transfer. These 4 unknowns are the temperatures inside and
outside of the receiver and the glass cover. The flow chart of this method can be seen in
the Appendix section (A.2). At first, guessed values are set to these 4 temperatures. Then
a while­loop is set where the condition is that is operating while the absolute difference
between the vector v that contains all 4 temperatures and the vector v1 which contains the
temperatures that have been set before of the start of the loop is less than a specific error
difference. The vector v1 is set to be a random number close to the vector v (e.g.v1=v+1).
The next step is to save the value of v to the v1 at the start of the loop, so it can be
compared in the next loop where the new vector v will be calculated at the end of the loop.
Then all the heat fluxes Q that have been mentioned in the previous section are calculated.
Then the vector f (2.87) is created that contains the 4 equations of the heat balance. All 4
equations were solved in the form of Q(T)=0 and are shown below (2.83,2.84,2.85,2.86).
Q1 : Qcon,r −Qkr −Qrrg −Qcrg = 0
(2.83)
Q2 : Qkr −Qcrf = 0
(2.84)
Q3 : Qrrg + Qcrg −Qkg = 0
(2.85)
Q4 : Qkg + Qcon,g −Qcgs −Qrgs = 0
(2.86)
f = [Q1; Q2; Q3; Q4]
(2.87)

the 4 temperatures Tro, Tri, Tgo, Tgi. So the next step is the calculation of the derivative

calculated by the partial derivative of each Q for each T (2.88), where i are the number

2.6
Assumptions
The calculation for this type of simulation required several assumptions, so it can be pos­
sible to be computed in an efficient and precise way. The list of the assumptions that have
been made follows:
• The heat flux that is reflected from the parabola’s mirror to the heating collection
element (HCE) is assumed that is interacting with the whole external area of the
tube. Normally only the bottom section of the tube receiving this heat.
• The temperature of the cross section area of the tube is uniform. This answered by
the previous point, as normally the bottom section of the fluid, the glass cover and
the receiver will have higher temperature than the upper section.
• The direction of the wind was assumed that it doesn’t have an impact on the heat
transfer when realistically it has. Different directions of the wind alter the heat trans­
fer between the glass cover tube and the air.
• The ambient air temperature Ta and wind speed vw were assumed constant through­
out the day.

be a minute or a second; depends on the time strep that is used. That’s why the heat
balance equations do not include the thermal inertia ( ˙mCp) of the elements of the thermal
fluid, the glass cover and the receiver. A more advance code could be used in a future

4.1.2
Flow charts of the code
In this section the flow charts of the two main loops that running the code are explained
and are shown in the appendix section (A.1,A.2). The inputs that have been set in the
main program are the date, place (coordinates), geometric characteristics of the PTC el­
ements, heat transfer fluid,optical and thermophysical properties of the PTC elements,
axis rotation (N­S. E­W). The variable inputs that the code has, are the DNI and the inlet
fluid temperature. So that is possible to analyse a whole day but also for a different inlet
fluid temperatures. The while­loop of the mean fluid temperature is shown in Appendix
(A.1). At first the inlet fluid temperature has been set (it is a known parameter), and the
mean fluid temperature Tf is calculated. Obviously, it is a guessed one as the outlet tem­
perature is still unknown. But it has to be calculated because the calculations of the heat
fluxes Q and thermpophysical properties of the HTF are calculated based on the mean
fluid temperature Tf. Another term is created Tfn which is the value that will be compared
in the while loop condition. The term eps that is shown in the condition of the loop is the
minimum error/difference between the mean fluid temperature that have been calculated
at the end of the loop with the one that it was at the start. That’s why the Tf has been set
equal to Tfn at the start of the while­loop. Then the Newton­Rapshson method follows
A.2. After the end of the while­loop of the Newton’s method, the performance charac­
teristics are calculated.The outputs of the Newton’s method are the heat fluxes and the
temperature of each element (fluid, receiver and glass cover). Therefore the outlet tem­
perature of the fluid can be calculated by equation 4.6, thus the mean fluid temperature.
The mean fluid temperature that has been calculated is saved to the term Tfn so it can
be compared to the Tf in the condition of the loop. This loop is ended when the absolute
difference between the new mean fluid temperature Tfn and the previous one Tf is less
than eps=0.001.
The flow chart of the newton method is explained in the previous section of: ”Implemen­
tation of the Newton­Raphson method to the code”.

be noticed which variables could change to minimize this. The radiative heat flux Qrrg
is proportional to the outer diameter and the length of the receiver. So the reduction of
those variables will decrease the radiative heat flux Qrrg , thus the heat losses. The glass
cover’s and the receiver’s emissivity has a proportional relation as well, according to the
equation 2.46. Obviously, any change to the design affects other heat fluxes as well, so
to optimize this type of model all type of heat fluxes are needed to be taken into account
to find the optimum design characteristics for the system. If the design characteristics
neglected from this equation there are only two variables that could be adjusted. The
reduction of the heat loss of Qrrg can be achieved with an decrease of the receiver’s and
glass cover’s emissivity. Another way to improve the system according to the equation
2.28, is by increasing the absorbance coefficient of the receiver. Higher concentrated
heat will benefited the whole system as a part of this heat will transform to the form of
conduction through the receiver, which is the heat flux that the heating fluid received.
Furthermore, an increase on the thermal conductivity of the receiver will be beneficial for
the system as well; which can be seen by equation2.33. As it can be seen by equation
2.29 the conductive heat flux Qkr is equal to convective heat flux Qcrf; thus a higher Qkr,
means a higher Qcrf. And this translates to a higher heat gain to the HTF.

Conclusions
The conclusions that can be drawn from this study are the multifarious behaviour of the
PTC system. One of the main purpose of this study was to create a simulation model that
could predict the performance characteristics of a PTC. The validation section shows a
reasonable precision to other studies and that verify the code that has been used. The
validation is based on 2 different studies from CAS IEE and Aalborg CSP. In the compari­
son of CAS IEE data the error of the average thermal efficiency is 1.01% and for the case
of Aalborg CSP is only 0.48%. That means that the model that has been created can be
used as a toll to extract performance characteristics for a future design of a PTC.
Moreover, the simulation results that are shown in the application’s section, are enough
to provide us with a good understanding of the system and how is operated in different
conditions. The effect of the latitude has a quite influence on the performance, as its con­
nection to DNI is direct. This can be seen by comparing the figures of the simulation on
the 25th of August for the three locations of Nicosia (4.4), Copenhagen (4.15), and Abu
Dhabi (4.16). Additionally, the pie charts that are shown in the applications section, are
able to provide the heat balance of the elements and can give a good understanding of
how the system could limit its heat losses.
A future study could include a dynamic model of the heat balance equations, and the TMY
data for a given location for a more accurate results. Furthermore, a further study could
include a sensitivity analysis that shows the influence of each parameter and it could be
used to design an optimized model of an existing PTC system.
