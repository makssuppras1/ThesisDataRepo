Abstract
Introduction Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is a chronic inflammatory demyelinating disease of the central
nervous system. Fatigue is considered the most prominent and debilitating symptom affecting up to
80% of patients. Yet, no effective treatment is available as the pathogenesis of fatigue is still poorly
understood. Different biomarkers have been hypothesized to have an association to fatigue, but
findings vary, and it is still unclear which brain regions and biomarkers drive fatigue. Event-Based
Modelling (EBM) might help identify patterns as they can reconstruct timelines for alterations
that may have started years before diagnosis.
Methods In this project, EBM was used to establish a timeline of micro- and macrostructural
changes associated with fatigue in patients with MS, using baseline data from the DanNORMS
study. Focus was on deep brain structures (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, thalamus) and
brainstem (pons, medulla, midbrain). Both standardized volume and quantitative susceptibility
mapping values were looked at. The project also included a replication of previously published
results by Eshaghi et al. [1] regarding the use of EBM to establish the progression of regional
atrophy in MS patients. This was done to assess the robustness of EBM.
Results In the replication study, EBM was used to determine a progression pattern for MS based
on the standardized volume of 20 brain regions, as a measures of regional atrophy. The same
progression ordering as the Eshaghi paper was not achieved and the EBM staging was found to
be negatively correlated with age. The EBM was relatively robust towards changes in number of
healthy controls but sensitive towards noise. In the fatigue study, the volume and iron measure of
seven brain structures were examined for their relation to fatigue. Few biomarkers showed trends
related to fatigue, disability or duration. EBM was unable to determine a consistent progression
pattern. Linear models determined a weak relation to fatigue.
Conclusion The EBM failed to identify a consistent underlying progression pattern based on the
selected biomarkers. Through complementary analyses caudate volume was found to be the primary
biomarker linking to fatigue. Through the replication attempt it was found that a high level of
attentiveness towards potential sources of noise in the data is required when employing EBM.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is a chronic inflammatory demyelinating disease of the central nervous
system. Demyelination reduces neuronal conductivity, resulting in a wide range of symptoms and
potential disabilities depending on the affected brain regions. The average age of diagnosis is 33 years,
and although life expectancy is shortened by about five to ten years [2], MS is a disease that people
live with for many years. The most prominent and debilitating symptom of MS is fatigue, which
affects up to 80% of patients and impacts both quality of life and productivity [3]. Yet no specific
treatment is available as the pathogenesis of fatigue is still unclear. Fatigue has been associated with
atrophy in brain stem[4], thalamus [3] and basal ganglia [5]. Furthermore, Susceptibility Weighted
Imaging (SWI), a technique increasingly employed to explore MS pathogenesis, has revealed notable
associations between disease duration and thalamic volume and iron [6]. Given the established
relationship between blood iron levels and general fatigue, a hypothesis has emerged suggesting
that changes in iron dynamics in the brain could influence fatigue. However, findings vary, and it is
still unclear which brain regions and biomarkers drive fatigue, and patients can experience severe
fatigue independently of disability level [7].
Advanced modelling approaches like Data-Driven Disease Progression Modelling (D3PM) might
help identify patterns in neurodegenerative diseases, reconstructing timelines for alterations that
may have started years before diagnosis. One method called event-based modelling (EBM) allows
estimation of disease timelines from cross-sectional data. This is especially important for a disease
like MS that has a long trajectory and limited non-clinical data such as SWI, making it difficult to
obtain sufficient longitudinal data to analyse the entire time course. However, with a sufficiently
large dataset of subjects at varying disease stages it might be possible to create a timeline on
an arbitrary time scale. EBM builds on the idea that disease progression is an accumulation of
different biomarkers gradually becoming abnormal, and that the disease progression is irreversible.
The model uses a control dataset to determine when biomarkers become abnormal and assumes
that the data represents the full disease progression and that subjects experience a similar sequence
of changes. It is thereby possible to determine the pattern with which a disease progress, and
potentially from this gain insight into which regional microstructural changes (i.e. iron changes)
and atrophy, that drive fatigue. This is explored in the hope of improving the understanding of
the mechanisms behind fatigue and potentially identify different phenotypes to ultimately aid the
development of treatments. As some patients experience severe fatigue while others mild fatigue,
unrelated to lesion-burden or disease duration, patients may represent different phenotypes and
potentially need different treatments.

Background
2.1
Multiple Sclerosis
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic inflammatory demyelinating disease of the central nervous
system (CNS) with a prominent neurodegenerative component. In MS, the immune system attacks
the spiralling protective sheaths, called myelin, that wrap around the nerves. Myelin is created by
oligodendrocytes and is made up of proteins and lipids that provide the electric insulation which
allows the electrical impulse to maintain its strength by skipping distances when travelling down
the axon. Damaged myelin leads to a slowed and potentially stopped electrical signal hindering the
communication between the brain and the rest of the body. This can lead to disabilities related
to the damaged neurons [8, 9]. Symptoms of MS vary based on nerve damage location and extent.
Some patients lose the ability to walk, while others have vision, balance, or speech issues [10]. A
standardized measure of the progression and disability level associated with MS is the Expanded
Disability Status Scale (EDSS), developed by Kurtzke JF in 1983 [11].
The diagnosis of MS is made based on fulfilment of different combinations of clinical, radiographic,
and laboratory criteria. The radiographic criteria focus on evidence of T2-hyperintense lesions (≥3
mm) disseminated in time and/or space [12]. MS is classified by the presence or absence of attacks
and remissions. The most common type, Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS), features temporary
relapses followed by remission and affects 85% of MS patients initially. Without treatment, about
half of these patients develop Secondary-Progressive MS (SPMS) within 10-20 years, characterized
by a gradual worsening of symptoms without remissions. Primary-Progressive MS (PPMS) affects
10% of patients and involves a steady decline from the start, with no remissions. A less common type,
Progression-Relapsing MS (PRMS), affecting 5% of patients, shows steady worsening regardless of
relapse and remission periods. Potential precursor diagnoses include clinically isolated syndrome
(CIS) and radiologically isolated syndrome [13].
Lesions in MS are regions of demyelination. These lesions can be seen in magnetic resonance images
(MRI) as hyper-intensities or white patches in FLAIR MRI, as can be seen in Figure 2. In relapsing
forms of MS, remyelination occurs in many lesions which can partly restore neuronal function
and prevent further disability. However, remyelination will be increasing incomplete over time. For
progressive forms of MS, remyelination might be sparse [14].

2.1.1
Multiple Sclerosis and Fatigue
For many patients with MS, fatigue is the most prominent symptom, affecting up to 80% of patients.
Fatigue is also often considered the most disabling symptom and impacts both quality of life and
productivity [3]. Fatigue appears independent of disability level. The aetiology of fatigue is complex,
and its pathogenesis is still unclear and debated. Studies have found some correlation between
fatigue and microstructural abnormalities and atrophy in basal ganglia [5], brainstem [4] and
thalamus [3, 15, 16]. Furthermore, SWI has revealed notable associations between disease duration
and thalamic volume and iron [6]. Given the established relationship between blood iron levels and
general fatigue, it has been hypothesised that iron changes in the brain could influence fatigue.
Rocca et al 2014 found atrophy in strategic brain regions to have a stronger association with fatigue
than measures of global brain damage [17]. Systematic reviews of published studies [3, 16], indicate
that brain atrophy in the thalamus plays a role. Bernitsas et al 2017 found that thalamus and
pallidum volumes were significantly lower in fatigued subjects (FSS ≥5.1) [15]. Calabrese et al 2010
reported lower thalamus, putamen, and caudate volumes in fatigued versus non-fatigued RRMS
subjects [5]. Gómez et al 2013 found that fatigued patients had more extensive grey matter (GM)
and white matter (WM) atrophy in the brainstem compared to the non-fatigued [4]. Further support
of the potential involvement of the brain stem comes from Barnden et al 2011 who found midbrain
WM volume decreases with increasing fatigue duration in a study on chronic fatigue syndrome [18].
Schweser et al 2018 found reduced magnetic susceptibility in thalamic subregions in RRMS patients,
linked to disease duration, indicating decreased tissue iron and suggesting an involvement of chronic
microglia activation [6]. In the normal brain, iron is stored in oligodendrocytes and myelin, but in
MS, its release in demyelinating lesions can worsen tissue damage. In addition, alterations in the

Several different scales exist to quantify fatigue, both general and disease specific [20]. One scale,
designed specifically for MS, was developed and tested against other fatigue scales by Penner et
al. in 2009 to asses MS-related cognitive and motor fatigue [21]. This fatigue scale for motor and
cognitive functions (FSMC) consists of a questionnaire of 20 questions, with 10 questions addressing
motor fatigue and 10 focusing on cognitive fatigue. Each question is rated on a 5-point scale, from
1 (does not apply at all) to 5 (applies completely). Consequently, the FSMC scale ranges from
a minimum score of 20 to a maximum score of 100. Based on these scores, fatigue levels can be
categorized accordingly: A total FSMC score below 43 indicates no fatigue, scores between 43 and
53 denote mild fatigue, scores from 53 to 63 represent moderate fatigue, and scores above 63 signify
severe fatigue. For details see Appendix A.
2.2
Magnetic Resonance Imaging
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a powerful and safe medical imaging technique primarily
imaging soft tissue. The technique utilizes the magnetic properties of the abundant amount of
hydrogen nuclei in human tissue, including in the brain. An external magnetic field (B0) is applied
to align the normally random orientation of hydrogen nuclei with the external field, resulting in a net
magnetization. By applying radio waves at the appropriate resonance frequency for the precessing
nuclei (precessing B0 at the Larmor frequency), a transversal magnetization can be created. This
is called excitation. After the radio frequency pulse is removed, the nuclei gradually realign with
the B0 field. This is called relaxation. Relaxation time varies depending on tissue structure, as
interactions between neighbouring protons affect the speed of relaxation and therefore depends on
tissue firmness. These variations result in different signal intensities from different tissues. MRI
sequences use the difference in relaxation properties by adjusting acquisition parameters such as
for example echo time (TE), which refers to the delay between the excitation and signal collection,
and repetition time (TR), which is the interval between successive excitations of the same tissue
slice. These acquisition parameters are used to weight the scans by tissue properties as expressed
by relaxation time. The tissue parameters include the time it takes tissue to regain longitudinal
magnetization (T1) or to lose transversal magnetization (T2). A commonly used T2-weighted
(T2w) sequence is the Fluid Attenuated Inversion Recovery (FLAIR) sequence which uses very
long TR and TE to create a T2 weighting and an inversion pulse to suppress the signal from

2.2.1
Magnetic Susceptibility
Magnetic susceptibility is a physical property that describes the extent to which a material becomes
magnetized when exposed to a magnetic field. Magnetic susceptibility is primarily determined by
the magnetization from orbital electrons compared to the MRI signal which originates from nuclear
magnetization [8]. Biological tissue can be either paramagnetic, such as haemoproteins and ferritin,
and have positive susceptibility or be diamagnetic, like water and most brain tissue, and have
negative susceptibility. The magnetic property of a tissue depends on its molecular contents and
microstructure. For example, haemoglobin is paramagnetic when oxygenised and diamagnetic when
deoxygenised. Air is also paramagnetic and therefore has a positive susceptibility which can create
large susceptibility gradients in areas where air cavities are close to the brain, such as the nasal
cavity [8].
The most common sequence for visualizing magnetic susceptibility is the spoiled gradient-recalled-
echo (GRE) sequence. The sequences can apply a single echo or multiple echoes to obtain magnitude
image(s) and phase image(s). The contrast of the magnitude images evolves over the echoes and
exhibits the exponential decay (T2* decay) of the tissue. From the inverse of this T2* relaxation
time, an R2* susceptibility map can be created. The phase images of the GRE sequence measure
the local frequency offset relative to the Larmor frequency, thereby reflecting the magnetic field
perturbations. From the phase images, an indication of iron concentration is obtained based on the
spin changes that occur around the iron molecules. From the phase image(s), a susceptibility map
can be calculated by means of quantitative susceptibility mapping (QSM) [8, 23].
QSM is a technique used for evaluating the magnetic susceptibility of tissue based on the fre-
quency shifts which can be seen in the phase images. Unlike magnitude data, phase information
accumulates with time causing phase wraps. A well-thought-out processing is therefore needed
to obtain anatomical information from the phase images. In a phase image, there will be several
phase wraps where the phase wraps from 0 to 2π (see Figure 3a). Phase unwrapping is therefore
necessary (see Figure 3b). Furthermore, the contribution of the surrounding (non-local) field, e.g.
high susceptibility difference between air and tissue, must be removed so that dipole inversion
can be performed on the unwrapped local phase image (see Figure 3c). To obtain a quantitative
map of the magnetic susceptibility, the non-local effects of the susceptibility source, which can
create field changes far away, need to be reverted back to the source. The field changes around a

2.3
Disease Progression Modelling
Data-Driven Disease Progression Modelling (D3PM) was first introduced in 2011 by Fonteijn et al
[25] to look at Alzheimer’s disease. D3PM is particularly aimed at reconstructing neurodegenerative
disease timelines where neurodegeneration can start many years before the time of diagnosis. The
novel element to the approach, compared to previous disease progression models, was that no a
priori division of patients into clinical stages was required. It became possible to describe disease
progression as a series of events using large-scale heterogeneous datasets. D3PMs are deemed
holistic statistical models at the intersect of unsupervised learning and traditional longitudinal
modelling. These models are defined by their two main features; first the ability to reconstruct the
disease trajectory on an arbitrary disease timeline and secondly that they are purely data driven
[26]. Under the overarching D3PM category, there are two groups of models distinguished based
on whether the available data is cross-sectional or longitudinal. The D3PM framework that allows
estimation of disease timelines from cross-sectional data is called event-based modelling (EBM).
EBM builds on the idea that disease progression is an accumulation of different biomarkers becoming
abnormal. Within EBM, biomarkers refer to any imaging or non-imaging marker of neurodegener-

Fitting the EBM requires evaluating the probability density function p(S|X) of a particular event
sequence given the data. As this is not readily available, Bayes’ theorem is used to obtain the
posterior distribution. Besides the prior p(S) and the marginal p(X), the likelihood of the data given
a particular sequence needs to be evaluated p(X|S). This likelihood is evaluated by fitting a mixture
model with two components on the data (see Figure 4). One component is fitted to the normal
(control) data and the other component provides the model for the abnormal distribution [25]. EBM
assumes independence between biomarkers, allowing it to fit a mixture model for each biomarker
and then determining the likelihood of a given sequence by the product of the probabilities for each
data point, with some biomarkers being abnormal and others being normal [28]. Over the years,
Page 8 of 49
==PAGE:17==
2
BACKGROUND
different combinations of components have been applied allowing for a range of biomarker data types.
In the original paper by Fonteijn et al. [25], a mixture of a Gaussian and a uniform distribution was
fitted to the data as the biomarkers were atrophy measures where unity corresponds to no volume
change and values less than one indicate atrophy. The uniform distribution was used to describe
the gradual decline in volume with disease progression [25]. Later, a mixture of two Gaussians
(GMM) was implemented [29] and most recently, a kernel density estimation (KDE) to allow highly
non-Gaussian data such as cognitive scores [30]. The complex nature of neurodegenerative diseases
calls for integrating diverse imaging and non-imaging biomarkers, whose integration is challenging
with traditional statistical methods but is a key strength of EBM [28].
The EBM requires a representative dataset covering the full progression of the disease in order
to determine a disease progression pattern purely based on cross-sectional data. Furthermore, the
model generally assumes a similar disease trajectory across individuals. However, more recently
alternative models which include a clustering element have been developed to allow for subgroup
specific disease trajectories e.g. defining different disease phenotypes [31]. Finally, as the EBM is
designed to estimate an underlying cumulative process, it follows that disease progression must be
irreversible.
Eshaghi et al 2018 [1] used EBM to look at the progression of regional GM atrophy in MS and
its association with disability accumulation. They looked at a cohort of 1424 subjects with an
average of 2.5 scans per patient from which the volumes of 24 GM regions were used as input to
the EBM. The MS cohort consisted of 20% CIS, 10% PPMS and 70% relapse-onset MS (includes
RRMS and SPMS). They found the sequence of regional atrophy to be consistent across the three
MS phenotypes and that the data-driven staging of atrophy progression showed that GM atrophy
spreads to involve more regions over time. Finally, they found no correlation between baseline EBM
stage and EDSS score. However, as they worked with some longitudinal data they found a significant
association between the annual rate of EBM stage and the annual rate of EDSS worsening.

Advanced Normalization Tools (ANTs) is an open source C++ library for medical image registra-

The FreeSurfer image analysis suite is an open-source neuroimaging toolkit. Five of the available
tools were used, all from FreeSurfer version 7.4.0. The Sequence Adaptive Multimodal SEGmen-
tation (SAMSEG) tool was used to create a segmentation mask of lesions in the DanNORMS images.
SAMSEG has an extension to robustly segment WM lesions in MS patients by accepting multi-

the BET tool was used for brain extraction [44]. Furthermore, a WM mask was generated using

boundary correction. Finally, by using the lesion segmentations from SAMSEG the lesions were ’filled’
in the T1w images using the lesion_filling tool [47]. lesion_filling uses intensities from the
non-lesion neighbourhood to fill the lesion area. It has been found that refilling lesions can improve
accurate brain volume measurements [47].
Manual segmentation of brain scans is time consuming and error prone. Segmentation software
has therefore been developed for automated segmentation. FSL’s FIRST and FreeSurfer’s image
analysis suite are examples of such segmentation tools. These are based on a combination of a
subject-independent atlas from manual segmentation of images and probabilistic computing to
determine the subject-specific shape and volume of each structure [41, 46].

3.3
Biomarker Processing
For the Replication Study, the biomarkers needed in the EBM was the volume of 20 ROI. 12 cortical
regions: posterior- and midcingulate, superior-, middle- and inferiortemporal, entorhinal, insula,
precuneus, cuneus, lingual gyrus, fusiform gyrus and precentral gyrus. Eight subcortical regions:
brainstem, thalamus, hippocampus, accumbens, putamen, amygdala, caudate and pallidum. The
volume of these regions were calculated from the FreeSurfer DKT atlas voxel-wise segmentation
in template space and the voxel dimension (1mm x 1mm x 1mm). The volume of regions in both
the left and right hemisphere were averaged between them.
The effect of the confounders age and sex was removed by fitting a linear regression model for each
biomarker on the controls [51]. On the full dataset, the residual of the fit was then used as the
biomarker value. This choice was made based on inspection of the slope describing the relation
between age and e.g. thalamus volume where the slopes were slightly different for the control groups
versus the abnormal groups. Therefore, as there might be some interaction between disease-related
and age-related effects that should not be removed, the controls were used to determine the potential
effect of age and sex.
Furthermore, since the SRPBS dataset comprised of data from 11 different sites, a linear regression
model was fitted to regress out effect of scanner protocol on the volume data. For some of the brain
regions, there were very clear differences between sites (see Figure 22 in Appendix C). Optimally, the
potential scanner effect between the different datasets, SRPBS, HCP and DanNORMS, should also
have been removed. However, as there was insufficient overlap in subject characteristics across the
three datasets, this was not deemed feasible. As a final step, the regional volumes were standardized
and after visual inspection a z-score threshold of five was chosen to remove outliers, resulting in 12
subjects being removed.
For the Fatigue Study, a similar approach was used to determine the volume of the ROIs: thalamus,
caudate, putamen, pallidum, midbrain, pons and medulla. The iron concentration measure based
on the QSM was calculated as the mean susceptibility within a segmented region. The mean
susceptibility within the regions was selected as there was no visible outliers affecting the mean
value. The QSM values were mean normalized by the whole brain QSM values. The volumes and
iron concentration measures were adjusted for effects of age and sex. The linear regression model
for each biomarker was fitted on the control group of the cohort. The subjects with fatigue scores
below 43 based on the FSMC questionnaire are defined as having no fatigue and therefore chosen
as the control group for the investigation of the mechanisms of fatigue.

3.4
Event-Based Model
In this project, the EBM implemented by the Progression Of Neurological Disease group (POND)
at University College London (UCL) was used (available at https://github.com/ucl-pond/kde_
ebm). This code was developed in connection with the article by Firth et al. [30]. The EBM consists
of three main steps to estimate a probabilistic sequence of biomarker events representing the
cumulative effects of neurodegenerative disease progression.
3.4.1
Detect Disease Progression Events
The first step involves fitting a mixture of two distributions depending on the nature of the data. For
this project, a mixture of two Gaussians was chosen as all biomarkers were continuous, standardized
and normally distributed. Depending on the assumptions of the data two approaches have been
adopted for fitting the Gaussian mixture models. In the original implementation by Fonteijn et al.
[25], they had a well defined control group and thereby fixed all control data to be part of the normal
component and then fitted the abnormal data such that some fell under the normal component
and other data made up the abnormal component. Later, Young et al. [29] used EBM to look at
sporadic Alzheimer’s disease where the cognitively normal group may have had presymptomatic
Alzheimer’s disease and they therefore fitted a mixture of two Gaussians using all subjects to obtain
the two models. In this project both approaches were used. In the Replication Study a mixture
of two Gaussians was fitted where one component was fixed to the control group. In the Fatigue
Study, all data was used to fit the two Gaussians as the control data was not as well defined, since
the categorization of MS subjects into no fatigue was based on the FSMC questionnaire.

3.4.3
Establish Certainty of Sequence
The initial sequence determined by the greedy ascent algorithm was used as starting point to
establish a positional variance diagram of the uncertainty in the maximum likelihood sequence.
The positional variance was determined by a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) process.
MCMC is a well established mathematical process dating back to the Metropolis algorithm first
described by Metropolis et al in 1953 [57, 58]. A Markov chain is a succession of memoryless steps
from one state to another. It is a stochastic process said to have the Markov property where the
probability of the next state is influenced only by the current state [59]. Monte Carlo methods are
computational techniques emerged from the research scientists of Los Alamos, the same group who
produced the first MCMC algorithm [58]. A Monte Carlo process refers to a simulation that draws
random samples from a posterior distribution of interest [60]. MCMC simulations combine these
two processes to allow for exploration of the posterior distribution.
The EBM employed in this project uses a MCMC algorithm to sample from p(S|X) to estimate the
uncertainty in the sequence. As with greedy ascent, the next sequence is created by perturbing the
current sequence i.e., swapping two events in the sequence at random. However, instead of selecting
the best sequence the next sample is selected with a probability determined by the difference in
likelihood between the current sequence and the next sequence. From 10,000 MCMC samples a
positional variance diagram was created by determining the proportion of MCMC samples where
each biomarker occurred in a specific position in of the sequence. Subsequently, the subjects were
staged by calculating the likelihood of all M + 1 stages by (5), given the subjects’ data and the
MCMC sampled sequence with the highest likelihood.
A progression pattern was thus established by EBM on the full dataset. However, as this might
not generalize well to another combination of subjects, a bootstrap method was employed as a way
of further determining the confidence of the progression pattern. Bootstrapping is a resampling
technique first introduced by Bradley Efron in 1979 [61] where B bootstrap samples are obtained
by randomly sampling N times (from a dataset of size N), with replacement. Hereby, B independent
bootstrap samples were generated as approximations of samples from the same distribution as the
original dataset. Fitting EBM to each bootstrap sample reveals the impact of slight data differences,
resulting in a more generalized progression pattern.

3.5
Alternative Models
A number of alternative models were applied in this project to evaluate potential relations be-
tween the biomarkers and fatigue. These models were employed as a supplement to EBM, a com-
plex method with its own limitations. These models were implemented using the Python library
SciKit-Learn [62].
Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression with one response variable, PLS1, can be used to construct
a linear combination of input variables allowing dimensionality reduction in the direction with high
variance and high correlation with the response variable. PLS takes the latent structure of both the
input variables and the dependent variable into account. The first component of PLS1 corresponds
to the latent structure in X that maximizes the covariance with the response variable [52, 63]. In
this project PLS1 was applied using PLSRegression with FSMC fatigue scores as the response
variable and the standardized volume and iron concentration measures for the seven ROIs as the
predictor variables. This results in a linear combination of the biomarkers that has the highest
correlation with the fatigue scores.
Elastic Net Regression is a linear regression model that combines Ridge regression and Lasso
regression. It was first proposed by Zou and Hastie [64] as a new regularization and variable
selection method. Lasso and Ridge regression models have a regularization term that shrinks the
regression coefficients either towards zero (L2 ridge penalty) or that shrinks some coefficients to be
zero (L1 lasso penalty) thereby doing a variable selection. The combination of these two shrinkage
methods aims at alleviating over-fitting. Elastic net regression was applied using ElasticNet from
the SciKit-Learn library.
The statistical significance of the results from these models was evaluated using a permutation
test, a non-parametric statistical test with few assumptions. It allows choosing the performance
measure or numerical value that best describes the data or hypothesis and use that as the test
statistic. Permutation tests relies on an assumption of exchangeability, meaning that under the
null hypothesis the response variables of the observations are interchangeable and would result
in the same distribution regardless. Through 10,000 permutations of the response variable and
subsequent evaluation of the fitted model, it is possible to evaluate if the test statistic of the model’s
performance on the original data is significantly better compared to the model’s performance on
randomized data [65].

5
Discussion
The primary aim of this project was to establish a timeline of micro- and macrostructural changes
associated with fatigue in patients with MS. To fulfil this aim, a secondary aim arouse, namely to
evaluate the robustness of EBM by means of a Replication Study.
5.1
Validity of Replication Study
The Replication Study was conducted to assess the robustness of EBM. Much effort was put into
making the replication as close as possible to the Eshaghi paper. However, a proper replication
was not feasible, primarily due to suboptimal data for this replication, which became increasingly
clear during the replication attempt. The dissimilarity in progression pattern was not in it self
unexpected as this could be ascribed to the difference in MS populations. The challenge was more
that the EBM staging obtained in the Replication Study was negatively correlated with age which
is inconsistent with the expected relation to disease progression.
Closer examination of Figure 7 indicates that in seven of the 20 brain regions, the mean volume of
the MS subjects exceeded that of the HC subjects. Although lesion burden may vary, the overall
trend in MS is a steady increase in atrophy, resulting in a consistent decrease in volume [66]. The
pattern seen in the data for the Replication Study thus deviates from the expected trend based on
existing literature and the findings of Eshaghi et al. Their results demonstrated that the HC group
consistently exhibited a larger volume, occasionally equal to that of CIS subjects (those with the
lowest disease burden). The discrepancy may be attributed to several factors as discussed below.
Firstly, the DanNORMS cohort included a substantial proportion of subjects newly diagnosed
with MS (40% duration ≤2 months) compared to the population investigated by Eshaghi et al.
In the Eshaghi study, the mean disease duration for patients with relapse-onset MS was 8 years,
while the DanNORMS fatigued group, those with the highest mean disease duration, had a mean
disease duration of about 5 years. This indicates that the DanNORMS cohort may not have been
as advanced in disease progression as the Eshaghi MS cohort, making the DanNORMS cohort less
different from the HC group, and a progression pattern thus more difficult to determine. In addition,
the DanNORMS dataset was much smaller, with only 193 MS subjects compared to Eshaghi’s 1214
(including 836 Relapse-onset MS patients). This limits clear disease representation, leading to less
clear biomarker distributions for seldom events. In other words, there might simply have been less
abnormal patterns to detect thereby making it more difficult to detect the underlying sequence.
Secondly, the HC dataset used for the Replication Study was suboptimal. Initially, the HC cohort
consisted only of the SRPBS dataset, as this dataset included a substantial number of healthy
controls with a wide age range that aligned well with the DanNORMS cohort. The age-matching
Page 36 of 49
==PAGE:45==
5

was the primary focus at first to handle the effects of age-related volume difference [67]. There
was no available information regarding the race of the participants in the SRPBS dataset, but
it is assumed to be primarily East Asian, whereas the DanNORMS dataset is presumed to be
predominantly Caucasian. Research has indicated that there are significant volume differences for
certain brain regions between East Asian and Caucasian populations [68, 69]. This difference in race
may affect the interpretability of the results, since the HC cohort was solely from the SRPBS dataset
and the MS cohort exclusively from the DanNORMS dataset, making it challenging to account
for potential confounding effects of race. It was therefore deemed necessary to find an additional
HC dataset to minimise potential race effects that could blur a disease progression pattern. For
this, the HCP young adult dataset was acquired. Originally, the HCP aging dataset was wished for,
but the needed approval was too difficult to obtain, and the HCP young adult dataset was used
instead. The HCP young adult dataset contains a large number of healthy subjects, all scanned
with the same scanner and following the same protocol. This is desirable as it removes scanner
effects between-subjects which can have a significant impact on volumetric measures [70]. The
HCP cohort was, however, not age matched with the DanNORMS cohort, as the HCP cohort was
younger than the DanNORMS cohort. In theory, the addition of HCP dataset to the pooled HC
data created a better matched dataset regarding race. However, the inclusion of HCP made the
HC dataset have a lower mean age than the MS cohort and the problem of accounting for scanner
effects persisted. Since there was no clear overlap between the three datasets, the effect of different
scanners/protocols could not be accounted for across the datasets. The SRPBS data was acquired
at multiple sites and site differences (as a proxy for scanner/protocol effect) could be accounted
for within this data. However, since the DanNORMS dataset only comprised of subjects with MS
any attempt at determining scanner effects for DanNORMS versus SRPBS or HCP would likely
also include disease effects.
Thirdly, it was not possible to decode all 24 brain regions mentioned in the Eshaghi paper and find
them in the FreeSurfer output, only 20 of the regions were found. This is, however, not believed
to have had any major impact on the results as the missing four regions would most likely not have
affected the ordering of the remaining regions.
Finally, Eshaghi et al. had longitudinal data (mean 2.54 scans per patient with SD of 1.04), while
only cross-sectional data was available for the Replication Study. This difference had several
consequences. As they had longitudinal data, Eshaghi et al. used a within subject template and
then adjusted regional volumes for intracranial volume (ICV). This was unfortunately not noticed
until late during the project. For the Replication Study, normalization was done by brining the
segmentations into a study-specific template space without explicitly adjusting the regional volumes
for ICV. The availability of longitudinal data also increase the data size to 3604 T1w scans in the
Page 37 of 49
==PAGE:46==

Eshaghi paper, making the amount of data substantially larger than what was available for the
replication. It furthermore makes it difficult to directly compare the results due to longitudinal
effects such as relating the rate of change in EDSS to the rate of change in EBM stage. In the
Eshaghi paper, a correlation between EDSS and EBM stage was reported for the full dataset, but
not when looking only at the baseline data, the latter being consistent with what was seen in the
Replication Study.
5.2
Considerations Regarding Event-Based Model
A key strength of EBM is that it is a data-driven approach that does not rely on predefined disease
stages, theoretically allowing it to infer objective characterization of disease progression. However,
as events are inferred based on biomarker differences in the dataset, the model may be at risk of
catching differences based on noise (other factors than those being studied). This could be noise
introduced by scanner differences or by age or race differences. Both examples have somewhat been
exemplified in Figure 13.
Another valuable strength is that a disease progression can be determined based on cross-sectional
data. However, the estimated sequence of events may not reflect the true underlying disease pro-
gression, as the sequence of events is determined only based on the specific data included in the
model. If the study cohort is biased by e.g. an over-representation of early-stage patients then the
resulting sequence might not generalize well. Furthermore, when using EBM with a dataset where
only some subjects have longitudinal data, the progression pattern might be biased by the event
ordering for these subjects over the general progression pattern, since the measures for the same
subject at different time points are assumed independent [25].
EBM assumes that biomarkers are independent of one another, meaning their progression is modelled
without considering interdependencies. This may hold for some biomarkers but not for all [28].
The EBM framework allows for a wide range of biomarkers to be included which can provide
a more comprehensive view of disease dynamics. However, the model does not account for how
the biomarkers might co-vary based on potential biological interdependency [31]. Ignoring these
relationships may lead to incorrect event ordering.
Moreover, EBM assumes a common disease trajectory across all individuals, which is a known
limitation of the model, as neurodegenerative diseases are often heterogeneous. Despite this, it
has been argued that the model can still capture an average trajectory that reflects key disease
characteristics [28]. Furthermore, newer implementations of EBM have attempted to alleviate
this limitation. An example of this is SuStaIn which includes Subtype and Stage Inference, first
introduced by Young et al [29]. SuStaIn aims at addressing the limitation by clustering subjects
into subgroups with common disease progression pattern. The problem of a single static progression

pattern has also been attempted addreesed by Venkatraghavan el al [27] in their implementation of
a discriminative event based model (DEBM). In their implementation, they introduced the concept
of relative distances between events to capture heterogeneity in disease progression speed between
individuals. These models are nice, but as with any model awareness of the model assumptions is
essential when using them.
Contrary to what was initially expected, EBM did not appear to be that sensitive towards number
of subjects in the dataset. The larger a dataset, the better the edges of the distribution is defined
which increases the likelihood that the ordering represents the true disease trajectory. However,
even on a small dataset with relatively distinct groups a consistent pattern will be found, although
more muddy compared to when more subjects are included, as seen in Figure 12.
5.3
Trajectory of Fatigue
The Fatigue Study aimed at establishing a timeline of biomarker changes associated with fatigue in
subjects with MS. It was therefore decided to only include subject with MS and then distinguishing
based on the experienced fatigue. This will inevitably make the biomarker difference between the
two groups smaller as all subjects will have some MS related changes. It does, however, increase
the likelihood that differences detected are in fact fatigue related.
The ability to detect biomarker changes related to fatigue will be highly influenced with how
well fatigue versus no fatigue is defined. The definition of fatigue is here based on the FSMC
questionnaire. The scoring is done based on 20 questions, rated from 1 to 5. A subjects with no
fatigue will therefore have a score of 20. Since Eshaghi et al. had around 10% of the total cohort
as controls, it was originally decided to use 22 as the cut-off to achieve around 10% of data as ’no
fatigue’ subjects. However, as this did not provide sufficient data to create meaningful distributions
it was latter decided to use a FSMC cut-off of < 43 as no fatigue [21]. This may seem somewhat
arbitrary and additional scoring based on other fatigue scoring systems [20] could potentially have
improved the splitting of subjects into fatigue and no fatigue. However, even when the EBM was
applied on only extremes, i.e., only no fatigue and severe fatigue subjects there was still no clearer
progression pattern.
A key assumption of EBM is that the data should represent an underlying progression, which may
not be fulfilled for fatigue. Machtoub et al. [71], based on a retrospective review of 483 patients
with MS, reported that around 63.6% of subjects had either stable or improvement in fatigue over
a two year period. Englund et al. [72] also found that the trajectories of fatigue over 11 years
for subjects with no, low, mild and severe fatigue remained stable while subjects with moderate
fatigue increased to severe fatigue. Subjects experiencing severe fatigue had on average an initial
worsening followed by slight improvement over the 11 years. This indicates that the assumption

of progression related to fatigue might not hold, and that there might be different subgroups with
different fatigue trajectory patterns. Instead of dividing subjects into whether or not they experience
fatigue, subjects could be divided based on their fatigue trajectory pattern (stable, improvement,
worsening over time) and then biomarker patterns could be assessed.
5.4
Fatigue Study Biomarker Considerations
For the Fatigue Study, it was decided to focus on basal ganglia, thalamus and brain stem and
to look at atrophy and iron concentration changes in these regions. This decision was based on
potential association to fatigue described in the literature [3–6]. More regions could have been
included, as EBM can handle many variables, although this may make the progression pattern a
bit more muddy. As an example, amygdala and hippocampus have been reported to be correlated
to fatigue scores [73] and could thus have been considered. Furthermore, since depression has been
reported as the best predictor of an increase in fatigue over time [74], depression scores could have
been included in some way, if a proper depression score had been available.
It could also have been relevant to look at more nuanced values for the regions, such as e.g. looking
at subregions, by hemisphere, using more nuanced QSM measures than mean QSM for a region, or
even a voxel-based approach. Early in the project, the monoaminergic nuclei (LC and VTA) , which
reside in the brain stem and have been connected to fatigue [75], were included in the EBM, but
this did not add anything. All the hemisphere specific DGM biomarkers were also tried included
in the EBM, but gave nothing major and no further focus was put on this. Finally, as caudate was
identified as being associated with fatigue, looking further into its subregions (head, body and tail)
could also have been relevant, but was not looked at.
Finally, as an alternative to the EBM model, more standard approaches were applied to investigate
potential associations between biomarkers and fatigue. As the data had low SNR, two linear
models (PLS and ElasticNet) with slightly different approaches were selected. PLS showed how
the biomarkers could be transformed to a new space reflecting the fatigue scores, while ElasticNet
performed feature selection. Both found caudate volume to be the strongest predictor of fatigue.
This finding is consistent with the initial data analysis.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
This thesis has explored Event-Based Modelling (EBM) as a technique for identifying progression
patterns of micro- and macrostructural changes in deep brain structures and the brainstem in
relation to fatigue in multiple sclerosis (MS).
The results of the Replication Study demonstrated that the EBM model with the available data
(MS subjects from DanNORMS and HC subjects from two open-access datasets) was able to detect a
consistent progression pattern, but it did not matched the progression patterns obtained by Eshaghi
et al. The discrepancy in progression pattern was not in it self unexpected as this could be ascribed
to the difference in MS populations. However, the EBM staging in the Replication Study was
negatively correlated with age, which contradicts the expected relationship with disease progression.
This does not invalidate the EBM approach but suggests that a high level of attentiveness towards
potential sources of noise in the data is required when employing EBM.
The results of the Fatigue Study demonstrated that the EBM model was able to distinguish no
fatigue subjects from subjects with fatigue, but failed to identify a consistent underlying progression
pattern based on the selected biomarkers. This would appear to be the consequence of a non-
progressive trajectory of fatigue in MS as well as a low signal-to-noise ratio in the biomarker values
for the DanNORMS cohort. Few biomarkers showed trends related to fatigue, disability or duration.
Complementary analyses, including linear models, indicated that caudate volume might play a role
in fatigue pathology.
Finally, this thesis highlights that despite its limitations, EBM remains a powerful data-driven
method for understanding disease progression. Future studies should explore the additional infor-
mation available in either subregions or additional regions found to be correlated with fatigue, as
well as elaborate on the nuances regarding fatigue trajectories.

Title: Machine Learning for event-based modelling of microstructural changes in deep brain nuclei and 
the brain stem for classiﬁcation of fatigue phenotypes in MS 
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a long-lasting inﬂammatory-demyelinating disease of the central nervous 
system. Fatigue is a common symptom of MS, where patients experience severe fatigue independently 
of the level of disability. [Tur C, Curr Treat Options Neurol., 2016] However, it is a poorly understood 
symptom with unclear mechanisms and lacking characterization of fatigue-speciﬁc phenotypes. In 
previous studies, fatigue has been found to have a link to atrophy and iron concentration alterations in 
brainstem [Barnden et al., NMR Biomed, 2011], thalamus [Carpone et al., MSJ, 2019] and putamen 
and caudate [Rocca er al, Neuroradiology, 2014]. Eshaghi et al (2018) developed an event-based 
model to determine a disease progression pattern based on cross-sectional data from a variety of 
patients. 
In this project, the aim is to utilize event-based modelling to establish a timeline of micro- and 
macrostructural changes related to fatigue, focused solely on deep brain structures (caudate, 
putamen, thalamus, globus pallidus) and the brain stem (pons, medulla, midbrain). From the event-
based microstructural patterns a supervised machine learning method is used with the aim of 
identifying distinct fatigue phenotypes. The modelling is initially performed on around 200 subjects 
with relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS) with varying levels of fatigue.  
Additional models might be implemented to compare and understand the strengths and weaknesses 
of the diƯerent models and their assumptions. 
 
Research questions:  
How can data-driven event-based modeling enhance our understanding of the mechanisms 
underlying fatigue in MS, and is an event-based model a superior descriptor compared to linear ﬁtting? 
Can microstructural changes in deep grey matter nuclei and the brain stem help identify distinct 
fatigue phenotypes in MS, and provide insights into disease progression patterns related to fatigue?

Appendix D: Code Overview
==PAGE:72==
The code need to recreate the results in this report are all available in Thesis_Code.zip. This
contains two folders: Analysis contains three Jupyter Notebooks for data analysis and modelling
and Preprocessing contains nine scripts containing different parts of the preprocessing steps.
Analysis:
• Fatigue_Study_data_processing.ipynb
Fatigue Study dataset creation with biomarker values extracted from preprocessed images.
• Fatigue_Study.ipynb
Data analysis and modelling related to the Fatigue Study
• Replication_Study.ipynb
Data analysis and modelling related to the Replication Study
Preprocessing:
• ANTsTemplate_sub.sh
Creation of study-specific template
• QSM_twopass.m
QSM two pass approach with functions from the QSM.m library.
• QSM_sub.sh
All steps of QSM processing including preprocessing as described in Figure 6.
• bash_QSM.sh
Script to run QSM_sub.sh for all subjects.
• LesionCleanUp.m
Remove all lesions consisting of less than six voxels.
• structuralPipeline_sub.sh
Pipeline for preprocessing of image to obtain lesion filling, desired segmentations and trans-
formation to template space.
• bash_structural_pipeline.sh
Script to run structuralPipeline_sub.sh for all subjects.
• SRPBS_to_Template.sh
Transform segmentations to template space.
• bash_SRPBS_to_Template.sh
Script to run SRPBS_to_Template.sh for all HC subjects.
