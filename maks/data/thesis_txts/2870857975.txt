Abstract 
The construction industry has consistently shifted towards enhancing circularity and 
sustainability due to its substantial contribution to global warming. Although the adopted 
modular and industrialized construction approach has definitely facilitated the circularity 
principle in construction. However, it has highlighted that there is a need for accurate and 
efficient environmental assessment. Life cycle assessment is a widely adopted methodology 
to assess sustainability; despite its effectiveness, assessment remained resource-intensive due 
to repetitive analysis of similar construction components across different projects. This thesis 
has bridged this gap by exploring the integration of Product Platform (PP) thinking into the 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) methodology to enhance sustainability in industrialized 
construction. 
The research has conducted both qualitative and quantitative analysis in close collaboration 
with Næste, a Danish modular shed construction company. The core concept for this project 
was that leveraging the principle of standardization within a construction project can 
significantly streamline the LCA. A unique framework was developed that simplifies LCA 
and emphasizes parametric and modular-based calculation. This industry partnership further 
helped to examine how standardized elements can aid in the efficient implementation of this 
developed framework, especially in the life cycle inventory analysis stage. A tool was 
developed using their practical case to validate this framework. Its findings illustrate that this 
integration not only reduces the redundant calculation but also allows the calculation of LCA 
dynamically with real-time changes. Thus, it saves both time and effort in conducting Life 
cycle inventory analysis for modular construction. 
This integration promotes more sustainable building practices through consistent and 
informed decision-making about material and design choices. The implications of this thesis 
are not just limited to theoretical contributions but also offer practical insights into the 
adoption of PP and LCA in construction. It also enhances comparability and transparency in 
sustainability reporting. It was identified that a product platform-based tool could potentially 
improve the dynamic LCA process and enhance real-time emission predictability. Although 
the tool has shown strong potential, it still needs to be further refined through more data, 
which opens avenues for future research. Its capability could be extended by transforming it 
into an emission configurator and adding a socio-economical aspect to make it more useful 
across different construction contexts.

1 Introduction 
The previous year 2024 has been recorded as the warmest summer on the European landmass, 
with the average global temperature rising by 1.54°C above the pre-industrial era. Global 
warming is responsible for this alarming heat wave around the world (Copernicus, 2024). To 
address this crisis, numerous countries are implementing strategies to reduce carbon 
emissions by adhering to the Paris Agreement’s target of limiting the global temperature 
increase to 1.5°C (Wijaya & Kokchang, 2023). Globally, three sectors are the major 
contributors to GHGs: Energy, Industry, & Agriculture (EEA, 2024; Henrique Morgado 
Simões with Gema Andreo Victoria, 2021). According to the UNEP’s Global Status Report 
for Buildings and Construction 2020, the construction industry is the prime consumer of the 
energy sector. It contributes to 39% of energy-related CO₂ emissions and consumes around 
36% of the energy worldwide  (Ian Hamilton and Harry, 2020). 
The construction industry accounts for both energy used in maintaining building operations 
and energy used in the production of building materials, making it a prime consumer of the 
energy sector. In Denmark, the construction industry contributes to 8% of total CO₂ 
emissions; and creates large amounts of solid waste that amounts to 58% of construction and 
demolition (C&D) waste. Only 15% of C&D waste is currently recycled (Munch et al., 2023). 
According to the EU’s Green Deal and Climate Action Plan, the circular approach is a prime 
tool that can be implemented to reduce waste and improve sustainability across industries, 
paving the way towards net-zero emissions by 2050. This circularity approach facilitates 
construction industries to reduce resource extraction, reuse materials, and minimize waste, 
which leads to sustainable urban development. Nevertheless, this approach has the potential 
to significantly reduce environmental impact while providing economic and social benefits. 
The term Circularity refers to the concept of creating closed-loop systems, where the 3R 
(reduce, reuse, recycle) principles are used to manage resources, products, and materials to 
minimize waste and environmental impact. The goal of this term is to extend the life cycle 
of products and materials by keeping them in use for as long as possible and reducing the 
need for new raw materials. The Circular Economy runs on similar lines, as it is an economic 
model incorporating the principles of circularity. A circular economy (CE) represents the key 
alternative to the linear ‘take-make-consume-dispose’ economic model, that still 
predominates in the construction sector (Zu Castell-Rüdenhausen et al., 2021a). To address 
the current environmental challenges of the traditional approach, the construction industry is 
majorly adopting the path of industrialized construction as a viable solution to the circular 
economy.  
Industrialized construction (IC) is a systematic manufacturing technique that uses off-site 
production, automation, and standardization to increase efficiency, reduce waste, and 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
 
improve sustainability in the construction sector. The shift toward IC focuses on the reuse 
and repurposing of building materials, reducing demolition waste, and integrating design for 
disassembly (DfD) strategies, allowing components to be used in different life cycles. 
Thereby, IC encourages circular economy thinking in the real sense. Through the integration 
of IC and the circular economy principle, the construction sector not only minimizes its 
environmental footprint but also improves its economic viability and resilience. However, 
one of the major persisting challenges for the construction sector is accurately assessing the 
environmental impacts of its processes and projects in a way that promotes circularity. 
LCA is a method and tool to evaluate the environmental impact and material usage associated 
with a product or system. During the evaluation, it considers all the stages from raw material 
extraction, the production phase, the use phase and the end-of-life (EOL) of the product. 
Moreover, within the construction industry, LCA is the most effective tool to develop an 
ecological design of the product (Dossche et al., 2017). However, the challenge with such a 
method or tool in the construction industry is that every project requires a unique LCA, even 
if many of the building components or structures are repeatedly used across different projects. 
This leads to inefficiencies in the LCA results and also increases the time, effort, and 
resources required to assess the circularity of construction projects. 
The product Platforms (PP) method standardizes components that can be reused across 
different models or boost efficiency by making the process less complicated. This model has 
been adopted by the automotive and electronic industries. Integrating this approach with 
LCA might prove beneficial for the construction industry, and this is the reason to perform 
this particular thesis study. 
 
1.1 Case  Description 
Næste is a Danish company specializing in constructing sustainable sheds using principles 
of circular economy. Næste was founded in 2018 by Niels Jakubiak Andersen, with a vision 
based on the principles of resource efficiency and socio-environmental responsibility.

prioritizing the reuse of construction demolition waste material to fulfil its goal and vision.

Næste has integrated the concept of circular economy into its business workflow. which

2.1 Circular Economy in Construction 
The term Circularity refers to the concept of creating closed-loop systems. The circular 
economy is a sustainability-driven economic model applying the principle of 3R: Reduce, 
Reuse, Recycle (Ghisellini et al., 2018) to manage resources, products, and materials to 
minimize waste and the environmental impact of a product. The circular economy offers a 
substitute for the traditional linear approach of the ‘take-make-consume-dispose’ economic 
model that still predominates in the construction sector (Zu Castell-Rüdenhausen et al., 2021; 
Uwuigbe et al., 2025). The prime goal of this approach is to extend the life cycle of products 
and their materials by keeping them in use for a longer period and reducing the need for 
virgin materials. This approach establishes the ground for economic growth (Uwuigbe et al., 
2025).  
Moreover, the circular economy (CE) principle also promotes the usage of recycled or reused 
materials, goods, and components to reduce waste generation (Fnais et al., 2022). Therefore, 
in recent years, CE has gained high relevance in the construction sector due to its 
considerable consumption of natural resources. According to (Kedir et al. 2024), the building 
industry in Europe accounts for 40% of GHGs, utilizes 50% of material extracted from the 
earth’s crust, and generates 45% of total regulated waste. It aims to innovate the entire chain 
of production, consumption, distribution, and recovery of materials and energy according to 
a cradle-to-cradle vision (Ghisellini et al., 2018). 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
 
Despite its potential, its implementation in the building sector is hindered by several barriers, 
such as the conservative and fragmented nature of the construction industry and the tendency 
for buildings to be developed under time and cost constraints (Fnais et al., 2022). 
Additionally, (Futas et al. 2019) noted that the absence of a standardized and measurable 
framework to evaluate and compare cradle-to-cradle (C2C) effectively leads to 
inconsistencies in practices within the industry. To address these barriers, various innovative 
practices are carried out in construction, where one of the approaches is prefabrication or 
Industrialized construction. (Kedir et al., 2024) demonstrate that Industrialized construction 
(IC) is emerging as an alternative to counter barriers, particularly by enhancing data flow, 
reducing fragmentation, and improving standardization. Thus, by promoting repeatable 
design elements and modular approaches, IC aligns with CE principles and facilitates more 
efficient material recovery and reuse strategies.  
Though awareness around sustainability is growing in this sector but the current practices 
remain fundamentally unsustainable in absolute terms. The circular economy has been 
identified as a crucial transition that could align the sector with absolute sustainable 
boundaries, particularly concerning waste management and resource efficiency (Thuesen et 
al., 2023). However, making CE a common practice in construction needs more regulatory 
support, technical innovation and industry-wide standardization efforts. 
 
2.2 Industrialized Construction 
Industrialized construction (IC) is a paradigm change from traditional on-site construction to 
a more efficient off-site production approach. According to (Kauppinen et al., 2024), IC is 
used as an umbrella term for prefabrication or modular or off-site construction, where parts, 
modules or even whole buildings are built off-site in a factory and then moved to the actual 
building site (Daly et al., 2025). Industrialized construction prioritizes efficiency, quality, 
and sustainability through methodologies such as prefabrication, standardization, and 
modular construction (López-Guerrero et al., 2022; Daly et al., 2025) identified that one of 
the major advantages of prefabricated or off-site construction is reducing waste in 
construction. Hence, it is evident that the IC approach aligns with the principles of the circular 
economy by reducing waste and improving resource efficiency (Abdalla et al., 2025).  
Standardization is considered as a cornerstone of modular and IC (Yashiro, 2014). The 
principle of standardization also guides the creation of uniform components corresponding 
to predefined specifications. It also helps to streamline the production process, reducing 
material waste and enhancing overall quality. However, the application of standardization 
varies from project to project. Some studies (Daly et al., 2025; Kamali & Hewage, 2016) 
highlight some of the inconsistencies that persist while applying standardization principles 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
 
in modular construction. Furthermore, (ibid) stressed that to achieve true modularity requires 
the implementation of standardization at multiple levels, including design, material selection, 
and assembly processes. 
The application of LCA in IC is critical for holistically evaluating the environmental impacts 
of construction processes (Daly et al., 2025). A major challenge in applying LCA to IC is 
that the existing LCA framework and data are not entirely built to handle standardized or 
modular building systems (López-Guerrero et al., 2022). However, the major hurdle that lies 
in this implementation is the lack of a unified framework that could ease the comparative 
evaluation of different IC designs. And the issue is compounded by the decentralized nature 
of construction (Kamali & Hewage, 2016), which complicates data collection and results 
standardization due to the involvement of various materials and construction methods. These 
challenges underscore the need for more tailored approaches that could balance 
standardization and customization to carefully examine the sustainability of IC buildings or 
structures (ibid).  
 
2.3 Life Cycle Assessment 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) has been a widely adopted method for quantifying the 
environmental impact of a product or process. It is used to keep track of the environmental 
impacts and carbon emissions of a product and process throughout its lifecycle, including 
transportation energy consumption, resource use, and waste generation (Onat et al., 2017; 
Barbini et al., 2022). The construction industry emits a huge amount of waste during 
demolition, which further exacerbates the environmental impact of the building industry. 
According to (Anand & Amor, 2017), we can significantly minimize environmental damage 
and unlock new value by integrating circular economy principles into the built environment. 
LCA not only helps to quantify the environmental pressures and the trade-off but also tells 
us the areas to achieve improvements considering the full life cycle of a product (buildings) 
from design to recycling (Fnais et al. 2022).  
However, despite its importance, the complexity and data-intensive nature of LCA impose 
significant challenges, limiting its broader adoption in the construction sector. This has 
resulted in continuous research attempts to simplify LCA techniques, making them more 
dynamic and accessible for IC. 
2.3.1 Challenges in Conducting Life Cycle Assessment in Construction 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is an intricate process that involves several critical steps. Many 
studies have identified challenges that are specific to construction, IC and the building 
industry when conducting LCAs. In order to conduct a full-fledged LCA of entire buildings 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
 
or projects, they have highlighted the complexities involved in data collection, data 
standardization, and integration of environmental assessments into the early-design stage. 
Some of the key challenges are mentioned below: 
1. Life cycle inventory analysis is very complex due to the numerous materials and 
processes involved (Anand & Amor, 2017; Kamali & Hewage, 2016) and it differs 
with building design (Ghattas et al., 2013). 
2. Due to the lack of methodology being scalable to various building designs and not 
accounting for its geographic locations (López-Guerrero et al., 2022), make the 
uncertainty predominant in study results (Schlanbusch et al., 2016).  
3. Conducting comparative LCAs of different ICs structures or with traditional 
structures (Munaro et al., 2020) remains challenging due to complexities in 
evaluating their differing material flows, transportation, energy consumption 
processes, and end-of-life (EOL) (Kamali & Hewage, 2016). 
4. Different LCA tools can generate inconsistent results for the same design problem 
because each tool utilizes various workflows and databases (Mora et al., 2020; Fnais 
et al. 2022) 
5. The Nordic building industry also finds building LCAs time-consuming and 
expensive. This is likely the reason for the strong interest in creating a common 
Nordic LCA database (Schlanbusch et al., 2016). 
These issues need to be addressed to help construction decision-makers select the best 
method of evaluating sustainability assessment under given circumstances to achieve 
sustainability goals (Kamali & Hewage, 2016; Anand & Amor, 2017). 
2.3.2 Development around Life Cycle Assessment 
Several studies, experts and research communities have executed continuous efforts in order 
to resolve the issues regarding inconsistency, transparency, comparability and data quality in 
multiple ways. 
Environmental Product Declaration 
Environmental product declaration (EPD) has been considered as the most standardized and 
verified source of environmental impact information on construction materials (Marrero et 
al., 2020). Its adoption has been encouraged by environmental regulations, certification 
schemes, and digital platforms (Gay et al., 2022). However, according to (Dixit et al., 2012;

2.4 Platform Thinking 
The term ‘platform’ has been used in different contexts and at various scales, ranging from 
specific products to solutions for multiple industries (Construction Innovation Hub and UK 
Research and Innovation, 2023). Platform thinking is recognized as an umbrella term 
characterized by several common assets, such as physical components, processes, 
knowledge, or relationships (Thuesen et al., 2023). These assets have been shared across 
products to enhance efficiency and provide a competitive advantage. 
The concept of platform thinking, primarily applied to manufacturing industries like 
automobile and aviation, has been expanded to include digital platforms in today's economy. 
This has revolutionized the way companies approach market opportunities (Parker et al., 
2016).  The book (Marc H. Meyer, 1997) says, “Since most companies design new buildings 
one at a time, the focus on individual schemes results in a failure to embrace commonality, 
standardization or compatibility across projects or programs of work.” 
 For example, a case study, “The High Rise Solutions (HRS) system by Mace” in 
(construction innovation hub and UK Research and Innovation, 2023), showcases the use of 
product platform thinking in construction. It has used a platform approach to standardize key 
components such as precast structural elements (e.g., columns, cores, stairs) and interior 
elements (e.g., bathrooms, utility cupboards). These components have been manufactured 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
 
offsite using a catalog of repeatable designs and preconfigured processes, ensuring 
consistency and efficiency across projects.  
Product Platform (PP) is a specific application of Platform thinking, which has further 
transformed the construction sector by promoting standardization, enhancing efficiency, and 
enabling customization. Companies like Modulous utilize PP to deliver modular, repeatable 
designs, balancing standardization and variety to optimize resources and create value for 
stakeholders (construction innovation hub and UK research and innovation, 2023). 
Moreover (Thuesen et al., 2023),  have emphasized that platforms thinking connecting (key) 
stakeholders can effectively drive innovation by reducing cost and managing complexity, 
which are critical in the construction industry. However (ibid), it has also highlighted some 
challenges involved in fully adopting CE within the construction sector. One significant 
challenge lies around material documentation; even though LCA is a well-recognized tool 
for assessing sustainable potentials and consequences; the complexity of generating data is 
an obstacle to fully integrating this method in decision-making and comparisons to 
conventional solutions (Ipsen et al., 2021). By leveraging such platforms, the construction 
industry can overcome existing barriers and significantly advance toward sustainability. 
 
2.4.1 Integration of Platform Thinking and Life Cycle Assessment  
While the integration of Platform thinking LCA is still being untapped in the construction 
industry, some examples from other industries demonstrate the potential of such an approach. 
As discussed in the paper (Helo et al., 2024), the integration of platform thinking with LCA 
was a structured approach to enhance sustainability and operational efficiency in the shipping 
industry. This study presents a product platform-level solution to capture key supply chain 
elements and calculate emissions for each product variant using a product configuration 
system (ibid). It shows that adopting the principle of modular designs and shared components 
with a platform could facilitate the shipping industry to conduct efficient LCAs. This 
approach not only saves time but also provides detailed insights into environmental impacts 
at a component level. This case study illustrates how this integration approach can provide 
detailed insights into the environmental effects of different product variants, paving the way 
for more sustainable supply chain practices.

3.1 Life Cycle Assessment 
3.1.1 Fundamentals of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
LCA is an internationally recognized methodology governed by ISO 140401 and 140442 
standards. The first ISO standard established the strategic guidelines for conducting LCA, 
whereas the second ISO standard facilitated the description of the inventory phase, impact 
assessment, and interpretation (Matthias Finkbeiner, 2006).  
According to the definition provided in the ISO standard, an LCA consists of four major 
phases, as shown in Figure 6: Goal and scope, Life cycle inventory analysis, Life cycle 
impact assessment, and Interpretation. 
 
Figure 6: Phases of Life cycle assessment methodology   
(Source: Adopted from Sustainable brandplatform, 2024) 
 
1 ISO 14040 
2 ISO 14044 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
 
 
1. Goal and scope definition: This phase is the foundational phase of LCA methodology, 
starting from defining the objective and the scope of the study. This stage also determines 
various other key elements, including the intended application of the product, the 
definition of the functional unit, and the system boundary. The intended function defines 
the purpose of the product to be studied and ensures the results are assessed within the 
practicality of the real world. The prime function of the functional unit is to create a 
reference unit for all the calculations in the whole process. It should be defined in a way 
that the unit must be quantifiable, representative and standardised. The system boundary 
helps to guide the study to determine which life cycle stages and processes are included 
in the scope of the study and ensures that only relevant flows are assessed. To conclude, 
this phase helps the user to clearly define the goal and boundaries of the study in order to 
minimise uncertainty or assumptions and improve comparability. It makes the LCA 
results more transparent and actionable. 
 
2. Life cycle inventory analysis: The second phase of an LCA is a systematic data 
collection process, which consists of all the relevant inputs and output data of a product 
life cycle (Dossche et al., 2017). As per the definition (Jolliet et al., n.d.) 
It is an inventory of elementary flows or emissions and extractions or 
the quantitative description of flows of matter, energy, and pollutants that cross the 
system boundary. 
 
In this phase, it is important to work with very accurate data, since the accuracy of the final 
results of the life cycle assessment strongly depends on the life cycle inventory (Dossche et 
al., 2017). To determine the LCI of any product, it is essential to first define the process flows 
of each element. The process flow represents the complete sequence of operations involved 
in material extraction, processing, transportation, assembly, use, and disposal. Each process 
flow consists of inputs (materials, energy, and resources) and outputs (emissions, waste, and 
co-products), ensuring that all environmental exchanges are accurately accounted. After 
collecting the data of elementary flow, this phase also accounts for the emission data of each 
unit process from the universally existing databases like ECO-invent, Gabi, etc. This phase 
is fundamental in ensuring that the LCA results are based on reliable, empirical data, enabling 
accurate environmental impact calculation. 
 
3. Life Cycle Impact Assessment: The third phase of LCA is the impact assessment. It 
translates LCI results into meaningful environmental impact outcomes. It involves the 
classification of impact categories with the characterization and aggregation of 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
 
emissions. It also evaluates used resources across the life cycle based on the data of the 
LCA phase and depending on which impact assessment method is used (Dossche et al., 
2017). LCIA consists of three key components: 
• Impact categories: All the inventory results from the LCI having similar effects 
(if all contributing to greenhouse gases) are grouped into impact categories. 
Approximately 16 categories are present, which gives a clear picture of which 
element is causing what impact (Jolliet et al., n.d.). 
• Characterization factor: The factors that translate each emission type into its 
equivalent environmental impact. (e.g. all greenhouse gas emissions are 
converted into a single unit kg CO₂ eq.) 
• LCIA methods: The LCIA methodological framework develops two methods for 
conducting impact assessments: the mid-point method (cause-effect) and the End-
point method (damage-oriented).  
Midpoints are considered intermediate points in the cause-effect chain of a 
particular impact category prior to the endpoint. To nuance the midpoint results, 
characterization factors can be calculated to reflect the relative importance of 
various specific emissions or extractions in the LCI. However, LCA studies that 
require a combination of impact categories will often acquire endpoint methods 
(Bare et al., 2000). 
 
4. Interpretation: This is the final phase of LCA and is considered a critical step in 
ensuring the results, which identify significant issues, evaluate results, draw conclusions, 
and provide recommendations (Dossche et al., 2017). It aims to identify the key hotspot 
causing more environmental impact and conduct sensitivity and uncertainty analysis to 
ensure the robustness of the study.  
 
However, it is important to know that ISO never aimed at defining the exact methods for 
creating an LCA. ISO 14040 states that one of the key features of an LCA is that 'there 
is no single method for conducting LCA (Finkbeiner & Finkbeiner, 2014) (Klöpffer, 
2012). 
 
3.2 Product Platform Thinking 
Product Platform (PP) thinking is also a systematic approach to product development that 
focuses on the principles of standardization, modularity, and scalability to facilitate efficient 
design, production and customization across a product family. A product family is 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
 
“A group of related products that share common features, components, and subsystems and 
satisfy a variety of market niches. A product family comprises a set of variables, features or 
components that remain constant from product to product (product platform), and others 
that vary from product to product” (Simpson et al., 2001) 
 
OR 
“a group of related products that share a common platform, allowing manufacturers to 
efficiently produce different models using shared design elements and production 
processes” (Marc H. Meyer, 1997) 
 
Thus, the core of PP Thinking is to have a common platform that provides the foundation for 
multiple product variations while ensuring that key components of the product family remain 
standardized and allow for controlled customization. In recent years, this thinking has been 
widely applied in manufacturing, the digital market (Thuesen et al., 2023), Engineering, 
construction, and industrial design, where each of them is trying to balance mass production 
efficiency with product diversity. Although every field working on this thinking has defined 
it differently, they all have converged on the fundamental idea that enables standardization, 
scalability, and efficient product variation.  
 
 
Figure 7: Fundamentals of Product Platform Thinking principle  
(Source: Self-developed by Author) 
 
The above Figure 7 illustrates the role of the Core platform in enabling Modularity, 
commonality, scalability, and flexibility  
 
1. Modularity: It means making products out of separate but replaceable parts so that 
different types of products can be assembled from standard parts that have already 
been set up (Baldwin & clark, 2000).  
2. Commonality: It makes sure that basic design elements stay the same across all 
versions of a product. This makes production more efficient and cuts down on costs 
(Thevenot & Simpson, 2007). 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
 
3. Scalability: It allows companies or industries to make their products more available 
by changing certain design factors (like size, materials, or functionality) while leaving 
the core platform as it is. 
4. Flexibility: It ensures that platform-based products can be adapted to changing 
customer needs or market conditions without requiring a complete redesign. 
 
 
In addition to these four fundamental principles, PP thinking follows two key concepts, 
according to (Simpson et al. 2001), in its core to perform its intended functionality.  
 
1) Fixed platform elements: These elements remain unchanged across the product 
family, which forms the backbone of the standardization process. They could 
include structural components, core manufacturing processes, and shared technical 
specifications. 
 
2) Scaling variables: These variables provide controlled variation and allow for 
customization within design limits. It includes size adjustments, material choices, 
different functionality, or optional features that enable the same base platform to 
cater to different market segments. 
 
In conclusion, PP Thinking is a structured approach that maintains a balance between 
standardization and customization, thus allowing businesses to streamline production, 
optimize resource use, and enhance scalability. By developing a common platform with 
predefined customization options, organizations can efficiently deliver a wide variety of 
products by reducing manufacturing complexity and enhancing sustainability. These days, 
as industries continuously move towards modular and flexible design approaches, this 
platform-based development could play an important role in driving efficiency, innovation, 
and environmental responsibility in modern product design.

4.1 Philosophy of Research: Pragmatism 
According to (Saunders et al., 2019), research philosophy is defined as “a system of belief 
and assumptions about how knowledge is developed.” It has also been clarified that even 
addressing some specific challenge in an industry or company could also be regarded as 
knowledge development. However, whether knowledge is being developed consciously or 
not, we take many considerations and assumptions at every stage (Burrell & Morgan, 2017). 
These assumptions ultimately shape the understanding of this study’s research question, the 
selected methods, and the interpretation of findings. This study adopted a pragmatic 
==PAGE:32==

approach, a research philosophy that focuses on practical solutions. This approach depends 
upon three fundamental underlying assumptions mentioned in (Saunders et al., 2019):  
Ontological, Epistemological, and Axiological.  
The study's ontological perspective describes how reality is perceived. This study has 
adopted a pragmatic ontological view, in which reality is both objective and subjective. For 
instance, Environmental impact has been influenced by measurable data and design choices. 
Epistemology refers to how knowledge is acquired and validated. This study’s pragmatic 
epistemology approach values knowledge from multiple sources and integrates both data and 
practical insights to deduce a comprehensive understanding of sustainability in construction.   
The Axiology pragmatic perspective examines how a researcher’s ideas, beliefs, and values 
shape the study (Saunders et al., 2019). This study values platform thinking and LCA 
methodology and recognizes the need to simplify the assessment method by utilizing a 
standardized technique. However, the prime focus remains on developing useful, data-driven 
solutions that assist industry stakeholders in real life. 
4.2 Research Approach: Abductive Approach 
This is the second layer of the research onion, addressing the question, “How does the 
research approach uncover the essence of the research problem and its goal’’(Chen & Huang, 
2019). The abductive approach has been taken in this research since it allows starting with 
incomplete observation and attempting to discover the most plausible explanation to the 
issues at hand. Given the goal of streamlining Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) through Product 
Platform (PP) thinking within industrialized buildings, the concept has already been utilized 
in the shipping industry. Thus, abductive reasoning is considered most suited to this study. It 
accommodates modification of existing ideas on the basis of case study proof and enables 
the generation of new perceptions where existing theory has constraints. This helps ensure 
that the proposed solution is both theoretically grounded and practically applicable within 
the construction industry.

6 Framework Development 
This chapter presents the development of an integrated framework based on the two theories 
explained in the previous section 3. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) has provided a 
scientifically structured methodology for assessing the environmental impact of a product, 
process, or system, and PP thinking facilitates the standardization and modularization of the 
products. PP also enhances the efficiency, scalability, and reusability of the product. By 
combining these two theories, the challenges persisting in the traditional LCA method can 
be eased. Furthermore, the integrated approach ensures that the environmental assessment of 
the product remains structured and comparable across various projects. 
 
Figure 14: Developed framework for Integration of Product Platforms (PP) with Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA) at each phase  (Source: Self-developed by Author) 
Figure 14 illustrates how this integration is implemented through a multi-stage approach, 
resulting in the development of a structured and reusable environmental assessment tool.  
The developed framework, referred to as the Product Platform & Life Cycle Assessment 
framework (PP&LCA), will be consistently used throughout this report. Since 
standardization is the core of PP thinking, its application with LCA ensures a consistent, 
scalable and repeatable assessment process. Each stage of LCA contributes uniquely to the 
environmental impact assessment. The structured alignment is also embedded at all four 
stages- Goal & Scope, Inventory analysis, Impact assessment and Interpretation. The blue 
boxes in the figure represent the phases of LCA methodology, where the linkage shows how 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
 
the PP thinking has been integrated. The yellow dialogue is a brief explanation of the 
consequences of integration action. This standardization framework optimizes sustainability 
assessments, enabling structured data management and automated environment impact 
calculation. 
• The Goal and scope define the foundation of any LCA process, setting a common 
objective, system boundaries, and assessment criteria. It ensures that a uniform 
methodology is used for all assessments, making the results more consistent, accurate, 
and comparable. Whereas, in traditional LCA, the goal definition and system boundary 
often bring a lot of inconsistencies in the assessment, which makes cross-project 
comparison difficult. However, through standardization, these uncertainties can be 
reduced by uniformly predefining goal definitions, system boundaries, assumptions and 
limitations for all products or projects. This consistency in the first phase helps to 
streamline the data handling part of the following phases and ensures that all 
sustainability assessments adhere to the predefined methodological principle. 
 
• The inventory analysis phase forms the most critical stage in the PP&LCA framework, 
as it consists of all the data related to exclusive material used, energy consumption, and 
transportation associated with a product, process, or system. This phase is crucial 
because it is used as the foundation for the impact calculation. Standardising this phase 
will ensure that the data collection will become more structured and allow for improved 
efficiency and scalability in sustainability assessment. 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
 
 
Figure 15: Detailed view of integration at the Life Cycle Inventory Analysis phase  
(Source: Self-developed by Author) 
 
Figure 15 illustrated above, is a detailed picture of how standardization is applied at the 
second phase of an LCA (of a product family). The process begins with the disintegration 
of a whole product or system into its smaller elements, components or modules and 
identifies where each will share common material composition and structural 
configuration. Another key concept behind this disintegration is that the components 
must be categorized in a way that ensures their reusability across different products or 
projects.  For example, the components can be classified into core structural units and 
functional modules. By following this approach, the need for repeated data collection for 
every new assessment is eliminated, making LCA more efficient and consistent across 
different or multiple configurations. Later on, each standardized component or module 
should store its predefined LCI inventory data, such as material properties, embodied 
carbon emission, transportation and energy consumption in a database. This database 
acts as a single source of emission data, known as a centralized database, ensuring that 
all LCA assessments conducted within the same goal and system boundary are retrieved 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
 
from reliable, pre-validated data. The use of a centralized emission database also enables 
automated retrieval of environmental impact value and will significantly reduce the 
complexity in conducting new assessments. This approach is particularly beneficial as it 
allows sustainability assessment to remain repeatable, scalable and comparable across 
multiple projects. 
 
• The impact assessment phase is responsible for translating LCI results into meaningful 
environmental impact outcomes. This phase helps to calculate the environmental burden 
associated with every product or project by linking the collected centralized inventory 
data to the relevant emission factor and impact category. Since a centralized inventory 
database was already established in the previous phase. Thus, the impact assessment can 
now be performed on predefined and uniform environmental impact factors of each 
component or module, eliminating the need to recalculate emissions for every 
assessment. Therefore, by applying PP thinking, several key aspects of the impact 
assessment process also become standardized, including the classification of 
environmental flow, characterization factors, aggregate of impact result and impact 
category. This structured and harmonized approach ensures that each component ‘s 
environmental burden is quantified consistently. Furthermore, allowing the scope 
modularity in standardized components ensures that different configurations can be 
evaluated dynamically. This dynamic evaluation enables real-time analysis and 
comparative evaluations that enhance sustainability driven design choices. 
 
• The interpretation phase ensures that the insights derived from the previous phases are 
presented in a structured and actionable manner and can be used to improve decision-
making. Since the framework relies on a structured and consistent dataset, it helps users 
compare the environmental performance of different design alternatives efficiently. 
Therefore, instead of performing an LCA calculation for every new configuration, using 
this approach, the user can modify the design (variable) parameter and instantly observe 
changes in environmental impact. Although the tool does not directly generate 
quantitative insights, but standardized dataset allows for the systematic tracking of 
sustainability performance over time. Additionally, the key feature of this phase is the 
Feedback loop, which shows the ability of continuous improvement of this approach. 
 
To summaries, the integration of PP thinking with LCA presents a transformative approach 
to sustainability assessment in industrialized construction. By establishing a standardized 
methodology and linking environmental data to predefined modular elements, this study 
developed a framework that is scalable, efficient and applicable to multiple projects.

7 Application of PP&LCA Framework in Næste Shed 
The developed framework aims to standardize LCA data, particularly inventory analysis, by 
creating a centralized database of reusable LCA for prefabricated construction components. 
By accommodating design variations and eliminating redundant LCA calculations for the 
recurring components, assessment efficiency and accuracy have been enhanced. 
This Næste case study of modular shed construction is a real-world deployment of this

System boundary: 
The system boundary as shown in Figure 18 of this study is defined using the cradle-to-gate 
approach (A1–A4 stages), which includes: 
• A1 (Raw Material Supply): This phase includes emission from the extraction or 
acquisition of raw materials.  
• A2 (Transport to Manufacturer): It covers the emissions associated with 
transporting raw materials from their source to the factory or production facility. 
• A3 (Manufacturing): This phase involves emissions related to the conversion of raw 
materials into the final product.  
• A4 (Transport to Site): This includes the emissions of transporting the finished 
product from the factory gate to the construction site.  
 
As a result of this integration, this system boundary will remain consistent across various 
configurations of shed construction ensuring that the LCA calculations account for the 
associated environmental assessment. 
Given that the shed was prefabricated and assembled on-site, the A4 phase is extended to the 
Næste fabrication site. Where needed, transportation to the specific construction site can be 
calculated and subsequently added to the A4 phase. This approach has ensured that the 
assessment consistently reflects only the standardized material and manufacturing impacts, 
making the LCA results directly comparable across different projects. 
Functional unit: 
The functional unit (FU) for this study was defined as "One unit shed constructed in 
Denmark." This FU was chosen because the shed's primary function is to provide a self-

shed configuration are calculated, the outcomes are represented “per shed” or “per m2 per

metric.

7.3 Parametric Tool Development 
Following the explanation of how the PP&LCA framework was implemented at the LCA 
level. This development of a parametric tool has been discussed in this section. The 
developed tool was referred to as the Næste tool, but both terms are used interchangeably 
across the report. The tool was designed in Microsoft Excel in order to process the modular 
shed configuration with dynamic parameters. It will automatically retrieve the predefined 
emission factors, compute material weights, and adjust impact assessments based on scaling 
variables. The aim was to create an efficient, scalable, and user-friendly system, ensuring 
that users can input shed specifications and retrieve real-time emission results. 
The following sections outline the tool’s architecture, data integration, and key 
functionalities. It will be showcased how the theoretical standardization principles previously 
discussed have been transformed into a real-life operational sustainability assessment tool.  
7.3.1 System Architecture and Development Approach 
The Næste LCA tool has been developed as a data-driven application that integrates the 
structure of a predefined Life cycle inventory database with a standardized emission database 
using simple mathematical operations.  
To develop the internal architecture, the product variant master technique has been 
incorporated strategically to manage complex product information and improve usability. 
According to (CampGay et al., 2022) the PVM framework has been considered critical to 
organize the different aspects of Life cycle assessment into a cohesive structure that improves 
the efficiency and clarity of sustainability evaluations. The internal engine is structured in 
three functional layers inspired by the conceptualized LCA framework model explained in 
(ibid):  
• the Centralized Data Layer,  
• the Geometric Layer, and  
• the Product and Process layer.  
Each layer has been designed to fulfill specific functional requirements of the automated 
LCA process. 
7.3.1.1 Centralized Data Layer: Environmental and Standardized Element 
The Centralized data layer has served as the foundation of the Næste tool’s architecture. It 
stores all the essential data regarding the standardized list of materials used in the shed 
constructions, with predefined material properties, Fixed shed elements, and carbon footprint 
values per kg CO₂eq. These data are obtained from comprehensive Life Cycle Inventory 
(LCI) datasets, described as a Centralized layer in the PP&LCA framework, as mentioned in 
section 6, ensuring robustness and reliability in tool calculation. The primary function of this 
==PAGE:63==
62 
 
 
layer is to store all the data that can be reused across different configurations of shed design 
but also have dynamicity in terms of changes in temporal factors like transportation, which 
is essential to carry out accurate assessments. 
Thus, when a user selects a specific shed element configuration in the process and product 
layer, the tool automatically retrieves the necessary data from this layer. Therefore, this 
automation eliminates repetitive calculations and ensures consistency across LCA 
evaluations, making the process more efficient and less prone to errors. 
7.3.1.2 Geometric layer: Dynamic Computation Layer 
The Geometric layer is referred to as the dynamic computation layer, where all dynamic 
processing is conducted. This layer has been developed to handle scaling variables such as 
material lengths and different design configurations. These dynamic and scaling parameters 
are considered central to the tool architecture, as they enable the modularity aspect of PP 
thinking. Through this capability, the tool is allowed to adapt to various user-specified 
configurations inputs and real-world conditions and get real-time results. The user input 
could be in terms of material selection, the number of elements used in a particular module, 
or their lengths. The tool will dynamically adjust the emission value of the given input. 
Key process of this layer: 
• Element volume and weight are calculated using predefined formulas based on user-
input dimensions and standardized material density, as described in section 7.4.1. 
 
7.3.1.3 Product and Process Layer 
The Product and process layer is referred to as the component or module layer within the 
tool’s architecture. This layer is designed to focus solely on calculating the final emission 
impact of each shed component, Foundation, roof, wall module, and fastener quantity based 
on the selected configuration. Additionally, energy consumed and final transportation to the 
assembly site could also be added to the final emission. The generated results enable users 
and stakeholders to make informed decisions about material choices and design 
modifications. 
 After integrating all three layers, a streamlined workflow is established from data retrieving 
to processing and final reporting. The final output sheet reports the shed's total emissions per 
m2. This structured approach not only simplifies the complex processes involved in LCA but 
also enhances the tool's scalability and adaptability, making it a robust solution for assessing 
sustainability in shed constructions.

7.4 Results 
7.4.1 Dynamic Life Cycle Assessment Calculation Performed by Tool 
After standardization mechanisms were defined at Næste’s modular construction level and 
within the LCA methodology in section 7.1 and, a solution was proposed in the form of a 
platform-based tool to operationalize these principles to compute Life Cycle Impact 
Assessment (LCIA) results efficiently. This tool followed a parametric setup. This parametric 
calculation refers to a computational method where a set of predefined parameters (fixed 
values) is used, and results are dynamically updated based on scaling variables (changing 
inputs). 
The modular nature of Næste’s sheds, as described earlier, enables a parametric approach to 
sustainability assessment, ensuring that recurrent components are assessed once while 
scaling variables are dynamically calculated based on real-time input parameters. Thus, this 
section will elaborate on how the tool processes material weights and LCI data, retrieves 
emission factors, and compiles emissions for a complete shed configuration. 
Since an LCA quantifies environmental impacts based on material usage, it is necessary to 
determine both the amount of material used and the associated emission factor for each 
material. The core principle behind this tool is to calculate emissions at the modular or 
component level before aggregating results to obtain the total emissions for the entire shed. 
This method allows the tool to leverage pre-stored emission data for all the components used, 
ensuring that only length variations, Number of units used, material substitutions, and 
configuration changes influence recalculations. To ensure accurate and scalable LCA 
assessments, the tool is designed by following the systematic approach of breaking down the 
shed into two primary structural categories as shown in structural analysis section 5.6: 
1. Substructure (Foundation Elements): Includes ground screws, element plates, corner 
plates, base plates, and Tystle plates, which serve as the primary load-bearing support system 
for the shed. 
2. Superstructure  
Wall Elements: The shed comprises load-bearing and non-load-bearing walls, organized 
into 15 modular sections (E1 to E15), each containing pre-defined exclusive components 
such as wooden frames, facades, and spacers. 
Roof Elements: Though Næste is constructing five types of roofs, this study considers only 
two predefined roof configurations due to the limitation of data as defined in section 5.6.  
Following the structural division of the shed into the substructure (foundation) and 
superstructure (walls and roof), reveals that the impact of the parametric methodology is

8 Discussion 
The developed approach provides a new lens through which the construction industry can 
assess and improve its sustainability practices by integrating PP thinking in their Life cycle 
assessment. The primary goal of this thesis is to improve sustainability across the 
construction project lifecycle by developing a conceptual framework that systematically 
incorporates these methodologies. The chapter has outlined the key findings of this study that 
significantly contribute to the theoretical and practical applications of the “PP&LCA 
framework”.  
8.1 Theoretical Contribution 
Framework Development 
The theoretical contribution of this thesis lies in the development of a novel framework that 
integrates Product Platform (PP) thinking into the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
methodology. It was observed that LCA is more of a linear and project-based analysis and 
requires new assessment for different projects. However, this gap was bridged by developing 
a modular and reusable system that can operate across different configurations of shed 
design. This approach shifts LCA from being a repetitive task to a system-based 
environmental assessment framework. It further enhances methodological clarity, reducing 
redundancy between the different LCA phases, and supporting scalability in environmental 
assessments. Furthermore, the thesis identified that standardization can be used as a data 
efficiency tool and a facilitator of strategic design since it can sync sustainability assessment 
with modular design principles. It helps to broaden LCA theory in a more dynamic and agile 
way, which is appropriate for enhancing circularity practices in IC. 
Streamlining Data Collection 
With the traditional approach, each new project often requires a fresh round of data gathering 
for all materials and processes involved, which is time-consuming and prone to inaccuracies. 
Whereas by adopting the standardization principle, most of the data regarding inputs (like 
raw materials and transportation) and outputs (such as emissions) need to be determined only 
once and later could be scaled and modified on a project basis. This approach was further 
supported by an automated tool, the Næste tool in this case, where users can input real-time 
design changes, and the tool dynamically calculates the updated environmental impacts. 
Whether the dimensions (of component or module) are stretched or shrunk, or the 
configuration is modified, the system provides instant feedback. As a result, the tool not only 
reduces the workload of collecting and recalculating environmental analysis but also allows 
for the evaluation of the environmental impact of their designs in real-time. 
 
==PAGE:76==
75 
 
 
Standardization of Inventory Data 
The PP&LCA integration results in harmonizing the inventory data by aligning the 
components and materials used across various construction projects. Data related to these 
materials, such as quantity, type, and environmental impact is predefined and can be reused 
in multiple analyses without re-calculation. It not only makes the LCA process fast but also 
enhances data accuracy. The use of standardized materials across projects reduces variability 
and the potential for errors that can arise from project to project.  
However, while gathering emission data for the Næste case, some difficulties were 
encountered in obtaining EPDs for all products (e.g., virgin wood, steel plates, and fasteners) 
used in the projects.  As a result, alternative databases had to be searched, which reduced the 
results' efficacy. EPDs are essential in this context because they serve as standardized 
documents that reduce anomalies in environmental flow data and impact calculation. This 
gap is not rooted in the developed methodology but instead reflects a limitation in the 
regulatory or industry-wide availability of EPDs. Therefore, there is a growing need to 
develop and evolve EPDs to cater for a broader range of construction products to maintain a 
uniform LCA approach in construction. This EPD approach is crucial in construction, where 
materials-related decisions significantly influence the environmental footprint of a project. 
Functional Unit 
Without a common and reproducible functional unit, comparisons between different shed 
designs become difficult. This issue has been addressed by standardizing the functional unit 
as “One unit of shed constructed in Denmark.” This approach significantly mitigates the 
identified challenges, providing a clear and common ground for all LCA calculations 
within the study. Consequently, the standardized functional unit also tackles issues like: 
• Completeness: This chosen functional unit is designed to show the final output of 
the shed construction process, as the shed is the final product that is delivered. This 
is considered useful when stakeholders are interested in the overall environmental 
footprint of constructing the entire shed. It further simplified communication with 
stakeholders who may not be familiar with more detailed aspects of LCA.  
 
• Comparability: The comparisons between different projects is improved when the 
same FU is used. It seems helpful in scenarios where different construction 
approaches (e.g., using different materials or configurations) were being evaluated 
for their environmental impacts. 
 
• Scalability: When different sizes or specifications of sheds need to be assessed, the 
environmental impact can be scaled proportionally based on the size of the shed. 
==PAGE:77==
76 
 
 
This means that the relationship between size and impact is linear or can be 
accurately measured. Hence, it ensures that this functional unit is scalable. 
The Næste tool further operationalizes this standardized functional unit and shows how a 
theoretical concept has been transformed into practical applications.  
Consistency of Results Across Methods 
In the LCIA, emission factors were derived from different sources, including Eco invent and 
EPDs. Certainly, some EPDs provided emission data using different methods compared to 
Eco invent methods (Impact World+). However, the consistency of the climate change results 
was not affected, even though the emission factors were derived differently.  This is because 
the characterization factor issued by the IPCC standardized reports (typically from 2012-
2013) and used across all methods. As a result, GWP comparable values were obtained 
throughout the analysis, and consistency in interpreting the results was ensured.  
 
Dimensional Flexibility in Standardized System 
It was observed that component-level changes, such as modifying module length, can be seen 
as deviations from rigid standardization. However, such changes can still be considered 
within the scope of PP thinking when implemented through predefined configurable 
parameters. In this developed framework, these dimensional alterations are considered 
scalable input integrated into the Standardized architecture of this tool.  
This means design flexibility has been provided without compromising the benefits of 
modularity or reusability. Furthermore, consistency in LCA is ensured, as all variant 
configurations were derived from a centralized database. Even when small alterations were 
being made, the logic of standardization was maintained, thereby supporting the 
sustainability-driven product platform-based solution.  
Observed Time Efficiency 
The notable outcome observed from the preliminary time efficiency calculation was that the 
PP&LCA had a significant reduce the time required for conducting LCA. Based on the 
defined assumptions, in Method 1, the estimated time for conducting traditional LCA ranged 
from 1 hour to 5 hours due to the complex process flow calculation for each material 
involved. Whereas the Næste tool takes around  2 hours 45 minutes because certain emission 
factors are directly taken from EPD’s available and eliminated to perform process flow 
calculation as compared to traditional LCA.  
In Method 2, the tool performance was tested on two shed designs of different sizes and roof 
types, where the length of each module was adjusted as per the size. It was observed that the 
total time taken for LCA calculation remained relatively the same across configurations but 
==PAGE:78==
77 
 
 
significantly reduced from the Method 1 Calculation. The potential reason was the 
centralized database was already defined, and the tool’s backend calculation engine was 
already configured; the user only performed certain operations using the Plug-and-play 
interface and selected inputs from the predefined data set.  
Although this analysis lacks peer-reviewed verification, despite this limitation, the findings 
indicate that a platform-based approach has the potential to significantly reduce the time and 
effort involved in environmental assessment. 
Tool Validation with Næste 
The developed tool was validated with the Næste team to evaluate its applicability and 
usability for them in their real design framework. The team confirmed that the tool’s modular 
logic and emission calculations were aligned with their current design structure and recurring 
components. However, feedback was gathered for future improvements, such as recycled 
wood is usually procured directly from the demolition site and subsequent processes like de-
nailing and resurfacing are carried out at their facility. Thereby, the associated emission of 
these processes will be reflected in the final calculation by incorporating the energy 
consumed during these steps. Additionally, they have highlighted that recently they are more 
inclined towards sedum roof type, which was not included in this study due to lack of prior 
knowledge. However, this sedum roof consists of small shrubs or plants that may act as a 
carbon sink, potentially reducing the total emission of their shed. Thus, to enhance tool 
accuracy and adaptability, this roof type must be included in future iterations, and further 
data should be collected to reduce the uncertainty in result caused by the number of 
assumptions considered, thereby improving its usability.   
8.2 Practical Contributions 
Besides the theoretical contribution, this thesis also offers practical contributions through the 
development of this integrated framework. A practical contribution can be seen in the 
implementation of this framework to develop a Platform-based solution, namely the Næste 
tool. This parametric tool was created for the company case, Næste, to address the key 
challenges in modularized or industrialized construction. The Næste tool was designed to 
streamline their LCA process for both existing and future shed constructions by leveraging 
the integration of PP&LCA. Moreover, this tool has also addressed several other key 
challenges of the Næste, such as repetitive recalculation, manual data collection for each shed 
design, and the lack of a standardized process for conducting LCA. The sustainability 
assessment of shed configurations has been made more reusable and consistent by developing 
a centralized environmental database and linking it to modular design logic. As a result, the 
effort needed for repeated sustainability analysis has been reduced, and decision-making has 
been supported in a more transparent and structured manner.  
==PAGE:79==
78 
 
 
Although the tool has been developed based on the modular shed data of Næste, its 
conceptual structure, and logic have been designed with broader applicability in mind. Thus, 
this integrated approach can serve as a reference for those companies that are operating 
around the common ground of PP principles, where similar material, component, and 
assembly techniques are used across multiple project variants. While the current tool has not 
yet been tested beyond the Næste’s context, it can act as a foundation or inspiration for 
developing customized automated LCA solutions. Through this way, LCA processes can be 
simplified, and sustainability practices can be improved across construction projects. 
However, it is noted that the current tool is at a nascent stage, and iterative development will 
be required to reach a duly automated and generalizable state.

9 Conclusion  
The study began with the aim of investigating the main research question,  
“ How can the development of the Product Platform simplify and standardize the Life Cycle 
Assessment process in Industrialized Construction?”. 
LCA is considered essential for evaluating the environmental impact of any construction 
project and is often repeated across projects even when similar component designs are used, 
which leads to inefficiency in time and effort and needs LCA assessments from scratch. To 
address this issue, the study has further delved into the notion of integration of PP thinking 
with LCA methodology. A case study was conducted in collaboration with Næste. This case 
was particularly suited to the goals of this thesis as its workflow aligns with the PP principles 
and its innovative approach toward circular construction. It also provided a practically 
functional scenario to apply and evaluate this integration. These aspects allowed an in-depth 
analysis of how standardized construction components influence LCA practices. The 
connection between the developed theoretical frameworks and the Næste case study has been 
critical in addressing the main research question of this thesis.  
To provide a comprehensive result to the main research question, the study has been further 
divided into three sub-research questions. The first sub-research question identified several 
challenges in carrying out LCA for modular construction, such as a lack of standardized 
methodology and repetitive assessments of identical building designs. Subsequently, the 
second one found that PP thinking can offer a solution to these challenges by predefining 
standard components and creating a reusable centralized LCI database at the component 
level, which reduces the need for reassessment from the elementary stage. This approach also 
brought more consistency and less effort in the assessment process. The third sub-research 
question observed that the integration of PP&LCA also improved time efficiency in the long 
term, as redundant work was avoided and assessment became easier to conduct when product 
platforms were used. 
A digital tool named the “Næste tool”, was created to support these findings and demonstrate 
how the developed  PP&LCA framework could be used in practical scenarios. It was 
designed to allow centralized data to be linked with a predefined list of standardized 
components so that assessment could be reused instead of recalculated from scratch. This 
tool provided a way to test how platform-based components could be configured with their 
already embedded environmental impact in order to reduce manual work and data 
duplication. 
Overall, the findings of the developed framework, sub-questions, and tool outcome 
confirmed that this integration could reduce workload, save time, and support more 
systematic and scalable LCA. However, maintaining a strategic balance between 
==PAGE:81==
80 
 
 
standardization and modularization., where standardization is helpful in unifying the 
assessment process, but flexibility will still be preserved for project-specific needs and 
context.  
Thus, this thesis provided an initial step towards understanding how standardized component 
and their associated environmental data can be aligned to streamlined LCA in industrialized 
construction. Apart from these benefits, the conclusion of this study is limited by a single 
case study with limited data availability regarding every element used in Næste shed 
construction. As a result, the tool may not provide accurate emission at the moment, and 
some iteration is needed according to the actual specification, such as different roof designs 
and material configurations. These limitations also open up new opportunities for future 
research, particularly regarding how far this platform-based tool could be developed to cater 
to a broad construction industry perspective.   
 
9.1 Limitation 
There were several aspects that limited this research and its contribution.  Firstly, the 
framework and tool were applied to a single case study; its findings were derived from a 
specific, exclusive list of materials and design logic, so its broader applicability to other 
construction types was not yet explored. Secondly, it is worth noting that the language barrier 
has hindered a deeper understanding of the Næste shed design and detailed knowledge of the 
construction element. Although secondary data provided a basic overview of their design 
workflow, most of the provided data were in Danish, except for the previous life cycle 
assessment report generated by the student. This led to another limitation: to conduct a 
comprehensive life cycle inventory analysis; certain assumptions had to be made regarding 
the material type, quantities used, and transportation distance. Therefore, its results will not 
reflect the actual carbon emission of the scenario but rather those of the assumed scenario. 
Thirdly, the scope of the LCA was limited to the A1-A4, use phase, and end-of-life were not 
considered due to case company requirements and time constraints. The study was 
exclusively focused on the environmental impact, and social or economic aspects were not 
assessed yet. As a result, a comprehensive Life cycle assessment was not conducted, which 
may bring variation in the overall sustainability outcome. Another significant limitation was 
the lack of data on recycled material used in Næste’s shed. Therefore, emissions from wood 
logs were assumed to be zero, and transportation till their facility is considered, while the 
energy consumed for resurfacing or machining is still unknown and must be considered in 
future analysis. Furthermore, the time efficiency of the Næste tool was assessed using 
publicly available LCA time estimates and tested across two self-created shed configuration 
cases to observe performance under varying design conditions. Therefore, validation is still 
needed through direct comparison with the traditional LCA tool applied to actual shed 
==PAGE:82==
81 
 
 
projects using the same module system. Lastly, the tool was developed in Microsoft Excel, 
so its integration with other tools like Revit has yet to be explored.  
 
9.2 Future Research Opportunity 
While conducting this study, many avenues have been identified that could bring 
opportunities for future research. The study provided a significant step forward in simplifying 
LCA using PP, contributing significantly to the theoretical aspect; however, the outcome is 
a tool-based solution that is more toward practical application to improve sustainability in 
industrialized construction. Firstly, this study was conducted on only a very specific case, 
Næste, which brings in a future need to test the applicability and adaptability of the PP&LCA 
framework with other IC settings. Secondly, the current was primarily focused on the Cradle-
to-gate scope due to the necessity of this case study. However, many aspects that occur in the 
use phase and End-of-Life could also be expanded to enable more comprehensive long-term 
sustainability. Furthermore, the current tool only focused on one impact category, climate 
change, while extending its scope to the other required and relevant categories to improve 
the robustness of the tool.  Thirdly, the developed tool’s internal architecture followed the 
similar fundamentals of the product variant master; on further development in a programmed 
way, this has the potential to evolve as a configurator. Fourth, the developed tool is Microsoft 
Excel-based to demonstrate its feasibility of parametric LCA in modular construction. 
However, to reduce the manual work that is needed in this Excel-based tool, other platforms 
like Revit could be integrated at an advanced level to make real-time adjustments during 
design and improve its usability and predictability with the architecture, designers, or 
customers. The current tool only focuses on the project’s carbon emissions, but it can be 
further developed for life cycle-related socio and economic parameters. Last but not least, a 
feedback mechanism could also be incorporated within the tool to help the user understand 
if their selected configuration input significantly alters expected outcomes and guide them 
on how to achieve optimal results.

made of Fir, forming the horizontal frame, along with spacer lists (LST) made of Kebony,

aesthetic layer, enhancing the module’s longevity and resistance to environmental 
conditions. Since the breadth and thickness of the wooden elements remain fixed, 
standardization allows for pre-stored LCA impact values. However, as the length of the 
component may vary depending on the shed dimension, the LCA tool will dynamically 
adjust impact assessment based on these changes.

of Kebony.
