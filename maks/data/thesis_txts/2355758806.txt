Abstract
Tennis is one of the most popular sports in the world and extensive research has been
conducted into modeling professional matches. The majority of the work focuses on
stochastic models that utilize the hierarchical structure of tennis. This thesis pro-
poses a machine learning based approach that captures more complex relationships
between circumstances that inﬂuence the outcome of tennis matches. We built a
random forest model with diverse input features that represent match conditions as
well as historical player performances based on multiple parameters. When evalu-
ated on two separate seasons, the model out-performs the oﬃcial rankings and a
small proﬁt is made when simulating betting on the predictions.

1.
Introduction
Tennis is the world’s most popular individual sport [1, 2]. The top level of men’s
professional tennis features more than 60 tournaments in over 30 countries each
year. In recent years, during the most popular event Wimbledon, over 1 billion
viewers tuned in worldwide.
With such a large number of people following the
sport, forecasting matches has a signiﬁcant potential amount of public interest.
Sports analytics ﬁrst became popular within baseball in the 1970s where it was
used to interpret and evaluate teams, individual players and tactics [51]. Due to
advances within automated data aggregation and computational capabilities as well
as early successes within the ﬁeld, sports analytics has seen an increasing popularity
in the last decade [51]. Because of the growing level of detail and availability of
data, sports analytics is not limited to simple statistical models. Machine learn-
ing algorithms are obvious candidates because of their ability to learn patterns in
complex and diverse data sets.
The online sports betting industry is massive with a market size of over 30 billion
U.S. dollars in 2013 and estimated to reach more than 50 billion U.S. dollars in 2018,
resulting in a doubling in only nine years [4]. It is interesting to examine whether
a market of that magnitude can be beat in terms of making a proﬁt, based on data
driven predictions. Additionally, evaluating a tennis forecasting model in terms of
its proﬁtability in such a large commerce is a good indication of its predictive power.
1.1
Project Statement
This thesis will attempt to predict tennis matches using machine learning techniques.
The oﬃcial tennis rankings are the only formal indication of the diﬀerence between
two players’ winning chances leading into a match. However, the method from which
this hierarchy is based neglects to consider multiple factors that could inﬂuence the
outcome. The objective is to develop a model that takes several of these overlooked
circumstances into account with the aim of exceeding the ranking-based predictions.
The majority of previous work within tennis modeling is based on independent
distributional assumptions regarding points and games that exploit the hierarchical

This thesis is initiated with an introduction to tennis in terms of the nature of the

2.1
Tennis
Tennis is a racket sport that can be played as both singles, which involves two
players, and doubles, where two teams of two players compete. We will focus on
modeling singles matches, since data is more available for this segment of the sport.
Tennis is played on a ﬁxed-size rectangular court divided by a net. The goal is
to play a ball free of the net and into the other side of the court that the opponent
either cannot reach or that forces the opponent to make an error. Any given ball
has a designated server and a receiver. The server has two attempts to produce a
legal service. The serve alternates between the two players. They each serve a full
game at a time. The nature of games and the general scoring system is explained
below.
2.1.1
The Scoring System
Tennis matches always have a winner and a loser. There are no ties. The scoring
system has a hierarchical structure consisting of points, games and sets. A player
needs to win minimum four points to win a game and minimum six games to win a
set. To win a match, a player must win either two or three sets, called best-of-three
and best-of-ﬁve set matches respectively, which depends on the tournament. The
Grand Slams, which is a combined term for the four most prestigious tournaments,
all consist of best-of-ﬁve sets matches. Table 2.1 shows an overview of the basic
scoring system.

2.1.2
Tennis Terms
During the course of a tennis match several standard situations and speciﬁc events
take place. For example, as mentioned in Section 2.1, every point has a server and
a receiver. The most important terms denoting these are described below.
First Serve Each point begins with a serve from one of the players. The ﬁrst serve
is the initial of the two serves that the serving player has at his disposal at
the start of each point. This is typically the serve where players take the most
risk. Consequently, if the serve is valid, it is often a challenge for the opponent
to return the ball in an optimal fashion.
Second Serve If the ﬁrst serve is not valid, the server shall serve again. The second
serve is usually played with more margin than the ﬁrst serve, since making an
error results in a lost point. Therefore, the receiver typically has a better
opportunity to get a good start in the point. Second serve points are usually
not as one-sided as ﬁrst serve points where the server usually dominates the
play.
Ace An ace occurs when the serve is so fast or well-placed that the receiver does
not even get in contact with the ball. Very good servers, such as the American
tennis player John Isner, get a lot of free points behind their powerful ace-
producing serves.
Double Fault A double fault happens when a players misses both of his serves and
results in the opponent winning the point without any open play taking place.

The Break and Break Point A player is broken when he loses his own service
game. A break point is when the receiving player has an opportunity to break

point results in breaking the opponent’s serve. The upcoming point has to be
converted by the receiver before the actual break is a reality.

2.2.1
Crucial Statistics
Points won on serve are a good measurement of a player’s level, since holding serve
is signiﬁcant in terms of winning tennis matches. Often, a tennis match is decided
by a few breaks of serve and, if a player sports good serving statistics, it indicates
a high level of play.
==PAGE:13==
2.2. DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE
7
With regard to the above, statistics on break point performances are imperative.
Breaking the opponent is one of the most diﬃcult elements in tennis, since serving
is a big advantage for most players. Giving away a lot of break point chances is
typically a sign of a player being under pressure when serving and in the match
in general. If a player performs well in these game-deciding situations it improves
his chances of winning matches, since defending well against break points makes it
easier to hold serve.
2.2.2
Serving the First Game of the First Set
The ﬁrst set is important in tennis, since in 80 % of the matches, the winner of the
ﬁrst set also wins the match. Additionally, serving the ﬁrst game of the ﬁrst set is an
advantage. It can put extra pressure on the opponent in crucial moments of the ﬁrst,
very important set. If the set is close, a common scenario for the non-ﬁrst-serving
player is serving while being behind 4-5 or 5-6. To stay in the set, the player has to
win his service game. In contrast, if the ﬁrst-serving player loses a service game at
the score 4-4 or 5-5, he has an extra chance to come back in the opponent’s service
game before the set is decided. It is a small yet deciding diﬀerence.
2.2.3
Importance of Surface
The surface of the court is in general an important factor in tennis. Diﬀerent playing
styles are better suited to speciﬁc surfaces, and some tennis players have optimized
their game accordingly. Spanish players are for example notoriously good on clay
where playing with a lot of topspin is an advantage because of the increased ball
bounces and the slower pace. Having a consistent baseline game and making mini-
mal errors are crucial on clay. In contrast, grass courts are fast and the ball does not
bounce very high, which favors ﬂatter strokes and more risk-taking tennis. There-
fore, the surface of the court can be imperative information for estimating winning
chances in a matchup between two players with diﬀerent playing styles.
The evolution of tennis courts in recent years has been towards more uniformity
across surfaces and in general, the courts have slowed down. The grass at Wimbledon
is not as quick as it once was and tournaments using the fast hard-court carpet
surfaces are all but extinct. The reasoning is more crowd-pleasing tennis with longer
rallies as well as consistent rivalries among the top players throughout a season and
across surfaces.

2.2.4
Tournament Importance
As mentioned in Section 2.1.1, the four Grand Slam tournaments are the most im-
portant events each year. These are the milestones that players measure themselves
by, and the winners go in the history books. The top players hold the Grand Slams
above all else and plan their entire season around performing well at them. The
possibility of a top player suﬀering a surprise loss is therefore minimal, since they
are all highly motivated at these tournaments.
It is mandatory for the 104 best ranked players to compete in Grand Slam events.
Consequently, players who specialize in for example clay tournaments are committed
to play Wimbledon, which is on grass. It is therefore often the case that the top ten
players, who are generally accomplished on all surfaces, face somewhat demotivated
opponents in the initial rounds, since they are only participating in the event due
to it being mandatory.
Surprising losses are more common in the smaller ATP 250 events. The amount
of points earned for winning the tournaments is practically irrelevant for the best
players, since it won’t really aﬀect their ranking due to the many points rewarded
in the major events, where they notoriously perform well. However, for a less high-
proﬁle player, winning matches in the smaller events is signiﬁcant in terms of gained
ranking and price money won. Additionally, the best players often receive appearance
fees in smaller tournaments, which is money they collect for simply participating
in the event. The tournament organizers are happy to pay the money, as the best
players attract spectators and commercial interests. Unfortunately, it is speculated
that players sometimes show up primarily to collect the appearance fees without
much incentive for performing well, thereby resulting in possible early exits.
2.2.5
Injuries and Weather
Returning from a signiﬁcant injury can aﬀect a player’s results where random losses
often occur in the beginning of the comeback. Players often need time to ﬁnd their
rhythm and regain consistency.
The weather can be important when competing in outdoor events. The wind and
temperature inﬂuence the path of the ball and certain players have more experience
in speciﬁc conditions than others, due to where they train in oﬀ-seasons and where
they learned to play tennis.

2.3
Betting Market and Biases
The following is a description of the sports betting market covering the nature of
odds, the concept of value betting as well as known betting biases.
The two most popular formats of sports betting are ﬁxed odds betting and
parimutuel betting [39]. In the former the bookmaker sets the odds prior to the event
and when a bet has been placed the potential winnings are ﬁxed. In parimutuel mar-
kets, winning bets receive a proportional share of all the wagered money. The odds
are therefore not set until all bets have been placed, and the potential winnings
depend on the total money wagered as well as the number of bets placed on the
winner. Tennis belongs to the ﬁxed odds category of betting, which is investigated
further below.
The emergence of the online betting industry has made it possible to not only
bet prior to a match but also while a match progresses. The latter is called live
betting. Only bets placed before the beginning of a match will be considered in this
endeavor, as live betting odds are not present in the utilized data sets.
2.3.1
Odds
Betting odds are presented diﬀerently depending on traditions relating to the coun-
try and the speciﬁc events. The two most prevalent are fractional odds and decimal
odds. Fractional odds are mainly a British phenomenon due to the fact that the UK
ﬁrst started using decimal currency in 1971 [15]. However, the British are beginning
to convert to the European standard of decimal odds inﬂuenced by the advance of
the online betting industry [15]. Table 2.4 shows an example of fractional and the
corresponding decimal odds from a tennis match from 2015.

2.3.3
Value Betting
The concept of value stems from the world of investment and is a method for picking
stocks. The idea is to pinpoint and purchase shares of a company at a price that
==PAGE:17==
2.4. RELATED WORK
11
is lower than the company’s actual underlying value for the purpose of making a
proﬁt in the long run [10]. When doing so, value-investors operate with a principle
called margin of safety where stocks are only acquired when the market price is
signiﬁcantly below the intrinsic value of the company [10]. The larger the margin
the smaller the downside risk. The success of the strategy relies on the investor’s
ability to determine a companies ”true” value which can be highly subjective [10].
The idea of value betting follows a similar approach where a bet is placed if it is
priced for less than it is worth. The worth of a bet is the actual chance of the event.
If the probability of a given outcome is greater than what the odds of the bookmaker
suggests, there is value to be found by wagering on that bet. By only placing bets
in this fashion, the return will be positive in the long run. As with picking stocks,
the success depends on the punter’s capability of determining the actual chance of
a given outcome. The more precise the calculated probabilities are, and the larger
the margin is, the better the chances are of wining over the long term.
2.3.4
The Favorite-Longshot Bias
Several studies have examined the existence of biases in the sports betting mar-
ket. The most prevalent pattern found is the favorite-longshot bias where bets on
favorites have higher expected value than bets on longshots. Favorites are highly
probable outcomes and longshots are highly improbable outcomes. The bias mate-
rializes in the odds set by the bookmakers where favorites are over-predicted and
longshots are underpredicted [44, 16].
There exist multiple theories explaining the reasons for the pattern. One is risk-
loving punters who are drawn to the improbable outcomes by the longer odds and
the adrenaline rush of betting on the unlikely occurence [45]. A related reason is a
better’s tendency to overestimate winning probabilities of longshots [45]. Bookmak-
ers take advantage of these phenomena and adjust the odds accordingly. A third
explanation is pertaining to risk-assessment by the bookmakers due to information
asymmetry based on two possible scenarios [35]. Firstly, they could potentially lose a
lot of money if mispriced longshots were exploited by punters with inside knowledge
of an event. Secondly, by reacting slower than the general public to new information
regarding an event. Bookmakers, therefore, protect themselves from these cases by
lowering the odds on longshots [35].

3.1
Supervised Learning
In 1959 Arthur Samuel coined the term machine learning as ”[A] Field of study
that gives computers the ability to learn without being explicitly programmed” [8].
In Samuel’s pioneering paper from 1959 he noted certain characteristics that make
a domain qualiﬁed for the branch of machine learning, later deﬁned as, supervised
learning [43]:
• The activity must not be deterministic in the sense that no known algorithm
should exist that guarantees a correct prediction of all outcomes.
• A deﬁnite end goal must exist and also midway goals which aﬀect the ﬁnal
goal.
• The rules of the domain must be well-deﬁned.
• Data must exist so the learning can be tested.
Let’s evaluate tennis as a domain suitable for supervised learning based on the
above criteria. Firstly, results of all tennis matches cannot be perfectly predicted.
There are too many unforeseeable factors that inﬂuence the outcome. Secondly, an
end goal exists in form of the winner of a match and midway goals as well such as
points, games and sets. Thirdly, the rules of tennis are well-deﬁned, since there is
no ambiguity regarding how a point is won and consequently a match. There are
clear guidelines regarding all occurrences in a tennis match. Lastly, historical data
sets of tennis statistics exist and are available. All criteria are thereby met, and I
can therefore conclude that tennis, according to the deﬁnition by Arthur Samuel, is
a domain qualiﬁed for the application of supervised learning.

3.1.4
Data Splitting
In machine learning the aim is to generalize well to novel data and avoid simply
memorizing inputs already given. The latter is called overﬁtting. It is therefore im-
portant to partition the data accordingly when selecting and optimizing the model.
This involves splitting of the data into a training set and a test set. The training
set is used in the learning phase. The test set is needed to assess the performance of
the trained model. Dividing the data ensures an objective estimation of the model.
If the same data were used for both training and testing, the performance would be
biased, since the model would be optimized and tested on the same data [40].
A prevalent way of avoiding overﬁtting is to use k-fold cross-validation where the
data is split into K parts of equal size. The kth part is used for validation and the
model is trained on the remaining K-1 parts. This is performed for all k = 1,2,...,K
subsamples with each subsample used for validation only once. The results are then
averaged to produce the ﬁnal assessment. Because the model is tested on diﬀerent
segments of the data in each iteration, the possibility of overﬁtting is reduced.

3.2.2
Stopping Criteria
The goal of a stopping criteria is to ﬁnd the optimal size of the decision tree. When
increasing the model complexity, which in the case of decision trees means expanding
the number of leaf nodes, we risk capturing noise in the training set which results in
overﬁtting [14]. The key is to ﬁnd the right balance of complexity since too simple
a tree will miss important classiﬁcation information.
The two most common approaches are max depth and min samples split [34].
The former limits the maximum allowed depth of the tree, forcing a given node
to be terminal when the depth reaches a certain threshold. The latter requires a
minimum number of samples at a node before splitting is allowed which results in a
node being set as terminal if it contains a number of samples below the limit.

Characteristics
Decision trees have many attractive characteristics which make them very practical.

can also handle diﬀerent types of data both categorical and ordinal [14]. Decision

Random Forest
The following explains the reasoning behind choosing random forest and describes
the inner workings of the algorithm and how it can be optimized.
3.3.1
Why Random Forest
Fern`andez-Delgado, Cernadas, Barro, and Amorim tested 179 classiﬁers from 17
diﬀerent families of classiﬁers used on 121 diverse data sets [20]. They found that
random forest was the best performing classiﬁer overall. Surprisingly, no work has
been published on tennis prediction based on random forest predictors. We have
therefore chosen to investigate the predictive capabilities of random forest within
the realm of tennis.
3.3.2
The Algorithm
Random forest is an ensemble method. Within classiﬁers, ensemble methods are a
machine learning system where separate models are trained in parallel whereafter
their outputs are combined to reach the ﬁnal classiﬁcation [42]. In the case of random
forest, the aggregated models are decision trees. The algorithm works as follows:
Initially a preselected number of decision tress are ﬁtted on diﬀerent samples that
are drawn from the training set, with replacement. This is called bootstrapping. At

3.3.3
Properties
Random forests inherit all the good characteristics of decision trees. In addition, the
algorithm improves upon the decision-tree related problem of overﬁtting the training
data. By using bootstrapping and averaging over many trees, random forests are
more robust in terms of sensitivity to noise in the data set [11]. Bootstrapping de-
correlates the trees by ﬁtting them with diﬀerent data sets. The trees in the forest
can, however, still be correlated. This happens if a small amount of the features are
very strong predictors which will cause them to be selected in the majority of the
trees in the forest. The random subset selection of features in each split neutralizes
this eﬀect by forcing the trees to choose from diﬀerent sets of attributes thereby
de-correlating the trees [24].

The standard way of accessing a player’s level is to refer to the oﬃcial ATP Rankings

several aspects of tennis matches which can aﬀect the outcome. The circumstances
overlooked by the rankings but considered in this project are outlined below. The
included elements are a product of the interview with Nielsen, covered in Section 2.2,

Surfaces
As previously described, tennis matches are played on diﬀerent surfaces that vary
in their characteristics, which in turn aﬀect the tennis game in terms of speed and
consequently optimal playing strategies. However, the ATP Rankings are not surface

Fast courts such as grass favor serve and volley playing styles and
players with accurate and well-placed returns and serves [27]. Slow courts such as

players that open up the court with a wide serve [27]. Barnett and Pollard analyzed

Form
The ATP Rankings do not distinguish between recent matches and matches played
12 months ago.
Results from the previous week might inﬂuence a player’s per-

Rankings. Mchale and Morton as well as Somboonphokkaphan et al. improved the
predictive capabilities of their models by including recent form [36, 46].
The Score
On the men’s professional tennis circuit the winner takes all. The score does not
aﬀect the points acquired from a match. This means that very close matches ending
7-6 6-7 7-6 are treated the same as clear victories such as 6-1 6-0. Clearly, the
ﬁrst match reﬂects a better-performing losing player than the second. Mchale and
Morton found that diﬀerentiating between close and one-sided victories increased
the accuracy of their model [36].
Standard Situations
Performing well in the standard situations of a tennis match described in Sec-
tion 2.1.2 has an impact on the outcome of a match. Somboonphokkaphan et al.
found that adding data based on a player’s performances in many of these standard
situations during a match improved their model [46] and O’Malley, Liu and Newton
and Keller based their entire models on serving statistics [25, 33, 37].
Level of Previous Opponents
As shown in Table 2.3, players are rewarded points based on how far they get in
a tournament as well as the series of the tournament. However, the levels of the
opponents are not individually accounted for. A player can potentially win an event
by beating only high-ranked opponents and he will receive the same amount of points
as he would if all opponents had been out of the top 100. Beating many high-ranked
players is most probably an indication of performing well and the level of a player’s
prior opponents is therefore included in the model.

4.3.4
Feature Representation
An optimal model must include performances from both players participating in
a match.
A possible solution would be to include both values of a statistic as
separate features.
However, inspired by previous work by O’Malley, Corral and
Prieto-Rodriguez as well as Clarke and Dyte, features are instead represented as
diﬀerences between the features of the two players [25, 19, 17]. As an example the
feature denoting the number of matches won is represented as below:
Matches Won = Matches Won1 −Matches Won2
(4.2)
where Matches Won1 represents the matches won by player 1 and Matches Won2
the matches won by player 2. Assuming that the diﬀerences between players are
essential, this construction simpliﬁes the tree growing process making it easier for
the decision trees to ﬁnd patterns as well as decreasing the computational time. To
verify the above assumption, a pilot study was done and results were in fact better
when using the diﬀerences as features.
4.3.5
Data Partitioning
Inspired by the work of Somboonphokkaphan et al., the data is split historically [46].
This is due to the fact that there is a time-element in our data set which would be lost
in conventional k-fold cross-validation where the order of the data is not considered.
More recent seasons better represent the current the state of tennis. Additionally,
the order of matches might be important. Training the model on matches played
after the ones used for testing could be misleading. The data is therefore partitioned
historically into two main groups: one used for feature selection and hyperparameter
optimization and another for evaluating the ﬁnal model. These are then further
divided into separate data sets. Table 4.5 shows the splits.
The data used for model selection is divided into ﬁve sets each using a diﬀerent
partition as test set to avoid overﬁtting to a single season. As in k-fold cross val-
idation, the results are averaged over the multiple partitions when estimating the
performance. The splits used for learning when evaluating the model do not appear
in the table because they are not ﬁxed at this point. The optimal number of past
seasons used to train the model is part of the model optimization and is tested in
Section 4.6. The evaluation of the model is done using two separate holdout sets to
give a more realistic estimation of its predictability compared to using only one.

4.4
Feature Selection
When selecting the features to inlude in the model, a straightforward approach
would be to evaluate them based on their correlation with match outcomes. How-
ever, features inﬂuence each other and seemingly irrelevant inputs can be useful
when combined with others [22]. Instead of assessing features individually, we will
therefore rank features based on their combined attribution to the predictions.
In the realm of random forests, several metrics exist that compute feature im-
portances. We have chosen to use the Gini Importance for reasons explained below.
4.4.1
Gini Importance
The Gini Importance is calculated using the Gini Index described in Section 3.2.1.
The diﬀerence between random forests and single decision trees, when splitting at a
node, is that only a random subset of the features is considered at each split when
chosing the best candidate. The depth at which features are used in a node can be
utilized to rank them, since features chosen at the top of a tree assist to a greater
extent to the ultimate prediction decision of a bigger portion of the input samples.
The impurity decreases are therefore weighted by the proportion of samples reaching
the node where a feature is used for splitting. All weighted impurity decreases are
added up for all nodes where a given feature is used, averaged over all trees in the
forest, and the features are ranked accordingly [11, 12, 34]. For variable Xj the Gini
Importance is deﬁned as:

4.4.2
Feature Elimination Algorithm
The Gini Importance becomes less stable when the variables are highly correlated,
which can result in inaccurate feature ranking [50, 21]. As a solution to this problem,
Gregorutti, Michel and Saint-Pierre apply a recursive elimination algorithm where
feature importances are updated at each step with improved results [21]. Inspired
by these ﬁndings, a feature selection process was implemented as follows:
1. Train the model and compute the Gini Importance
2. Test the model
3. Remove the least relevant feature
This procedure was continued until only a single feature remained.
Figure 4.1 shows the results of multiple runs on each of the data sets reserved
for model selection. The graph shows that the accuracy of the model is fairly stable
until around 40-50 features whereafter it ﬁrst slowly and then drastically declines.
Though the accuracy is not better around 50 features, the computational speed is
improved since less features will have to be considered at each split when growing
the trees. The 70 worst performing features were therefore removed from the data
set and the model was ﬁxed with the remaining 50 features.

The speciﬁc players participating also varies be-

circumstances could potentially inﬂuence patterns important for predicting match

previous seasons to inspect whether more recent seasons are better predictors.
As mentioned in Section 3.3.4, overall, Oshiro et al did not ﬁnd any improvement
above using 128 trees in the forest [38]. However, speciﬁc models did gain from
increasing the number of trees [38]. Our model is therefore tested with both the
previously chosen number of 128 trees as well as twice as many to see if the accuracy
improves. Figure 4.3 shows the mean accuracy taken over the ﬁve data sets with
both 128 and 256 trees combined with the number of prior seasons used for training.
It is clear that there is no improvement when increasing the number of trees beyond
128. The only diﬀerence would be added computational time. The value of 128
trees is therefore ﬁxed from here on.

look more stable when approaching the max of nine prior seasons. This could be
due to the fact that additional seasons equals more training samples making the
model less sensitive to possible season speciﬁc outliers. We ﬁx our model at nine
prior seasons.

5.2
Bias Testing
In this section the tennis betting market is investigated for the possible existence of
the favorite-longshot bias described in Section 2.3.4, on which to base an optimal
betting strategy.
Cain, Law and Peel found evidence of the favorite-longshot bias in tennis based
on 91 matches at Wimbledon in 1996 [16]. The results were unstable though, which
Cain et al. believed to be due to the small sample size. Lahvicka also investigated
the existence of a favorite-longshot bias in tennis based on a larger data set spanning
multiple seasons and found the bias to be strongest in high proﬁle tournaments [31].
Based on these ﬁndings, combined with the insights gained from the interview with
Nielsen in Section 2.2.4, the favorite-longshot bias is tested based on all tournaments
as well as the Grand Slams exclusively. A real-life betting scenario is assumed and
therefore only years prior to the seasons used for evaluation are considered.

from low to high probability outcomes [16]. Figure 5.2 shows the distributions based

5.3.2
Random Forest Based Value Betting
In this simulation bets are placed based on the probability of an outcome calculated
by the random forest model. As mentioned in Section 3.3.2, the predicted probabili-

the forest. This way, the likelihood of a given match result can be achieved instead
of only the labeled outcome. We use this fact to identify bets where our model
predicts a higher chance of winning than what the odds indicate, inspired by the
value betting concept described in Section 2.3.3. Assuming our model is accurate,
a larger margin will result in a better chance of winning the bet. Simulating bets
when the model’s estimated probabilities exceed the implied probabilities based on

Conclusion and Outlook
In this project, the aim was to apply machine learning techniques to predict the out-
come of tennis matches. The experiments proved that the random forest algorithm
was a competent forecaster with a prediction score around 68 percent. Compared
with using the oﬃcial rankings, the ﬁnal model achieved a higher accuracy, though
the increase was marginal. A possible reason for the limited improvement could be
the recent uniformity of the surfaces in tennis.
When simulating bets on the predictions, a modest proﬁtability was found in
multiple of the tested scenarios. The existence of the favorite-longshot bias in tennis
was conﬁrmed and betting on the models gave a higher return in all scenarios when
exclusively simulating bets on favorites. Based on the results, the favorite-longshot
bias was not conﬁrmed to be stronger in Grand Slam tournaments, though the model
was a bit more accurate in labeling these matches.
The strategy that gave the highest return was when bets were placed according
to probability diﬀerences between the model’s predictions and the betting odds. The
number of identiﬁed value bets was sparse though and the largest proﬁts were made
under high uncertainty because of the limited amount of optimal bets. There was,
however, an indication of a pattern where probability disagreements between the
odds and the model forecasting resulted in value opportunities.
6.1
Future Directions
The work done is this project can be extended in multiple ways. Nielsen recom-
mended additional features that could not be included due to limitations of the
available data. Incorporating a player’s return from injury would be interesting and
could very well improve the model since Nielsen ﬁnds that players are often more
inconsistent in the initial stages of a comeback. The weather conditions might also
be of signiﬁcance especially regarding wind and temperature levels.
Tennis has a very unique scoring system that involves a lot of intermediate
objectives. It would be interesting to test how machine learning could be utilized
for predicting in-play during the progression of a match. For example, if a player
45
==PAGE:52==

has a 5-4 lead in the ﬁrst set and the opponent is serving, how does this aﬀect the
predictability of the outcome? Additionally, the focus of the approach could be on a
set-to-set basis where the outcome of the following set could be predicted based on
the result of the previous set. This would, however, require far more detailed data
sets including intermediate scores as well as the end result.
Home advantage is a known factor in team sports due to fatigue from travel
aﬀecting the away team, the psychological factor of the crowd encouraging the home
team and a strategic factor of familiarity with the venue [48]. Though playing at
home is a diﬀerent phenomenon in tennis compared to, for example, soccer where
each team has their own stadium, it would be interesting to examine the importance
of competing in tennis tournaments in a player’s home country.
In terms of proﬁting on the basis of predictions, a model could be trained and
optimized according to the maximum return instead of the accuracy. The conﬁrmed
existence of the favorite-longshot bias proves that betting companies set the odds
according to factors other than the probability of the outcome. A model trained
solely on the basis of betting returns might have an improved chance of beating
the bookmakers by ﬁnding patterns where the odds do not accurately reﬂect the
probabilities of the outcomes.
