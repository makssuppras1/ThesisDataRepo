Abstract
This thesis focuses on the fitting and further development of a mathematical model simulating
action potential induced exocytosis. The changes introduced to the model and to the fitting method
aimed to help understand and explain the observations made in an electrophysiology experiment
of control and Unc13CaMWRWR mutant Drosophila larvae.
Three model versions were fitted to the experimental data, including a control and mutant type. In
the control type of the models, a novel Ca2+ dependent synaptic vesicle unpriming mechanism was
implemented, introducing stably and unstably primed vesicle states. The results of the simulations
showed good agreement with the experimental results, suggesting the validity of the introduced
mechanism. The mutant simulations of the first two models used a Ca2+ independent vesicle
unpriming mechanism with a constant unpriming rate. The results of the simulations indicated that
the Unc13CaMWRWR mutation promotes stable priming. Model 3 demonstrated that increased
Ca2+ influx to the presynaptic cell while keeping the Ca2+ unpriming mechanism of the control
model, can also produce similar results to the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant.
For models 2 and 3, a new fitting method was introduced, which involved fitting the number of
vesicles (a parameter of the model) separately to the individual cells of the electrophysiology ex­
periment. The variance of this parameter provides a possible explanation for variance in the ex­
periment.
Furthermore, model 4 introduced an alternative approach for calculating Ca2+ influx directly from
membrane potentials. This method offers flexibility to the simulations and could be valuable for
future investigations.

Introduction
Communication between cells in the nervous system occurs at specialized points called synapses.
These synapses facilitate the transmission of chemical or electrical signals between neurons, al­
lowing for information processing and transfer throughout the nervous system.
At a chemical synapse, the presynaptic cell, also known as the ”talking” cell, contains synaptic vesi­
cles (SV). These vesicles are small, membrane­bound structures that store and release chemicals
called neurotransmitters ([1]). Neurotransmitters are the signaling molecules that carry informa­
tion from the presynaptic cell to the postsynaptic cell, or the ”listening” cell. The area close to the
membrane in the presynaptic cell where the neurotransmitter release happens is called the active
zone (AZ) [2]. The process of a SV releasing its neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft (the space
between the presynapic and postsynaptic neuron) is called fusion. In fusion the SV merges with
the AZ plasma membrane and exocytosis happens (active, bulk transport of molecules, in this case
neurotransmitters). Before fusion, the SVs need to go through a functional maturation process
called priming, in which the vesicles localize (dock) to the AZ plasma membrane. The primed
vesicles make up the readily releasable pool (RRP)[3][4].
Various evolutionarily highly conserved proteins mediate both the fusion and the priming process
[5][6]. Some of these proteins act as Ca2+ sensors [7][8]. Cooperative binding of Ca2+ ions to
these sensors increases the probability of vesicle fusion (pVr). Since there is always a presence
of these proteins and Ca2+ ions in the active zone, fusion can happen any time. Fusion without
an external stimulus is called a spontaneous release [9]. The current generated by the spontaneous
release of the neurotransmitters from a single vesicle is called the miniature Excitatory Junctional
Current (mEJC, often called ”mini”).
An action potential (AP) is a rapid, transitory and propagating change in the membrane potential of
a cell. APs in the presynaptic cell trigger the opening of voltage gated Ca2+ channels located on the
AZ membrane, which leads to Ca2+ ion influx. The elevated Ca2+ levels induce the synchronous
fusion of the SVs in the RRP. The current generated by the synchronous fusion is called the evoked
Excitatory Junctional Current (eEJC) and it is the sum of the currents generated by the single vesicle
fusions in the reaction.
Synapses can be distinguished by their short­term plasticity (STP), the way they react to successive
AP stimuli. STP is crutial for temporal computational tasks [10]. Short­term facilitation (STF)
happens when the eEJC amplitude evoked by succeeding AP is larger than the previous one and
short­term depression (STD) happens when it is lower. It is suspected, that STD is caused by
significant depletion of the RRP following repeated APs and the lack of time to replenish the
pool the original levels before the subsequent AP arrives. Alternatively, STF is often attributed to
mechanisms increasing the RRP to higher levels before the successive APs, caused by the residual
Ca2+ from the initial AP [11][12][13].
Following an AP, the inflowing Ca2+ ions diffuse over time. Due to this, the locals Ca2+ con­
centrations are different in the vicinity of individual SVs. The difference in these concentrations
is a function of the distance between the SV and the Ca2+ channels [14]. The local Ca2+ con­
centration effects the pVr of the individual cells. In a study published in 2020, Kobbersmed et
al. showed that the distribution of SV­Ca2+ channel distances can be described with a Reyleigh
distribution integrated around a circle [15]. In this study, a mathematical model that considers the
properties described above to simulate AP­induced neurotransmitter release at the presynapse was
introduced. The model utilizes a Ca2+ sensor, that is capable of binding up to 5 Ca2+ ions. The
increase of ions bound to the sensor results in higher pVr. The rates of Ca2+ binding, the rate of

vesicle priming and unpriming, and the rate of fusion are Ca2+ concentration dependent and are
described by a series of differential equations. After spatiotemporal intracellular Ca2+ concentra­
tions are calculated, these equations are solved deterministically or used in a stochastic simulation.
The model outputs the changes of vesicle states over time (number of Ca2+ bound, primed or un­
primed) and the fusion events which can be used to calculate further properties of the model, such
as pVr and eEJCs).
In this thesis, a number of applications and improvements of the model are proposed:
1. (M)Unc13 (mammals: Munc13, other species: Unc13) is an evolutionarily conserved pro­
tein that mediates SV priming and fusion [16][17]. Mutating the Ca2+/Calmodulin inter­
action domain (CaM domain) of Unc13A results in changed electrophysiology of the neu­
romuscular joint (NMJ) of Drosophila larvae. The mutant (Unc13CaMWRWR) showed in­
creased initial eEJC aplitudes and a loss of STF compared to the control group [18]. In the
first model, a new way to calculate Ca2+ concentration dependent vesicle priming was in­
troduced, which considers the mechanism of Ca2+/CaM binding to Unc13A. An alternative
priming mechanism, indipendent of Ca2+ concentration, was used for the mutant version
of the model. After fitting the parameters, the control and mutant models reproduced the
electrophysiological recordings of the control and the mutant experiments.
2. The second model introduces a new way to fit the model parameters the experimental values.
3. After some parameter exploration, a new way to reproduce the changes in electrophisiology
observes in the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant was developed. The third model shows that in­
creased Ca2+ influx results in the same electrophisiological changes in mutant model, while
keeping the Ca2+ concentration dependent vesicle priming mechanism of the control model.
4. Mutation in the synapse can change the characteristics of AP. For example, in the Shaker
mutant Drosophila, the repolarization period of the AP is delayed [19]. AP induced Ca2+
currents through the Ca2+ channels can be approximated by a gaussian curve [20]. The
original model uses this approximation to generate the Ca2+ current for the simulation. In
the fourth model, an alternative way of calculating the Ca2+ current, directly from membrane
potential is introduced.

Results
Model 1: Fitting the model with new unpriming mechanism to
experimental electrophysiology data
Electrophysiology testing
Electrohpysiology testing on the NMJ of control and Unc13CaMWRWR mutant Drosophila larvae
was performed as part of a study by Jusyte et al [18]. 19 control and 29 mutant specimen were
investigated. In the experiment, motor neuron axons were stimulated by two successive APs with
a 10 ms delay. eEJC amplitudes (eEJC1 for the first AP, eEJC2 for the second AP) were measured
and paired pulse ratios (PPRs) were calculated. PPR is defined as the ratio between eEJC2 and
eEJC1 (PPR=eEJC2/eEJC1). PPR is larger than 1 for STF and lower than 1 for STD. This test was
repeated 10 times for each cell with 5 different extracellular Ca2+ concentrations ([Ca2+]ext=0.4,
0.75, 1.5, 3, 6 mM).
eEJC1 amplitudes increased and PPR values decreased at higher extracellular Ca2+ concentrations
for both the control and the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant. It was also observed that the mutants had
higher eEJC1 amlpitudes and lower PPR values than the control.
Model fitting and new unpriming mechanism
The observed physiological data described above was used as the reference to fit a version of the
mathematical model by Kobbersmed et al [15]. The model simulates exocytosis, triggered by
Ca2+ ion influxes into the presynaptic AZ. During a simulation, SVs bind and unbind Ca2+ ions
with a Ca2+ sensor, change between primed and unprimed states, and release neurotransmitters by
fusion. The rates of these reactions depend on the local internal Ca2+ concentrations. From the
fusion reactions eEJC1, eEJC2 and PPR values are calculated. The simulated eEJC1 and eEJC2
amplitudes depend on the size of the RRP before the APs, which in turn depends on the priming
and unpriming rates of the model.
For the control version of model 1, a new way to calculate the Ca2+ dependent unpriming rate
is introduced. The new mechanism considers two possible primed states: a stable one, when
Ca2+/Calmodulin is bound to Unc13A, and an unstable one, when there is no Unc13A binding.
The unpriming rate is derived from the slow unpriming rate of the stably primed SVs and the fast
unpriming rate of the unstably primed SVs (Figure 1A). The forward priming rate is constant. The
mutant version of the model is also introduced, which is fitted the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant from
the electrophysiological experiment. The mutant model has a constant, low and Ca2+ independent
priming rate.
The number of total release sites (Ntotal), the maximum charge influx induced by the APs (Qmax),
the number of Unc13A proteins bound to a stably primed vesicle (m), the Ca2+ binding affinity of
the stable priming reaction (KD), the unpriming rate from the unstable state (kunprime(Ca2+ = 0))
and the forward priming rate (krep) were free parameters determined by fitting the model (Table
1). For model 1, the fitting involved the minimization of a cost value, which was calculated from
the difference between the eEJC1 and PPR values of the simulation and the mean eEJC1 and mean
PPR values of the experiments.

We can determine how well the model reproduced the experimental values by comparing the simu­
lated and experimental eEJC1 and PPR values (Figure 1F and 1G). As observed in the experiments,
the simulated eEJC1 values of the mutant were higher than the eEJC1 values of the control. Both
the simulated and experimental PPRs are lower in the mutant, compared to the control. However,
the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant had higher PPR values than the mutant simulation at 0.4 mM and
0.75 mM external Ca2+ concentrations. At 0.4 mM the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant in the experi­
ment shows STF. In the simulated mutant the available release sites are always occupied by primed
vesicles in the steady state (Figure 2E) due to the constant low unpriming rate (Figures 2B and 2C).
This means, that eEJC1 will always have the maximal amplitude for a given AP, thus, this version
of the model can never show STF.
For the control simulation at low extracellular Ca2+ concentration0,s the initial RRP (or steady­
state percentage of primed vesicles, Figure 2E) is smaller than the RRP before the second AP
(Figure 1C) resulting in STF. This is because the residual Ca2+ from the first AP induces vesi­
cle priming, resulting in a larger pool of primed vesicles for the second AP. At high extracellular
Ca2+ concentrations, both in the control and mutant simulations, the steady­state percentage of
primed vesicles is close to 100%, which results in STD. The initial RRP (Figure 2E) depends on
the balance between the steady­state unpriming rate (Figure 2C and 2B) and the constant prim­
ing rate. The steady­state unpriming rate depends on the resting intracellular Ca2+ concentration.
At higher steady­state intracellular Ca2+ concentrations a higher percentage of Unc13 is bound
to the Ca2+/Calmodulin complex, which means more stably primed vesicles and lower unprim­
ing rate (Figure 2B). The steady state intracellular Ca2+ concentration is spatially constant in the
presynapse and it is a function of the external Ca2+ concentration (Figure 2A).
The vesicular release probability (pVr, Figure 2D) and the total release probability (Figure 1B)
were both calculated from the number of fusions in the first 10 ms of the simulation. However,
pVr only considered the probability of fusion for the primed vesicles. The intracellular Ca2+
concentrations and fusion rates of primed vesicles were the same for the control and mutant simu­
lations, which means their pVr was also equal. The total release probability shows the probability
of fusion for a SV in general. Since the pVr was equal for both models and the number of primed
vesicles was higher for the mutant, total release probability was also higher for the mutant.

Model 2: Alternative fitting of Model 1
To fit the model to the experimental values, deterministic simulations were used. For every param­
eter set, the deterministic simulation gives one eEJC1 and one eEJC2 value as the output. The PPR
value is calculated from these, so the PPR=eEJC2/eEJC1 equation is always true for simulations
in the fitting algorithm.
For the experiments, the cell to cell variances had to be considered. The amplitudes of eEJC1
and eEJC2 of the different cells showed linear correlation (r = 0.64, p < 0.05). This correlation
means that generally, cells with a higher eEJC1 also had a higher eEJC2. In model 2, the number
of vesicles was fitted to each cell separately to replicate the variance in the eEJC amplitudes. The
number of vesicles parameter in the deterministic simulation acts as a linear scaling factor for the
eEJC values, so the observed correlation between eEJC1 and eEJC2 was replicated. The number
of vesicles in the simulation does not effect the PPR. It was also fitted separately from the other
parameters, after the deterministic simulations were run with the given parameter set.
In the experiments, the PPR was calculated for each cell separately. Because of the variance be­
tween the cells, the PPR=eEJC2/eEJC1 equation does not stand for the mean PPR and mean eEJC
values. This means that it is impossible to fit the model to all three values at the same time with
one parameter set. A solution to this would be to vary a parameter to differentiate the model by
cells. However, if a changed parameter has an effect on the PPR, the simulation has to be run for
each cell separately, and that would be too time consuming. This means, that two from the three
values (eEJC1, eEJC2, PPR) had to be chosen for the fitting. Model 1 was fitted to the mean eEJC1
and mean PPR values so the observed dynamics of the model was preserved. Model 2 shows how
the parameters change, if they are fitted to the eEJC1 and eEJC2 values.
In summary, model 2 used the same priming and unpriming mechanisms as model 1. Outside of the
number of vesicles, the same parameters were fitted (Table 2). The number of vesicles was fitted
to the individual cells separately. The cost value was calculated from the differences between the
eEJC1 and eEJC2 values of the simulations and the eEJC1 and eEJC2 values of each cell in the
experiments.

An important observation of this fit is that even though the first two models have different pa­
rameters, they produced similar eEJC and PPR outputs. This means that if one parameter of the
model is changed, it can still be balanced out by the change of the other parameters. The Qmax of
model 2 was lower than the Qmax of model 1, resulting in a lower amplitude for the AP induced
Ca2+ influxes (Figures 3C and 3D). Since the mutant model has a 100% percent site occupation in
the steady­state, the model compensated for the lower Ca2+ influx with a higher number of total
vesicles (Figure 3A). The similar eEJC values also meant that the total number of fusions was sim­
ilar to model 1. The higher number of vesicles, but similar number of fusions meant that the total
release probability was lower (Figure 3B). The lower Ca2+ influx with similar number of fusion
means that the vesicular release probability was lower (Figure 4D).
The fitting of the parameters of the Ca2+ dependent unpriming was not as successful as for model
1. The unpriming rate constant of unstable priming was much higher (kunprim(Ca2+ = 0) =
596.95s−1) and the Ca2+ affinity of the stable priming reaction was much lower (KD = 28.18nM).
This resulted in higher unpriming rates and low release site occupation at low Ca2+ levels (Figures
4B, 4C and 4E). This caused a deviation from the control experimental eEJC1 and PPR values at
low external Ca2+ concentrations. This deviation may have been caused due to fitting the model
to eEJC2 values instead of the PPR and the model overfitting to eEJC2.
Overall, fitting the number of vesicles to the individual cells can be a useful tool to explain the
variance of eEJC values. Fitting the model to the eEJC2 values instead of PPR values caused a
deviation in the dynamics of the model at low external Ca2+ concentrations. However, the fact
that eEJC2 is the value that is primarily measured in the electrophysiological experiments, could
be a reasoning behind choosing it for the fitting.

the Ca2+ influx and the resting Ca2+ concentration ([Ca2+]basal) in the mutant simulation, while
keeping the Ca2+ dependent unpriming rate of the control.

Model 4: Alternative method to calculate Ca2+ influx
The first step of the simulations is the calculation of the internal Ca2+ concentrations. For this, the
Ca2+ influx through the Ch2+ channels is needed. In models 1­3, the Ca2+ current induced by an
AP was simplified with a Gaussian curve and the parameters defining the width and amplitude of
this curve were fitted. However, some mutations can cause a change in shape of the AP, for example
in the Shaker mutant Drosophila NMJ the repolarization takes a longer time [19]. To account for a
change like this, a new way of calculating the Ca2+ influx directly from the membrane potentials
was developed. This new method uses a Hodgkin­Huxlay model (HH model), which replaces the
synapse with an electrical circuit, with the Ca2+ channels represented as voltage gated resistances
[21].
First, 3 different signals were generated representing the membrane potentials during an AP with
normal, fast and slow repolarization (Figure 7A). The repolarization time of the normal signal was
multiplied by a factor of 0.5 and 5 to get the signals with fast and slow repolarization respectively.
The opening (α, Equation 5) and closing (β, Equation 6) rates of the Ca2+ channel were calculated
from the membrane potentials (Figure 7B). The channel activation was calculated from the opening
and closing rates (Figure 7C, Equation 11). The channel activtion is a dimensionless variable with
a value between 0 (channel is closed) and 1 (channel is open). The opening rates stay high during
repolarization, which causes the Ca2+ channel to stay open for a longer time for slow reporazation,
and close sooner for the fast repolarization. The Ca2+ current (ICa) is calculated from the channel
activation (Figure 7D, Equation 12). Longer repolarization time corresponds to longer currents
with higher amplitudes.
The calculated Ca2+ currents were used to simulate exocytosis. For this, parameters from the
control in model 3 were used. The maximal conductance (gmax) of the Ca2+ channel is a parameter
that linearly scales the amplitude of the Ca2+ current. Since this exocytosis simulation was only
for presentation, the value of gmax was arbitrarily chosen, so the simulation with the normal AP
repolarization time had similar eEJC1 values at low extracellular Ca2+ concentrations as the control
in model 3. The resting intracellular Ca2+ concentrations were calculated the same way as before.
This means that the RRP in the stable state will be the same as in model 3. Because of this the PPR
values from this simulation (Figure 7E) resemble the PPR values of model 3.
The eEJC1 values (Figure 7E) of the normal repolarization time show less variance at the different
extracellular Ca2+ concentrations than the control of the original model. This means there is less
variance in the Ca2+ influx calculated from the membrane potentials, than it was assumed by the
simplified version with the gaussian curve. To achieve a larger variance in the eEJC1 values, some
of the parameters need to be refitted so the model has a higher Ca2+ affinity.

Methods
Simulation flow
All the simulations were executed in MATLAB version R2020a. The simulations for the different
models consisted of the same four steps:
1. Deterministic Ca2+ simulations: The first step involves running deterministic Ca2+ simula­
tions where the internal Ca2+ concentration is calculated in both space and time within the
presynapse. This is repeated for all the desired extracellular Ca2+ concentrations.
2. Drawing SV Distances: SV positions (distance from the Ca2+ channels) are generated based
on an experimentally observed generalized gamma distribution [15]. These SV distances
determine the points at which the intracellular Ca2+ concentrations are read from the first
step. These concentrations are then used for the exocytosis simulation.
3. Ca2+ binding and exocytosis simulation: The simulation of the models for Ca2+ binding
and exocytosis is performed for each SV position determined in the previous step. The
Ca2+ concentrations determined in the first two steps influence the reaction rates, causing
them to change accordingly. Based on the reaction rates the binding of Ca2+ to a sensor and
the subsequent process of exocytosis are simulated.
4. Convolution with mEJC and Obtaining eEJC: The outcome of the exocytosis simulation is
a series of vesicle fusions. In this step, mEJC waveform is convolved with the result of the
exocytosis simulation to form the eEJC.
To generate Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6 stochastic simulations were used, and steps 2­6 were repeated
200 times per parameter set. Stochastic simulation originally was developed so the variances in
eEJCs and PPRs resulting from stochastic distribution of SV locations could be compared to the
variances of experimental eEJCs and PPRs. In this thesis, these variances were not analyzed and
deterministic simulations would have been sufficient. However, some of the methods for generat­
ing the figures were developed for the output of the stochastic simulations and there was no time
to recreate these methods for the deterministic simulation.

Running the exocytosis simulations
The deterministic simulations were run by solving the differential equation system of the rate equa­
tions in MATLAB with the ode15s solver for the predetermined 180 SV locations. The occurring
fusions in the 180 solutions were then convolved with the mEJC waveform and multiplied by
cnumves to give the eEJC.
The stochastic simulations used a custom written version of the Gillespie Algorithm ([24]) with
a minimal time step (µ). In this kind of simulation the vesicle states are recorded in a vector.
The algorithm calculates the reaction rates at the current time (t) for all the vesicles based on
their current state and the local Ca2+ concentrations. Then the algorithm finds the next reaction.
The vesicle state where the reaction happened is updated, the new vesicle states are saved with
a timestamp, and the new reaction rates are calculated. If the reaction happened after the given
minimal time step, no vesicle changed its state and the current time was updated with the time step
(t+µ). After the simulation is carried out the fusion reactions, are convolved with mEJC which
gives the eEJC.

Discussion
In this thesis, three versions of a mathematical model simulating AP induced exocytosis were fitted
to the data of an electrophysiogy experiment. In the experiment, short­term plasticity in control and
Unc13CaMWRWR mutant Drosophila larval NMJ was measured at different extracellular Ca2+
concentrations. All three model versions had a control type, that was fitted to the control of the
experiments, and a mutant type, that was fitted to the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant.
For the versions of the model, different changes to the simulation process and the fitting algorithm
were introduced. The goal of these changes were to incorporate assumed mechanisms of the vesicle
priming into the model, to gain additional information from the fitted model, to give possible
explanations to the changes in the physiology of the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant and to increase the
flexibility of model for future investigations.
The control simulations utilized a novel Ca2+ dependent unpriming mechanism with a stable and
an unstable primed state. This change to the model was introduced to account for the assumption
that Unc13A binding to Ca2+/Calmodulin stabilizes the primed vesicle.
Generally, the eEJC1 and PPR values of the control simulations of models 1­3 showed good agree­
ment with the experiments. This could indicate that the assumptions of the new priming mechanism
were correct. All 3 models also indicated that around 2 Unc13 molecules are needed to stabilize a
primed vesicle (m = [2.04, 1.89, 2.026]). The models also predicted values for the Ca2+ affinity
of the stable priming (KD = [40, 28.18, 33.46] nM) that are in the range of the estimates for the
affinity of Ca2+/Calmodulin binding to Munc13­1/ubMunc13­2 in vitro (20–100 nM) ([26], [27]).
The other free parameters showed a larger variance between the 3 models. Having too many free
parameters can cause this. This thesis was limited by only having the electrophysiology experi­
ments as a reference for the fitting. Some additional experiments are needed to fix some of the free
parameters.
Two possible mechanisms were offered in this thesis to explain the changed electrophysiology of
the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant.
Models 1 and 2 introduced a mutant type, that had a constant low unpriming rate, as opposed
to the Ca2+ dependent unpriming mechanism of the control. The eEJC1 and PPR values of the
simulations showed good agreement with the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant. However, due to the
always high number of primed vesicles in steady­state, this model can never show STF. The
Unc13CaMWRWR mutant had STF at low extracellular Ca2+ concentration, so the model deviated
from the experiment. The control models had low unpriming rates, when a high percentage of the
vesicles were stably primed, which corresponds to the constant low unpriming rate of the mutant
model. This leads to the prediction that the Unc13CaMWRWR mutation promotes stable priming.
The mutant simulation of model 3 showed, that by raising the Ca2+ influx and the resting intra­
cellular Ca2+ concentrations while leaving the Ca2+ dependent unpriming mechanism results in
changes in the output similar to the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant. In fact, the eEJC1 and PPR val­
ues of the simulation had better agreement with the experimental values than models 1 and 2 did.
This could be directly compared by the cost values of models 2 and 3, which used the same fitting
algorithm. The cost value of model 3 (74.11) was lower than the cost value of model 2 (79.28),
indicating a better fit. These results lead to the prediction, that the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant does
not lose the Ca2+ dependence of the unpriming mechanism. The other prediction of this model is
that Unc13CaMWRWR mutation leads to an increased Ca2+ influx into the presynaptic cell. This

prediction, however needs to be supported by in vitro experiments. The Michaelis­Mentens con­
stant of the equations for calculating Ca2+ influx and resting intracellular Ca2+ concentrations was
the parameter, that was fitted for this model. The original value of this constant was determined
by measuring presynaptic Ca2+ levels using a genetically encoded calcium indicator [15]. A sim­
ilar experiment could be conducted to compare the Ca2+ concentrations in the Unc13CaMWRWR
mutant and the control.
Another possible interpretation of the mutant simulation of model 3 is that the Unc13CaMWRWR
mutation changes the Ca2+ affinity of the reaction rates. Raising the Ca2+ affinity of the unpriming
mechanism and the Ca2+ sensor has similar effects as higher intracellular Ca2+ concentrations.
This interpretation may be more in line with the assumed role of Unc13 in the presynapse. If there
is no difference between the experimentally observed Ca2+ levels of the Unc13CaMWRWR mutant
and the control, it is highly possible, that this second interpretation is correct.
Models 2 and 3 used a modified version of the fitting algorithm from model 1. Models 2 and 3
were fitted to the eEJC1 and eEJC2 values of the electrophysiology experiments, while model 1
was fitted to the eEJC1 and PPR values. None of the two methods showed a clear advantage over
the other. One reason for fitting to the PPR is to preserve the dynamics of the model. A justification
for fitting to the eEJC2 could be, that it is the primary result of the elecrophysiology experiments,
while PPR is a calculated one.
Models 2 and 3 also provided a possible explanation for the cell­to­cell variance in the eEJC1
and eEJC2 values of the electrophysiology experiments. Measured eEJC1 and eEJC2 amplitudes
showed a linear correlation. The number of vesicles in the simulation acts a linear scaling factor
for the eEJC1 and eEJC2 values. The variance of the eEJC1 and eEJC2 values and their linear
correlation was replicated in models 2 and 3 by fitting the number of vesicles to each cell separately.
Unrelated to the physiological experiments, model 4 introduced an alternative way to calculate
the Ca2+ influx directly from membrane potentials. This could be a useful tool for future inves­
tigations, as it makes the model more flexible to change. The currents calculated by this method
showed a lower dependence on the extracellular concentrations. This could be compensated by fit­
ting some of the parameters of the model, for example, by raising the Ca2+ affinity of the reaction
rates.
