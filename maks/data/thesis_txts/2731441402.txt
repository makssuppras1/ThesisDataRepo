Abstract
The integration of charge points (CPs) from various manufacturers into a unified electric vehicle
(EV) ecosystem is a challenging and labor­intensive task. The current compatibility testing
process, managed by Central System providers like Monta, demands significant manual effort
and direct communication with CP manufacturers to address compliance issues, particularly in
adhering to the Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP).
This thesis presents the development of an innovative automated test tool designed to stream­
line the compatibility verification of CPs with the Monta Central System. This tool aims to signif­
icantly reduce manual interventions in the integration process. The primary focus is on assess­
ing CPs against the OCPP, the de­facto standard for communication in EV infrastructures. The
tool identifies discrepancies that hinder successful integration and evaluates the cybersecurity
of CPs, detecting potential vulnerabilities to enhance their security against cyber threats.
The tool’s development involved a detailed analysis of current CP integration practices, the
identification of key areas for automation, and the design of an architecture that supports au­
tomated testing processes. This thesis also explores the implications of automated testing in
the context of the rapidly evolving EV ecosystem. It considers aspects such as the scalability
of CP integration, the impact on the efficiency of the EV charging network, and the potential to
facilitate more robust cybersecurity practices.
Furthermore, this thesis discusses the broader implications of automating CP integration. It
examines how this innovation could transform the EV charging infrastructure landscape, high­
lighting potential benefits such as increased operational efficiency, reduced integration times,
and enhanced reliability and security of the EV ecosystem.
Overall, the outcome of this research significantly advances the field of EV infrastructure man­
agement. The developed tool not only enhances the efficiency and reliability of integrating CPs
into the EV ecosystem but also sets a precedent for future advancements in the automation
of compatibility testing, contributing to the broader goal of promoting sustainable and efficient
transportation solutions.

Introduction
In recent years, the rapid rise in popularity of Electric Vehicles (EVs) has brought about sig­
nificant advancements in the field of sustainable transportation. As an alternative to traditional
fossil fuel­powered vehicles, EVs rely on electricity as their primary source of energy. However,
the widespread adoption of EVs necessitates the development of a robust charging infrastruc­
ture to support their charging needs.
To understand the importance of integrating Charge Points (CPs) into the EV infrastructure, it
is essential to grasp the fundamentals of how EV charging works. Charging an electric vehicle
is comparable to refueling a conventional car, but, instead of visiting a gas station, EV drivers
rely on CPs. These CPs are located in a variety of places, including private residences, work­
places, public parking lots, and highways, to provide convenient access to charging facilities.
The charging demand from EVs is projected to rise significantly in the coming years [1], which
challenges the charging infrastructure to be comprehensive and robust.
The EV charging infrastructure comprises a network of CPs connected to a Central System.
There are several Central System providers, Monta is one of them. A Central System acts as
the control center, facilitating communication and coordination between CPs and the EV drivers
or service providers. It facilitates charging operations, monitors charging sessions, and enables
billing and payment processing.
The charging infrastructure needs to accommodate a variety of use cases [2]. For example,
private charging occurs at home or in residential areas, where EV owners have dedicated CPs
installed. This allows guaranteed access to a CP when the EV is safely parked at home and to
take advantage of better energy rates overnight.
On the other hand, public charging stations are available for EV owners who require charg­
ing while away from home. These stations are typically found in public parking lots, shopping
centers, and other high­traffic areas. Public charging stations provide flexibility and extend
driving range. Another important aspect of the EV ecosystem is roaming charging. Roaming
allows EV drivers to charge their vehicles at CPs outside of their usual charging network or ser­
vice provider. This capability ensures interoperability and flexibility, as EV drivers can access
charging facilities even when traveling into areas where their regular charging network may not
have coverage.
To ensure seamless integration and compatibility between the diverse range of CPs and the
Central System, a standardized communication protocol called the Open Charge Point Protocol
(OCPP) is used. OCPP defines the rules and procedures for communication between CPs and
the Central System, facilitating interoperability and successful charging operations.
The objective of this thesis is to develop an automated test tool that verifies the compatibility of
CPs with the central operating platform, thus streamlining the integration process and ensuring
efficient and reliable charging services for EV users. By automating compatibility testing and
addressing potential issues in CP integration, the practical implementation of this thesis aims to
enhance the functionality and interoperability of the charging infrastructure, fostering the growth
of the EV ecosystem and contributing to the widespread adoption of electric vehicles.
1.1
Motivation
The global adoption of EVs is accelerating at an impressive rate. By 2021, there were ap­
proximately 16.5 million EVs on the road, this is almost three times as many as in 2018 [3].

To accommodate this surge, over 1.8 million public EV Charge Points were available in 2021,
expanding from around 0.5 million earlier in the year [3]. The speed and efficiency of these
charging stations play a pivotal role in EV adoption since charging duration remains a signifi­
cant concern for potential EV users [4].
In response to the rapid growth of EVs and their charging infrastructure, there has been a con­
certed effort by both public and private sector in innovative initiatives to enhance the manage­
ment of EV Charge Points. Developed by the Open Charge Alliance (OCA), OCPP serves as a
communication bridge between EVs, their CPs, and the systems managing them [5]. Available
as an open specification, OCPP can be freely adopted by any organization. Since its inaugural
release in 2015, the protocol has seen multiple updates.
Notably, OCPP 1.6 introduced advancements like smart charging and integrated both the Java­
Script Object Notation (JSON) and Simple Object Access Protocol (SOAP) formats. OCPP
2.0 further refined the protocol, fully embracing JSON and incorporating enhanced security
measures and other features [6]. The most recent iteration at the time of this thesis is OCPP
2.0.1, which builds upon version 2.0 by further optimizing aspects like smart charging, security,
and overall extensibility [5]. The universal standard OCPP offers ensures a unified approach to
EV communication. This harmonization brings about reduced costs for businesses and fosters
efficiency, security, and reliability in both EVs and their charging stations [7].
An essential component of this infrastructure is the integration of CPs into a central Charge
Point management system. However, integrating CPs from various manufacturers into a unified
operating platform presents a complex and labor­intensive task, primarily due to the need for
compatibility testing.
The existing process of compatibility testing involves manual intervention and communication
between the operating platform provider, Monta, and CP manufacturers. This process aims
to address non­compliance issues, ensuring that CPs adhere to the OCPP. However, manual
testing is time­consuming, prone to human errors, and hampers the scalability of the integration
process.
The integration of CPs into a Central System is a critical part of the EV infrastructure, ensuring
seamless communication and operation. This integration, however, poses a complex chal­
lenge, predominantly due to the diversity of CPs from different manufacturers and the necessity
for compatibility testing. Compatibility testing is needed for new CPs that should be integrated
into the Central System, but also necessary when a CP that is already integrated gets a firmware
update. Currently, this testing process is primarily manual, involving direct interaction and coor­
dination between the Central System provider (Monta), and CP manufacturers. This method is
designed to identify and rectify non­compliance issues, ensuring that CPs adhere to the OCPP.
However, the manual nature of this process not only makes it time­consuming and prone to
errors but also significantly restricts the scalability of the integration process, a critical factor
considering the rapidly evolving nature of EV technology and the increasing variety of CPs.
The development of automated testing for CP integration is essential for several reasons:
1. Efficiency and Speed: Automated testing significantly reduces the time required for com­
patibility testing, enabling quicker integration of CPs into the EV charging network.
2. Consistency and Reliability: By eliminating human error, automated testing ensures con­
sistent and reliable results, fostering trust in the compatibility of CPs with the EV charging
infrastructure.
3. Scalability: As the number of CPs and their technological complexity increases, auto­
mated testing offers a scalable solution that can keep pace with industry growth and fre­

1.2
Goals of the Thesis
To overcome these challenges, this thesis proposes the development of an automated test tool
for charge points. The goal of this tool is to streamline the integration process by verifying the
compatibility of CPs with Monta’s Central System.
The developed tool will assess the CPs adherence to a chosen set of necessary core require­
ments of OCPP 1.6 [8]. The objective is to optimize this verification process, ensuring the tool
identifies possible discrepancies, and generates logs that would facilitate in rectifying the de­
tected issues.
Automated in this sense means that the user interaction and input is kept to a minimum and the
different steps of the test validate the CP’s response.
The main objective is split into several smaller sub­investigations:
1. How can compatibility of a charge point with a specific EV ecosystem utilizing OCPP be
automatically verified? At the time of writing the industry mainly uses version 1.6 of OCPP.
While OCPP 2.0.1 [5] is an emerging standard that will be used in the future, this thesis
will be based on the more widely used OCPP 1.6.
2. How can a system that automatically verifies compatibility of a CP with OCPP 1.6 be
built? In order to send and receive messages from the CP, the OCPP messages need
to be processed. This requires a software system consisting of a backend for the logic
and data storage, and a frontend that provides the User Interface (UI). The frontend will
communicate with the backend via its Application Programming Interface (API).
3. How can a system detect different levels of security of a CP against attacks? With the num­
ber of adopted EVs rising, a secure and available EV charging infrastructure is needed.
Antoun et al. [9] give a security assessment of the current infrastructure. Attacks are pos­
sible on all parts of the system: the EV, the communications medium, or the CP. Attack
vectors include Denial­of­Service (DoS), Man­in­the­middle (MitM) or impersonation [10].
As the communication between the CP and the Central System poses a possible security
risk, the objective of this thesis is to give an assessment of a CP’s security through the
test.
For future development, the goal is to enable CP manufacturers to independently test the com­
patibility of their CPs using the automated tool. This approach decentralizes the testing process,
further enhancing efficiency and scalability, and allows CP manufacturers to proactively en­
sure compliance and interoperability with the OCPP, therefore making the development cycles
quicker.
Overall, the development of an automated test tool for CPs represents a crucial step towards
optimizing the integration process, minimizing manual efforts, and ensuring compatibility and
security of CPs within the EV ecosystem.

The Need for Protocols
Given the quantity of manufacturers and stakeholders in the EV charging domain, consistent
communication protocols are indispensable. They facilitate seamless communication and en­
sure the reliability of the charging process across devices and networks. The primary objective
of protocols like OCPP is to make certain that devices made by various manufacturers can
communicate with a central management system such as Monta.

2.2.2
Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP)
OCPP is an application protocol that defines the communication process between EV Charge
Points and a Central System. The currently widely adopted version is OCPP 1.6 [8]:
• Event­Based Messaging: OCPP 1.6 uses event­based messaging, where messages are
sent in response to events such as a vehicle being plugged in, a charging session starting,
or a charging session ending. This is an asynchronous communication method.
• Message Types: The protocol includes a variety of message types for different functions
(e.g., start transaction, stop transaction, heartbeats, meter values).
• Simplicity: OCPP 1.6 is simple in structure, making it easy to implement.

The key differences from OCPP 1.6 to OCPP 2.0.1, which constitutes the protocol of the future,
are [5]:
• Smart Charging Features: OCPP 2.0.1 enhances smart charging capabilities, allowing
more control for Charge Point Operators (CPOs) and utilities, and supports ISO 15118 for

• Device Model: The Device Model in 2.0.1 provides improved monitoring and control, en­

• Tariff and Cost Information: OCPP 2.0.1 allows displaying tariff and cost information in

• Improved Security: It includes advanced security features like encryption and secure au­
thentication, some of which have been backported to 1.6.
• Data Handling and Transaction Efficiency: The protocol streamlines transaction handling
and mandates data compression, improving efficiency, especially for mobile­connected
charge points.
In essence, while OCPP 1.6 is sufficient for current EV charging needs, OCPP 2.0.1 introduces
improvements in smart charging, security, and data efficiency [5].
2.2.3
ISO 15118 and IEC 61851
While OCPP defines communication between the Central System and the CPs, the commu­
nication between the CP and the EV is standardized by ISO 15118 [12] and IEC 61851 [13]
standards, which serve complementary roles. While ISO 15118 specializes in the communi­
cation between the CP and the EV, incorporating advanced features like vehicle­to­grid (V2G)
communication and bi­directional charging, IEC 61851 focuses on the physical specifications
of the charging system, ensuring safety and compatibility standards [13].
ISO 15118 extends beyond basic communication, introducing functionalities such as Plug &
Charge for streamlined authentication and billing, and Smart Charging to optimize the charging
process in relation to grid capabilities and user preferences [14]. These enhancements facilitate
a more efficient and user­centric charging experience [12].
On the other hand, IEC 61851 provides the foundational framework for conductive charging
systems, detailing electrical and mechanical safety requirements, and standardizing connector
types. This ensures the reliability and universal compatibility of the physical charging interface
[13].
Together, ISO 15118 and IEC 61851 form a cohesive framework that enhances both the com­
munication efficiency and physical integrity of EV charging systems, reflecting an integrated
approach essential for the modern energy landscape.

they last read and read forward from that point to avoid missing any items on the topic.

In designing a robust, maintainable, and scalable software system, one must consider the foun­
dational principles of the architecture.
3.2.1
Backend
For this project, the ”Clean Architecture”, conceptualized by Robert C. Martin (often referred to
as ”Uncle Bob”) [29], was chosen as the guiding architectural principle for the system. This ar­
chitecture emphasizes the separation of concerns through distinct layers, ensuring that depen­
dencies point inwards and that the innermost layers remain independent of external frameworks
and devices.
The layers include:
• Entities: the core business objects of the system.
• Use Cases: the application­specific business rules.
• Interface Adapters: sit between the use cases and external systems, converting data to
and from forms the inner circles can use.
• Frameworks and Drivers: includes UI, databases, web servers, and other external in­
terfaces.
A notable feature of this architecture is the Dependency Rule, which dictates that dependencies
should only point inwards. This ensures that core business rules and entities are not tied to
external frameworks and can be reused and changed without affecting the outer layers.
To manifest the clean architecture in a more granular and practical sense, the system is orga­
nized into four primary components as shown in figure 3.1: Controller, Service, Repository, and
Model.
• Controller: acts as the intermediary between the client (UI or external system) and the
service layer. It receives client requests, processes them (e.g. validation or authentica­
tion), and hands them over to the appropriate service.
• Service: encompasses the application’s core business logic or operations. It communi­
cates both with the controller, receiving processed data from it, and with the repository, to
access or persist data.
20

• Repository: acts as an abstraction layer over the underlying data access mechanism or
service. It provides an API for the service layer to retrieve and store data, ensuring that
the service layer remains decoupled from the database specifics.
• Entities: represents the business model and rules of the application. They are the core
classes in the domain of the application, encapsulating the fundamental business logic
and data. Entities are typically used by the service layer to perform business operations
and can be persisted and retrieved from the database via the repository layer.
The interaction between these components abides by the principles of separation of concerns.
A client request first reaches the Controller, which then processes the request and forwards it to
the appropriate Service. The Service, upon needing to access or modify data, will interact with
the Repository. Finally, the Repository fetches or persists data in the database or data source,
using the Entity to represent the structure and rules of the data being handled.
This structure ensures that each component has a clear, distinct responsibility, leading to a
system that is more maintainable, scalable, and testable. Moreover, by abiding by the Clean
Architecture’s principles, the design remains flexible, allowing for changes in business rules,
user interfaces, or data sources without a massive overhaul of the entire system.

the frontend then requests the necessary steps for the test from the backend. After opening the
auto test page, the user initiates the test through the frontend. This triggers the establishment
of a WebSocket connection between frontend and backend. The backend then interacts with
the database to create a test and the first step associated with the specified chargePointId.
The backend enters a recursive function to handle each test step. This involves processing
the step and, depending on the step, may require user interaction (e.g., connecting a cable
to the CP and an EV or EV simulator). Optionally, the backend sends requests to the CP as
part of the current step, and the CP responds accordingly. After processing each step, the
backend updates the results in the database and creates the next step. These updates are
then broadcast to the frontend to keep the user informed of the test progress.

even after the system that uses the database is turned off or restarted. The primary objective
of a persistent database is to ensure long­term data retention.
MySQL is a relational database management system that stores data on non­volatile mediums,
ensuring its longevity. It ensures data integrity through atomic transactions and write­ahead log­
ging. MySQL provides tools for backup and recovery, handles concurrent access with locking
mechanisms, and supports long­term storage with scalability features [32]. These characteris­
tics solidify its role as a persistent database.

Key features of ngrok include:
1. Secure Tunnels: It offers encrypted connections for secure data transfer.
2. Ease of Use: With a simple command, users can expose local servers to the internet,
bypassing complex network configurations.
3. Web Interface: Provides a user­friendly interface for inspecting HTTP traffic and replaying
requests.
4. Multi­Protocol Support: Beyond HTTP/HTTPS, ngrok also supports TCP protocols.
Use cases for ngrok are varied, including web development, mobile app testing, and webhook
testing. In web development, it allows developers to share local sites with clients without public
server deployment. For mobile app testing, it exposes local APIs and services for real­time
testing on mobile devices. In webhook testing, it aids in debugging by exposing local servers to
third­party services.

Discussion
In this chapter, the implications and findings of this research are explored, addressing the key
questions and objectives set forth in the previous chapters. The impact of automation on scal­
ability, potential future work, and other main aspects of this thesis will also be discussed.
6.1
Automation Evaluation
Reflecting on the thesis objectives defined in section 1.2, an assessment is possible to whether
the goals have been achieved:
1. The compatibility of a CP with a specific EV ecosystem utilizing Open Charge Point Pro­
tocol (OCPP) can be automatically verified through the developed test tool. This tool
successfully establishes a communication protocol with CPs, testing their adherence to
OCPP standards.
2. To build a system that automatically verifies CP compatibility with OCPP 1.6, this thesis
implemented a prototype demonstrating how the compatibility testing process can be au­
tomated. Although the current implementation does not cover all scenarios, it provides a
foundational model for comprehensive automated testing.
3. The developed system can detect different levels of security of a CP against cyber attacks
by assessing whether the CP uses a secured protocol to connect to the test tool. However,
further development is required to evaluate the CP’s support for specific Security Profiles
as defined in OCPP versions.
The initial stage of testing CPs involves connecting the CP to the communication endpoint,
which varies significantly across different CP brands and models. This process can range from
using mobile apps for Bluetooth or WiFi connections to requiring a computer connection via
an Ethernet cable. This variability poses challenges in standardizing the test process, as the
connection can be time­consuming and error­prone.
6.2
Relevance for OCPP 2.0.1
While the test tool developed in this thesis is based on OCPP 1.6, its underlying methodology
remains relevant for OCPP 2.0.1. The future adaptation of this tool to OCPP 2.0.1 will require
adjustments to accommodate the different message formats and enhanced security features of
the newer protocol version. This evolution is crucial for maintaining the tool’s applicability in the
dynamically evolving EV infrastructure.
The current implementation’s security assessment is limited to verifying the use of a secured
protocol for CP connections. A comprehensive evaluation of CPs’ adherence to specific Secu­
rity Profiles, as defined in OCPP 2.0.1 and the security whitepaper for OCPP 1.6, necessitates
further development. This enhancement would allow a more robust assessment of CPs’ cyber­
security capabilities.
6.3
Security in the EV Infrastructure
Gerofalaki et al. [14] identify open security issues for OCPP 2.0.1. These concerns highlight
the importance of continuous improvement in security measures within the EV infrastructure.
A feature called AutoCharge was released by Monta recently. It is the predecessor of a feature
that will be implemented in the future, called Plug & Charge. AutoCharge enables EV drivers
to begin charging immediately upon plugging in, eliminating additional steps. This technology,

==PAGE:48==
compatible with most EVs using Combined Charging System (CCS) connectors, provides con­
venience akin to RFID cards, but with superior security and ease, as it only requires cable
connection.
The AutoCharge technology operates by transmitting the Vehicle Identification (VID) via the
Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP). Once the connection is established, the charging session
is automatically initiated without any further actions required. The CP and Central System man­
age user identification, authorization, and billing processes internally. The charging session
ends automatically when the charger is unplugged.
The main distinctions between AutoCharge and Plug & Charge lie in their security levels and
complexity. Plug & Charge is notably more secure thanks to its integration of the ISO 15118
standard. However, its implementation is more complex, necessitating collaboration among
various parties within the EV infrastructure to manage different certificates, ensuring interoper­
ability across all stakeholders. Although Plug & Charge is technically feasible, its widespread
adoption hinges on the creation of a solid PKI and the active involvement of car manufacturers.

7
Conclusion
This thesis has presented the development of an automated test tool for the integration of
Charge Points (CPs) into electric vehicle (EV) ecosystems, particularly focusing on compliance
with the Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP). The primary motivation for this research was the
rapid global adoption of EVs, which has led to a significant increase in public EV Charge Points
and highlighted the importance of efficient and reliable charging infrastructure. The existing
manual compatibility testing process, characterized by extensive labor and direct communica­
tion with CP manufacturers, was identified as a bottleneck in this rapidly evolving field.
The automated test tool developed in this thesis represents a significant advancement in the field
of EV infrastructure management. By streamlining the compatibility verification process, this
tool substantially reduces manual interventions, thus addressing the scalability issues inherent
in the current system. A detailed analysis of current CP integration practices and the design of
an architecture supporting automated testing processes were key aspects of this development.
The implications of this research are substantial for the EV ecosystem. The tool enhances the
efficiency and security of integrating CPs, setting a precedent for future advancements in the
automation of compatibility testing. This contributes to the broader goal of promoting sustainable
and efficient transportation solutions. The development of this tool addresses several critical
needs:
• Efficiency and Speed: Significantly reduces the time required for compatibility testing,
enabling quicker integration of CPs into the EV ecosystem.
• Scalability: Offers a scalable solution that can keep pace with industry growth.
• Cost­Effectiveness: Reduces manual labor and time­intensive processes.
• Enhanced Security: Incorporates a security check, making the cybersecurity of CPs
more visible.
In conclusion, this thesis has made significant contributions to the field of EV infrastructure man­
agement by advancing the automation of CP compatibility testing within the EV ecosystem. The
development of the automated test tool marks a crucial step towards optimizing the integration
process, not only addressing immediate operational challenges but also providing a foundation
for future innovation and standardization in this rapidly evolving field. While minimizing man­
ual efforts and ensuring compatibility and security of CPs, the complexity of integrating newer
OCPP versions and enhancing cybersecurity measures indicates that further work is necessary.
The ongoing development of technologies like AutoCharge and Plug & Charge underscores the
dynamic nature of the EV industry and highlights the critical role of research and development
in driving progress towards sustainable transportation solutions.
7.1
Future Work
Looking forward, the goal is to enable CP manufacturers to independently use the automated
tool for testing the compatibility of their products. This decentralization of the testing process
is expected to further enhance efficiency and scalability, allowing CP manufacturers to proac­
tively ensure compliance and interoperability with the OCPP. Such a development is crucial for
shortening development cycles and adapting to technological advancements in the EV sector.
The work presented in this thesis could be extended in several directions. A distinct set of test
steps has been analysed and automated in this project. The remaining steps of the testing
