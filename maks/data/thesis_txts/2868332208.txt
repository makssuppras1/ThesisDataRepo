Master Thesis
Abstract
As the building industry looks to the future, the service life span of concrete structures, such
as concrete bridges, is expanded to upwards of 200 years. Investigations into the residual
service life of concrete bridge pillars, have shown that the splash zone incurs serious chlo-
ride induced corrosion to the pillars, sometimes requiring repairs after 50 −60 years. This
thesis will examine the splash zone, and underwater anodes’ ability to provide ICCP to the
concrete pillar.
The thesis employs an experiment, where reinforcement bars embedded in concrete speci-
mens are impressed with different currents. The magnitude and time of impressed current
densities are varied between specimens, to simulate different parts of a concrete bridge pillar.
The lower and upper parts of the splash zone are investigated, along with a fully submerged
concrete pillar and a reference specimen without ICCP. This totals to four specimens, which
are placed in containers, while slightly submerged in ASTM synthetic seawater.
Performance assessment and post experimental analysis led to the following conclusions:
Specimen 1, simulating the lower part of the splash zone, required a numerically higher cur-
rent density to reach the same polarisation as Specimen 2, simulating the upper part of the
splash zone, indicating a more aggressive reduction of life span closer to the surface.
Visual inspection of reinforcement anodes yielded unexpected results, as most of the pre-
cipitated rust formed about the anodes’ electrical connection. Specimen 4, which had no
currents impressed, had no rust precipitations on its anodes.
Chloride profiles did not follow the expected trajectory of a diffusion profile. Additionally,
no tangible difference between the specimens’ chloride contents were found, as they all hover
around 0.15 wt % of concrete at a depth of 10 mm. Possible reasons are discussed, but not
verifiable using this paper’s results.
Apart from a few outliers, such as rust precipitating near the anodes’ connections, there
were very few differences between specimens during analysis. The experimental setup is ul-
timately deemed inadequate, requiring improvements to eliminate errors and uncertainties.
The complexity of the master thesis’ findings highlights the importance of further research
into the investigated subject, as both expected and unexpected results were observed.

1
Introduction
Master Thesis
1
Introduction
Concrete is a widely used building material that has been subjected to increased scrutiny in
recent years. The resource requirements and carbon dioxide release associated with manu-
facturing of concrete, is not sustainable and must be improved. In an effort to reduce the
carbon footprint of the concrete industry, the refinement of concrete structure design has
proven an important topic. Improved designs result in extended service life and a reduced
need for repairs. This paper will explore concrete bridges in marine environments, and pos-
sible improvements to their design of cathodic protection.
Figure 1: Residual service life of con-
crete bridge pillar. Picture provided by
project supervisors.
Concrete bridges are typically designed for a ser-
vice life period of 100 years, but the building in-
dustry seeks to expand this period to 200 years.
A concrete bridge’s service life is primarily gov-
erned by chloride induced electrochemical corrosion
of reinforcement, occurring in the bridge’s pillars
near the splash zone.
The intricacies of avoid-
ing corrosion in the splash zone is not solved in
the industry, as bridges with supposed service life
spans of over 100 years, have required repairs in
the splash zone after 50 −60 years.
The issue
is illustrated in Figure 1, where the residual ser-
vice life in the splash zone is estimated.
Indus-
trial experience has shown that cathodic protec-
tion is the most prevalent method, for prevent-
ing the onset of corrosion in reinforcement.
A
well functioning system of cathodic protection can
prevent the need for repairs, providing economical
and environmental relief.
Anodes installed in the
splash zone result in high installation and main-
tenance costs, and are typically avoided if possi-
ble.
This paper seeks to investigate the ability of anodes installed underwater to protect the
splash zone, through experimental work. The experiment entails impressing reinforcement
bars embedded in concrete specimens with different currents, to provide Impressed Current
Cathodic Protection (ICCP). The magnitude and time of impressed current densities are
varied between specimens, to simulate different parts of a concrete bridge pillar. The lower
and upper parts of the splash zone are investigated, along with a fully submerged concrete
pillar and a reference specimen without ICCP. This totals to four specimens, which are
placed in containers, while slightly submerged in ASTM synthetic seawater.

Problem Statement
Background and Methodology
This project intends to explore cathodic protection of concrete bridge pillars, specifically
in the splash zone located just above the water level, as the residual service life of existing
bridge pillars have proven to be significantly reduced in this area, as illustrated in Figure 1.
The reduced lifespan of the bridge pillars is caused by the fluctuating water level, and the
installed cathodic protection systems inability to effectively adapt. A better understanding
of cathodic protection of the splash zone is not only relevant for corrosion protection design
of future bridge pillars, but is also pivotal for the many concrete bridges around the world
currently facing this issue.
Experimental work is used to investigate underwater anodes’ ability to prevent the onset
of corrosion in the splash zone.
Four concrete specimens with three steel reinforcement
rods each, are cast with a high water-cement ratio of 0.55, and placed in ASTM synthetic
seawater. The steel rods are impressed with currents to facilitate a system of ICCP.
Specimen 1 simulates the lower parts of the splash zone, where the residual service life is
reduced to ≈100 years. A current density of 0.2 mA/m2 is impressed for 6 hours, followed
by a 6 hour period with no impressed current, repeating.
Specimen 2 simulates the upper layers of the splash zone, where residual service life is
reduced to ≈100 −200 years. A current density of 1.0 mA/m2 is impressed for 2.5 hours,
followed by a 21.5 hour period with no impressed current, repeating.
Specimen 3 simulates the fully submerged parts of a pillar. The specimens anodes are
subjected to a constant current density of 0.1 mA/m2 throughout the experiment.
Specimen 4 will serve as a reference specimen, and will not have a current impressed.
Objective
The experiment is evaluated using performance assessment measurements of potentials, cur-
rent densities, potential decays, and IR-drops, in combination with visual inspection, stereo
microscope inspection, and scanning electron microscope inspections of reinforcement bars,
and analysis of the concrete cover layers chloride contents. The chloride content present in
the water is also analysed. The paper seeks to investigate the following:
• Comparing performance assessment of different specimens.
• Compare visual inspections of specimens.
• The different systems of ICCP’s influence on chloride content in the cover layers.
• Possible improvements to the experimental setup.

This section contains the relevant theory for the paper. Initially, the electrochemical concepts
governing corrosion of steel in concrete structures are specified, along with their relevance
regarding deterioration of modern concrete structures. Finally, cathodic protection in form

3.1
Corrosion of steel
Premature deterioration of reinforced concrete structures is typically caused by corrosion
of the reinforcement steel. This paper seeks to investigate reinforcement steel exposed to
seawater, and will thus primarily investigate corrosion in wet environments.
3.1.1
Electrochemical corrosion
Reinforcement in structures is generally mild steel, an alloy of iron and carbon with trace
quantities of Mn, Cu and Si. The electrochemical corrosion of reinforcement steel can be
characterized as the oxidation of metallic Fe, in the presence of moisture and oxygen, into
Fe-oxides and hydroxides commonly referred to as rust.

3.1.4
Metal corrosion in seawater medium
The reaction rate of metal corrosion in a seawater medium is dependent on the composition
of the seawater[2]. Listed below are some of the most influential factors.
3.1.4.1
Salinity
Salinity refers to the amount of salts dissolved in a body of water. As these salts are dissolved
into ions, there is a direct correlation between the salinity and conductivity of a liquid[3]. An
increase in salinity will decrease the oxygen content present in the water. As the corrosion
reaction relies on oxygen, a higher salinity level will typically result in lower metal corrosion
rates.
3.1.4.2
Conductivity
Conductivity refers to a solutions ability to conduct an electrical current. Ions dissolved in
water act as conductors for the electrical current[4]. For seawater, the major charged ions are:
Sodium(Na+), calcium (Ca2+), potassium (K+), magnesium (Mg2+), chloride (Cl−), sulfate
(SO4
2−), carbonate (CO3
2−), and bicarbonate (HCO3
−). Nitrates (NO3
2−) and phosphates
(PO4
3−) are considered minor contributors to conductivity.
3.1.4.3
Dissolved oxygen
The electrochemical corrosion described in section 3.1.1 rely on the presence of oxygen. The
amount of dissolved oxygen in a body of water, will indicate the corrosion rate of the system.
3.2
Corrosion of steel in concrete
Concrete offers a plethora of beneficial and preventative factors regarding corrosion of steel.
The high pH alkaline environment impedes electrochemical corrosion. Furthermore, concrete
is fairly electrically inert and conducts electricity poorly. Despite these factors, corrosion
of steel in concrete still occurs regularly.
For concrete bodies submerged in water, two
mechanisms are typically at fault.
3.2.1
Ingress of chlorides
As concrete is a porous material, harmful elements such as chlorides will eventually be trans-
ported through the cover layer to the reinforcement. Chloride ions attack the reinforcement
steel, and break the protective passivation film. Chlorides are typically present in marine
environments, de-icing salts, or even in certain aggregates and admixtures.
Transport of chlorides through the concrete cover layer are mainly governed by two mech-
anisms; diffusion and capillary suction.
Diffusion accelerates the ingress of chlorides in
accordance with the water-cement ratio of the concrete, as a higher water-cement ratio re-
sults in a more porous concrete. Capillary suction accelerates the ingress of chlorides relative
to the amount of capillary pores present in the concrete.

3.2.2
Critical chloride content
The concentration of chlorides present in concrete, which causes electrochemical corrosion,
is referred to as the critical chloride content, Ccrit. The total chloride content is defined as
the chloride content relative to the mass of the cement or concrete. Ccrit can be a compli-
cated concept to predict, as it is influenced by a plethora of parameters. These parameters
include[5]:
• Steel-concrete interface
• Concentration of hydroxide ions in the
pore solution (pH)
• Electrochemical potential of the steel
• Binder type
• Surface condition of the steel
• Chemical composition of the steel
• Temperature
• Moisture content of the concrete
• Oxygen availability at the steel surface
• Water/cement ratio
• Electrical resistivity of the concrete
• Degree of hydration
• Source of chloride
• Type of cation accompanying the chlo-
ride ion
While Ccrit is dependent on many factors, the condition of the steel-surface interface, the
pH of the concrete pore solution, and the steel potential are considered the most influential
factors. In practice, conservative values of Ccrit are used as design goals. These values hover
around Ccrit = 0.05 wt % of concrete or 0.5 wt % of cement, and represent a lower bound
value, as electrochemical corrosion does not occur below these Ccrit values with high degrees
of reliability[6]. When regarding literature from around the globe, critical chloride contents
have been reported between 0.04 % −8.34 % chloride relative to mass of cement[5].

3.2.4
Carbonation
Carbonation describes the reaction between atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) with the cal-
cium hydroxide (CA(OH)2) inherent in the cement matrix. The carbonation process releases
acidic species such as H+, leading to a reduction in pH and alkalinity. The degradation in
alkalinity alone can initiate corrosion in the reinforcement steel, but the carbonation process
may also release bound chlorides in the concrete, increasing the concentration of chlorides
and enabling the depassivation of the reinforcement steel. Depassivation breaks the passive
film on the steel surface, and increases corrosion rates.
3.2.5
Corrosion mechanisms
The two most important corrosion mechanics in the study of steel embedded in concrete
structures, are described in this section.
3.2.5.1
Uniform corrosion
The simplest form of corrosion is called uniform corrosion. Anodic and cathodic reactions are
coupled to form numerous micro-cells on the steel surface and occur on the same area. This
type of corrosion is generally caused by the steel loosing its passive state due to carbonation
or critical concentrations of chloride, while sufficient humidity and oxygen are present to
sustain the resulting cathodic reaction[8].
3.2.5.2
Macro-cell corrosion
Macro-cell corrosion is also referred to as pitting or localized corrosion. Macro-cell corrosion
in concrete is mainly a result of localized ingress of chlorides, caused by a capillary pore
or an uneven diffusion through the cover layer. The local concentration of chloride will be
critical, and breaks the passivation film in a small area to form a cavity in the surface of
the steel. The cavity forms its own minuscule cathode-anode system, with the anode at the
bottom of the corrosion pit, and the surrounding passive steel acting as cathode[8].
3.3
Cathodic protection
Cathodic protection is used to protect steel or other materials prone to corrosion. Cathodic
protection is typically achieved by employing either Galvanic Cathodic Protection (GACP),
or Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP). GACP is commonly used in cathodic
protection of steel structures, but is not explored in this paper.
3.3.1
Impressed Current Corrosion Protection
ICCP is achieved through impressing two different objects placed in an electrolyte with a
positive and a negative current, respectively.
The experiment conducted in this paper seeks to protect a bar of reinforcement steel. A
titanium net covered with MMO is placed somewhere in the electrolyte close to the rein-
forcement steel, and the two metal members are connected through a power supply. The
experimental setup is described in greater detail in section 5, and a sketch can be seen in
Figure 8. The reinforcement steel functions as a working electrode and is impressed with a
negative current, while the titanium net acts as a counter electrode and is impressed with a

positive current. The potential difference between the two acting electrodes is large enough
to cause negatively charged ions, such as chloride, to be repelled from the working electrode,
while being drawn towards the counter electrode. Repelling chloride ions lowers the chloride
content around the working electrode, which reduces reaction rates of corrosion processes.
3.3.2
Current density
Cathodic protection for steel in buried or immersed concrete structures is governed by the
impressed current density[9]. The current density is dependent on whether corrosion has
initiated or not. Typical current densities range from 0.2 mA/m2 to 20 mA/m2 in new struc-
tures in water-saturated conditions.
The required current density also relies on the placement of the specimen. If part of the
concrete structure is exposed to air, the immersed part of the structure will require a higher
current density to reach a protective current. Atmospherically exposed structures will typi-
cally require current densities closer to 20 mA/m2.
The lowest possible current density to achieve satisfactory cathodic protection is desirable,
not only for economic purposes, but also because protective and overprotective potentials
cause bond degradation between steel and concrete[10].
This paper’s project is designed around preventative current densities at 0.2 mA/m2. Earlier
projects[11] at DTU have shown that half the current density is sufficient to reach preven-
tative potentials. A preventative current density of 0.1 mA/m2 will serve as this paper’s
baseline.
3.3.3
Performance assessment
To gauge the quality of cathodic protection employed in a system, potential difference mea-
surements between the working electrode and a reference electrode must be taken. This is
called performance assessment. DS/ISO 12696:2022 - Cathodic protection of steel in con-
crete[9] states the following: ”The performance assessment shall be in accordance with the
following criteria of protection for steel in concrete. For any structure, any representative
steel in concrete location shall meet any one of the criteria given in a) to c):”
a) An ”Instantaneous OFF” potential more negative than −720 mV with respect to
Ag/AgCl/0.5 mol/l KCl;
b) A potential decay over a maximum of 24 h of at least 100 mV from ”Instantaneous
OFF”;
c) A potential decay over an extended period (typically 24 h or longer) of at least 150 mV
from ”Instantaneous OFF” to a continuing decay, and the use of reference electrodes
(not potential decay probes) for the measurement extended beyond 24 h.
”Instantaneous OFF” refers to potential measurements taken between 0.1 s and 1.0 s after
switching to the direct current circuit open.

4.1.2
Alternatives to Portland Cement
The thesis ”Corrosion of Steel in Alkali-Activated Materials”[1] investigates steel corrosion
in concrete, with low-Ca and high-Ca alkali-activated materials as substitutes for Portland
Cement. Alkali-activated materials (AAM) have the potential to reduce waste and emissions
in the industry, but differ from traditional cement in terms of chemistry and microstructure.
Their long-term performance when regarding steel corrosion is poorly documented, and the
thesis seeks to amend this issue.
Key findings in the thesis:
• For simulated low-Ca alkali-activated binders, the high alkalinity results in improved
passivation of the steel, increased chloride threshold value, and changed the critical
chloride concentration required for pitting somewhat unpredictably.
• For simulated high-Ca alkali-activated specimen, the chemical structure of the passive
film layer is changed and forms an Fe-S complex.
The traditional electrochemical
performance assessment methods proved insufficient for interpreting data, and further
study is required.

4.2
Cathodic protection post-corrosion onset
This master thesis deals with pre-corrosion specimen protected by preventative current den-
sities. Once uniform corrosion starts, the efficiency of cathodic protection dwindles. The
properties of cathodic protection post-corrosion onset is investigated in the following section.
4.2.1
Active protection of steel
The article ”Active and passive protection of steel reinforcement in concrete column us-
ing carbon fiber reinforced polymer against corrosion”[15] investigates reinforced concrete
columns experiencing varying degrees of corrosion, while treated with non-protection, passive
protection, and active protection respectively. The active protection involved wrapping the
investigated column specimen in a carbon fiber reinforced polymer (CFRP), which served as
sacrificial anode for the concrete reinforcement. The passive protection involved wrapping
the column without connection to the reinforcement. Key findings in the article:
• By measuring steel potential, linear polarization, and electrochemical impedance spec-
troscopy, the efficiency of active protection was found to be close the levels of pre-
corrosion. As the deployed active protection acts as a sacrificial anode, the CFRP
wrap will deteriorate with time, resulting in reduced efficiency of the protection.
• The deployed passive protection successfully slowed the corrosion process, but was
unable to stop the corrosion.
4.2.2
Realkalisation of carbonated concrete
The article ”Electrochemical Realkalisation of Carbonated Concrete: An Alternative Ap-
proach to Prevention of Reinforcing Steel Corrosion”[16] investigates, whether reinforced
concrete experiencing carbonation can achieve realkalisation, through impressing the rein-
forcement with a current. Key findings:
• Electrochemical realkalisation is able to increase pH levels of carbonated concrete, but
not to levels measured pre-carbonation.
• Pozzalanic concrete, in this case concrete using Portland Pozzolanic Cement, is more
susceptible to carbonation than concrete using ordinary cement, in this case Portland
Compound Cement, or blast furnace slag.
• Pozzalanic concrete specimen were able to reach realkalisation at a lower current flow,
than concrete using compound cement or slag.
• Electrochemical realkalisation is a temporary solution, as it can only be applied for a
few days to a few weeks.
Electrochemical realkalisation also risks permanent damage to the applied structure, as the
high current densities of about 1 A/m2 can cause bond degradation between steel and con-
crete[10].

4.3
Summary of literature review
As indicated by the literature review, chloride ingress in concrete structures is a rich topic,
and there are many avenues to explore. Avenues such as exploration of analysis methods,
to better detect and understand chloride ingress rates and causes, and investigations into
chloride ingress in concrete using cement alternatives to reduce its carbon footprint, are
topics which will only become more relevant in the years to come, as the industry seeks
to reduce its carbon emissions. Other avenues could include experiments centered around
optimisation of systems cathodic protection, or investigations into possible reversal of elec-
trochemical corrosion.
These topics only scratch the surface of possible projects, underlining the complexity of the
issue. However, it is widely understood that further research is needed for many of these
topics, to better understand their intricacies. This thesis intends to build upon some of the

for future projects.

The settings used throughout the

• Open Circuit Voltage (OCV): Working electrode is disconnected from the power am-

trode over a duration of 5 min. The impressed current is measured against the potential.

5.3.3
Performance assessment
To gauge the quality of an anode’s galvanic protection, 24-hour potential decay[9] measure-
ments are taken during the experiment. Specimen 2 has 21.5 hour rest periods programmed
in-between periods of impressed current. These are considered sufficient for the 24-hour
potential decay measurements, as observations during the experiment show that most of the
potential decay has occurred at this point. Specimens 1 and 3 will have 24-hour potential
decays measurements once a week. Specimen 4 has no current impressed.
5.3.4
IR-drop
A well documented phenomenon in potential decay measurements is the IR-drop, which
causes a large deviation in the measured potential immediately following disconnection of
power. IR-drops should be subtracted, when regarding potential decays. For this paper,
IR-drop measurements are taken over a 10 minute span. A development in the magnitude
of IR-drops can signify a change in a systems resistance.

5.5
Post experimental analysis
After the completion of the experiment, each specimen was cut open to investigate the cover
layer and the anodes and their surroundings.
5.5.1
Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM)
A Zeiss Supra 35 Scanning Electron Microscope was used to perform the SEM.
A scanning electron microscope produces images of a specimens material composition, by
beaming the investigated specimen surface with electrons, and cataloguing the resulting K-
α emissions with their respective elements. Ideally, a SEM analysis will map the chloride
concentrations at different points of a specimens cross section.
Between cutting and SEM analysis, the concrete samples were placed in a desiccator with
soda lime and silica gel, which will cultivate a low humidity CO2 deficient environment and
prevent carbonization. Before the scan can commence, the surface of the specimen is cleaned
using 99.9 % ethanol to remove possible dust. The sides of the specimen are then coated with
aluminium, as concrete lacks the electrical conductivity for the SEM microscope to function.
5.5.2
AgNO3 titration
A Metrohm 888 Titrando High-end titrator with built-in buret drive was used to perform
AgNO3 titration. A detailed procedure of the titration is found in Appendix A.8. AgNO3
titration is used to determine the concentration of chlorides in a provided powder sample.
AgNO3 titration is preferable to other methods of chloride mass determination[11], as it is
able to include the bound chlorides inherent in the concrete. A chloride mass percentage
profile is established for each specimen. The cover layers’ chloride contents are examined
in increments of 1.0 mm, from the water exposed surface at 0 mm to the anode position at
10 mm. The cover layer is investigated directly under the middle anode and between two
anodes respectively. This will hopefully ascertain the effectiveness of each specimens cathodic
protection, as an anode subjected to cathodic protection should actively repel chloride ions.
Once the AgNO3 titration has concluded, a volume of AgNO3 required for neutralization is
determined. The amounts of chlorides in the sample can then be determined using (5) and
(6):
mCl = cAgNO3 · VAgNO3 · MCl

molarity of AgNO3, MCl = 35.45 g/mol is the molar mass of chloride, and msample is the
mass of the powder sample.

7.3.1
Chronoamperometry
The experiment originally planned to utilize chronopotentiometry, to set a specific current
density, to simulate the two different layers within the splash zone. After the first 13 days, it
was decided to change the potentiostat settings to chronoamperometry in an effort to salvage
the experiment. Chronopotentiometry was deemed not feasible in this experiment for
three reasons:
1. Measured potentials did not reflect anticipated values based on theory.
• May be caused by inherent resistances or loss of power somewhere in the system.
• May be caused by an error in the potentiostat. The datalogger functioned well
with the theoretical current densities.
• The deviation between the theoretical current densities, and the practical current
densities required to obtain protective potentials, were somewhere in the realms
of 100 −300x.
Page 79 of 94
==PAGE:95==
7
Discussion
Master Thesis
2. When attempting to adapt the chronopotentiometry settings to reach protective po-
tentials, the potentiostat would sometimes overloaded and short circuit.
• This tended to happen when more than one anode was impressed with a current.
3. Impressing more than one anode in a specimen gave varying results.
• At current densities in the realm of ≈10 −20 mA/m2, the different anodes were
clearly affecting each other and would act as catalysts for each other, resulting in
the steel potential becoming increasingly negative with time.
• The effect of this would vary between tests.
Chronoamperometry is a mostly suitable setting for simulating the layers within the
splash zone, as the simulations are reliant on the time intervals and the intensity of the
impressed current.

7.4
Post experimental analysis
The post experimental analysis is discussed in this section.
7.4.1
Cutting
It is common practice, when studying chloride content within concrete blocks, to avoid using
water-cooled saws when possible. Water-cooled saws risk tampering with test results, as the
excessive amount of water can remove water-bound chlorides from the concrete. Specimen 1
had some of its larger cuts done with a water-cooled Carat T-5010 Stone saw machine, while
the finer cuts were executed using a STRUERS Discoplan-TS cutting machine, which was
cooled using isopropyl alcohol. Unfortunately, the Discoplan-TS broke down towards the end
of Specimen 1’s cutting, and was not repaired in time for the remaining three specimens.
Specimens 2, 3, and 4 were all cut using the water-cooled Carat T-5010. The chloride content
examinations for Specimens 2, 3, and 4 are therefore subjected to an additional source of
possible error.
Another consequence of only using the Carat-T5010 was its wider blade
causing difficulties with the narrow cuts.
Cutting the specimens open for post experimental analysis led to some issues.
As was
showcased in Figure 82, the internal placement of the anodes led to accidental cutting of
anode A3.3.
7.4.2
SEM
Specimen 1 served as a test specimen with regards to SEM. Electrochemical corrosion of
the reference anodes A1.1 and A1.3 was detected through potential measurements during
November, and it was decided to terminate Specimen 1 and examine the anodes. The SEM
scans should result in a section cut through the specimen, showing chloride content through
the cover layer and around the anode.
Samples of the side anode A1.1 and the middle anode A1.2 were put aside in a desiccator
filled with silica-gel and soda lime, to remove moisture and carbon dioxide from the desicca-
tor. This eliminated the possibility of the steel anodes corroding, and onset of carbonation
in the concrete.
The SEM samples had their surfaces cleaned with 99.9 % ethanol, and had their sides coated
in aluminium to ensure conductivity.
When examining the SEM samples, it became evident that the Zeiss Supra 35 Scanning Elec-
tron Microscope was ill suited to examine concrete samples. The electrically inert nature of
concrete complicated the scans, as insufficient amounts of K-α emissions were bouncing back
into the microscope. The low element counts were an issue, as the software was programmed
to specifically look for chloride. It is entirely possible, that the microscope found elements
with similar K-α emissions as chloride, and labelled them as chloride to satisfy the software.
The microscope was unable to consider the uneven topography of concrete, resulting in some
scans having large blank spots due to the concrete’s aggregates.
It is also possible, that cleaning the samples’ surfaces with ethanol removed chloride. The
expert operating the microscope stated that the instrument was prone to measurement er-
rors, when the element mass percentage fell below 1.0 %.
The Zeiss Supra 35 proved inept at scanning the concrete specimen, as it is designed to
perform SEM scans on steel specimen. These factors contribute to the SEM results being
compromised, and SEM was not repeated for Specimen 2, 3, and 4.

7.4.6
Water content in specimens
The concrete specimens were cast with a water-cement ratio of 0.55, and were matured in
50 ◦C water, to decrease the concrete maturing time from 28 days to 7 days. The resulting
concrete specimens were likely saturated with water, meaning the pores in the concrete were
filled with water before the experiment began. The lack of a diffusion profile in Figure 91, and
the SEM scan analysis showing similar results on both sides of the inspected anodes, leads to
two different conclusions. Either the chloride ingress is diminished due to the concrete pores
being prematurely filled, resulting in less than desired chloride ingress within the experiments
time-frame, or the saturated environment has caused the chloride to diffuse past the steel
anodes. The second conclusion could explain the chloride profiles barely decreasing during
the first 10 mm. If deeper layers of the specimens had been investigated, a more definitive
conclusion to the lacklustre chloride profiles may have been reached.
7.5
Sources of error
The experiment faced a variety of possible errors, which will be compiled below.
7.5.1
Impressed currents
The downscaled dimensions of the anodes led to very small current densities, as current den-
sities are the defining factor. The potentiostat was initially programmed to deliver 0.553 µA
and 2.765 µA to Specimens 1 and 2. The small current densities increases the severity of
errors in currents. If an instrument has a deviation of ±0.1 mA for example, the relative
impact is much larger for experiments utilizing small currents.
7.5.2
Concrete specimens
The issues mentioned in section 7.1 led to deviations between anode positions in the spec-
imens.
These deviations created differences in cover layer depths and distance between
anodes. Variance in cover layer depth could result in onset of electrochemical corrosion at
different chloride contents. It has been established, that the anodes within each specimen
affected each other during periods of impressed current. Varying distances between the an-
odes makes it difficult to quantify the magnitude of this effect.
Another possible error during concrete casting was the application of oil to the concrete
mould. The oil was required to release the concrete specimens from the mould, but may
have left some residue on the specimens’ surfaces.
7.5.3
Titanium screw connections
From investigations of the titanium screws, it is evident that the connections cultivated rust
precipitations on all anode with impressed currents. Furthermore, it was noted that some
of the screws were loosened after removal from the concrete specimens. These connections
were verified using a handheld voltmeter, and were shown to be perfectly functional with
regards to electrical conductivity.

7.5.4
Water container
Keeping all four specimens in the same container led to many issues, when using a sensitive
instrument such as the potentiostat. The initial decision to keep the four specimens in the
same water container was based on an earlier project[11], which had a similar premise and
experimental setup. The aforementioned project does not mention stray currents as an issue,
as it did not use a potentiostat.
ICP results show that ICCP systems produce chloride, and will eventually pollute the water.
The amount of pollution is unknown, as water samples from before the experiment started
were not taken. The effect of the chloride pollution will depend on the time frame of the
experiment. Experiments which run for longer will be more affected, as an increasing amount
of chloride will be produced, leading to more chloride ingress and reduction of the pH level
of the water.
As the experiment seeks to emulate real world conditions, this is deemed
undesirable.
The water pollution issue could be amended by continuously replacing the
water.
7.5.5
Reusing ASTM seawater
This project faced an issue stemming from the decision to reuse ASTM synthetic seawater
produced for an earlier project[11]. This meant possible contamination of the seawater, and
a limited supply of extra seawater. Possible contamination is not possible to determine, as
[11] does not specify the chemical compounds present in the water from the start, and this
project did not perform water quality tests before or during the experiment. During the
late stages of the experiment, sufficient amounts of seawater had evaporated from the water
containers, resulting in the supply of extra seawater being insufficient. Bricks were placed in
the water containers to keep the water level at an appropriate level. Bricks are very porous
by nature and may have absorbed some of the harmful elements present in the water, such
as chlorides, leading to reduced chloride ingress in the concrete specimens.
7.5.6
Titanium wire resistance
The inherent resistance present in the used titanium wires was not considered. Using a hand-
held voltmeter to gauge 1 m of titanium wire, the resistance was measured to be 0.438 Ω/m.
7.5.7
Placement of counter electrodes
The decision to have the counter electrodes stuck to the bottom of their respective specimen
was short-sighted, as it introduced another element of uncertainty. The counter electrodes
could have served as a physical barrier for chloride ingress, and may even have provided the
concrete specimens with a form of active chloride content reduction, by applying a positive
current to the surfaces. The magnitude of these effects are unknown.
7.5.8
Potentiostat
The numerous setting changes made during the initial experimental period, resulted in very
volatile potentials for Specimens 1 and 2. These operational errors had long term effects
on the systems, and it is very likely that they caused the onset of electrochemical corrosion
in Specimen 1. The errors affected Specimen 2 less, as it was programmed for 2.5 hours of
impressed current a day, as opposed to Specimen 1, which totalled 12 hours of impressed

7.6.1
Potentiostat settings
The potentiostat is a very versatile instrument with a wide variety of programmable settings.
These settings can provide measurements for different investigations, or contribute to more
in-depth inquiries into the issues examined in this paper.
7.6.2
Concrete specimens
There are many possible alterations to the concrete specimens, which could be interesting
to explore.
Increasing the width of the concrete specimens would result in more space between anodes,
diminishing the effect of interference from multiple anodes in a single specimen.
Decreasing the width of the concrete specimens, while removing the redundant side anodes
could lead to more focused investigations of an anode embedded in concrete.
This paper’s results are lacklustre, as 3/4 specimens never experienced electrochemical cor-
rosion, despite casting the specimens with a water-cement ratio of 0.55 and keeping a short
cover layer depth of 10 mm. If future projects operate within a similar time-frame, the ingress
of chlorides could be accelerated even further to ensure onset of electrochemical corrosion.
If synthetic seawater was used to cast the concrete specimens, the concrete would inhabit a
considerably higher chloride content from the experiments inception, and the critical chloride
threshold would be reached faster.
The concrete specimens used 0 −2 mm sand as aggregates. Different aggregates with larger
grain sizes would result in increasing imperfections in the concrete, allowing for more air
pockets near the steel anodes. Projects could also investigate differences in methods of ma-
turing the concrete, by comparing specimens matured in water and specimens matured in
ovens, thus exploring the effect of saturated and dry pores.
To avoid the possible contamination from mould oil used during casting, an additional 10 mm
cover layer could be cast. The additional 10 mm would then be removed after casting and
maturing. This would also allow for an analysis of the chloride content, present in the con-
crete from the experiments start.
Environmentally friendly concrete is a very popular and relevant topic these days. Generally,
these eco-friendly concretes employ alternatives to cement to reduce their carbon dioxide
footprint, as cement requires an enormous amount of energy to produce. Chloride ingress
in concrete using alternatives to cement could prove an important topic, as the effect of
replacing cement is not fully understood yet.
7.6.3
Anodes
The anodes are also an explorable asset. Anodes with varying dimensions could be inves-
tigated for their respective critical chloride threshold. An entirely different anode layout,
such as a reinforcement mesh, could also be employed. This would reinforce the experiments

8
Conclusion
This master thesis sought to investigate underwater anodes’ ability to provide cathodic pro-
tection of the splash zone.
The experiment measured and evaluated potentials, current
densities, IR-drops, 24-hour decay potentials, and chloride content in reinforced concrete
specimens, and ran for 89 days.
The experiments initial setup saw operational and equipment errors, resulting in polarisa-
tion of the steel reinforcement anodes to −4000 mV in the most extreme case. The resulting
damage to the experimental specimens remains unknown.
Specimens 1 and 2 were controlled by a potentiostat, while Specimen 3 was controlled by
a power supply. The potentiostat exhibited flaws in its measurements, and an inability to
control two separate ICCP systems simultaneously, leading to performance assessment re-
sults, pertaining to Specimens 1 and 2, being virtually incomparable with Specimen 3.
Specimens 1 and 2 exhibited notable discrepancies in current densities required to achieve the
same polarization and 24-hour decay. For a 50 mV 24-hour potential decay, the discrepancy
in current density between the lower and upper splash zone was 1.6 mA/m2, with the upper
splash zone requiring less current. This translates to the lower splash zone receiving worse
cathodic protection than the upper splash zone at the same impressed current, aligning with
observed service life reductions in the splash zone, as the lower parts of the zone suffer the
largest reduction of residual life span.
SEM inspection of Specimen 1 yielded inconclusive results, despite Specimen 1’s potentials
indicating onset of corrosion, as the Zeiss Supra 35 proved inadequate at SEM analysis of
concrete specimens. Visual inspection and stereo microscope inspection of Specimen 1’s an-
odes led to unexpected results, as no visual corrosion was detected.
Notable precipitations of rust were found near Specimen 2 and 3’s electrical connections.
Specimen 4, which had no currents impressed, exhibited potentials indicating passivated
anodes, and had no visible rust precipitations.
Chloride profiles of the specimens do not follow the trajectory of a diffusion profile. Addi-
tionally, no tangible difference between the specimens’ chloride contents were found, as they
all hover around 0.15 wt % of concrete at a depth of 10 mm. Visual inspection of the cover
layers using AgNO3 spray, confirm the presence of chlorides for most anodes, but is unable to
determine differences in chloride contents. The lack of variation in chloride contents between
the specimens, indicates a flaw in the experimental setup. The precise flaw is explored, but
not possible to ascertain from the experiment.
Had the time frame of the experiment been increased, better measurements and conclusions
would likely be achieved, as chloride induced corrosion would have occurred. This is sug-
gested by the measured chloride contents all being above the critical threshold of 0.05 wt %
of concrete.
The complexity of the master thesis’ findings, highlights the importance of further research
into the investigated subject, as both expected and unexpected results were observed. New
considerations for future projects were established to eliminate possible errors, and to facil-
itate more quantifiable data.

A.1
Concrete recipe
A concrete recipe suitable for the project is derived. While the paper intends to explore
concrete bridge pillars, which employ concrete with a relatively low water-cement ratio and
a large cover depth, the time-frame of the experiment demands altered conditions. To achieve
useful data regarding cathodic protection, the corrosion is accelerated by lowering the cover
depth, and increasing the water/cement ratio of the concrete to increase the conductivity of
the research specimen. The Great Belt Bridge pillars are constructed using a water-cement
ratio of approximately 0.34, but this concrete recipe will use a water-cement ratio of 0.55.
There are no demands pertaining to the concrete strength.

specimens are cast to determine whether superplasticizer is necessary.
