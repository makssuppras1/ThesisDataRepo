Abstract
Optical phased arrays (OPAs) are a promising solution for solid­state beam steer­
ing in LiDAR and related applications. However, their performance suffers from
temperature­induced phase variations. This thesis proposes a hybrid waveguide
platform based on silicon nitride (SiN) and titanium dioxide (TiO2), aiming to pas­
sively reduce thermal sensitivity through material­level thermo­optic compensa­
tion.
Numerical simulations using Lumerical MODE, FDTD, and varFDTD demonstrated
that a carefully optimized SiN–TiO2 structure can significantly reduce the effective
thermo­optic coefficient while maintaining low loss and good modal confinement.
A complete simulation framework was established for evaluating MMI, Y­branch,
Mach–Zehnder interferometers, and one­dimensional OPAs under temperature
variation.
The proposed structures were successfully fabricated in a cleanroom environment
using electron­beam lithography, sputtering, and PECVD processes. Scanning
electron microscopy confirmed the structural fidelity of key photonic components.
Although the final optical measurements did not yield the expected far­field inter­
ference pattern, the results validate the feasibility of the hybrid fabrication process
and confirm the effectiveness of the athermal design in simulation.
This work provides a robust foundation for future refinement in phase control and
system­level integration of thermally stable OPAs.

1.1
LiDAR Systems and Optical Phased Arrays
1.1.1
LiDAR Applications and Market Trends
With the rise of autonomous driving, robotics, and advanced driver assistance
systems (ADAS), Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) has emerged as a piv­
otal technology for acquiring high­resolution, real­time 3D environmental data.[1]
Unlike cameras and radar, LiDAR can deliver accurate depth perception and ob­
ject contouring regardless of lighting conditions, making it a strong candidate to
complete the automotive perception suite.[2]
LiDAR systems emit laser pulses and calculate distances based on either time­of­
flight (ToF) or frequency­modulated continuous wave (FMCW) methods,[3] with
each providing distinct advantages in resolution, noise immunity, and Doppler
sensing. Driven by the demand for safer and smarter transportation, the LiDAR
market is projected to grow at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 19.27%,
reaching nearly 7 billion USD by 2030.[4] However, traditional mechanical LiDAR
systems face challenges in scalability due to their bulkiness, moving parts, and
cost.[5] These constraints motivate the development of solid­state LiDAR solu­
tions with improved reliability, miniaturization, and compatibility with CMOS pro­
cesses.
Among the various wavelengths used in LiDAR (850 nm, 905 nm, 1550 nm), the
905 nm band strikes a compelling balance between detector cost, eye safety, and
resolution.[6] It is compatible with silicon photodetectors and suitable for CMOS
integration, making it a popular choice for automotive­grade compact LiDAR sys­
tems. Moreover, under adverse atmospheric conditions, 905 nm wavelength Li­
DAR systems demonstrate greater robustness compared to 1550 nm systems,
making them preferable for outdoor and all­weather scenarios [7].
1.1.2
OPA for Solid­State Beam Steering

solid­state beam steering in integrated LiDAR. Inspired by their radio­frequency
counterparts, OPAs consist of an array of optical emitters, where the phase of each
element is controlled to steer the outgoing beam by constructive interference. In
the absence of mechanical motion, OPAs offer high­speed, programmable beam
control, scalability, and resilience to vibration—all essential features for automo­
tive and mobile platforms [8].

Phase Shifting in Optical Phased Arrays
1.2.1
Principles of Phase Shift and Thermal Sensitivity
In optical phased arrays (OPAs), beam steering is achieved by introducing phase
shifts across an array of emitters. Constructive and destructive interference in the

1.2.2
Compensation Techniques for Thermal Drift
Temperature­induced phase drift poses a major challenge to stable beam steering
in optical phased arrays (OPAs). This drift originates from variations in the effec­
tive refractive index of waveguides due to temperature changes, often caused by
on­chip heat accumulation or thermal gradients. These can arise from poor heat
dissipation, the presence of densely packed components, or localized heating of
active devices. To address this issue, four main categories of thermal compensa­
tion strategies have been developed.
One solution is deep trench isolation. By etching through the buried oxide layer
and partially into the silicon substrate, deep trenches are introduced between
temperature­sensitive regions and heat­generating components. This reduces
lateral heat diffusion and improves thermal isolation within the chip.[11]
A second structural approach uses heat shunts. These are thermally conduc­
tive pathways—often metallic—that divert excess heat away from active photonic
elements, stabilizing the local temperature near phase­sensitive waveguides.[11]
The third approach uses interferometric calibration. By incorporating a reference
interferometer within the optical circuit, phase drift can be monitored and com­
pensated either electronically or through software­based correction algorithms.
This technique improves precision but increases circuit complexity and layout foot­
print.[12]
The third and most power­efficient method is material­level athermal design. This
strategy involves engineering waveguide structures with materials exhibiting op­
posite thermo­optic coefficients. For example, silicon nitride (Si3N4) has a moder­
ate positive TOC, while titanium dioxide (TiO2) possesses a large negative TOC.
By optimizing the overlap between the optical mode and the surrounding mate­
rials, it is possible to achieve a near­zero net TOC, thus passively suppressing
thermal drift without the need for active control.[13]
In this work, we focus on the fourth method, integrating a Si3N4–TiO2 hybrid waveg­
uide structure into the OPA design to achieve passive thermal stabilization of the
phase front under environmental temperature variations.

1.4
Objectives and Innovations
1.4.1
Application of SiN–TiO2 to OPA Phase Stability
The main goal of this work is to apply the Si3N4–TiO2 hybrid waveguide strategy to
improve thermal stability in optical phased arrays. In OPA systems, precise phase
control across many elements is essential for accurate beam steering. However,
conventional Si3N4 waveguides suffer from thermal­induced phase drift, which de­
grades beam fidelity. By employing the hybrid athermal waveguide design in each
phase shifter arm, the influence of ambient temperature changes can be signif­
icantly reduced. This enables robust beam steering performance without active
thermal control.
1.4.2
Coupling Method
Efficient fiber­to­chip coupling is essential for integrated photonic devices. Two
mainstream approaches are commonly adopted: grating couplers and inverse
tapers.
Grating couplers use a surface­etched periodic structure to diffract incoming light
from a vertically aligned fiber into the waveguide. They support wafer­level testing,
occupy small footprint, and can be co­designed with photodetectors. However,
their efficiency strongly depends on etch depth, fill factor, and angle alignment.
They are also inherently polarization­sensitive and have limited bandwidth. More
importantly, grating structures introduce non­planar topography, which hinders
subsequent thin­film processes.
Inverse tapers, in contrast, rely on adiabatically narrowing the waveguide width to
expand the optical mode into the surrounding low­index cladding. This expanded
mode can be efficiently coupled to a lensed fiber aligned in­plane. Inverse tapers
offer broadband, polarization­insensitive coupling with lower insertion loss and
better tolerance to fabrication deviations.
In this project, inverse tapers are selected over grating couplers due to their com­
patibility with TiO2 deposition. The TiO2 overcladding must be uniformly sputtered
on top of the waveguide for effective thermo­optic compensation. Grating cou­
plers’ corrugated surface severely limits film conformity, leading to non­uniform
coverage and degraded device performance. In contrast, inverse tapers provide
a flat top surface that supports conformal TiO2 deposition, ensuring consistent
athermal behavior and high coupling quality. This makes inverse tapers the pre­
ferred interface structure in hybrid TiO2–Si3N4 waveguide systems.

2
Athermal structure analysis
This chapter compares different TiO2 integration strategies for hybrid Si3N4 waveg­
uides and analyzes their optical and thermal performance through simulation. We
evaluate the modal field distribution, single­mode condition, and thermo­optic be­
havior to determine the optimal configuration for athermal OPA applications.
2.1
TiO2 Deposition Strategies
Three TiO2 integration strategies are evaluated: patterned TiO2, conformal TiO2
coating, and multilayer TiO2–Si3N4 stacking. The patterned TiO2 method offers lat­
eral refractive index control but introduces fabrication challenges such as rough­
ness from lift­off and difficulty in etching due to TiO2’s chemical resistance. Though
not adopted experimentally, it is simulated for performance comparison.
The conformal TiO2 coating structure involves depositing TiO2 onto a patterned
Si3N4 core. This approach avoids direct TiO2 etching and achieves a relatively
symmetric vertical index profile by sandwiching the core between SiO2 and TiO2,
improving manufacturability.
The multilayer stack alternates Si3N4 and TiO2 layers to allow more freedom in
tailoring mode shape and TOC. Though difficult to fabricate, this design is included
for theoretical benchmarking.

coatings, it offers greater design flexibility and improved thermal stability for inte­
grated photonic circuits.

Among the evaluated TiO2 integration strategies, the conformal TiO2 coating emerges
as the most practical solution for athermal design. With a thickness of 50 nm, it
achieves near­zero TOC while maintaining acceptable modal performance. Al­
though higher­order modes may appear in wider waveguides, their impact can

Dioxide

be mitigated through inverse tapers and short passive paths in the OPA layout,
making this approach suitable for scalable implementation.
The optimized patterned TiO2 structure offers stronger confinement and achieves
negative TOC while preserving single­mode operation. Its tunability in both ge­
ometry and thermal response makes it attractive for precision designs, though
the need for accurate lithographic definition introduces fabrication complexity that
limits large­scale deployment.
Multilayer TiO2/Si3N4 stacks provide the highest design flexibility and can tailor
both optical and thermal characteristics with great precision. However, their intri­

Considering performance, tunability, and process simplicity, the 50 nm conformal
TiO2 coating is selected as the most balanced option for realizing the proposed

Simulation Workflow
This chapter presents the multi­stage simulation workflow used to evaluate the
optical performance and thermal stability of the proposed SiN–TiO2 hybrid waveg­
uide platform. Depending on the physical characteristics and scale of each struc­
ture, different simulation tools from the Lumerical suite were selected, including
MODE, FDTD, and varFDTD.
First, straight and bent waveguides were analyzed using Lumerical MODE. This
eigenmode solver was used to extract neff, calculate the effective thermo­optic co­
efficient (dneff/dT) and get loss for SiN–TiO2 hybrid straight and bent waveguide.
Second, the Y­branch splitter in the multimode interferometer was simulated using
Lumerical FDTD. Due to the complex field interactions and geometric discontinu­
ities in this region, full­wave time­domain simulation was necessary to evaluate
key performance metrics such as splitting ratio, insertion loss, and back­reflection.
These simulations guided the structural optimization of the Y­branch.
Finally, a complete MZI was constructed using the optimized components and
simulated using Lumerical’s varFDTD solver.
Since the MZI structure is rela­
tively large in size, conventional FDTD would be computationally expensive. The
varFDTD method, which combines mode expansion with finite­difference prop­
agation, provides an efficient and accurate solution for modeling long photonic
circuits, especially under temperature variation.
By selecting the appropriate solver for each structure, the simulation workflow
enables both detailed component­level analysis and efficient system­level evalu­
ation.

3.4
Mach–Zehnder Interferometer (MZI) Simulation
and Analysis
To evaluate the performance of the Y­branch in a realistic photonic circuit, a Mach–
Zehnder interferometer (MZI) was constructed and simulated using Lumerical’s
variational finite­difference time­domain (varFDTD) solver. Unlike the eigenmode­
based simulations previously performed using the MODE tool, which relies on the
finite­difference eigenmode (FDE) solver to compute local mode profiles, varFDTD
does not require solving Maxwell’s eigenvalue problem for each cross­section. In­
stead, it propagates a predefined input mode through the structure using a slowly
varying envelope approximation (SVEA), making it particularly efficient for long or
interferometric structures such as MZIs.
Compared to full FDTD, varFDTD offers a compromise between simulation accu­
racy and computational speed. While standard FDTD solves Maxwell’s equations
in the full time domain with rigorous field updates, it is often computationally expen­
sive for large­scale or system­level designs. varFDTD simplifies the time­stepping
process under the assumption of primarily forward propagation and weak refrac­
tive index variations, enabling rapid evaluation of passive photonic circuits with
reasonable accuracy.
In this work, MZI structures were composed of straight and bent waveguides, in­
corporating the previously optimized Y­branch splitter. To streamline layout gen­
eration, GDSII mask files were created using custom Python scripts developed in
PyCharm. This script­based approach enabled precise control over arm lengths,
bend radii, and port alignment. The generated GDS layouts were directly imported
into Lumerical, replacing manual structure building and enabling a repeatable and
automated simulation workflow.

Spin coating was used to apply uniform photoresist films onto 4­inch and 6­inch

resist viscosity, and environmental factors like temperature and humidity.
In this project, spin coating was employed prior to cleaving to deposit a sacrifi­

mechanical damage during breaking. Since the spin coater accommodates only
uncut wafers, this step was performed before the wafers were cleaved into smaller
chips. Subsequent chip­level processing steps used a manual spinner designed

Wafer Cleaving
The wafers were manually cleaved into smaller chips using a diamond­tip scribe.
A shallow scratch was made along the silicon ⟨100⟩crystal orientation, followed by
the application of mechanical stress to initiate a controlled fracture. The resulting
chips varied in size depending on the layout of the patterns and experimental
needs.
Manual cleaving offers a simple and efficient way to separate wafers into die­level
samples without requiring a dicing saw. This method is particularly suitable for
research­scale fabrication, where flexibility in sample size and minimal equipment
overhead are important. After cleaving, residual particles were removed using a
nitrogen gun to ensure clean surfaces for subsequent processing.
4.2.3
E­beam Resist Manual Spinning
Resist coating on cleaved chips was performed manually using a Spin coater:
Labspin. The resist was dispensed onto the chip surface, and spinning was man­
ually initiated. This setup allowed resist films to be applied consistently on non­
standard substrates. A soft bake was performed immediately after spinning to
remove residual solvents and improve film adhesion.
This manual approach ensured flexibility in handling multiple samples and mini­
mized resist waste during small­scale fabrication. Despite lacking automation, the
process offered sufficient uniformity for electron beam lithography.
4.2.4
Thermal Evaporator
A thin aluminum layer was deposited on top of the resist using thermal evaporation
to suppress charging during electron beam exposure. The resistively heated alu­
minum source was placed under high vacuum, allowing metal vapor to condense
directly on the sample surface.
This method provides directional deposition and precise thickness control, making
it suitable for thin film applications that require high uniformity. The aluminum layer
was removed during the development step.
4.2.5
Electron Beam Lithography
Electron beam lithography (EBL) was used to define nanoscale patterns for pho­
tonic devices. The process was carried out using the JEOL JBX­9500FSZ system,
which features a field emission gun operating at 100 kV. The system supports sub­
10 nm resolution and sub­nanometer beam positioning accuracy through electro­
magnetic deflection and interferometric stage control.
Resists used included both CSAR (positive tone) and AR­N 7520.17 (negative
tone). The exposure dose and beam conditions were optimized for each resist
type. The conductive aluminum layer minimized charge build­up on insulating
surfaces, ensuring consistent pattern fidelity. Complex patterns were written in a
single pass using digitally controlled beam paths.
After exposure, the resist was developed to reveal the pattern. The solubility of
exposed regions changed due to electron­induced reactions—chain scission in
CSAR and cross­linking in AR­N. The development step simultaneously removed

4.2.6
Development
After exposure, the samples were developed to remove either the exposed or
unexposed regions of resist, depending on whether a positive­ or negative­tone
resist was used. For CSAR resists, the developer ZED­N50 was used, which is
a solvent­based formulation optimized for high­resolution positive­tone imaging.
For AR­N 7520.17, development was performed using AR 300­46, an aqueous
alkaline developer suitable for negative­tone resists.
Following development, the samples were rinsed in deionized water and dried
with nitrogen. The development step also removed the aluminum conductive layer
applied prior to lithography. Clean resist profiles were critical to ensure accurate
pattern transfer during the subsequent dry etch process.
4.2.7
Dry Etching
Pattern transfer into the silicon nitride layer was performed using inductively cou­
pled plasma (ICP) etching in the Pegasus 4 system. The system supports both
Bosch and continuous etching modes; however, continuous mode was used in
this work to avoid scalloped sidewalls and ensure smooth, shallow features suit­
able for photonics.
While the Bosch process alternates etch and passivation steps to achieve high
aspect ratio profiles, continuous ICP etching maintains plasma and ion bombard­
ment throughout. This ensures stable and uniform material removal with good
anisotropy and minimal surface roughness.
In the ICP configuration, high­density plasma is generated by an RF coil, while the
platen RF bias accelerates ions toward the substrate. The ions assist in break­
ing surface bonds, enabling directional etching, while chemically reactive species
such as fluorocarbons react with silicon nitride to form volatile by­products. By
tuning power, pressure, and gas flow, the process was optimized for etch depth,
selectivity, and sidewall quality.
4.2.8
Plasma Ashing for Sample Cleaning
To remove residual resist and organic contamination, plasma ashing was per­
formed using a gas mixture of oxygen and nitrogen. In the system, the O2 and
N2 gases are ionized into a low­temperature plasma, producing reactive species
that break down carbon­based materials into volatile by­products such as CO and
CO2.
The presence of nitrogen in the plasma helps control ion density and energy dis­
tribution, reducing the risk of surface damage while maintaining ashing efficiency.
This cleaning step is essential before dielectric deposition, as it ensures high sur­
face quality and minimizes contamination­related defects in subsequent layers.
4.2.9
Sputter­System Metal­Oxide
TiO2 was deposited using reactive sputtering in a DC sputter system. In this pro­
cess, argon ions bombard a titanium target in an oxygen atmosphere. The ejected
titanium atoms react with oxygen, forming a uniform TiO2 film on the substrate.

Sputtering offers good control over thickness, stoichiometry, and uniformity. The
deposited TiO2 serves as part of a hybrid waveguide core material with a negative
thermo­optic coefficient. Unlike evaporation, sputtering ensures dense films with
strong adhesion and is less sensitive to surface topography.
Process conditions were chosen to minimize stress and enable reproducibility
across samples. The low­temperature deposition process makes it compatible
with previously defined resist and etched features, and the resulting optical prop­

4.2.10
E­beam Evaporator
Electron beam evaporation was used to deposit a thin layer of SiO2. In this pro­
cess, a focused electron beam is directed onto a SiO2 target inside a high­vacuum
chamber. The localized energy input causes the source material to melt and evap­
orate, and the resulting vapor condenses onto the cooler substrate in a line­of­
sight fashion.
E­beam evaporation enables precise thickness control and is particularly suitable
for depositing high­purity dielectric films such as SiO2. Compared to other depo­
sition methods like PECVD, it yields denser films with minimal hydrogen incor­
poration, although step coverage is typically limited. In this project, evaporated
SiO2 was used as an intermediate layer prior to further material deposition, where
optical quality and low contamination were prioritized.
4.2.11
PECVD
The final SiO2 top cladding layer was deposited using PECVD. The technique
introduces precursor gases such as silane and oxygen into a plasma chamber,
where they react to form SiO2 at low substrate temperatures.
In PECVD systems, plasma excitation enhances reaction rates, allowing uniform
film growth at reduced thermal budgets. This makes PECVD suitable for encap­
sulating photonic devices after patterning, without damaging underlying layers.
The deposited oxide provides optical confinement and protection. Additionally,
PECVD allows tuning of refractive index, film stress, and deposition rate through
adjustment of gas ratio, pressure, and RF power. These features were critical
for integrating the oxide with TiO2 and Si3N4 materials, helping maintain structural
integrity and optical mode stability in varying environmental conditions.

Optical Microscope

features.
Various illumination modes such as brightfield, darkfield, and polarized light were

This inspection step helped identify visible defects before proceeding to high­
resolution characterization.

Scanning Electron Microscopy

This thesis presents the design, simulation, and fabrication of an athermal optical
phased array (OPA) system based on a hybrid silicon nitride (Si3N4) and titanium
dioxide (TiO2) waveguide platform. The proposed approach leverages the oppo­
site thermo­optic coefficients (TOCs) of Si3N4 and TiO2 to suppress temperature­
induced phase drift in integrated photonic circuits.
The simulation workflow, carried out using MODE, FDTD, and varFDTD tools,
demonstrated that a 50 nm TiO2 overcladding layer can significantly reduce the
effective TOC while maintaining good optical confinement and low loss. Key com­
ponents such as straight and bent waveguides, Y­branches, and a 64­channel
OPA were modeled, with far­field steering performance predicted.
During fabrication, inverse taper structures with tip widths below 150 nm were
patterned and transferred using electron beam lithography and ICP etching. Al­
though inverse taper survival was not guaranteed across all dies, a sufficient yield
was achieved to enable partial coupling functionality.
However, due to process limitations and misalignment, the fabricated Mach–Zehnder
interferometers could not be optically tested, and thus no experimental verifica­
tion of the thermal phase stability was obtained. Meanwhile, although the OPA
structures were successfully fabricated and coupled, far­field measurements did
not show a clean interference pattern, likely due to phase mismatch or residual
fabrication errors.
Despite these challenges, the fabrication of a functioning inverse taper and com­
plete OPA layout confirms the feasibility of the proposed platform. The results
lay the groundwork for further process refinement and full integration of athermal,
chip­scale beam steering systems.
6.2
Future Work
While the core design and fabrication of the hybrid Si3N4–TiO2 platform were suc­
cessfully completed in this project, several key components remain to be opti­
mized. The Mach–Zehnder interferometer (MZI) intended for thermal character­
ization was not realized due to a combination of limited cleanroom access and
fabrication uncertainty. In future work, repeating the fabrication multiple times un­
der more stable process conditions would be necessary to obtain functional MZI
structures and perform temperature­dependent validation of the athermal behav­
ior.
The inverse taper structures, although successfully patterned, exhibited limited
survival rates due to their mechanical fragility during development and dicing. If
loss performance is not the immediate bottleneck, a practical strategy would be

to slightly widen the taper tip to enhance structural robustness without severely

For the optical phased array (OPA), although the entire layout was fabricated and
tested, no clear far­field interference pattern was observed. This suggests that
significant phase mismatch may exist across channels, possibly due to design
errors, etch nonuniformity, or unintentional excitation of higher­order modes if the
single­mode condition was not satisfied. In addition, the existing design did not
fully isolate the input taper region from the input light, making it difficult to ensure
that the observed far­field pattern arises solely from the OPA itself. A revised
GDS layout, with an optimized input taper region and well­separated propagation
length, could help ensure that the injected light couples properly into the guided
