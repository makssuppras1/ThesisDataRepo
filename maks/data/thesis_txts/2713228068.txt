2 Abstract  
 
Every year, 5.4 million people suffer from snakebites, of which 137,000 die and three times as many 
are left with morbidities. Snakebite is a disease of poverty, mainly affecting people living in rural 
tropical areas. In India alone, and the country focused on in this report, it is estimated that 58,000 
people die annually from snakebite. Current treatment is based on the immunisation of animals with 
increasing doses of venom. The animal’s immune system will produce polyclonal antibodies towards 
the given target venom, which is used in antivenom. While this method has saved many lives and 
limbs, it is very costly and often inaccessible to those most in need, and it comes with several adverse 
reactions due to its heterologous origin, among others. There is therefore currently a significant push 
towards not just improving current antivenom, but reinventing them entirely, e.g., as recombinant 
monoclonal binders. These binders can be alternative binding scaffolds such as designed ankyrin 
repeat proteins (DARPins), which when targeted against medically relevant venom toxins can yield 
specific binders that can be manufacturable via reproducible expression systems. Furthermore, their 
discovery can via cross-panning be directed towards discovering binders that can neutralise whole 
clusters of similar yet different toxins within a toxin family and across different species of snake, 
thereby reducing the number of binders required, which lowers the cost of a final antivenom.  
 
Phage and yeast surface display were used in this report to discover cross-reactive DARPin binders 
against cytotoxin (CTx) derived from the two Indian snakes, Naja naja and Naja kaouthia. Three 
rounds of phage display selection were performed using biotinylated CTx as antigen target. The first 
two selection rounds showed the presence and enrichment of specific binders; however, in the third 
selection, low enrichment was observed. In another strategy, pre-eluted phages were quantified with 
qPCR and showed a 14-fold enrichment over the negative selection and possibly signs of cross-
reactivity. However, data were difficult to interpret due to the high signal in the negative control. The 
DARPin binder sequences were subcloned into yeast, creating a yeast surface display library. 
However, the yeast library showed complications with induction. It was hypothesized that the 
DARPin library as a N-terminus fusion to the a-agglutinin matin protein could have affected the 
results. The use of the DARPin library is currently being investigated as a C-terminus fusion in the 
Tropical Pharmacology Lab.  
 
If possible, to overcome the complications seen in the phage and yeast surface display techniques in 
this report, discovered DARPin binders can contribute a cost-competitive and effective therapeutic 
solution to snakebite in India and set a promising precedent for the global treatment of snakebite 
envenomations.

3 Introduction 
 
3.1 Snakes and current situation in India 
Every year, 5.4 million people suffer from snakebites, resulting in 1.8 to 2.7 million cases of 
envenoming. Of these, 137,000 people die and three times as many are left with amputations and 
other permanent disabilities such as severe paralysis potentially prevent breathing, bleeding disorders, 
and irreversible kidney failure. Despite the large number of incidents and deaths, the medical needs 
related to snakebites have been neglected for many years. Snakebite as a category was removed from 
the World Health Organization (WHO) list of “Neglected Tropical Diseases” back in 2013 but 
returned to on the list in 2017. It is a disease of poverty, mainly affecting people living in rural tropical 
areas and in countries where the healthcare system is weak and medical resources are sparse. Most 
snakebites occur in Africa, Latin America, and Asia (see Figure 1). Asia alone accounts for 2 million 
(70%) of these global envenoming cases, and a single country and the country focused on in this 
report, namely India, accounts for half of the global deaths due to snakebites, with an estimated 
58,000 annual fatalities.  
 
 
 
Figure 1 showing the geographical distribution and the rough approximation of snakebite envenomings and deaths. The 
highest incidences occurs in Africa, Latin America, and Asia with India accounting for 70% og the envenomings in Asia. 
Map modified from  [1]. 
In India, 90% of snakebites are caused by the “big four” snakes (see Figure 2 A-D), which includes 
the Russell’s viper (Daboia russelii), Indian cobra (Naja naja), Saw-scaled viper (Echis carinatus), 
and Common krait (Bungarus caeruleus) [2][3]. Besides these four, the WHO has additionally added 
the Monocled cobra (Naja kaouthia)(see Figure 2 E) to their list of “Category 1: Highest medical 
importance”, which is their list of highly venomous snakes that are widespread or common and which 
cause numerous snakebites, resulting in high levels of disability, morbidity, or mortality [4].

In contrast to many other serious health conditions, functional treatment does exist for snakebites. 
Many of the serious consequences of snakebite are entirely preventable if only the right, safe, and 
effective antivenom were more widely available and accessible. At present, few countries have the 
capacity to actually produce snake antivenom of adequate quality, however, as it shall be stated later, 
these antivenom does not reflect the geographical variation in venom that occurs of some widespread 
species. With additional lack of capacity for the control of these antivenoms in rural countries, an 
inability to assess the quality antivenom occurs. Weak regulation is based on poor data on the number 
and types of snakebites occurring in India and have led to difficulties in estimating the actual needs. 
Poor quality antivenom has led to a loss of confidence in some of the available antivenoms by health 
managers, clinicians, and patients. Given the (misleading) low demand, several manufacturers have 
ceased production and the price of antivenom products has increased dramatically in the last 20 years, 
making treatment unaffordable for the vast majority of those most in need [7].

3.2 Snake families and venom  
 
Snakebite envenoming results from the injection of a highly concentrated toxin secretion – venom – 
by a venomous snake into humans. The composition of snake venom is a mixture of toxins which 
vary extensively within and between snake species. Venom is intrinsically ecological and is largely 
affected and evolved with the physiology of their prey animals and, to a certain extent, the predators 
it is positioned against. Variation also exists between age, gender, and size classes, season, and 
geographical region. Broad differences can therefore be found in the venom composition between 
genera within each snake family and between species within each genus [1]. As seen in Figure 3, the 
venom from the same cobra species (Naja kaouthia) but from two different parts of India can vary 
significantly in its venom composition. While India does host several antivenom manufacturers that 
produce monovalent (targeting one species) and polyvalent (targeting several species) antivenoms 
against the big four, the substantial geographical venom composition variation mentioned renders 
them largely ineffective against snakebites in regions different from those snakes used for 
immunisation. In relation to Figure 3, there is hence a chance that antivenom for the Naja kaouthia 
from region A will not be effective against the venom injected from another Naja kaouthia from 
region B [8].

There exist around 3,000 snake species in the world, of which 600 are venomous. These snakes fall 
into especially into two main families of snakes which are venomous to humans: The Viperidae (e.g., 
Echis carinatus) and the Elapidae (e.g., Naja naja) [9]. The pharmacological effects of snake venom 
can be classified into three main types: Hemotoxic, cytotoxic, and neurotoxins. The four major toxins 
involved in these three main groups are phospholipase A2 (PLA2s), snake venom-metalloproteinases 
and -serine proteases (SVMP and SVSP, respectively), and three finger toxins (3FTx). These toxins 
can either act alone or in combination with each other to cause multiple pharmacological effects in 
snakebite victims [10].  
 
The Viperidae family includes vipers and rattlesnakes, which have large and hinged front fangs that 
permit deep penetration and whose venom is mostly hemotoxic and/or myotoxic.  
The Elapidae family includes coral snakes and cobras, which have fixed front fangs and mostly 
neurotoxic venom [11]. Elapids mainly possess systemically acting neurotoxic venoms made up of 
3FTx inducing neurotoxic manifestations such as neuromuscular paralysis for the immobilising prey 
and defence. However, this paralysis is sometimes reversible if treated with acetylcholinesterase 
inhibitors. The Viperidae typically produce venoms containing predominantly enzymatic and locally 
acting toxins, such as SVMP, SVSP, as well as PLA2. Their venom is associated with bleeding from 
the bite site, blistering, immediate radiating pain, superficial soft tissue and muscle necrosis, 
coagulopathies, and hypovolemic shock (the heart unable to pump enough blood around in the body) 
[1]. 
 
3.3 Snake toxins 
Snake venom is a highly diverse and complex mixture of toxins, and differ in their size and 
conformation, and they exert a wide range of different effects. In the following section, the four main 
types of toxins, PLA2, SVMP, SVSP, and 3FTx, found in highest abundance will be explained. 
 
3.3.1 Snake venom metalloproteinases 
Snake Venom MetalloProteinases (SVMPs) are zinc-dependent proteinases ranging from 20 to 110 
kDa in size and exerts proteolytical processes. SVMPs contributes greatly to the hemorrhagic activity 
via destruction of the basement membrane components, allowing blood to escape, and coagulopathic 
venom activities via direct activation or cleavage of blood clotting factors [10]. For that same reason, 
SVMPs may have application in treating human conditions that involves abnormal blood clot 
formation [12].  
 
3.3.2 Snake venom serine proteinases 
Snake Venom Serine Proteinases (SVSPs) display molecular masses ranging from 26 to 67 kDa. 
SVSPs have the ability to rupture capillary vessels, which leads to systemic bleeding, which may 
progress to cardiovascular shock. However, their primary toxicity is to alter the hemostatic system of 
their prey/victim, causing edema and extreme pain. Hemotoxic effects include perturbations of 
platelet aggregation and blood pressure and blood coagulation [1], [10].

3.3.3 Phospholipases A2s 
Phospholipases A2s (PLA2s (phosphatidylcholine 2-acylhydrolase) have neurotoxicity and 
myotoxicity effects. The enzymes have a molecular mass of 13-15 kDa and are found both in Elapidae 
(PLA2 group I) and Viperidae (PLA2 group II). Both groups of PLA2s (I and II) share similar 
enzymatic properties and exert strong myotoxic effects, leading to severe necrosis and promotion of 
inflammation in the form of edema, cytokine production, pain, and intensifying hemorrhage by 
inhibiting coagulation [10].  
PLA2 toxins causes local myotoxic effects by promoting Ca+2-dependent hydrolysis of the 2-acyl 
ester bond in glycerophospholipids. Another activity is the presynaptic-acting neurotoxin 
phospholipase, where they causes systemic neurotoxic effects by binding pre-synaptic nerve cells, 
blocking the neuromuscular transmission which leads to acute muscle damage and in some cases 
paralysis [12].  
 
3.3.4 Three-finger toxins 
The three finger toxin (3FTx) family are non-enzymatic polypeptides which contain a 60-74 amino 
acid residue three-finger fold structure (see Figure 4 green) 
that is stabilised by four to five disulphide bridges, with 
four of them being conserved (see Figure 4 yellow). The 
characteristic feature of 3FTx is this distinct protein fold 
where three -stranded loops (resembling the three 
outstretched fingers on the hand) are extended from a 
small, globular, hydrophobic core, cross-linked by the four 
conserved disulphide bridges [13].  
The 3FTx differ in length, giving the isoforms of cytotoxins 
and -neurotoxin (referred to as short-chain (60-62 amino 
acid residues) and long-chain neurotoxins (66-74 amino 
acid residues)). The -neurotoxin exerts neurotoxic effects 
by binding post-synaptically at the neuromuscular 
junctions, interacting with ion channel receptors, including 
the muscarinic receptors, nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, 
and sodium channels, causing neuromuscular paralysis and 
respiratory failure in the snakebite victims [10], [13].

3.3.4.1 Cytotoxins 
Cytotoxins (CTx) belong to the 3FTx family and is approximately 60 amino acid residues (or 7 kDa) 
and exerts cytotoxic effects. These CTxs constitute as much as 40-70% of cobra (Naja) venom and 
function by killing cells by non-selectively disrupting cell membranes via pore-formation and are 
thus often associated with potent necrotic activity. It is believed that it is the hydrophobic core of CTx 
that represents the principal membrane-binding motif, and when bound, CTxs produce structural 
defects in the lipid bilayers, causing the formation of pores. CTxs exhibits activity on numerous cell 
types, including lymphocytes, erythrocytes, spleen cells, cardiac myocytes, and various tumour cells. 
The pathological consequence depends on the cell membrane proteins and the phospholipids found 
on the outer leaflet of the cell membrane of these types of cells.  
Some cytotoxins are known as cardiotoxins, which depolarize cardiac muscle cells. This involves the 
opening of voltage dependent Ca2+ channels, causing an influx of Ca2+ which leads to muscle 
contraction and eventually irregular heartbeats or the heart stopping [12].  
3.3.5 CTx as the key medically relevant toxin target.  
 
As mentioned above, snake venom is composed of a mix of biologically active proteins and 
polypeptides. So, how should one know what component to target when designing antivenom? At 
least two approaches are suggested to look at when identifying key toxin targets: The evolution of 
snake venom and/or by a toxicity score.  
 
Evolution is assisted by natural selection, which generates improved biologically functional protein 
molecules. Evolution thus favours the survival of snakes in their different environments. 
Understanding the evolutionary processes and ecological constraints that shaped snake venom to its 
current variability requires identifying the temporal and spatial variability distribution in venom 
composition between and within species. Therefore, identifying venomous snakes’ adaption to their 
natural ecosystems may aid in the identification of toxins that most likely must be neutralized to 
reverse the effects of venom, hence guiding the rational development of antivenom therapeutics [7]. 
As mentioned above in section 3.3.4.1 Cytotoxins, 40-70% of cobra venom consists of CTxs, These 
are hence very abundant and thus an important component that could be medically relevant to the 
target in an antivenom.  
 
The toxicity score is the other method that can be used to evaluate the relevance of toxins in whole 
venom. The score is calculated by dividing the relative toxin abundance in percentage in the venom 
by the lethal dose (LD50) value for the toxin in mice. The score is proposed to be a better parameter 
for ranking the medical relevance of a toxin compared to LD50 alone [15]. In an article by A. Laustsen 
et al. [16], 3FTxs (neurotoxins and CTxs) and PLA2s were found as the most abundant venom 
components in venom from Naja kaothia (77.5% and 13.5%, respectively), with 3FTx as the most 
medically relevant component. The most relevant lethal components in terms of toxicity score were 
α-neurotoxins, followed by CTxs [16]. A toxicity score for CTx was calculated in Naja kouthia 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
venom with a LD50 value of 1.41 µg/g and a toxin abundance in the venom of 19.2%, giving a score 
of 14 g/µg. The threshold value proposed for significant toxicity was set at 5 g/µg, and CTx was 
therefore evaluated to be significantly toxic. In comparison, the score for PLA2 was low due to its 
high LD50 values of 1.75 µg/g (hence less lethal) and low abundance in the venom, 4.6%, giving a 
score of <5 g/µg [17].  
 
As indicating, CTx is therefore a promising key medically relevant toxin that could be targeted by an 
antivenom and will be the main toxin focused on in this report.  
 
3.4 Current antivenom 
Currently, the only available antivenom treatment for snakebite envenomations is blood products 
consisting of animal-derived polyclonal antibodies. The serum therapy method dates back to 1894 
and was originally used to treat bacterial infections (tetanus and diphtheria), and paved the way for 
the introduction of antivenom therapy for envenomings caused by venomous animals, like snakes 
[18]. The production involves the immunisation of animals, like horses, with increasing amounts of 
the target venom(s) (see Figure 5). The horse’s immune system will then, over the course of a year, 
produce polyclonal antibodies towards the given target venom(s). These antibodies are isolated from 
the immunised horse plasma and used in antivenom [19]. While this method has saved many lives 
and limbs, it is very costly and the heterologous nature of the current antivenom can lead to high 
immunogenicity and thus pose a significant risk of adverse reactions in patients. 
 
 
 
Figure 5 Overview of the current traditional manufacturing process of antivenom. The process begins with 1) milking the 
snakes in order to obtain their venom. 2) This venom is then used to immunise larger animals like a horse over a longer 
period of time. 3) Blood is then drawn from the horse and 4) plasma and red blood cells are separated and 4) antibodies 
are isolated from the plasma. 5) Once the antibodies are purified and formulated, they are used for antivenom [19]. 
Figure made in BioRender. 
 
Of these animal-derived antibodies, it is estimated that only a fraction of them in current antivenoms 
have therapeutic value, as most of the isolated antibodies from the animal plasma are directed against 
antigens that are either unrelated to snake venom (e.g., towards viruses or bacteria) or related to 
components in the venom with negligible contribution to the venom toxicity. With low concentrations 
of binding and neutralising antibodies, high volumes of antivenom are needed to treat a snakebite, 
which increases the risk of side effects for the patient [20]. Additionally, due to the animal origin and 
varying immune responses, batch-to-batch variation occurs, which further complicates the 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
concentration of antibodies. These high antibody requirements necessitate the ongoing use of animals, 
like horses, which is both costly and ethically questionable [21]. 
 
Another problem is that the manufacturers producing antivenom use commercial snake venoms that, 
as mentioned, do not reflect the geographical variation that occurs in the venoms of some species [7]. 
As a consequence, the specificity and efficacy of these antibodies will inherently be linked to those 
venoms used for immunisation and toxin variations and will result in a reduced recognition and 
neutralisation of toxins from different venoms [21]. 
 
3.5 Future antivenom and cross-reactivity 
There is currently a significant push towards not just improving current antivenoms but re-inventing 
them entirely. As an alternative to animal-derived antivenoms, this could, i.e., be as therapeutics 
based on recombinant monoclonal binders that neutralise medically relevant toxins in snake venom. 
These binders could be human antibodies, or alternative binding scaffolds, such as nanobodies or 
designed ankyrin repeat proteins (DARPins). All of these formats share the benefit of being 
discoverable via high-throughput display technologies and being manufacturable via reproducible 
expression systems (see Figure 6). This way, most of the drawback of the current, traditional 
antivenom can be avoided. First of all, the antibodies or alternative binders can be fully human, 
mimicking the antibodies produced in the human body after being exposed to a foreign particle. The 
human origin will make the treatment less immunogenic and hence safer for human patients. 
Moreover, recombinant antivenom will consist of antibodies only with therapeutic value, thus 
avoiding the problem with non-therapeutic and unrelated antibodies in animal-derived antivenom, 
which poses a big health risk to snakebite patients. With the reproducible expression system, this will 
also solve the problems of low specific antibody concentration, the batch-to-batch variation as seen 
in animal-derived antivenom, and the cost will be reduced significantly, aiding the problem of 
unaffordable antivenom.  
The concept of recombinant antibodies has been demonstrated to be effective in 2018 by Laustsen et 
al., who, with an antivenom consisting of an oligoclonal mixture of recombinant human IgG 
antibodies, were able to, experimentally, neutralise dendrotoxin-mediated neurotoxicity from African 
black mamba venom in vivo [20].

The cost of animal-derived antivenom is a crucial factor to overcome as the patient-group mostly 
comes from low-income countries. In an article from 2020, it has been estimated by Jenkins & 
Laustsen [22] that a recombinant antivenom from monoclonal antibodies can be manufacturable at a 
cost-competitive or lower cost compared to current animal-derived antivenom. It is estimated that 
animal-derived antivenom costs USD 13-1,120 per treatment, whereas a monovalent recombinant 
antivenom treatment can be manufactured for USD 20-225 per treatment. Hence, almost 80% lower 
costs for the highest prices and potentially lower cost for manufacturing if using alternative protein 
scaffolds [22]. 
 
3.5.1 Cross-reactivity 
 
Antivenom against a specific snake species can be effective if the victim actually happened to see the 
snake biting. However, even if the snake was identified in every snakebite case, it would be an 
incredibly large task to discover molecular binders for each and every snake, taking both the inter- 
and intraspecific variations into account mentioned in section 3.2, which results in even more 
molecular binders needed. The cost for both the discovery, formulation, and manufacturing would 
likewise be expensive, not solving the aspect of expensive antivenom.  
 
Though binders are typically designed to bind a specific target and avoid unspecific binding, in some 
circumstances it can actually be an advantage to bind homologs or heterologous toxins [23]. With 
recombinant antivenom, it is possible to direct the discovery towards broadly neutralising binders 
that can neutralise whole clusters of similar yet different toxins within a toxin family and across 
different species of snake. This can be achieved via cross-panning (or consensus toxins) and results 
in fewer binders required in a final antivenom. This way, the binder will have a known specific 
therapeutic value for neutralising one or more toxins, which reduces the amount of binders needed to 
treat snakebites and hence the cost of the antivenom. But largely important too, if the snakebite victim 
is uncertain of which snake it was that bit [24]. As the big four snake species are among the snakes 
that cause the most envenomings in India, a cross-reactive binder towards a toxin from these snakes 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
could ensure high protection in one antivenom for victims unsure of the snake biting, as the chance 
of being bitten by one of these big four snakes is large.  
It should be noted that cross-reactivity does not necessarily mean that there is cross-protection in the 
form of neutralisation. This has to be investigated in vivo [25].  
 
3.6 Binder formats 
 
Different molecular binders can be used for the discovery of binders and for the purpose of cross-
reactivity in general. Antibodies have for long been a valuable molecular binder in therapy due to 
their high affinity and specificity. Due to their larger size, they have a long half-life (21-28 days) and 
likewise to their binding to the neonatal Fc receptor, which recycles the antibodies. Antibodies hence 
have the potential to neutralise systemically toxins effectively for many days. However, antibodies 
are also limited in several aspects, including their high manufacturing cost and large molecular size, 
giving them a low volume of distribution. Alternative scaffold exits and could hence enable entirely 
new efficacies compared to antibodies, with each having their own advantages and disadvantages, 
which would need to be evaluated for the specific purpose of binding. Such alternatives are, e.g., 
fragment antigen-binding (Fab) formats (the antibody binding region) without the Fc region from 
whole antibodies, which can obtain a smaller molecular size and thus a larger distribution volume, a 
shorter half-life (0.5-30 hours) without recycling, and faster tissue penetration. These can therefore 
be used for both systemically acting and locally acting toxins. Otherwise, nanobodies derived from 
Camelidae consist of only the heavy chains of antibodies yet still possess full antibody functions with 
high affinity and specificity and a high penetration rate into deep tissue. More recently, another 
alternative has been designed through rationally designed derivatives of other human proteins, 
namely DARPins [26]. 
 
3.6.1 DARPins 
 
The ankyrins repeat (AR) protein comes from a protein family with high variability-binding and high 
specificity-binding characteristics and has led to the development of the artificial recombinant-
binding protein: Designed ankyrin repeat proteins (DARPins). Natural AR proteins are among the 
most abundant binding proteins in the human genome and, when bound to a target, differ in functions 
varying from enzyme inhibition to simple anchoring of protein-protein interactions.  
Like natural repeat proteins, DARPins have been designed to feature a protein architecture consisting 
of a varying number of structural motifs (repeats). Each repeat consists of 33 amino acids and has 
been engineered by a consensus design approach, many of which were from the human genome. The 
hypothesis used was that those residues important for folding will be conserved, hence showing 
prominently in the alignment, whereas the residues involved in protein interactions will not be 
conserved. Seven of these amino acids residues is not conserved and are thus variable. A DARPin 
library consists of two caps: A N-cap repeat, and a C-cap repeat, and a varying number of library 
repeat domains (see Figure 7). Since each library repeat domain consists of 33 amino acids, where 
seven of these residues are variable, this creates a diversity of >107. If combined by two to four library 
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
repeat domains, it reaches a diversity above >1014 [27]. A DARPin library is hence generated by 
assembling an N- and C- cap to varying numbers (n) of edited domain repeats (NxC) (see Figure 8).  
The protein-biding residues in DARPins are the seven varying amino acids. These form a ß-turn, 
followed by two antiparallel α-helices (see Figure 8). The variation in DARPin libraries thus lies in 
these residues in the ß-turns.  
 
Besides the large diversity of the library, the consensus design has yielded idealised DARPins with 
improved properties, such as high stability and solubility against heat-induced or denaturant-induced 
unfolding, and very high expression levels in, e.g., Escherichia coli (E. coli). Here, expression yield 
has reached 200 mg/l of purified protein in simple shake flask culture (15 g/L with fermentation). 
Opposite of antibodies, which need disulphide bonds in their quaternary structure and post-
transcriptionally glycosylation, DARPins can additionally be efficiently produced in prokaryotic 
cells, which eases the production. The stability of DARPins allows for large-scale purification 
protocols, providing rapid and low-cost purification and production but also for applications not just 
intravenously but also orally, through inhalation or nasal administration. The thermal stability of 
DARPins is very high, usually with midpoints of denaturation varying between 66 and 95°C (the 
more repeats, the more stable).

The size of a DARPin is usually between 14 to 21 kDa (one module is just above 3.5 kDa), about one 
tenth the size of an IgG antibody (150 kDa). In regard to the small size of DARPins, they have 
considerably improved tissue penetration and can block, e.g., toxins closer to their sites of action, 
leading to a more potent inhibition compared to antibodies, which due to their larger size, are known 
to have relatively poor tissue penetration and act mainly in the circulation. As CTx are locally acting 
toxins, this is a highly appreciated and highly suitable feature of DARPins.  
 
DARPins-ligand complexes are typically cleared by the kidneys, and another great advantage of 
DARPins lies in their ability to clear unwanted molecules, such as small toxins, factors involved in 
blood clotting, or CTxs, from tissue or the circulation, fast. Whereas antibodies have a longer serum 
half-life which can potentially lead to accumulation of ligand-antibody complexes in the serum. 
However, if DARPins are cleared too fast, this can be regulated by PEGylation, allowing longer 
biological effects [29], [27], [30].  
 
DARPins have already shown promising results in therapeutic fields, including Abicipar pegol 
targeting vascular endothelial growth factor for macular degeneration, and recently, in February 2022, 
the first DARPin-based treatment was approved for treating COVID-19 infection. The multivalent

DARPin, Ensovibep, from Novartis and Molecular Partners, contains three covalently attached 
domains that bind to the receptor-binding domain of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein. Their data 
showed a reduced risk of hospitalization by 78% and lower viral loads after eight days after a single 
infusion than those receiving placebo [29].  
 
In order to evaluate if a DARPin library can actually bind CTxs toxins from whole venom, the whole 
venom extracted from a snake(s) needs to be fractionated and separated into its different components. 
For this purpose, RP-HPLC can be used.  
 
3.7 RP-HPLC 
Reversed phase (RP) high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is a method used to separate, 
identify, and concentrate molecules in a mixture based on their hydrophobicity. With the use of 
column chromatography, the components can be separated, followed by analysis by a computer.  
 
Initially, a polar, mobile phase containing solute is passed through a non-polar, stationary phase, e.g., 
a C18 column. Here, the analytes will partition between the mobile and stationary phases. The more 
hydrophobic the analytes are, the more they will be retained in the stationary phase. Opposite, the 
more polar the analytes are, the more they will be retained in the mobile phase. The retention of an 
analyte is adjusted by the composition of the mobile phase. With a high content of organic solvent, 
like acetonitrile, the mobile phase will reduce retention compared to with a high water content, where 
the mobile phase will increase retention [26].  
With the use of a gradient elution in RP-HPLC, a dynamic amount of organic solvent is used to 
achieve a steady increase over time. This way, analytes are eluted according to their increasing 
molecular hydrophobicity over a period of time shown. A series of peaks is drawn on a time axis. 
Each peak represents a different compound detected, and the area of the peak is proportional to the 
amount of the particular compound  [31]. Ones one analyte has been fractionated the desired fraction 
can be biotinylated – an important step when using phage display (see later in Section 3.9 Display 
methods).  
 
3.8 Biotinylation and MALDI TOF mass spectrometry 
The toxin used for antigen presentation in phage display (see later in Section 1.9) is managed through 
biotinylation and biotin’s binding with streptavidin. A biotin molecule is covalently conjugated to the 
toxin via a chemical linker. The ratio of biotin to toxins is crucial but a challenging task. If a toxin is 
over-biotinylated, the toxin will decrease its binding surface available and thereby prevent phages 
from binding. As shown in Section 3.9, the use of biotinylated toxin allows for the use of 
paramagnetic streptavidin-coated microbeads, which capture biotinylated antigens which provides 
better selection condition.  
 
The biotinylation reaction can be monitored by Matrix Assisted Laser Desorption/ Ionization Time 
of Flight Mass Spectrometry (MALDI TOF MS) - a method which can be used to determine the 
molecular mass of a compound in a mixture of molecules. Compounds are converted into charged 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
ions by soft ionization by a laser beam. The protonated ions are then accelerated at a fixed potential, 
and the ions separate from each other on the basis of their mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio. During time of 
flight (TOF) analysis, the m/z ratio of the ion is measured by determining the time required for it to 
travel the length of the flight tube before hitting a detector. Based on the information, a mass 
“fingerprint” is generated for the analyte.  
By knowing the mass of the biotin molecule, 473.15 Da, it is possible to measure the ratio of 
biotinylation by determining the difference in weight of un-biotinylated and biotinylated compounds 
by looking at the difference in masses obtained in the mass fingerprints [32].  
 
3.9 Display method 
Over the past decade, different display methods have been developed to screen libraries against ligand 
targets expressed on cell surfaces. Such a display method includes phage display, which will be used 
to discover DARPins’ ability to bind and neutralise CTxs from the big four snakes in a cross-reactive 
manner [33].  
 
3.9.1 Phage display and the M13 Bacteriophage 
The phage display technology essentially imitates the immune system in a test tube. The technology 
uses bacteriophages (phages) to display binders like antibodies or DARPins on their surface which, 
can then, when exposed to antigens, be analysed for their binding abilities. Central to the technology 
are the properties of the filamentous M13 bacteriophage. The M13 phage only infects Escherichia 
coli (E. coli) strains that express F pilus. This F pilus is used by the M13 phage for its attachment to 
E. coli. The M13 phage consists of nine genes (G) encoding 11 different proteins: Five coat proteins 
(see Figure 9) and six proteins involved in replication. It establishes a chronic infection in the host 
cell, which creates a continuous release of new M13 phages. Once the M13 phage infects E. coli, it 
does so via the attachment of its G3P coat protein to the tip of the mentioned F pilus on the surface 
of E. coli. Here, disassembly of the pilus will bring the M13 phage closer to the surface of E. coli 
where it will translocate its single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) into the bacterium. Next, replication of 
the M13 DNA takes place, followed by assembly and budding of the M13 phage, resulting in the 
release of new phages through a pore in the E. coli cell membrane.

It is possible to incorporate foreign DNA into the M13 phage such that genes encoding, e.g., DARPins

recognizing phages. By cloning large numbers of genes encoding the binder of interest, large phage 
display libraries can hence be created from which diverse binders can be selected.  
 
3.9.2 Phagemid and helper phages 
 
The genome of each phage normally encodes three to five copies of the G3P coat protein, resulting 
in a multivalent display of binder-G3P fusion proteins. For a monovalent display, an alternative so-
called “phagemid” vector can be used, consisting of a smaller minimal plasmid.  
The minimal plasmid contains three key elements:  
- 
An antibiotic selection marker (chloramphenicol) 
- 
The gene encoding the binder-G3P fusion protein  
- 
The phage origin of replication 
The E. coli harbouring a phagemid is, in order to produce the functional phage particle displaying the 
binder-G3P fusion, infected with a so-called “helper phage” (see Figure 10). This helper phage 
contains the nine genes of the phage genome needed for the production of capsid, assembly, 
replication, and budding.  
The E. coli is now ready to produce the phages. The helper phage is constructed in such a way that it 
contains a low copy number (“defective phage origin”) of the origin of replication and will hence be 
outcompeted by the phagemid’s origin of replication, resulting in phages consisting mostly of the 
phagemid DNA. Additionally, the phagemid encoding the binder-G3P fusion also competes with the 
wild-type G3P from the helper phage for incorporation into the phage. 90% of the produced phages 
will not display the binder protein, hence being “bald” phages, and the 10% that does will display a 
single copy of the binder protein. The number of binding proteins present depends on the induction 
level. A Lac promotor and Isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) is used for the controlling 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
of the binder-G3P fusion. These bald phages can be rendered non-infective in certain libraries by 
engineering the G3P (gene III) within the helper phage to be sensitive to trypsin. Trypsin can cleave 
the G3P and make the phage non-infection [34]. The purpose of having monovalent binding is to 
avoid having significant avidity effects, which are affected by the number of binding sites, and 
potentially low-affinity binders. The phagemid system reduces the avidity effects with a monovalent 
display and hence allows for stricter affinity selection [35].

3.10 Yeast display technology 
 
Another high-throughput molecular display method which can be used for the detection of specific 
binders from a library to a target is yeast surface display (YSD). YSD is a cell surface display 
technology, that uses yeast as the expression system and in where each individual yeast cell is 
transformed with a single vector which encodes a protein variant of interest genetically fused to a 
cell-surface anchor protein. The most common type of yeast used for YSD is Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae strain EBY100. Different cell surface anchor proteins can be used. However, the most 
common YSD systems fuse the binder library to the C-terminus of the α-agglutinin protein (Aga2P) 
subunit. However, the fusion of binder libraries can be fused to either the C- or the N-terminus of the 
cell surface anchor protein. Open translation and expression of the binder-Ag2p fusion construct. 
Aga2p adheres to the Aga1p protein by two disulphide bonds (see Figure 12). Aga1p is a ß 1,6-
glucan-anchored domain anchored in the cell membrane and stably found in the yeast chromosome. 
The induction of expression occurs through a GAL promotor. Opposite of phage display, which 
displays a monovalent display, YSD expression results in a display of up to 100,000 copies of 
DARPins on the surface of a single yeast cell. 
With the display of up to 100,000 copies of DARPins on the yeast cell surface, it is favourable to 
have a normalization tag implemented in the construct [39], [40]. For such purpose, the SpyTag and 
SpyCatcher system can be used. SpyTag can be fused to the binder-Ag2p construct and when 
expressed, the SpyCatcher fused to a fluorophore, e.g., mKate, can bind the SpyTag and asses the 
expression level when analysed via FACS (see below) (see Figure 12). On the other hand, to assess 
binding of the binder to the target, the target itself can be fused with another colour than the one used 
on the SpyCatcher, e.g. a green fluorescent protein (GFP), hence providing a two-coloured system 
[41].

3.10.1 FACS 
 
Fluorescence-activated cell sorting, FACS, is a technique which uses flow cytometry to purify cells 
based on their phenotype. As mentioned above, the cells presenting different binders on their surface 
can be targeted with specific fluorescence-conjugated targets, such as GFP-toxins, and an expression 
normalising binder with another fluorescence marker, e.g., mKate. When binding, the flow 
cytometry/ sorter is capable to excite and detect the light of the different fluorescent dyes, which is 
used to represent the different surface molecule markers at a single cell level (see Figure 13). When 
passing through the FACS detector, cells with the same phenotype are directed towards designated 
collecting tubes. FACS has the capacity to separate more than one population simultaneously, saving 
a lot of time but also reagents [42]. With a gating strategy, it is possible to analyse the cells of interest 
based on their affinity, cell size, and complexity. The light scatter is measured by two optical 
detectors: Forward scatter (FSC) and side scatter (SSC). FSC is measured along the path of the laser 
and allows for the discrimination of cells by size. SSC measures ninety-degrees angle relative to the 
laser and provides information about the granularity of a cell. When measured in combination (FCS 
vs SSC), the measurement allows for cellular differentiation. FACS can also identify single cells,

3.11 Yeast display vs Phage display 
 
YSD has collective advantages which includes its eukaryotic expression system capable of 
incorporating post-translational modifications such as the formation of disulphide bonds and quality 
control of protein folding. It offers low time and technical requirements modifications relative to 
other eukaryotic display system. It can, as mentioned, be included with epitope tags, which can allow 
for normalization of protein function to surface expression, and hence, the identification of proteins 
that are expressed at high levels as well as binding with varying/ high affinity to a target protein 
Lastly, it is compatible to the use of FACS, allowing quantitative measurements of equilibrium 
binding constants, stability, dissociation kinetics, cross-reactivity using colour systems, and 
analyzation directly without the need for subcloning, soluble expression, and purification [39], [40].  
 
Phage display take advantage of panning-based methods to screen libraries as large as 1012-1014 
variants, whereas YSD due to limitations in yeast transformation efficiency and screening technology, 
offer lower throughput currently of library sizes screened by FACS of 108-109 yeast cells (50,000 
cells per second). Hence if screening larger libraries larger than 108 variants in YSD, it is necessary 
to use other screening methods, like bead-based magnetic-activated cells sorting as seen in phage 
display, at first to reduce the library diversity before screening with FACS and hence increase 
throughput [39].

3.12 Aim of studies 
 
Current snake antivenoms derived from immunised animals comes with many drawbacks including 
high cost, high risk of immunogenicity due to its heterologous nature, batch to batch variation, and 
low therapeutic content. Therefore, the aim of this study is to discover high affinity and cross-reactive 
designed ankyrin repeat proteins (DARPins) binders against the medically relevant toxins, cytotoxins, 
from the Indian Naja naja and Naja kaouthia, two of the most frequently biting snakes in India. For 
this purpose, phage display and yeast surface display will be used. The prospect of this discovery is 
to use these binders in a recombinant antivenom against the Indian Naja naja and Naja kaouthia, a 
treatment that will be more efficient, more specific, less immunogenic, and most importantly, more 
cost-effective, providing help for those most in need.

5.3 Biotinylation 
In order to present antigens to phage display, the antigen needs to be biotinylated. Based on the SDS-
PAGE gels and proteomes from literature [47], [48], fractions containing CTx were selected for 
biotinylation. Biotin (EZ-Link, ThermoFisher cat. no: A39259) was dissolved in 170 µL milliQ water 
to a concentration of 0.02 M. From the selected fractions, 75 µL were mixed to a molar ratio of 1:1.5 
toxin to biotin molar mass and incubated for 30 min at room temperature.  
After biotinylation, hydrolysed biotin was potentially present, which could interfere with later 
experiments. Purification of the biotinylated toxins was performed using Vivaspin 500 (Sartorius, 
Vivaspin 500, cat. no: Z614009-25EA) tubes. The biotinylated toxins were pipetted into individual 
columns. The tubes were topped with 1x PBS and spun at 4,000 g for 5 min. This washing step was 
repeated twice in total. The expected molar mass addition of biotin was 473.15 Da.   
5.4 C18 Stage Tipping 
 
In preparation for MALDI-TOF, salts and other contaminants were removed from all biotinylated 
and unbiotinylated samples with the use of C18 stage tipping. For this purpose, a ProteoMASS 
Protein MALDI-MS Calibration Kit (see Table 14) was used. All buffers and calibration solutions 
came from this kit. Two C18 filters (prepared by the DTU Proteomics Core) were punched out with 
the use of a special syringe and transferred into the tip of a 10 µL pipette tip. The C18 filters were 
activated by adding 20 µL of Wetting/Elution solution (60% acetonitrile and 0.1% TFA). The solution 
was pressed down with the syringe until the solution was just covering the top of the filters, leaving 
the filters hydrated. The flow-through was being discarded. Next, the filters were washed with 20 µL 
Equilibration solution (0.1% TFA in MS grade water) and pushed through in the same manner. For 
the sample loading for MALDI-TOF, 5 µg of biotinylated sample was suspended in 20 µL of 1% 
TFA, added to the filter and pushed through, allowing the biotinylated sample to bind the filter. The 
filter was washed twice with 20 µL of Equilibration solution. For sample elution, the pipette tip was 
placed in a clean Eppendorf tube and added 10 µL of Wetting/Elution. The liquid was pushed through 
and collected. The sample were kept at 4°C until used for MALDI-TOF.

5.5 MALDI-TOF 
 
As mentioned in section 3.8, MALDI-TOF is a method which can be used to determine the molecular 
mass of a compound in a mixture of molecules. For this purpose, a Ground Steel plate (MTP 384) 
was cleaned with deionized water and 70% ethanol. A calibration solution (see Table 14) was 
prepared. On a target on the plate, 1 µL of calibration solution was added followed by addition of 1 
µL of Sinapinic acid matrix solution and mixed together thoroughly. The solution was allowed to dry 
and form crystals. On another target on the plate, 1 µL of each biotinylated C18 stage tipped sample 
was added, and likewise, 1 µL of Sinapinic acid matrix solution was added and mixed thoroughly. 
The calibration and the samples were allowed to dry on the target plate. The samples were analysed 
by the UltraFlex MALDI-TOF mass spectrometer (Bruker, USA) with the following settings: 1.00 
GS/s digitizer (Acquiris DP240), Pulsed Ion Extraction (250 ns of delay time), TOF-TOF acquisition 
at 60% laser intensity (500 shots), operated in positive linear mode, detector gain of 1821 V, ION 
source mode 1, and 25 kV of acceleration voltage. The machine was calibrated by shooting the 
calibration solution. After calibration, three rounds were performed, shooting each sample with 500 
shots. 
 
5.6 DARPin library 
 
The DARPin library used in this project was adapted from Binz et al, 2003 [49], and was provided 
by Cambridge University. The library consisted of triple repeat DARPins cloned between a N and C-
terminal capping repeats. Of the 33 amino acid residues mentioned in section 3.6.1, seven of these 
were allowed for mutation. In this library, these randomized mutations were at the positions 2, 3, 5, 
13, 14, and 33. All amino acids were allowed in these positions, except glycine and proline due to 
being structurally unfavourable, and cysteine, as it may form unwanted disulphide bonds. 
Additionally, position 26 was partly randomized, allowing only histidine, tyramine, or asparagine. 
With three designed repeat domains, N3C, there a theoretical library size of, 3.8•1023. Despite the 
great library size, a limiting step in phage display library size is the transformation efficiency of up 
to 1011 [39].

5.13 Transformation 
 
An S. cerevisiae EBY100 culture (kind gift from Elena Schäfer) was inoculated in 5 mL yeast extract 
peptone dextrose (YPD) media (see Table 14) and grown at 30°C overnight with 250 rpm. The 
following day, 1-2 mL of the overnight culture was used to inoculate 50 mL YPD media and grown 
at 30°C overnight with 250 rpm. 500 mL YPD media was inoculated to OD600 =1 and grown at 30°C 
with 300 rpm for 3 hours. The culture was aliquoted to 10 Falcon tubes and centrifugated with 3,000 
g for 6 min at 4°C. All centrifugations in the following were performed with these settings. The 
supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in a total of 100 mL ice cold miliQ water. 
The cells were centrifugated again, and pellet was washed in a total of 100 mL ice cold miliQ water. 
The cells were centrifugated again and pellet was resuspended in a total of 100 mL ice cold Sorbitol 
solution (see Table 14). The cells were centrifugated and the pellet was resuspended in 50 mL Lithium 
acetate buffer with DTT, LiOAc (see Table 14). The cell suspension was transferred in a baffled 250 
mL Erlenmeyer flask with a breathable lid and incubated at 42°C for 1 hour to initiate heat shock. 
Pellet was washed and resuspended twice in 50 mL ice cold Sorbitol solution. Cells were pellet one 
last time and resuspended in up to 4 mL Sorbitol solution. For each transformation reaction, 400 µL 
yeast solution was used and the cell suspension was mixed with 10 µg of plasmid DNA and 100 µg 
of boiled carrier DNA in form of single stranded salmon sperm DNA (Sigma Aldrich, cat. no: D9156). 
The mixture was transferred to a 2 mm gap electroporation cuvette and the solution was 
electroporated at 2.0 kV, 200 Ohm and 25 µFD (Eppendorf Eporatorm cat. no: 4309000019). The 
time constant was expected to be > 3.5 ms. After the electroporation, 2 mL of fresh YPD media was 
added immediately and incubated at 30°C with 280 rpm for 1 hour to recover. From the recovered 
culture, a serial dilution was set up ranging from 10-2 to 10-6. From each dilution, 100 µL of culture 
was plated on Synthetic Dropout (SD) medium-Trp plates (see Table 14), using tryptophan (Trp) as 
an auxotrophic marker. The rest of the culture was expanded into 50 mL of SD-Trp media.  
 
5.14 Induction of yeast cells 
 
From the recovered transformed culture, 2 x 107 cells were induced by adding to 75 mL Synthetic 
Drop-out media supplemented with 2% galactose, 0.2% glucose, and 25 µg/mL chloramphenicol.   
For control, the positive control 3G86 culture was added 75 mL of synthetic drop-out media + 2% 
galactose media (as induced positive control) and 75 mL synthetic drop-out media with 2% glucose 
final, no galactose (as uninduced negative control). The cultures were incubated overnight at 30°C 
with 280 rpm.  
 
5.15 Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) 
 
Of the induced cells, a worth of 2•107 cell culture was transferred to an Eppendorf tube and pelleted 
at 13,000 g for 1 min. The supernatant was removed, and the pellet was washed twice with 1 mL of 
PBS-A (PBS-Albumin, see Table 14). The cells were resuspended in 500 µL PBS-A supplemented 
with biotinylated CTx (Nn14 and Nk14 mixed) antigen at 250 nM and incubated with end-over-end

PBS-A supplemented with GFP-tamavidin at 200 nM and mKate-SpyCatcher at 500 nM. The cells

The cells were resuspended in 500 µL PBS-A.

7.1.1.3 Biotinylation verification with MALDI-TOF 
 
From the SDS-PAGE analysis, fraction 14, Nn14, was chosen for biotinylation. The addition of a 
biotin molecule to a toxin results in an addition of 473.15 Da to the molar mass of the toxin. This 
way, the degree of biotinylation can be investigated using MALDI-TOF analysis. The most optimal 
is to have 1 biotin per toxin. 
Fraction Nn14 was biotinylated in a 1:1.5 and a 1:1.75 toxin to biotin molar mass, and the result from 
MALDI-TOF is shown in Figure 25. For both ratios, a clear difference in the molar mass between the 
biotinylated and unbiotinylated reference is seen. Products with 1 and 2 biotin per toxin (1B and 2B, 
respectively) and a smaller amount of product with 3 biotin molecules per toxin is seen for both ratios. 
The difference between the peaks is very conserved at around 474 Da.

7.2 Phage Display  
 
With the biotin verified Nn14 and Nk14 fractions, phage display was conducted. First, the phages 
were panned against both Nn14 and Nk14, 500 nM each. Next, the output from the first selection was 
split and panned against 1000 nM of Nn14 and 1000 nM of Nk14, individually (A and B, 
respectively). These outputs were then used in a cross-panning setting, where the output from A 
(Nn14) was panned against Nk14, and opposite, the output from B (Nk14) was panned against Nn14.  
Additionally, the biotinylated Nk14 fraction was used to explore the properties of IPTG induction in 
overnight phage cultures where these were with and without added IPTG induction. 
 
After each phage display selection round, TG1 cells were infected with eluted phage outputs and 
plated onto enrichment plates to follow if toxin-specific binders had been obtained, demonstrated in 
the form of colonies.  
 
5.2.1 Phage display enrichment plates:  
 
The infected TG1 cells were plated onto phagemid selective 2TYGC plates containing 
chloramphenicol. Only TG1 cells carrying the phagemid is therefore able to grow. After each 
selection round, the colonies on the enrichment plates were counted, see Figure 30.

(without antigen) is obtained. In selection 2, an overall enrichment is seen and no CFU is counted for 
the negative selection. The phage output presented to Nn14 (A) antigens shows a 2.7-fold enrichment 
compared to selection 1, and the output presented to Nk14 (B) antigens shows a 1.4-fold enrichment 
over selection 1. In selection 3, the calculated CFU decreases with 5-fold compared to selection 1, 
and a 13- and 7-fold decrease compared to selection 2 (BA compared to A and B, respectively).

8 Discussion 
The aim of this project was to discover specific DARPin binder against CTx from the Naja naja and 
Naja kaouthia. For this approach, phage and yeast display was used. However, due to poor 
enrichment in phage display and lack of induction in yeast display among others, this aim of 
discovering specific binder was not obtained. This discussion will use the results to discuss the 
complications that arose and to draws conclusions along with suggestion on how to improve these 
complications.  
 
8.2 Preparation of toxins for phage display 
 
The first experimental work conducted in this project was the fractionation of whole venom from 
Naja naja and Naja kaouthia with the purpose of isolating CTx for phage display. The fractionation 
was carried out using RP-HPLC. The obtained reference chromatograms were compared to 
chromatograms and proteomics from literature [47], [48] and by high resemblance for both snakes, 
nine CTxs from Naja naja and four CTxs from Naja kaouthia was identified. The fractions were 
investigated with SDS-PAGE analysis, showing bands at ~7 kDa in reduced conditions corresponding 
to the correct size of CTx. The chosen fractions showed, beside the correct size band, signs of purity 
and high abundance. The biotinylation of the fractions were analysed with MALDI-TOF. The 
chemical linking of a biotin molecule to a toxin is beneficial as it allows for better presentation in the 
phage display panning. However, the degree of biotinylation is important, since over-biotinylation 
can result in a decreased binding surface area available thereby prevent phages from binding epitopes 
[32]. MALDI-TOF was used to assess the degree of the biotinylation. The expected mass of a biotin 
molecule is 473.15 Da. In the first round of biotinylation of the Nk14 fraction, the biotinylation failed, 
Figure 28, as no increase in the molecular weight was seen and visually by a complete overlap of the 
unbiotinylated reference and the biotinylated sample. The fraction was re-biotinylated in a 1:1.5 and 
1:1.75 toxin to biotin molar ratio, Figure 29, and resulted a successful biotinylation in both ratios. 
The difference between the peaks differs from the 473.15 Da, 511-578 Da, however, the differences 
are more or less consistent. This difference is most likely due to a poor instrument calibration. Despite 
an identical protocol, an extra included buffer exchange step between the two biotinylations, 
removing possible remnants of acetonitrile from the HPLC, could potentially be the reason for why 
the re-biotinylation suddenly worked, allowing for better enzyme linking of the biotin to the toxin. 
The Nk14 1:1.15 ratio biotinylation consists primarily of the addition of one biotin molecule per 
toxin, whereas the 1:1.75 ratio have a significant amount of toxin added with two biotin molecules. 
The 1:1.5 ratio is thus more favourable due to the ideal degree of biotinylation.  
The Nn14 fraction was biotinylated in a 1:1.5 and 1:1.75 toxin to biotin molar ratio and succeeded 
the first time, Figure 25, with very accurate addition of the biotin molecule with difference between 
the peaks of 472-474 Da. However, the addition of one biotin is almost as frequent as the addition of 
two biotin molecules, increasing the chance of over-biotinylation. The biotinylation of the fraction 
was still considered successful. Nn14 and Nk14 was thus chosen as antigen targets for the phage 
display panning. If optimal, both fractions should have been biotinylated in a 1:1 ratio.

8.3 Phage display 
With the biotinylated antigens, phage display was initiated. Three selection rounds were conducted. 
During the phage display selection, different issues were encountered hereof lack of enrichment and 
contamination by helper phages.  
 
Different methods were used for the quantification of total amount of phages and the amount of 
specific and non-specific binders, hereof enrichment plates, ELISA, and qPCR.  
 
8.3.1 Panning against CTx  
 
For the phage display with CTx, the first phage display selection against CTx (Nn14 and Nk14) 
resulted in a 22-fold increase over the negative selection, Figure 30. As the eluted phages containing 
the binders, are amplified, and enriched after each selection round it is expected that they should 
represent a larger proportion of the phage pool compared to the initial library input. This happened 
to be the case between selection 1 and 2, where the phage output from selection 1 was panned 
individually against Nn14 and Nk14 resulting in a 2.7- and 1.4-fold enrichment, respectively, over 
the first selection phage count. However, the same pattern was not seen between selection 2 and 3. 
Instead a 13- and 7-fold decrease was obtained compared to selection 2, and 5-fold decrease compared 
to selection 1. The decrease could of be due to the fact that the binders are only specific to the panned 
antigen in selection 2, and thus not showing cross-reactive properties when panned to the second/ 
opposite antigen in selection 3. Some cross-reactive binders are observed in selection 3, 2•105, and 
does therefore not exclude this possibility entirely.  
 
However, it was chosen to investigate the low phage count further. The low phage count in selection 
3 was hypothesised to be due to a helper phage contamination. Growth of the uninfected cells on the 
control plates containing kanamycin (selective for helper phages) had been observed after each 
selection round, see appendix 12.4. Helper phage contamination can decrease the transduction level 
of the TG1 cells and hence the amplification [52]. Though enrichment in the first and second selection 
round, the suddenly high number of CFU appearing on the control plate from selection 3 correlates 
with the significant decrease in enrichment shown on the enrichment plate from same selection. 
 
Another hypothesis was amplification bias where non-selective phages or phages without DARPin 
variants have different amplification efficiencies. Some phages can thus be amplified more efficiently 
than e.g., other more relevant phages, hence skewing the distribution of the phage output 
amplification [53]. This effect was not investigated in this report but is a general hurdle in phage 
display protocol.  
Generally, the DARPin library itself could present low diversity and lack of enrichment, however 
unlikely, as the same library used in this report has been successfully used in the discovery of 
DARPins against the human epidermal growth factor 2 (HER2) [54]. 
It should be noted that the protocol used for the phage display was prepared for the E. coli strain XL1-
blue. TG1 E. coli cells were used for this experiment and can therefore have affected the results

obtained due to difference in phage infectivity and/or production. Switching to another stain could 
thus be a possible improvement.

of enrichment. Something therefore indicates that complications occurred in the amplification of the 
phages eluted from selection 2.  
 
8.3.2 Induction by IPTG 
 
For the experiment with IPTG, the induction with IPTG was investigated on the monopanning against 
Nk14, where the overnight cultures amplifying the phages were with and without added IPTG. Phage 
titering of the outputs from the overnight cultures are seen in Figure 32. For all selection, enrichment 
above the negative selection is observed for both with and without IPTG. The titering shows that the 
addition of IPTG lowers the phage count for both selection 1 and 2. In contrast to the titering, the 
qPCR results of the total phage count in Figure 33A, shows that the addition of IPTG increases the 
total phage count compared to without the addition of IPTG. Consistent with the phage titering, the 
qPCR shows the highest phage count in selection 1 compared to selection 2, both for with and without 
IPTG, indicating poor amplification. Though addition of IPTG increases the total phage count 
significantly in selection 1, the number of specific binders from same selection with and without 
==PAGE:75==
 
75 
IPTG are the same. Only in selection 2, the number of specific binders for with IPTG is 2.9-fold 
higher than for without IPTG.  
 
The observed decrease in the phage count was hypothesised to be caused by over-induction when 
added IPTG. IPTG induces the DARPin-G3P fusion 
 
If the DARPin-G3P fusion is induced too much by IPTG multiple copies of the binder will be packed 
and show polyvalent display. The phages expressing multiple G3P copies will be selected to a greater 
extent in the binding selection compared to monovalent or bald phages due to avidity effects. 
However, when amplified in E. coli, the WT G3P is missing and a decrease in the infectivity can 
therefor occur [49]. For future investigation of the IPTG the effects could be explored with different 
concentration of IPTG. 
 
8.4 Pre-eluted phage display 
 
The decision to conduct qPCR on the pre-eluted selection beads after one round of phage display was 
based on the complication encountered throughout the phage display hereof the lack of enrichment 
and amplification between the selection rounds. The results in shown in Figure 36. The standard curve 
used for this qPCR was based on phage library size determined from the titering results, hence more 
reliable and quantitative qPCR analysis. The qPCR assessed the specific binders and showed a 14-
fold enrichment over the negative selection after one round of panning. This number was 65 times 
higher than the specific binders found in selection 1 from the post-eluted phage display output, 
indicating that the complications seen between the phage display selection could be found in 
processes after the elution, hereby the amplification and precipitation steps.  
 
8.5 Yeast surface display  
To discover high affinity binders, YSD was explored as an alternative method to phage display.  
For the construction the YSD library, the pre-eluted phages were used. The maximum library size of 
YSD is three times smaller than that of phages, and with the use of pre-eluted phages, a fraction of 
the unspecific binders was discarded [39]. The YSD vector was successfully constructed and 
transformed into yeast. When analysed with flow cytometry, the uninduced and induced samples 
(positive control and CTx samples in different volumes) did not differ from each other, which is 
unexpected. Expected is a linearly distributed population of cell with increasing mKate and GFP 
fluorescence signals, corresponding to different expression levels of binders. However, this pattern 
is not observed instead indication lack of induction and expression of DARPins on the yeast surface. 
The initial induction media used consisted of 2% galactose and 0.2% glucose. Glucose, as a repressor 
of the galactose [55], was hypothesised to inhibit the induction of the YSD construct. Another 
induction was hence performed using induction media without glucose, only galactose. However, this 
gave the same results, see Figure 42.

76 
The DARPin used in this report was fused to the N-terminal of the Aga2p subunit. As mentioned in 
section 3.10, the fusion of binder libraries can be fused to either the C- or the N-terminus of the 
Aga2p, however the C-terminus is commonly used [40]. It was therefore hypothesised that the fusion 
of the DARPin to the C-terminus instead could affect the lack of expression. DARPins used in YSD 
is relatively novel and the research examining the optimal terminus fusion is hence limited. However, 
in an article by Mohan et al. [56], DARPins were successfully used in YSD as a C-terminus fusion. 
It could therefore be worth to investigate the experiment with the DARPin library fused to the C-
terminus of Aga2p, a project which is already ongoing in the Tropical Pharmacology Lab.   
9 Conclusion 
 
RP-HPLC was used to fractionate whole venom from the two snakes and proteomes from literature 
was used to identify cytotoxins containing fragments. These fragments were analysed and confirmed 
by the use of SDS-PAGE. Biotinylation of the fragments were conducted in a 1:1.5 toxin to biotin 
molar mass and was confirmed by MALDI-TOF. This resulted in both fraction 14 from Naja naja, 
Nn14, and from Naja kaouthia, Nk14, was being used as antigen targets for the phage display 
campaign.  
 
Three rounds of phage display were initially performed resulting in two successful selection rounds 
with the detection and enrichment of specific binders between the two rounds. However, the third 
selection round resulted in a low phage count most likely due to a helper phages contamination, 
detected from the kanamycin control plates.  
qPCR was used to confirm and quantify the number of specific binders in the phage outputs from the 
different selection rounds and confirmed selective binder in the two first selection rounds and a 
possibility of cross-reactive activity in the phage output from selection 2 a Nn14, as selective binders 
towards both Nn14 and Nk14 was observed. However, due to the high signal in the negative controls 
the data was difficult to interpret. The negative controls showed large standard errors, and the 
potential cross-reactive phage output could hence be worth investing again. 
 
To investigate the cause of the poor enrichment, one round of phage display was performed but 
terminated before the elution step and analysed with qPCR. The analysis showed a 14-fold 
enrichment over the negative selection, indicating that the problem lay in the steps after the elution, 
hereby the amplification and precipitation steps. The sequence of the pre-eluted DARPins were 
sequenced with PCR and subcloned into E. coli. Results were not obtained in the time period of this 
project. Additional subcloning into yeast was introduced for the investigation of binders via yeast 
surface display determined by FACS. No difference was observed between the induced and 
uninduced positive control, indicating that induced had not occurred.   
 
Specific DARPin binder against CTx from Naja naja and Naja kaouthis was not obtained in the 
timeframe of this project, however, significant progress towards identifying potential specific 
DARPin binders was presented which with modifications of the yeast surface- and phage display 
techniques have the potential to be obtained.

10 Future perspectives 
 
Though specific DARPin binders towards CTx from Naja naja and Naja Kaouthia were not obtained 
in this project, the data showed the presence of binders, though, they were not able to be amplified. 
The full investigation of these potential binder is therefore of great interest. Optimisation of the phage 
and yeast surface display techniques are needed. Different strains of E. coli, than TG1, could be 
explored, e.g., XL1-Blue, in the phage display selection for which the protocol was originally 
designed to. Also, DARPins are novel within the field of YSD and the investigation of the fusion 
terminus is therefore also of high interest to be investigated in future work. Hence, if a C-terminus 
fusion to the Aga2P can result in better display of DARPins in the yeast surface display campaign 
than a N-terminus fusion.  
 
It is also of interest to repeat the phage display selections in order to confirm or reject the possibility 
of cross-reactive activity in the Pan 2 a Nn14 selection. The selection should be investigated with 
enough samples for a qPCR to obtained reliable results with appropriate standard errors. If these 
binders are truly cross-reactive, the binders would contribute greatly to a future development of a 
cross-reactive antivenom against Naja naja and Naja kaouthia, minimising the number of DARPin 
required.  
If any specific binders are obtained in general, these would have to be investigated for their 
neutralising properties. Along with the neutralising properties, the modifications of the DARPin are 
also to be investigated if used in an antivenom. Modifications include e.g. affinity maturation in order 
to investigate if a higher binding affinity can be obtained. Due to the small size of DARPins, which 
is considered an advantage in form of improved tissue penetration and fast clearance, DARPin have 
a short half-life. The half-life of DARPin can therefore also be investigated with modifications in 
form of PEGylation or coupling to human serum albumin to increase the half-life [30]. 
 
If possible, to discover and amplify specific DARPin binders, the aim is to implement these in a 
recombinant antivenom. Together this will ensure a cost-competitive and effective therapeutic 
solution over current antivenom to snakebite in India and set a promising precedent for the global 
treatment of snakebite envenomations.
