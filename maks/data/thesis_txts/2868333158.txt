Abstract
3D­printed concrete structures are a relatively new phenomenon, so the knowledge on the topic is
limited. However, the potential they possess cannot be denied. The development and implemen­
tation of this method result in several advantages. But at this stage in the process, there are many
unanswered questions.
This paper aims to investigate the unreinforced concrete interface strength. Interfaces are inevitable
when dealing with 3D­printed concrete. Therefore, this thesis will specifically investigate the time­
dependent interface strength, as it is thought that the strength of the interface will decrease over
time.
To investigate the interface strength, a link is created between a material test, a component test, and
a structural test. A cylinder is tested to determine the compressive strength. A unreinforced cast
and a 3D­printed concrete specimen is tested in a bi­axial compression to determine the interface
strength. The knowledge learned from these two tests will be used to analyze a structural 3D­
printed concrete beam.
The conclusion of these tests is that a link was created between the material and component test­
ing, but the link between the specimen and the beam was not fulfilled. It was found that the
time­dependent interface strength decreases significantly in the first two hours of the print time
gap. Furthermore, it was found that the rate of strength loss for cast and 3D­printed concrete was
equivalent. Finally, it was concluded that the 3D­printed beam had a similar bearing capacity to
that of a monolithic beam. However, the crack development for the beam was unique.
Throughout this experimental work, only the time dependency was investigated. There are several
other parameters that affect this strength, such as concrete mix and moisture content, among others.
It is recommended to gain more insight into 3D­printed elements before a standardized code design
can be published and implemented.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The implementation of 3D­concrete printing (3DCP) in the building industry has grown signif­
icantly since the 1990s, with the number of projects using 3DCP increasing exponentially [10].
Some significant advantages of using 3DCP include reduced production costs in terms of per­
sonnel and time efficiency, the ability to create more complex structures and shapes, less material
waste, and minimized human errors [15]. However, the complexity inherent in 3DCP leads to some
trade­offs. The quality of the extruded concrete is highly dependent on factors such as weather con­
ditions [5], which results in larger variances in mechanical properties from project to project. This
necessitates a more complex process of quality assurance compared to standard procedures, such
as in­situ or prefabricated concrete elements.
The literature on how to ensure stable mechanical properties for 3DCP is evolving, but norms
for estimating them are very limited. As of today, there are no clear norms for designing 3D­
printed concrete structures. An important mechanical property of concrete structural elements is
the interface strength between an existing concrete layer and a freshly extruded one.
In this MSc thesis, the objective is to investigate unreinforced interfaces in concrete structures.
The primary focus is the time dependency between the construction of each layer of concrete and
its effect on the interface strength. In general, there is limited knowledge on how ‘wet­on­wet’
concrete interface strength is affected by the ‘opening time’ of the concrete mixture. In the ex­
treme case, the new layer of concrete is cast (or printed) against a hardened surface, consequently,
the strength relies solely on cohesive resistance.
The hypothesis is that the strength of the interface decreases as the time between the layers in­
creases.
This thesis will experimentally investigate the time­dependent strength development in terms of
average shear stress as a function of ’opening time’ between the production of each layer. The
outcome of the experiments is to develop a model of the interface strength as a function of time
between constructed layers.
To verify the model, a structural element, e.g., a beam, consisting of several 3D­printed layers.
The beam will be tested under controlled conditions. The results will be compared to the bearing
capacity of a monolithic beam. Furthermore, an Digital Image Correlation analysis of the crack
development will be conducted.
The experiment to establish the time­dependent strength model was conducted using a specially
designed biaxial test on unreinforced concrete for both cast and 3D­printed specimens. The test
setup was designed and built as part of this project. For each specimen uni­axial compression test
will be performed for cylinders. The goal is to establish a connection between material, component,
and a large structural element. The test specimens and the 3D­printed beam in their experimental
setup are shown in Figure 1.1.
This thesis is part of a larger project aimed at investigating the behavior of 3D­printed concrete
as a concept for the construction of large structural elements. Furthermore, it contributes to the
research on methodologies for determining the strength of 3DCP structural elements.

CHAPTER 2. CONCEPT OF 3D CONCRETE PRINTING
2
Concept of 3D Concrete Printing
Today, concrete is the most used material in construction. The latest development in the history of
concrete is the growing use of 3D­printed concrete structures. In this thesis, 3D­printed concrete
will be referred to as 3DCP. 3DCP emerged in the 1990s, and since then, interest in this phe­
nomenon has been growing immensely. There are several reasons for this. 3DCP offers numerous
benefits such as building constructions faster, flexible architectural design, no formwork, and safer
and better working conditions, among others [20].
One of the key factors in 3DCP is the concrete paste, and even today, it is not well known what
the most ideal composition of the concrete paste should be. The paste is evaluated based on three
important factors: pumpability, extrudability, and buildability.
These factors describe the general process of 3D printing concrete. Fresh concrete is delivered to
or mixed at the construction site. Using a pump, the concrete is transported through the printer arm.
At the end of the printer arm, there is a printing head with a nozzle. From the nozzle, the concrete is
extruded in layers that form the final construction. Therefore, these factors are important to assess
when analyzing the concrete.
2.1
Pumpability
Pumpability is defined as a characteristic of the concrete mix that is workable enough to be trans­
ferred through the pumping system to the printing head while under pressure, maintaining its initial
properties the entire way.
This means that small­sized aggregate and very wet concrete would be ideal. However, when
the concrete is extruded, it needs to be relatively stiff to maintain its intended shape. Therefore,
pumpability is evaluated based on parameters such as pumping distance, pumping methodology,
and hose diameter.
The main concern regarding pumpability is that the concrete may segregate while being pumped.
This means that the wet part of the concrete may attach to the side of the hose, while the coarse
aggregate moves towards the middle. Consequently, there is a possibility of not maintaining the
initial properties and viscosity.
In recent years, a pumpability index has been developed by Tay et al [19]. The pumpability index
is dimensionless and is described as the ratio of the flow rate of the concrete mix to the flow rate
of water. The index was found to be proportional to the slump­flow value of concrete.
2.2
Extrudability
Extrudability is defined as the ability of the concrete mix to be extruded smoothly and unhindered
through the nozzle as a homogeneous paste [20]. Two important factors in extrudability are the
concrete mix design and the type of nozzle.
Extrudable concrete depends on the composition and size distribution of the dry aggregates. The
design should ideally have an optimal yield stress and low plastic viscosity.
Nozzles come in many shapes and are responsible for the formation of the extruded concrete layers.
The shape of the nozzle is primarily chosen based on its intended use. The most common shapes are
rectangular and circular. The rectangular shape offers great stability for longer stretches of printing,
while the circular shape provides more flexibility, for instance, in the corners of a construction.
The nozzle height is also important to consider. A greater height allows for faster printing of a full
construction, but it also makes it more difficult for the structure to support itself.

2.3
Buildability
Buildability is defined as the ability of the printed material to maintain its intended form when exit­
ing the printing nozzle and to support successive layers. The most important factors in buildability
are the concrete’s ability to maintain a stable shape and its strength. The concrete should be able to
support itself and, shortly after, be able to support a successive layer. Furthermore, it should mix
well with the underlying layer and not form any cold joints.
Parameters associated with buildability include the setting time of the concrete, print time gap
(PTG), green strength, and bonding [20]. Investigations into these parameters are ongoing world­
wide . One of the major questions being investigated is the printing speed. The printing speed
should be high enough to ensure good bonding between layers and prevent cold joints, but not so
fast that the individual layers do not have sufficient strength to support themselves. This question
regarding the optimal printing speed still needs to be answered, and experiments with different
PTGs need to be carried out.
2.4
Roughness
The roughness of an interface has, to an extent, a large impact on the bond strength of the interface
[17]. The adhesion of an interface has two different contributing aspects: specific adhesion and
mechanical adhesion. Specific adhesion covers chemical bonding strength and chemical interac­
tion, while mechanical adhesion covers interlocking between two surfaces. Both types of adhesion
depend on the true surface area of each side of an interface. The roughness of a surface affects
both specific and mechanical adhesion by changing the true surface area. Increasing the roughness
increases the true surface area, thereby increasing the interface strength. For this report, the effect
of roughness on the interface will not be thoroughly investigated.
2.5
The Time Dependent Interface Strength
This paper will investigate the interface strength with different PTGs. Interface strength is critically
important, as there is normally an interface every 10­40 mm of height [12], [7], whereas in this
project, the nozzle height is 100 mm.
Depending on the size of the desired structure, the printer arm will return to the same location at
different time intervals. This makes it essential to clearly identify the interface strength depending
on the time. Currently, there is limited literature on this topic. Therefore, a model will be presented
in this section. The model is based on the Mohr­Coulomb failure criteria described later in section
3.1.1.
The purpose of the model is to predict the interface strength with different PTGs. The model can
be represented by the following equation.
τ(t) = c(t) + µ(t)σ
(2.1)
Where, c(t), is the time­dependent cohesion of the interface, σ is the applied normal stress, and
µ(t) is the time­dependent friction coefficient. The model will be reevaluated after a series of
tests described in the upcoming sections. The test results could provide a basis for the insertion of
constants, depending on the measured shear and normal stresses. This will be discussed later in
the report.

Theory
3.1
Failure Criteria
Regarding the failure of concrete, there are no natural laws that predict the failure envelope of
concrete. The failure of concrete is described by hypotheses developed back in the 1700s [13].
These ideas have been experimentally tested over time and are now used as theories to describe the
failure criteria of concrete. Today, there are many failure criteria for different materials. Concrete
is a brittle material, and the most common failure criterion for brittle materials was developed
by Coulomb and Mohr. A brittle material is the opposite of a ductile material, where the tensile
strengths and compression strengths are similar. Brittle materials tend to have a higher compression
strength than tensile strength, which fits well with concrete.
Coulomb was one of the people who developed a hypothesis based on the observation that failure
often occurs along different sliding or yield planes. The resistance in this hypothesis is determined
by two parameters: cohesion and internal friction. This was later combined with Mohr’s general
theory, which assumes that failure in a material occurs when the stresses in a section satisfy the
condition.
f(σ, τ) = 0
(3.1)
In the equation above σ and τ is the normal and shear stress respectively. The two theories Mohr’s
failure envelope and Coulomb’s frictional hypothesis is illustrated in figure 2.1, p. 18 [13].
3.1.1
Modified Mohr Coulomb Failure Criteria
In this report, the modified Mohr­Coulomb failure criteria is used to describe the failure criteria
of the 3D­printed interface. The modification is a combination of the two theories mentioned in
the previous section. Today, it is the most common method to describe the failure envelope of a
concrete interface. It is specifically useful for materials such as concrete, which has a compression
strength fc much greater than the tensile strength ft.
The failure criteria is described with the model below.
τ = c + µσ
(3.2)
In the formula, τ is the shear stress, c is the cohesion, µ is the coefficient of friction and σ is the
normal stress. If the normal stress σ is a compressive stress, it gives a positive contribution, and
if it is a tensile stress, it gives a negative contribution. Note that in this paper that compression is
considered as positive.
Below, a figure is presented to illustrate the concept of the modified Mohr Coulomb failure criteria.

As mentioned above, the material can fail in two ways: sliding or separation failure. Separation

The Single Strut
For a given concrete element, a strut and tie model can be used. In a strut and tie model, the com­
pression forces are carried by concrete elements called struts, while the tensile forces are carried
by steel reinforcement bars, referred to as ties. This model is a lower bound solution, meaning it
assumes a statically admissible stress field and estimates a conservative bearing capacity, p. 13
[13].
Strut­and­tie models are as old as reinforced concrete theory itself [13]. Generally, the model is
used to calculate the load­bearing capacity of concrete elements. It works well for preliminary
designs but can also be applied to final designs. In this thesis, a system with a single strut will be
used and analyzed.

4.2
Beam Design
A reinforced beam is designed to compare the behavior of a structural 3D­printed beam element to
that of a monolithic beam element. This is done through crack analysis as well as comparison of
bearing capacity and interface stresses. The interface stresses are calculated to provide an indica­
tion of the interface strength of a larger and more realistic structural element, such as a 3D­printed
concrete beam.
The beam consists of four layers and a total of three interfaces. The interfaces are made with three
different PTGs: 0.5h, 2h, and 2h, respectively, for the three interfaces. The beam is tested in a
four­point bending test, which is designed to affect only one end of the beam, allowing the beam
to be tested twice.

mensions of the beam, which increased because of the printing method. The print head is 150x100,
but in this section, the beam is assumed to be approximately 450 mm tall, 175 mm wide, and 1800
mm long. Two steel rebars are placed in the interface between the first and second layer, with
diameter Ø16 used. Each beam is supposed to be tested twice, as the test setup is designed to pro­
voke a failure at only one end of the beam, thereby leaving the other end intact. The dimensions

Uniaxial Compression Test of Concrete Cylinder
A uniaxial compression test of the concrete cylinders is conducted to determine their compressive
strength. This is done using a servo­hydraulic machine, SHY­035­Toni [1], specifically designed
for uniaxial compression tests.
Figure 6.1: SHY­035­Toni Technik­280Ton, DTU Lyngby Denmark
The machine has round marks on the inside to indicate where the cylinder should be placed. The
total number of tests is listed in Table E.1. The cylinders measure 100 mm in diameter and 200 mm
in height. They have hardened for a minimum of 14 days. The cylinders from the cast specimen
were left in a water tank for curing. The water was approximately 20◦C. The cylinders associated
with the 3D printed elements were left at room temperature, fully exposed. They are tested on the
same day as the relevant unreinforced concrete specimens.
The test is conducted according to DS/EN 12390­3:2019 [2]. A standard compression test is per­
formed with a linearly increasing load of 0.6 MPa/s. Assuming a stiffness of 35 GPa [18], the
equivalent strain is calculated.

6.2
Biaxial Test of Unreinforced Concrete Interface
A biaxial compression test of the cast and 3D printed concrete elements is performed to deter­
mine the strength of the interface. The test is designed to closely resemble a direct shear test. The
interface strength of the unreinforced concrete is tested on the SHY­058­Daeyoung [3] test ma­
chine. The tests are carried out in the structural laboratory located on the campus of the Technical
University of Denmark.
Below are pictures of the test setup.
(a) View 1
(b) View 2
Figure 6.2: Pictures of the test setup.
The test machine is capable of providing biaxial compression. Each actuator can provide a force
of 2 MN [3] and a piston displacement of ± 100 mm. Many of the steel fixtures were found
in the structural laboratory and reused. As described in Chapter 5, new steel fixtures have been
designed, which can be seen in Figure 6.2b. The roller supports were also found in the structural
laboratory and provide support parallel to the actuator that provides the force. The rollers can move
perpendicular to the actuators.
The two actuators correspond to the horizontal and vertical forces, which are controlled individ­
ually. The vertical force will be applied linearly at a rate of the targeted vertical force divided by
1.5 minutes until it reaches the targeted vertical force. Once the targeted vertical force is reached,
it will remain constant throughout the test.
After the vertical force has reached its target, the horizontal force will start loading the specimen
at a linear rate of 1 mm/min, continuing until failure is achieved. The ultimate force is recorded
and used for further calculations.

Discussion
In this chapter, the results of both the biaxial test of the unreinforced concrete interface and the 3D
printed beam will be discussed. The current issues concerning the uncertainty about the strength
in unreinforced concrete interfaces and how to investigate it were introduced in the introduction.
These issues are addressed by assessing how successful the tests are in terms of gathering the
essential interface strength parameters, cohesion c and the friction coefficient µ. Furthermore, the
creation of an interface strength model, which is assumed as a function of PTG, is established and
discussed. The results gathered from the designed specimens for the biaxial test of the unreinforced
concrete interface are examined by comparing them to a more realistic and structural use case, in
the form of a bending test of a 3D printed beam.

8.3
Modified Mohr Coulomb Failure Criteria
The Mohr­Coulomb failure criterion is assumed to represent the failure criterion for an unreinforced
interface. Based on the results presented in Figure 7.4 and 7.8, it can be seen that the interfaces
follow this criterion.
This project aims to determine a model for time­dependent interface strength. In section 2.5, the
failure criterion were modified and a time variable was included. The initial form of the assumed
model Mohr­Coulomb failure criterion is written below.
τ(t) = c(t) + µ(t)σ
(8.3)
Here, both the cohesion, c, and friction coefficient, µ, are functions of t, where t is the PTG. This
model is being discussed in this section.
8.3.1
Revised Friction Coefficient and Cohesion
The friction coefficient were initially assumed to be a function of PTG. Considering the factors
affecting the friction coefficient, such as the surface roughness, concrete composition, and casting
circumstances (temperature and moisture content) none of these factors, except the moisture con­
tent, change with different PTG. This suggests that the friction coefficient is independent of PTG.
From the results shown in figure 7.4 and 7.8, the friction coefficient appears to be independent of
PTG, indicating a constant friction coefficient. Therefore, the model of the Mohr­Coulomb failure
criterion is now assumed to be written as the following:
τ(t) = c(t) + µσ
(8.4)
The new friction coefficients is found by taking the average of the friction coefficients listed in
figure 7.7 and Table 7.10. The new friction coefficient, µ, can be seen below.

8.3.3
Contextualization
The interfaces studied in this project result from two surfaces meeting, where one surface is more
hardened than the other. Furthermore, the bottom layer was exposed and left uncovered during the
PTG. The reason for the strength reduction is unknown. An excessive moisture loss can lead to
loss in tensile strength [9], assuming the tensile strength and cohesion is linked, this would also
give a reduction in cohesion. It is believed that during the PTG, the amount of free cement and
free water is limited as they have been used in the hydration reaction and have evaporated. This
leaves less material on the surface of the first layer for the second layer to react with. The casting
and printing of specimens were conducted indoors at approximately 20◦C. Therefore, the rate of
hydration and evaporation has been the same for both cast and printed surfaces.
Very little is known about the concrete used by the 3DCP industry, but it is assumed that print­
able concrete is accelerated to reduce the amount of concrete used. Often hardening acceleration
additives are added to the mix design in order to ensure the buildability [5]. If printing were to
be done outside, exposed to wind and weather, this could result in different rates of hydration and
evaporation, thereby affecting the strength loss rate. For 3DCP structures, there will always be
interfaces. It is clear that a good understanding of the strength loss rate for the concrete used is
very important during the design phase of the given structure.
Because of the various factors that could affect cohesion and the rate at which it declines, it is
difficult to contextualize the results seen in this project to what is expected on a concrete 3D print­
ing site. However, the model creates an expected curve progression of how quickly the interface
strength decreases. The specific values for cohesion, strength loss rate of cohesion, and friction
coefficient may need to be adjusted to the relevant mix design and 3D printing conditions.

8.4.1
Monolithic Reinforced Concrete versus Reinforced 3DCP
The concept of 3D concrete printing (3DCP) is still under development. Since its inception in the
1990s, new knowledge has been presented annually. However, many aspects of the final product
remain unknown. The ultimate goal is to integrate 3D printing into various design codes, making
it easier to produce buildings using 3DCP.
One of the objectives of this thesis is to compare the 3D­printed concrete beam with a monolithic
reinforced concrete beam. This comparison will be made by analyzing the test results from the
experiment described in section 6.3 and the design calculations presented in section 4.2. The cal­
culations assume a monolithic concrete beam.
The main results from the four­point bending test are presented in Table 7.11. The recorded failure
loads were 292 kN, 344 kN, and 174 kN, for test 1, 2 and 3 respectively. According to the design
calculations, a monolithic beam would experience a bending failure at 330 kN. Comparing this
with the 3D printed beam, the recorded failure loads for the first and second tests were rather close
to the design calculations.
Initially, after the first test, it presumably appear as a shear failure, and the second test, appear as a
bending failure. From the design calculations it is clear that the capacity of the monolithic beam in
shear and bending is close to each other, when excluding the effectiveness factor. When including
the effectiveness factor the shear capacity was utilized to a 153 % with a piston load of 330 kN.
It seems the effectiveness factor have had less influence on the bearing capacity of the 3D printed
beam than expected, since both failure mode have been recorded.
It should be noted that these results are based on three tests, so they are not entirely representative
of the broader picture. More experiments would be needed to fully validate the implementation of
3DCP. Additionally, in the third test, the beam recorded a failure load of 174 kN, showing a large
variation in failure loads. This specific test was a second testing for the same beam, meaning it had
already experienced stresses throughout the beam. It is believed that this prior stress significantly
contributed to the lower failure load, which should be considered in defense of the 3D­printed
beam.
During testing, it was observed that the beam behaved similarly to cast concrete. The first cracks
appeared at the mid­bottom of the beam and extended up to the first layer where the reinforcement
was placed. A primary concern when printing the beam with reinforcement bars was whether the
reinforcement would bond with the concrete. It was observed that, under bending, the bottom
interface would slowly detach, thereby losing the ability to carry tensile stresses. The initial de­
tachment would eventually develop into large openings in the first interface, leaving the first layer
as dead weight. This is referred to as the zipper effect and is discussed later in the chapter. This
issue was one of the biggest challenges encountered when working with 3DCP.
Another significant problem was the print method used in this experiment. It was observed that the
printhead moved at varying speeds and heights, as described in section E.6.2. This inconsistency
resulted in cracking of the concrete. However, it is believed that larger 3D printing companies have
more stable printing techniques. The method in this thesis involved manually pouring the concrete
into the print head, whereas larger companies use a pump to extrude the concrete. These are issues
that cast concrete does not experience.
Despite the several advantages of 3DCP, such as using less material and offering more design
freedom, it still needs to address the reinforcement issues observed in this experiment. One possible
solution is the use of fiber reinforcement, which would make the bottom layer less vulnerable where
the tensile reinforcement is placed. Nevertheless, the effectiveness of this approach remains highly
dependent on the bond between the relevant layers.

8.5
Sources of Errors
In this section, a summary of the essential sources of errors that might have affected the test results
of all elements are investigated. First, it should be noted that the intent of this project has been
to ensure that as many variables as possible were kept consistent, such as testing parameters, mix
design, specimen design, etc. However, as previously mentioned, difficulties in consistency were
encountered, especially with the 3D printing method. Furthermore, it is expected to encounter
unexpected sources of errors, particularly when attempting to develop a new test setup and speci­
mens.
8.5.1
Biaxial Test of Unreinforced Concrete Interface
For the biaxial test of the unreinforced concrete interface, several sources of errors have been
observed, potentially impacting the results. Notably, more errors were observed in the 3D printed
specimens compared to the cast specimens. First, the common sources of errors are identified and
discussed, followed by a discussion of the errors unique to the 3D printed specimens.
Mutual Sources of Errors
The most distinctive sources of errors that are discussed in this segment are listed below.
• Simplified stress distribution
• Height difference of specimens
• Difference in applied vertical force, F
• Difference in curing time and method
• Inclusion of different failures
• Size factor
• Lack of specimen repetitions
First and foremost, the model of the stress distribution assumed, as seen in subsection 4.1.1, is a
simplification of how the stresses develop within the specimen. This assumption is made to facili­
tate ’hand calculations’ for designing the test setup and estimating the interface stresses. However,
the stress distribution for the test setup in this thesis is rather complex to predict, especially across
the interfaces as the PTG increases. An essential factor that might influence the stresses in the
interface is the surface quality, which is not thoroughly investigated in this report.
The small variance in the actual height of the specimens is a result of them being cut into the
desired geometry. The test setup is designed for a specific height (see Figure 4.1); therefore, any
deviation leads to a smaller contact area of the horizontal piston. This results in increased contact
stress, making failure more likely to occur through crushing rather than interface failure.

To compensate for this, wooden plates were placed under the specimens to better fit the height,
although this is not ideal as it lowers the system’s stiffness. However, only small deviations of a
few millimeters were observed, so the impact of this error is not considered highly significant.
The maximum recorded vertical force, Fmax, used for the calculation of the interface stresses, τ
and σ, were significantly larger than the intended vertical force, see the test reports in Appendix D.
This discrepancy is mainly because the vertical piston did not have time to react to the failure
and could not move away in time, thus creating more resistance. However, the applied vertical
force was constant during the test, and the sudden peak was relatively low compared to the applied
horizontal force, P, as seen in the stress­displacement curves in Appendix D. Fine­tuning of the
piston could have helped resolve this issue.
In order to ensure more stable results the curing time and methods could have been equalized
more both between respectively the cast and 3D printed specimens themselves, and especially
when comparing cast specimen results with 3D printed specimen results. The compressive strength
most likely could be more similar if the cast specimens not were submerged in water, hence e.g.
the strength reduction models of the cohesion, seen in subsection 8.3.2, for cast and 3D printed
specimens would be more comparable with each other, as cohesion is dependent on the compressive
strength, cf. [13]. Furthermore, the total curing days varied from 24­28 days for cast specimens and
22­27 for 3D printed specimens. The CS­2 specimens only had 11 days of water curing compared
to the 14 days for all other cast specimens. This may have caused some inconsistencies in the
results. However, seen from Table A.1 the compressive strengths did not vary too much.
The failures modes identified in subsection 8.2.1, that deviates from the intended failure mecha­
nism described in subsubsection 4.1.3, have been included in the results. However, no correlation
of not­intended failure modes and unusual results has been discovered, when investigating stress­
displacement curves, failure load etc.
In general, relying on the compressive strength of the cylinders when analyzing results with other
geometries, which has been done with the specimens, one has to take into account of size and
shape effect. It is known that cylinder compressive strengths are higher than for rectangular and
larger specimens [16]. Therefore, the interface strength will presumably be lower and a one to one
comparison between fc and c is most likely not reasonable.
When conducting experimental investigations with unreinforced concrete, large spread in data is
expected due to the concrete’s composition, which includes varying aggregate sizes and place­
ments. Therefore, for more accurate results, it is preferable to have more samples and more repe­
titions within the same type of specimens.

• Cutting of specimens

8.5.2
Four­point Bending Test of Reinforced 3D Printed Concrete Beam
The sources of errors stemming from the printing method previously mentioned has also been
observed for the 3D printed beam. Other sources of errors that are evaluated as the most prominent
and influencing for the results of the 3D printed beam are listed below.
• Non­ideal test setup
• Testing the same beam twice
• Reinforcement placement
• Beam surface is out of plane
• Small sample size
• Simplification of failure mechanism
The test setup for the 3D printed beam was not ideal, in that the most optimal geometry of the
setup to ensure an interface failure was not obtained. The tested span length should ideally have
been longer to create a larger distance between support and force, hence angle of the strut, which
makes the system more likely to result in an interface than a shear failure. However, as this distance
increases, the likelihood of a bending failure also increases, reaching a limit. If the test is to be
conducted again, adding more reinforcement would decrease the likelihood of bending failure. This
would allow the distance between support and force to increase, thus increasing the likelihood of
an interface failure. This setup may better demonstrate how the interfaces act, as they would be
more exposed than in the test conducted in this report.
The test of the 3D­printed beams was designed to test the same beam twice due to limited resources.
However, the first test resulted in a beam that was not suitable for a second test. The cause of this
is believed to be the accidental locking of the targeted support in the horizontal direction. This
changed the conditions for the static model, making it different from the assumed conditions.
This issue might have been avoided by using a longer beam, providing more room for a second

comparable to the other tests, as the damage to the tested area of the beam would have been less.
Additionally, increasing the beam length would increase the reaction in the support of the intended
failure compared to the other support.
Additionally, before the second test, the beam was unintentionally loaded, which may have dam­

A significant factor contributing to the failure of the 3D printed beam has been attributed to the
placement of the reinforcement. As the rebars are placed between the first and second layers, sepa­
ration failure of these two layers has been identified as one of the root causes of the beam’s failure,
rather than the intended interface failure. The placement of the rebars was limited by the printing
method, as they could only be placed on top of a newly cast layer. A more optimal placement would
have been in the middle of a layer to ensure better adhesion of the concrete interface, minimize the
zipper effect, and result in higher moment capacity. However, this is practically impossible for 3D
printing, another solution would be to use fiber reinforcement.
As a result of the printing method and the setting of concrete, the width varies along the height of
the 3D printed beams. This means the surface used for the DIC analysis is not perfectly in plane,
unlike traditionally cast concrete when cast in forms. Therefore, analyzing the strains and cracks
may have resulted in some unknown inconsistencies. Consequently, some skepticism is necessary
when assessing the DIC analysis. Furthermore, cracking of the concrete induces more deviation
in the evenness of the pictured surface.
A larger sample of specimens would have been more optimal to obtain a more accurate and reliable
understanding of how a 3D printed beam behaves in a four­point bending test in terms of cracking,
failure mechanisms, etc. However, as seen from the major strain figures in subsubsection 7.4.3,
the crack development is not significantly different from each other.
Most importantly, the assumption that the 3D printed beam’s failure mechanism follows the strut
and tie model must be addressed. The complexity of the setup, with the previously mentioned
sources of errors, and the introduced significant uncertainty regarding predicting the actual failure
mechanism of the 3D printed beam. Another type of strut or model of the failure mechanism
might have been introduced. Therefore, this simplified model must be taken into account when
investigating this matter further.

CHAPTER 9. CONCLUDING REMARKS
9
Concluding Remarks
This thesis set out to investigate unreinforced concrete interfaces through experiments, specifically
examining how a Print Time Gap (PTG) affects interface strength. For this purpose, interfaces
from cast and 3D Concrete Printed (3DCP) specimens were tested. The specimens were designed
to provoke a sliding failure in the interface in a biaxial compression test. The design is considered
effective as it produced consistent interface failures while being manageable and easy to produce.
The results from the specimens showed that the friction coefficient, µ, seemed to be independent
of the PTG. However, the 3DCP specimens exhibited a higher friction coefficient, likely due to
increased surface roughness.
Cohesion was found to be highly dependent on the PTG, with the first two hours of ’opening time’
being significantly critical, as the cohesion was less than 50 % of an entirely ’wet­on­wet’ interface.
The 3DCP specimens were found to have lower interface cohesion compared to the cast interfaces.
The rate of decline in interface cohesion over PTG was found to be approximately the same for
both cast and 3DCP interfaces. The theoretical cohesion based on compression strength, matched
well with the measured cohesion, indicating a possible strong correlation between the test cylinders
and the test specimens.
As part of the investigation of concrete interfaces, two 3DCP beams were produced and tested to
failure. The tests showed a similar bearing capacity to a theoretical monolithic counterpart.
A significant difference between the monolithic and 3DCP beams was observed in the crack de­
velopment analysis. In the bottom interface, large separation cracks were seen forming from the
first bending crack and extending outward towards the supports. This phenomenon was named the
’zipper effect,’ as the bending crack initiated a separation between the layers as soon as it reached
the interface. The bending cracks were also observed to shift horizontally when intersecting with
the first interface. Regarding shear cracks, they were observed to be similar to that of a monolithic
beam.
The stress on each interface in the beam was analyzed using a strut­and­tie model. However, the
results from this model did not align with the expected outcome. The model revealed negative
cohesion at the beam’s interface, suggesting that the applied strut­and­tie model oversimplifies the
beam’s actual behavior.
The test method and setup used to determine the interface strength were found to be effective for
unreinforced interfaces. The test setup were convenient and allowed for testing both cast and 3D­
printed interfaces on a large scale. Cast specimens proved to be a good alternative for 3D­printed
specimens, as they exhibited the same decline in interface strength.
There have been discrepancies between the cast and 3DCP elements. The curing methods dif­
fered between the cylinders for the cast and printed elements, resulting in varying compressive
strengths. These curing differences also applied to the test specimens used for determining the in­
terface strength. To address this, the curing conditions for the cast elements should have matched
those of the printed elements, allowing for a clearer understanding of the impact of curing condi­
tions.
Furthermore, no monolithic interface was tested for the 3DCP specimens, resulting in the absence
of a reference for the printed elements. To better evaluate the performance of the 3DCP beam, a
monolithic cast counterpart could also have been produced and tested.
An essential limitation was the number of specimens, particularly for the 3D­printed ones. This
was mainly due to the printing method.

CHAPTER 9. CONCLUDING REMARKS
9.1
Future Work
In this thesis, the impact of PTG is the only parameter tested, leaving many unanswered questions
about how other factors affect interface strength. These factors could include mix design, tem­
perature, moisture content, and printing method. Understanding the impact of these parameters is
crucial for designing and verifying a 3DCP structure.
Establishing a better link between the elements and structures is critical for verifying the behavior
of a 3DCP structure. Therefore, a more accurate model for verifying the stress in the interfaces of
a beam is needed. Additionally, testing an exact monolithic cast structural counterpart could better
assess the differences in structural performance and behavior.
In summary, this thesis has provided valuable insights into the PTG dependencies of a concrete
interface. A robust method for testing concrete interfaces has been designed, built, and tested.
While insights into larger structures with interfaces have been gained, they have also raised more
questions than answers.

APPENDIX B. BIAXIAL TEST OF UNREINFORCED CONCRETE INTERFACE
B
Biaxial Test of Unreinforced Concrete
Interface
B.1
Purpose
The purpose of the test is to determine the cohesion and friction coefficient of a concrete interface
with different print time gaps of the interface.
B.2
Test Setup
The specimens are made of two parts for both cast and printed elements. These two parts are made
at different times, with the time between each layer referred to as PTG (Print Time Gap). The
surface area between each layer is referred to as the interface. The interface has an angle of 17.35◦
from the horizontal. In figure B.1, the interface can be seen as the line that runs from the left side
to the right side.
The test is conducted using the SHY­058­Daeyoung test machine [3]. This machine has two axes,
allowing it to apply both a vertical and a horizontal force simultaneously. It is equipped with two
rolling supports, enabling the element to move freely during the test. A plate is positioned at the
vertical applied force so that the force acts only along the interface. For the horizontal force, the
reaction is also designed so that the vertical force is applied solely over the interface.
Below a photo of a element inside the machine can be seen.
Figure B.1: SHY­058­Daeyoung, DTU Lyngby Denmark
B.3
Test Procedure
The test specimens are inserted into the machine, and then the vertical force is applied until it
reaches a given load, which it will maintain for the rest of the test. Next, the horizontal force is
applied at a constant rate until the test is over. The vertical force is applied at a rate of the targeted
vertical force divided by 1.5 minutes until it reaches the targeted vertical force. The horizontal
force is applied at a rate of 1 mm/min. The displacement and load of both pistons are recorded five
times in a second (5 Hz).

APPENDIX C. FOUR­POINT BENDING TEST OF REINFORCED 3D PRINTED CONCRETE BEAM
C
Four­point Bending Test of Reinforced 3D
Printed Concrete Beam
C.1
Purpose
The purpose of the test is to establish a link between a smaller unreinforced concrete interface and a
larger structural element, such as a 3D­printed beam. The investigation focuses on whether there is
coherence between the cohesion found in the specimen and the cohesion in the beam. Additionally,
the test aims to observe how the interfaces behave in a beam subjected to bending.
C.2
Test Setup
The beam is made of four concrete layers, resulting in three interfaces. These interfaces have
different PTGs. The PTG for the first layer is 0.5 hours, while for the second and third layers, it
is 2 hours. The beam is 1800 mm long, 450 mm tall, and 175 mm wide. It is reinforced with two
Ø16 steel rebars.
The test setup is designed so each beam could be tested twice, once at each end. The beam and the
two static models for the test can be seen below.
Figure C.1: Drawing of beam and test setup.
The rebars are placed in the first interface. After the first layer has been printed, the rebar is placed
on top of the layer and pushed slightly into it, aiming for a depth of 8 mm (half the diameter). Then,
the second layer is printed on top of the rebar. The first layer with the rebar can be seen below.
Figure C.2: A full length 1st layer with rebar.

C.3
Test Procedure
The test was conducted on the SHY­012­Instron [4]. The machine is fitted for a four­point bending

During the test, the machine displacement, the load, and the beam’s vertical displacement at the

regular intervals. The test was performed with a constant piston movement of 0.5 mm/min and
was stopped manually. See the sketch of the test below.

E.5
Procedure
E.5.1
Cast Specimens
Casting
For the casting of the cast specimens the following procedure is carried out.
1. The materials are weighed according to the mix design, seen in Table E.2.
2. The materials are poured into the mixer in the following order: aggregates, half of the sand,
cement, limestone and lastly the rest of the sand. Start the mixer and let it mix in 1 minute.
3. The water are poured into the mixer and the mixer continues to mix in 3 minutes.
4. The cylinder forms are filled halfway and the cast specimen forms are filled halfway of the
layer, while they are being vibrated. Then the concrete are stamped with the stamper.
5. The cylinder forms are filled all the way up and the cast specimen forms are filled till the
whole layer is filled, while still being vibrated and stomped.
134
Experimental Investigation of Unreinforced Interfaces in Concrete Structures
==PAGE:146==

6. Excess concrete are scraped off with the cylinder top.
7. The cylinder forms are closed with clamps.
8. The cylinder specimens are placed indoor at approximately 20◦C overnight.
9. The next layer is being cast the same way as prescribed for the first layer after the relevant
time for the PTG.
10. The cylinder specimens are closed with clamps and the cast specimen are covered with plas­
tic and weights.
11. The cast specimens are placed indoor at approximately 20◦C overnight.

Demoulding and Curing
For the demoulding and curing of the cast specimens the following procedure is carried out.
1. The specimens are removed from their forms and the forms are cleaned with a putty knife.
2. Oil is applied to the forms and put back in its place.
3. The test specimens are submerged in 20◦C water for approximately 14 days for curing.
4. The test specimens are taken out of the water after approximately 14 days and placed indoor
in approximately 20◦C until tested.

E.5.2
3D Printed Specimens and Beams
Casting
For the casting of the 3D printed specimens and beams the following procedure is carried out.
The mixing of the materials and casting of cylinders are the same procedures as described for cast
specimens.
1. The wooden floor plate is placed under where the printer will print the concrete.
2. The printer head is filled with concrete continuously while the speed of the printer head is
controlled to ensure a good printing quality.
3. Immediately after the layer is printed, the concrete is cut out to fit 3 specimens (750 mm) 4
times, having a total of 12 cuts and a total length of the concrete of 3000 mm. New concrete
is mixed and this step is done 4 times to get 4 layers on top of each other, where the time
between layer 2 and 3 has to refer to the PTG aimed for. Layer 1 and 2 are cast in succession
aswell as layer 3 and 4.*
4. After the first layer, the rebars are placed on top of the layer and gently pushed downwards
to ensure som adhesion between the rebars and the concrete. New concrete is mixed and
layer 2 is cast after the relevant PTG as well as for layer 3 and 4.**
5. The 3D printed concrete stand in place for at least 14 days indoors at approximately 20◦C.
*Only relevant for 3D printed specimens. **only relevant for 3D printed beams.
