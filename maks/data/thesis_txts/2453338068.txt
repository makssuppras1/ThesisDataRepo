Abstract
In recent years the amount of textual data have increased a lot with more
and more websites in multiple languages. This does not only gives us access
to huge amounts of information but also makes it almost impossible to ﬁnd the
relevant information. Therefore a recent trend have become to develop statistical
models that ﬁnd the information for us but these models have up until recently
depended on annotated data. This enabled the models to work only on a few
common languages such as English, Spanish, German and Chinese. But since
annotating data is expensive researchers have begun to investigate in models
that can leverage all the unannotated data which exists not only for common
languages but also for low resource languages such as Danish.
Therefore this thesis will start by introducing the Question Answering problem
and setting. Then I motivate the use of question answering models in practice
and introduce a number of models using neural networks to solve this task.
First I introduce a baseline model and afterwards introduce a more recent at-
tempt to improve language models to enable them to solve multiple tasks within
a subset of natural language processing. The improvements heavily rely on the
advances introduced in the machine translation domain.
I hope that the results I present in this work will motivate and help other
researchers to build better language models and to further improve the work on
multilingual language modelling in general. Since we today are close to human
performance on many natural language tasks I hope that by contributing I could
inspire others to also focus their work on multilingual language models. In the
future I hope that one would be able to ask a question in their own language
and then the machine would ﬁnd the answer in articles on all languages and
translate the answer back to the same language as the question.
When performing the literature review I were motivated to construct more gen-
eral purpose models rather than models that only can leverage the amount of
labeled data available and also exploit the huge amounts of unlabeled data freely
available online. This further inspired med to construct a bilingual model since
I noticed that many labeled datasets is only available in English so therefore
I constructed a small labeled Danish Question Answering (similar to the way
SQuAD is constructed) dataset to evaluate our model on.
==PAGE:4==
ii
As the ﬁrst step I constructed a set of models. First a baseline DrQA which
relies on the prior work in Recurrent Neural Networks and then I introduce
the transformer model BERT which relies on a large number of parameters
and a long pretraining procedure. I show that the BERT model gives a great
performance on SQuAD (a F1 score of 81) even when down-scaled and with less
pretraining data than in [DCLT18].
Secondly I investigate recent attempts to improve the transformer model by
introducing Lightweight Convolution and Dynamic Convolution to replace the
Self Attention layers in the transformer model. It was previously shown that
these improvements increase the performance of the transformer model on the
translation task both in terms of quality of the translation and in terms of the
speed. However I show that these improvements doesn’t do as well in a long
term dependency setting.
Finally I tested the models ability to work in a bilingual setting when pretrained
on a joint dataset ie. mixing the data from both languages. To enable the model
to learn the bilingual representation I constructed a joint Byte-Pair-Encoding
vocabulary such that the model would have the ability to create a representation
for words in both languages.
I hope that my analysis of the BERT model, extensions and applications to
both the Question Answering task and the bilingual Question Answering task
will motivate others to conduct more research into the topic and enable people
to apply our model to their beneﬁt in the future. I also hope that researchers
in the near future will overcome the drop in performance on other languages
as I discovered when I transferred our model to run on other languages than
English.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Recently in the ﬁeld of natural language processing, deep learning has proved
to be eﬀective. Many tasks that were problematic is now solved due to the work
on recurrent models and more recently attention models.
Before the deep learning revolution natural language processing models were
mostly based on hidden Markov models and decision trees. But these models
suﬀered from the inability to learn long term dependencies which was partly
solved by using recurrent deep learning models. One of the problems that re-
quire long term contextual information is extractive question answering where
a model is given a context and a question and the task is to ﬁnd the answer
in the given context. Recurrent models have not been successful in solving this
eﬃciently therefore the development of the attention mechanism was an impor-
tant step in Natural Language Processing (NLP). Attention was successfully
applied to question answering models and gained a large boost in performance
but even then it still wasn’t performing very good. The later attempts to solve
general natural language processing problems have been focused on the embed-
dings which greatly improved the models performance in all subﬁelds of natural
language processing.
Today we are gathering and produce huge amounts of data and this makes it
almost impossible to keep track of information and ﬁnding the relevant infor-
mation therefore systems enabling this would be of great use both for saving
time but also to enable the user to ﬁnd the relevant information. In this thesis
I will be focused on solving the issue of ﬁnding the relevant information to a
question by creating a reading comprehension model (also know as a question
answering model) but due to time constraints/computing resources this thesis
will leave out the document retriever (ie the model that ﬁnds the relevant docu-
ments) and therefore I assume that we are already given the document/context

that the questions regards and that the document contains the answers to the
questions. Another system should therefore be used to gather the relevant doc-
uments.
Previously attempts to solve this problem involve using tf-idf (which ﬁnds a

In this thesis the reading comprehension model is further extended to be used
for low resource languages by only requiring plain text for each of the languages

1.2
Natural language processing
Natural language processing is the ﬁeld that works with all kinds of language
related problems. In this thesis I will work on question answering but related
tasks is described below to give an overview of related tasks since they are all
relevant and often the models used in similar tasks can be repurposed to other
NLP tasks.
1.2.1
Translation
The translation task is about translating one sentence from one language into
another language. It is often also referred to as machine translation since in
NLP it is always the machine that does the translation. Many of the recent
breakthroughs in NLP comes from an eﬀort to solve the translation task. This
is seen in [BCB14], [WSC+16], [VSP+17] and [AS18] that is all based upon
solving the translation task but the results from these articles was adopted into
other subﬁelds of NLP to solve other tasks. Therefore this task also have a lot
of benchmarks and almost every year a new dataset is released on this topic.

1.2.2
Question Answering
This task is a very important and hard task since this requires the model to
learn the relationship between two sentences in order for it to determine where
the answer is and if it exists in the given context.
Other types of question
answering tasks exist and I will further explain this in section 2.4.
1.2.3
Language Modelling
Language modelling is the task of predicting the next token whether it might be
a word, subword or a single character. This type of task is also sometimes called
text generation since it generates text based on a prompt (a small piece of text).
Recently it was discovered that this task serves as a great pretraining task for
general purpose NLP models (or multi-task NLP models). In [RNSS18] and
[DCLT18] they show the strength of this pretraining task and how much impact
it have on other tasks. Another importance of this task is that it is completely
unsupervised which is good since these days we have a lot of unlabeled data but
little labeled.
1.2.4
Sentiment Analysis
The sentiment analysis task is about classifying certain pieces of text either
sentences or whole documents. This is done to get a quick overview on a larger
piece of text and enabled us to either automatically detect the topic in a text
or if it was used in an online forum to detect if the person was writing about
some banned context.
1.3
Objective
The objective of this thesis is to construct a model that ﬁnds a mapping between
the question and context to the answer. I will start by developing a baseline
model and thereafter construct a generative model to further increase the per-
formance. The generative model will be trained in a semi supervised context
and I expect that it will construct an internal language representation which
should enable the model to be even better for question answering. And ﬁnally
I will train the generative model for multilingual question answering. In the
thesis I will work on the following objectives:

1.4
Structure
Natural language processing have recently gained a lot of attention and the
number of publications have increased a lot. The increased attention is due
to the widespread of the internet both on computers and mobile devices and
the ability for people to search for information but still today they are limited
by their language.
In this thesis I will attempt to make progress in a way
that would enable everyone to search in their own language for information
that might not be available in their native language. When the transformer
model from [VSP+17] was introduced it gained a lot of attention and it have
since then almost completely replaced the RNN models that people usually used
since the transformer model showed superior performance when pretrained with
a lot of data as they did in [RNSS18] and [DCLT18]. Both papers showed that
transformer models are multi purpose models when pretrained a lot and provided
state of the art results on almost all NLP tasks.
They thereby also showed
that the transformer model is capable of constructing a rich representation of
language in general and therefore the next task is to construct this representation
across languages.
Therefore I will in this thesis start by constructing a question answering model
by ﬁrst looking at a baseline model for comparison to previous models. Af-
terwards I will use the new model BERT developed by [DCLT18] and train it
on my own data. I will then investigate similar models and its possibilities to
improve the transformer model.
Finally I will further improve the BERT model to work in a bilingual setting (two
languages). This will be done by gathering some Danish data from Wikipedia
and merging it with the English data to thereby train a joint model on both
languages without the model being aware that it is trained on two languages.
This would allow the model to create a joint representation for both languages
and thereby an universal representation. I expect that this will enable us to
create a good question answering model in a zero-shot setting.

2.1
Types of learning
For classiﬁcation problems there exist two types of learning methods:
• Supervised Learning: Training a model where we are supplied with
both the input data xi for i ∈[1, N] and a set of correct labels for each
of the samples in the input yi where yi ∈[1, C] where C is the number
of classes. The purpose of the training is then to learn a mapping f such
that f(xi) ≈yi. E.g. The model is given a picture of a dog and is asked
what is on the picture? Then we are also given the correct answer namely
it’s a dog to train the model to notice this.
• Unsupervised Learning: Training a model where we are only given the
input data xi but not the labels. The purpose of this type of model is
then to learn a mapping f that separates the data into C clusters. E.g.
We have some measurements from our customers and we want to produce
two sizes in T-shirts then we need to separate our data into two clusters
such that we can ensure that the sizes we create ﬁts all our customers.
• Selfsupervised Learning: A subset of unsupervised learning where we
in this case are given the labels from the input itself.
This could for
example be we wanted to predict the next word in a sequence given the
previous words ie. we are given xi for i ∈[1, n] and we want to learn a
mapping such that we can predict xn+1 which we also know.

further improve the performance of the model. I will use supervised learning

2.3.1

Masked language modelling (MLM) is a more recent attempt to solve the prob-
lem from language modelling by giving the model access to future words in
the sequence and was introduced in [DCLT18]. When the model gets access to
future words it is important not to leak information such as the current word

10% of the time the tokens is replaced with another token and 10% of the time
the token is kept. This is speciﬁcally designed such that the model would see all
tokens during training and keep the model biased towards the actual observed
word. Although this approach enabled us to train a bidirectional model (ie.
both look into past and future words) this also comes at a price. First this
creates a mismatch between the pre-training stage and the actual training since
the [MASK] token would not appear in the training data. Another downside is
that training a MLM model as opposed to a LM model is much slower since it
only predict tokens 15% of the time and therefore needs more steps to converge.
But increasing the number of tokens to predict would also heavily increase the
task complexity and therefore make it almost impossible to solve.

Next sentence prediction
Another task that [DCLT18] introduced is the next sentence prediction where
the language model is turned into a binary classiﬁcation model and is asked
that given two sentences how likely is it that the ﬁrst sentence is followed by the
second sentence. They show that this is very important for question answering

and therefore ensures that the model doesn’t always predict one of the labels.
The purpose of this task is to introduce the model to the relationship between
two sentences and thereby a building block for tasks like Question Answering
and Natural Language Inference.
2.4
Finetuning for Question Answering
Question Answering (QA) is the task of ﬁnding an answer to a given question.
There exists many types of QA tasks and they can be divided into the following:
1. In context QA. Ie. ﬁnding the answer within the given context. It is often
assumed that the answer exists in the context.
2. Answer selection. Choosing an answer given a set of possible answers.
3. Knowledge based QA. Finding an answer in the knowledge base (KB).
Here the task also consists of ﬁnding the right document in the KB.
In this thesis I have decided to work with the context QA and it is assumed
that the answer always exists in the context but it could easily be extended to
tasks that doesn’t require the answer to be in the context. Even though the
other tasks could be relevant as well I decided not to work with these due to
the limited time of this project.

Input encoding
There exists mainly three diﬀerent encoding types for language models.
• Word encoding. Encodes each word into an integer after applying some
tokenization.
• Character encoding. Encodes each character into an integer after applying
some tokenization.
• Byte-Pair encoding. Encodes each token into an integer after applying
some tokenization.
Each of the encoding schemes will be explained in this section.
2.9.1
Word encoding
A word encoding translates a word into an integer. One of the great advan-
tages of word encoding models is that it keeps the vector representation of the
sequences short (which is good computationally) but it comes with some disad-
vantages. One of the disadvantages is that all misspelled words would result in
a diﬀerent word encoding eg. if king = 1 then kung ̸= 1. Another disadvantage
is that the word encoding requires a large vocabulary to contain all words in
the data.
2.9.2
Character encoding
A character encoding translates each character into an integer. This enables the
model to account for misspellings by correctly interpret the two words spelled
slightly diﬀerent as the same word.

But such an encoding also comes with a cost and that is that
the vector representation of sequences is much longer since each character is
represented as an integer in the sequence especially if the sentence contains
many long words.
2.9.3
Byte-Pair encoding
Byte-Pair encoding (BPE) is an encoding method that is adapted from data
compression [SHB15]. The algorithm starts by initializing a vocabulary of char-
acters and each word is represented as a sequence of characters plus a special
end of word token. Then each symbol pair is counted iterativly and each oc-
currence of the most frequent pair is replaced with the joint characters. Eg. if
A and B was the most frequent pair it would be replaced with AB. Each of the
merge of characters represent a character n-gram. Frequent character n-grams
is then merged into a single symbol.
The algorithm can be run on any text after some simple preprocessing steps. The
preprocessing includes counting each word and collecting words into a dictionary
weighted by the frequency of each of the words. The Byte-Pair encoding takes
advantages from both the word encoding and the character encoding by having
no out of vocabulary problems and still it can represent long sentences using
either whole words or common subword tokens.

3.1.3
Prediction
After both the question and the document have been encoded this is fed into two
classiﬁers: the ﬁrst predicts the start of the answer index and the second predicts
the end of answer index. These classiﬁers are bilinear layers that capture the
similarity between the encoded question vector q and the encoded document pi.
These are computed as follows:
Pstart(i) ∝exp(piWsq)
Pend(i) ∝exp(piWeq)
To get the start and end of answer they take the argmax of the two functions
and ﬁnd the best span where Pstart(i) × Pend(i′).
3.2
BERT
BERT stands for Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers and
was ﬁrst introduced in [DCLT18]. Essentially BERT is taking the Transformer
Encoder from [VSP+17] and using more layers and pre-training it in a novel
way.
The transformer is build upon the encoder-decoder structure that used in [BCB14]
which is a structure that ﬁrst uses one model to encode the sequence into either
a sequence of vectors or a single vector which is then fed into the decoder that
relies on the encoded sequence to complete whatever task it is set to do.
Typically these types of encoder-decoder setups is built for machine translation
where the encoder is fed the sentence in one language and then the decoder have
to decode the sentence into another language. The normal setup of encoder-
decoders uses RNN’s (recurrent neural networks) to encode the variable length
sequences into a latent vector which is fed to the decoder which typically is also
a RNN that then translates the sentence.
The transformer model replaces the RNN’s with something called Multi-Head
Attention which builds upon Self-attention from [LFdS+17]. Multi-Head At-
tention is eﬀectively Self-attention that is projected h times.

3.2.1
Transformer encoder
The encoder of the original transformer model used N blocks where each block
in the encoder consists of two sub-blocks: the ﬁrst is a self-attention block and
the second is a feed-forward block. The feed-forward block which is essentially
two linear layers with a ReLU activation in between and is computed as follows:
FFN(x) = ReLU(W1x + b1)W2 + b2
where W1 ∈Rd×dff , W2 ∈Rdff ×d and d & dff are hyperparameters to be
set. The two sub-blocks are both surrounded by residual connections and layer
normalization. The transformer encoder also uses an embedding layer that in-
troduces the position of the word in the sequence. This is very useful since the
self-attention doesn’t know where the word comes from only that it is somewhere
in the sequence. This embedding is called positional encoding and is added to
a normal embedding layer. The positional embedding from the original trans-
former paper [VSP+17] use a ﬁxed positional embedding that is computed as
follows:
PEpos,2i = sin(pos/100002i/d)
PEpos,2i+1 = cos(pos/100002i/d)
where pos is the position and i is the dimension. But [DCLT18] uses a learned
positional embedding instead since it showed to be more powerfull. For tokeniza-

a split is denoted with "##".
3.2.2
BERT Pre-Training
The diﬀerence between BERT and the Transformer encoder is that BERT uses
more transformer blocks, more neurons and a learned positional embedding
instead of the ﬁxed positional embedding the Transformer uses. Other changes
are that BERT is pretrained in a special way that enabled it to be SOTA (state
of the art results) for many NLP-tasks. BERT is pretrained in a language model
(LM) fashion ie. its task is to predict missing words in sentences. But since
the classical way of doing LM is to predict the next word in a sentence is not
an eﬃcient way of learning, BERT uses something called Masked LM and Next
Sentence Prediction. The two methods is described in more detail in section 2.3.

Chapter 6
Discussion
In this thesis I introduced a collection of supervised and semi-supervised models
and applied them to a question answering task to test the architectural designs
and training methods.
Furthermore it was investigated if zero shot transfer
learning could be applied such that I could use the same model for multiple
languages in a question answering context. Now I will discuss the pros and cons
of the diﬀerent methods and pretraining variants.
6.1
Supervised vs. Semi-supervised
One of the focus areas of this thesis was to investigate if I could employ unsuper-
vised pretraining to further increase the performance of the models. This gave
us an opportunity to look into some architectural designs that allowed for this
type of pretraining. Although not all types of architectures was explored since
this would be too time consuming. One model I could have explored more is a
RNN model used in a generative setting but this was left out. Therefore this
thesis focuses on a baseline model namely the DrQA (which have proved very
eﬃcient in terms of performance vs. number of parameters but relies heavily
on manually crafted features) and a transformer architecture which previously
have proved to get a great performance although with a high parameter count.
Below is some of the listed pros for the semi-supervised models:
• Huge amount of unlabeled data is available
• Models can easily be extended to other natural language processing tasks
• Pretrained models require very few epochs to converge for other NLP tasks
• Builds an internal representation of language
==PAGE:60==
48
Discussion
And the cons is listed below:
• Semi-supervised/Generative models require a lot of parameters to enable
it for transfer learning
• The models require more training time in total however it doesn’t require
much ﬁne-tuning time for new tasks
Based on the above listed pros and cons I suggest using semi-supervised models
due to their transferability and if the pretrained models is published it requires
little work for others to ﬁne-tune the models for their task at hand.
I also
suggest to further increase the size of the semi-supervised models to enable them
to further improve the performance and the underlying language representation.
6.2
Multilingual transfer learning
Another focus area was to explore the ability of the semi-supervised models
to transfer the knowledge in one language into another language to thereby
not requiring any supervised data in the foreign language. This was explored
by training a semi-supervised model with unlabeled data in both (English and
Danish in this work) languages and afterwards ﬁne-tuning the model in English
and then testing the model in Danish.
The only unlabeled data that the model requires is some data for each of the
languages but today this is not a problem since much data is available on the
internet and this data doesn’t have to have some special structure. The only
requirement for the data is that it contains multiple coherent sentences but this
doesn’t seem to be a problem when exploring the internet. Luckily Wikipedia
contains a lot of languages and is free to download.

Eﬀect of the pretraining dataset
It was also explored if the pretraining dataset had an performance eﬀect on the

6.4
Eﬀect of the kernel size
It was discovered during training that the kernel size of the Lightweight convolu-
tion and Dynamic convolution BERT was especially diﬃcult to get right. They
both had a very limited performance which was quite unexpected although these
models were developed for translation and therefore not requiring long term de-
pendencies. I expect that this is due to the limited context window since the
convolution models doesn’t have the ability to look everywhere. Even though
it should have the ability to propagate the data through since the model have
multiple layers with increasing kernel size. Some of the results is listed below:
• Large kernel sizes is essential for the Question Answering task due to the
long term dependencies
• The receptive ﬁeld is not a good indicator of the models ability to handle
long term dependencies
After further experiments it seems that when the input is padded to the maxi-
mum sequence length the Lightweight and Dynamic convolution models fail to
get the answer right. So it seems that the convolutional models have a hard
time looking past the paddings and into the early parts of the input to ﬁnd the
answer. In the experiments I used the same sentence and increased the padding

When we look at the receptive ﬁeld which for the large model is 493 (calculated

Future Work
In this project I introduced the question answering task and some diﬀerent
architectures to solve the task. Some architectures relied on manually crafted
features while others relied on a large number of unlabeled texts. It turned out

In order for the models to improve even further on other languages it would have
been great to incorporate more data in Danish and use more of the English data
that was available but not used since it would require too much time to train
the models on. I expect that using more data would further enable the model

7.3
Larger models
Due to the limited computing power available on the DTU Compute GPU server
I decided to work on smaller models to be able to run the models in a reasonable
time-frame and to be able to ﬁt the model on the GPU. Therefore I would expect
that larger models would get an even better performance due to the ability to
learn a more complicated internal representation and also more details about
the topics encountered during pretraining.
7.4
Memory Transformer
Recently a team from Facebook AI released [LSR+19] a model that incorporates
an external memory into the BERT model by replacing a few of the linear layers
with a memory layer. The model works by constructing a large number of keys
and two queries that then performs k-nearest neighbours to ﬁnd the nearest
keys which maximize the inner product with the query. These are then used to
lookup in the memory and ﬁnd the corresponding values (memory slots). They
showed that it not only improves the performance of the model but also enabled
them to use halve the number of layers and still get the same performance
and thereby also halving the running time. Since this was released recently 10,
July, 2019 I didn’t have time to implement this and train the model before the
deadline of this thesis. Although I would expect an increased performance both
in terms of F1 score and in inference speed.
7.5
Unsupervised Question Answering
Also another team from Facebook AI released a new method to train a model for
Question Answering in an unsupervised way [LDR19]. They ﬁrst ﬁnd a random
context from one of the documents then they create an answer for that context
using either a noun or a named entity from the context as the answer. Then
==PAGE:65==
7.5 Unsupervised Question Answering

given the answer and the context they generate a question by a two step method
that ﬁrst generates a cloze question (a statement where the answer is marked
with a blank) and then they use some diﬀerent methods to construct an actual
question from the cloze question (this method is either an identity mapping, a
random ordering of the cloze prepended with a wh* word, a rule-based system
or a seq2seq model). This enabled the team to get an okay F1 score (56.4)
on SQuAD purely based on unsupervised data and not using the labeled data
available. I hypothesize that this could be used for language pairs that either
have no labeled data available or a very small amount available and therefore it
could have been very useful for this work.

the model. It might be due to the model is already saturated when using the

It was also discovered that the use of unsupervised pretraining of a generative
model improves the performance a lot and it replaces the need for manually
constructed features and thereby improves the generalizability of the model. I
found that using attention improves over RNN’s by giving the model access to
all tokens at every layer and thereby preventing the model from forgetting early
words late in the sentence. This did not only improved the performance of the
model but also enabled semi-supervised training and thereby the usage of the
huge amounts of unlabeled data that is available on the internet and the labeled
dataset we have available. I did also experiment with the same model but only
trained in a supervised manner (ie no pretraining) and this did not achieve the
same performance as the semi-supervised generative model. So in this thesis I
have shown the strength of unsupervised pretraining.
It was discovered that even though the ligthweight convolution and dynamic
convolution is faster than BERT they can’t match the performance on question
answering tasks. Dynamic convolution should incorporate some temporal infor-
mation however it seems that this is not enough but it is shown that increasing
the kernel sizes helps to improve the performance. After more experiments it
turns out that the amount of padding after the sentences matter since when
there is a lot of padding the convolution models have a hard time looking at the
ﬁrst part of the input so if the answer is in the ﬁrst part these models doesn’t
select the correct answer.
It is commonly known that increasing the model size helps to improve the per-
formance of the models a lot but since the available GPU memory was limited
and time was also limited I used the maximum size available. The time con-
straint was set to a maximum of 3 days of pretraining and afterwards 4 hours for
ﬁne-tuning since this enabled faster prototyping, debugging and hyperparam-
eter tuning. However if one were to use the Memory transformer model that
[LSR+19] introduced this would enable us to probably get the same performance
in terms of F1 score but with a much smaller model and thereby also a lot faster
model.
Furthermore it was shown that adding Danish unlabeled data and increasing the
vocabulary with some Danish words enabled the model for zero shot transfer to
a Danish version of the SQuAD task and still getting a great performance. I
notice that ﬁne-tuning the pretrained model on a small Danish version of SQuAD
does not improve the performance and I hypothesize that this is due to the
training data being too small (5 samples) but it might also be due to the learning
rate being to high or low to get any performance gains.
