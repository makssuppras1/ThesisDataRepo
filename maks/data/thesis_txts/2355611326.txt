Abstract
Facing great energy challenges over the next few decades, we must quickly ﬁnd ways to
utilize the renewable energies. Harvesting the sunlight and converting it to electricity is a
plausible way to do this. But the energy supply is intermittent and the excess amounts of
energy produced must therefore be stored in chemical bonds. Using renewable electricity
to split water for hydrogen and then hydrogenate CO2 to organic fuels that can use the
existing infrastructure is a good option.
To understand the catalytic properties of CO2RR (or CORR) on copper, a size-dependent
study on copper nanoparticles is begun. This leads to a promising design of a CO-TPD
cell added to our cluster source to allow characterization the reactivity of later produced
nanoparticles directly by probing with CO. During testing of the nanoparticles, a bet-
ter cleaning protocol for the substrates was developed and a transient methane product
formation was observed for the ﬁrst time.

The energy challenge
To present date, the world population has just about reached 7.5 billion peole – an increase
of one billion people over the last twelve years.1 The global total energy consumption of
2015 reached 13.15 billion tonnes oil equivalent2 corresponding to an average worldwide
energy consumption of 17.5 TW. This is estimated to rise to 30 TW by 2050 – that is,
almost a doubling of the current energy consumption.3 The larger part of this amount
of energy is generated by the burning of fossil fuels which release the greenhouse gas
carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere along with other pollutants like CO, NOx,
and SOx. With the projected increase of the energy demand, aptly named the ”terawatt
challenge”4, the estimated oil reserves available will last about a century, the same being
true for the natural gas reserves. Other gas and coal reserves are estimated to potentially
sustain several hundred years, but are much less energy eﬃcient and pollute more per
energy content. The concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere equilibrates with the near-
surface oceans within a few decades. The remaining amount of CO2 requires on the order
of a thousand years to vanish because it has no natural breakdown mechanisms in the
atmosphere*. Removal of the remaining anthropogenic CO2 is thus reduced to mixing by
convection – a scale dominated by the mixing of the near-surface ocean layers and the deep
ocean.3 The amount of CO2 released within the next decades will therefore accumulate
with levels already surpassing the natural cycle of the Earth for the last 650,000.5,6 The
exact consequences of these new high levels of CO2 is extremely diﬃcult to predict given the
highly chaotic nature of the climate system and the lack of previous experience to correlate
cause and eﬀect. Despite these uncertainties, the Paris agreement recently demonstrated
that not only is it the general consensus that the continued emission of greenhouse gases
can not be left uncontrolled, but that action must be taken. It is argued now that clean
energy has gained an irreversible momentum.7,8
Meeting the terawatt challenge and maintaining a high living standard in the future
in any sustainable way requires harvesting the energy oﬀered to us by the sun. Fusion
might surpass this claim one day, but the time range for that perspective is on a scale
comparable with the one doubling the worldwide energy consumption. Fission technology

exists today and can provide a constant supply of energy regardless of the state of the
weather, but it can not be scaled to single-handedly supply the energy demand by 2050.9
The amount of energy used in a year corresponds roughly to one and a half hours of
full sunlight and the technology for harvesting this energy exists today. Though the full
argument and implications of course are much more complex, covering ”only” 0.16 % of
the total land surface with 10 % eﬃcient solar cells would provide 20 TW of power.10 All
plants from massive trees to smaller water chestnuts harness the energy of the sun with
a conversion eﬃciency of only 0.5 % converting CO2 into chemical fuels. All grow by this
process. Herbivores devour these plants with only 10 % eﬃciency, while carnivores devour
the herbivores at an ever further eﬃciency loss of a tenth. Artiﬁcially utilizing the energy
of the sun – be it through solar panels or wind turbines – with an average conversion
eﬃciency higher than what the nature oﬀers on its own is the most sustainable way to
meet the energy demands of the near future. Given the intermittency of the sun and
wind, we need ways to store the excess electrical power produced at peak periods, so it
can be redistributed at periods where the production is lower than the demand. Battery
technology by itself is unfeasible for upscaling to the terawatt regime due to the scarcity of
materials often involved11 and the low energy density. Some places can use excess power
to pump water from one reservoir to a reservoir at a greater altitude thus storing the
energy as mechanical energy. The areas with this possibility is very limited and can only
be exploited locally like in geothermal areas. It seems agreed upon that the sustainable
solution in the foreseeable future will be to store the energy in the chemical bonds of
fuels.3,12–14
Given that a new energy infrastructure requires lots of both energy and time, we need to
start implementations sooner rather than later. An ensemble of energy technologies com-
plementing the strengths of each other will likely provide the full solution where individual
energy technologies would fall short.11
The need for catalysts
To speed up the transition, the renewable technologies must become economically viable
in order to outcompete fossil fuels in a way that is still convenient for our society — this
is just beginning to show its evidence in recent years.8 Storing the energy in hydrogen
bonds is especially attractive given the huge abundancy of hydrogen in the form of water
in the oceans.
Another element of abundance is nitrogen constituting 79 % of the air
we daily breathe. However, breaking these bonds to form new bonds for storing energy
is not easy. If it was easy, there would not be so big an abundance of these elements
and the resulting chemical fuels would store less energy.
The Haber-Bosch process of
hydrogenating atmospheric nitrogen with an eﬃciency of 50 % using a metal catalyst is a
century old and is today responsible for almost all production of ammonia. This constitutes
a few percent of the world’s energy consumption and is the reason a high global population
can be sustained without covering most of the useful land areas with farming.source? This
industrialization was only made possible by the discovery of the catalyst allowing the high
eﬃciency for the reaction.
A catalyst is something that catalyzes a reaction to make it proceed faster. Stable
2
==PAGE:13==
molecules are stable because they are in a state with low energy and will only tend to react
if the ﬁnal state oﬀers an even lower energy. Even then, a reaction might be slow because
the spatial changes necessary to go through would bring the electronic conﬁguration into a
too energetically unfavorable state — it has a high activation energy. An eﬀective catalyst
mediates this by oﬀering shortcuts in the reaction. The molecule may bind temporarily to
the surface of the catalyst thereby changing its chemical state which will lower activation
energies to some potential reaction steps and raise it for others. For this reason, a catalyst
can aﬀect the selectivity of a reaction by making some reaction steps more energetically
favorable than others. A few important terms used later are
- The activity of a catalyst is a measure of how good it speeds up a reaction and is
thus measured as the throughput or produced amount of reaction products per unit
time per unit catalyst.
- The selectivity of a catalyst refers to the distribution of reaction products. This is
measured as the partial activity with respect to a certain reaction product compared
to the total activity.
- The reactivity of a catalyst is a measure of how strongly it binds reactants, interme-
diates, or products.
Changing the reactivity of a catalyst may therefore inﬂuence both the activity and selectiv-
ity of the catalyst. Controlling the reactivity is therefore necessary to have a good catalyst
to make eﬃcient reactions that can compete commercially with existing technologies.
The pursuit for better catalysts
The ﬁrst step towards controlling is understanding. Well-characterized single crystals have
served to make detailed studies to enhance the understanding of the principles of catalysis.
Mapping the activity of a catalyst as function of the reactivity exhibits a maximum since
a too reactive catalyst will bind e.g. the reactants too tightly so they will not desorb again
or react further, or the surface is too noble so the reactants will not bind to the surface in
the ﬁrst place. The optimum catalyst lies somewhere in between the two cases (Sabatier’s
principle). With density functional theory (DFT), it was found that the d-band center of
the transition metals is a good descriptor of the reactivity and that the adsorption energy
of adsorbates scale linearly with this.15,16 This knowledge has since been used succesfully
to predict new and improved catalysts.17,18
In the splitting of water to produce hydrogen, platinum is the best pure metal catalyst
far surpassing any others. As platinum is very expensive and scarce, alloys of platinum
have been made to increase the activity and thereby reduce the loading of catalyst re-
quired to overcome the heavy overpotentials related to the scaling relations of the oxygen
evolution reaction.19 The active phase can in some cases be attributed to an outer shell
formation of pure platinum which would have a diﬀerent reactivity due to a strain induced
by the mismatch of lattice constants.20 This was then controlled with the ”lanthanide con-
traction” by using alloying with diﬀerent lanthanides and actinides to control the level of
strain.21 Based on pure platinum nanoparticles, it has been estimated that 30 % of the
3

world’s annual platinum production could be utilized in photoelectrochemical systems to
produce 1 TW worth of hydrogen.22 The hydrogen economy is thus not entirely out of
reach as of now. However, hydrogen is diﬃcult to store and needs an altered infrastruc-
ture from what we have now. Hydrogen produced from excess renewable energy could
thus instead be used directly to hydrogenate other elements.
The role of copper
Carbon dioxide can be converted into chemical fuels that can be used in the existing infras-
tructure while simultaneously closing the anthropogenic CO2 emission.23 The reduction of
CO2 has many diﬀerent reaction products, which makes it diﬃcult to distinguish between
the individual reaction pathways and ﬁnd selectivity to only one of them.24,25 Of the many
products, methanol and higher order hydrocarbons and alcohols are the most interesting
products as storage for energy. Of the pure metals, copper is the only catalyst showing
any signiﬁcant selectivity towards these.24 Examples have been shown of several catalysts
enabling an eﬃcient and practical reduction of CO2 to CO which is the ﬁrst reduction step
of CO2RR.26–28 Therefore mapping of the reaction pathways can be simpliﬁed a little by
studying the carbon dioxide reduction reaction through the carbon monoxide reduction
reaction (CORR).
In understanding the reactivity of copper, experiments have been done on well-deﬁned
surfaces to correlate activity and selectivity with the morphology of the surface.29,30 Reg-
ular planar copper electrodes will not make the conversion of CO2 to higher hydrocarbons
eﬃciently.24,25,30–32 A new type of copper electrode, oxide-derived copper (OD-Cu), was
prepared by reduction of Cu2O foil and proved to be eﬃcient in catalyzing the formation
of higher-carbon products.33 This instigated the identiﬁcation of an intermediate product
that is hard to detect,34 while CO-TPD experiments on OD-Cu identiﬁes the presence of
strong binding sites which may be supported by grain boundaries.35 The high roughness
of the OD-Cu surface makes it a diﬃcult model system in which to correlate reactivity
with activity and selectivity. Studying model systems of well-deﬁned size-selected copper
nanoparticles might support the bridge between well-deﬁned ﬂat or stepped surfaces and
the highly porous surfaces like oxide-derived copper. Mapping the reactivity of this system
could be a great step towards understanding the reaction pathways of CORR/CO2RR.
The object of this thesis
In this project, I keep a focus on making a well-deﬁned model system of size-selected
nanoparticles. These are made in the cluster source in the SurfCat group and ﬁltered by
mass using a lateral time-of-ﬂight mass ﬁlter. To map out the reactivity of these samples, I
will design a cell from scratch to be connected to the cluster source to do CO temperature-
programmed desorption (CO-TPD) on the samples made of nanoparticles under the same
ultra-high vacuum conditions the nanoparticles are synthesized in. Correlating this with
other characterization techniques like X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), ion scat-
tering spectroscopy (ISS), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and with the catalytic
properties measured with electrochemical mass spectroscopy (EC-MS) and electrochemi-

2.2
Production of Mass-Selected Nanoparticles
Mass-selected nanoparticles are produced in a UHV cluster source (Nanobeam2011, Birm-
ingham Instruments) consisting of a magnetron sputter head, an aggregation zone, a set of
electromagnetic lenses before a mass ﬁlter (MF), a lateral time-of-ﬂight mass ﬁlter (TOF-
MF), and a second set of electromagnetic einzel lenses connecting the cluster source to the
Omicron Multiprobe system in the preparation chamber. The cluster source is highlighted
in the top of ﬁgure 2.
In the middle part of ﬁgure 2, the principle of nanoparticle formation by gas-aggregation
is sketched. The magnetron sputter head is inserted into the aggregation zone which is
cooled by liquid nitrogen (LN2). Argon gas ﬂows around the target – solid block of the
metal or alloy you want your nanoparticles to consist of – and ﬂows through the aggregation
zone with an absolute pressure in the order of 0.01 to 0.5 mbar. The argon gas is partly
ionized and a concentric ring of permanent magnets behind the target coupled with a
negative applied potential means that the argon ions are continuously accelerated into
the target. By further collisions with neutral argon atoms, these may in turn be ionized
and the electron separated from the ion. Thereby a cascade eﬀect occurs producing a
plasma localized around the surface of the target depending on the conﬁguration of the
magnets in the magnetron sputter head. The continuous sputtering of the surface of the
target releases mostly single atoms from the target into the aggregation zone where they
aggregate into larger particles. A partially laminar ﬂow through the aggregation zone leads
the continuously forming nanoparticles through an exit aperture where the ﬂow undergoes
a supersonic expansion and the laminar part of the ﬂow is captured with a biased skimmer.
Haberland et al.37 states that the aggregation from single atoms is facilitated by metal
dimer formation. Due to energy and momentum conservation laws, the dimer formation
can not be facilitated by a two-body collision alone, but requires a three-body collision.
Hence, the argon gas cooled by interaction with the cooling shroud plays three important
roles: 1) It creates single atoms in the aggregation zone, 2) It supports metal dimer
formation leading to aggregation of nanoparticles by facilitating three-body collisions, and
3) It thermally equilibrates the aggregated nanoparticles. Helium can be introduced in the
aggregation zone from the side as illustrated in ﬁgure 2. Being light, the helium will not
contribute much to the ﬁrst point of sputtering the target, but will alter the aggregation
conditions by contributing to point two and three. The introduction of helium from the
side likely produces some diﬀerent kinds of ﬂows as compared to a pure argon gas ﬂow
introduced from behind the target. The nanoparticles continue to grow in size while in
the aggregation zone, and the main feature governing the mass distribution seems to be
the time spent in the aggregation zone. Various features can be changed to control the
mass proﬁle, and a few is listed here
- Increasing the ﬂow speed of the carrier gas
- Introducing helium into the carrier gas changes the mass proﬁle by the argon-to-
helium ratio
9
==PAGE:20==
- Changing the size of the exit aperture will alter the pressure thereby inﬂuencing the
ﬂow rate and the mean free path of the gases
- Changing the sputter power aﬀects both the current and the mass proﬁle
- Changing the length of the aggregation zone changes the residence time in the ag-
gregation zone
Only about one in every thousand atoms produced from sputtering is charged.37 How-
ever, afterglow from the sputter discharge and collisions with ionized rare gases will eﬀec-
tively charge a large fraction of the nanoparticles (30 - 80 % in the slightly diﬀerent setup
of Haberland et al.). This is the great advantage of the magnetron sputter head principle:
with a high sputter yield and subsequent heavy ionization, it is possible to produce intense
and continuous beams of charged nanoparticles. The resulting beam will contain a wide
range of masses, though, and a mass ﬁlter is typically put in the path of ﬂight to select
only well-deﬁned sections of the beam.
The principle of mass-selection by the lateral time-of-ﬂight mass ﬁlter (TOF-MF) is
sketched in the bottom part of ﬁgure 2. A slice of the entering beam is selected with an
entrance aperture. At the far end of the entrance aperture, a faraday cup array can be
used to measure the current of nanoparticles in the beam entering the TOF-MF. A set of
electromagnetic lenses including an X-Y deﬂector are put between the skimmer and the
entrance aperture. These can be used to focus the beam of nanoparticles using the faraday
cup array. A pulsed potential is applied between the bottom plate and center plate which
accelerates the particles upward according to their mass-to-charge ratio, m/z. Particles
with a small m/z will be accelerated faster than particles with a high m/z. This way, the
nanoparticles are spatially separated according to their masses, and the range of masses
able to pass through the grid between the center plates depends on the duration of the
pulsed potential. When the particles have passed the center plates and entered the top
region, a pulse identical to the accelerating pulse is applied between the top plate and the
center plate to decelerate the particles back to their original path of ﬂight. Only now,
the path of ﬂight has been displaced and the nanoparticles are sorted spatially according
to mass. By changing the conditions for which a given mass is able to pass through the
mass ﬁlter, a spectrum of the masses in the original beam of nanoparticles entering the
TOF-MF can be obtained. An exit aperture in the top region selects a narrow range of
masses to be transmitted beyond the TOF-MF. Issendorf and Palmer38 report a 50 %
transmission of mass-selected nanoparticles and clusters by optimizing the design of the
TOF-MF and the sequences of acceleration/deceleration pulses. This type of mass ﬁlter
oﬀers a better mass resolution and higher transmission of charged particles than a more
conventional quadropole mass ﬁlter (QMF).39 Furthermore, there are no restrictions on
the range of masses it is able to select. On the other hand, with the QMF exists the
possibility of disabling the mass ﬁlter to allow an intense beam of all the formed masses to
pass to the substrate. The lateral TOF-MF can not be simply disabled in this way since
it physically changes the path of ﬂight of the beam.
Until now, all particles have been assumed to carry only a single charge, but a small
fraction of the nanoparticles will be double-charged.
Since the TOF-MF ﬁlter masses
10
==PAGE:21==
by their mass-to-charge ratio, m/z, some of the nanoparticles from the aggregation zone
with double the mass being transmitted through the TOF-MF will still be transmitted
due to the same m/z. Therefore it is important to obtain a good mass proﬁle before
you start depositing nanoparticles on a sample. The optimal conditions are found as the
best trade-oﬀwhere a high current of the desired mass m1 is obtained while a minimum
amount of double mass m2 is produced. As rule of thumb, the ratio of single-to-double
mass m1/m2 ≥10 to get an acceptably low deposition signal from double mass due to
double-charged particles. The total loading by mass will still be the same though.
From the TOF-MF, the mass-selected beam of nanoparticles pass through two sets of
einzel lenses, which focus the beam near a deposition mask and sample holder (see ﬁgure
3). The deposition mask and sample holder are both isolated from the ground potential

2.4
XPS
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) is a surface sensitive characterization method
used to determine the elemental composition of a sample by exploiting Einstein’s photo-
electric eﬀect.
An ideally monochromatic beam of X-rays is directed at a target. The photons are
absorbed by core-level electrons in the target allowing them to leave the atom with a
kinetic energy, Ekin corresponding to the diﬀerence between the energy of the photon, Ep,
and the binding energy of the electron, Eb
Ekin = Ep −Eb
(5)
Using a magnesium anode X-ray source, the Kα line has an energy of 1253.6 eV and a full
width at half maximum (FWHM) of 0.7 eV which is mainly due to the Bremsstrahlung
produced by decelerating electrons at the X-ray anode. In this energy range, the emitted
electrons will have a mean free path in the range of a couple to ten monolayers following the
universal curve.42 This allows us to obtain an XPS spectrum as an elemental compositional
ﬁngerprint of the top couple layers of the sample. Although not as surface sensitive as
ISS, the identiﬁcation of the elemental composition is easier because each element often
is represented by several features rather than one. The energy range being scanned must
therefore be large enough to include several transition lines.
For quantiﬁcation, the background needs to be subtracted to isolate the transition lines.
The largest contribution to the background arises from inelastic scattering of energized
electrons in the sample. A commonly used type of background is the Shirley background,
which assumes that the extra background intensity at any binding energy is proportional
to the total peak area at lower binding energies. This type of background is computed
iteratively, but is not based on a physical model.
It simply tries to account for the
constant background signal seeming to tail a transition peak. The constant nature of this
background is due to the main contribution from inelastically scattered electrons. On the
basis that other models of accounting for the background are not more right physically,
choosing the type of background happens on a case-to-case basis. Quantiﬁcation of XPS
spectra should therefore not be expected to be accurate with an error less than 10 %43.
With the increased resolution obtainable by using synchroton radiation, more sophisticated
modelling of the background may prove beneﬁcial.
The interesting features in an XPS spectrum are the intense core-level transition lines
and Auger features. Auger peaks occur by electrons relaxing into the core-states left open
by the photoelectrons and in the process uses the gained energy to eject a second higher-
energy electron. These features should therefore be largely independent on the energy
17
==PAGE:28==
of the photons and can be studied by itself using a beam of electrons to excite bound
electrons in the sample.
In analyzing an XPS spectrum, all the peaks must be accounted for to know the com-
position of the sample surface. A number of occurring peaks with smaller intensity may
appear. The analysis is made more diﬃcult if these peaks are overlapping with peaks of
special interest. A few of the diﬀerent kinds of occurring peaks will be shortly described
here.
- Satellites originate from spectral lines of the X-ray source of smaller but noticeable
intensity. A given X-ray source will have certain spectral lines with diﬀerent relative
intensity.
Say, the source has a line 10 eV higher than the primary energy line,
but at 10 % intensity, then every core-level peak in the XPS spectrum will have a
corresponding satellite 10 eV lower in binding energy with 10 % intensity.
- Shake-up satellites may arise at higher binding energies than a core-level peak, be-
cause the excited states left in the sample after ejection of electrons will relax to
lower energy levels. It takes some time before the relaxation occurs, however, and
in the meantime the electrons will exhibit a screening eﬀect in the local electronic
environment. This changes the binding energy of the remaining surrounding elec-
trons.
- Ghost peaks may arise from the dual anode design by unwanted activation of the
opposite anode. These peaks will therefore appear at a ﬁxed distance (energy) from
the spectral core-level peaks depending on the elements in the dual anode.

CO-TPD
Temperature-Programmed Desorption (TPD) is a technique used to characterize the re-
activity of a surface. It works by adsorbing gas molecules on a cooled surface and then
measuring with a mass spectrometer the amount of gas desorbed upon subsequent heating
with a controlled rate. The temperature at which desorption peaks occur can then be
correlated with the binding energy of the gas molecules to the surface site. A mapping of
the active surface sites of the sample can thereby be achieved, where as heat is added to
the system, the sites oﬀering the lowest binding energy will release the gas molecules ﬁrst.

2.5.1
QMS
Quadropole Mass Spectrometry (QMS) is a mass ﬁlter technique used to detect the com-
position content of the gas in UHV conditions.
There are three main components in the QMS: A ﬁlament (1) is heated by a strong
current so electrons are emitted from the surface of the ﬁlament. When the electrons
collide with a molecule, there is a probability that the molecule will absorb the electron
and thus be ionized. The resulting ion are then accelerated to a quadropole mass ﬁlter
(2) which consist of two pairs of rods with a certain cross-sectional geometry surrounding
the path of ﬂight.
A steady potential diﬀerence is applied between two rods opposite
of each other, with a RF-signal applied between the two other rods opposite each other.
For a given ratio of potentials, only compounds with a certain range of mass-to-charge
ratios are allowed to pass the QMS. Other compounds will be deﬂected oﬀthe path of
ﬂight and collide with the rods. The compounds that are ﬁltered through the QMS are
measured by their collision with a ceramic (3) that causes a cascade of electrons that can
be measured as a signal. Along this process, there exist a probability for a compound to
fragment into smaller ionic compounds. Because of this eﬀect along with isotopic eﬀects
and the possibility of multiple ionization, compounds measured with mass spectrometry
will show signals at several mass-to-charge ratios with varying intensity. For example as
can be found in the NIST database, pure oxygen should show a main peak at m/z = 32
and have a corresponding peak at m/z = 16 corresponding to ionization of monatomic
oxygen. The latter peak is expected to be about 20 % the intensity of the main peak.
When used for TPD experiments, a sniﬀer is used as the path between the probed
surface and the QMS. The sniﬀer is a tube that narrows down at its end into a small
aperture. Thus if the sniﬀer is placed in a region with relatively higher pressure than the
pressure in the QMS, only a small fraction of gas molecules are likely to hit and enter the
small aperture. The sniﬀer can thus allow probing of gases in considerably higher pressures
than the UHV conditions the mass spectrometer requires by choosing an appropriate size
of the sniﬀer opening. To measure the desorption rate in a TPD experiment, the sniﬀer
is positioned very close to the surface of the sample. Done right, all signal will practically
come from the surface of the sample or the background gas in the QMS region.

3
Design of CO-TPD Cell
This section will describe why designing a CO-TPD cell is necessary, and take you through
the main steps of the process of its design and implementation.
Since the binding of carbon monoxide is an important intermediary step in the reduc-
tion of carbon dioxide, mapping the reactivity of copper with temperature-programmed
desorption using CO as the probing molecule will be of great value when correlating with
activity experiments to ﬁnd and understand the selectivity of copper in the reduction of
CO2.35 This will provide valuable information about the chemical properties of the sample,
which is hard to discern using the other surface-sensitive techniques like XPS, ISS, SEM
and STM available at our setup, and the TPD cell will thus be an important complement.
The technique has been used previously on the setup on platinum45,46 and ruthenium47
nanoparticles. These samples did not require cooling, however, as the interesting features
were found above room temperature. The CO-TPD spectrum of copper begins to show
features starting at around 100 −120 K and the sample thus needs to be cooled to around
the temperature of liquid nitrogen (LN2).35
Existing options would be to use the sample manipulator in the preparation chamber.
This is equipped with a PBN heater which makes it possible to heat samples to temper-
atures in the vicinity of 1100 K according to the manual. It has the extra option to be
equipped with a LN2-cooled appendage allowing minimum temperatures between 140 and
160 K, which is nowhere cold enough for the copper project. Rather than modifying the
existing and working high-pressure cell, we decided to design and build the new (CO-)TPD
cell from scratch.
Therefore, part of my project was to design a system able to cool a sample down to the
temperature of LN2 (77 K) while at the same time being capable of reheating the sample
to a few hundred centigrade within a few minutes.

some being absorbed or scattered along the way. The transmitted electrons are the ones

4.2
Cleanliness of Samples
The substrates used for the electroreduction of CO2 were glassy carbon disks as mentioned
previously.
They are conducting so they can transfer potential to the active catalyst
serving (and co-serve) as a working electrode (WE). Carbon is used because it oﬀers a
low-activity surface and mainly should produce hydrogen at the relevant potentials. Prior
to deposition of nanoparticles, each substrate is sputter-cleaned to remove adventitious
carbon from the surface and then characterized with ISS and XPS to conﬁrm that the
surface is reasonably free of any contaminants. In a study by Lum et al.49, they reported
that trace elements of especially 3d metals were often likely sources of CO2RR activity
reported in studies of carbonaceous electrodes. Characterizing impurities with ICP-MS,
they tested electrodes of glassy carbon and diﬀerent carbon materials dispersed on glassy
carbon: high-purity carbon, graphene oxide and carbon nanotubes. Repeated rigorous
rinsing in concentrated high-purity nitric acid showed that metallic impurity dissolution
was much diminished after the ﬁrst rinse. This was proposed as the treatment necessary
to remove the largest portion of contaminants.
In the ISS spectra prior to deposition, I could see trace concentrations to varying de-
gree of mostly 3d metals and some 5d metals. More rarely, but existent, was 4d metal
contaminations. The contaminations were in each case below the detection limit of XPS
consistent with the observations by Lum et al.49 In collaboration with Nicola Mazzanti,
we investigated the best ways to improve the existing protocol for cleaning the glassy
carbon substrates and thereby minimize the amount of contaminants the results of which
are summarized in ﬁgure 16. Rinsing for an hour in concentrated nitric acid would consis-
tently reduce contamination levels to below 200 CPS using an emission current of 10 mA
(see bottom part of ﬁgure 16). Testing with other acids for removal of ﬁnal trace contam-
ination left after the treatment with nitric acid proved unsuccesful. Likewise, prolonging
the nitric acid treatment beyond an hour did not prove more eﬃcient. Via sputtering
and polishing of two exceptionally clean samples (ISS signal ∼50 CPS at 10 mA emission
current), the contamination signals would increase two- to three-fold. This seems to in-
dicate, that whatever traces of contaminations present are inherently spread throughout
the bulk of the substrates. Further, it is likely that the treatment with concentrated nitric
acid extracts a large fraction of contaminants from the near-surface area, the eﬀect of
which is then negated upon subbsequent sputtering or polishing. Given the intercalation
possibility of carbon substrates and the low cross-section area of carbon in ISS, it may
be possible that whatever signal background trailing a small-magnitude peak results from
bulk contaminants. This concept correlates with the general peak behaviour of pure sub-
strates showing some degree of matrix eﬀects, and ﬁts with the observation that not only
would the peak intensity be reduced when switching the emission current to 1 mA, but
the trailing background would vanish completely.
As a result of the studies conducted, we decided on setting an arbitrary limit to 200 CPS
at an emission current of 10 mA and a chamber pressure 5·10−8 mbar helium as the thresh-
old for which glassy carbon substrates would be clean enough to use in measurements. As
illustrated in ﬁgure 16, by comparing this directly to the signal from a 5 % copper nanopar-
ticle deposition, we correlate this threshold with a maximum of 0.01 % contamination by
35
==PAGE:46==
surface concentration.
The treatment with concentrated nitric acid was therefore merged with the existing
cleaning protocol where we would (1) Polish for 2 min on polishing cloth with 1/4µ diamond
paste and wetting with isopropanol with a speed of 400 rpm; (2) 5 cycles of 5 min sonicating
in ﬁrst milliQ and then in isopropanol; (3) ﬁnish by rinsing in milliQ.
In the new cleaning protocol we would (1) start by rinsing in milliQ; (2) wash for an
hour in concentrated HNO3 and rinse in milliQ; (3) Polish for 2 min (same as step (1)
in the old protocol); (4) 2 cycles of sonicating 10 min each in ﬁrst isopropanol and then
milliQ; (5) wash for an hour in concentrated HNO3; (6) ﬁnish by sonicating in milliQ.
Usee of the new protocol seemed to provide consistently cleaner substrates than the old
one.

Simulations on Coverages
This section will explain how the simulations were carried out and illustrate how the re-
sults should be interpreted.
The why: The simulations are carried out following a very simple model in order
to simply catch the general trend in going from our assumption of HOMOGENEOUSLY
distributed nanoparticles to one of RANDOMLY distributed nanoparticles. Its purpose
in this paper is not to provide an accurate accounting of the physics in the deposition
process, but rather to provide the next step and give some statistical information about
the expected fraction of overlapping particles, the degree or type of overlap, and to provide
a correction to the calculated coverage during a deposition by using equation (2).
The model: As a ﬁrst step to gain deeper insight in making samples of randomly de-
posited nanoparticles, the model is kept at its simplest. The nanoparticles are considered
non-interacting, i.e. they are allowed to overlap physically* and the coverages sought for
are two-dimensional projected areas parallel to the surface. Counting the fraction of over-
lapping particles is easy. The trick of this simulation is estimating the degree or type of
overlaps. A ﬁrst approximation to the grain-boundary formation by overlapping particles
is done by simply adding up the areas occupied by one nanoparticle, the areas occupied by
two nanoparticles at once, and so on. This can be done in several ways depending on what
you want to highlight and this study was motivated by correcting the assumed projected

coverage. The areas are therefore added in levels of ”what a bird would see from the sky”
– in the ﬁrst level, what percentage of the surface is covered by nanoparticles? Stripping
that layer away, what remaining percentage of the surface is now seen? This is the level
two coverage. In a physical model, this corresponds to letting the already randomly dis-
tributed nanoparticles become interacting by pushing any overlapping area up to the next
level oﬀering empty space until all overlapping areas have been redistributed across levels
(approximating the three-dimensional situation) to become non-overlapping. This may
also be thought of as depositing the nanoparticles one by one, and if any nanoparticle
collides with one already there, the overlapping part sticks on top of the old nanoparticle
while the rest of the newly arrived nanoparticle continues to the lowest unoccupied level
as illustrated in ﬁgure 18.
The implementation:
Designed to be computationally light, a ﬁxed number of
nanoparticles are assigned random sets of coordinates for their center positions. For each
particle, a local grid of cells is drawn and checked for overlap with all particles. In case
of a cell overlapping with a nanoparticle, the value of the cell increases by 1 to reﬂect
the total number of nanoparticles overlapping with that particular cell. The case of a
nanoparticle overlapping with two other nanoparticles is sketched in ﬁgure 19. Having
counted the overlaps from all other nanoparticles, the coverage contribution from each
type of cell is counted and added into dummy variables and a new local grid is drawn
around the next nanoparticle. The counting is corrected for multiple contributions for
each group of nanoparticles. The dimensions used are normalized interpreted as nanopar-
ticles being simulated are 1 nm in diameter. Being purely random statistics, the results
are the same for any sizes (within the stated model).
To calculate a coverage with a
ﬁxed number of nanoparticles, the dimension of the substrate on which to distribute the
nanoparticles is calculated to match a target coverage such as calculated by equation (2)
with the total projected surface area of the nanoparticles. In other words: increasing the
target coverage means distributing the nanoparticles randomly across a smaller substrate.
Parts of nanoparticles lying beyond the edges of the deﬁned substrate are clipped to mimic
how microscopy images of nanoparticles will look in reality. For this reason, the summed
coverage will be slightly less than the target coverage.
To average out the randomness of the randomly distributed nanoparticles and be left
with the statistics, the results of a simulation of a target coverage is looped 20 to 40 times.
The resulting ensemble provides an average value with the standard deviation used as
errorbars. Convergence plots for determining the size of the local grids and the number
of deposited nanoparticles are shown in ﬁgure 20.
Rough results up to a target coverage of 300 % are presented in ﬁgure 21 while a more
detailed plot of the lower coverages is presented in the main text in ﬁgure ??. From the
results it is noted that even with small coverages like 5 % or 10 %, about 15 % or 30 % of
the particles will be overlapping to some degree and already at 50 % target coverage about
80 % of the nanoparticles will experience overlap of some degree with another nanoparticle.
Thus to minimize the possible eﬀect of grain-boundaries by overlapping nanoparticles, one
will have to go to very small coverages.
From these results, it can be roughly estimated that the type of grain-boundaries, will

will begin to become dominant. Likewise, from about 100 % coverage higher-degree grain-

of nanoparticles. Changing the way of counting to, say, fractions of clusters containing a
number of overlapping particles will show this eﬀect in a better way.

Automated Integration of Deposition Currents
This section describes how the ﬁnal version of the integrating deposition tool works.
It estimates the leak currents and integrates the net deposition current, because this
will make it a useful tool in helping to estimate the time left until a certain target coverage
is reached and thereby decrease the spread of resulting samples of certain coverages. The
core function of the script is integrating the total number of charges deposited during
the deposition. This number corresponds to the total mass and can be treated in several
ways, of which I use the convention used at the setup and express the number in term of
coverage assuming homogeneously distributed single-mass nanoparticles.
The design of the script is based on the general behaviour of deposition currents. The
most important ones are listed here:
(1) The deposition current is measured as an estimated leak current subtracted from
a total measured current. That is, the two items subtracted can not be measured
simultaneously, so during a deposition the beam of nanoparticles is deﬂected away
from the sample at random intervals to keep a running estimate of the leak current.
(2) Each set of data points ﬂuctuate naturally.
(3) The setup around the electrometer (see ﬁgure 3) is very sensitive. If a person walks
past within a meter or two or stands next to it, the deposition current will show a
wild ﬂuctuation peak sometimes surpassing the deposition current in intensity.
(4) The possible users of this script later, may do small things a little diﬀerently and will
not understand the ﬁner details of the script. User friendliness in terms of number of
inputs is therefore valued as highly as robustness to provide a useful tool for people
after me.
(5) The leak current may drift during a deposition.
(6) The deposition current may sometimes be very hard to maintain and can vary a lot
during a single deposition.
The implementation of automatic integration can be divided into the following steps:
noise ﬁlter, dividing the recorded raw current into regions of leak current (beam oﬀ) and
deposition current (beam on), applying an estimate of the leak current in the deposition
regions, obtaining the net deposition current by subtracting the estimated leak current,
and integrating this over the deposition time to obtain a total current number of charges
(read single-mass nanoparticles) deposited.
The noise ﬁlter is made by ﬁnding the standard deviation of the natural ﬂuctuations
of the signal in the ﬁrst ﬂat region. Calculating the gradient of the spectrum and averaging
over ﬁve data points, any value higher than the natural standard deviation is classiﬁed as
noise or the transition regions when turning the beam of nanoparticles on and oﬀand is
thus ﬁltered away. An optional input can change this condition by multiplying the natural
standard deviation by a factor (default is 1.0).

==PAGE:63==
Auto-detection of leak current measurements was the most challenging part.
The ﬁnal implementation uses a single input which is the ﬁrst measured net deposition
current given in units of picoampere (10−12 A). The start region of the ﬁltered current is
then scanned point by point to detect the ﬁrst change given as input by checking each
value with the next value and noting when the diﬀerence is bigger than a default factor
(0.60 = 60 %) times the input. If the noted diﬀerence is positive the region just scanned
was a deposition region; if negative, the region just scanned was a leak current region.
The next region scanned is then classiﬁed as the opposite type region. For example if the
ﬁrst region is found to be a leak current measurement (as in ﬁgure 22), the next region
is classiﬁed as a deposition measurement region and the regions are only compared for
an increase in current (meaning that the beam is switched oﬀresulting in less negative
current. The old limit serving as an input to the function is then updated to match the
newly found net deposition current. This method has proven quite robust when testing
on diﬀerent kinds deposition measurements. It will be challenged if a deposition current
suddenly drops in intensity too quickly for the auto-detection of the regions. This can
to some degree be alleviated by lowering the factor for detection of the regions. At some
point though, the detection limit will be so low that variations in the deposition signal that
can not be ﬁltered out will trigger a new region and bug up the results. The best practice
to avoid this is being aware of the problem and renewing the leak current measurements
if the deposition current suddenly drops fast.
Estimating the leak current is done by simply drawing a straight line between two
consecutive leak current measurements. This should be a good and simple approximation
in most cases, and in the rare cases exhibiting a noticeable drift of the leak current, it
is likely to underestimate the leak current. Corrections may be made in the future to
provide better estimates, but estimating instead the error in the output of the script is
more important.
Integrating the net deposition current with equation (1) is done by adding the
area outlined by two consecutive measurements, i.e. drawing a line from point to point to
outline the area of integration.
Estimating the remaining deposition time was added as a last helping tool during
a deposition. It calculates the remaining time to reach the next target coverage in the
predeﬁned list: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 40, 50, 75, 100, 150, 200, 300 %. The calculation is
based on the average net deposition current estimated in the lastly made deposition region.
See an example of the output in ﬁgure 22. The most critical thing to implement in the
code in the future will be an estimate of the error or the precision with which the coverage
is calculated. This might be achievable by dividing the error estimate into individual con-
tributors such as error from drift of leak current, error from long deposition measurement
neglecting checks of the leak current, neglecting contributions from the regions where the
beam is switched on and oﬀ, and maybe a contribution based on the larger ﬂuctuation of
the deposition current. Adding all these contributions in a more thorough analysis would
be very helpful in trusting the result from each deposition. However, even without this
estimate, the results published by this script is more accurate than any estimate done by
eye.
