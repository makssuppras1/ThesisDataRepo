Abstract
One of the most severe mass extinctions on Earth occurred at the Permian–Triassic bound-
ary. As the information from that period is sparse, there are many opinions about the
main trigger for the extinction. One explanation is the big eruption of the Siberian Traps
in the end-Permian period. The work in this thesis investigates the climate changes which
have been caused by eruption of the Siberian Traps. Simulations of the past climate are
performed using the DCESS (Danish Center for Earth System Science) model, which
divides the climate system of the Earth into several interacting modules: atmosphere,
ocean, land biosphere, lithosphere, and ocean sediments.
By using reported estimates for the duration and magnitude of the eruption of the Siberian
Traps, the model is able to reproduce some of the large decreases in carbon-13 and oxygen-
18 isotopes reported in the literature. Despite that, the simulation results show no sign
of the Siberian Traps for causing the mass extinction by itself. Neither the temperature
nor the pH and oxygen levels in the ocean reach critical values where the biodiversity
would have been signiﬁcantly aﬀected. Even when using extreme values for the modelled
eruption, no critical levels are reached. The eruption of the Siberian Traps could, however,
have worked as a trigger for a series of other events, which together could have caused
the mass extinction.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the last 540 million years life on Earth have suﬀered several catastrophes of rapid
climate change. Six of these catastrophes led to mass extinction, where a great amount of
the Earth’s fauna and ﬂora were destroyed and unable to handle the change [1, 2]. Five
of these mass extinction events1 are shown in Fig. 1.1, and they occurs in the periods:
Devonian-Carboniferous (360 Ma), middle-Permian (260 Ma), end-Permian (251 Ma),
end-Triassic (200 Ma) and end-Cretaceous (65.6 Ma) [2].

vertical lines illustrate how major extinction events are related to large eruptions. The

Triassic, J - Jurassic, K - Cretaceous and T - Tertiary. Modiﬁed ﬁgure from Bond and

A mass extinction is deﬁned as a death of the majority of the existing species within a
short geological time period. In a mass extinction signiﬁcantly more species dies than
the normal background extinction of the individual species [1, 6]. The mass extinctions
have been identiﬁed by comparing the presence of fossils before and after these events by
calculating the number of fossil in the geological formation. The fossils may be shells of
mussels, pollen and spores from plants, bones or skeletons of animals [1].
How extreme an extinction is, is not only evaluated from the loss of species, but also from
the ecological changes during such a crisis, which is characterized by the ’impact factor’
[7]. The impact factor is determined by the size of the changes in the diﬀerent ecosystems
when crisis is passed [1, 7]. The impact factor is divided into several categories: category
1, where the existing ecosystem collapses and is replaced by a new ecosystem after the
crisis. category 2, where the existing ecosystem will be disturbed, but will recover and is
not replaced by a new one after the crisis. Within category 2 there are two subcategories:
category 2a, where disturbances causes permanent loss of important components of the
ecosystem, and category 2b, where the disruption is temporary and the ecosystem is
reorganized after the crisis [1, 7]. The three mass extinctions at Late Devonian, end-
Triassic and end-Cretaceous are deﬁned as a crisis of category 2a. The end-Ordovician
crisis is of category 2b and lastly the end-Permian crisis is of category 1 [7].
The information about the main cause behind some of these extinction events is sparse
and is still debated. In the last two decades the interest in hypothetical processes which
could explain the mass extinctions within a short time scale have been growing [2, 8, 9, 10].
A wide range of kill mechanisms has been proposed. These kill mechanisms are supposed
to have had major eﬀects on the marine and terrestrial environments globally [2, 11], and
thereby causing a mass extinction, but not a total extinction. Some of the most supported
hypotheses includes a change in sea level, large scale volcanism [2, 10, 12, 13] or impact
from an asteroid or a comet [14, 15].
The end–Permian mass extinction
The mass extinction that occurred at the end-Permian (∼251 million years ago) in
Phanerozoic, represents the most serve biodiversity loss in the ocean and of the terrestrial
species [12, 16, 17]. Up to more than 60 % of genera did not survive into the Triassic
[4, 17], as shown in Fig. 1.1. Past suggestions for the reasons causing this mass extinction
have been volcanism (eruption of Siberian Traps) [2, 8, 13, 12, 18, 19, 20, 21], warming and
acid rain via volcanism, volcanic winter, anoxia in the oceans [12, 22], ocean acidiﬁcation
[2, 12, 23], warming via methane release from the seaﬂoor [10, 11, 12], oxygen depletion
or bolide impact [15]. All of these mechanism except the latter considers the Siberian
Traps as the initial trigger [2, 20, 22].
A complete understanding of the end-Permian mass extinction will ﬁrst be achieved when
the trigger mechanisms are deﬁned from geological data and incorporated with the kill
mechanisms [22]. A kill mechanisms is deﬁned as a process causing death, where a trigger
mechanisms is a critical disturbance, which leads to the kill mechanisms [22]. With this
understanding, better opportunities is obtained to evaluate the warm climate that may
await us in the future, hereby with the use of climate models. Therefore it is interesting
if one can simulate the past climate and imitate events such as the end-Permian mass
extinction.

The Permian Paleography and Paleoclimate
To understand the end-Permian climate it is important to understand the Permian pa-
leography and paleoclimate. At the end of the Permian period and in the beginning of
Triassic period, the continental masses was located very diﬀerent from today, see Fig. 1.2.
During the Permian and Triassic time period, all of the continental masses was gathered
in the supercontinent Pangea, stretching from the north pole to the south pole [1]. The
main mass was in the mid-latitude area of the Northern and Southern Hemisphere [24]
and Pangea was surrounded by the Tethys ocean at East, which was connected to the
Panthalassic ocean west of Pangea [1].
In the Early Permian period the climate was dominated by glaciation [25, 26]. Climatic
belts were developed, and the placement of the continental masses had an big impact
on the circulation patterns in the oceans and in the atmosphere [25, 26]. In the middle
Permian period the climate became warmer, and in end-Permian period there was a big
shift in the climate due to the mass extinction. The climate got much warmer, and there
did not appear to have been any ice at the poles [25, 27].

1.1
Climate Modelling
Modelling the Earth’s climate is a quantitative approach to study the interactions of the
features in the Earth’s climate, including changes in the atmosphere, oceans and on the
continents [9]. Over the last 20 years many diﬀerent climate models have been proposed for
investigating and simulating the environmental changes during mass extinctions, amongst
these the end-Permian climate and mass extinction [9, 10, 29]. The geological data for
this period is sparse and estimations for the CO2 levels during that period are very few.
Therefore modelling the end-Permian mass extinction has to some extent been important
in evidence of supporting the diﬀerent suggesters of kill mechanisms. Some of the early
climate studies are simple models as proposed by Budyko [30] and Seller [31] in 1969.
These models consider the balance between the net absorbed solar radiation and the net

emitted terrestrial radiation [9].
They are simple and fast computationally, but have
limited application due to their simplicity. The models have subsequently been expanded
with variables describe e.g. the ocean circulation, topography and vegetation. These
energy balance models have been used to describe the temperatures of Pangea in the
end-Permian [32].
One of the things which have been used for interpretation of the important changes in the
ocean–atmosphere system in the Permian period is the negative carbon isotope excursion
(CIE) at the Permian–Triassic boundary [9]. The amount of carbon isotope 13C, measured
relative to the amount of carbon isotope 12C, is described by the measure δ13C, in which
the ratio is compared to a well-known reference sample ratio. It can be a measure for
carbon of organic compounds, or in general carbon, included in carbonate minerals such
as shells in mussels [1]. A depth proﬁle of δ13C-values from excavations can be used for
mapping the variations in the carbon cycle in a geological period. The biological activity
prefers the light 12C isotope instead of the 13C isotope. Thus, if there is a lot of 12C in
the atmosphere or in the ocean, the organisms will use more of the light 12C isotope than
the 13C isotope. A big negative drop in the δ13C-isotope proﬁle can be explained by a
supply of the light 12C isotope, which e.g. could come from a volcanic release of CO2 or
a release of methane from methane hydrates.

1.2
Scope of the Thesis
In this project we will focus on the eruption of the Siberian Traps in the end–Permian pe-
riod. The major goal is to model and describe the end-Permian climate changes, including
the eruption, and to see if the model reproduces data for carbon-13 and oxygen-18 isotopes
as reported in the literature. We will discuss how the model contributes to the debate
about the Siberian Traps as being a primary kill mechanism for the mass extinction.
The modelling is carried out using the DCESS (Danish Center for Earth System Science)
model by G. Shaﬀer, S. Malskær Olsen and J. O. Pepke Pedersen [43]. The properties of
the eruption is determined from a review of literature on the Siberian Trap. The DCESS
model beneﬁts from having a low complexity compared to other Earth system models.
Furthermore the DCESS model has a low spatial resolution, which makes it possible to
investigate the Earth’s system changes on scale of years to millions of years [43].

possibility of considering other durations and magnitudes of the eruption. The possibility
of the Siberian Traps as being the main source of the mass extinction is discussed.

Chapter 2
The DCESS Earth System Model
This chapter provides a general overview of the DCESS Earth System Model, which is
used for the simulations. The DCESS model is primarily designed for simulating the
climate system of the Earth. However, the model does not present the precise Earth
climate system. It tries to calculate the condition of the climate from understanding the
underlying physic, given some boundary conditions [44, 45]. The model is a good tool
for understanding the past global changes of the climate and predicting the future global
change [43]. Furthermore, the model can present the evolution of the Earth system in a
wide temporal range from changes on a scale range of years to millions of years [43].
2.1
Model Overview
The model divides the Earth’s components into diﬀerent modules as shown in Fig. 2.1,
and the components are given as: atmosphere, ocean, ocean sediments, land biosphere
and the lithosphere module. The model contains a description of the dynamics within
each module and also the transport between the modules. The overall connection between
the modules is described brieﬂy below, followed by a description of the model geometry
and lastly a detailed description of the functionalities of each module. As the description
and implementation of each module in the DCESS model is quite extensive, the focus
in this chapter will be only to describe the parts of the DCESS model which are most
important for the work in this thesis. The description in this chapter is primarily based
on Shaﬀer et al. [43], which is also referred to for further explanations of the model. The
implementation of the code has been done in MATLAB.
In Fig. 2.1 the incoming short-wave solar radiation from the sun provides energy for the
Earth. A part of this energy is reﬂected back into space from the water and land surface,
clouds and backscattering by the air [45, 47].
Another part of the solar radiation is
absorbed by water vapour, dust and CO2 in the atmosphere or in the clouds. The rest is
absorbed by the Earth’s surface, hereby the ocean and the land biosphere [47].
The atmosphere consist of layers of gas covering the whole Earth [45]. The layers mainly
consist of nitrogen, oxygen, water, methane and carbon dioxide, and the chemical com-
position determines the absorptivity and emissivity [44]. The module considers transport
of heat and gasses between low-mid and high latitude areas in the atmosphere.

The ocean module contains the circulation and mixing between the diﬀerent parts of the

The land biosphere module describes leaves, wood, litter and soil biomass in the model.
It calculates the primary production on land and also calculates the land biomass changes
and land-sea gas exchanges of CO2 and CH4. The carbon cycle interact with the land
biosphere by storing the CO2 in leaves, wood, litter and soil [43].
The lithosphere module considers outgassing, weathering of carbonate and silicate rocks,
and weathering of rocks containing old organic carbon and phosphor [43].
In each module, the DCESS model calculates the dynamics of module-speciﬁc physical
variables, so-called tracers, such as the carbon content in the atmosphere or the tem-
perature in the diﬀerent ocean layers. The dynamics are calculated by a time-stepping
approach, where the change of each tracer is calculated for each step. This will be de-
scribed in details in Section 2.7.

2.2
The Atmosphere Module
The atmosphere module consist of eight tracers which have diﬀerent values in low-mid
(super-script L) and high latitude (super-script H) areas. In each area the tracers describe
a mean value across the given area. The tracers are given by: the near-surface atmospheric
temperatures, Ta, the partial pressures of CO2, CH4, N2O, O2, and also partial pressure
of the speciﬁc isotopes 13CO2, 14CO2 and 13CH4.
In the following section an explanation of the dynamics for the mean atmospheric tem-
peratures is provided, and in the subsequent section a description of the dynamics of the
rest of the tracers is given.

organic matters is decreasing exponentially with increasing depth.
2.4
The Ocean Sediment Module
The ocean sediment module treats dissolution of calcium carbonate and re-mineralization
of organic matter in the sediments from the ocean module. The ocean sediments are
composed of calcite and non-calcite mineral and reactive organic matter fractions. The
ocean is divided into two boxes of 55 ocean layers for low-mid and high latitudes, as
illustated in Fig. 2.4. Each layer is divided into bioturbated segments of 0.1 m thickness.
In the model re-mineralisation and dissolution are not considered below the bioturbated
layer. It is assumed that calcite and non-calcite minerals are well mixed in the bioturbated
layer. The relative organic matter fractions diﬀer over the bioturbated layer due to the
re-mineralization. Furthermore it is assumed that the bioturbation in the bioturbated
layer is constant [43].

Summary
The DCESS model divides the Earth into diﬀerent components: The atmosphere, the
ocean, the land biosphere, the ocean sediments, and the lithosphere. Each module contains
a set of tracers, which describe the properties which may be changed through time, such
as the temperature, partial pressure of gasses, carbon content etc. The dynamics of these
tracers are calculated by a time-stepping approach, which calculate the tracer changes in
each module.

When analysing the eﬀects of a volcano on the climate in the following chapters, it is im-
portant that the climate system initially is in a steady state. Otherwise it will be diﬃcult
to understand the eﬀects of an abrupt increase in CO2. A comprehensive calibration of

3.1
Calibration of the Model
In order to properly understand the upcoming results, it is important to know how the
DCESS model is originally calibrated. The model is calibrated against Earth system data
by testing the simulation of atmospheric greenhouse gas increase, global warming and
model tracers evolution before 1765. This is deﬁned as a pre-industrial steady state.
The model was calibrated in four steps, starting with calibrating only the atmosphere
module and then one by one including more modules in the following steps.
In the
calibration, parameters related to each module are estimated, such as transfer rates and
diﬀusion coeﬃcients.
The calibration is carried out by a ”trial and error” approach,
by which the free parameters in each module are estimated in order to get expected

Lastly, poleward heat and water vapour transport in the atmosphere were demanded

3.2
Permian–Triassic Steady State
In this work the model is re-calibrated to describe the steady state for the Permian–
Triassic period. The re-calibration is necessary, as the solar radiation in the Permian–
Triassic period was lower than the period for which the model was calibrated.
With a new value for the solar radiation, the previously calibrated model will no longer
be a steady state, as the incoming energy no longer balances the outﬂow of the model.
A new steady state could be sought by running the simulation for a suﬃciently long
time for the system to be in a new steady state. However, this turns out to be a time
consuming method, and the new steady may have signiﬁcantly (and undesirably) changed
the value of some of the tracers. In order to ﬁnd a reasonable new steady state, the re-
calibration procedure from Appendix A is followed instead. This was provided by J. O.
Pepke Pedersen. Summarizing the procedure, the model is run several times in order to
ﬁnd the new steady state. In each run, diﬀerent parameters are held ﬁxed and a new steady
state with these ﬁxed parameters are determined. The new steady state will, however,
have tracer values which diﬀer to a certain extent from the tracers in the pre-industrial
steady state.
In the Permian–Triassic period it is believed that the partial pressure of CO2 was down
to 500 ppm [9, 42]. When the model was run with the decreased solar radiation and with
500 ppm, the temperature decreased signiﬁcantly below values which were believed to be
present in the end-Permian period (a mean around 20 ◦C). To avoid the signiﬁcant decrease
in temperature, the partial pressure of CO2 was increased until the mean temperature
for low-mid latitude and high latitude area was approximately 20 ◦C, which were at a
partial pressure of CO2 around 1000 ppm. As the next chapters focus on how pulses CO2
changes the tracer values, the initial (absolute) value of the partial pressure of CO2 is not
believed to have a major inﬂuence.

Chapter 4
Simulating a Volcanic Eruption
Progressing from the steady state solution from last chapter, this chapter examines the
dynamics in the case of a sudden volcanic CO2-release with massive eruptions of basalt.
The investigation includes testing the main hypothesis of this thesis to see if such an
eruption could induce lowering of the oceanic δ13C and induce the greenhouse warming
that lead to a mass extinction.
However, before turning to the main hypothesis, the
results for several model variables such as temperature, carbon inventory and pH in the
ocean are illustrated. These both veriﬁes the validity of the model and provides a more
detailed overview of the system dynamics.
This chapter ﬁrst provides an overview of the estimates in the literature for the volume
of the basaltic magma from the Siberian Traps. Based on these, the volcanic CO2-pulse
for this investigation is created. The results from the simulation are presented, hereunder
atmospheric pCO2, atmospheric temperature, carbon balance, the inventory of the carbon
of the system, ocean temperature, oxygen concentration and pH in the ocean, and ﬁnally
the δ18O and δ13C dynamics.
4.1

Inventory of carbon
The total carbon in the system is divided into three components; the atmosphere, ocean
and land biosphere. In Fig. 4.6 (Upper left) the calculated CO2 inventory for these three
components is shown, illustrated with inventories for the whole Earth. The maximum
total carbon in the atmosphere is approximately 3,750 Gt C at the time t = 300 kyr, and
the maximum total carbon in the ocean is around 52,000 Gt C at the time t = 330 kyr.
The inventory in the ocean peaks at a later time, as the carbon release is made into the
atmosphere module. The maximum total carbon in the land biosphere is close to 1,800
Gt C at the time t = 280 kyr, which is illustrated more clearly in the zoomed-in-plot in
Fig. 4.6 (Upper right). The land biosphere is not eﬀected as much as the atmosphere and
ocean. This, and the earlier peak for the land module, may be explained by saturation-
like eﬀects as the net primary production in the land biosphere has a log-dependency of
pCO2 as shown in Eq. (2.18). Thus, the NPP increases slower, the higher the values of
pCO2.
Fig. 4.6 (Lower left) shows the change in carbon inventory for the atmosphere, ocean, and
biosphere, compared to the total amount of released carbon and the net carbon which
exits the system. The latter is calculated as the weathering minus the burial of carbon.
The maximum increase in carbon inventory for the ocean is approximately 7700 Gt C
obtained at the time t = 330 kyr, the change in carbon inventory for the atmosphere is
approximately 2200 Gt C at t = 300 kyr, and the change in carbon inventory for the land
biosphere is approximately 11 Gt C at t = 280 kyr. The ocean quickly absorbs the increase
amount of carbon in the atmosphere, whereas the land biosphere only absorb a small
amount. Agreeing with the chosen carbon pulse, the total amount of injected/released
carbon ends at 18,000 Gt C. In order for the system to reach a steady state after the pulse
has passed, the injected carbon exits the system again (illustrated with the dashed line),
although with a delay of ∼100 kyr.
The last plot in Fig. 4.6 (Lower right) shows the total change in the carbon inventory
in the atmosphere–ocean–land system. As a check of the calculations in the code, this
curve agrees with the total inﬂow in the system minus the outﬂow. At no time during the
release the total amount of carbon in the system has increased with more than 10,000 Gt
C, i.e. a little more than half of the carbon content of the total release.

pH level in the ocean with a CO2 release
The acidity of the ocean also has an eﬀect of the biological life in the ocean. The temporal
evolution of the pH in the ocean is illustrated in Fig. 4.9. The pH value is highest in the
top layer, both for the low and high latitude areas, and has a tendency to decrease with

parts of the ocean becomes just above 7.0, and where the pH has decreased with 0.2 due
to the CO2 release.
When the CO2 pulse is released in the atmosphere, a part of it is dissolved in the ocean and
reacts with water. Carbonic acid (H2CO3) is formed, and the carbonic acid will release its
hydrogen ions and form hydrogen carbonate (HCO−
3 ). The formed hydrogen carbonate
will again release hydrogen ions and form carbonate (CO2−
3 ). The more hydrogen ions in
the water, the more acidic the ocean will become, causing the lower pH-value.
The mean pH depth proﬁle in low-mid latitude and high latitude areas is shown for

high latitude region. In the low latitude region the pH decreases rapidly through the ﬁrst
1000 m and is almost constant for the following 1500 m. The drop in the pH-value is
not signiﬁcant does not seem to be suﬃciently large to kill the plans or organisms in the
ocean.

Summary
From a review of the information about the eruption of the Siberian Traps, one of the
largest estimates for the release is chosen for the simulations. By injecting 18,000 Gt
carbon over 600 kyr, signiﬁcant changes are observed in the carbon balance. The global
mean temperature increases is around 3 ◦C in the atmosphere and 4 ◦C in the ocean. The
pH in the ocean drops with 0.2 at max, and the oxygen content drops by 50 µmol/kg.
The drop in δ13C of ∼4  agrees with the lower range of the results in the literature,
but does not reproduce the drops in the higher range.

Chapter 5
Additional Investigations
The complexity of the DCESS model opens up for the possibility of examining other
variations of the volcanic eruption, which also may be possible events in the end-Permian
period.
These variations are performed both to test the model further in simulating
realistic events, but also to see if the results from the previous chapter may be signiﬁcantly
changed. First, a release of sulphur in the eruption is included in the model. Second, the
possibility of releasing the carbon as methane is examined. Last, the eﬀect of having an
underwater release of the carbon is considered.
5.1
Sulphur Release from Volcanoes
In the previous chapter the volcanic eruption was simulated only by a release of carbon
dioxide. During an eruption gaseous dust and other gasses are also released, see Table 5.1.
One other signiﬁcant component is sulphur, which together with the gaseous dust con-
tributes to the atmosphere aerosols. Volcanic dust and ash have a relative short lifetime,
because they quickly fall to the surface. Thus the focus in this section is to consider the
eﬀects of including release of sulphur during the eruption.
Sulphur emitted from volcanoes are typically on the form of SO2 for high-temperature
volcanic gases or H2S for low temperature volcanic gases [85]. However, H2S oxidates
to SO2 within one-two weeks after the eruption [86]. It is therefore assumed that all of
the sulphur from the volcanic is released as SO2 in the simulations. One estimate of the
emitted amount of sulphur from the Siberian Traps is provided by B. Black et al. (2012)
[77], who estimate degassing to be approximately ∼6300–7800 Gt S by assuming a total
magma volume of ∼3 × 106 km3.
In order to include the eﬀect of a sulphur release in the model, the code is modiﬁed by
subtracting a time-dependent term from the amount of ingoing radiation in Eq. (2.6)
(In the code, this is most easily done by adding a corresponding term to the outgoing
long-wave radiation instead, i.e.
adding an additional term to the right-hand side of
Eq. (2.7)). This is done in order to simulate the eﬀect of sulphur causing a cooling eﬀect
of the surface. The cooling occurs as SO2 transforms to H2SO4 (sulphuric acid aerosols),
which creates more cloud-cover in the atmosphere and thereby blocks the incoming solar
radiation for smaller periods and up to several years [87]. This eﬀect could in worst case

additional time-dependent term. Here a pulse containing 7800 Gt S is released over 600

Earth.

Chapter 6
Discussion
There has been a large variety in explanations for the mass extinction in Permian–Triassic
boundary. In this chapter, the results from this thesis are discussed in relation to the
ﬁndings in the literature.
Summarizing the results
The results in the previous chapters showed that a pulse of 18,000 Gt C released over 600
kyr did give a drop in δ13C of approximately 4  at the Permian–Triassic boundary for
a single pulse. The drop was induced by a change in pCO2 of approximately 1000 µatm
at max, corresponding to an average global temperature increase of 3.5 ◦C. A drop in the
δ18Oc proﬁle of -0.8  was obtained for simulating the volcanic eruption. By assuming
that the released carbon was in methane compounds instead of carbon dioxide molecules,
a shift in δ13C of approximately -7  was obtained, which is approximately 3  more
than for simulation the volcanic eruption with a CO2 pulse. Furthermore, a change in the
pH, oxygen level and temperature in the ocean was observed. These factors are important
drivers of the mass extinction. Due to the release of CO2, a reduction in pH and oxygen in
the ocean was found. This could be a driver for increasing the presence of marine hypoxia
and anoxia associated with the Permian–Triassic boundary [29].

section in South China [29], see e.g. Table 1.1. The Meishan section has the richest end–
Permian faunas, and the volcanic ash layers are interlayered with fossil bearing rocks,
which are well preserved [27, 33].

6.2
Temperature Increase and Change in δ18O
As described in Section 3.2, a change in the oxygen-18 isotope is believed to describe
a change in the temperature, with an 1 ◦C increase per 0.22  drop in δ18Oc. In the
literature, diﬀerent opinions exist about the magnitude of the temperature increase at
the Permian–Triassic boundary. Fig. 6.5 shows the oxygen isotopes for the Permian–
Triassic boundary as described by Korte et al. (2010) [34]. The oxygen isotope data is
obtained from Conodonts (an extinct eel-like animals) and Brahiopods (animals with sea
shells). The data shows a drop of around 1 , corresponding to a temperature increase
of around 4 ◦C from the end-Permian (Changhsingian) to early Triassic (Induan). Quite
consistently, Y. Cui et al. (2015) [9], tried from proxy climate data and existing paleocli-
mate simulations to get a better understanding of the link between the mass extinction
and climate change in the Perian–Triassic period. Y. Cui et al. ﬁnd a temperature in-
crease of 5-9 ◦C, based on proxy data, and a pCO2 rise of 4000 ppm during the event. Y.
Cui et al. found a temperature anomaly consistent with ∼1.5 doubling of the pCO2, and
they concluded that volcanism from the Siberian Traps was a reasonable explanation of
the observed ∼4  carbon excursion.
Earlier studies of oxygen isotopes of Brachiopod shells at the Permian–Triassic boundery
from Korte et al. (2005) [92] showed a decline in the late Permian of δ18Oc values by ∼
0.5  to approximately -3  and thereby a warming of approximately 16 ◦C. Oxygen
isotopes data from from Sun et al. (2012) [93] showed a large warming across the Permian–
Triassic boundary of a temperature increase of 15 ◦C over 0.8 Myr. These oxygen isotope
values are from Conodont apatites in Nanpanjiang Bassin, South China. The highest
ocean sea water temperature is reached in the early Triassic period and is estimated to

6.3
Levels of pH and Oxygen
The ocean acidiﬁcation, which was thought to be one of the kill mechanism to the mass
extinctions was not found to signiﬁcant in this study. The mean pH values did drop from
7.4 to approximately 7.2 under the release of 18,000 Gt carbon, but not to a pH-level that
could kill the marine life. In the extreme scenario with a very short pulse and a large
release from Section 6.1, the average pH in the ocean decreased by 0.6 to a level of 6.8
(data from simulation not shown). Even in this extreme scenario this decrease is believed
to be small in order to cause a mass extinction.
Hypoxia in the ocean was observed from the results in this work as in Fig. 4.8, where the
oxygen level in the low-latitude areas are reduced with up to around 30 %. Anoxia and
hypoxia conditions have been reported in the end-Permian [34, 95], and these conditions
might have contributed to the mass extinction.

6.4
Did the Siberian Traps Cause the Mass Extinc-
tion?
A. Knoll et al. (2010) [34] study the environmentally eﬀects of kill mechanism linked to
the extinction. He suggest that some of the main killers (triggered by the Siberian Traps)
in the ocean were acidiﬁcation, hypoxia and toxic sulphide concentration and a high level
of pCO2, which caused warming.
However he believes that hypercapnia (elevation of
carbon dioxide) can explain the extinction of marine life. A. Knoll says that this is not
likely the main killer of life of land and says it is not clear how anoxia could transmit onto
land [22, 96].
Korte et al. investigate the carbon and oxygen isotope excursion in Guryul Ravine (Kash-
mir), Abadeh (Iran), Bulla (Italy) and compare it to the Siberian Traps. Korte et al. sug-
gest that short lived events such a destabilization of methane hydrates or bolide impact
are unlikely to cause the shift in δ13C [2, 34].
R. Berner (2002) [10] examines some of the recent hypothesis for the mass extinction event
by looking at the consequence eﬀecting the carbon cycle model. Berner concludes that
volcanic degassing alone could not have induce a large enough drop in the δ13C. A release
of methane from methane hydrates could however explain the drop in the δ13C, but not
the high amounts of CO2 in the atmosphere. Berner (2002) suggests a combination of a
release of carbon from methane hydrates, volcanic eruption and maybe the impact of a
bolide to have caused the sudden mass extinction.
Bond et al. (2014) [2] concludes that the negative carbon isotope excursion found at the
Permian–Triassic boundary was too big to be from volcanic CO2 and CH4 alone. Bond et
al. also conclude that volcanic eruption did include warming, which is a powerful driver
of ocean anoxia.
K. Kaiho et al.
(2001) did study measurements of sulphur isotopes in rock from the
Meishan section at the Permian–Triassic boundary. The rocks from the Meishan section
was enriched with light sulphur, and K. Kaiho et al. conclude that it could be due to
the impact of an large asteroid hitting the Earth’s surface and this impact could cause a
massive release of sulphur from the Earth’s mantle to the ocean–atmosphere [15, 97].
Such an impact would not have destroyed the Earth, but it would have made a big
eﬀected on the Earht’s surface and in the climate of the Earth. The emitted sulphur
into the atmosphere could have caused acid rain, and dust and aerosols released into
the atmosphere, may have covered for the sunlight and inhibited photosynthesis. The
animals in the ocean and on land would not have had many changes of survival without
sunlight, even if it was of a short period of time. A site for the impact have even been
suggested in north-western Australia [22, 98], however these proposal are under sharp
criticism [99, 100].
Comparing the ﬁndings in this thesis with the opinions above, the simulation results seem
to support the statement that the single big eruption from the Siberian Traps did not cause
the mass extinction by itself. It could have worked as a trigger, but other mechanisms
or rapid eruptions might also have had an eﬀect in order to create the large changes in
δ13C. If the carbon release from the eruption instead was distributed over several short

but large eruptions, this could also have caused some more drastic changes in δ13C and in
δ18O. The eruption might also have been caused by an impact of asteroid or have acted
as a trigger to the mass extinction.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This thesis have investigated the climate changes caused by the eruption of the Siberian
Traps in the end-Permian period, and the observed mass extinction was discussion in
relation to the eruption. The link between large scale igneous province volcanism and
mass extinction has been recognised for a long time, especially for the end-Permian mass
extinction. There is still uncertainty about what was actually the main trigger for the
extinction.
To investigate the climate changes created by the Siberian Traps, the DCESS model was
applied. The model was adjusted to match the lower solar radiation expected at that
time. The adjustment was followed by a re-calibration to get the model in a steady state,
which was used as the origin for the rest of the simulations. A review of the information
about the eruption of the Siberian Traps was performed, such as the volume of basaltic
magma, released amount of carbon into the atmosphere, and the duration of the eruption.
This information was used to create a pulse with a carbon content in the high end of the
observations in the literature, such that the largest possible eﬀects would be obtained
within realistic estimates.
The eruption was simulated by releasing 18,000 Gt carbon into the atmosphere over a
period of 600 kyr. The increase in the global mean temperature in the atmosphere was
approximately 3 ◦C and approximately 4 ◦C in the ocean. This increase in temperature
does not seem suﬃciently large to cause a mass extinction.
Induced warming in the
ocean is one of the drivers for ocean anoxia. The oxygen level in the ocean dropped by
approximately 50 µmol/kg corresponding to 30 % decrease at max, which also does not
indicate a mass extinction. The pH value in the ocean decreased only by 0.20, by which
the acidity of the ocean did not reach a critical value. Even when trying with extreme
pulses where the 18,000 Gt carbon is released over only 20 kyr, still none of these values
seem to become critically high.
With the 600 kyr wide pulse, a drop in δ13C of around 4  was observed, and similarly
a drop of 0.8  in δ18Oc. The results fairly reproduces some of the smallest observed
changes in the carbon-13 and oxygen-18 isotopes reported in the literature, e.g. by Saun-
ders et al. [27]. The reported large changes in δ13C of around 8  cannot be reproduced
with this model, unless the extreme pulse with the very short duration is used. This is
with the exception that if the carbon was released as methane, such large changes might
64
==PAGE:65==
be obtained. But even with the short 20 kyr pulse, it is not possible to model the very
short spikes in δ13C data, which have been reported by e.g. Bowring et al. [38] or Cao
et al. [89]. This is due to a long re-normalization time for the Earth system to get back
into a steady state.
As sulphur is also a main component in volcanic gasses, the model was modiﬁed to include
a release of SO2. By using estimated values for the amount of sulphur from the Siberian
Traps, this had a cooling eﬀect due to a reduction in the incoming solar radiation, but the
eﬀect was quite small. The model was also used to consider the possibility of releasing the
carbon gasses under water instead of into the atmosphere. This gave a slightly smaller
increase of the partial pressure of CO2 than the release into the atmosphere, and the
temperature diﬀerence was small.
According to the ﬁndings in this thesis, the Siberian Traps were not alone causing the
mass extinction. The eruption could, however, have worked as a trigger of a series of other
mechanisms, which together could have caused the mass extinction. These mechanisms
are not included in this thesis, and it is important to remember that all results presented
here are within the speciﬁcations of the model. The discussion of the kill mechanisms is
however still an ongoing debate, which is interesting to follow when new model extensions
are being developed and new ﬁndings in the ﬁeld are made.
Further Ideas
There are many ways in which the model can be used for further analysis. The climate
sensitivity in this model was set to a temperature increase of 3 ◦C for a doubling of the
atmospheric pCO2, assuming a climate sensitivity more or less similar to today’s value.
The range for the climate sensitivity may vary from 1.5 ◦C to 4.5 ◦C per CO2 doubling
[101].
Therefore it would have been interesting to examine the eﬀect of varying this
parameter, as a 4.5 sensitivity would give a larger temperature increase. The increase may
be more than 50 % as feedbacks from melting of the snow may increase the temperature
even further due to feedback mechanisms.
In some cases in the literature the partial pressure of CO2 is expected to be lower than
the 1000 ppm used here; some says down to around 500 ppm [9, 42]. It would have been
interesting to re-calibrate the steady state to 500 ppm and see the results for this new set
up. With a lower CO2 content, an ice layer would form at the top of the ocean. Thus,
when injecting CO2 the above-mentioned feedback mechanisms will have an increased
eﬀect on the temperature rise.
A massive methane release from the sea-ﬂoor has been suggested as an explanation for
the mass extinction [37]. As the current state of the model only allows injecting methane
into the atmosphere as done in this thesis, it would be interesting to expand the model
to include underwater release to see if the system response would be changed.

Appendix A
Steady State Calibration Steps
The re-calibration procedure of the model is described below and was provided by J. O.
Pepke Pedersen. The re-calibration is performed to ﬁnd a steady state solution for the
system. In order to ﬁnd the new steady state, the model is run several times. In each run,
diﬀerent parameters are held ﬁxed, and a new steady state with these ﬁxed parameters
is determined. The model code consist of diﬀerent parts, which in turn are turned on/oﬀ
in the steps of ﬁnding the steady state. The procedure is as follows:
1. The partial pressure for CO2 is held ﬁxed at the desired value and the model is run
for 10 kyr.
2. The mean temperature for low-mid latitude and high latitude is determined for
these values. If the mean temperatures are in a steady state, they are prescribed
as standards and are held ﬁxed for the next step. If not, the pCO2 is again a free
parameter, and the temperature is held ﬁxed for which the simulation is run for 10
kyr or until the values are steady.
3. Now the temperature and the choice of pCO2 are ﬁxed, and the simulation is run
for 10 kyr to see if inputs and outputs are stable.
4. Next step is to get phosphate stable. This is done by allowing the phosphate inven-
tory to vary until a balance between input and output of the system is obtained.
After that step, then phosphate inventory is ﬁxed.
5. Getting carbon stable is more complicated, and the organic carbon burial should not
be in balance with just weathering of old organic carbon, since there is also volcanic
input of carbon that is a mixture of carbonate and organic carbon. Therefore the
organic carbon burial should be balanced by weathering of old organic carbon plus
volcanic input of carbon minus silicate weathering of carbon, as described in Section
2.6.
6. With pCO2 kept constant, the burial rate of carbon is varied by an iterative approach
until a balance is obtained.
7. The next step is to adjust DIC and ALK to get a stable state, which may change
pCO2 a little.
66
==PAGE:67==
8. Now the temperature and pCO2 again set as free parameters and the simulation is
run again.
9. If pCO2 varies too much, try to let all parameters for carbon vary.
10. Lastly, the simulation is run for at least 50 kyr to check that the system is in a
steady state.
11. If the value for δ13C varies too much, the small adjustments are made until the value
of δ13C is also steady.
