ABSTRACT 
Analysis of data from field studies have indicated that neutrality as defined in current 
standards is not necessarily ideal for all people as their preferences of neutral vary and 
may be influenced by season, climate, expectations and thermal perception and 
preferences. The adaptive thermal comfort model includes these factors in the design of 
indoor climate which in tropical and subtropical climates have been shown to have a 
potential for energy savings while the thermal comfort was maintained. [1] [2] Thus, this 
project aim to investigate in a temperate climate how the adaptive thermal comfort 
model could influence the design process and evaluation of the indoor climate, use of 
energy and architecture, with the case study of the new headquarter for Nordea Bank. 
To investigate the interface between the case study and general aspects of the adaptive 
thermal comfort model (ATC), double skin façade and element façade were compared as 
two design alternatives and variation of temperature set point and window area in IDA 
ICE were studied. These variations were made in relation to the design criteria for 
thermal comfort, daylight and energy described in standards and in the design stage in 
the detailed project of Nordea Bank. 
The studies of the parameter variation underlined opportunities by a design with the 
adaptive thermal comfort aspects. By a temperature offset of ±2°C in the temperature 
set point variation, the need for heating was reduced with 54%-65% and cooling with 
around 30%. Compared with the evaluation of PMV, aPMV showed an improved 
comfort category. This indicates that a design of temperature set points inspired by ATC 
have the potential for energy savings and justify great temperature ranges which were 
seen in the element façade with large window area. By the variation of the window area 
from 41%-77% opening area, the evaluation of the thermal comfort was also improved 
by the evaluation of aPMV compared with PMV. With an analyse of the daylight and by 
the evaluation of the thermal comfort, it was shown that larger window area can be 
justified by aPMV in both façade alternatives. In both variations and by the comparison 
of the double skin façade with the element facades, the element façade showed larger 
improvement by evaluation of the aPMV than PMV. ATC thus justify larger window area 
and tolerates wider temperature ranges and thereby a design of the element façade in 
relation to thermal comfort.  
In the interviews with engineers and architects it was indicated that the indoor climate 
in open plan offices will be designed according to the occupant and depend less on the 
external conditions. The design of façades such as double skin facades will distinguish 
more between internal and external conditions and reduce the potential for 
implementation of the adaptive aspects. In accordance with the potential and 
complications by ATC described in the interview and indicated by the studies in this 
project, it was concluded that there is a larger potential for implementation of adaptive 
aspects in the design with elements facades in residential buildings or single offices. In 
this type of buildings and by the element façade design, the influence by the external 
conditions and thereby greater temperature ranges are justified by ATC.

RESUME  
Analyse af data ved feltstudier har indikeret at en neutral temperatur som defineret i 
gældende standarder ikke nødvendigvis den ideelle temperatur for alle, da præferencer 
og definitionen af en neutral temperatur kan variere og være påvirket af sæson, klima, 
forventninger og termiske opfattelse og præferencer. Den adaptive termiske komfort 
model (ATC) inkluderer disse faktorer ved design af indeklima, hvilket er set i tropiske og 
subtropiske klimaer har potentiale for energibesparelser mens den termiske komfort 
opretholdes. [1] [2] Derved har dette projekt til formål at undersøge, om ATC i et 
tempereret klima kan påvirke design processen og evalueringen af indeklima, 
energiforbrug og arkitektur ved et casestudie af Nordeas nye domicilbygning. For at 
undersøge grænsefladen mellem casestudiet og de generelle aspekter ved ATC, vil 
double skin facade sammenlignes med element facade som to designalternativer og 
variationer af temperatur set point og vinduesareal vil blive studeret. Dette er i relation 
med design kriterier for termisk komfort, dagslys og energiforbrug som beskrevet i de 
gældende standarder og hovedprojektet for Nordea.  
Studierne af parameter variationerne understregede mulighederne ved et design 
inspireret af ATC. Ved variation af temperatur set point på ±2°C, var energiforbruget af 
varme reduceret med 54%-65% og af køling med omkring 30%. Sammenlignet med 
evalueringen med PMV, resulterede aPMV i en forbedret indeklima kategori. Dette 
indikerede, at et design af temperatur set points inspireret af ATC har potentiale for 
energibesparelser og retfærdiggør store spænd i temperaturer, som ses ved element 
facaden med stort vinduesareal. Ved variation af vinduesarealet fra 41%-77% var 
evalueringen af det termiske komfort også forbedret ved evaluering af aPMV i forhold til 
PMV. Dagslysanalyser viste at større vinduesarealer kan retfærdiggøres ved evaluering 
af aPMV ved begge design alternativer. Ved begge parameter variationer og ved 
sammenligningen mellem double skin facade og element facade, viste element facaden 
større positiv effekt ved evalueringen af aPMV i forhold til PMV. Dette indikerede, 
hvordan den ATC retfærdiggør et større vindues areal og tolererer et større spænd i 
tempererer og derved i relation med termisk komfort et design med element facade.  
I interviews med ingeniører og arkitekter blev det indikeret at indeklima i 
landskabskontorer i fremtiden vil blive designet i forhold til brugeren og mindre i forhold 
til udendørs forhold. Et facade design ved eksempelvis double skin facade differentierer 
mere mellem udendørs og indendørs forhold og reducerer derved potentialet ved 
implementering af adaptive aspekter. Med udgangspunkt i fordele og ulemper ved ATC, 
som blev beskrevet i interviewet og belyst ved studier i dette projekt, blev det 
konkluderet at der er et større potentiale ved implementering af adaptive aspekter i et 
design af boligbyggeri og enkeltmandskontorer. Ved denne type af bygninger og ved et 
design med element facade, vil udendørsforhold have en større indflydelse og derved 
kan større temperaturudsving retfærdiggøres ved ATC.

INTRODUCTION 
Current standards are based on two fundamental assumptions 
 
Optimum temperature corresponds to a ‘natural’ thermal sensation 
 
‘Acceptability’ is associated with thermal sensation defined in ASHRAE and DS 1752 
as the seven-point scale. [3] 
Analysis of data from field studies have indicated that neutrality as defined in current 
standards is not necessarily ideal for all people as thermal sensation outside the seven-
point scale does not necessarily leads to discomfort. People’s preferences of neutral 
temperatures vary and may be influenced by season, climate, expectations and thermal 
perception and preferences. In relation to the outdoor temperatures, the adaptive 
thermal comfort model includes these factors in the valuation of thermal comfort and 
goes beyond physics and physiology as fundamental factors. As seen in tropical and 
subtropical climates, that a design inspired by the adaptive aspects has the potential for 
energy savings while the thermal comfort is maintained. [1] [2]  
Thus, this project aims to investigate in a temperate climate how the adaptive thermal 
comfort model in accordance to DS/EN 15251 could influence the design process and 
evaluation of the indoor climate, use of energy and architecture. The study will be 
carried out in collaboration with Henning Larsen Architects and take base in a case study 
of the new headquarter for Nordea Bank.  
To investigate the interface between the case study and general aspects of the adaptive 
thermal comfort model, the façade design and variation of temperature set point and 
window area in IDA ICE will be studied in relation to the design criteria for thermal 
comfort, daylight and energy as described in standards and in the design stage in the 
detailed project of Nordea Bank.  
The project is delimitated to the design proposal, factors and criteria evaluated in the 
design process of Nordea Bank. Thereby, natural ventilation as a criterion for the 
adaptive thermal comfort model will not be included but discussed, double skin façade 
and element façade will be compared as two design alternatives and variations will be 
made in relation to parameters described in the design process. The studies will be 
evaluated in relation to interviews made with engineers and architects concerning the 
adaptive thermal comfort model and the general and future design of facades and office 
buildings.

BACKGROUND AND LITERATURE  
4.1 
THERMAL COMFORT 
Thermal comfort is one of five subareas defining indoor climate. [4] 
 
Thermal – temperatures as cold, heat, draft etc.  
 
Atmospheric – pollution of the air, smell, fragrances, particulate matter etc.  
 
Acoustics – sound, noise generated by occupants and transmitted through 
construction etc.  
 
Visual – light and sight as for example glare, daylight, colours and view to the 
outside.  
 
Mechanical – ergonomic and vibrations at frequencies outside the audible area.  
Thermal comfort and our acceptability of thermal conditions are influenced by the 
metabolic rate, clothing insulation, air temperature, mean radiant temperature, air 
speed and humidity. How we perceive, regulate and adapt to our environment can be 
divided into the following subcategories.  
 
Physiology  
Physiology explains the mechanisms of living and how muscles and organisms 
function. In general, muscular activity is associated with heat production. The more 
active we are the more heat we produce. Over time, the generated heat will 
equalise with the heat losses to the surrounding environment. The physiological 
mechanisms affect our comfort and have an influence on the overall satisfaction of 
the indoor thermal environment.  
 
Psychophysics 
Psychophysics describes the relationship between physical stimuli, our sensation 
and the ways the brain interprets them. Thermal sensors give information to the 
brain about skin temperature and the skin condition.  
 
Physics 
The human body mainly loose heat to the environment by convection as air 
currents, radiation as infrared heat transfer and by evaporation of sweat in warm 
environments. 
 
Behaviour 
Behaviour includes changing clothing, posture, or metabolic rate, moving between 
thermal environments or making use of thermal controls to change the 
environment. Within behavioural interactions, the time has importance and is 
divided into four periods – immediate which is to change in clothing, within-day 
that describes environmental changes during the day, day-to-day as we learn from 
one day to the next and longer term which describes the seasonal changes. [5]

STATIC THERMAL COMFORT MODEL  
According to the static thermal comfort model (PMV/PPD) described in ISO 7730, a 
human being’s thermal sensation is mainly related to the thermal balance of the 
person’s body.  [6] 
PMV 
It is possible to predict the thermal sensation for persons by calculating the predicted 
mean vote (PMV). The PMV-index is influenced by six parameters: the metabolic rate, 
clothing insulation, air temperature, mean radiant temperature, mean air velocity and 
humidity. The PMV is an index that relates a numerical value with the sensation of 
warmth expressed on a 7-point thermal sensation scale (Table 1). [6]

4.3 
ADAPTIVE THERMAL COMFORT MODEL 
By introducing PMV / PPD in various comfort standards as ISO 7730 [6] and ASHRAE 55 
[9], the model became the leading model for HVAC engineers and other building 
designers for the specification of an acceptable thermal indoor climate. This model has 
over the recent years been challenged with the view of different climates, cultures and 
energy efficiency. The most famous of the alternative models is known as the ‘adaptive 
thermal comfort model’ (ATC) which was already published in 1970s in papers by 
Humphreys and Nicol and are expressed by the adaptive principle “if a change occurs 
such as to produce discomfort, people react in ways which tend to restore their 
comfort”. [10] [11] 
In the 1980s ASHRAE funded a series of field studies of thermal comfort in 160 office 
buildings spread across four different climate zones with an outdoor temperature range 
of -5 to 33 °C. The data from these studies were aggregated in ‘ASHRAE RP-884 – 
developing an adaptive model of thermal comfort and preference’ as a database of 
thermal comfort field studies. All statistical analysis were performed separately for 
HVAC and naturally ventilated buildings (NV). The analysis indicated that the PMV 
successfully predicted the comfort temperatures in HVAC buildings in contrast to NV 
buildings, where there were differences between comfort predicted by PMV and actual 
comfort temperatures due to psychological adaption, habituation and expectation. [12] 
On 1998, the adaptive thermal comfort model was explained by de Dear and Brager by 
physiological, behavioural and psychological factors. [13] 
 
Behavioural – personal, environmental, technological or cultural.  
Personal adjustment includes adjustment like changing clothing, activity, moving 
between locations. Technical/environmental adjustment describes adjustments of 
the surroundings such as open/closing windows, changing setting of HVAC system, 
or blocking air diffusers. Cultural adjustments include scheduling activities, siestas, 
adaptive dress codes etc.   
 
Physiological – genetic adaption or acclimatisation.  
Genetic adaption describes adjustments in the genetic heritage over a time period 
longer than a life time. Acclimatisation is a process where the individual adjusts to 
changes in the environment over a short time period of days or weeks.  
 
Psychological – habituation or expectations.  
Psychological feedback describes how habituation and expectations influence one’s 
perception and reaction to sensory information. This is also known as ‘perceptual 
adaption’ which describes repeated or chronic exposure of an environmental 
stressor which influence the sensation’s intensity.  [13] 
Physiological, psychological, social and behavioural actions will occur and will vary from 
climate and culture. Adaptive actions in response to temperature may be classified in 
five categories: [14] 
 
Regulation of the internal heat generation.  
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
 
Regulation of the body heat loss.  
 
Regulation of the thermal environment. 
 
Selection of a different thermal environment.  
 
Regulate or change the habits or expectations.  
The adaptive thermal comfort model states that there are factors beyond fundamental 
physics and physiology that play an important role in thermal perception, expectations 
and thermal preferences. Occupant expectations to the indoor climate influence the 
thermal sensation, satisfaction and acceptability. Occupants are not considered as 
passive receivers, in the sense of impressions, but as an active part of a dynamic thermal 
environment. This is creating a dynamic character of comfort and discomfort where in 
addition to the thermal environment it is also needed to consider a dimension of time. 
[12] [14] 
As a part of the building design process, adaptive interventions can be subdivided into 
‘adaptive opportunities’ and ‘adaptive constraints’. Opportunities and constraints 
relating to the building envelope (placement of windows or solar control), interior layout 
(occupants placement in the room, single offices or open plan office) or climate control 
system (de- or centralized HVAC system, settings of set points or condition of control – 
centralized or individual). [13]  
By adjusting the inlet air temperature set point in accordance with the seasonal 
variations and the outdoor climatic condition, the indoor temperatures will by adaptive 
opportunities break with the general range of room temperature between 20 – 24 °C all 
year round. Static and dynamic control methods are two methods to implement an 
adaptive approach in the control of indoor temperature. By the static control strategy, 
the inlet temperature was risen 1°C higher than normal during summer and by the 
dynamic strategy the temperatures were adjusted in direct response to the outdoor 
conditions by a multi-agent control system. Both methods showed energy savings 
around 6%, though with slightly better occupant satisfaction by the dynamic approach. 
[15]

4.3.3 
THE ADAPTIVE PREDICTED MEAN VOTE MODEL (APMV) 
The adaptive predicted mean vote model (aPMV) is based on the static thermal comfort 
model (PMV), but includes people’s characteristics and behaviour by describing the 
underlying structure, mechanisms and dynamics of the mind. It describes the relation 
between the input/output and the effects of adaptive feedbacks such as adjusting 
clothing. [16] 
The aPMV model explains the PMV by an adjustment of the thermal sensation in warm 
or cool conditions assuming that the majority of occupants feel comfortable at relatively 
higher temperatures during warm seasons and lower temperatures during cold seasons. 
Thereby the aPMV considers adaptive aspects both in the heating and cooling seasons.  
Defining the adaptive coefficient equal to zero in the case of no adaptive actions, the 
aPMV will we equal to the PMV, Fanger’s static thermal comfort model. Illustrations of 
the two alternative models are shown in Figure 5 - Figure 6. [16]

4.4 
DOUBLE SKIN FACADES  
Already in 1903 at the Steiff Factory in Giengen, Germany, the first double skin curtain 
wall was reported. [18] A double skin façade is an additional part of building envelope 
which is installed over the existing façade and is defined by two transparent surfaces 
separated by a cavity, which functions as an air channel. The double skin façade can be 
categorized according to the function of the cavity: [19] 
 
Active wall.  
An extra skin is added to the inside of the building envelope where air from the 
inside can pass through this cavity and return to the ventilation system. Energy 
absorbed by blinds will be removed by this ventilated system and the internal 
surface temperature of the inner glass can be kept close to the room temperature 
both during summer and winter, leading to increased comfort at the zone close to 
the external façade. This makes it recommendable in colder climates. (Figure 7) 
 
Naturally ventilated wall.  
The extra skin added on the outside of the envelope will fuction as additional 
thermal insulation during periods with little or no solar radiation. In periods with 
solar radiation the temperature will rise in the cavity and it will be ventilated by a 
buoyancy effect, reducing the solar heat gains. This requires a design of ventilated 
openings to reduce the temperature difference between the outside air and the 
heated air in the cavity. This type of double skin façade is not recommended in 
warmer climates. (Figure 8) 
 
Interactive wall.  
The interactive wall is similar to the naturally ventilated wall but with the difference 
that the ventilation is forced. The ventilation in the cavity does not depend on the 
buoyancy effect alone, which makes this system work in warmer climates, too. 
(Figure 8) [19] 
 
==PAGE:21==

The double skin facades can also be categorized according to the type and geometry of 
the cavity:  
 
Box window type.  
In this type, horizontal and vertical partitions divide the double skin façade into 
smaller elements. (Figure 9) 
 
Shaft box type. 
The shaft effect in this type is created by vertical shafts and connection between 
box elements and increases the buoyancy effect. (Figure 10) 
 
Corridor façade. 
Vertical partitions are added for acoustical reasons, fire security or ventilation.  
 
Multi story double skin façade. (Figure 11) 
No horizontal or vertical partitions are added between the two skins, and thereby 
the buoyancy effect is realized by large openings near the ground and roof of the 
building. (Figure 12) [20]

In all categories the cavity between the second skin and the original façade will function 
as a thermal buffer zone. This buffer zone will during the heating season serve to 
insulate the building by reducing heat losses and enabling passive thermal gain from 
solar radiation. For south orientated systems, this solar radiation can be used for heating 
purposes. During the cooling season the warm air can be removed by the buoyancy 
effect in the cavity which must be vented correctly to avoid overheating. The key criteria 
here are the width of the cavity and the size of the ventilation opening in the outer skin. 
[22] [23] 
Some of the advantages of all types of double skin façade can be mentioned:  
 
Acoustic insulation 
==PAGE:23==
15 
 
 
Thermal insulation both during heating and cooling due to the outer skin. 
During heating season the double skin façade will serve as insulation by increasing 
the external heat transfer resistance. During cooling season the warm air inside the 
cavity can be extracted when it is ventilated (naturally or mechanically) 
 
Energy 
Double skin façades can save energy on heating by thermal insulation and cooling 
by the removing heat by the cavity when properly designed 
 
Better protection of the shading or lighting devices.  
 
Reduction of the wind pressure effects.  
 
Transparency 
There is often a desire from the architects to use larger percentage of glazing 
surfaces in the facade. Ventilated double skin facades reduce solar gains in cooling 
season and provide thermal insulation in heating season, allowing larger window 
areas.  
 
Natural ventilation can be implemented even in high rise buildings.  
 
Thermal comfort  
During the heating season the internal part of the façade can maintain 
temperatures that are more close to the thermal comfort level. In the cooling 
season the cavity has to be designed so the temperatures does not increase 
dramatically. [24] 
Following disadvantages can be mentioned:  
 
Higher construction costs.  
Constructional and investments costs for standard and double skin façade in the 
Central Europe is stated as: [25] 
 
Standard façade 300 to 500 Euro/ m²  
 
Double Skin Standard 600 to 800 Euro/ m²  
 
Double Skin with adjustable air in and outlet 700 to 1000 Euro/ m²  
 
Double Skin with openable exterior sashes 800 to 1300 Euro/ m² 
 
A double skin façade incur higher cost in construction, cleaning, operation, 
inspection, servicing, and maintenance compared to a standard façade. Additional 
maintenance and operational costs in Central Europe are stated as: [25] 
 
Standard façade 2.5 to 3.5 Euro/ m² and cleaning operation  
 
Double Skin façade 4 to 7.5 Euro/ m² and cleaning operation. 
 
Reduction of rentable office space as a waring size of the façade results in a loss of 
useful space.  
 
Overheating problems if the cavity in the double skin façade is not properly 
designed and thereby not ventilated enough to remove heat in the cavity.  
 
Increased weight of structure.  
 
Daylight.  
There can be a reduction of the quantity of the light in the rooms because of the 
additional external skin and the compensatory effects by a design with larger glass 
area. [24]

5. 
METHODOLOGY  
This project aims to study the theory of adaptive thermal comfort and how this theory 
can be implemented in the evaluation the indoor climate in Danish offices with the base 
in a case study of the new headquarter for Nordea Bank. To fulfil this aim both 
qualitative and quantitative approaches are needed.  
5.1 
LITERATURE REVIEW   
As a part of the qualitative research, the literature helps to substantiate the research 
problem and understand the theory behind. Moreover, the literature not only 
substantiates the problem but also suggests possible questions or hypotheses that can 
be asked or addressed. Together with case study and interviews, a review of adaptive 
thermal comfort literature developed and shaped the project. [34] 
5.2 
CASE STUDY 
Case study research can lead to an understanding of objects, methods or theories. It can 
extend experience or add strength to what is already known. [35] An office building for 
Nordea Bank was in this project the case object. The theory of indoor thermal 
environment in this building was studied and understood as a base for further design, 
parameter variation and evaluation by the adaptive thermal comfort theory. With 
inspiration from the design criteria and process of Nordea Bank, it was chosen to 
compare the double skin façade with the element façade and to study a variation of 
temperature set points and window area with a base in the two design alternatives – 
Kastenfenster 77% opening area and element façade 41% opening area with internal 
shading.   
The building was in cooperation with Henning Larsen Architects chosen as a 
representative example of a modern office building in a temperature climate. The 
building includes areas with requirements to the indoor environment which vary from 
traditional office buildings. Thus, the study was focused to a limited area of the building 
which was designed according to traditional standards in relation to the design of the 
systems controlling the thermal climate and the design of the façade. This enabled a 
general evaluation of the static and adaptive thermal comfort theory in the study of the 
thermal indoor climate and façade design. The design process of Nordea and the 
reflections made can be put into perspective to a general design of office buildings in 
relation to the aspects described in the interview and the related literature. 
Uncertainties and confounding factors in the analysis based on the case study will be 
discussed.

SIMULATION  
By simulations, “a reality” can be obtained and research knowledge of the "reality" can 
be established by a reproduction and analysed quantitative. [36] With a base in the

double skin façades and the effect of the opening area in relation the adaptive thermal









Inspired by a static control method, the parameter variation of set points was made in 
steps of 0.5°C in the four base models to study the influence on the use of energy and 
indoor thermal comfort. The window area was increased with steps of 5m2 window area 
from 41% to 77% in the Kastenfenster and the element façade with internal or external 
shading. This was to study the influence on the energy use and the indoor thermal 
environment in relation to the daylight. In connection with the overall and specific 
objectives, both parameter variations were studied and evaluated in relation to the 
theory about double skin façade, PMV, adaptive thermal comfort categories and aPMV. 
IDA ICE 
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) was used as simulation tool. It is a program for 
simulation of building energy consumption, indoor air quality and thermal comfort, 
validated according to CEN 13791. It takes into account thermal models, CO2 and 
moisture calculations, vertical temperature gradients and wind and buoyancy driven 
airflows through leaks and openings. [37] In the late versions of IDA ICE it is possible to 
simulate ventilated double skin facades and other complex glass facades according to 
ISO 15099 [38] such as the Kastenfenster façade, which is described in the case study 
(paragraph 4.5).

6. 
INTERVIEWS 
A design process today is based on the Danish standards, regulations, codes and 
guidelines. Both in the design process for engineers and architects, within new 
construction and renovation. Interviews with Rambøll, MOE, ALECTIA and both CASA 
and Henning Larsen Architects (HLA) indicated that standards like DS 474, DS 464, DS 
15251 and DS 1752 are used in the design of indoor climate and HVAC in new buildings. 
Standards like DS 418, DS 474 and DS 557 are widely used within renovation as the 
interview with Rambøll in the department of existing buildings indicated that there 
within this field is a stronger focus on research in building physics and surface 
temperatures.  
The adaptive thermal comfort model was less known within the department of existing 
buildings in Rambøll compared with the departments of new constructions in Rambøll, 
the other engineering companies and HLA. Compared with CASA Architects which 
represent a smaller company, the larger companies Rambøll, MOE, ALECTIA and HLA 
were familiar with the adaptive thermal comfort model. The interviewed had become 
acquainted with the model by the standard DS/EN 15251, conferences and one by a 
competition proposal, where the adaptive model was used to argument for the indoor 
climate and to show ‘the state of the art’.  
The interviewed had different opinions about the adaptive thermal model, its potential 
and how the adaptive aspects have been and can be implemented.  
Especially ALECTIA, MOE and HLA indicated that the adaptive aspects are used within 
single offices and residential buildings by control of temperature, ventilation and 
shading in contrast to larger open plan offices where the indoor climate often is 
regulated by a CTS. In open plan offices where the occupants have the opportunity to 
regulate the external shading and the ventilation system, it can result in discomfort by 
unstable temperatures, ventilation and draft. This has been experienced by the 
interviewed from MOE at open plan offices in NIRAS.  
Potentials of the adaptive theory were pointed out by the interviewed from ALECTIA, 
MOE and Rambøll. The adaptive theory obliges with the changes in the outdoor 
temperature in contrast to the current standards which only distinguish between 
heating and cooling seasons, and therefore may create discomfort during the transition 
periods. Another potential within the adaptive aspects was described by the interviewed 
from ALECTIA where adaptive aspects of thermal environment control in form of 
individual fans and opening of window were added in an existing building. These were 
added as solutions to indoor climate problems as there were not economic resources or 
possibilities to change the existing systems or construction.  
When the interviewed were asked if the adaptive thermal model could be implemented 
in the future design processes, their responses were different. The interviewed from 
Rambøll in the department for new construction, commercial buildings and 
sustainability had a clear opinion about the adaptive theory. According to him, the 
future design process will depend less on the outdoor temperature especially regarding 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
larger office buildings and headquarters. The focus will be on flexibility and interaction 
between rooms and on the occupants and their needs.  
Like other qualitative factors that influence the occupants in an office, the interviewed 
from ALECTIA pointed out that the adaptive model needs to be better operationalised 
before it can be implemented in the future design processes. During this interview, the 
upcoming concept of flexible and activity based work stations was also discussed in the 
adaptive context. [39] The occupants in these environments may be more sensitive to 
comfort due to changes in the psycho-social work environment [40], which will cause 
challenges in the implementation of the adaptive model. Moreover, it was pointed out 
that it has to be considered if it is justifiable that the occupant’s environment will 
deteriorate and maybe decrease their productivity.  
In contrast, the interviewed could also see the potential in the adaptive model and the 
adaptive thinking. Especially, when it concerns buildings where the opportunities to 
improve the existing systems and construction are limited. It was mentioned, that 
adaptive aspects might be possible to implement in larger office buildings, though with 
greater care. A larger potential in the adaptive model and aspects were expressed by the 
interviewed from MOE and HLA, who were of the opinion that the adaptive theory is the 
correct and future way to consider indoor thermal comfort. But for it to be a part of the 
future design process it has to be implemented in standards and maybe even in the 
simulation programs. This is a change in the engineering methods, which HLA found 
necessary to ensure a general acceptance and use of the adaptive thermal model. An 
acceptance and insight of the theory that varied between companies, its size, fields and 
individuals who during this interview underlined the problems and the potentials of the 
adaptive aspects.

includes adaptive aspects during the heating season and therefore resulted in an 
improved indoor thermal sensation compared to the ATC.

SUMMARY 
The internal temperatures during the cooling season showed same tendency in three of 
the models – Kastenfenster 77%, element façade 41% with internal shading and element 
façade 77% with external shading. In contrast, the element façade 77% with internal 
shading showed higher temperatures due to a large opening area, solar radiation and 
internal shading. During the heating season the minimum temperatures were the same 
in all models as the effect from the convector maintained the temperatures within the 
defined set point range.  
This maintenance of the temperature during the heating season affected the energy use 
for heating and differentiated between the models. The Kastenfenster 77% and element 
façade 41% with internal shading had the lowest need of energy for heating compared 
to the element façades 77% with internal or external shading. Similarities between the 
Kastenfenster 77% and element façade 41% which corresponds with the design 
comparison described in the design process of Nordea and the theory of double skin 
façade and how the buffer zone serves as insulation during the heating season. 
The need for cooling was higher in the Kastenfenster 77% compared to the element 
façade 77% with external shading. This corresponds to paragraph 0 where it is described 
how the design of the cavity can influence the need for cooling during the cooling 
season and in the worst case cause overheating. In accordance to the described design 
process of Nordea, the Kastenfenster 77% had a lower need for cooling than the 
element façade 77% with internal shading.  
The PMV in all models show the same result during the heating season. In the cooling 
season the element façade 77% with internal shading exceeded category III in contrast 
to the other models which showed same tendency below category II. The aPMV were in 
all models within category I or II in contrast to the PMV which exceeded category III. This 
indicates how the adaptive thermal model equalizes and accepts extreme temperatures 
and conditions during the cooling season and by aPMV during the heating season too. A 
design process with a base in the adaptive thermal comfort theory therefore have a 
potential for energy savings and justify temperature fluctuations which is seen in the 
element façade with large window area and internal shading.

SUMMARY 
The study of the temperatures at the variation showed, how the operative temperatures 
by the internal shading are higher but also easier affected by the variation. Which 
indicate how the internal conditions by the internal shading are more affected by the 
solar radiation and therefore gain more by a reduction of the window area.  
The Kastenfenster has the lowest use of energy for heating but also gain the least by 
increase of window area, which indicates the effect of the double skin façade and how it 
serves as insulation and reduce the influence of the external conditions.  
In contrast, the Kastenfenster had a higher need for cooling than the element façade 
with external shading due to the design of the cavity in the double skin façade. This 
underlined by the highest reduction in the need for cooling by the reduction of window 
area, as the effect by the cavity and solar radiation were reduced.  
The Kastenfenster façade and the element façade with external shading showed the 
same pattern in PMV and aPMV, within category II and I respectively. The element 
façade with internal shading showed more fluctuating temperatures during the cooling 
period, and were at PMV outside category III and at aPMV within category II during 
peaks. All which improved with a reduction of the window area.  
Below 55% of opening area, the daylight would not fulfil the requirement of 2% at all 
work stations. With daylight and PMV (category II) or aPMV (category I) as criteria, the 
Kastenfenster façade and the element façade with external shading enabled window 
areas up to 77%. With PMV (category II) and daylight as design criteria, it was not 
possible with a façade design with internal shading, where aPMV (category I) enabled a 
window area of 55%.

DISCUSSION  
The results of the IDA ICE and daylight analysis will be discussed in relation to the static 
and adaptive thermal comfort theories which will include evaluation and comparison 
with the design process of the façade design and the implementation of double skin 
façade. The project was limited to the design factors and criteria evaluated in the design 
process of Nordea. Thereby, natural ventilation as a criterion for the adaptive thermal 
comfort model was not included which result in a discussion about the relevance of the 
adaptive aspects, factors and actions in mechanical cooled office buildings. Although, it 
will be discussed how or if the adaptive aspects could influence the façade design of 
Nordea and by interviews reflect on the general and future design of facades and office 
buildings.  
 
ANALYSIS OF THE INDOOR THERMAL CLIMATE AND COMFORT, ENERGY USE AND DAYLIGHT.  
By the variation of the temperature set point, the operative temperature curves showed 
a clear influence and difference in the cooling and heating seasons by the temperature 
set point variation. Compared to the static thermal comfort model, the adaptive comfort 
categories indicated a larger tolerance to high temperatures in the cooling season, 
which especially were seen in the element façade 77% with internal shading due to a 
large opening area, solar radiation and internal shading. During the heating season the 
minimum temperatures were the same in all models as the effect from the convector 
maintained the temperatures within the defined set point range. (Figure 36 - Figure 37, 
Table 29 - Table 26, Appendix 3) 
This maintenance of the temperature during the heating season affected the energy use 
for heating and differentiated in the results. The Kastenfenster façade 77% and the 
element façade 41% with internal shading had the lowest need for heating but highest 
percentage reduction. The similarities indicate how a double skin façade with opening 
area of 77% insulate and reduce the influence of the external conditions as a façade with 
a lower opening area of 41%. (Figure 38 - Figure 41, Appendix 3) 
The need for cooling was higher in the Kastenfenster 77% compared to the element 
façade 77% with external shading. This corresponds to paragraph 0 where it is described 
how the design of the cavity can increase the need for cooling during the cooling season 
and in worst case cause overheating. In accordance to the described design process of 
Nordea Headquarter, the Kastenfenster 77% had a lower need for cooling than the 
element façade 77% with internal shading. For all models, the percentage reduction of 
energy for cooling was 30%. (Figure 38 - Figure 41, Appendix 3) 
During the heating season, PMV was within category II-III and aPMV within category I. In 
the cooling season the element façade 77% with internal shading exceeded PMV 
category III and were within aPMV category II. The other models were within PMV 
category II-III and aPMV category I-II. With an evaluation of the aPMV, the results 
showed improved comfort category. Therefore, a design process with a base in the

temperature ranges which are seen in the element façade with large window area. 
(Figure 42 - Figure 43, Appendix 3) During the cooling season, this evaluation by aPMV 
corresponds with the ATC comfort categories. During the heating season, the ATC 
comfort categories corresponds to the static thermal comfort model and PMV, which 
are described in DS/EN 15251. Lower set point variation during the heating season can 
therefore not be justified by the evaluation of the ATC and the comfort categories 
defined in DS/EN 15251. (Figure 36 - Figure 37) 
 
In the variation of the window area, the operative temperatures with the Kastenfenster 
facade and the element façade with external shading were similar. In contrast, the 
operative temperatures with the internal shading fluctuated more and were easier 
affected by the variation of the window area. The operative temperatures with the

The Kastenfenster façade showed the lowest use of energy for heating which was

conditions during the heating season. (Figure 52 – Figure 54, Appendix 4) 
The Kastenfenster facade had a higher reduction at 22% in the need for cooling

external shading. This indicates how the design of the cavity increased the need of 
cooling and by a reduction of the area of the double skin façade, the influence of the 
cavity was reduced too. (Figure 52 – Figure 54, Appendix 4) 
Due to the effect of the convector during the heating season, all the models showed the 
same tendency where the PMV were within category II and the aPMV within category I. 
During the cooling season, the Kastenfenster façade and the element façade with

increased as indicated in the literature. Moreover, it would smoothen the transition 
periods between heating and cooling seasons, which there were differentiated more 
between by the temperature set point variation in this project. The settings of the

0.55W/m2W. As the changes in all three facades were the same, the conclusions derived 
from the results are unchanged, but the changes and tendencies might have been more 
clear and easier to compare and conclude on.

THE ADAPTIVE THERMAL COMFORT MODEL - NATURAL VENTILATION  
Both the adaptive thermal comfort model in ASHRAE Standard 55 and DS/EN 15251 
state that the adaptive theory and the comfort limits can only be applied when the 
thermal conditions in the room primarily are regulated by opening of windows. In this 
project, natural ventilation was not included as a parameter to change as the project 
was limited to the design factors and criteria evaluated in the design process of Nordea.  
The adaptive principle states that “if a change occurs such as to produce discomfort, 
people react in ways which tend to restore their comfort”. [11] Occupants can react and 
regulate their body heat loss by adjusting their clothing, regulate their thermal 
environment by individual control or regulate their habits or expectations. Their thermal 
expectations to the thermal indoor climate can influence the thermal sensation, 
satisfaction and acceptability. These are actions described in relation to the adaptive 
thermal comfort theory, which also partly occur in the general theory of thermal 
comfort. It can thereby be discussed if factors like behavioural adjustment, physiological 
(acclimatisation) and psychological (habituation and expectation) will influence the 
perception of the thermal comfort in office buildings with any kind of thermal 
regulation. Occupants may be active parts of a dynamic thermal environment and define 
a dynamic character of comfort and discomfort.  
Studies of parameter variations in this project underlined the opportunities and 
advantages by a dynamic thermal comfort and control. By a temperature set point offset 
of ±2°C, the need for heating was reduced up to 65% and within aPMV comfort category 
I-II, and the need for cooling was reduced by 30% and within aPMV comfort category I. A 
==PAGE:75==
67 
 
window area of 77% could be justified with the design of Kastenfenster and element 
façades with internal or external shading. During the heating season the aPMV for all 
façade designs were within category I. ATC and aPMV comfort categories during the 
cooling season were mainly within category I, only above 60% opening area in the 
element façade with internal shading it was within category II. With a design of the 
façade and indoor climate inspired by the adaptive aspects, these studies indicates a 
potential of energy savings and improved daylight by lager opening areas without a 
deterioration on indoor thermal climate.  
In contrast, analysis based on field studies of 160 office buildings, which are aggregated 
in the ASHRAE RP-884 database, indicated that the PMV successfully predicted the 
comfort temperatures in HVAC buildings in contrast to naturally ventilated buildings. It 
was stated, that due to the psychological adaption, habituations and expectations the 
comfort predicted by PMV and the actual comfort were different in NV buildings. This 
precludes the use of adaptive aspects within mechanical cooled buildings.  
 
THE ADAPTIVE THERMAL COMFORT MODEL – INTERVIEWS AND IMPLEMENTATION  
The interview with engineers and architects indicated how the adaptive thermal comfort 
model was known, but not an integrated part of the indoor climate design. The adaptive 
thermal comfort model was known from DS/EN 15251, conferences and competition 
proposals where the adaptive aspects were added to the indoor climate evaluations as 
the ‘state of the art’.  
The interviewed had different opinions about the adaptive thermal comfort model, its 
potential and how the adaptive aspects have been and can be implemented. The 
interviewed indicated that the adaptive aspects have been used and have the potential 
within single offices and residential buildings by control of temperature, ventilation and 
shading. One had the opinion that the adaptive thermal comfort theory is the correct 
way to consider indoor thermal comfort, but for the adaptive aspects to be 
implemented in the design process, the interviewed indicated that the theory has to be 
better operationalised and implemented in standards and simulation programs to 
change the engineering methods.  
In contrast, the interviewed described the complications by implementation of the 
adaptive aspects in open plan offices where the requests about the indoor climate from 
the building owners require a CTS controlled environment. One even experienced 
discomfort, unstable temperatures and draft as consequences of occupant control of the 
ventilation and external shading in an open plan office.   
In open plan offices, the risk of influence on the psycho-social work environment is 
increased. Occupants might be more sensitive to fluctuating temperatures and 
influenced by the adaptive aspects, which might have a negative impact on their 
productivity. The interviewed indicated how there is and will be an increased focus on 
occupant, flexibility and the interaction between the occupants and between the rooms 
==PAGE:76==
68 
 
in the building. Therefore, the indoor climate will be designed according to the occupant 
and depend less on the external conditions. The design of façades as for example double 
skin facades will distinguish more between internal and external conditions and reduce 
the potential for implementation of the adaptive aspects. In accordance with the 
potential and complications by the adaptive thermal comfort described in the interview 
and in paragraph 0 about the adaptive thermal comfort theory, the studies indicated a 
larger potential for implementation of adaptive aspects in the design with elements 
facades in residential buildings or single offices. In these cases the influence by the 
external conditions and thereby greater temperature ranges are justified by the 
adaptive thermal comfort model.

CONCLUSION  
Studies of the parameter variation in this project underlined opportunities and 
advantages by a design with the adaptive thermal comfort aspects. By a temperature 
offset of ±2°C in the temperature set point variation, the need for heating was reduced 
with 54%-65% and cooling with 30%. PMV showed comfort categories which exceeded 
category III where aPMV mainly were within category I-II. With an evaluation of the 
aPMV, the results showed an improved comfort category, and it can thereby be 
concluded that a design of temperature set points inspired by the adaptive thermal 
comfort theory has the potential for energy savings and justify great temperature ranges 
which are seen in the element façade with large window area and internal or external 
shading.  
By the variation of the window area, the aPMV for all façade designs during the heating 
season were within comfort category I and PMV category II. During the cooling season 
the PMV exceeded category II where aPMV comfort categories mainly were within 
category I. As the element façade with internal shading as an example, it was not 
possible with a façade design with internal shading in relation to PMV (category II) and 
daylight as criteria, where aPMV (category I) enabled a window area of 55%. It can 
thereby be concluded that a larger window area in general can be justified by the 
evaluation of the thermal comfort by aPMV.  
In both variations and by the comparison of the double skin façade with the element 
facades, the element façade showed larger improvement by evaluation of the adaptive 
model. This indicates how the adaptive thermal comfort model tolerates a wider 
temperature range and thereby a design of the element façade in relation to thermal 
comfort. With an acceptance and implementation of adaptive aspects in a design of the 
façade, indoor climate and open plan office as in Nordea Headquarter, it can be 
concluded from the results that there in general is a potential of energy savings and 
improved daylight by lager opening areas, while the thermal comfort can be maintained.  
The interviewed indicated that the indoor climate in open plan offices will be designed 
according to the occupant and depend less on the external conditions. The design of 
façades such as double skin facades with its advantages will distinguish more between 
internal and external conditions and reduce the potential for implementation of the 
adaptive aspects. In accordance with the potential and complications by the adaptive 
thermal comfort described in the interview, it can be concluded that there is a larger 
potential for implementation of adaptive aspects in the design with elements facades in 
residential buildings or single offices. In this type of buildings and by the element façade 
design, the influence by the external conditions and thereby greater temperature ranges 
are justified by the adaptive thermal comfort model.

PERSPECTIVE - PRODUCTIVITY  
It was in the interview mentioned that a change in the consideration of thermal comfort 
could influence the psycho-social work environment. Thus, it has to be justified if the 
occupant’s environment will be deteriorated or the occupant’s productivity and work 
performance influenced by implementation of adaptive aspects. Productivity is defined 
as the extent to which activities have provided performance in terms of system goal. 
[10] It is closely related to the physiological thermoregulations and psychological 
processes, which are described both in the general and in the adaptive theory of 
thermal comfort.  
Some research states that the implementation of the adaptive thermal comfort model in 
temperate climates will have no effect on productivity or consequences for the mental 
performance, provided that the adaptive opportunities are available and occupants do 
not have high expectation to their indoor environment. This is substantiated by 
considering thermal sensation rather than temperature as a variable for prediction and 
estimation of thermal effect on performance. Using thermal sensation as a 
representative of the thermal exposure to quantify the effect on performance of the 
thermal climate, the adaptive thermal comfort model resulted in lower energy 
consumptions without mechanical cooling and at the same time, the occupant’s 
performance was only slightly lower despite large temperature variation. [11] [1] 
With the consideration of the temperatures as the variable for estimation of the thermal 
effect on performance, another view of the subject described how the neutral comfort 
temperature are in close relation to the productivity and performance. Perceived 
productivity depends on whether the indoor environment is experienced comfortable or 
uncomfortable in relation to a neutral comfort temperature, which established by the 
adaptive thermal comfort theory and can cause temperatures up to 30°C. [10]  
In an additional paper, it is distinguished between the optimal comfort temperatures, 
the productivity, the efficiency and the rate of the occupants work. It is described that 
performance might not necessarily be optimal under the thermal comfortable 
conditions or under alternating temperatures, as it is needed to distinguish between the 
percentage errors and of the rate of work. The percentages of errors might not change 
with the temperature while the rate of work might be lowest at the temperature the 
occupants found comfortable according to the adaptive thermal comfort theory. A high 
productivity and performance of the work describes a high rate of work with an 
acceptable percentage of errors. This is not necessarily obtained at the optimal thermal 
comfort applied by the adaptive thermal comfort theory. [43]  
The understanding of thermal comfort effects on productivity and work performance 
remain ambiguous and since there is no common agreement on the definition and 
methodology to measure productivity in offices, which reflects in different results and 
opinion as described. As indicated, it is argued that warmer environments decrease the 
productivity, that there is a positive effect on productivity when people have adaptive 
opportunities and that it is not the indoor air temperature but the perception of general 
==PAGE:79==
71 
 
thermal comfort which influence the productivity. This indicates the uncertainties about 
the definition and measurement of productivity. As indicated in this project there are 
advantages by the implementation of the adaptive thermal comfort theory in office 
buildings in temperate climates. These advantages have to emphasized and 
operationalised since the salary costs of occupants a typical commercial building are 
about two orders of magnitude more expensive than the operational energy and 
maintenance. Thus, as reflected on and mentioned in the interview, the advantages by 
the adaptive thermal comfort model have to be evaluated with the influence of the 
productivity. [10] [11]
