Abstract
In recent years, there have been many GDPR violations, concluding the year of 2023
with a total amount of €2,055,170,6421 in fines with Article 25 ”Data protection by design
and by default” being among one of the most serious and commonly violated regulations.
Some known solutions for assessing GDPR compliance are the interview style or event
log auditing of the system processing which both are reactive solutions and are considered
non-ideal for large organizations such as IKEA where there is great divergence among
hundreds of applications. Therefore, in this thesis, the focus is on creating a solution that
is accessible and can be performed by non-GDPR experts to take a proactive approach
to the compliance check. Based on IKEA’s infrastructure, an abstraction was developed
that fosters adaptability by other organizations to describe the data processing of the or-
ganization’s systems. This was utilized to design the verification criteria and algorithms
that are used in automation to perform compliance checks for Article 25. The verification
algorithms successfully flag violations on a prototype system, therefore proving that con-
tinuous integration pipelines could include similar implementations to perform compliance
checks prior to the release of any new data processing features.
Keywords: Process Mining, General Data Protection Regulation, Compliance Checking,
Software Engineering.

Introduction
In recent years, there have been many General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) vio-
lations2 and often they involve major organizations with certain instances of these viola-
tions resulting in significant fines. For example, Facebook (Platforms Ireland Limited)3 in
November of 2022 with a fine of €265 million, Instagram (Meta Platforms, Inc.)4 in Octo-
ber of 2022 with a fine of €405 million and TikTok (TikTok Limited)5 in September of 2023
with a fine of €345 million. These examples are just a small sample of violations from
some of the most well-known corporations but numerous other companies have faced
multiple fines with just May 2023 amounting to €1,208,852,6406 in fines. A commonly
found GDPR Article in the violations in GDPR Enforcement Tracker7 is Article 25 which
defines the ”Data protection by design and by default”8. For example, this was the case
for Clearview AI9 that used geolocation and biometrics but such usage of information
was not legally based. In another case, Facebook 10 did not implement privacy by de-
sign which resulted in a “significant” number of personal data to be scrapped and shared
online. Other cases, such as one involving TikTok11 showcase ”dark patterns and by de-
fault settings” that lead to users accepting less privacy-friendly conditions which ultimately
placed under-aged users’ account information on the public by default12.
Problem Statement: Based on the violation records, it is apparent that often the system
infrastructures are not created to be compliant by design which results in infringements of
the GDPR Article 25. A known solution for assessing compliance has been interviews of
questions13 between legal teams and engineering teams which are considered demand-
ing in both time and human resources. Another solution is to use event-based compliance
frameworks similar to the one presented by Barrientos et al. 2023 [1] which do not cover
organizations that can not efficiently collect and process log events due to their size and
divergence of systems. Such an example is IKEA’s system infrastructure, which consists
of 800 different applications across the world which practice the interview style, which
has proven to be inefficient, as well as they have also considered the implementation
of an event-based compliance system but deemed it as not feasible due to the afore-
mentioned reasons. Furthermore, event-based compliance frameworks are by nature
performed after the collection of event logs, indicating a reactive method of assessing the
infrastructure’s compliance therefore introducing delays in the discovery of non-compliant
processes, leading to potential vulnerabilities.

Background
1.1
Process Mining
Based on the book ”Process Mining: Data Science in Action” by Will Van der Aalst 2016 [3],
process mining is a new concept that combines the aspects of data mining and process
analysis by analyzing data from event logs to learn how business processes are per-
formed. This technique allows organizations to identify deviations and opportunities for
improvement by providing an evidence-based understanding of the execution of a pro-
cess. Finally, process mining extracts insights and helps with the optimization of real-life
processes by analyzing numerous data generated by everyday business operations such
as logs, events, databases, etc..
1.1.1
Process mining types
There are different types of process mining:
Process Discovery: This involves learning a process model from event log data, offer-
ing insights into the actual workflows without prior assumptions. For example, the Alpha
Algorithm, as described by Mikolajczak and Chen 2005 [4], discovers a model from order-
ing event logs based on their causality and constructs a directed graph that represents a
process flow.
Process Enhancement: This involves the improvement of an existing process model by
integrating insights derived from event data. For example, these insights could be related
to bottlenecks, resource allocations, the discovery of process variations and more.
Conformance Checking: This type compares observed behaviour of a process against
modelled behaviour to verify if the real-life executions adhere to the predefined processes,
aiding in compliance and auditing. For example, some techniques are fitness checking to
calculate how well the events follow the model, token replay to identify deviations, trace
alignment analysis and more.
Operational Support: This aspect provides warnings, predictions and recommendations
by applying process mining in real-time using current data. For example, this can provide
information for root cause analysis to identify the source of a problem and predictive an-
alytics to forecast trends and issues.
1.1.2
Process model types
In the same book, the discussion about process models essentially is broken down into
two main types:
Informal Process Models: These models resemble sketches of ”boxes with arrows” that
are used for describing complex processes in a comprehensible format, as they don’t have
strict syntax and they are not suitable for technical analysis. For example, an informal
model could be a simple flowchart created for a presentation.
Formal Process Models: These models have strict syntax and semantics and are used
for in-depth process analysis such as its efficiency, bottlenecks and compliance. Exam-
ples include Petri nets models as described in the same book, BPMN models as described
by Dumas et al. 2018 [5], UML diagrams as described by Engels et al. 2005 [6], etc..

1.1.3
Dynamic Condition Response Graphs
Distributed Dynamic Condition Response graph (DCR), as described by Hildebrandt and
Mukkamala Oct. 2011 [7], is a declarative and event-based process model which incor-
porates roles and events connected by directed arrows which represent four main types
of relationships: condition, response, include, and exclude. A condition relation means
that an event can only happen if another event has happened before; a response relation
means that an event must happen after another event has happened; an include relation
means that an event enables another event to happen; and an exclude relation means
that an event disables another event from happening. The events can also be assigned
to roles which enable different actors to participate in the process execution. Conclud-
ing, DCR graphs enable the modelling of processes that are flexible and simulate real-life
processes such as health care, government, businesses, etc..
1.2
General Data Protection Regulation
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)1 came into effect in May of 2018 and is
a European legislation that protects the user’s personal information privacy. These laws
regulate the data handling, processing, storing and sharing within and across organiza-
tions, as well as the transfer of data to non-EU organisations. Also, GDPR violations
impose harsh fines on organizations with penalties reaching into the tens of millions of
euros2.
1.2.1
Data protection by design and by default
The Article 253 of the GDPR with the title ”Data protection by design and by default”,
requires data controllers to adopt the data protection principles by design, meaning from
the early stages of planning a new processing operation as well as throughout its lifecycle.
Therefore personal data must be collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes
by default which enforces that only the data, necessary for each purpose, are collected
and processed.
1.3
System Infrastructure
A system infrastructure4 consists of physical and virtual elements like hardware, software
and networks which provide a stable environment for the IT operations to bring value to the
organization and the users. As defined by the GDPR, a system infrastructure is classified
as a data controller and processor, thereby it is obligated to comply with the regulations.
Software: This includes the programs and applications that run on hardware and provide
logic and data processing.
Hardware: These are the physical devices that provide computing power, storage capac-
ity, network connectivity and user interface.
Network: This includes the systems that connect hardware and software components
together and allow data transferring.

Related Work
2.1.1
A compliance-based architecture for supporting GDPR
accountability in cloud computing
The paper presented by Zhou, Barati, and Shafiq 2023 [8] introduced an architecture
designed to ensure GDPR compliance within the context of cloud computing. This frame-
work utilizes blockchain technology and introduces a domain model that places emphasis
on GDPR’s accountability aspect. Moreover, the research entailed experimenting with a
cloud-based infrastructure that integrated a blockchain-based data integrity system. The
prototype presented showcased its efficiency in handling high-throughput requirements
within cloud environments. However, it’s worth noting that the discussions mention the
need for future work in scaling the solution to assess its effectiveness. This is because de-
centralized blockchain technology introduces significant costs and throughput constraints
when applied on a larger scale.
2.1.2
Automating GDPR Compliance Verification for Cloud-hosted
Services
A cloud-based service is presented by Barati, Theodorakopoulos, and Rana 2020 [9]
which is used to showcase the automation of GDPR compliance checking on a purchase
order system based on time constraints. The paper identified temporal logic formulas in
order to verify the GDPR rules against the system activities. These activities are repre-
sented as abstracted automata and indicate the purpose of data processing which are
mapped to time constraints to indicate the causality of their completion.
2.1.3
Verifying compliance in process choreographies: Foundations,
algorithms, and implementation
In the paper presented by Fdhila et al. 2022 [10] a Decomposition Algorithm is presented
for a compliance check of business processes on a distributed scheme, with multiple
partners that request privacy through minimum communication and information sharing
between them during the compliance checking. The algorithm utilizes the transitivity prop-
erties of business processes expressed in first-order predicate logic and more specifically,
Linear Temporal Logic1. However, the paper also emphasizes the importance of con-
ducting an empirical study to systematically evaluate use cases from different application
domains, comparing the quantitative differences between manual and automated compli-
ance approaches in the context of local compliance checking assertions derived from the
decomposition of global compliance rules.
2.1.4
Learning to Map the GDPR to Logic Representation on
DAPRECO-KB
The paper presented by Nguyen et al. 2022 [11] provides an example of Logic usage to
translate GDPR regulations by contracting a semantic parser that employs a combination

2.1.5
Business Process Compliance Using Reference Models of Law
The framework presented by López et al. 2020 [2] describes how to design and verify pro-

ulation document are translated into two distinct DCR graphs in order to compare and
perform the GDPR compliance assessment. The understandability is achieved through
the usage of graphs, which serve as a user-friendly medium to express the conditions,
flow and time relations that are specified on a regulation document from GDPR or an
organisation process.
2.2
Motivation
In the context of GDPR, software services fall into the categories of data controllers and
processors because they involve the exchange, processing, and storage of data across
various services, therefore they are obligated to respect the GDPR regulations. According
to IKEA, compliance checks on service infrastructures are conducted manually through
internal interviews between IT and Legal teams which are time-consuming and demand
the involvement of multiple parties within an organization, highlighting the need for au-
tomation. Ultimately, the automation of this process would allow for more frequent and
accurate compliance checks.
Throughout the thesis duration, weekly meetings were held with a Senior Security Engi-
neer from IKEA, Panagiotis Vasilikos. He provided valuable insights into IKEA’s compliance-
checking processes by explaining real examples. An illustration of such processes in-
volves manual compliance checks conducted through interviews between legal and IT
employees. This approach proved to be time-consuming and lacked flexibility for the
different services across IKEA’s 800 digital products. Additionally, these interviews were
conducted every several months resulting in a reactive approach to compliance checking.
Consequently, by the time of the subsequent review, there was a chance that engineers
had released new processes in production, which could potentially be non-compliant. The
current compliance check method has created the need for a more effective and scalable
approach which should be proactive and seamlessly integrated during the development
stage, instead of being intermittently applied after release while in production.
This current thesis was developed in response to the insights described earlier from IKEA
and seeks to automate compliance checking. It aims to translate GDPR legal documents
in a comprehensive format using DCR Graphs in order to extract the compliance verifica-
tion conditions and then design them into proactive automated compliance checks. The
purpose of this automation is to make it easier and more efficient for engineers, who may

2.3
Research Aim
Concluding, ensuring that organizations and their systems meet their privacy require-
ments typically involves manual privacy assessments conducted through interviews. It’s
important to note that the individuals involved in this process are not experts in GDPR
compliance checks, and this manual approach is both inefficient and detached from the
digital product development process.
Furthermore, it’s evident that there hasn’t been much exploration into a proactive auto-
mated approach to GDPR compliance methods for service infrastructures, especially one
that is closer to engineers and can continuously be applied in real-time during the devel-
opment phase.
Therefore, this thesis’s aim is to explore the feasibility of GDPR compliance checking
on a system infrastructure based, on the ”GDPR Compliance Check Framework using
Reference Models of Law” as presented by López et al. 2020 [2].

3.1
Problem Identification
In order to justify the thesis aim, the first step is to identify the problem to investigate.
The problem addressed is related to the lack of methods to perform compliance checking
on infrastructure systems, that are not overly complicated, proactive and continuous. In
the IKEA case, encompassing 800 digital products, the currently used method lacks the
adaptability to reflect all system processes, as the questionnaires and the interviews are
mostly standardised.
The initial approach involved recording events from different processes for the purpose
of performing Conformance Checking techniques in order to assess the fitting of real-life
processes to the reference model of law. However, following discussions with Panos, it
became clear that applying this method across all 800 systems poses more challenges in
terms of scalability. It requires a considerable amount of implementation time, potentially
impacting the productivity of the team and not aligning with the practical requirements of
the case at hand. More importantly, this method would still entail a reactive approach to
compliance checking, with assessments being carried out only after the release of code
and the collection and analysis of events.
Consequently, the research for this thesis shifted towards a different approach, focusing
on developing a compliance check method that is proactive and closer to the engineers.
This solution allows to interpret the data handling aspects of a process during feature
development without affecting the features’ implementation time. In addition, this method
allows developers to denote the process actions of data storage, sharing, collection, and
other related activities before release, enabling real-time, proactive compliance checking.
To facilitate this, Panos designed the Privacy Containers language, which in the future will
be incorporated into IKEA’s infrastructure with the help of IKEA’s security team.
In more detail, IKEA’s Privacy Containers language presents the idea of Privacy Infrastruc-
ture as Code. This enables engineers to comprehensively document the privacy metadata
of a digital product using Terraform1 files. Being system agnostic, Terraform is versa-
tile, operating indifferently on various cloud platforms or without any cloud provider at all.
IKEA’s security team has been actively working on incorporating Privacy Containers with
the aim of eventually replacing manual compliance checks with an automated process.
In conclusion, the thesis aim is to address the issue mentioned in the section 2.3 which
is to design an automated method for the compliance check which could be performed
proactively and continuously.

4.1.1
Requirements
Based on Article 25 of GDPR, The organization’s obligation is (1) to identify the different
categories of business purposes that fit the business needs and (2) to set a list of the
required personal data that are allowed to be processed for each purpose. During the
design of the purposes, a data protection officer (DPO) needs to be involved.
The IT’s obligation is to describe the system’s purposes for processing personal data for
every system and its sub-processes.
Therefore, for every part of the infrastructure where personal data are used, the system
must have a declaration of:
- Purpose description of the data processing
- Used Data Categories
- Storage Retention period
- Origin of personal data
- Sharing of personal data to other defined system purposes

4.2.3
Compliance Assessment v0.1
The compliance assessment determines whether the system processing is compliant with
the allowed processing defined by the Organization and the PDO. The allowed process-
ing describes all the allowed Purpose Policies (Purpose_Policies) as well as what data
are allowed for each purpose. The algorithm compares all the system’s Compute Con-
tainers (Compute_Containers) against the Purpose Policies (Purpose_Policies) to de-
tect any deviations that can be considered violations. For a system to be fully compliant,
all its destination systems must also be compliant, therefore any destination system’s
proc_def that uses the shared data, must also be compared against the Purpose Policies
(Purpose_Policies).
4.2.3.1
System Compliance
Compliance status relies on the extent to which individual processing definitions
(proc_def) conform to the verification criteria. If all proc_def meet the Verification
Criteria, the Verification algorithm [1 and 2] yields a True outcome; conversely, a
False result is given if at least one proc_def does not adhere to at least one of the
Verification Criteria.

4.2.8
Demonstration of violations
In the violations tree [4.4], moving from left to right, there is an initial Start node that leads
to a hierarchical structure that is based on the class diagram [4.3]. In more detail, the
Definitions, as the first nodes branching from the Start node, represent the proc_def com-
ponent. Then each Definition node branches out to its components which then branches
recursively until the last leaves. To verify the purpose compliance of all the proc_def com-
ponents, the verification algorithm examines all their components, including data usages
(Data_Usages), data subject (data_subject), data categories (Data_Categories), process
definition destinations (ProcDef_Destinations). Then it proceeds to explore recursively
all destination proc_def components within ProcDef_Destinations. The verdict of pur-
pose compliance is inheritable, meaning that the non-compliance of one child component,
results in non-compliance for its parent components. Through this approach, the algorithm
flags cases where process definitions share data with non-compliant destinations.

IKEA’s Instance
Usually, organization systems face some limitations in regard to their frameworks and their
system capabilities. Based on insights given by Panos for the IKEA’s infrastructure, the
privacy framework has an upper bound of 52 distinct Purposes of Usage (POU) per sys-
tem that can be recognized with the PDO which correlates to the Purpose_Policies in the
currently presented components. Taking into consideration that there are approximately
800 different systems, then the upper bound of the number of policies in Purpose_Policies
would be p = 41, 600. However, the privacy framework is expected to be upgraded reach-
ing 100 distinct POUs per system in the future as well as the information defined in the
Compute_Containers can be more fine-grated and could include more information than
what is defined in the Purpose_Policies, thus introducing a possible high time complexity.

Discussion
In this section, there will be a discussion of whether the research aim that was described in
the methodology has been fulfilled. The thesis aim was stated as "Explore the feasibility of
GDPR compliance checking on a system infrastructure, based on the ”GDPR Compliance
Check Framework using Reference Models of Law” presented by López et al. 2020 [2]
which was broken into the following steps:
1. Translate the GDPR Article 25 legal text into DCR graph with systematic steps based
on the Compliance Check Framework.
2. Layout the requirements, assumptions and a scenario of an organization and its sys-
tem infrastructure.
3. Formalize the Privacy Containers components.
4. Define the verification criteria for the compliance checking.
5. Design an algorithmic approach to perform the compliance checking based on the
verification criteria.
6. Demonstrate violations discovered from the compliance checking via an example im-
plementation.
All the above steps were completed in order to explore the feasibility of the compliance
check in infrastructure systems. The proposed approach introduced an automation of the
compliance checking of Article 25 to be adapted as a proactive check during the continu-
ous integration in the development phase of system features. The implementation of this
approach is assisted by the representation of the legal text into a DCR graph based on
the framework presented by López et al. 2020 [2], which provides comprehensive step-by-
step activities to verify the compliance checking of a system. In addition, the assumptions
and requirements highlight that Article 25 requires the identification of its prerequisites in
order to perform an accurate compliance check. Subsequently, the formalization of the
Privacy Containers provided an abstraction which can be extended and may be used by
other organizations to adopt a similar solution. Furthermore, the definition of the verifica-
tion criteria based on the requirements of Article 25 and their application in the verification
algorithms showcase how to design an automation for compliance checking. Moreover,
the demonstration of violations extracted by a prototype implementation of the verification
algorithms offers a user-friendly method for non-GDPR experts, such as engineers, to
flag non-compliant processes for the implemented processes. Lastly, the evaluation of
the algorithm’s time complexity showcases a time complexity dependent on the number
of processes and their shared destination, which in regards to the case of IKEA’s infras-
tructure and framework, presents a suitable possible solution. In conclusion, based on
the above, the feasibility of performing compliance checking of a system’s infrastructure
is proven successful.
The outcomes presented in section [5] can be mapped to real-life scenarios where the
development team is detached from the legal concepts and the automation of the compli-
ance check could catch any violation prior to the release of a new feature. Outcome(1):
This example would be possible when an engineer is defining a new process definitions
which has not yet been recognized by the PDO. Outcome(2): This outcome could repre-
sent the example of a process definition being updated with new usages that are not yet
recognised by the PDO in any purpose policy which should be raised as a violation alert.
Outcome(3): This scenario represents cases where the actual processing requires more

data than initially agreed and therefore this would trigger another session with the PDO
to approve the need for processing those data categories. Outcome(4): Finally, the pro-
cessing definition ”Definition-HR_Employee_Alerts” is an example of a process definition

The limitations: An obvious limitation in the research was the accessibility of event logs,

like Article 25. After some discussions with Panos, it was apparent that the implications
of collecting events from 800 systems, as in IKEA’s case, storing and processing them

checking continuously via event logs would not be viable. To showcase an alternative
solution that does not need event logs and therefore is also proactive, this thesis used the
Article 25, which is commonly violated and is the most approachable candidate for such
solution.
Another predicted limitation was the time constraint, due to this the verification criteria and

the version 1. Thereby, leaving out other principles, like the data retention check, which

Conclusion
The application of GDPR in the infrastructure side of an organization poses many chal-
lenges for the companies. In the thesis problem space, the presented work explores the
feasibility of GDPR compliance checking on a system infrastructure based on the ”GDPR
Compliance Check Framework using Reference Models of Law” presented by López et
al. 2020 [2]. The focus was on developing an automation for performing compliance
checking on system data processing that is proactive, in order to replace periodic manual
interviews. Consequently, compliance checks can be shifted from being conducted a few
months post-release to before the release of a process. In addition, compliance is more
flexible to the specific process, involves fewer people and takes less time.
The proposed approach addresses the research aim which is introduced in section [4].
In particular, the current solution focuses on GDPR Article 25 and utilizes an abstraction
of the Privacy Container language introduced by IKEA. In addition, it maps the require-
ments of Article 25 to verification criteria and verification algorithms which successfully
flag non-compliant processes. Moreover, the time complexity of the algorithms is ana-
lyzed based on the case of IKEA. The feasibility of the compliance check is proven based
on a prototype implementation and the demonstration of extracted violations.
7.1
Future work
Based on the limitations that were encountered during the thesis, for future work it would
be beneficial to investigate a way to perform compliance checking without event log pro-
cessing to other articles that seem to require event processing, such as Article 51. More-
over, it would be a valuable addition to complete the automation for the compliance check
of Article 25 by extending the verification criteria and algorithm with more versions to in-
clude all the compliance principles, such as check for retention period, pseudonymization
of data etc.. Finally, the evaluation of the algorithms could be supplemented with a use
case benchmark to verify their scalability and robustness.
