Abstract
Advalight is a company creating laser systems that use Sum Frequency Generation (SFG)
to create 589 nm laser to treat a large variety of skin conditions. To increase SFG output
power, laser beams are guided through an LBO crystal with a series of mirrors to maximize
beam overlap. Advalight recently developed a new laser module, posing a new challenge.
The laser alignment process evolved from a 2D alignment problem to a 3D alignment
problem, increasing the difficulty of manual alignment. The purpose of this study is to
assess whether employing an automatic alignment to solve this issue is feasible. The
project describes and builds a mock­up based on Advalights ADVATx laser module, uses
image analysis to automatically align the lasers and compares the outcomes to manual
alignment. The results show that automatic alignment requires less time and achieves
a more accurate alignment than manual alignment. However, as the prototype used in
the project is a simplification of the laser module, further experiments are required in
conditions more similar to the new ADVATx system.

Introduction
Advalight is a medical device company that makes laser systems that combine 1064 nm
and 1319 nm lasers to create a 589 nm laser using an LBO non­linear crystal. Prof.
Bjerring, a laser dermatology professor, believed that current dermatology technology,
such as dye and CO2 lasers, were expensive and required extensive maintenance [1].
Therefore, Prof. Bjerring looked for solid­state lasers capable of producing high power
yellow laser in the range of 589 nm due to its high absorption in hemoglobin as shown
in Figure 1.1. Green laser at 532 nm is another wavelength used in dermatology due to
its absorption in hemoglobin. However, green lasers also have a higher absorption in the
epidermis due to high absorption in melanin as illustrated in Figure 1.2 and therefore does
not penetrate the skin to the same depth as 589 nm [2].

Optimization algorithms are used to find a global minimum or maximum by using a set of
inputs to an objective function. The difficulty of using optimization algorithms is defining
complexity of the problem, defining the cost function, choosing the best algorithm and
termination condition or maximum run time. In addition, the cost function is a limiting
factor as the only output value from the laser module is the power output which gives
limited information on the improvement between optimization steps and can lead to low
total variation leading to finding a local, rather than the global maxima.
Using single­parameter optimization based on SFG power output, there are several lim­
itations that must be considered: allowing to wide movement of the mirrors may cause
the lasers to hit the edge of the crystal causing damage. On the other hand, a too narrow
movement range can prevent the optimizer from locating an alignment that will produce
the maximum output power. In addition, to achieve optimal SFG, phase matching must
be achieved by temperature tuning, angle tuning of the LBO crystal, and temporal overlap
between the two pulsed lasers. If these parameters are not set correctly, the optimizer

may believe that the alignment is incorrect. Finally, if the lasers do not overlap, no output
is produced.
Another method for optimizing laser alignment through an LBO crystal is to use image
analysis, which involves directing the lasers at a detector, such as a camera, measuring
the distance between the two beams, and adjusting the mirrors in a feedback loop. This
approach has two main difficulties. First, by using one detector depth perception is missing
where the two lasers might overlap on the detector but separate after due to angular
misalignment. Second, finding the exact center of the beam as it can be affected by
noise, spread and saturation of the detector.

2.1.1
Non­linear Optics
For many years, the relationship between the polarization of a medium and the applied
electric field was thought to be linear. One of the main reasons this assumption was held
was the strength of the electric fields used at the time, which were not nearly as strong
as the fields inside the atoms and molecules and therefore did not give enough response
to be considered. However, this changed with the invention of lasers. Non­linear optics
is the result of laser beam interaction with materials [8].
In linear optics, a light wave acts on a molecule, which absorbs it and emits its own
light wave. However, non­linear materials have crystalline asymmetrical structures which
cause inversion asymmetry around their axes. When an intense light field enters a mate­
rial, it causes a displacement of the electrons inside the material, which begin to wiggle
due to the electromagnetic energy from the light field. However, in a non­linear material,
the wave also generates a non­linear polarization wave.

response, the electron cloud is pulled in a straight line while in a non­linear response it is
pulled diagonally [9] [10].

2.1.2
Second Harmonic Generation
SHG is a process in non­linear optics where two photons of the same frequency interact
with a non­linear material and a new photon is emitted with the combination of the photons’
wavelength, i.e., with the sum of energy of the two fundamental photons. SHG was first
demonstrated by Peter Franken et al. in 1961 at the University of Michigan when they
focused a ruby laser with a wavelength of 694 nm into a quartz sample, which resulted
in an output wavelength of 347 nm [11]. SHG has recently been employed in biological
applications, where it can now be used to image certain polysaccharides and myosin as
well as to image collagen in a variety of applications [12].

2.1.3
Sum Frequency Generation
SFG is a second order nonlinear optic process where two photons are combined into
one with the sum of their angular frequencies ω1 + ω2 = ω3, where ω1 and ω2 are the
angular frequencies of the input photons, and ω3 is of the output photon. The basic SHG
is a special case of SFG where ω1 = ω2. SHG is a more common type of SFG due
to the fact that only one input beam is required, while when ω1 ̸= ω2 two laser beams
are required. However, the important differences of SFG compared to SHG is due to
requiring two lasers they must be aligned properly to achieve high conversion, and the
phase matching condition must account for the two fundamental waves and the resulting
SFG wave. Figure 2.4 shows two lasers aligned through a mirror to traverse a non­linear
crystal and the resulting output laser [13] [10].
Figure 2.4: Sum frequency generation. Two lasers are combined into one through a non­
linear crystal [10]
The power generated by SFG with two lasers can be describes as
P3 = a ∗P1 ∗P2
(2.2)
Where P1 and P2 is the power of the input beams, P3 is the power of the output beam,
and a is the effective conversion efficiency which among others depends on factors such
as phase matching, length and material of the non­linear crystal, beam size, and spacial
overlap of the beams.

of the material, the phase matching parameter and the parameters of the interacting field.

2.2
Optimization Techniques
To maximize the output power via beam overlap, two main approaches are discussed in
this project. The first is the use of mathematical optimization, discussed in section 2.2.1,
which maximizes the output power by systematically choosing a different orientation of the
mirrors. This can be used to adjust the laser beams to find the maximum power generated
by the system. The second is the use of image analysis, discussed in section 2.2.2, which
can be used to find the laser beam hitting a detector and move it based on a reference
beam to match its position and angle.
2.2.1
Mathematical Optimization
Mathematical optimization or mathematical programming is concerned with finding the
best solution to maximize or minimize the objective while honoring the constraints. In
1827, Jean­Baptiste Joseph Fourier published a method for solving linear inequalities,
and Carl Friedrich Gauss proved that elementary row operations could be used to solve
a set of linear equations. Their work demonstrated that by using mathematical tools, one
can solve real­world problems. At that time, these tools could only solve small problems.
Today, however, much larger problems can be solved using computers [17].
Optimization models have three major components:
• Decision variable: These are factors controlled by the decision maker and have a
range of potential values, for example the mirrors’ orientation.
• Objective function: These function define the criteria for evaluating the solution. It
is a mathematical function that uses the decision variables to convert a solution into
a numerical evaluation of that solution. The function also specifies a direction of
optimization, whether to maximize or minimize the result, for example the output
power produced by SFG.
• Constraints: These are a set of functional equalities or inequalities that represent
the restrictions on what numerical values can be assigned to the decision variables,
for example the maximum angle allowed for the mirrors to not exceed the crystal
dimensions.
Mathematical optimization has a wide variety of applications in fields such as economics,
engineering, mechanics, and machine learning. There are three main ways to solve prob­

2.2.2
Image Analysis
Image analysis is the extraction of information from an image. It can include tasks such as
finding shapes, detecting edges, removing noise, counting objects or calculating statistics
for texture analysis or image quality.
Hough Circular Transform
Hough transform (HT) is a feature extraction method first introduced as a method of de­
tecting complex patterns of points in binary image data [19]. The transform determines
specific values of parameters which characterize the patterns. Spatially extended patterns
are transformed so that they produce spatially compact features in a space of possible
parameter values. The HT converts a difficult global detection problem in image space
into a more easily solved local peak detection problem in a parameter space [20] [21].
Circle Hough Transform is a specialization of Hough transform which is used to detect
circles in imperfect images. A circle can be described by the equation
(x −a)2 + (y −b)2 = r2
(2.5)
Where the center coordinates are a and b, and the radius is r. For each circle in an image,
these parameters must be determined. The transformation produces an edge image by
thresholding the image and applying any edge­detecting technique. The transform works
by marking each pixel along the edge that has been determined in the image with a circle
with a predetermined radius. During the transform, an accumulator is utilized to tally
the number of times a circle has traversed it. The location of the circular center is then
determined by the largest value in the accumulator.
Cluster Analysis
Cluster analysis is the task of grouping elements together in groups called clusters. It
is used in exploratory data analysis and is a common technique in statistical data sci­
ence [22]. Cluster analysis is used in many fields, such as machine learning, pattern
recognition, information retrieval, bio­informatics, data compression, computer graphics
and image analysis. A centroid of a cluster can be found by taking all the data points and
averaging all the x and y points [23] [24]. This technique can be used to approximate the
center of any shape in an image by binarizing the image and taking the resulting pixels
as data points.

Method
This chapter describes the process of constructing the prototype of the setup including
the process that led to the final setup and algorithms used throughout this project. The
sections in this chapter describe the material required for the test setup, the 3D compo­
nents designed an printed, and the algorithms used. Section 2.2, describes two possible
approaches for the model. As time only allowed to evaluate one, the choice was made
to proceed with image analysis rather than mathematical optimization. This choice was
made based on several reasons. First, the approach is faster to set up and to deliver
results as this setup requires less sensitive components. Second, this approach is less
dangerous as it uses low power green lasers which cannot damage the skin. Third, this
approach does not require the use of an LBO crystal, YAG crystal and Q­switches which
can be damaged or destroyed if used improperly.
Figure 3.1 shows the area of interest in the ADVATx laser module. This is the area where
the two lasers are aligned before passing through the LBO crystal. To achieve a good
conversion, the two laser need to be co­linearly and spatially aligned.

The setup uses two cameras to detect the laser beams, which can then be used to calcu­
late the line equations of the beams, and four motors (two for each mirror mount) that turn
screws on the mirror mounts, which control the horizontal and vertical tilt. The detectors
are set at different distances from the beam splitter to measure two different points of the
laser. The setup works in two stages. In the first stage, the reference beam shown in red
in Figure 3.2 will be turned on and pass through Mirror 1 and hit the two detectors forming
the reference points on the cameras. The coordinates of the laser hitting the detectors
are saved. In the second stage, the reference beam is turned off and the second beam
shown in green is turned on. It is then aligned with the reference beam using the motors
to control the tilt of the mirrors. This setup is a proof of concept that shows an automatic
alignment is able to produce acceptable alignment in an acceptable time. The fulfilment
of these parameters will be discussed in section 5. In addition, the final product must fit
in the ADVATx laser module, and be able to be attached and detached from the system.
To keep costs low Advalight would like to use materials already existing in their storage.
Therefore, the stepper motors are the same stepper motors used in their laser module,
NEMA8 SCA2018M0804­A (appendix A.2). However, due to shortage of electronic com­

3.2
Algorithms
This section discusses the algorithms used to capture images of the laser beam and
calculating the angles to adjust the mirrors. Section 3.2.1 describes the approaches taken
to pre­process and locate the center of the laser beam on the detector. Section 3.2.2
describes the calculations made to find the line equations of the lasers, and calculate the
difference in direction and position, and in section 3.2.3 calculating the steps required by
the motor to adjust the mirrors.
3.2.1
Laser center
After removing the lenses from the camera, the image of the laser beam on the detector
is shown in Figure 3.3. However, the beams high intensity on the detector causes the
detector to shut down to prevent damage. To dampen the beam, a Neutral­Density (ND)
filter was placed in front of the beam splitter. The image captured after damping is shown
in Figure 3.8.
Figure 3.8: The image obtained from the detector after dampening
The first approach used to find the center was the Hough circular transform, which was
discussed in section 2.2.2. Figure 3.9 depicts the resulting circle. The images show the
pre­processing stages. First, the green layer is filtered. Next, the image is binarized.
Lastly, an edge detection algorithm is run on the image. For a single image, this process
is quite reliable. However, the system works in a feedback loop, requiring a new image
to be captured and processed every few seconds. The problem with this approach is
that the laser beam changes slightly between each captured image and, in addition, it
can have a more oval shape than a circle. This causes the center found by the Hough
transform to vary between images, making it difficult to accurately align the beam to the
reference beam. Additionally, the Hough transform is a time­consuming operation since
it first determines the beam’s circumference, even though just the center of the beam is
needed for the analysis. Therefore, another approach was taken to locate the center of
the beam by using cluster analysis as described in section 2.2.2.

The pre­processing to find the cluster center is shown in Figure 3.10. First, the green
light layer is filtered. Next, thresholding is used to turn the image to black and white. The
image is then subjected to an area opening algorithm to reduce noise [27]. Then, area
closing is performed to remove small holes inside the circle [27]. Lastly, all the columns
and rows with a white pixel are averaged, which reveals the center of the cluster or beam.
This technique leads to a more consistent approximation of the beam center. In addition,
this method does not require calculating the radius of the circle and therefore requires
less computation time.

conversion of the angle to steps on the motors. According to both the motors data­sheets,
they provide an angle of ≈1.8 ± 5% degrees per step. According to the data­sheet of
the mirror mounts, the change in angle is ≈11 mrad/rev or ≈0.6 deg/rev. Therefore,
the conversion is 360

Discussion
The effects of noise and laser stability on the estimation of the laser center are shown in
Section 4.1. The results in Figure 4.1 demonstrate that the laser is not drifting but rather
revolving about a specific location because the distribution is uniform around the axis
center. Additionally, the vertical axis has a wider dispersion where the computed angle
can go up to 0.010 degrees, as opposed to the horizontal axis where it is closer to 0.05.
Three main elements are thought to be responsible for this noise. First, the laser stability
causes the laser to move between each image taken. Second, changes in light cause an
aperture change in the camera, changing the beam shape on the detector and changing
the estimated center. Third, the laser is held on a hinge, which can move slightly due to
vibrations. The reason believed to cause a larger spread on the vertical axis is that there
is a lower pixel density, which causes smaller changes to have a larger effect. The results
also show that the calculation for mirror 2 is more sensitive to small variations. This is due
to mirror 2 being further away from the detector, and therefore smaller changes have a
larger effect. These findings have implications for future experiments in that there should
be a spread of around 0.003, 0.010 on mirrors 1 and 2 on the vertical axis and a spread of
around 0.001, 0.05 on mirrors 1 and 2 on the horizontal axis. There are four techniques
to reduce the noise levels. First, by utilizing a more stable laser. Second, by managing
the room’s lighting conditions. Thirdly, by giving the lasers a more secure mount. Lastly,
capturing several images, calculating the beam center and averaging the results.
The mirrors’ stability, as well as the motors’ horizontal adjustments, are shown in Figures
4.2 and 4.3. While the mirror and motors do not produce more noise than the laser stability
on average, they do produce a few outliers with a substantially higher correction degree.
This can be caused by five reasons. The first reason is that the drill bit adapter between
the motor and the mirror may be a little loose, resulting in a different first motion end
position, but this motion then stabilizes. The second reason is that, as a result of trouble
accelerating and insufficient torque to spin the mirror mount screws, the motors may be
missing steps, which causes an angle difference and outliers. The third reason is that the
drill bit on the motors may cause the mirrors to move slightly, causing the beam to move
on the horizontal axis. Fourth, the drill bit adapter is pressure­fitted to the motor, which
can cause the adapter to slip on the shaft when turning. Finally, the motors provide an
accuracy of ±5% and the mirror mounts, while not specifying the exact variation, state
that one rotation is ≈11mrad, which can result in a larger spread. Similar results can
be seen in Figures 4.4 and 4.5 showing the mirrors, and motors stability on the on the
horizontal axis. To eliminate these affects three improvements can be implemented. First,
Using motors with more torque. Second, using longer bit drill adapters with a spring can
relieve pressure on the mirror mount, reducing mirror twisting. Finally, by using a shaft
with a smooth side, the adapter can be fitted with a screw to keep it from slipping. The
implications of these results for the subsequent experiments include a somewhat wider
spread and a few outliers.

6
Conclusion
This project introduced Advalight’s inception and mission, the ADVATx laser system de­
veloped by Advalight, and the issues that arise from changing to the new design of the
ADVATx laser module. The issue is that the alignment process of the two lasers cannot
be seen as a 2D problem anymore and is now a 3D problem. A way to solve this problem
was proposed by using motors attached to the mirrors to automatically align the lasers.
The approach used is a simplified prototype of the laser alignment segment derived from
the ADVATx laser module, using 2 detectors to calculate the line equation of the laser and
calculate the difference in angle and position between the two lasers, and using the motors
to adjust. Several conclusions can be drawn from the results. Noise affecting the laser
caused by the laser stability and environmental effects can cause a noticeable change in
calculation, especially on the vertical axis. In addition, mirror 2 is more sensitive to these
changes. Due to the motor missing steps or loose drill bit adapters, mirror movements
can sometimes move an erroneous number of steps. However, this is not the general
trend. There was a discrepancy in the calculations, causing the angle of mirror 1 to be
wrongly calculated after a movement of mirror 2 as shown in Figure 3.12. Adding this to
the calculations caused a substantial improvement. The automatic alignment was faster
and more accurate than an Advalight employee alignment using the same prototype, re­
ducing the alignment time by up to two minutes and increasing the overlap percentage by
up to 22%. These are promising initial results. However, several improvements can be
made to improve performance further, such as using more stable lasers, stronger motors,
and improvements to the algorithm to locate the laser center.
6.1
Future work
The setup presented in this project is a simplification of the actual setup in the ADVATx
system. First, the lasers in the setup are green visible laser pointers, while in the system
they are infrared lasers with wavelengths of 1064 nm and 1319 nm. Therefore, one of
the next steps will be to change the detectors from HD cameras to IR cameras. Second,
the setup does not use a lens to focus the lasers before passing through the LBO and
does not use an LBO. The addition of these two components will require calculating the
focal length of the lens and calculating the refraction caused by the crystal. Advalight
requires the setup to be modular to mount and unmount the laser modules, therefore a
new design of the motor mounts is required. Additional algorithms to locate the laser
center can be tested and compared. To optimize the alignment, this project only used
image analysis. However, another approach proposed that can be tested and compared is
mathematical optimization with the output power as the decision variable, and the effects
of different prior manual overlaps can be tested to see how they affect the automatic
alignment. Lastly, more experiments are required with prototypes similar to or close to
the Advalights ADVATx laser module.

POLARIS-K05 Usage Tips 
When mounting the POLARIS-K05, or any other high performance opto-mechanical 
device, it is important to follow a few principles: 
1)
Match Materials: Due to its relatively low coefficient of
thermal expansion, stainless steel was chosen as the
material from which to fabricate the Polaris mount.
Whether mounting the POLARIS-K05 to a post, a block, or
directly onto a table, we recommend using mounting
components fabricated from the same material.
2)
Use a Wide Post: The Polaris’s performance is optimized
for use with a Ø1" RS series of posts. These posts provide
two planes of contact with the mount. These planes of
contact help to confine the bottom of the mount during
variations in the surrounding temperature, thereby
minimizing potential alignment issues.
3)
Mount an Optic only when the Mount is out of a Setup:
Since an optic is prone to movement within its mounting
bore, all optics should be mounted with the Polaris out of
the setup to ensure accurate mounting that will minimize
misalignment effects. First, ensure that there is no
grease, dirt, or dust in the optic bore or on the optic
itself. If any of these particulates are present, remove
them with clean compressed air and/or clean the bore
and optic with acetone or methanol. Next, with the
mount orientated at ~45° so that it stands on its corner
adjuster, place the optic into the bore. Lightly tap the
edge of the faceplate with the plastic handle of a
balldriver. This will adjust the optic’s position so that it
makes the correct contact with the two contact lines and
the locating lip. Finally, use a balldriver or hex key to
engage the optic retaining screw, preferably to the
specific torque described in principal #5.
4)
Front Plate’s Position: Although the mount is designed to
allow adjustments of up to 9.5°, to achieve the best
performance, it is recommended that the front plate be
kept as parallel as possible to the back plate. This ensures
the highest stability of the adjustments.
5)
Mount as Close to the Table’s
Surface as Possible: To minimize
the impact of vibrations and temperature
changes, it is recommended that your setup
have as low of a profile as possible. Using short posts
will reduce the Y-axis translation caused by
temperature variations and will minimize any
movements caused by vibrations. The POLARIS-K05
kinematic mirror mount can also be mounted directly
onto an optical table surface. By doing so, the
instability introduced by a post will be eliminated.
6)
Polish and Clean the Points of Contact: We highly
recommend that the points of contact between the
mount and the post as well as the post and the table
are clean and free of scratches or defects. For best
results, we recommend polishing the table’s surface
with a polishing stone and then polishing the bottom
and top of the post as well as the bottom of the mount
using a polishing pad (Item # LFG1P).
7)
Try to Minimize Deflection Caused by the Hand while
Adjusting: When making adjustments, we recommend
applying as little force as possible. There is no
detectable play on the POLARIS-K05 screws by feel;
however, one would still want to apply pure torque on
the adjusters instead of torque and a directional force.
This will ensure the shortest settling time and greatly
reduce the chance of alignment change after
adjustment.
8)
Use specified Torques for the Mount and the Mirror
Clamping: Since the mirror expands at a different rate
than the mounting mechanism, it is essential to hold
the optic firmly in place. A torque of 6 to 10 oz-in
[0.375 to 0.625 lb-in; 0.04 to 0.07 N∙m] applied to the
optic’s mounting setscrew (Torque Wrench Item #
TD24) has been found to produce optimal results.
Furthermore, when tightening the Polaris mount to a
Ø1” post, a torque of 14 to 16 lb-in [1.6 to 1.8 N∙m]
(Torque Wrench Item # TD75) was found to be optimal
and minimized the mount’s slippage attributed to
temperature changes.
Not Recommended:

Do not take the adjusters out of the bushings as it can cause the threading to be
contaminated, greatly reducing the mount’s fine adjustment performance.

Do not pull the front plate away as it might stretch the spring
beyond their operating range or crack the sapphire seats.

The A4984 is a complete microstepping motor driver with 
built-in translator for easy operation. It is designed to operate 
bipolar stepper motors in full-, half-, quarter-, and eighth-step 
modes. Step modes are selectable by MSx logic inputs. It has 
an output drive capacity of up to  35 V and ±2 A. The A4984 
includes a fixed off-time current regulator which has the ability 
to operate in Slow or Mixed decay modes.
The ET package meets customer requirements for no smoke 
no fire (NSNF) designs by adding no-connect pins between 
critical output, sense, and supply pins. So, in the case of a 
pin-to-adjacent-pin short, the device does not cause smoke 
or fire. Additionally, the device does not cause smoke or fire 
when any pin is shorted to ground or left open.
The translator is the key to the easy implementation of the 
A4984. Simply inputting one pulse on the STEP input drives 
the motor one microstep. There are no phase sequence tables, 
high frequency control lines, or complex interfaces to program. 
The A4984 interface is an ideal fit for applications where a 
complex microprocessor is unavailable or is overburdened.
During stepping operation, the chopping control in the A4984 
automatically selects the current decay mode, Slow or Mixed.

Device Operation. The A4984 is a complete microstep-
ping motor driver with a built-in translator for easy operation 
with minimal control lines. It is designed to operate bipolar 
stepper motors in full-, half-, quarter-, and eighth-step modes. 
The currents in each of the two output full-bridges and all of the 
N-channel DMOS FETs are regulated with fixed off-time PWM 
(pulse width modulated) control circuitry. At each step, the current 
for each full-bridge is set by the value of its external current-sense 
resistor (RS1 and RS2), a reference voltage (VREF), and the output 
voltage of its DAC (which in turn is controlled by the output of 
the translator).
At power-on or reset, the translator sets the DACs and the phase 
current polarity to the initial Home state (shown in Figures 8 
through 11), and the current regulator to Mixed Decay Mode for 
both phases. When a step command signal occurs on the STEP 
input, the translator automatically sequences the DACs to the 
next level and current polarity. (See Table 2 for the current-level 
sequence.) The microstep resolution is set by the combined effect 
of the MSx inputs, as shown in Table 1. 
When stepping, if the new output levels of the DACs are lower 
than their previous output levels, then the decay mode for the 
active full-bridge is set to Mixed. If the new output levels of the 
DACs are higher than or equal to their previous levels, then the 
decay mode for the active full-bridge is set to Slow. This auto-
matic current decay selection improves microstepping perfor-
mance by reducing the distortion of the current waveform that 
results from the back EMF of the motor.
Microstep Select (MSx). The microstep resolution is set by 
the voltage on logic inputs MSx, as  shown in Table 1. The MS1 
pin has a 100 kΩ pull-down resistance, and the MS2 pin has a 
50 kΩ pull-down resistance. When changing the step mode, the 
change does not take effect until the next STEP rising edge.
If the step mode is changed without a translator reset, and abso-
lute position must be maintained, it is important to change the 
step mode at a step position that is common to both step modes in 
order to avoid missing steps. When the device is powered down 
or reset due to TSD or an over current event the translator is set to 
the home position which is by default common to all step modes.
Mixed Decay Operation. The bridge operates in Mixed 
decay mode, at power-on and reset, and during normal running 
according to the ROSC configuration and the step sequence, as 
shown in Figures 8 through 11. During Mixed decay, when the 
trip point is reached, the A4984 initially goes into a fast decay 
mode for 31.25% of the off-time, tOFF . After that, it switches to 
Slow decay mode for the remainder of tOFF. A timing diagram for 
this feature appears on the next page.
Typically, mixed decay is only necessary when the current in the 
winding is going from a higher value to a lower value as determined 
by the state of the translator. For most loads automatically selected 
mixed decay is convenient because it minimizes ripple when the 
current is rising and prevents missed steps when the current is falling. 
For some applications where microstepping at very low speeds is 
necessary, the lack of back EMF in the winding causes the current to 
increase in the load quickly, resulting in missed steps. This is shown 
in Figure 2. By pulling the ROSC pin to ground, mixed decay is set 
to be active 100% of the time, for both rising and falling currents, and 
prevents missed steps as shown in Figure 3. If this is not an issue, it 
is recommended that automatically selected mixed decay be used, 
because it will produce reduced ripple currents. Refer to the Fixed 
Off-Time section for details.
Low Current Microstepping. Intended for applications 
where the minimum on-time prevents the output current from 
regulating to the programmed current level at low current steps. 
To prevent this, the device can be set to operate in Mixed decay 
mode on both rising and falling portions of the current waveform. 
This feature is implemented by shorting the ROSC pin to ground. 
In this state, the off-time is internally set to 30 µs.

to a predefined Home state (shown in Figures 8 through 11) and 
turns off all of the FET outputs. All STEP inputs are ignored until 
the ¯R¯  ¯E¯  ¯S¯  ¯E¯  ¯T¯   input is set to high. 
Step Input (STEP). A low-to-high transition on the STEP 
input sequences the translator and advances the motor one incre-
ment. The translator controls the input to the DACs and the direc-
tion of current flow in each winding.  The size of the increment is 
determined by the combined state of the MSx inputs.

Internal PWM Current Control. Each full-bridge is con-
trolled by a fixed off-time PWM current control circuit that limits 
the load current to a desired value, ITRIP . Initially, a diagonal pair

through the motor winding and the current sense resistor, RSx. 
When the voltage across RSx equals the DAC output voltage, the 
current sense comparator resets the PWM latch. The latch then 
turns off either the source FET (when in Slow decay mode) or the 
sink and source FETs (when in Mixed decay mode).

RSx and the voltage at the VREF pin. The transconductance func-
tion is approximated by the maximum value of current limiting,

Shorted-Load and Short-to-Ground Protection. 
If the motor leads are shorted together, or if one of the leads is 
shorted to ground, the driver will protect itself by sensing the 
overcurrent event and disabling the driver that is shorted, protect-
ing the device from damage. In the case of a short-to-ground, the 
device will remain disabled (latched) until the S¯  ¯L¯  ¯E¯  ¯E¯  ¯P¯   input goes 
high or VDD power is removed. A short-to-ground overcurrent 
event is shown in Figure 4.
When the two outputs are shorted together, the current path is 
through the sense resistor. After the blanking time (≈1 µs) expires, 
the sense resistor voltage is exceeding its trip value, due to the 
overcurrent condition that exists. This causes the driver to go into 
a fixed off-time cycle. After the fixed off-time expires the driver 
turns on again and the process repeats. In this condition the driver 
is completely protected against overcurrent events, but the short 
is repetitive with a period equal to the fixed off-time of the driver. 
This condition is shown in Figure 5.
If the driver is operating in Mixed decay mode, it is normal for 
the positive current to spike, due to the bridge going in the for-
ward direction and then in the negative direction, as a result of the 
direction change implemented by the Mixed decay feature. This 
is shown in Figure 6. In both instances the overcurrent circuitry is 
protecting the driver and prevents damage to the device.
Charge Pump (CP1 and CP2). The charge pump is used to 
generate a gate supply greater than that of VBB for driving the 
source-side FET gates. A 0.1 µF ceramic capacitor, should be 
connected between CP1 and CP2. In addition, a 0.1 µF ceramic 
capacitor is required between VCP and VBB, to act as a reservoir 
for operating the high-side FET gates.
Capacitor values should be Class 2 dielectric ±15% maximum, 
or tolerance R, according to EIA (Electronic Industries Alliance) 
specifications.

monitored. In the case of a fault condition, the FET outputs of the

Application Layout
Layout. The printed circuit board should use a heavy ground-
plane. For optimum electrical and thermal performance, the 
A4984 must be soldered directly onto the board. On the under-
side of the A4984 package is an exposed pad, which provides a 
path for enhanced thermal dissipation. The thermal pad should be 
soldered directly to an exposed surface on the PCB. Thermal vias 
are used to transfer heat to other layers of the PCB.
In order to minimize the effects of ground bounce and offset 
issues, it is important to have a low impedance single-point 
ground, known as a star ground, located very close to the device. 
By making the connection between the pad and the ground plane 
directly under the A4984, that area becomes an ideal location for 
a star ground point. A low impedance ground will prevent ground 
bounce during high current operation and ensure that the supply 
voltage remains stable at the input terminal.
The two input capacitors should be placed in parallel, and as close 
to the device supply pins as possible. The ceramic capacitor (C7) 
should be closer to the pins than the bulk capacitor (C2). This is 
necessary because the ceramic capacitor will be responsible for 
delivering the high frequency current components. The sense 
resistors, RSx , should have a very low impedance path to ground, 
because they must carry a large current while supporting very 
accurate voltage measurements by the current sense comparators. 
Long ground traces will cause additional voltage drops, adversely 
affecting the ability of the comparators to accurately measure the 
current in the windings. The SENSEx pins have very short traces 
to the RSx resistors and very thick, low impedance traces directly 
to the star ground underneath the device. If possible, there should 
be no other components on the sense circuits.
