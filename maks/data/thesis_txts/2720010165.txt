Abstract
This thesis found a knowledge gap in the current literature when regarding electric vehicle (EV) charging
behaviour and its effect on load profiles. Most literature is based on either surveys or internal-combustion-
engine vehicle data, or a combination of the two, which makes the conclusions questionable when using
it to assess EV behaviour. This thesis has analysed and modelled user behavior based on real charging
data, provided by the charge point operator (CPO) SPIRII with the motivation being to study the potential
flexibility provided by the EV fleet, as well as the influence of the fleet on the aggregated load profile. This
is studied from the perspective of the distribution system operator (DSO) seeking to avoid congestion and
voltage issues with regard to the integration of the fleet in the grid, as well as the perspective of the CPO
seeking to optimize the EV consumption and use the potential flexibility to sell frequency control ancillary
services to the transmission system operator (TSO).
The behavior of EV and plug-in hybrid electric vehicle (PHEV) users were characterised, with the main
differences found to be energy charged per session and the frequency of sessions. The probability of plugging
in and out at different hours, the session duration, as well as the frequency of sessions was found, which
when assessed together gives an insight into the potential flexibility provided by the vehicle.
The biggest influence on a users smart charging was found to be the potential savings when comparing
to performing a dumb charge. Aside from the potential financial savings, the influences were the sessions
duration and the time of plug-in. The most accurate way to predict smart charging was found to be with
aggregated data, where the percentage of smart charges at a given time was predicted, as opposed to
predicting individual sessions.
Lastly, while it was determined that while the effect of smart charging increases the peak loading and
decreases the load factor, it also moves the load into the hours of low consumption for the power grid. This
result shows the potential gain for the DSO in getting more users to perform smart charging, and thereby
moving their load and unburdening the grid in peak hours of consumption.
Keywords
Electric Vehicles, Behavioral Modelling, Energy Systems, Aggregated Load Profiles

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter presents the motivation behind this thesis, a literature review of similar studies, and lastly
the thesis outline.
1.1
Motivation
In 2017, 27% of the total greenhouse gas emission from the EU came from the transportation sector
[1]. With the EU’s goal of reducing their total greenhouse emission by 55% in 2030 compared to 1990,
decarbonization of this sector becomes paramount [2]. An important and necessary step towards the
decarbonization is the adoption of electric vehicles (EVs). Lithium-ion battery technology, which is used
in modern EVs, has significantly evolved in the last decade, which has decreased the price and increased
the driving range of EVs. These factors, together with lawmakers giving financial incentives to buy an
EV, have made EVs an attractive alternative to internal-combustion-engine vehicle (ICEV). The aim of the
current government policy in Denmark is to stop the sale of new ICEVs in 2030, and plug-in-hybrid vehicles
(PHEVs) in 2035 [3]. In figure 1.1.1, the yearly number of newly registered EVs and PHEVs is shown,
which clearly shows the rapid market growth.

The transition from consumers owning ICEVs to EVs comes with an increased load demand, as well
as a risk for increased coincidence factors [5], which can be a challenge for the distribution grid [6]. This

can cause both voltage and congestion issues, which can result in the need for expensive investment in
infrastructure for the distribution system operator (DSO) [7]. Due to these potential problems, studying and
understanding how EVs can be integrated in the power system becomes crucial.
EVs can be seen as a flexible load, where both the charging power and the time of charge can be
controlled. This has the potential to flatten the demand curve of the distribution grid [8] and thus, unburden
the system. Being able to accurately model realistic charging profiles and user behaviour, and therefor the
demand by the EVs on the distribution network, becomes a very important part of infrastructure planning
[9]. Not only the power demand of EVs, but also where in the grid they are plugged in and at what time is
important. Realistic charging profiles are also valuable for charge point operators (CPOs) desiring to use
their EV fleet to optimize their consumption. This can include minimizing electricity cost via spot price
optimization, offering frequency control ancillary services (FCAS) to the transmission system operator
(TSO), or congestion services to the DSO [10]. All these services can be offered since EVs can spend several
hours every day being plugged in, where power can be controlled.
As the widespread increase of privately owned EVs is fairly new [4], there is a clear lack of studies or
available resources that derive charging profiles and infer user behaviour based on real charger data. Instead,
many studies derive charging profiles based on other sources including surveys, historic ICEV data, smaller
EV trials, or combinations of the three to overcome the lack of data.
This thesis uses a large collection of real data collected directly from a widespread network of residential
chargers. The data is used to analyse and understand residential charging behaviour, as well as creating
different load profiles based on behavioural differences. With the charging profiles the intent is to realistically
simulate and assess the effect of using different charging control methods, and model user behaviour.
1.2
Literature Review
Several studies have tried to analyse charging behaviour and create charging profiles based on different
methods and data sources.
Modelling driving patterns, and based on that deducting the necessary charging profiles, has been
performed by [11]. This paper is based on data from the "Test-an-EV" project by the CPO Clever in
Denmark. In this project 198 EVs were lent to residential users for two years. The data collected was
charging time and daily travel distance. The data did not include the charged kWh and the charging power,
which had to be derived from other parameters. The project was conducted from 2011 to 2013, where
few EVs were owned and a public charging infrastructure was not available, which may have influenced
charging behaviour. This project involved 24 out of 98 municipalities in Denmark, so the number of EVs per
municipality was low and only a small part of the country was represented. The project had participants
sign up voluntarily, which might mostly include early adopters of EV technology, and might not reflect the
behaviour of the general population.
Both [10], which analyses the EV driving and charging behaviour of a large EV fleet, and [12], which
analyses potential earnings from grid services, are based on data from 7,163 EVs located in the United States.
These studies only use data from the 24 kWh Nissan Leafs model, which by today’s standard is a small
battery for an EV, and might require more frequent charging. The data consists of GPS data, as well as when
and how many kWh the EVs charged. The studies are constrained both by not knowing the power profile, as
well as assuming the EVs are plugged in the entire time they are parked. It is also uncertain how well this
data translates to a Danish context, and charging with different EV models.
Many studies rely on surveys when real data is not available. One example is [13], which tries to identify
charging patterns and uses it to develop strategies for users with a photovoltaic system. The study is based on

a survey about charging behaviour of Danish EV owners. This can give a skewed picture as the participant
of the study may give a more positive description of their charging behavior than is actually true. Super
users and early adopters might be more inclined to answer such surveys, which again might not reflect the
behaviour of the general population.
Several papers have made their analysis based on the Danish National Travel Surveys [14], which
investigate transport behaviour of car owners in Denmark. The latest survey was done from 2014 to 2015
and it does not differentiate between ICEVs and EVs. A paper that tries to model charging demand profiles
based on the survey is [15]. This paper derived the load demand of potential EVs from the average daily
driven distance found in the survey. As a result, an estimate of the necessary kWh to charge was found. A
similar method is used by [16] which is based on the UK National Travel Survey [17] and tries to determine
plug in behavior. [18] and [19] tried to determine plug-in behaviour based on driving data collected from
a 76 cars in Winnipeg, Canada. Similar in all four studies is the assumption that driving behaviour and
life habits do not change when transitioning from an ICEV to an EV. This is problematic as EVs do not
necessarily follow the same driving patterns as ICEV and arrival and departure times may not coincide with
plug-in and plug-out times. In addition, while the daily driven distance might give in indication of how many
kWh is it necessary to charge, it does not reveal when the charge occurs and if it is at a public or residential
charger.
[20] and [5] use travel data from Denmark to get the driving behaviour, and combine it with plug-in/plug-
out data gathered from from Nissan Leaf EVs in Japan to assess grid loading in Denmark due to EV charging
profiles. Only the 24 kWh Nissan Leaf model was used, which is an older model with a relatively small
battery. The charging power and actual time spent charging is not included. Instead a single phase charge of
3.7 kW was assumed. With all the assumptions, as well as combining lacking plug-in/plug-out data from
older EVs in Japan with driving behavior derived form a travel survey in Denmark, the accuracy of the
derived charging profiles is uncertain.
A different approach was taken by [21], which analyses meter-values from households with an EV. By
looking at the hours with power peaks, the study tries to estimate the power consumption of the EV, and
from that the power profiles. By only looking at the meter values, the study only has access to the energy
consumption per hour, and not the precise charge power or the kWh charged. The study also has a limited
sample of only 14 households, as well as 32 public electric vehicle supply equipment (EVSE). Having
to estimate the kWh, while not having the power and the small sample size makes accuracy of the power
profiles questionable.
Very few studies are based on real charging data, and none have a comprehensive enough sample. The
rest have sources of varying quality, often surveys or ICEV data, and with a range of assumptions to make
up for the lack of data.
1.3
Research Questions
The thesis seeks to answer the following questions:
• What are EV and PHEV users charging behavior based on an analysis of real data?
• How can the usage of smart charging be modelled?
• What is the effect of the use of dumb and smart charging in aggregated load profiles?

CHAPTER 2
Technical Aspects of EV Charging
This chapter provides the technical background behind EV charging. Section 2.1 presents the electro-
technical aspects, section 2.2 presents the communication between EV and EVSE, section 2.3 presents
different control methods, section 2.4 present the communication method and flow of information between
EVSE and central management system (CMS), and lastly section 2.5 presents what data is available for the
analysis based on the prior sections.
2.1
EV Charging
EVSEs supply the EV battery with electric power. This is either with AC power, utilizing the EVSEs
onboard charger and AC-DC converter, or directly with DC power. DC power facilitates a high charging
rate, but comes at the cost of expensive power electronics, as well as the need for a large grid connection,
which only makes it a viable solution for certain commercial applications. Residential sites usually have AC
chargers with a 16 A installation capacity, which facilitates charging with either 11 kW 3-phased or 3.7 kW
1-phased.
The EV inlet is connected to the EVSE socket outlet through a charging cable which facilitates the flow
of power as seen on figure 2.1.1. The official AC cable connector standard in the EU is the type 2 connector,
which follows the IEC 62196 international standard and allows for a maximum of 43 kW 3-phased charging.
The connector consists of two pins for communication, a neutral pin, a ground pin, and the three phase pins
as seen on figure 2.1.2.
Figure 2.1.1: Charging of vehicle with AC power [22]

A charging session starts once the EV is detected by the EVSE. An EVSE can either be set up to offer
power immediately, or to require authentication first. This is most often done via a RFID card or through
an app. When the EV is fully charged the power will go to zero, but the session continues. The charging
session does not stop until either the EV is unplugged, or the user stops it in the associated app.
2.2
Communication between EV and EVSE
All communication between the EVSE and the EV is done via the two communication pins on the
connector. The proximity pilot (PP) pin tells the EV when the EVSE is connected, which signals the EV
to lock its connector in the inlet. The EV can read the cable’s power ratings on the resistance between the
PP and the ground pin. The control pilot (CP) pin is used to communicate current limitations and request
an EVSE mode. Current limitation is implemented via a 1 kHz PWM signal set by the EVSE, where the
duty cycle corresponds to the maximum allowable current for the EV to draw. The EV can request an EVSE
mode by setting a resistor between the CP and the ground pin. This can, for example, be to signal that the
EV is connected and ready to charge, or to stop the current charge session. This is the only communication
that happens between the EVSE and the EV. The EVSE has no information about the type of vehicle, its
power capabilities or the state of its battery. The EVSE sets a maximum allowed current import, and the EV
draws power based on that.
2.3
Charging Control
The only charging session parameters the user can directly control are when charging starts, and, in some
cases, a limit to how much power can be drawn. It is often desirable to delay the start of charging to move it
outside the hours of peak power consumption. This can be both to free up power, for example while cooking
and doing other power-intensive activities, or to move power consumption to hours where energy prices are
cheaper. Delayed charging can either be done from the EVSE side by only offering power, or by the EV side
by only drawing power, when desired. The CPO can use different methods to offer delayed charging. A
simple method is to have the EVSE only offer power in specific time slots, for example between 22:00 and
06:00. Another method is to set the EV to wait a certain amount of hours before requesting power. Some
CPOs use more dynamic methods based on the daily energy prices, where the EVSE will only offer power in
the hours when the energy is cheapest. In the case of a delayed charging, the EVSE will start a session once
the EV is connected, but power will only flow when requested.
2.4
Communication Between EVSE and CMS
Most chargers communicate via Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP), which is a free and open-source
standard for communication between the EVSE and the CMS of the CPO that operates it. The standard makes
==PAGE:17==
2.5. DATA AVAILABLE FOR ANALYSIS
7
it easy for EVSEs from different vendors to communicate with a range of different CMSs. OCPP allows for
authentication, remote control, and logging of charging sessions. OCPP also supports load balancing, as
well as a range of other features, but this depends on what features the CMS and EVSE supports. How much
information is sent under a charging session is not strictly set by the OCPP standard, and therefore depends
on the specific EVSE model. The data used in this thesis is collected from the EVSE model Zaptec Go. It is
a 22 kW AC residential charger made by the Norwegian technology company Zaptec.
When a charging session is started on the Zaptec Go a Start Transactions message is sent to the CMS, and
the internal EVSE energy consumption counter is set to zero. The EVSE then sends a message containing
the session meter values every minute during the session. The meter values contains values for the energy
consumption since the sessions started, the active power import, and the current import on the different
phases. Furthermore, it contains the session-ID and the timestamp for the message. When the session ends
the EVSE sends a Stop Transactions message with the final energy consumption.
2.5
Data Available for Analysis
The analysis of the thesis is based on data gained from the meter values from all the charging sessions
from a network of 2786 residential Zaptec Go EVSE made from September 9 2021 to September 9 2022.
The data was provided by the Danish CPO company SPIRII, and processed using Python.

CHAPTER 3
Analysis of Residential Charging Behaviour
This chapter provides an analysis of residential charging behaviour. Section 3.1 presents the motivation,
section 3.2 presents the data processing and the different parameters investigated, while section 3.3 presents
the different results of the analysis.
3.1
Motivation
To better asses the impact of the mass integration of EVs into the power system it is necessary to
analyse and understand EV charging behavior. Both the overall behaviour, but also grouping the EV users in
groups to identify behavioural differences. Analysing and thereby developing an initial understanding of
residential charging behaviour, can be used to identify the potential for exploiting the flexibility provided by
EV charging, and to find patterns that can be used when predicting charging behaviour. For this analysis,
the users are grouped into categories of user who own EVs and PHEVs. The chapter presents the charging
behaviour found as well as how it differs for the two groups.
3.2
Method
Before the analysis can be done, the raw data needs to be processed, filtered, and clustered into groups.
This section presents the procedure.
3.2.1
Data Processing
A number of sessions with insignificant charged energy were removed from the analysis. Such sessions
may be the result of user unfamiliarity with the equipment or technical problems, and their inclusion may
distort the final results. The sessions were filtered based on the following criteria:
• Ppeak > 1 kW
• Qsess > 1 kWh
Furthermore, only data from EVSEs that had over 10 sessions were used, as a limited number of sessions
would not give an accurate picture of user behaviour. As a result, 26.5% of all sessions were filtered out. For
each session the parameters, as seen in table 3.2.1, were calculated based on the meter values.

There are different objectives considering when smart charging. The objective of the user is to to optimize
the charge cost by moving the charge into periods when the energy price is at its lowest. As seen in figure
3.3.5, many users will plug in their vehicle during the peak hours between 16:00 and 20:00, which in general
have high energy prices. This group will have a clear economic incentive to delay the charge and move into
the low peak period later in the evening.
The objective of the DSOs is to avoid congestion and voltage issues and in general improve the reliability
of the distribution grid. By having users smart charge, the extra loading is moved outside of the peak hours,
which can decrease the peak loading. Note that the objectives of the user and the DSOs do not always
overlap, as in a case where the cheapest price is at the hours with peak loading of the grid. In this case the
DSOs wants the user to move the charge to low peak periods, but the user have no incentive to delay the
charge. In contrary, as the vehicles primary function is mobility, the user has an incentive to charge it as
fast is possible. But in most cases the cheapest prices will fall in the low peak periods, which aligns the
objectives of the user and the DSOs.
An objective of the CPOs can be to use the vehicle as a power buffer, and use the flexibility provided
by the user smart charging to perform FCASs for the TSO. By changing when the charge takes place, and
potentially changing the power, the CPOs can perform frequency regulation, and sell it on the market for
FCASs. The user will have to have an incentive to perform smart charging and for them to allows the CPOs
to control the charge.
Where the prior chapter investigated user behaviour with the perspective of potential flexibility, this
chapter investigates what specifically influences users decision to perform smart charging and to what
accuracy is it possible to predict this behaviour based on the data available.

4.2.3
Energy Price of Sessions
To investigate the correlation between energy prices and the use of smart charging, the total energy
price in DKK per kWh needs to be calculated. The kWh price is based on the historic spot price data, the
electricity tax, DSO and TSO tariffs. This is calculated per hour k and day d as:
λk,d = (λspot(k, d) + cTAX + cDSO(k, d) + cTSO) · VAT
(4.1)
Here λspot being the kWh spot price, cTAX being the electricity tax, cDSO and cTSO being the DSO and
TSO tariffs respectively. λk,d is the energy price per kWh with all taxes, tariffs and VAT included. All
parameters are in DKK
kWh . The historic spot prices were downloaded from the Energy Data Service website
[24], which is a database with energy data run by Energinet. The tariffs were based on the prices from the
DSO Radius [25]. The TSO tariff is flat, contra the DSO tariff that is dependant on the time of day. The
DSO tariff is split into hours of low and peak load. Every day from April to September is considered low
load, as well as every day from October to March in the period 20:00-17:00. Every day from October to
March in the period 17:00-20:00 is considered peak load.

To find the importance of the different features when it come predictive power an analysis based on
recursive feature elimination (RFE) is done. RFE is a feature selection algorithm, and is a method that can be
used to investigate which parameters are the most relevant when predicting the target variable. The algorithm
takes two hyper parameters: the number of parameters to selects, and the prediction algorithm used to test
the performance. The algorithm works by removing one feature at a time, fitting the model, and then testing
the performance. The feature resulting in the lowest loss of performance is then removed, and the algorithm
is run again with one less feature. This continues until only the selected number of features is left.
A gradient boosting classifier was chosen as the prediction algorithm, and a single feature was selected
as the target. Gradient boosting works by combining a group of weaker machine learning algorithms into
a strong predictive model. The amount of dumb sessions is down sampled, so the data includes an equal
amount of sessions with non-smart and smart charging. This is done to avoid the algorithm being trained
with a bias towards the dumb charges which as the majority class is clearly over represented in the data. The
data is split into 75% train, and 25% test data.
The results of the RFE can be seen in figure 4.3.2. This figure shows both the selected features at each
loop, and the achieved accuracy when prediction the smart charge target. For example the first feature to
be removed is Weekend, and one single feature at a time is removed until only Sopt is left. Only using
Sopt an accuracy of 73% can be achieved. Adding ∆sess increases it to 74.4%, startsin increases it to 76%,
flex increases it to 76.5%, and ∆charge increases it to 77.5%. After this point the accuracy only increases
insignificantly when adding more features.

CHAPTER 5
Influence of Smart Charging on Aggregated
Load Profiles
The prior chapter presents an analysis of what accuracy the percentage of smart charges taking place at
different times could predicted. This chapter analyses how smart charging influences the aggregated load
profile, both to compare how load is shifted, and how the peak loads of the system differ. Section 5.1 present
the motivation for analysing the influence of smart charging on load profiles, section 5.2 presents the data
processing necessary, and lastly section 5.3 presents the results of the analysis.
5.1
Motivation
The electrification of the transportation sector, with integration of EVs and PHEVs, increases demand
and the potential for increased peak loading on the power grid. This additional demand can lead to congestion
which causes overloading on transformers and lines, as well voltage problems and increased active power
losses [26]. Voltage drops can decrease the quality of the service for the end user, and the extra loading
on the electric equipment can lead to degradation and failure. Knowing the load profile, and assessing the
potential future load profile as the number of EVs integrated in the grid increases, becomes important for
different parties.
The DSOs can use this information when planning new infrastructure to avoid inefficient infrastructure
utilization. As the assets of the distribution grid have to be dimensioned so it can handle peak loading,
understanding how smart charging influences the load profile have an economic value. Both for dimension-
ing the electric equipment to avoid overloading and degradation, but also for avoiding unnecessary grid
reinforcement by assuming a worst case scenario where all user charge daily at peak hour, instead of a
realistic assessment of the load profile based on real data.
The CPOs can use the load profiles when offering FCASs to the TSO. Here the CPOs act as an aggregator
and works as an intermediary between the end user and the TSO. The CPOs can use the EVSEs flexibility as
a resources, which it can gather and control and offer as a service to the TSO. To do this the CPOs need to
be able to predict the expected load profiles including potential deviations. For an accurate prediction it is
important to understand what influence smart charging have on the aggregated load profile.
5.2
Method
The load profiles are generated based on the the meter values from all sessions made by a sample group
of users. The sample consists of all sessions made between September 2021 and September 2022, by users

whose charger was installed before September 2021. This is done so only users who have had a charger for
the entire duration of the year is considered, which avoids that the load increases as the number of EVSE

profiles.

and the length shows the utilization rate.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion and Future Works
Section 6.1 presents the conclusion while section 6.2 presents future works.
6.1
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to model and understand EV user behavior while assessing the flexibility
potential of EVs, as well as the influence user behavior has on aggregated load profiles. First, the behavior
of EV and PHEV users and their difference was assessed. The duration of their sessions was found to be
very similar, with the majority of them being between 1-5 and 9-16 hours. The probability of charging every
day was found to be 70% for PHEVs, and 40% for EVs. This is consistent with PHEVs having a smaller
battery, and therefore more often requiring a charge. The plug-in time and plug-out time was found to be
very similar for both groups. For both groups the probability of plugging in before 18:00 was found to be
60%, while the probability of plugging out before 12:00 was 60%. When looking at the normalised charge of
sessions there is a clearer difference. The probability of a session charging under 80% of the battery capacity
was 65% for PHEVs while it was 95% for EVs. Once again this can be explained by PHEVs having smaller
batteries that are more likely to deplete.
It was further investigated what influences a user performing smart charging and, to what accuracy, the
usage of smart charging can modelled. The main parameters influencing smart charging were found to be
when the session was started, the duration of the session, the ratio between charge and session duration,
and the optimal savings. The amount of smart charges increased for sessions starting later in the day, with
the percentage of sessions being smart charges peaking between 19:00 and 20:00 at 42%. After 20:00 the
percentage decreased again until between 02:00 and 03:00, where there was only 3%. Following this, the
percentage increased once more. There is a clear correlation between smart charging and session duration,
where the percentage of smart charges increased in concert with session duration. The percentage of smart
charges increased from 21% with a duration of 7-8 hours to 41% with a duration of 14-15 hours. The same
tendency was found for the ratio between session and charge duration, where the percentage increased as
the ratio increased. At a ratio of 40-50%, 25% were smart charges, which increased to 48% for a ratio of
10-20%. Lastly, the correlation between the optimal saving and probability of smart charging was found.
Here it was discovered that as the savings increased, the probability of a charge being a smart charge also
increased. For a savings between 0 and 2.5 DKK, 21% of sessions were smart charges, which increased to
48% for a savings between 25 to 25.6 DKK. The smart users were found to be slightly more influenced by
these parameters.
After training a model based on all parameters to predict the use of smart charging, the optimal saving

was determined to be the most important parameter with an accuracy of 73%. Using all four parameters
resulted in an accuracy of 78%.
Two models were then trained using only the parameters that would be available before the session took
place. These are the start time of the session, the energy price at the start time, and the user classification.
The first model, based on prediction of individual sessions, was assessed to have en RMSE of 15.73% points
when aggregating the result to a percentage of smart charges at a given time. The second model, based on
predicting smart charging on an aggregated scale, had a resulting RMSE of 11.02% points. Consequently,
the best accuracy was found when predicting smart charging usage at an aggregated scale.
Finally, the effect of dumb and smart charging on the aggregated load profiles was assessed. First, the
difference between the true and a theoretical dumb profile was determined. When inspecting a single week,
it was discovered that the dumb profile was shifted 1-2 hours back in time compared to the true profile. The
LDC was found to be similar with a slightly higher peak for the true profile. The load factor was calculated
to be 34.50% for the true profile and 27.82% for the dumb profile.
Then the difference in loading for three different user groups was assessed. The users were grouped
based on how often they performed smart charging into groups of non-smart, semi-smart, and smart users.
The shape of the LDC was similar for all three groups, with the peak values increasing for the semi-smart
and further for the smart users. This was also reflected in the load factor which was found to be 45.21% for
non-smart users, 40.31% for semi-smart users, and 38.70% for smart user. To further analyse the increase in
peaks, the average load throughout the day was plotted for the groups together with the grid consumption for
DK1 and DK2. Here it was found that even though the smart user group had higher peaks, the average load
was shifted out of the peak hours of grid consumption, which resulted in the load being high when the grid
consumption was low. This tendency was lower for the non-smart and semi-smart groups which still had
high loading at the peak hours of the grid. This reveals a clear potential gain for the DSO in getting more
users to perform smart charging.
6.2
Future Work
There are several areas of user behaviour that can be investigated further. It would be interesting to
perform a behavior analysis similar to that in chapter 3, but using varying demographics. The demographics
of interest could be smart and non-smart users, or by using geographic data, the behavioral difference for
different parts of the country
In addition, if a larger sample size was used, the predictive power of the models could potentially be
improved by using more date features. The date features considered could be the month or week of the
year. Integrating data from more years would also make it possible to see how the behaviour of users have
potentially changed.
