Abstract
We have less than 10 years to reach the 2030 goals for a more sustainable planet. Corporations
play a big role in this and we are seeing them stepping up to the challenge. However, this de-
mands large scale change - yet studies show that 75% of change projects fail. One tool used in
several ﬁelds, corporate sustainability included, to diagnose organisational capabilities and im-
prove them is the maturity model. Yet, very little research has been done explicitly connecting
the tool to change management. In this thesis that connection is explored through the research
question: “How can a sustainability maturity model be designed to function as an eﬃcient change
management tool for external change agents aiding corporations in becoming more sustainable?”.
Given the nature of the research question the methodology applied in this thesis doubles as
an initial hypothesis for a potential answer. The methodological approach is based on a recog-
nised maturity model development framework by Maier et al. (2009) with a few adaptations to
include the change management angle. The framework was utilised over 3 steps;
1. A literature study to build the main content of the model and 2. A practitioner study to
develop the design and application process
These two steps resulted in a 5 stage maturity model consisting of 31 sustainability criteria and
an application model focusing on consultant to client ownership change through self-determination
motivational elements. In addition, self-determination motivational elements were added into
the ﬁnal design of the model.
3. A case study with DFDS to evaluate and improve the model
The evaluation showed that the model could not yet be seen as an eﬀective change manage-
ment tool for corporate sustainability. To remedy this 6 maintenance points were suggested to
improve both the model design and application process.
While further testing is still needed to evaluate if the created maturity model is an eﬀective
change tool, it is concluded that the above development process is not the optimal approach.
Instead, a new adaptation of Maier et al.’s (2009) development framework and an entirely new
development approach focusing more on the change management angle are reviewed as poten-
tial answers in the ﬁnal discussion. However, both will need further work and testing before any
ﬁnal recommendation can be made.

Resume
Vi har mindre end 10 år til at nå 2030-målene for en mere bæredygtig klode. Virksomheder
spiller en stor rolle i dette, og vi ser dem tage udfordringen op. Dette kræver dog ændringer
i stor skala - alligevel viser undersøgelser, at 75% af ændringsprojekter mislykkes. Moden-
hedsmodeller er et værktøj, der bruges inden for ﬂere områder, inklusiv bæredygtighed, til at
diagnosticere organisatoriske evner og forbedre dem. Alligevel er der udført meget lidt forskn-
ing, der eksplicit forbinder værktøjet med ændringshåndtering. I denne afhandling undersøges
forbindelsen gennem forskningsspørgsmålet: "Hvordan kan en bæredygtighedsmodningsmodel de-
signes til at fungere som et eﬀektivt forandringsstyringsværktøj for eksterne forandringsagenter, der
hjælper virksomheder med at blive mere bæredygtige?".
I betragtning af forskningsspørgsmålets type, fungerer den anvendte metode i denne afhan-
dling som en indledende hypotese for et potentielt svar. Den metodologiske tilgang er baseret
på et anerkendt modehedsmodeludviklingsværktøj af Maier et al. (2009) med et par modiﬁka-
tioner til at inkludere ændringsstyringsvinklen. Udviklingsværktøjet blev anvendt gennem 3 trin;
1. En litteraturundersøgelse for at opbygge hovedindholdet i modellen og 2. En ekspertun-
dersøgelse for at udvikle anvendelsesprocessen og modeldesignet
Disse to trin resulterede i en 5-trins modenhedsmodel bestående af 31 bæredygtighedskriterier
og en anveldelsesmodel med fokus på konsulent til klient ejerskabsændring gennem selvbestem-
melsesmotiverende elementer. Derudover blev selvbestemmelsesmotiverende elementer tilfø-
jet til det endelige design af modellen.
3. Et case-studie med DFDS til evaluering og forbedring af modellen
Evalueringen viste, at modellen endnu ikke kunne anses som et eﬀektivt ændringsstyringsværk-
tøj til at øge virksomheders bæredygtighed. For at forbedre modellens design og anvendelsespro-
ces blev 6 vedligeholdelsespunkter foreslået.
Selvom der stadig er behov for yderligere test for at evaluere, om den udviklede modenhedsmodel
er et eﬀektivt ændringsværktøj, konkluderes det, at ovennævnte udviklingsproces ikke er den
optimale tilgang. I stedet gennemgås en ny tilpasning af Maier et al.’s (2009) udviklingsværktøj
og en helt ny udviklingsmetode, der fokuserer mere på ændringsstyringsvinklen, som potentielle
svar i den endelige diskussion. Begge vil dog have behov for yderligere arbejde og afprøvning,
inden en endelig anbefaling kan fremsættes.

1.
Introduction
“Ours can be the ﬁrst generation to end poverty – and the last generation to address climate change
before it is too late.” (United Nations 2015b)
This was said by UN Secretary-General Ban-Ki Moon in 2015, highlighting the importance of
the work to be done in just one generation. Five years, 17 Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs) (United Nations 2015a), a global signing of the Paris Agreement (UNFCCC 2016) and
a warning from the The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) (IPCC 2018) later
this statement has not become any less relevant. In 2020 COVID-19 closed down most of the
world leading to the largest ever annual fall in CO2 emissions (CarbonBrief 2020). However, it
resulted in a great social and economical expense with an estimated equivalent of 195 million
job losses (UN News 2020) showing us that lockdown of the economy/economic activity and
people is not a good way to solve climate change. We need sustainable development instead.
The term sustainable development was ﬁrst coined in 1987 where the Brundtland report de-
ﬁned it as: "development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of
future generations to meet their own needs” (Commission 1987). A deﬁnition that is still widely
used today (Gerasimova 2017). According to Holden et al. (2013) four primary dimensions can
be derived from the Brundtland Report: 1) safeguarding long-term ecological sustainability, 2)
satisfying basic human needs, 3) promoting intergenerational and 4) intragenerational equity. In
addition they argue against another popular way of perceiving sustainability - the Triple Bot-
tom Line; a framework coined in 1994 by J. Elkinton where sustainability is seen as a balance
between an economical, social and environmental dimension (Elkington 1997). Holden et al.
(2013) states that economic growth should not be a primary dimension as it is in the Triple Bot-
tom Line. Instead it should be seen as a means to achieve the four primary dimensions, with
the argument that continuous improvement of the world’s highest standard of living cannot be
considered environmentally sustainable in the long run (Holden et al. 2013).
Yet, this is where an important distinction needs to be made. From what angle are we consid-
ering sustainability? Holden et al. (2013) focuses mostly on policy makers while J. Elkington’s
(1997) Triple Bottom Line was created mainly for corporations. Both are powerful and entangled
groups when it comes to reaching the goals of this generation. This thesis will focus on corporate

sustainability as companies have an increasingly important role in ensuring a sustainable devel-
opment of our planet (Larderel 2003). They aﬀect everything from emissions to social welfare
on a daily basis and can often make decisions faster than possible in governance. In addition
companies are seen increasingly taking on this responsibility with examples such as Microsoft
announcing a goal to be carbon negative by 2030 (Microsoft 2020), HM setting a goal of be-
ing climate positive throughout their entire value chain by 2040 (HM Group 2019), and Maersk
targeting net-zero CO2 emissions from operations by 2050 (Maersk 2019). All of these while
also targeting social issues such a equality, health, education and job security (Microsoft n.d.)
(Maersk n.d.) (HM Group n.d.).
Despite this increasing commitment the deﬁnition of corporate sustainability is currently “...as
nebulous as the carbon dioxide swirling around our planet” (Frecè and Harder 2018, p. 1). A result
of which can be seen in the statements above where terms such as ‘carbon negative’, ‘climate
positive’ and ‘net-zero CO2 emissions’ are used to describe quite similar goals. Yet, one thing
remains a constant: In two recent separate papers that collectively highlight over 50 diﬀer-
ent literary deﬁnitions or descriptions of corporate sustainability most of them refer to Triple
Bottom Line as the core of that deﬁnition. Both of these papers also use the Triple Bottom
Line framework in their own analysis and description of corporate sustainability (Meuer et al.
2019)(Bergman et al. 2017). The Triple Bottom Line also inspired popular sustainable accounting
tools such as the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) and Dow Jones Sustainability Indexes (DJSI)
(Elkington 2018). This showcases the immense impact this framework still has on how corpo-
rate sustainability is viewed and assessed today.
However, just as with Holden et al. (2014), the framework has been criticised by Milne et al.
(2005) for not including the limits to the scale of economic and human development. In addi-
tion Milne et al. (2005) argues that while the Triple Bottom Line does promote improvements in
business reporting and behavior it fails to confront the fact that some products, processes and
even entire industries may prove to be unsustainable even when run eﬃciently. In 2018 J. Elk-
ington released an article (Elkington 2018) critically reviewing the impacts of his own framework
acknowledging that in most of the current practices the economic bottom line still dominates,
demonstrating a failure to bury the single bottom line paradigm. He also highlights that the Triple
Bottom Line was never supposed to be just an accounting tool, the outset was system change.
J. Elkington describes the original intention as “It was originally intended as a genetic code, a triple

Considering the undeniable entanglement of corporate sustainability and the Triple Bottom Line,
the framework will serve as the underlying understanding of corporate sustainability in this the-
sis. However, given the critique and the issues with how the framework is currently interpreted
it should be stated that the aim is to work with J. Elkington’s originally intended meaning.
While the Triple Bottom Line is still an understanding up for interpretation I want to argue that
considering the nature of corporations this might be a good thing. Marrewijk and Werre (2002)
suggest that a value based approach where companies deﬁne their own level of ambition and
thereby approach to corporate sustainability is preferred to a ‘one-deﬁnition-ﬁts-all’. Similarly
Frecè and Harder (2018), highlight the positive reinforcement from having a deﬁnition of cor-
porate sustainability in line with the company’s identity instead of a ’one-deﬁnition-ﬁts-all’. Al-
lowing companies to interpret corporate sustainability might be what enables them to properly
adopt it, like with Microsoft, HM and Maersk expressing their goals with diﬀerent terms but
ultimately all moving towards a similar destination.
This will inevitably lead to both more and less ambitious and sophisticated deﬁnitions of sus-
tainability. Rego et al. (2015) express a worry that because of globalisation most companies are
connected by transnational supply chains, stating that the diﬀerences in corporate sustainability
eﬀorts might cause the more sophisticated integration’s to be weakened by the less sophisti-
cated. Something that could challenge the notion that corporations should create their own
deﬁnition of corporate sustainability. Yet, I want to challenge that, as there are several cases
where companies with more sophisticated eﬀorts strengthen the less sophisticated integration.
An example of this is when Walmart made a sustainable suppliers index that forced their many
suppliers to work with sustainability, using their power in the ﬁeld to heighten the level of sus-
tainability of their entire supply chain (Emhart et al. 2013). For companies with less power,
an argument is that with the increase in environmental legislation (UN Environment programme
2019) and consumer demand on sustainable goods (Whelan and Kronthal-Sacco 2019), the com-
panies with a higher level of sophistication in their sustainability are still rewarded. Therefore, I

believe that a more optimistic view on the matter is justiﬁed.
Regardless of which deﬁnition of sustainability a corporation should work towards one thing
is certain: it demands change. Microsoft, HM, Maersk and all other companies will have to
change to become more sustainable than they were yesterday. Organisations are always chang-
ing, some even argue that the only constant in an organisation is change (Jabri 2017, p.2), yet
studies consistently report that a 75% of all change initiatives fail, either not managing to deliver
the anticipated beneﬁts or being abandoned entirely (Anand and Barsoux 2017). With only a
timeline of one generation, this success rate needs to go up for corporate sustainability change
projects. This is why I ﬁnd it important to explore the eﬀects of considering change manage-
ment in connection with corporate sustainability in this thesis.
One of the most well known ways of considering change management is Lewin’s (1951) three-
step process of unfreezing the existing level of behavior, moving to a new level, and ﬁnally
refreezing the behaviour at the new level (Hayes 2018, p. 21). Later, Dawson (2003) and Kantor
et al. (1992) have argued that the refreezing stage is not relevant for organisations operating
in turbulent environments. Instead the organisations need to be ﬂuid and adaptive to which
the notion of refreezing seems counter-intuitive (Hayes 2018, p. 21). Jabri (2017) presents the
perception that change is like a river, it is never the same river you step down into.
While the above mentioned present quite diﬀerent ways of perceiving organisational change,
one thing remains the same: something will trigger the change (Jabri 2018 p. 4). For corpo-
rate sustainability known triggers include reducing operating costs, enhancing reputation, stake-
holder pressure, government regulation/legislation and top management commitment (Renukappa
and Egbu 2007).
After recognising the need for change the change process will start. Hayes (2018) deﬁnes this
process with four phases; 1) ‘diagnosing what needs to be changed and formulating a vision of the
preferred future state’, 2) ‘planning and preparing for change’, 3) ‘implementing the change and re-
viewing the progress’ and 4) ‘sustaining the change’. A process that can be carried out fully by
either an internal or external change agent or in a combination of the two.
In this thesis the main focus will be the ‘diagnosing and vision creation’ and the ‘planning and
preparing’ phases. It is in these two phases that the ambition level and interpretation of how
the company will work with sustainability are decided, and can therefore be seen as where the
==PAGE:17==

ﬁeld of corporate sustainability and change management overlap the most. In the phases after,
’implementation’ and ’sustaining’, it can be assumed that the procedure will mostly be within the
ﬁeld of change management. Something that will also be highly important for success, but to
limit the scope of this study they will not be included.
A tool used in several ﬁelds for diagnosis of an organization’s capabilities and goal setting of
how these capabilities can be improved is the maturity model (Pöppelbuß and Röglinger 2011).
A maturity model typically includes a sequence of levels (or stages) that form an anticipated, de-
sired, or logical path from an initial state to maturity (Becker et al. 2009). They are primarily used
to assess as-is situations, to guide improvement initiatives, and to control progress (Röglinger
et al. 2012). The level of maturity in a company will represent its capabilities in relation to a
speciﬁc ﬁeld or domain (Rosemann and Bruin 2005). The use of maturity models have proven
successful in numerous applications with a growing base of research supporting the positive
correlation of maturity and organizational performance (Storbjerg et al. 2016).
Maturity models are also used in the ﬁeld of corporate sustainability. This combination has been
linked a considerable number of times in literature - with the earliest example being a study by
Marrewijk and Werre (2002) where a maturity model-like evaluation matrix was presented. A
newer example is the ‘Organizations Maturity - Research Framework’ by Odwazny et al. (2019),
a model that has integrated the SDGs into the framework.
Despite the maturity model being a well known diagnostic tool there is very little literature
connecting the tool to change management practices. In an article by Maier et al. (2011) called
‘Maturity grids as tools for change management: practitioners guidance through ﬁeld experience’, it is
argued that maturity models can aid change managers with diagnosis, reﬂection, and improve-
ment purposes; highlighting the tools ability to engage people in the change process. While
stating what can also be derived from linking maturity models and organisational diagnosis, the
article by Maier et al. (2011) seems to be the only one explicitly connecting change manage-
ment and maturity models in writing. However, even in this article the connection is not further
elaborated beyond the mentioned points.
Based on this it can be assumed that little to no research has been done connecting change
management with sustainability maturity models. The only evidence I have been able to locate
is in an article by Pigosso et al. (2017) where ‘implementation and change management’ is de-

scribed as a phase/tool in their maturity based approach to sustainability implementation. A
reversed view compared to the notion that maturity models are tools for change managers or
at least a tool in organisational diagnostics.
I believe exploring this connection could be an interesting perspective into the merging of corpo-
rate sustainability and change management for more successful sustainability change projects.
In addition, sustainability maturity models and similar tools seem to be on the rise, one paper
even advocates the creation of SDG maturity models as an important tool for monitoring their
development in organisations (Marcovecchio et al. 2016).
The exploration will begin at the maturity model design phase as maturity model design has
been criticised for limited documentation in how to create a model that is theoretically sound,
useful, rigorously tested and widely accepted (Maier et al. 2011). It is also found that several of
the critiques of the tool can be traced back to the design (Pöppelbuß and Röglinger 2011). While
the critique is older than most of the sustainability maturity models existing today, considering
the aim of connecting the model to change management, it seems appropriate to design a new
sustainability maturity model for this purpose.
This maturity model will be developed for use by an external change agent. This distinction
is made as external and internal change agents have quite diﬀerent possibilities and challenges
(Jabri 2017, p. 61). I have therefore chosen to only focus on one to narrow the scope. The ex-
ternal agent was selected as it is closer to my personal frame of reference being in a startup that
aids corporations in becoming more sustainable. In addition, it is my experience that companies
are often using externals, this can e.g. be seen in the sheer number of programmes supporting
small and medium sized enterprises (SMEs) in getting external aid to improve their sustainability
(Bluetree 2020).
The aim of this thesis will therefore be to answer the following research question:
How can a sustainability maturity model be designed to function as an eﬃcient change
management tool for external change agents aiding corporations in becoming more sustainable?

3.
Initialising planning and development
In this chapter the initial development of the corporate sustainability maturity model is elab-
orated. To provide further context for this development the following section will introduce
relevant theory about maturity models in relation to corporate sustainability.
Maturity models are used in several ﬁelds for diagnosis of an organization’s capabilities and
goal setting of how these capabilities can be improved (Pöppelbuß and Röglinger 2011). A ma-
turity model typically includes a sequence of levels (or stages) that form an anticipated, desired,
or logical path from an initial state to maturity (Becker et al. 2009). They are primarily used to
assess as-is situations, to guide improvement initiatives, and to control progress (Röglinger et al.
2012). The level of maturity in a company will represent its capabilities in relation to a speciﬁc
ﬁeld or application domain. When fully mature, a company should have reached the highest
level of capabilities in regards to this domain (Rosemann and Bruin 2005). The development
of the ﬁrst maturity model began in 1986 where the Software Engineering Institute (SEITM) at
Carnegie Mellon University started working on a process maturity framework that would help
organizations improve their software processes (Paulk 2009). Since then hundreds of maturity
models have been proposed by researchers and practitioners in various domains such as knowl-
edge management and business process management (Pöppelbuß and Röglinger 2011). This
includes the ﬁeld of sustainability as well.
In 2014 Poli et al. (2014), created a sustainability maturity model to understand how much
an enterprise is oriented and eﬀectively operates in a sustainable way. They also stated that
a completed tool for evaluating sustainability maturity seemingly had not been developed be-
fore. This was explained by sustainability being more of a theme that aﬀects all activities and
company functions while regular maturity models are usually applied to processes. However, as
highlighted in the introduction, the sustainability maturity model framework can be traced back
to Marrewijk and Werre (2002), while not explicitly stated in the article, the authors present a
maturity model-like evaluation scheme. In 2005, Cagnin et al. also created a maturity model
with ﬁve levels called the Sustainable Business Maturity Model. Later, a 2010 publication by
Baumgartner and Ebner (2010) presented a detailed four level maturity model for economical,
social and environmental sustainability in companies. Lastly, in 2011 Schneider and Meins in-
troduced a sustainability maturity model in the format of a 2x2 matrix.

There was therefore some movement in the ﬁeld before Poli et al.’s (2014) model. Still, most
publications on the matter are after Poli et al.’s (2014) model (Machadoa et al. 2017) (Odważny
et al. 2019) (Rocha-Lonaa et al. 2017a) (Szczuka 2016) (Silvestre et al. 2016) (Loss and Rodriguez
2016). This shows an increasing interest in corporate sustainability maturity, with a special fo-
cus in 2016 and 2017 which could be due to the Paris agreement and the SDG’s being formed
in 2015 and 2016. However, this is just speculation.
With these eleven existing models one could question the need to develop yet another cor-
porate sustainability maturity model. However, ﬁve of the aforementioned models only present
corporate sustainability maturity on an overall 3-5 level scale, meaning that no means (e.g. crite-
ria) are given for the actual assessment of how a corporation would be rated (Rocha-Lonaa et al.
2017a) (Szczuka 2016) (Machadoa et al. 2017) (Poli et al. 2014) (Loss and Rodriguez 2016). The
2x2 maturity matrix by Schneider and Meins (2011), provides a framework to use with diﬀer-
ent ways of measuring corporate sustainability. It would therefore have to be combined with
a second model. The remaining ﬁve models do provide an assessment framework, consisting
of a set of criteria that can each be judged on a 3-5 level scale to determine the ﬁnal maturity.
However, four of these provide little description of how these criteria were made. Considering
the scope of this thesis this is something an external consultant should be able to justify when
bringing a model to a company. Finally there is the 2019 model by Odważny et al. (2019) who
does describe what their 11 maturity criteria were based on. They were identiﬁed through other
maturity models in the literature and the international standard: PN-EN ISO 9004:2000 which
focuses on quality management systems (ISO 2000). However, little detail is provided on why
these particular 11 criteria were chosen to measure the mature sustainable company and what
lies behind the detailing of these criteria.
Given this, the aforementioned critique of maturity model design, and that none of the existing
sustainability maturity models cover use in a change management perspective, designing a new
model from scratch to answer the research question still seems valid.
To further work with the maturity model design it was necessary to select what type of ma-
turity model would ﬁt the aim of this study. According to Fraser et al. (2002) maturity models
can be divided into 3 groups:
1. Maturity grids. These typically describe the maturity characteristics for each activity at

each maturity level with a text description.
2. Hybrids and Likert-like questionnaires, which describe maturity by using questions con-
cerning good practices.
3. Capability Maturity Model (CMM)-like models. These follow the same formalized and in-
ternationally recognized standard format, with process areas that each include common
features, goals, and key practices.
The maturity model developed in this project will be a maturity grid type, which is what Maier
et al.’s (2009) development framework is targeted at. To elaborate why this choice was made, a
three area distinction between maturity grids and models (type 2 and 3) from Maier et al. (2009)
will be used:
Work orientation: Maturity models identify best practices for speciﬁc processes and evaluates
the maturity of an organisation based on how many of these practices have been implemented.
Maturity grids, on the other hand, apply to companies across industries. They identify the char-
acteristics that any process and every enterprise should have in order to design and deploy
high-performance processes. As the aim of this project is to create a model that can be used
by an external change agent the application will most likely be with several companies cross
industries. Therefore, the maturity grid type model is a better ﬁt.
Mode of assessment: Generally, maturity models use Likert scales or binary yes/no-based ques-
tionnaires and checklists for assessment. In contrast, maturity grid assessments are usually
structured around a matrix or a grid. Levels of maturity are allocated against key aspects of per-
formance or key activities, thereby creating a series of cells. An important feature of maturity
grids is that the cells provide a descriptive text explaining the characteristic traits of performance
at each level. Considering the complexity of sustainability, providing descriptive text is a pre-
ferred option. This is also seen in most of the 11 sustainability maturity models described earlier.
Intent: Most maturity models follow a standard format and are internationally recognised and
can therefore be used for certiﬁcation. Maturity grids are usually less complex and do not aspire
to provide certiﬁcation. A maturity grids’ assessment may be used as a stand-alone assessment
or as a part of a broader improvement initiative. Considering the scope of this thesis, creating a
model that can be used for certiﬁcation does not seem relevant.
Based on the distinctions above the maturity grid appears to be the ideal form of maturity model

mechanism and the type of formulation.
Information source
The chosen information source for the model was the second body of literature deﬁned during
the process area selection. This consisted of 17 articles with multidimensional descriptive cri-
teria or similar for corporate sustainability. These criteria were synthesised using an adaption
of the aﬃnity diagram method (Holtzblatt and Beyer 2017) sorting similar descriptions from
diﬀerent articles into categories which were afterwards named. All groupings with 3 or more
separate sources after sorting were chosen to be a criteria in the model. See an illustration of
the method in ﬁgure 3.2. The sorted descriptions can be seen in appendix 2.

Type of formulation
As the cell text was formulated on the basis of descriptions from other literature, the nature of
these descriptions determined the formulation. Due to this the type of formulation is best de-
scribed as prescriptive. Prescriptive formulation is characterised as speciﬁc and detailed courses
of action suggested for each maturity level of a criteria by Maier et al. (2009). Despite the for-
mulations being of prescriptive nature it was decided that the texts should still allow for some
interpretation to match the opposition to ‘one-description-ﬁts-all’ taken in the introduction. In
practice this meant avoiding to much detail on the course of action. In addition, it was decided
that the narrative should be ﬁrst person, as it was assumed that this would allow the company
to relate more to the descriptions.

3.3
Discussion
The purpose of this project step has been to deﬁne the initial draft for the maturity model result-
ing in 31 criteria with ﬁve maturity steps each. Compared to some of the other existing maturity
models, Odwazny et al.’s (2019) model is made up of 11 criteria, Baumartner and Ebner’s (2010)
has 19 criteria and Marrewijk and Werre’s (2002) has 17 criteria - 31 criteria seem to be a lot.
In addition, in some cases multiple of the criteria from these models were used to deﬁne just
one of the 31 criteria. An example being 3 criteria from Baumartner and Ebner’s (2010) model;
’Ethical behavior and human rights’, ’No controversial activities’ and ’no corruption and cartel’ that
were all grouped as a part of the ‘Ethics’ criteria in this model. Another example is the ‘Drivers
for sustainability’ and ‘External drivers for sustainability’ from Marrewijk and Werre (2002) which
were both grouped together under the ‘Drivers for sustainability’ criteria in this model.
==PAGE:53==
42 Initialising planning and development
Considering that clusters like this were made it is even more interesting that the total of criteria is
this much grander. One explanation could be the inclusion of non-maturity criteria descriptions
such as the 44 indicators from Harik et al. (2015), the 96 criteria from the E-sat tool analysed by
Aarcha and Øystein (2018), and the 39 evaluation questions by Asif et al (2010). However, de-
spite including frameworks with such a grand number of indicators, criteria and questions, none
of the 17 frameworks used to deﬁne the criteria contributed to all 31 (see table 3.6). The frame-
work being included in most is the E-sat tool (Aarcha and Øystein 2018) which was included in
24 of the 31 criteria. In addition, none of the 17 sources has a complete overlap in their corpo-
rate sustainability criteria. This highlights the argument made in the introduction, that corporate
sustainability is an ambiguous topic with no common deﬁnition yet. One could speculate that
if non-academic frameworks such as the SDGs or Global Compact had been included it would
have been possible to see more of a pattern. Potentially heightening the credibility that these
exact criteria equals a fully mature sustainable company.
However, given that these 31 criteria have a clear origin from published academia and con-
sidering that all criteria are based on at least 3 existing sources, it does have some credibility.
Yet, it is still important to be aware that while lower than some non-maturity frameworks, this
model still has substantially more criteria than the other corporate sustainability maturity frame-
works. It therefore makes sense to do a veriﬁcation as a part of the practitioner study to see if
any criteria would potentially be deemed irrelevant or less important.
The same goes the other way, even though there are a lot of criteria in this model some were still
cut; evaluation, production, pricing, competition and relation to governance. However, it can be
argued that evaluation is included in the ‘Knowledge management’ and ‘Internal measurement’
criteria . Production will naturally have been considered to reach higher sustainability maturity
in ‘Product’, ‘Emissions’, ‘Water’ etc. Pricing could relate to the ‘Customer relationship’ criteria,
and competition and relation to governance may be included in the ‘Stakeholder relationship’
and (in some cases) the ‘Collaboration’ criteria.
Yet, similar arguments can be made for the interconnectedness of the 31 selected criteria. For
example reaching stage 5 in the ‘Supply chain’ criteria is connected to e.g. the ‘Social impact’ and
‘Environmental management’ criteria, with none of them able to reach stage 5 maturity without
each other. Would it make sense to combine them then? For now these will stay separate, but
this does promote a need for continuous iterations to properly determine the best criteria to
==PAGE:54==
43 Initialising planning and development
measure corporate sustainability maturity.
The same is the case with the cell text. The test of looking for additional material to detail
the ‘Training’ and ‘Health and safety’ criteria came out somewhat positive. While not mountains
of new material was uncovered, it did add some other perspectives that were included in the
cell text. It could therefore be argued that the model could have beneﬁted from an even more
in depth literary analysis after the criteria had been determined. Yet, doing this for all the cri-
teria would have demanded a lot of additional time spent on this part of this process. As the
additional sources are not in the format of criteria, indicators or similar, they need more inter-
pretation and analysis to ﬁt into the brief cell texts of the maturity framework.
Finding additional material was also attempted for the ‘Diversity’ criteria, as none of the cell
texts for this ended up being directly tied to the 17 sources used. However, it became too time
consuming and instead the cell text got based mainly on the overall maturity keywords deﬁned.
Given that the development described in this chapter was only one of three parts in this thesis
project it made sense to make this cut. Still, looking into more speciﬁc literature for the indi-
vidual criteria might be interesting for future iterations. Especially considering that both the
practitioner study and the case study could help specify which criteria might need further work
- ensuring a focused eﬀort.
3.3.1
Limitations
One limitation to this study is the choice to only focus on published academia for the information
source, this excluded considerations towards some of the more popular frameworks such as
the SDGs. Another limitation in regards to the published material is that only free articles and
papers available from DTU Findit and Google Scholar were included, this might have excluded
potentially relevant material. A potential bias is that some of the articles used to develop the
overall maturity keyword framework, also had criteria in the same article that were used to
develop the cell text, e.g. (Landrum and Ohsowski 2018) both present ﬁve overall corporate
sustainability maturity stages and 4 criteria. This might have caused that more of these criteria
descriptions were used in the cell text development. A deﬁnite bias in regards to the cell text
is that it is solely based on my personal interpretation of the criteria text in the 17 sources, as
it was not possible to ﬁnd an objective method. Appendix 2 where the reasoning behind each
cell text is written, is an attempt to remedy this. Another potential bias is that the word cloud
==PAGE:55==
44 Initialising planning and development
method does not account for synonyms. There might, therefore, be relevant keywords which
were left out due to this.
3.4
Conclusion
In this chapter the development of the base sustainability maturity model was completed using
phase I and phase II (decision points 1-3) of Maier et al.’s (2009) development framework. This
resulted in 31 sustainability criteria each with 5 descriptions of stage 1 to 5 maturity. These
were built on a keyword based overall sustainability maturity guideline and 17 existing published
frameworks portraying sustainability maturity criteria or similar. However, 31 criteria is high
compared to existing sustainability maturity frameworks. It was therefore decided to further
validate these as a part of the practitioner study, which will be elaborated upon in the next
chapter.
3.5
Process reﬂection
Applying Maier et al.’s (2009) framework to develop this part of the maturity model worked as
expected. Phase I - planning was straightforward to ﬁll out for the aim of this thesis and following
the steps for decision points 1-3 in Phase II - development was also possible in relation to the
research question. The main issue of this part is the potential of having spend time developing
cell text for too many criteria. Including a speciﬁc validation/veriﬁcation step as a part of this
development step could be interesting to explore.

4.
Finalising planning and development
The purpose of this chapter is to describe the practitioner study and the results here-oﬀ. The
study were carried out to add the change management angle to the model and the applica-
tion process. It focused on detailing the administration mechanism decision point of Maier et
al.s model (methodology, table 2.1). The administration mechanism deﬁnes how the maturity
model is applied to reach the aim of the model. In other words it describes the process that a
consultant and a company will go through to improve the sustainability maturity by using the
model.
In addition, the practitioner study was used to deﬁne the ﬁnal design of the model. This de-
cision option was added to the original development framework by Maier et al. (2009) to allow
for a deeper exploration of design options. An external change agent are evaluated by his/her
ability to build trust, provide high quality services valued by the client, friendliness, approacha-
bility and motives for intervening (Hayes 2018, p. 93). Something that can be expected to have
an eﬀect on the success of the administration of the model. However, given that the maturity
model will also be novel to the client, it can be assumed that the design here-oﬀwill eﬀect how
the company perceives the consultant who brings the model in as the main tool. In product
design it is known that people make associations regarding a product’s quality based on the em-
bodied design (Chakrabarti and Gupta 2007). Based on this it is anticipated that considering the
design of the maturity model might allow for more control on how the model and the consultant
is evaluated, and thus its function as an eﬃcient change management tool.
Finally this practitioner study sought to validate the 31 criteria established in chapter 3, as it
was noted that the current format is quite grand compared to other corporate sustainability
maturity models. In addition the decided options for the planning phase will be reviewed based
on the insights gained from the practitioners. The next section will explore the methods used
for the study.
4.1
Method
The practitioner study had the format of one-on-one workshops, where I functioned as facilita-
tor and dialogue partner with one participant. This format was chosen as it allowed more input
from one participant than a workshop with more people present. Logistics also had an inﬂuence

planning with the maturity model. In addition, the workshops should result in a design of the
model and a ﬁnal decision on which of the 31 criteria to include in the model.

Seven of the participants worked as sustainability consultants (or similar) while the other four

professionals in the sustainability ﬁeld. More information about the participants can be found

4.3
Discussion
The purpose of this practitioner study was to ﬁrst of all determine an administration mechanism
to use in the case study, secondly to design a model which would support the administration
in reaching the aim, and ﬁnally to validate the 31 maturity criteria. I would argue that using
practitioners to work with these decision points was successful, they provided a lot of great
insights into the practicalities of using the model. However, the individual exercises and possibly
even the workshop format in itself could have been designed more optimally. Yet, given that this
step in the development covers the change management angle, and that it is new to Maier et
al.’s (2009) development framework, it could be seen as expected that it would be hard to create
the ideal format right away. Instead this should be explored as a learning for better answering
the research question. To comply with this while still developing a ﬁnal maturity model draft
to test in the case study, this discussion will focus on how each of the intended aims (criteria
validation, administration mechanism and design) could have been approached diﬀerently, while
also interpreting the results to develop the model from these.
4.3.1
Validation of the criteria
Exercise 2 in the workshop was meant to test if any of the criteria stood out from the rest,
however, none did so signiﬁcantly. Instead, the main takeaway was that the 11 participants had
==PAGE:66==
55 Finalising planning and development
selected very diﬀerently. It can be argued that the participants were not given enough time to
properly assess the criteria given the short time frame of the exercise. The results might have
been diﬀerent had they been sent the criteria in advance. The sample size of 11 people can also
be critiqued for being too small a sample size to properly determine anything of signiﬁcance. In
addition, considering that the topic of corporate sustainability is still rather ambiguous (Frecè
and Harder 2018) and the vastly diﬀerent backgrounds of the participants, the mixed outcome
might have been expected. However, it could also be argued that the mixed backgrounds is a
strength and represents the diversity of angles/views on sustainability which all are important
and therefore the mixed outcome could well be the most valid outcome.
Still, another critique of the method is that it focused on the perception of importance. Should
corporate sustainability criteria be deﬁned on what the company thinks is important? While it is
argued in the introduction that a company should choose their own interpretation and ambition
in relation to sustainability, this should still be within some boundaries. Especially when it comes
to the mature sustainable company, while ‘water’ might be more important to some companies
compared to others, it should still be considered a part of the general sustainable company.
Instead it might have made sense to link and connect the criteria. This was already done to
some extent when justifying why the ﬁve criteria; evaluation, production, pricing competition
and relation to governance were sorted out (discussion, chapter 3). Similar arguments could be
made for the criteria suggested by the participants, e.g. that the business model will naturally
have to be considered to reach higher levels of maturity in e.g. ‘emission’, ‘economics’ and ‘social
impact’. It could have been interesting to let the participants group or make similar justiﬁcations
to limit the number of criteria. This was already done by the two participants who suggested
combining criteria without any nudge from layout of the exercise. For application, the Venn di-
agram method could have been used to deﬁne possible logical relations between the diﬀerent
criteria (Design Method Toolkit n.d.). This would however take longer than the original work-
shop time, considering the amount of criteria to consider. This indicates that it might be optimal
to carry out the criteria validation separately from the administration mechanism in future matu-
rity model design processes. However, for this model it will not be possible and the ambiguous
results will be used as a conﬁrmation that all 31 criteria add some kind of merit to corporate
sustainability maturity.

4.3.2
Deﬁning the administration mechanism
An administration mechanism is described by Maier et al. (2009) as deﬁning that the maturity
model is e.g. scored through a survey and the improvements are e.g. determined in a workshop.
However, based on the results from the practitioner study this does not appear to be the best
way to enable successful change. Instead the focus should be on individualising the administra-
tion mechanism so it ensures a transfer of ownership. Yet, as the workshop was designed with
the aim of designing one set mechanism, one could question if this can rationalisation of the
results could be seen as valid, as important angles might have been lost because the exercises
were designed with a diﬀerent aim.
I want to argue that it is valid due to the application of the think-out-loud method which al-
lowed for stray conversation and comments from the participants. All while the speciﬁcness
of the exercises e.g. prototypes and storyboards functioned as good boundary objects for this
conversation. Due to this the nature of the results became much more qualitative than originally
intended. It was while trying to condense these qualitative points into quantitative results that
the impossibility of this became clear and the second rationalisation came to be. However, as
this result was unexpected more work needed to go into exploring how transfer of ownership
can be linked to change management and maturity. The rest of this discussion section will there-
fore focus on this.
The importance of ensuring a change in ownership is also highlighted in academic sources. An-
derson (2012, p180) states that if an external change agent fails to transfer the ownership to
the client, the change won’t last beyond the current engagement. So when the change agent
leaves, the organisation will go back to its previous ways. In addition, joint authorship and co-
ownership are also seen as high quality relational practices (Lambrechts et al. 2009). A focus on
ownership might therefore also be a good practice considering the diﬃculty of establishing a
good change relationship between companies and external consultants (Hayes 2018, p93). Sec-
ondly, the scope of this project can be related to the doctor/patient model of consulting where
the doctor ‘consultant’ diagnoses and prescribes a solution to the patient ‘client’, who is then
trusted to implement the solution (Cash and Minter 1979). Anderson (2012, p87) argues that
this approach is rarely successful for neither the diagnosing nor the implementation. However,
he writes that this can be salvaged if the consultant and the client jointly agree on the diagnosis
and implementation strategy. Again showcasing the importance of creating joint ownership of
==PAGE:68==
57 Finalising planning and development
the maturity model process.
It can be expected that at the beginning of the collaboration between the consultant and client
the ownership of the maturity model will solely be in the hands of the consultant as it is novel
to the company. Ensuring that the company takes over the maturity project can therefore be
seen as a gradual process, which can be seen portrayed in ﬁgure 4.2.
Figure 4.2: Visualisation of gradual consultant/client ownership change.
The question to explore is therefore how to enable this process as an external consultant. I
want to argue that this can be seen as a motivational process where the client is motivated to
gradually take over the ownership. One well known motivational theory is self-determination
theory and the motivational continuum (see ﬁgure 4.3) created by Ryan and Deci in 2000.
Figure 4.3: The Self-Determination Continuum Showing Types of Motivation With Their Regulatory Styles, Loci of Causality, and
Corresponding Processes by (Ryan and Deci 2000). Color adapted.
While it has not been possible to ﬁnd literature portraying a direct link between ownership and
self-determination theory in change management, the link has been made in the ﬁeld of exer-
cise. Here it has been shown that clients take more ownership of ﬁtness programs that have
been created considering self-determination theory motivation (Edmunds et al. 2009). In addi-
tion, self-determination has been linked with increased work performance (Moran et al. 2012).
==PAGE:69==
58 Finalising planning and development
Krarup (2019) highlights how the framework can be used to guide the change manager when
motivating employees. She states that a change leader can motivate as far as the ﬁnal step of
extrinsic motivation (see ﬁgure 4.3), while it is only the employee him/herself that can achieve
intrinsic motivation. Considering that the maturity model is an external tool, the clients motiva-
tion in the beginning of the project might be closest to the external regulation. An interpretation
of linking the feeling of ownership with Ryan and Deci’s theory is that by moving right in the
regulatory styles, the more likely the client will be to feel ownership of the model.
This movement from left to right is supported by fulﬁllment of the three innate human needs:
autonomy; the right to self-determination, perceived competence; the need to experience com-
petence and mastery in one’s tasks and relatedness; good and healthy social relations (Krarup
2019).
The four ways to support company ownership deducted from the practitioner study can also
be related to the innate human needs. Letting the company make the decisions in the process
is a way to ensure ‘autonomy’. Including several stakeholders can be linked to ‘relatedness’ by
ensuring that the sustainability manager is not alone with the project after the consultant leaves.
Focusing on the transaction is a way to ensure that the company has the ’competencies’ to carry
out the project when the consultant leaves. Finally the design of the model can be linked to all
three innate human needs which will be explored further in the next section.
Given that it was not possible to ﬁnd any change management literature exploring this, I am
highly aware that this interpretation of the link between ownership and self-determination the-
ory is somewhat speculative at this point. However, after a conversation with Hanne Jessen
Krarup, who has worked in the ﬁeld of change management for over two decades, it was de-
cided that this connection was interesting to explore, as it seemed close to her experience of
what happens in practice. Yet, it should be made clear that the decision to utilize this link to
help deﬁne the administration mechanism for the maturity model is highly experimental. How-
ever, I believe being experimental is justiﬁed given this being a new take on maturity model
development. The ﬁnal interpretation used for this project can be seen in section 4.3.4.2 ’The
administration model’.

4.3.5
Limitations
The workshop was very time restricted, being originally designed to mainly gather quantitative
data, however it became clear that the true value was in the qualitative comments from the
participants. Some valuable comments/insights might therefore have been cut due to trying
to keep up with time. In addition it should be pointed out as bias that I functioned as both
the creator and facilitator of the workshops. By taking an active part in all workshops I might
have unintentionally aﬀected the course of dialogue with the diﬀerent participants. Finally,
the ownership transfer model was created linking ownership and self-determination theory, the
limitations of the experimentalness of this has already been highlighted. However, it should
also be mentioned that there was not any grander research on motivational theory carried out.
Instead due to time restrictions the research was very speciﬁc and narrowly focused on self-
determination theory, possibly excluding other relevant considerations that might beneﬁt the
ownership transfer.
4.4
Conclusion
This chapter explored how the practitioner study was utilised to determine the administration
mechanism and design decision points from the adapted version of Maier et al.’s (2009) devel-
opment framework. In addition the study was used to edit the Phase I - planning decision points
==PAGE:77==
66 Finalising planning and development
and to validate the 31 criteria deﬁned in chapter 3. It was decided to keep all the 31 criteria for
the case study as the results did not indicate that any should be removed, however, the method
for reaching this conclusion was critiqued and a diﬀerent approach would be recommended for
another development. The study also showed that it would not be ideal to deﬁne a set admin-
istration mechanism in regards to the change management perspective. Instead the ownership
transfer model was developed which will be used as the guideline to deﬁne an individualized ad-
ministration process for the case study. Finally, 23 of the participants’ design suggestions were
used in combination with regards to the three innate human needs to develop the ﬁnal design
of the maturity model.
4.5
Process reﬂection
Just as in the previous chapter Phase I - planning was straight forward to work with and adapt
to the aim. However, the administration mechanism decision point does not ﬁt with the aim of
this study, considering that this is the part where change management is considered, deciding
on one set administration mechanism does not work. Instead creating a model such as the
ownership transfer model to guide the creation of an individualised administration might be
preferable. I would argue that adding the design decision point to the framework was validated
by the interest the practitioners showed in the design, however, that this part should probably
have been carried out separately from the administration mechanism. This would have allowed
to create an understanding of what the administration should be before trying to make the ideal
design for it. The same goes for the validation of the criteria, because this was done while also
working out the two other decision points very little time was available to do the validation
properly. It might have made sense to do this separately, before developing the administration
and design.

5.2.3
Breakdown analysis
Given that none of the four success criteria could be interpreted as entirely fulﬁlled in the case
study, a breakdown analysis was carried out using the survey answers to ﬁnd potential places
==PAGE:87==
76 Evaluation and maintenance
for improvement in the model and the application process. Based on this 3 main breakdowns
were determined:
1. The model lacked proper relatedness in the initiation phase
Other people than the head of CSR were not included until the end of the process. It is my inter-
pretation that because the format of the model and the process during the initiation phase was
not perceived as shareable, this aﬀected the next steps. While the opportunity for relatedness
was acknowledged in the remaining phase, the option was only used in the ﬁnal two and also
shortest phases. My interpretation is that because it was not a natural part from the start, it felt
strange to include people in the diagnosing. The next window to include people was therefore
ﬁrst when the process went from diagnosing and choosing the focus for improvement to vision-
ing/planning, which due to study limitations only consisted of one meeting.
2. Hard to understand and work with the scores leading to the consultant making the anal-
ysis and improvement suggestions
A major breakdown was when the process had to be changed from the initial intention of having
the company analyse and work with the scores using the predesigned template (vision 1+2 tab).
The head of CSR stated that she did not feel that she had the proper insight and understanding
to carry out the analysis. Instead, I as the consultant, ended up doing the analysis of the results.
Something which were made clear from the practitioner study would not be ideal to increase
client ownership. My interpretation is that this stunted the takeover of ownership, which would
also align with the motivation never growing after the ‘initiation phase’. From the survey re-
sponse it was also clear that there had been an issue with the way the scores were portrayed.
3. The model was not aligned with the head of CSRs understanding of sustainability
The head of CSR actively disagreed that a full model score equals a fully mature company. De-
spite agreeing to being able to relate the criteria in the context of DFDS. This might have aﬀected
her ability to take in and identify with the model. Something which potentially inﬂuenced that
the head of CSR only felt neutral about the tool’s usefulness to increase maturity at DFDS.
It is my interpretation, that these breakdowns might have caused the lack of motivational growth
throughout the 4 phases and thereby the missing ownership feeling from the head of CSR. These
breakdowns will be further explored in the discussion.

5.3
Discussion
This discussion will focus on the three breakdowns with the purpose of gaining a deeper under-
standing of why they happened and how they can be remedied. Finally the limitations of this
study will be disclosed.
5.3.1
Integrating relatedness in the initiation phase
Looking at the 23 design suggestions included in the ﬁnal maturity model, only two considered
relatedness and both relied on the format Google Sheets (chapter 4 table 4.4). This shows that
considerations towards relatedness in the design of the model might have been at the low end.
Considering Martin et al.’s (2018) framework for self-determination focused design in MOOCs,
relatedness had the least suggestions of the three innate human needs (chapter 4 ﬁgure 4.3);
‘provide opportunities to connect with others’, ‘a warm and friendly style’ and ‘use an internal frame
of reference’. It might therefore be that there in general are fewer options to design for related-
ness. This was also conﬁrmed by an unsuccessful literature search to ﬁnd additional relatedness
design criteria. Despite this, more could have been done to increase the relatedness support in
the model design. It was, for example, suggested in the practitioner study that the model was
created in a survey-like format so more people could be included and the results could easily be
compared (appendix 6: table 1.6, table 21.3). This might have helped ‘provide opportunities to
connect with others’, however it was not included due to time limitations.
In addition, relatedness could have been considered further in the administration process. It
was clear that the participants in the practitioner study saw a big value in including more peo-
ple in the process. Something that was challenged by the COVID-19 situation, yet more eﬀorts
could have been done to remedy this. Ford and Ford (1995) believes that communication is, in
fact, the medium within which change occurs. Being an active participant throughout the pro-
cess, I can also attest to the conversations in the ﬁnal meeting being of great value. However,
just as with the design, the relatedness in the administration mechanism process could be boiled
down to ‘letting the head of CSR know she could invite people’ and ‘doing material in DFDS col-
ors’. Something which clearly was not enough.
Jabri (2017) presents monologic ’speaking to’ (individual) and dialogic ’speaking with’ (relational)
agency, where monologic focuses on change being in the hands of one or two persons using pre-
determined methods and dialogic based change on open ended conversations about the change
==PAGE:89==
78 Evaluation and maintenance
process. A theory could be that the nature of the maturity model is inherently monologic. This
type of agency is based on the assumption that reality can be identiﬁed and investigated through
predetermined methods Jabri (2017 p69) - a category in which maturity models can be said to
belong. Something that might make it harder to use the tool for relational purposes. Yet, this
does not mean that the tool cannot be used in relational agency. Maier et al. (2011) highlights
that the maturity model’s ability to engage people in the change process is what makes it an in-
teresting change management tool. I would also argue that the model can be used as a boundary
object between people in the change process.
Still, more eﬀort than originally thought might be needed to make the maturity model a proper
relational tool. It will therefore be important to ensure that this is actively considered far beyond
the eﬀorts made in this case study, both in the design and the administration process.
5.3.2
Creating a better more understandable scoring
In the maturity model development framework by Maier et al. (2009) no decision points ac-
tively address the scoring and how to read it. The same goes for the development process of
this model. There was one exercise in the practitioner study which addressed the score format
deciding on average, spiderweb and the 2x2 matrix with a trendline. Yet, there was little to no
work on how this scoring system should be interpreted. Only the 2x2 matrix and the trendline
had some interpretation rules as they were based on articles by Moldavskaa and Welob (2019)
and Schneider and Meins (2011). However, little thought was given to topics such as; ‘when is
an average score of a category signiﬁcantly higher or lower than the overall average?’ and ‘what
understanding is the spider web diagram supposed to bring beyond providing an overview?’. The
two vision tabs (chapter 4 ﬁgure 4.9) were created to allow the company to work actively with
the criteria in their own context. However, using this function was rejected by the head of CSR,
due to doubt in her ability to work with them in a reasonable timeframe. It is unknown if this
was due to the vision tabs being a poor idea, because of the scoring not being understandable
enough or simply due to lack of time and resources.
I want to argue that having a scoring system that the company can easily work with and in-
terpret is crucial for reaching the ‘company takeover’ phase of the ownership transfer model.
Otherwise the consultant will remain as the sole expert, just as what happened in the DFDS
case study. Yet, it has been diﬃcult to ﬁnd any literature about how to work with score in-
==PAGE:90==
79 Evaluation and maintenance
terpretation in a sense relevant to the scope of this model. Also, neither of the two reference
articles that uses Maier et al.’s (2009) development framework, Moultrie et al. (2016) and Stor-
bjerg et al. (2016) consider how to interpret the maturity scores for further use.
In addition, when I as the consultant analysed DFDS’s score no speciﬁc method was used. The
analysis was based on my insights, experience and ‘gut feeling’, a diﬀerent person might have
provided a completely diﬀerent analysis. One can argue if ‘gut feelings’ are really the right ap-
proach for interpreting the results - it does allow to take in the individual case more holistically,
but it also leaves room for a large amount of uncertainty and subjectivity. Considering that do-
ing a ‘gut feeling’ interpretation was deﬁnitely not a viable option for the head of CSR who had
just been introduced to the framework, it can be assumed that few managers will enjoy working
with the scores without some sort of interpretation guidelines. Given that no framework for
creating such guidelines seem to exist, it might be necessary to ﬁrst create said framework and
then develop the interpretation guidelines.
5.3.3
Aligning with companies interpretation of corporate sustainability
According to Frecè and Harder (2018) being able to identify with a deﬁnition of corporate sus-
tainability is important for a successful integration. Yet as mentioned in the thesis introduction
there is still no consensus beyond the Triple Bottom Line. This was also clear looking at the 17
reference sources for the cell text, where none of them had the same focus on all areas.
One thing that was interesting is that the head of CSR stated in the survey that sustainable
maturity could be achieved before reaching stage 5 maturity in all 31 criteria. Indicating that the
model either has unnecessary criteria and/or too ambitious stage 5 descriptions. This follows
the note from chapter 3, that all other sustainability maturity models have signiﬁcantly fewer
criteria. In addition it supports the conclusion from chapter 4 that the criteria validation should
have taken a diﬀerent format.
However, given that no consensus exists on corporate sustainability, it might be a lot to ex-
pect that it will be possible to create a model that encompasses corporate sustainability as it is
perceived by the individual sustainability manager - or just in general as well. While reassessing
these 31 criteria will be necessary, it might also be interesting/necessary to consider other ways
of getting the company to feel that their deﬁnition of corporate sustainability is represented in
the model. An example from Moultrie et al.’s (2016) maturity model is that they allowed compa-
==PAGE:91==
80 Evaluation and maintenance
nies to score N/A or irrelevant. In addition, they added the question ’can you inﬂuence this as a
designer?’. One participant in the practitioner study also suggested weighting the criteria based
on relevance or impact (appendix 6: table 14.14). This will eventually demand a discussion of
potential boundaries in order to stay true to actual corporate sustainability. However, as a deﬁ-
nition of what this is remains unidentiﬁed, working adaptation possibilities to some extent can
be justiﬁed.
5.3.4
Limitations
This study has several limitations. Firstly, COVID-19 made it so all meetings except the last had
to be digital. This was not the original intention and deﬁnitely altered the process. Secondly,
DFDS was asked to participate in the case study and has since stated that they would not usu-
ally purchase consultancy services to do a project like this. The initial and ongoing motivation
might therefore be diﬀerent from a company who has actively purchased and decided to use
a consultant. This also aﬀected the level of detail possible for the planning phase, which may
have inﬂuenced the possibility for a proper ownership transfer. Thirdly, I participated actively
as the consultant while also being the model creator and the person analysing the project, this
might have introduced some bias in the results. Having been a third party observer might have
allowed me to evaluate the process from a diﬀerent more objective angle, it would also allow for
the consultants experience to be included. In addition, my inexperience as a consultant might
have aﬀected the decisions made during the case study and thereby the head of CSR’s percep-
tion of the administration process and the model. Fourth, the ownership transfer model, which
was used both for creating the administration mechanism and the survey to validate it, is self-
created and not an existing well-known and accepted model. Should this model get ‘debunked’
then the case study and the analysis will be invalid. Fifth, the ﬁnal evaluation survey was done all
in one go at the end of the project, meaning that the head of CSR had to remember how she felt
in each phase all at once, which might have biased the results. Sixth, the ﬁnal recommendations
for maintenance are based on only one case study. Had more been carried out or had it been
with a diﬀerent company, these might have been diﬀerent. Finally, the study with DFDS does
not have a baseline maturity model without the change additions to compare it to, which could
have been used to challenge the takeaways.

5.4
Final maintenance recommendations
Based on the discussion above I have created a list of six suggested points to maintain the ma-
turity model improving/eliminating the 3 breakdowns. The assumption is that implementation
of these points will lead to the fulﬁllment of the success criteria in a second case study.
1. Redesign model to allow for better relatedness. Use the 3 points from Martin et al.’s (2018)
design framework for self-determination as potential inspiration.
2. Add more relatedness when designing the individual administration process for a company.
Dialogic change agency can be used as potential inspiration.
3. Develop a framework for how to make a score interpretation guideline for maturity mod-
els. Note that this will be a larger process.
4. Develop score interpretation guidelines and integrate them with the model.
5. Reassess the 31 criteria to see if some can be merged. Potentially use the Venn diagram
method for this.
6. Add an additional element to the maturity model that allows companies to feel that the
deﬁnition of sustainability is more tailored to them.
Including all of these maintenance points will demand a greater additional development process,
which is outside of the scope of this thesis. However, plans to do further work with the model
after the hand-in of this thesis has been made (described in chapter 8 Future Work). To allow
future readers access to future improved versions of the model, they will be uploaded to this
folder when available.
5.5
Conclusion
In this chapter the case study with DFDS was explored as part of the evaluation and maintenance
phase of Maier et al.’s (2009) development framework. The case study process was carried out
using the ownership transfer model as guidance. This model was also used to evaluate the pro-
cess success in addition to the evaluation criteria. Based on the results it was concluded that the
model is not yet an eﬀective change management tool. To mitigate this, a breakdown analysis
was carried out establishing three main breakdowns. To mitigate these, six points for mainte-
nance was recommended. It was estimated that if these maintenance points are implemented,
the possibility of the model working as intended on all evaluation criteria will be higher.

The purpose of this thesis has been to answer the research question “How can a sustainability
maturity model be designed to function as an eﬃcient change management tool for external change
agents aiding corporations in becoming more sustainable?”
For this an adaptation of Maier et al.’s (2009) maturity model development framework was used
in a three step process consisting of: an initial planning and development with base in a literature
review, ﬁnalising of the planning and development from a practitioner study and evaluation and
maintenance using a case study. A simpliﬁed overview of how the adaptation of Maier et al.’s

from it is a potential answer to the research question addressed in this project. However, eval-

therefore argue that the process tested in this thesis is not an eﬃcient change management tool.

The main changes to the original framework are as follows:
1. The development phase has been divided into three separate phases; cell text develop-
ment, administration development and design.
2. The score interpretation guideline has been added as a part of the cell text development.
3. There is no longer one deﬁned administration mechanism. Instead goals and a set-up
process are deﬁned.
4. Validation of the individual development phases added in addition to the overall evaluation
of the ﬁnal outcome.
5. Maintenance changed to being ongoing in all phases.
In this section I will discuss how these 5 changes might make this adaptation a potential answer
to the research question.
Three development phases
As previously mentioned Maier et al’s (2009) development framework never had the change
management tool angle. While the same authors argue that maturity models can be seen as
useful change management tool (Maier et al. 2011), it is not an active part of their develop-
ment framework. The model therefore had to be adapted to be relevant for answering the
research question. In this project the administration mechanism was interpreted as the element
which should include the change management perspective, and the added design ‘decision point’
would serve as support for this. However, this led to the two elements being lumped together
in the practitioner study - not doing either of them any justice. Had the two been separated, it
might have been possible to do more thorough work, and the case study might have been more
successful in achieving the aim.
Still, this process did conﬁrm the relevance of adding the design decision point to the develop-
ment framework as all participants in the practitioner study clearly had opinions on the design
and plenty of improvement suggestions. This was something that was expected as it is known
that good visual design stimulates the users perception of quality (Chakrabarti and Gupta 2007).
What was unexpected to me was that it did not end up being possible to deﬁne a ‘one-size-ﬁts-
==PAGE:99==
88 Discussion
all’ administration mechanism - something I realised after reﬂecting and reading through all the
comments by the participants. Had more eﬀort been put into understanding the nature of or-
ganisations in regards to the aim of the model before designing the workshop, then this might
have been apparent from the start.
The cell text and criteria development (described in chapter 3) ended up being quite thorough
work, and no reason to change this part of the process was found. My theory is that because
this part got its own step in this process, more time could be devoted to that one feature. Given
that, the importance of both the administration mechanism and design to reach the aim of this
study was conﬁrmed by the practitioner study. I would argue that in a future iteration of this
process these two parts should also have their own separate development step, and to show
this they would have their own phase in the development framework.
The score interpretation guideline
As highlighted in chapter 5 the scores and lack of help to properly interpret them clearly inﬂu-
enced the head of CSR impression of the model, which aﬀected the entire process. It therefore
makes sense that considerations towards this should be included in the process. However, as
previously mentioned this focus is not included in Maier et al. (2009) framework or in the two
adaptations of it by Moultrie et al. (2016) and Storbjerg et al. (2016). Nor was it possible to ﬁnd
any other literature about this. However, this could be inﬂuenced by the short available time
frame to research it, or wrong search keywords due to not having a reference for this.
In this thesis process the little consideration made towards the results was included as a part
of the design decision point. Yet, I want to argue that it should be included as the last decision
point of the cell text development phase, as knowing this would be beneﬁcial when developing
the administration mechanism and the design. In addition my recommendation is that it should
be added after the cell text validation as it would make sense to know the exact criteria and
their descriptions before looking into the scores. However, given that very little is still known
about how this part of the development would be carried out exactly, further research should
be made uncovering the exact placement that best beneﬁts the aim of the research question.
Administration goals and set-up
A clear takeaway from the practitioner study is that the administration mechanism should be
individualised. Instead the ownership transfer model was created - focusing on achieving a com-
==PAGE:100==
89 Discussion
plete ownership change through motivational interventions from the self-determination theory.
This provided a set-up process for the administration at DFDS. The new administration phase
decision points were inspired by this.
An option could also have been to make the ownership transfer model a permanent part of the
administration. However, this model only represents one way of considering a successful con-
sultant/client project. In addition to ownership transfer Anderson (2012, p179) presents nine
other reasons why change interventions fail such as; unclear or over-ambitious goals, poorly
designed intervention, and resistance to change - none of which are considered when using the
ownership transfer model.
In general it can be argued that the focus on change management in the currently designed
maturity model is somewhat narrow. Given that this was never properly considered in the de-
velopment framework even with the adaptations made for this thesis. By changing this part of
the development, hopefully this will get more attention and therefore create a better change
management tool.
Ongoing validation and maintenance
The purpose of adding ongoing validation in each development step is to ensure that this part
gets its own focus. Not like the process carried out in this thesis where the criteria validation
became a part of the practitioner study. Something that did not succeed properly as it ended up
being forced into a small exercise to keep time. In addition, the current development framework
by Maier et al. (2009) only has evaluation after the development. This might work with the
previous format, but with the administration and design taking on a bigger role it becomes a lot
to evaluate at the end. This also showed in the evaluation with DFDS where little attention was
given to the individual criteria, the cell text, and the design. All elements that could have been
interesting to have further insights on for improving the model.
Using ongoing validation is inspired by the Lean Startup Method (Ries 2011), where the Mini-
mum Viable Product (MVP) is developed and then improved by testing and validating diﬀerent
hypotheses. Here, the criteria and cell text can be seen as the MVP, and the two other develop-
ment phases as improvements. An argument for this approach is that research based maturity
models are critiqued for taking a long time to develop and that the relationship with the audi-
ence of the model is often lacking (Lasrado et al. 2016). An argument against this could be that
==PAGE:101==
90 Discussion
it might take longer before the full model could be tested holistically in a real-life setting.
One important takeaway from using Maier et al.’s (2009) framework as the base is that the
change management angle became quite under represented compared to the sustainability an-
gle. While it is the intention that adaptation point 1) and 4) will change this, there is also the
possibility that it is not the ideal development framework, considering that the change perspec-
tive was never originally at part of it. The next section will therefore explore the option of an
entirely new development framework.
2. Creating a new maturity model development framework
As mentioned above it can be questioned if Maier et al.’s (2009) development framework is the
ideal base for designing a maturity model to function as an eﬃcient change tool. Most of the lit-
erary research in this thesis has been focused on deﬁning corporate sustainability for the model
and little on considering it as a change tool. Something that was a natural result of the devel-
opment structure. An argument could be that Maier et al.’s (2009) framework just needs more
adaptations such as the ones proposed in the section above. However, it can also be seen as
forcing a framework to take a direction it was not created for. In addition, even with the above
changes, the cell text aka ’the sustainability angle’ is still the base/MVP of the development,
meaning that change management will always be second within that framework.
It could therefore be interesting to develop a model where the change management perspec-
tive is the primary focus, with corporate sustainability being secondary. To explore this angle I
did a small literature study. This uncovered areas that might be interesting to start with when
developing a sustainability maturity model from this perspective:
1. The sustainable change process
2. Why change projects fail
3. Change management and diagnostic models
This is in no way an exhaustive list or necessarily the most relevant of starting points. The pur-
pose is mainly to utilise these points to portray an opposing approach to answer the research
question than the one used in this thesis. The three areas are elaborate upon below.
The sustainable change process
Corporate sustainability has been a topic for quite a while. Therefore there is quite an exist-
==PAGE:102==
91 Discussion
ing pool of literature exploring the change process towards sustainability. E.g. Doppelt (2003)
has developed a wheel of change towards sustainability and Sroufe (2017) a model showing
the integration and organizational change towards sustainability. Understanding the workings
of sustainable change might help scope when the maturity model should be applied and how it
can be best used.
Why change projects fail
As mentioned earlier Anderson (2012) has 10 total points on why change projects implemented
by external change agents fail. Doppelt (2003) presents seven sustainability blunders includ-
ing; ‘siloed’ approach to environmental and socioeconomic issues, and confusion over cause
and eﬀect. Just as how the importance of creating ownership transfer became a large part of
the model developed in this thesis. Focusing on what usually goes wrong and where a maturity
might mitigate or potentially cause increasing damage could be a potential way to scope the
model.
Change management and diagnostic models
Maturity models are not the only diagnostic model used in change management. For example,
a well known diagnostic model is the Strengths Weaknesses Opportunities and Threats model
(SWOT). While maturity models and change management are not explored much, several au-
thors consider diagnostic models in general. E.g. Hayes (2018, p122) speciﬁes weaknesses,
strengths and uses for these models, and Hassin (2010) describes data gathering methods for
diagnosis, warning signals, and the diﬀerent steps to consider. By looking into these points it
might be possible to create the basis for a successful change management tool.
By starting with this change based focus it might be possible to discover what a company will
need from a sustainability maturity model to move forward with sustainability. One potential is-
sue is that there might be a disconnection between what a company needs to move forward, and
what is actually best from a sustainability perspective. Going back to J. Elkington’s (1997) the
Triple Bottom Line, several companies would probably say that they have successfully adopted
this approach. However as J. Elkington points out in his own critique of the Triple Bottom Line:
“Whereas CEOs, CFOs, and other corporate leaders move heaven and earth to ensure that they hit
their proﬁt targets, the same is very rarely true of their people and planet targets. Clearly, the Triple
Bottom Line has failed to bury the single bottom line paradigm.” (Elkington 2018). If the focus is suc-
cessful change, then the path of least resistance - the economical path, might become the true
==PAGE:103==
92 Discussion
driver undermining the balance between the three dimensions which were originally intended.
However, if the alternative is that the change fails, then maybe it is the lesser of two evils.
Preferably some kind of balance is achieved where the change is successful without compro-
mising too much on the original understanding of the Triple Bottom Line of sustainability. Still,
whether the new adaptation of Maier et al.’s (2009) development framework, the altered focus
approach, or an unknown third option is the optimal answer to ‘How can a sustainability maturity
model be designed to function as an eﬃcient change management tool for external change agents aid-
ing corporations in becoming more sustainable?’ will need further testing and exploration before a
ﬁnal recommendation can be made.
6.1
Limitations
A limitation of this study is that there is no consideration on whether or not the maturity model
is a good change management tool. Instead the study is based on the assumption that it can
be a good change management tool with the right design and application process. Should this
not be the case it will never be possible to answer the research question. Secondly, the suc-
cess criteria deﬁned in chapter 3 and 4 are based on the thesis scoping and learnings from the
practitioner study, rather than a detailed literature study on what deﬁnes an eﬃcient change
management tool. This might have biased the evaluation of the maturity model. Thirdly, this
study only focuses on two change phases, there might have been interesting/relevant perspec-
tives for reaching the aim by investigating other phases as well.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
==PAGE:105==
94 Conclusion
7.
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to answer the research question: “How can a sustainability matu-
rity model be designed to function as an eﬃcient change management tool for external change agents
aiding corporations in becoming more sustainable?”. For this an adaptation of Maier et al.’s (2009)
maturity model development framework was used as the main methodology. This was done in
a three step process;
The ﬁrst step focused on developing the cell text and criteria for the model primarily through a
literary study. This resulted in a ﬁve stage maturity model with 31 criteria.
In the second step a practitioner study was completed to develop the administration, ﬁnalize
the design and validate the 31 criteria. Based on the study, it was concluded that there should
not be a set administration mechanism, instead the ownership transfer model was developed to
be used as the guideline for deﬁning an individualized administration process. The ﬁnal model
was designed using suggestions from the practitioner study and considerations towards the
three innate human needs. All 31 criteria were kept in the model, as it was not possible to ﬁnd
evidence for removing or adding criteria.
In the last step the model and administration through the ownership transfer model were eval-
uated in a case study with DFDS. The results were mixed, indicating that the maturity model
had not yet been designed to function as an eﬃcient change management tool. This lead to
six recommended maintenance points focusing on increasing relatedness in the administration,
design and interpretation of the results and working further with the maturity criteria in the
model. Given that maintenance is an included part of the development framework, until these
six points are implemented and the model re-evaluated it cannot be said with certainty if the
tested approach to design the maturity model can be seen as an answer to the research question.
Despite this it was concluded in the ﬁnal discussion that if the development could be re-done,
the above approach could be improved from the learnings from this thesis process. It was there-
fore argued that the approach of this thesis is not the optimal answer to the research question.
Instead the ﬁnal discussion explored two other development process as potential answers:
1. A new adaptation of Maier et al. ’s (2009) development framework based on the learn-
==PAGE:106==
95 Conclusion
ings from the process described in the thesis. These adaptations were divided into 5 themes; 1)
Dividing the development phase into three separate phases; cell text development, administra-
tion development and design, 2) Adding a score interpretation guideline as a part of the cell text
development, 3) There is no longer one deﬁned administration mechanism. Instead goals and a
set-up process/model are deﬁned, 4) Ongoing validation and 5) Ongoing maintenance.
2. A completely new maturity model development framework not directly dependent on an
existing one where the change management perspective is the primary focus and sustainability
the secondary.
Still, whether or not these two approaches or a third approach is the optimal answer to the
research question will demand further research before a ﬁnal conclusion can be made.

8.
Future work
In many ways the work completed in this thesis work can be seen as initial in the ﬁeld of combin-
ing corporate sustainability, change management and maturity model (diagnostic/measurement
models) design. Therefore, while investigating the topic several angles have been uncovered that
could be interesting to explore further, which could not ﬁt into the scope of this assignment. This
chapter will ﬁrstly detail the planned future work which has been scheduled to happen after the
hand-in of the assignment. Secondly, points I as the author, have found might be interesting to
explore to expand the work within this ﬁeld.

project into the startup. So far the planned tasks are:
1. Explore if the maturity model (criteria and cell text in particular) can be used for developing
new e-learning material and data collection points.

8.1.3
Further research on the ownership transfer model
The creation of the ownership transfer model was an unexpected outcome of the practitioner
study. Yet the assumption behind it about combining self determination theory and ownership
for increased success in consultant/client change projects ended up aﬀecting both the model
design, the administration, and evaluation in the case study. Purposely, to not stray too far from
the scope of the thesis, little time was devoted to discussing and validating the model. However,
after a conversation with change consultant and author Hanne Jessen Krarup, she expressed
interest in the model as a general change management tool for consultants, and proposed doing
further research to potentially write an article about it. This would also potentially add credibility
to the model (and thereby the case study), and possibly provide new insights for how to best
administer a maturity model for successful change.
8.2
Potential future work
This is work/angles which could be interesting to explore to take the learnings from this thesis
further, but are not oﬃcially planned to be carried out.
8.2.1
Best angle for answering the research question
In the discussion two new potential paths for answering the research question were explored.
It could be interesting to investigate these angles more, e.g. talk with practitioners and other
stakeholders, to ﬁgure out which approach would be ideal to try in the case of a second iteration
of the maturity model design project.
8.2.2
Connecting change management with other sustainability frameworks
The focus in this thesis has been combining maturity models and change management. However,
maturity models are just one of many sustainability measurement/diagnostic frameworks. Other
famous frameworks such as the SDG’s or GRI might also beneﬁt from this combination. Given
that the whole purpose of this particular study has been to ﬁnd a way to help companies with
sustainable change, it might make sense to explore if there could be an even bigger impact
==PAGE:110==
99 Future work
working with the more well-known frameworks, or if maturity models are the best option for
this scope.
8.2.3
Explore a diﬀerent maturity criteria pool
The 31 criteria created in this study were developed based on a literature study focusing solely
on published criteria (or similar) about corporate sustainability. It could be interesting to explore
if diﬀerent criteria would be determined with another focus. Or, if one of the more popular
mainstream frameworks such as the SDG’s could be used as inspiration instead - exploring if
using the recognisable SDG’s would have an impact on the models ability to function as an
eﬃcient change management tool.
8.2.4
Reevaluate the cell text
Due to time restrictions, the cell text for the 31 criteria did not get evaluated beyond understand-
ability and stage to stage progression. Given that it was possible to add additional perspectives
to the ‘Training’ and ‘Health and safety’ criteria by doing additional research, it might be inter-
esting to consider this option for the remaining criteria. Possibly also doing research outside of
academia. In addition, it might also add additional value to test if the formulation of the cell text
has an inﬂuence on how the company perceives it, and if it therefore has an inﬂuence on the
eﬀectiveness of the model.
8.2.5
Explore the development process methods
The overall development process in this thesis was guided by the adaptation of Maier et al.’s
(2009) framework. However, to carry out this development several methods were used. These
were selected based on my experience from the Design and Innovation bachelor and master
studies. It could be interesting to explore in more detail which methods would be ideal for the
diﬀerent phases of the maturity model development. For example it was questioned in chap-
ter 4 if choosing the most/least important criteria was actually the best approach for criteria
validation. Similarly, it could be compelling to explore diﬀerent levels of co-creation in the de-
velopment process, to see if that has a positive eﬀect on the ﬁnal result.
