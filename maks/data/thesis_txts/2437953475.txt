Abstract
Rapid sand filters(RSFs) are a common used water treatment technology applied in
Denmark and other countries and is favourable in most cases due to no use of chemi-
cals, high filtration rate, lower sludge reduction and low operational and maintenance
costs. Typical groundwater contaminants found in Danish groundwater include com-
pounds like ammonium, hydrogen sulfide, methane, iron, manganese and low levels
of assimilable organic carbon (AOC). The removal process of these groundwater con-
taminants are both physical, chemical and biological. The removal of ammonium
is entirely biological, whereas among the metals (Fe and Mn), these are removed
chemically and biologically in the RSFs, but the contribution for each process is not
clear. Most of the methane and hydrogen sulfide is stripped during aeration, but the
remaining is likely to serve as biological substrates in the RSFs. These groundwater
contaminants and their contribution to biological oxidation in the RSFs, which results
in growth, is not yet clear. The growth potential of substrates entering the RSFs have
been quantified by recording the total cell concentration (TCC) using flow cytometer
(FCM). The growth potential for these substrate was conducted on a full-scale RSFs
at Islevbro and Slangerup waterworks, and the growth potential for the substrates
resulted in different net growth values. The actual growth contribution for each sub-
strates to the microbial communities in the RSFs has not been able to quantify. The
microbial community needs energy in order for cell division and growth. The free
energy of substrates entering the RSFs has been calculated and it has been detected
that metals(Fe and Mn) and nitrogen species are exergonic, hence they can provide
energy for the guilds located in the RSFs, which results in growth. As of today, only
the yield of ammonium oxidizers is available and not for metal oxidizers. This means
that the energy input to the AOB can be quantified, hence also the microbial growth
between each filtration step.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Clean and readily available water is one the most important natural resource on earth
and is used for household, agricultural and industrial purposes. Groundwater is the
leading global source of water for human consumption and the Danish drinking water
supply is entirely based on this (Søgaard and Madsen 2013).
The Danish drinking water production consists of a simple treatment method;
groundwater is pumped up from various wells to the waterworks and goes through an
aeration and filtration process called rapid sand filtration (RSF). RSF is a widespread
treatment technology to produce drinking water from groundwater (Gülay, Tatari, et
al. 2014) and is favourable in most cases; no use of chemicals, the filtration rate is
higher, lower sludge reduction and low operational and maintenance costs (Teker-
lekopoulou, Pavlou, and Vayenas 2013). The major contaminants found in the anoxic
Danish groundwater include inorganic compounds like NH+
4 ,H2S, Fe, Mn, CH4 and
low levels of assimilable organic carbon(AOC) (Søgaard and Madsen 2013), and all
can potentially serve as energy sources for microbial growth in the RSFs (Gülay,
Tatari, et al. 2014). The contaminant removal in the RSFs is chemical, physical and
biological. The removal of ammonium is the major biological process in the RSF and
has been studied in detail. Among the metals, Mn2+ and Fe2+, which is converted
to Mn4+ and Fe3+ during filtration, is biological and chemical oxidation, but the dis-
tribution of these is not clear (Gülay, Tatari, et al. 2014). The removal processes of
methane and sulfide occurs mostly in the aeration step but the remaining is likely to
serve as biological substrates in the RSFs (Gülay, Musovic, et al. 2016). The amount
of total organic carbon (TOC) that enters and leaves the drinking water treatment
plant (DWTP) is typically the same in influent and effluent (Gülay, Musovic, et al.
2016), while the amount of assimilable organic carbon(AOC) that results in growth,
entering and leaving the filters is not clear (E. I. Prest et al. 2013).
These microbial guilds that catalyse oxidation of CH4, H2S, Mn2+, Fe2+ and AOC
present in the RSFs and in charge of the biological oxidation processes, resulting in
growth, are poorly understood and not well examined. Flow cytometry (FCM) is
a rapid tool which has the ability to calculate the cell concentrations directly from
water samples. The excessive growth of guilds can be applied with FCM to measure
microbial growth during the water treatment in the RSFs (Gatza, Hammes, and E.
Prest 2013). The purpose and motivation of this study is to find a systematic approach
to assess which groundwater contaminants contributes as biological substrates and
provide substantial energy to the growth of the microbial communities in the RSFs.

1.3
Rapid Sand Filters
Figure 1.2: A typical RSF from a Danish drinking water treatment plant (Tatari

The most common sand filtration technology used in Danish groundwater treat-
ment is rapid sand filtration (RSF). Figure 1.2 illustrates a RSF from a Danish
waterwork (Tatari 2014). The term is used to distinguish it from slow sand filtration,
an older technology with much slower filtration rate. RSF is also favoured in many
other European countries, due to the minimized amount of chemicals needed, low cost
and its high filtration rate (Crittenden, Hand, and Howe 2012). RSFs is applied to
remove solid precipitates from the water and it is at this treatment stage that most
of the oxidation reactions of the chemical species takes place (Søgaard and Madsen
2013), see table 1.2.
The RSFs usually involve a pre-filter and after-filter phase. The pre-filter consists
of a sand-bed with coarse particles and is used to remove large compounds and Fe2+
whereas the after-filter consists of finer sand particles to remove smaller contaminants
and Mn2+ and NH+
4 , organic compounds and the residual iron from pre-filter (Tek-
erlekopoulou, Pavlou, and Vayenas 2013). The removal process of these groundwater

Nitritation
The nitritation is the first step in the nitrification process where ammonia (NH3)
is biologically oxidized to nitrite (NO−
2 ) (EPA 2007). This transformation process
is carried out by ammonium-oxidizing archaea (AOA) and ammonium-oxidizing bac-

1.3.2
Iron oxidation in Rapid Sand Filters
Iron is one of the most abundant element in Danish water supplies and exist in two
forms, Fe2+ and Fe3+.
Microorganisms oxidizing Fe2+ are in competition with chemical iron oxidation
since they want capture Fe2+ to obtain energy. Because of the neutral pH and highly
oxic conditions present in the groundwater, the chemical oxidation of iron generated
by aeration is in favour for these conditions. Sometimes the chemical oxidation can
be supported by iron oxidizing bacteria in the RSFs, which increase the oxidation
rate even further. The biological oxidation of iron is not considered as important in
RSFs, yet it has been discovered that genera like Gallionella ferruginea, Leptothirx,
Nitrospira and Siderocapsa are the documented Fe oxidizing organisms in the RSFs
(Beek et al. 2016; Tekerlekopoulou, Pavlou, and Vayenas 2013) .
1.3.3
Manganese oxidation in Rapid Sand Filters
Manganese in groundwater is present as soluble Mn2+, a common constituent in
Danish groundwater due to anoxic conditions and relatively low/neutral pH. Soluble
Mn2+ is typically formed by dissolution of compounds such as Mn-OOH and MnO2
under anoxic conditions in the groundwater. The concentration of dissolved Mn(II)
in groundwater is controlled by redox reactions between Mn2+ and Mn3,+, Mn4+
and pH (Søgaard and Madsen 2013). The transformation of manganese in RSFs is
assumed to happen via chemical oxidation and biological oxidation, although the
latter is poorly understood and examined.
The chemical oxidation of soluble manganese is catalyzed by metal oxide surface
in the sand grains in the RSFs. The hydroxyl groups on the metal oxide surface (Me-
OH) attract the Mn2+, which promotes oxidation. The metal surface is most likely
iron oxides in first filtration step where manganese co-precipitate with iron, whereas
the second filtration step is pre-coated with manganese oxide which helps to oxidize
the adsorbed Mn2+ (Søgaard and Madsen 2013).
Previous studies on manganese-oxidizing bacteria (MOB) have found that the mi-
crobial contribution in RSFs are bacterial genera that are taxonomically diverse from
each other, these include Crenothrix, Hyphomicrobium, Leptothrix, Metallogenium,
Actinobacteria Siderocapsa, and Pseudomonas (Søgaard and Madsen 2013; Teker-
lekopoulou, Pavlou, and Vayenas 2013).

1.4
Energy input reflects the microbial community and
growth in RSFs
The groundwater is the main energy source and seed bank for the microbial commu-
nities and the RSFs need different kinds of nutrients for successful culture growth
of microorganisms. Typical microbial nutrients that enter the RSFs and dominate
the living systems are hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), carbon (C), nitrogen(N), phospho-
urs (P), sulfur (S) and selenium (Se). The dry weight of a bacterial cell consists of
approximately 50% carbon and around 13% nitrogen (Madigan et al. 2015). The
contaminant removal performed by microbes in the RSFs is determined by several
factors that affects microbial community in the RSFs. These include pH, temperature,
dissolved oxygen(DO) concentrations and the nutrients available in the groundwater.
These parameters are essential for the microbial community to thrive, otherwise these
factors will influence the growth rate and removal of contaminants in the RSFs (Tek-
erlekopoulou, Pavlou, and Vayenas 2013). Yet, the actual effects of the microbial
communities in the groundwater and the substrates entering the RSFs and responsi-
ble for the different transformation processes are poorly understood and has not yet
been fully investigated.
The biological oxidation that occur in the RSFs are accompanied by changes in
energy. To identify how much energy is avaialble, the free energy, ∆G′, the ability
to do work, can be examined. The change in free energy is expressed as ∆G′, and
a negative in arithmetic sign, means that the reaction will proceed with the release
of free energy, which the cells may conserve as ATP. This energy-yielding reaction
is exergonic.
Conversely, if ∆G′ is positive, the reaction requires energy in order
to proceed, these types of reactions are endergonic and will not occur spontanously
(Madigan et al. 2015).

1.5
Aim of this study
The objective of this study is to provide a more clear understanding of the ground-
water contaminants and their contribution to growth of the microbial communities
and the amount of contaminants that is removed biologically in the RSFs. The po-
tential free energy provided by each chemical compound will be linked to the growth
potential meaurements to determine if the microbial community growing on the sand
reflects the removal yield and energy input. In order to do this the following tasks
were performed.
1. Establish and test a method for measuring growth potential.
2. Sampling of chemical species at different locations at the RSFS.
3. Measurement of growth potential associated with different substrates at full
scale RSFs.
4. Calculate the energy input and removal yield in the RSFs and relate this to the
growth potential.

before the TCC measurement, binding to the cells which allows efficient separation
of small bacteria cells Gatza, Hammes, and E. Prest 2013. A common way to utilize

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
In this final chapter, the final conclusions regarding the aim of this present study will
be answered, which comprise to the research question;” Do energy input reflect the
microbial community structure of full scale groundwater fed rapid sand filter”. The
following was concluded:
• The growth potential of the bottled Evian water was almost proportional with
the acetate concentration, and based from earlier studies (E. Prest et al. 2016),
this can be used as a straightforward method to quantify the bacterial growth.
Thus, the linearity between the acetate and growth potential from this experi-
ment needs improvement if used for later growth potential studies.
• The measurement on growth potential for the different groundwater substrates
(nitrogen species, metals, AOC) when modifying the water samples showed that
the growth potential varies a lot, but in most cases the un-filtrated water sample
had the highest net growth due to most substrates in the water. Since the net
growth potential for each treatment process had such high variance, it is difficult
to detect how much each substrate contributes to the microbial growth in the
RSFs, and more research of this issue is therefor needed.
• The variance in incubation time showed that the negative net growth values
had already reached the stationary phase, and that the maximum net growth
potential may be reached already after maximum 2 days, due to no more energy
input left for biological oxidation, hence many negative net growth results.
• The calculated free-energy input in the RSFs showed that the detected concen-
trations for metals and nitrogen species are exergonic, hence can provide energy
for the guilds located in the RSFs, which results in growth. As of today, only
the yield of ammonia oxidizing bacteria(AOB) is available and not for metal
oxidizers. This means that the energy input to the AOB can be quantified,
hence also the microbial growth between each filtration step.

2. Reducing agent: 1.4 mol (9.73 g) hydroxylamine-HCl in a 100 mL of 2M HCl.
Conncentration(II) = 2 mol/L , Volume (II) = 100 mL , concentration (I) =
10.2 mol/L Calculate Volume(I) = 74.6 mL HCl , 25.4 mL water.
3. Buffer: ammonium acetate: 10 mol/L , adjust the pH to 9.5 with ammonium
hydroxide.

Measurement of TCC with FCM from water samples
1. Transfer 500 uL of the water sample into a labelled 3.5 mL Sample-tube (dilute
if needed)
2. Pre-warm for 3 min at 37°C in the heating block
3. Add 5 uL of SYBR Green I to the tube and vortex briefly
4. Incubate in the dark for 10 min at 37°C
5. Vortex briefly and measure on FCM with the correct settings
6. Distinguish between bacteria and background with electronic gating.
