Abstract
Decentralized power systems have received a good attention regarding the need of more eﬃcient
power systems with emissions as low as possible due to rising global climate and environment
concerns. These type of power systems oﬀer an on-site power production that can be estab-
lished close to the user end omitting the losses in transmission and distribution. Cogeneration
systems based on solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) oﬀer potential beneﬁts such as low emissions,
high eﬃciency and very low noise levels, and can also be established as decentralized units in
urban areas. Such systems are suitable for meeting the heat and power demand of commercial
buildings such as hospitals, hotels and campuses, municipal buildings, domestic buildings for
single or multifamily dwellings. A micro combined heating/cooling and power (CHCP) system
based on SOFC, Stirling Engine and Absorption Chiller with a net electric capacity of 250 kWe
has been studied. Natural gas has been used as the main fuel that is fed into the anode side
of an SOFC after its puriﬁcation and reforming. Under the study, various plant conﬁgurations
have been investigated and plant parameters were analysed for the optimization of the plant
performance. A thermal eﬃciency (LHV) of approximately 59% and combined heat/cooling
and power eﬃciency, so called CHP or plant eﬃciency of 83.7% were obtained for the optimized
system. Compared to other fuel cells, one advantageous feature of the SOFC is its fuel ﬂexibil-
ity. Therefore, the optimized plant was also investigated with dimethyl-ether (DME), methanol
and ethanol. Ethanol demonstrated approximately the same thermal eﬃciency as the natural
gas. The thermal eﬃciency with DME and methanol were relatively lower. A techno-economic
analysis has also been conducted to estimate the investment cost of such a plant and evaluate
the economic viability.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Cogeneration, also known as a combined heat and power (CHP), is not a new concept but is the
simultaneous production of electrical (power) and useful thermal energy from a single energy
source such as oil, coal, natural or other combustible gas, biomass or solar energy. It has been
considered worldwide as the major alternative to traditional/conventional ways of generating
heat and electricity independently in terms of energy saving and environmental conservation
[1]. The concept of cogeneration can be related to power plants of various sizes ranging from
small scale for residential or light commercial applications to large scale systems for industrial
purpose to fully grid connected utility generating stations. Sites that make use of both elec-
tricity and heat are the ones that would beneﬁt from the CHP systems [2]. A good example
would be building applications which usually require heat energy supply as well.
The rising concern of global problems such as ozone depletion, climate change and green house
gas emissions has raised the demand for more eﬃcient and less pollutant-emitting energy tech-
nologies on a global scale. As one of the responses, the concept of decentralised/distributed
power generation has received a signiﬁcant interest in recent years. The production and dis-
tribution from these systems is established close to the end user making them decentralised,
which increases the security of energy supply and minimizes the costs and emissions associated
with transportation, transmission and distribution [3]. Small scale CHPs, also known as micro-
cogeneration or micro-CHP, as a form of decentralized systems has attracted much attention as
an emerging technology due to its potential of high energy eﬃciency and reduction of associated
greenhouse gas emissions [4].
Micro-CHP systems are particularly suitable for commercial buildings such as hospitals, ho-
tels and schools, domestic buildings for single or multifamily dwellings, industrial facilities, as
well as local heat networks [2, 4]. They possess the potential to reduce energy demands of
the residential sector from space heating, domestic hot water heating, and electricity [4]. In
comparison to single-family application, cogeneration systems for multi-family, commercial and
industrial applications tend to beneﬁt more from the thermal/electrical due to load diversity in
the multiple loads served, reducing the need of storage. Whereas for a single-family installation,
such systems are technically challenging because of the improper match between the thermal
and electrical loads, thus requiring a storage facility or connection in parallel to the electrical
grid. However, one of the most promising targets in the application of CHP lies in energy
production for buildings, where small- and micro-CHP system is usually installed [5].
The most common cogeneration technologies, also known as prime mover technologies, suitable
for residential and small-scale commercial applications are [2, 4]:

The reciprocating IC engine, micro gas turbines and micro Rankine cycles are the most well
established technologies for small- and micro-CHP applications. IC engine based cogeneration
systems for residential applications demonstrate an electrical eﬃciency in the range of 20−26%,
with a potential CHP eﬃciency up to 90% [2, 4]. Micro gas turbines which are a scaled down
version of combustion turbines provide reasonable electrical eﬃciency of about 30% and an
overall cogeneration eﬃciency of 80% [2]. They are capable of running on multi-fuel with low
emission levels, heat recovery potential and need minimal maintenance. Existing micro turbine
systems range in size from 25 to 80 kW [2]. The micro Rankine cycle based CHP systems
mostly available in the markets are at prototype level with an electric power size ranging from
1 to 10 kW, with a corresponding thermal output ranging from 8 to 44 kW which use water or
an organic ﬂuid as the working ﬂuid [4]. The electric eﬃciency ranges from 6% to 19%, with a
overall CHP eﬃciency that is always higher than 90% [6, 7]. These mature technologies have
been extensively studied and numerous studies can be found (for example, Refs. [6–11]).
Fuel cells, which convert fuel directly into electricity through an electrochemical process, ap-
pear to oﬀer low emissions, high eﬃciency and very low noise levels. Fuel cell based cogenera-
tion systems are either based on the low temperature polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells
(PEMFC), which operate at about 80◦C, or on high temperature solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC)
operating at around 800 −1000◦C [2], and have an ability to generate electricity and heat with
an overall eﬃciency of around 80−90% [4, 12]. The electrical eﬃciencies have been reported in
the range of 30 −50% [2]. Fuel cell systems also have excellent part load performance in both
power generation and cogeneration applications.
Stirling engines are external combustion engines, meaning the fuel is burned outside to provide
heat that drives the engine. Stirling engine based micro-CHP systems have some beneﬁts with
respect to IC engines such as continuous external combustion, high thermodynamic eﬃciency,
noise reduction and long maintenance intervals [1]. The electric eﬃciency of Stirling engine
based cogeneration systems for various applications vary between 10 −33% and the total eﬃ-
ciency varies in the range 70 −90% [1, 2]. Although the Stirling engine eﬃciency may be low,
it is considered to oﬀer high reliability and costs are low [13]. However, as the Stirling cycle
more closely resembles to Carnot cycle, it has the potential of achieving higher eﬃciency than
those of Rankine and Joule cycles. An electrical eﬃciency of 50% is expected, 40% has been
presently reported [2].
In addition to these prime mover technologies, Thermo photovoltaic (TPV) generators have also
emerged as a micro-CHP system. These need a properly designed boilers, that use a surface
radiant burner, by placing photovoltaic cells (PV) cells in front of the surface radiant burner

The cogeneration systems based on IC engines are available at reasonable cost, that are well
proven and robust. On the other hand, they exhibit higher emission and require more periodic
maintenance than competing technologies. Although the electrical eﬃciency of IC engine is
higher compared to micro-turbines and Stirling engines, the electrical eﬃciencies of these two
are also not that behind and oﬀer a compact system with less moving parts, lower noise and
emissions. The micro Rankine cycle also has an eﬃciency more or less in the same range of
micro turbines but are often considered complex system. The fuel cells demonstrate very good
electrical and overall eﬃciency and are capable of producing power and energy in environment
friendly fashion. From the sustainability point of view, the fuel cells seem transcendent, which
has also become an important feature to be considered in the current energy technologies.
1.1
Scope of Work
The aforementioned cogeneration technologies when operated alone for the production of both
heat and power are unable to achieve the full potential of the fuel utilization i.e., high utiliza-
tion of the heat and power. This can be addressed by the implementation of hybrid systems
by combining two prime mover technologies to yield relatively higher utilization from same fuel
input. Some of the common hybrid system combinations that have been widely studied are,
solid oxide fuel cell as a topping cycle and micro gas turbine, or micro Rankine cycle, or Stirling
engine as a bottoming cycle. Due to the high operating temperature of SOFC the oﬀ-air and
unused fuel exit with a high temperature. This is suitable to fed in the burner of micro gas
turbines, or can be further burned in a burner to produce heat to generate steam for a micro
Rankine cycle or the heat can be used directly in case of Stirling engine.
The gas turbines and Rankine cycles being mature technologies, numerous studies have been
performed on hybrid systems of SOFCs integrated with gas turbines and Rankine cycle (for
example, Refs. [14–17]). However, comparatively few investigations have been done on a hybrid
system with SOFC and Stirling engine (Refs. [18, 19]). Due to the high operating temperature
of SOFC, the cell components are pushed to their limits and requires materials that can with-
stand in such environment which are usually very expensive to manufacture. Low operating
temperature SOFCs have been the focus of various researchers as well as of manufacturers as
in their next generation product lines because lowering the temperature will minimize man-
ufacturing costs, thus making the technology less costly. On the other hand, low operating
temperature of SOFC will be less favourable to gas turbines and Rankine cycle as a bottoming
cycle, consequently might be less beneﬁcial over time. Thus, SOFC integrated with Stirling
engine stands as a good alternative that can operate even with low temperature SOFC.
Cooling needs such as chilled water and air conditioning also have been equally important as

Technological Backgrounds
In this chapter, the technical information of the technologies of the system to be investigated
i.e. solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC), Stirling engine and absorption chiller are explained.
2.1
Fuel Cell Technology
The history of fuel cells dates back to the 1830’s, when in 1839 William R. Groove reported
an electrolytic cell consisting of two platinum strips immersed in sulphuric acid, one in contact
with hydrogen and other with oxygen, that provided electricity for a short time [24, 25]. Since
then the development in this ﬁeld has been carried on and has led to exploration of diﬀerent
fuel cell types. As some highlights, the development of solid oxide fuel cells began with the
initial discovery of a ceramic material consisting of 85% ZrO2 (Zirconium-dioxide) and 15%
Y2O2 (Yttrium oxide), the so called ‘Nernst Mass’, by Walther Nernst in the late 1890s [26];
the alkaline fuel cell has been described since at least 1902, but it was only in the 1940’s and
1950’s to be proved to be viable power units by F.T. Bacon at Cambridge [27], and was used
in the spacecraft for the Apollo missions; the proton exchange membrane fuel cell was ﬁrst
developed by General Electric in the U.S. in the 1960’s [27].
The various current fuel cell technologies in diﬀerent stages of development include alkaline
fuel cells (AFC), polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells (PEMFC), direct methanol fuel cells
(DMFC), phosphoric acid fuel cells (PAFC), molten carbonate fuel cells (MCFC) and solid oxide
fuel cells (SOFC). Table 2.1 presents the brief overview of ions moving through an electrolyte,
operating temperature ranges and their applications.

heating systems, and therefore has been applied extensively in the residential sector [27].
2.1.1
Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC)
Fuel cells are electrochemical devices that convert the chemical energy content in a fuel into
electricity without any direct combustion [28, 30]. SOFC is a solid- state power system that
uses solid, non-porous metal oxides as an electrolyte where the ionic conduction occurs. A
typical SOFC is illustrated schematically in Figure 2.1, which essentially comprises two porous
electrodes separated by a dense, oxide ion conducting electrolyte. Oxygen gas molecules on the
cathode side react with incoming electrons coming from the external circuit to form oxygen
ions, which migrate through the oxide ion conducting electrolyte to the anode. At the anode,
oxide ions react with H2 to form H2O (and/or CO2), liberating electrons, which ﬂow from the
anode through the external circuit to the cathode to produce electricity. Provided that both
fuels and oxygen are supplied constantly, the continuous electrochemical reactions can steadily
generate electricity [28, 29].

The most common electrolyte material used for SOFCs is Yttrium-stabilized ZrO2 (YSZ). Other

platinum (Pt) and gold (Au), and transition metals such as iron (Fe) and nickel (Ni) have been

characteristics (Table 2.2). In order to meet them, a composite of Ni-YSZ was found as a

The two most common designs of SOFCs are the tubular and planar (Figure 2.2) in terms
of cell structure. With emerging technologies and advancement in fuel cell technology, these
two designs have adopted a broad range of scientiﬁc and engineering disciplines resulting in
many interesting variants but still within the category of these two geometries. Some of them
==PAGE:22==

are monolithic SOFC with co-ﬂow and cross-ﬂow conﬁguration (in Ref.[30]), segmented cell in
series design (in Ref.[31]) and ﬂat tubular design (in Ref.[29]).
The tubular design is a hollow tube with one end closed, and the tube wall consisting of cath-
ode, electrolyte and anode in a concentric arrangement with cathode as the inner tube and
anode as the outer tube with electrolyte in the middle. An interconnection strip runs along
the length of the cell. Air is fed through the inner most tube that will direct the air through
the inner passage of the cathode and the fuel ﬂows on the anode side. In the planar design,
a cell consists of thin and ﬂat disks (square or rectangular planes) of cathode and anode on
either side with an electrolyte in the middle. Then the cells are placed in series and connected
by interconnection (or Bipolar) plates, which is ribbed on both sides to form the passage for
the fuel and air. The single biggest advantage of tubular cells over planar cells is that they
do not require any high-temperature seals to isolate oxidant from the fuel, and this leads to
very stable performance of tubular cell stacks over long periods of time (several years) [28, 29].
However, at 1000◦C their power density (0.2−0.3 W/cm2) is much lower compared with planar
cells (upto 2 W/cm2 for single cells and at least 0.5 W/cm2 for stacks) [28, 29, 32]. Due to this
lower power density they are only suitable for stationary power generation and less attractive
for transportation applications [32].
The SOFC that has been considered in the system to be analysed is based on the planar type.

Stirling Engines
The Stirling engine was invented and patented by Robert Stirling in 1846, and became known
by the name Stirling engine as early as 1884 after suggestion by Fleeming Jenkin [44].
A
Stirling engine is a heat engine or mechanical device that operates on a closed regeneratrive
thermodynamic cycle, with cyclic compression and expansion of the working ﬂuid at diﬀerent
temperature levels [13, 44]. The typical working ﬂuids are air, hydrogen or helium. The Stirling
engines developed in the early stage were huge and ineﬃcient, but since its invention it has
adopted various designs and performance optimisation and the eﬃciency has achieved signiﬁ-
cant improvement and can use any high temperature heat source [13].
One major diﬀerence that diﬀerentiates a Stirling engine from an IC engine is that it is an
external combustion engine. This means, the fuel is externally combusted and the heat energy
in return is utilized to power the engine. Because of an external combustion, in addition to the
multiple fuels it can aslo be powered by heat from the combustion of any materials, ﬁeld waste,
1In case of direct methanol fuel cells, for which fuel crossover is signiﬁcant, and, for fuel cells operating with
more complex fuels, the description of the fuel cell eﬃciency requires additional considerations.
==PAGE:27==
2.2. Stirling Engines
14
renewable energy sources like biomass and solar energy. Furthermore, the products from the
combustion do not enter the engine and the external combustion is a well controlled process.
Stirling engines in comparison to IC engines oﬀer possibility of higher eﬃciency, less exhaust
emissions, fuel ﬂexibility, quieter operation with low noise and vibration and long maintenance
free operating periods [2, 13].
The Stirling engines have not completely entered commercialization and availability is limited.
However, several companies are involved in the development of such engines suitable for small-
scale CHP applications [2] such as SOLO (Germany based company), DTE Enery Technologies,
Sunpower Inc., WishperTech from New Zealand and Norway based Sigma.
Presently, the
investment cost of a Stirling engine cogeneration unit is twice as high as an IC engine driven
cogeneration unit of the same capacity. However, with respect to maintenance costs, Stirling
engines are considered to be economical ones [2]. Moreover, the investment and maintenance
costs are expected to drop with the mass production of such units. As of Stirling engines’
potential, an electrical eﬃciency of 50% is expected, presently about 40% reported, and the
overall eﬃciency of 65-80% with power to heat ratio between 1.2 and 1.7 [2]. It is also capable
of operating under part-load conditions.
2.2.1
Stirling Cycle
The Stirling engine functions on an idealised Stirling cycle which involves an isothermal heating
and cooling processes as in the Carnot cycle. In diﬀerence to the Carnot cycle, the Stirling
cycle substitutes the two isentropic processes with two constant-volume processes, also known
as a regeneration process. This process involves the heat absorption from the hot working
ﬂuid, storing it and releasing back to the cold working ﬂuid during the transfer of working
ﬂuid between the hot and cold spaces in the engine. This is accomplished by a regenerator, an
internal heat exchanger and temporary heat store placed between the hot and cold regions. It
can be as simple as a metal mesh or foam, and requires important properties such as high surface
area, high heat capacity, low conductivity and low ﬂow friction for an optimal performance. Its
primary purpose is to elevate the thermal eﬃciency by retaining within the system that heat
which would otherwise be lost to the surroundings, thereby reducing the irreversibility of the
system. The P-v and T-s diagrams of a reversible Stirling cycle are illustrated in Figure 2.5
along with a simpliﬁed theoretical model in Figure 2.6 to correlate the cyclic process.
Figure 2.5: P-v and T-s diagrams of Stirling cycle.
==PAGE:28==

Figure 2.6: Simpliﬁed executive model of Stirling cycle [44].
The ideal Stirling cycle consists of four thermodynamic processes acting on the working ﬂuid:
• Isothermal expansion (1 →2) - In this step, the working ﬂuid undergoes isothermal
expansion absorbing the heat from the hot source. As a result, the left piston moves
outward, doing work and the gas pressure drops. The expansion space and the associated
heat exchanger are maintained at a constant high temperature (TH).
• Isochoric cooling (2 →3) - The working ﬂuid is passed through the regenerator where
it cools, transferring the heat to the regenerator for use in the next cycle. During the
process both pistons move to the right maintaining constant volume until the entire ﬂuid
is forced into cold space. As heat is transferred to the regenerator, the ﬂuid temperature
drops to TL.
• Isothermal compression (3 →4) - The working ﬂuid undergoes isothermal compression
as the right piston moves inward which increases the temperature and pressure. The heat
is then rejected (isothermally) to a heat sink at low temperature (TL), while the pressure
rises.
• Isochoric heating (4 →1) - Both pistons move to the left at a same pace maintaining
constant volume forcing all the working ﬂuid back through the generator where it recovers
much of the heat transferred in (2 →3). Due to heat recovery, the gas temperature
increases to TH and the cycle completes.
2.2.2
Types of Stirling Engine
Based on the mechanical design characteristics, the three common types of Stirling engines are:
Alpa-, Beta- and Gamma-conﬁgurations. All three types use the same thermodynamic cycle
despite their dissimilar conﬁgurations.

displacer cylinder through the heater, generator and cooler.

harnessed by a linear alternator [2]. As the free piston drive avoids mechanical contact and
reduces the moving parts, the major advantages of it include quiet operation, zero wear and
maintenance due to no friction resulting in long life, continuous power operation and potential
high eﬃciency.

2.3
Absorption Chillers
A vapour compression cycle is the most common way of producing cooling eﬀects which utilizes
a mechanical power to drive the compressor. An alternative way of producing cooling eﬀects is
through an absorption cycle which is driven by thermal energy. Absorption cyclec are similar to
vapour compression cycle, except instead of a vapour compressor, it uses a thermal compressor
consisting a desorber (also known as generator), solution heat exchanger (SHX), regulating
valve, absorber and solution pump. Vapour compression chillers use reciprocating, screw and
centrifugal compressors which most often are driven by electric motors, although can also be
powered by natural gas engines or steam turbines [21]. The thermal input to absorption chillers
can be from direct ﬁred burner integrated with chiller or indirect thermal sources such as hot
water, steam, or combustion exhaust.
The three distinct absorption technologies available are: a) water/lithium bromide chillers, b)
ammonia/water chillers and c) ammonia/water/hydrogen chillers [46]. The former two are the
most common ones. The water/lithium bromide absorption cycle uses lithium bromide (LiBr)
as an absorbent and water as a refrigerant. As the water is used as the refrigerant, this type of
cycle is limited to refrigeration temperatures above 0◦C. Absorption machines based on water-
LiBr are typically conﬁgured as water chillers for air-conditioning systems in large buildings.
Ammonia/water chillers use water as an absorbent and ammonia as a refrigerant and this allows
much lower refrigeration temperature due to ammonia’s much lower freezing temperature. So
ammonia/water chillers are used when lower cooling temperatures are required (for example,
refrigerated warehouse for cold storage) compared to water-LiBr systems. However, due to the
toxicity of ammonia, its use is limited to well-ventilated areas. Absorption chillers are most cost
eﬀective at sites such as hospitals, hotels, large commercial buildings, and colleges, campuses
that have signiﬁcant year-round conditioning loads [21].
Many working ﬂuids other than water-LiBr and ammonia-water have been tested for absorption
machines. Some of them are water/sodium hydroxide, water/sulfuric acid, ammonia/sodium
thiocyanate and many more [46]. However, none of these proposed combinations have gained
popularity because of their inability to compete with the properties exhibited by conventional
ﬂuids (water-LiBr and ammonia-water) which are desired in an absorption working ﬂuid. It is
apparently not possible to ﬁnd a ﬂuid mixture that meets all of the criteria [46]. Thus, one
must consider the compromises presented by the existing ﬂuids.
So far, ammonia-water and water-LiBr seem to be the best mixtures to fulﬁl the most criteria
required for absorption cycle ﬂuid properties (see properties [46] in appendix-A.2). However,
one drawback with ammonia-water ﬂuid is that the vapour formed in the generator also tends
to contain water vapour to a certain extent, so this water vapour content needs to be decreased
==PAGE:32==
2.3. Absorption Chillers
19
in the refrigerant. To do this a water separator, so called rectiﬁer is installed after the genera-
tor, and in large plants the generator is designed as a distillation column. On the other hand,
there is no such issue in the case of water-LiBr working ﬂuid, as LiBr is a salt (with a vapour
pressure almost zero [47]), and therefore vapour formed in the generator contains only water.
This simpliﬁes the system design as no rectiﬁcation is needed to remove LiBr-vapour. How-
ever, water-LiBr solution requires appropriate selection of LiBr concentration in the solution

In this study, an absorption chiller unit based on water-LiBr is considered as it is well-suited
for chilled water production that the unit is intended for.
2.3.1
Single-eﬀect Working Cycle
A single-eﬀect absorption chiller typically consists of an evaporator, absorber, generator, con-
denser, solution heat exchanger, solution pump and regulating valves as major components
along with control and other auxiliary systems. A process ﬂow diagram of a single-stage water-
LiBr absorption chiller is presented in Figure 2.8 showing these components.
Figure 2.8: Single-eﬀect water/lithium bromide chiller piping chart schematic [48].
The working cycle is broken into several components to simplify the explanation of how the
cycle works.
==PAGE:33==
2.3. Absorption Chillers

Desorber/Generator: Heat source (steam, hot water or exhaust gas) is supplied to the gener-
ator which heats up the mixture of water-LiBr. This produces refrigerant vapour (water vapour
incase of water-LiBr solution) which moves to the condenser leaving concentrated water-LiBr
solution. This concentrated solution ﬂows back to the absorber via a solution heat exchanger
where it transfers its heat to the dilute water-LiBr solution coming from the absorber to the
generator.
Condenser: The refrigerant vapour is cooled down in the condenser through a cooling stream,
usually the same cooling stream that enters the absorber. Separate cooling streams can also be
used for the condenser and absorber as per the requirement. The refrigerant vapour condenses
into a liquid form and is transported to the evaporator passing through an expansion valve,
where the pressure and temperature of the refrigerant is reduced to a very low level.
Evaporator: After the expansion valve, the water as a refrigerant enters into the evaporator
at a very low pressure and temperature. Since a very low pressure is maintained inside the
evaporator the water remains in a partial liquid- and vapour-state. This water refrigerant ab-
sorbs the heat from the medium to be chilled and gets fully evaporated.
Absorber: The fully evaporated water from the evaporator ﬂows in the absorber. Due to
the strong aﬃnity of LiBr for water vapour, the concentrated water-LiBr solution entering the
absorber absorbs the vapour to form again a weak water-LiBr solution. The absorption process
is exothermic (i.e., it releases heat) [21, 47], and so a cooling medium (e.g., cooling water from
a cooling tower) is required to carry away the heat. The resultant dilute solution collects in
the bottom of the absorber, which is then pumped back to the generator by a solution pump
and the cycle continues.
The heat exchange process between two liquid streams in the solution heat exchanger involves
only sensible heat transfer, meaning no phase change takes place under normal conditions. The
solution heat exchanger is regarded as one of the key components to have a major impact on
the performance of absorption machines as it utilises the energy within the system which would
be wasted otherwise thereby reducing the external heat input. Moreover, its use reduces the
quantity of heat rejected, or cooling demand of the absorber.
2.3.2
Double-eﬀect Cycle
The multiple-eﬀect absorption cycle makes use of multiple generators (for example, two in
double-eﬀect cycle). The main reason for implementing multiple generators is to achieve im-
provements in the coeﬃcient of performance (COP) by taking the advantage of higher avail-
ability of high temperature heat source [46, 47]. In fact, the one limitation of single-eﬀect
absorption cycles is its inability to utilize the higher availability of high temperature heat
source. The COP of a real absorption machine is essentially constant due to the irreversible
eﬀects associated with the heat transfer, and the typical cooling COP of a single-eﬀect water-
LiBr chiller is around 0.7 [46]. The low COP of single-eﬀect cycles makes it diﬃcult to compete
economically with vapour compression systems (except in low temperature waste heat appli-

cations where the input energy is free). The double-eﬀect cycle utilizes the same heat energy
twice, so the cooling COP is in the range of 1.0 to 1.2 [46], making it much more competitive.

2.4.2
Compressors
Compressors, as well as pumps and fans, are devices used to increase the pressure of a ﬂuid.
Unlike the turbines, these devices need a supply of work or power from an external source
through a rotating shaft. Heat transfer from turbines is usually negligible since they are typi-
cally well insulated. Heat transfer is also negligible for compressors unless there is intentional
cooling. Compressors have a wide range of applications due to their various designs. Based
on the construction and operation features, they are mainly classiﬁed in two types: ‘Positive
Displacement Type’ and ‘Dynamic Type’. A mechanical displacement of a ﬁxed volume of air
into a reduced volume occurs in Positive Displacement Type and is further sub-divided in Re-
ciprocating and Rotatory (screw, scroll, vane) compressor. Whereas in Dynamic Type, the gas
is accelerated to a high velocity, and then decelerated to convert the kinetic energy to potential
energy. Centrifugal and Axial compressor lie in this category in spite of their rotatory charac-
teristic. In centrifugal compressors, the deceleration happens in the diﬀuser, and the gas leaves
the compressor in a direction perpendicular to the drive shaft. While in an axial compressor,
the deceleration occurs in the stator blade passages, before the gas leaves the compressor in
the axial direction. The centrifugal and axial compressors are the common types used in power
generation systems.

2.4.5
Desulphurizer
From the electrochemical point of view, hydrogen is the ideal fuel for fuel cells, also because
the oxidation of hydrogen forms water which is environmentally favourable. Although natural
gas is not the only fuel that can be used to employ hydrogen, but it is the preferred fuel in fuel
cells for small stationary power plants because of its availability for commercial, light industrial
and residential applications [50]. Chemically, natural gas is composed of hydrocarbons of low
boiling point and this composition might vary depending upon its geographical sources of ori-
gin. Generally, methane (CH4) is the compound with the highest concentration in natural gas
about 85-95%, ethane (C2H6) about 13%, propane (C3H8) about 3%, butanes (C4H10) and so
on [27, 51]. In addition to these hydrocarbons, natural gas also contains contaminants such as
nitrogen, carbon dioxide, helium, sulphur in form of hydrogen sulphide and carbon sulphide in
small quantities. More speciﬁc chemical composition of natural gas from diﬀerent geographic
regions such as North Sea, Qatar and Indonesia can be found in Ref. [27].
Natural gas contains organic sulphur compounds and so if these are not removed tends to cause
sulphur poisoning of the metal catalysts in the fuel reformer, as well as the anodes. This will
result in performance degradation of reformers and fuel cells due to deactivation of catalysts.
Since the metal catalysts are highly vulnerable to sulphur poisoning, good handling of sulphur
removal is required to ensure adequate lifetime of fuel processors and anode catalysts. It is
suggested to limit the sulphur content to less than 0.01 ppm [50]. However, the requirement
of fuel purity can vary as per the fuel cell design and operating temperature. For instance,

showing diﬀerent tolerances for same fuel cell type could be discovered, but the discrepancies

Dynamic Network Analysis (DNA) is the main simulation software [55] that has been used
in the simulation of various plant conﬁgurations of the considered micro CHCP plant. The
software initiated with a master thesis project [56] and is a result of an ongoing development
process at the thermal section of the mechanical department of the Technical University of Den-
mark. It receives continuous updates to increase its applicability to diﬀerent energy systems.
The program includes a component library and algebraic equation systems. The component
library contains various component models such as heat exchanger, burners, gasiﬁers, turbo
machinery, dryers, decanters, energy storages, engines, valves, controllers and more specialized
and utility components.
DNA is a component-based simulation tool where the components of the system are connected
together with nodes and the physical and thermodynamic conditions are applied. Each com-
ponent has a speciﬁc syntax for deﬁning the inputs, outputs and constraints. Based on the
node numbering, the model is solved by mathematical equations that includes the mass and
energy conservation for all components as well as the relations of thermodynamic properties of
the ﬂuid involved. Figure 2.15 presents an overview of the software’s computation procedure.

30◦C, it seems well suited to be integrated in radiant ﬂoor heating for the cooling water circuit.

this study and would be only considered as a source of cooling water supply that serves as an

the net power was too low for the target net power of 250 kW. Thus, from the net power
curve, the stacks’ range of 150 and 190 seems reasonable as the net power is very close the
target power and the number of stacks is signiﬁcantly reduced from 250. This big reduction
signiﬁcantly reduces the purchase cost. The fuel input can be slightly increased for the stacks

4.3.2
Fuel Utilization Factor
The maximum SOFC power output was observed at a utilization factor of 0.82. It decreased
slowly by both increasing and decreasing the utilization factor from this factor, however de-
creased relatively quickly at the higher utilization factor. As the SOFC eﬃciency is directly
dependent on the SOFC power, the same pattern could be seen in SOFC eﬃciency curve. It
==PAGE:67==
4.3. Overall Plant Analysis
54
should be noted that the variation of utilization factor does not have anything to do with the
fuel input, and relates to the fuel consumption in the SOFC reactions. So, the decrease in
the utilization factor means less fuel is used in SOFC thus less power will be generated. On
the other hand, high utilization factor tends to cause concentration losses because of increased
concentration of fuel which also declines the SOFC power. As less fuel is utilized in the SOFC
for a lower utilization factor, more fuel is fed into the burner, thus more energy for the Stirling
engine and therefore, the Stirling power increases. In addition, more auxiliary power is used
during higher fuel utilization and is reduced for lower fuel utilization. As a result, the net
power changes by an insigniﬁcantly amount for lower utilization factor since the decrease in
SOFC power is balanced by the increased Stirling power. However, the plant eﬃciency tends
to increase all the way up to the utilization factor of about 0.88 and just starts to decline. This
behaviour is due to the increased heat transfer in DHW and the cooling eﬀect of absorption
chiller that improves the overall plant output.
Figure 4.12: Eﬀect of fuel utilization factor on the plant power output and eﬃciency.
Although some factors might be improved by decreasing the fuel utilization factor, it will be
uneconomical to operate with a too low utilization factor as the investment cost of SOFC stacks
is relatively higher. It is recommended that the utilization factors is not below approximately
0.75 [19]. As an alternative, the number of stacks could be reduced with higher utilization
factor to get the same power output, which would be rather more economical. In this case,
as per the thermal and plant eﬃciency, a utilization factor between 0.8 −0.82 seems optimal,
which also lies in the scale of higher fuel utilization.

the anode inlet and 180◦C for the cathode inlet with respect to the SOFC operating temper-

the SOFC operating temperature i.e., both the anode- and cathode-side temperature were also

reduced when lowering the SOFC operating temperature and vice-versa. It is the more logical
way of performing the analysis because for the planar SOFC types, an overly small diﬀerence
between the inlet and average operating temperature may lead to cell fatigue due to thermal
stress, and can permanently damage the cell.
Increasing the cell operating temperature decreases the SOFC power output. Consequently,
the net power and the plant eﬃciency also decreases. Increasing the cell temperature facili-
tates more heat for the Stirling engine from the burner, but from the temperature and mass
ﬂow observations (see in App A.5.) the burner exit temperature also decreases. This was due
to the excess air ﬂow in the burner that cooled the process. However, increased exhaust gas
mass ﬂow dominated the temperature drop in return increasing the Stirling power. Overall,
the decrease in SOFC power also dominates over the increase in Stirling power for higher op-
erating temperatures. On the other hand, the thermal eﬃciency and net power increased as
the operating temperature decreased and reached a maximum value at 670◦C, beyond which
it again decreased. This trend is due to the reduced cooling demand for maintaining the cell
temperature at a lower SOFC operating temperature. As a result, less power is consumed by
the air compressors as represented by the power consumption curve.
The cell operating temperature range of 700 −750◦C seems reasonable to be selected for the
optimization in spite of the highest thermal eﬃciency being at 670◦C. This is because it would
be too optimistic to go below such a low temperature, even around 700◦C with respect to the
current SOFCs in operations. Therefore to be more realistic, the average of this range i.e.,
730◦C could be used for the optimal plant performance.

Chapter 5
Techno-economic Analysis
The successful completion of a thermal design project requires estimation of the major costs in-
volved in the project (for example, total capital investment, operation and maintenance (O&M)
expenses, and cost of the ﬁnal products) considering various assumptions and predictions re-
ferring to the economic, technological, and legal environment and using techniques from engi-
neering economics [66]. This chapter deals with the techno-economic analysis conducted for
the micro CHCP plant. The total cost estimates of various components have been calculated
through the component cost models and assumptions suggested and used in various studies of
similar thermal systems. In addition, a discounted cash ﬂow model was used to evaluate the
economic feasibility using economic indicators such as net present value (NPV), internal rate
of return (IRR) and payback time (PBT).
5.1
Component Cost Models
For the techno-economic analysis of the plant, the purchase cost for every component, annual
capital cost, annual operation and maintenance cost, and the annual total cost need to be deter-
mined. The component cost functions that have been used to obtain these costs are presented
in Table 5.1, which were proposed in Refs. [67, 68].
For the air compressors (centrifugal), the corresponding costs are determined as a function
of the maximum power required. For the counter-ﬂow heat exchanger, the purchase cost is
dependent on heat exchanger area, and the area is expressed as the ratio of heat transferred
to the product of thermal conductivity (k) and log mean temperature diﬀerence. k values of
35 W/m2 for gas-gas heat exchangers and 135 W/m2 for gas-liquid heat exchangers [68] have
been assumed. The cost function for the SOFC stack relates the purchase cost to the cell area
and operating temperature, and the purchase cost of SOFC auxiliary components (chambers,
mixers, valves,etc.) is expressed as 10% of the SOFC stack cost divided by the auxiliary com-
ponent’s eﬃciency. The capital cost of an inverter is simply based as a function of the fuel cell
power (in kW) to be converted.
The component cost function of the pre-reformer is based on the reformer volume (VPR) and the
ﬁnned area of pre-reformer (APR,fin). The ﬁnned area can be assumed as 10% of the volume of

of the process. The reaction time can be assumed from Refs. [68, 69] to be 1 second for the gas
cleaner and 0.5 seconds for the methanator. However, as the reformer volume is problematic

Chapter 6
Conclusions
A micro CHCP system based on SOFC integrated with a Stirling engine and absorption chiller
with a net power output of 250 kWe was studied. Various plant conﬁgurations and parame-
ters were investigated for the optimization of the plant performance. The main motive behind
employing an absorption chiller in the system was to produce a cooling eﬀect simultaneously.
This even resulted in a better way of utilizing the fuel’s full potential throughout the year as
the heat demand in the summer tends to decrease, hence the heat from the plant can be used
in the absorption chiller for generating cooling eﬀect, chilled water in this case.
From the parameter analysis of the absorption chiller unit, the LiBr concentration and hot
outlet temperature of the concentrated water-LiBr solution after the solution heat exchanger
were the parameters identiﬁed as those having the greatest impact on the COP. A larger diﬀer-
ence in the concentration of dilute and rich water-Libr solution improved the performance as
it increased the steam (refrigerant) temperature and quantity which in turn enhanced the heat
transfer in the low desorber, condenser, evaporator and absorber. However, the concentration
diﬀerence should not be too large as to cause crystallization issues. Lowering the hot outlet
temperature after the solution heat exchanger increased the heat utilization within the system
for the same external heat input thereby increasing the system output.
The parameter analysis of the SOFC module showed that for a given fuel mass ﬂow, the power
and thermal eﬃciency increase with the increase in the number of SOFC stacks. However, after
a certain number of stacks the gain in the power was not much. The SOFC power increased
with the increasing fuel utilization factor. However, after a higher fuel utilization (about 0.82)
the power output decreased due to concentration losses. The higher fuel utilization on the
other hand resulted in less fuel availability for the bottoming cycles, and operating the SOFC
with lower fuel utilization factors will be not be economical due to its high cost. Thus, an
appropriate balance should be conﬁgured. The SOFC and plant performance also improved
with decreasing the SOFC operating temperature. However, further lowering of operating tem-
perature again decreased the SOFC power output. In addition, lowering the anode and cathode
inlet temperature also improved the plant performance due to less cooling demand.
Optimizing the SOFC and Stirling engine module conﬁguration from ‘TYPE-A1’(considered
at the very beginning) to ‘TYPE-A3’, the thermal eﬃciency increased from 50.5% to 57.09%.
The performance of the overall plant (TYPE-A3 with absorption chiller) was even improved
by reconsidering the parameters values as shown by the parameter analyses. This increased
both the thermal and CHP eﬃciency of the optimized plant approximately by 2% compared
to TYPE-A3. Moverover, the optimized plant even required less number of stacks of i.e., 150

(reduced from 250), and a lower fuel mass ﬂow to get the net power of 250 kWe which was very
economical.
As the SOFC is capable of operating on various fuels, the performance of the optimized plant
conﬁguration was also investigated with DME, methanol and ethanol so that a comparison
could be made with natural gas. The plant power output was ﬁxed to 250 kWe. The results
obtained showed that the ethanol demonstrated almost the same thermal eﬃciency as the nat-
ural gas. The thermal eﬃciency with the DME and methanol were relatively lower than that
obtained with ethanol. However, approximately the same eﬃciency was obtained for DME
and methanol. Nevertheless, ethanol could be a good alternative to natural gas among the
additional investigated fuels.
In all the plant conﬁgurations including the optimized conﬁguration, the exhaust gas from the
Stirling engine was utilized in the DHW at ﬁrst and then in the absorption chiller, meaning
they were in series connection. Re-conﬁguration of the absorption chiller in a parallel connec-
tion to the DHW beneﬁted the system as it allowed extra ﬂexibility to respond to the changing
heating and cooling demand with respect to the climatic variation without any disturbance in
the system production. The exhasut gas from the Stirling was split into two streams, one going
to the absorption chiller and the other to the DHW. The splitter valve directed the desired gas
mass ﬂow in each unit.
The techno-economic analysis conducted for the micro CHCP plant demonstrated a total capital
investment cost estimate of about $ 848,261 (5,428,871 DKK) with 30% subsidies assumed. The
SOFC resulted as the most expensive component of the system showing a cost share of around
40% of the total purchase equipment cost. The results obtained for the economic indicators
i.e., net present value (NPV) of $ 437,048 (2,797,107 DKK) and internal rate of return (IRR)
of 9.36% showed the economic viability of the micro CHCP plant with respect to the economic
assumptions. The payback time of the investment was found to about 11.8 years.
