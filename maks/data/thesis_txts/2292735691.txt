Abstract
The use of models in chemical and biochemical engineering has a vital importance
in the design, simulation and optimization of products and processes. However,
often they represent a bottle neck in industrial projects since they often ar every
time-consuming and require a lot of expertise. For this for reason, development
of systematic methodologies is key to reduce the time and resources required to
build models. In this project the systematic modelling methodology suggested by
Heitzig, M., (2011) is adopted to develop the kinetic model of the kinetic model
for the enzymatic hydrolysis of triglycerides.
Three diﬀerent model-candidates were initially considered, however two of them
were discarded during the model discrimination. One of them was discarded be-
cause did not satisfy the objective of the modelling. The other one because it
required to estimate too many parameters, and not enough experimental data was
available. The model scenario that was not discarded considered the a plausible
simpliﬁcation of the entire mechanism of the reaction and the adsorption of the
enzyme to the interphase. The eigenvalue report sensitivity analysis performed on
the model revealed that it could be simpliﬁed by assuming that all the enzyme
was immediately adsorbed to the interphase. Then, the parameters were identiﬁed
according to the identiﬁability analysis. The parameter ﬁtting was performed ac-
cording to the statistical analysis of the experimental data. Finally the uncertainty
of the parameters and its propagation in the model output was also addressed.
Finally the identiﬁed model was applied to simulate two process scenarios. In
the ﬁrst process scenario is a system with 3 CSTR in series. The overall conversion
achieved by this system according to the model is 77.8 %. Furthermore, the results
of the simulation could be validated with the experimental data, showing that the
model can performed satisfactory. The second model scenario is a counter current
system with 3 CSTR and two settlers. The simulations revealed that the overall
conversion achieved using this system was of 89.2 % which is notoriously higher
than the conversion obtained by the system with 3 CSTR in series.

Chapter I
Introduction
A model is a representation of certain phenomena that occur in reality. In the
process of developing a model, the modeller translates the events of the real world
into a virtual world with the purpose of gaining understanding of the real world. A
formal deﬁnition of model was provided by Cameron & Gani (2011) as ’a represen-
tation of a real or virtual physico-chemical, economic, social or human situation
in an alternate mathematical or physical form, for an envisaged purpose’.
Models are created for a speciﬁc purpose, which has to be well deﬁned in the
beginning of the process. In order the model to serve the purpose, it is neces-
sary that it describes certain characteristics of the real system (Sales-Cruz, M.A.,
(2006)):
• An adequate and valid structure that represents the relations between inputs,
outputs and internal variables.
• A correct response of the outputs to changes in the inputs.
• A correct dynamic.
During the process of developing a model, the modeller will take some deci-
sions regarding the physical assumptions and the mathematical approximations
that are going to be included in the model. In other words, the modeller will
decide whether a certain phenomena is to be included in the model and how accu-
rately it needs to be described. This may result in several candidate-models with
diﬀerent degrees of complexity. The quality of the candidate-models is evaluated
in terms of performance on serving the purpose.

I.1
The role of models in chemical engineering: need for
systematic modelling methodologies.
In the context of chemical and biochemical engineering models are widely used
to design, simulate and optimize processes. They have a great value in pushing
forward the development of novel and improved products and processes.
The
main contributions of the use of models is their potential to replace resource-
demanding, time-consuming and cost-intensive experiments in the optimization
of product-process behaviour (Heitzig, M., (2011)). The great potential of the
model-based approaches raises from the reciprocal relation between the models
in the virtual world and the systems in the real world. Indeed, valid models are
created from the observations in the real world, and then they are applied to
simulate and ﬁnd optimized systems. The results of the application of the model
are then interpreted and used to guide experimentation in the real world towards
the optimized solution. This relation is summarized in Figure I.1.

are combined to make a process model. Finally, additional models such as oper-
ational, costs, sustainability or environmental impact models are combined with

Due to the complexity of the models, their development and application often
becomes non-trivial and results in very time-consuming and cost-intense processes,
requiring numerous steps and a lot of expertise (Heitzig, M., (2011)). Foss et al.,
(1998) stated that the modelling is the most time-consuming factor in an industrial
project involving model-based process techniques. Moreover, this was conﬁrmed
by Preisig (2010), who stated that modelling is one of the main bottle-necks in
the development of a processing plant. For this reason, development of systematic
methodologies is key to reduce the time and resources required to build models. A
systematic methodology approach is a well deﬁned step-by-step procedure that re-
duces the modelling process into a list of tasks (Papadakis E., (2013)). Several sys-
tematic methodologies have been developed during the last years (Marquardt, W.,
(1996)), (Foss et al., (1998)), (Preisig, H.A., (2010)), (Cameron & Gani, (2011))
or (Heitzig, M., (2011)). The diﬀerent methodologies diﬀer on the number, order,
detail and degree of complexity of phases and steps. For example, whereas Foss
et al., (1998) consider the modelling environment before the development of the
conceptual model, Marquard, W., (1996), Cameron & Gani, (2011) and Heitzig,
M., (2011) do not consider it as a step, since the environment does not aﬀect the
conceptual model. Some of the methodologies are summarized in the Figure I.3.

I.2
Model identiﬁcation: challenges and needs.
The model identiﬁcation is a phase of the model development where unknown
parameters are estimated from the experimental data. This phase is included in
diﬀerent manners in many the modelling methodologies (Marquardt, W., (1996)),
(Foss et al., (1998)), (Cameron & Gani, (2011)) (Sales-Cruz, M.A., (2006)) (Heitzig,
M., (2011)). For example, whilst Marquardt, W., (1996) considers the parameter
estimation as a unique step to identify the models, Cameron & Gani (2011) include
4
==PAGE:23==
a sensitivity analysis as a previous step to the parameter estimation. Heitzig, M.,
(2011), extended the approach by Cameron & Gani (2011) including an extra step
between the sensitivity analysis and the parameter estimation where the linear
relations between sets of parameters are identiﬁed in order to reduce the amount
of parameters to be estimated (identiﬁability analysis).
The model identiﬁcation phase is crucial in the process of model developing
since it often aﬀects the performance of the model. Despite the fact that a lot of
eﬀort is being centred in this ﬁeld (Montgomery, D.C, (2013)), often it turns out
to be challenging to obtain satisfactory estimates of the parameters. The reason is
that the parameter estimation is very dependent on structure and the information
about the experimental data and on the type of model. The commonly used criteria
(such as least squares or maximum likelihood estimation) often are not applicable
or do not guarantee the best estimations of the parameters due to the poor quality
of the experimental data.
However, the performance of the estimation can be
improved by selecting other criteria. The election of the alternative criteria must
be based on the information about the data points, the structure of the model
and the purpose of the model. This information is obtained through a thorough
analysis of the experimental points. In this analysis, questions like ’is it possible
to know the error distribution of the experimental data?’, ’what is the reliability
of the experimental data?’ or ’are all the points in the experimental data equally
reliable?’, must be answered. Moreover, information about the structure of the
data points must be also collected. For example ’are the experimental points giving
enough information to fulﬁl the goal of the model?’. The results of the analysis
will not only guide the estimation of the parameters towards better estimations,
but also can be used to guide the experimentation in order to improve the quality
of the data.
I.3
Motivation and objectives of the thesis.
Models are virtual representation of the real world developed for a speciﬁc pur-
pose. In the context of chemical engineering they are widely used in the design
and optimization of novel and improved processes. Its importance lies in the fact
that they can replace resource-demanding, time-consuming and cost-intensive ex-
periments in the optimization of product-process behaviour. Often, the models
in chemical engineering combine properties and kinetics models with mass and
energy balance models of the unit operations in the process. However, often the
modelling becomes a bottleneck in the process design and optimization, since often
they are very time-consuming and require a lot of expertise. This situation mo-
tived the development of systematic methodologies that can reduce the time and
5
==PAGE:24==
resources required to produce the model. A vital step in the diﬀerent systematic
model methodologies is the model identiﬁcation, in which the parameters of the
model are identiﬁed from experimental data. In this context, a thorough statistical
analysis of the data is a crucial preliminary step for a satisfactory identiﬁcation of
parameters.
The objectives of this project are:
• To adopt a systematic methodology for the model development, which will
be used to construct, identify, validate and apply a kinetic model of a case
study.
• To analyse the experimental data in order to guide the identiﬁcation of the
parameters.
• To discuss and test diﬀerent criteria for the parameter estimation in order
to ﬁnd the best ﬁt.
• To apply the identiﬁed kinetic model to simulate diﬀerent process alterna-
tives.

Chapter II
Literature review
The model identiﬁcation is the phase in the model methodology where the set
of parameters of the model are identiﬁed and evaluated. First of all, the model
identiﬁcation begins with an analysis of which parameters in the model can be
identiﬁed from the experimental data. A parameter can be identiﬁed from a given
experimental set if the the model is sensitive to the parameter, and if the param-
eters in the model are not linearly correlated between them. These properties are
analysed by the sensitivity and the identiﬁability analysis. Once the set (or sub
set) of identiﬁable parameters has been determined, the parameter estimation of
them is performed. In the parameter estimation, the values of the identiﬁable pa-
rameters are determined from the experimental data set. However, the identiﬁed
parameters are not absolute values, but they have associated an uncertainty. This
is measured by the intervals of conﬁdence. Finally, the uncertainty on the param-
eters of the model is propagated and generates uncertainty in the predictions. The
uncertainty of the predictions of the model is analysed in the uncertainty analy-
sis. All the analysis mentioned in this introduction are discussed in this section,
with the objective to illustrate the diﬀerent methods and alternatives used in this
report.
II.1
Sensitivity analysis.
The sensitivity analysis is a study of the changes on the model prediction due to
changes on the variables of the model. It provides vital information that can be
used in the parameter estimation problem, to identify which parameters are sensi-
tive (and therefore can be identiﬁed). This is very important because the presence
of non-sensitive parameters can lead to the reduction of the model. Furthermore,
the sensitivity analysis can also be used in optimization problem, to identify which
are the variables that have a greater impact on the desired variables (Cameron &
Gani, (2011)).

II.2
Identiﬁability analysis.
The objective of the identiﬁability analysis is to identify the linear correlations
between the parameters of the model. The parameters that are correlated can
not be identiﬁed together because the eﬀects of the change on one parameter can
be compensated by the change of another parameter. The collinearity between
parameters has been extensively studied in many ﬁelds of science and engineering
((Stewart, G. W., (1987)), (Weisberg, S., (1990))). In the context of regression
diagnostics, a simple and eﬀective method to evaluate the collinearity relations
between the parameters of the model was suggested by Brun, R., et al., (2001). Is
not the objective of this section to provide a thorough description of the mathe-
matical development of the diagnostic method, but to give an overall description
in order to illustrate it.

II.3
Parameter estimation.
Parameter estimation is a problem that appears in many diﬀerent ﬁelds of science
and engineering where mathematical models need to be identiﬁed by using exper-
imental observations. The objective of the parameter estimation is to identify the
variables of the model that can not be measured directly by experimentation and
that need to be estimated from other measurable variables. In order to do that,
the relations between the measurable and the non-measurable variables and their
time dependency (in dynamic systems) need to be described in a mathematical
form, using algebraic equations (AE) and/or diﬀerential equations (DE). The ba-
sic concept of the parameter estimation is to estimate a vector of non-measurable
values (θ) such that the diﬀerence between the experimental values (yexp) and the
predicted values (ysim(θ)) is minimal in some sense (Sales-Cruz, M.A., (2006)).
Therefore, the parameter estimation problem can be restated as an optimization
problem. A general statement of an optimization problem is shown below.
The objective is to:

In the context parameter estimation, a vector of parameters that minimize
an objective function (a function of the errors), and that satisﬁes some equality

sumptions and knowledge about the measurement errors. The conditions required
to apply a maximum like- lihood criterion are often not present, e.g. an incorrect
model structure results in systematic errors.
II.3.1
Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE).
The maximum likelihood estimation is a statistical-based estimator that ﬁnds the
vector of parameters (θ) that most likely produced the experimental data (yexp)
given a probability density function of the errors in the measurements (p(yexp|θ)).
The likelihood function (L(θ|yexp)) is a reversion of the roles of yexp and θ in
p(yexp|θ), as shown in Equation II.5 and represents the likelihood that a speciﬁc
vector of parameters θ has to produce the experimental output.
L(θ|yexp) = p(yexp|θ)
(II.5)
L(θ|yexp) is the objective function, and the optimal ⃗θ is found after maximiz-
ing it.
For computational convenience, it is common to use the log-likelihood
(log L(θ|yexp)) to calculate the MLE estimate. Because the logarithm function is a
monotonically increasing function, it does not change the points of the maximums
and the minimums of the likelihood function.
Even though the MLE estimates is a very powerful tool to estimate parameters,
it requires to know the probability density function of the errors in the measure-
ments. It is often that the probability density function is not known. In these
11
==PAGE:30==
cases, the MLE estimation can not be applied to estimate parameters and other
methods have to be used.
II.3.2
Ordinary least squares estimation (OLS).
The ordinary least squares (OLS), is one of the most popular methods for parame-
ter estimation. Its importance is established by the Gauss-Markov theorem, which
states that OLS is the best unbiased estimator for linear models in which errors are
uncorrelated, have zero expectation and equal variances. The OLS criteria ﬁnds
a vector of parameters (θ) that minimize the distance between the experimental
(yexp) and the simulated (ysim(θ)) data points. The objective function of the OLS
criteria is shown in Equation II.6.

Although being one of the most used methods, the OLS estimation does not
always guarantees the best results as many of the assumptions of the Markov-
Gauss theorem do not hold. For instance, it is common to ﬁnd highly non-linear
systems, or systems where the error is not independent or the standard deviation is
not constant, (Bard, Y., (1974)). In addition, diﬀerent ranges in the residuals may
cause the lose of information of the smaller residuals since the larger residuals will
domain over the smaller ones. This situation occurs when variables with diﬀerent
dimensions or scales are used (Bard, Y., (1974)). Under these circumstances, the
OLS estimates often performs unsatisfactory and alternative solutions have been
developed in order to obtain better estimates. Some of the most used solutions
are described in the following sections.

II.4
Statistical analysis of the uncertainty of the ﬁtting.
The statistical analysis of the estimated parameters are a group of mathematical
tools that aim to evaluate the quality of the estimated parameters beyond the
value of the objective function. It is not the objective of this section to provide an
thorough description of the diﬀerent methods, which can be ﬁnd in books of infer-
ence and analysis such as Montgomery, D.C, (2013). However, a brief explanation
of the methods used in this project will be provided.
The experimental data is always associated to an experimental error, in a
consequence, the predictions of the model identiﬁed from that data will have an
uncertainty associated (Belsley, D. A., (1991)), (Sales-Cruz, M.A., (2006)). In
order to determine the uncertainty of the model, ﬁrst the intervals of conﬁdence
and the correlation of the parameters need to be calculated (Sales-Cruz, M.A.,
(2006)).
The interval of conﬁdence of a parameter θ is deﬁned as an interval
in which, with a certain probability level, the real value of the parameter θ will
fall. Hence, if the 95% interval of conﬁdence of θ is θ and θ, then with a 95% of
probability the real value of θ will be between θ and θ. In the context of parameter
identiﬁcation, the interval of conﬁdence provide very useful information about the
quality of the parameters and of the uncertainty of the predictions. The interval
of conﬁdence is calculated by using the expression shown in the Equation II.12
((Seber, G. & Wild, C., (2003)), (Sales-Cruz, M.A., (2006))).

Chapter III
Methodology
The systematic methodology framework suggested by Martina Heitzig (2011) has
been adopted in this project in order to develop the kinetic model. It is an itera-
tive process consisting of ﬁve phases (modeling objective and system information,
model construction, model identiﬁcation / discrimination, model evaluation / val-
idation and model application) each of them containing several steps (Heitzig, M.,
(2011)). This methodology in implemented in the Software ICAS-MoT, which has
been used in this project. A brief description of the software is also provided in
this section.
III.1
Adopted methodology for model development.
In this section, a brief description of the method is provided. The Figure III.1
summarizes the main steps in each phase.
A more detailed description of the
method is given in Heitzig, M., (2011).
PHASE I: Modeling objective and system information.
Step I.1. Modelling objective.
Objective. To state the objective of the model with respect to the model per-
formance.
To state the variable to be predicted, the required accuracy in the
predictions. State the degree of detail required. To establish the availability of
experimental data.
Step I.2. System information and documentation.
Objective. To collect and document the information related to the system un-
der study. The information about the system includes functional description an a
sketch of the system, its conditions, a list of relevant phenomena, the modelling
17
==PAGE:36==
problem, the possible assumptions and possible model-scenarios of interest.
Step I.2.1. Functional description / sketch of the system.
Objective. To provide functional description and a sketch of the model in order
to get a more complete overview of the system.
Step I.2.2. Provide system conditions.
Objective. To collect the relevant conditions of the system for the application
of the model.
Step I.2.3. List phenomena in the system that might be of importance.
Objective. To collect a list of all potentially inﬂuential fenomena in order to
get a more detailed idea of how the model can be modelled.
Step I.2.4. Collect information on modelling of system/ problem.
Objective. To collect all information (if available) on how the system can be
modelled/has been modelled before and how similar systems have been modelled.
Step I.2.5. List possible assumptions.
Objective. To document of possible assumptions in order to get a more detailed
idea on how the system can be modelled.
Step I.2.6. Collect preliminary system/process/reactor data.
Objective. To collection and documentation of preliminary data on the system.
To establishment of how the desired system behaviour can be monitored quanti-
tatively and what kind of experimental data could be generated for later validation.
Step I.2.7. Select model-scenarios of interest.
Objective. Derive model-scenarios to be developed.

PHASE II: Model construction.
Step II.1. Model scenario, documentation and concept.
Objective. To establish a description of the model scenario based on which the
model equation will be derived.
Step II.2. Derivation of model equations.
Objective. Derive the equations for the model-scenario based on description
provided in the Step II.1. and the information in PHASE I.
Step II.3. Translation of the model.
Objective. To transform the equations into a format that is readable by a com-
puter.
Step II.4. Analysis of the model.
Objective. To ensure the consistency of the model. This step consists of 7
sub-steps.
Step II.4.1. Equation classiﬁcation.
Objective. To obtain the number and types of equations. The equations are
classiﬁed either as algebraic equations (AE) or as diﬀerential equations (DE).
Step II.4.2. Pre-classiﬁcation of variables.
Objective. To classify the variables according to their type.
Step II.4.3. Degree of freedom analysis.
Objective. To calculate the degrees of freedom of the AE and the DE. To check
that the degrees of freedom allow the model to be solved.
Step II.4.4. Variable values.
Objective. To provide the values of the variables needed to solve the model.
Values for known variables are value of the variable, values for unknown variables
are initial guess, values for dependent variables are initial values and values for
19
==PAGE:38==
parameter can either be the value of the parameter or an initial guess for later
optimization.
Step II.4.5. Incidence matrix derivation and analysis.
Objective. To create and analyse the incidence matrix in order to optimize the
equation ordering and the solution strategy.
Step II.4.6. Solution strategy.
Objective. To derive the solution strategy, to identify the required solvers and
connect them to the model.
Step II.4.7. Veriﬁcation.
Objective. To verify that the model is reﬂecting the system. (variable units,
typing mistakes in variable values). Verify that the model gives the right output
based on a speciﬁc input.
PHASE III: Model identiﬁcation / discrimination.
Objective. To identify the parameters of the model and to discriminate diﬀerent
candidate models based on the available experimental data.
Note. In situation where the modeller does not need to identify parameters of the
model and/or to discriminate between diﬀerent candidate models, the modeller
can proceed to PHASE IV.
Step III.1. Collection and analysis of experimental data.
Objective. To analyse the experimental data collected in PHASE I. To study
the statistical properties of the experimental data. Re-design the experiments in
order to improve the identiﬁability of the model and the discrimination between
diﬀerent candidates.
Step III.2. Sensitivity analysis.
Objective. To identify the impact of each parameter on the output of the model.
To identify which parameters have no impact on the model under the experimental
conditions. To reduce the size of the parameter estimation problem and to increase

the signiﬁcance of the parameter estimation.
Step III.3. Identiﬁability analysis.
Objective. To identify subsets of parameters that are non-collinear and there-
fore identiﬁable. To reduce the size of the parameter estimation problem and to
increase the signiﬁcance of the parameter estimation.
Step III.4. Parameter estimation.
Objective. To estimate the value of the unknown parameters from the available
experimental data.
Step III.5. Statistical analysis.
Objective. To asses the quality of the model identiﬁcation.
Step III.5.1. Conﬁdence intervals.
Objective. To calculate the intervals of conﬁdence of the estimated parameters.

Chapter IV
Case study
In this chapter, the systematic methodology developed by Heitzig, M., (2011) nd
summarized in the previous chapter, is applied with the objective to develop a
kinetic model for the enzymatic hydrolysis of fat. All the steps described in the
Section III.1 and shown in the Figure III.1, were followed in order to construct,
identify, validate and apply the kinetic model. During the process of developing a
model, three model-scenarios were selected according to the information available
and the objective of the modelling. Then, during the discrimination phase two of
them were discarded, and the resulting model scenario was identiﬁed. In the iden-
tiﬁcation process, several ﬁtting criteria were tried in order to obtain satisfactory
parameters. The model was then validated with independent experimental data
and ﬁnally applied to simulate various process set-ups.
IV.1
PHASE I. Modeling objective and system informa-
tion.
IV.1.1
Step I.1. Objective of the model.
The objective of the modelling is to develop and identify a valid dynamic model
for the kinetics of the enzymatic hydrolysis of fat into fatty acids. The model
will be applied to predict the conversion degree of the reactions in diﬀerent set-
ups, which include both batch and continuous operations. Therefore, the model
needs to predict the concentrations of the diﬀerent species over time for diﬀer-
ent concentrations of enzyme. The model also needs to predict the steady state
concentrations of the species and the time required to reach it. The available ex-
perimental data consists of measures in a batch reactor of the degree of conversion
of the reaction at diﬀerent times in a batch reactor. The measures were taken at
diﬀerent conditions (enzyme concentration, water content, etc.).

Step I.2.4. Collect information on the modelling/ system problem.
The enzymatic hydrolysis of triglycerides into fatty acids or similar systems, such
as the biofuel production from triglycerides, is a process that has been extensively
studied in the literature. In the following section a review of how these systems
have been modelled in the literature is provided. The review is done by explaining
how the diﬀerent authors approach each of the phenomena described in the previ-

Eﬀect of the mixing speed on the interfacial area.
It is not the objective of this section to provide an in deep description of how the
mixing speed aﬀects the interfacial area. However, since the interfacial area is an
important factor in the system, it is relevant to mention that increasing mixing
speeds decrease the particle size, increasing the area.
Adsorption of the enzyme to the interfacial area.
Several studies show that the enzyme has a signiﬁcant increase in the catalytic ac-
tivity when it is adsorbed to the interphase between the organic and the aqueous
phase ( Martinelle, M., et al., (1995)). The reversible adsorption of the enzyme
to the interface is modelled in the literature by assuming the Langmuir isotherm
model, which assumes that the enzyme-adsorption rate is proportional to the bulk
enzyme concentration and to the free interfacial area (Tsai, S.-W. & Chang, C.S.,
(1993)), (Jurado, E., et al., (2008)), (Al-Zuhair, et al., (2003)), (Price, J. et al.,
(2014)).
In this model, an equilibrium between the adsorbed enzyme, free in-
terfacial area and the bulk enzyme is established. This situation is described in
Equation IV.1

Some authors suggest a simpliﬁcation of the Langmuir isotherm, in which a
linear relation between the concentration of enzyme in the interphase and the
concentration of enzyme in the bulk (Martinez, O., et al., (1992)). However this
assumption only holds in situations where the ratio between the concentration of
bulk enzyme and the free available area is low (Hermansyah, H., et al., (2006)).
Adsorption of the substrates and products to the interfacial area.
The diﬀerent compounds in the system are also adsorbed to the interface follow-
ing the Langmuir isotherm model (shown in Equation IV.1). This phenomena is
not considered by most of the authors. Hermansyah, H., et al., (2006) consider
a linear relation between the compounds adsorbed to the interface and the com-
pounds in the bulk. Despite the fact that this assumption only holds when the
concentration of each compound is very small compared to the available free area,
it is considered to be a valid assumption since the volume occupied by the diﬀerent
compounds is very small. Tsai, S.-W. & Chang, C.S., (1993) and Al-Zuhair, et al.,
(2003) only considered the adsorption and desorption of the the fatty acids. They
assumed that the interfacial concentration of fatty acids, is proportional to the
30
==PAGE:49==
concentration of fatty acids in the bulk. Moreover, Al-Zuhair, et al., (2003) also
assumed that the interfacial concentration of fatty acids is very low, and hence it
occupies a negligible fraction of the total interfacial area. Price, J. et al., (2014),
and Fedosov, S. N. et al., (2012) address this problem by neglecting the adsorption
and assuming that the concentration of all the compounds in the interphase is the
same than their concentration in the bulk.
Mass transfer between the organic and the aqueous phse.
The transfer of the diﬀerent compounds in the system between the organic and
the aqueous phase, is governed by an equilibrium of their concentrations at each
phase. This phenomena is not considered by most of the authors who assume
that the mas transfer between phases is negligible at the operational temperature
((Fedosov, S. N. et al., (2011)))
Enzymatic hydrolysis of triglycerides into fatty acids.
The overall chemical reaction is shown in Equation IV.2.
T + 3 W

(IV.2)
where T stands for triglyceride, W for water, F for fatty acid and G for glycerol.
However, the mechanism of the reaction is more complex than the reaction shown
in Equation IV.2. It is suggested that the reaction takes place in three steps, each
of them with a Ping-Pong Bi-Bi mechanism (Price, J. et al., (2014)), (Jurado,
E., et al., (2008)), (Cheirsilp, B., et al., (2008)), (Fedosov, S. N. et al., (2012)),
(Al-Zuhair, (2005)). In a Ping-Pong Bi-Bi type of mechanisms two substrates are
consumed in order to make two products. In the ﬁrst reaction, the triglyceride (T)
interacts with the enzyme and produce the complex enzyme-triglyceride (E-T).
Then, the reaction takes place, producing the complex enzyme-diglyceride-fatty
acid (E-D-F). At this point, the diglyceride is released as a ﬁrst product leaving the
acylenzyme (E-F). Then, a molecule of water (W) interacts with the acylenzyme
releasing a free fatty acid (F) as a second product and leaving the free enzyme.
In the second and third reaction, the mechanism is the same but diglyceride and
monoglyceride are used as a ﬁrst substrate, and monoglyceride and glycerol are
released as a ﬁrst product respectively. The second substrate and second products
are the same in all the reactions. An schematic representation of the reaction
mechanism is shown in the Figure IV.2

Inhibition of the enzyme.
32
==PAGE:51==
Enzyme inhibition by the substrates or by the products is a very common phenom-
ena in biotechnological processes that need to be considered. Inhibition is usually
modelled by adding an extra reaction where the inhibitor interacts with the en-
zyme creating a complex enzyme-inhibitor. For the seek of simplicity, most of the
authors do not consider the eﬀect of inhibition in the models. Hermansyah, H., et
al., (2006), demonstrated that some lipases are inhibited by certain types of fatty
acids (i.e. oleic acid). They suggested a model that included a competitive inhibi-
tion by fatty acids. This resulted in a much larger model that could satisfactory
predict the concentration of products and substrates at diﬀerent concentration of
inhibitor. However, despite the complexity of the new model, it only improved
the results slightly, compared to simpler models without inhibition. Regarding
to inhibition by glycerol, some authors also report lower activities of immobilized
lipases when high concentration of glycerol are present in the mixture. However,
it is thought that the lower activities are due to mass transfer diﬃculties rather
than enzyme inhibition (Fjerbaek, L., et al., (2009)).
Deactivation of the enzyme.
The enzyme deactivation is the lose of the enzymatic activity with the time
due to a changes in the structure of the enzyme. This phenomena is neglected
by most of the authors since the enzyme deactivation is not relevant under the
experimental conditions (Jurado, E., et al., (2008)).

is considered to follow a Langmuir isotherm. The reaction kinetics considers
the entire mechanism (Price, J. et al., (2014)). This model-scenario also has
a wider range of application than the ﬁrst model-scenario, and provides a
more accurate description of the reaction mechanism.

x. The fat hydrolysis is the only chemical reaction occurring in the system.
xi. The each reaction follows a Ping-Pong Bi-Bi mechanism.
xii. All reactions are reversible.
xiii. No inhibition of the enzyme due to any product of the reaction.
xiv. It is assumed that the vegetal oil is only composed by triglycerides.

the ratio of the maximum absolute real part and the minimum absolute real part
of the eigenvalues of the system’s Jacobian matrix is greater than 1000 (Equation
IV.49).
max|Re(λi)|
min|Re(λi)| > 1000
(IV.49)
The results of the eigenvalues analysis suggested that the system can be re-
garded as stiﬀ, since the ratio described by Equation IV.49 was of 6.16·1023. This
means that the system has diﬀerent time scales, and consists of modes that reach
steady state very fast and modes that reach it slower. This situation is visual-
ized in Figure IV.5, where the bulk enzyme IV.5i reaches the steady state almost
immediately, whilst the glycerol IV.5f or the fatty acids IV.5d take longer. This

IV.3
PHASE III. Model Identiﬁcation / Discrimination.
The objective of this phase, is to identify the parameters of the model and to
discriminate between the diﬀerent model-scenarios selected. The model identi-
ﬁcation and discrimination is an iterative process where the diﬀerent steps are
repeated through several rounds. First, the experimental data and the diﬀerent
model-scenarios were analysed. As a consequence, of the analysis of the data, two
of the three model scenarios were discarded (model-scenario 1 and model-scenario
3). The model-scenario 2 was analysed in order to see which parameters have more
impact in the output (sensitivity analysis) and which parameters subsets could be
identiﬁed (identiﬁability analysis). The results of sensitivity analysis revealed that
the model could be simpliﬁed since it was insensible to some of the parameters
(the arguments for the simpliﬁcation were also supported by the eigenvalue report).
The results from the identiﬁability showed set of parameters that were not linearly
correlated. The results of the previous steps were used in an iterative process to
re-estimate the parameters. Several strategies for the parameter estimations were
used in order to ﬁnd the best set of parameters that describe the model. Finally,
the results were analysed statistically to deﬁne the interval of conﬁdence of the
parameters and the uncertainty of the model.

Eﬀect of the stirring rate. The stirring rate aﬀects the interfacial area of the
system. At lower stirring rates the water drops are larger and the interfacial area
is smaller. When the stirring rate increases, the drop size decreases and the in-
terfacial area becomes larger. This will have an eﬀect on the reaction since the
larger the interfacial area, the greater amount of enzyme will be able to get ad-
sorbed. However, at one point the interfacial area will be large enough that all the
enzyme is adsorbed there. At that point an increase of the interfacial area won’t
increase the available enzyme adsorbed to it, and the system won’t be limited by
the transfer of the enzyme to the interphase. This situation is well represented
in the experimental set (experiments 2-5), Figures IV.6a, IV.6b, IV.6c and IV.6d.
The experiment 3 (with a stirring rate of 350 rpm) the conversion is lower than
in the other 3 experiments (2,4 and 5) in which the conversion is very similar.
This suggests that at 350 rpm the system is limited by mas transfer, whilst at
higher stirring rates (550, 850 and 1000 rpm) the system is not limited by mass
transfer and the conversion are almost the same. Therefore, regarding the mod-
elling, the experiments 2, 4 and 5 can be considered to be the same experiment
since all of them are performed under the condition of no mass transfer limitations.
Eﬀect of the enzyme concentration. The eﬀects of the enzyme concentration
was studied in the experiments 6-9, and the results are shown in Figures IV.7a,
IV.7b, IV.7c, IV.7d. Higher enzyme concentrations result in faster reactions with
higher degree of conversion.
Eﬀect of initial water content. The eﬀect of the water content in the mixture

In the parameter identiﬁability analysis, the collinear interdependencies be-
tween the diﬀerent parameters of the model are studied with the objective of iden-
tify a subset of parameters that is approximately not collinear (Brun, R., et al.,
(2001)). When a set of parameter are collinear, they can not be identiﬁed together
with one experimental data set, because the eﬀect on changing one parameter
could be compensated by a change in another parameter. The collinearity index
of a subset of parameters (k) is described by the collinearity index γk (Equation
II.3). A large value of the collinearity index implies that a set is is poorly identi-
ﬁable. An empirical threshold of γk equal to 15 was used in this study, meaning
that each subset of parameters with a value of γk lower than 15 will be consid-
ered as approximately non-collinear. This value was selected from the literature
62
==PAGE:81==
in other cases similar to the one under study ((Price, J. et al., (2014))). Finally
an extra measure for the identiﬁability of subsets is used, ρk. It combines the in-
formation from the sensitivity analysis and from the collinearity index. It is used
to discriminate between subsets of the same size with similar γk, great values of
ρk represent subsets of parameters with more signiﬁcance and better identiﬁability.

estimation. In order to do that, ﬁrst of all the diﬀerent ﬁtting options are brieﬂy
discussed and then examples of ﬁttings using diﬀerent criteria are explained and
compared.
The parameter estimation is an iterative process that requires of several rounds
of iteration, in which some of the previous steps (sensitivity analysis and identi-
ﬁability analysis) will be repeated until a good estimate of parameters is found.
In the process of parameter estimation, the selection of a good ﬁtting criteria is
crucial for the quality of the results. In order to illustrate the importance of the
ﬁtting criteria, in the quality of the results, some examples of ﬁtting with diﬀerent
criteria are discussed in this section.
The ﬁrst step is to choose the objective function used for the parameter esti-
mation. Among the diﬀerent objective functions that were available, the relative
least squares was selected to do the parameter estimation EQUATION. This is
because the relative least squares presents a balanced ﬁt between the small errors
and the large errors in contraposition with the ordinary least squares (Equation
II.6) which weights more the large errors than the small ones. In the current ex-
perimental set, there are compounds with diﬀerent scales of concentration, (the
free fatty acids range from 0 to 80 g/goil, whilst the monoglycerides range from
0 to 4 g/goil). Using ordinary least square to ﬁt all the compounds results in an
unsatisfactory ﬁttings for the compounds with with lower concentrations. On the
other hand, the relative least squares provides a much more balanced ﬁtting of all
the compounds. However, this objective function might contain large terms only
because the denominator tends to zero. This situation might become a problem
since the objective function will make more eﬀort to ﬁt small values in detriment of
larger values. Nevertheless, this unbalanced situation can be balanced by adding
weights to each data point, in order to specify how much a speciﬁc point is de-
sired to weight in the overall ﬁt. Therefore, the selection of the weight becomes a
crucial factor to obtain satisfactory ﬁts. In this context, several criteria of placing
the weights were tested in order to ﬁnd the an optimal ﬁt and will be compared
in this section. The right manner to place the weights according to the reliabil-
ity of each data point. Thus, a good assessment of the reliability of each point
is crucial. In this case, the statistical analysis of the experimental data revealed
that the standard deviation of the initial points is notoriously higher than at the
64
==PAGE:83==
later points. In consequence, the weights of each data point were provided in-
versely to their standard deviation. In addition, the results of two other criteria
are also shown, in one of them the same weight was placed to all the points, and in
the other the weights were determined according to the value of the experimental
points, so larger values had larger weight than lower values. A summary of the
diﬀerent ﬁttings, showing the experiments used for the ﬁtting, for the validation,
the parameters obtained in each ﬁtting and the value of the objective function is
shown in Table IV.22.

PHASE V. Model Application.
The kinetic model developed during the previous phases was applied to simulate
two diﬀerent scenarios in order to predict their behaviour under diﬀerent condi-
tions. The ﬁrst scenario consists of 3 CSTR in series, which operate until steady
state. For this scenario there was available experimental data, which could be
used to validate the model. The second scenario to be simulated consists of a
countercurrent system with 3 CSTR in series that operates at steady state. No
experimental data was available for this system.
IV.5.1
Simulation of 3 CSTR in series.
In chemical and biochemical engineering, continuous operational modes are often
used in order to increase the productivity of the batch processes, which usually
have lower productivity. When a system runs in a continuous mode reaches a
steady state, where the variables remain constant in time. Hence, the reactor can
keep a certain level of productivity uninterruptedly through a certain operational
time.
The achieved steady state depends on average amount of time that the
chemicals stay inside the reactor: the residence time (τ). The residence time will
determine the yield of the chemical reaction (if the molecules stay very long in the
reactor, the yield will larger). However, very large residence time will also result
in lower productivities even if the if the yield of the reaction is high. A solution
to achieve large productivities with lower residence time is to use several reactors
connected in series. In this way the outlet of the ﬁrst reactor becomes the inlet
of the second one, and so on. In this way, high overall yields are achieved using
relatively small residence times. Moreover, the fact of operating in continuous also
represents a great operational advantage compared to the batch processes because
systems at steady state are easier to control.
A continuous operation set up is is investigated in this section. A system of 3
CSTR in series is simulated with the objective of analyse the yield of the entire
process. The results of the simulation can be validated with experimental data
80
==PAGE:99==
obtained from the same set up. The system is presented in Figure IV.19 and the
operational conditions are listed below.
1. At time 0h, the CSTR 3 starts working in a batch mode.
2. At time 14h, the CSTR 2 starts working in a batch mode.
3. At time 17h, the CSTR 1 starts working in a batch mode.
4. At time 24h, the 3 CSTR start working in a continuous mode.
The reason that the reactors are started ﬁrst in batch mode for a certain time
and then switched to continuous is to reduce the required time to achieve steady
state by approaching the initial concentrations pf the continuous mode to the con-
centrations at the steady state.
In order to simulate the system, the kinetic model for the fat splitting devel-
oped in the previous phases was used to simulate the diﬀerent stages of the current
system. The simulation was divided into two stages, the ﬁrst one corresponds to
the batch operation mode and the second one to the continuous mode. In the ﬁrst
part, the kinetic model was used to simulate the batch mode of each reactor for
the corresponding operational times. The second part, which corresponds to the
continuous operation, was modelled including the kinetic model into mass balance
for a continuous system. The ﬁnal value of the variables of the ﬁrst stage were
used as initial values for the variables of the second stage.

Step V.1.4. Steady state analysis.
The stability of the steady state was tested ﬁrst of all by calculating the eigenvalues
of the Jacobian matrix at the steady state solution (Heitzig, M., (2011)). If the real
part of all the eigenvalues is negative, then the steady state is stable. The result
of the eigenvalues analysis revealed that all th e real parts of the eigenvalues of the
Jacobi at the steady state were negative, meaning that the steady state is stable
and will be driven back to a steady state solution after a disturbance. This, result
was simulated graphically by making disturbances on the model, and analysing
the behaviour at the steady state. First, the steady state was calculated from the
initial conditions. Then, one of the conditions (the residence time) was disturbed
by a 10 %, and the new steady state was calculated. Finally, the residence time
was setted to the original value and the steady state was calculated again. If the
ﬁnal and the initial steady states coincide, then the steady state of the system is
stable. This analysis was made for the %w/woil of triglycerides and for each of the

IV.5.2
Simulation of the counter current system.
In the previous section it was discussed the possibility to include a fourth CSTR
to increase the overall yield of the process. However, the simulation showed that
only an 1.82 % extra yield would be achieved. Hence, from an economical point
of view, it might not be worth to include a fourth CSTR in the process. It was
also discussed that the reason of the decrease of the individual yield in each re-
actor is that each additional reactor receives a mixture that is every time closer
to the equilibrium. In this section, an alternative topology of the system with 3
CSTR in series is investigated in order to study if the overall yield can be increased
compared to the 3 CSTR in series. The objective of the new topology is to make
each reactor more eﬀective by displacing from the equilibrium the mixture at the
inlet of each CSTR. A diagram of the new system is shown in the Figure IV.24.
The system consists of 3 CSTR in series, the ﬁrst one receives the fresh oil and a
recirculation stream, with enzyme, water and glycerol. The second CSTR receives
the output from the ﬁrst one. The output of the second CSTR is separated, the
aqueous fraction (including water, enzyme and glycerol) is discarded, and the oil
phase goes to the third CSTR, which also receives fresh enzyme and water. The
output of the third CSTR is separated again, the oil fraction (which is rich in
fatty acids) is the ﬁnal product, whilst the aqueous fraction is recirculated into
the ﬁrst CSTR. The objective of this set up is to increase the individual yield of
each reactor by changing the conditions.

Chapter V
Conclusions and future work.
The systematic modelling methodology suggested by Heitzig, M., (2011) was ap-
plied to construct, identify, validate and apply a mechanistic model for the kinetics
of the enzymatic hydrolysis of fatty acids with the objective of predicting the con-
centrations of triglycerides, diglycerides, monoglycerides and fatty acids at any
reaction time when diﬀerent concentrations of enzyme are used in the reaction.
In the process of developing the model, three diﬀerent model-scenarios with
diﬀerent degrees of complexity were considered as a possible candidates. The ﬁrst
model-scenario, considered only a very simple model of the kinetics of the reaction.
It summarized the fat hydrolysis in three reversible reactions where the triglyc-
erides, diglycerides or monoglycerides reacted with water to produce fatty acids
and glycerol. The second model-scenario considered the adsorption of the enzyme
to the interphase and the kinetics of the reaction. The adsorption was assumed to
follow the Langmuir isotherm model, and the kinetics of the reaction was a plausi-
ble simpliﬁcation of the full mechanism of the reaction. The third model-scenario,
considered the adsorption of the enzyme to the interphase (described by a Lang-
muir isotherm) and the kinetics of the reaction (considering the entire mechanism
of the reaction). The ﬁrst and the third model-scenarios were discarded in the
model discrimination phase. The ﬁrst model-scenario was discarded because was
not applicable in the desired range of applicability (speciﬁed by the objective of
the modelling), and the third model-scenario because there was not enough exper-
imental data points to satisfactory identify the parameters of the mode.
In the model identiﬁcation/discrimination phase, the sensitivity analysis of the
model-scenario 2 revealed that it could be simpliﬁed since the model was not sen-
sitive to the parameters controlling the adsorption rate. This result was supported
by the eigenvalues report, which showed that the adsorption reached the steady
state a lot faster than the rest of the model. Hence, the model was simpliﬁed by
assuming that all the enzyme was immediately adsorbed to the interphase. The
97
==PAGE:116==
parameters of the resulting simpliﬁed model were then identiﬁed by parameter
estimation. The statistical analysis of the experimental data set turned out be
crucial for the selection of a proper criteria for the parameter estimation. In or-
der to illustrate this situation three diﬀerent ﬁtting criteria were tried. In the ﬁrst
one, all the data points weighted the same, in the second one the lower data points
were weighted less and in e third one the points that showed a higher standard
deviation were weighted less. The results showed that the last criteria resulted in
the most satisfactory ﬁtting. The models were also evaluated qualitatively against
the experimental data and for diﬀerent enzyme concentrations, and they were val-
idated in diﬀerent experimental scenarios. The results of the validation permitted
to ﬁnd the applicability range of the model.
Finally the model was applied to simulate two diﬀerent processes scenarios.
The ﬁrst process scenario consisted of 3 CSTR in series and the second one of 3
CSTR in series with two settlers and one recirculation. The results of the simula-
tions with the 3 CSTR showed an overall conversion was 77.8%. Furthermore, the
results of the simulation were veriﬁed with experimental data of the system, show-
ing a satisfactory match between the prediction of the model and the simulated
data. The simulations for the second process scenario showed that the counter cur-
rent system had an overall conversion of 89.2%, which is notoriously higher than
the overall conversion of the 3 CSTR. The reason was that in the counter current
system each reactor worked with higher eﬃciency because the reaction mixture in
the inlets was not as close to the equilibrium as it was in the 3 CSTR system.
In conclusion, in this work a mechanistic model for the enzymatic hydrolysis
of triglycerides into fatty acids was developed using the systematic methodology
suggested by Heitzig, M., (2011). The use of the methodology leaded to a kinetic
model that describes the mechanism of the reaction, can be identiﬁed with the ex-
perimental data and satisﬁes the objective of the model. Moreover, the importance
of the statistical analysis of the experimental data was also demonstrated in the
results of the ﬁttings: the parameter set estimated using the statistical information
performed notoriously better than the other estimates. Finally, the application of
the model revealed that the model can potentially be applied for simulation and
the optimization of the process.
An extension of the applicability range of the model could be of interest in the
future work. For instance, the temperature could be an factor to study in since
it could increase the overall conversion of the system. Also, the inhibitory eﬀects
that the substrates or products might have on the enzyme activity can also be
studied by performing the proper experiments. Regrading the applications of the
98
==PAGE:117==
model, it can potentially be used to optimize the enzymatic production of fatty
acids and to simulate diﬀerent process scenarios. For instance it can be combined
with a more accurate description of the settlers (which were considered as ideal in
this work), in order to obtain more accurate prediction of the separation.

# -------------------------------------- MODEL FOR ·CSTR IN SEIES -----------------------------------------# 
 
 
 
# -------------------------------------- Definition of the system -----------------------------------------# 
 
#                               -----                    -----                    ----- 
#                         Q0   |     |        Q1        |     |        Q2        |     |        Q3 
#                       -----> |  V1 |      ----->      |  V2 |      ----->      |  V3 |      -----> 
#                              |     |                  |     |                  |     | 
#                               -----                    -----                    ----- 
#                               CSTR 1                   CSTR 2                   CSTR 3 
 
# --------------------------------------------- Considerations --------------------------------------------# 
 
# The system is considered dynamic. 
# The solution of the first CSTR becomes the input for the following CSTR. 
# We are interested in the solution at the steady state. 
# All reactors do the same reactions. 
# The enzyme does not leave any reactor. 
# The volume is constant. 
# 
 
# ---------------------------------------------- Reaction rates -------------------------------------------# 
 
r1[j] = k1*C_T[j]*C_E[j] - kr1*C_D[j]*C_EX[j] 
r2[j]  = k2*C_D[j]*C_E[j] - kr2*C_M[j]*C_EX[j] 
r3[j]  = k3*C_M[j]*C_E[j] - kr3*C_G[j]*C_EX[j] 
r4[j]  = k4*C_W[j]*C_EX[j] - kr4*C_F[j]*C_E[j] 
 
# ------------------------------------------------ CSTR [j] -----------------------------------------------# 
 
dC_T[j] = -r1[j] + Q/V * (C_T[j-1] - C_T[j]) 
dC_D[j] = r1[j] - r2[j] + Q/V * (C_D[j-1] - C_D[j]) 
dC_M[j] = r2[j] - r3[j] + Q/V * (C_M[j-1] - C_M[j]) 
dC_F[j] = r4[j] + Q/V * (C_F[j-1] - C_F[j]) 
dC_W[j] = -r4[j] + Q/V * (C_W[j-1] - C_W[j]) 
dC_G[j] = r3[j] + Q/V * (C_G[j-1] - C_G[j]) 
dC_E[j] = r4[j] - r1[j] - r2[j] - r3[j] + Q/V * (C_E[j-1] - C_E[j]) 
dC_EX[j] = r1[j] + r2[j] + r3[j] - r4[j] + Q/V * (C_EX[j-1] - C_EX[j]) 
 
# --------------------------------------- Mass % in the oil phase -----------------------------------------# 
 
w_T[j] = V*C_T[j]*MW_T 
w_D[j] = V*C_D[j]*MW_D 
w_M[j] = V*C_M[j]*MW_M 
w_F[j] = V*C_F[j]*MW_F 
 
wp_T[j] = w_T[j]/(w_T[j]+w_D[j]+w_M[j]+w_F[j])*100 
wp_D[j] = w_D[j]/(w_T[j]+w_D[j]+w_M[j]+w_F[j])*100 
wp_M[j] = w_M[j]/(w_T[j]+w_D[j]+w_M[j]+w_F[j])*100 
wp_F[j] = w_F[j]/(w_T[j]+w_D[j]+w_M[j]+w_F[j])*100 
 
# --------------------------------------- Mass % in the water phase ---------------------------------------#
