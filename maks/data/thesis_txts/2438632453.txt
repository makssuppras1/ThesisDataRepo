Abstract 
The origin of this project stems from a course at DTU in conjunction with the hearing aid company Widex. 
The company, with headquarters in Lynge, has the third highest market share in Denmark and strives to 
provide customers with the best hearing aids making the most advanced and natural sound. Their goal is 
to pursue a continuous innovation by investing in R&D. This course focused on the development of a 
design which incorporates the functionality of a push button for an ITC hearing aid into the battery drawer 
component of the hearing aid. With the button currently located to the left of the battery drawer, a button 
integrated solution would lead to a reduction in size. The motivation behind this design task was to save 
space within the hearing aid while still maintaining functionality. Reasons for this are two-fold; firstly, 
there is cost-saving demands in creating ever compact, multifunctional electronic devices. This currently 
is one of the main drivers of innovation within the industry. Secondly, there is customer demand for 
smaller, more discreet products. With piezoelectric materials, components can be reduced and scaled 
down to one stationary component, opposed to a moving button mechanism which is currently used. This 
proposal of piezoelectric push button was chosen as the most favourable proposed solution within the 
course by the representatives of Widex, and it offers great potential for innovation if successful.  
Two distinct methods of incorporating a piezoelectric push button into the battery drawer component 
were considered. The first proposal was based on the injection moulding of a small piezoelectric element. 
The element could either be fabricated using films that are over-moulded into the battery drawer, or 
formed from piezoelectric feedstock powder as part of a double-shot moulding process. The second 
method involved the integration of an off-the-shelf or outsourced piezoelectric element being 
incorporated into a battery drawer component. The first proposal was more favourable as creating this 
integrated piezoelectric button functionality from one molding process would facilitate a cost-effective 
method for mass production. It would also negate the reliance on external suppliers.

Introduction 
This research project will analyse the potential commercial viability of developing a push button for 
hearing aid applications utilising piezoelectric elements. Different methods of fabricating these 
piezoelectric elements will be explored, with the initial ultimate objective of creating a functional 
prototype device. At the outset, the proposed aim was to fabricate samples made from a piezoelectric 
material using thermal powder pressing and micro injection molding. The inspiration for this research was 
to establish a cost effective method of mass producing push button functionality into a battery drawer 
housing component, subsequently saving space within an ITC hearing aid. Given that piezoelectric 
components of the scale required are currently commercially available, the secondary aim of this research 
was to analyse how these components could be integrated into an ITC battery drawer housing. This 
research will examine and summarise current relevant research and established technologies that have 
successfully produced piezoelectric materials for similar applications. The potential of micro-injection 
molding for piezoelectric materials will be discussed, and the development and experimentation process 
of a piezoelectric push button integrated into an ITC battery drawer will be described.   
This initial aim of developing sample push buttons from micro-injection moulded piezoelectric material 
was to pave the way for incorporating this technology into a multi-shot injection moulding operation. This 
process would mould the active piezoelectric material for the button on top of the moulded polymer 
which forms the rest of the battery drawer component. This is a process already implemented for the 
production of micro components by companies such as Makuta (Makuta Micro Molding, u.d.). Injection 
moulding is already used by hearing aid companies such as Widex to produce polymer components. If a 
button could be injection moulded in a second moulding operation, then it could be incorporated into the 
already existing process chain, reducing the number of operations required to install a standard push 
button into the limited space available in the battery drawer. An injection moulded push button would be 
theoretically simpler to design given there would be fewer geometrical constraints and more room 
available to work with. Injection moulding is a much more cost-effective method of producing 
piezoelectric materials than other conventional methods such as tape casting. It has a much quicker 
turnover and is much more conducive to mass production. In addition, injection moulded samples found 
in the literature often have improved homogeneity of microstructure. There are a number of materials 
suitable to this process, most commonly used are PZT (Lead Zirconate Titanate), PVDF (Polyvinylidene 
fluoride), P(VDF-TrFE) a co-polymer of PVDF, and Barium Titanate.  
Injection moulding of piezoelectric materials is a hybrid process incorporating steps from more 
conventional powder injection moulding steps. Currently, there are several methods of fabrication. One 
method utilizes thin films over-moulded into a passive polymer. Other methods combine a piezoelectric 
powder into a feedstock material with a binder which follows more conventional ceramic and powder 
injection moulding techniques. Due to the very high costs of initial tooling, this is a process which is more 
suited to low-cost, high-volume products with high throughput.  
A state of the art review on research into compact micro piezoelectric push buttons and similar 
technology, unsurprisingly revealed that there are no current feasible prototypes of such a devices. There 
are none that could feasibly be retrofitted onto an ITC battery drawer component and there are none 
currently used within the hearing industry. There are also no current patents on micro piezoelectric push

buttons used for this application. It is worth noting that the initiation of innovative progress in 
piezoelectricity is largely material based and not facilitated by market pull (Piezoeletricity: Evolution and 
Future of a Technology) (W. Wersing, 2008). It is difficult for a novel technology to succeed in a given 
application if it fails to deliver three advantages. With a natural tendency of inertia for existing solutions 
to survive, it is no wonder that the high costs of R&D, even just considering raw materials, have prevented 
further prototype development from progressing.  
A lack of processing capabilities available at DTU, specifically for binding and sintering steps, combined 
with the high cost of raw materials in both film and powder form, prevented the fabrication of moulded 
piezoelectric samples being developed. Whilst a prototype design of a battery drawer component that 
could potentially fit a mounted piezoelectric element was developed and 3D printed, development of a 
fully functional prototype ITC battery drawer with integrated push button functionality proved 
unsuccessful. This can be attributed mainly to the brittleness of piezoceramics fabricated at the required 
scale, and inadequate electrical properties of the procured piezoceramic elements when applying the 
direct piezoelectric effect. While neither of these aims could be fully realised throughout the duration of 
the project, successful development of these technologies is certainly not impossible. Although a first 
iteration prototype device using a supplied piezoceramics element could not prove functional, the report 
identifies aspects that can be modified and optimised for successful development of this device. With 
further R&D resources employed, there is potential to develop prototypes made from layered PVDF-TrFE 
films or stacked multilayer piezoceramic, which offer improved piezoelectric and mechanical properties.  
Aims 
Upon completion of this project, an analysis of the feasibility of fabricating an injection moulded 
piezoelectric push button for ITC hearing aid applications will be established. If feasible, samples should 
be produced and tested, with this technique optimised. Another aim of the project is to assess the 
feasibility of utilising an outsourced piezoelectric element as a push button integrated into an ITC hearing 
aid battery drawer. The ultimate objective is that this will culminate in a functional prototype of this 
component.  
Background 
Piezoelectric Materials 
Applications 
It is well known that piezoelectric materials have been used extensively for transducer and sensor 
technology, notably in the communication, defense and medical industries. In the hearing aid industry 
specifically, they have been used for transducers (Higuchi Lab, u.d.), speakers (Gustavo Corrêa Martins, 
2014) and even been researched for energy harvesting applications (Voix, 2014), where ear canal dynamic 
motion is transferred to power from a polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) based piezoelectric film . 
Piezoelectric materials are found in a plethora of application-specific shapes: plates, disks, rods, tubes and 
foils. These elements are purpose designed for static, dynamic or resonant applications. These materials 
however have not been used for more simple applications such as push buttons. Currently there are no 
available patents for these micro-scaled push buttons proposed in this report that are applied to hearing 
aid components. The design of the developed prototype button outlined in this report drew inspiration 
from this finger-powered wireless piezo switch (Bush, 2010), which uses a similar concept.

The Piezoelectric Effect 
The functional cornerstone of this proposed design lies in the application of the piezoelectric effect; 
specifically, the direct piezoelectric effect. This phenomenon occurs in certain crystalline materials with 
no inversion symmetry, mostly ceramics. The effect can be described as the linear electromechanical 
interaction between the electrical and mechanical states of these materials. They have a unique 
characteristic that when a mechanical strain is applied, a corresponding electric field is produced. When 
compressed or deflected, causing mechanical strain, the material’s molecular structure aligns itself such 
that a momentary potential difference develops across it, creating a current. This is known as the direct 
piezoelectric effect. This process is reversible, meaning that when a voltage of the same magnitude is 
applied across the crystal, the material will deflect/compress by a predictable value of strain. This is called 
the indirect or converse piezoelectric effect. The direct effect is the basis for many sensing devices and 
applications, and thus the phenomena lends itself well to push button applications.  
In piezoceramics, the effect originates from the perovskite crystal structure with individual structures 
composed of a smaller tetravalent metal ion boxed within a divalent metal ion and O2 lattice. It is the 
predetermined asymmetry which facilitates the effect, and this usually only exists below the Curie 
temperature of the material. Below this temperature, each crystal has an associated dipole moment, and 
local adjoining regions of these dipole alignments are called domains. The direction of polarisation of 
neighbouring domains is random, and so there is no net polarisation. To achieve this polarisation, a strong 
DC electric field is applied across the element, usually at slightly below the Curie temperature. During this 
process, commonly referred to as ‘poling’, domains closely aligned to the field expand at the expense of 
domains that are misaligned with the field, and the element expands in the field’s direction. The electric 
field is then removed, and the dipoles are then mostly locked in a nearly aligned configuration. The 
element is now permanently polarised. The increase in length after this process is normally within the 
micrometer range. This process is outlined below (Piezoelectricity: Evolution and Future of a Technology; 
L.E Cross, W Heywang, A Ballato)) (L.E Cross, 2008).   
Other piezoelectric materials developed for experimentation are, Barium Titanate (BaTiO3), Lithium 
Niobate (LiNbO3). These materials however are limited in their piezoelectric and ferroelectric properties 
and thus are seldom commercially available. PVDF is an example of a ferroelectric piezoelectric polymer. 
It has however much lower ferroelectric properties than PZT.

It is this poling process above which is responsible for the effect taking place. Mechanical compressive or 
tensile strain on the element leads to a change in the associated dipole moment. Compression along or 
tension perpendicular to the polarising direction generates a voltage with the same polarity as this poling 
voltage. Vice versa, tension along or compression perpendicular to this direction of polarisation creates a 
voltage with the opposite polarity to the poling voltage. When a piezoelectric element is being operated 
in this mode, it effectively works as a sensor, converting mechanical energy into electrical energy. Values 
of compressive stress, voltage or field strength generated by the applied stress are linearly proportional 
up to a certain stress value. This point is dependent on the material properties. This is also the case for an 
applied voltage and a generated strain.  
If a voltage with the same polarity and direction as the poling voltage is applied to a piezoceramics, the 
element will lengthen and its diameter will decrease. If a voltage is applied which is of opposite polarity 
to the poling voltage, the element will shorten and its diameter will increase. If an alternating voltage is 
applied, the element will expand and contract in a cyclic manner, at the same frequency of this applied 
voltage. When the element is operated in this mode, it acts as an actuator converting electrical energy to 
mechanical energy.  
The representation of piezoelectric materials can be described using the constitutive equations based on 
the widely accepted IEEE standards. It is assumed that the behaviour of piezo materials is linear, which is 
a good assumption at low electric fields and mechanical stresses. However, if operated under high electric 
fields and mechanical stresses, considerable non-linearity occurs, and this can lead to depolarisation. In a 
depolarised ceramic specimen, all the domain walls are oriented randomly. This often occurs at stresses 
at 20-30 % of the yield stress of the material, long before plastic deformation or fracture will occur (Piezo 
Design: Forces & Stiffness in Piezoelectric Actuation) (PI Ceramic, 2012). The constitutive equations 
assume that the total strain in the transducer is the sum of the mechanical stress and the controllable 
actuation strain caused by the applied electric voltage. The axes of the elements are denoted by letters 
with 1 referring to the x axis, 2 referring to the y axis and 3 corresponding to the z axis. Axis 3 signifies the 
direction of initial polarisation of the piezoceramics, while axes 1 and 2 lie in the perpendicular plane. This 
is demonstrated best below in figure 2.

Relative Dielectric Constant KT 
The dielectric constant is one of the defining parameters for characterizing piezoelectric behaviour. It 
represents the extent to which a material enables electric flux through it. It is described as the ratio of the 
permittivity of the substance to the permittivity of free space. A material with a higher dielectric constant 
will permit more electric flux. This phenomenon allows materials to hold their electric charge for a certain 
period. The ‘ij’ subscript signifies the direction of the electric field applied or that charge is collected in

Piezo Element as a Push button 
The design incorporates a small piezoelectric element which, when deflected by the user, creates small 
voltages which can be detected as a signal passing through the chip. The voltage of the signal varies with 
the applied force or pressure, so higher applied forces result in higher voltages produced. This enables 
the button to function as a switch for multiple programs or settings, as signals of varying time or voltage 
can be attributed to different functions. The advantages of using a piezoelectric element, is that it can be 
made thinner and thereby take up a small amount of space in the battery drawer compared to a 
mechanical pushbutton. Piezoelectric switches do not have any moving parts and thus provides the same 
functionality with fewer parts. Given it is stationary, this element should therefore be less prone to ware 
and more resistant to cyclic loading than conventional mechanical switches. The lifetime of piezoelectric

switches is therefore longer than that of mechanical switches and can typically be used for tens of millions

Fabrication of Piezoelectric Materials 
Conventional methods for forming piezoelectric materials consist of tape casting, and powder pressing. 
The former is usually used to produce multilayer piezo components, made from stacked elements, and 
sometimes thin films, which are often made from polyvinylidene fluoride PVDF (Piezoelectric Tape Cast 
Materials) (CTS Corporation, 2016). These multilayered devices are best suited to actuator applications 
due to the lower levels of voltage required for large deflections. The latter technique is normally used to 
fabricate monolayer piezoceramics, devices with one layer of active piezoelectric and electrodes on 
opposing side, and these are best employed as sensors (Thin Film Technologies for Manufacturing 
Piezoelectric Materials) (Wasa, 2010).  
Preparing piezoelectric ceramics using powder pressing consists of mixing specific portions of component 
metal oxides in a fine powder form. To form a uniform powder, the mixture is heated and then mixed with 
an organic binder where it is formed into more specific shapes. These elements are then heated for a 
period at a specific temperature, sintering the particles to from a dense polycrystalline ceramic structure. 
Once the elements are cooled they can be cut, trimmed and electroded (Fundamentals of Piezoelectricity) 
(Fleming, 2006).    
In the literature, there are two distinct methods of producing piezoelectric components with injection 
moulding techniques. The most prominent method involves a powder injection moulding process with 
much of the same elements as conventional methods for powder injection moulding ceramics, metals and 
composites. There is a limited selection of piezoelectric materials available in powder form as raw material 
that are processable and have been studied. These consist mainly of ceramics, including Barium Titanate 
and PZT, with some studies involve the doping of PVDF and P(VDF-TrFE).  
Another method employed is the back injection of piezoelectric films or fibres within an injection moulded 
polymer matrix. This method is more common in the processing of PVDF based components for 
components in sensor, actuator or MEMS based applications, such as multilayered actuators.  
Direct powder injection moulding techniques 
The injection moulding process of piezoceramics shares much in common with PIM and CIM techniques. 
The steps consist of feedstock preparation, injection moulding, debinding and sintering. Like other powder 
technologies, the key step in the process involves choosing the appropriate powder for moulding. The 
properties of the feedstock are determined by the specific surface area, particle shape and size, size 
distribution, and powder purity. As with conventional PIM, the feedstock preparation involves the 
materials in powder form being mixed with a binder material before being moulded. A high concentration 
of piezoceramics powder is mixed with a thermoplastic binder to control the viscosity of the feedstock. It 
is necessary that the feedstock is homogenous and free of agglomerates. In conventional powder 
metallurgy method, further machining processing steps followed by cleaning are required to achieve the 
desired shape of the final part. With complex shapes, cutting and machining of piezoceramics is often 
time consuming. In addition, there are further costs in relation to the die and material wastage.  
The injection moulding process for piezoelectric components utilises similar equipment to that used for 
polymer injection moulding, involving concurrent heating and pressurization. Close monitoring and 
control of the process is essential to minimise the possibility of defects. The moulding process does not 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
result in a finished product. Instead, a green body is produced. The debinding step is then required to 
remove the binder material. This occurs in two distinct steps. Immersion removes the soluble component 
of the binder, and facilitates the development of pore channels which allow removal of the remaining part 
of the binder. Thermal debinding of the insoluble component follows this and results in the brown body. 
After the debinding process, sintering of the brown body is needed. Sintering parameters are dependent 
on the electrical properties and the type of the powder ceramic used. Weighting and visual inspection are 
common methods of quality control during the green, brown and sintered states. This step is key to 
identify any surface cracks, pores, voids, impurities, distortions skin marks and incomplete parts (Ceramic 
Injection Molding; Zdravko Stanimirović and Ivanka Stanimirović) (Stanimirović, 2012).  
Utilising CIM methodology for piezoceramics eliminates additional the machining and cleaning steps in 
conventional methods. Furthermore, CIM produced components can have much more complex shapes 
with more homogenous microstructure. Recycling of feedstock material can also further contribute to 
cost reduction. 
I. Stanimirović and Z. Stanimirović successfully produced piezoelectric ceramic samples made from 
PbZrTiO3 and Barium Titanate (BaTiO3), which are both commercially available, using ceramic injection 
moulding (CIM). Conventional methods for forming these piezoelectric components uses powder 
metallurgy to press the parts into dense ceramics with good strength. Subsequent machining to shape, 
electroding and poling operations are then required. The fabrication process used in this study 
incorporates traditional powder pressing methods with polymer injection molding and consists of 4 
distinct steps: feedstock preparation, ceramic injection molding, debinding and sintering.  
To prepare the feedstock, these two materials were combined with a wax-based binder. A feedstock 
containing 10.5% binder material. The binder was composed of 9% was, 1% low melting temperature wax 
and 1% stabilizer. This binder to powder ratio is commonplace among piezoceramics powder injection 
moulding processes. This feedstock is then transformed into a melt which can then be injected into the 
mould cavity where it cools to form the desired shape.  
After molding, the debinding step is two-staged, with solvent debinding followed by thermal debinding. 
This involved gradual heating to prevent thermally induced defects such as micro-cracks, slumping and 
blistering. The parts are then sintered at atmospheric temperature and in the presence of a lead source 
to minimise lead loss from PZT that occurs around 800oC. The dielectric constant of piezoceramics is 
heavily dependent on the sintering temperature. With conventional powder metallurgy, the dielectric 
constant increases with sintering temperature because of the increase in PZT grains. With CIM process, 
the dielectric constant also increases with sintering temperature, but until 1250oC where it starts to 
decrease due to the decrease in density as a result of lead loss. Thus microstructure development and 
grain size changes vary with sintering temperature.  
After the sintering process, silver pated electrodes were applied to the PZT samples using screen printing 
and firing in air. Polarisation and electrical characterisation of the specimens was the final stage of 
fabrication. The samples produced achieved the necessary dielectric and piezoelectric properties required 
which were reflective of that found in literature. They had an improved microstructure homogeneity and 
had lower production costs due to the elimination of the machining step and the use of recycling 
feedstock.

The direct powder injection moulding processes are more well-known and studied in the literature, and 
are more suited to the production of a monolayer piezoelectric element. Though careful control of process 
parameters is required for optimisation, the process involves fewer steps and is less complicated than 
that of back injecting thin films and fibres. However, both procedures could be viable options for 
producing a micro-scaled piezoelectric push button integrated into an ITC hearing aid battery drawer. 
With P(VDF-TrFE) being a more suitable material due to its higher voltage coefficient and better 
mechanical properties; specifically high mechanical strength and good flexibility. As with piezoelectric 
based powders, commercially available PVDF based films are too expensive to carry out this practical

The PVDF copolymer PVDF-TrFE has a superior g33 constant to all other piezoelectric materials. This was 
the first choice of material to experiment with creating samples using moulding techniques similar to this 
research (Manufacturing of Poly(vinylidene fluoride) and Evaluation of its Mechanical Properties) (Esterly, 
2002). Although the lower d33 constant means that less charge will be deposited on the electrodes of the

The procedures outlined by I. Stanimirović & Z. Stanimirović and Bowen & French could potentially be

The ultimate aim was to develop PVDF-TrFE samples using hot press and injection moulding techniques 
that can ultimately be incorporated into an ITC hearing aid battery drawer. The samples would be 
subsequently poled and tested for piezoelectric characteristics. Optimisation of the moulding stage would 
be required to achieve beta-phase crystallinity. The important properties that would be measured are the 
piezoelectric strain constant (d33), piezoelectric stress constant (g33), and dielectric constant (KT).  
However, without binder materials or capabilities for preparing these samples from powder based 
piezoelectric materials available, it was not possible to follow methods of composite injection moulding

the literature on the processing steps, inducing piezoelectric properties and experimental results from 
studies. Furthermore, the high cost of raw materials meant this approach could not be further examined. 
With no option to produce samples from moulding techniques, it was then decided that further research 
would be channeled into developing a prototype battery drawer with an outsourced piezoelectric push

This section will provide an overview of the materials which were initially considered for injection

Piezoelectric Materials 
As discussed previously, there are a number of piezoelectric materials studied in the literature that are 
suitable for injection moulding processes. Out of these, the most common are PZT and PVDF. These are 
the two materials that were considered more in detail for this application due to their higher piezoelectric 
properties.  
Lead Zirconate Titanate (PZT) 
Lead Zirconate Titanate (PZT) is the most widely used piezoelectric ceramic for monolayer elements, 
mainly in actuator and sensing technology. It has good pyroelectric and piezoelectric properties, which 
make it one of the most widely used and effective material for piezoelectric elements across industries 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
such as aerospace, automotive and medical. A fracture toughness of around 1 MPa.m1/2 is commonly 
quoted in literature, although there is considerable variation (Fracture toughness anisotropy of PZT: J.M 
Calderon-Moreno, F Guiu, M Meredith, M.J Reece) (Moreno, 1997). It is available as fine powder and 
pellets for feedstock, as well as ceramic elements. One of the material’s most limiting factors is its 
brittleness. With the demand for electronics to be reduced in size and volume, compacting components 
with higher functionality density, there remains a difficulty to scale down these components without 
reducing mechanical strength of the elements due to defects such as cracks, pores and inclusions. This 
warrants piezo elements of a certain size unreliable. Injection moulding of these materials in a polymer 
matric improves the processability and piezoelectric properties. Piezo composites with polymer-matrix 
fillers offer improved electrical insulation, high dielectric breakdown strength, lower brittleness and 
improved manufacturability of more complex shapes (Depolarization field effect on dielectric and 
piezoelectric properties of particulate ferroelectric ceramic-polymer composites) (Ma, 2015).   
Polyvinylidene Fluoride (PVDF) 
Polyvinylidene Fluoride (PVDF) is a fluoropolymer and it has the best piezoelectric properties out of the 
polymers that show the effect, with a high dielectric constant. The material can be fabricated with high 
crystallinity of up to 65% when it is of low molecular weight. A higher crystallinity in PVDF is directly 
impacting on the material’s degree of piezoelectric activity (Piezoelectricity and pyroelectricity in 
polyvinylidene fluoride - A model, M. G. Broadhurst, G. T. Davis, J. E. McKinney, and R. E. Collins) 
(Broadhurst, 1978). Its mechanical properties are also remarkably high for a fluorinated thermoplastic. It 
has good impact strength, the highest heat deflection temperature, flexural strength and modulus. The 
material’s yield strength and creep resistance is also high for fluoropolymers that are injection mouldable 
(Polymer Technology Dictionary) (Whelan, 1994). PVDF generally has a higher fracture toughness than 
PZT at around 2 MPa.m1/2 although this value for PZT varies in the literature. While the voltage constant 
g33 of PVDF is not as high as the value for its co-polymer PVDF-TrFE, the value found in literature is 
generally higher than that of PZT. Moreover, the material’s flexible nature means it could be a more 
suitable material for providing better tactile feedback to the user, which is an important feature for 
customers. Both polymers are available as fine powder, pellets and in thin films (PVDF piezoelectric 
voltage coefficient in situ measurements as a function of applied stress) (Gusarov, 2015).

Design and Development 
Design Constraints 
The CAD models of the original battery drawer component and the faceplate, the latter of which is shown 
in figure 4, were provided by the company at the start of the course. These identified the constrained 
dimensions to be kept unchanged during the whole project. The part is required to be durable within its 
environment and take up as little space within the hearing aid as possible. The device must not interfere 
with the conductive plates on the faceplate on either side of the battery, and must connect to a conductive 
plate/chip on the baseplate by the rear of the battery drawer. The part should be adequately tactile to 
facilitate user friendliness. Since a high percentage of users are likely to be over seventy and thus likely to 
have low fingers sensitivity, the final solution should enable simple battery changing, approximately 
weekly, without an extra tool. Interchangeability of the product with a battery drawer without push 
button functionality is a key design target. Another design aim is to keep the part as discreet as possible.

Piezoelectric Element 
Given that push button functionality not a common application for piezoelectric elements, there was little 
information in the literature on designing materials for this application. This meant it was difficult to 
determine which material parameters of the device were the most important to consider for this 
application.  
The pure function of the piezoelectric element was one of the first aspects to be determined upon 
consideration of a device as push button incorporated into an ITC hearing aid component. The element is 
the most basic form of sensor, which sends a signal to the circuit indicating a change in setting once it is 
pressed. The governing phenomena at play is the direct piezoelectric effect, as described in the abstract. 
A monolayer piezoelectric element for the button functionality was first considered as opposed to a 
multilayer device. The most common and well-established material for these single layer parts is PZT, due 
to its superior piezoelectric properties such as high dielectric constant and coupling factors. Moreover, 
other piezoelectric elements that could potentially be suited to this are difficult to source commercially. 
Due to the shape of the battery drawer and the geometry of the top surface, which offered the largest 
space to work with, a rectangular element was chosen as the most desirable. This geometry enabled the 
area available for the piezoelectric element to be optimised. For the element to tactile, it must provide 
sufficient area for pressing, but while maintaining the required voltage output for applied force. There is 
a trade-off here between tactility and performance. Increasing area to make the element more tactile also 
decreases the voltage output per applied force as seen in equation 14.

Using the von Mises stress criterion gives a stress of 45883 Pa in 2D and 44330 Pa in 3D. Compared to the 
yield stress of PZT in the literature, these principle stresses are extremely low. This however is a yield 
criterion better suited to ductile materials rather than brittle materials. It is more likely that the mode of 
failure is not driven by yield strength to principle stresses, but by fracture toughness. Failure mode for 
ceramics is very dependent on both intrinsic and extrinsic material factors. It is difficult to obtain a simple 
failure stress due to the micro and nano scale cracks that are often distributed stochastically throughout 
==PAGE:29==
21 
 
the specimen. These cracks contain stress concentrations which are much higher than the principle 
stresses the material experiences on a macroscopic level. To determine the size, distribution, frequency 
and location of these cracks and pores, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) should be used (Investigation 
of Partial Discharge and Fracture Strength in Piezoelectric Ceramics) (Hang, 2014).  
The performance of piezoceramics is limited due to their proneness to cracking, which has impeded their 
use in high power applications. (Piezoelectricity: Evolution and Future of a Technology; L.E Cross, W 
Heywang, A Ballato) (L.E Cross, 2008) The problem of cracking near the elctrodes of PZT specimens is a 
known phenomenon, highlighted in Designing Cofired Multilayer Electrostrictive Actuators for Reliability 
by Winzer,  Shankar and Ritter (Winzer, 1989) and Effects of electric field on the fracture toughness (Lynch, 
2013). Stress concentrations of high magnitudes can develop at the tip of these cracks. The tensile 
strength for brittle materials is a function of many parameters, including size, which are both intrinsic and 
extrinsic to the material (Advances in Electronic Ceramics II) (Priya, 2009).    
There is an innate link between the microstructure of a material and its ability to resist fatigue. Fatigue in 
ferroelectric materials, mechanical or electrical in origin, causes microscopic material modifications. Both 
mechanisms can yield similar or even the same changes due to the electromechanical coupling. Reduction 
in the domain switchability and the development of internal fields and offsets are the major defects that 
arise from fatigue. Defects of large enough size can be sufficient to alter the dielectric constant of the 
material. Even larger defect sizes can prevent polarization within a certain volume. During fatigue, a 
cluster of defects begin to emerge. These clusters most commonly occur in the form of oxygen vacancies 
or as defects at grain boundaries, domain walls, microcracks or electrode contacts.   
In cracked PZT specimens, the ferroelectric domain switching is caused by high stresses in the vicinity of 
the crack tip, within a finite zone ahead of the crack front. Whenever this zone is penetrated by the crack, 
toughening takes place in the area behind the crack tip. Compressive stresses then close the crack and 
effectively shield the tip from the external load. Some attempts to quantify the fracture behaviour of PZT 
experimentally have been made. A few focus on the distribution of shielding stress after crack growth, 
others involve measuring the domain switching zone using atomic force microscopy, interference 
contrast, and X-ray diffraction.  
More investigation is required to determine the fracture toughness of these PZT samples. Although the 
forces involved in Vickers hardness testing would likely be too high to avoid complete fracture for these 
samples. Fracture toughness measurements using the Vickers method involves indenting the material on 
a surface that lies normal to the direction of polarisation (New considerations about the fracture mode of 
PZT ceramics) (Guillon, u.d.). Measuring the dimensions of a crack notch, in tandem with SEM micrographs 
to determine the size, distribution and shape of existing pores, micro cracks and impurities at grain 
boundaries, of a known applied force, enables the hardness to be quantified. These measurements are 
then used along with the young’s modulus of the material to calculate its fracture toughness. In depth 
experiments to determine the fracture behaviour of PZT is described in Fracture toughness anisotropy of 
PZT (Moreno, 1997), Effects of electric field on the fracture toughness (KIc) of ceramic PZT (Lynch, 2013) 
and PZT based piezoelectric ceramics with enhanced fracture toughness (Jing Si Yang, 1998).   
In polarised ferroelectric ferroelastic materials, a significant change in electric potential on the material’s 
surface accompanies the domain switching mechanism. When domain switching takes place, it alters the 
local poling condition and thus the macroscopic piezoelectric coefficients of the specimen. The relation 
==PAGE:30==
22 
 
between domain switching and fracture toughness of ferroelectrics, including PZT, under applied electric 
and elastic fields still requires further exploration.   
Fracture toughness Kic is the governing failure mode for ceramics. Failure happens when the stress 
intensity factor Ki of the most prominent crack within the component exceeds the fracture toughness. 
The fracture toughness of ideally brittle materials is identical to the stress intensity factor that causes the 
onset of stable crack growth but is independent of the crack extension.  
To improve the design of the piezoelectric element, the thickness can be increased to 0.5mm, which is a 
standard thickness offered by Piezo systems. Another option is to introduce a brass shim and use a stacked 
two layer device in which the shim is sandwiched between two 0.191 or 0. 267 mm elements secured with 
adhesive. These are both standards thickness of plates and sheets offered by Piezo Systems. This supplier 
also offers two layer transducers with a brass shim between with thicknesses of 0.51 mm and 0.66 mm 
(Piezo Systems: Piezoelectric brass bending sensors) (Piezo Systems Ltd, u.d.). They recommend use of a 
shim as one option in strengthening these brittle elements (Piezo Systems: Technical/FAQs) (Piezo 
Systems Ltd, u.d.). Both of these solutions were modelled in ANSYS to estimate the stress in the element 
under loading conditions.  
One other option for a piezoelectric element is using a thin PVDF or PVDF-TrFE film with metallised 
electrodes. This was considered initially, but these films are expensive and elements this size are not 
commercially available in off-the-shelf formats. For a film 40 microns thick and with the same area as the 
element currently used, voltage output calculated from 1 N of applied load is calculated to be 0.3 V, using

Flexible PCB 
To connect the piezoelectric element to the chip and the rest of the circuit, a variety of options were 
deliberated on. First of all, thin wires similar to that supplied by Piezo Systems were considered. The 
limitations with wires is that they can become easily entangled, especially with the hinge. Another 
problem is nesting them neatly within the battery drawer to protect them. On top of this, it could be 
difficult to incorporate a modular battery drawer sub assembly that can be easily detached from the hinge 
and replaced with a regular battery drawer component. This was one of the aims of the final design. Wires 
would need to be soldered, or at best soldered to a plate that is connected to the battery drawer 
component. This requires additional and unnecessary steps in the manufacturing process. A conductive 
insert that is soldered onto the piezo element and slides into the battery drawer was also considered. This 
was deemed to be difficult to achieve given the limited space available. The third option considered was 
electroplating the back or side walls of the battery drawer to provide electrical connectivity from the 
element to the rear side of the component. The capabilities of doing this on the surface of the component, 
along with the limited space available, and subsequently securing the electrical connections to the 
elctrodes of the piezo element brought about more difficult problems to overcome. The best solution thus 
seemed to be a flexible printed circuit board which the piezoelectric element could be soldered to, as 
eluded to in the previous section. This can be made to ultrathin thicknesses, can contain small conductive 
tracks that are protected by a thin film, can be easily incorporated into the battery drawer, passing 
underneath a slot in the hinge clip. These can be fabricated to the small dimensions required for this 
application, with a range of suppliers available for this. This was something that could not be achieved for 
a functional prototype for this course. With major trouble in establishing working relationships with 
suppliers, no prior knowledge of PCB design, and a limited window of time to obtain parts and create 
samples, this was not possible to incorporate into a physical prototype. A similar device to that seen below 
would be used. PI Ceramic Ltd (PI Ceramic, 2012) offer integration of piezo components with flexible PCBs, 
so this was viewed as a good potential solution.

FEA Simulations 
A static structural finite element analysis (FEA) was carried out using ANSYS to simulate the applied 
compressive stress on the piezo element. The element was modeled as a rectangular plate under applied 
normal force of 2 N, and was imported as a STEP file. The vacuum sputtered electrodes were assumed as 
negligible. To begin with, a coarser (low) standard mesh was used due to the simple geometry. A zero 
displacement constraint was applied to the bottom face and a normal force of 2 N was applied to the top 
face to represent the loading on the component during functional testing. Lead Zirconate Titante was not 
available as a material choice in ANSYS, so a new material with the properties available had to be added 
to the library. It must be noted however that the output of an FEA simulation is only as accurate as its 
input values. Attempts were made to obtain more comprehensive material information from the supplier, 
but they never responded despite numerous efforts. There was also little information on this value 
available in the literature. However, the Young’s modulus of PZT-5A (Navy type II) was provided. This value 
in the 3 direction is 52 GPa. An approximate Poisson’s ratio of 0.31, obtained from Boston Piezo Optics- 
Ceramic Materials (Boston Piezo Optics Inc., u.d.), was used. It was assumed that the material had 
constant isotropic elasticity.

The stacked two-layer element device with a brass shim between has a lower maximum stress during 
flexural loading of 130 MPa compared to 141 MPa. The two layer element has a slightly higher maximum 
stress of 0.97 MPa compared to 0.94 MPa under compressive loading while fully supported on the bottom 
surface.  
The flexural simulations use the structural conditions of the edges being simply supported. This loading 
simulation gives a better idea of the component’s low resistance to bending moments, which produce 
large shear stresses within the part. Under loading at 10 N, the maximum stress of 141 MPa is above the 
generally accepted value of the material’s yield strength in the literature, and will almost certainly 
fracture. That said, the loading constraints are at the extreme. The part should not be implemented as a 
beam simply supported at the very edges. This value will be reduced if the component has more support 
around the bottom surface.  
It is difficult to determine from these simulations which solution would be more durable. With some room 
to increase the thickness without making the entire component top heavy, a stacked 2 layer piezoelectric 
element with a brass shim of increased thickness may be a better solution. It can be imagined as removing 
an aspect of brittle material with a portion of stronger material. The main problem with this however is 
the use of adhesive and the electroding, which will ultimately make the fabrication of this more complex 
and more expensive. More testing should thus be carried out to determine the optimum thickness of PZT 
plate to be used.

Given that tensile, compressive or flexural strength is not the governing mode of resisting failure, the 
fracture toughness and impact strength requires more attention. This however is governed by microscopic 
phenomena which are highly variable and difficult to predict or simulate without empirical data.  
 
Characteristic Strength Testing 
Due to the physical limits imposed by the geometry and brittle nature of the piezoelectric elements, it 
wasn’t possible to conduct tensile, compressive or flexural testing. The results of tensile and compressive 
testing can vary significantly based on the test specimen and design of the loading fixture to ensure the 
loading is homogenous. No suitable test fixtures were available to the size required for any of the tests. 
Regardless of available fixtures, the clamping force necessary for compressive and tensile tests would 
most likely break the samples before testing. Additionally, to effectively test these PZT samples for tensile 
and compressive strength, samples are required to be in the form of a dumbbell shape with reduced gauge 
section. This helps to obtain the desired stress state throughout a constant cross-section. The samples in 
this research are not of that shape and are too small and brittle to be cut to this shape. This however is 
less of a problem for carrying out flexural testing. A three point bending test could be carried out using 
this flexure bend fixture (Instron, u.d.). Measurements of flexural modulus, flexural strength and flexural 
yield strength could be measured with the Instron Model 5942 or 5944 MicroTesters and along with this 
fixture. This just requires the specimen to rest on two anvils. It has a span of 3-10mm and has the ability 
to work within a temperature range of -75°C to 300°C. This fixture was not available at DTU and there was 
not enough within the budget to procure or create a fixture suitable.  
It is possible that the loading conditions on the element during operation may result in depolarisation. 
Considering that some of the elements fractured during operation, there is substance to this hypothesis. 
That said, it is difficult to ascertain whether the stresses within the parts at failure would have been within 
the 20-30 % mark of the material’s yield strength, which is a fairly simple but reliable estimation of when 
depolarisation occurs. This however is extremely material dependent and so testing would need to be 
carried out to determine if, when and to what extent depolarisation takes place under certain loading 
conditions. This is a phenomenon both dependent on the strength of electric field and the temperature 
the material is subject to.  
 
Depolarisation 
To test the samples for depolarisation, one practical method is to assess the element’s dielectric 
properties by measuring its charge constant d33. In the literature there are a number of methods outlined, 
some more direct and reliable than others. It is possible to get accurate measurements of the strain 
coefficients d31 and d33 using laser inferometry (Laser Interferometric Displacement Measurements of 
Multi-Layer Actuators and PZT Ceramics) (L. BURIANOVA, 2005). Another method is the Berlincourt 
method (Characterisation of Ferroelectric Bulk Materials and Thin Films, Markys G. Cain) (Cain, 2014) 
which uses a quasi-static d33 meter (PiezoTest d33 PiezoMeter System) (Piezotest, u.d.) to measure change 
in d33 coefficient with loading to show effect on depolarisation/degradation of samples. This technique is 
used by Jun Sae Han, Chang Woo Gal, Jae Man Park, Seong Jin Park (Jun Sae Han, 2017).  
The dielectric constant can be quantified either statically, quasi-statically or dynamically. Due to problems 
with controlling electrical boundary conditions, static tests are now rarely used for piezoelectrics. Dynamic 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
tests involve analyzing how alternating electric fields or mechanical stress being applied to the samples 
effect their performance. Pyroelectric charges can produce drift in static readings, and so dynamic and 
quasi-static methods can eliminate this error. The quasi-static method works by exciting the sample of 
known geometry and shape with small oscillations and the charge output measured is divided by the 
applied force amplitude. (Characterization of Ferroelectric Bulk Materials and Thin Films) (Cain, 2014).  
A simpler but less accurate and reliable method of roughly gauging the dielectric constant KT3 is by 
measuring its capacitance. This can be achieved by using a capacitance bridge circuit or an LCR meter. In 
this study by M. Islam, R. Seethaler and M. Alam, an LCR meter is used (Characterization of piezoelectric 
materials for simultaneous strain and temperature sensing for ultra-low frequency applications) 
(Mohammad Nouroz Islam, 2015). A similar method is also employed in Micro-powder injection moulding 
of barium titanate (Onbattuvelli, 2011). This guide describes the process of measuring the piezoelectric 
parameters, with the capacitance bridge technique being employed (Measuring Properties of 
Piezoelectric Ceramics) (SPARKLER CERAMICS PVT. LTD, u.d.). When the capacitance is measured, the 
value can be used to calculate the dielectric constant using equation 24 (Piezoelectric Transducers for 
Vibration Control & Damping: Chapter 2 - Fundamentals of Piezoelectricity; Moheimani and Fleming) 
(Fleming, 2006). The area A, thickness t and 𝜖𝜖0 is the permittivity of free space are known, where C is the

Testing and Experimentation 
Functional Testing 
The unique nature of this application meant it was difficult to fully test and characterize the performance 
of this piezoelectric element. The scarcity of available procedures and experimentation in literature led to 
difficulties in fulfilling an ideal test cascade. Nevertheless, experiments were carried out to determine the 
performance of the procured piezoelectric elements, designed to fit into an ITC hearing aid battery 
drawer. The objective was to obtain data that quantifies the relationship between an applied force and 
an applied load, with the aim of proving the component performs under customer operational conditions. 
Current output was a secondary parameter to characterise. As specified by Widex, low values should be 
detected in future products with ever improving signal processing technology.  
Before beginning any experimentation of the designed micro piezoelectric elements, the practice 1”x1” 
larger piezo elements provided in the solder and flux kit were used to optimise the attachment procedure 
of the wires to the nickel electrodes. These larger and much less representative piezo elements were used 
as a proof of concept and procedure. Upon successful attachment of the wires to the practice piezo 
element using a standard soldering iron and just a table top vise, it was realised that much more precision 
and accuracy was necessary.

the pressure applied by each finger press, the voltage was recorded for each individual light press once 
the voltage dissipated to zero. For ten sequential very light presses of the large piezo element, the voltage 
spike ranged from a minimum of 0.15V to 0.46V. Pressing the element slightly firmer led to maximum 
voltage of 1.2V for one single press. The current produced here peaked at 0.3 mA.

Once the practice piezo element was functionally tested and could be confirmed to be producing small 
amounts of voltage and current under small presses, the micro piezo elements were tested.  
 
Figure 27 - Set up showing micro piezo element held in place connected to the nodes of the multimeter.  
==PAGE:51==

Upon functional testing of the micro piezo element while it was connected to a multimeter, initial voltages 
of 0.05 to 0.1 V were detected. This is around 10 times below the minimum detectable threshold of the 
external circuit that this button system will be connected to, but is in line with the small levels of voltage 
predicated from forces of around 1 N. The multimeter was then switched to measure current, and 
although the tapping was extremely light, no current was detected. Reasons for this could be that the 
applied pressure was greater than the threshold within which the element remains polarised. Applying a 
pressure of around 20-30% the yield stress of the material causes depolarisation of the aligned dipoles of 
the molecular structure within the material, which are responsible for the piezoelectric effect (Piezo 
Design: Forces & Stiffness in Piezoelectric Actuation) (PI Ceramic, 2012). This could have contributed to 
the lack of current being measured. This is anomalous as it appears to break Ohm’s law. Thermal shock 
from the soldering, which was not successful upon the first attempt, could have caused some 
depolarization. Heating up the piezo element beyond its Curie temperature will lead to depolarisation and 
reducing the piezoelectric and dielectric performance of the element.  It is recommended that the 
soldering process takes 1-2 seconds at most to reduce the amount of heat transferred to the piezo 
element. It is known that repetitive thermal stress due to excessive soldering can cause depolarisation 
(Handling and Electrical Contacting of Piezoceramic Components - PI Ceramics) (PI Ceramic, 2012). It is 
also possible that charge leakage from conductive residual flux liquid, sweat or water vapour in the air 
could cause these discrepancies. While I have some prior experience in soldering, and ensured that extra 
care was taken during the procedure, this technique is best carried out by an expert to minimise the risk 
of permanent damage to these delicate elements. Solder size and location can also contribute to the 
damping effect. If the solder is more central and larger, it will have an increased damping effect, which 
could influence the performance of the samples (American Piezo - Connecting to PZT components) (APC 
International Ltd, 2016). Another possible reason is that the internal resistance inside the meter was too 
large, leading to the current detected being negligible. The current produced in piezo elements of this size 
under the small strains applied would be in the µA, making them difficult to measure. Although both 
scenarios are difficult to measure but both viable, it is very likely that the depolarisation threshold was 
met before the current could be measured. To try and obtain a higher value of voltage, the applied tapping 
pressure was increased, it is possible that this led to depolarisation. Upon trying to obtain a current by 
increasing the applied pressure slightly, the element fractured unexpectedly. The applied force could not 
be measured. Although some minimal voltage could be detected, depolarisation would have occurred 
before the element would have encountered stresses large enough to cause fracture.

Although the surface roughness measurements were largely carried out on the top surface of the nickel 
plated electrode, this procedure can be repeated for non-electroded samples. One common mode of 
failure in these piezoelectric elements is at the interface between the piezoelectric layer and the 
electrode. Interface failure is common due to imperfect adhesion conditions. A piezoelectric component 
with a rough surface is more likely to have micro-stresses at this location, and a rough surface is more 
likely to lead to delamination of the electrode. This failure mode is common in the literature, and 
especially with components that are prone to fatigue due to cyclic loading. The relationship between this 
requires more in depth study.

interface surface between the piezoelectric PZT layer and the electrode leads to less conductance. Thus 
there is a mandate for further investigation into the effect surface roughness has on delamination, 
piezoelectric properties, and failure modes.

Micrographs 
Perhaps the most important objective of using the Alicona microscope is to assess the degree of micro-
cracks and porosities in the samples. Micro cracks in the surface topography of the samples can lead to 
micro-stress concentrations and discontinuities that can lead to rapid crack propagation under loading. 
Micro-stress represents a homogeneous stress state distributed throughout the entire surface of the film 
and substrates (The Microscopic Origin of Residual Stress for Flat Self-Actuating Piezoelectric Cantilevers) 
(Jeong Hoon Lee, 2011). In fact, most PZT based actuators fail due to microcracks near the electrodes 
==PAGE:74==
66 
 
when they are not adequately bonded to the surface of the piezo element (Designing Cofired Multilayer 
Electrostrictive Actuators for Reliability) (Ritter, 1989). When a critical stress value is reached, a crack 
reaching the terminal will propagate, causing fracture.  The magnitude of the stress concentration is 
dependent on the shape, size and orientation of the defect involved. Larger defects with low crack tip 
radius leads to an increased stress concentration. The strength of ceramics is therefore assumed to be 
limited by the largest defect within the material. Defects on the scale of microns are often enough to 
influence performance.  
Using the Alicona, the fracture surface and the smooth unbroken edges of some piezoelectric samples 
were analysed. Images were taken at different magnifications from 5x to 50x zoom.

Discussion 
The ultimate aim of this research was to develop a functional prototype of a piezoelectric push button for 
applications in the hearing aid industry. The idea was spawned during a course at DTU in January 2018, 
and the concept was pursued after there was particular interest from Widex representatives, especially 
because it was a new approach within the industry. The technology could potentially have advantages 
over conventional buttons used, as it can integrate button functionality into the battery drawer 
component without taking up extra space. It is more compacted, consisting of one single stationary 
component, rather than a button mechanism. A piezoelectric based button also enables more control 
over the input variables. This is because the output signal is dependent on both the magnitude of the 
force length of time it is applied for. This could enable different systems to be activated with different 
button presses, such as double tapping or holding the button down, producing different signals.  
The design and development process of a piezoelectric push button for ITC hearing aid applications has 
been outlined in detail. A current state of the art review into micro injection moulding of piezoelectric 
materials showed that this was a process that could be used to feasibly produce a piezoelectric material 
with good electrical and mechanical properties, suitable for push button applications at this scale. The 
electrical output of this element at this scale should be able to match that which is required. Injection 
moulding is a process widely used by companies such as Widex. Combining the moulding of the composite 
or polymer push button into a double-shot moulding process that moulds the battery drawer component 
beforehand would facilitate a time and cost effective approach to mass manufacturing. Bottlenecks often 
arise in the process chain where manual processes are involved. Removing these sometimes tedious steps 
could rapidly reduce throughput, and thus reduce cost. The polymer PVDF, and its co-polymer PVDF-TrFE 
have been shown to be the most suitable for this process. Moreover, combining the ferroelectric and 
piezoelectric superiority of PZT with mechanical properties of PVDF and/or P(VDF-TrFE), through micro 
injection moulding techniques could overcome the shortfalls of both materials for applications in the 
micro domain. PZT is limited by its brittleness while PVDF is more easily mouldable for complex shapes, 
and provides more flexible and less brittle material properties. Merging these properties in a composite 
material could make it a viable solution to integrating push button functionality into an ITC battery drawer 
component. With the demand for sensors and transducers to push the limits on size, especially within the 
medical industry, this technology could fulfil this demand. However, samples could not be produced using 
powder injection moulding based procedures described in literature, due to budget and processing 
capabilities available. The commercial viability of this technique is also in question because of the high 
cost of raw material.  
Although a functional prototype of an ITC hearing aid battery drawer with integrated piezoelectric push 
button could not be realised, the feasibility of this technology is evident. The proposed design 
incorporates a thin piezoelectric element with a flexible PCB that slots into the battery drawer and 
connects the button to the chip. The modular design features contact connections to allow the battery 
drawer sub assembly to be removed from the rest of the hearing aid and be replaced with another battery 
drawer. This was one of the main design objective in developing this concept.  
==PAGE:82==
74 
 
Solutions to some crucial problems faced during the design process have been realised; a piezoelectric 
element of this size can produce a signal with the required voltage output at reasonable pressing forces 
(just over 5 N for the output target of 0.85 V), within the general range accepted in literature. A 
piezoelectric element of this scale can fit into an ITC battery drawer component without excessive changes 
to its shape and without compromising the overall volume of the hearing aid. The mechanical durability 
and strength of the PZT element, in particular its brittleness at ultra-thin thicknesses, are critical issues 
that prevent its usability in its current form for this application. Steps to better understand of the failure 
mode of this element have been outlined and have been applied to improve the design. The element in 
question is 0.127 mm thick for context, which is the thinnest possible thickness of PZT element offered by 
Piezo Systems. Designs have been proposed with elements of increased thickness, and of a 2-layer 
element with a strengthening shim, that could offer potential solutions, without detriment to the 
electrical output or available space. Importantly, a procedure to follow for designing compacted 
piezoelectric push buttons, that could have applications across the micro domain, has been established. 
This is an application that has been scarcely studied within the industry, and so as a preliminary study, 
there are some promising possibilities. However, to be implemented successfully requires a more in-depth 
R&D approach. With limited budget, capabilities and timeframes, a PZT element was the only proposal 
that could be studied within the scope of this project.  
A better understanding of the failure mode of brittle PZT piezoelectric elements is required to optimise 
the design of these elements for this application. The stresses experienced during testing in this project 
could have developed micro-stresses within the material’s microstructure high enough to cause some 
depolarisation of the dipoles responsible for the piezoelectric effect. The brittle nature means that 
microscopic behaviour within the material’s microstructure, particularly the presence of micro-cracks and 
pores near electrode surfaces, have an influence on the materials macroscopic properties. This makes 
simulating and predicting the material’s behaviour under different loading conditions more complex. 
Thus, a more thorough analysis of the materials under these loading conditions is required to be better 
equipped at designing these elements. This should consist of characterizing the piezoelectric response to 
applied forces, as well as determining the element’s operational limitations from depolarisation and 
characteristic strength. As outlined in this research, this should include tensile, compressive, flexural, and 
fracture toughness testing. A more thorough study on the influence of the element’s microstructure to its 
macroscopic behaviour is required. A relationship should be established between dielectric 
measurements and applied stress or loads to understand the element’s functional polarisation range.  
As described in this research, potential solutions could also lie in utilising PVDF based films, which may 
offer improved mechanical performance, particularly with impact strength and flexibility. These films have 
also been successfully over-moulded into injection moulded base materials, as described in the ‘Back 
injection of piezoelectric films into polymer structures’ section in the Background. Combined with higher 
voltage coefficients, meaning a higher output voltage per applied force, there are good prospects of this 
being a successful avenue for further research. A PVDF-based solution could potentially be a better 
candidate than traditional sensor elements based on PZT elements.  
Conclusion 
The existing research into these technologies analysed in this project show that piezoelectric materials 
can be used to create compound components using micro injection molding. Promising combinations of 
piezoelectric materials, particularly in composite form, have been demonstrated to be processable for 
==PAGE:83==
75 
 
multiple component micro injection molding. This technology could be used to develop micro components 
with improved functionality density. Specifically, integrating push button functionality into the battery 
drawer component of an ITC hearing aid by utilising injection moulded piezoelectric material.  The 
increased geometrical and shaping freedom this offers, combined with a potentially more streamlined 
and efficient manufacturing method, provides opportunities for micro devices with integrated 
piezoelectric functionality. In the hearing aid industry, this could open up novel applications for push 
button functions, enabling functionality to be combined without additional steps in the assembly process 
and without compromising the compacted size of the device. It is recommended that different material 
and process combinations should be tested and optimised to assess the validity of the technology for this 
application. The development process of an ITC battery drawer with integrated piezoelectric push button 
using outs-sourced PZT element has been detailed. An element of this scale can provide the electrical 
requirements while fitting accordingly into the battery drawer, without compromising the space available. 
Though ultimately unsuccessful for an element of ultra-thin thickness, due to the highly brittle nature of 
this thin element, potential solutions to this limiting factor have been described. With a strengthened PZT 
element, either with increased thickness or a strengthening metal shim, there is room for optimisation. 
There is potential for PVDF films, with perhaps more suited mechanical and piezoelectric properties for 
this application, to be an alternative into the design solution. These could be either manually assembled 
post-moulding or integrated into injection moulding steps.
