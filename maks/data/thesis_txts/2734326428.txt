Abstract 
Fungal-derived peramine, a pyrrole-containing non-ribosomal peptide with insect deterrent 
effects, offers a promising alternative to chemical insecticides. While peramine biosynthesis 
is mainly attributed to Epichloë spp., its distribution among Hypocrealean Entomopathogenic 
Fungi (HEF) remains unexplored.  
Bioinformatics and phylogenetics analysis identified Metapochonia bulbillosam MUCL8257 as 
a potential peramine producer within HEF. This study explored the heterologous expression 
of perA, the gene encoding peramine synthetase (PerA) from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257, in S. 
cerevisiae. Despite utilizing both hydrophilic interaction liquid chromatography (HILIC) and 
reversed-phase high performance liquid chromatography (RP-LC) analytical methods, 
peramine production was not detected in S. cerevisiae.  
These findings confirm the broader distribution of perA among HEF but highlight the 
challenges of its heterologous expression in S. cerevisiae. Further research focusing on co-
expressing accessory enzymes, optimizing expression strategies, and employing other HEF 
strains with peramine producing capabilities holds promise for unlocking peramine production 
in S. cerevisiae, paving the way for environmentally friendly biocontrol solutions.

3. Introduction 
3.1 Fungal classification 
The fungal kingdom includes heterotrophic eukaryotic organisms distinguished by the 
presence of cell walls composed of chitin and glucan. This kingdom comprehends numerous 
species able to thrive in almost every habitat, rendering fungi the second largest kingdom after 
bacteria1. Although it is estimated millions of fungal species on Earth, only approximately 
150,000 have been documented to date by the scientific community2. This immense diversity 
of fungi, coupled with their multi-niche distribution, correlates with the fact that fungi possess 
distinct and unique biochemical pathways3, 4. Fungal biochemical pathways give rise to a wide 
array of substances with diverse applications. 
The fungal kingdom is categorized into four major phyla: Chytridiomycota, Zygomycota, 
Ascomycota, and Basidiomycota which are represented in Figure 11.  The phylum Ascomycota 
stands as the largest and includes over 60% of all described species. Among these species, 
some can be pathogenic to plants, animals, and humans, as exemplified by Candida albicans5, 
while other Ascomycota such as Penicillium oxalicum var. Armeniaca CCM 8242 hold 
industrial relevance as it produces the food colorant Natural RedTM6.  
 
3.2 Entomopathogenic Fungi (EPF) Definition. Application as biocontrol 
agents and EU regulations 
This study focuses on entomopathogenic fungi (EPF), a group comprising fungi capable of 
infecting and causing diseases in insects. The prefix "entomo-" comes from ancient Greek, 
ἔντομος (éntomon) to refer to insects. EPF can infect insects and arachnids (non-insect 
arthropods) such as mites, spiders, and ticks7.

EPF have garnered significant attention due to their potential in biological pest control for 
agricultural and forest pests, as well as insects and arachnids responsible for human and 
animal diseases8, 9, 10, 11, 11, 12, 13, 14. They are utilized in various biocontrol approaches, 
including augmentation (inundation and inoculation), classic, and conservation pest 
management strategies. Importantly, EPF pose minimal risks to beneficial organisms such as 
bees, earthworms, and parasitoid wasps, and they also present few safety concerns for human 
exposure15, 16, 17, 18. Consequently, government regulators are likely to classify them as "low-
risk" biocontrol agents19.  
To reduce dependency on chemical pesticides and promote sustainable agriculture, the 
European Commission, the autonomous executive body of the European Union (EU), has 
established the toolbox integrated pest management (IPM), prioritizing natural methods over 
chemical pesticides and encouraging the development of sustainable alternatives20.  The 
adopted regulations facilitate the use of EPF as biocontrol agents. EPF is a safe and effective 
alternative to chemical pesticides and can play a key role in organic agriculture. With this, the 
European Commission will make EPF more accessible to farmers to protect their crops in a 
more sustainable way21. 
EPF-based biopesticides can be applied using existing spray equipment22, and they possess 
the potential to reproduce within or in close proximity to the targeted pest population, offering 
a degree of self-sustaining control23. As alternatives to synthetic chemical pesticides, they help 
to reduce the selection pressure for the development of pesticide resistance in pest 
populations and can even reverse the expression of resistance once it has emerged24. 
Interestingly, while EPF can be found across diverse fungal phyla and subphyla, they do not 
constitute a monophyletic group. However, most of the species utilized for biological pest 
control primarily originate from two groups: the Hypocreales within the Ascomycota phylum 
and the Entomophthoromycota group7. This project focus on the Hypocrealean 
Entomopathogenic Fungi (HEF) typically represented by the genera Beauveria, Cordyceps, 
Epichloё, Metarhizium, Ophiocordyceps and Tolypocladium among others. 
3.3 Definition of Specialized Metabolites (SMs). Fungal SMs and Significance  
Due to the immense biodiversity of fungi and their complex biochemical pathways, they are 
an abundant reservoir of known and novel natural product (NP) bioactive compounds. All 
these NPs, including many other small-molecule fungal metabolites, fall under the category of 
specialized metabolites (SMs)25 which have low molecular weight, typically between 100 and 
5,000 Daltons (Da)26.  
==PAGE:13==
Heterologous expression of a fungal insecticide 
13 
 
Traditionally, SMs were referred to as "secondary metabolites" because they were believed to 
be non-essential for an organism's growth and reproduction, unlike primary metabolites such 
as amino acids, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids27. This terminology "secondary 
metabolites" is no longer considered accurate and the term "specialized metabolites," SMs, is 
more appropriate because it better reflects their key role in the organism's survival and 
facilitating interactions with its environment28. Therefore, in this work we will refer to secondary 
metabolites as specialized metabolites (SMs). 
SMs have diverse functions that range from stress protection to defense against competitors28. 
Some SMs act as toxins that are either host-specific against plants or non-specific ones. HEF 
fungi also uses SMs to facilitate mutualistic interactions, for example the symbiont Epichloё 
festucae produces indole alkaloid SMs that protect its ryegrass host from herbivores29. Some 
of HEF biocompounds serve as defenses against competing microorganisms living in the 
same ecological niche, exemplified by beauvericin from Fusarium fujikuroi that affects the 
bacterium Ralstonia solanacearum30.  
3.4 Industrial production and applications of Fungal SMs 
The production of SMs is a significant area of research, engaging organic chemists, molecular 
biologists, and bioinformaticians alike. In the past, fungal secondary metabolism research was 
driven by pharmaceutical companies seeking novel bioactive compounds for commercial 
applications31. The introduction of recombinant DNA techniques in the 1980s revolutionized 
fungal specialized metabolism research, leading to significant advancements in genetics and 
biochemistry27. Then studies in the late 80s revealed that genes responsible for various SMs 
are often clustered together, with pathway-specific regulatory genes embedded in these 
clusters and accessory genes serving various functions, including transportation, self-
resistance, and others3. Since the release of the first genomes of filamentous fungi at the 
beginning of the 21st century, the genomic era has revived interest in fungal bioactive 
compounds27.  
Fungal SMs are used in a wide variety of applications, including the production of 
pharmaceuticals, crop protection agents, and industrial chemicals. Penicillin G (1), griseofulvin 
(2), cyclosporin A (3), and lovastatin (4) which are examples of fungal-derived SMs with 
antibacterial, 
antifungal, 
immunosuppressant, 
and 
cholesterol-lowering 
properties 
respectively (Figure 2)4.

3.5 Natural Product Gene Clusters (BGCs) and Definitions of their Classes  
The synthesis of SMs is the consequence of the expression of biosynthetic gene clusters 
(BGCs), which are genes that are co-localized and act in concert within a biosynthetic 
pathway. BGCs are activated or deactivated in response to certain conditions27. These 
clusters include  core genes encoding the core enzymes which synthesize the central 
structural motif for that class of SMs. These core proteins functions are generally preserved 
across species27. BGCs can also contain accessory genes that encode tailoring enzymes for 
transportation, resistance mechanisms, modifying the structure, adding functional groups, or 
regulating the production of the SMs35. For example, the lovastatin biosynthesis pathway from 
A. terriusis regulated by the transcription factor LaeA—Zn(II)2Cys6 localized within the 
lovastatin BGC36. The biosynthesis of SMs can involve the catalytic activity of one or more 
enzymes or sometimes large multifunctional polypeptides. In cases where multiple enzymes 
work together to biosynthesize an SM37,38.  
Fungal SMs are classified into a few major chemical classes depending on the precursors and 
enzyme classes involved in their biosynthesis3. In fungi the most found key enzymes are 
polyketide synthases (PKSs), non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs), terpene 
synthases (TSs), and dimethylallyltryptophan synthases (DMATSs) which synthesize 
polyketides (PKs), non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs), Ribosomally synthesized and 
posttranslationally modified peptides (RiPPs), terpenes, and indole alkaloids, respectively. 
These core enzymes are characterized by highly conserved domains with different catalytic 
activities27. The main classes of secondary metabolites found on fungi are described below. 
Polyketides. Fungal PKs are typically synthesized by type I polyketide synthases (T1PKS) and 
in some cases by type III polyketide synthases (T3PKS). PKS are multidomain proteins akin 
to fatty acid synthetases (FAS) that synthesize fatty acids (FAs). Both PKS and FAS utilize 
acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA to construct carbon chains of varying lengths. However, they 
differ in β-carbon reduction; the reduction is optional in PKs, while in FAs the β-carbon is fully 
reduced8. 
Fungal PKSs contain three essential domains, including ketoacyl-CoA synthase (KS), 
acyltransferase (AT), and acyl carrier protein (ACP) domains. A starter AT loads the starter 
unit,  typically acetyl-CoA or propionyl-CoA and transfers it onto the ACP. Then, ACP tethers 
the growing chain and moves it to the KS domain of the current module.  Then AT loads and 
activates an extender unit (usually malonyl-CoA) which is transferred to the ACP. Then the 
KS catalyzes the decarboxylating of the extender unit (malonyl) that provides the 
energy/electron for the condensation reaction, which joins the starter and extender units 
releasing CO2. New extender units can be loaded and be added to the growing chain. The

number of condensation rounds (iterations) that the individual PKSs perform is not predicted 
yet.  The developing PK chain attached to an ACP is sometimes modified by enzyme β-KS, 
ketoreductase/ketoacyl reductase (KRs), enoyl/hydroxyaxyl dehydratase (DHs), enoyl 
reductase (ER) domains, which reduce ketone groups to aldehydes to alkenes and to alkanes 
respectively when present39. The T3PKS is an exception, as it only presents multifunctional 
KS domain but not ACP or AT domains.  
Among accessory enzymes, Phosphopantetheinyl Transferases (PPTases) are of great 
significance in the biosynthesis of several types of fungal NPs including PKs and NRPs40. 
PPTase converts the inactive apo-form of PKSs into their active holo-form by attaching 
coenzyme A to the ACP domains respectively. This activation enables the ACP domains to 
attach extender units and synthesized PKs respectively40,41. The cleavage of the PK chain 
from the PKS can be performed through different mechanisms depending on the presence of 
a final thioesterase (TE) or terminal reductase (R) domain. TE releases the PK via a claisen 
cyclization of a PK intermediate, cleaving the thioester bond between the ACP and the PK and 
leading to a C-C bond formation that generates ring structures42. Fungal TE can edit the 
product chain hydrolyzing acyl groups tethered to ACP by PPTase which corrects defective 
posttranslational modification (PTM) processes of non-reducing PKS41,43. R belongs to the 
SDR superfamily and uses Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (phosphate), NAD(P)H as a 
cofactor to release PKs or NRPs and have a Rossmann fold structure that allows for binding 
NAD(P)H cofactors. R domains work by transferring a hydride and proton from NAD(P)H to 
the target molecule resulting in either aldehyde that can spontaneously cyclize or in a 
alcohol42.  
In bacteria, most T1PKS are multi-modular with separate modules for each step and 
methylations in carbon chain are derived from methyl-malonyl CoA extender units. However, 
in fungi T1PKS are iterative, using one module for biosynthetic repeated reactions; the active 
sites are used repeatedly, and methyltransferases can add methylations in the carbon chain. 
Diversity in fungal polyketide structures arises from the number of iterations, reduction 
reactions, extender unit usage, and cyclization of the nascent polyketide chain for aromatic 
PKs8. Lovastatin (4) (Figure 3) is biosynthesized by an iterative T1PKS, LovB, in collaboration 
with an ER, LovC44 
Peptides can be synthesized through either non-ribosomal or ribosomal pathways.  
Non-ribosomal peptides. NRPs are synthesized by NRPSs, large enzymes that compromise 
multiple domains. Each module of the NRPS recognizes, activates, and binds a specific amino 
acid as a thioester to the 4'-phosphopantetheinyl transferase (PPTase) that is linked to every 
module via a conserved serine. Then, the amino acids are linked via peptide bonds, and the

resulting peptide is released at the final module's C-terminal end. The precursor amino acids 
can be derived from both proteinogenic and non-proteinogenic amino acids. Key domains 
involved include A (adenylation), PCP/T (PCP as peptidyl carrier protein or T as thiolation), C 
(condensation). A selects and activates amino acid monomers, C catalyzes the peptide bond 
formation and T together with a with a phosphopantetheine group transfers substrates into the 
catalytic site.  Additional domains include TE, R, and E (epimerization) among others. E is 
involved in the for the conversion of L-amino acids to D-amino acids. TE and R domains are 
involved in the NRP cleavage were described at the PKS section.  Diversity in NRPs stems 
from differences in peptide length, cyclization, and variations in domain functions8. 
For instance, the first fungal NRPS discovered was δ-(l-α-aminoadipyl)-l-cysteinyl-d-valine 
synthetase (ACVS) which catalyzes the initial step in β-lactam antibiotic biosynthesis, such as 
penicillin G (1) and cephalosporin C (8) (Figure 3)45. Another example is the cyclic peptide 
from Tolypocladium inflatum, cyclosporin A (3) (Figure 3), an immunosuppressive drug used 
in post-organ transplant treatment. The cyclosporin NRPS does not have a TE domain and 
releases the peptide through cyclization and some modules feature an extra methylation 
domain (nMT) between the A and T domains for amino acid methylation46. 
Up to 90% of the fungal NRPSs utilize a terminal C domain for the peptide cleavage which 
catalyzes intramolecular cyclization. But fungal NRPSs can also include TE and R at the C-
terminus. PPTases also play a role in the biosynthesis of NRPs40, converting the inactive apo-
form of NRPSs into their active holo-form by attaching coenzyme A to the T. This activation 
enables the T to attach extender units and synthesized NRPs40,41.  The cleavage of the final 
NRP chain from the megasynthases is carried out by TEs. In fungal NRPSs, TE also release 
the product, regenerating misloaded T and removing short acyl chains41,43. Most of the fungal 
R belongs to the NAD(P)H-dependent SDR superfamily with results in either aldehyde that 
can spontaneously cyclize or in a alcohol42.  
Ribosomal peptides. Ribosomally synthesized and posttranslationally modified peptides 
(RiPPs) are a type of peptide NP that are synthesized by the ribosome. Their biosynthesis 
involves translating a precursor peptide, that then undergoes PTMs to yield the mature peptide 
which is then released by proteolysis47.  The biosynthesis pathways of RiPPs are encoded in 
the genome by BGCs, containing one gene that encodes the precursor peptide and 
neighboring genes that encode enzymes responsible for PTMs48. 
RiPP precursors consist of two parts: N-terminal leader sequence and a C-terminal core 
peptide48. The leader sequence acts as a recognition motif for modifying enzymes, which will 
introduce PTMs onto the core peptide.  In eukaryotes, a signal sequence preceding the leader 
peptide leads the transport of the precursor to cellular compartments48. The core peptide

contain a recognition sequence located at the C-terminus, that facilitates the excision and 
cyclization of the core peptide49  
Despite PTMs were also associated with NRPs, RiPPs undergoes single residue modifications 
such as methylation, hydroxylation, acetylation, epimerization, glycosylation, and the 
formation of disulfide, lanthionine, thiazole, and oxazole bonds which PTMs are responsible 
for the chemical diversity of RiPPs50 PTMs typically occur in a sequential manner, starting 
from the N-terminus and proceeding towards the C-terminus and the intermediate modified 
peptides remain bound to the PTM enzyme throughout the process51.  
However, RiPPs are a relatively small group of secondary metabolites within the fungal 
kingdom, with only four distinct families: amatoxins/phallotoxins, borosins, dikaritins, and 
Epichloëcyclins. Asperipin-2a (9) (Figure 3) is a dikaritin produced by Aspergillus flavus. 
Epichloëcyclins are unique to HEF, mainly synthesized by Epichloë endophytes47. However, 
Epichloëcyclin’s structure is not available yet and a more precise elucidation of their chemical 
structure is required52. The role of Epichloëcyclins is to facilitate interactions between Epichloë 
spp. and their grass hosts in both mutualistic and pathogenic associations53.  
While the identification of BGCs involved in the biosynthesis of NRPSs and PKSs can be 
achieved by analyzing protein domain structures, locating the genes responsible for the 
biosynthesis of terpenes and alkaloids is more difficult due to the divergence of their primary 
sequences3. 
Terpenes. Terpenes, known for their aromatic properties in plants, are also synthesized by 
fungi. Terpenes consist of multiple isoprene units and can adopt linear or cyclic structures, 
saturated or unsaturated forms, and undergo various modifications. Typically structures 
contain carbon skeletons represented by the formula (C5)n and classified in monoterpenes 
(C10), derived from geranyl pyrophosphate; sesquiterpenes (C15), derived from farnesyl 
pyrophosphate; and diterpenes(C20)54 and carotenoids originating from geranylgeranyl 
diphosphate (GGPP). Terpene synthesis is carried out by TSs that are classified as 
prenyltransferases or terpene cyclases (TCs). TCs, that convert diphosphates into terpenes55. 
Terpenes have various biological functions, such as attracting pollinators, deterring 
herbivores, and serving as precursors for more complex compounds like steroids and 
alkaloids56. 
Austinol (10) (Figure 3) and calidodehydroaustin are insecticidal terpenoids produced by 
Aspergillus spp. Austinol is produced by A. nidulans in favorable fermentation conditions 
whereas calidodehydroaustin is synthesized by A. calidoustus but only under unfavorable 
conditions for industrial production57.

Trichothecenes (12) (Figure 3) are a family of toxic terpenoids found in various HEF and plant 
pathogenic fungi, including Fusarium, Isaria, Trichoderma, and Trichothecium among others58. 
Trichothecenes play a role in the pathogenesis of certain Fusarium spp. on various crop 
plants, making them significant mycotoxins concerning food and feed safety59. In contrast, 
trichothecene production by Trichoderma arundinaceum contributes to its biological control 
activity against some plant pathogenic fungi60. 
Indole alkaloids. Indole alkaloids are a diverse group of nitrogen-containing compounds that 
are found in plants, bacteria, fungi, and animals. One of the most common ways that indole 
alkaloids are produced is through the enzymatic conversion of tryptophan (Trp) into other 
compounds61.  
Tryptophan is synthesized from through the shikimic acid pathway, involving the intermediates 
anthranilic acid and indole-3-glycerol-phosphate which is the indole donor and can be coupled 
with serine (Ser), forming Trp.  Trp can be decarboxylated to tryptamine, which can then be 
prenylated to form 4-dimethylallyl tryptophan (4-DMAT) or it can be directly incorporated into 
NRPSs and PK-NRP hybrid compounds. 4-DMAT can then be converted through oxidation 
reactions to form the ergoline ring, a fused tetracyclic structure that is a key component of 
ergot alkaloids. Trp, indole-3-glycerol-phosphate, tryptamine and 4-DMAT are itself precursors 
for the indole/indoline moieties in fungal indole alkaloids61. 
There four main classes of indole alkaloids based on the enzymes involved in their 
biosynthesis; peptide indole alkaloids synthesized by NRPSs, hybrid indole alkaloids 
synthesized by PKS-NRPS, indole diterpenoids and indole alkaloids derived from other 
building blocks61.  
Peptide indole alkaloids synthesized by NRPS are indole-containing NRPs that are 
synthesized by NRPS that incorporate Trp to NRPs. Indole alkaloids synthesized from NRPSs 
can be classified into either dipeptide indole alkaloids that involves the formation of 
diketopiperazine (DKP) or tripeptide indole alkaloids that forms an anthranilate-containing 
tripeptide that undergoes cyclization to contain a tricyclic pyrazinoquinazolinedione core. 
Dipeptide indole alkaloids are synthesized by dimodular NRPS that typically joins Trp to 
another amino acids such as proline (Pro), histidine (His), phenylalanine (Phe), alanine (Ala) 
or another Trp. The resulting linear dipeptidyl thioester undergoes intramolecular cyclization 
through the attack of the free amino group of Trp on the aminoacyl thioester, forming a 2,5-
diketopiperazine ring. This cyclization reaction is likely catalyzed by the C-domain, the final 
module in the NRPS assembly line, but can also occur spontaneously61. Tripeptide indole 
alkaloids are usually synthesized by trimodular NRPS that typically joins β-amino acid 
anthranilate to Trp, resulting in tripeptidyl alkaloids that undergo macrocyclization and release

NRPS. C-like catalyzes nucleophilic attack of the aniline nitrogen of anthranilate on the

tripeptidyl alkaloids61. 
In fungi, hybrid indole alkaloids are synthesized by hybrid iterative PKS-NRPS, typically the

Indole diterpenoids are a group of compounds found in HEF, often produced alongside ergot

Indole alkaloids derived from other building blocks include asterriquinines, ergot alkaloids and

3.6 Peramine Molecule, Biosynthesis and Insecticidal Properties 
Peramine is known for its insecticidal properties and has been described as pyrrolopyrazine 
alkaloid67–75. However, its biosynthesis is dependent on a NRPS system and therefore 
classified as a non-ribosomal-dipeptide-derived SM78. It presents unique structural elements, 
including a lipophilic ring system and a hydrophilic guanidinium group, which have not been 
identified in any other insect feeding deterrents77. Its biosynthesis also differs significantly from 
that of other secondary metabolites. While ergot alkaloid, indole diterpene, and loline 
production are governed by well-defined biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs), peramine 
biosynthesis potentially relies on a single protein called peramine synthetase (PerA). PerA is 
a two-module NRPS encoded by the 8.3 kb (kilobase pair) gene perA found in Epichloё 
festucae (Figure 4.1)78.  
Despite Tanaka et al. (2005) suggested that peramine biosynthesis exclusively depends on 
the PerA protein, the mechanism responsible for creating the oxidized pyrrole group remains 
unknown. This pyrrole group has garnered significant interest since it cannot be formed 
through the conventional NRPS-dipeptide biosynthesis pathway using proline as a substrate. 
Berry et al. (2019) discussed two potential mechanisms for the formation of the pyrrole ring. 
Their theory involves the action of a second enzyme that oxidizes the released dipeptide, while 
the theory from Tanaka et al. (2005) suggests that PerA catalyzes all the steps and might 
incorporate an oxidized proline analogue, 1-pyrroline-5-carboxylic acid (P5C), as a substrate. 
While the latter approach is plausible, Berry et al. (2019) challenged to provide genetic 
evidence that negates the participation of a protein encoded by a distant, non-clustered 
gene78. 
To date, the proposed biosynthesis pathway for peramine follows a multi-step mechanism and 
involves a series of enzymatic and spontaneous reactions described in Figure 4.2: 
(i,ii) In the initial steps, the adenylation domains (A1, A2) facilitate the selection, adenylation, 
and thioester tethering of the putative 1-pyrroline-5-carboxylic acid (P5C) and arginine (Arg) 
substrates individually to the 4’phosphopantetheine (PPT) cofactor of their respective 
thiolation domains (T1, T2). 
(iii) Following this, the methylation domain (nMT) catalyzes the N-methylation of arginine that 
is tethered to the T2 domain. 
(iv) The central condensation domain (C2) mediates the peptide bond formation between the 
tethered aminoacyl substrates. This results in the formation of a dipeptidyl intermediate that is 
retained on the second PPTase before being released as an aldehyde by the C-terminal 
reductase domain (R).

(v) The reductive release of the dipeptidyl intermediate in the form of an aldehyde is facilitated 
by R domain. Additionally, a trans-to-cis peptide bond isomerization of the free dipeptidal 
intermediate occurs during this step. 
 (vi, vii) Subsequently, spontaneous cyclization, rearrangement, and air oxidation reactions 
take place, leading to the final formation of peramine78.

Peramine's insecticidal properties present a green and eco-friendly alternative to traditional 
chemical pesticides used in agriculture. It serves as a potent insect-feeding deterrent against 
Coleopteran and Lepidopteran pests, such as the pastoral pest Listronotus bonariensis 
(Argentine stem weevil)79 and Ichneutica mutans (New Zealand cutworm)80. Analogs of 
peramine have demonstrated feeding-deterrent activity against adult weevils, highlighting the 
significance of the PPZ ring system in determining this feeding-deterrent property73.  
While peramine enhances the grass's defense against insects, it is essential to evaluate its 
impact on other biotic factors, notably livestock. Peramine is commonly found alongside other 
alkaloids, such as ergovaline and the indole-diterpene lolitrem B within endophyte-infected 
grasses. Ergovaline and lolitrem B can be toxic to grazing mammals, causing fescue toxicosis 
and ryegrass staggers disease, respectively. However, peramine, despite its activity against 
insect pests, has no known toxic effects on grazing livestock74. High concentrations of these 
alkaloids would not be effective as biocontrol agents. In fact, the inclusion of ergovaline and 
lolitrem B pellets in the diet of grazing mammals resulted in a 13% loss of livestock81.  
While peramine may share certain structural and functional characteristics with alkaloids or 
indole-diterpenes, its primary classification is as an NRPS-derived compound due to its 
biosynthetic pathway (Figure 4.2). It's important to avoid generalizing peramine as a toxic 
metabolite as it lacks toxicity compared to antimammalian alkaloids like ergovaline and lolitrem 
B from Epichloë endophytes74,81.  
Peramine is not only safe for grazing livestock but also demonstrates insect-pest target 
specificity and harmlessness to other beneficial insects for agriculture, such as pollinators82,83. 
In contrast, the aforementioned ergovaline, loline, and lolitrem B alkaloids are toxic to a broad 
range of insects species69. Additionally, none of the Epichloë alkaloids and their intermediates, 
including peramine, present antifungal activity67,84. Easton (2007) proposed that P5C 
substrate may be originated from the grass hosts, and therefore, the substrate supply and 
consequent production of peramine can be regulated by the plant hosts85. 
Contrary to previous report from Schardl et al. (2013), PerA is not exclusively encoded by 
Epichloë spp. In fact, other HEF such as Cladonia grayi, Fusarium acuminatum, Fusarium 
langsethiae, Metarhizium majus, Metarhizium rileyi45 and Ophiocordyceps sinensis also 
presents potential BGCs for peramine production78. Furthermore, a potential perA homologue 
found in M. rileyi encodes an functional PerA, suggesting the presence of PerA-like proteins 
in multiple strains. Genomics analysis of the perA gene order revels a perA-centered gene 
cluster within the genomes of C. grayi, M. majus and, M. rileyi. This implies that in these 
species, peramine, which serves as the final product in Epichloë spp., likely acts as the initial

component of the peramine gene cluster, in which associated genes are responsible for

3.7 Heterologous expression of fungal NRPS. A comparative analysis of hosts. 
Exploring many of the fungal NRPS remains a challenge due to undiscovered fungal species, 
cultivation difficulties, and silent BGCs under laboratory conditions. However, molecular 
biology techniques have provided a solution: heterologous expression86. This method consists 
in introducing a gene from one organism into a different culturable host organism that does 
not naturally possess the gene. Heterologous expression enables the expression of fungal 
BGCs in non-native hosts, enabling the production of novel and valuable compounds. The 
typical workflow for heterologous expression includes introducing the target gene into the host 
using a vector, culturing to allow gene expression, and characterizing the resulting product86.  
To successfully utilize molecular biology techniques, an ideal cell factory host should possess 
several key traits: genetically engineerable, lab-culturable with consistent growth, established 
growth control tools, and a Generally Regarded As Safe (GRAS)25 status with no toxic 
byproducts. Other factors such as scalability, high titer, rate, yield (TRY)87 and/or source of 
substrate might be considered for the selection of the most suitable cell factory88. The selected 
cell factory might be capable of expressing the perA gene and producing peramine with 
minimal modifications to reduce potential host-related stresses.  
The following section evaluates the potential of filamentous fungi, yeast, and bacteria as cell 
factory hosts for heterologous expression of peramine. Each host is compared, assessed for 
its advantages and disadvantages, and supported with specific examples. 
Filamentous fungi cell factories that previously achieved peramine expression. 
Filamentous fungi, including species like Aspergillus spp. and Penicillium spp., are potential 
hosts candidates for peramine expression. They share similarities with native peramine 
producers, potentially enhancing peramine’s activity and stability due to the maintenance of 
natural PTMs and proper protein folding, enhancing its activity and stability78. As multicellular 
eukaryotic microorganisms, fungi possess the capability to perform complex PTM 
modifications, a crucial factor for ensuring the correct functioning of certain proteins89. 
Despite their advantages, filamentous fungi typically exhibit slower growth rates compared to 
faster-growing microorganisms like bacteria and yeast. This slower growth can impact both 
the time required for protein production and the scale at which it can be achieved. Another 
potential challenge is that fungal expression systems may have fewer well-established genetic 
==PAGE:27==

tools and protocols compared to yeast and bacteria, which makes difficult the heterologous 
expression process. The sustainability of fungal-based expression systems can vary 
depending on the specific fungal strain used and the cultivation methods employed90.  
Yeast as the preferred host. 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae is one of the most common cell factories in academia and industry, 
has been used for alcoholic fermentations for over 4000 years. This microbial workhorse is 
used to produce a wide range of compounds from ethanol and succinate to pharmaceutical 
proteins, and fungal NPs such as NRPs and PKs91. Its rapid growth rate translates into high 
protein yields in short time frames, a valuable trait for efficient production.  
As a single-celled eukaryotic fungus, yeast exhibits similarities with multicellular fungi, making 
it suitable for expressing complex eukaryotic products like peramine. It is simpler to engineer 
with multicellular fungi and its well-characterized metabolism aids in production optimization 
and byproduct minimization. Its genetic manipulability opens doors to tailored product 
expression92. The possibility to transform yeast with exogenous DNA and the highly efficient 
homologous recombination capabilities of S. cerevisiae make it a leading candidate for 
recombinant production of pharmaceuticals and valuable chemicals. Numerous techniques for 
genetically modifying S. cerevisiae, including transformation-associated recombination 
cloning (TAR-cloning)93, long terminal repeat-guided cloning, and CRISPR/Cas9, have been 
developed for enhanced productivity and potentially contributing to a more sustainable 
production platform91. Yeast offers scalability, making it adaptable from laboratory to industrial 
production, thus aligning with the principles of large-scale sustainability91. Such as fungi, yeast 
can also be cultivated using various sustainable substrates, such as agricultural waste 
products, which could reduce its environmental footprint90.  
S. cerevisiae, unlike many other fungi, is not naturally producer of PKs or NRPs as it lacks an 
essential PPTase as Sfp is in bacteria or NpgA in fungi, which are required for the post-
translational activation of PKSs and NRPSS91. Engineering S. cerevisiae with a PPTase is 
therefore essential for the heterologous production of those SMs. Although Sfp-type PPTases 
from bacteria are available, NpgA from Aspergillus nidulans is often preferred for the 
heterologous production of fungal NPs in yeast due to its fungal origin91. 
Bacteria as an alternative microbial chassis. 
Bacteria, notably Escherichia coli, are known for their high growth rates and the ability to 
produce proteins in large quantities rapidly. Their well-established genetic manipulation tools, 
numerous expression vectors, and available strains make bacterial systems cost-effective 
compared to eukaryotic systems, which may reduce resource consumption94. Bacterial

4. Aim of the study 
Berry et al. (2019) and Tanaka et al. (2005) demonstrate that peramine biosynthesis relies 
solely on one specific pathway protein, PerA. This project aims to establish a biosustainable 
production platform for peramine in S. cerevisiae through the heterologous expression of perA. 
To achieve this is required the following objectives. 
1. Phylogenomic analysis of HEF with perA.  The first step is to use phylogenomic 
approaches to investigate the genetic components and relationships within the 
peramine biosynthetic pathway across diverse fungal species. This research will 
highlight new peramine producers and their associated biosynthetic genes. 
 
2. Heterologous expression of Peramine in S. cerevisiae for Sustainable 
Production. The second but primary aim is to successfully express the peramine 
biosynthetic pathway's key NRPS, PerA, in S. cerevisiae by using the budding yeast 
as heterologous host. 
 
In summary, this research aims to establish a sustainable peramine production platform while 
also expanding our understanding of peramine biosynthesis through phylogenomic 
exploration, paving the way for the discovery of novel congeners with similar bioactivity.

5.3 Heterologous expression of perA from synthetic DNA from Metapochonia 
bulbillosa: perA insert, vectors, assembly DNA constructs, cloning.

Cloning methods and (assembly transformation and verification) 
This study followed a common cloning procedure for heterologous expression approaches. 
The first step was to transform E. coli DH10B with the DNA of the plasmid or construct via 
bacterial heat shock transformation described by Sambrook and Russell (2006), mixing 0.5 
µL of pure DNA (usually 10 pg - 100 ng) with 50 µL of competent cells98. The E. coli 
transformants were selected in LB agar supplemented with ampicillin (100 µg/ml). Then 
Plasmid DNA with a length shorter than 15 kb was extracted following the indications of 
NucleoSpin Plasmid QuickPure Kit (Macherey Nagel), drying the silica membrane for 3 min 
and using 75 µL of MILLIQ© water instead of buffer AE with an incubation of 5 min at room 
temperature. Plasmid DNA equal or larger than 15 kb was extracted following the first three 
first steps (cell harvesting, lysis and clarification of lysate) described in NucleoSpin Plasmid 
QuickPure Kit (Macherey Nagel) and followed by a manual DNA precipitation with isopropanol, 
without a silica membrane column, washing with 70% ethanol after the precipitation, and 
resuspending DNA samples in 75 µL of MILLIQ© water. After purification, the DNA was 
confirmed via Restriction Enzyme (RE) digestion with High Fidelity (HF) REs and with 
rCutSmart™ Buffer, both provided by New England Biolabs, NEB. Next, 20 µL from the RE 
digestion were loaded onto a 1% agarose gel and subjected an electrophoresis according to 
the protocol described by Sambrook and Russell (2006) to visualize the RE digestion efficacy 
and identify the fragments that correspond to the bands at expected size. Those with the 
expected sized were selected and the DNA concentration was measured with Nanodrop. Each 
plasmid or genetic construct was storage at -20°C for further use. Positive transformants cells 
were stock in 50% glycerol at -80°C as well. 
5.3.1 
Cloning pESC-LEU in E. coli for use as control and vector backbone 
The expression vector pESC-LEU (provided from YNP lab; Agilent)109 was cloned into E. coli 
DH10B via bacterial heat shock transformation. After its purification, the plasmid was validated 
via EcoRI-HF (#R3101L) digestion and those with the expected sized were selected. pESC-
LEU was used as a vector backbone for perA assembly and as a control DNA in further 
assemblies. pESC-LEU was used in S. cerevisiae transformation as described in the 
subsection “S. cerevisiae transformation with pESC-LEU-perA” below. 
pESC-LEU is a dual expression vector, with genetic elements needed in E. coli and yeast 
expression systems (Figure 7A). The genetic elements allowing its selection and expression

extract the cell pellet, washed with water, and then dried in a Heratherm™ oven at 80°C for 3 
days. The final dry cell weight (DCW) was measured, and the average and standard deviation 
of triplicates of each flask per different days were used for the Scatter plot of the growth curve.

Extractions were performed on the 5th day of fermentation. Yeast cells were separated from 
the supernatant by centrifugation at 6000 rpm for 10 min. Then the supernatant was separated 
and transferred to a glass tube. The supernatant fraction was then subject to partition with 
ethyl acetate (ratio 1:1) in a separatory funnel. It was shaken and kept on the support until the 
mixture got well separated. The resulted supernatant (aqueous phase) was separated from 
the organic phase. The aqueous phase was re-extracted with ethyl acetate. This step was 
repeated three times. The two extracted organic phases were combined and evaporated using 
the rotary evaporator system.

instrument112.  
The chromatographic separation for the second method (HILIC) was achieved using a Waters

Bioinformatics and comparative genomics approaches identified seven potential peramine 
producing HEF strains (Table 3), including two Epichloë spp., E. coenophiala MB805488 
(YNP76) and E. typhina MB165057 (YNP37). However, due to the prior report of peramine 
production in the Epichloë genus Berry et al. (2019), these two strains were excluded from 
further research. 
The antiSMASH analysis of Metarhizium flavoviride DSM11336 (YNP50) revealed a perA 
cluster that differ from the reported PerA from E. festucae Fl1 as the nMT (methylation) domain 
is missing and it also contains two additional C (condensation) and E (epimerization) domains 
that are not present in the reported PerA. Additionally, the MIBiG similarity score for peramine 
was low (0.25) and no peramine was detected with the BLASTP algorithm. These results 
suggest that this Metarhizium perA cluster is not involved in peramine production. 
Two Metapochonia strains, M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 (YNP45) and M. bulbillosa MUCL34289 
(YNP46), were also characterized. The antiSMASH fungal version analysis of M. bulbillosa 
MUCL8257 predicted an incomplete perA sequence, lacking one of the two A (adenylation) 
domains typically found in PerA. Consequently, a new PerA prediction was done using 
Augustus gene calling with perA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 as query and the closely 
related fungal species, Trichoderma atroviridae as reference. Then, from the annotated PerA 
sequence, a codifying DNA (cDNA) was obtained by reverse transcription and by codon 
optimization for S. cerevisiae (GenScript). The new cDNA sequence for perA was subject of 
new antiSMASH predictions which revealed the expected two A domains in the NRPS. Based 
on their perA core lengths (7698 nucleotides, nt in M. bulbillosa MUCL34289 and 8375 nt in 
M. bulbillosa MUCL8257), the absence of introns, and the more similar domain organization 
with the reported PerA from E. festucae F11, M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 was selected for further 
investigation.  
Two Ophiocordyceps strains, O. aphodii MB504221 (YNP81) and O. gracilis MB504277 
(YNP53), were also analyzed. perA in O. aphodii MB504221 contained one intron while in O. 
gracilis MB504277 had three. The domain organization in O. aphodii MB504221 was the same 
as in the reported PerA. However, in O. gracilis MB504277 presented an additional C domain 
and lacked an nMT domain. These results, together with a higher MiBIG score for peramine 
in O. aphodii MB504221, described O. aphodii MB504221 with superior peramine biosynthetic 
potential over O. gracilis MB504277.

6.2 Comparative genomics and phylogenomic analysis with PerA 
6.2.1 
FUNGISON of annotated PerA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 as query and perA from 
Epichloë festucae F11 as reference  
The amino acid sequence of PerA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 predicted with antiSMASH 
after Augustus gene calling and codon optimized for S. cerevisiae was compared to the YNP 
fungal database to find the conserved core of perA and the gene context in homologues. 
FUNGISON analysis showed that the 36 species belonging to Epichloë genus share core 
identity of 50% with PerA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 (Figure 9). PerA from M. bulbillosa 
MUCL8257 shared 100% identity with the gene context (accessory genes upstream and 
downstream perA) of E. festucae F11, which was reported by Berry et. al (2019) as native 
peramine producer. This suggests conserved identity and synteny between PerA from M. 
bulbillosa MUCL8257 and peramine-producing Epichloë spp. making M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 
a potential peramine producer candidate.

MUCL8257 and a 100% similarity and identification of all accessory genes in E. festucae F11.

6.2.3 
IQ-TREE phylogenetic analysis 
The evolutionary relationship between PerA from different HEF was achieved with IQ-TREE 
using PerA from M. bulbillosa. The HEF sequences were aligned with MAFFT as described in 
Table 2. The phylogenetic tree was annotated in FigTree v1.4.4. The output from PerA from 
Metapochonia bulbillosa MUCL8257 is highlighted in blue and Epichloë ssp. are group on the 
top within a triangle (Figure 11). Xylariales sp. PMI 506  was used to annotate an outgroup as 
it belong to Xylariales but not Hypocreales order121. The phylogenetic tree compromise other 
HEF species with peramine orthologs and their evolutionary distances among them: Fusarium, 
Metapochonia, Simplicillium, Tolypocladium, Trichoderma and Metarhizium. The nodes show 
UFBoot (Ultrafast Bootstrap Approximation) values from 89 to 100% (except for a value of 
60% in the Trichoderma koningiopsis terminal clade) which supports with high level of 
confidence the representation of the branches in the phylogeny122. Thus, this phylogeny 
represents the evolutionary relationship and similarities in PerA.  
PerA is conserved at basal groups from HEF represented by Fusarium spp. and Trichoderma 
spp. in the tree. Tolypocladium is reprsented in a clade near to HEF´s basal groups. The tree 
also revealed some species from Clavicipitaceae family (belonging to Metarhizium, 
Metapochonia and Epichloë genera), some from Ophiocordycipitaceae family (belonging to 
Ophiocordyceps genus) and some from Cordycipitaceae family (belonging to Simplicillium

genus) which diverged from the basal groups and which are described as plant endophytes. 
The species Metarhizium majus, Metarhizium rileyi and Ophiocordyceps aphodii are 
represented as sister species of Epichloë spp. based on sequence similarity of their PerA 
protein. As Epichloë spp., are described as producers of peramine by Berry et al. (2019), these

These results show that PerA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 shares identity among HEF, in

precursor by Berry et al. (2019) instead of Pro, P5C moiety could not be detected in any of the 
analytes. The analyte detected at the peak of RT 1.67 min in EIC appeared in cells fraction of 
S1 and control C1 and the one at RT 1.80 min was detected at S3. These ions share the same 
MS2 pattern in which peaks present similar intensities and at the same m/z values (70.0650, 
98.0599, 125.0708, 167.1178 and 195.1128 m/z). However, the presence of this ion in C1 
discarded it from further analysis and correlation with a metabolite from the peramine producer 
strains.  
Other ions found at m/z 272.17 compromised an Arg-Pro derived structure that made it a 
potential peramine intermediate (Supplementary 9). The ion was found in both RPLC and 
HILIC analysis with a protonated formula [C11H21N5O3 + H]+ and protonated exact mass of 
272.172265 u. However, deeper analysis of this metabolite in FreeStyleTM could detect the 
presence of this ion in C1 which discards the possibility of being a product from perA 
expression in 1C_NpgA-perA samples. A summary of detected peramine-related ions, 
including m/z 254.16 and 272.17 and other candidates found in HILIC and RPLC modes is 
shown in Supplementary 8 together with their structure prediction and MS2s. Despite their 
structural similarities with peramine, none of the analytes were unique for 1C_NpgA-perA 
samples as they were present in C1 and thus they were discarded for further analysis. 
By using GNPS we could generate molecular clusters containing compounds with similar MS2 
fragmentation pattern. Some clusters containing metabolites with m/z values close to the exact 
mass of protonated peramine were found. Among the clusters, cluster 5217 connected 
dipeptide molecules with spectral match to Pro-Arg or Arg-containing molecules which could 
potentially be peramine intermediates (Supplementary 9A). The nodes were annotated with 
the different GNPS groups of which some nodes could be observed to be unique to 1C_NpgA-
perA samples analyzed by HILIC and RPLC methods (groups G3 and G4 coloring in green 
and orange respectively) and their parent masses shown in Supplementary 9B were then 
analyzed in SIRIUS. However, no structures were identified for the nodes appearing only at 
1C_NpgA-perA samples. Overall, GNPS analysis did not cluster peramine standard with other 
detected ions.

7. Discussion and Future Perspectives 
The initial genome mining of hundreds of Hypocrealean Entomopathogenic fungi (HEF) strains 
identified seven strains (Epichloë coenophiala MB805488, Epichloë typhina MB165057, 
Metapochonia bulbillosa MUCL34289, Metapochonia bulbillosa MUCL8257, Metarhizium 
flavoviride DSM11336, Ophiocordyceps aphodii MB 504221, Ophiocordyceps gracilis 
MB504277 with accession numbers YNP76, YNP37, YNP46, YNP45, YNP50, YNP81 and 
YNP53 respectively), with perA gene sequences and associated domain organizations 
suggestive of their peramine biosynthesis potential.  
Among these strains, the perA cluster from Metarhizium flavoviride DSM11336 YNP50 was 
excluded as its antiSMASH-predicted domain organization differs from the previous described 
PerA of E. festucae F11, what suggests that the strain is unlikely to produce peramine. 
Although Berry et al. (2019) described perA orthologs in some Metarhizium, this specific strain 
differs and was not related to other peramine producers by phylogenetic analysis. 
Two Ophiocordyceps spp. were also analyzed as this genus has been described to present 
perA homologues by Berry et al. (2019). PerA of O. aphodii MB 504221, shares a more similar 
domain organization with the reported PerA from E. festuca FI1. Remarkably, PerA NRPS in 
Ophiocordyceps spp. (O. aphodii and O. robertsii) are closely related to the one of Epichloë 
spp., even closer than certain Metarhizium spp. that were previously described as perA 
homologue78. This finding would suggest that PerA NRPS in O. aphodii and O. robertsii could 
produce peramine and they could be implemented in further heterologous expression 
approaches. 
Another possible peramine producer strain was M. bulbillosa MUCL8257, however it is 
nucleotide sequence, and therefore the resulting NRPS, was shorter than the previously 
described in Epichloë spp., being a potential candidate to investigate. Subsequently,  perA 
sequence from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 was manually annotated with Augustus software, and 
the protein sequence obtained was transformed in optimal coding DNA (cDNA) using reverse 
transcription and codon optimization software of GenScript, revealing more similarities to 
Epichloë spp.  
FUNGISON analysis revealed conflicting, results regarding the synteny of PerA.  While 
comparing M. bulbillosa MUCL8257´s PerA to E. festucae F11´s genome, FUNGISON 
identified gene context similarities and synteny between PerA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 
and from Epichloë spp. However, a broader analysis with PerA from various strains (O. aphodii 
504221, M. bulbillosa MUCL8257, M. bulbillosa MUCL34289, E. coenophiala MB805488, E. 
brachyletri E4804 and E. festucae F11) and M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 and E. festucae F11 as 
==PAGE:60==
Heterologous expression of a fungal insecticide 
60 
 
references could not detect any synteny relationships among PerA of the listed strains, 
including M. bulbillosa MUCL8257. These results show that synteny is not very conserved, so 
only strains that are closely related share gene context, but others do not. The 100% similarity 
observed between M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 and E. festucae F11 might be an isolated case, 
and the gene context surrounding PerA different across other strains or can be spread in the 
genomes. In order to assess peramine production potential of M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 and 
PerA orthologs within HEF, other phylogenetic methods were used in this study 
The IQ-TREE phylogenetic analysis of PerA from Metapochonia bulbillosa MUCL8257 shows 
that peramine biosynthesis is conserved at both the core level and at the gene context, 
correlating with the first FUNGISON analysis between M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 and the 
genome reference of E. festucae F11. As IQ-TREE used Xylariales sp. PMI 506 as outgroup 
for belonging to Xylariales order, it can be inferred the evolutionary events that resulted in the 
PerA synteny across HEF. Peramine is a distinctive molecule of HEF and appears at the 
Xylariales sp. PMI 506 and the majority of HEF´s basal groups such as Fusarium spp. and 
Tolypocladium, Trichoderma spp. which could explain that PerA is conserved at the basal 
species level.  
As peramine functions as an insect deterrent, it at the same time, defends the plant from 
herbivory resulting in an indirect beneficial effect for the plant health. The presence of PerA in 
the most diverged species, belonging to Metarhizium, Metapochonia, Simplicillium, 
Ophiocordyceps and Epichloë genera could be attributed to horizontal gene transfer events, 
where perA core and part or whole gene context could have been transfer interspecifically, 
resulting in peramine expression in those species to promote symbiosis with plants and the 
survival of endophyte fungi. This hypothesis can be supported by the fact that fungal species 
such as Metapochonia bulbillosa, detected as potential peramine producer or Epichloë spp. 
are commonly described as endophytes. Additionally, there are no duplicated genes and 
therefore, no duplication events can be reported for the presence of PerA.  
To test the hypothesis of PerA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 being an ortholog of E. festucae 
and possibly with a similar product to peramine, heterologous expression approaches were 
performed. Detection of peramine would have supported this hypothesis and suggested 
synthesis between PerA sequences from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 and Epichloë spp. The 
gene sequence perA, that hypothetically encodes PerA in M. bulbillosa was used for 
heterologous expression in S. cerevisiae via replicative expression vector, pESC-LEU. Then, 
the transformants carrying the construct pESC-LEU with perA (1C_NpgA-perA) were utilized 
in fermentation experiments to induce the expression of perA. The expression of perA is 
controlled by the GAL1/10 inducible promoter and CYC1 terminator. GAL1/10 promoter should 
==PAGE:61==
Heterologous expression of a fungal insecticide 
61 
 
have only induced the expression of peramine in presence of galactose which was added to 
the medium in the fermentation experiments. 
Expression of perA in S. cerevisiae was supported by the distinct growth profiles between 
strains expressing perA (1C_NpgA-perA) and control (1C_NpgA-pESC-LEU), that grew on 
same galactose-containing media (SC-Leu with 2% galactose). This suggests the observed 
slower growth in 1C_NpgA-perA was not due to differences in carbon source utilization.  
Fermentation experiments revealed comparable growth patterns among the three perA 
expressing strains (S1, S2, S3) but a decrease in dry cell weight (DCW) compared to the 
control (1C_NpgA-pESC-LEU). This reduction suggests that perA expression impacts S. 
cerevisiae cells, possibly due to metabolic demands associated with its expression under the 
GAL1/10 promoter, redirecting energy from growth towards peramine production. 
Alternatively, peramine itself might possess slightly antifungal activity. Further investigation 
into the toxic effects of peramine in S. cerevisiae is needed. Performing bioassays with 
increasing peramine concentrations would help to elucidate the cause of the observed growth 
reduction. 
While Berry et al. (2019) expressed and detected peramine at the fifth day of fermentation 
from the heterologous expression host P. paxilli, this study did not detect peramine in S. 
cerevisiae transformants using the same fermentation time and same extraction methods. The 
differences between P. paxilli and S. cerevisiae fungal factories could also be reflected in 
different times for peramine production. Future experiments could be addressed to optimize 
the fermentation process, analyzing different time points of the production, and improving perA 
expression.  
Metabolomics analysis could not detect the protonated mass of peramine, suggesting that 
either peramine BGC was not fully expressed in the S. cerevisiae samples, or peramine was 
synthesized but subsequently degraded. The absence of peramine could also be explained 
by an insufficient disruption of the cell wall, which prevents peramine from being extracted.  
The dipeptide ion at m/z 254.16 resembles a potential peramine intermediate. However, the 
presence of this intermediate in both 1C_NpgA-perA samples and the 1C_NpgA-pESC-LEU 
control, with similar intensity, and its absence in media control fractions indicates that is not a 
product of the perA expression but a product from S. cerevisiae native metabolism. This 
dipeptide differs from the described peramine intermediate proposed by Berry et al. (2019), 
who suggest that the intermediate is formed with P5C instead of Pro. The absence of genetic 
evidence supporting this mechanism led Berry et al. (2019) to propose an alternative pathway 
involving an oxidative enzyme that oxidizes the pyrrole group of peramine intermediate. The 
oxidative enzyme could be encoded by another gene located in other BGC different from the 
==PAGE:62==
Heterologous expression of a fungal insecticide 
62 
 
perA. Further molecular biology experiments could incorporate genes encoding fungal 
oxidative enzymes, leading to the formation of oxidized pyrrole groups and increased 
production of peramine-related metabolites. 
Arg and Pro are proteinogenic monomeric building blocks of the primary metabolism of S. 
cerevisiae´s primary metabolism. If peramine biosynthesis utilizes Arg and Pro (rather than 
Arg and P5C, as proposed by Tanaka et al. (2005)), the dipeptide ion detected at m/z 272.17 
could be attributed to PerA activity. However, this ion was not unique to S. cerevisiae strains 
with perA, as it was also detected at similar intensity in the control strain, lacking perA but 
absent in the media control fractions. This suggests that this dipeptide ion together with the 
ion at 254.16 m/z are not directly related to peramine biosynthesis but rather represent 
byproducts of other metabolic pathways in S. cerevisiae. 
In S. cerevisiae, the L-proline biosynthesis pathway finishes with the conversion of P5C into 
Pro, which is mediated by the enzyme delta 1-pyrroline-5-carboxylate reductase (PRO3)123. 
This reaction favors Pro production, resulting in a lower abundance of P5C relative to Pro. In 
this study, S. cerevisiae strains were grown in (SC)-Leu which is a P5C-deficient media, which 
could explain the absence of peramine-related compound as the peramine precursor P5C was 
absent in the media. Further feeding experiments with P5C could saturate PRO3 that would 
not be able to convert all the P5C to Pro and therefore the remaining P5C will be available for 
peramine biosynthesis. Additionally, a change of the PRO3 promoter by a weak constitutive 
promoter could also increase the pool of P5C that could be used as substrate for peramine 
biosynthesis. P5C is spontaneously converted to L-glutamate-5semialdehyde in the proline 
degradation pathway. Metabolic Flux Analysis (MFA) could evaluate the risks of minimizing 
Pro levels or maximizing P5C in the medium, in order to increment P5C for both primary 
metabolism and peramine biosynthesis.  
SIRIUS analysis exhibited limitations while determining the sample origin of the analytes. It 
failed to identify the presence of ions at m/z 272.17 in the control samples, maybe because it 
appears in the low abundance. FreeStyleTM, provides a more definitive assessment of analyte 
presence or absence. Moreover, SIRIUS provides a percentage of structural similarity 
because it searches for similar compounds in databases. Although SIRIUS cannot assure the 
structure when the similarity score is below 95%, it  can help to identify motifs of the analyte.  
By contrast, the molecular formula predicted from SIRIUS can be confidently used to calculate 
the exact mass for further analysis in FreeStyleTM. 
GNPS analysis could not cluster peramine standard to any of the peramine analogs detected 
ions, which means even the structure related compounds we detected doesn´t share similar 
fragmentation pattern with peramine enough to get clustered. However, further analysis

63 
 
should keep integrating GNPS analysis in the metabolomics pipeline, as it creates molecular 
networks in which future experiments could cluster peramine with its analogs or intermediates. 
Other arguments that explain the absence of peramine in LC-MS could be due to peramine 
degradation by the heterologous host along the fermentation. In order to test if the absence of 
peramine could be due to degradation, a production curve should be done during fermentation, 
extracting samples of peramine every day. 
This study used the S. cerevisiae strain 1C_NpgA, engineered to express the Aspergillus 
nidulans npgA gene, which encodes a phosphopantetheinyl transferase (PPTase). This step 
is crucial because S. cerevisiae lacks its own PPTase and npgA expression is essential  for 
the activity of nonribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) like PerA124. Previous research by 
Berry et al. (2019) successfully expressed heterologous perA genes in Penicillium paxilli, 
which possesses its own native PPTase (PaxM) and associated regulatory elements (5'-
promoter and 3'-terminator from paxM) essential for NRPS activity78,125,126.  
The native P. paxilli´s PPTase might be orthologous to the npgA expressed in 1C_NpgA strain, 
suggesting both hosts could be suitable for PerA expression. Further investigation into the 
functional similarity and evolutionary relationship between these two enzymes could confirm 
their roles in enabling heterologous NRPS expression in S. cerevisiae. Moreover, Cacho and 
Tang (2019) demonstrated that engineering S. cerevisiae with npgA is crucial for expressing 
other fungal NRPS genes such as fumiquinazoline F synthetase TqaA/Afu6g12080 in S. 
cerevisiae 124. Therefore, the presence of npgA in our strain shouldn't be a limiting factor for 
peramine production.  
Another alternative that could explain the absence of peramine in LC-MS data, could be 
because the core of PerA may not be sufficient for peramine synthesis and certain accessory 
genes could play a role in its expression. As Berry et al. (2019) cited that peramine cluster 
genes are common among host species with perA homologues forming the peramine´s BGC. 
This observation is also proved by the FUNGISON analysis in this study. The accessory genes 
also described in other species are predicted to function as MFS transporter, Cytochrome 
P450 monooxygenase and two oxidoreductases (NAD- or NADP-dependent oxidoreductase, 
SDR reductase and Ferritin/ribonucleotide reductase-like protein). Future cloning experiments 
should not only consider the core of perA but the accessory genes for peramine production. 
Although Berry et al. (2019) concluded that the biosynthesis of peramine in P. paxilli required 
only PerA, this could be because P. paxilli already possess oxidoreductases, oxigenases and 
or MFS transporter that S. cerevisiae, the heterologous host in this study, doesn’t. Therefore, 
future experiments should consider cloning the accessory genes in S. cerevisiae and not only 
the core perA.

Overall, the results of this study shows a promising candidate strain, M. bulbillosa MUCL8257, 
as potential peramine producer. Further successful heterologous expression of perA in S. 
cerevisiae should incorporate the expression of the peramine accessory genes found 
upstream and downstream perA NRPS gene and not solely dependent on the perA core as 
Berry et al. (2019) suggested. If the hypothesis is true further experiments should design 
different heterologous approaches with the perA from M. bulbillosa MUCL8257 depending on 
the type of heterologous expression host; if the host are filamentous fungi such as P. paxilli, 
the peramine expression will be achieve by solely PerA as Berry et al. (2019) achieved. If S. 
cerevisiae is used as the cell factory it might require the expression of some accessory genes 
involved in the biosynthesis peramine. 
 
8. Conclusion  
In conclusion, the distribution and evolution of peramine synthetase (PerA) among 
Hypocrealean Entomopathogenic Fungi (HEF) have been elucidated in this study. PerA non-
ribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS) from Metapochonia bulbillosa MUCL8257 is 
phylogenetically related to the one described in Epichloë spp. The bioinformatic studies prove 
that the gene context of peramine´s BGC is identical to the gene context in E. festucae F11. 
Despite their synteny, peramine was not detected during its heterologous expression in S. 
cerevisiae. Many questions have been set up during the development of this research and the 
analysis of its results, exposed in the discussion.  
The data reported in this study helps broaden the knowledge of peramine distribution among 
fungal strains, molecular biology strategies to heterologously express peramine in S. 
cerevisiae, extraction methods and metabolomics tools for peramine detection.
