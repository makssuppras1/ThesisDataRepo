Abstract
The scope of this project is to design, develop and test an automated sea ice concen-
tration model using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN). The thesis provides an
overview of the theory behind the Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and Passive Mi-
crowave Radiometer (PMR) instruments as well as introduces sea ice formation and
it’s active and passive electromagnetic signatures. A description is provided of the Au-
tomated Sea Ice Products, ASIP, data set with scenes encompassing Sentinel-1 SAR,
AMSR2 and a sea ice chart. The Artificial Intelligence methods applied and how the
architectural U-Net CNN is implemented and tested is introduced. Experiments are
initially carried out training the U-Net model on SAR data. Afterwards, data fusion
attempts are made, fusing SAR and the system’s incidence angle, SAR and AMSR2
and finally, the SAR, the incidence angle and AMSR2 data.
Results conclude that
the U-Net architecture trained on Sentinel-1 SAR data produces highly detailed sea ice
concentration maps with open water, intermediate sea ice concentrations as well as full
sea ice cover. The main obstacle of the model is the incapability to correctly classify
areas of high sea ice concentration where low variance occurs in the SAR HH and HV
polarization images. The SAR incidence angle was found to improve results, but not
fully mitigate this issue. Fusing SAR and AMSR2 at the input were seen to correctly
classify areas of low SAR HH and HV variability and was the overall best performing
model. Adding the SAR incidence angle to the U-Net trained on SAR and AMSR2 was
not seen to improve results using this architecture but performed better than the U-Net
trained on just SAR data.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Arctic is rapidly changing due to global warming. The American National Oceanic
and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) have reported that the temperature in the
Arctic is increasing at twice the rate compared to the global average [1]. The impact
on the environment is profound with glaciers withdrawing, ice sheets diminishing, per-
mafrost thawing and the sea ice cover decreasing. Particularly, the latter have opened
for activities in the otherwise remote and inaccessible regions of the Arctic. Observa-
tions of the extent of the Arctic sea ice cover has shown that it is decreasing across
the entire year. Seasonal minimum and maximal cover are present in September and
March, and have respectively diminished by 31 % and 6 % in 2019 [1] compared to 1995.
Meanwhile the average sea ice age has also decreased.
This has created new opportunities in the Arctic with renewed tourist interests,
resource prospects and new trading routes.
With large economic interests, political
agendas are increasingly encompassing the Arctic and multiple nation states are scram-
bling for influence and control [2]. For example, of the remaining global undiscovered oil
and natural gas reserves, 22 % are thought to be in the Arctic [3]. If ships were to sail
through the Arctic sea, the economic cost of shipping from Northeast Asia to Northwest
Europe has been estimated to decrease by 10 % [4]. The journey time from e.g. South
Korea to the Netherlands [2] could be reduced from 48 days through the Suez Canal,
to 35 days. This could make the Northern Sea Route (NSR) one of the busiest in the
world in the future [4].
Traversing the remote Arctic region by ship has never been easy and has cost many
sailors their lives. The iconic history of the Titanic that sank in April 14–15, 1912,
killed about 1,500 people [5] and it highlights the dangers of the Arctic sea. In addition,
numerous occurrences of ships being trapped for weeks, months or sunk in sea ice have
been recorded. To help navigation along the Greenlandic coasts the Danish Meteoro-
logical Institute (DMI) have developed tools that allows a contemporary vision of sea
ice. These so-called ice charts help many routine fishing vessels, freight transports to
remote villages and tourist ships safely navigate. The charts highlight objects such as
icebergs and the amount of sea ice also called the sea ice concentration (SIC) in an area.
However, these charts cover only the surrounding waters of Greenland and are produced
by hand. In the past, data availability has been a limiting factor in the ice chart produc-
tion chain. This has changed with the launch of the European Space Agency’s (ESA)
Earth Observation (EO) programme, Copernicus [6] and Sentinel missions [7] that re-
leases an abundance of data. The next logical step in improving Arctic sea navigation
is to increase coverage, release frequency and create sea ice products that target specific
user-cases, which ultimately will make Arctic voyaging safer. To accommodate these

points, an increase of analysis resources is necessary, which can be generated from either

With the rise of machine learning, Artificial Intelligence (AI) and an abundance of
computing power, it seems only appropriate to use these methods to develop new sea

The division is designed to be a catalyst for radical innovation in EO [8].
There have been multiple earlier attempts at classifying sea ice using computational
algorithms. Initially different mathematical features were calculated and fed into a neu-
ral network to choose the optimal combination of features to predict the sea ice concen-
tration. However, these attempts have not shown sufficient success to be implemented
at places such as DMI.
The next frontier in automatizing sea ice classification is the Convolutional Neural
Network (CNN), which has shown promising results. To aid the development of such an
algorithm, DMI and the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) (DTU Space & DTU
Compute) have created the Automated downstream Sea Ice Products (ASIP) project,
which has become a part of the ESA AI4ARCTIC initiative. The project’s aim is to
partially or fully automate the sea ice chart generation from Copernicus Sentinel-1 SAR
imagery [9]. For this purpose, the ASIP data set has been compiled encompassing a
large selection of scenes with overlapping Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR), Passive
Microwave Radiometer (PMR) measurements and an equivalent DMI ice chart to train
potential algorithms. This will be the foundation for this project.
1.1
Problem Definition
The problem to be solved is how best to automate SIC charting utilizing Earth Observa-
tion and AI. However, there are extensive data sources and CNN architectures, which
could be applied. Comparing all these different approaches is not feasible. Therefore,
the focus is on utilizing the popular U-Net CNN architecture with data from the ASIP
data set, which ultimately match end user-needs. The ASIP data set contain both SAR
and PMR data. Experiments are made to assess the performance of using only SAR
as to establish a baseline, followed by fusion of SAR and the system’s incidence angle.
Then the SAR and PMR will be fused and afterwards the SAR, PMR and the incidence
angle. The models are assessed on the accuracy, the confusion matrix and the statistical
prediction to the ground truth fit termed the R2-coefficient. In addition, quantitative
and qualitative assessments on final inference visualizes model usefulness and versatility.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical Measurement
Approach
This chapter serves to introduce measuring techniques related to the Synthetic Aperture
Radar (SAR) and the Passive Microwave Radiometer (PMR), to give an understanding
of the data used in this project. Initially, the SAR’s operational geometry, scattering
mechanisms as well as backscattering properties and speckle are introduced. Afterwards,
the measuring principle behind PMR is presented. The SAR is the primary data source
while the PMR is subsidiary and therefore the focus on the SAR is emphasized.
2.1
Synthetic Aperture Radar
Radar has been utilized for monitoring sea ice conditions in Greenland since early 1960s
[10] mounted on aircrafts and Synthetic Aperture Radar or SAR have been onboard
satellites for the same purpose since 1980s. The SAR is an active system that generates a
propagating electromagnetic radar pulse with microwave frequencies and is transmitted
using an antenna/transmitter. The signal propagates through space and interacting
with an object will cause part of the signal to be absorbed and reflected, depending on
the dielectric properties of the surface. The signal propagates back to the system and
is measured by a receiver. Some antennas function as both transmitter and receivers.
The SAR system also utilize the electromagnetic polarization of the radar wave. A dual-
polarized SAR transmit electromagnetic signals in alternating sequences of horizontal
(H) and vertical (V) polarization and measures using polarized antennas. This produces
four combinations of transmission and receiver channels, HH, HV and VV, VH.
SAR operates independently of sun light by providing illumination in the form of the
radar wave, which allows for uninterrupted observations during the Arctic polar night.
Another advantage of microwave frequencies is the ability of the electromagnetic radar
pulses to penetrate through thick clouds and rain with low interference from atmospheric
effects [11]. Combined, this allows monitoring with minimal disturbance and maximal
measurement time.

2.1.1
SAR Geometry
A SAR is operated on a moving platform such as an aircraft or satellite to map and cover
large areas. The SAR is mounted with an inclination towards the measurement surface
as illustrated in figure 2.1 - so called side-looking. Valuable images are generated by
alternating between transmitting and receiving radar signals and arranging reflections
line by line. The view of the SAR can be imagined as a 2D field with two coordinates,
the (slant) range r and the along track, x, denoted azimuth. The distance between the
radar and a point on the surface is the slanted range, R. The distance from the ground to
the SAR is the height H and the ground range is denoted y. The coverage is determined
by the antenna footprint and it’s gain pattern. The swath is the area mapped by the
SAR.
The inclination of the SAR can also be expressed as the incidence angle. It expresses
the angle between the range and the perpendicular point towards the ground and is
illustrated as θ in figure 2.2a. Evidently, the incidence angle changes depending on the
ground range distance from the SAR.

Scattering Mechanisms
The received radar signal is largely dependent on the surface properties. Different mate-
rial will absorb varying amounts of the incoming electromagnetic wave and will scatter
the wave in various directions ([13], p. 202). The SAR can only measure the reflected
wave that returns to the physical location of the receiving antenna. Figure 2.3 exhibits

2.1.3

Each SAR image consists of measurement pixels and denoted resolution cells. A cell’s

cell is a sum of the amplitude and phase components contributions from all the scatter-
ers in the cell, that contain both destructive and constructive elements. The reflected
signal for N scatterers can be expressed as [14]:

Improving uniformity in SAR images can be accomplished by utilizing speckle filters

2.2
Passive Microwave Radiometer
Like the SAR, PMR is also capable of operating independently of the sun, but instead
of using an active illumination, the PMR measures the electromagnetic illumination
coming from the Earth’s surface. All matter emits electromagnetic radiation in various
quantities depending on temperature, dielectric properties of the material and the inci-
dence angle. The quantity of radiation also varies across the electromagnetic spectrum
([15], p. 7). The radiation thus expresses useful information of an object and gives the
motivation for measuring it. The PMR is capable of measuring in the electromagnetic
microwave spectrum and it consists of an antenna, a wideband receiver, and a recording
device. However, the measurements are not ideal, as only theoretical objects absorb all
incoming radiation and emits an equal amount with respect to its physical temperature.

CHAPTER 3
Sea Ice
Sea ice is a complicated physical phenomenon in the high Northern and Southern regions
of Earth with ever changing conditions and distinct features. Observing sea ice over
longer time-periods is difficult as it is constantly moving, melting, and freezing. The
Arctic region is covered by between 11 % to 15 % of sea ice and permanently exist above
72◦C latitude [17]. Generally, two types of sea ice are distinguished - First-Year Ice (FYI)
and MultiYear Ice (MYI), each with diverse electromagnetic signatures, differentiating
between themselves and open water. Understanding the sea ice composition and present
features is key to interpret the electromagnetic signatures. To address this, this chapter
provides an overview of ice types, formation, and features. This is followed by a practical
example of the electromagnetic signatures of open water and sea ice in both SAR and
PMR images.
3.1
Sea Ice Formation and First-Year Ice
FYI can form in both calm and turbulent seas. When water in the mixed ocean layer
becomes sufficiently cold, it nucleates in the upper water columns and forms a dispersed
disk of needle-shaped ice crystals that is termed frazil ([18] p. 48). The less dense frazil
floats to the sea surface in calm waters. Here, it merges and forms a greasy-like ice
floating on top of the water and resembles oil slick. As salt does not freeze, the frazil
crystals will expel the natural ocean salt and therefore consist of nearly purely fresh
water. With continuous growth, a very thin and dark cover of sea ice is formed and
termed nilas. The ice cover will become visibly lighter as the thickness increases. Due
to ocean movements from currents or wind, a process termed rafting can occur, which
pushes the nilas together, causing sliding over and under other ice layers. The cover
becomes more stable with increasing thickness and the bottom surface becomes smooth,
which forms the so-called congelation ice [19]. To further increase the thickness, more
congelation ice needs to be added as new frazil ice is unable to form under the ice.
If the ocean on the other hand is rough, the accumulation of frazil ice turns into
a slushy circular disk called pancake ice [19]. The turbulent sea pushes the pancake
ice together forcefully, creating raised ridges or edges. Bending, fractures and ice pile
up can occur due to ridging.
This forms a keel structure on the bottom of the ice.
Eventually, a coherent ice sheet is cemented together from pancake ice. Congelation ice
typically has a smooth bottom surface where pancake ice has a rough one. Over time
with continuous growth, FYI will eventually form.
FYI is relatively uniform with a surface salinity that remains above 6 % through
==PAGE:28==
14
3 Sea Ice
the winter ([18], p. 51). During winter, snow accumulates on the surface and the ice
continues to grow. With increasing temperatures during spring and summer, FYI melts.
Depending on the severity of the local temperatures and ice thickness, FYI may melt
completely. If the ice remains frozen over the summer till winter, it becomes MYI and
exhibit different properties.
3.2
Multiyear Ice
During the summer melt, new and distinct features are created in the ice with pieces
of drained, saline, and freshwater ice. Features encapsulate hummock fractures, melt
ponds and relict snow ice surfaces [20]. When the surface snow and ice melt, it washes
through the top ice layer, which lowers the salinity and creates small air pockets. MYI
hummocks that has gone through multiple summer melts can be sufficiently fresh to
drink.
Due to ocean currents and atmospheric circulation, MYI is more common in the
Arctic compared to the Antarctic waters. The currents in the Antarctica waters cause
most sea ice to migrate North to warmer waters and the inevitable melt [20]. In the
Weddell Sea, some MYI persists. The relatively landlocked nature of the Arctic Ocean
has allowed for extensive MYI covers to form. Sea ice in the Arctic is driven by wind
and ocean currents moving it across the Arctic. Particularly, the Fram Strait East of
Greenland funnels a lot of sea ice into the Atlantic Ocean.

backscatter depending on the surface roughness. Snow on smooth surfaces exhibit a
relatively larger response compared to snow on rough surface, which is most evident at
higher frequencies. Furthermore, the electrical properties of snow changes depending on
the moisture level. In addition, melt pools may occur during summer melt.

73).

the surface appears rougher with thicker snow covers that create stronger backscatter
in comparison to FYI. In addition, MYI is also lower in salinity, which allow the radar
wave to penetrate further into the ice. For these reasons, MYI will generally exhibit a
stronger electromagnetic response in the SAR image.

The ASIP Data Set
This chapter provides a characterization of the data used in this project, namely the
Automatic Sea Ice Products or ASIP / AI4ARCTIC data set. The data set was created
to allow multiple actors to develop automatic sea ice products for a wide array of applica-
tions. The data set has been compiled by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU),
Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) and HARNVIG Arctic and Maritime. The initial
version of the data set was released the 6th of March 2020 [21] and the second version
released on the 2nd of October 2020 [9] (this reference includes the data set manual),
which is compromised of 461 scenes. This project utilizes a pre-release of the second
version with only 438 scenes, but otherwise identical to the official release. Onward, this
pre-release will be referred to as ASIP V2 or simply ASIP, interchangeably.
Each scene contain a Sentinel-1 dual polarised (HH and HV) SAR image, auxiliary
image parameters, the corresponding sea ice chart derived from the image and complete
with an ice code look-up table and AMSR2 PMR measurements onboard the JAXA
GCOM-W satellite. Brightness temperatures have been resampled to 2 km grid-spacing
and aligned geographically to match Sentinel-1 measurements. The components of the
ASIP data set is described in the following sections, initiated with the Sentinel-1 SAR
specifications, followed by the AMSR2 PMR and finally the DMI sea ice chart is intro-
duced.
4.1
Sentinel-1 SAR
The Copernicus program is one of the most ambitious and far reaching Earth observa-
tions programs and developed in collaboration between ESA and the European Com-
mission. The observer suite is made up of more than 30 individual satellites [6] and
expanding as ESA is developing more Sentinels. Currently 7 Sentinels orbit the Earth
[22] and two are equipped with 5.405 GHz center frequency (5.5 cm wavelength) C-band
SAR instruments - Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B.
Multiple operational modes exist for the Sentinel-1 satellites; the Stripmap (SM),
Interfeometric Wide swath (IW), Extra-Wide swath (EW) and Wave (WV). SM, EW
and IW products supports both single polarization (HH and VV) and dual polarization
(HH+HV and VV+VH) while WV only operates with VV polarisation over open ocean.
SM is utilized for small islands and emergency management, EW monitors coastal re-
gions including oil spill, ship traffic and sea-ice [23].
ASIP SAR data is exclusively generated using the EW operational mode. Multiple
processing chains are available for EW data, so called Level-0,1 and 2. Level-0 is raw

data, Level-1 is pre-processed, centroid estimated and single look complex focused [24].
This includes Ground Range Detected (GRD) products, which are used in the ASIP
data set with medium resolution (GRDM). GRD is focused, detected, multi-looked
and projected to the ground range [24] with no phase information. Furthermore, dual

4.2
AMSR2 PMR
The intensity of microwave emissions, scattering from the surface and the atmosphere
of the Earth are accurately measured by passive microwave radiometry. The Advanced
Microwave Scanning Radiometer 2 (AMSR2) is one of the contemporary instruments
providing these measurements. Every 1.5 seconds the antenna rotates and creates a
1450 km swath. The instrument measures both V and H polarization in the frequencies
listed in table 4.1. Having 7 frequency bands enables different microwave emissions from
the ice to be measured. The 6.925 GHz and 7.3 GHz frequency bands are included to
complement each other due to the observation of commercial radio transmission noise
in the frequency spectrum.
Selected AMSR2 data for the ASIP data set is based on the geographical and tempo-
ral overlap with Sentinel-1 coverage. The temporal window is seven hours preceding the
Sentinel-1 SAR image [9] and if multiple AMSR2 acquisitions are available, the most
recent is selected. Overlapping brightness temperature pixels are resampled to align
with Sentinel-1 pixels by applying a Gaussian weighted interpolation from the Python
Pyresample library [9]. Resampled AMSR2 pixels match the coordinates of every 50th
pixel in the Sentinel-1 swath, providing brightness temperatures for every 2 km. As
indicated by table 4.1, lower frequency channels are more upsampled than higher fre-
quencies. Restraining from upsampling to higher pixel density allows the ASIP data set
user to choose how to incorporate the AMSR2 data best in a model. AMSR2 swaths
may have missing pixel areas due to lack of temporal overlap.

5.1
Feedforward Neural Network
The feedforward neural network or Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is a fundamental part
of deep learning models with the goals of approximating a function that can correctly
map an input x to a category y. The mapping can be expressed as y = f(x : θ) and finds
the value of θ that gives the best function approximation ([29], p. 168). Information
flows through the function, starting with input x, then computed to define the function
and finally output as y. This is a forward flow, which gives rise to the terminology.
Deep learning models are often associated with networks as they are created by a
multitude of functions that are interconnected. For example, a model is created in a
chain with three function, f (1), f (2), f (3), forming f(x) = f (3)(f (2)(f (1)))) ([29], p. 168).
Each of these functions are layers and can be visualized in figure 5.1. f (1) is called the
input layer, f (3) is output layer and in between them, f (2) constitutes a hidden layer.
This layer is called hidden because the layer performs calculations that are not visualized.
The length of the function chain describes the depth of the model. It is key, that the
algorithm decides itself how to utilize the layers to create a model that produces the
best result.

5.2
Gradient-Based Learning
The goal of deep learning algorithms is to optimize through minimization (or maximiza-
tion) of a function f(x) by adjusting x. In minimization this function is usually termed
criterion, loss, cost or error function. The derivative f ′(x) gives the slope of f(x) at x
and specifies how to change x to make small improvements in the output. By changing
x in small steps with opposite sign of the derivative, the function f(x) can be minimized.

Adam
The learning rate is a difficult hyperparameter to select and may not work equally each

5.3
The Convolutional Neural Network
The CNN is a type of deep learning algorithm that has been very successful in computer
visions tasks. The name comes from the applied linear mathematical operation called
convolution that replaces at least one layer of typical matrix multiplication used in neural
networks. A CNN consists of two parts, a feature learning segment, and a classification
segment. Feature learning utilizes multiple chains of convolutional layers that in our
case refers to the layer that performs the convolutional operation (simple terminology),
which is followed by an activation function. Other useful operations are pooling layers.
The feature learning segment has the purpose to reduce the input into compressed and
simplified information that is easier to process, but without losing important features.
Classification is achieved by evaluating the different feature maps and select the optimal
combination of features. There exist several classification approaches depending on the
task. This could be a fully connected layer, e.g. figure 5.1 or a convolutional layer.
The different operations of the CNN will now be elaborated, starting with the convo-
lutional operation, followed by the activation function, pooling, the classification layer
and finalized with the introduction of the utilized CNN architecture.

The convolutional operation leverages three concepts that improves learning and
computational efficiency and demands. These are sparse interactions, parameter sharing
and equivariance.
5.3.1.1
Sparse Interactions
As mentioned earlier, neural networks typically apply matrix multiplication with param-
eters representing each interaction between the layer input and each individual unit in
the layer. This implies that every unit interacts with every input as in the case of figure
5.1. Convolution on the other hand draws advantage from sparse interactions, which
limits the number of interactions between layer input and layer units ([29] p. 335). It
is achieved by applying a kernel that is smaller than the input similar to figure 5.4. It
is possible to detect small meaningful features that allows us to store fewer parameters
that reduces the memory requirements and enhances statistical efficiency while fewer
computational operations are required.
5.3.1.2
Parameter Sharing
In a traditional neural net layer, there are a great number of units with individual
parameters that are used only once when computing the output of the layer. The kernel
can reuse the same parameter across the entire image and thus share it. This implies
that it is not necessary to learn a separate set of parameters for every location and
restricted to learn only one. The benefit of this is the reduction in storage requirements
([29], p. 335).
5.3.1.3
Equivariance
The type of parameter sharing mentioned in section 5.3.1.2 introduces the equivariance
property. This implies that if the input changes, the output changes the same way. If
we move objects in the input, the 2D feature map created by the convolution will be
moved the same amount in the output. However, convolution is not equivariant to all
transformations. Scaling or rotation of an image requires other mechanisms such as
pooling.
5.3.2
Activation Function
The activation function is a mathematical operation that determines whether neurons
in a layer will activate (whether to pass information onward or not) depending on a rule
or threshold. Individual artificial neuron operations are linear in design. Therefore, to
create complex relations between input and output, non-linearity is introduced through
the activation function. This allows the network to represent almost any type of function
([29], p. 172).

Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) is the default recommendation ([29], p. 174). This is
due to the relative ease of optimization from its similarity to linear units and being
computationally efficient. The ReLU function is described as:
g(z) = max{0, z}
(5.8)
This outputs zero in half its domain, which makes it non-linear. This gives a large
derivative when the unit is active. The activation function is illustrated in figure 5.5. In
practice, if a neuron forwards a negative value, the information is not passed forward.
If it is positive, the information continuous.

5.3.3
Pooling
The pooling operation is similar to convolution. It too utilizes a kernel with dilation,
stride and can be padded to avoid shrinking image size. The output size can be calcu-
lated with the same approach to equation 5.7. However, it applies a different mathe-
matical operation and is typically summary statistics of the windowed area. In figure
5.6, often utilized pooling functions are displayed - max and average pooling. These
operations extract the maximum value in the window and calculates the average of the
area, respectively. Generally, pooling enables the representation to be robust to small
translations or changes in the input e.g. as rotation or movement of pixels and makes
it invariant ([29], p. 342). The invariance to translation implies that the values of the
pooled output does not change if the input is altered slightly. This can be an advantage
for detecting certain features, but not the exact location of them. If pooling is applied
to the convolutional output, the algorithms can learn, which transformations to become
invariant to.

Classification Layer
The classification section of the network evaluates the feature maps to produce the final

argmax function is used afterwards to find the class with the highest probability, thereby
classifying the pixel.

to figure 5.1, that predicts one pixel at a time. A different approach emerged in 2015
[33] using convolutional layers with a 1x1 kernel to do this evaluation, which segments
the entire image instead of a single pixel at a time. This approach has been found to be
more computationally efficient and slightly more accurate for sea ice classification [34].
There are many popular and well documented architectures for segmentation prob-

5.4
Regulization Techniques
In deep learning, we are interested in a performance measure, P, which may be the
number of correctly guessed outputs. By reducing the cost function J(θ), we hope to
improve P. However, blindly reducing the cost function, deep learning models can risk
overfitting, which lower the training error, but increases the validation error. Reguliza-
tion techniques are used to avoid this by fitting a function appropriately. Four such
techniques are Batch Normalization, Dropout, L2 Regulization and early stopping.
5.4.1
Batch Normalization
During model training the gradient is updated for each parameter under the assumption
that other layers are constant. However, all layers are updated simultaneously under the
assumption that each other are constant. This can lead to weights changing drastically
from layer to layer, which can lead to unexpected results. By normalizing the training
batch, layers are adjusted to the same scale, this speeds up learning and higher learning
rates can be applied. Batch normalization normalizes all units in a layer the following
way ([29], p. 318):
H′ = H −µ

where H is the original unit value, µ is the layer unit average and σ is the standard
deviation. Batch normalization can be applied on any input or hidden layer in a network.
5.4.2
Dropout
Dropout is a powerful and computationally inexpensive method of regularizing a model
that minimizes the risk of overfitting and can be utilized to train many variants a model
([29], p. 259). This is achieved by randomly deactivating or dropping out units in the
layers. The layer is then trained without the dropped units. Due to the probabilistic
behaviour, the next training iteration, new units will be deactivated. The method can
be applied to any or all layers but is not used on the output layer. Dropout and batch
normalization can be applied together. However, some disharmonies exist between the
two functions, as dropout removes information and may skew the mean and variance
used to batch normalize. To mitigate this, one approach is to apply dropout after batch
normalization [38].
5.4.3

5.4.4
Early Stopping
A common observation when training large models with tendencies to overfit, is the
training loss decreasing continuously over time, but that the validation loss reaches a
minimum point and begins to grow. By saving model parameters as checkpoints after
loss calculations, it is possible to return to that model version, once a full training cycle
is complete. This ensures that the best performing model can be chosen. The strategy is
commonly known as early stopping ([29] p. 246) and one of the most popular regulization
strategies due to its effectiveness and simplicity.

5.5
Previous SIC Algorithm Attempts
As in multiple other fields, the emergence of deep learning has changed the approach
to image classification and segmentation. Previously, attempts at automating sea ice
charting involved using features, which were hand-designed by experts followed by a
neural network to determine the optimal combination and weighting of the features.
Early attempts with using neural networks to classify sea ice started as early as 1999
with [41] using a Pulse-Coupled Neural Network and improved with [42]. In 2005 [43]
initial attempts were made at fusing satellite data to classify sea ice that illustrated
superior results to singular data sources. In [44] J. Karvonen attempted to fuse SAR
and AMSR2 data with hand-crafted features that a MLP used to estimate sea ice con-
centration. First attempts at applying CNN to classify SIC from Dual-Pol SAR images
(RADARSAT-2) was carried out by Lei Wang [45] in the Beaufort Sea. In [46], Lei Wang
compared the SIC classification performance and highlighted the improvements of CNN
in relation to MLP. These two CNN architectural structures perform classification using
a fully-connected layer to do pixel-wise classification. A computational improvement was
highlighted in [34] by Lei Wang with slightly improved accuracy achieved by utilizing
the FCNN.
Recent advancements have fused SAR and AMSR2 data in a CNN SIC algorithm.
In [47] authors present a FCNN architecture applying a DeepLabv3 style network [48]).
The input data is SAR HH/HV 300 x 300 pixels i.e. 12 km x 12 km and 7 AMSR2
frequency channels with both H/V polarizations amounting to 14 total channels in 6
x 6 pixels (also 12 km x 12 km).
6 convolutional layers with the ReLU activation
function is applied together with two instances of dropout and batch normalization and
Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) is then applied with four different dilation

6.2.2
Patch Size
The patch size decides the scale of all samples. Larger patch sizes are generally desired
for high model prediction performance with the trade-off of requiring significantly more
computational memory and time. It should also be noted that patch size only impacts
the training and validation performance and not the final inference due to the applied
inference technique. This will be presented and discussed in section 6.6.
To fully utilize the deepest layer of the implemented U-Net model’s receptive field of
140 pixels presented in section 6.5, the patch size should be at minimum this size. With
a receptive field of 140 pixels and a resolution of 80 m, pixel classification is performed
using features with sizes of 11.2 km x 11.2 km.
The patch size should also be in order of 2n because the pooling layers half the image
size four times. Otherwise, information will be lost during the decoder section of the
network (number of pixels must be integers) and additional padding must be carried
out during skip connections to ensure equal feature map size when concatenating. This
leaves two ideal and practically feasible (in terms of available memory and data) patch
sizes, 256 x 256 and 512 x 512 pixels.

6.4
Data Distribution and Augmentation
The 294 scenes remaining for training and validation contain 28.023 256 x 256 patches,
which are split into two separate sets. A common approach to creating a validation set
is to randomly sample a percentage, e.g. 10 % of pixels. However, due to the skewed
nature of this class distribution, the validation set would reflect this with a high number
of open-water pixels. The model needs to be tested on whether it can correctly classify
intermediate sea ice concentrations and full sea ice cover and not just open water. The
validation set size is selected to be 10 % of the least represented class, i.e. 10 % SIC,
with equal distribution across all classes and the threshold is thus ∼4M pixels. Patches
are selected randomly and only one full patch of water or 100 % SIC and a maximum
of 6 patches per scene is added to the validation to ensure a broader representation.
This ensures that the algorithm is validated equally on all classes with data across
different regions and seasons. The subsequent class distribution of the original training
and validation data is illustrated in figure 6.3a blue bars and 6.3b, respectively. The

10 % - 90 % SIC and this makes it desirable to include data augmentation for these
classes. However, it is paramount to perform augmentation after the initial training and

6.5
U-Net Implementation
The U-Net overarching architecture, as described in section 5.3.5 is implemented in the
Deep Learning Python library PyTorch version 1.6. Layer specifics from figure 5.7 is
changed to accommodate the design choices in section 6.2. A U-Net version with initial
feature maps of 32 is illustrated in figure 6.4 and the design choices accommodated.
To retain the initial input size at the output, padding is applied at every convolution
as described in section 5.3.1.
This ensures that it is not necessary to crop feature
maps during the skip connection concatenation step (feature maps must be equal size).
However, zero padding adds no information of what is beyond the image horizon and
skews predictions in a certain direction. Instead, it is assumed that the scene extends
beyond the horizon. Such an approach is implemented using replication padding, which
extend the image with replications of border pixels. In other words, the border pixels
are copied or replicated.
During size doubling in the ascending portion of the U-Net as described in section
5.3.5, the deconvolution is applied. However, this may cause a checkerboard phenomenon
to emerge as described in [56]. A computationally cheap and effective alternative exist
- upsampling using bilinear interpolation, which also double the image size.
After each convolutional layer batch normalization and the ReLU activation function,
described in section 5.3.2, is applied. This is accompanied by the dropout regulization
method, following the recommendations from [38] as discussed in section 5.4.2. The
initial layers and levels of the U-Net have a limited number of feature maps to dropout.
Therefore, an incremental dropout rate is applied with an initial rate of 10 % in the first
level, increased to 20 % in the second level, and 25 % in the third, fourth and bottom
level. The ascending portion mirror the dropout rate.
The classification is carried out by the 1x1 convolutional kernel that transforms the
previous convolutional feature maps into 11 features maps, one representing each class
with varying negative and positive numbers. The softmax function is applied to these
feature maps to map these values into probabilities between 0 and 1. Hereby, the model
assigns a probability of each pixel belonging to a certain class. The final step is to select
==PAGE:64==
50
6 Algorithm Design and Implementation
the most certain probability (the highest) using the ArgMax function. This collapses
the 11 feature maps into a single map with the final predictions corresponding to the
segmentation output map.
The model parameters such as layer width (number of feature maps), whether to
use certain types of regulization, learning rate etc. has been selected based on extensive
trial and error. For each consecutive model run, one specific parameter is altered and
run for between 20 and 100 epochs to determine an increase or decrease in performance.
For sake of simplicity and clarity every attempt will not be summarized. Through this
approach, the before mentioned parameters, model width and a learning rate, ϵ = 1·10−4
was selected. The L2 regulization, described in section 5.4.3, was incorporated with the
AdamW [40] optimizer with λ = 1 · 10−4. Final model experiments run for 100 epochs
and was chosen as little to no improvements occurred after this.

6.5.2
Metrics
Choosing how to evaluate the algorithm is key to determine its performance. A common
metric is the model’s accuracy, which is a measure of the total True Positives in compar-
ison to the total number of predictions. However, this is an insufficient metric in case
of imbalanced data sets. In addition, accuracy only measures whether the prediction is
exact without considering the proximity to the correct answer.
In case of sea ice, there are two fallacies regarding accuracy. Firstly, images often have
an abundance of water pixels or 100 % SIC (imbalanced data). Therefore, the accuracy
may be very high, because the model can predict these two types of classes very well
but may not have correctly predicted any of the less represented classes. Secondly, SIC
can be viewed as a linear function from 0 to 100. Therefore, if the SIC in a pixel is 80 %,
a prediction of 70 or 90 % is far superior to a prediction of 0 % SIC. Therefore, relying
only on accuracy is not optimal. To reflect this, the regression R2 or Coefficient of
Determination metric will be utilized as well. R2 is a measure of how well the regression
predictions fit the true classes in comparison to using the mean as a prediction. It is
defined as [58]:

where SSres is the residual sum of squared model error (total squared error of our
model) and SStot is the sum of squared mean errors (total squared error if the mean was
used to predict). The R2 value can be 1 or a negative value and it can be interpreted as
a percentage [58]. A value of 1 entails a perfect model fit, 0 implies SSres = SStot and
equivalent to the model and mean performing equally well. If the R2 is negative, the
model is a worse fit than the mean. Hereby, the best performing model will be selected
based on the ability to achieve the highest possible R2 score on the validation data set.
To further supplement the accuracy, the confusion matrix will be utilized.
The
confusion matrix is not a measure, but gives insight into the accuracy by showcasing the
accuracy for each class and what wrong predictions are classified as [59]. Each predicted
class can be directly compared to the true classes. If 100 % accuracy is achieved, the
diagonal of the confusion matrix would contain all predictions, implying all predicted
classes are equal to the true classes. With the 11 SIC classes, the matrix will be 11x11,
with rows representing the true classes and columns the predicted.
6.5.3
Hardware Setup and Training
Graphical Processing Units (GPU) for this project were accessible on a Lenovo Desktop
equipped with two Intel Xeon Gold 6136 Central Processing Unit (CPU), 192 GB of
RAM and two NVIDIA QUADRO RTX 8000. Each GPU have 48 GB of high-speed
memory. This allows for relatively large models, batch sizes, efficient running times, and
large inference images. The batch size was selected to be 64 - an order of 2n, which is
generally recommended. This size allowed the model to train individually on a single
GPU while allowing for maximum speed. Large computational performance gains were
acquired using the PyTorch multiprocess dataloader. The loader takes advance of the
CPU and GPU being separate entities that can run in parallel independently of each
other. While the GPU is training the model, the CPU will fetch and prepare the next
batch. Because fetching and preparing data on a single CPU thread is the bottleneck in
the training pipeline, the GPU finishes single batches before the next is ready. This is
mitigated by utilizing build-in CPU multi-threading. This allows for multiple parallel
threads to fetch and prepare batches simultaneously. In effect, the GPU can train the
model uninterrupted and thereby, training is bottlenecked by the GPU capability, which
is the ideal scenario.

Inference is the final step in model creation.
It is the act of testing the model on
new and unseen data.
In context of SIC chart maps, it is tested whether a model
can correctly predict segmentation of SIC on large images contrary to small patches.
During inference, the model is not trained and therefore no backpropagation is required,
freeing up memory. Furthermore, dropout is disabled and batch normalization does
not track individual batch mean and variance, but instead uses the running mean and
variance previously obtained during model training. Since FCNNs are not bound by

7.5 Comparisons and Review
71
7.5
Comparisons and Review
This section will highlight some of the strength and weaknesses of the different mod-
els implemented and compare the results. Additionally, biases in the reference truth
ice charts are discussed and potential solutions are proposed.
The metrics used for
evaluation are examined and other considerations are summarized.
7.5.1
Model Intercomparisons
With results available, the four U-Net models can be compared. According to the vali-
dation R2-score and scene inference, the performance order is SAR < SAR + incidence
angle < SAR + PMR + incidence angle < SAR + PMR. There is a clear advantage
of supplying the model with AMSR2 data, which predict slightly more consistently and
does not classify 100 % SIC as open water in regions with low variability in the SAR
image. However, there are a couple of downsides as well. Firstly, using the PMR seems
to decrease the resolution of the model prediction slightly. This is likely due to the
poor AMSR2 resolution. The temporal spacing between the SAR image and AMSR2
measurements could also pose an issue, where sea ice has moved between data capture.
Similarly, in section 6.3 it was mentioned that a small pixel shift occur in the AMSR2
data. The models do not seem too affected by these to factors but may shift the ice
edge slightly. From an operational perspective, adding an additional data source re-
quires a more extensive data pipeline and makes the supply chain dependent on two
data sources. If there are periods of no sufficient spatial and temporal overlap between
Sentinel-1 and AMSR2, the model would be unable to produce ice charts. A similar
scenario would occur if the satellite operating the AMSR2 instrument was undergoing
maintenance or seized to operate etc. Because of these reasons, having an algorithm
using both data sources can be unreliable in certain situations and likely a backup model
using just Sentinel-1 SAR data should be available. Therefore, more research should be
carried out using only the Sentinel-1 system to find potential solutions to the low SAR
variability issue. If sufficient performance is obtained, this type of model could provide
higher resolution while reducing data pipeline complexity and vulnerabilities leading to
ease of adoption.
There are multiple adaptations, which could be applied to the U-Net model that may
improve the prediction of open water instead of 100 % SIC. Applying different types of
loss functions could allow the model to better understand the regressional nature of SIC.
An example could be to apply a similar regression-type binary cross entropy function
such as the one utilized in [47]. Another option could be to add an auxiliary term to
the current loss function, which takes regression into consideration.
Experimenting with the use of batch normalization may also be beneficial. During
training, the U-Net model uses batch normalization, which is an excellent regulization
technique. However, it may lower potential amplitude differences and make the model
rely more on textural features in the images. Another option would be to increase the
receptive field of the model to include more pixels. If the receptive field is increased,
the training and validation patch size could also be increase i.e. 512x512 pixels. From
==PAGE:86==
72
7 Results and Discussion
personal experience, when identifying whether areas with low SAR variance is water or
ice, it is beneficial to locate the ice edge and backtrack to the location of doubt. The
model may gain a similar capability with a larger receptive field, which could be applied
by adding an additional level in the U-Net.
7.5.2
Reference Truth Biases and Potential Solutions
Inspecting the results of the U-Net models, it is clear none of them are perfect replicas
of the DMI ice charts. There is a general tendency for the predictions to follow the
diagonal line in the confusion matrices. However, there are notable predictions swaying
away from it, particularly to neighbouring classes. As mentioned in section 4.3, the ice
charts have uncertainties of ∼20%. If the inference error image of scene #2 using the U-
Net trained only on SAR data presented in figure 7.3, is produced again acknowledging
the uncertainty of 20 % by adding a margin of error of this magnitude, the error image
in figure 7.12 is obtained. The image is left with the same error features B-D as in figure
7.3. As discussed in section 7.1, these error areas could be contributed to higher detail
or resolution of the model compared to the ice analyst. Particularly, the sea ice edge
errors are visible in this image and clearly illustrate the model classifying the ice edge
with pixel-wise precision. This version achieves an accuracy of 75.05 % and an R2-score
of 83.25 %. Likewise, the U-Net trained on both SAR and PMR data attain an accuracy
of 74.99 % and an R2-score of 84.59 %. These scores are very respectable. It should
be noted, that applying these kinds of error margins should be carefully implemented
particularly for open water, as not to lure vessels into areas suspected of being ice-free
and then encountering sea ice.
Generally, a very high accuracy without acknowledging the estimated 20 % uncer-
tainty in the DMI ice charts should not be expected for this type of model, except when
classifying scenes with solely open water or complete sea ice cover. Rather having a very
high accuracy should be reason for concern. The reference truths have been compiled
by many sea ice analysts with varying experience and who produce ice charts using a
creative process. Therefore, the model is trained on reference truths that likely contain
many different personal biases.
Particularly the intermediate sea ice concentrations
could be affected by this. Further investigating the inference scenes, some of the true
ice charts’ labelling may be debatable. For instance, scene #1 has been labelled as to
contain relatively large areas with SIC of 100 %. Inspecting the SAR HH, HV and the
AMSR2 89 GHz V channel in figure 6.5 or appendix A.1- A.2, at least to the untrained
eye, it does not appear that much sea ice is present in the scene. It may have been more
appropriate to label this area with lower SIC. Naturally, there is the possibility that
a thin cover of sea ice is present, which is not apparent to an untrained eye or to the
algorithm. Arguably, this scene is very difficult to label, due to the strong wind and the
sea ice being scattered in various concentrations throughout the scene. Therefore, the
ice analyst may have labelled the scene very conservative to ensure safe navigation in
the area. Removing biases or at least converging them, could be achieved by applying
semi-supervised model techniques. Using such models, could yield better generalized
representations and potentially produce better results ([29], p. 244).

7.5.3
The Use of Metrics for Evaluation
Another aspect to be discussed is the choice of metrics used to evaluate these models.
Clearly the accuracy is not a very effective approach to determine model performance
if a 20 % uncertainty bias is present in the reference truth. Naturally, by training on a
large enough data set, the bias should be diminishing. However, the results do not seem
to fully indicate this. The R2 coefficient is arguably better than the accuracy, but as
seen when evaluating the #3 inference scene, this metric has issues too. Other popular
segmentation metrics includes the Jaccard index, also known as Intersection over union,
which is a statistical measure of similarity between two sample sets as described in [61].
However, this is also a discrete classification metric and does not reflect proximity to
the correct answer. A possible solution could be to weight certain predictions as more
important, such as open water and 100 % SIC. However, this raises the question of how
it should be weighted without adding a bias from the model creator. Applying an error
margin of 20 % uncertainty could also be implemented. Yet another solution could be to
limit the amount of SIC classes. This could be achieved by increasing the incremental
concentration change to 20 % or 30 %. This would arguably also make it easier for
end-users to interpret the ice charts.
Overall, metrics are a good approach to simplify large quantities of data and is a
==PAGE:88==
74
7 Results and Discussion
pragmatic approach to evaluation. In this project, inspecting the inference visually have
been a very useful tool to evaluate the model performance. However, this approach is
significantly slower and may add another bias. For instance, choosing a specific model
version based on the inference results directly adds a bias of what the model creator
perceives as the best solution. Therefore, using such an approach should be carried out
in close collaboration between the model creator and sea ice analysts.
7.5.4
Other Aspects and Considerations
Two additional considerations could be taking or implemented to improve the model
performance and testing of it. Firstly, the validation approach described in section 6.2
where validation and training patches are separated from the same scenes at random.
This can be improved to avoid potential spatial biases. The model could have been
trained on data that ultimately is spatially correlated to the validation data.
This
could be accomplished by splitting training and validation at scene level instead of at
patch level. Therefore, creating a validation set comprising of multiple scenes scattered
across seasons and regions could be a superior approach. In this manner, the validation
data does not have to follow the patch size layout of the training data. However, this
process makes it more difficult to have a balanced distribution of classes. Creating such
a validation set could be used as an official common approach to compare different sea
ice concentration models. No such approach is available, and this is one reason why it is
not possible to directly compare the results of the models created here to other models,
such as the ones in [47].
The second aspect is, that two ASIP data sets exists, Version 1 and 2. As this thesis
only took advantage of V2, the amount of training data could be potentially doubled.
An obstacle of the initial ASIP version, was the SAR processing chain. Particularly in
the SAR HV channel, the transition between swaths was very noisy and apparent as
illustrated in figure 3.4. Results in [47] suggests that the transition also affected the
final inference as small areas of grained pixels occur. However, by having both ASIP
versions, the model should be capable of learning to ignore these swath transitions and
may improve overall model robustness, which is the usual argument for during noise
data augmentation [62].

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This project was carried out with the purpose of investigating potential means of au-
tomating sea ice concentration charting around Greenland using supervised learning
methods.
The relevant theory of the measurement techniques have been presented,
encompassing the active SAR and passive PMR instruments. An overview of sea ice
formation, types and theoretical as well as practical electromagnetic signatures was illus-
trated. The Automated Sea Ice Products, ASIP, data set was presented with the unique
scene composition combining Sentinel-1 SAR and AMSR2 and an equivalent ice chart
created by the Danish Meteorological Institute. The sea ice chart was described with
uncertainty assessments and a practical example from North Eastern Greenland was pre-
sented. A theoretical description was given of the artificial intelligence methodologies
applied, such as optimization, the Convolutional Neural Network operation layers and
regulization techniques. The proposed U-Net architecture was highlighted and previous
attempts at automating sea ice concentration charting was summarized. An overview
of the algorithm design and implementation was outlined, which described the choices
made regarding resolution, training and validation patch size. Further highlights include
the data distribution and pipeline as well as the approach to inference and model exper-
iments carried out. Finally, results of the experiments were presented and discussed.
Experiments conclude that the U-Net architecture described in section 6.5 is capable
of producing highly detailed sea ice concentration maps with open water, intermediate
sea ice concentrations as well as full sea ice cover. The inference scenes are shown to
resemble those created by professional sea ice analysts, but with finer resolution and at
times highlighting sea ice not classified by the ice analyst. Furthermore, the model could
identify the location of the ice edge with higher precision. An obstacle in using the SAR
trained U-Net is the incapability of identifying high concentration sea ice in areas with
low variability in the SAR HH and HV polarization images, which may be misclassified
as open water. This will be a topic for future research. Adding the SAR incidence
angle yielded improvements to the U-Net performance and was shown to mitigate the
misclassification of high sea ice concentration slightly.
Fusing SAR and PMR was found to produce best results when done at the input.
The results are superior to the SAR-only trained U-Net, due to the capability of cor-
rectly classifying areas with low SAR HH and HV polarization variability. However,
the resolution was seen to be lower and less precise. Another downside is the added
complexity cost and dependence in terms of the data pipeline needing to acquire data
from multiple sources. Adding the SAR incidence angle to the fused SAR and PMR
U-Net did not produce improved results with the utilized architecture.
Overall, the different U-Net models were able to generalize well on the training data
==PAGE:90==

8 Conclusion
and produce useful sea ice concentration maps. The fusion of SAR and AMSR2 was
able to produce the best results, but with accompanying downsides, that leaves room for
further research into models applying only SAR data to be desired. Particularly research
into improving the U-Net ability to identify high sea ice concentrations in areas with
low SAR HH and HV variance are needed.
Obtaining sufficient performance could
provide sea ice charts with higher resolution while reducing data pipeline complexities
and vulnerabilities. This area of applied artificial intelligence for Arctic use-cases is still
at an early stage and many experiments with model hyperparameters, loss functions
and architectures are yet to be carried out.

CHAPTER 9
Future Work
There are many directions future research into the topic of using Artificial Intelligence
to automate sea ice concentration chart production. The different aspects can be sep-
arated into model architecture, data set improvements and input data. In terms of
model architectures, experimenting with different receptive field sizes should be carried
out. This could involve comparing Convolutional Neural Network architectures such as
the U-Net and DeepLabV3. Other types of loss functions and hyperparameter tuning
could also be attempted. In terms of improvements for the U-Net models developed in
this thesis, the immediate future tasks should involve solving the models inability to
classify high sea ice concentrations in areas with low variability in the SAR HH and
HV polarization channels. Experiments with increasing the patch size to 512 pixels
and adding another level to the U-Net may also improve performance by increasing the
receptive field. Finally, research into semi-supervised learning techniques could further
improve performance.
Further research into the data input, such as how best to fuse Sentinel-1 SAR, the
system incidence angle and AMSR2 could be done. Using more data by including the
earlier ASIP V1 data set and include further data augmentation, such as injecting various
noise elements, could also improve the model’s ability to generalize. Implementing a
spatial cross validation set could see improvements in testing and should be a made
available to the sea ice community for ease of model comparison and potential future
competitions.
Discussions with sea ice analysts on how best to evaluate the model predictions,
both quantitatively and qualitatively, would be beneficial e.g. how should the model
best factor in the 20 % uncertainty bias in the ice charts. Is the obtained precision in
terms of sea ice edge location and classification resolution acceptable?
