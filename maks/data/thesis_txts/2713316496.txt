ABSTRACT 
As for now, the operation of filtration units at the recovery process of the Novo Nordisk facility is 
subjected to various disturbances from upstream and is heavily based on knowledge derived from 
experience. Operating and improving industrial filtration processes requires high level of process 
understanding. Mechanistic models are an excellent form of building knowledge for a system as they 
are based on physical phenomena through first principles. 
The aim of this MSc thesis is to improve and enhance the conceptual understanding, quantify the 
operational space, evaluate the realized performance of the production batches compared to the 
expected one and eventually, to identify opportunities for process optimization. 
A filtration unit published in literature was used as a case study. The purpose was to apply 
Mechanistic Analysis of Process Systems (MAPS) workflow and explore potential application of such 
a model. Finally, the method was applied to develop a dynamic model for an industrial case crossflow 
filtration system. 
The mechanistic model was built using the osmotic pressure model to estimate the flux. It 
successfully describes the dynamics of the system and provides insights in terms of phenomena; for 
example, it shows that concentration polarisation and fouling are the main reasons for flux decrease 
through operation. A sensitivity analysis was performed on inlet variables to map the operational 
space, showing that an increase on angular velocity of discs implies a higher pressure difference 
needed for maintaining the constant flow operation. 
Application of the model for pore blocking prediction was successfully achieved. It showed that, an 
operation strategy based on cleaning the membrane when pore blocking is identified, could account 
for at least a 12.28 % of permeate volume increase.

ABSTRAKT 
For nuværende, er driften af filtreringsenheder ved genvindingsprocessen på Novo Nordisk-
anlægget udsat for forskellige forstyrrelser fra forudgående processer og baseret på viden udledt af 
erfaring. Drift og forbedring af industrielle filtreringsprocesser kræver høj grad af procesforståelse. 
Mekanistiske modeller er en fremragende form for opbygning af viden til et system, da de er baseret 
på fysiske fænomener gennem første principper. 
Formålet med denne kandidatafhandling er at forbedre og forbedre den konceptuelle forståelse, 
kvantificere det operationelle rum, evaluere den realiserede ydeevne af produktionsbatcherne i 
forhold til den forventede og til sidst at identificere muligheder for procesoptimering. 
En filtreringsenhed publiceret i litteraturen blev brugt som et casestudie. Formålet var at anvende 
Mechanistic Analysis of Process Systems (MAPS) workflow og udforske mulig anvendelse af en 
sådan model. Endelig blev metoden anvendt til at udvikle en dynamisk model for et industrielt case 
crossflow-filtreringssystem. 
Den mekanistiske model blev bygget ved hjælp af den osmotiske trykmodel til at estimere fluxen. 
Den beskriver med succes systemets dynamik og giver indsigt i form af fænomener; f.eks. viser den, 
at koncentrationspolarisering og tilsmudsning er hovedårsagerne til fluxnedsættelse gennem drift. 
En følsomhedsanalyse blev udført på indløbsvariabler for at kortlægge det operationelle rum, hvilket 
viser, at en stigning i vinkelhastigheden af skiver indebærer en højere trykforskel, der er nødvendig 
for at opretholde driften med konstant flow. 
Anvendelse af modellen til forudsigelse af poreblokering blev opnået med succes. Det viste, at en 
operationsstrategi baseret på rensning af membranen, når poreblokering er identificeret, kunne 
tegne sig for mindst en stigning på 12.28 % af permeatvolumen.

1. Introduction  
The introduction to this MSc thesis is divided into four sections. A general introduction to Novo 
Nordisk A/S is made, followed by the background and motivation of this project and the problem 
definition. Lastly an overview on the thesis structure is presented. 
1.1. 
Novo Nordisk A/S 
Novo Nordisk is one of the biggest pharmaceutical companies in Europe [4], founded in 1923 in 
Denmark, where it is headquartered. With a focus on diabetes healthcare and other serious diseases 
such as obesity and rare endocrine and blood diseases. It employs more than 47,000 people around 
the world in their production sites, and research and development centres. Novo Nordisk supplies 
the 50% of the insulin in the world, helping more than 34 million people suffering from diabetes. Its 
social responsibility strategy is focused in three critical areas: preventing the rise of type 2 diabetes 
and obesity, providing access to affordable case for vulnerable patients in every country and driving 
innovation to improve lives. As of today, 1 in every 11 people in the world has diabetes, a quantity 
that is projected to rise to 1 in every 9 by 2045 [1,2,3]. 
Novo Nordisk puts a lot of effort into improving its production process at all levels. One of the points 
where improvement is necessary is the recovery process of its Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient 
(API). It is essential that full knowledge on the operation step is achieved, not only to reach an 
accurate prediction on the performance of the process but also to be able to maximize the recovery 
of the product. 
1.2. 
Background and motivation 
A typical process for API production consists of three main stages: fermentation, recovery, and 
purification. The fermentation step is where API or a precursor is produced by biochemical reaction. 
The recovery and purification steps are where the product of fermentation is captured, modified, and 
eventually purified to meet the expected quality.  
The recovery process consists of unit operations such as centrifugation, chromatography, 
crystallization, and advanced filtration units. Dynamic Crossflow Filtration (DCF) is part of the 
advanced filtration operations.  
Right now, the operation is subjected to upstream disturbances, and it is based on knowledge 
derived from experience. By advanced knowledge, the non-linear behaviour of the system can be 
mapped, it can be defined how different variables affect the system. The operation and improvement 
of industrial filtration systems require high level process understanding, which can be provided by 
==PAGE:12==

the use of mechanistic models. They are built based on first principles and can be used to represent 
and describe the dynamics and non-linearities of the system. Identifying data that can be used to

model. 
The developed model will be used to provide advanced understanding, to quantify the operation 
space and the effect of inlet variables of the system. Additionally, the temporal spatial model can be 
extended into a compartmental model to offer insights into the homogeneity within the unit.

Problem definition 
The aim of this MSc thesis is to reach a high-level process understanding of an industrial filtration 
unit through mechanistic modelling. Then apply the model in the recovery stage in the IM2 facility 
and identify opportunities for process improvements. The thesis will result in: 
1. Advanced process understanding of an industrial filtration unit 
2. Suggestions for improving and optimization in terms of operation and efficiency 
3. Investigate the application of the model to other recovery and purification processes with 
similar filtration units 
4. Application of the model in means of process monitoring. Potential fault detection and 
diagnosis. 
To fulfil the objective of the thesis, the following scope of the process has been defined: 
1. Literature review of filtration processes

3. Model development and understanding 
4. Production data analysis 
5. Mapping of operational space 
6. Model application

2.1. 
Mathematical Modelling of Process Systems 
2.1.1. Mechanistic Modelling Framework 
A factory can be understood as a dynamic system. The different processes can take place at the 
same time and are interrelated, thus planning and scheduling are crucial for the overall performance.  
The dynamics in a production process can be captured at different scales of resolution, as shown in 
Figure 1. To successfully represent the system, modelling at every scale must be initiated to a certain 
extent, as they are needed to assist the decision making at different scales. 
The analysis of a system like this, which considers numerous variables, is quite complex. Solving 
large systems of equations at this level is not usually feasible, both because of the computational 
and combinatorial aspects.  
That is why the resolution and complexity change along the multiscale. From XAPS to DAPS, as the 
scale increases, the models become less complex but with a larger number of equations. X-ray 
analysis evaluates the whole multiscale by analysing data to find out where improvement can be

Mechanistic analysis provides the highest resolution of the dynamics at a unit-operation scale using 
first order principles, while hybrid analysis uses a combination between first order principles and data 
analysis to determine the control structure of the process at a flowsheet scale. Capacity analysis 
evaluates the closure of production mass balances at a facility scale. Lastly, decision analysis is 
intended to deal with strategic and production planning both at short and long terms. 
Combining all these scales a digital twin of an API manufacturing process is defined, and it is used 
to analyse production dynamics to a high degree of resolution. 
2.1.2. Modelling: Classification and structure of models 
Modelling is the representation of a real physicochemical, economic, social, or human situation in a 
mathematical or physical form. Three concepts are included in the definition of modelling: The 
subject of interest (system, S), the purpose and the representation form of the model [9]. The system 
from a modelling perspective is represented by Figure 2.  
 
==PAGE:16==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 10 of 68 
 
 
Figure 2. System representation modelling purposes [9] 
 
The principal components of a model are shown in Figure 2. The system (S) includes the principal 
entities of within the limits of model consideration and their interconnections, including the 
mechanisms operating in that system. Then there are inputs (u), which affect the behaviour of the 
system, and outputs (y), which reflect internal properties of the system, such as quality measures or 
production rates. Parameters (p) are constant variables of the system. States (x) are variables that 
characterize the properties of the system, and disturbances (d) are variables that affect the system 
uncontrollably [9].  
There are basic requirements that every model should fulfil as defined by Madni et al. (2018): 
completeness, consistency, and traceability. By completeness it is understood that all elements of 
the model should be sufficiently specified. Consistency is referred to the implementation of common 
concepts into the model. And traceability mean that all concepts of the model were derived from 
common standards or guidelines [10,12].  
 The modelling goal is to simulate real systems by means of a model with sufficient accuracy that it 
will help answer questions about the original system. It provides with system insights, which are 
useful for process understanding and system optimization. Eliminates the need of performing 
numerous experiments to get process understanding on the system, and when needed it gives 
information on which experiments should be defined for a given system [9,11]. 
As mentioned in chapter 2, there are different approaches for defining process models. All of them 
should be performed following a well-defined sequence of steps as the general one presented in 
Hangos et al. (2001) and Gani et al. (2012), from which Figure 3 is elaborated. The nature of this 
sequence is inherently iterative: one must repeat any previous step in case of unusual results and/or 
behaviour of the system at any point later in the process. 
 
==PAGE:17==

By this method, the aim is to find a mathematical model that represents the system, given the process 
system, the modelling goal, and the validation criteria. 
Mathematical models should be derived by applying the conservation principles of mass, energy 
and/or momentum, and their connections to the surroundings. A process may be divided into 
different sub-systems where each one is defined by boundaries and connections to other sub-
systems. According to this, model equations can be classified into 3 groups: Balance, constitutive, 
and constraint equations [13]. 
The equations that represent each type of model should be defined from specific properties of the 
system and modelling objectives. These will result on different assumptions and needs of the model, 
that will define the boundaries and connections of the system. As assumptions on a system are 
subjective to itself, a process could be represented by various versions of a model [13].

In this classification, aspects that influence the equations for the system were included. Starting from 
a unit level, some characteristics to consider are those related to the mode of operation, the type of 
process taking place (i.e., reaction or separation based) and the phases present on the system. It 
should be mentioned that when considering the type of process various options can be selected: For 
the reaction-based processes, heat transfer is an important variable while for the separation-based 
processes, different driving forces could be defined, such as pressure or equilibrium concentration. 
Moving on to a process level, spatial and temporal variations are considered. First, considering the 
existence of temporal variation, models can be classified into dynamic and steady state. In a dynamic 
system, variables are time dependant while in a steady state one they do not change with time.

2.1.3. Types of models based on their approach 
Mechanistic models are based on first order principles and well-established theories of the system 
that is being considered. These models are mathematical descriptions of a process, that act as an 
in-depth description of the underlying phenomena and of the process itself. They can be considered 
a “white box” model, where the inner workings of the system are known. On the other side, there are 
the so called pure “black box” models. In them the system inner workings are unknown and relations 
between the variables are found through data analysis and/or experience [5,6]. 
In mechanistic models the number of parameters is usually fixed and they might have a physical or 
empirical interpretation depending on the sophistication level of knowledge. Statistical models are 
determined exclusively from data, so number and nature of the parameters are flexible and not fixed 
in advanced by knowledge [5]. 
The combination of these two approaches is known as hybrid modelling, and it makes the solution 
of systems more efficient and faster. These hybrid models would lie on the classification of “grey 
models”, which appeared for the first time in the 1900s [5]. They are used when the mechanistic 
models cannot explain part of the phenomena of the system and/or when it is expensive to prove 
the accuracy of the model; hybrid models cover gaps in the understanding of a system.

Knowledge of the mixing behaviour of multiple axial and radial impellers is needed for creating and

To provide the basis for process optimization, design, and control, these flow models are often

The necessity to overcome modelling limitations lead to the development of compartmental 
modelling. Compartmental models constitute an intermediate approach between systemic models, 
where only the global behaviour is considered, and CFD. This type of modelling offers a less

volumes called compartments. The selection of compartment specifications will influence the 
accuracy of the results [15,20].

compartmental models were developed. With this approach several systems and phenomena were 
evaluated, presenting differences in the choice of compartmental definition criteria. This shows the 
dependency of this type of modelling and the system in consideration [15,16]. 
Conclusions on key advantages of compartmental modelling were presented in Jourdan et al. (2019), 
which included the following. A compartmental model can describe several phenomenological 
models and it offers a better representation of local phenomena. Moreover, the deviation between a 
CFD model and a compartmental model was small in comparison to the reduction in calculation time 
[15]. 
Numerous compartmental models have been developed over the last years, for a great variety of 
equipment. Iliuta et al. (2007) built a multicomponent compartment model to account for the detailed 
hydrodynamics of a bubble column reactor [17]. Du et al. (2015) developed a reactor network for the 
==PAGE:25==

Page 19 of 68 
 
modelling of a catalytic reactor [18]. And Öner et al (2018) scaled-up a pharmaceutical crystallization 
process based on a compartmentalization approach [19].  
From the work they performed it is proved that compartmental models can be applied to a wide range 
of systems, affected by different phenomena. But there are not common guidelines for the creation 
of compartmental models, as the number and properties of compartments will be highly dependent 
on the system under analysis. 
Lastly, Bisgaard et al. (2021) showed that the development of a compartmental models is 
independent of the experience of the modeler, as the necessary information is already contained in 
the collected data. Information obtained from a compartmental model can be used for providing 
potential optimizations of processes [20]. 
To sum up, compartmental models are a relevant approach to consider when representing 
distributed parameter systems, where spatial variation is considered. They constitute an 
intermediate approach between systemic models and CFD, what results on a lower computational 
demand.

These two configurations can be better explained by considering their mechanism of retention.  
In dead end filtration, the pressure that is applied to the system drives the feed through the 
membrane. The desired product is retained over the membrane surface in form of a filtration cake, 
while the particle-free fluid goes through the membrane constituting the filtrate flow. In this type of 
configuration, the feed and filtrate move concurrently along the filter generating one product stream 
every batch. As the filtration progresses, the filter cake becomes thicker and consequently the 
filtration rate decreases. At this point cleaning or replacing of the membrane is necessary, so filtration 
must be interrupted. This discontinuous mode of operation is a major disadvantage when handling 
process streams with a high solid content [13,24]. 
On the other hand, in the crossflow configuration two streams are generated: the one that contains 
the solid particles, known as retentate, and the one free from particles, known as permeate. The 
feed stream moves perpendicularly to the permeate one, as represented in Figure 8. This results on 
an efficient fluid management by controlling the thickness of the concentration polarisation layer by 
crossflow velocity, and not that much by TMP. Crossflow filtration presents a better ability in handling 
wide particle size and solid concentration variations, yielding a higher product purity [13,24]. 
It is of relevance to this thesis to mention the possibility of performing filtration by using a centrifuge.

2.2.2. Phenomena counteracting filtration 
The main phenomena counteracting filtration are filter cake formation, fouling and concentration 
polarisation. Along this section, their characteristics will be defined. 
Cake formation accounts for the deposition of solute particles on the membrane surface. This build- 
up over the membrane causes additional resistance to filtration, which increases with time. As the 
resistance increases, the flux through the membrane decreases. Moreover, the solutes deposited 
over the cake layer will result on a concentration polarisation layer because of concentration 
gradients [26]. 
Fouling is understood as the deterioration of the membrane performance, caused by interactions 
between feed and membrane surface. Usually, this is translated into an increase of membrane 
resistance, which would lead to a decrease in permeate flow. When particles encounter the 
membrane, they cause pores to block. It is an irreversible phenomenon, so cleaning of the 
membrane is necessary to reverse it. Membrane fouling is still an empirical science, due to the huge 
variation in membrane and feed properties, so its prediction cannot be modelled from general 
equations [13]. 
Concentration polarisation is defined by the IUPAC as the “concentration profile that has a higher 
level of solute nearest to the upstream membrane surface compared with the more-or-less well-
mixed bulk fluid far from the membrane surface” [19]. It consists of the existence of concentration 
gradients at the membrane-solution interface, that result from the selective transfer through the 
membrane. It can be differenced from fouling by two aspects: the molecules involved, and the 
timescale, which for concentration polarisation is normally less than a minute from the beginning of 
operation [28].

2.2.3. Shear enhanced filtration 
As mentioned in Section 2.2.2, concentration polarisation and cake formation are two of the main 
drivers of resistance to filtration. By using high shear forces, the effect of cake resistance and 
concentration polarisation are reduced [31]. 
High shear rates can be created at the membrane by introducing a moving part, such as a rotating 
or vibrating membrane. This is known as dynamic or shear enhanced filtration, and its improvement 
in filtration performance has been studied by Villafaña-López (2019) [32] and Hwang (2019) [33]. 
Both conducted experiments on cross flow filtration using rotating discs to increase the shear rate, 
confirming that its effect on separation rate of filtration was advantageous.

Frenander (1996) evaluated the filtration of protein from cells in a fermentation broth by shear 
enhanced cross flow microfiltration [34]. Their findings were also promising, higher protein recovery

Shear rate enhanced filtration is a promising technology to consider when resistances due to 
concentration polarization and cake formation are greatly influencing the overall performance of the 
filtration operation.

3. Methodology 
In this chapter the MAPS workflow is introduced, to further explain the objectives of each of the steps 
that are needed to develop a mechanistic model. 
 
3.1. 
Problem Definition 
Prior to developing a model, it is necessary to define what is the problem, why it is important and 
how to solve it. This includes identification of the targets of the problem and the information that is 
available and relevant to the case, as well as the limitations that the process presents. To define the 
problem under study it is required to obtain previous process understanding on the system to be 
able to identify relevant information about it, to decide if a mechanistic model of the system is 
necessary and the most suitable model. 
3.2. 
MAPS workflow 
This is the methodology that will be followed to develop the mechanistic model for both the literature 
and the industrial study case. The infinity loop shown in Figure 12 highlights the nature of mechanistic 
models. It should be continuously updated when any change in the process occurs, so the workflow 
is re-followed every time that the model needs to be improved or that it fails to represent of the 
system.

In sections 3.3 to 3.9 each of the steps in the MAPS methodology will be defined by stating the 
objective of each of them and how to perform it. 
3.3. 
System Description 
The objective of this step is to represent the physical boundaries of the system and to describe it in 
terms of phenomena and assumptions. 
3.4. 
Model Development 
Once the phenomena and assumptions have been listed, the evaluation of different theories that 
can represent that phenomena is made. Then the mathematical equations describing the 
phenomena and the assumption of the system are listed. 
When the mathematical model is developed, a DOF analysis is performed to identify which variables 
need to be specified to solve the system. 
3.5. 
Data Analysis 
Historical development and production data is analysed to obtain an initial understanding of the 
dynamics of the system and initial values for the parameters of the model are calculated.  
==PAGE:35==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 29 of 68 
 
These model parameters depend on the specific system that is being considered, and on the state 
of the system, so they are generally not known a priori and eventually they to be rigorously estimated.  
3.6. 
Initial Solution 
The objective of this step is to test if the model captures the dynamic/or steady state behaviour of 
the system, by solving the system using an initial set of parameters calculated in section 3.5. It is not 
necessary that the model is complete and accurate at this point [9,11]. 
In case the model does not capture the behaviour of the system, the initial values for parameters 
should be calculated again or the mathematical expressions defining the phenomena should be re-
evaluated and may be changed [9, 11] 
3.7. 
Parameter Estimation 
Once the model has been proved to capture the system dynamics, true values for the parameters 
are estimated using data from small/large scale experiments.  
3.8. 
Model Validation 
When the true values for the parameters have been estimated, the model performance is tested with 
data sets that have not been used during estimation. The model is solved using this new data set 
and compared to actual measurements of the variables to test the accuracy of predictions.  The 
objective of validation is to prove that the predictions of the model are valid and that it can be used 
on any data set for the given system [9, 11]. 
3.9. 
Model Application 
The validated model can be used in several applications, such as process understanding, monitoring, 
control, optimization and performing simulation scenarios [11]. The model will provide high-level 
understanding of the process and will accurately represent the system. Optimal values for the 
operation variables that influence the overall process can also be determined with this model.

The system consists of a cross flow filtration at a laboratory scale, with a 0.02 m2 membrane and a 
reservoir size for the feed solution of 1100 mL. Although it operates in batch mode, the model 
development will be made for a general system considering inlets and outlets, to later simplify it to a 
batch case and show this way how a specific case can be derived from the general system. 
Some assumptions presented in Krippl et al. (2020) will be made to solve the system. The variables 
that influence the performance of the filtration are the transmembrane pressure, the crossflow 
velocity, and the initial concentration of the bulk fluid; and the transmembrane pressure and 
crossflow velocity are constant through the operation. The desired product is contained in the 
retentate side, and the retention coefficient (R) of the membrane is 1, meaning that none of the 
product goes through the membrane to the permeate side.

6. Summary and discussion 
In this chapter, a summary of the results obtained through the development of this thesis, along with 
their challenges and gains are presented for each of the main parts: literature review, literature case 
study and industrial case study. Challenges and gains from their development are also presented.  
 
6.1. 
Literature Review  
Through the literature review, an overview of modelling strategies and membrane separation 
processes was developed.  
Mathematical models were classified at a unit level, to account for general specifications of the 
operation unit, and at a process level, to account for the dependence of variables on time and space. 
This classification was intended to create a guide on how to select an adequate model when a 
system of any type is considered. Moreover, the general structure of models was defined by 
presenting the types of equations needed to build a model. 
Space-dependent models were further related to black box, white box, and grey box concepts. These 
concepts are the basis for building hybrid models, that could present different structures depending 
on how the individual models are related.  
An application for temporal models was also introduced. Compartmental modelling is an interesting 
strategy to represent a temporal-spatial system by means of a temporal model. Compartmental 
models present lower complexity and computational requirements when representing that type of 
systems. 
Lastly an overview of different membrane separation technologies was presented through a 
classification based on their driving force. Followed by a more in-depth investigation on the 
phenomena on filtration and theories that could be used for flux prediction. 
6.2. 
Literature Case Study 
The MAPS workflow was followed in this case study on cross flow filtration to show its applicability 
on a system of this characteristics and to explore its applications on an industrial case. 
The development of a hybrid model showed the potential of statistical data analysis combined with 
mechanistic approaches. But it also presented some limitations. To build a statistical model a large 
quantity of data is necessary, but in this case study the data set was limited so specific methods for 
training of ANN had to be used. Moreover, the developed statistical model is constrained in the 
==PAGE:55==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 49 of 68 
 
training space of the ANN and cannot be used to improve the operating conditions outside of that 
space. 
Validation of the model was performed by comparison of the predicted concentration and the 
experimental values from literature. The results obtained were better than expected. The regression 
coefficient between measured and calculated concentration showed better results for the ANN 
developed in this thesis although its flux prediction was less accurate than the one of the ANN 
developed in literature.  
Sensitivity analysis showed that the model is most sensitive to initial concentration, followed by TMP 
and CF. As the initial concentration is very important, the design and control of the process in terms 
of stable and desired concentration is important to maximize the use of plant capacity. An opportunity 
can arise in monitoring the inlet concentration and controlling the filtration unit to operate optimally 
for the given inlet concentration. 
An optimization scenario was developed to find optimal values of TMP and CF that would maximize 
the final concentration of the product. The optimization space was constrained by the training space 
of the statistical model, so the solution obtained may be a local one. The specific values obtained 
were 2.7 bar for TMP, which is the upper limit of the space, and 265.05 mL/min for CF. Through the 
increase of training space of the ANN or by development of a fully mechanistic model, these results 
may be improved and an alternative maximum could be found. 
A last application case was to develop a compartmental model, accounting for time-space variations 
in the system. An evaluation on different number of compartments was performed from 1 to 50 
compartments, and it was found that the final concentration of the product decreased as more 
compartments were considered. This was not the expected outcome of this scenario, as the final 
concentration should be the same as in the temporal model. The compartmentalization is made by 
diving the system into smaller regions of study, but if the combination of all compartments is seen 
as a black box, the outlet concentration should be the same. The increase on the detail of 
calculations of this approach, combined with the change in concentration gradients could explain 
these differences. Nevertheless, the potential of a compartment approach when considering a 
temporal-spatial model was shown. 
6.3. 
Industry Case Study 
The MAPS workflow was followed to develop a model to investigate the global behaviour of a 
dynamic crossflow filtration system. Definition of relevant variables was made by analysis of the 
dynamics of the system, which were found to be the expected ones according to literature. TMP was 
==PAGE:56==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 50 of 68 
 
defined as the driving force of filtration, which was varied along operation to maintain a constant 
permeate flow through operation. 
Production data was used to identify regions where single parameters could be calculated. From 
those values, parameter estimation was performed over 8 batches through the period of May 2022-
June 2022. Some challenges were found when estimating filtration resistance, as high variability was 
present in this parameter. In the case of membrane resistance, a mean value could not be used due 
to the large difference between batches, so the specific value for each of them was determined. In 
the case of concentration polarisation and fouling resistance, also large differences were found 
between batches. A median value was used in this case, to allow for further predictions of the model. 
Future investigation on filtration resistance is advised as a conclusion of data analysis. 
Model validation was performed over 7 batches, showing a mean RMSE of 0.168. Despite the large 
variations on total filtration resistance, the results are considered accurate for the batches analysed 
except for one of them, whose RMSE increased up to 0.258. 
The variability found in the resistance parameters define an area of improvement. First evaluation 
on how the cleaning of the membrane is being performed is needed to identify potential 
improvements in cleaning, i.e., evaluation of frequential cleaning to improve filtration. Second, 
investigation on fouling and concentration polarisation is needed, as they are the main resistances 
counteracting filtration. 
A sensitivity analysis was performed on the angular velocity of discs. It was done by considering a 
steady-state scenario, so resistance and viscosity were taken as constants. This way the effect of 
angular velocity on the required pressure difference to maintain constant flux through operation could 
be determined. The analysis showed that for constant flux operation the pressure difference 
increases with angular velocity, what was the expected behaviour as the rotation of discs counteract 
filtration.  
An uncertainty analysis on the total filtration resistance and on the fluid viscosity was performed to 
map the operational space. Accounting for maximum and minimum values for these parameters, the 
variation on permeate flow and pressure difference could be determined. A higher effect of changes 
in viscosity was found. 
An optimization scenario was defined to maximize the permeate volume over a batch duration, 
subjected to angular velocity of discs. The finding of this scenario showed the importance of having 
an expression that could relate the total filtration resistance to the angular velocity of discs, as without 
it an optimal value for the velocity could not be found. 
==PAGE:57==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 51 of 68 
 
Application of the model for pore blocking prediction was performed by evaluating the permeate flow 
over the batch. By evaluation of batches that presented a decrease on permeate flow at some point 
in operation, it was decided that a decrease of 0.1% over a period of at least 10 minutes was the 
criteria to determine pore blocking. The resulting tool was used to evaluate if it can be advantageous 
to clean the membrane when there is blocking of pores, and then continuing operation. Permeate 
volume over the duration of a batch was calculated for the case of pore blocking and continuing 
operation, and for the case of pore blocking and cleaning of the membrane. Two batches were 
evaluated. The first presented pore blocking in the middle of the batch, and it showed a decrease in 
permeate volume of 11.47% when cleaning the membrane. The second batch presented pore 
blocking at the beginning of operation, and it showed an increase of 12.28% in permeate volume 
when cleaning the membrane and continuing operation. Further development of this tool will be of 
high interest to predict the time at which pore blocking occurs and evaluate if it is better to clean the 
membrane or to continue the filtration. 
Lastly the prediction of retentate concentration was performed. Due to the current lack of information 
on how production measurements on concentration are done, the model could not be validated. 
Therefore, investigation on how and at what point of operation are the samples taken is needed. 
Once this information is clear, the corresponding material balances can be set up to validate the 
predicted concentration with production data.

7. Conclusion and perspectives 
In this chapter the main conclusions from this MSc thesis are presented. Followed by the future 
perspectives of the use of this model, including challenges and future improvements of the current 
work are presented. 
 
7.1. 
Conclusion 
The recovery process is subjected to many upstream disturbances. Stabilizing and improving the 
operation of the recovery units require advanced process understanding. In that way, and with the 
help of mechanistic models and production data, the system is analysed, quantified and 
improvements can be proposed.   
The objective of this MSc thesis was to reach a high-level process understanding of an industrial 
filtration unit through applying a method for Mechanistic Analysis of Process Systems (MAPS).  
Initially, a case study from literature was used to apply the methodology and show the applicability 
of such model. The methodology was successfully applied and the final model was used in three 
application scenarios. First a sensitivity analysis on relevant input variables was performed, showing 
that initial concentration has a significant impact on the output of the system, followed by TMP and 
CF. An optimization case was solved to identify the optimum input variables that yielded maximum 
end concentration. And a compartmental model considering spatial variation of concentration was 
constructed and solved. 
Then the industrial case study was modelled following the MAPS workflow. A temporal model 
representing an advanced filtration unit was developed to investigate the global behaviour of the 
system. Relevant phenomena and relations between variables of the system were defined. To solve 
the model, measured data was analysed to identify regions where single parameters could be 
calculated. Such as CIP regions of constant permeate flow to calculate membrane resistance, start-
up of the filtration process to calculate the initial viscosity and regions of permeate flow over the 
filtration operation to calculate concentration polarisation and fouling resistance. From those values, 
parameters were later estimated from production data. 
The developed model with the estimated parameters was validated against 7 batches, showing a 
mean RMSE of 0.168. The validated model was further used in three different applications. A 
sensitivity analysis was performed on the angular velocity of discs, showing that for constant flow 
operation the pressure difference increases with angular velocity. Uncertainty analysis was done on 
==PAGE:59==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 53 of 68 
 
estimated parameters, i.e., total filtration resistance and fluid viscosity, showing that an increase in 
these parameters lead to a required higher pressure difference to maintain constant permeate flow. 
 An optimization scenario was defined to maximize the permeate volume over a batch, subjected to 
angular velocity of discs. The results of this case were that the optimum velocity of discs was null.  
From Marke et al. (2020) it is known than lower angular velocities lead to an increase in filtration 
resistance, so the findings were not the expected ones. The specific relationship between angular 
velocity and filtration resistance is still unknown, but if it is found the optimisation of angular velocity 
will be successful. 
Application of the model to predict pore blocking in the membrane was successfully performed by 
evaluating the permeate flow over the batch. The basis for creating a tool that will identify pore 
blocking and the best operating strategy in terms of stopping the operation when blocking 
phenomena is found, has been created. As a last application, prediction of concentration was 
performed by means of the mass balance equations for a filtration unit. The unit considered in this 
thesis is connected to other units, whose operation depends on the outlet of this system. Predictions 
on concentration would contribute to the improved planning of further units of the process.  
The development of this mechanistic model has defined the framework in which further application 
of the system could be done. It has also provided with a model that can be used not only for other 
filtration units in IM2 recovery process, but also in other recovery processes that use a filtration step 
of this nature. 
7.2. 
Perspectives and future work 
This work has provided process understanding over unknown phenomena of a filtration system that 
had not been considered before. Future work should be focused on continuing to explore the 
behaviour of the system, as there were still some aspects of it that could not be accurately modelled.  
From the developed model, the relationship between concentration and viscosity could not be 
described. This relationship is an important area for future investigation. It will be appropriate to make 
several measurements on concentration and viscosity at different states of filtration, as well as locate 
sensors at the inlet of the filtration unit to allow for predictions on operation. This will allow to test 
both viscosity estimation and concentration prediction, performed in this thesis. 
One of the main drawbacks found from the model was its impossibility to make accurate predictions 
on fouling and concentration polarisation phenomena. Further research on total resistance of 
filtration is proposed, so that a general equation representing the evolution of resistance can be 
found. It may be adequate to have a relationship depending on torque or flux, instead of the time 
==PAGE:60==
Advanced Model-Based Process Understanding of Industrial Filtration Unit 
Page 54 of 68 
 
dependant equation found in this thesis from production data. To do so, experimental work can be 
conducted to test different values of flux and/or torque and angular velocities over membrane fouling. 
Improved understanding of the system could be achieved by developing a compartmental model that 
represents the temporal-spatial variations of concentration through the equipment. As it consists of 
several membranes mounted in various shafts, concentration is expected to vary from the membrane 
disc that is closer to the inlet, to the disc that is closer to the outlet. 
There were found several batches greatly affected by pore blocking. A future area of application of 
the pore blocking prediction tool developed in this thesis is to optimise the cleaning times between 
batches. Focusing the investigation on how many times and for how long should the membrane be 
cleaned over fixed period, i.e., for a consideration of one month of operation. This will result on better 
filtration performance due to less frequent blockage of membrane pores. 
The prediction of concentration sets the basis for future improved monitoring of the recovery process. 
Knowing the concentration of the product that will constitute the inlet of further units will allow to 
predict the disturbances that will affect these units. Moreover, by monitoring the inlet to the filtration 
unit will allow also to predict its outputs and stabilize the recovery process.
