Summary (English)
Biometric systems, and especially facial recognition systems, have become ubiq-
uitous in our daily lives, serving as a reliable means of authentication for personal
devices.
Recently, deep learning has significantly improved the performance
of facial recognition technology, albeit these approaches are dependent on the
quality and quantity of the data used for training. As such, the verification
performance of these systems on children is reported as subpar as there exists
no large-scale unbiased database of childrenвЂ™s faces, due to privacy concerns.
However, automatic recognition of children from faces has numerous applica-
tions, including the potential to find missing or kidnapped children or analyze
child sexual abuse material.
Therefore, this thesis aims to address the need for a database of childrenвЂ™s
faces by using generative adversarial networks (GANs) and face age progression
(FAP) models to synthesize a realistic dataset. To generate high-quality adult
and corresponding child faces, the thesis performs an evaluation of various state-
of-the-art FAP methods, considering several criteria, including the preservation
of subject identity across ages. The final solution involves a software pipeline
consisting of multiple steps and models. The pipeline initially utilizes Style-
GAN3 to sample adult subjects, which are subsequently progressed to children
of varying ages using InterFaceGAN, enabling direct manipulation of subjects in
the StyleGAN3 latent space. Intra-subject variations, such as facial expression
and pose, are created by also manipulating the subjects in their latent space.
Additionally, the pipeline allows to evenly distribute the races of subjects, al-
lowing to generate a balanced and fair dataset with respect to race distribution.
Using the pipeline, a database was created, consisting of 1652 subjects and a
==PAGE:4==
ii
total of 188,832 images. The significant number of images is due to each subject
being present at various ages and with many different intra-subject variations.
The thesis evaluates the performance of various facial recognition systems on the
synthetic database and compares the results of adults and children at different
ages. The study reveals that children consistently perform worse than adults, on
all tested systems, and the degradation in performance is proportional to age.
Additionally, the study uncovers some biases in the recognition systems, with
Asian and Black subjects and females performing worse than White and Latino
Hispanic subjects and males. Overall, this thesis provides valuable insights into
the performance of facial recognition systems on children and highlights the need
for unbiased, diverse data sets to train these systems. The synthetic database
created through this work can serve as a valuable resource for researchers and
practitioners in the field.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The use of biometric systems, particularly facial recognition systems, in vari-
ous domains such as surveillance, airports, and personal devices, has been well
established. These systems have proven to be highly effective and accurate in
verifying the identity of subjects [RGEA21, WD21]. However, as facial recogni-
tion becomes increasingly integrated into our daily lives, it is crucial to consider
the potential for biases and discrimination against certain demographic groups.
Previous research has investigated this issue [DRD+20], but less attention has
been given to the effect of age on the recognition of childrenвЂ™s faces. This area is
essential, as there are numerous potential applications for well-functioning face
recognition systems for children. The police can use it to find kidnapped or lost
children. Another use case is an automated process for analyzing seized child
sexual abuse material (CSAM) to recognize the victims. In 2019 more than 70
million CSAM videos and images were obtained1. This issue is an increasing
problem with 17 million reports of CSAM in 2019, which saw a dramatic in-
crease to 29.3 million reports in 20212. Due to this immense amount of data it
is necessary to have automated systems for identifying persons in the material,
which require effective face recognition systems.

Introduction
The emergence of deep learning in recent years has also shown to be extremely
useful in face recognition [WD21]. A caveat of these models is that they need a
huge amount of data to get state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance and are compu-
tationally expensive to train. This amount of data needed has become a growing
concern due to increased legal and political scrutiny surrounding the privacy is-
sues associated with large datasets of individualsвЂ™ faces [Ris22, LCS+22, Har21].
The current databases used for research in this area are often limited in size,
constrained, and focused on specific ages or races3 and are also unavailable to
the public due to ethical and privacy concerns. This issue is further exacerbated
when it comes to children due to heightened focus on protecting their rights4.
One possible solution to address the conflicting demands of extensive data re-
quirements and privacy rights is the use of synthetic data. Generative models,
deep learning algorithms capable of generating synthetic data, have received
significant attention in recent years and have demonstrated promising results.
When utilizing these models, it is possible to create a database of artificially
generated childrenвЂ™s faces, which can then be used to train and/or evaluate fa-
cial recognition systems. There has even been evidence of the ability of these
models to perform face age progression (FAP), changing the appearance of an
individual at a younger or older age based on a single image [SYTZ20]. An ex-
ample of such an image generated by a generative model is shown in Figure 1.1.
This image is then progressed and regressed into 11 different age groups by four
different FAP methods.
This thesis endeavors to create a synthetic database of facial images of children
and develop accompanying software pipeline that allows for the creation of new
databases with configurable variables such as the age and race of the subjects.
For the dataset to be useful for face recognition systems, there will need to be
multiple variations of the same subject to compare them. To generate these
distinct subject variations within each age group, modifications will be made to
attributes such as illumination, pose, and facial expression, which also is con-
figurable in the pipeline. Additionally, the project investigates the performance
of facial recognition systems on this children dataset and compares it to the
performance of the same subjects in adulthood. Furthermore, subsets of the
dataset are created to see if gender5 or race influences the performance.

1.1
Research Questions
In order to measure exactly how verification of children performs on face recog-
nition systems, compared to the same subjects at adults age, a larger database
is needed. This database should contain the same persons (subjects) both at
adult age, but also at different child ages. With such a database it allows to
measure exactly how much, if any, the performance of said systems degrades as
the age progresses. As no such database exists of real life facial images across
various ages a synthetic one will be created. A crucial detail when creating this
synthetic database is that the identities should be preserved across ages, in order
to fairly measure the progression impact. The following research questions are
addressed by the thesis:
вЂў RQ1: How can new generative models be used to generate synthetic chil-
dren faces?
вЂў RQ2: How can generative models be used to create intra-subject vari-
ations to children images without losing the characteristics of the face
(identity)?
вЂў RQ3: How well do the state-of-the-art face recognition systems work on
childrenвЂ™s faces?

вЂў RQ4: How do state-of-the-art face recognition systems perform when
evaluating images of the same individuals at different ages, specifically as
children and adults?
вЂў RQ5: How does the race and gender of the subject impact the perfor-
mance of state-of-the-art facial recognition systems?
1.2
Thesis Contribution
The generated dataset, which is generated through the use of a software pipeline,
will be made available to the public domain to facilitate additional research
opportunities. This dataset will serve as a benchmark for evaluating the per-
formance of facial recognition systems with regard to varying age groups and
ethnicities. Additionally, the source code for the data generation pipeline will
also be disclosed, providing a foundation for further research into synthetic data

A biometric system in general contains some core components regardless of
operation mode and which biological characteristic is used [ISO21]. These com-

Data Capture: The component which captures the biometric characteristic.

Signal Processing: This component converts the signal from the data capture

identification.
Data Storage: Is the storage for the enrolment process where the biometric
reference features are saved for later to be used in verification or identifi-
cation.
Comparison: Is the component which handle the comparison of the biometric
references and probes. Often a specific distance or similarity score is used.
When used for verification, the comparison is made between the provided
reference and probe. For identification, the probe is compared to all or
many references from the data storage.
Decision The final step is where the decision is made based on some defined
policy.
For verification, the decision of if it is a match is based on a
defined threshold for the comparison score. Identification can be handled
in several ways, but one way is to filter the comparison scores based on a
threshold, and from this candidate list, the decision is made.

2.1.2
Face Recognition Systems
Face recognition systems are a specific and popular type of biometric sys-
tem. These systems can be used for verification and identification by extract-
ing features from the face. Face recognition systems have proven to provide
human-like accuracy of recognition, and even surpassing humans in certain
tests [RSB+18, WD21]. It also has the pro of not needing a specialised sen-
sor for biometric purposes but just a camera, which most devices come with
build in, making it a very popular biometric system.
When looking at face
recognition systems they can be categorized into three different categories. This
categorization is based on how the approaches work and which different features
from the face they use [KJAFA20].
Local Approaches:
Approaches that are using some specific facial features.
These models could focus on critical points like the eyes, mouth and nose
or specific areas or patches on the face. An example of a popular approach
is SURF (speeded-up robust features) [BETVG08, CLH+15].
Holistic Approaches:
Approaches that are looking at the whole face. Often
they convert the face to a lower dimensional space, where comparisons can
be made. These kind of approaches includes convolutional neural networks
(CNN).
Hybrid Approaches:
A third approach is combining the two other approaches
by both looking at specific features and the whole face, e.g.
as done
in [Kod14].
Many of the most popular, and best performing, face recognition systems today
take a holistic approach by using deep learning (DL). The details and inner
workings of DL and CNN will be explained in Section 2.2.1. These DL-based
systems works by learning patterns in a vast amount of facial images (the more
==PAGE:26==
12
Fundamentals
the better, and usually on datasets of 1 million+ images). At some point the
systems learn to generalize well enough that it can recognize humans that it has
never seen before among many different subjects. Some notable DL-based open
source face recognition systems are ArcFace [DGZ18] and MagFace [MZHZ21],
which have state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. These systems work by taking
some facial image as input and then outputting a low dimensional feature vector,
e.g. of 512 dimensions. Prior to feeding the image to the systems only the face
should be included and it should be aligned, which can be done by using a
face detector. The concept is then that two feature vectors of the same subject
should be very similar and can be compared with some similarity measure. A
con of this approach is that it is not exactly known what the neural networks
learns in order to recognize humans through facial images, as it is a black-box
approach.

Deep Generative Models
In this section, an overview of the theory behind deep learning and generative
models will be presented.
The general idea of generative models is to have
some model learn patterns in the specified training data X and then use this
model to generate entirely new synthetic data, similar to what is observed in
X. This concept is often done with deep learning algorithms, hence the naming
of deep generative models. And in the context of image analysis and computer
vision, as this thesis lies within, CNNs are the go-to network used. And thus
first an introduction to deep learning and CNNs will be provided as theory
for understanding generative models. Afterwards, the specific generative model
GAN will be explained, and a newer kind of model named diffusion model has
also been experimented with and will thus be explained briefly.

Diffusion Models
In recent times a newer variant of generative models named diffusion models
have become quite popular, notably through [HJA20]. The idea is quite simple;
gradually add more noise to an image, and have a neural network learn to reverse
the process, thus going from noise to image. In more details a Figure explaining
the process can be seen in Figure 2.6. It starts by having some data point x at
step 0 denoted as x0, which could be an image, and then adds some amount of
noise over T steps. This process can be formalized as a Markov Chain. To find
the image with some amount of noise at step t can be formulated as the condi-
tional probability: q(xt|xtв€’1), and as seen, only the data point at the previous
time step t в€’1 is needed. The reverse process, going from noise to image, can
be formulated as q(xtв€’1|xt), but that is intractable to compute, and thus some
model is needed to estimate it. This estimation can be denoted as: pОё(xtв€’1|xt).
Here a neural network of some kind with parameters Оё (its weights) can be used,
which can then be trained to reconstruct and thus remove noise from an image.
Once a neural network has been appropriately trained to do this reconstruction,
it can eventually produce a high-quality synthetic image from pure noise, sim-
ilar to the trained generator of a GAN as explained in Section 2.2.2. A caveat
of diffusion models is that they are slow compared to GANs regarding training
and inference, as the diffusion process is computationally expensive.

3.1
Facial Image Generation
GANs have proven to be especially good at learning complex data distributions,
and in particular of faces [KST+22]. This is largely due to the strength of the
specific generative model and the vast amount of research done to further en-
hance it. Since the original GAN was proposed in [GPAM+14] it has been used
for facial image generation, and since then the progress has been extensive. As
mentioned in Section 2.2.2, deep convolutional GAN (DCGAN) was proposed
in [RMC16], using CNNs instead of standard neural networks, greatly enhanc-
ing the quality of generated faces. Since 2016 many new variants of the GAN

architecture have emerged which are able to produce even more impressive (re-

Adversarial Networks (BEGAN) were proposed in which the authors take in-
spiration from control theory. They propose a novel equilibrium method for
balancing adversarial networks, and thus finds a way to get a more stable way
to train GANs, greatly increasing the quality of generated faces. In [KALL18]
the authors propose ProGAN (Progressive Growing of GANs), being the first to
successfully generate high resolution (10242 px) synthetic faces of high quality.
The novelty of the architecture is to grow both the generator and discriminator
progressively. Thus starting from a low resolution and then adding new layers
to the model, which improve details as training progresses.
Later one of the most known generative models for facial image generation was
proposed, namely the StyleGAN [KLA18] from 2018.
The model is trained
on the FFHQ dataset, which the authors have created by scraping images
under permissive licenses on the website Flickr. The model outperformed all
other GAN-based image generation models when it was first introduced, both
with respect to minimizing the so called Frechet Inception Distance (FID)
[HRU+17], a function used to determine the quality of synthetic data, but also
when looking at the visual quality of the images. In their model, they present an
intermediate latent code denoted w from the latent space W of 512 dimensions.

instance normalization (AdaIN). This module applies the style from the w to
the specific layer. This makes it possible to combine multiple imagesвЂ™ styles by
applying their w code at different layers. A visualization of this can be seen in

3.2
Face Age Progression
GAN-based architectures have not only proven their worth in generating syn-
thetic images but also for doing face age progression. Grimmer et al. [GRB21]
have somewhat recently written a comprehensive survey on deep face age pro-
gression, noting that GANs indeed produce remarkable face ageing results (which
was also seen in Figure 1.1). Many of the covered FAP models in this section
are based on GANs in some way.
In InterFaceGAN [SYTZ20], the authors do not directly train a new GAN to
do FAP but instead investigate the w and z latent space learned by the original
StyleGAN trained on the FFHQ dataset. The researchers train a linear model
in which a boundary is learned to, e.g. change the age or gender of a generated
image directly in the latent space. In [APW+22], Alaluf et al. retrain Inter-
FaceGAN on the StyleGAN3 latent space thus taking advantage of the improved
architecture for generating faces.
In [OESF+20], the authors handle FAP by proposing a new GAN architecture
trained with labelled age groups (e.g. 0-2 or 50-69), enabling FAP by giving an
input image and specifying the wanted age group. He et al. [HLY+21] note that
many of the GAN-based FAP approaches end up with an entangled latent space
in which they then manipulate the age. In their work, they instead propose a
model where they disentangle key characteristics while modifying the age, also
in different age groups. In [CN22], the authors also take a GAN-based approach
to FAP learned with different age groups. AgeGan, an architecture proposed
in
[SZG+22], uses a dual condition GAN architecture, where one generator
converts input faces to other ages based on an age group condition, and the
dual conditional GAN learns to invert the task.
In [APCO21], Alaluf et al. propose an architecture where age is approached
as a regression task, rather than discrete age groups.
Their model learns a
non-linear path to disentangle the age progression from other attributes. Li et
al. [LJA21] also focus on continuous ageing and use an age estimator as part of
a GAN-generator in a novel architecture.

Authors from Disney Research in [ZCS+22] propose a FAP model that does
not use a GAN but instead a U-Net, translating in an image-to-image manner
together with a provided age, and note promising results. A caveat of their
model is that it is only possible to progress down to the age of 20, due to the
training data used.
Many of the models proposed here, such as in [OESF+20, HLY+21, APCO21,
ZCS+22], are all end-to-end solutions, meaning that they take an image and a
wanted age (or age-group) as input and then outputs an image. In [SYTZ20],
and thus also in [APW+22], an image is manipulated directly in the latent space
of the StyleGAN variant, which then skips the part of inverting or translating
an image into latent space, which otherwise may come at a loss.
3.3
Latent Space Manipulation
When using StyleGAN or similar models, the intermediate latent space can
be explored and manipulated.
As mentioned in Section 3.2, InterFaceGAN
[SYTZ20] is a method for learning linear directions in latent space to modify
specific attributes. They learn these by fitting a Support Vector Machine (SVM)
to some classified data. In [HHLP20], they propose a different way to find these
directions. They use Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to find directions
in the latent space. While linear boundaries produce very promising results,
they do not come without problems. Moving too far in a given direction can
cause loss of identity, and as the w latent space of StyleGAN is quite entangled,
sometimes other attributes get affected [SYTZ20]. In [AZMW21], the authors
propose a conditional non-linear approach, by using Continuous Normalizing
Flows (CTF), to change attributes of subjects in the latent space of StyleGAN2.
However, these flows need more training compared to InterFaceGAN and are
slower at inference.
It has also been shown in [KJU22] that it is possible to manipulate specific
attributes in the latent space of diffusion models.

creasing limitations on access to public face recognition datasets, datasets such
as MS-Celeb-1M and MegaFace have been withdrawn due to ethical reasons
[LCS+22, Ris22]. The use of synthetic data has emerged as a possible alterna-
tive. Multiple papers are studying the possibilities of using synthetic data to
evaluate and train face recognition systems. In [ZGR+21], the authors investi-
gate the face quality differences between synthetic images generated with Style-
GAN and StyleGAN2 and authentic face images from the FRGC-V2 dataset
[CSX+18], filtered to reflect the quality of images used in border control. The
first experiment in the study demonstrates that StyleGAN and StyleGAN2 have
similar performance regarding face quality and biometric recognition. In the
later experiment, comparing synthetic data with real data, they show that Style-
GAN2 have slightly worse performance regarding face recognition and worse fa-
cial quality. However, the authors argue that the differences are minor; therefore,
synthetic data can be used to test facial recognition systems. In both [CFPM21]
and [GZR+21], the researchers are investigating creating synthetic intra-subject
variation for synthetically generated images to be used in mated comparison.
In [CFPM21], they mimic the pose, facial expression and illumination varia-
tions from the Multi-PIE dataset [SBB02] using InterFaceGAN [SYTZ20]. In
[GZR+21], they are also exploring changes in the pose, expression and illumina-
tion, but they are also proposing a non-deterministic way of generating mated
samples denoted PCA-FR-Guided sampling. It explores the latent space using
PCA to find relevant directions while minimising the identity changes using a
pre-trained facial recognition system. And thus, this method depends on the
performance of the facial recognition system. Both the papersвЂ™ solutions show
promising results regarding mated comparison on synthetic data. However, in
both papers, the authors emphasise the issues of creating extreme enough intra-
identity changes, which are comparable with real datasets. In [CFPM21], they
argue this could be due to the limitation of the data used to train StyleGAN2.
In SFace [BHS+22], the authors try to overcome this limitation by retraining
a conditional GAN based on StyleGAN2-ADA [KAH+20]. They train on the
biometric dataset CASIA-WebFace [YLLL14] using the identity as the class
label. They use this generator to generate a biometric dataset with multiple
intra-subject variations for each synthetic identity. This dataset is used to train
three facial recognition systems using three different learning methods. The first
one uses only the synthetic data, the second is training using knowledge transfer
from a pre-trained system on the synthetic data, and the final is to combine
the first two approaches.
The last approach achieved the best performance
comparable with an facial recognition system trained on authentic data. This
shows the potential for training on synthetic data. However, the worse results in
the facial recognition system trained without relying on the pre-trained model
show that further enhancement is required.

The paper [SRM+] uses a subset of the ITWCC [RBS15] and LFW [HMBLM08]

3.5.2
Race & Gender
In [KAKAQ20], the researchers investigate bias in some of the most popular
face analysis databases and systems by performing a systematic review. They
found that databases have tendencies not to have enough variability regarding
ethnicity, culture, race and gender. These issues propagate to facial recognition
systems, which heavily depend on these datasets.
In the NIST facial recognition vendor test [GNH19], multiple COTS systems
are evaluated on the demographic effect on the performance. They evaluate the
performance of four different datasets collected by the US. Government. They
found that, generally, for western developed facial recognition systems, people
from Africa and East Asia had the highest false positive rate, and those from
Eastern Europe had the lowest. For systems developed in China, people from
East Asia had the lowest false positive rate. They found that the gender bias
across all systems and datasets is consistent, with males having a lower false
positive rate than females.
In both [WDH+19] and [RLH+20], they propose a racially balanced dataset, de-
noted RFW and BFW respectively, for benchmarking racial bias in facial recog-
nition systems. The RFW dataset is a subset of the MS-Celeb-1M, which has
been withdrawn [LCS+22, Ris22]. The BFW is based on the VGG2 [CSX+18]
dataset, which also has been withdrawn due to legal issues. This makes it prob-
lematic to use them as a public benchmark set.
In [WDH+19], the authors
try to verify racial bias in commercial and open-source facial recognition sys-
tems by using the RFW dataset. Like [GNH19], their results also showed that
western developed systems performed better on white people and had worse
performance on people from Asia and Africa. Furthermore, systems developed
in East Asia performed better on Asian people. They also show by retraining
ArcFace [DGZ18] on a more equalized dataset, that they can reduce most of the
bias. This underlines the importance of fair and equal datasets. In [RLH+20],
they use the dataset BFW to validate if the off-the-shelf system Sphereface suf-
fers from any bias against gender or race. In their experiment, they found that
both race and gender affect the performance of verification.

Age Span The ages a model is able to transform a subject into. Some models

Image Size The resolution of the output images in pixels. It can be a constrain

is needed.
Inversion needed Whether or not a model first needs to invert an image into
a latent space prior to transforming the image. All of the images were
generated using a generative model and certain models are able to use
the latent space representation of an image as input instead of the actual
image when transforming. This option of using the latent removes the
necessity of inverting the image, which can reduce the quality.
Artifacts Artifacts found in images post transformation. When using a FAP
model artifacts such as unwanted lines, blurs or figures can appear in the
generated image. These artifacts are visually inspected.
Shape of Head Transformation How much the head shape of a subject is
transformed when progressing. A humans head shape changes drastically

throughout its lifespan, where the skull on a young child is proportionally

Hair Transformation How much the hair is transformed of a subject when

score was calculated for each of the progressed images for each of the mod-

detector was used to crop out the face prior to calculating the BRISQUE
score. All images were resized to 2562 pixels since some models are capped
at this resolution. It should be noted that BRISQUE is not necessarily an
accurate measure of the quality of facial images since the algorithm is not
trained for that purpose. Even though the faces were cropped, there can
still be some noisy background which can impact the score.
Run Time The time in seconds each model uses to transform an image on
average. It is important to have models with low inference time when
generating a huge database. The run time was measured by looking at
each model, summing up the inference time and averaging it. The inference
was computed on the Technical University of DenmarkвЂ™s (DTU) High-
Performance Cluster (HPC)2 using a single NVIDIA Tesla V100 32 GB
GPU.

LFS
LFS is also an end-to-end model which takes an input image and a desired
age group and outputs the image, with a resolution of 2562px, transformed to
that age group. The age groups go all the way down to 0-2 years old and the
age estimator often agreed with this transformation. The model segments the
background out of images before doing FAP and thus output images have a gray

their models ability to do head shape transformation, which also can be seen
in Figure 4.3. The model also transforms the hair to look more childish. A
caveat is that in some cases the model produces artifacts, e.g. if looking at

at the images in the left middle of Figure 4.3. The inference time is at 0.748s

4.5.4
DLFS
DLFS is also an end-to-end model which builds further upon LFS, by disentangling
the latent representations to model non-linear aging paths as noted in their
paper [HLY+21]. A caveat of this end-to-end approach for both LFS and DLFS
is that there is a loss when inverting the image into their internal latent space,
which could worsen the identity preservation. The model uses the same aging
groups as LFS, outputs an image with a resolution of 2562px and also segments
out the face of the image resulting in a gray background. Visually the shape
of the head and the hair is transformed in a manner that looks like a natural
aging process as can be seen in Figure 4.3. The model produces some artifacts
as can be seen in the younger images of the person in the top right of the figure.
In general the model visually produces similar results to LFS but in some cases
better looking images, e.g. looking at the transformations of the two bottom
reference images in the left of Figure 4.3, although the identity preservation
looks somewhat off in these images. The BRISQUE score is the highest of all
the models, but not by much compared to LFS. The inference time is at 0.741s

4.5.5
Chosen Model
Based on the assessment, it was concluded that InterFaceGAN [SYTZ20] re-
trained on StyleGAN3 [KAL+21] as done in [APW+22], denoted as TTTC/In-
GAN, produces the most promising results. SAM can only progress images down
to the age 5 which is not sufficient as the final database should contain children
younger than that. Besides, as seen in Figure 4.3, the ageing process does not
always seem very natural as neither the head nor hair is transformed, and as
discussed the age estimation is not very precise. LFS and DLFS transform the
head and hair of subjects, making it look somewhat realistic and can progress to
a young age. Though there are, at times, artifacts in the images after progress-
ing, the quality of the two modelsвЂ™ youngest child images is somewhat lacking,
and there is often a loss of identity throughout the progression. This identity
loss can also be seen in the identity preservation score, where the two models
perform significantly worse than TTTC/InGAN and SAM. SAM has the best
IP score, by a tiny margin compared to TTTC/InGAN, by having the small-
est average cosine distance, but that is to be expected since the images do not
change much post-transformation. InterFaceGAN produces visually acceptable
progression images concerning head & hair transformation and has good iden-
tity preservation. It also has satisfying age flexibility as it can transform images
into a very young age, which has been verified with the age estimator. By us-
ing InterFaceGAN it is possible to alter images conditioned on any given latent
space direction. This capability can then be used to do intra-subject variation
in the form of e.g. pose changes. This is in contrast to the other approaches,
where images would have to be inverted into the latent space of StyleGAN3
first, which would worsen the results, or use an entirely different approach. A
shortcoming of InterFaceGAN is that if an image is moved too far in a given
age direction, identity loss and artifacts can appear when generating the needed
database. An algorithm is needed to determine when to stop moving to prevent
going too far.

age progression models. The most promising model was, based on the evaluated
criteria, deemed as InterFaceGAN retrained on the latent space of the more
recent StyleGAN3. This model will be used in Chapter 5.

5.1
Methodology
As mentioned the wanted result is a children facial image database. The database
should consist of the same n subjects at different ages, and at ages as low as
1-3 years old. These images of a subject at various ages can be used as ref-
erence images and then adding some kind of intra-subject variation to each of
the reference images can be used as probe images. To end up with this kind of
database a certain methodology has to be followed, and for this thesis the three
following steps were identified:
==PAGE:54==

1. Generate initial adult faces
2. Progress every adult to children at different ages
3. Create intra-subject variations versions of all faces
In more detail a image generator will be used in step 1, a face age progressor in
step 2 and some kind of latent manipulation in step 3. This is a quite abstract
way of putting it and some of the steps can be quite complex, which will be
explored further in the following sections.
5.2
Overview
The pipeline for creating the desired biometric dataset consists of different steps,
also denoted as components, and are defined as follows:
Sampling This step handles the generation of synthetic faces, thus creating
the initial database.
Filtering The filtering step handles the filtering of the initial database, remov-
ing poor quality and unwanted images.
Race Distribution As the generation of the initial faces is random, the distri-
bution of the subjectsвЂ™ races may be skewed. This step evenly distributes
races in the database.
Age Transformation Progressing an adult into a child is a key concept in this
thesis. This step does that by progressing an adult into a child in different
age groups.
Intra-Subject Transformation To biometrically benchmark a database, ref-
erence images need corresponding probe images, which this step is respon-
sible for creating.
Post-Processing This step will do some automatic cleaning. It ensures that
the same seeds are present in all different age groups and tries to remove
poorly transformed images.

subject is still good enough. Especially when transforming subjects to a lower
age, the output can sometimes be inadequate.

The algorithm takes the most represented race and changes a random subject
with this race into the least represented race, and keeps doing this until the
races are uniformly distributed. In more detail the algorithm takes a database
of images and latent vectors sampled from StyleGAN3, where the race of each
of the subjects has been classified with some algorithm initially. In this project
the race classifier by [SO21] has been used, which was also used to train the
boundaries.
A dictionary of the database is then created where each of the
races are keys and the corresponding values are stacks of subjects with that
race. The algorithm enters a loop (line 5), which terminates once there is an
equal amount of subjects as each race В±0.1%. Note that this error margin is
largely dependant on the database size, a large database would need a very small
error margin, while a small one would need a larger (e.g. 2-5%) to not get stuck
in a while true loop. In the body of the loop the most and least represented races
are calculated, and afterwards a random subject from the most represented race
is chosen. The latent vector wsubject of this random subject is then neutralized
with respect to the race it is to be changed to. This neutralization ensures that
when the subject is moved in the specific race direction, the subject will not be
transformed too much in the direction thus resulting in unrealistic image. The
chosen subject then gets transformed into the least represent race with amount
ar (line 10), where the amount depends on the race since some boundaries are
stronger than others. The newly transformed subject is then neutralized with
respect to smile, yaw and pitch, in the same manner as when the subject was
originally sampled. Finally the subject with its new race is saved. An example
of the distribution of the races before and after can be seen in Figure 6.7. From
the figure it can be seen that 70% of the subjects sampled are classified as white,
where only 0,5% are classified as black. It should be noted that a caveat of this
approach is that it is largely dependant on the race classifier. Each of the race
boundaries are trained with the classifier, and the same classifier is also used
to classify the races prior to the algorithm. Human inspection of the subjects
races may not always agree with what the classifier and algorithm outputs.

Post Processing
As mentioned in Section 5.8, the post-processing step of the database has two
objectives; the first is to remove subjects which are not present in all age groups
and the second is to discover and remove poorly transformed subjects.
The pipeline uses many different classifiers throughout the different compo-
nents, to predict age, gender etc. At times these components can fail, e.g. if
they cannot detect a face. Such a scenario may especially happen when dealing
with younger subjects, as some of these classifiers are not trained properly for
children. When a classifier fails the image is simply not saved, which then results
in a subject potentially missing in one of the age groups while being present in
the others. Once the pipeline is finished the intersection of all seeds across age
groups is found:
S = {A в€©AG0 в€©AG1 в€©AG2 в€©AG3 в€©AG4}
(6.6)
Where A is the seeds of subjects as adults, AG0 is the seeds of subjects in age
group 0 etc. Seeds which are not in this set S will then be removed across the
database, ensuring that the database only consists of subjects present in all age
group.

database should look as close as possible to ones of non-synthetic data. The

transformed. The statistics of this circle such as radius etc. can be treated as

7.2
Face Recognition Systems
The database described in Section 7.1 will be evaluated with multiple facial
recognition systems to determine the performance difference of facial recognition
systems on children. The impact that race and gender may have will also be
evaluated by said systems to investigate whether age may impact these factors as
well. The facial recognition systems under investigation are ArcFace [DGZ18]1
and MagFace [MZHZ21]2, both state-of-the-art open-source solutions, and a
commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) solution. ArcFace is essentially a loss function
well fitted for facial recognition where some CNN, like one from the ResNet
family [HZRS16], can be used as a backbone. MagFace is a more recent extension
of the ArcFace loss, which claims slightly better performance. Both systems
perform state-of-the-art on well-known face recognition datasets according to
their papers [MZHZ21, DGZ18]. MagFace and ArcFace work by extracting a
feature vector from a facial image. Some similarity measure can then be used to
calculate if two feature vectors are similar and thus of the same person. Before
doing the facial recognition with the two systems, face detection and alignment
was done with RetinaFace [DGZ+19b], a state-of-the-art face detection system.
The COTS system is proprietary; thus, further details cannot be provided of it.

shown that when changing the age of subjects it has a significant impact on the

Chapter 9
Discussion
This chapter starts out by discussing the observed results in Section 9.1. After-
wards possible future work in form of things that could be improved, new paths
to explore and what the thesis can be used for is discussed in Section 9.2.
9.1
Results
In this section, a brief discussion of the results will be done. The results will
also be compared to similar experimentsвЂ™ results on authentic data, as described
in Chapter 3.
9.1.1
Full Dataset
As seen in Section 8.1 from the observed results of the full dataset, the mated
scores are stable across the different age groups and in general have quite a
high mean. The progression highly impacts the non-mated scores across age
groups, which causes a performance decrease as the subjects gets younger when
verification metrics are measured. This drop in performance was a common
==PAGE:108==
94
Discussion
pattern across all three tested face recognition systems. Performance signifi-
cantly decreases as the subjects get younger, with a notable increase in EER
and different FNMR values. A common threshold in biometric verification is hav-
ing a FMR at 0.1% [Res15], which is why it makes it interesting to investigate
the FNMR(FMR=0.1%) metric. It corresponds to asking the question; if we ac-
cept 0.1% false positives, how many false negatives would we then have? By
looking at MagFace in Table 8.2, it was seen how this was a mere 0.037% for
the adults, but by progressing these same adults down to an age group of 16-
13, i.e. as teenagers, it rose steeply to 0.70%. And when they are progressed
to the youngest children of age 1-4, it rises to 2.4915%. This change in score
demonstrates how large a performance decrease can be observed when the same
identities get younger. On the youngest children, the COTS system had the
best performance, according to EER and the different FNMR values. Although
when looking at the adults COTS performed the worst. It is unknown what
kind of data is used in training this system, but it could indicate that the system
has seen images of young children before.
As this analysis of the performance across ages is based on synthetic data, the
question arises whether these same observed results would happen if it was
tested on facial images of authentic persons at different ages. As mentioned
in Section 3.4 synthetic data performs similarly to authentic data when tested
with facial recognition systems. Furthermore, in Section 3.5.1, several studies
were described in which a performance drop was also observed in real data
of children, compared to the performance of adults.
Notably, in the recent
paper [MSG23], a performance decrease in younger children is seen compared
to adults. The authors also note that different childrenвЂ™s identities are harder
to discriminate for the different facial recognition systems that they test. They
do see a performance increase by fine-tuning a system on their child database.
These results are comparable with the results observed in this thesis, but this
dataset has the benefit of being synthetic.
9.1.2
Race & Gender Subsets
In Section 8.3, it was observed how subjects of Black and Asian race in general
perform worse than ones of White and Latino-Hispanic. It was also seen that
all races have a performance decrease as they get younger. This again brings
up the question of whether the synthetic data plays a role and if the subjects
of Black and Asian are not varied enough. As described in Section 3.5, racial
bias is a known problem within facial recognition systems. In [GNH19], NIST1
has performed a vendor test with a specific focus on the performance and bias
1https://www.nist.gov/

95
of commercial face recognition systems concerning demographic. In the report,
they note several of the same observations regarding race, age and gender of
real data that we have done with synthetic data. Notably, very young children
have worse verification performance than adults, and the same with females
compared to males. Regarding race, they note that FNMR is often highest for
African subjects when using high-quality photos. Furthermore, they also report
performance problems with subjects of Asian race, albeit it depends somewhat
on the image quality. Again, in [WDH+19], the authors note that in their racial
test of scraped data, different SOTA systems and APIs perform the best on
Caucasian, followed by Indian, and the worst on Asian and African. These are
the same findings as from Section 8.3. While this is not direct proof that the
synthetic database generated has the same properties as real-life ones, it still
has some of the same tendencies.
Overall the results indicate that facial recognition systems are not robust to
younger subjects and that racial and gender bias is a general problem across
age groups.
9.2
Future Work
This section describes improvements and different related topics which could be
pursued within the subject of the thesis.
9.2.1
Post Processing
Many current quality assessment tools have a bias against children. The process
of post processing of the pipeline to remove poorly transformed children was
explained in Section 6.7. For this purpose, a principal component analysis was
done, and the two most significant principal components were used to determine
if a child was poorly transformed. While the approach works decently, it does
not always catch all of the outliers, which could be because the task is more
complex than what the two components can represent in specific scenarios. It
could be interesting to utilize more, or all, principal components in a different
manner than proposed to determine outliers and see if that would catch them
all. Alternatively, other methods to ensure the quality of the generated images
which also works for children could be investigated.
==PAGE:110==
96
Discussion
9.2.2
Better Classifiers
As mentioned several times throughout the thesis, the generated databaseвЂ™s
quality mainly depends on the used classifiers. The different learned boundaries
are only as good as the classifiers used for training, e.g. the better a classifier
is to classify a subject correctly, the better the pipeline is to do the transfor-
mation. This is also true for the age transformation step. The transformation
step is largely dependent on the selected age estimator, as the algorithm only
terminates when a given age is reached. This age relies solely on the age es-
timator used, which also could explain why some children get transformed too
far at times, as the target age should have been reached before it happened.
As seen in the age estimation test in Section 6.1.1, different age estimators have
been tested to find the best-performing one for children. However, classifiers
and estimators keep getting better. Thus newer, better-performing alternatives
will probably emerge in the future. Due to the pipelineвЂ™s loosely coupled design,
the used classifiers could easily be replaced with new ones.
9.2.3
More Intra-Subject Variations
In Section 6.6, the different possible intra-subject variations, which the pipeline
enables, were explained. These variations were chosen since they are very typical
for biometric databases. Not all of the variations used in creating the synthetic
database were as extreme as one would like, e.g. having a yaw from the complete
side of a face. Real-life databases, such as the MS-celeb-1M [GZH+16], which
previously have been used for training facial recognition systems such as ArcFace
and MagFace, contain extremely unconstrained probes. Creating these extreme
probes is not possible with current StyleGAN versions trained on FFHQ. This
is because the FFHQ database, which StyleGAN3 is trained on, is not that
heterogeneous and does not contain e.g. very extreme poses. An interesting
path would be fine-tuning StyleGAN3 on such an unconstrained database or
just a subset of one, allowing a latent space where more extreme boundaries
could be trained.
9.2.4
Non-Linear Transformation
Learned linear directions in the latent space are used extensively to change
everything from age to pose of subjects in the pipeline. While this approach
produces impressive results, it is interesting to pursue non-linear methods, which
may reduce entanglement problems. An effort was made to retrain the non-linear
==PAGE:111==
9.2 Future Work
97
StyleFlow model [AZMW21], on the more recent StyleGAN3 architecture. How-
ever, the results were not consistent enough compared to the linear approaches.
Still, this or other non-linear approaches could be investigated further.
9.2.5
Improving Recognition of Children
A main contribution of this thesis is the ability to generate a database of syn-
thetic children reference images at desired ages, races and generate different
intra-subject variations for probes. Being able to generate such databases be-
comes increasingly important as more and more facial database become re-
tracted due to privacy issues as mentioned in Section 3.4. As seen from the
observed results, facial recognition systemsвЂ™ performance degrades significantly
the younger the subjects get. In future research, a larger generated children
database could be used to fine-tune existing facial recognition systems that per-
form poorly on certain ages and races. Afterwards, the fine-tuned system could
be verified on existing facial image databases of real children.
9.2.6
Text to Image Generative Models
Recently text-to-image generative models have seen an enormous rise in popular-
ity, both in academia and for the common person, through public easily accessi-
ble APIвЂ™s and websites. Some notable ones are the models DALL-E [RPG+21] &
DALL-E 2 [RDN+22] from OpenAI. Others include Stable Diffusion 12 and Sta-
ble Diffusion 23 from the CompVis research group from Munich and Stability-AI,
which builds upon Latent Diffusion [RBL+22] which was tested in Section 4.2.
DreamBooth by Ruiz et al. [RLJ+22] from Google Research allows for fine-
tuning a text-to-image model on a given subject, which then allows for the
generation of images of this subject in any scenario described in text. This con-
cept could generate very unconstrained variations of a synthetic reference image,
which other current state-of-the-art approaches fail to do. Albeit the concept
may be hard to scale, as fine-tuning each subject is computationally expensive
for a large dataset. An example of the output of a fined tuned text-to-image
model can be seen in Figure 9.1, the model is trained on one of the reference
subjects with corresponding probes from the generated dataset.

9.2.7
Longitudinal Studies
This thesis does not focus on the longitudinal study of face recognition perfor-
mance on children, albeit much research has been done within the domain, as
seen in Section 3.5.1. The proposed pipeline also enables the possibility of creat-
ing an extensive database of the same subjects, where the reference image could
be the sampled image, and the probes could be the reference image at multiple
ages. It could be interesting to benchmark such a varied synthetic database for
longitudinal purposes, where unnecessary components of the pipeline such as
intra-subject variation could simply be toggled off.
9.2.8
Further Bias Analysis
The effect of gender and race on biometric performance was tested in different
facial recognition systems where bias was indeed observed.
Further analysis
could be done on this topic. An obvious approach could be analysing the effect
of genders within the various races at different ages. A larger database is likely
needed to get meaningful results, which is possible with the pipeline, albeit
computationally expensive.

real children data. Future work of the thesis was also discussed with multiple
proposals to investigate further.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The confluence of a growing emphasis on privacy with respect to individuals
in datasets and the demand for expansive datasets to effectively train facial
recognition systems emphasises the need for synthetic datasets.
Particularly
children datasets are needed by the police and related bureaus for finding lost
children and analysing child sexual abuse material (CSAM). This thesis explored
different approaches to achieve this and used them to design a piece of software,
denoted as a pipeline, for creating a children dataset to examine the performance
of face recognition systems. This thesis answers the research questions proposed
in Section 1.1, which will be presented separately below.
RQ1: How can new generative models be used to generate synthetic children
faces?
Due to the lack of children datasets, no one has trained a dedicated generative
model for sampling children faces. The most common approach is to gener-
ate an adult face and use a face age progression model to alter the subjectвЂ™s
age. In Chapter 4, an evaluation of the different generative models and face age
progression methods was done. The most promising generative model was clas-
sified as StyleGAN3 [KAL+21], which had a high face quality score and was the
most visually appealing. Different state-of-the-art face progression algorithms
were evaluated, and the InterFaceGAN [SYTZ20, APW+22] approach showed
the most realistic results. Combining these two models with an algorithm to
==PAGE:114==
100
Conclusion
control the age progression made it possible to create a model that can generate
realistic faces at controllable ages.
RQ2: How can generative models be used to create intra-subject variations to
children images without losing the characteristics of the face (identity)?
The underlying architecture of StyleGAN3 made it possible to alter the imagesвЂ™
latent space, enabling the change of face attributes, such as yaw, pitch and illu-
mination. Different approaches were used and explored. Both linear [SYTZ20]
and non-linear [AZMW21] techniques were trained and evaluated. The linear
method with InterFaceGAN, also used for age progression, showed the best and
most robust results. Boundaries from [APW+22] were used in combination with
self-trained boundaries for attributes like pitch, races and facial expressions.
RQ3: How well do the state-of-the-art face recognition systems work on young
childrenвЂ™s faces?
As mentioned, the created pipeline enabled the creation of synthetic facial im-
ages of children aged as young as 1-4 years old. These images were biometrically
evaluated with three different state-of-the-art facial recognition systems, namely
ArcFace and MagFace, which are open-source solutions and a proprietary com-
mercial off-the-shelf system COTS. The obtained results show that the systems
perform quite differently when it comes to recognizing very young children. Bio-
metric verification with ArcFace resulted in an equal error rate EER of 0.75%,
0.60% for MagFace and 0.48% for COTS on ages 1-4. As seen, ArcFace performs
the worst with a 25% larger EER than MagFace and 56% larger than COTS. Sim-
ilar when observing the FNMR(FMR=0.1%) ArcFace would produce 3.44% false
non-matches and thus reject 3.44% probes which should have gained access.
With MagFace, the rate was 2.49% and 1.78% for COTS.
In general, it can be seen that the performance changes quite a lot depending on
the system, where COTS shows better verification results than both ArcFace
and MagFace on very young children. MagFace shows slightly better results
than ArcFace, which is interesting as their training dataset is almost identical.
RQ4: How do state-of-the-art face recognition systems perform when evaluat-
ing images of the same individuals at different ages, specifically as children
and adults?
The same set of subjects was initially sampled as adults (identified as ages 20+)
and then progressed to the following age groups: 16-13, 13-10, 10-7, 7-4 and
4-1. And thus, the same biometric evaluation was done on the same subjects
at different ages. The observed results from this comparison showed that the
==PAGE:115==
101
age had an impact on both the open-source face recognition systems as well as
the commercial one. Common for all systems was that the younger the subjects
got, the worse verification performance could be observed, and thus the worst
was for the youngest children of ages 4-1. More specifically, the results from
MagFace could be observed as having an EER of 0.07% for the subjects as ages
20+, 0.27% for 16-13, 0.32% for 13-10, 0.37% for 10-7, 0.42% for 7-4 and 0.60%
for 4-1. The same patterns could be found in the different FNMR values, e.g. as
observed in FNMR(FMR=0.1%) where ages 20+ has a rate of 0.04%, 0.70% for
16-13 and ages 4-1 has a rate of 2.49%.
Based on the findings, it can be concluded that even when just progressing
the adults down to teenage age, significant performance degradation can be
seen in verifying the identity of subjects.
This degradation in performance
is proportional to age, where the gap in performance between the oldest and
youngest age group is relatively large.
RQ5: How does the race and gender of the subject impact the performance of
state-of-the-art facial recognition systems?
The generated synthetic database was also biometrically evaluated with respect
to gender and race across ages.
The obtained results show that gender and
race have an impact on the performance of all tested face recognition systems.
Common for all face recognition systems is that subjects of Black and Asian
race, generally, have worse verification performance than those of White and
Latino Hispanic, and Middle Eastern and Indian are somewhere in between.
This race effect is present for both adults and children, albeit for the very
youngest children, it is not as evident. Concerning gender, in general, females
perform worse than males as adults and in all but the youngest age groups.
In the youngest age group, males perform slightly worse than females in the
open-source systems. In the commercial system, the same pattern of females
performing worse is still there.
Race and gender bias can be seen as a problem in face recognition systems, even
for children. However, the bias is not as evident for the youngest child group,
possibly due to the significant performance decrease already present from the
age alone.
