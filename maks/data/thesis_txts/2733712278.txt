Abstract
Future wireline data center interconnect requires data rates faster than the current gen-
eration of 112 Gb/s. This thesis investigates the design of a low power continuous time
linear equalizer for 224 Gb/s high-speed interconnect. To propose a design this project
examines design requirements based on the case of a next generation Nvidia MSN-4700
high-performance network switch. To come up with a design state-of-the-art receivers
are examined. This project presents a source degenerated differential amplifier loaded
by a T-coil.
The design was characterized for insertion loss between 6.5 dB to 23.6
dB at the Nyquist frequency. This work proposes a programmable bias current which
makes the power consumption adaptive with regard to the channel length. Furthermore,
a programmable load resistor is used in combination with the bias current to fit high-
frequency content of the frequency response of the continuous time linear equalizer. The
mid-frequency content is controlled by a programmable degeneration capacitor. A design
for a continuous time linear equalizer with a data rate of 224 Gb/s is proposed. The design
shows an adaptive power consumption of 45.5 nJ/b for the long channel case and down
to 0.89 nJ/b for short channel case.

Introduction
The ever-developing internet has become a central part of both work and private life for
billions of users. The cloud used to be accessed for tasks such as running a website where
a single node in datacenter could process the data required. However, recent years have
shown a push towards using the cloud for data intensive tasks such as training artificial
networks, modelling, and running simulations. These tasks requires multiple network
nodes running in parallel with access to the data on each node. To avoid large latency this
pushes the demand for high-speed interconnects. A simple example of this is illustrated
in fig. 1.1. The servers are connected with a switch at the top of each rack and the
wires connecting the servers are the high-speed interconnects. Because there is a limited
amount of input and output pins on processing units the data must be serialized before
transmitted from, e.g., Server X to Server Y. To do this, a serializer-deserializer (SerDes)
is used respectively at the transmitting switch and the receiving switch. If 1 byte of data
is sent from Switch X to Switch Y, the interconnect should ideally operate at 8 times the
speed of the processing units to keep latency low. For 1 byte it might not be a problem if
the interconnect is slow but data intensive networks shares large quantity of data between
servers, and thus latency can slow down the computation process a lot. Thus, the high-
speed interconnect is essential for datacenters to function.

State-of-the-art interconnects are operated at Gigahertz (GHz) frequencies which com-
plicates signal integrity. Electrical signals have frequency dependent losses causing a
severe attenuation of high frequencies and a small attenuation of low frequency signals.
When bits are transferred, the frequency content of the signal varies, causing different
levels of attenuation. To avoid this, recent generations of interconnect has made use of
optical cables, which have very small losses, even for optical cables of several meters
of length [1]. Optical wires requires an electrical-to-optical (E/O) chip which is placed at
the output of the switch as shown in the top part of fig. 1.2. The signal is however still
attenuated by the path from the processing unit to the E/O chip, as depicted in the lower
section of fig. 1.2.
The frequency dependent attenuation is significant in the high frequency components,
such as the two pulses in the center of the depicted bit pattern. This makes it difficult for
the E/O chip to differentiate between a low and a high bit which will cause bit errors. If the
trace between the processing unit and the E/O chip is short, the signal can be recovered.
The recovery of the signal is done by the analog front end (AFE) of the E/O chip and
assisted by digital equalizers. One of the important blocks of the AFE is the continuous
time linear equalizer (CTLE) which recovers the signal by amplifying the high-frequency
components of the signal and attenuating the low-frequency components as illustrated in
fig. 1.3.

Equalizers are fundamental for high-speed interconnect, to ensure signal integrity. Thus,
future generations of high-speed interconnects are without equalizers operating at the
required data rate. Datacenters were estimated to use 1% of the global electricity con-
sumption in 2018 and this number is only expected to go up in the coming years[4]. Inter-
connects are used in manyfold in a datacenter meaning any design should be low power.
This means that any design made for future generation of datacenter hardware should
be designed towards low power. Therefor, there is a need for a low power CTLE design
suitable for the next generation of datacenter high-speed interconnect.
1.1
Problem statement
This project focuses on the design of a low power CTLE for the next generation of AFE
and is made in collaboration with the company Nvidia. Nvidia offers high-speed intercon-
nect as one of their products. Since network switches play a crucial role in high-speed
interconnect, this project will work with the design of a CTLE designed for the output of
high-speed switch from Nvidia. The design of the switch is based on the current gener-
ation MSN-4700 high-performance switch, shown in fig. 1.4. The main purposes of this
project is to specify requirements for a CTLE design for 224 Gb/s interconnect based on
the MSN-4700 network switch and make a design proposal for this CTLE.

2.1
Basic concepts
2.1.1
Data rate
Before looking into high-speed interconnects, the terminology is introduced. It is important
to distinguish between the bit frequency and the Nyquist frequency of a piece of intercon-
nect. The bit frequency relates to the speed of a high-speed interconnect which is the
maximum transferred bit per second (b/s), the current generation of interconnect has a bit
rate of 112 Gb/s. The Nyquist frequency is the maximum frequency expressed by a data
rate. This illustrated by the signal in fig. 2.1. As T=2Tbit it is evident that a circuit with
a desired bit rate of 1/Tbit has a Nyquist frequency of 1/2Tbit. Bit frequency is typically
used when talking about the speed of a piece of interconnect where the Nyquist frequency
is typically used for describing the circuits [6].

2.1.2
Data format
Data can be transmitted in different data formats. The most simple format is the non re-
turn to zero (NRZ) which sends one bit per symbol. If the number of bits per symbol is
increased, the data rate would increase while maintaining the Nyquist frequency. One
method of encoding more bits in a symbol is pulse amplitude modulate (PAM). Increasing
the number of bits per symbol comes with a power penalty raising concerns for amplitude
margins [7]. High-speed interconnect are standardized in the 802.3 [8] ethernet standard
which uses a PAM 4-level (PAM4) where two bits are endcoded in each symbol, as illus-
trated in fig. 2.2. It is evident that PAM4 uses half the time to send a bit pattern compared
to the NRZ format, thus doubling the data rate. With 2 bits per transmitted symbol an
interconnect with the data rate 224 Gb/s will have a symbol rate of 112 GBaud/s and a
Nyquist frequency of 56 GHz compared to the Nyquist frequency 112 GHz required for a
NRZ format [6].

to-peak signal amplitude. This means that the amplitude per signal level is decreased

2.1.4
Eye diagram
High-speed interconnect does not only face noise on the amplitude, but temporal distur-
bances. A temporal disturbance can shift the signal causing a misinterpretation of the
value therefore also increasing SER. To check if this is an issue, an eye diagram is used.
This diagram shows multiple bit transitions used as overlay on top of each other forming
an eye between the symbol levels. The eye diagram of a system is created by sending
a long pseudo random bit sequence (PRBS) through the system which is sampled every
other bit period, overlaying each sample on the previous samples [10]. A simple example
of this is shown in fig. 2.4 where 4 periods are sampled. From this it can be seen that an
eye is formed in the middle and with a larger eye there is a better amplitude margin and
thus a better SER.

2.2
High-speed interconnect
High-speed interconnect is implemented by a SerDes circuit with a channel between the
serializer and deserializer. This project focuses on the design of a CTLE placed after the
serializer, before the optical channel at the E/O chip. This project concerns the serializer
of the SerDes. The goal of this subpart of the circuit is for the E/O chip to convert the bits
from the serializer into an optical signal. The serializer outputs an electrical signal which
is fed to the AFE of the E/O chip through a channel as illustrated in fig. 2.5. The AFE is
connected to a decisions circuits in the E/O chip. Since the sender and the receiver do not
share a clock signal, a clock and data recovery (CDR) block is used to convert the analog
signals to digital. The digital output of the CDR leads to a driver for the E/O block which
ideally outputs an optical signal with identical bits to the transmission from the serializer.
This however, only works if the CDR is able to decipher the signal and equalizers are
used to recover the signal after the channel. Most of the recovery is done by the CTLE
placed at the AFE but this is not the only equalizer. The CTLE is assisted by equalizers
both in the serializer and in the CDR block. These equalizers are feed forward equalizers
(FFE) which adds delayed versions of the input to the signal with different weights, or a
decision feedback equalizer (DFE) which uses knowledge of previous bits to adjust the
current symbol. This can be used to cleanup an eye diagram for ISI [2]. Thus, equaliza-
tion of the signal is done using multiple blocks.
The serializer has been presented as a single ended system but for high-speed applica-
tions a differential output is used. A differential signal reduces noise because any common
mode noise is ignored, and unequal rise and fall times are cancelled as the signals always
rise and fall opposite to each other [2].

2.3.1
Attenuation from conductor losses
The conductor loss is an ohmic loss but it scales with frequency because of the skin effect.
The skin effect describes that for high-frequency current the current density is pushed to
edge of the conductor, as illustrated in fig. 2.7A. This happens because the varying input
current, Iac,in, creates a magnetic field, H, in the middle of the conductor. This creates
eddy currents which nulls out the current in the center of the conductor. Higher frequencies

The bridged T-coil

at high frequencies as the bridging capacitor CB then acts as a short.
The T-coil is useful for termination of transmission lines as it ideally will terminate in a
purely resistive load for all frequencies[18].

2.5
High-speed CMOS technology
For faster devices it is desired to have smaller transistors. The smallest available tech-
nology for this project is Global Foundries fully depleted 22 nm SOI process (GF22FDX).
This process is a silicon on insulator (SOI) process where silicon islands are put on top
of an insulator. Each device is separated from the substrate using a layer of buried oxide
(BOX), as shown in fig. 2.12. This removes the need of doping the channel to create
a depletion zone and therefore the name fully depleted [20]. As different doping con-
centrations is one of the parameters which causes variation, the fully depletion reduces
the process variation. The layer does not only isolate each device but it also reduces
the junction capacitance from source/drain to bulk as the insulating BOX increases the
distance between source/drain and bulk. While the SOI devices are able to operate at
higher speed, the insulation is a poor thermal conductor, and so the thermal properties
are reduced compared to a bulk process [21]. Another feature which is enhanced in SOI

2.6
Literature study
A few design proposals for the next generation of high-speed SerDes have already been
proposed in the litterature. This section introduces the work of these two design proposals
and two designs of the current generation of 112 Gb/s devices.
Two of the most cited designs of the current generation of CTLE designs from Journal of
Solid State Circuits, are [24] and [25]. In [24] they are showing a 4 pJ/b receiver design
for a channel with 35 dB of attenuation at Nyquist and in [25] they propose a receiver
design for a channel with 36 dB attenuation at the Nyquist Frequency. The design of [24]
utilizes a feed-forward equalizer (FFE) to reduce the amount taps for the decision feed-
back equalizer (DFE). In this circuit the AFE uses a CTLE with two parts: An active part
implemented by a complementary set of differential amplifiers and a passive part which is
loaded by a passive stage. The active stage consists of multiple parallel slices that can be
turned on and off, thus changing the gain. The passive stage consists of an RLC network
were L and C sets a resonance frequency used for peaking. The resonance frequency
can be moved by changing R since this changes the quality factor of the inductor, which
is known as Q-shaping.
In [25] a new structure for DFE is proposed which overcomes design issues for many-
tap-implementations. This design uses a CTLE with source degeneration and inductive
peaking. This circuit also uses a passive attenuator between the input stage and the
CTLE. The input of the attenuator is AC-coupled to the CTLE which gives 4 dB extra
high-frequency gain without power or noise penalties.
The design proposals for the 224 Gb/s CTLEs can be collected in two projects which have
published a series of articles building up to the most comprehensive designs which pro-
posed in [26] and [27]. In [27] a design is proposed where the AFE consists of an input
stage implemented with a T-coil. This feeds the CTLE, which is using source degenera-
tion but instead of using a shunt inductor for high-frequency control, they use a T-coil, see
fig. 2.13B. In this design the last stage of the AFE is a VGA with 8 settings to bring the
signal back to the correct full scale. This system is a design for a channel up to 25 dB of
attenuation at Nyquist.
Another receiver design is proposed in [26] which is shown in fig. 2.13B. This design uses
an AFE with an input stage made with ESD protection implemented by a LC line. This
feeds into a CTLE made by a complementary differential pair utilizing the same current
through the PMOS and NMOS devices to obtain a larger gm with the same current. The
CTLE shapes the frequency response using source degeneration for the mid-frequency
bands and inductive peaking for the high-frequency bands controlled by Q-shaping. This
design has been made for medium to long reach channels ranging from 20.6 dB to 37 dB
attenuation at Nyquist frequency.

difficult case the following two subsections examines the necessary requirements to fit

CTLE order
The first thing to consider is the order of the CTLE. In the ideal case the CTLE is designed
with a number of zeros and poles. Here, the number of zeros will define the order of
the CTLE. An ideal first order CTLE contains one zero and two poles. The options of
fitting a first order CTLE are limited, as the position of the zero sets the only place where
frequency breaks towards an increasing slope. Alternatively a higher order CTLE can be
used to obtain a better fit as shown in fig. 3.5 but this circuit would increase the complexity
of the design. To test if it is possible to use a first order CTLE for this attenuation, different
values of the zero and poles are sweept and compared with the transmission line losses.
As shown in fig. 3.6, it is not possible to obtain a perfect fit with a first order CTLE but the
fit is close to being within the specification. This indicates that a first order CTLE can be
used and the complexity of a second order CTLE can be avoided. By looking at fig. 3.6 it
can be seen that if the frequency response is tilted a bit the fit would become better. This
can be done by adding a slope.

3.2.5
Input common mode
For the CTLE to provide gain active devices will be used and these must be biased by
a common mode voltage. For this section it is assumed that the CTLE is a differential
amplifier. Furthermore it is assumed that the channel is AC coupled to the input such that
the common mode on the input can be set to the level that is fit. This level should ensure
that the active devices are in the correct operating region.
3.2.6
Group delay
As the symbol period in this circuit is rather short, 1/56 GHz=8.93 ps, the amount of
allowed group delay is limited. For this project it is assumed that a group delay below half
the symbol is good. Thus the group delay is limited to 4.465 ps.
3.2.7
Power
As this is a low power design it should not use more power than the other proposed
designs for the next generation. Of the two proposed designs the one with the lowest
energy is [27], where 17.1 mW is used for the AFE. In that design each branch of the CTLE
uses 4 mA with a 0.9 V power supply. As mentioned earlier this system operates with a
0.9 V supply as well meaning that the maximum current per branch is 4 mA, assuming
a single stage CTLE. With these assumptions the requirement of the power draw is 7.2
mW.

3.2.9

The input and output impedance of the CTLE should match the stage before and after to
avoid reflections as these will travel back and forth on the channel and act as a disturbance
on other bits. The stage before the CTLE is the input stage which acts as a termination
of the transmission line. If the input impedance of the CTLE is large it is more difficult
to design the input stage as reflections from impedance mismatch distorts the signal.
It is assumed that 10% of the SNR budget can be used on reflections from impedance
mismatch between the input stage and the CTLE. This means that the reflected signal
must be attenuated by more than 40.61 dB. In [27] the CTLE is made with a 20 fF input
impedance. This value is assumed for this project as well. To test the signal strength of
the reflection the input stage is implemented using a T-coil designed for constant resistive
load. This has been done with a termination resistor of 50 Ωand a load of 20 fF in parallel
with 80 fF from ESD protection as suggested by [27]. The return loss of this setup is

3.3
Technology specifications
Apart from making requirements for the block the devices for this project should also be
chosen. This is a high-speed circuit and the fastest transistors should be used. The
GF22FDX comes with a range of transistor models based one measurements between
100 MHz and 110 GHz called milimeter wave (mmw) devices. The rest of the device
models are based on measurement with a maximum frequency of 40 GHz and might
therefor lack parasitics who would influence this circuits behaviour at 56 GHz. Thus it is
decided to use the mmw devices.
3.3.1
Metal choice
The metal stack used for this project should contain the right layers for the required de-
vices. The biggest concern is to make the inductors. To allow a good layout of inductors
it requires wide metal layers, such as Q1 and Q2. From the reference guide of the mmw
devices this reduces the options to four metal stacks. Among these it is only metal stack
19 which has both Q1 and Q2. Metal stack 19 is chosen for this project and the details of
the metal stack are: 10M2Mx5Cx1Jx2QxLB.
3.3.2
Transistor comparison
It is decided to primarily use the same type of transistors. To pick the right one the Ft
is compared between the low threshold devices. The Ft for these devices is found by
sweeping the gate voltage to find the one which results in the the largest Ft. As the circuit
heats up during operation the Ft comparison also considers two different temperatures.
The device comparison is plotted in fig. 3.11. Now knowing the gate voltages required
for the highest Ft the next test is to verify that the devices can operate at this frequency
without overheating. It is assumed that 20 ◦C is a good upper bound for the self-heating of
the devices. The self-heating of each device is compared in fig. 3.12 where VDD is swept
while the gate voltage is set individually per device from the findings in fig. 3.11. From
the two plots it can be seen that the slvnfet (super-low-Vth nfet) device has the highest
Ft but it also runs a bit warmer than most of the other devices. As all of VDD is dropped
across the devices this means that the slvnfet device can operate with a voltage drop of
0.43 V across it without getting too warm. As the CTLE is operated with VDD=0.9 V it
would require almost half of the supply to drop across a single device of this size before
it becomes a thermal issue. Thus it is decided that slvnfet devices will be used for this
project.

Design
This section describes the design of the CTLE. The first part describes choice of topol-
ogy which is followed with a description of the test setup. The next part describes the
design as the sub blocks are put together and hand tuned to prove the design concept
can work for the long channel. This is followed by a description of how the CTLE is made
programmable. After that, Process, voltage and temperature (PVT) corners are evalu-
ated and a simple layout is used to get a size estimate. The active components are mmw
devices with parasitics estimated and inductors have an estimated resistance of 0.5 Ω.
Resistors and capacitors are ideal.
4.1

The design of the CTLE must ensure the ability to equalize a wide band of frequencies
and be adjustable to fit different channels. This is done by using two sub blocks, one to
cover the low- and mid-range frequencies and another which covers the high frequencies.

at the high frequencies. The T-coil can be seen as a transimpedance output stage, as it
amplifies an input current Iin to an output voltage Vout. The two blocks can be combined

by the temperature variations a large low frequency error is introduced if it should fit at
the Nyquist Frequency. Accordingly is the CTLE robust in the temperature range 0 ◦C to
40 ◦C.

uses inductors with no coupling as there was no pdk inductors with coupling and this floor
plan is only used to get an area estimate meaning the presented floor plan is an upper
limit of the area. If a layout is made with coupled inductors, matching that of the design,
it will become much smaller, since two mutually coupled inductors approximately take up
the same amount of space as one inductor, which is shown in design proposal in [17].

factor of 2 if bias circuits are not considered part of the CTLE.

Discussion
The outcome of this work is the proposed design of a low power CTLE. This design has
been fitted to a range of channels showing a maximum error less than 1 dB. The input
impedance of 18 fF is close to the specification of 20 fF, and the SNR of 55 dB is above
the required 40.61 dB. The a programmable bias current reduces the power consumption
of the CTLE for the short channel case to 0.222 mA/5.6 mA · 100% = 3.9% of the long
channel case. The maximum power consumption of 45.5 nJ/b is larger than the specifi-
cation 32.14 nJ/b. The short case on the other hand is meeting the requirement as the
power specification as it is 0.89 nJ/b. It can be discussed if the design is within the power
spec but the results show that power can be reduced depending on the channel length
especially if there are more short channels than long channels.
With only 222 mA in each branch the short channel the headroom of the input pair is re-
duced headroom to only 20 mV. This is an issue when the expected output swing is 48
mV. The eye diagram of this case, see appendix F.4, shows a 100 mV peak-to-peak out-
put signal for the short case. That the signal level is as expected might be caused by the
inductors allowing dynamic signals to exceed the supply voltage. The issue is with low
frequency signals which might be clamped. This has not been investigated any further in
this project.
During the transient evaluation of the design it was discovered that the attenuation caused
by the channel deviated from what was seen in the AC analysis. A simple setup with the
channel model showed -23.67 dB at the Nyquist frequency in an AC analysis where a
transient amplitude only dropped from 1 V to 0.17 V, corresponding to -15.4 dB. This is
problematic for verifying the results found in the AC analysis in a large signal analysis.
As the CTLE had multiple settings for a range of losses it was decided to test the setting
of the CTLE which fitted the transient attenuation of the CTLE. As the channel model for
the longest case showed 18 dB of attenuation at the Nyquist frequency a CTLE setting
for 18.13 dB was used and the results of this was presented fig. 5.6. These results show
a peak-to-peak voltage of 100 mV which is close to the expected output swing of 94 mV
and an eye which is disturbed by smaller eye shapes on each signal level, this looks like
ISI. It is assumed that this is caused by the group delay with a size of 8.3 ps which is close
to the symbol period of 8.9 ps. It is assumed that this part of the signal can be recovered
after the CTLE. Based on these observations of the transient analysis is it assumed that
the CTLE design works for a channel with the expected transient losses. This indicates
that the results of the frequency response are correct. If any further work is made on
this design a new transmission line model should be used to to allow for better transient
testing.
Another note on the transmission line model used in this project is that it is non-linear at
the low frequencies where the skin effect dominates, see fig. 5.1. This makes it difficult
to obtain a good fit at the low frequencies. This can be seen in the error plot in fig. 5.2
where the behaviour below 5 GHz introduces close to 0.15 dB out of the total 0.73 dB of
error. This behavior has made tuning quite difficult especially in the short channel case.
It should be investigated if it can be assumed that the energy of the signal at the low fre-
quencies is small enough to justify fitting only above 5 GHz.
The design has been evaluated in PVT corners in the case of the long channel. This
showed that with 5% variation of VDD the maximum error -0.6 dB to -1.1 dB. In process
variation backgate biasing is used to compensate for variations with the largest error being
A low power continuous time linear equalizer for 224 Gb/s PAM4 signals
53
==PAGE:60==
-1.1 dB in the sf corner. In the temperature corner the design was test with temperatures
in the range 0-100 ◦C. This introduced an error spanning from -0.47 dB to -2.3 dB. This
could not be fixed by assuming a temperature coefficient at the current source indicating
that the issue might not only be with the current. It could also be caused by changes in
the load resistor and it should be investigated if a temperature dependent control signal
to the load could compensate the error induced by the temperature corner. It is noted that
most of the errors from PVT occurs because there is a drop in magnitude at the Nyquist
frequency. Some of the error could be countered by tuning the CTLE to a slightly higher
gain at the Nyquist frequency than -0.73 dB obtained in this project.
This CTLE is referred to the output and not ground meaning that the power supply rejection
ratio is assumed to be poor and the next stage must take this into account to reduce the
noise output of the AFE.
This design uses a degeneration capacitor CS for fitting mid bad frequencies similar to
[26] and [27]. Where this design differs is the programmable ibias used to lower the power
consumption for short channels. This is used in combination with a variable RL which
changes the peaking of the T-coil but it also affects the DC gain. The combination of ibias
and RL makes it possible to maintain a constant DC gain among all the channels. One
of the difficulties of using RL to control the high frequency gain is that it also changes the
voltage drop across the load and the initial thought was that this could be countered by
changing the bias current as well. This however was not possible, meaning that this setup
has a non-constant IR drop across the load and each setting must be examined to verify
that the output does not clip. Especially clipping at the short channel case was an issue
during the tuning process. This design has been designed to drive a stage with a similar
input impedance, 20 fF, compared to the 80 fF in [27]. The results of this work is compared
with the other proposed designs for 224 Gb/s interconnect which is shown in table 6.1.
This comparison is of the CTLE from the receiver designs to allow for a fair comparison.
This work introduces the possibility of reducing power consumption for the short channel
but in the long channels case it still uses a lot of power especially as the load is smaller
than [27] and the channel attenuation is slightly smaller as well which makes it difficult to
make a direct comparison.

Conclusion
This project has considered the requirements and design of a CTLE with a data rate of 224
Gb/s. The requirements of the CTLE of this project is based on an attenuation assumption
of the PCB trace in the MSN-4700 switch and design performance of previously proposed
designs. This work investigates the option of having a programmable bias current in
combination with a programmable active load. This combination allows for a constant
DC gain and reduces the power consumption of short channels. This work shows a final
design of a CTLE that is adjustable to channels between 6.5 dB and 23 dB of attenuation.
The design can adjust to the channels in 32 two settings within an error of -1 dB to 1 dB.
The design has been examined in PVT corners and it was found that process variation
can be compensated by using back biasing in the SOI process while voltage variations
only have little effect on the circuit. However, temperature variations introduce an error
at the output up to 2.3 dB. Further work is required to identify how the biasing network
could be made temperature dependent to compensate for error caused by the temperature
variation. The results of this project shows that the power consumption of a CTLE can be
made adaptive by setting the bias current dependent on the length of the channel. This
shows a reduction in power from 45.5 nJ/b to 0.089 nJ/b when changing from the long
channel to the short channel. In conclusion, this work shows that it is promising to design
a low power CTLE for a data rate of 224 Gb/s by making a CTLE with adaptive power
consumption.

Future work
To improve the design proposed in this project the following future work can be carried
out:
• Make a test setup where the AC and transient analysis does not deviate. This would
make it is easier to verify any further design work.
• Consider power supply rejection ratio.
• Consider common mode gain of the system.
• Investigate the use of a model with linear behaviour below 5 GHz to allow for a better
fit at the Nyquist frequency.
• Check if self-heating is a problem for any of the devices.
• Test largest error between each setting to check if any settings require returning and
check PVT corners for each setting.
• Test the PVT corners for all settings of the CTLE and change any if it is required.
• Investigate if inductive peaking combined with adjustable bias current could be an
option.
• Check the short channel settings for clamping in case of non-dynamic input signals.

I
Project Evaluation
I.1
Project description
The aim of this project is to design a low power CTLE for a 224 Gb/s PAM4 communication
channel. The CTLE will be designed for the next generation of data center interconnect.
This means that the CTLE design must have a higher data transfer rate than the current
generation of 112 Gb/s. The design must be done with concerns to the power consumption
as data centers are responsible for a significant amount of the world’s energy consump-
tion, 3-4% [https://www.aflhyperscale.com/articles/what-makes-hyperscale-hyperscale/].
The work will revolve the CTLE block. Bias circuits and inductors may be based on mod-
els. Main challenge to be investigated is minimizing power consumption while meeting
the speed requirement of 224 Gb/s. The CTLE will be designed towards use in a switch
from NVIDIA. Part of this project is to define the specification requirements for the CTLE
block for this use case. The work will be made in a 22-nm silicon on insulator (SOI) CMOS
process.
I.2
Learning objectives
The initial learning objectives of this project are:
• Understand principles of high-speed serdes systems.
• Describe a variety of performance measurements for a CTLE.
• Investigate current international state-of-the-art relevant CTLE designs to make a
performance summary.
• Compare relevant CTLE topologies to pick a well-suited topology for the application.
• Derive block level assumptions based on reasonable system level assumptions.
• Work in a technical problem-solving at a high level through cross-disciplinary team-
work and can work with and manage all phases of a project – Including preparation
of timetables, design, solution and documentation.
• Design a CTLE in a state-of-the-art CMOS technology with bias circuits based on
schematics or VerilogA models, inductors may be based on EMX models or realistic
lumped models.
• Examine design performance in process, supply voltage and temperature corners.
• Evaluate the performance of the design based on performance metrics.
• Propose a layout of the design and make parasitic extraction of critical high speed
devices.

Time plan

Evaluation
The initial plan of this project was to spend approximately 1/5 of the time on theory, 1/5
of the time on block level requirements, 2/5 of the time on design and layout and the last
1/5 on the documentation. As the topic was new the amount of time for theory reading
worked out well but this section was cut short by one week to start the work on the block
