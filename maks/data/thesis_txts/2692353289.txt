Abstract
The goal of this project is to evaluate the 5G performances when used with drones in a
real scenario. 5G is the newest cellular network technology and should be able to provide
ultra-low latency and ultra high speed. In order to test its capabilities, we focused on
two different situation: how altitude affects the connection when operating the drone
remotely outdoor and to what extent the 5G can support a ROS network for autonomous
operation indoor.
To achieve our goals, we focused on different metrics: for the outdoor flight we
considered the received signal power, the client-server latency and the data bandwidth,
while for the indoor flight we focused on the ROS messages latency and on the drone
response when following commands.
Our results show how altitude gives a beneficial effect to the connection, both in
terms of received power and latency, moreover, during the indoor test we concluded that
5G technology is comparable with a LAN connection for autonomous operation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter we are going to first present the goal and objective of this project,
secondly describe the problems and limitations that we encountered and finally present
the structure of this report.
1.1
Objectives
Since its announcement in 2012, 5G technology has soon attracted a lot of interest,
mainly because it is a substantial technological evolution of the current LTE. Its capa-
bility of providing ultra-high data speed and ultra-low latency has the potential to give
an great impact on multiple fields, from critical ones like tele-medicine[1] to consumer
oriented ones like smart-homes full of IoT devices[2].
What we want to outline with this project is how 5G performs when used in combi-
nation with an areal vehicle, both when controlling it remotely with manual commands
and when it is performing autonomous operations. To do it, we set up two different tests:
one was an outdoor test to evaluate the performances of 5G when flying at medium al-
titude (about 60 meters), the other was an indoor test where a drone had to complete
a track passing through a series of hoops autonomously.
For the outdoor flight, the main goal was to verify that, despite the antenna orienta-
tion in the sky, the 5G signal was of a better quality than the one at ground level, due
to the absence of physical obstacles leading to free-space propagation. Meanwhile, with
the autonomous test, we wanted to see the capability of 5G to handle a ROS network,
while comparing its performances with a cabled LAN connection.
The tests were performed while imagining two distinct real world applications: for
the outdoor flight we thought about the case were a drone operator could be seated in a
remote station with access to a series of drones connected to 5G. This would allow him
could control them without being physically present with almost non-existing delay and
high quality video-stream information. All of that using the existing commercial network
and without the need of expensive satellite connection. One example of this case could
be the delivery of life-saving drugs or equipment, a series of drones are deployed around
a certain area and one (or more) operator could stand in the control room and send the
necessary package while evaluating live the situation and giving the necessary first aid
information. Meanwhile, one application of autonomous operation using 5G could be
the exploration of areas for rescue purposes, small drones with the necessary equipment

1.3
The IP address problem
To allow communication between devices over the Internet, it is necessary to have an
IP address for each of them. However, as of today, the number of IPv4 addresses is
exhausted [3] due to to the limitation of a protocol that is almost 40 years old and can
have only 232 unique addresses. Due to this shortage, ISPs tend to not provide public IP
addresses to their costumers, but to aggregate them using a technique called CNAT. We
asked to all main ISPs in Denmark, but none of them was able to provide a data SIM
with both 5G and a public IP. The solution that we adopted to overcome this problem
was using the DTU VPN, however, introducing a VPN, despite making the process of
connecting devices easier, adds a delay and instability element to the connection.

2.3
5G key features
5G technology, or NR, is the fifth generation radio standard for mobile communication.
Its development started in 2012 and it is managed by an organization known as 3GGP,
like the previous technologies [7].
The scopes of 5G, according to 3GGP and synthesized by ITU recommendation
==PAGE:15==
2.4 5G design principles
5
IMT-2020 [8], are:
• Enhanced Mobile Broadband (eMBB): This corresponds to the expansion of the
current LTE/3G technologies to allow improvements on critical aspects of the user
experience, like higher data-rates and high-traﬀic volumes.
• Ultra-reliable and low-latency communications (URLLC): This is the most relevant
aspect for this research, since it is strongly interconnected with the human to
machine and machine to machine communication. It means that the connection
needs to be reliable under any condition with low latency, which is exactly what
we want from a connection to a remotely operating drone.
• Massive machine-type communication (mMTC): This metric is primarily referring
to scenario where a large number of devices is involved, regardless of the data-
transfer speed for each device. A typical example of this could be a large network
of IoT objects, like what a smart city neighborhood could be [2], [9].
2.4
5G design principles
One of the main characteristic of 5G, which is also one of the differences with LTE, is
the usage of a wider spectrum of frequencies in which it can operate [7][10]. This way of
working at ultrahigh frequencies is known as millimeter-wave (mmWave), and it allows
5G to reach top speeds of over 5Gb/s [11]. However, higher frequency means higher
channel degradation, as expressed by the following equation of the available attenuation
[12]:
aCh =
(4πd/λ)2
gAnt,TxgAnt,Rc
(2.1)
Where d is the distance between two antennas, λ is the wavelength of the transmitted
signal, gAnt,Tx,gAnt,Rc are the gains of the transmitting and receiving antennas.
Another key feature of NR is the massive(Multi Input Multi Output, also called
MIMO. To achieve MIMO, each 5G cell transmits information in different beams, in a
technique called beamforming. Each beam will be then directed to the most appropriate
receiver to reach the maximum performance. However, if there are many antennas, the
beams are narrow and beam tracking can fail [7]. The concept of beamforming could be
seen at figure 2.1.
Moreover, energy eﬀiciency must be considered, since one of the goals of 5G is the
connection of many devices at the same time. Currently one of the problem ”is the
amount of transmissions carried by network nodes regardless of the amount of user
traﬀic” [13]. Those are also known as ”always-on” signals and they include methods to
detect the base cell and broadcast of system information among others. However, this
kind of transmission introduces some negative factors. For instance intracell interference
and an overall reduction of energy eﬀiciency are two of them. 5G is designed with the

2.5
Drone controller
When performing the autonomous flight inside ASTA, the drone is working thanks to a
controller based on the one proposed by Lee in [15]. The interest of this implementation
is that it needs to constantly evaluate the position feedback from the drone, and it needs
to get this information in the smallest amount of time. This can be use as the perfect test
to evaluate 5G performances, only with an ultra-low latency the drone will be stable. We
used a position controller running on the remote machine, while an attitude controller
was running onboard.

are going to use what we described here as a starting point to develop our strategy for
the field tests.

3.4
Measured metrics
To evaluate the 5G performance and to see if it is a suitable solution for operating
drones, we focused on different metrics for the indoor test and the outdoor one. The
power of the received signal, the connection latency, and the data transfer speed were
considered for the outdoor test, while the ROS message time and the drone pose/yaw
were monitored for the inside flight.
3.4.1
Signal power
Due to the nature of the two different technologies (LTE and 5G), the signal power can
not be used as a direct comparison between the two of them, however it can still be
used to see how the connection varies from ground level to medium altitude. This case
is relevant due to the fact that to maximize the area covered with beamforming, the 5G
antennas are usually tilted to point at ground level [5]. However, it must be noticed
that when flying, the signal is not encountering any obstacles, so the channel condition
is close-to-free-space propagation. That is why this metric is especially interesting when
considering the outdoor flight. Specifically, we monitored two values for both LTE and

3.4.2
Latency
One of the promises of 5G is to offer ultra-low latency, so we decided to log the response
time from the flying drone to a remote location. We used a Raspberry Pi that was
connected to DTU network through an Ethernet cable, to minimize latency from that
side. Furthermore, since during the flight we needed to have a working connection to
keep the 5G connection alive, we collected the data from a ping test to google.dk website,
that gave us an overview of the latency during the whole test.
Performing the autonomous fly inside ASTA, we also monitored the ROS delay time,
which is defined as the difference between the creation of one ROS message in the control
machine to its reception from the remote node in the drone. To minimize errors, before
running the tests, both machines have been synced to the same time server (running on
the control machine) using a cable connection.

Having low latency is especially helpful when performing critical operations. For
instance, it can be employed for close space inspections, since it allows to obtain im-
mediate feedback from the UE to the base station, both for autonomous operation and
when remotely operated by a controller.
It is worth remembering that during these tests all measures are affected by the delay
introduced by the VPN, as explained in section 1.3.
3.4.3
Data bandwidth
5G should offer higher data transfer speed than the LTE technology, as it happens
with the latency. To measure this, we used an open-source tool called iPerf34. This
tool consists of two different elements: a client running on the drone, which is used to
measure upload speed and a server, that was installed in the Raspberry Pi inside DTU.
We carried out several trials at different altitudes, each of them had a duration of 10s
and 15 parallel TCP connections, obtaining multiple measures.
A higher data speed proved to be associated with more possibilities of controlling the
drone remotely, similarly to the latency. One possible application is to live streaming
a 4K resolution video, to give the operator a better image of what is happening on the
drone side.
3.4.4
Position and yaw
To measure the performances of 5G in the autonomous flight, we collected the data of
the position of the drone in the Optitrack reference system. We then compared it with
the set position to see how the latency affects the movements of the drone. We also
considered the yaw, but this time the drone’s measure was not directly taken from the
Optitrack system, but estimated from the other position parameters. We collected the
data both with 5G and a direct Ethernet connection, so we could compare them and
analyze the differences in the errors obtained.
3.5

the drone was flying at around 10m in autonomous mode using ROS and the Optitrack
sensors. The main goal of the indoor test was to see if 5G could support a ROS network,
so we focused on measuring the latency and the drone state during the flight. Second, a
series of measures that were taken outside, with the goal of evaluate how altitude affects
the 5G performances.

To sum up, the process that we followed to conduct the tests was:
Step 1 Establish SSH connection between base station and onboard computer (Intel
NUC).
Step 2 Enable VPN connection to DTU network, to allow measurements of latency and
data transmission speed, as well as for configuring the ROS parameters. This is
done in order to have a static know IP address as explained in the introduction
(1.3).

dows).
Step 5 Start the scrip to collect data from the modem (Windows) and to measure latency
and data-transfer rate (Ubuntu).
Step 6 Fly the drone in one of the specific test scenarios. There are four:
a) Outdoor and indoor base level (1 meter high), remote control.
b) Outdoor medium altitude (≈30 meters), remote control.
c) Indoor autonomous flight using the ASTA optitrack support.
d) Outdoor flight (≈60 meters), remote control.

4.5
Future works
One important development for the future would definitely be to repeat the measure
for bandwidth and point-to-point latency to acquire valid results. It would also be a
major improvement to have the possibility of repeating all tests without using the VPN,
however this is a more complex problem and it is something that probably will be solved
only when the current Internet infrastructure updates to its IPv6 stage. Another point
would then be to repeat all the tests when the commercial availability of the ultra-low
latency 5G is more widespread, since now all our test were not conducted using the
maximum capacity of this technology (at least according to the TDC coverage map
[19]). Finally, to test the real capability of 5G to handle massive amount of devices, it
would be interesting to test how a swarm of drones, like the CrazyFlie, perform while
multiple devices are connected to the same 5G cell.
4.6
Summary
Despite we could not use all the measures that we took from the outdoor test, we could
however prove that 5G is in fact a revolutionary technology. We think that the figures
4.7, 4.8, 4.9 and 4.13 showing how the signal and the performance change while moving
down, represent clearly why we can consider the possibility of using 5G for remote
operations.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
When considering both tests, it can be seen that the objectives described in the intro-
duction are partially fulfilled, although it is not possible to present a complete overview
of 5G performance when flying outdoor.
From the collected data, it can be deduced that altitude is not affecting 5G technology
in a negative way, and it is indeed improving its performance, opening the possibility for
operating drones remotely through a cellular network. This conclusion is based on signal
power evolution and the results from the ping test to google.dk. The lack of data for the
point-to-point latency and for the data transfer speed is why we think that the objective
has been only partially reach. This is due to the fact that one of the assumed real word
implementation that was considered (one operator controlling the drone remotely) is
mainly based on those two metrics.
Regarding the autonomous test, we were able to reach our goals: we had a working
ROS network that was maintained by 5G and the performance of it were comparable
to the one of a wired connection. That gives the opportunity to use the existing infras-
tructure that is the cellular network to integrate the drones for autonomous operation.
The findings suggest that 5G technology applied to drones can be a path to follow
in order to achieve SDG 11. The results of the indoor test shows that it will be possible
to have networks of autonomous drones monitoring the environment, avoiding natural
distastes like fires or flooding thanks to preventive inspection. The widespread of 5G
in the urban and pre-urban area will allow to use the drones as a means to transport
goods and services from centralized operative station to the surroundings.
Overall, this project gave us new knowledge on how the drones interact with 5G and
it gave us the possibility to explore the world of network infrastructure related to mobile
IoT. It is also a starting point for future work, that can be based on what we learned
about communicating with the modem, especially the AT command set.
