Abstract
Power system states are constantly changing due to high heterogeneousness of power
loads and large-scale renewable energy systems penetration. It has become quite chal-
lenging to achieve accurate power systems states with efficiency. Reverse flow of power
and sudden rise in voltages due to renewable energy source integration is causing the
power flow in distribution grids to become more complex which is resulting in the increase
of occurrence of critical bus voltages. It is very necessary to improve the observability of
distribution systems in order to identify these critical feeders. Data-driven methods can
be used to enhance the situational awareness of the power systems operator. Newly
available data sources such as smart meters are beneficial for such methods. A voltage
estimation method based on neural network is presented in this paper which is robust
under analyzing real electric vehicle charging data for 2years from DTU(Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark) campus and by extracting the load characteristics. Backpropagation,
short for ”backward propagation of errors,”,which is the proposed method to train artifi-
cial Neural network to forecast voltage of the bus. The effectiveness of this method is
displayed by applying on a representative Danish distribution network from a published
paper[1] as well as by developing a European LV distribution network from Cigree [2].
Validation of the proposed method is done by making a comparison between the results
of the actual Voltage of the bus to the Neural network forecasted voltage with various
types of errors such as Root mean square error(RMSE), Mean square error(MSE), Mean
absolute error(MAE), Mean absolute percentage error(MAPE).

Introduction
Distribution system state estimation techniques are presented for performance control
and management of an active distribution network. Since transmission and distribution
networks differ in terms of topology configuration, the number of buses, line parameters,
and the number of measurement instruments, a transmission system state estimation can-
not be used directly in distribution networks. Therefore, the appropriate state estimation
algorithms should be suggested based on the primary characteristics of the distribution
network. Since technical problems and poor decisions may arise in the control center due
to inaccurate distribution state estimation results, accuracy, computational efficiency, and
practical implications should be considered when designing distribution state estimation
techniques.
1.1
Background
Due to a shortage of smart measurement devices, the widespread use of both stochas-
tic and non-stochastic distributed generations (DGs), and the designs of radial or weakly
meshed distribution systems, Active distribution networks (ADNs) require constant ob-
servation, energy management, and control. Approaches for state estimation have been
developed to assess how distribution networks are currently functioning. To show and
monitor all the electrical values in ADNs, state estimation (SE) algorithms provide a con-
nection between measuring devices and control centers. State estimation techniques
have been used for many years in transmission grids. However, they cannot be used
directly in distribution grids. A suitable approach should be implemented in accordance
with the characteristics of distribution networks. In the following scenarios [3], transmis-
sion and distribution networks diverge considerably. One: Unlike transmission buses,
where measurement equipment is put on every bus, measurement instruments are often
installed on restricted buses. Two, although the architecture of transmission grids is highly
meshed, the distribution grids are often constructed in radial or weakly meshed networks.
Three factors set distribution networks apart from transmission grids, including their high
bus density and unbalanced layout. Four: Decoupled SE algorithms for transmission net-
works are impractical in distribution grids because of the low X/R ratios in the distribution
networks. As a result, suitable state estimation techniques must be made available to
establish a connection between measuring devices and a control center in order to eval-
uate the performance of ADNs. When supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA)
systems first went into use in 1990, initially deployed, the first distribution system state
estimate (DSSE) approach was published [3]. Since insufficient data and measurement
errors can be identified from the DSSE results, DSSE plays a crucial role in the manage-
ment of ADNs. The quality and quantity of actual measurements received from measuring
devices, such as SCADA, phasor measurement units (PMU) [4], micro PMU (PMU), etc.
[5], are necessary for a precise assessment of a system’s states. However, due to finan-
cial limitations, it is impractical to install the measuring devices on every bus. Therefore,
it is only sometimes necessary to use as many real measurements to estimate the states
of ADNs.

2.1
Principle of Load Monitoring
The behavior of the load determines how the power system operates. The generating,
transmission, and distribution systems are designed to handle the load under all circum-
stances. A balance between production and consumption is required at all times. In
the same way that it is impossible to consume power without available production, it
is also impossible to generate electricity without demand. A few imbalances might put
the security of the electrical grid at risk. As a result, proper countermeasures should be
implemented to maintain the system, considering economic and reliability criteria, i.e.,
generation adequacy and system balancing with always-available reserves. Therefore,
a deeper understanding of load and its impact on the performance of the power system
over shorter and longer timescales is crucial.[6] Power system evaluation In general, load
values are required for three essential tasks: system stability studies, short- and long-
term system planning, and planning for system op- rational dynamics. Each of those
applications requires a unique strategy. For instance, economic development scenarios,
predictions, and other socio-economic factors are considered in long-term system plan-
ning. In research on the stability of power systems, the load is modeled while considering
the impending impact of system behavior on contingencies. In this thesis, the modeling
and management of the load are discussed in relation to the modeling and analysis of
the operational dynamics of the distribution network. Load is stochastic, varies often, and
is influenced by consumer behavior and weather. It is obvious that lower-level loads are
more stochastically oriented than higher-level loads when examining the nature of loads
from different levels. Although the load cannot generally be controlled, there are some
instances in which it is possible, such as when using electrical tariffs or the load depen-
dence on operational parameters (voltage and frequency). By adjusting the load peak
values, equating the load curve, and other controllable factors, it is possible to reduce
system loading and have an impact on system performance. An excellent example of this
is the demand-side management strategy. A load may be seen as the amount of energy
consumed by a certain customer, substation, area, or system. Depending on how the
load is viewed, for example, a substation load is created by adding together various con-
sumer loads, which causes load parameters, the shape of the load curve, dependence on
temperature, and stochasticity to differ somewhat from those for individual loads. A thor-
ough understanding of the load makes it possible to distribute resources and expertise
more effectively between various sectors and applications. Load monitoring and analysis
are crucial in the power system. For instance, it is feasible to determine the transmission
restrictions or the requirement for balance power in the network based on a short-term
projection of a substation’s or a region’s demand. In order to improve the system in urgent
situations, it is possible to evaluate the potential network functioning and identify potential
solutions. In the long run, it is crucial to predict the load as precisely as possible to pre-
vent potential system security issues caused by the lack of expenditures in prior years.
Utilizing the value of the load, it is possible to simulate various system operation cases
and scenarios to evaluate and identify the best solution while considering various contin-
gencies.[7]

2.3.2
Box plot
The box plot sometimes referred to as the box and whisker plot or the five-number sum-
mary plot, is a form of a graph that aids in data visualization. It displays the distribution
in less detail than a histogram. However, it is constructive for determining whether a
distribution is skewed and whether the data set contains any potential outliers (strange
observations). Because the center, spread, and broad range of the distribution are im-
mediately visible, a box plot is excellent for comparing distributions.[8]
Figure 2.2: Statistical Box plot
The lower and higher quartiles are displayed on the left and right sides of a box and
whisker plot, respectively. The box encompasses the interquartile range, which includes
50% of the data. The median is the vertical line that divides the box in half. A dot or a
cross may also serve as an additional mean indicator on the box plot. The whiskers are
the two lines that extend beyond the box and go from the lowest to the lower quartile (the
box’s beginning) and from the upper quartile (the box’s end) to the maximum.

Data Driven Method
The conventional Distribution State Estimation(DSSE) methods fail to address the high
uncertainty and monitor every change and topology configuration due to the high pene-
tration of non-stochastic renewable energy resources, highly variable loads in the power
grid, and the integration of electric vehicles into the grid [10]. Additionally, in ADNs (Active
distribution networks) with high penetration of DGs, conventional pseudo-measurement
generating methods that address the lack of actual measurements suffer from high un-
certainty levels. Additionally, the traditional DSSE approaches, which rely on distribution
network parameters and models, are complex, time-consuming, and extremely sensitive
to initial conditions [11]. Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML)-based intelli-
gent, data-driven, and model-free state estimation methods have been developed recently
to address these problems by speeding up computation and improving the accuracy of
the output. ML may be applied in various ways, including supervised, unsupervised, and
reinforcement learning [12]. The input data and their associated output (label) are pro-
vided in supervised learning methodologies . During the training stage, the relationship
between the inputs and outputs is described as a function.. Methods for classification and
regression are two instances of supervised learning. No output in unsupervised learning
matches the input data [13]. A few examples of unsupervised learning techniques include
clustering and dimensionality reduction algorithms. Clustering analysis may be used to
classify the unsupervised learning issue of discovering patterns in energy usage. An-
other machine learning method that aims to perform the best possible action to maximize
reward in a specific scenario is reinforcement learning.

Neural Network
The artificial neural networks closely resemble the human brain. Because of the neural
network’s exceptional efficiency in processing data and making output predictions quickly
and accurately, we employ it in state estimation. The neural network model either has one
layer (input and output are directly connected), two layers (the first layer is for data recep-
tion and the second layer is for output results), or many layers (input layer, hidden layer(s),
and output layer) that are connected in parallel).[18]There are many algorithms for train-
ing neural networks. However, the one that minimizes error is the back-propagation al-
gorithm, in which the error is determined by calculating the difference between the target
and the measured output. The weights are adjusted and propagated backward from the
output to the input. The network is then trained to provide the desired result.[19]

5.1
Backpropagation Method to train Neural Network
In 1986, Rumelhart, Williams, and Honton discovered Backpropagation Neural Network.
To increase the accuracy of the neural network model, backpropagation works well to im-
prove the accuracy of the neural network model predictions by using the backpropagation
technique. For artificial neural networks, a backpropagation method is a supervised learn-
ing process where we may adjust the weight functions and raise the model’s accuracy.
The cost function is decreased using the gradient descent approach. The mean-squared
difference between the expected and actual data is decreased. This kind of algorithm is
typically used when training feed-forward neural networks for a set of data whose classi-
fications are known to us.[20]
Backward propagation is also known as the spreading of error backward to increase pre-
cision. The backpropagation procedure must be used whenever the forecast we obtained
from a neural network model differs significantly from the actual output in order for us to
gain improved accuracy. Multi-layered neural networks (MLNs) are often used in feed-
forward neural networks. The data flow from the input layer to the hidden layer to the
output layer.[21]
Typically, MLPs(Multi layer perceptron) are arranged into units known as layers. A per-
ceived artificial neural network has three layers: an input layer, several hidden layers
(which might be zero), and an output layer. Have at least one hidden layer in the case of
MLPs(Multi layer perceptron), with each layer comprising several perceptions. The figure
5.1 shows a feed-forward neural network with two hidden layers. Since there are no loops
in the computation graph, feed-forward neural networks have the property that informa-
tion moves forward through the network. Another way to describe it is that the layers are
interconnected so that none of their outputs depend on one another. This is seen in the
following figure 5.1, where each layer (apart from the input layer) is only linked to the layer
directly to its left.

5.3
Training of Multi layer perceptron
During training, each sample for training is fed to the model, and the neural network ad-
justs the weight and bias at each neuron in the hidden layers according to the training val-
ues. Every time a batch of 24 data is being trained. The neural network runs each batch
multiplied times to adjust the neuron weight and bias so that the output value matches
the training output values (voltage) as accurately as possible. In this process, the neural
network calculates the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the calculated and output
layers values and tries decreasing the MSE values in each iteration. If the neural network
finds the difference of MSE values to be less than 0.00005 for ten consecutive iterations
(epochs), then the training process stops. Thus a model is created using the training val-
ues.
Now this model is used to predict the maximum and minimum voltage values from the
given testing data (maximum and minimum load). Here the maximum and minimum load
values are fed into the model, and the model gives output based on what it learned from
the training.

7
Conclusion
The Backpropagation approach has been put out in this study as a way to estimate the
state of low-voltage distribution grid systems. The feed-forward neural network is used
in the suggested technique to estimate the grid bus voltage’s state. The suggested tech-
nique estimates the state at any moment quickly and accurately, whereas the existing
power flow methods require a very long time to execute. Three separate training sets
(Socket-1 single phase, Socket-1 three-phase, and Socket-2 three phase electric vehicle
charging load from DTU campus) were used for the testing and training of the neural net-
work. In order to validate the suggested strategy, it has been compared to the actual bus
voltage from the low-voltage transmission network. The backpropagation method can
quickly and accurately predict the grid’s voltage state. The suggested approach might be
helpful for system operators to track the real-time performance of smart grids. Future re-
search will focus on modeling large distribution networks for renewable energy sources,
such as solar and wind production systems. In addition, we will assess the condition of
smart grids using deep learning techniques.

Project Description
The objective of the report is to Develop data-driven low voltage network state estimation
based on load characteristics using machine learning models. Develope the load flow
characteristics from real electric vehicle charging data for 2 years from DTU campus has
been used which later is tested in two different grid in MAtpower to obtained different bus
voltages to train the ANN(artificial neural network) to forecast voltage for state estimation
The tasks of the project include the following,
1. Review the published method on state estimation based on data driven method, un-
derstand and test machine learning models with published examples;
2. Develop testing feeder cases and apply the published methods on the testing feeders;
3. Analyze real electric vehicle charging data for 2 yrs from DTU campus and extract the
load characteristics;
4. Develop and evaluate the applicability of data driven method LV grid state estimation
and identify the needed enhancements;
5. Propose application workflow of the state estimation including the input data, algorithm
framework, parameter settings, and training procedure.
