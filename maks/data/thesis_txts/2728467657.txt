Abstract
As the digital landscape continues to evolve, the importance of cybersecurity strategies
becomes essential for organizations striving to safeguard their sensitive information and
assets. This master thesis digs into the collaboration between two relatively novel re­
sources: Purple Teaming and the MITRE ATT&CK Framework, offering a comprehensive
analysis of their integration in fortifying an organization’s cybersecurity structure. The in­
sights gathered from this thesis allow them to elevate their cybersecurity resilience. By
seamlessly integrating both approaches into their practices, organizations gain a compet­
itive edge against sophisticated cyber adversaries. The research emphasizes the need
to adapt continuously and embrace proactive cybersecurity awareness and collabora­
tion culture to face emerging threats confidently. With this approach, it is possible to
effectively strengthen cyber defenses, shield critical assets, and ensure a robust security
posture that thrives amidst the dynamic and constantly changing digital environment. As
threats become more modern and prevalent, the significance of leveraging collaborative
efforts like Purple Teaming and the ATT&CK Framework practices cannot be overstated.
The research collection leaves no doubt about the remarkable advantages that arise from
combining these innovative approaches. Improved threat detection capabilities, targeted
defense strategies and proactive incident response workflows are among the benefits one
can obtain.

Introduction
Recently, the cyber landscape has witnessed an alarming surge in sophisticated and tar­
geted cyber­attacks, posing significant challenges to organizations across various indus­
tries. According to Rick Blaisdell, 60% of them experienced a cyberattack in 2021/2022
[1]. To fortify their defenses against these evolving threats, cybersecurity profession­
als have turned to innovative approaches, such as Purple Teaming (PT) and the MITRE
ATT&CK framework. Along this thesis, we shall explore the diverse benefits of combining
both in Threat Detection and Incident Response (TDIR).
PT is an emerging practice that involves a collaborative effort between offensive­focused
”Red Teamers” and defensive­focused ”Blue Teamers”. This relatively new approach cre­
ates an environment where both teams join forces to identify vulnerabilities and weak­
nesses in the infrastructure. By simulating real­world attack scenarios, PT augmentss the
ability to proactively detect and respond to potential threats. This collaboration bridges
the gap between offensive and defensive measures and nurtures a strong relationship
that empowers the organization to build a robust and resilient security infrastructure to
protect its assets. More detailed information is shown in section 3.
The MITRE ATT&CK framework, which will be further discussed in section 4, stands as
a comprehensive knowledge base, cataloging various adversary Tactics, Techniques and
Procedures (TTPs). This framework enables gaining of valuable data into the tactics em­
ployed by adversaries. By aligning defensive strategies with specific attack techniques,
the MITRE ATT&CK framework facilitates a systematic and structured approach to cyber­
security, ensuring that security teams stay one step ahead of potential attackers.
As the cyber environment continues to evolve, it is imperative to adapt defensive strate­
gies continuously. Nowadays, it is more important to prevent attacks than to remediate
them. The combined forces of PT and the MITRE ATT&CK framework offer a potent re­
lationship, equipping organizations with the necessary tools to stay resilient against the
most modern threats.
This paper will delve into the key advantages of integrating PT with TDIR strategies, in
conjunction with the MITRE ATT&CK framework. We will explore how this amalgama­
tion of approaches is able to enhance Threat Detection (TD) capabilities, streamline IR
workflows, and ultimately strengthen their overall security posture. The integration of both
tools is further discussed in section 6.
1.1
Context
Cybersecurity threats have evolved rapidly in recent years, becoming increasingly sophis­
ticated and targeted. Malicious actors, including cybercriminals, hacktivists, and nation­
state actors, employ a wide range of tactics to breach and compromise sensitive informa­
tion and critical infrastructure. The high­profile data breaches, ransomware attacks, and
other cyber incidents reported in the media underscore the urgency of fortifying defenses
against these threats. These threats are introduced in section 2.1
Traditional approaches that rely solely on reactive measures and perimeter defenses are
proving inadequate in the face of advanced and persistent attacks. The rapid advance­
ment of technology, the growing complexity of IT ecosystems, and the increasing reliance
on interconnected systems have created new ways for cyber threats to exploit.

In response to this evolving threat landscape, cybersecurity professionals have recog­
nized the need for proactive and collaborative defense strategies. This recognition has
led to the emergence of PT as an innovative approach in the field.
PT emphasizes the importance of cooperation and communication between offensive­
minded ”Red Teamers” and defensive­focused ”Blue Teamers.” Unlike traditional Red
Team (RT)­Blue Team (BT) exercises, where the two teams operate independently, PT
promotes a collaborative and iterative process. Red Teamers, who emulate attackers,
work together with Blue Teamers, who are responsible for the organization’s defense, to
identify vulnerabilities, assess security controls, and strengthen IR procedures. Simul­
taneously, the MITRE ATT&CK framework has gained prominence as a comprehensive
knowledge base that catalogues adversary TTPs.
The integration of PT practices with the MITRE ATT&CK framework has emerged as a
powerful and strategic approach to proactively enhance their cybersecurity capabilities.
By combining the collaborative nature of PT with the structured insights provided by the
MITRE ATT&CK framework, one can develop a more proactive, adaptive, and targeted
approach to cybersecurity.
In this context, this thesis seeks to explore and analyze the benefits of this integrated
approach, showcasing how it empowers organizations to improve their TD capabilities,
optimize IR workflows, and ultimately strengthen their overall security posture.

2.1
Overview of Cybersecurity threats
In this section, we delve into the diverse landscape of cybersecurity threats, providing a
comprehensive understanding of the various types that are faced in the digital realm. Un­
derstanding them is essential for developing effective strategies and mitigating potential
risks.
Threats encompass a wide range of malicious activities and attacks that target digital
assets, systems, networks, and individuals. They constantly evolve and adapt as cy­
bercriminals develop new techniques and exploit vulnerabilities. Common ones include
malware, ransomware, phishing attacks, DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attacks,
insider threats, and Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs). Additionally, they can be cat­
egorized as external (originating from outside the organization) or internal (arising from
within the organization) [2].
The impact can be severe and far­reaching. Organizations face financial losses, reputa­
tional damage, legal consequences, and disruptions to critical operations in the wake of
successful cyberattacks. Additionally, incidents can lead to breaches of sensitive data,
compromising the privacy and security of customers and stakeholders.
2.1.1
Types of Cybersecurity Threats
In this subsection, we explore the different types of cybersecurity threats in more detail
[2]:
• Malware: Malicious software, such as viruses, worms, trojans, and spyware, de­
signed to infiltrate systems, steal data, and cause disruptions.
• Ransomware: A specific type of malware that encrypts a victim’s data, demanding
a ransom for its decryption.
• Phishing Attacks: Social engineering attacks that deceive users into revealing sen­
sitive information, such as passwords or financial details, through fraudulent emails
or websites.
• DDoS Attacks: Coordinated efforts to overwhelm a target’s online services, render­
ing them inaccessible to legitimate users.
• Insider Threats: Threats arising from current or former employees, contractors, or
business partners who misuse their access to compromise data or disrupt opera­
tions.
• APTs: Sophisticated and stealthy attacks, often state­sponsored, that persistently
target specific groups for espionage or data theft.
Understanding the various threats is the first step toward effective mitigation. Organiza­
tions must implement a multi­layered defense approach, including robust security poli­
cies, secure network architecture, regular software patching, employee awareness train­
ing, and the use of advanced cybersecurity technologies such as Intrusion Detection and
Prevention Systems (IDPS) and endpoint protection.

2.2
Different Components of an Organization’s Cybersecurity
Strategy
The ”Overview of Cybersecurity Threats” presented in section 2.1 provides a comprehen­
sive understanding of the diverse and evolving landscape of cyber threats that are faced.
This awareness is crucial in developing an effective ”Cybersecurity Strategy” that can de­
fend against these dangers. The different components of an organization’s cybersecurity
strategy, such as CTI, IR, access control, and network security, are specifically designed
to address the wide range of threats identified in the overview. In this paper, we will fo­
cus on both CTI and IR. CTI allows persistent vigilance by continuously monitoring and
analyzing emerging threats and adversary tactics, while IR plans provide a structured ap­
proach to detect, manage, and mitigate the impact of security incidents when they occur.
Access control and network security measures are implemented to prevent unauthorized
access and data breaches, effectively thwarting potential cyber attacks outlined in the
threat overview. The cohesive integration of these components in the cybersecurity strat­
egy ensures a proactive and holistic defense, keeping the institutions well­prepared to
combat the dynamic landscape of attacks.
An organization’s strategy encompasses a wide array of components that collectively work
towards building a robust defense against cyber dangers and ensuring the confidentiality,
integrity, and availability of critical assets. Each component plays a vital role in the over­
all cybersecurity posture. Below are the key components of an effective cybersecurity
strategy:
Threat Intelligence
CTI involves the systematic collection, analysis, and dissemination of information related
to potential cyber threats, including emerging malware, vulnerabilities, and adversary tac­
tics. It helps to stay informed about the evolving threat landscape and assists in making
informed decisions regarding defensive measures and IR strategies.
IR
An IR plan outlines the procedures for identifying, managing, and mitigating incidents. It
provides a clear and coordinated approach to handling security breaches, data breaches,
and other cyber incidents.
Having a well­defined IR plan enables people to respond
promptly and effectively, minimizing the impact of security breaches [3].
Security Awareness Training
Security awareness training educates employees about the best practices, common threats,
and social engineering techniques. It empowers employees to recognize and report suspi­
cious activities, reducing the likelihood of falling victim to phishing, ransomware, and other
cyber attacks. Educated and vigilant employees are essential in bolstering the overall se­
curity.
Access Control and Identity Management
Effective access control mechanisms, including strong authentication and authorization
practices, limit access to sensitive data and critical systems only to authorized personnel.
Identity management ensures that user identities are managed securely throughout their
lifecycle, reducing the risk of unauthorized access and identity­related breaches.

Vulnerability Management
Vulnerability management involves identifying, prioritizing, and remedying vulnerabilities
in software, applications, and systems. Regular vulnerability scanning and patch man­
agement are essential to minimize the risk of exploitation by malicious actors.
Network Security
Network security measures, such as firewalls, intrusion detection/prevention systems (ID­
S/IPS), and secure network architecture, protect the network infrastructure from unautho­
rized access and cyber­attacks.
Data Protection and Encryption
Data protection involves implementing encryption and other security measures to safe­
guard sensitive data both at rest and in transit. Encryption ensures that even if data is
compromised, it remains unintelligible to unauthorized parties.
Security Monitoring and SIEM
Continuous security monitoring and Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)
provide real­time visibility into network and system activities, enabling the early detection
of security incidents and threats.
Security Governance and Risk Management
Security governance establishes policies, procedures, and guidelines for practices, while
risk management assesses and manages cybersecurity risks.
Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery Planning
Business continuity and disaster recovery planning ensure that the organization can con­
tinue critical operations in the face of cyber incidents or disasters.
Compliance and Regulatory Adherence
Adherence to relevant cybersecurity regulations and industry standards ensures that the
organization meets legal requirements and industry best practices.
Physical Security
Physical security measures protect data centers, critical infrastructure, and other sensitive
areas from unauthorized access.
Employee Training and Certifications
Encouraging employees to undergo cybersecurity training and certifications enhances
their expertise and skills in handling challenges.
Cybersecurity Culture
Creating a CC within the organization encourages a shared responsibility for security
among all employees, promoting good practices. Further details are discussed in section
5.2.
Continuous Improvement and Adaptive Defense
A continuous improvement approach and adaptive defense strategy ensure that the or­
ganization evolves to meet new and emerging threats effectively.

Predecessors of Purple Teaming and its Origins
The ”Different Components of an Organization’s Cybersecurity Strategy” play a signifi­
cant role in enhancing an organization’s overall cyber resilience and IR capabilities. These
components encompass various proactive and defensive measures, such as CTI, IR plan­
ning, access control, and employee training, which collectively aim to detect, prevent, and
mitigate cyber attacks. As organizations seek to strengthen their cybersecurity posture,
the concept of ”Predecessors of Purple Teaming and its Origins” comes into focus. Before
its formalization as a cybersecurity practice, the integration of RT and Blue Teaming prac­
tices emerged as predecessors. RT simulated real­world cyber attacks, and Blue Team­
ing was responsible for defense and IR. The fusion of these concepts laid the foundation

2.3.1
Red Teaming and Blue Teaming
Red Teaming can be traced back to military war games and simulations, where RT played
the role of adversaries, seeking to breach the defenses of the Blue Teams representing
friendly forces. These exercises helped military organizations understand potential vul­
nerabilities and weaknesses in their strategies, tactics, and defenses. They have also
been used to test physical security of nuclear facilities and the Department of Energy’s
National Laboratories and Technology Centers in the USA, for example. During the 1990s,
experts started employing RT­BT exercises as a method to evaluate the robustness of in­
formation security systems [4].
In the cybersecurity context, RT emerged as a form of ethical hacking, where skilled pro­
fessionals, known as Red Teamers or ethical hackers, simulate realistic cyberattacks on
systems and networks. The objective is to identify vulnerabilities and weaknesses before
malicious attackers can exploit them. This approach provided valuable insights into an
organization’s defensive capabilities and exposed areas that required improvement.
On the other hand, BT represents the defensive side. BT are responsible for maintain­
ing the security of systems, detecting and responding to threats, and implementing safe­
guards to protect against cyberattacks. By analyzing the tactics and techniques employed
by Red Teamers, Blue Teams enhance their ability to defend against real­world cyber dan­
gers.
2.3.2
The Integration of Red and Blue Teaming
The concept of integrating RT and BT, where offensive and defensive cybersecurity teams
collaborate, began to gain recognition and popularity in the early 2000s. The idea was to
combine the expertise and perspectives of both teams to create a more comprehensive
and realistic testing environment.
The cooperative approach sought to improve the traditional unidirectional assessment
process, where RTs conducted assessments without direct engagement with the defen­
sive teams. By promoting active communication and collaboration between RTs and Blue
Teams, one could better understand the tactics used by adversaries and develop more
effective defense strategies.

The Emergence of ”Purple Teaming”

3.1
Definition and Principles of Purple Teaming
PT can be defined as a coordinated cybersecurity exercise that brings together offen­
sive and defensive security teams to simulate real­world cyber­attacks and validate the
effectiveness of security controls. Unlike traditional RT assessments, where they act as
attackers and the BT responds to detect and mitigate the threats, PT promotes collabo­
ration between these teams throughout the assessment process [7].
For the successful execution of PT, it is imperative that the involved stakeholders collabo­
ratively establish well­defined parameters encompassing the exercise’s scope, objectives,
goals, timing, and regulations. The efficacy of PT hinges upon the transformation of the
working relationship between the BT (BT) and the RT into a dynamic collaboration, foster­
ing an enriched learning journey and mutual comprehension enhancement. Overcoming
potential barriers arising from distinct knowledge bases and expertise among stakehold­
ers is vital. Effective information exchange necessitates the adaptation of language to
ensure a shared comprehension among diverse teams [8].

Open­mindedness and mindfulness among stakeholders play a pivotal role in promoting a
conducive environment for candid discussions and embodying cooperative conduct. This
is particularly facilitated by the RT’s proactive role in setting an example. The RT should

PT activity relies on four different principles:
1) Active Collaboration: Its core principle is active collaboration between the RT and
the Blue Team. Both teams work together closely, openly sharing insights, tactics, and
findings. The RT shares their offensive techniques, while the BT provides valuable feed­
back on their detection and response capabilities. This collaboration develops a learning
environment, leading to continuous improvements in the security measures.
2) Continuous Learning and Improvement: It is not a one­time event but a continuous
learning process. It emphasizes the need for ongoing assessments, RT exercises, and
regular debrief sessions to identify weaknesses and refine defensive strategies. The in­
sights gained from each iteration empower the organization to adapt rapidly to emerging
cyber threats.
3) Realistic Attack Simulation: It strives to emulate real­world attack scenarios to chal­
lenge the security infrastructure authentically. By simulating actual adversarial techniques,
organizations can identify blind spots and weaknesses that may not surface in standard
security testing. That is where the MITRE ATT&CK framework comes into action, further
explained in section 6.
4) Focus on the critical issues:
It focuses on the most critical assets and potential
attack vectors. By prioritizing risk­based testing, the assessment aligns with the organi­
zation’s specific security concerns and resource allocation.
The way PT exercise is structured is: the RT constructs an adversary emulation mirroring
real­world cyber­attacks to pinpoint defense gaps and evaluate coverage. Utilizing the
RT’s report, the BT assesses risks and prioritizes gaps for mitigation. Through log data
analysis and security control configuration, the BT detects and prevents attacker activities.
This interactive process fosters adaptive defense strategies against evolving threats, with
the RT conducting re­tests of the adversary emulation to assess the efficacy of updated
defenses. This collaborative approach ensures robust cybersecurity measures for the
organization [9]. How to conduct a PT exercise is written in section 3.4.

Types of Purple Teaming
Oakley [12] categorizes this practice into two distinct types: one wherein either the at­

3.3.1
Reciprocal Awareness
The Reciprocal Awareness represents a classic archetype within the spectrum of exer­
cises. In this context, both the RT and the BT possess some degree of understanding
about each other’s roles and actions throughout the engagement. Typically, their aware­
ness levels are nearly equal, making an environment of shared knowledge.
Advantages:
Unlike more adversarial approaches, this category encourages collaboration and mutual
understanding, as both teams are aware of each other’s roles, mitigating the potential
for conflict or misunderstandings. This transparent exchange of information allows both
the RT and Blue Teams to learn from each other’s tactics, strategies, and responses,
contributing to a more holistic comprehension of cybersecurity challenges. Furthermore,
the real­time sharing of operational details enables prompt improvements. The BT can

Disadvantages:
While transparency contributes to a cooperative environment, the RT’s ability to emulate
real­world attacks may be somewhat constrained. The BT’s awareness of impending

3.3.2
Unwitting Host
In this scenario, the defensive BT assets are intentionally kept less informed about the
activities of the offensive RT. The only information they might know is the start and end
dates of the assessment along with the focus. The objective is to challenge the BT’s
detection and response capabilities in a realistic manner.
Advantages:
With limited information about the RT’s activities, the BT is compelled to rely on its detec­
tion skills and resourcefulness. This hones their ability to detect and respond to real­world
threats effectively. Furthermore, true attack emulation executed by the RT increases the
sophistication of their tactics. This preparation readies the BT for advanced and intri­
cate cyber threats, fostering a continuous improvement cycle in their defense strategies.
Importantly, this exercise also provides realistic response testing, simulating real­world
scenarios that offer a comprehensive assessment of the IR procedures under uncertain
conditions. This holistic approach ensures that both teams refine their capabilities and
contribute to a more resilient cybersecurity attitude.
Disadvantages:
While the benefits of limited information exchange are evident, it’s important to acknowl­
edge potential challenges. The inherent lack of information can inadvertently create a
competitive atmosphere between the Blue and RT. This perception of the RT as an ad­
versary may hinder effective collaboration and information sharing, undermining the co­
operative spirit essential for successful PT. Moreover, the risk of misinterpretation looms
large. With only partial insights, the BT might misjudge the nature or extent of an attack,
leading to less accurate response strategies that could prove detrimental in a real cyber
incident. Striking a balance between secrecy and collaboration is crucial. An overempha­
sis on concealing RT activities might divert resources from broader organizational security
goals, funneling efforts into hidden threats at the expense of addressing a more holistic

3.3.3
Unwitting Attacker
The Uninformed Attacker represents an unconventional approach. In this scenario, the
RT, acting as the attack emulators, operates with minimal knowledge about the capabil­
ities and activities of the Blue Team. Remarkably, the BT is clandestinely informed of
the RT’s actions. This setup enables real­time monitoring of the RT’s assessment by the
defensive apparatus.
Advantages:
The BT’s concealed awareness of the RT’s actions allows real­time observation, facilitat­
ing comprehensive monitoring and analysis of attack emulation. This setting replicates
genuine attacks, yielding accurate data on the natural progression of tactics. Additionally,
the BT can inject challenges to test RT responsiveness, promoting a dynamic assessment
environment.
Disadvantages:
Limited RT awareness compromises their adaptability to advanced defensive responses,
potentially affecting the simulation of sophisticated attacks. Orchestrating such scenar­
ios demands precise coordination to maintain covert BT monitoring as the assessment
progresses seamlessly.
Roles and Responsabilities:
The BT in this engagement plays a dual role—providing covert observation of the RT’s
actions while subtly introducing challenges to test their response. The RT, unaware of the
monitoring, operates as if conducting a standard assessment, providing genuine insights
into their attack methodology.

Red­Handed Testing

of PT exercises. Derived from the concept of being ”caught red­handed,” this approach
involves the RT willingly aiming to be detected by the Blue Team. The primary goal is to
comprehensively assess the organization’s defensive capabilities through all stages of an
attack, from initiation to detection and post­identification analysis.
Advantages:
Red­Handed Testing offers a comprehensive evaluation of security posture, assessing
BT’s response acss attack lifecycle stages. Conflict­driven learning emerges from this
potentially adversarial setup, fostering understanding and skill improvement through Red­
BT interactions. This realistic approach, intentionally allowing detection, closely emulates
real­world scenarios, enhancing insights into actual attack responses.

Disadvantages:

Catch and Release
The Catch and Release introduces an approach that focuses on testing both RT and BT
capabilities in a dynamic scenario. In this paradigm, the objective is two­fold: to assess

Advantages:
The Catch and Release engagement offers a dual team assessment by testing RT and

the BT promptly transitions into IR mode, attempting to mitigate the threat and eliminate

Carrying out a CTI is essential in order to conduct a PT exercise, since the methods,
tradecraft and TTPs will be specifically tailored for the organization. CTI consists of a few
steps described below.
Understand the Target Organization
To understand the target organization, a provider should simulate threat actors’ prepara­
tory steps, creating an initial view of vulnerabilities. This will later identify attack vectors
tied to people, processes, and technologies within the organization’s digital landscape.
Identify the Adversary to Emulate
The CTI team will want to research the adversary’s CIO (Capability, Intent, and Opportu­

Intent: Unchangeable, industry­specific motive behind adversarial actions, often data theft
in security intelligence, varying slightly per intrusion.
Opportunity: Relies on timing and target familiarity, occasionally intersecting with vulner­
abilities, such as 0­day awareness, yet not exclusively.
Capability: Adversaries’ aptitude to achieve goals using available resources, encompass­
ing skills, financial support, and technical prowess.

Utilizing diverse sources, including Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs),
for example, is important in this step.

The most important position within these roles is that of the Engagement Manager, often

records minutes, notes, and action items, while also facilitating valuable feedback ex­
change. Subsequently, in the Lessons Learned phase, they compile and communicate

Al. [5], the EC can be the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO).
The financial dimension is overseen by the Head Of Security, responsible for approving
the exercise and its corresponding budget, reinforcing the strategic significance of these
exercises within organizational structures.

their crucial role in the second phase of the PT process.

4.1
Overview of the MITRE ATT&CK Framework
In this section, we provide a comprehensive overview of the MITRE ATT&CK framework.
ATT&CK is an acronym for Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge. It
is a widely recognized and extensively used product of MITRE Corporation, that catalogs
adversary behaviors and tactics. It was first introduced in 2015 as a response to the need
for a standardized and comprehensive reference that would provide insights into the tac­
tics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) (explained in section 4.1.1) used by threat actors
across the entire attack lifecycle. The framework’s structure, objectives, and significance
in the cybersecurity domain will be further explained below [15].
Structure of the MITRE ATT&CK Framework
The framework is organized into matrices and sub­matrices. The three primary Matrices
in the framework are the Enterprise Matrix, the Mobile Matrix, and the Industrial Control
System (ICS) Matrix. The Enterprise Matrix has sub­matrices such as Windows, Linux,
and MacOS matrices along with the Cloud, Containers, and Network. The Mobile Matrix
has sub­matrices which are IOS and Android.

Objectives of the MITRE ATT&CK Framework
• Provide a Comprehensive Knowledge Base: The framework serves as an exten­
sive repository of real­world adversary behaviors, tactics, and techniques, providing
cybersecurity professionals with valuable insights into how adversaries operate.
• Improve Threat Intelligence and Detection Capabilities: By understanding attacker
conducts documented in the framework, organizations can improve their threat intel­
ligence capabilities and enhance their ability to detect and respond to cyber threats.
• Assist in Cyber Defense and IR : The framework aids in developing effective cy­
ber defense strategies by highlighting potential attack vectors and tactics. It also
supports IR efforts by offering guidance on detecting, investigating, and mitigating
specific adversary techniques.
Benefits of the MITRE ATT&CK Framework
• Standardization: The framework provides a standardized and consistent language
for describing attacker behaviors, facilitating clear communication and collaboration
among cybersecurity professionals.
• Realism: The framework is based on empirical data and real­world observations of
adversary behavior, making it highly relevant and practical for cybersecurity opera­
tions.
• High Community Engagement: The framework is actively maintained and updated
bi­anually [16] based on contributions and feedback from the global cybersecurity
community, ensuring its accuracy and relevancy in the current times.
4.1.1
Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs)
Together, the tactics, techniques, and procedures, also known by their acronym TTPs, in
the MITRE ATT&CK framework serve as a comprehensive repository of cyber adversary
conducts, enabling organizations to better understand the methodologies of attackers and
enhancing their defense and detection capabilities [15]. By mapping observed attacker
behavior to specific tactics and techniques within the framework, cybersecurity practi­
tioners can identify potential weaknesses in their defenses and develop more effective
strategies to detect and respond to cyber threats. The TTPs also have an ID next to their
names.
Tactics
Tactics represent high­level objectives that threat actors aim to achieve during an attack.
The framework defines 14 tactics: reconnaissance, resource development, command
and control, initial access, execution, persistence, privilege escalation, defense evasion,
credential access, discovery, lateral movement, collection, exfiltration, and impact [16].
Each tactic encompasses a set of techniques and procedures that adversaries utilize to
accomplish their objectives.
Techniques
Techniques are the specific methods or approaches employed by adversaries to carry out
the tactics defined in the framework. They represent the ”how” of an attack, providing
a more detailed view of adversary behavior. For example, under the ”execution” tac­
tic, techniques may include techniques for executing malicious code, employing scripting
languages, or using malicious macs. The MITRE ATT&CK framework includes numerous
techniques that cover a wide range of offensive actions employed by adversaries.

Sub­techniques
Sub­techniques are specific descriptions of how a technique is executed by an adver­
sary. Sub­techniques offer more context and insights into the variations of techniques
that different threat actors may use to achieve their goals. One example can be the
so­called ”Spearphishing Attachment” (id T1566.001) [17], which falls under the ”Initial
Access” tactic and ”Phishing” technique (id T1566) and involves targeted cyberattacks
through malicious email attachments. Adversaries craft convincing emails, tailor­made
for specific individuals or groups, containing seemingly legitimate files. When recipients
open the attachments, malicious code is executed, granting attackers to enter the or­
ganization’s systems. As a way of countering this threat, organizations should prioritize
user awareness training (id M1017) to recognize suspicious emails and implement robust
email security measures, such as advanced filtering and attachment scanning, to detect
and prevent successful initial access attempts via spearphishing attachments.
Procedures
Procedures are defined as a more precise execution method adopted by adversaries
for techniques or sub­techniques. A procedure example of the ”Phishing” technique (id
T1566) can be Hikit (id S0009) [18], a malware used for spearphishing by the group Ax­
iom. In the MITRE ATT&CK framework, procedures are classified based on real­world in­
stances of techniques in the ”Procedure Examples” section found on the technique pages.

4.1.2
Other current frameworks
While the MITRE ATT&CK framework has gained significant recognition and adoption in
the cybersecurity community, it is essential to compare it with other current frameworks
to understand its unique advantages and limitations.
One of the well­known alternatives to the MITRE ATT&CK framework is the Cyber Kill
Chain (CKC) framework, developed by Lockheed Martin in 2011. According to the MITRE
official website [16], both are complimentary, since the first is considered to have a low­
level definition of the attacker’s behavior, while the latter has a higher­level one. The
CKC essentially focuses on the different stages of a cyber attack, ranging from initial re­
connaissance to data exfiltration [19]. While both frameworks share a focus on adversary
conducts, the MITRE ATT&CK framework provides a more comprehensive catalog of spe­
cific tactics and techniques used by threat actors across various platforms and operating
systems, instead of focusing on the stages themselves.

Pyramid of Pain
An indicator of compromise (IoC) serves as evidence indicating the occurrence of a cy­
berattack, like a breach or intrusion. Digital forensics specialists pursue IoCs to decipher
the attack’s methods and attributions [21].
The Pyramid of Pain introduces a conceptual framework for comprehending cybersecurity
threats by categorizing IoCs across six distinct tiers. This concept, introduced by security
researcher David Bianco in 2013 [22], offers a profound insight into how organizations can
optimize their cybersecurity strategies. The PP categorizes different types of indicators
that adversaries leave behind, ranging from easily accessible to highly intricate.
Figure 5.1: The layout of the PP
• Domain Names, IP Addresses, Hash Values: At the base of the pyramid are the
most easily changed elements for attackers like IP addresses, domain names, hashes
of malicious files, and other artifacts that can be quickly altered. Defenders often
use these to identify ongoing attacks and mitigate threats. However, their value is
limited because adversaries can change them easily.
• Network/ Host Artifacts: Network and Host Artifacts encompass observables re­
sulting from adversary actions within your network or on individual hosts. These
indicators help differentiate malicious behavior from legitimate activities. While they
are not so easy to change, there are other resources even harder to modify.
• Tools and Infrastructure: Moving up the pyramid, the Tools and Infrastructure level
represents the specific software, malware, and infrastructure used by adversaries.
This includes exploit kits, malware families, command and control servers, and other
elements that facilitate attacks. Attackers may need more time and resources to
modify their tools and infrastructure, making this layer more valuable for defenders.

Cybersecurity Culture
CC refers to the collective attitudes, behaviors, and practices within an organization that
prioritize and promote cybersecurity awareness, preparedness, and responsibility among
all members. It encompasses a shared understanding of the importance of cybersecurity
and a commitment to maintaining a secure environment, both online and offline. A strong
CC is an integral component of an organization’s efforts to mitigate cyber risks and protect
its sensitive information and assets [24]. A lot of steps are required to promote it, some
of them are listed below.
Central to CC is awareness and education. Organizations must ensure that all employees,
from executives to front­line staff, understand the significance of cybersecurity threats and
their potential impact. Regular training programs and awareness campaigns familiarize
individuals with common attack vectors, phishing attempts, and safe online practices. This
education empowers employees to recognize and respond to potential threats proactively.
A robust CC fosters collaboration and open communication among all departments and
levels of the organization. Effective cybersecurity is not solely the responsibility of the IT
team; it’s a collective effort. When different teams work together to identify vulnerabil­
ities, share insights, and devise strategies, the defense mechanisms become stronger.
Transparent communication ensures that potential threats are promptly reported and ad­
dressed.
In a cybersecurity­conscious culture, individuals adopt a proactive mindset. They actively
seek ways to improve security, report suspicious activities, and suggest enhancements
to existing security protocols. Proactive individuals anticipate potential risks and take
preventive measures, minimizing the exposure to threats. This mindset extends to regular
software updates, password hygiene, and the secure use of digital tools.
Besides, it instills a sense of accountability and ownership among employees. Each indi­
vidual takes responsibility for their online actions, adheres to security policies, and safe­
guards sensitive information. Encouraging accountability helps prevent careless mistakes
that could compromise security and reinforces the notion that cybersecurity is everyone’s
concern.
Cyber threats constantly evolve, and this culture embraces continuous learning. Orga­
nizations provide ongoing training, resources, and opportunities for individuals to stay
updated on emerging threats and best practices. This enables employees to adapt to
new attack methods and technologies and respond effectively.

Challenges and Considerations
Successfully integrating PT with the MITRE ATT&CK Framework offers organizations a
proactive and dynamic approach to improving their detection and incident response capa­
bilities. However, addressing challenges and considerations is essential to maximize the
benefits of this collaboration. These challenges are further described in the subsections
below.
6.5.1
Realism and Evaluation
Organizations may encounter various challenges that revolve around striking a delicate
balance between realism and controlled testing. While the objective is to simulate real­
world attack scenarios, ensuring that these exercises do not inadvertently disrupt ongoing
operations or compromise sensitive data becomes paramount.
1. One of the central challenges lies in determining the threshold at which the exer­
cise’s realism may cross into the territory of causing actual disruption. Emulating
sophisticated attack techniques could lead to unintended consequences if not man­
aged carefully. It is necessary to assess the potential impact on critical systems,
applications, and services. While the goal is to identify weaknesses, the exercise
should not ruin the organization’s ability to function.
2. Striking a balance means designing engagement scenarios that are controlled and
predictable to some extent. The exercise should not deviate so far from established
protocols that it becomes challenging to manage. Overly aggressive engagement
scenarios might trigger excessive false positives in detection mechanisms, leading
to alert fatigue for security personnel.
3. Achieving realism requires customizing scenarios to align with the organization’s
industry, infrastructure, and threat landscape. However, tailoring should not com­
promise the exercise’s goals. Organizations operating in highly regulated industries
must navigate compliance requirements.
4. Ensuring that key stakeholders understand the objectives and potential impacts of
the exercise is crucial. All the teams involved should be informed and aligned. Re­
alistic simulations could potentially cause panic among employees if not communi­
cated effectively. Clear communication that the exercise is controlled and part of a
proactive security strategy is essential.
5. Organizations must define what success looks like for each exercise. Metrics might
include the identification of vulnerabilities, response time improvement, and increased
detection accuracy. The challenge is to measure success without overly glorifying
the RT’s efforts at the expense of causing undue anxiety or unnecessary resource
expenditure. The percentage of the BT detection of the RT actions does not reflect
necessarily on how much the system is secure.

Throughout the thesis, it was evident that the integration of the MITRE ATT&CK Frame­
work enriched threat detection capabilities significantly. The classification of adversary
tactics and techniques provided a comprehensive and standardized vocabulary for both
teams. As a result, the Blue Team can proactively detect and respond to potential threats
by leveraging insights gained from Purple Teaming exercises. The risk­aware approach
enables organizations to prioritize critical assets and focus on securing high­priority threat
vectors, effectively managing limited resources to address emerging risks.
However, this relationship also comes with challenges, particularly in maintaining a bal­
ance between realism and controlled testing, as well as ensuring a strong cybersecurity
culture. With planning, continuous improvement, and the dedication of skilled profession­
als, organizations can harness the potential of Purple Teaming and the MITRE ATT&CK
framework to fortify their defenses, stay ahead of evolving threats, and foster a proactive
and resilient cybersecurity ecosystem.
Another vital aspect highlighted was the instrumental role of cybersecurity culture in driv­
ing successful integration. A strong cybersecurity culture nurtures collaboration, contin­
uous learning, and a proactive mindset. By encouraging open communication, teams
exchange valuable knowledge and observations, refining their detection and incident re­
sponse workflows iteratively. Furthermore, a culture of accountability empowers individu­
als to take ownership of cybersecurity, ensuring collective responsibility for safeguarding
critical assets.
In conclusion, the integrated approach of Purple Teaming and the MITRE ATT&CK Frame­
work has proven to be a game­changer in the realm of cybersecurity. By embracing this
relatively new methodology, organizations can not only strengthen their cyber defenses
but also cultivate a proactive cybersecurity culture. The continuous learning and iter­
ative improvement driven by the collaborative approach enable organizations to adapt
smoothly to evolving cyber threats. Ultimately, this thesis has shown the potential of Pur­
ple Teaming and the ATT&CK Framework as indispensable tools in an organization’s tools
to protect against cyber adversaries and navigate the complexities of the digital landscape
with confidence and resilience.
