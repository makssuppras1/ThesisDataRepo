Abstract
This thesis investigates the automated disassembly processes of snap fits of various
shapes and sizes, which were 3D printed in PLA. The disassembly was conducted
using a UR3 collaborative robot (cobot) equipped with a Robotiq 2F-140 gripper and
FT-300S force sensor.
The primary focus of the research is to analyze the impact
of key parameters such as pulling force, gripping force, and the fixturing of parts on
the working table during the disassembly process.
Additionally, the integrity of the
snap-fit parts post-disassembly is evaluated to determine the feasibility of reusing these
components, thereby contributing to a circular product life cycle.
The study includes
an analysis the current state of automated disassembly and the significance of snap-fit
joints in sustainable manufacturing practices.
The experimental setup includes the
design and fabrication of various snap-fit prototypes using CAD software and 3D printing
technology.
The prototypes are designed to simulate real-world applications and are
tested under controlled laboratory conditions to ensure consistency and reliability of
the results.
The experimental results were analyzed to understand the relationship
between the disassembly parameters and the efficiency and reliability of the process.
The findings indicate that the design of snap fits, the type of fixture used, and the
accuracy of the applied forces significantly impact the disassembly performance. These
insights contribute to the broader goal of developing robust automated disassembly
methodologies that can be widely adopted in manufacturing and recycling industries.
This research enhances the understanding of how automated systems can be optimized
for disassembling 3D-printed components, promoting advancements in robotics and
manufacturing processes.
By improving the efficiency and reliability of automated
disassembly, this study supports the implementation of circular product life cycles,
reducing waste and conserving resources.

Introduction
1.1
Background
In today’s world, the rapid growth in the usability of plastic products across various
industries and modern society brings significant advantages and utility. However, this
widespread adoption comes at a cost: substantial environmental impact due to plastic
waste. The global production of plastic has massively increased, leading to proportional
degradation of natural ecosystems. Consequently, there is an urgent need for sustainable
practices, including effective recycling and reusability of plastic products [1].
The
escalating environmental challenges and the growing emphasis on sustainability have
driven industries to explore innovative approaches to reduce their carbon footprint. One
promising strategy is remanufacturing, a process that involves restoring used products to
a ”like-new” condition. This process is particularly relevant in the context of disassembly,
a critical step in remanufacturing that ensures components can be effectively reused. [2]
Remanufacturing is an integral part of the circular economy, significantly contributing
to resource conservation and reduction of environmental impacts.
Unlike traditional
manufacturing, which typically relies on new materials, remanufacturing utilizes used
components, thereby conserving the energy and materials initially invested in their
creation.
This process not only reduces waste but also minimizes the need for raw
material extraction and processing, which are energy-intensive and environmentally
detrimental [3].
An analysis on the energy intensities associated with manufacturing and remanufacturing
plastics highlights substantial energy savings. Manufacturing new plastic components
typically requires a significant amount of energy for the extraction and processing of raw
materials. In contrast, remanufacturing plastics, such as through recycling processes,
consumes much less energy. For example, recycling plastic saves up to 88% of the
energy compared to producing new plastic from virgin materials.
The environmental
benefits extend beyond energy savings. Remanufacturing avoids the CO2 emissions
associated with raw material extraction and processing. By recycling and remanufacturing
plastic waste, it is possible to significantly reduce greenhouse gas emissions, thus
contributing to climate change mitigation efforts. [4]

The role of disassembly in product design and end-of-life processes is critical.
By
facilitating the easy separation of components, disassembly supports the reuse and
recycling of materials, helping to reduce waste and conserve resources. The increasing
focus on sustainable practices in manufacturing and waste management highlights the
importance of developing efficient disassembly processes. Studies show a positive trend
in recycling practices and energy savings due to reusability. As illustrated in fig. 1.2, the
amount of plastic waste ending up in landfills is decreasing, thanks to increased recycling
efforts and improved material recovery processes.
Effective disassembly is crucial for the remanufacturing process.
It ensures that
components can be retrieved without damage, maintaining their integrity for refurbishment
and reuse.
Innovations in disassembly, particularly of complex assemblies like 3D
printed snap-fit joints, can enhance the efficiency of remanufacturing processes. These
innovations can reduce the time and energy required for disassembly, further amplifying
the environmental benefits of remanufacturing [3].

1.2
Problem Statement
This thesis subject focuses on a relatively new yet critical area of research: the factors
influencing disassembly processes, with a particular focus on the unfastening of snap-fit
joints.
There are two way the disassembly processes can be done, manually or
automatically. Manual disassembly processes often suffer from a lack of predictability,
control, and precision, presenting significant challenges in various industrial applications.

Conversely, while automated disassembly processes can offer enhanced consistency and
efficiency, they often face difficulties in generalization and adaptability across different
product cases. Even though currently the manual disassembly is still more applied in the
industry, because it involves less layout and infrastructure changes, robotic disassembly
can become highly effective when some specific factors like accessibility, modularity and
flexibility is achieved. [9]
The inherent costs associated with automated disassembly can be perceived as
a disadvantage for companies,
especially given the need for customization to
accommodate specific product designs.
However, as industries increasingly pivot
towards more sustainable business models, the relevance of this study becomes
evident. Improving recycling methodologies through effective disassembly and material
separation is essential for advancing sustainability in manufacturing.
1.3
Research Questions and Objectives
This research aims to advance the understanding of disassembly processes, contributing
to sustainable manufacturing practices.It is preferable that the process would be
non-destructive and keeping the integrity of the parts as intact as possible. The questions
that this thesis is trying to answer are:
• What are the advantages of the automatic disassembly process compared to a
manual process?
• How to predict the variation factors in an automatic disassembly process?
• Where could this study be applied?
• What are the factors defining the end-of-life conditions for parts that were
disassembled?
• How do those factors variation affect the product?
• How do those variation factors affect the disassembly process?
• Could the effect of those parameters be avoided or minimized?
The primary objectives of this thesis are:
• To develop a fully functional experimental for a robotic disassembly process setup
that is able to provide qualitative data regarding disassembly forces in various
conditions.
• To build an efficient robot program that is consistent and easy to replicate under all
the required situations.
• To gather consistent data from the sensors attached to the robot arm for a better
understanding of the disassembly process.
• To create an overview of the influencing factors on the disassembly processes
aiming to develop a predictable and controllable process that could be widely
spread.
1.4
Scope and Limitations
This study focuses on the automated disassembly of snap-fit joints made from PLA
plastic using various designs. While the research aims to provide broad insights into
the disassembly process, certain limitations must be acknowledged:

• The scope is limited to snap-fit joints and does not cover other types of fasteners.
• The experiments are conducted under controlled laboratory conditions, which may

different materials or manufacturing methods.
1.5
Structure of the Thesis
The thesis is structured as follows:
• Chapter 1 - Introduction: This chapter provides an overview of the thesis topic,
detailing the background, main motivations, and objectives. It also introduces the
work conducted throughout the thesis.
• Chapter 2 - Literature Review: This chapter reviews the current state of the
industry concerning sustainability, snap-fit design, disassembly procedures, and
end-of-life conditions. It offers a comprehensive analysis of the debated topic.
• Chapter 3 - Experimental Setup: This chapter explains the experimental part of
the study, including the equipment used and how it facilitated data collection. It also
addresses the limitations of each component used in the experiments.
• Chapter 4 - Results: This chapter presents all the relevant data gathered during the

of each.
• Chapter 5 - Discussion: This chapter highlights the key findings from the results,
presenting the final tool developed.
It also provides recommendations for best
practices in decommissioning and disposal.
• Chapter 6 - Conclusion: This chapter summarizes the conclusions of the thesis
and suggests areas for future research.

Literature Review
2.1
Current Situation
The current understanding of automated disassembly processes for snap fits, particularly
those manufactured via 3D printing, is relatively limited.
Traditional disassembly
techniques often require manual intervention and can be labor-intensive, especially
with complex geometries like snap fits. Automating these processes holds promise for
efficiency gains and supports the circular economy model by facilitating easier material
recovery and recycling. Industries are increasingly adopting modular design principles to
facilitate easy disassembly and reassembly, making reuse more feasible. This approach
aligns with circular economy models that prioritize the reuse of materials and parts,
creating closed-loop systems. Investments in advanced recycling technologies that can
handle complex materials and shapes, especially in high-tech industries, are also being
encouraged.
Figure 2.1: Product circular life cycle by introducing disassembly
Beliefs surrounding automated disassembly methods emphasize the potential for
reducing labor costs, improving process repeatability, and enabling scalable disassembly
for diverse part geometries.
However, challenges persist in optimizing parameters
such as pulling force, gripping force, and fixturing to ensure reliable and damage-free
disassembly.
Existing research suggests that integrating robotics can streamline

Even
small variations in product design can necessitate significant changes in the disassembly

The robustness is crucial for ensuring reliability and durability across various industrial

Several parameters regarding robust design need to be considered
when building a whole disassembly process. These parameters include accessibility,

Key
principles of robust snap-fit design include:
• Selection of materials with suitable mechanical properties.
• Geometric precision and tolerance management.
• Designing for variability to ensure flexibility and adaptability.
• Incorporating features that aid automated processes and prevent damage over time.
Robust design theory, particularly the application of minimal constraints theory to
interfaces, is essential. One of the robustness principles can be observed by examining
the number of contact points in snap-fit joints. A snap-fit design with more contact points
adheres better to this principle, ensuring fewer controlled dimensions and more reliable
performance.
Conducting these tests essentially performs a robustness evaluation to determine which
snap-fit design offers better performance stability, tolerance absorption, and overall
reliability. This evaluation provides insights into the most suitable design for automatic
disassembly while aligning with robust design principles by striving for consistency and
insensitivity to variations.
A robust design also prevents failure due to variations. Analyzing different principles of
snap-fits can help determine which design is less likely to fail under various manufacturing
and assembly conditions. In manufacturing, a robust design typically results in lower
defect rates.
If a design with more contact points demonstrates higher tolerance to
variations and consistent disassembly forces, it could lead to fewer defects during
production, translating to cost savings and higher quality products.
Testing multiple
designs allows for quantification of these potential benefits.
Over time, repeated disassembly and assembly may wear out the contact points.
A
design with more contact points could distribute this wear more evenly, resulting in
longer-lasting components. Comparing designs with different numbers of contact points
helps assess which one maintains its integrity better over prolonged use. Additionally, a
snap-fit design with more contact points may better absorb manufacturing and assembly
variations than a design with fewer contact points. This is because multiple contact points
can accommodate slight mismatches and still function correctly.
All these principles from robust design theory can be tested through various experiments,

2.3
Snap fit joint design
Snap fit joints are widely recognized for their efficiency, cost effectiveness, and
ease of assembly, making them a referred choise in many manufacturing processes.
The integration of snap-fit designs in additive manufacturing (AM) introduces unique
opportunities and challenges, which have been explored in various studies and
guidelines. This section reviews the principles of snap-fit design, particularly focusing on
their application in additive manufacturing and their potential for automated disassembly
processes. Each of these applications this kind of joints are used for, have the primary
advantage of snap-fits, which is their ability to provide a secure closure that can
typically be engaged and disengaged multiple times without significant wear or loss of
functionality.
The primary types of snap-fit joints include cantilever snap-fits, annular
snap-fits, ball and socket snap-fits, and torsional snap-fits. The design of a cantilever
snap-fit includes a beam (the cantilever) anchored at one end, with the other end free
to flex. This arrangement allows the free end to engage with a notch or ledge on the
mating part, securely holding it until disengaged. They are particularly appreciated in
designs where assembly and disassembly need to be simple and quick, without requiring
tools or excessive force.
The annular snap-fit is particularly suited to applications
where a durable, often watertight seal is needed, and where the parts may need to be
disassembled without damage for maintenance or refilling purposes. The ball and socket
snap-fit involves a spherical element fitting into a socket, often used in applications
requiring a range of movement.
The torsional snap-fit typically consists of a circular
or semi-circular clip that flexes torsional to snap over a mating part.
This design is
highly effective for applications requiring a secure fit that can also be quickly and
easily released, often enhancing the user experience by simplifying the assembly and
disassembly processes without the need for tools.[10] [11]
When focusing specifically on snap fits, those have some main functions such as:
• To provide attachment between parts
• To establish part location, alignment, and orientation
• To transfer service loads
• To eliminate degrees of freedom
• To absorb tolerance between parts

Fixture principles
The usage of fixture elements within the robotic setup constitutes an essential factor
influencing the outcomes of experimentation. These fixtures serve the purpose of securely
positioning test samples during testing procedures. In the context outlined, one facet of
the part is affixed to the table during the disassembly phase.
The design specifications of such fixtures necessitate certain attributes. Primarily, they
should enable the secure immobilization of the part while minimizing the degrees of
freedom necessary for manipulation. In the case of the disassembly setup focused on
this research, the fixture should suppress all degrees of freedom on the bottom part
of they prototype. Stability is paramount in ensuring accurate experimental conditions.
Additionally, an ideal fixture design should exhibit a degree of flexibility to accommodate
variations in the shapes and sizes of products under examination.
Some of the advantages that come with the fixture of the part in this process are: an
increases in the productivity due to high accuracy of the process and reduces the costs
of production, reduces quality control expenses, less skilled labour is required and it may
improve the safety factor in the workplace.

so, the snap-fit features exhibited increased strength and resistance to breaking. If the
parts were printed with the snap-fit features aligned parallel to the layers, the inter-layer
bonding would be weaker, leading to potential failures under stress.

4.2
Theoretical calculation
The calculation of the unfastening process for cantilever snap fits, as illustrated in fig. 4.2,
can be applied to both new and existing product designs to ensure they can be unfastened.
This step is crucial to verify that the necessary disassembly force does not exceed the
robot’s maximum load capacity.
First, it is important to ensure that the chosen production material is suitable for
the application.
Key input values include the modulus of elasticity, maximum strain,
and coefficient of friction.
Next, it is essential to verify that no other parameters or
environmental factors affect the unfastening process. If these conditions are not met,
destructive disassembly may be required, making the equations inapplicable and
necessitating a repeat of the process.
Subsequently, decisions must be made regarding the geometrical parameters of the
snap-fit design, such as mean length, mean thickness, deflection, and retention angle.
These elements are integral to calculating the deflection force.
The calculation can
vary significantly based on the snap-fit design, particularly considering the ratio of beam
length to beam thickness, the cantilever’s position relative to the part, and any applicable
variation factors. Once these variables are acknowledged, the deflection force can be
determined.[11]
Based on these parameters, the retention angle is calculated and compared to the critical
case, which represents the scenario where the chosen angle results in excessively high
disassembly forces. If the values fall within the acceptable range, the disassembly force
can be calculated. Otherwise, some parameters must be reviewed, as the unfastening
process may not be feasible or could lead to a destructive outcome.

4.3.1
Repeatability
The advancement in automated disassembly processes has become important in the
context of sustainable manufacturing and end-of-life product management. A key aspect
of this development is the reliable and efficient disassembly of snap-fit designs, which are
commonly used in various industries due to their cost-effectiveness, ease of assembly,
and recyclability. As these processes evolve, understanding the factors that influence
their effectiveness and consistency becomes crucial.
This chapter focuses on the
repeatability testing of automatic disassembly processes for different snap-fit designs,
with a particular emphasis on the potential impact of wear and tear on disassembly force.
Snap-fit connections, which rely on elastic deformation to assemble and disassemble
components, are integral to modern manufacturing. They are designed to be repeatedly
engaged and disengaged, which makes them suitable for products requiring regular
maintenance or parts replacement.
However, the mechanical properties of these
connections can be affected by repeated use, leading to variations in the force required for
disassembly. This variability poses challenges for automated systems, where precision
and consistency are required.
One of the central hypotheses guiding this research is that the disassembly force will
decrease after multiple testing cycles due to cumulative wear.
Wear can manifest in
various forms, such as material abrasion, plastic deformation, and surface roughness
alterations, all of which can affect the ease of disassembly. By quantifying these changes,
we can better predict the lifespan of snap-fit connections in practical applications and
develop strategies to minimize wear effects.
The repeatability experiment was conducted on the 2 contact points cantilever snap-fit
design, 3D printed in PLA with an Ultimaker printer, to assess how multiple repetitive
tests influence the disassembly force.
The graph in fig. 4.8 indicates that repetitive
disassembly affects the components being disengaged, as evidenced by a tendency for
the disassembly force to decrease over time.

Discussion
The entire project was initiates with the concept of product lifecycle robustness in
mind.
Remanufacturing by using recycled parts offer significant potential from a
sustainability point of view.
However, this process is labor-intensive and challenging
to automate.
A typical remanufacturing process follows several steps:
monitoring,
product inspection, disassembly, cleaning, part inspection, replacement, reassembly
and testing.
Each of those steps require precises measurements and monitoring
to ensure that remanufacturing is feasible and that the resulted product meets the
same quality standards as a new one. Even when quality expectations are met, it is
important to consider the rapid pace of technological advancement.
Market changes
and evolving quality perception must be acknowledged to address demand variability
effectively. The research described in this report focused on the disassembly phase of
the remanufacturing process. While all the steps before and after disassembly are as
important for the producing successful, reusable product, this study specifically aimed
to enhance understanding and efficiency in the disassembly process. The experiments
conducted have provided valuable insights into the factors influencing the disassembly
processes of snap-fit joints. The key findings illustrate how variations in gripping force,
pulling force, the direction of applied force, and fixture principles impact the disassembly
process.
Additionally, other factors such as surface quality, printing tolerances, and
the stability of the working table play significant roles. This discussion is structured to
address these findings under the context of robust disassembly design principles.
5.1
Result analysis
The results collected through data acquisition reveal a specific behavior of the system
during disassembly. By interpreting this behavior, the following insights were identified:
• The theoretical results calculated for all the snap-fit designers discusses are higher
thaa the values obtained through experiments.
It is assumed that this is due
to the deformation of the parts during gripping, which creates higher tolerances
between the two parts that are to be pulled apart.
This suggests that more
precise calculations can be achieved by physically measuring the deformation, a
measurement that is challenging given the design of the cubes incorporating the
snap-fits.
• Repetitive assembly and disassembly processes on a product assembled with
snap-fit joints tends to result in decreasing disengaging forces. This is likely due
to changes in surface roughness over time, material deformation, and wear that
occurs after multiple cycles.
• The difference in disengagement forces between two identical prototypes, printed
on different 3D printers, Ultimaker and Bambuslicer, can be attributed to variations
in print quality, resolution and resulted surface roughness. Bambuslicer, known for
high precision and tighter tolerances, produce parts with smoother surfaces and a
more snug fit, resulting in lower friction and reduced disassembly forces. In contrast,
Ultimaker printers may produce parts with slightly less surface precision, leading to
higher friction and increased forces required for disengagement.
• The disassembly force detected for prototypes fixed with a rigid fixture is higher than
for those with a spring-based fixture. The rigid fixture provides no means to absorb
TECHNICAL FOUNDATION FOR CIRCULAR PRODUCT LIFE CYCLES
55
==PAGE:66==
or redistribute the applied force, leading to higher direct forces on the snap fits. In
contrast, a spring-based fixture absorbs, stores, and gradually releases some of
the applied force, reducing the peak forces required to disengage the snap fits and
making the disassembly process smoother and less force-intensive. Additionally,
the spring-based fixture offers more flexibility, which may help eliminate errors such
as small misalignment that can increase the disassembly force.
• The similar results obtained by pulling on the direction of the disengagement, or
applying the force with an angle, for a prototype fixed in a spring based fixture,
proofs that the springs are able to compensate for the misalignment induced.
• Misalignment in the pulling force applied to the prototype has a greater impact on
the process than misalignment in the positioning of the part on the working table,
which is represented by misalignment in the fixture. An interpretation for this would
be that when the fixture is misaligned, the box or snap-fit parts may not be perfectly
aligned, causing the force to be distributed unevenly. However, the overall structural
integrity of the snap-fit remains relatively intact, allowing for some compensation in
the force distribution. While a misalignment in the pulling force directly affects the
direction and magnitude of the force applied to disengage the snap-fit. This causes
the force to be applied at an angle, reducing its effectiveness and increasing the
resistance encountered during disassembly. The misaligned pulling force fails to
provide the necessary direct disengagement, making the process less efficient and
requiring more force to overcome the additional resistance.
• The forces required for symmetrical four contact point cantilever snap fits are
higher than those for non-symmetrical designs. This phenomenon underscores the
influence of symmetry on the disassembly force. In symmetrical snap-fit designs,
all contact points experience uniform force distribution during disengagement. This
uniformity ensures predictable and stable disengagement forces, even if higher, due
to the simultaneous engagement of all contact points. Conversely, non-symmetrical
snap fits, which incorporate two different types of snap fits—two deflected and two
rigid—exhibit a distinct behavior. The asymmetry in the design leads to improper
and unpredictable contact between the parts.
In a non-symmetrical setup, the
forces are unevenly distributed, with the deflected snap fits absorbing more force
initially due to their flexibility, while the rigid snap fits resist disengagement longer.
This imbalance results in increased friction and mechanical resistance at different
stages of the disengagement process.This highlights that non-symmetrical snap fits,
despite potentially lower initial disengagement forces at individual contact points,
culminate in a more challenging and less robust disassembly process due to the
compounded resistances and force imbalances.
• When too many variation factors are involved in an automatic disassembly process
(e.g., unsymmetrical design, gripper misalignment, tilt force applied, tilt fixture),
the process becomes very difficult to control and unpredictable. This can lead to
increased wear on the disassembled product, higher energy requirements for the
process, and potential danger for operators around the working area.

Fixture principles
One of the examples is the fixture principles considered at the beginning. The initial
prototypes utilized three pins for positioning and fixing the part on the working table while
the robot performed the disassembly. However, lateral pressure proved to be a failure.
The friction forces, combined with the pressing forces for a 3D-printed fixture similar to

the gripper’s position, the specific case study, and the position of the cables during the
experiment. The force for each case is determined by calculating the mean of the values
that are expected to be zero at the beginning when the robot is in the ”home position” and
subtracting this from the entire process.

Conclusion
The conducted research underscores significant advancements in the automated
disassembly of snap-fit joints,
especially those manufactured using 3D printing
technologies.
This study emphasizes the advantages of the automatic disassembly
process over manual methods, particularly in terms of efficiency, consistency, and
cost-effectiveness.
Automated systems can disassemble products much faster than
human workers, significantly increasing throughput. Robots perform disassembly with
high precision and consistency, reducing the variability associated with human labor,
and lowering operational costs by reducing the need for manual labor.
Additionally,
automated systems enhance safety by handling hazardous tasks, thus reducing the
risk of injury to human workers. The findings of this research can be applied in various
fields, including sustainable manufacturing, product end-of-life management, and
waste reduction.
Optimizing snap-fit designs and automated disassembly techniques
can improve recycling processes, supporting the development of a circular economy.
Industries such as electronics, automotive, and consumer goods can benefit from efficient
disassembly processes, enhancing the reusability of components. By facilitating easier
recycling and reuse of plastic components, this study contributes to reducing plastic
waste and environmental impact.
Key factors that define the end-of-life conditions for disassembled parts include material
degradation, surface quality, and fixture type. Repeated disassembly cycles can lead to
material wear and deformation, impacting the reusability of parts. Changes in surface
roughness over time can affect the integrity and performance of snap-fit joints.
The
type of fixture used during disassembly, whether rigid or spring-based, can influence
the forces applied and the potential for part damage. Variation factors can significantly
impact both the product and the disassembly process, with factors such as misalignment,
force application angles, and fixture type increasing wear on disassembled parts and
reducing their lifespan. High variability in disassembly forces can lead to inconsistent
results, making the process less reliable and harder to control.
To minimize the impact of variation factors, several strategies can be employed: ensuring
symmetrical and robust snap-fit designs to reduce force variability, maintaining controlled
conditions and precise robotic programming to enhance process consistency, and using
flexible, spring-based fixtures to absorb and redistribute applied forces, thereby reducing
peak disassembly forces and improving alignment.
This research provides valuable
insights into the automated disassembly of snap-fit joints, highlighting the importance of
design, environmental control, and material properties. The findings contribute to the
development of more efficient and sustainable recycling processes, supporting broader
sustainability goals. Future research should focus on refining control mechanisms and
exploring new materials and geometries to further enhance the reliability and efficiency of
automated disassembly systems for developing the proofs for long term cost-efficiency.
