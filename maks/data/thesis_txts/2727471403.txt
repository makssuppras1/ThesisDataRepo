Abstract
Growing amounts of renewable energy sources (RES) in the power system combined with
variability in consumption patterns add stochasticity to the power grid. Because of this,
statistical parameters as the mean, value at risk (VaR) and conditional value at risk
(CVaR) are becoming increasingly important for risk assessment in power systems.
Monte Carlo simulation is a well-known random sampling technique widely used for
estimating statistical values such as the mean, value at risk (VaR) and conditional value
at risk (CVaR). Monte Carlo simulation is a valuable tool but computationally
burdensome, while quantum computing offers a potential advantage for solving complex
problems where Monte Carlo is used. This thesis theoretically and empirically compares

Introduction
The energy transition is currently centered around replacing fossil fuel sources with
renewable energy sources (RES). The goal is to add as much RES in the next five years
as has been added over the previous two decades [1]. This widespread adoption of RES
in the power grid brings forth computational challenges.
RES are subject to
intermittency and variability due to factors like weather conditions, time of day, and
seasonality. Thus, the prediction of RES generation patterns are more complex than for
fossil fuel sources which can be switched on and off depending on when electricity is
needed. Like generation patterns, the electricity demand is becoming more variable due
to for example electric vehicles and distributed energy resources, such as rooftop solar
panels, small-scale wind turbines, and energy storage systems.
These variable generation and demand patterns add stochasticity to the grid. For that
reason, the characteristics of the power grid can to a larger extent be described using a
probabilistic approach instead of using traditional deterministic methods. A probabilistic
approach takes into account the uncertainties associated with renewable energy generation
and consumption patterns. Statistical parameters, such as the mean, value at risk (VaR)
and conditional value at risk (CVaR), are becoming more important to understand the
potential risks for a power system.
Monte Carlo simulation is a valuable tool for analyzing systems with uncertainty and
variability across a wide range of fields. It has long been acknowledged as a reliable method
to estimate parameters like the mean, VaR and CVaR of a probability distribution by
employing random sampling. In case of power systems, Monte Carlo methods are useful
for probability distributions, like wind forecast errors, line loadings, electricity demand
patterns and voltage profiles, among others. The downside of Monte Carlo simulations is
that they come with a huge computational burden. Minimizing the computation time of
Monte Carlo simulations is essential to enable faster analysis, decision-making, and risk
assessment.
Quantum Computation (QC) has shown an advantage over classical computation when
it comes to solving some specific complex problems, such as doing computations for
approaches that require a probability distribution, like Monte Carlo methods [2].
Quantum Monte Carlo has been proven to provide a quadratic speedup compared to
classical Monte Carlo simulation [3].
IBM has demonstrated that the quantum
equivalent of Monte Carlo, from now on called Quantum Amplitude Estimation (QAE),
can be applied to sample from a known distribution and estimate the mean, VaR and
CVaR in a financial context [4].
In the context of power systems, the mean, VaR and CVaR could be used to estimate line
loadings. The expected line loading in a power system can be estimated with the mean.
The VaR serves as a measure to assess the risk that a certain threshold for line loading is
exceeded. For example, if a 95% VaR is 85% loading of the lines, this means that there
is a 5% chance that the line loading will exceed 85%. The CVaR represents the average
expected loading if the VaR is surpassed. For example, if there is 2.5% chance that the line
loading is 90% and 2.5% chance that the line loading is 92%, the CVaR would be 91%.
Brynjar et al. has demonstrated the computation of a DC Power Flow on a quantum
computer [2]. Building upon this progress, the next step could involve leveraging QAE
techniques to detect line overloadings.

The canonical QAE algorithm was first introduced in 2000 [5] and since then different QAE
algorithms have been developed. The canonical version uses a form of Quantum Phase
Estimation (QPE) which is computationally intensive and unfeasible for implementation
on current and near-term devices belonging to the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum
(NISQ) era [5].
That is why in the past years new algorithms have been developed,
eliminating the need for QPE. These modified versions are better suited for implementation
on current quantum computers. A variant of QAE known as Iterative Quantum Amplitude
Estimation (IQAE) was introduced in 2019 by Grinko et al. [6]. In 2020, Nakaji et al.
introduced an alternative algorithm called Faster Amplitude Estimation (FAE), which, at
that time, presented a faster computation requiring fewer samples while acquiring the same
accuracy [7]. However, in 2021, an updated version of IQAE was proposed with improved
theoretical bounds. This enhancement granted IQAE a theoretical advantage over FAE. In
[8], another algorithm was introduced, called Maximum Likelihood Amplitude Estimation
(MLAE). The mentioned algorithms differ in how they derive the different values for the
powers of the Grover operator and how they combine the results into a final estimate. In
[9], the performance of IQAE and MLAE is compared using Qiskit, the open-source toolbox
for quantum computing [10]. Here, quantum samples (oracles) and the circuit depth for
both are compared for a certain degree of accuracy using Monte Carlo integration. In [8],
MLAE is also demonstrated for Monte Carlo integration, whereas IQAE and FAE in [6]
and [7] are tested for numerical experiments.
The goal of this thesis is to compare the performance and quantum advantage of the
three mentioned QAE algorithms for estimating the mean, VaR and CVaR for various
probability distributions relevant for power system applications.
The next step would
involve the integration of these methods into the power flow circuit of [2] which generates
unknown probability distributions and applying QAE to estimate line flows.
Although, it has been previously mentioned that QAE algorithms would theoretically
provide a quadratic speed-up compared to classical Monte Carlo methods, it should be
remarked that quadratic speedups will not enable quantum advantage on early
generations of quantum computers in this NISQ-era, unless there is a significant
improvement in quantum error-correction [11]. Nevertheless, even a modest speedup in
solving optimization problems would be enormously valuable in economic and societal
value [12]. Another limitation in this research is the size of publicly available quantum
computers via IBM [13], as the public is restricted to devices with 7 qubits (quantum
bits).
This report starts with a short overview of some fundamental quantum computing
concepts, explaining some underlying principles to understand the computational power
of quantum computing in Chapter 2. In this chapter, the quantum amplitude estimation
algorithms will be further explained.
This is followed by a theoretical comparison
between QAE algorithms and the classical Monte Carlo simulation in Chapter 3, to show
the advantages of using QAE algorithms over the classical Monte Carlo simulation.
Chapter 4 focuses on the classical Monte Carlo simulation and its application to estimate
the statistical parameters of various probability distributions and how the amount of
samples relates to the accuracy of the estimates. This is relevant for comparing the QAE
algorithms with the classical approach.
Chapter 5 delves into the methods used to
estimate the mean, VaR and CVaR with QAE and how the algorithms are compared. In
Chapter 6, the results for the QAE algorithms are presented for estimating the mean,
VaR and CVaR. This is followed by a discussion on these results in Chapter 7 and
finally, a conclusion in Chapter 8.

2.2
Classical algorithms
2.2.1
Monte Carlo simulation
Monte Carlo methods are computational tools that use random number sampling to
evaluate an outcome. Monte Carlo simulation is part of these methods. In Monte Carlo
simulation, a large number of random samples is generated for the input variable(s) of
the model, typically following specified probability distributions and with those the
outcome of a certain problem is estimated. By generating random samples over and over
again between the defined minimum and maximum values, the simulation provides
possible outcomes of the problem. The more random samples are used for the simulation,
the more accurate the possible outcomes will be.
Monte Carlo simulation is used to
analyze complex systems or models where uncertainty and randomness play a role.
2.2.2
Maximum Likelihood estimation
Maximum likelihood estimation uses observed data to estimate parameters of an assumed
probability distribution by maximizing the likelihood that the observed data was generated
by the probability distribution.

then be one that maximizes the likelihood of getting the observed data. The probability

2.3
Quantum Amplitude Estimation algorithms
The quantum alternative of a Monte Carlo simulation is called Quantum Amplitude
Estimation (QAE). Theoretically, QAE shows a quadratic speed-up over classical
algorithms [14]. Four different QAE algorithms will be discussed in this report. Initially,
the canonical QAE has been invented in 2000 and in the past years three QAE
algorithms have been introduced which are more suitable for quantum computers in the
NISQ era:
Maximum Likelihood Amplitude Estimation (MLAE), Iterative Quantum
Amplitude Estimation (IQAE) and Faster Amplitude Estimation (FAE). In this section
the working of the QAE algorithms will be explained, but a more detailed explanation
about their quantum advantage is given in Chapter 3.
Although the focus of this thesis lies on the algorithms as mentioned above, QAE is still
being actively researched.
Examples of other, even more recent, QAE algorithms are
Real Quantum Amplitude Estimation [15], Variational Quantum Amplitude Estimation
[16], Improved Maximum-likelihood Amplitude Estimation [17], Random-Depth Quantum
Amplitude Estimation [18] and Dynamic Amplitude Estimation [19]. In [15], the QAE
algorithm is extended to be sensitive for the sign of the amplitude. For the purpose of

4.1
Probability distributions relevant for power systems
Various probability distributions are employed in power systems to deal with the stochastic
nature of the grid. Normal distributions are often applied to model measurement errors.
In the case of power systems, normal distributions can be used to estimate parameters such

in power systems, this is useful for the distribution of voltage across system nodes. Another
example is for modeling transmission line failure, where uniform distributions can be used
to represent the uncertainty in failure rates or repair times within a specified range. That
is why in this thesis, these different probability distributions are investigated: the normal
distribution, Weibull distribution and uniform distribution.

6.4
Increasing the amount of qubits
The more qubits are used for loading the probability distribution, the more accurate the
probability distribution can be represented. That is why the algorithms were also tested
for estimating the mean of the distributions loaded onto 7 qubits. This is expected to
impact the average relative error of the estimate, but an increase of qubits also comes at
the expense of an increased circuit depth.
6.4.1
Average relative error
The QAE algorithms were also ran for estimating the mean of the probability
distributions loaded onto 7 qubits.
However, using 7 qubits instead of 4 for the
probability distribution, the average relative error of the estimate of the mean did not
seem to be affected significantly. In fact, the average relative errors were more or less
similar. The results can be found in the Appendix (Table 10.1 and 10.2). Although, this
might be the case for estimating the mean, it is expected that for estimating the value at
risk and conditional value at risk, the increase of qubits significantly affects the accuracy
of the estimate. This was observed for increasing the number of qubits for estimating
VaR and CVaR of the uniform distribution, where the average relative error was
significantly lower than for the other distributions loaded onto 4 qubits. The reason for
this, is that for VaR it is crucial to accurately represent the distribution with more
qubits in order to be able to estimate the tail.

Discussion
The results provided in the previous chapter show potential for the application of QAE
to estimate the mean, VaR and CVaR for probability distributions relevant to power
systems.
The empirical comparison shows that MLAE and IQAE provide a speed-up
compared to classical Monte Carlo simulation. For the tested probability distributions,
the IQAE and MLAE algorithms were able to provide a more accurate estimate for the
mean using less samples for all distributions. In case of N(0.1, 0.001), MLAE and IQAE
gained an advantage over CMC around вҲј3В·103 samples, while for the other distributions,
this happened from вҲј3 В· 102 samples. FAE, however, did not show any advantage for
the investigated range of samples. The investigated range of samples was set to вҲј102 to
вҲј3В·105. Theoretically, the three QAE algorithms have been proven to provide a quadratic
speed-up in terms of the amount of samples needed for a specific accuracy compared to
classical Monte Carlo simulation.
Theoretically, MLAE and IQAE seem to be the preferred algorithms in terms of number
of oracle queries and accuracy. For MLAE, theoretically, a lower bound is provided for the
amount of samples corresponding to a certain accuracy, while for IQAE an upper bound
is given. IQAE and MLAE, in practice, though, seem to give similar results in Figure
6.2. It should be noted that the confidence interval for IQAE is much smaller for IQAE
than for MLAE, as can be observed in Figure 6.4. The downside of IQAE is, however,
that its circuit depth cannot be controlled. For current quantum computers, the depth of
a circuit still matters significantly due to the amount of noise generated by the quantum
computers.
For the normal distribution and the uniform distribution similar trends are visible for the
empirical and theoretical comparison while for the Weibull distribution at some point, the
quantum advantage decreases. One of the reasons for this might be that the mean can
not be estimated more accurately when more oracles are used due to the limited amount
of qubits with which the probability distribution is represented. It could be that for the
other distributions, the same happens when investigating an even larger range of oracles.
It is assumed that increasing the amount of qubits would solve this problem.
In this thesis, the simulator is assumed to give representative results in terms of oracle
queries. However, noise induced by quantum computers result in large relative errors (up
to 7 times larger than for the simulator) for a probability distribution loaded onto 4 qubits.
Assuming that noise will be reduced in the future, the results of simulator are trusted,
but current implementation and real use of these QAE algorithms might be unfeasible yet
for current quantum computers due to the large relative error. Furthermore, the circuit
depth increases drastically for increasing the amount of qubits onto which the probability
distribution is loaded. This means that circuit depth will generate even more noise and
larger relative errors could be expected. Running the QAE algorithms on a simulator with
the increased amount of qubits also takes significantly more time.
The relative estimation errors of the mean and VaR seem still acceptable for QAE
algorithms, although this depends heavily on the application for which they are used.
However, for the CVaR relative estimation errors seem too large (up to 21.21%) when
using 4 qubits for the probability distribution.
Again, this problem might be solved
when using more qubits. In [9], it is also stated that for 14 qubits results become more

accurate for IQAE and MLAE compared to classical Monte Carlo integration than for 10
qubits.
Although, IQAE and MLAE have proven to provide a speed-up compared to classical
CMC empirically, it has to be noted that this advantage is still not visible in terms of
runtime. While it takes several minutes to do one iteration of a QAE algorithm on the
simulator, it takes around 10-20 seconds to perform it on a quantum computer. However,
CMC just needs several seconds in classical computation time, so there is not yet a clear
advantage in runtime. The advantage is thus purely seen in computation complexity.

Conclusion
This thesis has proven that it is possible to estimate the mean, VaR and CVaR for certain
known probability distributions leveraging QAE techniques. The QAE techniques show
a practical quantum advantage, where IQAE achieves the most accurate estimates for a
certain accuracy among the tested algorithms.
The investigated probability distributions include two normal distributions with different
standard deviations, a Weibull and uniform distribution.
Normal distributions are
widely used in power systems for modeling loads and errors. The Weibull distribution
resembles the trend of the wind speed and is therefore relevant for estimating the
available wind power. Uniform distributions are able to model values that are equally
likely to happen and are commonly employed for modeling uncertainty. One example is
for modeling transmission line failure, where uniform distributions can be used to
represent the uncertainty in failure rates or repair times within a specified range.
Theoretically,
the investigated QAE algorithms outperform classical Monte Carlo
simulation quadratically with regards to the amount of samples needed in order to
achieve a certain accuracy. In theory, FAE needs more samples for gaining a quantum
advantage over classical Monte Carlo than IQAE and MLAE, which is supported by
practical results. When run on the simulator of the quantum computer, the empirical
trends for the QAE algorithms align with theoretical expectations. Empirically, IQAE
and MLAE outperform classical Monte Carlo for the investigated range вҲј102 вҲ’3 В· 105
samples, while FAE does not.
Literature states that around 10000 iterations would
provide suп¬Җiciently accurate estimates for probabilistic power flow in power systems but
is acknowledged to be computationally very intensive [27].
IQAE and MLAE show an advantage in terms of computational complexity for estimating
the mean of the four types of probability distributions. It was found that for estimating the
mean a normal distribution with a very small standard deviation N(Вө = 0.1, Пғ = 0.01), an
order of magnitude more samples were needed for IQAE and MLAE to outperform classical
Monte Carlo than for the other distributions. Classically, the required amount of samples
for a certain accuracy of the estimate depends on the type of probability distribution.
This also holds for estimates provided by QAE and the quantum advantage thus relies on
which type of probability distribution is used.
It should be noted that the quantum advantage is not seen yet in runtime on a quantum
computer. Furthermore, the relative estimation error when estimating the mean on a real
quantum computer was much larger than when ran on the simulator. The comparison for
the QAE algorithms is thus based on experiments run on the simulator. Although IQAE
and MLAE provide similar results in terms of relative estimation errors and required
samples, the empirical confidence interval of IQAE is smaller. A disadvantage of IQAE is,
however, that its circuit depth cannot be controlled, while this is possible for MLAE and
FAE.
The QAE algorithms were able to estimate the VaR and CVaR for the normal distributions
and the Weibull distribution. Though, the relative estimation errors were large (up to
21.21%). One explanation for this is that the probability distribution was loaded onto 4
qubits and this does not represent the probability distribution accurately enough. This
matters more when estimating the tail of a distribution (VaR and CVaR) rather than when
estimating the mean. Already when expanding the loading of the probability distribution

Expanding the probability distribution to more qubits shows promising potential for the

increased circuit depth. Large circuit depths are problematic in this NISQ-era because of
the noise generated by the quantum computer in the gates. If noise can be significantly
reduced in the future, the speed-up of QAE algorithms will be more visible in practice.

Future Work
In this thesis, QAE has been applied to known probability distributions, while in fact when
using Monte Carlo methods, the underlying probability distribution is not always known.
Connecting this work with [2], QAE techniques could be used to estimate parameters of
unknown line loading distributions in a small power system.
Furthermore, noise mitigation techniques could be applied to reduce the relative estimation
errors and improve the estimate as provided by QAE algorithms. For example as in the
work of [19], where IQAE is applied in combination with state-of-the art error mitigation
and circuit optimization techniques.
Other existing QAE techniques as Real Quantum Amplitude Estimation [15], Variational
Quantum
Amplitude
Estimation
[16],
Improved
Maximum-likelihood
Amplitude
Estimation [17], Random-Depth Quantum Amplitude Estimation [18] and Dynamic
Amplitude Estimation [19], have not been included in the comparison in this thesis. This
is an active area of research, but there has been no literature to my knowledge that gives
an overview and compares the performance of all of these QAE algorithms. This presents
an obstacle for individuals seeking to apply QAE techniques in practical scenarios, as
they are unable to determine which technique is most effective for specific use cases.
An important goal in the field of quantum computing is to ensure the accessibility of
knowledge to a broad audience, to enable a widespread adoption of existing technologies
and exploration of their applications, since we are still in an early stage of using quantum
computation for real applications. While Qiskit has already implemented certain Quantum
Amplitude Estimation (QAE) algorithms, it is important to note that not all of the above
mentioned techniques are currently available as functions in Qiskit. A next step would be
to make all QAE techniques readily accessible for everyone through the Qiskit platform.
