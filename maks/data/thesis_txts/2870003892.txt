Abstract
The shipping sector is undergoing significant technological advancements., with vessels be-
coming more interconnected and reliant on automation, artificial intelligence, and real-time
data. While these advancements improve the intelligence and efficiency of shipping, they
also increase the vulnerabilities of ships to escalating cyber threats. GPS spoofing, ran-
somware attacks, system intrusions, and communication hacks are no longer hypothetical
risks they are real dangers that can disrupt global supply chains, compromise safety, and
cost millions in damages. Despite these challenges, most cybersecurity measures in the mar-
itime industry still rely on centralized security models, which are slow to detect threats and
leave ships vulnerable to large scale attacks. This research proposes a Blockchain-Enabled
Cybersecurity Risk Assessment Framework for cyber ship to change that. This innovative
approach utilizes the immutable capabilities of blockchain, along with decentralized threat
intelligence and automated security, to create a robust and proactive cybersecurity frame-
work. By adhering to key international standards of NIST CSF and ISO/IEC 27001, we
guarantee not just compliance but also a notable enhancement of our security protocols.
Let’s make our Cyber-ship safe from cyberattack’s. To test how well this approach works,
we analyzed real-world cyberattacks such as the Black Sea GPS spoofing incident (2017),
the DNV ShipManager ransomware attack (2023), and the USS Roosevelt AIS spoofing case
(2020). The framework integrates AI-powered anomaly detection, blockchain oracles for ver-
ifying threats, and decentralized security monitoring helping ships detect and respond to
cyber risks in real time. Recent findings indicate that blockchain technology has the poten-
tial to greatly improve maritime cybersecurity including cybership. Reducing dependence
on centralized control systems and enhancing real-time threat detection can significantly
enhance security responses. Although barriers such as scalability and regulatory compliance
continue, addressing these challenges collaboratively might result in a more secure future for
cyberspace.Moving forward, further research should explore how AI and blockchain together
can make cybersecurity even smarter, how fleets can adopt this system on a larger scale,
and how emerging technologies like quantum computing may shape the future of maritime
security.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The maritime sector is undergoing a radical digital transformation, with global maritime
digital technology projected to be worth $345 billion by 2030, up from an earlier estimate
of $279 billion.
This surge in technological adoption is driven by autonomous shipping,
enhanced satellite communications [1], IoT-enabled smart port infrastructure [2], and the
integration of cyber-physical systems in vessel operations [3]. At the heart of this revo-
lution are cyber-ships digitally interconnected, AI-driven vessels that leverage autonomous
navigation, real-time communication networks, and remote-controlled operations.
While
these innovations promise efficiency, reduced human dependency, and seamless global trade,
they also introduce a new frontier of cybersecurity challenges. Unlike traditional vessels,
cyber-ships depend entirely on cyber-physical infrastructure, integrating navigation, propul-
sion, cargo management, and communication systems within IT and OT networks. This
interconnectivity significantly increases cyber vulnerabilities, exposing programmable logic
controllers (PLCs), onboard sensors, and critical communication channels to cyber intrusions
[4]. Unlike cybersecurity threats in the automotive industry, where compromises are typ-
ically contained within a single vehicle, cyberattacks on cyber-ships can have catastrophic
global repercussions by disrupting entire fleets, port operations, and global supply chains
[5].
Despite these risks, existing maritime cybersecurity solutions rely on centralized security
architectures, creating single points of failure [4]. These traditional approaches such as fire-
walls, periodic audits, and manual threat assessments are reactive rather than proactive,
failing to detect and mitigate threats in real time. Without a standardized cybersecurity
risk assessment framework, identifying and responding to these cyber threats remains a chal-
lenge, leaving vessels and fleet operations exposed [6].
To address these cybersecurity gaps, the industry is exploring decentralized solutions like
blockchain technology, which offers a more resilient approach to securing cyber-ship systems
[7]. Blockchain provides a tamper-proof, decentralized security model that enhances mar-
itime cybersecurity by enabling real-time threat intelligence sharing, immutable security logs,
and smart contract-driven automation [8]. This research proposes a Blockchain-Enabled Cy-
bersecurity Risk Assessment Framework, aligning with global maritime cybersecurity stan-
dards NIST CSF 2.0[9], IMO MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3 [10], and ISO/IEC 27001 [11]), ensuring a
scalable and proactive approach to securing the next generation of cyber-ship operations.

1.1
Problem statement
The rapid digitalization of the maritime industry has significantly increased the complexity
and interconnectivity of cyber-ship systems, which integrate Information Technology (IT)
and Operational Technology (OT) networks. While this interconnectivity enables seamless
fleet management, real-time navigation updates, and optimized cargo handling, it also widens
the attack surface for cybercriminals. A single security breach can compromise multiple
shipboard subsystems, disrupt port operations, and have cascading effects on global supply
chains. Cyberattacks in maritime environments differ from traditional IT breaches as they
can impact physical operations, leading to navigation failures, communication blackouts,
and even the potential loss of human lives. The following are some of the most pressing
cybersecurity threats facing cyber-ship systems:
• GPS Spoofing and Navigation Manipulation: Navigation systems in cyber-ships
rely on Global Positioning System (GPS) signals for accurate positioning. However,
GPS spoofing techniques allow attackers to broadcast false signals, misleading the ves-
sel’s onboard navigation systems. This can result in route deviations, grounding, or
even collisions, endangering crew safety and cargo security. Some documented GPS
spoofing incidents include unauthorized vessel redirections in the Black Sea and Per-
sian Gulf, where ships unknowingly deviated from their planned courses due to signal
interference.
• Ransomware and Malware Attacks: Cybercriminals increasingly use ransomware
to target shipboard IT networks, encrypting critical data and demanding ransom pay-
ments. Such attacks can disable essential functions such as cargo tracking, engine mon-
itoring, and financial transactions, leading to massive operational disruptions. One of
the most notorious examples was the 017 NotPetya ransomware attack, which affected
Maersk’s global operations, causing estimated losses of $300 million. Additionally, mal-
ware infections have been used to infiltrate port control systems, delaying shipments
and causing supply chain bottlenecks.
• Remote System Hijacking via VSAT Exploits: Cyber-ships rely on Very Small
7
==PAGE:11==
Aperture Terminal (VSAT) satellite communication networks to exchange data with
ports, fleet management centers, and maritime authorities. However, vulnerabilities in
VSAT systems can allow hackers to intercept, manipulate, or block communications,
potentially leading to full remote hijacking of ship operations. Attackers can exploit
weak encryption in satellite transmissions to inject malicious commands, disabling
essential control functions such as autopilot, cargo cranes, and engine control units.
• Fleet-Wide Cyber Threats and Supply Chain Disruptions: Unlike conventional
vessels, cyber-ships operate within a highly interconnected fleet ecosystem, where ship-
board networks are linked to cloud-based fleet management systems. A single malware
infection can rapidly spread across multiple vessels, disrupting trade routes and caus-
ing significant economic losses. The 2018 COSCO Shipping cyberattack demonstrated
the potential scale of fleet-wide threats when hackers disabled communication systems
across multiple global shipping offices, forcing manual operations and delaying ship-
ments.
• E-navigation and ECDIS Exploitation: Modern vessels rely on Electronic Chart
Display and Information Systems (ECDIS) for automated navigation. However, these
systems are vulnerable to cyber intrusions that can manipulate displayed routes, disable
alarms, or cause navigation blackouts. A compromised ECDIS system can mislead the
ship’s crew, causing unintended maritime accidents.
• Insider Threats and Supply Chain Attacks: Cybersecurity breaches are not al-
ways caused by external attackersinsiders (e.g., crew members, contractors, or vendors)
can exploit privileged access to compromise shipboard networks. Insider threats may
involve deliberate sabotage, unauthorized software installations, or unintentional mal-
ware infections due to poor cybersecurity hygiene. Additionally, supply chain attacks
targeting third-party vendors supplying software updates or electronic components
have become a growing concern.
Challenges in Existing Maritime Cybersecurity Measures
Despite these evolving threats, the maritime industry still relies on centralized and reactive
cybersecurity measures, which are often inadequate in addressing real-time cyberattacks.

Blockchain Technology
Blockchain technology has emerged as one of the most transformative innovations of the
digital age[13], fundamentally changing the way transactions, data storage, and security are
managed.
Initially developed as the backbone of Bitcoin by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2008,
blockchain has since evolved beyond cryptocurrency into a versatile tool with applications
spanning finance, supply chain management, cybersecurity, healthcare, and maritime trade.
At its core, blockchain is a distributed, immutable ledger that records transactions se-
curely across a decentralized network[14]. Unlike traditional systems that rely on centralized
control, blockchain operates on a peer-to-peer (P2P) architecture, ensuring transparency,
security, and resistance to manipulation[15].
The technology is particularly valuable in
environments where trust between parties is critical but difficult to establish.
By elimi-
nating intermediaries and introducing cryptographic security measures, blockchain enables
tamper-proof record-keeping and automated contract execution, making it a game-changer
for industries dealing with high-value transactions, regulatory compliance, and cybersecurity
concerns.
2.1
Blockchain Categorization
Blockchain networks can be classified based on their access control model, which determines
who can participate in transaction validation and who has the authority to append new
blocks to the ledger. This classification directly impacts the security, efficiency, and gover-
nance of a blockchain system. Broadly, blockchain networks fall into two primary categories:
permissionless (public) blockchains and permissioned (private/consortium) blockchains.
A permissionless blockchain is an open and decentralized network where anyone can join,
validate transactions, and propose new blocks without requiring approval from a central
entity.
These blockchains operate similarly to the public internet, allowing unrestricted
participation and data transparency. On the other hand, a permissioned blockchain functions
like a secured corporate network (intranet), where only authorized entities can validate
transactions and maintain the ledger. This model is commonly used in regulated industries
such as finance, supply chain management, and maritime cybersecurity, where controlled
access and compliance with security standards are essential.

2.2
Blockchain Components
Blockchain technology, at its core, integrates several well-established principles from cryp-
tography, distributed computing, and record-keeping systems. It combines cryptographic
primitives (such as hash functions, digital signatures, and asymmetric-key cryptography)
with the concept of an append-only ledger, ensuring that once recorded, data cannot be
altered or removed. Understanding these fundamental components is essential to compre-
hending how blockchain achieves security, decentralization, and trust.
This section explores the core elements of blockchain, including cryptographic hash
functions, transactions, asymmetric-key cryptography, addresses, ledgers, blocks, and block
chaining mechanisms.
2.2.1
Cryptographic Hash Functions
Cryptographic hash functions play a vital role in blockchain security, serving as a one-way
transformation that converts an arbitrary input into a fixed-length output, known as a hash
digest. A cryptographic hash function H(x) satisfies the following key properties:
1. Preimage Resistance: Given an output y, it is computationally infeasible to find an
input x such that:
H(x) = y.
(2.8)
2. Second Preimage Resistance: Given an input x1, it is computationally infeasible
to find another input x2 such that:
H(x1) = H(x2).
(2.9)
3. Collision Resistance: It is computationally infeasible to find any two distinct inputs
x1, x2 such that:
H(x1) = H(x2).
(2.10)
A widely used cryptographic hash function in blockchain technology is SHA-256 , which
generates a 256-bit digest, commonly represented as a 64-character hexadecimal string:
H(x) = SHA-256(x).
(2.11)
Since the output space of SHA-256 contains 2256 possible values, the probability of a
collision is extremely low, making it an effective tool for securing blockchain transactions
and blocks.
Cryptographic Nonce
A nonce (Number Used Once) is a unique number appended to the input of a hash function
to obtain a new hash value:
H(data + nonce) = digest.
(2.12)
This technique is extensively used in Proof of Work (PoW) consensus mechanisms, where
miners iteratively change the nonce value until a valid hash is found that meets a predefined
difficulty target.

2.2.2
Transactions
A transaction represents an operation recorded on the blockchain, such as the transfer of
digital assets or the execution of a smart contract. Each transaction is structured as follows:
• Inputs: References to previous unspent transactions used as the source of funds.
• Outputs: Specifies the recipients and the amount of digital assets being transferred.
• Digital Signature: Verifies the authenticity of the sender.
• Transaction Hash: A cryptographic fingerprint generated using SHA-256:
Transaction ID = H(Inputs||Outputs||Signature).
(2.13)
Transactions in blockchain networks undergo validation by network nodes to ensure com-
pliance with protocol rules before being appended to a block.
2.2.3
Asymmetric-Key Cryptography
Blockchain technology employs asymmetric-key cryptography, also known as public-key cryp-
tography, to secure transactions and authenticate participants. Each user possesses:
• A private key kpriv, known only to the owner.
• A public key kpub, derived from the private key but publicly shared.
The relationship between the two keys is defined as:
kpub = f(kpriv),
(2.14)
where f is a mathematical function such that it is infeasible to compute kpriv given kpub.
A commonly used algorithm is the Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA),
which ensures secure authentication in blockchain transactions.
2.2.4
Addresses and Address Derivation
Blockchain addresses serve as unique identifiers for users and are derived from their public
keys using cryptographic hash functions. The process is as follows:
Address = H(kpub).
(2.15)
This derivation enhances security by ensuring that the public key is not directly exposed
during transactions.

A ledger in a blockchain network is an immutable record of transactions, organized into
blocks. Traditional centralized ledgers rely on a trusted third party, while blockchain utilizes
a decentralized ledger, maintained through distributed consensus.
Mathematically, a blockchain ledger can be represented as an ordered sequence of blocks:
L = {B1, B2, ..., Bn}.
(2.16)
2.2.6
Blocks and Block Structure
Each block in the blockchain contains a set of transactions and metadata for ensuring security
and integrity. A block is structured as follows:
• Block Header: Contains metadata including the previous block’s hash.
• Block Data: Includes transactions validated for inclusion.
• Nonce: A variable used in PoW for hash computation.
A block Bi is linked to its predecessor by including the hash of the previous block Bi−1:
Bi = H(Bi−1, Ti, N).
(2.17)
2.2.7
Chaining Blocks Together
The integrity of the blockchain is maintained by chaining blocks together using cryptographic
hashes. If any block’s content is altered, it changes the hash of that block, causing a cascading
effect on all subsequent blocks. The blockchain is structured as follows:
H(Bi) = H(Bi−1, Ti, N).
(2.18)
Since modifying a past block requires recomputing the hash of every subsequent block,
the blockchain remains tamper-resistant.

Consensus Models
A key aspect of blockchain technology is determining which participant publishes the next
block.
This decision is governed by consensus mechanisms, which ensure that all nodes
in the network agree on a single, consistent state of the blockchain. Different consensus
models exist depending on whether the blockchain is permissionless (open participation) or
permissioned (restricted participation).
In permissionless networks, multiple nodes compete to append the next block, usually
for financial rewards such as cryptocurrency incentives or transaction fees.
In contrast,
permissioned networks often rely on a predefined, trust-based mechanism to assign publishing
rights. The primary goal of consensus models is to establish a trustless and decentralized
agreement, even among mutually distrusting participants.

• Ti represents the transaction data in the current block.
• N is a random nonce adjusted iteratively.
• Target is a dynamically adjusted difficulty level.

Smart Contracts
The concept of smart contracts dates back to 1994 when Nick Szabo defined them as “a
computerized transaction protocol that executes the terms of a contract” [16]. The primary
objectives of smart contracts include:
• Enforcing contract terms without intermediaries.
• Reducing execution costs and improving efficiency.
• Ensuring deterministic and trustless execution.
Smart contracts extend blockchain technology by embedding self-executing code within
a blockchain network, enabling decentralized automation of predefined conditions.
2.4.1
Structure of a Smart Contract
A smart contract consists of:
• Code: Defines functions and conditions for execution.
• State: Stores variables and persistent data.
• Transactions: Invoke functions and modify state.
The contract is deployed on the blockchain via cryptographically signed transactions.
Each node in the network executes the contract’s logic to maintain consistency across the
distributed ledger.

3.1
Maritime Cybersecurity Overview
The maritime industry is experiencing a transformative shift through the adoption of mod-
ern technologies designed to improve efficiency and navigation. From IoT-enabled devices
to autonomous ships and automated port systems, these advancements have revolutionized
global maritime operations. However, alongside these innovations comes a growing array
of cybersecurity risks. Cyber threats such as ransomware, phishing, denial-of-service (DoS)
attacks, and GPS spoofing now pose significant challenges, with the potential to disrupt
global trade, cause financial losses, and compromise the safety of vessels, cargo, and crew.
The maritime sector’s reliance on legacy systems, many of which were not designed with
cybersecurity in mind, leaves critical infrastructure highly vulnerable. These systems often
lack the robustness needed to counter sophisticated cyberattacks, creating significant secu-
rity gaps. Moreover, outdated or inadequate security practices fail to keep pace with the
rapidly evolving threat landscape, making it difficult for maritime organizations to protect
themselves against increasingly complex attacks.
Human factors further exacerbate these challenges. Poor communication, insufficient cyber-
25
==PAGE:29==
security training, and operational fatigue increase the likelihood of mistakes, such as falling
victim to phishing scams or misconfiguring critical systems. Many maritime organizations
prioritize operational efficiency over long-term cybersecurity measures, treating cybersecu-
rity as an afterthought rather than a fundamental requirement. Additionally, the complexity
of emerging technologies, such as autonomous ships, AI-driven logistics, and interconnected
IoT devices, introduces new vulnerabilities and expands the attack surface for cybercrimi-
nals.
While regulatory frameworks such as the IMO’s International Ship and Port Facility Se-
curity (ISPS) Code and the IACS Unified Requirements (E26 and E27) provide essential
security guidelines, they often fail to keep up with the rapidly evolving nature of cyber
threats. Smaller operators, in particular, face difficulties understanding and implementing
these regulations due to limited resources and expertise, further widening the cybersecurity
gap across the industry.

3.2.1
Vessel Security:
Vessel security is fundamental to maritime cybersecurity, impacting all aspects of the mar-
itime network. Crew members, often the first to detect security threats, play a key role in
maintaining security. Well-trained crews who are familiar with protocols can significantly
enhance a vessel’s security, but human factors, such as neglecting procedures, can introduce
vulnerabilities [17]. Vessel security is governed by international laws, conventions, and in-
dustry standards, which establish requirements for ships, ports, and government agencies.
While many incidents stem from failing to follow regulations [18], compliance should be seen
27
==PAGE:31==
as a baseline rather than the goal. Upon entering a port, vessels must adhere to the port’s se-
curity procedures. With the increasing digitization of port operations, including automated
systems for cargo handling and vessel traffic management, new cybersecurity risks emerge
[19]. Risk assessment methodologies help identify potential threats and develop security
measures and contingency plans, safeguarding human life and health [20]. Vessel operations,
influenced by time pressure and the ”just-in-time” philosophy, may lead to hasty security
decisions, prioritizing temporary fixes over long-term solutions [21]. Given the complex inte-
gration of systems from various industries, producers must be aware of supply chain-related
hazards[22]. This study focuses on the security of the Integrated Bridge System (IBS), a
potential entry point for attacks. The IBS is crucial for controlling vessel operations, relying
on various integrated systems such as navigation, communication, and sensors. While inte-
gration improves efficiency, it also introduces risks: a breach in one system could compromise
the entire network [23].
3.2.2
Port Security
The global port security market is projected to grow significantly, reaching $173.59 billion
by 2031, driven by the need for advanced security solutions and recognition of port security
as vital to global trade infrastructure. Port security encompasses policies, practices, and
systems that protect operations, infrastructure, and surrounding areas from threats like
theft, terrorism, and smuggling [24]. As cyber-physical threats increase, the future of port
security will likely focus on integrating cyber and physical measures [25]. Effective port
security requires combining technological, procedural, and physical defenses to address both
traditional and emerging threats. Studies have explored port infrastructure risk in detail
[26][27], emphasizing the importance of security management in mitigating various risks.
Key elements of port security include emergency plans, surveillance, perimeter security,
access control, and cargo screening. Access control systems use biometric technologies and
sophisticated credentials, while perimeter security employs barriers and intrusion detection
to safeguard port facilities .
3.2.3
Supply chain
Supply chain security involves laws, practices, and technologies designed to protect goods,
information, and assets from threats like theft, terrorism, and natural disasters [28]. The
maritime supply chain is complex, connecting ports across countries and continents, involv-
ing stakeholders like freight forwarders, shippers, and customs officials. Challenges such as
worker strikes, natural disasters, equipment failures, and cyberattacks can significantly im-
pact security and operations [29]. Cybersecurity threats, like attacks on logistics systems or
data breaches, are major concerns [30]. Physical threats like piracy, cargo theft, and smug-
gling remain unresolved [28]. As climate change increases vulnerability, ports and shipping
routes face more catastrophic weather events [31]. Operational risks, including equipment
failure and human error, further affect supply chain efficiency. Since over 80% of global
trade relies on shipping [32], any disruption would have far-reaching consequences. Supplier
security is also critical, particularly regarding equipment that supports various maritime
systems. Malicious actors can exploit vulnerabilities in these supply chains, potentially in-

==PAGE:32==
stalling harmful software or replacing original parts with counterfeit ones, increasing security
risks [28][29].
3.2.4
Risk Assessment Method
Risk assessment is crucial for identifying and reducing potential threats, preventing acci-
dents, minimizing financial losses, and adhering to regulations. This section highlights five
primary approaches: quantitative, qualitative, semi-quantitative, data-driven, and emergent
methods. Quantitative methods provide numerical estimates of risk probabilities and con-
sequences, including machine learning techniques like Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
networks [33] and probabilistic models such as Bayesian networks [34]. These methods are
useful for comparing risks and evaluating mitigation strategies but often require extensive
data and face challenges in estimating financial or reputational impacts [35]. Qualitative
methods focus on identifying risks without numerical quantification. Examples include Haz-
ard and Operability Analysis (HAZOP) [36] and Systems Theoretic Process Analysis (STPA)
[37], which are particularly valuable for complex systems or early-stage assessments. Semi-
quantitative methods combine qualitative insights with scoring systems, such as the Human
Factors Analysis and Classification System (HFACS) [38] and Failure Mode, Effects, and
Criticality Analysis (FMECA) [39]. These approaches strike a balance, offering detailed as-
sessments without the full complexity of quantitative models. Data-driven methods analyze
large datasets to uncover trends in maritime threats, such as using Geographic Information
Systems (GIS) for geospatial analysis [40]or association rules-based data mining techniques
[41]. Emerging tools like knowledge graphs [42] also show promise in integrating complex
data sources. Standardization efforts are advancing, with initiatives like IACS Unified Re-
quirement E22 [43], which structures failure impact assessments, and IACS Recommendation
171 [44], offering guidelines for systematic risk assessments. However, the maritime sector’s
complexity and limited expertise in cybersecurity remain significant challenges [45].
By
leveraging these methods and adhering to evolving standards, the maritime industry can
address its diverse risks more effectively.
3.2.5
Regulatory Frameworks/Laws
The IACS Unified Requirements E26 and E27 [46][47] are critical cybersecurity standards
designed specifically for the maritime industry. E26 focuses on ensuring the overall cyber
resilience of ships, while E27 addresses the cybersecurity of individual onboard systems and
equipment. These standards cover every stage of a vessel’s lifecycle, from design and con-
struction to operation and maintenance. By addressing key areas like network segmentation,
access controls, malware protection, and incident response, they provide practical guidance
for shipowners, manufacturers, and system integrators to reduce cybersecurity risks. The
International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code [48] is another cornerstone of
maritime security. It sets an international framework to detect and prevent security threats
affecting ships and port facilities, assigning clear responsibilities to governments, local au-
thorities, and industry players. Despite their importance, these regulations have limitations.
For smaller operators or those without cybersecurity expertise, the complexity of these stan-
dards can be overwhelming. Additionally, the fast pace of technological advancements often
29
==PAGE:33==
leaves regulations struggling to keep up. For example, the ISPS Code, originally created to
address terrorism threats, doesn’t fully account for modern cyber risks. Many shipping com-
panies meet only the minimum requirements, which can leave ships vulnerable to evolving
cyberattacks. These gaps highlight the need for continuously updated and more adaptable
regulatory frameworks to keep pace with the changing threat landscape
3.2.6
Human factors
Human factors play a major role in maritime incidents, accounting for a significant 65.8%
to 80% of them. These factors cover a range of elements, including cognitive aspects like
decision-making, situational awareness, and mental workload, as well as physiological factors
such as fatigue and stress [49]. Interpersonal issues, such as poor communication or coordi-
nation, also contribute, alongside the growing complexity of human-machine interactions due
to increased automation [50].Fatigue, a common problem in the maritime industry, affects
cognitive abilities, leading to slower decision-making and a reduced ability to respond to ur-
gent situations [51]. In addition, poor team communication or confusing interfaces between
humans and machines can cause mistakes that escalate into accidents [52]. At a broader
level, a weak safety culture that prioritizes operational efficiency over long-term safety can
put vessels at risk. On the other hand, a strong safety culture encourages proactive risk
management, making it easier to identify potential issues before they turn into accidents
[53]. A focus on human factors, across both individual and organizational levels, is key to
enhancing maritime cybersecurity and overall safety.
3.3
Emerging threats in maritime industry
The maritime industry is increasingly relying on digital technologies to streamline opera-
tions, enhance efficiency, and improve communication between vessels and ports.
While
these advancements have transformed maritime logistics and navigation, they have also in-
troduced serious cybersecurity risks. With more interconnected systems controlling critical
functions like navigation, cargo management, and communication, cyber threats have be-
come a growing concern. A security breach could disrupt trade routes, delay shipments,
compromise sensitive data, or even endanger crew safety. The industry’s heavy dependence
on cyber-physical systems, many of which were not originally designed with cybersecurity
in mind, makes them an easy target for cybercriminals. As threats continue to evolve, the
maritime sector must prioritize stronger security frameworks and proactive risk mitigation
strategies to safeguard operations from potentially devastating cyberattacks.
3.3.1
Malware Attacks
Malware, particularly ransomware, is one of the most prevalent threats in the maritime sec-
tor, targeting critical systems like navigation, cargo tracking, and communication networks.
These attacks encrypt essential data, halting operations until a ransom is paid, often re-
sulting in delays, financial losses, and safety risks. A notable example is the 2017 NotPetya
attack on Maersk, which disrupted global operations and caused losses estimated at hundreds
30
==PAGE:34==
of millions of dollars [54]. Malware can infiltrate systems through phishing emails, infected
USB devices, or unpatched vulnerabilities in older infrastructure. The industry’s reliance
on legacy systems and third-party software expands the attack surface, making vessels and
ports more susceptible [55]. As shown in 3.1a, malware, including ransomware, accounts for
46.5% of all maritime cyber incidents between 2017 and 2024, making it the most frequent
type of attack. The increasing frequency of cyberattacks in the maritime sector highlights
the urgency for robust cybersecurity defenses. A single malware infection can cause weeks
of disruption, massive financial losses, and reputational damage. As cybercriminals become
more sophisticated, maritime operators must remain one step ahead, implementing preven-
tative security measures to protect their operations from falling victim to the next major
cyberattack.
3.3.2
DoS and DDoS Attacks
DoS and DDoS attacks are significant threats in the maritime sector, designed to overwhelm
networks and disrupt critical systems like GPS, communication, and navigation. By flood-
ing systems with excessive traffic, these attacks can paralyze port operations or onboard
systems, causing delays, financial losses, and operational chaos [56]. DDoS attacks are par-
ticularly damaging, as they leverage botnets of compromised devices to generate massive
traffic surges, making them difficult to stop.
As shown in 3.1, DoS and DDoS attacks
make up 26.7% of all reported maritime incidents between 2017 and 2024, highlighting their
widespread impact. Vulnerable legacy systems and the growing adoption of IoT devices on-
board ships and in ports expand the attack surface, further increasing risks. Mitigating these
threats requires multi-layered defenses, including firewalls, intrusion prevention systems, and
cloud-based DDoS mitigation services. Regular system updates, vulnerability assessments,
and advanced traffic monitoring tools are also critical. With cyberattacks growing in scale
and sophistication, the maritime industry must remain proactive to ensure the safety and
resilience of its operations.
3.3.3
Phishing and Social Engineering Attacks
Phishing and social engineering attacks exploit human vulnerabilities within maritime orga-
nizations to gain unauthorized access to networks or sensitive information. Cybercriminals
use fraudulent emails, messages, or websites to deceive employees into revealing login creden-
tials or downloading malicious software. These attacks bypass technical defenses by targeting
individuals, making them particularly difficult to detect and prevent[57]. In the maritime
context, social engineering can have severe consequences, such as unauthorized access to
ship control systems, cargo manifests, or financial transactions. These breaches can disrupt
operations, compromise sensitive data, and expose organizations to significant financial and
reputational losses.
3.3.4
GPS spoofing
GPS spoofing and jamming are significant threats to the maritime industry, given the sector’s
heavy reliance on satellite-based navigation systems for accurate positioning and routing.
31
==PAGE:35==
Spoofing occurs when attackers transmit fake GPS signals, tricking a ship’s navigation sys-
tem into following an incorrect route. In contrast, jamming disrupts GPS signals altogether,
leaving vessels unable to receive accurate location data [58]. These attacks can have severe
consequences, such as hijacking vessels, rerouting them into dangerous waters, or even caus-
ing collisions. For example, a spoofing incident could manipulate navigation systems to lead
ships into pirate-controlled waters, while jamming could incapacitate vital systems during
critical maneuvers. Given the reliance on GPS for nearly all aspects of modern maritime
navigation, these threats pose serious risks to vessel safety and operational integrity.
3.3.5
Intrusion Attacks
Intrusion attacks, targeting unauthorized access to critical maritime systems, account for
8.8% of reported incidents between 2017 and 2024, according to the data. These attacks
often exploit vulnerabilities in legacy systems, insecure remote access points, or unpatched
software, compromising sensitive data and operational processes. In the maritime sector,
intrusion attempts can disrupt navigation systems, expose cargo manifests, or tamper with
financial transactions, creating significant safety and operational risks.
3.4
Major Cyber attacks in cybership
The maritime industry faces unique and evolving cyber threats. Cybercriminals have been
targeting the maritime sector, which includes ports, ships, shipping firms, and maritime
authorities, more and more in recent years. The maritime industry is becoming more and
more susceptible to sophisticated cyberattacks, thus recent occurrences need to be closely
examined. This section therefore concentrates on cyberattacks that have happened in the
last ten years, specifically from 2014 to 2024. A thorough analysis has been conducted on the
twenty chosen maritime events. Our goal is to contribute to a better understanding of the
changing cyber threat landscape by exploring particular categories of contributing elements
that may have been missed or insufficiently addressed in existing literature.
GPS Jamming in South Korea: [59]: In 2016, South Korea experienced a series of
GPS jamming attacks that affected hundreds of ship, causing significant disruptions to nav-
igation. The attacks, believed to have originated from hackers in North Korea, highlighted
the vulnerability of GPS systems.
Navigation System Attack [60]: In February 2017, hackers seized control of a German-
owned container vessel’s navigation systems for 10 hours, rendering the captain unable to
maneuver. The vessel’s IT system was compromised, and onboard experts had to intervene
to restore control. Industry sources suggest the attack was an attempt by pirates to divert
the ship for boarding and ransom.
GPS Spoofing in the Black Sea [61]: In 2017, alarm bells rang out across the Black Sea
when over 20 vessels reported their GPS positions as being inland at an airport, reported
by U.S. Maritime Administration. This wasn’t an isolated glitch over 20 ships reporting

the same false location, coupled with their positions bouncing back and forth between the

Austal Data Breach [62]: In mid-October 2018, Austal, Australia’s shipbuilder and de-
fence contractor, suffered a major data breach.
Attackers purchased stolen Austal login
credentials on the dark web and these credentials were used to gain access to Austal’s Aus-

GNSS Spoofing in Port of Shanghai [63]: In 2019, several incidents of GNSS (Global
Navigation Satellite System) interference were reported in Chinese coastal areas, including
the Port of Shanghai.
This interference can disrupt or falsify GNSS signals, potentially
affecting a vessel’s navigation and communication equipment. It has been proposed that
the Chinese government might be behind the GPS spoofing incidents, either as a security
measure to conceal oil terminals and important government facilities or to evade surveillance
of oil imports.
AIS Spoofing in Polish Waters [64]: In November 2020, the Automatic Identification
System (AIS) location data for the USS Roosevelt was spoofed. Threat actors, potentially
linked to Russia, manipulated the AIS data to show the vessel near the Russian enclave of
Kaliningrad, when it was actually elsewhere. The false AIS data could serve to create a
narrative portraying Russia as the victim of encroaching naval activity.
Attack on Voyager Worldwide [65]: In 2022, Voyager Worldwide, a leading maritime
technology company, fell victim to a cyber attack that resulted in the complete shutdown
of its navigation services and solutions. This major incident affected over 1,000 shipping
companies worldwide that rely on Voyager’s technology and services.
DP World Australia Attack [54]: In November 2023, DP World, a key player man-
aging 40of Australia’s maritime freight, announced a suspension of operations at its port
terminals in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, and Fremantle due to a cybersecurity breach.
Responding to the cyber threat, the company took immediate action by isolating its com-
puter systems from external networks, effectively shutting down operations. This resulted
in a standstill, with approximately 30,000 shipping containers being left stranded.
The increasing number of cyber-ship attacks highlights the growing vulnerabilities of
modern maritime systems. Incidents such as AIS and GPS spoofing, ransomware attacks,
and unauthorized system breaches demonstrate that cyber threats are no longer hypothet-
ical but have real-world implications for navigation safety, fleet security, and global trade
operations. These cases emphasize the urgent need for enhanced cybersecurity measures,
including blockchain-based security frameworks, AI-driven anomaly detection, and decen-
tralized threat intelligence sharing. As cyber threats evolve, maritime industries must adopt
proactive defenses to ensure the integrity, safety, and resilience of digital maritime operations.

3.5
Key Vulnerabilities in Cyberships: Academic In-
sights Through Cyber Attack Demonstrations:
Academic cyber attack demonstrations are usually conducted in simulated environments or
with custom-developed software designed to mimic maritime systems and networks. Some
academic research takes a theoretical approach, modeling maritime systems to identify poten-
tial attack vectors, even if these attacks have not been fully tested in large-scale simulations.
These demonstrations often target specific components of maritime systems3.2.
AIS Threats Overview: Balduzzi et al. [66] conducted an in-depth security assessment
of the Automatic Identification System (AIS) and classified its threats into three primary
categories: i) spoofing, ii) hijacking, and iii) availability disruption attacks. Kessler et al.
[67] expanded on this by providing a detailed analysis of potential cyber threats to AIS,
referencing earlier studies [68][69][70] that explored vulnerabilities in the Automatic Depen-
dent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) system, which serves a similar purpose in aviation by
enhancing situational awareness for aircraft. Additionally, Amro and Gkioulos [71] intro-
duced a novel cyber attack concept that leverages AIS as a covert channel for transmitting
command-and-control messages and deploying malware into maritime systems.
VDR Threats Overview: Harish et al. [72] performed a detailed multi-pronged attack
on a commercially available Voyage Data Recorder (VDR). Their approach included using a
USB Rubber Ducky for physical infiltration, leveraging the Metasploit framework to exploit
system vulnerabilities, simulating ransomware to demonstrate data encryption risks, and
employing Nmap to detect exploitable network weaknesses. Hopcraft et al. [73] underscored
significant cybersecurity gaps in current VDR systems and standards, highlighting their
susceptibility to ransomware attacks, malware infections, denial-of-service (DoS) attacks,
insider threats, and data breaches. S¨oner et al.[74] utilized failure modes and effects anal-
ysis (FMEA) to identify cyber vulnerabilities and assess potential attack scenarios against
VDRs. Their study concluded that the Data Acquisition Unit (DAU) is the most vulnerable
component, followed by remote access solutions and the bridge control panel. FMEA results
ranked false data injection, command injection, and viruses as the three most critical threats
to VDR security.
RADAR Threats Overview: Junior et al. [75] demonstrated how attackers can carry out
electronic attacks (EA) on ship radar systems by sending commands that create false radar
echoes in specific patterns. Wolsing et al. [76] developed a detailed taxonomy of potential
network-based cyber attacks on marine radar, supported by a simulation environment for
testing. Their taxonomy covers attacks like denial-of-service, image transformations (scal-
ing, rotation, translation), and manipulations such as adding, removing, or relocating radar
echoes. Longo et al. [77] showed how malware can exploit weaknesses in standard protocols
like NMEA and ASTERIX, which lack authentication and encryption, to autonomously and
covertly target radar systems. Longo et al. [78] also introduced a threat model combining
cyber attacks with false flag operations. These attacks alter radar data to mimic electronic
countermeasures (ECM) such as jamming or blip enhancement, making it appear as though
35
==PAGE:39==
the attack is coming from another vessel. This misdirection hides the true nature of the
attack, disrupts radar operations, and shifts blame to other entities while showcasing the
attacker’s capabilities.
VSAT Threats Overview: Santamarta [79] identified critical security vulnerabilities in
widely used satellite communications (SATCOM) terminals, presenting real-world attack
scenarios where these flaws could be exploited to leak sensitive military data, interfere with
distress communications, spoof navigation data, and disable safety systems. In the maritime
context, VSAT systems face significant risks, including data manipulation, spoofing, and
communication disruptions. Pavur et al. [80] highlighted severe security weaknesses in the
implementation of maritime VSAT networks. These vulnerabilities could enable attackers to
alter critical data, such as ECDIS navigational charts and AIS vessel tracking information,
potentially compromising ship safety. In some cases, attackers could even hijack active TCP
sessions and modify data in real time.
GNSS Threats Overview: Grant et al. [81] conducted practical experiments demonstrat-
ing the impact of GPS jamming on maritime navigation systems. Bhatti and Humphreys
[82] showcased how an attacker could take control of a maritime surface vessel by broadcast-
ing counterfeit civil GPS signals. Medina et al. [83] collaborated with the German Federal
Network Agency to execute a dedicated jamming measurement campaign on the Baltic Sea,
further validating the risks of GNSS disruptions.
ECDIS Threats Overview: Svilicic et al.[84] [85] [86][87][88] extensively studied the cy-

applying consistent vulnerability scanning methods across all three papers, each with a dis-
tinct focus.

underlying the ECDIS. Svilicic et al. [85] proposed a framework for assessing cyber risks

create an ideal environment for malware propagation.

Blockchain has gained significant traction in the maritime industry, primarily for enhancing

This section reviews key blockchain implementations in maritime operations and highlights
their limitations in addressing cybersecurity threats.
TradeLens: Enhancing Supply Chain Security and Transparency:
TradeLens,
a blockchain-based shipping platform developed by Maersk and IBM, aimed to digitize and
secure global trade transactions by leveraging Hyperledger Fabric. The platform facilitated
real-time data sharing between key maritime stakeholders, including shipping lines, ports,

clearance processes. Despite its innovations, TradeLens primarily focused on logistics and
operational efficiency, rather than addressing cybersecurity threats targeting shipboard IT
and OT systems. Additionally, the platform struggled with limited industry-wide adoption,
as many major shipping companies hesitated to join due to concerns over data sharing and
competitive disadvantages. Ultimately, TradeLens was discontinued in 2023, underscoring
the challenges of achieving blockchain adoption at an industry-wide scale.

ensures secure and tamper-proof ownership transfers of cargo using smart contracts [92]. By
digitizing shipping documents, CargoX eliminates manual paperwork delays, reducing fraud
risks and improving transaction speed in international trade. However, CargoX primarily
focuses on securing trade documentation rather than protecting shipboard IT and OT sys-
tems from cyber threats like ransomware, GPS spoofing, or unauthorized access.

enhance logistics security and operational efficiency. Through Naviporta, a decentralized
logistics platform, the port enables secure cargo tracking, automated digital transactions,

3.7
Overview of NIST Cybersecurity Framework
The National Institute of Standards and Technology Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF)
has become an essential guideline for strengthening cybersecurity across industries, includ-
ing maritime operations. Initially introduced in 2014 and later updated to version 1.1 in
2018, the framework was developed through collaboration between industry and govern-
ment to establish a flexible and adaptable approach to cybersecurity risk management [95].
NIST CSF is structured around five core functions—Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond,
and Recover—allowing organizations to assess and improve their cybersecurity resilience.
Recognizing its value, the United States Coast Guard (USCG) has actively advocated for
the adoption of NIST CSF in maritime operations, particularly in high-risk areas such as
Maritime Bulk Liquids Transfer (MBLT), Offshore Operations, and Passenger Vessel Oper-
ations [96]. NIST CSF 2.0: A New Approach to Cybersecurity Risk Management In 2023,
NIST released CSF 2.0, marking a significant evolution in cybersecurity risk management.
Unlike its predecessors, CSF 2.0 expands its scope beyond critical infrastructure, making it
universally applicable to all organizations, including those in the maritime sector [97]. The
most notable update is the introduction of a sixth core function, ”Govern,” alongside the
existing Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover functions. The Govern function
emphasizes the integration of cybersecurity into organizational governance, ensuring that
cyber risk management aligns with business priorities, regulatory requirements, and oper-
41
==PAGE:45==
ational strategies. Previously, some governance elements were included within the Identify
function, but in CSF 2.0, they are clearly distinguished as a separate category to highlight
the strategic role of cybersecurity leadership in decision-making. The introduction of en-
hanced resources, such as quick-start guides, industry-specific profiles, and extensive NIST
mappings, makes CSF 2.0 a valuable tool for maritime organizations seeking to strengthen
cyber resilience. By adopting the Govern function, maritime entities can better structure
their risk management frameworks, improve accountability, and enhance communication of
cybersecurity concerns [97]. Application of NIST CSF in Maritime Cybersecurity The Inter-
national Maritime Organization (IMO) has emphasized the importance of cybersecurity by
integrating best practices into the International Safety Management (ISM) Code, positioning
cyber risk at the same priority level as other safety risks [6]. The NIST CSF complements
these best practices, allowing port operators, shipowners, and maritime logistics providers to
develop robust, risk-based cybersecurity strategies that address both general cyber threats
and industry-specific vulnerabilities [98].
The International Association of Classification Societies (IACS) has taken further steps to
enhance cybersecurity in ship design and operation by introducing two Unified Require-
ments (UR E26 and UR E27). These requirements, which apply to new ships contracted
from January 1, 2024, focus on integrating cybersecurity into vessel design, construction,
and operational phases. UR E26: Ensures the secure integration of IT and OT systems,
emphasizing the ship’s collective cyber resilience. UR E27: Focuses on securing onboard
equipment, particularly addressing the role of third-party suppliers in maritime cybersecu-
rity [99]. These advancements mark a significant milestone in protecting maritime assets
from cyber threats, ensuring that cybersecurity is embedded into the entire lifecycle of a
vessel.Industry Adoption and Maritime-Specific Implementations To simplify and enhance
cybersecurity compliance, ABS Advanced Solutions has developed a specialized cybersecu-
rity solution for maritime facilities regulated by the USCG. This solution incorporates NIST
CSF’s core functions to streamline cyber risk management, incident response planning, and
compliance with Facility Security Plans (FSPs). It also assists Facility Security Officers, IT,
and OT personnel in effectively implementing cybersecurity controls [100].
Despite the growing adoption of NIST CSF 2.0, there remains a lack of extensive literature
on its maritime applications due to its recent release. However, the framework’s integra-
tion with IMO guidelines, IACS requirements, and ABS cybersecurity solutions signals a
strategic shift toward a more structured and resilient cybersecurity posture in the maritime
sector. These efforts collectively contribute to establishing a more secure digital maritime
environment and pave the way for widespread industry adoption of NIST CSF best practices.
3.8
Overview of ISO 27001
ISO/IEC 27001, the international standard for Information Security Management Systems
(ISMS), provides organizations with a structured framework to protect critical information
assets. The standard defines a comprehensive approach for managing cybersecurity risks,
ensuring that organizations establish security controls, policies, and procedures that align
with international best practices.
Supported by the broader ISO 27000 series, ISO/IEC
27001 is widely adopted across various industries, including the maritime sector, where secure
42
==PAGE:46==
shipboard networks, port IT systems, and digital trade platforms are critical for operational
continuity[101][102]. At the heart of ISO/IEC 27001 lies the Confidentiality, Integrity, and
Availability (CIA) triad, which ensures that:
• Confidentiality- Only authorized personnel can access sensitive maritime data, such
as ship navigation logs, cargo tracking systems, and port access credentials.
• Integrity – Data remains accurate, consistent, and unaltered, preventing cybercrim-
inals from modifying or corrupting essential operational data.
• Availability – Ensures that critical information and systems remain accessible when
needed, preventing operational downtime due to cyber incidents.
The integration of ISO/IEC 27001 in maritime cybersecurity extends beyond technical safe-
guards and includes organizational security measures.
The technical perspective focuses
on firewalls, encryption, and multi-factor authentication (MFA) to prevent unauthorized
access. The social aspect acknowledges that human error is a major cybersecurity vulner-
ability, emphasizing the importance of training maritime personnel to recognize phishing,
insider threats, and malware risks. The sociotechnical approach integrates both technology
and human behavior, ensuring that security measures are practical and adaptable to real-
world maritime operations [103].
A defining strength of ISO/IEC 27001 is its risk-based approach to cybersecurity.
The
framework requires organizations to systematically identify, assess, and mitigate security
risks, ensuring that potential threats are addressed before they escalate into major inci-
dents. In the maritime industry, this means conducting regular security audits on shipboard
IT/OT systems, including navigation platforms, electronic chart display systems, and cargo
tracking software. Organizations are also required to document their cybersecurity policies,
ensuring that they evolve in response to new and emerging threats. Additionally, ISO 27001
compliance aligns maritime companies with global data protection laws, such as the General
Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), helping them avoid legal penalties [104].
ISO/IEC 27001 provides four major business benefits, making it a cornerstone of maritime
cybersecurity strategies[105]:
• Regulatory Compliance:Maritime cybersecurity regulations, including IMO’s Cyber
Risk Management Guidelines, emphasize ISO 27001 as a best practice for security
governance.Ensures protection against fines and penalties for failing to secure critical
maritime systems [25][106].
• Competitive Advantage: Achieving ISO 27001 certification enhances a company’s cred-
ibility, demonstrating a strong commitment to cybersecurity best practices.
Cer-

operations[102].
• Cost Savings & Risk Reduction: Proactive security measures prevent financial losses
from cyber incidents, including data breaches, ransomware attacks, and operational
disruptions.Helps reduce recovery costs and downtime associated with cyber threats
[106].

Despite its advantages, implementing ISO/IEC 27001 presents several challenges[106][107].
The certification process requires detailed security assessments and audits, making it resource-

ing, requiring maritime firms to regularly update their risk management policies. Lastly,

training crew members and port staff on evolving cyber risks.
3.9
Maritime security Standards
While maritime security training exists, cybersecurity is often not systematically addressed,
despite the growing threats in the industry. Maritime standardization frameworks are tradi-
tionally classification-based, ensuring that products and solutions are categorized according
to function and necessity. However, cybersecurity must become an integral component of
maritime operations, embedded within planning, implementation, and lifecycle management.
Rather than being treated as an isolated IT concern, cybersecurity should be integrated into
ship design, port operations, and digital trade infrastructures to ensure resilient and secure
maritime systems [108].
A number of international standards have been introduced to enhance maritime cyberse-
curity, including ISO/IEC 27001:2022, ISO 22301:2019, and NIST guidelines. These frame-
works provide structured methodologies for managing cyber risks, ensuring compliance, and
improving overall security in maritime operations. One of the most significant additions is
ISO 23806:2022, titled “Ships and Maritime Technology – Cyber Safety”. This standard
establishes comprehensive cybersecurity requirements that address vessel cyber risks across
their entire lifecycle. It mandates the identification and assessment of cyber threats and
integrates preventive measures within a company’s Safety Management System (SMS). Fur-
thermore, it aligns with MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3, which provides Guidelines for Maritime Cyber
Risk Management, reinforcing best practices for mitigating cyber threats within shipping
companies and port operations [109].
Beyond ISO and NIST standards, independent maritime authorities and cybersecurity
organizations have also developed best-practice guidelines. Several white papers and industry
reports have been published to help standardize cybersecurity practices across maritime
operations. For instance, The Institute of Engineering and Technology (IET) has released
two notable cybersecurity frameworks:
• Code of Practice: Cyber Security for Ports and Port Systems - This provides
a structured approach to securing critical port infrastructures, ensuring the resilience
of cargo handling, communication networks, and IT management systems.

• Code of Practice: Cyber Security for Ships – This guideline focuses on shipboard
cybersecurity, detailing measures for protecting onboard digital systems, navigation

These frameworks serve as valuable industry standards, helping maritime organizations
strengthen cybersecurity governance and develop effective risk mitigation strategies.
As
cyber threats continue to evolve, the integration of ISO, NIST, and maritime-specific cyber-

of digital maritime infrastructures [109].

nent, and cryptographically verified, making regulatory compliance more efficient and
trustworthy.
Both NIST CSF and ISO/IEC 27001 provide foundational security practices, but they lack
built-in mechanisms for trust and integrity verification. Blockchain technology addresses
these gaps by:
• Providing Immutable Security Logs – Unlike traditional logging systems, blockchain
ensures that cyber incidents, risk assessments, and compliance records cannot be al-
tered or deleted.

The Ship Model
Ships are highly complex and interconnected structures, composed of multiple subsystems
that work together to ensure operational safety, efficiency, and security. The rapid digiti-
zation of the maritime industry has introduced numerous advantages, such as automated
navigation, real-time monitoring, and efficient cargo handling. However, this digital trans-
formation has also significantly increased the attack surface of modern ships, making them
vulnerable to cyber threats.
Understanding the architecture of a ship is fundamental to analyzing its cybersecurity risks.
A ship’s infrastructure consists of core systems, navigation and bridge control, communica-
tion networks, propulsion and power management, cargo handling mechanisms, safety and
monitoring systems, and administrative and crew support functions. Each of these systems
is susceptible to cyber intrusions, hardware malfunctions, and data manipulation.
This chapter presents a structured model of a ship by identifying its key components and
explaining their operational significance. Figure 3.1 offers a visual representation of a ship’s
core systems and how they interconnect.
4.0.1
Core Infrastructure Systems
The core infrastructure of a ship serves as the foundation for all onboard operations. It
includes electrical distribution, cabling, networking, and essential communication systems
that support other functional units. Since most ship systems rely on this infrastructure, any
attack or malfunction at this level can have cascading effects across the entire vessel.
Key Components:
• Cabling and Network Infrastructure: The ship’s internal wiring connects all major
control systems, ensuring seamless data exchange between navigation, safety, and en-
gine control units. It includes both fiber-optic and copper-based cabling, making it
vulnerable to signal interference, hacking, and hardware failures.
• Emergency Switchboard: Ensures that critical electrical systems, such as navigation
lights, power management, and emergency alarms, remain functional in the event of a
main power failure.

==PAGE:52==
Figure 4.1: Structure of ship[110]
• Power Management Systems (PMS): Responsible for distributing power efficiently be-
tween different subsystems.
A compromised PMS can lead to power shortages or
failures in critical ship operations.
• Main Switchboard: A centralized panel that manages the ship’s overall power supply,
acting as a bridge between power generators and ship systems.
• Signal Light Column: Provides visual signals for navigation and alerts, which are
crucial for collision avoidance and communication with nearby vessels.
A cyberattack targeting core infrastructure could disable power, disrupt communications,
and even render navigational tools inoperable, significantly increasing the risk of maritime
disasters.
4.0.2
Navigation and Bridge Systems
The bridge system is the central control unit of the ship, where officers monitor, navigate,
and manage ship operations. The digitization of bridge systems has significantly improved
maritime efficiency, but it has also made ships more vulnerable to cyber threats, GPS spoof-
ing, and unauthorized remote access.
Key Components:
• Bridge Control Console: Houses all primary control interfaces for ship operations,
including steering, engine controls, and autopilot functions. Any cyberattack on this
system could result in the loss of navigational control.
• Nautical Decision Support System: A software-based system that analyzes real-time
data to assist in decision-making, such as route optimization and weather forecasting.
49
==PAGE:53==
• Global Maritime Distress and Safety System (GMDSS): A radio-based emergency com-
munication system that enables ships to send distress signals during emergencies. If
compromised, ships may fail to send SOS alerts.
• Automatic Identification System (AIS): Used to broadcast a ship’s position, speed, and
direction to nearby vessels and ports. However, AIS can be manipulated by hackers,
leading to false tracking data or even the disappearance of ships from radar.
• Electronic Chart Display and Information System (ECDIS): A digital navigation sys-
tem that replaces traditional paper charts. Vulnerabilities in ECDIS could lead to false
navigational data, causing incorrect routing or collisions.
• Voyage Data Recorder (VDR): The maritime equivalent of a ”black box”, which records
all key navigation and communication data. Attackers could attempt to erase or alter
data to cover their tracks after a cyberattack.
Radar and Sonar Equipment: Used to detect obstacles, other vessels, and underwater threats.
If hacked, attackers could manipulate radar signals, rendering objects invisible to the crew.
Attacks on navigation and bridge systems can mislead the crew, disrupt safe passage, and
lead to vessel hijacking or accidents.
4.0.3
Communication Systems
Ships rely on multiple internal and external communication networks to stay connected
with ports, satellites, and operational headquarters.
A secure communication system is
vital for coordinating logistics, monitoring real-time vessel performance, and responding to
emergencies.
Key Components:
• Satellite Communication Systems (SATCOM): Ensures high-speed data transmission
via Iridium, Inmarsat, VSAT, and Globalstar. Cybercriminals can exploit SATCOM
to intercept communications, manipulate GPS data, or inject malware.
• Internal Communication Network: Used for crew-to-crew communication, integrating
radio, VoIP, and onboard email systems. Unsecured networks can be hacked to spread
misinformation or disable emergency alerts.
• Fleet Management System: Allows shipping companies to track and manage fleet
movements remotely. Cyber intrusions could alter vessel routes, disable tracking, or
delay shipments.
Communication systems are particularly vulnerable to signal jamming, eavesdropping, and
network hacking, which can sever communication links between the ship and shore control.

4.0.4
Propulsion, Machinery, and Power Control
A ship’s propulsion system is essential for movement and maneuverability. Cyberattacks
targeting propulsion systems can disable engines, tamper with fuel supply, or manipulate

7.1
Mathematical and Cryptographic Validation
Ensuring the security and integrity of blockchain technology is critical for its adoption in
maritime cybersecurity. This section provides a mathematical foundation for blockchain’s
immutability, cryptographic security, and Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT), proving that
the framework is theoretically robust.
7.1.1
Blockchain Immutability and Hashing Integrity
Blockchain immutability is guaranteed through cryptographic hashing. Each block contains
the hash of its predecessor, ensuring that any modification to a previous block would alter its
hash and invalidate the entire chain. This mechanism is defined mathematically as follows:
• Let Bi represent the ith block in the blockchain.
• The hash of block Bi is computed as:
H(Bi) = H(Ti||Pi||H(Bi−1))
(7.1)
where:
– Ti represents the transactions within block i.
– Pi denotes other block metadata (timestamp, nonce, etc.).
– H(Bi−1) is the hash of the previous block.
Any attempt to modify a historical block Bk (k < i) results in a completely different
H(Bk), invalidating all subsequent hashes, thus preventing unauthorized alterations.
84
==PAGE:88==
7.1.2
Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT) for Security
Blockchain consensus mechanisms ensure that even if some nodes act maliciously, the network
remains secure.
This property is maintained through Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT),
which ensures that consensus can be reached despite up to f faulty nodes in a network of n
nodes, provided:
n ≥3f + 1
(7.2)
This means that at least two-thirds of the nodes must be honest to maintain blockchain
integrity. The framework leverages a Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (PBFT) consensus
mechanism, which operates as follows:
1. Pre-prepare Phase: A leader node proposes a new block.
2. Prepare Phase: All nodes validate the proposal and share their agreement.
3. Commit Phase: If at least 2f +1 nodes approve, the block is appended to the chain.
4. Finalization: The block is considered confirmed and immutable.
PBFT ensures that even if malicious nodes attempt to manipulate the blockchain, they
cannot influence consensus unless they form a majority, which is mathematically prevented

8.3
Challenges and Limitations
While the Blockchain-Enabled Cybersecurity Risk Assessment Framework presents a trans-
formative approach to maritime cybersecurity, its practical implementation poses several
challenges that must be addressed for widespread adoption. These challenges stem from
technical, regulatory, operational, and economic constraints, which, if not properly miti-
gated, could hinder the effectiveness of blockchain-based security frameworks in maritime
operations. Below, the key challenges and limitations are outlined, along with potential
strategies to address them.
8.3.1
Integration with Legacy Systems
The maritime industry operates with highly heterogeneous and often outdated IT infrastruc-
ture, making the integration of blockchain and AI-driven security models complex. Many
vessels still rely on traditional centralized database systems and legacy navigation and risk
management platforms that were not designed to accommodate decentralized technologies.
Retrofitting these systems with blockchain-based security protocols requires significant tech-
nical adaptation and phased implementation strategies.
Potential Solution: The adoption of hybrid blockchain models that work alongside
existing security infrastructures can facilitate gradual integration. Additionally, API-based
blockchain interoperability layers can enable legacy systems to communicate securely with
blockchain networks.
126
==PAGE:130==
8.3.2
Scalability and Performance Constraints
The maritime sector generates large volumes of real-time data, including AIS transmissions,
radar logs, cargo tracking information, and cybersecurity incident reports. Storing and pro-
cessing this data on a blockchain network presents scalability challenges, particularly in
high-traffic maritime environments where performance efficiency is critical. Moreover, con-
sensus mechanisms such as Proof of Work (PoW) or Proof of Stake (PoS) require significant
computational resources, which may not be practical for vessels with limited processing
power.
Potential Solution: Implementing off-chain storage solutions and lightweight consen-
sus algorithms such as Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (PBFT) or Delegated Proof of
Stake (DPoS) can enhance blockchain scalability without overloading shipboard computing
resources.
8.3.3
Regulatory and Compliance Barriers
Although blockchain aligns with global cybersecurity frameworks such as IMO MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3,
NIST CSF 2.0, and ISO 27001, its regulatory acceptance in maritime security remains un-
certain. The decentralized nature of blockchain creates challenges in governance, legal ac-
countability, and data jurisdiction, particularly when ships operate across multiple inter-
national regulatory zones. Additionally, smart contracts, while automating cybersecurity
enforcement, lack clear legal recognition in many jurisdictions, raising concerns about their
enforceability in the event of cyber incidents.
Potential Solution: Industry-wide standardization efforts must be undertaken to align
blockchain-based cybersecurity solutions with maritime regulations and compliance frame-
works.
Collaborative efforts between IMO, national maritime authorities, cybersecurity
agencies, and blockchain consortia will be critical in establishing guidelines for decentral-
ized maritime security.
8.3.4
Computational and Energy Constraints
Blockchain security frameworks, especially those integrating cryptographic validation and
AI-driven anomaly detection, require high computational power. Given the limited onboard
energy resources on maritime vessels, deploying a resource-intensive blockchain network for
cybersecurity could lead to operational inefficiencies.
Potential Solution: Implementing low-energy consensus models such as Proof of Au-
thority (PoA) or Hybrid Blockchain Architectures can ensure efficient energy utilization
while maintaining strong cybersecurity protocols.
8.3.5
Data Privacy and Confidentiality Concerns
While blockchain enhances data integrity and transparency, data privacy remains a major
concern in maritime operations. Sensitive ship data, such as route plans, cargo manifests, and
crew information, may be stored on a blockchain network. However, public or semi-public
127
==PAGE:131==
blockchain architectures could expose this information to unauthorized access or industrial
espionage.
Potential Solution: Utilizing permissioned blockchain architectures with zero-knowledge
proofs and multi-layered encryption can protect sensitive data while maintaining the integrity
of cybersecurity logs. Additionally, role-based access controls within the blockchain network
can ensure that only authorized entities can retrieve confidential information.
8.3.6
Internet Connectivity and Network Reliability
Maritime vessels frequently operate in remote oceanic regions where internet connectivity
is unreliable or intermittent. Many blockchain-based security functions, such as real-time
threat intelligence sharing and data synchronization, require continuous network availability.
Limited connectivity could delay threat alerts and disrupt real-time cybersecurity enforce-
ment mechanisms.
Potential Solution: Implementing offline-capable blockchain solutions, such as edge
computing nodes and satellite-based blockchain synchronization, can mitigate reliance on
continuous internet access. Ships can store and verify cybersecurity events locally and syn-
chronize with the global blockchain network once connectivity is restored.
8.3.7
Industry Adoption and Stakeholder Trust
For blockchain-based cybersecurity frameworks to succeed, adoption must extend beyond in-
dividual shipping companies to include port authorities, fleet operators, insurers, regulatory
bodies, and cybersecurity agencies. However, lack of familiarity with blockchain technology,
coupled with resistance to change, may hinder adoption across the industry.
Additionally, trust in decentralized security governance models remains a challenge.
Many maritime stakeholders still rely on centralized decision-making, and transitioning to
blockchain-enabled autonomous cybersecurity enforcement may face skepticism.
Potential Solution: Pilot projects, industry collaborations, and regulatory sandbox
initiatives can demonstrate the practical benefits of blockchain-based cybersecurity. Con-
ducting joint cybersecurity drills between fleets, ports, and maritime cybersecurity agencies
will also help build confidence in blockchain-based risk management.
