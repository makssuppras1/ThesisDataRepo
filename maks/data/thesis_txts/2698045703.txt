Abstract
With the rapid growth of renewable energy, lots of small photovoltaic (PV) prosumers
emerge.
Due to the uncertainty of PVs, there is a need for aggregated prosumers
to predict solar power generation and whether solar power generation will be larger
than load so that they can make bid/offer decisions.
In recent years, deep learning
has emerged and rapidly grown as a promising alternative in renewable power output
forecast and load forecast.
However, its application in real-life operation is limited
by overconfident predictions and few transparency.
In this thesis, two interpretable
neural networks are proposed to solve the problem: one binary classification neural
network and one regression neural network. To interpret the neural networks and gain
insights in the inner workings of the neural networks, global feature importance and local
feature contributions are examined by five gradient-based methods. The attribution map
methods of Gradient, Gradient * Input, Integrated Gradients, Expected Gradients, and
DeepLIFT are implemented and applied with different baselines for understanding the
neural networks and ensuring that they utilize reasonable input-output correlations to
make predictions. It has been shown that the methods generally produce reasonable
interpretations that are well matched with human domain expertise and intuition and
that they provide valuable insights into model inner workings in both local and global
prediction of the networks. Moreover, aleatoric uncertainty and epistemic uncertainty of
neural networks are estimated using Bayesian neural networks. The estimated uncertainty
can successfully detect abnormal cases when predictions might fail. Neural networks,
interpreted by the gradient-based methods and complemented with estimated uncertainty,
provide robust and explainable forecasting for decision-makers.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
In the past few years, Artificial intelligence (AI) has become a key enabling technology
for the sciences and industry. Nowadays, the Machine learning (ML) algorithms have
already achieved excellent performance on an increasing number of complex tasks [1].
They not only outperform humans in complex Computer vision (CV) tasks [2] [3] [4] or
Natural language processing (NLP)[5], but also do amazing jobs in strategic games, such
as Alpha Go [6] who beat the 18-time world-champion Lee Seedol in 2016. Moreover,
machine learning has also become crucial tools in scientific research and industry fields,
such as genomics [7], drug discovery [8] and medical diagnosis [9], etc.
Besides, AI methods have also been widely applied in energy systems, the applications
include: electric network operation and control [10], energy management and control [11],
demand response [12], energy generation [13]and load forecasting [14]. AI methods have
been critical in the modernization of the electrical grid and making it a ‚Äôsmart‚Äô grid [15].
Nevertheless, one challenge AI models face in these fields is the lack of transparency and
explainability because of the absence of a simple understanding of the inner workings of
the models. The problem is originated from the black-box nature of models due to their
nested nonlinear structures [1]. Numerous engineers in energy industry are hesitant to
deploy AI-based techniques considering their lack of insight and explainability, which
can help understand their dynamic decision-making procedure [15]. Explainable artificial
intelligence (XAI) addresses the concern by enhancing the explainability and transparency
of the AI models and thus ‚Äôwhitening‚Äô the black-box prediction results. One method to
interpret the models is dedicated to examining the contributions of specific input features
to the final output predictions by attribution maps.
In addition, the knowledge of uncertainty is also fundamental to the development of
robust and interpretable machine learning techniques. Traditional models such as neural
networks only provide point estimations, so it remains unknown to us how confident the
model is about its predictions. The uncertainty estimation of models allows for applying
alternative approaches or making robust and conservative decisions when predictions
are in doubt.
This thesis focuses on the above-mentioned two goals, interpretability and uncertainty
estimation, applying interpretable neural networks along with Bayesian neural networks
(BNN‚Äôs), and it does so through the problem of forecasting of aggregated renewable
generation and control of the aggregated load as a guiding example.

1.2
Research Directions
In the coming years, wind turbine generators and photovoltaic (PV) solar panels will
generate a major part of the electricity in Europe. While wind farms tend to be large
and provide the network with their power production at medium and high voltage
levels, solar power capacities tend to be smaller and spreader at lower voltage levels
of the overall network, typically at the distribution grid level. Generating power at the
distribution grid level, which may be consumed locally or going up to higher voltage
levels, is relatively new and creates new challenges in distribution grid operation and
management. Unlike big PV farms which can sign a contract with the system operators
and participate in the wholesale electricity market to make profits, the owners of small
PV panels are not considerable parties for the system operators. As a result, small PV
producers can only benefit from the net metering scheme [16] and sell excess power for
a much lower price than big PV farms‚Äô owners. In recent years, the situation gets even
worse. For example, in Denmark, with progressive reductions in the feed-in tariff level
of the net metering scheme, currently, the only advantage for new PV prosumers under
the Danish net metering scheme is the compensation of self-generation and consumption
within an hourly basis, with total or partial exemptions on certain tariffs and taxes [16].
To mitigate the problem and help small PV prosumers make more profits, one
method is to virtually aggregate small PV producers via electronic services and sign
one collaborative agreement with the system operator to participate in the electricity
market. Besides, another advantage of this method is that load aggregation of different
prosumers would lead to a less variable load, thus increasing the rate of self-consumption
of PV systems [17].
As the output of PVs is intermittent, there is a need for customers to know their solar
power generation and whether solar power generation will be larger than load so that
they can determine to sell surplus energy to system operators or buy energy to fulfill
their electricity needs or cut down their loads. However, few of the service functions are
collecting the weather and load data of small PV prosumer households and providing
directives on selling power for aggregated small PV owners.
A number of research efforts have been exerted to build models to predict solar power
generation and load with the focus on either reducing forecasting errors or improving
computation eÔ¨Äiciency.
The traditional methods for solar power/load prediction are
classical statistical algorithms, such as linear (polynomial) regression and Auto-Regressive
and Moving Average model (ARMA). In [18], the authors present a linear regression
model to predict solar radiation using temperature data. In [19], the authors propose
an hourly ARMA model to predict the power output from a PV panel.
Recently,
a great number of researchers work on the development and improvement of several
machine learning algorithms in solar power forecasting. The algorithms include Support
vector regression (SVR), Gradient boosted regression tree (GBRT), and Artificial neural
network (ANN), etc. In [20], the researchers present a support vector regression model
to produce solar power forecasts on a rolling basis for 24 hours ahead. In [21], a GBRT
model is proposed using historical power generation data and relevant meteorological
variables. The study in [22] suggests an artificial neural network for producing solar

power forecasts. Moreover, the study in [23] and [24] applies Long Short-Term Memory
(LSTM) and Convolutional neural network with LSTM (CNN-LSTM) to provide more

While the literature on machine learning in
solar power and load forecasting has increased substantially in recent years and the
machine learning algorithms have proven to be able to predict the power output more

Thus, the interpretations of the models are urgently needed, which can both support

several contributions towards this goal.
1.3
Main contributions
The main contributions of this thesis are the following:
‚ÄĘ Two neural networks are developed: a PV power generation forecasting model,
based on the regression neural network, and a load control model, based on a
classification neural network. Both models can be used to predict whether solar
generation will be larger than the load to help small PV prosumers make decisions.
‚ÄĘ Both local and global feature importance analysis are conducted for the designed
classification and regression neural networks. From the feature analysis, several
interesting dependencies between the features and the outputs are discovered,
which match with the physical phenomena behind the PV generation process.
‚ÄĘ Five gradient-based methods are implemented to interpret the designed models
and determine the major causes why models fail in some cases. Recommendations
on building robust neural networks are also given.
‚ÄĘ A Bayesian neural network is designed to estimate two types of uncertainties:
aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty of the solar power forecast model, with which
abnormal PV generation hours can be detected. Thus, when it comes to cases that
our models might fail, these hours can be considered more carefully.
Moreover, the core idea of the thesis has been summarized and written as a paper
which has been submitted as a conference paper to IEEE International Conference on
Communications, Control, and Computing Technologies for Smart Grids. The submitted
paper is currently under review and can now be seen on arXiv [25].

2.1.1
Feedforward Neural Networks
Feedforward neural network, also called multilayer perceptron (MLP), is one of the
quintessential deep learning models. The goal of a feedforward neural network is to
approximate some function f‚ąó. A feedforward neural network defines a mapping y =
f(x; őł) and learns parameters values őł which achieves the best function approximation
[27].
These models are called feedforward neural networks because information always
flows forward through the model and is never fed back. The model is associated with a
directed acyclic graph describing how the functions are composed together. For example,
in Fig 2.1, three functions f (1), f (2), and f (3) are connected in a chain, to form f(x) =
f (3)(f (2)(f (1)(x))). f (1) is called the ‚Äôfirst layer‚Äô of the network, and f (2) is called the
second layer, and so on. In this case, the model only has three layers, so f (3) is called
the output layer. The node associated with each computation (each circle shown in
Fig. 2.1) is denoted as a ‚Äôneuron‚Äô. The neurons are organized in layers with connections
from one layer feeding into the next. In this way, information is processed sequentially
(layer-wise) in the network: Firstly, the input pattern (which is just a vector x = (x1,...,
xM)) is fed to the input layer such that neuron i in the input layer is given an activation
equal to xi. The activation is then propagated to one or several hidden layers and finally
to the output layer consisting of one or more neurons corresponding to the coordinates
of the output vector.

2.1.2
Activation functions
There are several choices for the nonlinearity transformation h. The transformations are
also called activation functions. In deep learning, the default recommendation is to use
the rectified linear unit, or ReLu, defined by the activation function h(z) = max{0, z}
for it tends to show better convergence performance and typically learn much faster [29].
However, one drawback of ReLu is that it cannot learn via gradient-based methods on

2.1.4
Back-propagation and training methods
As is illustrated in section 2.1.1, The process when the initial information provided by
input x propagating through layers and finally outputting y is called forward propagation,
while back-propagation allows the information to flow backward from the cost through
the network to compute the gradients.
The calculation of back-propagation is quite simple, so it will only be introduced
briefly here. To compute the gradient of some scalar z with respect to one of the input
x in neural networks, we begin from the gradient with respect to each parent of z which
can be computed by multiplying the current gradient by the Jacobian of the operation
that produce dz. Continuing multiplying by Jacobians, traveling backward through the
neural network in this way until finally reaching x, the gradients can be obtained. For
any node that may be reached by going backward from z through two or more paths,
simply sum the gradients arriving from different paths at that node.

2.1.5
Regularization techniques
In neural network training, one of the most intractable problems is overfitting, which
means a situation where the model performs exceptionally well on the train set, but fails
to predict accurately using test data. The reason is that the model tries to learn all
details and even noise from the training set, which ultimately leads to poor performance
on the unseen data from the test set. In the machine learning field, many strategies
have already been developed aiming to reduce the test error, possibly at the expense of
increased training error. Those strategies are known as regularization.
L2 & L1 Regularization
One idea of regularization is to limit the capacity of models by penalizing the coeÔ¨Äicients.
In neural network, it actually penalizes the weight matrices of the nodes. L1 and L2
regularization are the most common regularization techniques of this type. Both of them
update the cost function by adding another parameter norm penalty term. Applying L2
regularization, the cost function is changed to:
JL2(w) = J(w) + őĽwTw
(2.29)
where lambda is the regularization parameter. L2 regularization is also known as weight
decay as it forces the weights to decay towards zero.
In L1 regularization, the absolute value of the weights is penalized:
JL1(w) = J(w) + őĽ||w||1
(2.30)
In comparison to L2 regularization, L1 regularization results in a more sparse solution,
which means some parameters might have an optimal value of zero [27]. This property
has induced L1 regularization being used extensively as a feature selection mechanism.

Dropout can also produce very good results and is thus the most frequently used
regularization technique in the field of deep learning.

Early stopping is probably the most commonly used regularization technique in deep
learning for its simplicity and eÔ¨Äiciency.
The idea originates from the observation
that when training large models with suÔ¨Äicient representational capacity, training error
decreases steadily, but test error rises again after some epochs. Fig. 2.5 can be a good
example to illustrate this phenomenon. It can be seen that after a certain epoch (labelled
by a vertical dotted line in the figure), training error continues to decrease while testing
error goes up again.

This means that a better model (with better test error) can be obtained by simply
stopping training and saving the model parameters at the point in time with the lowest
test error (Early termination point in Fig. 2.5).
Early stopping is an unobtrusive form of regularization since it is simple to use the
early technique without damaging the learning dynamics: the only thing need to do when
implementing early stopping is to maintain a copy of the best parameters. However, this
cost is generally negligible, because the best parameters are saved infrequently and never
read again during the training process, the occasional writes will only have little effect
on the total training time.

Grid search, together with random search are the two most widely used strategies
for hyperparameter optimization in neural networks [34]. In grid search, every possible
configuration of the parameters is tried. The steps of grid search are:
1. Define a grid with n dimensions which map for n hyperparameters.
2. For each dimension, define the range of possible values.
3. Search for all the possible configurations and find the best one.
It can be easily seen that with more dimensions, the more time complexity of grid search
will explode.
Thus, although this method guarantees to find the best configuration,
it is still not the best option. Instead, it‚Äôs better to use Random Search. Sampling
hyperparameters randomly with appropriate probability density has been shown to be
more computationally eÔ¨Äicient as for most data sets, only a few of the hyperparameters
really matter, but different hyperparameters are important on different data sets [34].
Random Search does a random search on step 1 to pick a point randomly from
the configuration space. The intuition of how random search works better is that the
hyperparameter space can be explored more widely compared to grid search (especially
for the more influential variables), which helps us to find the best configuration in fewer
iterations.
For example, as is shown in Fig. 2.6, for grid search, it‚Äôs easy to notice
that even if 9 models are trained, only 3 values of the important parameters are tested.
Whereas, by sampling the hyperparameters randomly, it is extremely unlikely that the
same parameter values will be chosen more than once, which leads to more evaluations
of important parameters (9 models are trained with 9 different parameter values).

In this section, motivations and theoretical background of interpretability methods will

2.2.1
Motivation
Nowadays, artificial intelligence (AI) systems based on machine learning have shown
immense successes in various fields thanks to the improvements and breakthroughs in
deep learning methodology. Although DNNs have excelled on a variety of different tasks
[2] [5] [6] [7] [8], challenges still exist which slow down or even hinder the prevalence of this
technology in some applications. One of the biggest challenges is the lack of transparency
and explainability.
The black-box nature of DNNs has hindered the application of
these models, which reduces the trust in and verifiability of the decisions made by the
models [1], especially in fields where explanation and reliability are crucial, such as
medical prognosis, self-driving car controlling, and assessing risks in finance. For all
these tasks, the decisions taken will have profound effects on people‚Äôs lives or have
substantial economic losses if predictions fail, thus the importance of interpretability
are enhanced and more precise guarantees need to be enforced.
In Europe, the regulations are quite strict in this sense.
In 2018, General Data
Protection Regulation (GDPR) took effect and restricted automated individual decision
makings by algorithms which significantly affect users. In Articles 13, 14, and 22, GDPR
has newly created a right of nondiscrimination and a right to explanation, which means
users have every right to be provided with an explanation of the algorithmic decision
==PAGE:29==
2.2 Interpretability
17
made about them by. In this regard, non-explainable machine learning models are de
facto banned in some domains and the deployment of neural networks is posed with the
biggest challenge for their nonlinear complex architectures [35].
AI has been used in the context of energy systems and the energy market domain
for at least two decades.
In the field of renewable generation and load forecasting,
interpretability of models can help detect improper biases in the model or the data.
Besides, interpretability can help to identify the key features in predictions so that
we can prioritize for which features we need the most accurate prediction and further
improve models‚Äô performance and eÔ¨Äiciency. For this reason, explanation can become an
important part of the training and validation process of future AI models [1]. Moreover,
interpretability can help to convince the field that data-driven approaches are eÔ¨Äicient
and reliable. The interpretability of both the inner workings of models (how models make
predictions) and individual cases (why such an output prediction is made) can provide
reasonable and convincing explanations for model output and give further support for
decision-makings.
2.2.2
Overview of interpretability methods
Interpretability in machine learning can be achieved in different ways. The first method
is to use models that are inherently interpretable, like linear models [36]. Although
this way is the easiest and fastest, the price is a trade of interpretability for limited
predictive power. The second way is to design models capable of outputting predictions
and explanations at the same time [36]. Training such models is very challenging because
they require real explanations to train.
More importantly, since the interpretation
generator itself is a trained model, the problem of assessing fairness and accuracy is
only transferred from the operational model to the interpretation model. Finally, an
interpretation method can be built on the basis of existing models. Potentially, this
allows generating explanations for any existing black box models without the need to
retrain and without sacrificing the performance of the best model.
Another important dimension to consider is the scope of interpretability methods.
Global interpretability is about understanding how the overall model makes decisions,
which input patterns it captures, and how to transform these patterns to produce
output [37]. On the contrary, local interpretability focuses on explaining the reasons
for specific decisions and justifies the model predictions on a case-by-case basis [37].
Global interpretability is very useful for detecting deviations that might lead to unfair
or discriminatory behavior of model, but it is diÔ¨Äicult to achieve as the number of
interactive parameters increases [36].
For this reason, most of the researches only
focus on the local interpretability of deep neural networks, such as attribution methods
[38] [39] [40] [41].
Attribution methods assign an attribution value, sometimes also
called ‚ÄĚrelevance‚ÄĚ or ‚ÄĚcontribution‚ÄĚ, to each input feature of a network. The detailed
introductions will be covered in the next section.

can know which of the network input features have a greater impact on the predicted

2.2.4
Baseline
When using attribution methods to estimate different features‚Äô contributions, the focus
is mainly on the marginal effect of a feature and how the output would change without
that feature. Besides, it has also been emphasized that humans assign blame to a cause
by comparing the outcomes of a process including or not including such cause [39]. As
a result, many attribution methods require the definition of a baseline to indicate the
==PAGE:31==
2.2 Interpretability
19
absence of information. Unfortunately, in deep neural networks, there is no suitable
method to remove one or more features from the input, as most network architectures
assume fixed input dimensions.
Although one alternative is to retrain the network
with a reduced set of input features, this will generate a different model whose internal
mechanism is not necessarily related to the original model we focus on.
To solve the problem, many attribution methods tend to select a baseline to simulate
the absence of a feature instead of removing it. The criterion of choosing baseline is that
the baseline should best represent the absence of information, such as the black image
in computer vision domain. With the chosen baseline, attributions can be computed
with respect to it, replacing each feature with its baseline value when the absence of the
feature needs.
Attributions are greatly affected by the choice of the baseline as the attribution
values are calculated as a function of the difference between the input and the selected
baseline instead of the input alone. How to choose an appropriate baseline for different
domains is still an open research question today. For many attribution methods, the
zero baseline is the canonical choice [43] [39]. If the data has been normalized by a
standard score in data preprocessing, the zero baseline can be seen as the mean of the
training set. Thus, sometimes, the expected value of each feature over the training set
can also be used as a baseline [41].
Baselines with low information content, referred to as uninformative baselines, are
often seen as ideal baselines and are widely applied in classification problems with
many classes. However, for binary classification, uninformative baselines are often not
well defined. Apart from using uninformative baselines, it can also be useful to use
informative baselines, that is, select baselines with high information content to highlight
the most important feature attributions resulting in the observed outcome divergence.
For example, in credit scoring applications, a person with ‚ÄĚperfect credit‚ÄĚ can be selected
as the baseline, the explanations are then around deducting points due to sub-optimal
factors like having ‚ÄĚshort credit history‚ÄĚ or ‚ÄĚmany open accounts with balances‚ÄĚ [44].

Perturbation-based methods directly calculate the attribution of an input feature or a
set of input features by removing, masking or altering the features, and then running
a forward pass with the new input, assessing the difference compared with the original
output. This technique has been applied to Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
in the domain of image classification in 2014, visualizing the probability of the correct
class as a function of the position of a grey patch occluding part of the image [43]. The
simplest perturbation method is to compute attributions by setting one feature to zero
baseline and then measure the effect of this perturbation on the target output [43]. The
method is often referred to as occlusion.

2.2.6

Instead of considering the model as a black-box that cannot be inspected, gradient-based
methods take advantage of lower level access to the model‚Äôs computational components
and of the knowledge of how information flows in a DNN. Most often, gradient-based
methods compute the attribution values for each input feature in the forward propagation

2.2.6.2
Motivations of advanced gradient-based attribution methods
The advanced gradient-based methods are mainly motivated by three main reasons:
Smoothing noise, avoiding the saturation problem and fulfilling desirable axioms. For
the noises in sensitivity analysis and Gradient ‚ąóInput, a possible explanation is that the
derivative of the function yc may fluctuate sharply at small scales. In other words, the
apparent noise in a sensitivity map may be due to essentially meaningless local variations
in partial derivatives [46]. The reason can be attributed to the discontinuity of the ReLu
activation functions commonly used in neural networks.
Another problem is called saturation, which has been previously reported in [47].
Saturation problem means the gradients of input features might have small magnitudes
around a sample even if the network depends heavily on those features when the network
function flattens after those features reach a certain magnitude [48]. Several approaches,
DeepLIFT [41], Integrated Gradients [39] and Expected Gradients [40], have attempted
to address this potential problem by more global descriptions.
Finally, the last motivation is the fulfillment of some desired properties. There exists
various kinds of approaches to construct attribution maps.
However, one significant
challenge in designing an attribution technique is that they are often hard to evaluate
empirically [39] which complicates the consideration of which method best explains the
actual inner workings of the models. It is hard to tease apart the error stemming from
the misbehavior of the model versus the misbehavior of the attribution method [39]. To
compensate for this shortcoming, several researchers have taken an axiomatic approach
and proposed different axioms that attribution methods should satisfy [39], all of which
are introduced below:
‚ÄĘ Sensitivity(a)
For every input and baseline that differ in one feature but have different predictions,
then the differing feature should be given a non-zero attribution [39].
‚ÄĘ Implementation Invariance
==PAGE:35==
2.2 Interpretability
23
The attributions are always identical for two functionally equivalent networks. Two
networks are functionally equivalent if their outputs are equal for all inputs, despite
having very different implementations [39].
‚ÄĘ Completeness
The attributions add up to the difference between the output of F evaluated at
the input x and the output of function value evaluated at the baseline x0 [39].
‚ÄĘ Sensitivity(b)
If the function implemented by the neural network does not depend mathematically
on some variable, then the attribution to that variable is always zero. This is a
natural complement to the definition of Sensitivity(a) [39].
‚ÄĘ Linearity
If a function f(x) is a linear combination of two sub-networks f1 and f2: f(x) =
a ¬∑ f1 + b ¬∑ f2, the attributions of f(x) should also be the weighted sum of the
attributions for f1 and f2 with weights a and b respectively [39]. In other words,
linearity requires the attributions to preserve the linearity within the network.
‚ÄĘ Symmetry-Preserving
Two input variables are symmetric w.r.t. a function if swapping them does not
change the function.
An attribution method is symmetry-preserving, if for all
inputs that have identical values for symmetric variables and baselines that have
identical values for symmetric variables, the symmetric variables receive identical
attributions [39].
‚ÄĘ Explanation continuity
Given a continuous prediction function to be explained fc(x), it also produces
continuous attributions Rc(x) [36].
With all motivations, several advanced gradient-based methods are proposed and
will be introduced in the later section.

2.2.7
Evaluation of Interpretability methods
Unlike metrics of model performance such as accuracy, the criteria of interpretability
often cannot be completely quantified.
Moreover, there is little consensus on what
interpretability in machine learning is and how to evaluate it for benchmark [37]. Prior
work on evaluating attribution methods generally falls into four categories: 1) measuring
sensitivity of explanations to model and input perturbations, 2) inferring correctness
of explanation from accuracy drop upon feature removal, 3) evaluating explanations
==PAGE:38==
2.2 Interpretability
26
in a controlled setting with some knowledge of feature importance, and 4) evaluating
explanations based on human visual assessment [49]. In this thesis, the focus will mainly
be on the first three categories of methods to evaluate models‚Äô interpretability because
the attribution maps of solar power and load forecasting are not easily visualized as in
computer vision domain so that option 4 is not suitable in this case.
2.2.7.1
Human evaluation
Evaluation of different attribution methods is often based upon human studies which
assess whether the rankings are meaningful to humans. More specially, this approach
either asks humans to use interpretability methods to perform a task or evaluates
how often humans can correctly predict model behavior after seeing explanations [50].
Different attribution methods can generate different explanations even with the same
input and the same model. Thus, commonsense and instinctive explanations to expert
knowledge about the significance and influence of features are often used as a qualitative
assessment.
In [51], the authors showed the utility of explanation by conducting a
comprehensive evaluation with simulated and human subjects,where they measured
the impact of explanations on trust and associated tasks. In their experiments, they
examined whether the interpretability of models can help non-experts decide if one
should trust a prediction, choose a better model, and improve an untrustworthy classifier.
However, it should be cautious when involving humans in the evaluation loop for
interpretability. The lack of robustness for neural networks has been revealed in work
[52] that image classifiers, for instance, fail spectacularly once the images are subjected
to adversarial changes that appear minor to humans. Adversarial samples suggest that
neural networks are much more sensitive to small variations than the human brain. Thus,
it is unreasonable to expect the image recognition tasks to be wildly different, but the
corresponding explanations fail to reflect this difference [36].
2.2.7.2
Ablation test
One simple way to evaluate the interpretability of models is to see whether ablating
the input features as ranked by their importance decreases the prediction accuracy or
confidence, which is called ablation test [48]. Top-K ablation test substitutes the K most
relevant features with their baseline values and observes the degradation in performance.
Let x ‚ąąRM be a sample point with attribution values Rc ‚ąąRM and gK(x, R) be a
function which replaces the K most influential features with their baseline values. Then
the Top-K ablation metric Abl ‚ąíK is expressed as:
ABI ‚ąíK(Rc, x) = f(x) ‚ąíf(gK(x, Rc))
(2.44)
Thus, the attribution map with higher Abl-K values shows that these features are more
important for the predicted output, compared to the baseline. However, the ablation
test has also some significant drawbacks. One drawback is called feature correlation,
which means that when ablating one feature in the data set, similar performance might
be obtained because similar information is also stored in another correlated feature, but
==PAGE:39==
2.3 Uncertainty quantification
27
that does not mean the ablated feature is not important. Another disadvantage is that
it is unclear whether the degradation in model performance comes from the distribution
shift or because the features removed are truly informative [48].
There are also some other evaluation metrics proposed in some other literature. For
example, in [49], the researchers propose a variant of an ablation test in computer vision.
Firstly, they ablate pixels in the training and test sets, and then retrain a new model
on the ablated data and measure by how much the test set performance drops. This
method has the advantage of better capturing whether or not the methods highlight
the pixels that are the most important for predicting the output class, however, it has
the drawback of higher computational expense for the need to retrain the model several
times [48]. This metric may also get confused by feature correlation.
In this thesis, we choose to carefully perturb the samples with one feature at a time.
Thus, how the different methods attribute this ablated feature is used as a qualitative
evaluation.
2.3
Uncertainty quantification
The knowledge of uncertainty is essential to the development of robust and interpretable
neural networks. A general regression/classification neural network can only provide a
point estimation, which means the network is ‚Äúequally confident‚ÄĚ in all samples, even
in those ones the model provides inaccurate or wrong predictions. The overconfident
predictions made by neural networks might be harmful or offensive in some cases, for
example, a classifier could give the wrong medical diagnosis with such high confidence
that the data is not flagged for human inspection, or a language model could output
offensive text that it confidently believes is non-problematic [53].
Thus, as neural
networks have become increasingly essential in larger decision making pipelines, the
needs for representing model uncertainty is of crucial importance [54]. The estimated
neural networks uncertainty can not only help detect abnormal cases model might
fail so that uncertain inputs and special cases can be treated explicitly, but also can
help to improve accuracy of model and make better decisions.
For the solar power
generation forecast considered in this thesis, because of the high uncertainty of solar
power generation, the captured uncertainties can help detect cases that predictions might
fail so that decision makers can make more robust decisions considering the estimated
uncertainties.
2.3.1
Uncertainty decomposition
The uncertainty associated with machine learning models can be broadly classified into
two types, which represent two inherently different sources of uncertainty: aleatoric
uncertainty and epistemic uncertainty.
The overall uncertainty of any model is a
combination of the above two types of uncertainty.
Aleatoric uncertainty refers to the irreducible error of the uncertainty, which means
the error cannot be reduced by choosing a better model because the uncertainty originates
==PAGE:40==
2.3 Uncertainty quantification
28
from the non-deterministic nature of the sought input/output dependency. Aleatoric
uncertainty refers to the variability in the outcome of an experiment, which is due to
inherently random effects and captures the uncertainty concerning information that our
data cannot explain [55]. For example, in lab experiments, even when all input values
are similar, the values measured after multiple trials are never the same. This type
of uncertainty is a typical aleatoric uncertainty. As opposed to uncertainty caused by
randomness, uncertainty due to a lack of knowledge about the perfect predictor, for
example, caused by uncertainty about the parameters of a model, is called epistemic
uncertainty. Epistemic uncertainty captures our limited understanding of the real-world
process for which we are building the model, as we are not able to capture all input
features that affect the target variable [55].
In principle, epistemic uncertainty is a
reducible error which means this uncertainty can be reduced on the basis of information.
2.3.2
Bayesian neural networks
In this thesis, BNNs (Bayesian neural networks) are applied to estimate the mentioned
two types of uncertainties. A brief introduction of Bayesian neural networks and how to
use BNNs to estimate the two types of uncertainties will be covered in this section.

3. Average instantaneous measurements of the PV module temperature in 0C.

3.2.1
Implementation of neural networks
Conventionally, the data set is divided into training and test sets. 10% of the data are
randomly shuffled for testing and the rest are used for training. The motivation behind
random shuffling is that the performance of neural networks is dependent on how many
previously unseen data samples are contained in the test set. By randomly shuffling the
data, it can be guaranteed that the model trains on as many data samples as possible to
avoid overfitting. When randomly shuffling the data set, a common random seed is used
so that different models can be compared and validated with a consistent train/test split.
As a result, the training and testing sets are the same for the regression and classification
models. Note that for classification neural network training, all features introduced in
Table 2.8 are used while for regression model, HLoad is not used because the regression
neural network is to predict solar power generation.
In the training of neural networks, as introduced before in section 2.1, Adam is chosen
to be the ideal optimizer because of its good performance in practice shown by empirical
results [32]. Relu is chosen to be activation functions for all layers except the output
layer because its faster and better convergence performance [30]. Moreover, the most
common choices: MSE (mean square error) and binary cross entropy are set as the loss
function for regression and classification model correspondingly. To avoid overfitting,

3.2.3
Load control and Decision makings
When there is imbalance between aggregated load demand and aggregated solar power
generation, bid or offer decisions need to be made by customers.
When there are extra solar power generation after satisfying self-consumption, the
power surplus can be sold to electricity market. However, when aggregated solar power
generation is not suÔ¨Äicient for self-consumption, there are two methods to handle this.
The first way is to curtail part of the unnecessary load demand so that the remaining
demand can be fully satisfied by solar power. However, this method is hard to implement
in the case of prosumers aggregation, as it is hard and troublesome to decide which
households and which part of load should be controlled. Besides, it is quite expensive
for prosumers to curtail their load demand considering their dissatisfaction. Another way
is to simply buy electricity from power market which is also the preferred option in this
thesis. With the predicted aggregated solar power generation and load, the exact amount
of energy to trade can be obtained. However, with the interpretability and estimated
uncertainty of models which will be introduced later, more favourable decisions can be
made. As the bidding/offering strategy is not the main focus of this thesis and the
strategy should be determined by customers‚Äô preferences: whether customers would like
a robust decision or more profitable but risky decisions, the details of strategy are out
of scope of this thesis.

3.4
Uncertainty quantification
The neural networks only provide a point estimation of solar power generation. As a
result, the regression neural network in section 3.3 is ‚ÄĚequally confident‚ÄĚ in all described
TP, TN, FP, FN cases, while hours during a day are not equivalent in terms of solar
irradiance. How to quantify the confidence of neural network predictions for safer and
more robust decisions still remains a question. In this section, uncertainty quantification
of neural networks is proposed to address this problem.
Uncertainty estimation of models allows increasing eÔ¨Äiciency of the decision-making
process. To be more specific, the designed neural networks can perform autonomously
in hours with low uncertainty. On the contrary, in those hours with high estimated
uncertainty, the models can request the intervention of the domain expert or simply
take a safer and more robust decision to avoid potential risks. As introduced in section
2.3, aleatoric uncertainty and epistemic uncertainty are quantified by designing Bayesian
neural networks. The epistemic uncertainty is estimated simply through Monte Carlo
sampling, while the aleatoric uncertainty is estimated by placing a distribution over the
output of the model output, so both predictive mean ňÜy as well as predictive variance
(aleatoric uncertainty) ŌÉ2 can be estimated.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
In this thesis, several contributions towards interpretability and uncertainty estimation
of neural networks in aggregated solar power generation and load forecast are made. In
order to help aggregated prosumers make bid/offer decisions in the electricity market,
two neural networks are designed to provide directives for them: one regression neural
network for solar power forecast, and one classification neural network to predict whether
solar power generation will exceed load demand in hourly resolution.
Firstly, through an elaborate hyperparameter tuning process and model training,
the final regression model has an RMSE of only 142.51 KWh, and the classification
model has an prediction accuracy of 91.4%. When in the implementation of models, to
avoid negative solar power predictions in the regression model and reduce unwanted false
positive cases in the classification model, some improvements are made to the networks
for better performance of models.
However, without becoming human interpretable, neural networks can hardly be
implemented in such critical infrastructures as power systems. To interpret the designed
classification and regression neural networks, both global and local feature analysis are
conducted. In the thesis, five gradient-based attribution methods: Sensitivity, Gradient
* Input, Integrated gradients, Expected gradients, and DeepLIFT, with two different
baselines: extreme baseline and average baseline, are implemented.
The analysis of
global feature importance is conducted using five methods. It can be concluded that
all five methods apart from Sensitivity method agree that load and solar generation of
the previous hour and solar irradiance have the greatest impacts on the outcome of the
neural network. After a quantitative ablation-type evaluation of the different methods
with different baselines, DeepLIFT with extreme baseline turns out to be the most
effective method in highlighting the most important features for both models. Based on
the interpretation of DeepLIFT with extreme baseline, influences of different features
to model output as well as the inner workings of two networks are revealed. Besides,
several interesting dependencies between the weather parameters and outputs of the
neural networks are also discovered.
After deeper research on individual cases, a better understanding of different features‚Äô
contributions to the output in different situations is obtained.
The main source of
inaccurate forecasts is also discovered: the sudden dramatic change of solar irradiance,
which can be caused, for example, by cloud movements. To resolve this problem, one
Bayesian neural network is implemented to determine the uncertainty of PV output
in each hour. Thus, those hours with high uncertainty can be known beforehand and
considered more carefully by the domain expert. Neural networks, interpreted by the
gradient-based methods and complemented with estimated uncertainty, can provide
==PAGE:68==
4 Conclusion
56
more robust and explainable forecasting for decision-makers.
This work can be seen as the first step towards the creation of continuously learning
neural networks for PV forecasting. Currently, the designed neural networks can perform
autonomously in hours with low uncertainty, while in the hours with high uncertainty,
the models can request the intervention of the domain expert.
The neural network
can learn from the decisions made by the human expert, which will further enhance
the neural network performance and make a step towards autonomous forecasting and
decision-making algorithms performing on the above human level.
