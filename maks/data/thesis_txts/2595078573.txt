Abstract  
Cancer is currently the second leading cause of death globally. Traditionally, treatment of cancer has 
been limited to three pillars: Surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation. In recent decades, the field of 
immunotherapy has emerged as the fourth pillar. The immune system is known to survey the body. 
If infected or malignant cells are recognized by the immune system, it will kill the cells in order to 
defend our body from further advancement of disease. Thus, these features can be harnessed in 
therapies to help us fight cancer.  
Adoptive cell transfer (ACT) is one of the rapidly growing branches of immunotherapy in which great 
clinical advances have been obtained. ACT is based on the expansion and manipulation of immune 
cells harvested from patients, to generate tumor-reactive cells that can potentiate antitumor immunity. 
Both tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) and peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) have 
been extensively explored as sources of immune reactive T cells. The tumor-draining lymph node 
(tdLN) has been suggested as a clinically relevant cell source, since sentinel lymph nodes are often 
removed during surgery to assess whether the cancer has spread. In addition, the tdLN is described 
to be an immune privileged site for presentation of antigens to the immune system.  
This thesis set out to establish a method for generating tumor-reactive T cells derived from tdLNs. In 
data presented in this thesis, ACT of tdLN cells demonstrates a great antitumor response and 
prolonged survival in lymphodepleted tumor-challenged mice. Analysis showed that donor-derived 
CD8+ T cells infiltrated the tumor and both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells proliferated in vivo after injection. 
Intratumoral administration of a Toll-like receptor 7 agonist (TLR7a) formulated into a controlled 
drug release matrix augmented a significant tumor remission and complete responders showed long-
term immunity upon re-challenge. Lastly, applying cell-tethering IL-12 to tdLN cells also enhanced 
the survival in mice with medium and large tumors. Overall, this thesis evaluates the use of tdLN for 
ACT in preclinical cancer models and demonstrates that adjuvant therapies such as TLR7a and IL-
12 can further improve the outcome of the therapy.

1 Introduction 
Immunotherapy is defined as therapy that stimulates or suppresses the immune system to fight 
cancer, infectious- and other diseases, according to National Cancer Institute’s Dictionary (1). 
Due to tremendous research and treatment advancements during the last decade, 
immunotherapy has been named the fourth pillar of cancer therapy as an addition to surgical-, 
radio- and chemotherapy (2).  
The growing interest in the field is noticeable with a 91% increase in immune-oncology agents 
currently in development or approved between 2017 to 2019 (from 2.030 to 3.876) with cell-
based therapies undergoing the largest growth during these years (3). A market research 
company called ‘Reports and Data’ has forecasted that the global cancer immunotherapy 
market will grow to USD 153,03 Billion by 2027 due to the increasing demand from hospitals 
and clinics (USD 78,04 Billion in 2019) (4).  
Clearly, there has been a growing focus on immunotherapy during the last few decades, 
however, the notion that the immune system is involved in cancer prognosis was described 
around 1900 by both Handley, W. S. and Da Fano, C. reporting lymphocyte accumulation as a 
defensive mechanism (5). Already in 1892, a surgeon named William Coley reported that 
injecting a cocktail consisting of heat-killed S. marcescens and S. pyogenes into sarcomas 
(tumors of connective tissue origin) resulted in a delayed tumor growth inhibition, a bacterial 
vaccine unknowing of its immune response triggering (6). Today, several cancer 
immunotherapy platforms are approved by the US Food and Drug-administration such 
oncolytic viruses, treatment vaccines, cytokine therapy, bispecific antibodies, immune 
checkpoint inhibitors as well as cell-based therapies (7). Overall, immunotherapy offers a range 
of opportunities to target the cancer specifically, while limiting the pronounced damage done 
to healthy tissues, which is seen in other cancer therapies, such as chemotherapy (8) 
Importantly, activation of the adaptive immune system can establish “immunological 
memory”, and thus function both therapeutically and as a ‘safeguard’ from relapse. This is an 
important aspect, as the majority of cancer deaths results from disseminated disease that has 
not been removed by treatment. 
1.1 Cancer Immunoediting  
In 2002, Dunn et. al introduced the concept of cancer immunoediting, which is useful to 
understand and describe immunotherapy and its challenges. Immunoediting is divided into 
three phases: Elimination, equilibrium, and escape phase (9-11). Leading up to the elimination 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
2 
phase is the transformation of a normal cell into a neoplastic cell. A hallmark of a neoplastic 
cell is genome instability leading to accumulation of genetic alternations which eventually can 
result in expression of altered proteins. These altered proteins allow the cell to be perceived as 
“foreign from self” and thus activate immune cells. Mutated proteins, termed tumor-specific 
antigens, will after degradation be displayed on the surface by major histocompatibility 
complex class I (MHC I) molecules or released after cell death and taken up by antigen-
presenting cells (APC) for display on MHC II or MHC I molecules (12-14). Other origins of 
tumor antigens are abnormalities epigenetic regulation, which can cause to altered protein 
expression patterns. This can cause expression of germline-specific genes or overexpression of 
nonmutated proteins normal present in low levels on other cells called tumor-associated 
antigens (15-17). Under normal immunosurveillance, the innate and adaptive molecules and 
cells will initiate an immune response to the transformed cell. This phase is termed the 
elimination phase. A main component herein is the adaptive response by cytolytic T 
lymphocytes, CD8+ T cells, which recognize both tumor-associated- and tumor-specific 
antigens displayed on neoplastic cells and causing cell-lysis and thus, eradicate the cancer cells 
and prevent further tumor growth (18, 19).

However, an inadequate immune response can lead to sporadic survival of cancer cells. These 
enter the equilibrium phase in which the immune system controls the progression of the tumor 
while the tumor cells are present in a dormant state in which editing occurs. A continuous 
insufficient immune control can lead to a selective pressure that promotes the outgrowth of 
cancer cells with properties to evade the immunosurveillance, defining the escape phase. Some 
of the main escaping strategies include loss of neoantigen expression (20, 21), loss of "2 
microglobulin in the MHC class I molecule, (22, 23), and mutational defects in the antigen-
processing machinery such as the components Tapasin (24) and Transporter associated with 
antigen presentation 1-protein (25) hindering neoantigen loading. Other mechanisms include 
establishment of an immune suppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) which is described 
in greater detail in Section 1.3 later in this thesis. An illustration of phases and mechanisms is 
provided in Figure 1. 
1.2 Adoptive T cell transfer  
The goal of adoptive cell transfer (ACT) is to reinitiate an immune response by reintroducing 
high numbers of activated, tumor-specific T cells to eliminate the tumor and hence, overcome 
the equilibrium. The process of ACT can be divided into three parts: Firstly, T cells are isolated 
from a donor, often autologous cells from the patient itself. Secondly, the isolated cells will be 
cultured ex vivo which allows for manipulation of the cells during expansion e.g. stimulating 
differentiation towards a preferred functionality (26-28) or genetically modifying them to 
enhance the antitumor effects (29, 30). Finally, cells are re-infused into the patient to mediate 
eradication of the tumor. This step is often accompanied by other therapies in combination to 
enhance the antitumor response (31). Most clinically established is supplementation with 
interleukin-2 (IL-2) to promote survival of the transferred T cells. Different sources of T cells 
as well as activation and expansion approaches have been taken which all have their advantages 
and challenges – an overview is provided in Figure 2 
Figure 2.

1.2.1 Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes – A source for tumor-specific T cells  
A pioneer within ACT, Steven A. Rosenberg and his group started in the late 1970s to study 
how to isolate lymphoid cells from disaggregated solid tumors and expand these later named 
tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL). They found that these TILs were cytotoxic when co-
cultured with tumor cells of the same tumor which the TILs were isolated from (32). In 1994, 
Rosenberg et al. published a clinical study reporting the treatment of metastatic melanoma 
patients with TILs. Each patient received 1011 TILs intravenously and high dose of IL-2 
simultaneously. They reported an objective responses rate of 34% in the 86 patients included 
in the study. In addition, they found a significant inverse correlation between patients’ 
responses and the number of days the TILs had been cultured. Thus, shorter culturing cause 
great antitumor potential of TILs (33). Later studies have supported this observation (34-36).  
Melanomas are of particular interest in TIL-therapy as they possess a high mutational load (37) 
causing a high chance of producing immunogenic antigens (38). Additionally, autologous TILs 
from other epithelial cancers with high mutational burdens, such as gastrointestinal cancers, 
have demonstrated T cell responses against neo-antigens of the patients’ tumor. Satisfying 
results have been obtained in TIL treatment in conjugation with irradiation and IL-2 
administration for melanoma patients, with up to 50-70 % responders reported in by Dudley et 
al. (2008) (39). However, these results have not been possible to translate into to epithelial 
cancer patients in clinic (29, 38, 40). Although recently, encouraging results have been 
achieved in metastatic breast cancer (41) colorectal cancer (42) and human papilloma virus-
positive cancers, in which a study reported two out of 18 cervical cancer patients showed 
complete response (43).  
The commonly used culturing method for isolating and expanding TILs is developed by 
Rosenberg’s group. To isolate TILs, tumors are dissected and split into smaller pieces while 
necrotic tissue are removed. Fragments are placed in tissue culture plates containing growth 
media during culturing. Subsequently, T cells are stimulated with the growth cytokine IL-2, to 
outgrow CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in the culture (44). Later protocols describe the addition of 
anti-CD3 to culture to further activate the T cells in an antigen-independent manner (45). The 
CD3 stimulation induces expression of high affinity IL-2 receptors on T cells, and thereby, 
which enables T cells to grow at lower concentration of IL-2 (46). However, the addition of 
IL-2 during expansion should be administered with caution since it can also promote outgrowth 
of suppressive T cells and induce activation-induced cell death of T cells (47). These 
procedures increase the purity of the TILs product by selectively expanding lymphocytes while 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
5 
letting contaminant cells die due to lack of survival factors. However, a disadvantage of 
stimulating TILs antigen-independent, is that non-tumor specific “bystander” T cell clones are 
expanded, thus not only enriching the antigen specific clones. Studies have uncovered that 
frequently, only a small number of TILs mount a response against the neoantigens presented 
on the tumor (17, 38). In general, TILs have been reported as a source of polyclonal T cells for 
ACT that elicit a great antitumor response.  
1.2.2 Genetically engineered T cells – A method for generating potent specific T cells  
An advance in ACT-therapy emerged when laboratories discovered how to genetically 
engineer T cells isolated from peripheral blood in vitro during expansion to be tumor-specific. 
One of the approaches is to transduce T cells with specific genes encoding a chimeric antigen 
receptor (CAR) directed against a tumor-associated antigen (48). CARs typically consist of an 
antigen-specific extracellular domain formed by a single-chain variable fragment linked to an 
intracellular domain consisting of CD28 co-stimulatory domain and CD3-zeta T cell activation 
domain in conjunction (48, 49). In a newer generation, the co-stimulatory CD28 domain is 
further connected with stimulatory domains such as 4-1BB or OX40. Several further versions 
have been developed such as CARs with two targets, an inhibitory receptor recognizing normal 
tissue, and CARs with a suicide switch to turn-off the response (50). An advantage in ACT 
therapy with CAR-T cells is that they do not require epitope presentation on MHC molecules. 
CAR-T therapy has presented great accomplishments in the last decade (51) . In a clinical trial 
by Maude et al., 2014, thirty relapsed acute lymphoblastic leukemia patients were treated with 
CD28-4-1BB CD19-directed CAR-T cells resulting in complete remission of 27 (90 %) and a 
durable response up to 24 months with no adverse events registered. Discouragingly, leukemia 
relapses were observed due to loss of CAR-T cells in circulation and escape of CD19-negative 
variants (52). Downregulation or loss of antigen expression is one of the main obstacles with 
CAR-T cells due to their monospecific target. The immune escapes following CAR-T therapy 
have been reported in several studies (14, 51, 53, 54) 
Although CAR-T cells have shown great clinical potential, the therapy has been associated 
with some severe adverse events, sometimes even resulting in fatal outcomes. A frequent side 
effect has been cytokine-release syndrome (CRS) caused by a massive immune reaction 
resulting in elevated systemic pro-inflammatory cytokine levels such as IL-6 and interferon-
gamma (IFN-#). CRS symptoms are high fever, fatigue, hypotension and cardiac arrest and 
even neurotoxicity. These symptoms require administration of corticosteroids and anti-IL-6 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
6 
(tocilizumab) to reverse the condition (50, 55). Another consequence of the specific CAR is 
on-target/off-tumor toxicities that have been observed in a lung carcinoma patient in which 
tissues expressing low-level of target resulted in respiratory failure (56). Overall, CAR-T offers 
great opportunities in target immune responses, especially in hematological malignancies while 
there is still a need for further development to translate the results into solid tumor and 
decreasing the cost of manufacturing process (50, 57).  
1.2.3 Tumor-draining lymph node cells 
Another alternative and less explored approach, compared to TILs and CAR-T cells, is the use 
of T cells from the tumor-draining lymph node (tdLN) as a source of tumor-reactive cells (58-
62). The principle behind this, is that various antigens released from dying tumor cells are taken 
up by APCs which present the antigen to lymphocytes in the tdLN. As a result, CD8+ and CD4+ 
T cells are primed and expanded in the tdLN, thus the tdLN makes a great source of enriched 
tumor-specific T cells. A study by Matsuda et al. 2019 reported that the numerous TCR 
clonotypes were shared between the tdLN and the tumor, thus indicating a polyclonal 
specificity (63).  
Using tdLN as a source for ACT can be an alternative when there cannot be expanded enough 
TILs from dissected tumor. A handful of clinical studies have been carried out using tdLN to 
treat solid tumors postoperatively (64). Notably, in a Swedish clinical study, researchers 
isolated the tdLN from colorectal patients for ex vivo culturing before refusion. Four out of 
nine patients with stage IV cancer showed complete responses, and increased the median 
survival from 0.8 (using conventional treatments) to 2.6 years when receiving tdLN cells with 
no side effects observed (65). Generally, exploration of tdLN for ACT-therapy is still sparse 
even though the microenvironment of the tdLN in relation to cancer has been heavily described 
(66). The next section will focus on cells and factors which have been shown to be involved in 
inhibiting T cell function and promoting an immunosuppressive TME as well as possible 
interventions to reverse these mechanisms.

1.3 Enhancing the immune response of adoptive cell transfer  
In 1968, Hellström et al. studied how tumor-specific lymphocytes isolated from progressing 
tumors of cancer patients were able to kill cancer cells in vitro. They speculated whether the 
reason for the contradicting observation of lymphocytes having no effect in patients despite 
their tumor-killing capabilities was due a suppressive immune TME (69), later termed the 
Hellström Paradox (70). The role of a suppressive TME in the escape phase has later been 
uncovered by several studies (14, 71, 72). In tumors with a high number of TILs, described as 
“hot” tumors, a mechanism of the adaptive immune resistance is the upregulation of the 
programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) on tumor cells often in response to constative IFN-# 
exposure (14). PD-L1 binds to immune checkpoint molecule PD-1 that is expressed on T cells 
and block proliferation and impairs T cell function, an inhibitory immunoregulating pathway 
(72, 73).  
In addition to the tumor itself, specific lymphocytes and stromal cell types have also been 
revealed to promote the suppression of the immune response. One of these, regulatory T cells 
(TReg), are known to secrete immunosuppressive factor, transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-
") and IL-10, express PD-L1 and deplete IL-2 by expressing a high-affinity IL-2 receptor (74, 
75) resulting in dysfunctional T cells. Although TGF-" has primarily been described in an 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
8 
immunosuppression context such as converting naïve CD4+ T cells into TReg  cells (76), studies 
have also reported TGF-" to be related to expansion of CD8+ TILs and promoting cell-lysis 
(77).  
Another TGF-" secreting cell-type is the myeloid-derived-suppressor cell (MDSC), a diverse 
group of myeloid progenitor cells and immature myeloid cells with immunosuppressive 
function (78). Studies have suggested that MDSCs can promote the development of TReg cells 
by IL-10 and TGF-" secretion (79) and reduce CD8+ T cell infiltration (80). Tumor-associated 
macrophages (TAM) have also been shown to regulate the TME both with pro- and anti-tumor 
effects. A subset of TAMs called M2-like TAMs, have also been shown to secrete IL-10, TGF-
" and in addition, limit the tumor infiltration of CD8+ cells (81). The next section will cover 
strategies which have been used throughout this thesis to reverse the suppressive TME to 
increase the efficacy of ACT-therapy.  
1.3.1 Preconditioning before adoptive cell transfer – Creating a “niche”  
Lymphodepleting preconditioning has proven to enhance the outcome of ACT when performed 
prior to both CAR-T and TIL therapy (49, 54, 75, 82, 83). The two most frequent 
lymphodepleting routines in clinic are total body irradiation (TBI) and the administration of 
the chemotherapeutic agents, cyclophosphamide (CPX) and fludarabine. Extensive research on 
preconditioning has demonstrated that CPX decreases the endogenous lymphocyte population 
transiently and thus, decreases the number of TReg cells in the TME resulting in a better outcome 
for ACT-therapy (84, 85). Similarly, in Yao et al. (2010) they demonstrated that 
preconditioning with chemotherapy and TBI resulted in a decreased number of TReg cells and 
that the effect correlated with the dose of TBI (86).  
Irradiation has shown to promote activation of splenic dendritic cells (DC) reported as 
upregulation of MHC class II molecules and activation marker CD86 in mice (87). Some 
evidence from a study by Paulos et al. (2007) also suggest that TBI can kill microbes present 
in gut microbiota causing a release of lipopolysaccharides triggering toll-like receptor (TLR) 
4 on DC, leading to DC activation (88). Similar observations have been reported by Wrzesinski 
et al. (2010) (89).  
Lymphodepletion also cause an increased availability of IL-7 and IL-15. This happens because 
most of the endogenous immune cells that normally binds the cytokines are abolished after 
lymphodepletion. Under normal circumstances, these cytokines are presented to regulate the 
homeostasis of the immune system. Removal of the endogenous cellular ”sinks” by either TBI 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
9 
or chemotherapy have been reported to cause homeostatic antigen-independent proliferation of 
the transferred T cells and enhanced antitumor efficacy (90). Increased availability IL-7 and 
IL-15 have been reported to support the survival and the memory phenotype of adoptively 
transferred T cells and that the effect is absent in IL-15-/--deficient mice (91-93). However, 
lymphodepletion preconditioning a not also a beneficial. The list of potential side effects is 
long and includes pulmonary complications, neutropenia, renal insufficiency, secondary 
myelodysplastic syndrome, secondary malignancies etc. (8, 94) 
1.3.2 Combining adoptive cell transfer with adjuvant therapy  
TLR7 agonist treatment  
A strategy for overcoming a tumor suppression of transferred T cells, is to stimulate an innate 
immune response to further promote the adaptive immune response. Toll like receptor (TLR)-
7 is an intracellular endosolic receptor that recognizes single-stranded DNA. TLR7 both 
expressed on plasmacytoid DCs (pDC) and to a lesser extent on myeloid-derived DCs in mice. 
However, the difference in TLR7 expression patterns between human and mice are still debated 
(95, 96). Upon TLR7 stimulation, the adapter protein MyD88 is then recruited which allows 
docking of kinase proteins which mediates in transcription by activating NF-%B and IRF7 that 
varies immune mechanisms.  
TLR7 stimulation induces secretion of large amount of type I interferon, IFN-! from pDCs 
(97). Activation of TLR7 on DCs results in upregulation of MHC I and MHC II and the 
costimulatory molecules CD80 and CD86, which are necessary for T cell activation and 
survival. Besides IFN-!, an increased IL-12p70 secretion also occurs as a result of TLR7a 
stimulation (98) which has been correlated with lower PD-1 expression on CD8+ T cells (99). 
Administration of TLR7 agonist (TLR7a) has been suggested to promote tumor recruitment of 
specific CD8+ T cells by C-X-chemokine ligand 10 (CXCL10), CXCL11, CXCL13 and C-C-
ligand 2 (CCL2) secretion activated through IFN-! (100, 101). IFN-! has also been associated 
with enhanced cross-presentation of exogenous antigens to CD8+ T cell by conventional DCs 
(cDC) (101-103). CD8+ T cells have also been reported by Salerno et al., (2016) to be able to 
respond directly to TLR7 ligands. Sensing the TLR7 ligand resulted in a rapid but limited 
secretion of IFN-# which activated macrophages (104). 
Resiquimod (R848), is a TLR7/8a molecule approved for topical use in melanoma and 
cutaneous T cell lymphoma patients (105) and has also been tested as an adjuvant for cancer 
vaccines (106, 107). Oral administration of R848 has also been explored in clinical trials in 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
10 
which severe IFN-related toxicities were observed such as fever and lymphopenia (108). To 
limit adverse effects of TLR7a, research groups have been exploring different approaches to 
improve DC-targeting by liposomal formulation (109) or by intratumoral injection (110). Work 
by our research group has been done in formulating TLR7a into a bio-compatible gel to be 
delivered intratumorally which further creates a favorable release profile and benefits drug 
penetration in the tumor (111) 
IL-12 administration 
As noted above, IL-12 release by APCs is involved in antitumor effects of the adaptive immune 
system (112, 113). IL-12 increases IFN-# production in T cells which further stimulate IL-12 
secretion by DCs and creates a positive feedback loop. In vitro expansion of CD8+ T cells with 
IL-12 can give rise to memory phenotypes and caused long-term memory CD8+ CD62L+ T 
cells leading to improved efficacy of ACT in vivo (114-116). Preclinical studies have also 
demonstrated that IL-12 is associated with polarization of CD4+ into Th1 cells, orchestrating 
antitumor responses (112, 113). The promising potential of IL-12 in preclinical trials have not 
been meet in clinical trials, where toxicities have revealed a very narrow therapeutic window 
(112). To minimize toxicities associated with systemic IL-12 administration during ACT, 
researchers have recently developed CAR-T cells that constitutively secrete IL-12 and in this 
way, reduce the systemic exposure to IL-12 (30, 117-119). Another clinical study by Kang et 
al. uses the strategy of transducing fibroblast cells with an IL-12 encoding gene and 
subsequently, injecting the cells into cancer patients showing limited adverse events and 
reduction in tumor sizes (120). However, some toxicities have been reported even with ACT 
of engineered T cells, thus, there is still a need for finding a safe way to harness the potential 
of IL-12 in immunotherapy (112, 118).

2 Aim and objectives  
As of today, preclinical studies of ACT are often performed with TCR-transgenic mouse models, 
in which the RAG-gene and TCR sequence has been genetically altered to give a uniquely high 
proportion of specific T cells with already known targets. The most used models include the OT-1 
and OT-2 T cells recognizing OVA (a model “neo-epitope”) or PMEL cells recognizing the gp100 
antigen (a model “self-epitope”). As useful as these models are, they are having some importance 
divergences from the clinically used T cell products. Furthermore, they have the clear disadvantage 
of limiting pre-clinical research to tumor models expressing either OVA or gp100 as part of their 
proteome. 
The aim of this thesis was to establish a protocol for ex vivo selection and expansion of tumor-
reactive T cells in a syngeneic murine model of cancer. To generate tumor-specific cells, donor 
mice were challenged with CT26 colon carcinoma tumor cells. The hypothesis was that tumor-
antigen exposure taken place in the tdLN will prime T cells that can be isolated, differentiated 
and expanded into tumor-reactive cells.  
To validate this protocol the objectives are to firstly, characterize the phenotypes of the ex vivo 
expanded tdLN cells. Secondly, to evaluate the in vivo antitumor potential of the these tdLN 
cells upon ACT and optimize the precondition prior to treatment. Lastly, to investigate the use 
of tdLN cells in combination with two novel drug delivery platforms; An intratumoral TLR7a, 
R848, formulated into a bio-compatible gel developed by our group Colloids- and Biological 
Interfaces Group at DTU Health Tech. And another IL-12 cell-tethering drug delivery platform 
kindly provided by Repertoire Immune Medicines (former Torque Therapeutics). The 
formulation of these compounds lies out of this thesis’ scope.  
Establishing this protocol will broaden the number of syngeneic murine cancer models in our 
group that can be used to study ACT of polyclonal T cells and the combination of ACT with 
other cancer drug therapies.

3 Methods and materials  
3.1 Cancer cell line  
The CT26 colon carcinoma cell line (CT26.WT CRL-2638, American Type Culture 
Collection) from the BALB/c strain was chosen as the murine syngeneic tumor model cell line 
throughout this thesis. The cells had been tested negative for pathogens upon arrival. Cells 
were cryo-preserved in liquid nitrogen in a freeze-medium consisting of 90 % Fetal Bovine 
Serum (FBS, #10500064, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and 10 % Dimethyl sulfoxide (#D2650, 
Sigma-Aldrich). Upon use, 6x106 cells were thawed in a 37°C water-bath and transferred to a 
TPP Tissue Culture Flask (#90076, Buch & Holm) with 37°C RPIMI-1640 Medium, 
Glutamax™ Supplement (#61870, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was supplemented with 10 % 
FBS and 1 % Penicillin-Streptomycin (10,000 U/mL) (#15140, Thermo Fisher Scientific) 
referred to as RPMI-1640 hereafter. Cells were passaged by washing with sterile Dulbecco’s 
phosphate buffer saline without Ca2+ and without Mg2+ (PBS) (#ALB-L0615, Biowest) to 
remove excess medium. Next, Trypsin-EDTA 0.25 % was added (#T4049, Sigma-Aldrich) and 
the flask was kept in incubator at 37°C for 1 min to detach adherent cells followed by the 
addition of RPMI-1640 to stop the trypsination. To subculture cells, up to 90 % of cell 
suspension was removed (depending on the confluency of cells) and fresh 37°C warm RPMI-
1640 was added. Cells were subcultured every second day until harvest as described 
previously. Whenever the cells were not handled, they were maintained in a humidified 5 % 
CO2 incubator at 37°C, unless otherwise stated.  
At the day of inoculation, cells were harvested at their exponential growth phase by 
trypsination with Trypsin-EDTA 0.25 %, washed with Hanks’ Balanced Salt Solution 
(#14025092, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and centrifuged at 300xG for 5 min. The cell pellet was 
resuspended in HBSS to a final concentration of 3x106 cells/mL. Countess™ II Automated Cell 
counter (#AMQAX1000, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used to determine cell density and 
viability using the supplied cell counting slides and trypan blue solution 0.4 % (#C10228, 
Thermo Fisher Scientific) for exclusion of dead cells. Cell suspension was kept on ice until 
inoculation.  
3.2 Animal tumor model 
Female 6-week-old BALB/cJrJ mice were purchased from Janvier Labs (Le Genest, France) 
and housed at the Department of Experimental Medicine, University of Copenhagen. At all 
times, mice had access to chow and water ad libitum. Mice were acclimatized for at least one 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
13 
week and were at the age of 7-9 weeks when included in experiments. All animal experiments 
were approved by and conducted under the guidelines of The Danish Animal Experiments 
Inspectorate in compliance with the directive 2010/63/EU of the European Parliament on the 
protection of animal used for scientific purposes.  
3.2.1 Tumor challenge 
To establish the syngeneic murine tumor model, BALB/c recipient mice were subcutaneously 
injected with 3x105 viable CT26 cells suspended in 100 µL HBSS suspension (3x106 cells/mL) 
into the right flank under inhalation of anesthesia with ~5 % sevoflurane (#FDG9117, Baxter) 
mixed with 35 % O2 in N2. Tumors were allowed to grow for 9-13 days before mice were 
randomized and assigned into treatment groups based on tumor volume to avoid biased volume 
distribution. Treatment was carried out the following day.  
A complete responder was defined as a mouse that is tumor-free on day 100 after inoculation. 
In some studies, the antitumor immunity of complete responders was challenged by inoculating 
mice on the distal left flank. The rechallenge was performed with the same cell linage, number 
of cells, and by the same procedures as the initial tumor challenge. Rechallenges are indicated 
with vertical dotted lines on Kaplan-Meier curves throughout this thesis. 
3.3 Generation of in vivo-primed tumor-draining lymph node cells  
To obtain tumor-specific T cells, BALB/c donor mice were inoculated with the same batch of 
CT26 cancer cells and on the same day as tumor challenged mice. The inoculation was 
performed as described Section 3.2.1, however, cells were injected in both right and left flank 
to maximize the yield per donor mice.   
3.3.1 Isolation of tumor-draining lymph node cells 
After 5-9 days, donor mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation and the tdLN (here inguinal 
lymph nodes) were removed and suspended in an Eppendorf tube containing MACS’ Tissue 
Storage Solution (#130-100-008, Miltenyi Biotec) placed on ice. To obtain a single-cell 
suspension, lymph nodes were mechanically dispersed by filtering them through a 70 µm cell 
strainer (#43-10070, pluriSelect) with the piston from a syringe while flushing with room 
temperature sterile PBS. Cells were centrifuged at 500xG for 5 min. and the pellet was 
resuspended in RPMI-1640 supplemented with 1% Insulin-Transferrin-Sodium selenite Liquid 
Media Supplement 100x (ITS, #I3146, Sigma-Aldrich).  
==PAGE:22==
 
 
14 
In addition, inguinal lymph nodes from mice that were not inoculated with CT26 were isolated 
and were used as a control for unspecific lymph node cells, hereafter entitled non-tumor bearing 
mice lymph node (nLN).  
3.3.2 In vitro activation and expansion of tumor-draining lymph node cells 
24 hours prior to tdLN harvest, 6-well tissue plates were coated with 0.5 µg/mL InVivoMab 
anti-mouse CD3ε (clone: 145-2C11, #BE0001-1, Nordic BioSite) and 5 µg/mL InVivoMab 
anti-mouse CD28 antibodies (clone: 37.51, #BE0015-1, Nordic BioSite) dissolved in room 
temperature sterile PBS.  
tdLN cells in the single-cell suspension were plated at a concentration of 1-2 x 106 cells/mL 
into the pre-coated 6-well plates and incubated for 24h in 37°C in 5% CO2 at day 0. At day 1, 
20 ng/mL recombinant murine IL-2 (#402-ML, R&D Systems) and 5 ng/mL IL-7 (#407-ML, 
R&D Systems) were added to the cell media to stimulate the cells and incubated at 37°C for 
another 24h in 5% CO2. The following day (day 2), cells were flushed off the bottom of the 
coated plates with room temperature PBS and resuspend to 0.5x106 cells/mL in RPMI-1640 
supplemented with 1% ITS as well as additional IL-2 and IL-7 for a final concentration of 20 
ng/mL and 5 ng/mL, respectively. The cells were transferred to an uncoated 6-well plate and 
incubated for another 24h. On day 3, cells were resuspended in 25 ng/mL IL-21 (#594-ML, 
R&D systems) containing RPMI-1640 further supplemented with 1% ITS to a final 
concentration of 0.25x106 cells/mL. Cells were transferred to tissue flasks and incubated until 
harvest 24h later. nLN cells underwent same activation and expansion produce as described 
above.  
3.4 Adoptive cell transfer therapy using tumor draining lymph node cells 
3.4.1 Adoptive transfer of expanded tumor-draining lymph node cells  
After 4 days of expansion, tdLN cells were prepared for ACT. Firstly, cells from flasks were 
harvested into tubes before being spun for 5 min at 500xG. Secondly, cells were washed in 
HBSS and spun again at 500xG for 5 min. Lastly, cells were counted on the electronic cell 
counter and resuspended in HBSS to a final concentration of 100x106 viable cells/mL kept on 
ice. ACT treatment was performed by intravenously injecting 100 µL cell suspension in the 
tail vein of the recipient mouse, equivalent to 10x106 tdLN cells unless otherwise is stated.

3.4.2 Total body irradiation and chemotherapeutic lymphodepletion prior to therapy 
To enhance the antitumor effect of tdLN treatment, TBI was carried out as a non-myeloablative 
lymphodepletion preconditioning. Pairs mice were placed in a circular animal chamber placed 
in the center of radiation chamber of the X-RAD320 (Precision X-Ray, North Branford) to 
equally spread the exposure. The initial radiation dose was chosen to be 5 Gy at a rate of 1 
Gy/min as described by Kueberuwa et al. 2018 (30). However due to sudden systemic toxicity 
signs such as weight loss and diarrhea, acute radiation sickness was suspected as a consequence 
of radiation exposure. In the later experiment with TBI, the dose was lowered to 3 Gy with a 
rate of 1 Gy/min, which was safer and only caused minor weight loss.  
As an alternative preconditioning, 2 mg cyclophosphamide (CPX) (Sendoxan® injection 
solution, Baxter) was administered by intravenous injection into the tail vein. The dose is 
equivalent to ~100 mg/kg. Preconditioning with CPX or TBI was performed a day prior to 
ACT or/and R848-gel treatment.

4.1 Characterizing ex vivo expansion of tumor-draining- and non-tumor-
draining lymph node cells  
The procedure used for ex vivo activation and expansion has been development by my co-
supervisor Ditte Jæhger (Colloids and Biological Interfaces). Up until this study, it had only 
been used for expanding TCR-transgenic T cells transgenic murine strains. Thus, first thing to 
asses was whether it was feasible to expand tdLN cells into an amount T cells that would be 
usable for ACT. The aim was to produce at least 10x106 per lymph node as other studies 
investigating tumor draining-lymph node cells (tdLN) for ACT in similar models had reported 
tdLN cell doses ranging from 10x106 to 30x106 cells (123, 124). Further, the aim was to expand 
tdLN cells with a central memory phenotype with low degree of exhaustion. Earlier studies 
had suggested ACT of central memory CD8+ resulted in a better antitumor outcome, showed 
long-lived persistence and memory to antigen challenge after treatment (125, 126). Thus, the 
goal was to clarify whether central memory T cell (TCM) phenotype was generated after tdLN 
cell expansion.  
The objective was to characterize the tdLN cells before and after expansion and compare their 
expansion capacity and phenotype with LN cells from non-tumor-bearing mice. To test this, 
lymph nodes from tumor-bearing mice (tdLN) and non-tumor-bearing mice (nLN) were 
harvested 10 days after tumor cell inoculation. In short, lymph node cells were activated and 
expanded ex vivo by plating them into anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 coated wells, and stimulating 
the cells with IL-2, IL-7 and IL-21 as described in methods (Section 3.3). Six tdLN and six 
nLN were pooled in pairs during cell isolation, however one of the nLN cultures failed to 
expand, thus only two biological replicates are represented in nLN-group from this experiment. 
Total number of cells were counted, and the cell product was analyzed by flow cytometry 
before expansion (day 0) and after expansion (day 4). Figure 3A provides an overview of the 
experimental timeline.

also includes expansion data (uncolored symbols) from six other independent cell expansions 
used for in vivo experiments described later in this thesis.

4.2 Efficacy evaluation of tdLN cells  
Next, the immunotherapeutic potential of the tdLN cells were examined. An in vivo efficacy 
analysis study was set up by ACT tdLN cells into established tumors to test the antitumor 
properties of the cells. The hypothesis was that if the tdLN cells was tumor-specific and 
demonstrated antitumor phenotypes, transferring the cells to tumor-bearing recipient would 
inhibit tumor growth and prolong survival.  
4.2.1 Initial adoptive T cell transfer study of tumor-draining lymph node cells 
To evaluate the efficacy of the cells, six-week-old BALB/cJrJ mice were inoculated with CT26 
colon carcinoma cells and divided into donor and recipient groups. tdLN were harvested from 
donor mice on day 10 and further activated and expanded as previously described in methods 
(Section 3.3).  
On day 14, the tumors on recipient mice had reached an average size of 49.07 ± 22.44 mm3. 
Due to a limited number of expanded cells, only 7.5x106 cells were intravenously injected into 
recipient mice although 10x106 cells were the goal. Figure 4A outlines the experimental 
timeline for the efficacy study.  
Figure 4B displays the mean tumor growth of the tdLN cell recipient group (tdLN) compared 
to the untreated group. Here, there was a tendency for a minor inhibition in the tdLN-group. 
Comparing individual mouse tumor growth curves between groups in Figure 4C, only one 
mouse responded to treatment in the tdLN-group with complete eradication of the tumor until 
day 105 where the tumor relapsed. No difference in survival curves between tdLN-group and

mouse in each group was euthanized due to superficial ulcerations on the tumor surface. 
Moreover, no weight differences were observed in the tdLN-group when compared with 
untreated mice (Figure 4E)  
Overall, the results from the initial in vivo efficacy study suggested no significant improvement 
in survival was observed when ACT tdLN cells to tumor-bearing mice (P = 0.0556). One 
responder was observed among tdLN cell treated mice which motivated the further

4.2.2 Enhancing the antitumor response by preconditioning with total body irradiation  
Previous studies have reported that lymphodepleting TBI can improve the antitumor immune 
response of tumor-reactive T cells (77, 88, 90, 127). Lymphodepletion of the recipient prior to 
ACT have been shown to promote a more favorable environment for tumor-reactive T cells to 
overcome the tumor burden (75, 90).  
As described in previously literature, 5Gy was chosen as the optimal dose for lymphodepletion 
during the experimental setup (30, 128). In brief, both donor and recipient mice were inoculated 
and tdLN or nLN were harvested and cultured from day 8-12. On day 11, recipient mice were 
measured randomized into groups with an average tumor volume of 45.9 ± 21.5 mm3. Every 
recipient group received 5Gy at a rate of 1Gy/min except for the untreated control group. 
On day 12, recipient mice were treated with either 8x106 tdLN cells (TBI + tdLN), an 
equivalent number of nLN cells (TBI + nLN) or no cells at all (TBI). The dose of 8x106 nLN 
cells was chosen due to lower expansion of nLN cells compared to tdLN cells. The nLN-group 
acted as a treatment control to examine the tumor-specificity of tdLN cells. Efficacy analysis 
followed and survivors past day 144 were re-challenged by the same cancer cell line to examine 
the long-lasting antitumor immune response. An overview of the experimental setup is 
visualized in Figure 5A.  
The TBI + tdLN-group showed a substantial tumor growth delay after day 22 while 
administration of nLN showed no growth inhibition compared to TBI-group or the untreated 
group (Figure 5B). Interestingly, every non-censored mouse from the TBI + tdLN-group 
survived longer than any other mouse in the study. However, two tumors started to relapse after 
day 40 and another two tumors relapsed after day 80 in the TBI + tdLN-group (Figure 5C). 
2/6 mice in TBI + tdLN-group displayed complete remission and survived tumor re-challenge 
at day 144 without any tumor growth after re-exposure to the cancer cell line compared to no 
survivors in other groups (Figure 5D). Unexpectedly, mice began to show signs of toxicity 10 
days after TBI and several mice were euthanized due to acute weight loss (>15% loss between 
measurements) and overall a large drop in body weight was observed in every TBI exposed 
group. Mice that survived past day 30 regained their normal weight increase (Figure 5E).

proliferation, infiltration and antitumor properties of both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells(99, 136,

as monotherapy are observed (Figure 7C). CPX + tdLN + R848-group had a median survival 
time of 125 days compared to 37.5 days (CPX) and 35 days (CPX + tdLN). Encouraging

complete responders compared to 1/8 responder in each CPX and CPX + tdLN-group.

4.4.3 Evaluating the antitumor efficacy of tdLN in Deep-Primed™ IL-12 technology 
platform 
To further test the tdLN cells antitumor potential were evaluated in in combination with Torque 
Therapeutics established Deep-Primed™ IL-12 drug delivery platform (also known as 
PRIMEIL-12).  
Previous preclinical studies of IL-12 have proposed it as a promising candidate for 
immunotherapy as it possesses several antitumor properties both when administered during ex 
vivo expansion and when given intravenously (142, 143). However, in a clinical setting, the 
use of IL-12 has been limited due to complications observed when administered both 
intravenously as well subcutaneously (144-147). Here, the Deep-Primed™ IL-12 platform 
provides safe delivery of IL-12 with an improved efficacy (148).  
To examine the therapeutic efficacy of Deep-Primed™ IL-12 tdLN cells, tumor challenged 
mice were treated with tdLN either as monotherapy, with systemic co-administration of soluble 
IL-12 or as Deep-Primed IL-12™ tdLN cells (Figure 9A#1). Deep-Primed™ IL-12 tdLN cells 
were generated according to Torque Therapeutics’ protocols by tethering Deep IL-12 particles 
onto tdLN cells (Figure 9B). No overt toxicity or severe weight loss were observed in any 
treatment groups (Figure 9C). Tumors reached an average volume of 32 mm3 ± 9.96 on day 9 
(a day prior to treatment). Deep-Primed IL-12 tdLN cells induced a growth inhibition in 7/8 
mice compared to 5/7 in tdLN + Syst. IL-12 group or 3/7 when tdLN cells were given as 
monotherapy (Figure S5) Deep-Primed IL-12 resulted in a delayed growth overall (Figure 
9D). In addition, mice treated with Deep- Primed IL-12 tdLN resulted in 6/8 complete 
responders compared to 3/8 for tdLN + Syst. IL-12 or 2/7 for tdLN as monotherapy. All 
complete responders survived an additional re-challenged (Figure 9E).  
Remarkably, all groups that received tdLN cells had complete responders. Though, it is 
important to mention that the tumor burden was low in every group. To further evaluate Deep-

5 Discussion 
Many cell-based approaches have been investigated for treating cancer. While great 
accomplishments have been obtained in hematological cancers using genetically engineered 
CAR-T cells, researchers and clinicians have been unable to translate these results into 
treatment of solid cancers (29). Main obstacles in ACT therapy of solid cancers are the loss of 
antigen-presentation or antigen-expression due to selective pressure, leaving no target for the 
transferred T cells (14, 67, 149, 150). Another challenge of ACT is the hostile 
immunosuppressive TME, affecting T cell infiltration and function (89, 151). One way to 
reverse the immunosuppressiveness is by administering immune stimulating drugs to support 
infused T cells (99, 105, 152, 153).  
While the use of CAR-T and TILs for therapy are well-described, using tdLN as a source of 
tumor-reactive cells are less described, but offers an alternative to the more established options. 
In breast and colorectal cancer patients, one or more tdLN is resected during the tumor surgery 
to evaluate whether the cancer has spread. Thus, resected tdLN offer a clinically relevant source 
of potential tumor-reactive T cells (154). It has also been reported that tdLN cells perform 
better than peripheral blood cells and TILs when stimulated with autologous tumor homogenate 
(155).  
The aim of this thesis was to generate polyclonal tumor-reactive T cells from tdLN with 
therapeutic efficacy against the murine cancer model, CT26. To further enhance the efficacy 
of the transferred cells, irradiation- and chemotherapeutic lymphodepleting regimens were also 
investigated. Finally, ACT based on this approach was investigated in combination with  
immunotherapies developed in the group as strategies to overcome the suppressive TME.   
5.1 Adoptive cell transfer of tdLN demonstrates antitumor potential  
5.1.1 Ex vivo expansion protocol generates T cells with memory phenotype  
The initial objective of this thesis was to generate T lymphocytes with a low-degree of 
differentiation and exhaustion, since these parameters are important for the cells’ antitumor 
efficacy during ACT (156-158). A study by Gattioni et al. (2005) from Rosenberg’s lab 
evaluated how the differentiation of CD8+ T cells correlates with the efficacy of ACT-therapy. 
Paradoxically, despite demonstrating strong tumor cell-killing in vitro, terminally 
differentiated effector CD8+ T cells show minor efficacy in vivo compared with TCM cells 
(CD62LHigh/CCR7+) (159). The finding that the degree of differentiation is inversely correlated 
with the efficacy of T cells has also been reported in other studies (34-36, 126, 156). Thus, the 
==PAGE:47==
 
 
39 
high percentage of TCM cells obtained after expansion (E+G) indicates a favorable cell-product 
for ACT-therapy in vivo. Additional markers such as Ly6C, C-C Chemokine receptor 7 
(CCR7), CD122 and transcriptional factor 1 and 7 (TCF7/TCF1) have been described to 
characterize a TCM population. Adding especially CCR7 in the phenotyping, would be 
beneficial to include in the phenotype panel in future work in order to optimize the presentation 
of TCM gating in flow (159-164). Interestingly, whereas IL-2 stimulation has been shown to 
drive differentiation into an effector-like phenotype, the addition of IL-21 during expansion 
has been suggested to be key factor for suppression of T effector-type differentiation and 
maintaining the low-differentiation state (156, 162, 165). Thus, a possible explanation for the 
high degree of TCM in the population, is the addition of IL-21 on day 3 (Methods section 3.3)  
Analysis of PD-1 expression in TNaïve, TEM and TCM populations after expansion showed a 
gradient increasing intensity of PD-1 expression from TNaïve to TEM. It would have been 
beneficial to include a sample of high PD-1 expression, such as positive PD-1High-controls 
which could be prvoked by a prolonged culturing process with IL-2 stimulation and autologous 
tumor lysate. In general, the represented data is in accordance with findings described in a 
study by Duraiswamy et al. (2011), in which they reported CD8+PD-1High expression to be 
associated with TEM (CD8+CCR7-) compared with TCM (CD8+CCR7+) phenotype (166). In the 
last decade, researchers have investigated the addition of inhibitors of Akt and Glycogen 
Synthase Kinase-3" (GSK-3 "), which are involved in T cell differentiation pathways and the 
regulation of PD-1 expression of T cells (167-170). In a preclinical study by Crompton et al. 
(2001), Akt inhibition during ex vivo expansion of T cells caused expansion of a larger 
proportion of T cells with a memory profile defined by CD62L expression and resulted in slight 
enhanced efficacy in vivo (168).  
Altogether, the memory phenotype and exhaustion level displayed a low degree of 
differentiation, which is in accordance with presented studies is optimal for antitumor efficacy. 
To further advance the proportion of TCM cells harvested from the expansion, addition of Akt 
and/or GSK-3 " inhibitors could possibly increase the therapeutic potential of the cells. Sorting 
of favorable cell phenotypes could also enhance the potential, although it would increase the 
workload of the culturing process.   
5.1.2 In vivo-priming with tumor cells generates tumor-reactive T cells  
The initial efficacy study of tdLN cells (Figure 2) was unable to demonstrate significant 
antitumor effects. This outcome was contradictory to previous studies by Ammori et al. (2015), 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
40 
in which they reported the use of tdLN cells having a substantial antitumor effect in the murine 
syngeneic model, 4T1. However, although they use a different tumor model, a possible 
explanation for the observed efficacy, is that the treatment starts on day 3 after tumor 
inoculation, hence the tumor volume is expected to be small (62). The observation that the 
tumor challenge at the time of treatment strongly correlates with the outcome, is indicated in 
Figure 7D-G, in which tdLN cell treatment alone resulted in 2 complete responders in small 
tumors and none in larger tumors, without any preconditioning. In the current study, treatment 
with tdLN cells alone either with or without preconditioning showed inconsistent antitumor 
effects. No improvement in survival was observed of tdLN cell treatment compared to 
untreated or CPX preconditioning, as can be seen in Figure 4 and Figure 7. Likewise, as can 
be seen in Figure 9, when tdLN cells were administered together with systemic IL-12, no 
differences were observed. However, in the experiment presented in Figure 5, ACT of tdLN 
cells mediated a superior antitumor efficacy when compared with nLN cells from non-tumor 
bearing mice, indicating that the in vivo vaccination of tdLN cells are tumor-reactive to some 
degree. Additionally, in tumor growth curves presented in Figure 8B-C, tdLN cells in 
combination with CPX shows a tendency to inhibit tumor growth. The inhibition is not 
significant when compared with preconditioning with CPX alone.  
An explanation for unpredictability of tdLN cell treatment might be that the proportion of 
tumor-reactive tdLN cells are relatively low (and possibly variable) in tdLN cells. Low 
specificity has been observed in TILs in Cohen et al. (2015) (171). To generate highly specific 
T cell clones, immunogenic antigens must be presented by APCs to T cells with cognate T cell 
receptor (TCR). The immunome of the CT26 model used in this thesis was mapped by Castle 
et al. (2014). Here, they found that the gp70 antigen, also known as AH-1, a tumor-specific 
antigen is the highly expressed and is the dominant epitope. The group later tested CD8+ T cells 
response to neoepitopes found in the CT26 cancer cell line. Here, CD8+ T cells from 
splenocytes harvested 28 days after CT26 tumor inoculation, only mounted a significant 
immune response against the AH-1 antigen when exposed to a library of predicted CT26 (172).  
A method to antigen-dependent expand tdLN cells could be to pulse APCs with AH-1. These 
AH-1 presenting cells would then be co-cultured during expansion to increase the number of 
tumor-reactive T cells. Two studies have used the approach of designing artificial APCs 
presenting tumor antigens on their surface. These artificial APCs are then added to the culture 
during expansion of a pool of T cells (173, 174). Of particular interest, Perica et al. (2015) 
designed three CT26-derived candidate neoepitopes conjugated to MHC class I molecules 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
41 
present on surface these artificial APCs (Ld-SAF: SAFFSSFLM), (Ld-FPB: FPSENKHVY) 
and the AH-1 epitope (Ld-A5). They show an increased binding of cells expressing the cognate 
TCR of CD8+ T cells. Not only is this approach labor intensive, another disadvantage is that it 
only results in outgrowth of CD8+ T cells (173). Other researchers have used peptide-pulsed 
DCs to selectively induce tumor-specific T cell outgrowth during culturing (175) or used whole 
autologous tumor lysate (65, 176, 177). Finally, Dijkstra et al (2018) reported a protocol of co-
culture T cells together with tumor organoids that had the potential to generate antigen-specific 
T cell outgrowth (178). However, in the case of CT26 cells, it would only be CD8+ T cells, 
which would be enriched, since the CT26 does not express MHC II as previously described. 
Another consequence is that it might result in exhaustion of T cells due to continuous exposure 
to the tumor cells. 
All in all, these approaches could maybe increase the number of tumor-specific T cells, but it 
would lead to a significantly increased workload and be more costly. This would also require 
a sensitive in vitro cytolytic-assay to quantitively measure the amount of tumor-specific T cells. 
The current cytotoxic killing-assay standard, 51Cr-release assay, has a low sensitivity and is 
impractical due to handling of the waste isotopes (179). Thus, there may be a need for a more 
highly sensitive assay, to measure tdLN cell responses against CT26, due to an expected low 
number of tumor-specific T cells. Once again it is important to bear in mind that determining 
the antitumor potential of tdLN cells in vitro does not always translate directly to in vivo 
efficacy (159).  
5.1.3 CD4+ and CD8+ T cells’ engraftment in tumor and tumor draining lymph node 
As no functional MHC II molecules are expressed in the CT26 model, only CD8+ T cells are 
expected to be directly involved in tumor recognition. This is in line with the results presented 
in this study, showing that the majority of donor-derived T cells found in the tumor bed were 
CD8+, even though CD4+ T cells comprised >60% of the injected cell product (Figure 6B). 
Still, donor-derived CD4+ T cells made up of a smaller proportion of infiltrated cells (Figure 
6C). In pancreatic adenocarcinoma, CD8+ but also CD4+ T cell tumor infiltration has been 
described as a positive prognostic marker for clinical outcome after surgery (180). 
The role of CD4+ T cells cannot be excluded in the antitumor mechanism observed in the 
current study. Numerous studies have investigated the role of CD4+ T cells in tumor responses. 
In Tran et al. (2014), they report a clinical case of a patient receiving mutation-specific CD4+ 
T cells harvested from TILs, induced stabilization of the disease for more than 1 year (132). 
Similar observations have been made in a preclinical study by Bracci et al. (2007). Here, they 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
42 
found CD4+ T cells to be required for the antitumor immunity in the B16 melanoma model 
(128). Supporting this finding, a study by Saida et al. (2015) found that donor-CD4+ T cells 
derived from tdLN are required for antitumor efficacy in MHC class II negative tumor-bearing 
mice. Here mice were given a CD4+-depleted cell-product, which augmented significant tumor 
regression, whereas CD8+-depleted cells showed no antitumor immunity (181), consistent with 
a later study from the same research department (182). 
The notion that CD4+ T cells can play a critical role in MHC class II negative tumors has been 
described by Oseendrop et al. (1998). Herein, they report that vaccination with a MHC class 
II-directed peptide resulted in tumor eradication, mediated by intrinsic CD8+ cytotoxic T cells. 
CD4+ T cells were suggested to promote cross-priming of tumor antigens to CD8+ cytolytic T 
cells (183) in line with the findings from Schoenberger et al. (1998), which suggested that 
CD4+ T cells activate APCs through CD40L/CD40 axis (184). A possible explanation 
described is that the increased CD40L expression on CD4+ T cells is caused by TCR binding 
to MHC class II on lymph node-resident cDC1 during antigen-presentation. Subsequently, 
CD40L binds to CD40, stimulating IL-12 secretion and expression of the co-stimulatory 
molecules, CD80 and CD86, ultimately led to priming and differentiation of CD8+ T cells 
(129). Furthermore, CD40L-dependent activation of cDCs by CD4+ T cells, has been reported 
to be involved in recruitment of naïve T cells to the tdLN (185). The recruitment of naïve T 
cells to be primed in the lymph node by cDC is potential mediator of the increase in endogenous 
CD8+ T cells infiltrating the tumor in mice treated with tdLN cells, although no increase in 
CD8+ T cells in the lymph node was observed (Figure 6C-D).  
Together, the reported studies indicate that CD4+ tdLN T cells might play a critical role in 
enhancing the antitumor response through recruitment and cross-priming of CD8+ T cells, even 
though the majority of tdLN-derived T cells infiltrating the tumor were CD8+. No conclusion 
can be drawn from the experiments presented in this thesis since CD4- and/or CD8-depleted 
tdLN cells was not evaluated.   
5.2 Combination therapies improve the therapeutic potential of tdLN cells  
5.2.1 Lymphodepleting preconditioning enhances the tdLN-treated efficacy  
Strategies to increase the potential of ACT have been explored in numerous studies. An 
established approach is to deplete endogenous lymphocytes prior to treatment by administering 
systemic chemotherapeutic agents or by TBI of patients, which have improved the responses 
of ACT therapy (39, 53, 83, 186, 187). Consistent with literature, an improved efficacy was 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
43 
observed when recipient mice were exposed to TBI (Figure 5B-D) or CPX (Figure 8B-C) 
prior to tdLN treatment, compared to the initial study where no preconditioning regimes were 
performed (Figure 4B-D). Unfortunately, complete responders were observed as a 
consequence of preconditioning with CPX, which blurred the effects of tdLN cells themselves 
(Figure 7B-D, Figure 8A-C). Thus, TBI seemed to be a better preconditioning regimen to 
investigate the effects of the adoptively transferred tdLN cells (Figure 5B-E). However, strong 
toxicities were observed when exposing mice to 5 Gy TBI, which have not been described 
previously (30) This led to a lowered dose in the following experiment (Figure 8D-F),. How 
this lowered dose affected the lymphodepletion is uncertain, since the number of lymphocytes 
in the peripheral blood were not assessed before and after preconditioning. However, later we 
discovered that a study from Douglas Grahn (1958) had reported a lethal dose-50 (within 30 
days) for the BALB/c mouse strain to be approximately 5 Gy compared to 6.3 Gy in the 
C57BL/6 strain, which may explain the toxicities observed. It should be taken into account that 
Grahn also showed that younger BALB/c have a higher threshold (188), so age is also a 
consideration. Nonetheless, the intensity of the preconditioning correlates with the antitumor 
efficacy of the transferred cells as reported by Wrzesinski et al. (2010) (89).  
Data from donor-derived CD4+ and CD8+ T cells engrafted in the lymph node (Figure 6D-E) 
suggests that adoptively transferred tdLN cells proliferate in vivo, indicated by low CellTrace 
Violet intensity measured. This may be caused by tumor antigen exposure to tdLN cells in the 
lymph node, which cause activation and proliferation of tdLN cells. Alternatively, this 
proliferation can be explained by a homeostatic proliferation, caused by the lymphodepletion 
as previously described in several studies (90, 128, 189). Hence, the data is inconclusive and 
must be interpreted with caution. The underlying mechanisms is endogenous lymphocytes 
under normal conditions act as so-called cytokine sinks. After lymphodepletion, the 
competition for the homeostatic cytokines, present in the secondary lymphoid organs, are 
lowered, resulting in proliferation of the transferred cells (75). In Gattinoni et al. (2005) they 
report that the homeostatic proliferation diminished in IL-7 and IL-15 deficient mice and was 
correlated with an impaired efficacy of adoptively transferred T cells. The antitumor immunity 
could be restored by administering recombinant IL-7 and IL-15 (90). Both IL-7 and IL-15 have 
been reported to be important to homeostasis of T cells and maintenance of naïve and memory 
populations (190, 191). Thus, there may be beneficial effects of homeostatic proliferation in 
establishing antitumor immunity. 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
44 
Since both CPX and TBI are cytolytic to some degree, it has been proposed that immunological 
killing of tumor cells, caused by the preconditioning regimes, can cause a release of antigens. 
Antigens may be endocytosed by cDC1 cells and subsequently cross-presented to adoptively 
transferred tumor-reactive CD8+ T cells (162). Furthermore, studies have reported 
lymphodepleting preconditioning to support APC function during ACT. Brown et al. (2004) 
reported that irradiation conditioning can cause secretion of tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-
!) from macrophages and CD3+ T cells (192). In support of these findings, a study by Torihata 
et al. (2004) showed that a TNF-! exposure to DCs following irradiation resulted in an 
upregulation of the costimulatory molecule, CD80, through NF-%B (193). In addition, 
upregulation of CD86 caused by irradiation is reported in Zhang et al. (2002), who additionally 
find an upregulation of MHC class II molecules on CD11c+ DCs. This led to an increased 
stimulation in CD4+ T cells in vitro and activation of donor-derived T cells in vivo (87). 
Evidence from these studies indicates, that even though there is a decrease in the DC population 
after preconditioning, the DCs get activated and further support the adoptively transferred cells 
and possibly play a role in enhancing the antitumor efficacy. Hence, the preconditioning might 
not also give “space” for the transferred lymphocytes, but also activate endogenous DC to help 
boost the antitumor response. 
5.2.2 TLR7 agonist strengthens the antitumor potential of tdLN-therapy 
Different adjuvant therapies have been explored to support adoptively transferred cells 
combatting the immunosuppressive TME (103). In the current study, intratumoral 
administration of the TLR7/8a, R848, was evaluated in combination with treatment with tdLN 
cells. Here, a significantly improved survival was observed when combining ACT of tdLN 
cells with intratumoral administration of TLR7/8a, R848, in lymphodepleted mice (Figure 7B-
D). It cannot be ruled out that R848 administration alone was responsible for the seen efficacy, 
which limits the interpretation. Nevertheless, in a following study, bulk splenocytes without 
red blood cells from complete responders were transferred into tumor-challenged mice to 
evaluate the antitumor immunity of the responders’ T cells. Interestingly, mice receiving 
splenocytes from R848-cured mice had a significantly reduced survival time compared with 
mice receiving splenocytes from tdLN and R848-cured mice. Data also indicated that the 
complete responders that had been treated with combination therapy displayed a greater 
immunity than in complete responders that had been treated only with tdLN cells (Figure 8D-
F), suggesting that the stimulation of TLR7/8 is involved in improving the antitumor immunity.  
==PAGE:53==
 
 
45 
It can be hypothesized that R848 enhances the immunity by improving the presentation of 
tumor antigens released from tumor-killing, caused by the tumor-reactive T cells. This 
hypothesis is in accordance with a study published by Vascotto et al. (2019). The study 
examined the adaptive immunity of CT26 tumor-bearing mice treated intravenously, 
administering with multiple doses of the TLR7a, SC1. Here they demonstrate that TLR7a 
mediated tumor control of relatively small tumors (20-50 mm3) in a CD8+-dependent manner. 
Additionally, antitumor effects were mediated in by pDC activation and IFN-! secretion. The 
study also reported that both SC1 and R848 increased the number of AH-1-specific CD8+ T 
cells in the blood, spleen and tumor, supporting the idea that R848 can cause enriched tumor-
specific cells in vivo. This effect was also seen in splenectomized mice, indicating that the 
priming could take place in the lymph nodes (100). This is also supported by Nishii et al. (2018) 
(135). Related, Crespo et al. (2013) reported a mechanism of IFN-!-dependent cross-
presentation by cDC1 (CD8!+) cells to naïve CD8+ T cells, induced by TLR7a (PolyU) 
stimulation. In addition, TLR7a stimulation promoted stability of the MHC I:peptide complex 
on the DCs. Lastly, they identified that triggering cytolytic T cell responses by TLR7a required 
the presence CD4+ T cells (102).  
5.2.3 Cell-tethered IL-12 increases the potency of tdLN cells  
IL-12 have been reported to possess several immunoregulating properties (112). In a preclinical 
study from 1993, Brunda et al. showed that systemic administration of IL-12 augmented 
significant antitumor and antimetastatic effects in various murine cancer models. Here, they 
identified that the presence of CD8+ T cells was important for the antitumor response 
augmented by IL-12 (143).  
IL-12 may also play a key factor in reshaping the TME. A study conducted by Kerkar et al. 
(2011) showed that administration of IL-12 can reshape MDSCs into a pro-inflammatory 
profile, capable of both promoting the recruitment of CD8+ T cells, but also cross-present tumor 
antigens to the CD8+ T cells, which led to tumor regression (194). Similar findings were 
reported by Steding et al. (2011) where IL-12 also promoted upregulation of the costimulatory 
molecules, CD80 and CD86 on MDSC, indicating a shift toward an immunostimulatory 
phenotype. The study also reported that IL-12 increased the percentage of IFN-#+ CD8+ T cells 
in the tumor (195). Overall, these findings have been indicating IL-12 as a promising cytokine 
with antitumor properties. 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
46 
Significant clinical results of IL-12 treatment are yet to be reported together with the fact that 
systemic administration of IL-12 has been correlated with severe toxicities (112, 144-146). 
Therefore, safer approaches of IL-12 administration are currently needed. Thus, in this thesis, 
a delivery technique of cell-tethering IL-12 (Deep IL-12), developed by Torque Therapeutics, 
was evaluated together with tdLN cells for the first time. In the two efficacy experiments 
presented in (Figure 9A-G), tdLN cells primed with Deep IL-12 show superior tumor efficacy 
compared with tdLN cells alone and in combination with systemic IL-12, indicating that the 
IL-12 augments a boost in the tdLN cell antitumor immunity. It could be argued that the 
observed effect was caused by the Deep IL-12 particles acting as passengers on tdLN cells and 
eventually activated endogenous cells in the TME. Since no groups were treated with Deep IL-
12 particles alone, this explanation cannot be ruled out. The data implicates that the addition 
of Deep IL-12 correlates with notable antitumor efficacy, both in small (Figure 9D-E) and 
larger tumors (Figure 9F-G) without any prior preconditioning.

6 Conclusion 
The purpose of this thesis was to establish and evaluate a method for ex vivo-generation of 
tumor-reactive T cells, which could be used in ACT. In order to do so, tdLN from tumor-
bearing mice were chosen as the cell source. The hypothesis was that by challenging donor 
mice with tumor cells, T cells harvested from their tdLN would be primed for tumor-specificity 
and thus, promote tumor regression when transferred to tumor-challenged mice. Secondly, the 
study set out to explore whether additional therapies such as lymphodepleting regimes as well 
as the TLR7 agonist gel, R848 and cell-tethered IL-12 could further support the adoptively 
transferred T cells in augmenting an antitumor response.  
On the basis of the data presented in this thesis, tdLN cells can be harnessed as a viable source 
of tumor-reactive T cells with antitumor potential. Overall, with the approach of in vivo-
priming, ex vivo expansion and differentiation of tdLN cells, it was feasible in generating a 
high percentage of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells with a favorable memory phenotype. Transfer of 
these cells into tumor-challenged mice augmented tumor regression, prolonged survival and 
long-term memory when combined with lymphodepleting regimens. The effects could further 
be enhanced either by applying a cell-tethered IL-12 to the surface of the cells or by 
administrating a R848-gel intratumorally. The results from the TME analysis indicated that 
CD8+ T cells were involved in direct recognition of the tumor cells while CD4+ T cells might 
also be involved. Several studies have reported CD4+ T cells to be critical factor in the 
antitumor response, however this current study did not further investigate the involvement of 
CD4+ T cells.   
The mode-of-action of the adoptively transferred cells is yet to be elucidated and additional 
research is required to reveal the underlying mechanisms for the observed antitumor efficacy. 
A natural progression of the work would be to examine the tdLN cells’ tumor-specificity by 
evaluating specific tumor cell-killing in vitro. In addition, the depletion of CD4+ or CD8+ T 
cells from the expanded cell product before infusion would provide further details that could 
help unravel the mechanisms causing the observed antitumor immunity. Future research may 
also explore the use of tdLN cells in other tumor- and metastasis murine models to broaden 
knowledge of the adaptive immune response and TME. A better understanding of the tumor 
models in relation to ACT, would improve translation of preclinical data into clinical setting, 
ultimately led to development of better immunotherapeutic treatments for cancer.
