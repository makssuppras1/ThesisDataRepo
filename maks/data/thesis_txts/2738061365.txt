Abstract
In this thesis, two optimization models are posed for calibrating camera orientation based on a digital
elevation model (DEM) for golf broadcasting. The first method is a segmentation-based approach relying
on provided aerial segmentations of the golf course. The second method is a feature-based approach relying
on a provided aerial photo of the course.
Both models employ a differentiable renderer to render the surface mesh (DEM). This renderer is capable of
relating a rendered 2D image back to the spatial 3D data it was rendered from. The camera’s orientation is
the optimizable variable. An image is rendered for a specific orientation for which a loss function compares
it to a reference image. After computing the loss, the differentiable renderer relates the loss back to the
mesh and adjusts the camera’s orientation to minimize loss.
For the segmentation-based model, the DEM is ”colored” according to a provided aerial segmentation,
resulting in a rendered segmented image.
The two can be compared using the Focal Tversky Loss by
segmenting corresponding features in the reference image.
For the feature-based model, the DEM is ”colored” according to a provided aerial photo, resulting in a
rendered image that resembles reality. By using a feature extractor on the rendered image and the reference
image, the feature vectors can be compared to compute the loss.
Different techniques and pre-trained
networks (VGG-16 and DINOv2) were explored for feature extraction. The middle blocks of DINOv2 showed
the most suitable results for feature extraction, with cosine loss being the best choice of loss function.
For both models, hyperparameters were determined by a random hyperparameters search. Both models were
tested on 17 cameras near a green and 17 images near a tee area, all on the same course at TPC Summerlin
in Las Vegas. The segmentation-based models showed better results than the feature-based model and the
existing solution, using IoU as a metric. This difference was shown to be statistically significant using the
Wilcoxon signed-rank test. The feature-based model showed a similar result to the current implementation
for tees but a lower performance for greens.
Both models posed, along with techniques explored in this thesis, show promising results that can be gener-
alized to sports broadcasting and camera calibration in general.

Introduction
1

Introduction
In the realm of sports broadcasting, camera calibration is a crucial component. Many broadcasters have
incorporated graphics to enhance the viewing experience and provide clearer, more precise, and visually
compelling sports content.
An example is the use of virtual offside lines at the 2024 UEFAs European
Championships, which informs the viewer and aids the referee. Thus, it is crucial that these lines are drawn
correctly, as it influences the match, which is only possible if the camera is calibrated with high precision.
In contrast to football, golf is a sport experiencing decreased viewership. A big reason for this is that it is a
difficult sport to watch on TV, especially given the ball is so tiny and vanishes quickly into the background
after being struck. To enhance the viewing experience, Trackman provides a ball tracer that appears on
the broadcast following the ball trajectory. To this end, Trackman mounts 36 radars for each tournament
to track the flight of the ball. While the radars have a high precision, the data is captured in the radar’s
internal coordinate system and has to be related to “real world” coordinates.
Currently, unit calibration is a semi-manual process. It involves the use of a GPS for translation and an
inclinometer for an initial guess for tilt and roll. Additionally, several real-world 3D coordinates are measured
for easily identifiable objects on the course to calibrate the pan value. The orientation is then manually
adjusted using a built-in camera in the radar unit to align the projected coordinates to the respective object
in the image. This process is time-consuming and susceptible to human error, highlighting the need for a
more efficient and accurate method.
This thesis explores the feasibility of a fully automated camera calibration using a digital elevation model
(DEM). For the scope of this thesis, the calibration is limited to the orientation (tilt, pan, and roll) of the
built-in camera in the radar unit. By finding the orientation that aligns the projected DEM on the image,
the radar coordinate system can be related to the real world, as the real-world 3D coordinates of the DEM
are known, as well as the translation and orientation of the camera, with respect to the radar. For this
purpose, two models are posed: a segmentation-based model and a feature-based model.
While the scope is limited to the orientation of the built-in camera in the radar unit, the techniques explored
are highly relevant for other tasks, e.g., optimizing focal length for broadcasting cameras or fine-tuning
measured camera position.
The two methods posed, rely on a differentiable renderer[1] for optimization. The renderer can create an
image of what a camera with specified extrinsics would see on the surface mesh. Comparing the rendered
images to an image taken by the radar unit, the differentiability of the renderer can be used to optimize
the orientation to create a rendered image that most resembles the actual image, providing an estimate of
the radar unit camera’s orientation. The difference between the two models is what is rendered and how
the resemblance is measured. For the segmentation-based model, the rendered and the reference images

Related work
Many papers have been released on the topic of automatic camera calibration for sports broadcasting and
sports analytics[5][6][7]. However, most are concerned with sports such as football, basketball, and ice hockey,
i.e., sports with a well-defined court. The court will always have the same lines for all these sports, with
roughly the same dimensions. A common approach is to model the court as a planar annotated mesh to fit
on the image, using some feature points or lines on the court in the broadcast image[5][6][7]. While this has
some commonalities with the posed segmentation-based model, a golf course does not contain a fixed set of
lines, and the mesh of the field is not a plane. Thus, unlike other sports, one annotated 3D model can not
be created to fit all holes. For calibration evaluation, all forenamed examples use intersection over union as
a metric, which is a common approach in sports camera calibration and will also be employed in this thesis.
The Focal Tversky Loss is chosen for the segmentation-based model, as it is differentiable and has shown
good performances for segmentations with imbalanced classes, e.g., segmentation with a high proportion of
background pixels. The Focal Tversky Loss have been shown to improve performance for segmentation of
imbalanced data sets compared to traditional loss function such as DICE Loss and Tversky Loss[2].
For the feature-based model, VGG-16[3] and DINOv2[4] are explored as feature extractors. While no papers
were found using either as a feature extractor for rendered images, both have been used in many settings,
showing high performance and versatility. VGG-16 is explored as it, since its introduction in 2014, has
become a popular and well-established method for feature extraction, still showing good performences e.g.
in a study that used VGG-16 as a feature extractor for MRI scans and fed to a neural network to classify
dementia[8]. DINOv2 is explored as it is one of the latest innovations in feature extraction. While DINOv2
is not as well established as the VGG-16, it has already shown great results and potential as a feature
extractor[4].
Further, its versatility has been explored in a study, showing DINOv2, besides being able
to extract valuable features from natural images as it was trained on, also performed well on radiological
images[9].

Theory
This section provides an overview of the theory utilized and applied in this thesis. First, the Pinhole camera
model is introduced, as it lays the foundation for this thesis, relating the 3D world to a 2D image. Next, as
image rendering is an important part of both models posed, the theory behind it is introduced, including how
the renderer process can be differentiable. Then, the architectures of VGG-16 and DINOv2 are explained.
Lastly the Wilcoxon singed-rank test is introduced, for comparing performances of posed models.
3.1 | The Pinhole Camera model
A camera is a mapping between the 3D world and a 2D image. In other words, a camera projects 3D points
(real-world coordinates) into a 2D space (image plane). Different camera models have been proposed to
describe this mapping, all building on different assumptions about the camera. For this thesis, the pinhole
camera model is assumed. Therefore, understanding the pinhole model is essential for relating a point’s
real-world coordinates to its corresponding coordinates in the image plane.
3.1.1 | Model assumptions
The pinhole model is built on some assumptions regarding the camera. When taking a picture, the lights
emitted on an array onto an image plane are captured. With the pinhole model, a barrier with a pinhole is
placed between the rays and the medium see Figure 1.
Figure 1: Simple illustration of pinhole camera.
As seen from Figure 1, an effect of the camera is that the image will be upside down. To make computations
easier, the pinhole model assumes that the image plane lies in front of the pinhole. The distance between

3.2 | Image rendering
Image rendering is the process of generating an image from a 2D or 3D model using a computer program.
In the case of this thesis, rendered images are created from 3D surface meshes (the provided DEM), i.e., a
surface mesh consists of 3D points (vertices), connections between them (edges), and polygon areas formed
between edges (faces). In this thesis, the surface mesh represents the golf hole, and the faces are all triangles.
The aim is to place a camera somewhere on the mesh with a specified orientation and render what the camera
sees.
3.2.1 | Rasterization
Section 3.1 explained how a 3D point could be projected into a 2D plane (an image). However, this does
not consider the points’ positions in the 3D world, specifically occlusion. It is essential to determine which
parts of the mesh are visible from a specific viewpoint, a problem known as the visibility problem. The
rasterization rendering technique addresses the visibility problem.
Rasterization consists of two main steps[12]. Each triangle in the mesh’s vertices is projected from the 3D
space into a 2D representation (the image). After projecting the vertices, the pixels are looped over to see if
they lie within the resulting 2D triangle; if so, they get the same color as the triangle. For the rasterization
algorithm, the way pixels are looped over varies, depending on implementation, and is an important factor
in the algorithm’s speed. The rasterization algorithm is suited for GPUs and can run in parallel, allowing
fast rendering.
In practice, when rendering an image, one starts by creating a frame buffer, i.e., a 2D array the size of
the image one wants to render (for color images, a tensor, as elements in the array are three-dimensional
vectors). The array is initialized as black pixels; the color is then changed if it is contained in a projected
triangle. This results in an image of the rendered mesh with a black background.
The z-buffer method[12] is used to address the visibility problem. A 2D array matching the size of the image
is created, the elements of which are initialized with a very large number. Every time a triangle is projected,
its distance to the camera is recorded. If a pixel lies within the projected triangle, the corresponding index
in the z-buffer is checked. Suppose the distance between camera and projected triangle is smaller than the
number in the z-buffer. In that case, the value in the z-buffer is updated to match the distance, and the
pixels are colored according to the triangle. This way, the z-buffer records the distance between the object
projected to a pixel and the camera. If the distance is greater than the value in the corresponding index of
Page 9 of 97
==PAGE:17==
Rasmus L. T. Henriksen
Theory
the z-buffer, the object must lie behind what is already projected. Thus, it must not be visible, and the pixel
should not change color. When all objects have been projected, the z-buffer holds the distance between all
objects in the scene and the camera, a so-called depth map.
3.2.2 | Differentiable rendering
While a render can relate the 3D world to a 2D image, sometimes it is desirable to relate the rendered 2D
image back to the 3D world. A differentiable renderer can propagate gradients backward from a rendered
image to the 3D mesh. This allows rendering to be embedded into deep learning pipelines[13]. The issue
is that most renderers are not differentiable because the rasterization step is discrete. Different frameworks
able to render using differentiable functions and back-propagate signals to mesh vertices and their attributes
have been introduced. In this thesis, the renderer from PyTorch3D is utilized. The rasterization step in
PyTorch3D is based on a framework introduced in an article by Liu et al. (2019) [1].

3.3 | Feature extraction with pre-trained networks
A core component of image analysis and computer vision is the ability to extract valuable features from
images.
A feature can be explained as a measurable distinct attribute or characteristic from an image.
Features are an important tool in helping the computer understand what is happening in an image.
Convolution is a well-established and efficient method for extracting features from an image. The choice of
kernel decides the features captured, as different patches in the image respond differently to different kernels.
For example, a patch containing an edge will have a high response when applying a Sobel kernel. To capture
multiple features, one can convolute an image with a collection of kernels (a filter bank), which, combined
with pooling, can reduce the data dimension, thus creating a less redundant dataset.
While filter banks are easy to implement and interpret, they are limited because the kernels are fixed and
must be manually selected. Thus, some features may be overlooked if the filters are incorrectly chosen. A
Convolutional neural network (CNN) such as VGG-16 addresses this issue by having ”learned kernels.”
3.3.1 | VGG-16
Convolutional neural networks (CNN) are a class of deep neural networks designed for working with im-
ages[14].
CNNs are feed-forward neural networks, but they differ from other neural networks in that they use convo-
lutions (correlation filters), the weights of which are the optimized parameters when training the network.
CNNs consist of multiple layers: an input layer, convolution layers for feature extraction, pooling layers for
down-sampling, and fully connected layers for the final prediction.
An issue with CNNs is that the quality of the features extracted is determined by the training data and
complexity of the model (with large models being computationally hard to train). Thus, an alternative to
building and training a CNN from scratch is to use a pre-trained CNN, i.e., downloading the architecture
and weights of a (large) network already trained on a large, diverse data set. An example of such a network
Page 11 of 97
==PAGE:19==
Rasmus L. T. Henriksen
Theory
is the VGG-16 model, which will be used as a feature extractor in this thesis. Even though it has been
trained on different data than utilized in this thesis, the features captured generalize well to other data sets,
even outperforming hand-crafted representations[3].
VGG-16 is chosen because of its proven performance. For instance, it achieved high accuracy in The ImageNet
Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC)[3], where it had to classify 14 million images into 1000
different classes. Furthermore, since its introduction in 2014, it has been well-researched and remains a
popular choice of feature extractor.
The network architecture was posed by the Visual Geometry Group from the University of Oxford in 2014[3]
and can be seen in Figure 6.

As seen in Figure 6, VGG-16 takes an input image of size 224×224×3 and returns a vector of size 1000,
representing the probability of each class from the (ILSVRC). The network consists of 13 convolution layers
and three fully connected layers. Illustrated in Figure 6, the convolution layers appear in 5 blocks with similar
dimensions for layers within. Each block is followed by a max pooling (with stride 2) for downsampling,
halving the height and width while maintaining the number of channels. The blocks of convolution layers
contain 64, 128, 256, 512, and 512 filters, respectively.
All convolutions use same padding to keep the
dimension of the preceding layer. All filters are size 3 × 3, meaning all convolutions are 2D convolutions
with n-dimensional input with n being the number of channels in the preceding layer, i.e., each kernel takes
n-dimensional input and returns a 2D image. This is achieved by applying the kernel in all n layers and
performing an element-wise sum. After each convolution, ReLU is used as an activation function. After the
final max pool, the output is flattened to a vector of size 25088 and fed to a fully connected neural network
with three layers, which makes the final classification.
For feature extraction purposes, the final output of the model is less interesting than what goes on in
individual layers. Each convolution layer builds a feature map, where different features are captured. Because
the kernels in each layer are learned by training the model extensively, interpreting precisely what features

input image[16][17]. Thus, features from early layers can be more suitable for some tasks than later, more

3.3.2 | DINOv2
An alternative to CNNs as a feature extractor is vision transformers (ViTs). ViTs have a very different ar-
chitecture to CNNs and have shown better performances in various visual tasks when compared to[18][9][19].
A significant difference in the architecture of the two is that where CNNs use pixel arrays for the entire
image, the ViT divides an image into smaller patches of a fixed size, e.g., 14×14×3 (for RGB), to create a
series of smaller images. This is because ViT utilizes a transformer encoder model, which is relied heavily
on for natural language processing, like, for instance, the ChatGPT AI chatbot[20].
To use a transformer model, the image has to be represented in a certain way to be fed to the transformer
model. After dividing the image into multiple patches, each image patch is embedded, i.e., flattened into
a vector, and projected into a lower dimensional space using a fully connected neural network layer. The
embedding can be thought of as a feature for the image pad. Then, a positional embedding is added for
each patch embedding, with information on the patch’s position in the original image. This step adds global
awareness to the model. The patch embedding is then summed and fed to the transformer encoder, along
with the positional embedding.
The transformer encoder consists of multiple identical layers. Each layer has two main sub-layers: a Multi-
Head self-attention mechanism and a feed-forward neural network, specifically a Multi-Layer Perceptron
(MLP). The self-attention layer computes attention weights for all pixels, considering the entire sequence
of patch embeddings, which is then fed to the MLP to introduce non-linearity. The attention scores are
a weight that determines how much the model should focus on specific patches compared to others. With
these, the model can capture the global context of each patch.
As for CNNs, ViTs can be difficult to train, and pre-trained models on large data sets are available online.
This thesis uses DINOv2[4] by Meta AI Research. DINOv2 was designed as a feature extractor to be used
as a backbone in traditional computer vision tasks such as image classification. Thus, it produces features
that generalize well to different data sets without any fine-tuning. They achieve this by using self-supervised
learning on a curated image dataset. The DINOv2 is available in different sizes; for this thesis, the base
size is used. The base model uses a 14×14 patch embedding and has 12 ViT blocks that each return a
feature vector of size 768, which can be extracted from the model. Two versions of DINOv2 are available,
one trained with registers and one without. Registers have been shown to increase performance[21]. Hence,

3.4 | Wilcoxon signed-rank test

4.2 | Reference coordinate-systems for data
Trackman operates in four different coordinate systems. Understanding these are important, in order to
understand the nature of the data provided for this study. The four different coordinate systems are: UTM,
SCS, ACS and CCS. Note that all four are right handed Cartesian coordinate systems. Further, no scaling
is needed between coordinate systems, as all axes are in meters.
• The UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator) coordinate system is well established and will therefore
not be explained in detail. For the scope of this thesis, it is enough to know that, within a zone, the
x-axis and y-axis are oriented so that moving one meter east on a map will increase the x-coordinate
by one, and moving one meter north will increase the y-coordinate by one. The z-axis can be defined
arbitrarily but usually describes the height of a point above sea level.
• SCS (Site Coordinate System) is defined by Trackman. The coordinate system’s origo is the center
of the tee line, i.e., the spot from which a player will take his initial shot. The coordinate system is
defined so that the x-axis faces the direction of play (indicated by a point stated as the center of the
fairway), the y-axis faces up, and the z-axis to the right. SCS is closely related to UTM, as the UTM
coordinate of the SCS origo and a reference point on the x-axis is known.
• ACS (Antenna Coordinate System) is the internal coordinate system of the radar. The x-axis is parallel
with the face of the radar, the y-axis points vertically up and the z-axis are parallel to the ground
when assuming no roll.

5.1 | Correcting files

5.1.1 | Current solution
When drawing the ball tracer on broadcasting today, two cameras need calibration to align with the mesh;
one is the built-in camera explored in this thesis, which is responsible for gathering accurate radar data. The
other is the broadcast camera, which produces the images on which the tracer is overlayed for audiences.
At the moment of writing, both cameras’ tilt and roll are initialized using values from an inclinometer.
Next staff on sight measurre the 3D coordinates of several objects deemed easily identifiable in the image,
examples include the pin or a specific feature on a tree.
For the broadcast camera, the calibration process involves measuring several points that vary in distance and
position in the image. This is done to achieve the best possible calibration of orientation and focal length.
The objects are manually annotated in the image, and the 3D coordinates are projected onto the image. The
calibration is then refined by minimizing the pixel-distance between the projected and annotated points.
The focal length of the built-in camera in the radar unit does not need calibration, as this is done during
production. When adjusting the orientation to match known 3D coordinates, only one coordinate is used
(usually the pin for images of the green), and only the pan is adjusted. The tilt and roll value is kept as
sensor values.

5.1.2 | Adjusting tilt and pan
The orientation in the provided files could be subject to minor errors. As just explained in Section 5.1.1, the
tilt and roll values are sensor values, which can have some uncertainty. Further, when adjusting the pan, the
tilt and roll are fixed, which can lead to the pan being slightly off. To correct minor deviations, a method
is introduced to adjust the tilt and pan to fit the pin. Ideally, more points would be used, but the only
coordinate provided is that of the pin. For the same reason, only calibrations for greens can be corrected.
As the 3D coordinate of the pin is known, and it is easy to annotate, parameters can be optimized to
minimize the pixel distance between the projected pin Pp and the annotated pin Pan. Using the pinhole
camera model the pin coordinate is projected onto the image. Letting the orientation parameters between
ACS and SCS be a variable θ, the projected pin can be described as a function of this variable Pp(θ). One
can then define a loss function from the pixel-wise distance between the projected and annotated pin
L(θ) = ∥Pan −Pp(θ)∥2
As the coordinates in the image plane are two-dimensional, only pan and tilt are optimized as having three
variables, and two equations have infinite solutions.
The Nelder-Mead method[25] is used to minimize the loss function. This thesis will not go into depth with
this method as an implementation in Scipy is used, but the following provides a broad overview.
The Nelder-Mead optimization for a function of 2 variables works by defining three probing points from an
initial start guess (in this case, file parameters), for which the function is evaluated. The points are ordered
according to function value
f(x1) ≤f(x2) ≤f(x3).
Treating each point as a vertex in a triangle, the centroid xo of all points, but the biggest (x3), is computed.
Then the reflected point xr are computed
xr = xo + α(xo −x3).
With α > 0. If f(xr) < f(x3), x3 is replaced by xr and the process is repeated, otherwise x2 and x3
are moved closer to the best performing point, making the triangle smaller, before repeating the process.
Eventually, the function will converge towards a local minimum.

Open source with active community and support: It is easy to find documentation for implemented

Additional parameters of renderer
PyTorch3D’s built-in mesh renderer takes two inputs besides the mesh to be rendered, namely the rasterizer
and a shader.
The shader controls the coloring of faces and determines what methods to use for handling shade cast by
an artificial light source. The goal of the renderer is to create renders resembling the reel images provided.
Introducing an artificial light source and shadows poses two problems. First, information about the sun’s
positions and intensity at the moment the reel image was taken would be needed to cast realistic shadows.
Further, the height map only captures the ground, with trees and houses removed; there is nothing in the
mesh to cast the shadows seen in the reel images. Hence, the renderer is created without a light source or
shadows. In terms of coloring the faces of the triangles, the method explained in section 3.2.2 is used.
The rasterizer takes two parameters: cameras and raster settings. Cameras consist of all parameters for
the camera, i.e., image size, focal length, principal point, and the translation and rotation matrix for the
camera. For image size, the size of the undistorted cropped image is chosen. For focal length, the adjusted
value computed by getOptimalNewCameraMatrix when undistorting is chosen. Raster settings also take the
image size along with the parameter blur radius, which controls the blur around the edges of the shapes
projected; this is set to 0 for no blur.

5.4.1 | Image preprocessing methods
As explained in section 3.3.1, VGG-16 takes inputs of size 224×224×3; hence, both render and reference
images must be resized. Further, the images are normalized using mean [0.485, 0.456, 0.406] and standard
deviation [0.229, 0.224, 0.225], which is the values from Imagenet, on which VGG-16 was trained. Before
normalizing and resizing, three different image processing methods are proposed. As the render only renders
the surface with a black background, there is no information for comparison above the horizon in the image.
Each method addresses this issue. The first method is to create a mask using all the black pixels in the
rendered image to set all corresponding pixels to 0 in the reference image, thus ”painting” the sky black.
The second proposed method is simply to crop the top third of the rendered image and reference image,
as the horizon tends to be located around this position. The last posed method is to take the image and
render created in the first method and then crop the rows that sum to 0, i.e., crop all rows with only black
pixels. By cropping the images, more information is kept when resizing the image, as the sky contains no
information of value. As a control, not preprocessing the background is also investigated.

5.5.2 | Intersection over union
Intersection over union (IoU) is a widely used metric in image segmentation[32][33]. Say one have a predicted
class map C, and a ground truth class label map Cgt, the IoU metric then evaluates the quality of the
prediction by
IoU = |C ∩Cgt|
|C ∪Cgt|.
(11)
The IoU is limited to [0, 1], where 1 is a perfect match. As explained in Section 5.3, the renderer can create
a segmented render image given a specific orientation. When rendering the images, edges are blurred as the
color of the faces of the triangles in the mesh is interpolated from the vertices. These can be treated as
probabilities. When computing the IoU, pixels are rounded to the nearest integer (0 or 1). Computing the
IoU between the segmented rendered and reference images measures the quality of the camera orientation
used to render the image. The IoU score is not differentiable, so several differentiable loss functions like
DICE-loss or FTL have been devised to mimic it.

e.g., people or vegetation. As the height map won’t contain, e.g., people or vegetation areas occluded in
the reference image, it will be rendered, leading to some discrepancies between renders and reference image.

5.6 | Appropriate start guesses
Realistic tilt, pan, and roll start guesses are devised to test the two posed models for tees and greens.
For the tees, the radars have very similar setups. For radar functionality, they are placed directly behind
the player and have a slight tilt so that the player’s shots are perpendicular to the radio waves. As the radar
is behind players facing the shot direction (x-axis of SCS), 0 is used as a start guess for pan values, leading
to the biggest deviation from the file being approximately 6 degrees. Inspecting the tilt for all 17 tees, the
radar’s tilt is always within the narrow interval [0.139, 0.143]. Thus, the start guess for tilt is set to the
median of 0.141568. Setting up the radar is done on a flat surface. Thus, the roll is minimum therefore, a
start guess for the roll is 0 (for the 17 tees, it is always within [−0.00282, 0.003592]). Thus the start guess
for tilt, pan, and roll for tees are 0.141568, 0, and 0, respectively. Note that this start guess does not require
knowledge of any coordinates or sensor values.
For greens, the radar setup varies across holes. Trackman uses a continuous-wave radar (CW radar) which
measures the Doppler frequency in the radial direction with respect to the radar. For optimal performance,
it should be oriented to face the position of the shot, a fair assumption is that the radar faces the fairway,
as it is from here that a player is expected to hit the ball onto the green. Thus, as a start guess for the pan,
the angle between the SCS’s x-axis and the vector from the radar coordinate to the fairway coordinate are
computed. Using this as a starting guess will lead to the most significant deviation from the file being below
4.5 degrees. Again, as for the tees, the roll can be assumed to be 0. Unfortunately, no valid start guess for
the tilt could be devised. Thus, the sensor value from the provided file is used. Thus, to get the start guess
for the green, the tilt sensor value, along with the 3D coordinate of the fairway, is necessary.

5.7 | Hyper-Parameter Optimization
The segmentation-based model and feature-based model both contain several hyper-parameters. This section
explains how these can be optimized to get the best performance from the models. This can be thought
of as an optimization of the models themselves. For both models, Stochastic Gradient Descent is chosen
as an optimizer. This introduces two hyper-parameters, namely the learning rate and momentum. The
Focal Tversky Loss has two hyper-parameters for the segmentation-based model, α and γ (equation 9). For
the feature-based model, the choice of background preprocessing, loss function, and feature layer can all
be thought of as hyperparameters. However, to limit the search space, a separate analysis is done to find
the choice of background, loss function, and three candidate layers to extract features (Section 5.7.2). The
three candidate layers will then be treated as a hyperparameter. Hence, the segmentation and feature-based
models will have 4 and 3 hyper-parameters, respectively.
5.7.1 | Random hyperparameter sweep
A common approach for hyper-parameter optimization is to do a grid search. However, the search space can
become huge with four hyper-parameters, resulting in a time-consuming optimization. Instead, a random
search is used, which has been shown to achieve similar or better results in less computation time[34]. Instead
of having a fixed list, hyper-parameters are drawn randomly from specified intervals (uniform distributions)
to create random combinations, train the model, and evaluate performance. In the case of this thesis, the
structure is as follows. First, 100 random combinations are created, and for each, the model is trained for a
short duration (50 epochs) on 5 cases, returning some optimized parameters that are evaluated based on IoU
for tees and angle deviation from the pin for greens. The median score across all five holes will be assigned to
the respective combination. Having done this for all 100 combinations, this process is repeated for the three
best-performing combinations, this time trained for a longer duration (300 epochs), the best of which will
be the final choice of hyper-parameters. Separate hyperparameter optimizations are performed for tees and
greens. When performing the optimization, the momentum parameter is drawn from a uniform distribution
U(0, 1), and the learning rate from a log-uniform distribution U(ln(0.000001), ln(0, 01)). The log-uniform
distribution is chosen to increase instances of lower learning rates to have roughly as many instances between,
e.g., 0.00001 and 0.0001 as 0.001 and 0.01. For the segmentation-based model, α ∼U(0.5, 0.9) γ ∼U( 1
3, 1),
both are chosen based on the findings of Abraham (2019)[2], that showed α > 0.5 and γ < 1 improved
performance on imbalanced datasets, but that γ should not be to low. Note that γ = 1 results in Tversky
loss and α = 0.5 DICE loss.

5.7.2 | Reducing hyperparameters for feature-based model
Before performing a hyperparameter sweep for the feature-based model, the two choices of feature extrac-
tors VGG 16 and DINOv2 are examined. The aim is to find a combination of loss function, background
preprocessing method and feature extraction layer/block, to implement in the final feature based model.
Keeping the roll and tilt values from the sensor (the files), the pan value is varied to see how different
combinations perform. The loss function should experience a minima close to the correct pan value for a
good choice of all of these. The features extracted from each of the 13 convolutional layers in the VGG 16
and all 12 blocks of transformer layers in the DINOv2 are tested.
For all combinations, 2 sweeps are performed: a 360 degree sweep and a close sweep. The 360 degree sweep
tests values of the pan between −π and π with a step size corresponding to 1 degree. The reasoning behind
this test is to provide an understanding of the global behavior of features extracted, chosen loss functions,
and preprocessing methods. The close sweep aims to provide an understanding of the behavior close to the
true parameter. For images of greens pan values tested are within the pan from the file parameter ±0.4
radians (≈±23 degrees), while for tees the values tested are between −0.2 and 0.2 radians. In both cases,
the step size corresponds to just under 0.1 degree for more precision.

increases with its distance from the camera, a lower learning rate is expected for greens compared to tees.

too big a step, leading to the rendered segmentation no longer overlapping with the segmentation in the
reference image. This results in the FTL being a constant 1, causing the gradient to be the zero vector,
meaning the optimizer can’t return to an overlap and further optimize.

When using the file parameter, the camera faces the direction for which the mesh is defined several hundred
meters ahead. When turning the camera 180 degrees, it will face a direction of the mesh that is only defined
for roughly 30 meters, resulting in a rendered image with less surface. Thus, it appears that a global minima
will either appear close to the file parameter, or when the rendered image contain the most black pixels.
This could explain why the MSE and L1-norm perform worse than the cosine loss variations (Table 4. VGG-
16 extracts features from the entire image. If less mesh is rendered, less information can be extracted from
the image, as the background contains no information. Thus, the activation maps across layers will have
a larger portion of small values as no features are extracted. If the same layer cannot extract meaningful
features from the reference image, the corresponding activation maps will also contain many low values.
Comparing every value in both maps with equal weight, like MSE and L1-norm does, neglects the impact of
the few extracted features from both images. Cosine loss does not have this issue. Cosine does not compare
values in the feature vector but rather their directions. This suppresses the effect of low activation values
and emphasizes the high activation values. Thus, what is rendered is emphasized more when compared with
the reference image.
The architecture of DINOv2 is different from that of VGG-16 and is not as impacted by the choice of loss
functions. Unlike VGG-16, which pays equal attention to the entire image, DINOv2 creates attention maps
to find where in the image to focus, giving the image scores of what to emphasize. Thus, it will know to pay
attention to what is rendered when extracting features. As what is rendered will look less like the reference
image, the elements in the feature vectors should deviate, meaning the MSE and L1-norm are valid loss
functions.

The fact that the segmentation-based model outperforms the others is not surprising. It indirectly optimizes
the IoU by using the Focal Tversky loss. Further, it is optimized for all three orientation parameters, whereas
the file uses the sensor values for tilt and roll, with only the pan adjusted. Thus, the segmentation-based
model has more freedom to move the mesh around and fit it to the reference segmentations.
It is important to note that the segmentation-based model is limited by the quality of segmentations on both
the aerial and the reference images. For this project, the reference images were segmented by hand, which
is not ideal as it is time-consuming. Further, as the IoU is computed by using the same segmentations as
the segmentation-based model used for optimization, it can have some bias. Ideally, an expert would do
the segmentation to compute the IoU, and the segmentation for the model would be found in an automatic
way. Still, this test has shown that given a segmentation of the reference image and the surface mesh, the
segmentation-based model can optimize the camera’s orientation to align the two.
While the feature-based model has a lower median than the file parameters, the difference is not statistically
significant. The feature-based model contains no bias with regard to the IoU score, as it is not incorporated
into the model. Where the segmentation-based model seeks to align segmentations of features, and the file
pan has been optimized to fit one point, the feature-based model compares the entire rendered image to
the reference image. Thus, while the segmentation model results in better fits of the segmented feature (as
indicated by the IoU), the actual rendered image, in general, may resemble the reference image more using
the optimized orientation from the feature-based model, something not captured by the IoU score.

Conclusion
Two methods are proposed to compare DEM features with a reference image. The DEM only contains
spatial information, but can be mapped to other information using additional data. One posed method for
extracting features is to segment the mesh into features of interest using a provided aerial segmentation of
the course in UTM coordinates. Information about the segmentation is stored as color values in the mesh,
coloring vertices white if they belong to a segmentation and black if not. Another posed method is to add
information to the mesh by mapping it to an aerial photo of the course by coloring it accordingly. The
results of both methods are the DEM containing spatial data and color information representing either a
segmentation (based on provided aerial segmentation) or real-world colors (based on provided aerial photo).
A renderer is introduced to relate the mesh to a reference image. By modeling the camera using the pinhole
model and using a rasterization algorithm, the renderer can render an image based on a specified position and
orientation of a camera, with only vertices and faces visible to this camera. Thus, any data from the DEM
that will not appear in the image and contribute with no visible features to the reference image are removed.
With this, the spatial data in the DEM have been mapped to either a position in the image or discarded.
For the first posed method, the result will be an image with all the visible parts of the segmentation as white
pixels, with the rest being black. By segmenting the corresponding features in the reference image, it can be
related to the rendered image and, therefore, the DEM. For the second posed method, the rendered image
will resemble the reference image, as it is colored according to the surface of the real world. Thus, one can
relate this to the reference image by extracting features from both and comparing them. For this purpose,
the VGG-16 and DINOv2 were introduced, with DINOv2 showing the best results for extracting features to
relate the rendered image to the rendered.
Based on the two approaches of relating features from the DEM to the reference image, two optimizer models
were introduced: a segmentation-based model and a feature-based model. Both models utilize a differentiable
render, meaning the rendered image can be related to the spatial data of the mesh. By letting the orientation
of the camera be the optimizable variable, the models with render and image give an orientation for the
camera. From the rendered image and a reference image, a loss is computed and used to adjust the camera’s
orientation to minimize the loss.
The segmentation-based model was based on the first posed method of inferring segmentation-based infor-
mation to the DEM. One can create a segmented reference image by segmenting the objects corresponding
to the segmented objects introduced to the DEM. The Focal Tversky Loss was introduced as a loss function.
The feature-based model was based on the second posed method for relating the features of the DEM to the
reference image. Having rendered a ”realistic” image given a specific orientation, features were extracted
from the rendered image using DINOv2 and compared to features extracted from the reference image, with
Page 68 of 97
==PAGE:76==

the cosine similarity loss used as a loss function.
To test the two models, cases were separated into greens and tees, of which there were 17 each. Having
derived the best hyperparameters for both models, the optimized orientations were tested against provided
file parameters, representing the current solution. The optimized parameters for greens were additionally
compared to file parameters, with the pan and tilt adjusted.
Two metrics were introduced to compare
calibrations: the IoU and the angle of deviation from the projected pin and the actual pin.
The segmentation model consistently showed the highest performances based on the IoU score. This differ-
ence in performance between the segmentation-based model and the feature-based model and file parameters
was shown to be statistically significant using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test. In contrast, the difference be-
tween the segmentation-based model and the corrected file parameters was not statistically significant. The
segmentation-based model could effectively converge to an orientation that best aligns the aerial segmen-
tation with the segmentation on the reference image. However, while this ensures a high IoU score and
calibration precision for the segmented features, the same is not guaranteed for the rest of the image. As the
segmentation-based model is blind to anything but segmented areas, the calibration was sometimes off when
considering the entire image. This was mainly caused by the optimized roll parameter when the image only
contained segmentations close together or located in the center of the image. Several steps can be taken to
improve the model. One is to have more segmented features in diverse locations in the image. As this is not
always possible, a penalty could be inferred on the loss function based on the size of the roll parameters, as
prior knowledge tells us that it should be close to zero. One last option is to keep the sensor value of the
roll, not optimizing it, as a comparison between the segmentation-based model’s IoU score and the corrected
file revealed no statistical significance, even though the corrected file had kept the sensor value for the roll.
The feature-based model consistently showed the worst performances. However, no statistical significance
was shown between the IoU for the tees and the file parameters.
While it did not perform as well as
the segmentation-based model, it did not have the same issues with the roll. As the feature-based model
considers the entire image, it will not prioritize aligning the bunkers if it comes at the expense of the rest of
the image. Unfortunately, no metric was devised that captures the calibration for the entirety of the image,
which would perhaps have been favorable to the feature-based model. Performances of the feature-based
model could improve if provided with a more recent, higher-quality aerial photo. As the one provided was
three years older than the reference images, there can be discrepancies between what the mesh renders and
what the reference camera sees. Further, in some cases, people are present in the reference images. Usually,
this is not the case when calibrating. As people will not appear in the rendered image, the feature-based
model may perform better for images of empty courses.
To further explore the performance of the posed models and the current solution, a metric must be devised
that represents the entire image better than a single point (angle deviation from the pin) or a small, not
diverse area. If more coordinates like the pin were available, a metric similar to the angle deviation from the

pin could measure the deviation for more points for a more complete evaluation. This is similar to how the
broadcast cameras are calibrated today, meaning this data is currently available to Trackman.
At the time of writing, calibration of the radar unit requires sensor values for tilt and roll values and
measured 3D coordinates of real-world objects along with manual annotations of the same in the image. For
both models introduced, the optimization of the tee requires no sensor values, and the green requires only
the tilt value and the coordinate of the center of the fairway. As introduced, the segmentation model requires
manual segmentation of the reference image, a task that could be automated, while the feature-based model
is already fully automated. Further, both models are not limited to optimizing the orientation. Using the
pipeline set up in this thesis, other parameters can be calibrated, e.g., the position of the camera or the focal
length, which can have other applications within Trackman and in camera calibration in general.
