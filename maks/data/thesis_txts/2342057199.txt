Abstract 
 
The group of diseases commonly referred to as cancer is a global threat to humanity and 
touches the lives of most people. The current standard of care includes several treatment 
options, e.g., surgery, radiation and chemotherapy. An emerging therapeutic approach is to 
use the immune system to fight cancer. The fundamental idea is to utilize the fact that T cells 
recognize tumor cells as foreign based on the presence of certain peptides, or epitopes, 
displayed by their major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules. Thus, the 
identification and characterization of targetable tumor epitopes is an important step in 
developing new immunotherapeutic approaches. Transcriptome analysis usually focuses on 
known genes, therefore it is of interest to explore the intergenic regions of our DNA. This 
study analyzes and compares the expression of intergenic regions of chromosome 20 in Head 
and Neck Squamous Cell Carcinoma- (HNSC) tumor and normal samples to identify 
potential tumor-specific expressions that might lead to novel immunotherapeutic approaches. 
Differential expression analysis (DEA) was used to perform the analysis and comparison. 
The results of this study is that the transcription profiles of the intergenic regions are 
statistically different between HNSC tumor samples when compared to normal samples.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Immunotherapy is an emerging technology in the fight against cancer. The idea is based on 
the fact that the immune system is capable of recognizing and destroying tumor cells [1]. T 
cells recognize tumor cells as foreign based on the presence of certain peptides, or epitopes, 
displayed by their MHC molecules. Thus, identification of potential targetable tumor epitopes 
is an important step in developing new immunotherapeutic approaches. Neoepitopes derive 
from mutated versions of normally expressed genes or from non-mutated proteins that are 
expressed at an abnormally high level. In the past, the focus on identifying neoepitiopes has 
been on the genes and not on the intergenic regions.  
 
In general, cells exhibit widespread transcription of genomic regions that are not linked to the 
transcriptome. It has been called the dark matter in the genome and that field started when 
researchers performed tiling experiments on large genomic regions. Similar studies were then 
compared to try to gain insights into the existence of the transcription and answer questions 
such as if it is simply a biological noise, biological- or experimental artifacts or maybe 
undiscovered genes.  
 
Computer gene predictions, which have been shown to be reliable [2], often discard open 
reading frames shorter than a certain threshold. Many have a threshold around 300 
nucleotides, which corresponds to 100 amino acids. The human genome is full of so called, 
short open reading frames (sORFs), which are usually ignored in transcriptome analysis. 
Research has shown that some of them are indeed transcribed, and even translated into 
peptides [3]. The sORFs have been observed on both strands and often have alternative start 
codons [4].  
 
With that in mind it was determined to explore the intergenic regions with the hope of 
discovering epitopes derived from tumor-specific expression of sORFs in the intergenic 
regions.

1.2 Cancer 
The American Cancer Society describes cancer as being a group of diseases that are 
characterized by an uncontrolled growth and spread of abnormal cells [5]. Both internal and 
external factors cause cancer. Examples of external factors are tobacco consumption, 
infectious organisms, and unhealthy diet. Examples of internal factors are inherited genetic 
mutations and immune conditions [5]. These factors may act in sequence or in combination to 
cause cancer. Cancer, or more precisely, the cells that make up the cancer share many, if not 
all, common characteristics that were described by Douglas et. al. in the year 2000, when 
they published the famous paper, "The Hallmarks of Cancer" [6]. The six hallmarks are: 
sustaining proliferative signaling, evading growth suppressors, resisting cell death, enabling 
replicative immortality, inducing angiogenesis and activating invasion/metastasis. 
 
The aforementioned risk factors that are known to cause cancer cause genetic instability that 
in turn increases genetic diversity within the cell population. It is this genetic diversity that is 
a premise for the cells to acquire the hallmark abilities listed above.  
 
In 2011, Douglas et al. published an updated review of the hallmarks of cancer were they 
introduce two additional traits: reprogramming of energy metabolism and evading immune 
destruction [7]. 
 
The gravity of cancer is undebatable and the numbers speak for themselves. Roughly 1.7 
million new cancer cases are expected to be diagnosed in 2016 in the United States (US) and 
about 600 thousand to die from it. In the US, cancer is the second leading cause of death after 
heart disease [5]. Furthermore, the World Health Organization expects the number of new 
cancer cases to rise about 70% over the next two decades1. 
 
1.2.1 HNSC 
The current study focuses on Head and Neck Squamous Cell Carcinoma, which affects 
~600,000 patients per year worldwide [8]. The disease is terminal for half of the people 
diagnosed despite current standard of care treatment that includes surgery, radiation and 
chemotherapy [9].

Human papillomavirus (HPV) infection is known to cause HNSC, especially the high-risk 
type HPV-16 [10]. The virus expresses oncoproteins (E6 and E7) that inactivate p53 and 
retinoblastoma (pRb), which are tumor-suppressor proteins [11]. 
 
A TCGA research group profiled 279 HNSC samples. They summarized copy number 
alterations (CNAs) and somatic mutations found in the samples and discussed consequences 
on downstream pathways. They further characterized human-papillomavirus-associated 
tumors by identifying mutations and novel alterations resulting in loss of important genes [9]. 
 
1.2.2 Cancer Therapies 
Over the years, many drugs have been developed to fight cancer. Between 1949 and 1971, 30 
drugs were approved for cancer patients. By the year 2007, more than 300 drugs have been 
developed and are available for use in treatment [12].  
 
Immunotherapy was first attempted by William B. Coley, a surgeon in New York, in the year 
1891. He injected live and inactivated bacteria in order to stimulate antibacterial immune 
cells to kill nearby tumor cells. Paul Ehrlich proposed the theory of cancer immune 
surveillance in 1908. The theory is that the immune system suppresses tumor formation and 
was heavily debated until 2001, when experimental evidence in support of this phenomenon 
was provided by Robert Schreiber, Lloyd Old and others2. They showed that mice that were 
unable to synthesize interferon-gamma, a critical player in immune signaling system, were 
more susceptible to develop certain cancers [13].  
 
The immune response can also be tumor promoting and virtually all neoplastic lesions 
contain immune cells [14]. Inflammation can supply bioactive molecules that contribute to 
the hallmark capabilities, including growth factors (sustain proliferative signaling), survival 
factors (limit cell death) and enzymes that facilitates angiogenesis, invasion and metastasis 
[7]. Additionally, inflammatory cells can release reactive oxygen species that increase the 
mutation rate and therefore the genetic variety that tumor cells need to acquire the hallmarks 
[15].

1.3 Short open reading frames 
Open reading frames (ORF) are a stretch of potentially translatable sequence that begin with 
a start codon and end with a stop codon. A translatable ORF is often referred to as a coding 
DNA sequence (CDS) and results in it's protein product. sORFs are distinguished from 
regular ORFs by their size. sORFs can be translated into proteins in various regions, for 
example in the 5' leader and 3' trailer sequences of a transcript, within or overlapping a 
previously known ORF and in various transcripts including long non-coding transcripts 
(lncRNAs), intergenic and antisense transcripts [4].  
 
The size of sORFs ranges from its theoretical lower limit of two codons (start and stop) and 
the upper limit is ill-defined, but is usually less then 300 nucleotides. It has been shown by 
computational [16] methods and ribosome profiling [17] that non-annotated sORFs are most 
likely translated, but those kind of studies are indirect since they do not directly detect the 
presence of the sORF-encoded polypeptides (SEPs).  
 
To identify the SEPs directly, one research group developed a strategy that combines 
peptidomics and massively parallel RNA sequencing in 2013. They uncovered 90 SEPs and 
86 of them were novel [3] and the analysis was on human cells. Additionally, they estimate 
that SEP abundances range from 10 - 1,000 molecules per cell. 
 
Currently, computational tools exist that predict potential protein coding sORFs. One such 
tool is the sORFfinder [18]. sORFfinder uses selective constraints and hexamer composition 
bias between coding and non-coding sequences to predict the coding potential of the sORFs. 
This program is computationally intensive and feeding it the whole human genome has not 
been done.  
 
The number of sORFs in humans is most likely enormous. In [19] 593,586 sORFs (less than 
100 amino acids) were identified in intergenic regions in Drosophila melanogaster using 
bioinformatics analysis of non-coding euchromatic DNA. When a coding potential criteria is 
added, that number drops significantly.  
 
One research group did a genome-wide search for sORFs using sORFfinder on the Mus 
musculus genome. sORFfinder identified over 40 million sORFs and categorized 
==PAGE:15==
 
5
approximately 2.4 million of them as having high coding potential. Most of the sORFs were 
located in the intergenic region (~70%). sORFfinder took between 5 and 14 days analyzing 
one chromosome, depending on its size, using up to 50GB of memory [20]. 
 
1.4 Intergenic Regions 
The most recent assembly of the human genome (GRCh38) has over 3 billion bases, more 
than 20 thousand coding genes and 25 thousand non-coding genes3. The stretches of DNA in 
the human genome that are located between genes are referred to as intergenic region.  
 
In humans, 95% of the genome does not code for proteins, which is a considerable percentage 
when compared to more primitive species, e.g., bacteria (15%), Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
(30%) and Caenorhabditis elegans (70%) [21]. It seems that the fraction of non coding DNA 
increases with the complexity of an organism. 
 
The intergenic region in the human genome is extensively transcribed [22][23]. Genome-
wide transcription was initially measured using tiling microarray experiments, which 
revealed large amount of transcripts laying outside of known genes [23]. The term 'dark 
matter' has been coined to describe the transcripts on those regions and their use and function 
is not fully understood. Those regions have also been called 'junk DNA'.  
 
One of the first tiling microarray experiments was performed on human chromosomes 20 and 
22 and found that 47% of the positive probes (they used 60mer probes in 30-bp steps with 
samples derived from six different conditions) were outside the known exon positions. Out of 
those 47%, 22% were within introns and 25% were within intergenic regions [24]. This 
caught the attention of other research groups which performed similar experiments but the 
reliability of the results were debated because the overlap (of the positive probes) between 
studies varied a lot [23].  
 
In trying to figure out the dark matter transcription there are many potential explanations, 
e.g., biological noise, experimental artifacts and novel non- and protein-coding genes. Non-
coding transcripts have been shown to play a huge role in the regulatory system. As a 
molecule it has the ability to reversibly bind to virtually any other RNA or DNA molecule by

nucleotide complementarity. Many RNA regulatory systems have been characterized, mostly 
for small RNAs, but also for lncRNAs [25][26][27].  
 
The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE) stated in 2012, after a decade long research, 
that 80% of the human genome serves some purpose. Another study in 2013 corroborated the 
observation of pervasive transcription across the human genome done by the ENCODE 
project [28]. That group analyzed 127 data sets from 23 different tissues and many conditions 
and found that > 85% of the genome is transcribed [28]. Most of the long intergenic non-
coding RNAs (lincRNA) that the group identified were novel (i.e. not annotated), or > 94%, 
in spite of their stringent filters. They identified two contributing factors, first the RNA-seq 
read depth and second, the diversity of tissues, which allows for detection of rare and tissue 
specific transcripts. 
 
An important question in this field is if the 'dark matter' transcripts are functional or simply a 
result of noisy transcription. Those discussions are out of the scope of this thesis but the high-
level description of their function includes roles in high-order chromosomal dynamics, 
telomere biology, subcellular structural organization and regulating the expression of 
neighboring protein-coding genes [26].

2.2 Programs used in the project 
2.2.1 Quality control & trimming 
Trim Galore7 is a wrapper tool around Cutadapt [30] and FastQC8 and was used to quality- 
and adapter-trim the raw fastq files.  
 
It was used in paired-end mode with all parameters set to default. Quality Phred score cutoff 
was at 20 and the adapters were auto-detected as from the Illumina TruSeq platform 
('AGATCGGAAGAGC') and were removed. Maximum trimming error rate was at 0.1 or 
10%, which is number of errors divided by the length of the matching region and translates to 
one error per adapter in this case. Errors here can be mismatches or indels (insertions and 
deletions). The minimum required sequence length for a read before it and its mate* gets 
removed was 20 bases (* in paired-end mode, reads come in pairs. When talking about a read 
in a pair, the other read is referred to as its mate). Along with quality trimming, the FastQC 
program was applied and its output visualized to confirm that everything looked normal. 
 
The base quality trimming algorithm subtracts the given cutoff threshold from the individual 
base qualities and computes the partial sums for all indices and cuts the sequence at the index 
with the minimum sum. For example, if we have a sequence with the following quality values 
and a cutoff set to 20: 
52, 50, 36, 37, 18, 17, 21, 14, 12, 13 (base qualities).  
After subtracting it would be: 
32, 30, 16, 17, -2, -3, 1, -6, -8, -7.  
And trimming from the right end would give the sums: 
(70), (38), 8, -8, -25, -23, -20, -21, -15, -7.  
The numbers in the parenthesis would not be computed because the sum before it is above 
zero. The minimum is -25, so the last six bases would be trimmed of in this hypothetical 
example.

The algorithm for finding the adaptors involves alignments and is based on semiglobal 
alignment. Sequences are allowed to drift over each other, which differ from global 
alignment were the sequences are compared from end to end. The error is only calculated in 
the overlap unlike in global were all differences are penalized. Instead of an alignment score, 
where mismatches and indels are penalized differently, Cutadapt uses unit cost where 
mismatches and indels have the same penalty. 
 
2.2.2 BWA 
Burrows-Wheeler Aligner (BWA) is a software package for aligning sequences from next 
generation sequencing (NGS) platforms to a reference database [31]. It has three algorithms: 
BWA-backtrack (aln), BWA-SW and BWA-mem. The first algorithm performs gapped 
global alignments and was designed for short Illumina reads (less than 100bp) and was used 
in this study.   
 
BWA uses the Burrows-Wheeler transform algorithm which rearranges the reference genome 
enabling fast exact matching between the read and the reference. The algorithm alters the 
query sequence such that it matches the reference exactly and aborts if the penalty exceeds a 
given threshold. BWA supports mismatches and indels and its default output is in the 
sequence alignment map (SAM) format.   
 
First the reference genome is indexed then the alignments in the suffix array coordinate are 
generated resulting in *.bai files, one for each fastq file in a sequence pair. Finally, the 
alignments are generated in the SAM format using BWA sampe. 
 
Default parameters were used. Maximum edit distance was 2%, which translates to 2 
differences for 15-37bp reads and to 3 differences for 38-63bp reads and differences are both 
mismatches and indels. One gap in total was allowed and gap opening, mismatches and gap 
extensions had 11, 3 and 4 in penalty respectively.  
 
If a read has many possible best alignments, one is reported randomly and the other 
alignments are in the BWA specific XA flag (up to three alternative reads are reported, if 
more, then the XA flag will not be written). If a read has many possible best alignments, it 
==PAGE:20==
 
10 
will get a mapping quality of zero (BWA specific). Mapping quality is the Phred-scaled 
probability that a read is aligned in the wrong place and is calculated as:  
𝑝 = 10&' () 
where 𝑞 is the mapping quality and 𝑝 is the probability of the alignment being incorrect. For 
example, a mapping quality of 40 would result in 𝑝 = 0.0001, which means there is a 0.01% 
chance that the read is aligned incorrectly.  
 
2.2.3 Bedtools 
Bedtools was used throughout this study. Bedtools is ideal when one is working with 
genomic features in BED format. Bedtools allows for easy manipulation, comparison and 
annotation of genomic features [32]. Bedtools has functions such as intersect, which 
identifies features that intersect, and a coverage calculation function. In this project it was 
used to split the human genome into windows and find all reads that intersect with known 
whole genes. Bedtools' coverage function was used to count reads per window and to 
calculate per base coverage.  
 
2.2.4 Samtools 
Samtools was used throughout this study. Samtools is a software package that uses SAM and 
binary alignment map (BAM) formats of alignment files. Samtools has many functions, e.g., 
sorting, indexing, merging and extract alignments with a particular flag [33]. In the current 
project, it was used extensively to manipulate BAM files and to get mapping statistics. 
 
2.2.5 Picard tools 
Picard tools, developed by The Broad Institute is another command line tool that is suited for 
manipulating high throughput sequencing data and formats such as BAM/SAM9. Picard has 
many functions but only one was used in this project, FastqToSam, that converts BAM files 
into fastq files.

2.3 Differential expression 
edgeR (empirical differential gene expression in R) is a Bioconductor package for differential 
expression analysis [34] on RNA-seq data and was used in this project. The package is not 
strictly for gene counts but is in general useful for experiments that generate counts, as is the 
case in this study, where the genome was split into windows (hereafter, genes will be referred 
to as windows). The only requirement for comparing groups using edgeR is that at least one 
of the groups has to have a biological replicate. This study has three groups containing 2, 20 
and 314 replicates.  
 
The input to edgeR is a raw count table, i.e. not normalized, and a vector that specifies the 
experimental group for each samples (NT, TP or MT).  
 
In this study the generalized linear model (GLM) pipeline was used as it offers more 
flexibility than the classical model when there are more than two groups. The GLM 
framework has two testing methods: likelihood ratio test and quasi-likelihood (QL) F-test and 
the latter was used here because it accounts for window-specific variability [35]. The final 
step is to actually test for significant differentially expressed (DE) in each window. False 
discovery rate (FDR) is controlled by performing a multiplicity correction using the 
Benjamini-Hochberg method on the p-values [36]. 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
12 
The typical edgeR workflow consists the following: load in gene counts -> normalize -> 
create design matrix -> estimate dispersion -> fit a quasi-likelihood negative binomial 
generalized log-linear model to the count data -> F-test to identify differentially expressed 
genes.  
 
2.3.1 Normalization 
The goal is to identify DE windows rather than to quantify expression levels or in other 
words, identify relative changes in expression but not absolute expression levels. Because of 
that, technical influences should not be important because they should cancel each other out 
(between samples), if they are unrelated to the experimental conditions. The main 
normalization that needs to be carried out are the technical factors on a sample level (sample-
specific effects), for example sequencing depth. 
 
Another technical influence in DEA is composition bias that is best described with an 
example. Assume two samples A and B that have the same sequencing depth and all genes 
are expressed equally apart from one gene in sample A that is highly expressed. That will 
leave fewer reads to spread over the remaining genes because the highly expressed gene 
consumes a substantial proportion of the total library size. That might lead to a DE result that 
some of the other genes in A might appear to be down-regulated. Such composition bias 
needs to be adjusted. 
 
edgeR has the function calcNormFactors that adjust for varying sequencing depths and 
eliminates the composition bias. For composition bias, edgeR estimates the log-fold 
differences of the counts between samples and computes a set of scaling factors, the trimmed 
mean of M-values (TMM) normalization factor [35]. The scaling factors minimize the log-
fold changes between samples for most windows. The product of the scaling factors and the 
original library size defines the effective library size, which is used in all downstream 
analyses. 
 
Other factors like GC content and gene length (though irrelevant in this study) do not change 
from sample to sample and have little effect on the DEA. 
 
==PAGE:23==

2.3.2 Modelling variability in a quasi negative binomial framework (dispersion) 
edgeR uses the negative binomial (NB) distribution, which has been shown to be powerful for 
modeling over-dispersion [37], to model read counts for each gene in every sample.  
 
edgeR estimates a common dispersion for all windows and then applies an empirical Bayes 
(EB) strategy for squeezing the tagwise dispersion towards the common dispersion.

biological and technical sources into account. The variance of the count 𝑦,- is a quadratic

Fold change is calculated as a fraction. If we have two samples, A and B and A has 100 reads 
in a window and B has 50 reads, then logFC for A/B would be 100/50 = 2, or twice as much. 
If it would be the other way around, i.e. A would have 50 reads and B 100 reads, the fold 
change would be A/B = 50/100 = 0.5. If log2 would not be used then the scale would range 
from 0-1 (when A would be down-regulated, given that B was the reference) and from 1 to 
==PAGE:25==
 
15 
infinity (when A would be up-regulated). But log2 changes the scale so the FC is always 
equally far from the equality. In the case above the log2FC would be 1 and -1, respectively. 
 
CPM is read count in window divided by library size and then multiplied with a million. For 
example, a library size of 10 million reads and a window with 6 reads would result in a CPM 
of 6 ((60/10,000,000) * 1,000,000). Then the average CPM is calculated over all the samples 
and log2 of that value is reported.  
 
2.4 Data exploration 
2.4.1 Multidimensional scaling 
The data can be explored by generating multidimensional scaling (MDS) plot. The limma 
package of R has a function called plotMDS that performs MDS and plots the result [39]. 
MDS visualizes the differences between the expression profiles of all samples in two 
dimensions. In brief, the MDS algorithm calculates the distance between each pair of the 
samples as leading fold change, defined as the root-mean-square of the largest 500 log2-fold 
changes between that pair of samples. 
 
2.4.2 Principal component analysis 
Principal component analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction technique that is widely 
used in data analysis. PCA emphasizes the variation and brings out strong patterns in a 
dataset. In this study it was used to explore and visualize the data. PCA is not scale invariant, 
so the counts were normalized before the PCA was applied.

2.6 Strategy 
The original goal was to identify sORFs in the intergenic regions and compare expression, 
based on RNA-seq, between healthy and cancer samples. Optimally, one would simply map 
the reads, after quality filtering, to the sORFs and then generate a count table that would be 
used in any downstream analysis.  
 
The author of this thesis is not aware of the existence of a catalogue of sORFs and their 
coordinates in the human genome, as was mentioned in the introduction. Using a program for 
this purpose like sORFfinder, would take weeks if not months to run to build the necessary 
data set. Hence another approach was taken.  
 
The next strategy chosen was to map the reads on the mRNA reference and then remap the 
unmapped reads to the genome with the purpose of getting rid of reads originating from 
known genes. Once this was done, the next problem was to identify regions of interest in the 
genome. The first idea was to calculate the coverage over the whole genome and plot it to see 
if any hotspots would emerge. Given the sheer size of the human genome it was readily 
discovered that plotting so many points was not feasible, hence it was decided to divide the 
genome into 10,000 bases long adjacent windows. Reads were counted per windows and 
several visualizations techniques explored, e.g., plotting in R/excel and using genomic 
viewers (IGV and SeqMonk). To make a long story short, that approach was not successful 
because no clear hotspots emerged and the visualizations were chaotic and confusing. 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
17 
Since a simple visualization approach did not work to identify regions of interest, a DEA was 
performed to identify windows that differ significantly between healthy and cancer samples 
and it turned out to be promising.

3.7 Differential Expression Analysis 
The regular input into expression analysis is a table containing counts for all genes for every 
sample. This study focuses on intergenic regions were no genes have been described by 
definition. Instead of counting reads per gene it was decided to split the human genome into

Next, a count table was generated using Bedtools' coverage that returns the depth and breadth

4  Discussion 
This study analyzed expression in the intergenic regions for primary-, metastatic- and normal 
samples. The DEA resulted in 263 windows that differ between TP and NT and 180 of them 
are up-regulated in TP. The intergenic region is expressed and those areas are not annotated 
so the function of those transcripts is mostly unknown but the TP and NT seem to have a 
different expression profile in the intergenic regions. 
 
4.1 Mappers 
The tool used for mapping is important since all downstream analysis is based on the 
alignments. The decision of choosing a mapper was between Bowtie and BWA. Both are 
really fast but a comparison study of the mappers showed that BWA is more sensitive and 
aligns larger number of reads, hence BWA was chosen [42]. 
 
BWA is not a splice-aware mapper. When the mRNA is extracted it has most likely been 
spliced and a read spanning a splice junction might be troublesome to map if that particular 
isoform is not in the reference. The raw data consisted of paired-end reads where the 
individual read length was 48 bases. Given the original goal of identifying sORFs there was 
no need of having a splice aware mapper because short peptides would not contain introns. 
 
The STAR aligner, which is splice-aware, was used on one sample. The alignments from 
BWA and STAR were compared for one window and no differences were detected. Thus, 
using a splice-aware aligner is probably not worth the extra computation time. 
 
4.2 Number of replicates 
The number of samples for the groups in this study vary a lot. It would have been interesting 
to see if any pattern would be observed if more metastatic samples were utilized in the study, 
since they were only two. There might be a pattern in the metastatic samples because they 
probably differ somewhat from the TP. DEA comparing primary and metastatic samples have 
been done previously for different cancer types. In 2008, a research group performed a gene 
expression profiling of primary and metastatic melanoma and identified several genes that 
were significantly DE [43]. Another group, working with primary and metastatic breast 
tumors showed that the gene expression profiles were strikingly similar [44]. With regards to 
==PAGE:47==
 
37 
HNSC cancers, one group, in 2007, compared expression profiles for metastatic and primary 
tumors and found 160 genes that were differentially expressed [45].   
 
4.3 Whole genome analysis 
The aim was to do a genome-wide survey of potential tumor-specific epitopes. The quality 
filtering, both mapping steps and gene filtering were done on the whole genome but further 
downstream analysis was only carried out for chromosome 20 due to lack of time. It seems 
likely that similar patterns would emerge for the other chromosomes, and it would be 
interesting to do a whole-genome analysis.  
 
4.4 Introns 
The gene filtering step was added to the workflow quite late during the study. The mapping 
steps were supposed to filter out all reads originated from known genes but the suspicion 
arose that introns might not be present in the mRNA reference. Given that the focus of this 
study was on the intergenic regions then reads mapping to the introns would be unwanted.  
 
When a RNA molecule is cleaved after transcription in cells, polyadenylation starts and the 
polyadenylation machinery is linked to the spliceosome, which removes introns from the 
RNA [46]. The RNA sequences are extracted via polyadenylation, therefore it seems that 
introns are already spliced out and therefore not in the mRNA reference. 
 
After both mapping steps and the resulting BAMs were filtered using whole gene 
coordinates, it turned out that 83% of the reads fell into the known genes. First we assumed 
that those reads were all coming from the introns but the fraction of reads was surprisingly 
high. In the introduction a tiling experiment was mentions were 47% of active probes lay 
outside of exons and out of the 47%, 22% of them were from introns and 25% from the 
intergenic region.  
 
To see how many of the discarded reads were coming from the introns a BED file was 
downloaded from the UCSC containing only the intron coordinates. A single BAM file 
(before gene filtering) was then filtered using intron coordinates and the numbers compared 
with the results after whole gene filtering. Total reads on chromosome 20 were ~150,000 for 
this sample and after intron filtering they were ~52,000 but after whole gene filtering the 
==PAGE:48==
 
38 
remaining reads were only ~14,500. So the suspicion turned out to be true that plenty of reads 
were actually coming from the introns and according to the numbers there also seem to be 
genes that are in the whole gene file from UCSC that are not present in the mRNA reference.  
 
4.5 Workflow 
The workflow used may have been more complicated than necessary. The steps were as 
follows: quality trim and adapter removal → mapping to the mRNA reference → extract 
unmapped reads → convert them to fastq files → mapping using the whole genome as 
reference → filtering reads out that intersected known genes coordinates. Perhaps it would 
have been sufficient to map all reads to the genome after filtering and then do the whole gene 
filtering and skip the mapping to the transcriptome.  
 
4.5.1 Converting BAM to fastq files 
After the first mapping step, all singletons were lost when converting the BAM files into 
fastq files. In retrospect, there is a relatively simple solution to this problem which is to 
extract the singletons in a separate file. Then map the paired-end and singletons separately, 
in paired-end and single-end mode, respectively, and merge them afterwards. That would 
have avoided the loss of the singletons. 
 
4.6 Further work 
A framework has been developed to identify windows that differ between TP and NT. The 
aim was to predict potential tumor-specific non-gene epitopes. Next steps towards that goal 
would be to narrow the windows into segments that contain the bulk of the reads, e.g., the 
~2,000 bases in the window that was analyzed in section 3.8. Those sequences would then be 
fed into a program that translates the sequences into all peptides of appropriate length for 
MHC binding and their binding potential to MHC predicted. Finally, the peptides could be 
confirmed experimentally using a peptidomics approach.
