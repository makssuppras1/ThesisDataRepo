ABSTRACT 
The oil and gas drilling (OGD) industry is infamous for its high mortality rate and for accidents with 
disastrous consequences. Human errors are regarded as one of the key causal factors in the majority 
of the cases. Taking human error into consideration during risk management is nothing new in OGD 
but whether it is done enough and how it should be done are commonly debated topics in recent 
publications.  
 
An investigation of techniques for managing human error related risks in the OGD industry has been 
carried out. Nine of the most commonly applied techniques for risk assessment in OGD were 
investigated together with six of the most promising techniques for human reliability analysis (HRA). 
The first nine were quickly found unfit for detailed human error analysis and so only the six HRA 
techniques were investigated in-depth. The in-depth investigation comprised three levels of 
evaluation: first level was based on descriptive literature, the second level on other comparative 
studies and the third level on actual application of the most propitious techniques. 
 
The first level showed that the nine general safety analysis techniques are not capable of 
describing human error to the same detail as the six HRA techniques, even though the nine 
today predominantly are used by the industry for the purpose 
 
The second level indicated some clear tendencies across industries, on how experts deem 
the usability of the HRA techniques 
 
The third level consisted of three tests, each being a HRA of a typical OGD procedure. The 
tests proved the application potential of the HRA techniques  
 
The six HRA techniques are: Action Error Analysis (AEA) - A Technique for Human Error Analysis 
(ATHEANA), Cognitive Reliability and Error Analysis Method (CREAM), Human Error Assessment 
and Reduction Technique (HEART), Human Factors Analysis and Classification System (HFACS) and 
lastly Technique for Human Error Rate Prediction (THERP). 
HRA was in conclusion found as a beneficial but neglected part of risk management in OGD and will 
continue to be so for as long as oil and gas are demanded by society. No single best HRA technique 
can be distinguished, the quality of the results differ based on the analysed system and the resources 
available for the analysis. 
AEA, ATHEANA, CREAM and HFACS all possess qualities encouraging their use in the different 
situations encountered in OGD. However, an optimal OGD HRA technique has yet to be adapted 
before a consistently high safety level can be reached.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 MOTIVATION AND SCOPE 
The petroleum industry has grown rapidly in the past 150 years, from being as simple as digging 
holes to offshore operations so advanced that they technologically can be compared with rocket 
science.  
The petroleum industry is very diverse with sectors such as oil and gas exploration, retrieval, 
transportation, refining and storage. Due to time and resource constrains of this study, the scope was 
narrowed down to one of the core sectors, drilling, which is part of oil and gas retrieval.  
Oil and gas drilling (OGD) is commonly known as a dangerous undertaking. Not only for the people 
on the rig whom can be directly in harm’s way but also for the entire society which can be affected by 
the economic and environmental consequences of accidents. There has on average been a major 
accident every fifth year with 11 fatalities and loss of platform since the first offshore well was drilled 
(Hollaway & Johnson, 2014). The stakes are high but oil and gas are valuable resources clearly worth 
taking risks for. However, it is within everybody’s interest that all risks are reduced as much as 
reasonable possible.  
Risks are controlled through risk management which is part of health, safety and environmental (HSE) 
management. Before risk management, risks were rarely discovered sooner than noticeable 
accidents started to occur, which meant that risk reduction could not effectively be made in advance 
of accidents. Risk management has undergone important developments since it first appeared as a 
field of study after Second World War. Many different theories have been developed on how to 
assess risks, each with different taxonomies and focuses.  
The scope of this study was narrowed down to one of the most recent developments of risk theory, 
which is the focus on risks caused by human unreliability. Human reliability analysis (HRA) is carried 
out in order to incorporate human error in risk management. HRA has proven very successful in many 
industries but are not yet well enough implemented in the oil and gas industry (Hollaway & Johnson, 
2014; Li, Cho & Ren, 2014; Poblete, 2014; Fraser et al., 2015; Gilroy & Dumolo, 2013; St John, 2015; 
Thorogood et. Al., 2015; Gould, Ringstad & Van der Merwe, 2012). 
Most of the techniques for carrying out HRA were developed for the nuclear power plant industry and 
later proven generic, which allowed adaptation to other high risk industries (Everdij & Blom, 2013). It 
is however, not a necessity that a technique can be transferred directly to other industries not even 
after it has been proven generic (Chandler et al., 2010). Sometimes the empirical models do not 
translate – in other cases the taxonomies mismatch the context of the new industry. Investigating the 
usability of HRA techniques for application in OGD HSE was therefore set as the overall objective of 
this study.

Chapter 2 gives the technical background on oil and gas drilling industry. By focusing on historical 
and future technological development rather than specific technicalities. This chapter has another 
focus, which is the impact new technology has on risk and human errors. It is also including an

2 BACKGROUND ON OIL AND GAS DRILLING 
2.1 EVOLUTION OF OIL AND GAS DRILLING 
Section 2.1 shows how the technology of OGD constantly has been changing historically.  
 
2.1.1 PRE-DRILLING ERA 
The initial purpose of accessing the subsurface was not oil and gas but food and water. People of 
prehistoric cultures (and present-day Palaeolithic cultures) were digging holes manually without the 
use of any tools (Carter, et al. 1961). Specifics from this period are very uncertain but since the 
invention of the wheel (around 4000 B.C.) and up until the end of 600 B.C. people are believed to 
have started digging with the aid of simple tools such as shovels, baskets attached to ropes of leather 
and simple pulleys (Carter, et al. 1961). 
 
2.1.2 EARLY DRILLING ERA 
Digging for brines (with oil as a bi-product) can be tracked back to the Chou Dynasty (China) 600 
B.C.. By 1500 A.D. the Chinese were so skilled that they reached depths up to 2,000 feet (Carter, et 
al. 1961).  
The first dedicated oil production can be tracked back to the third and fourth century in the Middle 
East where oil was freely accessible on the surface (oil lakes) (Al-Hassani, 2008).  
In the sixth century hand dug pits were made, especially in Baku, Azerbaijan. Baku was the most 
famous oil producing area of that time. Oil demands rose when alternatives to incineration were 
invented. Tar was for instance (starting in the10th century) used for covering dirt roads and treatment 
against insect bites (Al-Hassani, 2008).  
The first technological boom came in the period 1800 to 1859, both in Europe and America. In this 
period many different pneumatic drilling machines were developed. With these new machines, wells 
up to 2,000 deep and two feet in diameter ended out as routine jobs (Carter, et al. 1961).  
The first distillation of kerosene from crude, the first refinery and the invention of the kerosene street 
lamp (all by Polish Ignacy Łukasiewicz in the early 1850’s) started the first international oil boom 
(Fleig, 2005). Pioneering inventions of this period were: the square hollow kelly (1844) and circulation 
fluid to remove cuttings (1845) (Carter, et al. 1961).

2.1.3 MODERN DRILLING ERA 
Beginning of modern drilling era 
The name, modern drilling era is widely accepted but there is some disagreement to the exact starting 
time. It starts with the first commercial well but whether it is defined as the first high productivity well 
(either Baku in 1848, Poland in 1853 or Romania in 1857) or the first well drilled with a steam engine 
driven rig (the Drake well, USA 1859).  
The period of pneumatic drilling ends in 1880 and is where most of the general principles applied in 
modern day drilling were invented. Still pneumatic drilling stood for nearly all successfully drilled wells, 
regardless all the new technologies (Carter, et al. 1961).  
Pioneering inventions of 1848-1880 were: combined gear driven rotary and pneumatic machine 
(1865), diamond coring (1869), fluid driven rolling cutter bits (1866) and rotary system with gear 
driven hoisting (1869) (Carter, et al. 1961) were introduced. 
 
Second part of modern drilling era 
There was an important transition period between 1880 and 1929, known for the shift between 
pneumatic and rotary drilling systems. Semi-portable and mobile rigs were developed and rotary 
drilling proved superior. A typical rig from 1915 is shown in Figure 1 (notice the amount of exposed 
moving parts compared to a modern day rotary rig as seen in Appendix B). A lot of important rotary 
drilling equipment which in some places are still used today – was developed in this period. 
Equipment such as chained tongs, which replaced rope and sticks and the elevator system which 
made dual action while tripping possible (Carter, et al. 1961).  
Drilling often resulted in large oil and gas gushers when over-pressurized reservoirs were penetrated. 
These gushers posed a huge risk, so it became mandatory to use blowout prevention by the year 
1929 in the state of California (soon followed by other developed countries). This resulted in a boom 
of blow out prevention technologies (Carter, et al. 1961).  
The pioneering inventions of this period were: grip rings for continues feed (1883), roller bearing 
bushing (1886) & two drum hoist (1890) (Carter, et al. 1961).

Transition between modern drilling era and today 
The period from 1929 up until the end of the 1950’s was influenced by the invention of the one-
package internal combustion engine which allowed for new powerful and mobile rigs (Carter, et al. 
1961). Other emerging fields were well logging and geophysical exploration.  
These 30 years also hosted the first hydraulic fracking, first horizontal well and the first real offshore 
platform. 
The general technological development of the 20th century can be seen in the technological time line 
shown in Figure 2. The offshore oil and gas boom started in the second half of the 20th century, when 
the move into offshore required many new technologies both on exploration, drilling, stimulation, 
completion, production and transportation (as can be seen in Figure 2). This period was also the 
dawning of tar sand production (Alberta, 1967), deep water drilling (with the first deep water drilling 
vessel the Glomar Challenger which launched 1968) and arctic drilling (which can be marked with the 
opening of the trans Alaskan Pipeline 1977) (SPE, 2003).

2.2 TECHNOLOGY OF OIL AND GAS DRILLING 
Section 2.2 gives an understanding of the diversity of technology and equipment used in OGD today 
(present risk picture). Additionally it gives a background on why new technologies are invented and 
what the future might bring which will help to understand how the risk picture changes over time.  
 
2.2.1 TECHNOLOGY TODAY 
Many new technologies have been implemented since the beginning of the century thanks to the 
development of cheap super computers that have initiated an era of digitalization with robots, 
automation, big data models and clever equipment (British Petroleum, 2016).  
Communication technology has greatly improved, so that the expensive personnel (engineers, 
managers etc.) rarely have to be at the platform. They can now do their job by monitoring real time 
data from onshore offices, thereby save costs and avoid the hazard of having more people on the 
platform (Baker Huges, 2016). However, not having key decision makers on-site increase risk in 
emergency situations especially if radio transmission is lost.  
Mobility is still a key performance has been enhanced greatly the last decades. Today heavy duty 
onshore rigs can be moved by helicopter for drilling in remote areas or can be disassembled easily 
and transported by trucks. Offshore drilling vessels can easily move across oceans. These are either 
self-propelling drill-ships, towable jack-up rigs or towable semi-submersible rigs (Transocean, 2016, 
Saipem, 2016 & Maersk Drilling, 2016). 
Directional drilling made it possible to drill with a controlled variated angle e.g. along strata in a 
complex geology. This is now possible due to equipment improvements and implementation of 
logging while drilling and mud/electromagnetic telemetry (Halliburton, 2016).   
New technologies are either supportive (improving existing technologies) or disruptive (replacing the 
need for old technologies). The risks picture is altered either way but more severely with disruptive 
technologies. 
A supporting technology in offshore drilling is the dual derrick vessels. These vessels can carry out 
both drilling and cementing or drill two holes at once thereby increasing effectiveness by simply 
adding an extra derrick to the normal single derrick vessel. The risks of a dual derrick vessel are 
different from a design perspective and due to the simultaneous operations that will affect each other. 
New risk assessments are needed for dual derrick operations, even though the risks of each derrick 
itself remains almost the same, the possibility of interactions needs to be taken into account. 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
A disruptive technology in offshore is coiled tubing drilling and workover, which can be used instead of 
drilling workover with jointed drilling pipes (Baker Huges, 2016). In moving to coiled tubing the design 
changes together with most of the related tasks, which gives a completely different risk picture. -  This 
is well reflected when comparing conventional drilling with coiled tubing drilling incident reports (IADC, 
2016). 
Another well-known disruptive technology is the top drive rotary systems which in almost all cases 
outperformed the traditional rotary table. Again, a new type of accidents occur with top drive 
compared to rotary table (IADC, 2016). Most new offshore platforms have both top drive and rotary 
table systems installed so that the rotary table can be used in cases where it is more suitable 
(Transocean, 2016, Saipem, 2016 & Maersk Drilling, 2016).  
The most game-changing disruptive technology today is automation. Automation is attractive due to 
the fact that less people give less risk and less downtime. E.g. iron roughnecks are replacing chained 
tongs and their operators and automatic pipe handling is replacing floor hands at the catwalk and/or 
the derrick man. Fully automated drilling rigs are called multi machine control (MMC) systems. With 
MMC the driller carriers out normal operations single handed without leaving the dog house (Maersk 
Drilling, 2016). Remotely controlled rigs have even been proposed but are still not operating 
anywhere. The reasons is that maintenance and inspection still have to be done manually, which 
nevertheless requires people on the vessel. Another limiting factor of remotely controlled rigs is the 
fear of being fully reliant on a signal between the rig and the people controlling it remotely1. 
 
2.2.2 DEVELOPMENT OF NEW TECHNOLOGY 
All oil and gas initiatives are big economical investments, mostly made by international oil companies 
(IOCs) and are therefore governed by the price of oil. Changes in oil price are governed by the 
balance between supply and demand: If demand is higher than supply prices will rise and vice versa. 
Oil has always been more valuable than gas and is often considered a bi-product, which explains why 
gas prices usually follows oil prices. 
The activity level of the oil industry is depending on the international oil prices, new technological 
initiatives are consequently started when the oil price forecasts are promising. This relation is due to 
the fact that a big part of oil reserves are found in challenging locations, where it is more expensive to 
retrieve. For example few companies will dare to start development of an arctic field in a market with 
oil prices below 75 USD per barrel when the breakeven price is 75 USD per barre. The typical oil 
break even prices (shown in Figure 3) gives an approximated view on what kind of locations oil are 
feasible during specific price environments.  
Most IOCs were turning to ultra-deep, artic, tar sands and fracking before mid-2014 when the oil price 
was 100 USD per barrel. This is simply because the easy accessible part of the oil and gas reserves

2.3 HUMAN ERRORS IN OIL AND GAS DRILLING 
Section 2.3 gives an overview of the general extent of human errors in OGD, major accidents and few 
brief statistics followed by an investigation of the effect of automation on human error in order to 
evaluate if human error analysis will become obsolete as automation becomes more dominant in the 
industry. 
 
2.3.1 INCIDENTS AND ACCIDENTS 
 “There is increasing evidence that human error is a major contributor to system risk: approximately 
50%–80% of the incidents and the accidents in these safety-critical systems have been associated 
with human error” (Liu & Li, 2014, pp 1706).  
This tendency is confirmed when reading through some of the thousands of available incident reports 
(IADC, 2016; OSHA, 2016) where most cases result in hazardous situations, near misses or minor 
injuries. However, severe incidents (accidents) with consequences such as career ending injuries, 
fatalities, serious impact on environment (and economy) are not rare.  
When looking at the overall statistics of Gulf of Mexico between year 2003 and year 2010 there were 
128 fatalities (fatality rate 7 times higher than average of other US workers), 1,349 serious injuries 
and 858 fires and explosions (Hollaway & Johnson, 2014).  
The worst recent accidents with human error as either the direct cause or as a main contributing 
factor, are briefly described here:  
April 2010: Deep Water Horizon, Gulf of Mexico. Accident led to 11 casualties and the single largest 
offshore oil disaster ever to occur in US territory. As the Macondo well had to be abandoned a cement 
plug was installed to seal off the reservoir, the test which had to determine if it was done properly was 
wrongly interpreted by the staff, whom concluded that the plug was tight (Roberts, 2013 and US 
CSHIB, 2014). When the abandonment was initiated a kick occurred, the crew failed to identify the 
kick and act before a blowout occurred approximately one hour after (Roberts, 2013 and US CSHIB, 
2014). The staff could have hindered the accident, if it was not for these two human errors 
(Tabibzadeh & Meshkati, 2014).  
July 2005: Mumbai High North, 160 km west of the coast of Mumbai, India. Accident led to 22 
casualties and material damages worth of $280 million (Daley, 2013). A worker was injured on a 
multipurpose support vessel, due to bad weather evacuation by helicopter was impossible. The 
captain made an ill-considered decision to board the platform on the windward side (during the 
storm), to reach medical attention on the platform. However, the thrusters could not keep the vessel 
from the platform in the strong wind, the ship’s helipad collided with the gas riser which then broke. 
Gas was released from the riser and ignited, the whole platform was set aflame (Daley, 2013). 
==PAGE:22==
12 
 
March 2001: Petrobas Platform 36, 150 km off the coast of Brazil. Accident resulted in 11 casualties a 
1,500 𝑚3 oil spill and loss of a $496 million platform (Wander, 2008). The rig which was an old drilling 
rig converted into a semi-submersible production rig was running much higher than capacity. When a 
valve failed and oil and gas started to fill up the emergency drain tanks. The tanks were placed inside 
the rig’s pontoons, without proper insulation due to dangerous planning. The leakage resulted in 
several explosions which prompted 1,723 critical alarms simultaneous in the control room. The 
operator was overwhelmed by the alarms and unable to execute an effective emergency procedures 
(Wander, 2008). The lack of a hazard identification study prior to the accident (Wander, 2008) and 
poor situation awareness (Roberts, 2013) are cited as casual factors. 
July 1988: Piper Alpha, North Sea, South of the Shetland isles. It is the most devastating disaster in 
the North Sea with 167 casualties, lost platform and a total cost of $3.4 billion (Li, 2014 & Paté-
Cornell, 1997). The production was running higher than supposed and the system pressure was 3 
times higher than the initial design intended. The high pressure consequently led to a malfunction in 
one out of two gas pumps (Paté-Cornell, 1997). The dayshift failed to communicate that a safety 
valve had been removed due to maintenance to the night shift. The nightshift operator acted in 
ignorance of the problem and restarted the pump which resulted in a gas leak. The gas was ignited 
and due to bad design of the platform a chain of events quickly immobilized the whole rig’s 
firefighting, communication and evacuation systems (Paté-Cornell, 1997 & NEB, 2011). 
February 1982: Ocean Ranger, on the Grand Banks of Newfoundland accident caused 84 casualties, 
loss of a semisubmersible drilling platform (NEB, 2011). A large wave hit the platform during bad 
weather and water entered the ballast control room where water destroyed the automatic ballast 
control system. The platform capsized when the automatic ballast control system malfunctioned and 
water was let into the pontoons. One of factors that allowed the tragic development to happen, was 
that the personnel lacked specific knowledge on how to operate the ballast control system manually 
(NEB, 2011). 
March 1980: Alexander L. Kielland, Norwegian North Sea. Accident led to 123 casualties and loss of 
an accommodation platform (Officer of the watch, 2013). The platform was a semisubmersible type 
with five floaters, one of the braces (a supporting steel beam) was badly welded, which allowed 
cracks to develop (Moan, 2010). Annual inspection failed to identify the crack (which extended to 50 
% of the beam) as a hazard (Officer of the watch, 2013). A relatively small wave (compared to the 
design wave) had the beam succumb and the whole rig capsized in a matter of 15 minutes (Moan, 
2010 & Officer of the watch, 2013).  
 
2.3.2 HUMAN ERROR AND AUTOMATION 
Automation is a disruptive technology which is winning advance in most process controlled industries 
today. Automation is an old phenomenon which started in the industrialization where machines 
==PAGE:23==
13 
 
replaced men. A new wave of automation is dawning, more sophisticated machines (robots) are 
capable of replacing more complex human tasks. Development of systems that combines sensors 
and algorithms can now replace or greatly simplify cognitive human tasks - tasks which are otherwise 
performed by highly educated decision makers and specialists (Maersk, 2016).  
There is a major upside in automation: as expensive labour cost is reduced, human reliability is made 
obsolete and time is saved. Automated drilling systems are today up to 10% faster than conventional 
manned drilling rigs (Abrahamsen, 2015).  
Automation is rather new for the OGD industry, it is split in automation on the drill floor and 
implementation of advisory systems. An advisory system is a software that automatically interpret 
data from downhole sensors, the rotating system, the derrick weight and the circulating system 
combined with some manual inputs (equipment specifications). Advisory systems can among other 
things give recommendations on optimal mud weight, control torque and RPM, warn on a potential 
well integrity issue, identify kicks and more.    
Advisory systems are well implemented in other industries especially aviation and this invites a 
comparison of the lessons learned.  
There have been several catastrophic accidents caused by human factors in automated environments 
(Parasuraman & Miller, 2004). One of the most common human errors in aviation is mode confusion 
(Bredereke & Lankenau, 2002), which occurs when the operator is unaware of a (to him) hidden 
automated function and actively is working against the automation, thinking it is a malfunction or 
simply not recognising it. 
A recent study was carried out testing several qualified drillers in different simulated systems with 
different degrees of automation (Iversen et al., 2013). The study concluded that:  
 
Automated systems require a new kind of driller different from the ones seen in conventional 
drilling. They need in-depth academic knowledge of most functions in the process to avoid 
mode confusion 
 
As the system is implemented and the drillers get more confident in it, the risk of not noticing 
unwanted behaviour is increased (because overconfidence to the system builds up) 
 
A higher degree of automation is generally better because it leaves the driller with fewer 
distractions from actually supervising the process behaviour 
 
As the industry becomes more automated, the number of required personnel is reduced. However, as 
fewer people are involved in the process, the reliance on each individual increase – this is called the 
irony of automation (McLeod, 2015). This is the reason why human error cannot be neglected until the 
day where humans are completely made obsolete.

3 RISK MANAGEMENT AND HUMAN ERROR 
3.1 RISK MANAGEMENT IN OIL AND GAS DRILLING 
Section 3.1 describes how risk management is carried out in OGD. First, generally how it is part of 
HSE and further on, by going more into depth on each of the three sub divisions, analysis, 
assessment and management.  
 
3.1.1 OVERVIEW 
Risk management is a very central part of a company’s HSE profile.  It is the intelligence foundation of 
the whole safety culture in a company. Risk management is a structured way for decision makers to 
manage company’s activities in a safest possible manner. Risk management’s structure and goals 
varies depending on geographical location, company policies and type of managed assets. 
Management always has to be made for a defined system. A system can be everything from: 
complete design of a new platform (with all its equipment, staff and procedures) to a minor 
modification on an existing platform to the implementation of a new IT system in a control room to a 
modification to a procedure.  
The three most frequently applied standards for risk management in OGD are: API RP 14J, ISO 
17776:2000(E) and NORSOK STANDARD Z-013 (Poblete et al., 2014).  
The organisation of risk management is officially (however, not always practically) divided in three 
categories: risk analysis, risk assessment and risk management (as visualized in Figure 4).  
Risk management is depending on size based on a number of risk assessments. Each risk 
assessment can be based on several risk analyses. Risk analysis in turn can be carried out with one 
of many techniques, each with different scopes, strengths and weaknesses. This organisation for risk 
management is not universal and as different countries have different standards, there is a big 
variation in regulations e.g. between drilling in Norwegian North Sea and Nigeria Delta Plane.  
OGD is a high risk industry but risk management is still not applied everywhere. Some small private 
landowners with few resources to spare simply cannot afford it. Not all projects are big or advanced 
enough for the owners to see the benefits from it.

15 
 
 
Figure 4 Typical risk management setup  
 
3.1.2 RISK ANALYSIS 
Risk analyses are the backbone of risk management. The term covers all kinds of risk related 
analyses. A risk analysis can be explained by the four basic steps: 
The first step is planning the analysis: this is the preliminary work, the scope/boundaries of the 
analysis (thereby what can be managed) have to be clarified. It is also where the specific techniques 
have to be planned. 
The second step is system description: all relevant data should be collected and presented. 
Blueprints, work drawings, flowcharts of processes and procedures should be prepared for the 
analysists. Incident reports and incident statistics are also very useful but not always available for 
prospective analysis. For human error analysis of existing systems, onsite observations and 
interviews are generally required. 
==PAGE:26==
16 
 
The third step is hazard identification: a hazard is a normally contained energy that if released will 
have undesirable economical and safety consequences. All the identified hazards can either be listed 
or described by an incident realization model. A typical incident realization model describes the 
source of hazard, the hazardous event, the affected zone, how the event can be contained/controlled, 
who or what can get in harm’s way and the hazardous condition’s stretch in time. All overseen 
hazards will not be properly managed as was the case with the Petrobas P-36 (see section 2.3.1).  
Fourth step is risk estimation: all hazardous events poses a risk, which can be estimated based on 
frequency, probability and the severity. Risk estimation can either be qualitative (based on judgment 
and estimated as a point on a scale) or quantitative (based on statistics or expert judgments and 
estimated as a numeric).  
 
3.1.3 RISK ASSESSMENT 
A risk assessment is an extension of a risk analysis, it requires all the work done in a risk analysis. 
Were the risk analysis part can be purely theoretical or epistemological, a risk assessment requires 
fifth step risk evaluation.  
Risk evaluations are carried out differently whether the risk estimation was done qualitatively or 
quantitatively. For a qualitatively estimated risk, the evaluation has to be based on the experts’ 
opinions. For a quantitatively estimated risk screening criteria are used. The critical risks are selected 
and risk reduction measurements are recommended (Poblete et al., 2014).  
 
3.1.4 RISK MANAGEMENT 
As previously stated, both risk assessment and risk analysis are included in risk management. Risk 
management includes implementation of risk reduction measures, establishment of functional 
requirements and model revision (Poblete et al., 2014).  
When the system is online, near miss and incident reports should start to accumulate and give a 
database. This data have to be hold against the functional requirements. Action is required if any risk 
is higher than allowed or if some unforeseen risks are encountered. When operation is initiated, most 
capital expenses have already been paid and general design changes become very costly. This is 
why a good hazard identification is very beneficial from the economical point of view. 
In risk management, risk assessments are supposed to be iterative however, they rarely are. This is 
due to lack of resources and the fact that most risk management is carried out in the design phase 
where time is of the essence.

3.2 ROLE OF HUMAN ERROR IN OIL AND GAS DRILLING RISK 
MANAGEMENT 
Section 3.2 is an introduction on how human error can be analysed, the theory behind the techniques 
and typical human error mitigation in OGD. 
 
3.2.1 HUMAN RELIABILITY ANALYSIS  
Human error is a trigger which can release a hazard and cause an incident or accident.  All manual 
actions (both during normal and abnormal operation), inspection jobs and maintenance jobs all have a 
potential for failing due to human unreliability which is why it in high risk industries is profitable to 
include HRA in risk management.  
Most risk analyses do not include human error or only to some extent. Specialized techniques are 
developed for analysing (and sometimes assessing) human error, these techniques are called human 
reliability analysis (HRA) techniques. 
 
3.2.2 APPROACHES FOR MODELLING HUMAN ERROR 
There are four main approaches to analysing human error. The traditional safety engineering 
approach (TSEA), the ergonomics approach (EA), the cognitive system engineering approach (CSEA) 
and the sociotechnical systems approach (SSA) (Embrey, 1994). All These approaches can be 
applied individually or supplementary to one another.  
The oldest and simplest approach is the TSEA which sees human error as a consequence of unsafe 
behaviour or incompetence i.e. the blame is on the individual who is performing the task. According to 
the TSEA, behaviour modifications such as persuasion (safety campaigns) or punishment 
(disciplinary actions) are always the solution (Embrey, 1994).  
In the EA human error comes from a mismatch between the system demands and human capabilities, 
either because: the task/design is too complex/error-intolerant because the workload is too high or 
because the environment in other ways has a bad influence. In the EA risk mitigation is performed by 
introducing modifications to the system (Embrey, 1994). EA can be described as fitting the job to the 
person and is in some sense the inverse to the TSEA which is fitting the person to the job.  
The most important approach is the CSEA, which was developed by Jens Rasmussen. Most human 
reliability analysis techniques are to some extend based on the theory behind the CSEA. It divides 
human failure into two categories, errors and violations. Two sorts of violations exists and neither can 
==PAGE:28==
18 
 
be modelled. Violations can be routine (people acting based on wrong knowledge) or exceptional 
(people breaking procedures on purpose) (Rasmussen, 1983).  
Errors are the core of CSEA and are modelled based on the complexity of the task performed by the 
so called SRK-model (skill-, rule- and knowledge-based errors). An individual can solve tasks at one 
of the three behavioural levels: skill based (autonomous actions like riding a bike), rule based (familiar 
problems with a known solution like stopping for red light) and knowledge based (improvisation in 
unfamiliar environment, such as solving a problem for the first time) (Rasmussen, 1983). There are 
associated errors to each behavioural level: skill based errors are called slips while rule- and 
knowledge- based errors are called mistakes (Rasmussen, 1983).  
The benefit of the CSEA is that it gives the possibility to systematically incorporate the difficulty of a 
task and differentiate between normal operations and scenario investigation (process disturbance).  
The SSA looks for causes outside the immediate context of the operator as human work performance 
often is affected by other than the individuals wish to stay safe. The SSA realize that errors are also 
provoked by cultural, social and management aspects (Embrey, 1994). This could be dangerous 
incentives such as bonus agreements for getting job done fast or a “macho attitude” between the 
employees. SSA gives a possibility to look at human errors from an organization-wide perspective but 
is therefore not applicable for specific actions as the three other approaches.  
 
3.2.3 RISK REDUCTION AND HUMAN ERROR  
A lot is being done in order to avoid human errors in the OGD industry. Unacceptable risks can be 
reduced either by removal or mitigation. Risk reduction can either be carried out prospectively (before 
accidents occur) or retrospectively (as reaction to incident patterns or accidents) (Li, Cho & Ren, 
2014).  
Risk reduction is usually initiated by managers with economic incentives at the beginning of a project. 
In extreme cases risk reduction can be demanded by other stakeholders and this could be society 
applying pressure on politicians whom by the means of regulatory legislation forces operators to take 
action. Almost all major oil and gas disasters, including the six described in section 2.3.1 released a 
reactive wave, which caused governments all over the world to create new legislations. All trying to 
hinder similar accidents ever to occur again, each time stricter than previously.  
Managers can practically carry out human error risk reduction with a set of different corrective actions, 
the rough frequency of the different types of corrective actions are shown in Figure 5. These actions 
can be divided into three levels (Brindle, 2014): 
The first level is physical risk reduction. A dangerous tool, machine or module can be replaced with or 
modified to a safer version. Replacing and modifying equipment is often expensive and causes 
downtime. Physical risk reduction is usually not very attractive and other solutions are sought unless 
the posed risk is very critical (Brindle, 2014).  
==PAGE:29==
19 
 
The second level is function specific risk mitigation. Which is the creation of rules that ideally prevents 
risky behaviour. Typical solutions are revising/modifying procedures, providing training or developing 
new procedures. It is a cheap way of reducing risk and can be applied on all human tasks. The 
problem with new training and procedures is that they are not always effective (Brindle, 2014). 
Reducing risk at the second level is the most frequent choice by operating companies (Brindle, 2014) 
but is limited to risk mitigation. 
The third level is organization-wide risk reduction. This is usually done when second level 
measurements are no longer having the desired effect. Third level involves changing how employees 
are being disciplined both with positive- and negative- enforcement. A recent tendency in the industry 
is changing from – punishment of safety violations into rewarding willingness to report. The reason 
why this is believed to be feasible is that the management to some extend is blind on the actual safety 
level on the floor and needs a clear image in order to manage risks optimally (Bridle, 2014 & Krauss & 
Casey, 2014).

3.3 TECHNIQUES APPLIED IN RISK MANAGEMENT 
Section 3.3 will describe the different types of risk assessment/analysis techniques and it serves as a 
background material to chapter 4. 
3.3.1 GENERAL DESCRIPTION OF RISK MANAGEMENT TECHNIQUES 
There are many techniques for carrying out a risk assessment in a systematic fashion. The 
techniques are based on different theories and covers different parts of the assessment. Some 
techniques are very specific, forcing the analyst(s) to work in great detail and explore many possible 
scenarios, while other are very superficial rushing the analyst(s) through the assessment in a very 
time efficient manner. Some techniques are very open and rely on experts experience on the system, 
while other are thoroughly describing every step along the assessment leaving almost nothing to 
experience. Usually a technique will cover one or more of following topics: 
 
System description 
 
Hazard identification 
 
Scenario description 
 
Risk estimation  
 
Risk evaluation 
 
Risk reduction 
 
System description is meant for easing understanding and gives a structured base for the other steps. 
System description techniques are usually resulting in flow diagrams, blueprints or detailed 
walkthroughs – with clear boundaries limiting further work to the area of interest. 
Hazard identification is the most creative process in the risk assessment, for this reason a lot of 
techniques have built in guidewords which stimulates the analysts’ imagination in a systematic 
fashion. Hazard identification techniques are divided in bottom-up and top-down.  
Bottom-up techniques are deductive, they connect all small nodes in a system and slowly exhaust the 
system for every possible hazard (ideally). They are therefore very thorough, laborious and usually 
limited to normal operations.  
Top-down techniques are inductive, they look at the whole system and identify critical nodes based on 
generic hazards. They are usually faster than bottom-up techniques but rely in higher degree on the 
analysts’ experience. 
An uncontained hazard will result in an incident which can escalate into an accident. Scenario 
descriptions aim to describe exactly how a hazardous event will unfold or retrospectively how they 
unfolded. Scenario description techniques identify consequences and secondary consequence based 
==PAGE:31==
21 
 
on the previously identified hazards. A usual goal is to identify barriers, which are intended for 
hindering the accident to happen or unfold.  
Risk estimation in its quantitative form is the product of the approximated likelihood of an event and 
the approximated severity of the consequences. In qualitative risk estimation likelihood and severity 
are graded on a scale (e.g. high to low or 1 to 5), which are then combined in a risk matrix that show 
the total risk on e.g. a colour scale. The total qualitative risk is then found from the matrix and 
presented for each identified hazardous event. 
The goal of risk evaluation is to decide on which risks the resources should be spend. How evaluation 
is performed depends on whether the estimation was done qualitatively or quantitatively. Qualitative 
risks require specialist’s opinions or anchor points observations from previous accidents. Quantitative 
evaluation can be performed by comparing risks to screening criteria (defined by authorities or the 
management).  
After the critical risks are evaluated a decision process is started, design alterations are proposed to 
reduce risk. Risk reduction recommendations are usually limited by a principle such as “as low as 
reasonably possible” (ALARP). When resources are sparse, cost benefit analyses are used to decide 
which risk reduction measurements are more effective and which to discard. 
 
3.3.2 HUMAN RELIABILITY ANALYSIS TECHNIQUES 
The overall process of HRA techniques is basically the same as normal risk analysis – it has the 
same amount of steps, each with similar goals. Hazard identification is, however, called error 
identification and is made in a slightly different way because the focus is on finding all critical human 
failures rather than identifying the hazards and consequences. The way risk is estimated is also 
different, in the sense that the goal in most cases is to find something corresponding to the human 
error probability (HEP) and rarely as much on quantifying the severity of the consequences. HRA 
techniques are built on different models describing the mechanisms that make humans unreliable 
(Chandler et al., 2006). The underlying models are variating a great deal and so is the process of the 
techniques. 
HRA techniques are divided in first-, second- and third- generation – based on when they were 
developed or which school they belong. The first generation techniques were based on Allan Swain’s 
work from the 1960’s, which in short states that human errors are provoked by surrounding 
influences, called performance shaping factors (PSFs). The second generation is based on Jens 
Rasmussen’s work from the 1980’s, which describes errors as cognitive dysfunctions. The core model 
of the second generation is the so called skill-, rule- and knowledge- based errors (SRK) model. The 
third generation was started with Jim Reason’s work in the 1990’s, which included management and 
socio technical factors. Reason’s work initiated what today is called modern safety management.

4.1.1 BOW-TIE ANALYSIS 
Bow-tie analysis (as described in Hamzah, 2012 and ISO 17776:2000) was adopted to the oil and gas 
industry in the aftermath of the Piper-Alpha accident in 1988 (the accident is described in section 
2.3.1), as an in-depth barrier investigation tool.  
Bow-tie diagrams are meant for creating a model which can communicate a complex safety system to 
the operators without overwhelming them. The technique got its name because of the characteristic 
look of the final diagrams. 
==PAGE:33==
23 
 
Bow-tie is: 
 
A hazard based scenario description technique 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out by a workgroup  
 
Usually used for qualitative risk estimation and evaluation 
 
Mainly used for identifying barriers, precautionary tasks and consequences 
 
Bow-tie outline: 
The technique is applied on a known hazardous event, usually identified with a “hazard identification” 
(HAZID) analysis and flagged as critical. On the left side of the event are all the causes that can 
trigger it and on the right side of the event are all the consequences that can follow.  
The bow-tie analysis should identify implemented barriers (between each cause and the event) and 
all recovery measures (between the event and each consequence). Finally, it identifies which 
precautionary tasks (operation, maintenance, management and engineering) are needed in order to 
sustain the reliability of each barrier and recovery measure. 
Bow-ties are generally made on critical hazards due to high level of detail they provide and due to the 
amount of work they therefore require. 
 
Bow-tie and human error: 
The bow-tie analysis is normally applied for incorporating human factors into risk analysis (McLeod, 
2015 and Poblete, 2014). It is, however, limited to identification of critical precautionary tasks, barriers 
and preventive measures that are dependent on human performance (Hamzah, 2012 and Poblete, 
2014). It does not include human error related risk estimation nor evaluation. 
Bow-ties is actually a product of the third generation of HRA and is by the industry often considered 
human error analysis technique. The focus put on human error in bow-tie is insufficient when 
comparing the outline with any of the actual HRA techniques (presented in section 4.2).   
 
4.1.2 EVENT TREE ANALYSIS 
Event Tree Analysis (ETA) (as described in Rausand, 2004) is a scenario based technique that aims 
to find all possible consequence scenarios. It looks at all implemented barriers and the effect it will 
have if they fail or succeed. The purpose of ETA is to estimate quantitative risk, based on empirical 
failure probabilities. ETAs are typically used to completely combine a risk estimation. It will give the 
==PAGE:34==
24 
 
probability of recovering if a hazardous event occur and thereby giving a base to decide which areas 
are considered safe and which needs improvement. 
 
ETA is: 
 
A scenario based technique 
 
A prospective quantitative risk estimation technique 
 
Mainly used for quantification of specific cause consequence sequences 
 
ETA outline: 
First step in a qualitative setup: an already identified hazardous event with known consequences is 
analysed – this is the initiating event. There are for each pivotal event two possible outcomes – 
success or failure. Each pivotal event serves as a barrier (e.g. a pressure gauge, an emergency 
release valve or a smoke detector). All barriers can either succeed or fail – each outcome will led to a 
new barrier or a final consequence. 
Second step is quantification of event tree: first the frequency of the initiating event has to be 
estimated or found. Then the probability of failure and success for each outcome for each barrier has 
to be estimated. This can either be done based on statistics or with a forecast method such as the 
Delphi method (workgroup method for interactive forecasting). The total frequency is calculated for 
each final consequence.  
 
ETA and human error: 
ETA can be used on mechanical barriers (such as smoke detectors or pressure relief valves) or 
human actions (such as use of fire extinguisher or activation of alarm). The probabilities have to be 
estimated by HRA technique if applied on human error. Creating ETAs is one of the first steps in a 
technique for human error reliability prediction analysis (THERP) (which is described in section 4.2.6). 
 
4.1.3 FAILURE MODES, EFFECTS AND CRITICALITY ANALYSIS 
Failure modes, effects and criticality analysis (FMECA) (as described in Rausand, 2004) is an 
addition to the failure modes and effects analysis (FMEA), which is qualitative risk analysis. FMECA 
is, however, with a quantitative (criticality) addition. The FMECA technique originates from the US 
military and was developed after Second World War. It gives best results when carried out as a 
workshop (RAC, 1994). However, workshop is not an absolute necessity.  
==PAGE:35==
25 
 
 
FMECA is: 
 
Node or task based hazard identification, risk estimation and evaluation technique 
 
A prospective technique  
 
Mainly used as a standalone technique 
 
FMECA outline: 
FMECA can be performed on either hardware (bottom-up) or functional (top-down) basis however, 
hardware basis is the most common. The hardware approach is systematically going through each 
components in the system, while the function approach is looking at general functions in the process 
(RAC, 1994). Each step (function or hardware) is treated individually and all possible failure modes 
are written down. A failure mode is defined as something is operating or acting against its purpose. 
After that, potential consequences and the possible safeguards for each failure mode are identified 
and written down. It is possible to go directly to recommendations for risk reducing measurements 
from this point (if applying FMEA). 
Criticality analysis:  
Each failure mode is converted into a cause-barrier-consequence relationship. All of these are 
investigated in the criticality analysis. The criticality analysis is carried out differently depending on the 
available resources. It can be either expert judgment, statistics or some other background for 
estimating probabilities. Evaluation is then logical, once the risk for each failure mode has been 
quantified. 
 
FMECA and human error: 
FMECA is relatively open technique and can potentially be angled to include human errors – even 
though it is not what it is meant for. If FMECA was converted into a technique focusing on human 
error it would have similarities to the action error analysis (AEA) (Bligård & Osvalder, 2013) (AEA is 
described in section 4.2.1).  
 
4.1.4 HAZARDS AND OPERABILITY ANALYSIS 
The hazard and operability (HAZOP) (as described in DS/EN 31010-2010 and McKay, 2011) is a 
strict workshop based technique with very clear guidelines. It is fundamentally meant for systematic 
hazard identification and qualitative risk estimation and evaluation.  
==PAGE:36==
26 
 
HAZOP was developed for chemical processes and reactors, it assumes that there is no risk when 
the process is running without deviations (McKay, 2011). This makes it inappropriate for manual, 
physical, flexible tasks and scenario analysis. 
 
HAZOP is: 
 
Node based bottom up hazard identification and evaluation technique 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out in a workgroup  
 
Mainly used as a standalone technique 
 
HAZOP outline: 
A workgroup is carefully chosen, with participants of different backgrounds – together covering the 
most important technical aspects of the investigated system. A skilled facilitator is needed to keep the 
analysis within the guidelines. 
For HAZOP the system should be described with as high resolution as possible – preferable down to 
individual valves and tubes etc. The ideal base is blueprints, work drawings and process flow 
diagrams. The workgroup systematically walks through all nodes, while a list of guidewords are 
prompted for each node. The guidewords are universal consequence modifications e.g. TOO MUCH, 
TOO LITTLE or REVERSE which are applied to parameters of the system such as pressure (giving 
for example TOO HIGH PRESSURE). The significant ones are systematically discussed, guided by 
the facilitator. The potential causes for the deviation, consequences of the deviations and existing 
safeguards are found and documented. Finally risk reduction measures are proposed and used in 
further risk management. 
 
HAZOP and human error: 
The traditional HAZOP is not applicable for investigating human errors but a modification called 
“Human HAZOP” is made for the purpose (Ellis and Holt, 2009). This is however, not a very 
commonly applied human reliability analysis technique (Bell & Holroyd, 2009).

4.1.5 HAZARD IDENTIFICATION  
Hazard identification (HAZID) (as described in Rambøll, 2011 and ISO 17776:2000) is a strict 
workshop based technique with very clear guidelines. It is basically meant for systematic hazard 
identification and limited qualitative risk estimation and evaluation. 
HAZID is a quick technique compared to e.g. HAZOP. It is often used prior to a hazard/event based 
analysis. The structure of HAZID helps the team to identify more types of hazards, especially those 
which are not part of normal operations (unlike to FMEA and HAZOP).  
HAZID is: 
 
Facility based top down hazard identification technique 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Usually used prior to a bow-tie analysis 
 
HAZID outline: 
A workgroup is carefully chosen, with participants of different backgrounds all together covering the 
most important technical aspects of the investigated system. A skilled facilitator is also necessary to 
keep the analysis within the guidelines. 
HAZID is a top-down technique, a hazard register (with items such as hydrocarbons under pressure, 
dangerous equipment with fast revolting parts etc.) is walked through and for each item the workgroup 
identifies relevant nodes. For each relevant node potential escalation, safeguards and consequences 
of escalation are written down. Finally a simple analysis of the risk is made, based on a risk matrix.  
 
HAZID and human error: 
HAZID does not include human error as a specific point but has some limited potential. If the facilitator 
intentionally guides the analysis towards finding safety criticalities exposed to human error. This 
would, however, require a lot from the facilitator or a complete modification of the HAZID structure. 
The quality of the results is questionable and not backed up by the literature.  
 
4.1.6 JOB SAFETY ANALYSIS  
Job safety analysis (JSA) (as described in Glenn 2011 and OSHA 2002) also known as job hazard 
analysis, is a simple technique that addresses each step in a procedure preferable in the shape of a 
==PAGE:38==
28 
 
task analysis (TA) repeatedly until the step is exhausted for potential hazards and corresponding 
safety measures. JSAs are ideal for including workers in risk and barrier identification and for analysis 
of laborious tasks. JSA is however, not applicable for processes and facilities. 
 
JSA is: 
 
Task based top down hazard identification and evaluation technique 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out by workers 
JSA outline: 
The technique is applied on a TA. Each action is targeted separately by list of guidewords of hazard 
categories (such as SHARP, ERGONOMICAL and KINETICS). The words guide the analysist to 
identify hazards, consequences and safeguards. All critical findings are written down and the analysis 
continues with the next action in line. 
 
JSA and human error: 
The technique works with human error but is limited to the ergonomics approach (EA as described in 
section 3.2.2). It identifies possible errors and evaluates them based on expert judgement. The 
simplicity of JSA makes it un-suitable for complex- and cognitive- tasks.  
 
4.1.7 MULTIPLE PHYSICAL BARRIERS  
Multiple Physical Barrier (MPB) (as described in Fraser et al. 2015) is also known as the success tree 
analysis because it to some extent is the opposite of a fault tree analysis. This method is a newer 
simplified version of the bow-tie analysis developed for the upstream oil and gas industry (exploration 
and production, including OGD). It is meant for giving operators an overview on key functions which 
are essential for maintaining safe operations. MPB has the same purpose as a bow-tie diagram but is 
a simplified version. 
 
MPB is: 
 
Hazard (HAZID) based scenario investigation technique  
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out in a workgroup  
==PAGE:39==
29 
 
 
MPB outline: 
This technique is the inverse of most other risk analysis techniques, in the sense that it focuses on 
maintaining a safe operational state. It starts at a specific safe state such as well integrity and then 
looks directly at safety measures (barriers), instead of looking barriers through threats and 
consequences. This means that consequences are completely out of focus and thereby risk in the 
original sense of the word. 
MPB is organised in a tree structure with the desired state in the top and all safety barriers linked 
through AND/OR gates. This method is quick to perform and is more intuitive to understand than a 
bow-tie analysis (Fraser et al. 2015).  
 
MPB and human error: 
It identifies critical human actions which are necessary to guarantee safe operations. This technique 
could support other HRA techniques but will not function as a standalone technique for HRA.  
 
4.1.8 QUANTITATIVE RISK ASSESSMENT  
Quantitative risk assessment (QRA) (as described in Rambøll, 2014) are a broad description of in-
depth techniques for estimating severity of a hazardous events (specific consequences). 
 
QRA is: 
 
Scenario investigation 
 
Prospective techniques 
 
QRA outline: 
There is no specific outline for a QRA, it varies on the type of system and the available resources. 
QRAs aim to model the exact consequences of an accident, in order to fully understand the severity. 
A drilling rig accident would typically be modelled with computational fluid dynamics, wind roses, fire 
models, evacuation schemes and structural models. It provides a base for quantifying the severity in 
the risk, which is usually done in USD. Even loss of life is converted into money. The QRA is also 
used for creating evacuation plans and defining recovery measures.  
 
==PAGE:40==
30 
 
QRA and human error: 
A QRA is generally needed to provide quantification of severity of the consequences of human errors 
for large systems. 
 
4.1.9 SIMULTANEOUS OPERATIONS 
Simultaneous Operations (SIMOPS) (as described in IMCA, 2010) is a technique for assessing two or 
more operations which have potential of contributing to each other’s risk. It is frequently used in 
offshore operations, when several vessels need to operate close to each other. It should, however, be 
used in all projects which have dangerous tasks overlapping, such as maintenance during operation.  
The SIMOPS is probably the most extensive risk analysis imaginable due to complex interactions 
between more systems.  
 
SIMOPS is: 
 
Operation based technique 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out in a workgroup  
 
Complete risk assessment technique 
 
SIMOPS outline: 
The actual outline of SIMOPS is outside the scope of this study. The reader is referred to IMCA, 2010 
for description of the technique.  
 
SIMOPS and human error: 
Can be used for addressing communication and supervision, both for identifying human errors and for 
assessing the severity of the error consequences (Poblete, 2014).

4.1.10 
STRUCTURED WHAT-IF 
What-if or the structured what if taxonomy (SWIFT) (as described in Tilney, 2010) is a workshop 
based technique. The focus is on creating a list of tailored questions (guidewords). The questions are 
created by the facilitator and they are specific to a given system. The technique does not require 
detailed system description and therefore be implemented very early in a design phase. But SWIFT 
strongly benefits from system description if the facilitator is not an absolute expert on the exact 
system.  
The SWIFT is a relatively quick technique because it can be focused on safety critical aspects. The 
quality of the analysis is, however, strongly dependent on the facilitator’s ability to form the right 
questions. 
 
SWIFT is: 
 
Top-down technique based on tailored check list questions  
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out in a workgroup  
 
A qualitative hazard identification and risk evaluation method 
 
SWIFT outline: 
First an experienced analyst has to be assigned as facilitator. An important part of the SWIFT analysis 
is in the preliminary work, which involves creation of the SWIFT-questions supported by checklists 
and procedures. In order to obtain concise questions the facilitator will use TAs, design specifications, 
generic checklists, process descriptions, standards and incidents reports. The guidewords could for 
instance be: what if a strong gust of wind comes or what if the load is too high. 
A group of specialists from different sectors of the project is chosen, the SWIFT-questions have to be 
kept secret to all participants until the start of the analysis. The facilitator walks through parts of the 
system and prompts the questions, which ideally should have the workgroup discuss/explore potential 
hazards, consequences and safeguards –which in the end helps to make recommendations for risk 
reduction.  
==PAGE:42==
32 
 
 
SWIFT and human error: 
The flexibility of SWIT contains a potential for including human error but the success is very much 
dependent on the facilitator’s capabilities. 
 
4.1.11 
TASK ANALYSIS & HIERARCHICAL TASK ANALYSIS 
The task analysis (TA) is an extended version of a typical system description. TA makes it possible for 
a specialist to describe a task that requires a line of human actions. It gives an easy accessible 
(sometimes technical) overview on the process, which can be used for personnel unfamiliar to the 
task. TAs are required as a base for most HRAs but are also used in some of the normal risk analysis 
techniques such as JSA.  
There are different variations of TAs, the two most common ones are the basic TA and the 
hierarchical task analysis (HTA).  
 
TA & HTA are: 
 
System description techniques 
 
Basis for most HRA techniques and JSA 
 
TA & HTA outline: 
TA techniques are different from a simple system descriptions (blueprints, flow diagrams etc.). A TA 
will analyse all actions and determine what information is necessary in order to commence further risk 
analysis.  
In the ordinary TA tasks are broken down into actions in chronological order. In the HTA a task is 
broken down into actions in overall flow (chronological) with sub flows (not necessarily chronological).  
Each action can again be divided into subtasks – this step is required by some HRA techniques. It is 
common that each action has a thorough description and that pictures are provided, if available.

4.2.1 ACTION-ERROR ANALYSIS 
Action error analysis (AEA) (as described in Taylor, 2015) is a 2nd generation technique somewhat 
comparable to HAZOP or FMEA in its error mode identification (Bligård & Osvalder, 2013). It is a 
generic tool with the quantitative side is rooted in chemical plants (Taylor, 1994). The quantitative part 
of the technique was specified and validated recently in Taylor, 2016. 
AEA is differentiated from the other HRA techniques in that it is supported by a latent hazards 
analysis and that it has a focus on error identification. Latent hazards could be equipment failures 
either unknown or overlooked by the operator such as a weak step on a ladder or toxic fumes in a 
confined space.  
 
==PAGE:44==
34 
 
AEA is: 
 
Comparable to  
o 
Systematic Human Error Reduction and Prediction Approach (SHERPA) 
o 
Predictive Human Error Analysis (PHEA) 
o 
Human HAZOP 
o 
Action Error Method (AEM) 
 
Working with actions from a TA  
 
A prospective technique 
 
Carried out in a workgroup with experienced participants 
 
Best known for its qualitative side but includes quantification based on error causes 
 
Including aspects from EA and CSEA 
 
AEA outline (as described in Taylor, 2016 and Øien & Rosness, 1998): 
AEA requires a TA or a HTA, the technique is two parted: an action error mode analysis and an action 
error causal analysis.  
Action error mode analysis has following steps: 
 
Identify possible modes of error for each action – by choosing from a checklist with 12 modes 
of error 
 
Do a latent hazard analysis, which can be carried out in one out of two ways  
o 
By tracing all affected objects for the given action and see if they or something 
“upstream” could be deviating from normal  
o 
By a checklist approach 
 
Determine consequences by carrying out a cause consequence analysis for each action-error 
mode combination 
 
Flag safety critical consequences 
 
Identify safety measures/barriers for each safety critical cause-consequence relationship (e.g. 
a safety valve or a safety boots) 
 
Make recommendations for risk reduction (new consequence prevention barriers)  
 
Action error causal analysis (should only be carried out for safety critical cause consequence 
relationships): 
 
Characterize the action type, based on a list of 16 generic cause groups 
 
Identify possible error causes, based on experience or from tables of typical error causes  
 
Identify relevant performance influencing factors from a list. The list contains situational 
factors, task/equipment specific factors, physiological factors and idiosyncratic factors 
==PAGE:45==
35 
 
 
Identify safety measures/barriers for the causes (e.g. a work schedule that reduces fatigue or 
an advisory system which simplifies an action)    
 
Make recommendations for error prevention (new cause barriers) 
 
A quantitative part of AEA is also available and it relies on a database – Taylor, 2016 provides a 
generic plant accident data base and software suited for this purpose. Both latent hazards frequency 
and HEP are combined in order to estimate the total risk.  
 
4.2.2 A TECHNIQUE FOR HUMAN ERROR ANALYSIS 
A technique for human error analysis (ATHEANA) is a 2nd generation technique created for the 
nuclear power industry. ATHEANA is meant as a standalone HRA technique. It has never been 
validated (Bell & Holroyd, 2009) probably due to the fact that the technique completely relies on 
experts’ judgments. 
ATHEANA requires a human reliability analysist, a person responsible for safety and several technical 
specialists. The technique is divided in two parts, a qualitative and a quantitative.  
 
ATHEANA is: 
 
Selecting an appropriate plant disturbance event 
 
Mainly a prospective technique but can be applied retrospectively 
 
Carried out in a workgroup with experienced participants 
 
Both a qualitative and quantitative HRA 
 
Including aspects from EA and CSEA  
 
ATHEANA outline (as described in NUREG-1624): 
The qualitative part has focus on a base case that could for instance be an accident scenario 
involving expected operator actions. This will make a foundation for the probabilistic risk assessment 
(PRA) model.  
The PRA includes three aspects: human failure events (HFE), unsafe acts (UA) and characterization 
of the error forcing context (EFC). HFEs are all the things that can go wrong due to human 
unreliability. UAs define which actions that actually has a hazardous potential. The EFC covers the 
circumstances that forces a HFE to happen, circumstances such as a misinterpretation, having to 
perform an action without appropriate training or not being able to see a necessary object due to bad 
==PAGE:46==
36 
 
lighting.  
The qualitative part of ATHEANA follows these steps: 
1. Work out definition and interpretation of the investigated system 
2. Formulate the scope of the HRA 
3. Describe the base case, include expected operator response and key decision points 
4. Identify HFEs and UAs during the course of the scenario 
5. Identify system vulnerabilities, a hazard is only existing where HFEs and UAs collide 
6. Describe EFC  
a. Based on list of keywords (physical context) 
b. Based on potential human errors at key decision points (organizational context) 
c. Based on misleding system signals, or in other words unnecessary cognitive 
challenges from equipment (ergonomically context)  
7. Identify complicating factors 
8. Evaluate recovery potential, this should be done as a probability if quantitative analysis is to 
be carried out 
9. Interpret analysis based on qualitative part or skip this step and go to quantitative part  
 
The second part is quantification of the PRA, which is performed by multiplying severity and 
probability. In ATHEANA the probability of a hazardous HFE is defined as the product of all EFC 
probabilities, the UA probability and the probability of not recovering.  
The quantitative part of ATHEANA follows these steps: 
10. Assess probability of all particular EFCs 
11. Assess conditional probability that an UA and an EFC will collide and allow a HFE 
12. Assess the probability of not recovering from the HFE 
13. Calculate the HEP as the product of the three probabilities above 
 
At last, design modifications are incorporated to the base accident scenario in an iterative manner 
until a screening criteria is achieved.

4.2.3 COGNITIVE RELIABILITY AND ERROR ANALYSIS METHOD 
Cognitive reliability and error analysis method (CREAM) introduced in Hollnagel, 1998 is a 2nd 
generation HRA tool. CREAM has already been applied in off-shore drilling in USA (Chandler et al. 
2006) but no public follow up is existing. Validation of CREAM is ongoing (Bell & Holroyd, 2009). 
CREAM is special because it distinguishes between genotypes/causes for errors and 
phenotypes/error modes (Everij & Bloom, 2013). The technique can be applied as the basic technique 
or in an extended version (Hollnagel, 1998).  
 
CREAM is: 
 
Similar to  
o 
Bridge reliability and error analysis method (BREAM) – derived from CREAM 
o 
Driver reliability and error analysis method (DREAM) – derived from CREAM 
 
Working with actions from in a TA/HTA structure 
 
Can be carried by a sole analysist  
 
A quantitative HRA 
 
Based on CSEA  
 
CREAM outline (as described in Hollnagel, 1993): 
CREAM is TA based – all actions in the TA has to be divided into subtasks before the technique can 
be applied. CREAM can be used in its basic format as a screening technique or in its extended format 
as an in-depth technique. 
CREAM as a screening technique: 
 
Each subtask has to be fitted to a subtask type out of list of 15 types 
 
Identify the state of each of the nine performance shaping factors (PSF) for each subtask and 
their contribution to the operator’s mode 
 
Determine the operator’s mode based on the operator mode diagram. The mode can be 
either tactical, strategic, opportunistic or scrambled – each mode has 95 % confidence 
bounds for HEP 
==PAGE:48==
38 
 
 
CREAM as an extended version includes the basic version with the following additional steps: 
 
Each subtask is specified as containing at least one out of four activity specifications 
(observation, interpretation, planning or execution) 
 
13 different error modes are distributed over the four activity specifications, both errors of 
commission and omission are included, the irrelevant are eliminated 
 
Look up PSF contribution factors based on activity type and PSF state 
 
Calculate modified HEP based on PSFs and base HEP for each error mode, as the sum of 
HEPs of all error modes for each subtask 
 
Screen the quantified risks and make recommendations for reducing the human error 
 
4.2.4 HUMAN ERROR ASSESSMENT AND REDUCTION TECHNIQUE  
Human error assessment and reduction technique (HEART) (as described in Williams, 1986) es a 
simple alternative to the (at that time) most common HRA tool technique for human error rate 
prediction (THERP) (described in section 4.2.6). The main reason for the development of HEART was 
that THERP is too time consuming for many applications. HEART has a mathematical model for 
calculating probability of failure of an action based on negative contributions from the surrounding. 
HEART is a validated technique (Bell & Holroyd, 2009). 
 
HEART is: 
 
Similar techniques 
o 
Nuclear action reliability assessment (NARA) 
o 
Controller action reliability assessment (CARA) 
o 
Nuclear-electric human error assessment and reduction technique (NE-HEART) 
o 
Standard plant analysis risk – HRA (SPAR-H) 
 
Task based 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Can be carried out by a sole analysist 
 
A quantitative HRA 
 
Including aspects from CSEA 
 
==PAGE:49==
39 
 
HEART outline (according to Williams, 1986): 
This technique does not need a task decomposition in order to function as most other HRA 
techniques.  
 
Tasks have to be matched to one out of nine general tasks based on a short description (all 
generic tasks have a base HEP and corresponding 95 confidence intervals) 
 
Define the error producing context (EPC) for each task by picking a number of independent 
factors out of a list of 38 (each with a given HEP contributing effect) 
 
Calculate the total HEP for each task as the product of all effects from the error producing 
context and the base HEP 
 
4.2.5 HUMAN FACTORS ANALYSIS AND CLASSIFICATION SYSTEM 
Human factors analysis and classification systems (HFACS) (as described in Wiegmann and 
Shappell, 2001) was developed by U.S Navy for aviation mishap investigation – with an aim on 
mitigating human errors.  
HFACS is an incident investigation and classification technique based on the swizz cheese model for 
mishaps which was developed by James Reason. 
HFACS is already applied in the petroleum industry with published, successful results (Hollaway & 
Johnson, 2014). It is included as the only complete retrospective HRA technique. 
 
HFACS is: 
 
Scenario based 
 
A retrospective technique 
 
Carried out by a sole analysist 
 
Including aspects from EA, CSEA and SSA 
 
HFACS outline (as described in Wiegmann and Shappell, 2001): 
The technique aims to mitigate or prevent human error by addressing a mishap database. All mishaps 
should ideally be from the same industry and well documented. HFACS investigates incidents and 
categorizes the causes in four different levels. When all causes are found for a specific incident, the 
data is stored. As more incidents are investigated a foundation for prospective analysis is building up. 
This data can be modelled statistically and the models will be fitted for this specific industry. 
==PAGE:50==
40 
 
First level is unsafe acts (UA): the incident was allowed because of one or more UAs, these have to 
be described as either human errors (skill-based, decision-based and perceptual) or violations 
(routine and exceptional). This is following Rasmussen’s SRK-model (see section 3.2.2).   
Second level is preconditions for unsafe acts: here the motivation or cause leding to the UAs have to 
be described. It can either be caused by environmental factors (physical and technological), operator-
condition (mental- and physiological- state/limitations) and personnel factors (resource management 
and readiness).  
Third level is unsafe supervision: this can either be inadequate supervision, plan inappropriate 
operation, failure to correct known problem and supervisory violations.  
Fourth level is organizational influences: this is divided into resource management, organizational 
climate and operational process. 
 
4.2.6 TECHNIQUE FOR HUMAN ERROR RATE PREDICTION 
Technique for human error rate prediction (THERP) (as described in Swain & Guttmann, 1983) is the 
mother of all HRA techniques, it is a very thorough technique and also very time consuming 
compared to most other methods. It is a first generation technique with an in-build validated database 
for generic rate predictions. THERP was initially developed for nuclear power plants but is applied in 
many other industries today due to its general nature.  
THERP has been applied in off-shore oil drilling in USA (Chandler et al. 2006) but no public follow up 
could be found. 
 
THERP is: 
 
Task based 
 
A prospective technique 
 
Can be carried out by a sole analysist 
 
A quantitative HRA 
 
Including aspects from EA and SSA 
 
THERP outline (as described in Kirwan, 1994): 
THERP provides guidance for all steps including TA and error identification. The technique can be 
divided in a qualitative and a quantitative part, even though it does not make sense only to carry out 
one part. 
==PAGE:51==
41 
 
Qualitative part: 
 
Gather plant/process and event specific description 
 
Create the TA (see section 4.1.11 of description of TA) 
 
Identify safety critical actions 
 
Make HRA event trees for incidents caused by failed safety critical human actions (see 
section 4.1.2 for description of ETA) of caused by plant incidents (in which case THERP is 
used to assess human errors involving failure to prevent the accident) 
 
Quantitative part: 
 
Find nominal HEPs for all safety critical human actions in the event tree, these are found in 
the database 
 
Assess PSF for each safety critical human action. The PSFs are training level, stress and 
experience 
 
Find the dependencies of each PSF for each action from the THREP database 
 
Calculate HEP for each safety critical human action in the HRA event tree based on nominal 
HEP and PSF dependencies  
 
(If possible) incorporate findings in general risk assessment and evaluate human reliability 
sensitivity of the system

5 EVALUATION OF TECHNIQUES 
5.1 FIRST LEVEL – DESCRIPTIVE LITERATURE  
Section 5.1 is an evaluation of all techniques based on their descriptions from chapter 4 in the light of 
the obtained background knowledge from chapter 2 and chapter 3. 
 
The descriptions of the 19 techniques were provided as a base of comprehension, which was used in 
the evaluation of the techniques. Most descriptions includes an introduction plus a brief on the general 
use, underlying models and a quick walkthrough (outline). It can be seen that none of the 11 general 
safety analysis techniques are suited for a proper HRA but some of them have aspects which could 
serve as a useful support for the dedicated HRA techniques:  
 
The closest candidate for actual HRA is JSA , with its capability of identifying hazards related 
to the man machine interface and evaluate the risks 
 
A group of indirectly applicable techniques include bow-tie, HAZID, MPB and SWIFT. All 
useable for identifying critical human activities 
 
Some directly supportive techniques are HTA, TA and ETA which all are necessary for 
structuring the HRA 
 
Out of the six dedicated HRA techniques, HFACS was the only one which have been used in OGD 
with publicly available documentation. This means that unlike the five others, the work of collecting 
case data and/or statistics for HFACS (regarding this study) has already been completed. HFACS 
was therefore chosen as a suitable retrospective HRA technique and not investigated in-depth as the 
rest.  
 
The reader is referred to Table 1 for the briefest possible specific overview of the descriptive part. The 
table shows an evaluation of the applicability of 16 of the chosen techniques, all listed together with 
their base i.e. the starting point of a methodology. Also all of them with description of their applicability 
on each of the five regular risk assessment topics (introduced in section 3.3.1) and their potential or 
use, regarding HRA. The applicability of each the five topics is described with a few words and colour 
graded based on how much attention the topic is granted in the individual methodology. 
No single technique is capable of doing a full risk assessment for a complex system, especially not if 
human errors have to be included with a high level of detail (as seen in Table 1). Some techniques 
such as FMECA, HAZOP, JSA and SWIFT covers most topics but they will still not prove suitable for 
==PAGE:53==
43 
 
all kinds of systems, over a spectrum of conditions. The specifics about each technique is elaborated 
in chapter 4.  
It is necessary to pick a combination of techniques each time a new assessment is designed. It is not 
possible, however, to pick a single al-around winning combination, even though there are a few 
advantageous ones.  
For example for risk management of a bigger facility without dedicated focus on human error, a 
combination could be HAZID for initial identifications of the most severe hazards and bow-tie for in-
depth barrier & consequence analysis and to present the results. The bow-tie diagrams could be 
extended with ETAs for quantification of risks that are difficult to evaluate qualitatively. This setup will, 
however, be very inefficient for risk management of e.g. a simple task on a drilling rig, where most 
hazards come from ergonomics and wear. For this system a JSA would prove more suitable. 
The tendencies get even more blurred when human reliability is included in the risk assessment. All 
HRA techniques aim to estimate human contribution of risk but the means for doing so are very 
different. Both the theoretical approach (as described in section 4.2), the base and the included topics 
are varying. Another important difference is the underlying HEP-models, the techniques rely on 
different databases or as ATHEANA, on generation of probabilities.

5.2.1 EVALUATION OF AEA AND HFACS 
AEA and HFACS were not part of any of the eight big comparative studies, which included the other 
four techniques. AEA appeared in three comparable studies and the conclusions from these are 
presented directly:  
 
Everdij & Bloom, 2013 (which is a catalogue over techniques for HSE management, 
especially human error related techniques) describes AEA as a technique applicable for 
error/hazard identification & risk evaluation. It also states that AEA has a focus on hardware, 
human factors and procedures. This corresponds well with the description of the technique 
(see section 4.2.1)  
 
Bligård & Osvalder, 2014 (which is a study of how to improve AEA and two other HRA 
techniques) states about AEA “the weaknesses occur primarily in three areas: deficient 
coupling to theory in the field of cognitive ergonomics, deficient high-level perspective in the 
analysis (main focus on operations in the HTA analysis, not on more general tasks and 
functions) and defectiveness in the reporting of results, especially for large numbers of 
results.” 
 
Troola, 1993 writes: “AEA is an analysis of the technical system, and does not analyse the 
operator behaviour. The thoughts and intents of the human being, i.e. reasons for mistakes, 
are not examined.”

retrospective analysis of incident reports written by incident investigators (Wiegmann & Shappell, 
2001) and is therefore incomparable with the other techniques. HFACS is therefore not included in 
detail at this stage of the study.

5.3.1 PROCEDURE OF TESTS 
The three tests and two corresponding TAs that section 5.3 is based upon are imitated HRAs and are 
not part of a regular risk assessments.  
Regular assessments have the goals to identify critical hazards/human errors, evaluate them and 
reduce their associated risk – all specific to a given system (facility, procedure, technology etc.). 
These tests are made with the sole purpose of evaluating the applicability of the HRA techniques in 
an OGD context.  
Ideally all six techniques should be tested in a manner allowing for cross comparison on four defined 
areas:  
 
Error identification & causal analysis capability 
 
Taxonomy goodness of fit  
 
Quantitative predictability  
 
Post-accident/retrospective analysis capability

time. The ideal test setup and the limitations of the achieved test setup are discussed in chapter 6 and 
the tests are described individually in section Error! Reference source not found., section 5.3.4 and 
ection 5.3.5. The three tests were partly carried out in a workgroup with following participants:

5.3.2 OVERVIEW OF TEST-RESULTS 
This part aimed to test all the HRA techniques against each other according to the exact needs of this 
study. Time- and resource- constraints limited this investigation down to the three best candidates. 
AEA was tested on the procedure for making a connection and CREAM and HEART were both tested 
on the procedure for a soft shut-in. This was done in a way that allowed for qualitative evaluation of 
AEA (test 1) and both qualitative (test 2) and quantitative (test 3) evaluation of CREAM and HEART. 
The first test proved that AEA is a very suitable technique for error identification and qualitative 
evaluation of human error related risks.  
The second test showed that CREAM is highly suitable for OGD related systems and that it is far 
more effective than HEART, which is equally time consuming and less accurate in taxonomy and 
underlying models. 
The third test showed that both CREAM and HEART gave decent results, when used for HEP 
estimation of blowout frequencies. It was however, not possible to properly verify either technique due 
to uncertainties in the setup of the analyses. 
 
5.3.3 TESTING ACTION ERROR ANALYSIS 
Scope of test 
First test aimed to test: human error identification-, consequence investigation- and qualitative 
evaluation- capability of AEA in a typical OGD context.  
The aim of the test was not to identify and evaluate all specific risks because neither sufficient time 
nor sufficient technical expertise were available to do so. The scope was rather to test the 
==PAGE:60==
50 
 
methodology of AEA in order to increase the foundation on which the final evaluation was based 
upon. 
AEA’s potential counterpart for this test is ATHEANA but an unmet demand of technical resources 
hindered the idea of applying ATHEANA. This is why AEA was applied solely in this test. 
 
Description of System  
The chosen system for analysis is – a normalized procedure for “making a connection”, as it is done 
on a modern manual rotary drilling rig. There are many different types of rotary drilling rigs, each 
giving different procedures for the task. A brief explanation for the chosen setup follows: a drill string 
is mainly made up of drill pipes of 30 feet length, which mean that every time the drilling crew has 
progressed 30 feet a new pipe has to be attached. Attaching a new pipe is called “making a 
connection” (the procedure is described stepwise in a TA in appendix B). 
Making a connection is a well-known task and so is this specific procedure for it. The procedure has 
not changed a lot since the introduction of the rotary table in the 1950s (see section 2.1) and it can at 
a basic level be assumed that all possible accident types have already occurred. It is therefore likely 
that all feasible risk reducing measures are already well known. Making a connection is therefore not 
a very exciting procedure to make a risk assessment on, at least not in the eyes of the operating 
company. It is, however, a core procedure in drilling and is a good sandbox system to test the 
applicability of a technique on. Making a connection fits this test because it allows to test how 
applicable the HRA technique is and the findings can quickly be related to incident reports from e.g. 
the international association of drilling contractors (IADC, 2016) or occupational safety and health 
administration (OSHA ,2016).  
 
Analysis 
The analysis is presented in appendix C. Each unique step from the TA (Appendix B) was used as 
queue in the AEA. The official session with the entire work group, lasted six effective hours including 
introduction and debriefing: 13 actions were analysed out of the 27 that make out the complete 
procedure. Discussion of applicability was performed during the analysis and the facilitator was taking 
notes on the analysis process. The last 14 actions were finished by an individual analysist (NB), who 
also carried out processing and reporting of the results.  
Several different critical hazards and potential errors were identified where the most critical ones were 
errors by the driller with fatal consequences such as underbalanced well (potential kick and blowout) 
and impact of travelling block with the crown-block. The safety recommendations were mostly training 
and procedural but with some hardware recommendations as well.  
==PAGE:61==
51 
 
Applicability 
When comparing the results of the HRA to accident reports (IADC, 2016), it is seen that relatively 
many of the important errors directly linked to the procedure are included in the analysis. This was 
interpreted as a sign that AEA offers good guidance on human error identification – especially when 
considering the fact that none of the analysts had any practical experience or in-depth technical 
knowledge on the work related to “making a connection”.  
There is a group of incidents which are not captured in the analysis. All incidents related to very 
specific errors which requires a very good technical understanding to identify. The typical resulting 
accidents are very coincidental e.g. “a driller started a new shift without knowing that the previous 
driller had controls prepared for an atypical operation” or “a roughneck is unaware that a longer snug 
line is attached due to some previous work”. Both example accidents have proven fatal. These 
accidents are difficult to identify with AEA unless an experienced participant is present or if the TA has 
an exceptional level of detail. 
There was another very prominent incident group, which was systematically unnoticed by the 
analysis. This group can be characterized as incidents caused by either abnormal circumstances 
(such as unrelated personnel working on the rig floor or in the derrick) or failure of hardware remotely 
related to the procedure. This was interpreted as a sign that AEA is strongest for normal operations 
including abnormal operations defined by procedures. 
The technique has a systematic approach well suited for assessing the isolated context of a specified 
procedure. The applicability was somewhat limited because no specific rig layout was chosen, AEA 
requires work drawings and equipment specifications in order to be used for effective consequences 
investigation. 
Another observation was that each error type can led to very different consequences depending on 
the analysists’ technical understanding, some of the initially identified consequences turned out not to 
be physically possible. The analysis will give wrong results if the physics are obscure to the analysts. 
Some fields that caused troubles were the physics concerning: swabbing of the string, the kelly cock 
valve and forces regarding mud flow. This is another reason why the workgroup benefits from a 
member with practical experience. 
It was noticed that after a few hours of analysis, the analysists tend to jump over crucial steps and 
focus on the obvious. This introduces a pressure on the facilitator, whom has to be vigilant and strict 
in order to keep the analysis on track. In the test it leds to a few overlooked critical human errors, 
which were later spotted during a later stage of the analysis. It is therefore crucial to have an 
experienced facilitator. 
The TA needs a complete level of detail in order for the AEA to capture all possible errors. The TA 
can, as probably with all other task based HRA techniques, greatly bias the analysis or force the 
participants to overlook certain aspects. The quality of the results was greatly reduced where actions 
==PAGE:62==
52 
 
were either skipped of clustered in the TA. AEA is similar to HAZOP, it requires full description of the 
assessed system including detailed definition of all equipment before it can be successful. 
The key of AEA is having sufficient resources. AEA is a powerful technique, with good qualitative 
potential but only if the right team is assembled. A member with technical experience, an experienced 
analysist and detailed system description are the three requirements. The technique seems very fit for 
OGD problematics, the error modes are flexible (meaning that they can be modified after demand) 
unless the build-in quantitative database is to be used.  
 
5.3.4 QUALITATIVE TESTING OF CREAM AND HEART 
Scope of test 
Second test was aimed to evaluate qualitative HRA applicability of HEART and CREAM for a mixed 
cognitive and laborious procedure, as these often are seen in OGD. In other words, the taxonomies 
was to be evaluated in an OGD context. 
It was ideally intended that AEA, ATHEANA and THERP should have be included in the quantitative 
assessment for a complete test and this is planned for subsequent studies. The organisation of the 
method trial is discussed in section 6.1.3.  
 
Description of System 
The chosen system is a normalized procedure for realizing and acting on a kick. Kick control is a vast 
subject, the identification- and shut-in- procedures varies considerably based on the circumstances. 
Most important circumstances are equipment (rig type and downhole equipment), formation properties 
(rock strength and fractures), kick severity (pressure difference and kick volume) and time of 
occurrence (during drilling, production, wire-lining etc.).  
Following set of circumstance was chosen in order to specify the analysis:  
 
Modern, fixed offshore, rotary table drilling rig 
 
Very capable and experienced drilling crew 
 
Good well integrity (casing in place), deep well 
 
Strong formation 
 
Kick exceeding kick tolerance (well will start to flow if not shut-in) 
 
Kick occurring while crew is tripping out, with the drilling bit far from the bottom 
 
Swabbed in kick – mud pressure reduced to below formation pressure due to removal of drill 
string 
 
==PAGE:63==
53 
 
The full procedure for this type of kick is determined based on examples from kick control literature 
(Mitchell, 2001; Rigtrain, 2001; ADS, 2002), the procedure can be seen in the TA in appendix D. 
  
Analyses 
The qualitative analyses lasted for two hours each four in total, excluding brakes, prior the test, 
automated CREAM and HEART excel sheets were developed in order not to waste time on writing 
and calculating. Action 1 and 2 were analysed first with CREAM and immediately after with HEART.  
The results are presented in appendix E and F (as part of the complete quantitative test, test 3). The 
main focus of this session was to apply the two techniques on the same subtasks and discuss how 
the techniques performed compared to each other benefitting from the knowledge and experience of 
the two senior analysts (RT and IK). 
Both methodologies, estimated HEPs and the taxonomy were discussed and discussions were written 
down meanwhile.  
 
Applicability 
Both techniques are resource light, in the sense that they do not require analysists with great 
technical- or academic- knowledge. They are almost equally fast to apply (with the use of automated 
tools), analysis of a subtask takes less than 10 minutes, if no problems or disagreements are 
encountered. The tests lasted longer due to the fact that all steps in the methodologies were 
discussed during the analyses. 
Results of both techniques are very dependent on the quality of the underlying TA. Each subtask 
need good explanation of operator- and system- conditions, unless the analysists have full 
understanding of the procedure in advance, which was not the case. 
Neither of the techniques include structured error identification or identification of system malfunctions 
(latent hazards). In this sense HEART is completely limited as it cannot describe errors at all. CREAM 
is limited to the 13 human error types, which are a bit too roughly described to count as an actual 
error identification. HEART, however, offers some standard remedial suggestions, which in some 
sense could have made error identification obsolete (the error is unimportant if the cause is removed). 
Unfortunately these suggestions are fuzzy and impractical – they did not prove appropriate for the 
given context.  
The CREAM error modes required interpretation for some of the generic cognitive task types. 
However, CREAM’s mathematical model is open and flexible in the sense that error types can be 
excluded if they are not sound. This was appropriate rather often as seen in Appendix E.  
==PAGE:64==
54 
 
CREAM was found to be a generally a good technique for the task studied. Two critique points are 
that it could benefit from a more oil and gas (drilling) specific glossary and benefit from some known 
anchor points in petroleum industry incident databases (quantification was however, not at focus in 
test two). CREAM could serve in a hybrid version together with AEA (or possible ATHEANA) and 
benefit strongly from its error identification. 
The underlying model of HEART is inflexible, it only allows modification though it’s two inputs which 
are generic tasks and the EPC. This is a big problem as they in many cases turned out almost 
equivalent due to overlap in description. 
HEART has a weakness in that the terminology of the general tasks are not fitting very well, most of 
them overlap to some extend and it is hard to pinpoint the exact action type. This will definitely give 
issues with reproducibility and also cast doubt on the precision of the results. The mathematics 
behind HEART are also questionable for instance, it is possible to get HEP higher than 1 because of 
some of the very high EPC contribution factors.  
HEART does not recognise the existence of a positively contributing context. The EPC is even 
skewed, as all points are in-depended. Factors such as fatigue should have some dependency 
incorporated (affect other parts of the EPC). All points in the EPC are furthermore linear which is also 
not realistic e.g. it should be distinguished between being fatigue and being very fatigue. Furthermore 
many of the points in the EPC seem to be irrelevant for OGD. 
It was agreed by the end of the session that CREAM is a better alternative than HEART when applied 
in OGD. HEART could prove more effective if a new version was created similar to NARA which is 
HEART modified for specific use in the nuclear industry. However, the resources are probably better 
spend on modifying CREAM, which unlike HEART is not excessively simple. 
 
5.3.5 TESTING PREDICTABILITY OF CREAM AND HEART 
Scope of Test 
Third test was an extension of the second test, it aimed to verify the predictability of HEART and 
CREAM. The two techniques were tested on the same system and their individual results were 
compared in order to evaluate which technique is more suited for quantitative use in the OGD 
industry.  
 
Description of System 
The system is the most commonly used (standardized) procedure for shutting in a well when a 
swabbed in kick occurs, followed by a volumetric bleed kill (see full description in section 5.3.4 and 
appendix D).  
==PAGE:65==
55 
 
The procedure was divided in ten unique subtasks. The kill procedure was set to be the most difficult 
standard kill procedure – a volumetric bleed (Rigtrain, 2001). The volumetric bleed was divided into 
eight subtasks, of which six are similar to some of the previously analysed subtasks. 
This analysis should roughly capture the total human error contribution of a blowout that occurred 
while tripping out of hole. This makes it possible to compare (or verify) a combination of estimated 
blowout probability and statistical kick frequency with statistical blowout frequency.  
 
Analyses 
The quantitative analyses lasted a week, automated CREAM and HEART excel sheets were 
developed in order not to waste time on reporting and calculating. All subtasks from appendix D were 
analysed first with CREAM and later with HEART. The results were combined in two simple event 
trees (see Figure 8 and Figure 9) which illustrates the estimated HEPs, estimated consequence 
frequencies (from appendix E and appendix F) and how the total blowout frequency was calculated (in 
accordance to the TA from appendix D).  
The two event trees have the same pivotal events. The sequences were simplified in the sense that 
following points were neglected: 
 
Equipment failures i.e. no latent hazards 
 
Recovery potential from human errors i.e. all human errors are irreversible 
 
Complexity of human errors i.e. no in-depth study of secondary consequences 
 
The frequency of the initiating event, a swabbed in kick, was converted from kick data (Wylie & 
Visran, 1994) which already was divided in time of kick- “kick occurred while tripping out of hole” was 
used. The final estimated blowout frequency is the compared with SINTEF data (Holland, 1997), 
which states the frequency of a swabbed in blowout. 
Total frequency of failure according to the CREAM analysis was 0.003 which means that a swabbed 
in blowout will occur once every 330 well. Applying HEART on the exact same system predicted a 
frequency of 0.007 or that a swabbed in blowout will occur once every 139 well. According to the 
SINTEF database, swabbed in blowout frequency is 0.00164 or once every 609 well. This means that 
CREAM in this particular case was 185% of the real data while HEART was 437% of the real data. 
 
Applicability 
The main purpose of this test was to verify or indicate the quantitative potential of the two HRA 
techniques. Both techniques gave conservative estimates, CREAM almost a factor two and HEART 
==PAGE:66==
56 
 
more than a factor four over the real blowout frequency. This is an indication that CREAM is a more 
precise method but that neither method is completely off. There is some uncertainty to the test 
method due to the fact that the SINTEF database is not distinguishing between hardware failure and 
human errors and due to the simplified ETA (this is discussed more thoroughly in section 6.1.3)  
The test leds to HRA of 12 unique subtasks which provided further insight on the qualitative 
applicability of the techniques (in extension to test 2 explained in section 5.3.4). Some observations 
were noted but not discussed because the third test was not performed in a work group.  
CREAM qualitatively seen is generally very applicable, the nine PSFs cover the context of OGD well. 
It would, however, make more sense to apply the technique on a specific crew, operating a specific 
rig owned by a specific company than as an analysis made on a generic case. The taxonomy is very 
intuitive and the learning curve is very steep.  
HEART qualitatively seen is a bit doubtful. The EPC lacks positive contributors and many of the 37 
points are not really suitable outside of a control room. The generic tasks are too general and not 
covering the context of OGD very well. Bad generic tasks sometime result in a transverse workflow – 
meaning that the base HEPs are used instead of the actual task descriptions to classify the given 
subtask. The benefit of HEART is that it offers standard remedial measures for each of the 37 
contributions and has a build in 95-confidence interval.

6 DISCUSSION 
The study had to use many different resources in order acquire the best possible foundation for 
optimizing how human error is included in OGD risk management. This was done over two rounds. The 
first was an investigation of the industry and how risk management is done – this part was objectively 
done and hence does not require any discussion. The second part was about picking the most common 
and most promising risk assessment techniques for including human error and thereafter evaluating 
them in order to find the best suited for OGD – this part was done subjectively and the discussion is 
therefore directed on this part.   
The overall evaluation was carried out based on a three levelled investigation: the first level was the 
descriptive literature, both primary literature and secondary literature. The second level was the 
comparative literature, which is credible sources’ attempt to evaluate the different techniques' 
performance against each other – typically in a given context e.g. medical care or space travel. The third 
level was testing the techniques in the context of OGD and comparing the results against two experts’ 
evaluation and publicly available data.  
 
6.1.1 FIRST LEVEL – DESCRIPTIVE LITERATURE  
The first level of evaluation which included what could be found in descriptive literature, contributed with 
the following knowledge, which was used in the overall evaluation: 
 
Background of techniques  
 
Methodology of techniques 
 
What are the intended and practical uses of the techniques 
 
Descriptive literature is either directly written by the inventor(s) of the technique (primary source) or by 
users of the technique with the aim of teaching others the practical use of the technique (secondary 
source). Both primary and secondary sources are necessary in order to understand the theoretical and 
practical limits of a technique. The primary literature has one big weakness in that it is described by the 
inventor, who often has a close relationship with his/hers technique. Primary literature is therefore likely 
to be biased. Secondary descriptive literature, is also expected to be biased to some extent, due to the 
fact that the authors already chose to use the techniques and might not be aware of the potential of 
other techniques. Secondary literature, however, has some advantages. Firstly it describes how others, 
typically with a more practical approach, apply the technique. Secondly it reveals which industries use 
the technique and typically includes some examples why the technique is applied in this specific field of 
work.  
==PAGE:70==
60 
 
It is important to understand the basic concepts and methodologies of the techniques before trying to 
understand other people’s evaluations and especially before trying to apply the techniques. 
Descriptive literature serves as good first references, which allow early elimination of bad candidates. 
For instance, a technique with no mention of e.g. human actions cannot be used for structured HRA2. 
This fact was used to identify which of the investigated techniques had potential for analysis of human 
error. It was at this stage that all the ordinary techniques used in risk assessment (techniques from 
section 4.1) and HFACS were excluded from the more time consuming investigation done in the next 
two levels.  
 
6.1.2 SECOND LEVEL – COMPARATIVE STUDIES 
Adding the second level to the overall evaluation, which included findings from comparative studies, 
contributed with the following knowledge used in the overall evaluation: 
 
Vast body of expert opinions on 
o 
Common uses of techniques 
o 
Quality of applying the technique for different uses 
o 
Strengths and weaknesses of techniques 
o 
Comparison of the different techniques’ performance  
 
Quantitative estimation precision  
 
Generic and specific (to a given industry) application  
 
All the chosen HRA techniques are generic techniques (Bell & Holroyd, 2009), they are all except 
ATHEANA and HFACS, based on models with a statistical foundation from different industries. Generic 
application of most of the techniques have been validated in different studies (Bell & Holroyd, 2009). 
However, this does not prove that the statistical background of the methods are suitable for analysis in 
OGD. The biggest challenge the petroleum industry faces with estimation of human reliability, is the lack 
of OGD specific statistical background (Li, Cho & Ren, 2014; Gilroy & Dumolo, 2013; Thorogood et al., 
2015).  
Most HRA techniques were invented for risk analysis on nuclear power plants. For example, when 
generic techniques are adapted to aviation, one of the main issues is that the typical tasks are different 
between the two industries (Chandler et al., 2010). The mathematical foundation is invalidated when the 
generic tasks (model inputs of most techniques) do not match with the analysed task.  In many ways 
aviation and nuclear power plants are not alike. A very central difference is the response time which in 
most cases is much longer in the control room at a nuclear power plant than in the cockpit of an 
airplane. Also the contexts which the operators are acting in, are generally very different between the

two. Regarding OGD (which technology is described in section 2.2.1) the before mentioned issues gives 
doubt that nuclear power plant HRA techniques will translate directly. It is therefore difficult to make 
parallel conclusions on the specific applicability between industries, based on comparative studies. 
However, the overall evaluation would gain an extremely strong foundation if it were possible to 
incorporate all previous comparative studies in a way that allowed some degree of parallelism between 
their respective contexts and the context of OGD.  
The most extensive study is an example that indicates the potential untapped resources of the previous 
comparative studies: Forester et al., 2013 the Halden nuclear reactor study was an international 
cooperation of 19 nations (by the year 2013). The facility opened in 1958 and has since then served as 
a research facility with different risk related projects running constantly until now. The latest project was 
the international HRA empirical study, which included experimental staff, operator crews, HRA teams 
beside of the researchers doing the project. The necessary resources and time for making a study the 
size of the Halden project, are many orders of magnitude larger than those available for this study. The 
Halden project was specifically minded on comparing HRA techniques for application in nuclear power 
plants, so conclusions are therefore not parallel to application of HRA techniques in OGD.  
A total of 21 individual technique evaluations (observations) were taken from the Halden nuclear reactor 
project and six other studies. The studies were each based on different experience bases, some of them 
rather similar (such as nuclear power plants and chemical plants), others very different (such as aviation 
and nuclear power plants). It was observed that studies with similar experience bases also gave similar 
results and vice versa. The observations were spread over the four HRA techniques, ATHEANA, 
CREAM, HEART and THERP. The idea was to analyse the relative amount of studies which had similar 
conclusions about each technique. It was assumed that a statement is likely to be universal (also true for 
OGD) if it had a clear trends across all the studies. A clear trend was for instance when ATHEANA in 
four out of four studies was characterised as good for incident realization and with low reproducibility. In 
other cases the picture becomes too obscure, when for instance CREAM is said to be strong and weak 
quantitative by an equal amount of independent studies. 
The body of observations was fuzzy, which made very precise statistical analysis difficult. Fuzzy in the 
sense that observations from different industries were biased for their respective industries and 
therefore not directly comparable. Another problem was that the studies were all formulated slightly 
different. Two authors might mean something different or have a different scale e.g. when grading the 
required resources for a technique. No standards exist when evaluating HRA techniques which makes 
direct comparison of different study conclusions challenging.

6.1.3 THIRD LEVEL – TESTS 
The third level of evaluation which included what could be concluded based on actual application (tests), 
contributed with the following knowledge, which was used in the overall evaluation: 
 
Application capability of techniques in OGD 
 
Specific complications with application of techniques in OGD 
 
Fit of taxonomy to OGD 
 
Quantitative precision of techniques for OGD 
 
Comparison of techniques in an OGD context 
 
Test organisation 
A well-defined picture of the available methods was drawn based on the literature study (the two first 
levels) but nothing could be considered certain before the methods had actually been tested. An ideal 
test setup for comparing all six techniques with each other was formulated but due to lack of both 
resources and time this was not completed. The setup was as follows (underline indicates which parts 
were completed): 
 
Test qualitative use of AEA and ATHEANA on same system 
 
Test taxonomy of CREAM, HEART and THERP on same system 
 
Test predictability of AEA, ATHEANA, CREAM, HEART and THERP 
 
Test post-accident analysis capability of CREAM, HFACS and THERP 
 
AEA and ATHEANA should have been compared because they are supposed to be capable of doing 
structured error identification. ATHEANA is according to the research, done in the first two levels, very 
reliant on the analysts’ technical knowledge and very time consuming and was therefore not tested at all 
here.  
CREAM, HEART and THERP should have been compared in order to test which of the strict quantitative 
techniques’ taxonomies were better suited for OGD. THERP being the oldest, most time consuming and 
most difficult technique was not included due to an attempt to optimize the time spent in this study. 
AEA, ATHEANA, CREAM, HEART and THERP should all have been compared on their precision in 
predicting HEPs for an accident with a known frequency (anchor point). However, again, ATHEANA and 
THERP were both excluded due to the previously mentioned reasons. AEA was excluded with the same 
explanation as with THERP. 
==PAGE:73==

The first three tests were valued higher than a post-accident analysis, which is why CREAM, HFACS 
and THERP were not compared in a retrospective analysis. 
 
Implications with TAs 
OGD is a restrictive industry and therefore specific material on processes and equipment is usually not 
publicly available. Oil and gas operators tend to keep this material classified. It has therefore not been 
possible to obtain detailed drawings or procedures for the TAs. It was even challenging to find high 
video quality footage on how the procedures are carried out. This study could have benefitted from 
direct collaboration with an operating company or simply by an interview with a driller or other 
experienced platform worker.  
The TAs were made based on drilling- and kick control- textbooks and publicly available “Fair-use” 
copyright protected videos. General textbooks do not usually show exact practical procedures and 
videos filmed by e.g. roughnecks are often done with a motive of showing off at the cost of safety. The 
lack of high quality material can be justified when it is clearly stated: that the tests are intended to 
investigate the applicability of techniques in an OGD context not meant for actual risk management of a 
specific case.   
 
Test 1 Qualitative application of AEA 
AEA requires at least one practical participant in the work group. This definitely impacted the workflow 
and results of the test, especially as the quality of the TA foundation was questionable. It was, however, 
possible to ignore the limitations on technical competences and evaluate the methodology’s goodness of 
fit, by the aid of the two specialists’ knowledge. 
 
Test 2 Qualitative application of CREAM and HEART 
This test went without any issues, other than those related to the TA. 
 
 
Test 3 Verification of quantitative predictability of techniques 
This test has the potential of being highly useful for further HRA in OGD. It could potentially verify the 
use of the techniques for OGD if the test was carried out in complete detail with all the necessary 
resources. Actual verification was however, not met, even with the very promising results prediction 
error less than a factor ten in both cases. The reasons why this test does not count as a full validation 
and why the predictions cannot be fully relied upon are: 
==PAGE:74==
64 
 
 
Implications with base of TA (as discussed above), make it hard to create precise subtasks 
 
The SINTEF data base (used for blowout frequency) includes both human errors and hardware 
malfunctions. This meant that the actual blowout frequency due to human error is estimated to 
be between  50 % and 80 % of the used frequency (as mentioned on page 11) 
 
The actual kill procedure was the largest contributor to the total HEP. In this test the most 
difficult kill method was used which increased the estimated blowout frequency. In order to 
make a more realistic estimate all four alternative methods (explained in appendix D) have to be 
analysed separately and then weighted by a factor reflecting how frequent the methods are 
applied  
 
Human errors can in a lot of cases be recovered. This was neglected in the predictions due to 
the unknown effect of time/urgency. Some kicks will cause an uncontrolled flow in a matter of 
minutes and others are much slower. This fact makes recovery probabilities complex to model 
 
The errors are believed to have contributed in the following way: 
Point I did not necessarily cause any error but makes the result untrustworthy and is not acceptable for 
an actual verification.  
Point II results in a lower actual blowout frequency caused by human error (in SINTEF data), which 
increases the gap between the predictions.  
Point III justified part of the error but will be extremely difficult to quantify statistically. It could be 
eliminated if expert opinions were used to estimate how frequently the different kill methods are applied 
and then analysing each kill procedure separately.   
Point IV is a critical grey zone e.g. if a kick is detected and the roughnecks fail to insert the casing slips 
properly before the driller starts lowering the string, it will almost certainly be recoverable. The same 
goes for many of the other actions but it is very hard to estimate how much time is allowed for recovery 
at the different stages. 
To summarize: there are different errors affecting the result in opposite directions, cancelling the error to 
some extent. Great refinement of the analyses is needed before the individual error contributions can be 
quantified.

7 CONCLUSION 
7.1 ACADEMIC CONCLUSION 
An accident is in a sense a manifestation of a badly managed risk. Historically seen OGD has been a 
high risk industry, the number of accident has not decreased despite technological advances and a 
greater focus on safety. Accidents, especially offshore, have extensive impacts on all stake holders. 
Lives are lost, the environment is severely damaged and the operating companies lose valuable assets. 
Many of the accidents seen in recent times had human error accredited as one or more causal factors. 
Human errors are important and they should be given more attention in OGD risk management than is 
common today. 
Risks including those caused by human unreliability, are introduced every time new technologies are 
developed. The stream of new technologies and thereby also human error caused risks can be assumed 
to continue for as long as it is profitable to drill for oil and gas.  
Also, human error in general has been in focus since the Second World War. The development has 
been very strong especially for nuclear power plants and aviation. For these industries, specialized HRA 
techniques, which can estimate HEPs and be used for finding risk reduction measurements, have been 
developed.  
Human error is a highly debated topic in the oil and gas industry. Ordinary risk analysis techniques are 
still applied when implementing human error in risk management. It is therefore a necessity to evaluate if 
the techniques already applied by the industry, are thorough enough and if not which techniques should 
be used instead. 
A total of 15 techniques applied in risk management were chosen for investigation in this study, with the 
aim of finding the most suitable techniques for including human error in risk management. They were 
divided in nine ordinary risk analysis and risk assessment techniques, which were the most common 
from the OGD industry as well as six specific HRA techniques. None of the nine techniques are 
considered sufficient for implementing human error in risk management. The overall individual 
evaluation of each HRA technique is given in Table 2.

7.2 ACHIEVEMENTS 
The results of the research are compared to the objectives stated in section 1.2. 
 
The main achievements of this study were: 
 
I. 
It was confirmed that human error is a major contributor to OGD accidents and therefore 
exceedingly important to include in risk management. In fact human has been found to be 
involved in over 80% of the major accident in drilling, see chapter… 
II. 
It was found that the role of human error in OGD risk management is mostly dealt with by 
means of human factors engineering with ordinary safety analysis methods. Dedicated HRA is 
largely neglected in the industry. 
III. 
The feasibility of applying HRA in OGD was demonstrated by assessing the results of actual 
application of HRA. It was possible to identify critical errors which could led to accidents and 
which could have been hindered by HRAs.  
The effectiveness of HRA is strongly dependent on the available resources, access to practically 
experienced personnel and to high-quality descriptive material.  
IV. 
Various HRA and general safety analysis techniques were investigated. Just a subset were 
qualified to be included in in-depth study excluding all the general safety techniques. The 
selected methods were all found to provide significant input to the evaluation of risk. The criteria 
of selection were the ability to work with chosen tasks in insufficient detail to provide relevant 
feedback. 
V. 
The evaluation of techniques showed clear strengths and weaknesses of all the investigated 
techniques. Selection of techniques should be based on of the analysed system’s 
characteristics (see section 7.3 for guidance on choice of technique). 
VI. 
The overall assessment showed that: 
a. Human error should definitely be included in risk management, by the implementation of 
HRA. 
b. With HRA, availability of resources has a huge significance, comparable to ordinary 
safety analysis techniques such as HAZOP. 
c. Results of HRA analyses are valuable not only for determination of risk level with 
approval purposes but also for finding risk reduction measurements.

7.3 RECOMMENDATIONS FOR SAFETY PRACTITIONERS  
Up to 80 % of accidents in the oil and gas drilling (OGD) industry are caused by human errors (see 
section 2.3.1), including several devastating accidents such as the Deep Water Horizon blowout. This 
should be reason enough to understand the potential of reducing human errors and thereby the need for 
human reliability analysis (HRA).  
Human error analysis should therefore be a part of risk management in the OGD industry for as long as 
new initiatives continue to be implemented. The application of general safety analysis techniques3 
should continue as before with an increased focus on identifying critical human tasks. All safety critical 
tasks should then be analysed with the most suitable HRA technique(s). The result of the HRAs are then 
directly applicable in regular risk estimates. This should be proceeded by a general identification of 
hazards using methods such as HAZID or HAZOP, or by collection of a representative set of accident 
reports. HRA should always be proceeded by observations in the field and in the control room and by 
interviews with operators if the plant actually exists.  
This study identified four suitable HRA techniques out of the six investigated. Each HRA technique is 
proposed for different circumstances and with different purposes: 
 
ATHEANA  
o 
For response to accident scenarios (e.g. in the early planning stage of a new 
implementation)  
o 
For accident scenarios where procedures cannot yield a realistic description (e.g. an 
explosion or a wave exceeding design criteria)  
 
AEA 
o 
For qualitative analysis of well-defined procedures (everything with a finished design 
and corresponding procedures)  
o 
For structured error identification 
 
CREAM 
o 
For estimating human error probabilities of human actions 
 
HFACS 
o 
For retrospective incident investigation (post-accident analysis) 
o 
For database establishment  
None of these techniques were directly invented for OGD and might not be long term solutions. 
Specially designed techniques (or modifications of old ones) should be developed for OGD purposes, 
based on OGD incident statistics.

7.4 SUGGESTIONS TO FURTHER RESEARCH 
Extension of HRA technique investigation 
The optimal test setup (explained in section 6.1.3) was not achievable. It is recommended to complete 
the test setup as described but little more can be achieved without corporate support.  
In order to fully optimize the way human error is included in risk management in OGD, more techniques 
have to be investigated. A list of some interesting techniques is given here but many more techniques 
could be included: 
 
Accident sequence evaluation program (ASEP)  
 
Connectionism assessment of human reliability (CAHR) 
 
Success likelihood methodology (SLIM)  
 
Commission errors search and assessment (CESA) 
The next step after choosing the best out of all the candidates is to tailor the techniques to the OGD 
industry’s needs. Whether this should be done as research or through actual application is not known. 
One thing that can be done before implementation by the industry is creation of hybrid techniques. Much 
potential lies within hybrid HRA techniques. The term covers when several complimenting HRA 
techniques are combined in order to get more cons without having to run more analyses.  
This study managed to identify the potential of combining AEA and CREAM as a hybrid but creating a 
hybrid is a trial and error process. Methodologies have to be incorporated in a new structure and more 
research is required if the underlying models need modifications.   
 
Verification of predictability of HRA techniques 
An obvious choice for actual verification of the techniques would be not only to analyse the soft shut-in 
procedure for a swabbed in kick – but to analyse the whole spectra of kick types and their respective 
shut-in procedures. This would in a matter of a few months give several more predictions, which would 
allow for some more sophisticated investigation of the error and thereby make verification or rejection 
possible. The four previously discussed flaws should however, to some extend be remediated before 
commencing any serious verification attempt.

APPENDIX D – TASK ANALYSIS 
SHUT-IN WHILE TRIPPING  
There are many different procedures for shutting in a well, they differ based on equipment and 
circumstances. Soft shut-in is the most frequently used procedure when: the kick occurs while tripping 
out of hole (or in some cases while pulling out the string while making a connection), due to swabbing 
and on a fixed rig (onshore or offshore). Figure 1 shows the generalized procedure for a shut-in of a 
well on a fixed platform which is performing a tripping operation. 
The task analysis (TA) is based on Mitchell, 2001; Rigtrain, 2001; ADS, 2002.  
Pictures are taken from: 
Appendix B, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NsFTVfZQ6lg , 
http://www.efcgroup.net/downloads/BOP-Control-System-BC0114001A.pdf  and 
http://www.rigserv.com/pdf/CompleteBrochure2004.pdf  
The TA is supposed to serve human reliability analysis (HRA) with CREAM and HEART. 
 
 
Figure 10 soft shut-in procedure for a fixed drilling rig 
==PAGE:136==
126 
 
Action 1: 
Detection of kick – swabbing in a kick while tripping out of hole. Figure 2 shows an overview of the 
detection process. There are 7 common causes for a kick, but this analysis is limited to one cause, 
swabbed in kick. Swabbing happens as the drill string is pulled out of the hole. The available volume 
to the mud is increased when pipe is lifted out of it. If the volume is increased but the amount of mud 
stays the same the height of the mud column falls. With a lower mud column height comes a lower 
mud-pressure. If the pressure inside the well is smaller than inside the formation flow will start. 
 
Figure 11 kick detection (Mitchell, 2001, Rigtrain, 2001 and ADS, 2002) 
 
Subtask 1.1: 
Identify increase in pit volume of trip tank – a special mud tank is used while tripping in order to 
monitor the volume of mud in the hole. If a kick is present it means that formation fluid have entered 
the borehole. If the kick size exceeds the predefined kick tolerance shut-in must be initiated. 
This is typically don every 3 lengths of drill pipe and every length of drill collar. 
==PAGE:137==

Figure 12 trip-tank with level indicator 
 
Subtask 1.2: 
Spot mud return rate increase – spotting an increase in mud return flow rate can indicate a kick 
almost instantaneous. Modern drilling control panels with digital displays will have a mud inflow rate 
indicator as seen in figure 4.

Action 8: 
Choose right kill method or not to kill if well is not flowing. The three most frequent methods are 
volumetric bleed, bullheading and off bottom kill.  
All three kill methods have several variations depending on specifics of the well and formation. All 
sharing the purpose of getting the kick out of the wellbore. This can be done off bottom on bottom or 
while stripping down to bottom. It can be done by pushing the kick back in the formation or by 
circulating the kick out of the hole and it can be done with different mud weight sequences. 
All the variation procedures vary some, but all have one in common: commencing a successful kill 
requires execution of following three general types of subtasks: 
 
Subtask 8.1: 
Assess the situation and decide on the right method. 
 
Subtask 8.2: 
Execute the kill – which include handling of both mud pump, surface choke, BOP rams, HCR valve, 
safety valve and tripping in the drill string. This correspond to 2.1, 2.2, 3.2, 4.1, 5.1 and 6.1. 
 
Subtask 8.3: 
Monitor and interpret pressures during what sometimes can be a very time consuming procedure.

APPENDIX F – SOFT SHUT-IN ANALYSIS WITH HEART 
WARNING: this document is not meant for educational purposes, a good knowledge on 
application of HEART is necessary for comprehension. 
 
Technique: 
Human Error Assessment and Reduction Technique 
 
Methodology: 
The HEART HRA analysis were carried out in excel. A standard sheet was created which based on 
class of general task and state of the 37 point in the EPCs calculates the basic HEP and the 95-
confidence interval. 
First HEART was used qualitatively in a short session with all three analysists, with the purpose of 
evaluating the technique in the context of oil and gas drilling.  
Then HEART was used to predict the frequency of blowouts from the occurrence of swabbed in kicks. 
 
Participants: 
J. Robert Taylor – Inventor of AEA 
Igor Kozine – Senior researcher DTU 
Nicki Christian Blådal-Hansen

NB only

28-2-2016 08:00 – 18:00 Checking results 
29-2-2016 08:00 – 18:00 Presenting results

First part 
The discussion yielded following conclusions 
 
HEART is a resource light technique  
 
The terminology is generally not good and would need a thorough rework before it suits oil and 
gas drilling  
 
Mathematics are unsound – independent points of EPC are multiplied with likely outcome of 
HEP>1 
 
Model of HEART does not capture dependencies of some points in EPC (such as fatigue which 
will influence the effect of nearly all other points in EPC)  
 
All points in EPC are linear, some should def. be non-linear (varying effect depending on the 
severity) 
 
Most of the points in EPC were found to be irrelevant for the 2 cases 
 
Action classifications are ambiguous and overlapping, the technique would benefit from more 
classes
