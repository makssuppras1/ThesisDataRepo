Abstract
Human–Robot Collaboration (HRC) increases the need for systematic, early­stage safety
analysis, yet existing practice often relies on manual documentation and expert judge­
ment, making traceability and change management difficult. This thesis addresses these
practical limitations by enhancing the Function­Centered Hazard Identification Approach
(F­CHIA) with a structured cross­domain dependency representation.
The main contribution is a DMM­enhanced F­CHIA framework that formalizes the relation­
ships between system functions and functional safety requirements using a DMM(Domain
Mapping Matrix). In the proposed workflow, hazards are identified through function de­
viations and their associated causes and consequences, and each resulting requirement
is explicitly linked back to the originating functions through the function–requirement ma­
trix. This representation supports systematic coverage checking, improves bidirectional
traceability, and enables clearer impact analysis when the system design changes.
To operationalize the framework, this thesis develops and implements an open­source
software tool that supports F­CHIA analysis workflow. The tool provides an interactive
diagram workspace for conducting F­CHIA modeling, a synchronized table view for pro­
ducing auditable records, and an automatically generated function–requirement DMM for
traceability inspection. In addition, it integrates requirement­to­standard linking to sup­
port compliance­oriented documentation and demonstrates extensibility by connecting
F­CHIA results to FTA(Fault Tree Analysis).
The approach is evaluated by reconstructing a published case study on a semi­autonomous
agricultural tractor. The results show that the tool can reproduce the baseline hazards
and requirements while improving function–requirement traceability and reducing manual
consistency work.
Keywords:
HRC; Functional Safety; F­CHIA; DMM; Hazard Identification; Traceability;
Open­Source Tool

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
HRC is becoming a defining feature of modern industrial and service systems, enabling
humans and robots to share physical workspaces and jointly perform tasks that were pre­
viously automated or manually executed in isolation. Compared with traditional industrial
automation, HRC promises substantial benefits in flexibility, productivity, and ergonomics,
but it also introduces new forms of safety risk. Direct physical proximity, dynamic task al­
location, software­driven control, and uncertain human behavior create hazards that are
no longer adequately addressed by conventional protection strategies such as physical
guarding or strict separation.
International safety standards, particularly ISO 12100, provide a well­established frame­
work for machinery risk assessment and risk reduction. However, while such standards
effectively describe what must be achieved in terms of safety, they offer limited guid­
ance on how to perform hazard identification for highly adaptive, software­intensive, and
human­interactive systems. Traditional hazard analysis methods such as HAZOP, HARA
and FMEA remain essential, but they were primarily developed for static, component­
oriented systems. As a result, they struggle to express hazards that arise from functional
deviations, contextual changes, or dynamic human–robot interaction.
To address these limitations, Wu et al.[1] proposed the F­CHIA, which shifts hazard anal­
ysis from component failures to system functions operating under varying contexts. By
systematically identifying tasks, functions, realizations, deviations, causes, and conse­
quences, F­CHIA enables hazard reasoning early in the design phase, before detailed
implementations exist. This makes it especially suitable for modern HRC systems, where
functional intent and interaction semantics are as important as physical structure.
Despite its conceptual strength, F­CHIA is currently limited by its reliance on manual and
document­based workflows. Relationships between functions, hazards, and safety re­
quirements are typically maintained using tables or spreadsheets, resulting in poor trace­
ability, limited scalability, and difficulty in managing design changes. Moreover, F­CHIA
provides no formal mechanism for representing cross­domain dependencies, such as
links between functions and requirements. These limitations restrict its applicability in
large­scale engineering projects and hinder its adoption in modern, collaborative system
development processes.
1.2
Research Problem and Proposed Approach
This thesis addresses these challenges by integrating F­CHIA with DMM, a matrix­based
method for representing relationships between different system domains. The proposed
F­CHIA–DMM framework formalizes traceability between system functions and functional
safety requirements, enabling structured dependency analysis, consistency checking,
and change­impact evaluation. Building on this framework, this work further introduces
an open­source software tool that operationalizes the methodology through interactive
modeling, automated traceability management, and integrated FTA.
1.3
Contributions
This thesis makes two primary contributions:

Literature Review
2.1
HRC and Its Safety Challenges
Human–robot collaboration (HRC) offers significant productivity and flexibility benefits in
manufacturing, but it also poses inherent safety challenges. Unlike traditional industrial
robots confined behind fences, HRC involves humans and robots working in close prox­
imity, which increases the risk of accidental collisions or other hazardous interactions if
proper safeguards are not in place [2]. In Fig. 2.1, HRC can be categorized into differ­
ent levels—coexistence, cooperation, and collaboration—characterized by progressively
tighter spatial and functional coupling between humans and robots. As the level of inter­
action increases, so does the complexity of safety assurance and risk management.
Figure 2.1: Levels of HRC [3]. (a) Coexistence: human and robot work in shared space
without shared goals; (b) Cooperation: they operate on a shared goal with limited inter­
action in shared space; (c) Collaboration: they work simultaneously on a shared object in
a shared space.
Ensuring safety in such shared workspaces is complex: advanced sensors, real­time
monitoring, and intelligent control algorithms are required to prevent injuries without un­
duly hindering productivity [4]. In addition, increasing mental stress owed by operators
due to the robot procimity [5]. However, many current HRC implementations still lack rig­
orous safety measures. A recent review by Arents et al. [3] found that 1/4 of surveyed
HRC applications included no specific protective actions, and over 1/2 did not reference
any safety standards. This situation does not stem from a lack of willingness to address
safety issues. Rather, the stringent demands on system safety and the resulting un­
certainties regarding the safety aspects that must be complied with significantly hinder
progress [2]. Although the international standard ISO12100 [6] provides general princi­
ples and guidance for risk assessment, and ISO/TR14121 [7] offers examples of methods
of performance, expert knowledge and practical experience remain indispensable. This
reliance poses a substantial challenge for small and medium­sized enterprises and orga­
nizations, which helps explain why safety standards are not addressed in the majority of
HRC applications.
Against this background, ISO 12100 may serve as an appropriate entry point for exploring
a simple, low­threshold tool to support systematic risk assessment in HRC systems.
2.2
ISO 12100: Principles for Machinery Safety
(EN) ISO 12100 [6] is the most important standard for risk assessment, whose primary
purpose is to provide designers with an overall framework and guidance for decisions

during the development of machinery to enable them to design machines that are safe for
their intended use.
This International Standard specifies basic terminology, principles and a methodology for
achieving safety in the design of machinery. It specifies principles of risk assessment and
risk reduction to help designers in achieving this objective. These principles are based
on knowledge and experience of the design, use, incidents, accidents and risks associ­
ated with machinery. Procedures are described for identifying hazards and estimating
and evaluating risks during relevant phases of the machine life cycle, and for the elimi­
nation of hazards or the provision of sufficient risk reduction. Guidance is given on the
documentation and verification of the risk assessment and risk reduction process.
ISO 12100 also define key terms used throughout the standard, ensuring consistent un­
derstanding of safety­related concepts in machinery design. ISO 12100 is classified as a
Type­A safety standard, which means it outlines general safety principles that apply to all
categories of machinery. It is part of a broader hierarchical structure of machinery safety
standards that also includes Type­B: covering generic safety aspects and safeguards, and
Type­C :focused on specific machinery or groups of machines. This standardization hi­
erarchy enables modular and scalable application of safety measures. This International
Standard is also intended to be used as a basis for the preparation of type­B or type­C
safety standards.
Central to ISO 12100 is a risk assessment and reduction methodology that involves the
following key steps: 1) determining the limits of the machinery, including intended use
and reasonably foreseeable misuse; 2) identifying hazards and hazardous situations; 3)
estimating the risks; 4) evaluating the risks; and 5) reducing the risks as needed. This
structured process ensures that all phases of the machine’s life cycle—from design and in­
stallation to operation, maintenance, and decommissioning—are adequately addressed.
The standard places strong emphasis on defining terms and concepts clearly to facilitate
a shared understanding among stakeholders. These include definitions related to types
of hazards (e.g., mechanical, electrical, thermal), types of guards and protective devices,
and concepts such as residual risk, safety functions, and human­machine interaction.
ISO 12100 prescribes a three­step approach to risk reduction, reflecting a hierarchy of
control measures: 1) Inherently safe design measures: These aim to eliminate hazards
at the design stage through careful consideration of geometry, material selection, control
systems, and ergonomic principles. 2) Safeguarding and complementary protective mea­
sures: These include physical guards, protective devices, emergency stop functions, and
measures for rescue or safe access. 3) Information for use: Where residual risks remain,
appropriate warnings, instructions, signage, and training must be provided to inform users
of remaining hazards. However, such information must not be seen as a substitute for
design­based or technical safety measures. But It does not deal with risk and/or damage
to domestic animals, property or the environment.
In summary, ISO 12100 establishes a harmonized and legally robust framework for ma­
chinery safety that not only facilitates compliance with EU law but also contributes to the
development of safer, more reliable, and user­friendly machines. Its structured method­
ology, emphasis on lifecycle considerations, and alignment with the Machinery Directive
make it an indispensable reference in the field of machine design and industrial safety
engineering.

2.3
Review of Safety Analysis Methods and Tool Support
Although ISO 12100 establishes the overarching process for hazard identification and
risk reduction, it provides primarily high­level principles and leaves substantial flexibility in
how hazards are identified and analysed in practice. This becomes a limitation for HRC,
where hazards often emerge from software­driven decision­making, perception uncer­
tainty, dynamic task allocation, and close physical interaction. Consequently, engineers
commonly rely on established safety analysis methods to operationalize ISO 12100’s in­
tent. Classical approaches (HAZOP, HARA, and FMEA) provide structured techniques
for identifying and evaluating hazards from complementary perspectives, while more re­
cent approaches such as STPA and F­CHIA extend coverage toward interaction­related
hazards and functional deviations.
2.3.1
HAZOP
The Hazard and Operability Study (HAZOP) is one of the most established Process Haz­
ard Analysis techniques, originally developed for large­scale process plants. It provides a
structured, qualitative, and systematic method for identifying deviations from a system’s
design intent that may lead to hazards or operability issues [8]. A multidisciplinary team,
guided by an experienced leader, applies predefined guide words such as No, More, and
Less to explore how deviations in key parameters or behaviors may arise and what their
causes and consequences might be. IEC61882 [9] formalizes this “guide­word exami­
nation,” ensuring thorough, node­by­node coverage of system functions and documenta­
tion of deviations, causes, consequences, and safeguards[10].Figure 2.2 illustrates the
standard HAZOP workflow, from dividing the system into study nodes to documenting
deviations and recommendations in the final HAZOP report.
Figure 2.2: Typical HAZOP procedure, showing node selection, guide­word application,
and documentation of identified deviations [11].
According to ISO 12100 [6], hazard identification must include both the system’s intended
use and reasonably foreseeable misuse of the machine. HAZOP, by systematically ex­
ploring deviations from design intent, therefore aligns with and complements this require­
ment by offering a repeatable process for analysing unintended operations and misuse.

Although HAZOP was originally developed for process industries, several studies demon­
strate that it can be effectively adapted for HRC. Guiochet et al. show that HAZOP can
be combined with UML behavioral models to analyze hazards arising from human–robot
interaction sequences, software­driven behaviors, and deviations in control logic, thereby
extending the method beyond traditional process parameters [12].
A broader review
by Huck et al. confirms that HAZOP­based approaches are increasingly used in indus­
trial HRC risk assessments, particularly when adapted to capture interaction dynamics,
unsafe control actions, and context­dependent deviations characteristic of collaborative
robotics [13]. These studies support the applicability of HAZOP in robotics when guide
words and process variables are reinterpreted in terms of robot motion, perception, control
actions, and human–robot interaction behaviors. Overall, HAZOP supports systematic
deviation coverage in HRC when guide words are mapped to interaction­relevant vari­
ables, but it may require complementary techniques to capture complex human­driven
interaction hazards.
Commercial tools such as PHA­Pro [14] and Ansys Medini Analyze[15] support docu­
mentation and traceability. Recent work highlights increasing efforts to automate HAZOP
through expert systems, simulation­based approaches, integrated multi­method tools,
and AI techniques such as ontologies, Bayesian networks, and NLP [16]. Although chal­
lenges remain—such as knowledge­base construction and limited validated datasets—
automation shows promise for improving completeness and efficiency, particularly during
early design.
2.3.2
HARA
Hazard Analysis and Risk Assessment (HARA) is widely used in the automotive and
robotics domains [17]. Its main objective is to evaluate the safety relevance of functions
and operating scenarios, particularly in systems incorporating electronic and software­
based control. HARA identifies hazards arising from functional failures and classifies risk
based on severity, exposure, and controllability, yielding safety goals and Automotive
Safety Integrity Levels (ASILs).
HARA extends ISO 12100’s principles by introducing a quantitative, software­oriented
approach to risk estimation. It is particularly valuable in robotic systems with complex
interactions between perception, control, and environment—such as unintended robotic
arm motions or perception module failures. For instance, a detailed HARA was applied
in a Level 4 automated vehicle scenario, demonstrating systematic hazard­identification
and ASIL derivation using ISO 26262 methodology [17]. This aligns with broader findings
that ISO 26262’s software­related processes require adaptation when applied to com­
plex, data­driven or non­deterministic systems [18], further underscoring the importance
of rigorous hazard analysis in such environments. In HRC, HARA is particularly useful for
scenario­based risk prioritisation and deriving safety goals for software­based functions,
but it is less explicit in eliciting detailed interaction­driven causal mechanisms.
Tools like Ansys Medini Analyze [15], IBM DOORS [19], and Cameo Safety and Reliability
Analyzer [20] offer integrated HARA workflows.
2.3.3
FMEA
Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) is a systematic, bottom­up methodology used
to identify potential failure modes, their causes, and their effects on a system, with the ob­
jective of assessing and prioritizing risk so that corrective measures can be implemented
to improve safety and reliability [21]. In FMEA, each failure mode is evaluated using the
Risk Priority Number (RPN), defined as the product of occurrence, severity, and detection,
allowing analysts to rank failures and allocate resources to the most critical issues. When

2.3.4
STPA
Systems­Theoretic Process Analysis (STPA) is a modern hazard analysis technique de­
veloped by Leveson as part of the STAMP accident causality framework. Unlike traditional
methods based on reliability theory, STPA is grounded in systems theory, treating safety
as a problem of inadequate control rather than component failure [26]. This shift enables
STPA to identify a wider range of causal scenarios, including unsafe interactions among
non­failing components, software design errors, human–automation coordination issues,
and system­level control flaws [27].

STPA aims to identify hazardous system behaviors by analyzing the control structure that
governs system operation. The technique uses functional control diagrams and a set of
guidewords related to inadequate control actions. The analysis proceeds in two steps: 1)
identifying unsafe control actions (UCAs) that could lead to hazards; 2) determining the
causal factors—such as flawed control logic, incorrect or missing feedback, timing issues,
or degraded controls—that could produce those UCAs [26, 28].
Compared to traditional techniques such as FTA, STPA provides more explicit analyt­
ical guidance and captures scenarios not involving component failures. Studies have
shown that STPA can identify all failures found by fault tree analysis while also revealing
additional causal factors related to software behavior, system interactions, and design
assumptions [28]. A further advantage of STPA is its applicability early in system devel­
opment, when detailed component designs or failure modes may not yet be defined; it
can also be applied retrospectively to analyse existing systems or incidents. In HRC, this
control­structure perspective is advantageous for identifying unsafe interactions and co­
ordination flaws, although the method can be modelling­intensive for large collaborative
systems.
A variety of STAMP­based tools catalogued by MIT’s PSASS provide support for control­
structure modeling, unsafe control action generation, and traceability management during
STPA [29].
2.3.5
F­CHIA
The Function­Centered Hazard Identification Approach (F­CHIA) is a more recent method­
ology developed to address the limitations of traditional methods such as FTA, FMEA, and
HAZOP, especially for adaptive and human­interactive systems [1].
F­CHIA extends ISO 12100’s risk assessment philosophy by introducing a structured
function–context–hazard model. It shifts the focus from component failures to deviations
in system functions under varying operational contexts. The method involves identify­
ing human and robotic tasks, defining associated system functions, describing how each
function is realized (e.g., via sensors, control logic, or actuators), and applying guidewords
such as no, part of, or other than to explore potential deviations. Each deviation is then
analyzed for its causes, consequences, and mitigations, with direct linkage to functional
safety requirements.
F­CHIA offers several advantages: 1) Function­level abstraction: Enables hazard analy­
sis early in concept design, before detailed implementation exists; 2) Context­awareness:
Explicitly accounts for human behavior and environmental variation; 3) Lifecycle integra­
tion: Supports continuous traceability from concept to operation. This makes F­CHIA well
aligned with HRC contexts where hazards emerge from functional deviations.
However, F­CHIA lacks mature tool support. Its workflow relies on manual documenta­
tion, which limits scalability and integration with model­based workflows. This creates a
gap between methodological strength and practical usability—particularly compared with
traditional methods that benefit from robust commercial tool ecosystems.

their suitability for HRC differs because collaborative robotics combines close physical
interaction with software­driven control and context­dependent behavior. Classical safety
analysis methods such as HAZOP, HARA, and FMEA remain integral to structured risk
assessment and are widely used within the ISO 12100 lifecycle. In HRC, however, their

3.1
Limitations of the F­CHIA Framework
Although F­CHIA provides a structured and function­oriented method for analyzing haz­
ards in HRC, several limitations reduce its effectiveness in modern engineering workflows.
1) Lack of Structured Traceability Between Functions and Functional Requirements: A
major limitation of the original F­CHIA framework is the absence of a formal mechanism
for representing the relationships between system functions and the functional require­
ments derived from their deviations. In practice, these relationships are often managed
through spreadsheets, making bidirectional traceability difficult to maintain. As system de­
signs evolve, this lack of explicit linkage complicates validation, change impact analysis,
and regulatory compliance. 2) No Formal Representation of Cross­Domain Relationships:
F­CHIA focuses on identifying hazards from functional deviations, but it does not provide a
structured method for mapping functions to other system artifacts such as hazards, design
constraints, or requirements. This restricts engineers from analyzing how changes in one
domain influence elements in another, reducing the robustness of the safety reasoning
process. 3) Manual and Document­Based Workflow: The current F­CHIA process relies
primarily on static documentation—typically in spreadsheets or tables—to record func­
tions, deviations, causes, and consequences. This manual workflow limits traceability,
increases redundancy, and poses challenges for large­scale or multidisciplinary projects.
It also prevents integration with model­based design environments or continuous engi­
neering pipelines, which are increasingly common in modern safety­critical system de­
velopment. 4) Challenges in Change Management and Scalability: As systems grow in
complexity, the volume of identified functions, hazards, and requirements expands sig­
nificantly. Without a structured mechanism to manage interconnections and propagate
changes, updates to one part of the system can easily become inconsistent with others.
This challenge becomes particularly evident when multiple engineers or teams collabo­
rate across design domains, leading to fragmented safety analysis artifacts and potential
loss of information fidelity.
In summary, F­CHIA provides strong function­oriented hazard identification but lacks the
structural mechanisms needed for traceability, scalability, and cross­domain consistency.
To overcome these limitations, this chapter introduces the Domain Mapping Matrix (DMM),
a flexible and systematic tool for representing relationships between functions and safety
requirements.
3.2
Overview of DMM
The DMM is a rectangular, matrix­based method for representing relationships between
two distinct domains [30, 31]. The DMM provides a structured mechanism for capturing
how elements in one domain influence, depend on, or correspond to elements in another.
Unlike single­domain matrices, a DMM takes the form of an n×m matrix whose rows and
columns represent different categories of system artifacts.
In systems engineering, DMMs commonly appear as allocation or traceability matrices
(e.g., requirements­to­functions, functions­to­components). Such matrices are empha­
sized in standards like IEEE 1220­2005 because they help maintain coherence between

Approach (F­CHIA) in Early­Stage Safety Engineering

==PAGE:20==
requirements, functions, and implementations [32, 33]. Danilovi’c and Browning further ar­
gue that these matrices are essentially DMMs, as they formalize critical cross­domain re­
lationships that shape system architecture and guide design decisions [30, 31]. Since their
introduction, DMMs have been applied beyond traditional allocation/traceability, rang­
ing from product architecture–organization linkages [34] to diverse domain pairs such
as systems–organization and requirements–architecture mappings [35].
DMMs can also be used to visualize dependencies between teams or projects, revealing
interdependencies that affect coordination and resource planning. Clustering patterns
within the matrix help identify tightly coupled groups and areas where information ex­
change is critical [35]. Combined with complementary within­domain tools, such as DSMs,
the DMM provides situational visibility that increases transparency across domains and
reduces uncertainty during system development. [36]
3.2.1
Concept and Representation
A Domain Mapping Matrix (DMM) is an n × m matrix in which: 1) the rows represent
elements of one domain (e.g., system functions, persons); 2) the columns represent el­
ements of a second domain (e.g., safety requirements, tasks); 3)and the matrix entries
denote the existence, type, or strength of a relationship between the two.
Because the matrix relates two distinct sets of elements, DMMs are typically rectangular
rather than square. They allow information defined in one domain to be systematically
transformed and interpreted in another, providing a structured foundation for reasoning
across system boundaries.
A simple example is provided by DSMweb.org [37], where a DMM is used to map persons
to tasks in a process model. As shown in Figure 3.1, each row corresponds to a team
member and each column corresponds to a process activity; marked cells indicate which
person is responsible for which task. The resulting matrix reveals responsibility allocation
patterns and highlights potential coordination needs between individuals and activities.
Figure 3.1: Example of a Domain Mapping Matrix (DMM) mapping persons to tasks [37].
This example demonstrates why the DMM is well suited for representing and analyzing
cross­domain relationships in engineering. Whether used to link requirements to func­
tions, functions to components, or people to tasks, the DMM provides a compact and
analyzable structure for cross­domain traceability in complex systems.
3.2.2
Analytical Capabilities
Although structurally simple, the DMM enables several analytical operations that are par­
ticularly relevant to safety­critical system development: 1) Traceability analysis: clarify­

ing the origin, justification, and cross­domain dependencies of each requirement [34]; 2)
Criticality assessment: identifying which functions interact with or generate multiple high­
impact requirements, thereby revealing safety­critical functional clusters [31]; 3) Change­

a function is modified, added, or removed [30]; 4) Completeness checking — verifying that

3.2.3
Applications in Safety and Systems Engineering
DMMs have been widely applied in systems and safety engineering as a means to struc­
ture complex, multi­domain relationships. In safety­critical domains such as automotive,
aerospace, robotics, and other cyber–physical systems, DMMs are used to organize func­
tional architectures, allocate safety requirements, and verify the consistency of safety
artefacts across lifecycle stages [38].
3.3
Motivation for Integration with F­CHIA
The DMM directly addresses key limitations inherent in the original F­CHIA methodology.
F­CHIA effectively identifies system functions, functional deviations, resulting hazards,
and the corresponding safety requirements, but it lacks a formal mechanism for linking
these elements across domains. Consequently, the relationships among functions, haz­
ards, and requirements remain implicit and fragmented, often dispersed across multiple
documents.
Integrating the DMM closes this gap by establishing explicit and analyzable relationships
between system functions and their associated safety requirements. In doing so, it en­
sures structured traceability across the entire safety lifecycle, maintains consistency dur­
ing iterative design updates, reduces documentation errors, and provides a solid basis for
tool­supported, model­based safety engineering.
3.4
Integration Strategy: Linking F­CHIA and DMM
Integrating F­CHIA with the Domain Mapping Matrix yields a systematic, model­based
framework referred to as the F­CHIA–DMM framework. This enhanced framework formal­
izes the mapping between system functions and safety requirements, thereby improving
clarity, traceability, and maintainability.
3.4.1
Conceptual Integration Framework
Within the integrated framework, each system function identified during hazard analysis
is assigned to a row of the DMM, and each derived functional requirement is assigned to
a column. The matrix entries then indicate whether a given requirement is derived from,
mitigates, or otherwise relates to its corresponding function. This structure transforms F­
CHIA from a primarily document­driven approach into a model­based approach in which
cross­domain relationships are explicit, inspectable, and amenable to analysis.
3.4.2
Function–Requirement DMM
The resulting Function–Requirement Matrix enables backward traceability from each safety
requirement to its originating function, supports the identification of safety­critical functions
that influence multiple requirements, and allows rapid impact assessment during design
iterations. At the same time, it provides a systematic means of checking the completeness
and internal consistency of the overall safety analysis.

Workflow Integration
The enhanced workflow proceeds as follows. First, F­CHIA is applied to identify system
functions, their potential deviations, the resulting hazards, and the corresponding safety
requirements. As these requirements are derived, the DMM is populated with the asso­

3.4.4
Benefits of the F­CHIA–DMM Integration
Together, F­CHIA and the DMM provide explicit and maintainable traceability, scale to
larger and multi­domain systems, and support more effective change management. The
integrated framework aligns naturally with model­based engineering practices, reduces
redundancy in safety artefacts, and lowers the likelihood of human error in documenting
and maintaining the safety analysis.

4.1
Motivation and Design Goals
While the DMM­enhanced F­CHIA framework conceptually addresses the methodologi­

availability of a structured and accessible software environment. The motivation for de­

4.1.1
Motivation
Traditional implementations of F­CHIA rely heavily on static documentation, typically in
spreadsheets or text­based reports [1]. As the number of system functions, hazards, and
requirements increases, maintaining consistency and traceability becomes difficult and
error­prone. Engineers must manually cross­reference tables to identify which safety re­
quirements are linked to which functional deviations—a process that is both time­consuming
and susceptible to human error.
The integration of DMM conceptually solves this problem by providing a structured model
for capturing dependencies between functions and requirements. However, without a
dedicated tool, these benefits remain theoretical. Manual DMM construction quickly be­
comes infeasible for complex systems, where even small design changes may propagate
across multiple functions and requirements. Hence, a software platform is needed to auto­
mate these relationships, visualize interdependencies, and ensure continuous traceability
throughout the system lifecycle.
Furthermore, most existing safety analysis tools—such as Medini Analyze [15], APIS IQ­
FMEA, or ReliaSoft Xfmea—are commercial and closed­source. Their license costs and
proprietary data structures create barriers for academic use, open research collaboration,
and early­stage system design. This motivates the creation of an open­source, transpar­
ent, and extensible tool that supports DMM­enhanced F­CHIA while remaining accessible
to the broader research and engineering community.

4.2
Architecture Overview
The DMM­enhanced F­CHIA tool is implemented as a lightweight, single­page web ap­
plication(SPA) [39] developed with React [40], TypeScript [41], and Vite [42]. Its architec­
ture is designed for clarity, extensibility, and offline operation, allowing analysts to perform
functional safety analysis entirely within the browser without server dependencies.
Navigation across analysis stages—diagram modeling, tabular editing, DMM visualiza­
tion, FTA, and in­app documentation—is coordinated through a browser­based router,
enabling a seamless workflow within a unified interface. Each workspace is implemented
as a modular component, sharing a common data backbone to ensure that changes in
one view (e.g., the function diagram) immediately propagate to others (e.g., the safety
table or DMM).
State management is handled through a family of composable, zone­scoped stores built
with Zustand [43]. Each “zone’’ represents an isolated workspace for a subsystem or
task context, maintaining its own nodes, edges, layout state, and analysis artifacts. This
design keeps large systems tractable by segmenting analyses while ensuring persistence
through browser­local storage, thus supporting offline usage and rapid context switching.
Interactive modeling and visualization are powered by React Flow [44], which provides
a flexible canvas for representing functions, deviations, and relationships. The diagram
engine integrates automated layout utilities via elkjs [45] and validation callbacks that en­
force node­type integrity, ensuring consistent hazard modeling. All visual representations
are synchronized with a shared intermediate data model, which is also used to generate
DMM matrices and traceability tables.
The FTA component reuses the same visualization engine to maintain architectural con­
sistency. Although the current FTA implementation is limited in scope, it demonstrates
how the tool can be extended to incorporate additional safety analysis methods in the
future. Planned extensions include automatic report generation and export capabilities,
which will allow users to produce structured safety documentation directly from the stored
analysis data.

Results
This chapter evaluates the DMM­enhanced F­CHIA framework and its software imple­
mentation using an HRC case study previously reported in the literature [1]. The original
work presents a manual, document­based safety analysis of the collaborative cell, in­
cluding the identification of system functions, functional deviations, hazards, and safety
requirements. In this thesis, that study is used as a baseline for assessing the benefits of
a structured and tool­supported analysis approach.
The aim of this chapter is not to repeat the manual analysis, but to reconstruct and extend it
using the proposed F­CHIA–DMM tool and to compare the resulting artifacts. Specifically,
the chapter addresses the following questions:
• To what extent can the tool­based workflow reproduce the functions, hazards, and
safety requirements reported in the original manual study?
• How does DMM­based representation improve traceability between functions and
safety requirements?
• What advantages does the tool provide for change­impact analysis, consistency
checking, and scalability compared to the manual approach?
To answer these questions, the chapter first summarizes the HRC case study and the
corresponding manual F­CHIA analysis from the literature (Section 5.1). Section 5.3 then
describes how the case is modelled in the F­CHIA–DMM tool and presents the resulting
diagrams, tables, and Domain Mapping Matrices. Finally, Section ?? compares the man­
ual and tool­based analyses and discusses the observed benefits and limitations of the
proposed approach.
5.1
Case Study Overview
The case study used in this thesis is directly adapted from the semi­autonomous agricul­
tural tractor analysed by Wu et al. [1]. The machine was originally developed in Denmark
for use in vineyards and similar fields and is intended for the European market, where it
must satisfy CE­marking requirements under the Machinery Regulation 2023/1230/EU.
In the original study, this tractor serves as the running example for applying the F­CHIA
method in combination with other safety analysis techniques.
The tractor operates in two modes: a manual mode, in which an onboard operator controls
the vehicle via a control panel, and an autonomous mode, in which navigation, obstacle
avoidance, and task execution rely on perception systems such as cameras, LiDAR, and
GPS. The machine typically runs on relatively flat vineyard terrain and must navigate
precisely between rows of grapevines while potentially carrying various implements for
soil maintenance, pesticide spraying, pruning, and related tasks. Operation is primarily
in daytime but may be affected by adverse weather (e.g. rain, fog) that degrades sensor
performance and complicates safe navigation.
In this thesis, the safety analysis focuses exclusively on the tractor’s main body—including
its electrical system, mobile base, and attachment interfaces—while the design and safety
of agricultural attachments (e.g. sprayers) are treated as out of scope. This is consistent
with the system boundary adopted by Wu et al. [1], but here the case is re­used as a
benchmark for evaluating the proposed tool­supported F­CHIA–DMM workflow.

5.2
Baseline: Manual F­CHIA Analysis from Literature
In the original study, Wu et al. [1] apply the F­CHIA method to the agricultural tractor
described in Section 5.1 using a traditional, document­based workflow. The analysis is
carried out with textual descriptions and tables in a word processor, without any dedicated
modelling or analysis tool support. Their results therefore provide a natural baseline for
assessing the benefits of a tool­based, model­centred approach.
Wu et al. first decompose the collaborative workspace of the tractor into several hazard
zones that reflect different parts of the machine and its environment. In the published case
study, four zones are introduced: ”base zone”, ”front zone”, ”electrical system zone”, and
”surrounding zone”. The paper uses the base zone as the main running example in the
results section.
Within the base zone, the authors define three representative operational tasks that char­
acterise how the operator and tractor interact in manual and autonomous modes: Task 1—
switching between the autonomous mode and the manual mode using the control panel;
Task 2—autonomous navigation in the field, avoiding humans, other machines, and envi­
ronmental obstacles such as stones and vines; and Task 3—controlling the tractor mobile
base in the manual mode. For these tasks, Wu et al. follow the F­CHIA process flow:
starting from the tasks and design intent, they identify the system functions that sup­
port human–robot collaboration (e.g. mode management, perception of the environment,
motion control), refine them into implementation means and critical attributes, and then
systematically derive possible functional deviations. For each deviation, they document
potential causes and consequences and judge whether it can lead to hazardous events
for the operator or bystanders.
The results of this manual analysis are presented in tabular form. For each task and zone,
the tables list the system functions, functional deviations, hazards, and the corresponding
safety requirements. Wu et al. further map the hazards and requirements to relevant EU
directives, regulations, and standards. This mapping is an important part of their result,
illustrating how F­CHIA can guide the selection of normative safety measures already in
the early design stages.
Finally, Wu et al. take the navigation system from Task 2 in the base zone as an example
of how the F­CHIA results can be integrated with more traditional safety analyses. The
navigation­related functions and hazards are used as input to a fault tree of navigation
system failures. In the present thesis, the focus is on reconstructing and extending the F­
CHIA part of this manual analysis using the proposed F­CHIA–DMM tool; the subsequent
quantitative evaluation of the navigation system is therefore not reproduced.
5.3
Tool­Based F­CHIA–DMM Results
This section reports how the manual F­CHIA analysis presented by Wu et al. [1] was
reconstructed using the proposed F­CHIA–DMM tool. To focus on the most extensively

zone” introduced in Section 5.2. The same three operational tasks—mode switching, au­
tonomous navigation, and manual driving—are modelled in the tool to enable a direct
comparison with the manual analysis. The automatically generated report for this recon­

5.3.1
Overview of the Tool­Generated Report
After completing the F­CHIA modelling of the base zone in the tool, a report can be gen­

5.3.2
Advantages of Tool­Based F­CHIA–DMM
Although the underlying information is largely the same as in the manual F­CHIA analy­
sis, representing the base zone using a React Flow–based diagram and an associated
DMM offers several important advantages in terms of process discipline, transparency,
and traceability.
Enforced analysis workflow.
The tool constrains user interaction so that the F­CHIA
process must follow the prescribed workflow defined in the F­CHIA flowchart(figure 4.4).
This prevents users from arbitrarily changing the analysis order, such as defining func­
tional safety requirements first and then retroactively constructing hazardous consequences
and causes to fit them. By enforcing the intended sequence from function, via hazardous
situations, causes, and consequences, to requirements, the tool helps maintain method­
ological rigor and reduces the risk of biasing the analysis toward preselected solutions.
Improved visibility of hierarchical relationships.
The tool provides a clear visual
overview of the relationships between tasks, functions, and other model elements. For
example, for the task “manual driving,” the associated functions can be identified immedi­
ately in the diagram, which significantly reduces the cognitive effort required to understand
the hierarchy and dependencies between model elements. By zooming into a specific
function, the modeler can directly observe its incoming and outgoing dependencies as
well as its linked requirements. This supports local reasoning (e.g., “what does this func­
tion depend on, and how is it protected?”) that is difficult to achieve in purely tabular
representations.
More granular traceability between causes, consequences, and requirements.
The
tool enables a more granular representation of the relationships between causes, haz­
ardous consequences, and functional safety requirements. In the original study by Wu et
al. [1], the results tables often map multiple causes to multiple hazardous consequences,
which are then linked to multiple requirements in a single row. While this consolidation
is acceptable for summarising results, it can be confusing during modelling and review,
because an individual requirement does not necessarily mitigate all causes listed in the
same row.

In the F­CHIA Diagram Workspace, each cause and each hazardous consequence is pre­
served as an independent node, and edges explicitly capture which requirement mitigates
which cause or consequence. This structure makes it easier for engineers to check and
refine the causal chains. For example, the realization “Camera detection with image pro­
cessing algorithms and learning approach” has eight identified causes, but only three rows
of requirements in the results tables of Wu et al. [1]. This makes the cause–requirement
mapping difficult to verify in the manual representation. In the tool­based model, the cor­
responding cause, consequence, and requirement nodes and their links can be inspected
directly, allowing inconsistencies or gaps to be detected more easily.
Integrated selection of directives, regulations, and standards.
The Table Workspace
supports the selection of relevant directives, regulations, and standards for each func­
tional safety requirement. This can be performed either manually by the user or assisted
by a large language model accessed through the OpenAI API [48]. These regulatory ref­
erences are displayed directly alongside the corresponding requirements in the function­
centric hazard identification table.
This integrated view is more practical than the approach in Wu et al. [1], where standards
corresponding to requirements are listed separately. Traditionally, engineers must be
highly familiar with the relevant directives, regulations, and standards in order to manually
match them to the requirements. With the support of large language models, the tool
can now suggest candidate standards automatically, significantly reducing the workload—
especially for less experienced engineers who are not yet familiar with the full body of
applicable regulations.
Function–Requirement DMM for enhanced traceability.
The Function–Requirement
DMM explicitly captures the many­to­many relationships between functions and functional
safety requirements. A single function may be supported by several requirements, and
conversely, a single requirement may be linked to multiple functions. In the manual anal­
ysis of Wu et al. [1], these relationships are mediated by intermediate analysis steps and
table structures, which can weaken traceability,particularly for requirements that apply to
several functions and therefore deserve higher priority.
The DMM makes such patterns visible at a glance. For example, in the present case
study, the requirement ”Set up an independent emergency stop function…” is linked to
two different functions, indicating its high criticality and priority. Without the DMM, this
relationship between the requirement and multiple functions is easy to overlook in manual
documents.
Taken together, these advantages show that the tool­based F­CHIA–DMM representa­
tion preserves the substance of the manual analysis while providing better support for
consistent modelling, review, and traceability.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the application of function­centered safety analysis to HRC
systems, with a particular focus on improving traceability, structure, and practicality in
early­stage functional safety engineering. Motivated by the limitations of conventional
safety analysis methods in handling complex, software­intensive, and context­dependent
systems, this work adopted F­CHIA as a theoretical foundation and extended it with a
structured dependency­management mechanism.
To overcome the limitations of traditional, document­based F­CHIA workflows, this the­
sis proposed an integrated F­CHIA–DMM framework, which uses DMM to systematically
capture relationships between system functions and functional safety requirements. By
explicitly modeling cross­domain dependencies, the proposed framework improves trace­
ability, enables consistency checking, and supports structured reasoning across the func­
tional safety lifecycle. This enhances both analytical transparency and engineering rigor,
especially in early design phases where system architecture and implementation details
are not yet fixed.
Building on the methodological contribution, this work further introduced an open­source,
web­based software tool that operationalizes the proposed framework. The tool provides
interactive modeling, traceability visualization, and integrated FTA within a unified en­
vironment. Compared with traditional spreadsheet­based workflows, the tool improves
usability and scalability while reducing the manual effort required for managing safety
artifacts.
The effectiveness of the framework and tool was evaluated using a published HRC case
study. The results demonstrate that the DMM­enhanced approach improves the clarity
of function–requirement relationships and facilitates structured reasoning about hazard
propagation and mitigation strategies. Compared with manual analysis, the tool­based
workflow enhances traceability, reduces redundancy, and enables more consistent safety
modeling, especially when system changes occur.
Overall, this thesis contributes a complementary perspective to existing safety analysis
methods by emphasizing functional intent and cross­domain dependency management.
The proposed approach helps bridge the gap between conceptual safety analysis and
practical engineering workflows, thereby supporting more robust and systematic design
of safety­critical human–robot systems.
6.2

A further direction for future work is the application of the proposed F­CHIA–DMM tool
in safety engineering education. Previous work on HAZOP pedagogy has shown that in­
teractive and tool­supported teaching modules can significantly improve student engage­
ment and learning outcomes compared with conventional slide­based instruction [50, 51].
The enforced analysis workflow, visual dependency modelling, and structured traceabil­
ity provided by the tool align well with the requirements of teaching systematic hazard
identification methods.
In an educational setting, the tool could be extended with a dedicated learning mode
offering guided workflows, predefined case studies, and automated report generation to

Key
Requirement Text
R1
Provide at least two distinct means of access to the mode switch (for redundancy and safety, e.g., a mechanical button reachable when the operator is outside the tractor and an access on the control panel), while minimizing the complexity introduced by multiple access
points
R2
Provide 100% knowable instructions in the user manual on how to switch modes (for good decision support when autonomous mode fails to start, to reduce possible human errors), considering the diverse educational levels of the intended users. Instructions shall be
provided in at least two formats: (1) diagrams showing the appearance and location of the access points, and (2) textual descriptions explaining the operations in detail. Multiple languages and clear definitions of terminology shall be provided to meet the needs of users
with different educational backgrounds
R3
Set up an independent emergency stop function that shall stop all hazardous motions and should be clearly marked and easily accessible. It shall only be reset by a deliberate manual action that does not cause a restart after resetting but shall only permit a restart to
occur. The following information shall be provided: (a) stop category according to IEC 60204-1:2016 + AMD1: 2021. (b) span-of-control of the emergency stop; (c) maximum response time for the emergency stop, as measured from input state change until the termination
of the hazardous function of the tractor. (d) maximum stopping time for the emergency stop, as measured from input state change until the termination of hazard function(s) of the tractor
R4
Set up two image quality assessment modules: a subjective test module where humans can access captured images and provide evaluations, and an objective test module where computational software enables comparison between reference and target images. Use
images with rain droplets, snow particles, and dust to simulate extreme conditions. Compare the modules’ outputs with known benchmarks for images in similar conditions. The verification and validation methods are review of the documentation and information for use,
practical tests, and simulation tests. Record the test conditions. Quality assessment is expected to start once autonomous mode starts. The captured images shall be tested for brightness, resolution, contrast, noise, and blur. The system shall provide instant feedback on
whether the series of input images is valid for autonomous navigation. If not, autonomous mode shall be disabled
R5
Test the lighting system. The verification and validation methods are visual inspection, observation during operation, review of specifications and information for use, and practical tests
R6
Specify limitations on maximum driving and turning speeds for different types of risky weather conditions in autonomous mode in the user manual, providing 100% fully clear instructions for manually selecting operating conditions or setting speed limits (for good decision
support to reduce human errors), considering the diverse educational levels of the intended users
R7
Set limitations on sensing and automation-related zones in the user manual, providing 100% fully clear instructions (for good decision support to reduce human errors), considering the diverse educational levels of the intended users
R8
Analyze sensor performance classes and test the detection capabilities of the sensors and software before implementation in the real application. Set specific, measurable criteria to assess the performance of the sensors and software. Record the test conditions
R9
Provide 100% knowable instructions in the user manual on how to perform the calibration of onboard sensors (e.g., camera, depth camera, and LiDAR in this case study), for good decision support to reduce possible human errors, considering the diverse educational
levels of the intended users
R10
Test the trajectory planning algorithm before implementation on the machine. Set specific, measurable criteria to assess the performance of the trajectory planning algorithm in both simulation and real implementation. Record the test conditions
R11
Calibrate the path at first use. Provide 100% knowable instructions in the user manual on how to perform the calibration (for good decision support to reduce possible human errors), considering the diverse educational levels of the intended users.
R12
Test the control system in manual mode before implementation and record the tests
R13
Define limits and requirements for operators
R14
Provide protective measures in the cab
R15
Design the seat and the control panel in accordance with relevant standards to fully meet human factors engineering requirements
R16
Provide 100% knowable instructions on the control systems in the user manual (for good decision support to reduce possible human errors), considering the diverse educational levels of the intended users
