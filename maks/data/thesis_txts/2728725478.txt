ABSTRACT
The European Union (EU) has set the goal to achieve climate neutrality by 2050. In order to facilitate the transition to
a greener economy, the EU has implemented various policies and frameworks, including the RePowerEU Plan. Especially
the transportation and industry sectors, which are regarded as hard-to-abate sectors, necessitate a wide range of measures to
reduce emissions. Renewable fuels, particularly green hydrogen and green ammonia, are viewed as viable solutions to this
problem. With this in mind, the European Commission recently released two Delegated Acts which define the conditions under
which the produced fuels can be regarded as renewable. This master thesis project focuses on examining the implications of
these Delegated Acts on the production of green ammonia within Power-to-X Hubs located in different ports across four EU
countries; Denmark, Germany, Greece and Spain. Two scenarios are explored: the first assumes that the Power-to-X Hub is
directly connected to renewable energy sources without grid connection and the second assumes that the Power-to-X Hub’s
electricity is sourced from a Power Purchase Agreement and the Day-Ahead Market given a specific price threshold. The aim
of this study is to investigate whether specific EU countries have a competitive advantage in green fuel production and identify
impactful factors on the investment decisions and production costs. By expanding the EnerHub2X modelling framework to
accommodate investment decisions, a minimum-cost optimization of the yearly operations and technological investments for
the annual production of 30ktons of green ammonia is performed. The Levelized Cost of Ammonia is calculated for each case
and the results are compared to derive conclusions. In the first scenario, the port in Denmark exhibits the cheapest ammonia
production cost of 531e/ton and the one in Spain the most expensive cost with a value of 941e/ton.
In the second scenario, the Power Purchase Agreement price is the main driver of the ammonia production cost as the
electricity provided by the agreement accounts for over 90% of the total energy needs of the Hub. The port in Denmark
showcases the most inexpensive cost of 760e/ton and the port in Germany the most expensive cost of 928e/ton. The energy
deriving from the Day-Ahead Market is very limited due to the imposed threshold. However, the cases of Denmark and Germany
are the ones mostly affected by the grid price as they exhibit higher frequency of price dropping below this threshold. Finally, a
modelling approach comparison is conducted to explore differences between a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming and Linear
Programming formulation and discuss the benefits of each approach.

I. INTRODUCTION
Recent studies reveal that climate change is becoming
an “existential threat” to humanity, with possible warming
above 2◦C in the next three decades and beyond 4◦C- 6◦C
within the next several decades [1]. As the threat posed by
climate change and environmental degradation is of utmost
importance to Europe and the world, it requires a new set of
growth strategies and initiatives that can lead to a modern,
resource-efficient, and competitive economy. Since 2019, the
European Union (EU) is paving the path towards achieving its
climate neutrality by 2050 through the application of strategic
plans and legislative packages such as the ”European Green
Deal” [2], ”NextGenerationEU Recovery” plan [3], ”Hydrogen
Strategy” [4] and ”Fit for 55” package [5]. On 2022, the
unstable situation created by the geopolitical landscape on
Eastern Europe led the EU to further accelerate its efforts for
the clean energy transition. The ”REPowerEU” is a plan that
aims to make Europe independent of fossil fuels by 2030 and
one of its main targets is to diversify the EU’s energy supply by
promoting the use of renewable hydrogen in the near future. To
reach these targets, the plan suggests a hydrogen accelerator
to increase the capacity of the electrolyzers to 17.5GW by
2025 in the EU and produce 10 million tonnes of renewable
hydrogen locally, and a modern regulatory framework for
hydrogen [6].
In line with the REPowerEU Plan’s hydrogen targets, the
Commission issued two Delegated Acts, under the latest
version of the Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) [7], that
describe detailed rules about what constitutes renewable hy-
drogen in the EU. They are part of a comprehensive regulatory
framework for hydrogen in the EU which includes energy in-
frastructure investments, state aid rules, and legislative targets
for renewable hydrogen. The proposed Acts will guarantee that
all renewable fuels of non-biological origin (also referred to as
RFNBOs) are generated exclusively from renewable electricity
sources [8]. The first Delegated Act indicates under which
conditions hydrogen, hydrogen-based fuels or other energy
carriers can be regarded as an RFNBO while the second
Delegated Act offers an intricate framework for evaluating the
life-cycle of greenhouse gas emissions from RFNBOs, taking
into account various factors, such as the upstream value chain
activities, the processing and transportation activities [8].
It is evident that the EU’s regulatory actions towards hydro-
gen generation and in general green fuel generation indicate its
willingness to develop a European hydrogen ecosystem with
international standards. As hydrogen and renewable fuels are
expected to play a vital role in the green transition that Europe
is striving for, solutions that contribute towards the growth of
their necessary technology as well as the maturity of their
production must be fostered.
One of the most promising topics for the promotion of
renewable fuels is the Power-to-X (PtX) strategy. Power-to-X
refers to a set of technologies that make use of the hydrogen
which is produced with electricity. This hydrogen is utilized
directly as fuel in industrial proposes or in the transportation
sector or it can be transformed into other carriers such as fuels,
chemicals or materials [9]. Traditionally, hydrogen is produced
through electrolysis, a method which uses electricity to split
water into oxygen and hydrogen. This technique is usually
based on fossil fuels such as coal (brown hydrogen) or natural
gas (grey hydrogen) to provide the required electricity [9].
However, as the EU policies implicate, the focus should be
placed on the electrolysis that is powered using renewable
sources, mainly wind and solar energy. The resulting green
hydrogen can be utilized as a building block for the generation
of other green fuels, such as ammonia and carbon-based fuels
(methane, methanol).
Green Ammonia (or e-Ammonia) can be produced by
combining nitrogen with green hydrogen and it is a crucial
product of PtX technologies as it exhibits high energy density
and hydrogen content [10]. Firstly, it can directly be used as
feedstock or fuel for various applications such as fertilizers,
power generation or maritime fuel. Secondly, ammonia can
be regarded as a hydrogen carrier and be converted back
to hydrogen. However, there are some limitations regarding
the ammonia production in PtX. The re-conversion back to
hydrogen (crackling) is an energy-intensive process, reaching
up to 13-34% of the energy contained in the hydrogen [10].
Furthermore, it is toxic and corrosive, meaning that safety
measures must be in place. As ammonia is already produced
and traded on a global scale, substantial ammonia infrastruc-
ture exists that can be exploited to further promote the trade
of green ammonia. Ports with ammonia terminals can be the
front-runners for the development of PtX energy hubs.
Carbon-based fuels are generated from the chemical re-
action of hydrogen with carbon under extreme pressure and
temperatures. The products that are formed, such as methane
and methanol, can be utilized as fuel similarly to the already
available fossil fuels. The main issue of carbon-based fuel
generation in PtX is the origin of the utilized carbon, pre-
dominantly CO2, and whether it is biogenic or not because
will determine if the resulting fuel is carbon-neutral [9].
Energy hubs with PtX technologies is a topic that has been
extensively researched over the last two years, both in terms of
investments and operations. Multiple models based on Linear
Programming (LP) or Mixed-Integer Linear Programming
(MILP) have been utilized in different studies, as discussed
in Section III, to simulate the dynamics within a PtX hub and
estimate the production costs of various green fuels. Yet, the
recently issued Delegated Acts under RED II, which certify
green hydrogen production, will highly affect the level of PtX
investments and their allocation across the EU as they impact
the whole PtX value chain. Furthermore, EU countries exhibit
substantial differences in crucial parameters in regards to in-
vestments decisions, such as renewable production availability,
cost of capital, and grid prices. In light of the new criteria for
RFNBOs, this study will try to answer the following three
research questions.
• What are the differences between the MILP and the LP
models in terms of investment and operations for a PtX
energy hub under the RED II Delegated Act?

• Is there a head start for certain locations within the EU
for the generation of green ammonia in PtX energy hubs
when there is a direct connection to a renewable energy
(RE) plant, as outlined in the RED II Delegated Act?
• Is there a head start for certain locations within the EU
for the generation of green ammonia in PtX energy hubs
when the electricity is purchased from a Power Purchase

Act?
II. POLICY DESCRIPTION
The first Delegated Act specifies four ways to source
electricity so that it can be used for the production of RFNBOs.
These options will enable the electricity to be considered as
fully renewable and the resulting fuel as RFNBO.
Direct connection to a RE plant. In this option, it is
ensured that the generated renewable electricity is directly
used by the electrolyzer. Grid connection is possible but a
smart metering system has to be installed to monitor the
consumption. Furthermore, after 01.01.2028 the criterion of
additionality must be met, which means that the renewable
energy plant must not have commenced operation more than
three years before the electrolyzer. This criterion ensures that
plant is built specifically for the electrolyzer’s operation and
prevents the utilization of existing renewable capacity for the
RFNBO production [11], [12].
Purchase of grid electricity in a bidding zone with
more than 90% renewable share. Fuel producers may source
electricity from the grid in bidding zones where the average
share of renewable electricity surpassed 90% in any of the last
five calendar years. The only constraint in the case, is that the
maximum operating hours of the electrolyzer may not exceed
a threshold of hours which is calculated by multiplying the
average share of renewable production in the bidding zone
with the number of hours in a year [11], [12].
Purchase of renewable electricity through a PPA. In
this case, a RE operator supplies electricity to the hydrogen
producer through the public grid under the conditions of
a PPA. The additionality criterion must be also met after
01.01.2028, unless the grid emission intensity is less than
64.8g CO2eq/kWh. Additionally, the conditions of temporal
and geographical correlation must be fulfilled. The temporal
correlation condition dictates that the energy consumed by
the RFNBO production unit must be on the same one hour
period it was injected by the RE operator. Before 01.01.2030,
a less restrictive condition is in place and a monthly temporal
correlation is allowed. In any case, if the fuel is produced
within an hour when clearing price of electricity is lower or
equal than 20e/MWh or lower than 0.36 times the price
per ton of CO2, the temporal correlation is always met and
the fuel is considered as RFNBO. Finally, the geographical
correlation is satisfied when the RE plant is situated in the
same bidding zone as the electrolyzer, in an adjacent zone
with more expensive electricity prices or in an offshore bidding
zone [11], [12].
Electricity consumption that avoids the shutdown of
a RE plant for a redispatch measure. When the grid
operator foresees an overload of the transmission capacity and
a RE plant must be downward dispatched, the energy can be
redirected to power an electrolyzer instead. The produced fuel
is regarded as RFNBO because it contributes to an optimized
utilization of the RE capacity [11], [12].
III. LITERATURE REVIEW
Since 2007, there have been extensive research efforts
focused on modelling energy hubs, with a wide range of
variations. These variations encompass aspects such as the
end-product of the energy hub value chain, optimization of
investment and operation, utilization of different power tech-
nologies, and the geographical locations of the hubs. Initially,
the research was mainly focused on power and heat co-
generation systems as well as gas systems. In [13], the concept
of energy hub was introduced along with a novel methodology
and a mathematical model for optimizing coupled power flows
of different energy carriers. In [14], the study proposes an
optimal expansion planning model for energy hubs containing
electricity, gas and heat generation systems. Given specific
expansion options, the suggested planning model provides a
flexible framework that energy planners can utilize, enabling
them to assess energy efficiency, environmental impact, and
reliability performance of various energy hubs. Similarly,
[15] presents a comprehensive framework for the long-term
generation and transmission expansion planning in energy
hubs (referred as multi-carrier energy systems or MCES)
and focuses on CHP (Combined Heat and Power) and gas
energy systems using a MILP model to minimize the total
investment and operations costs. The analysis results highlight
the importance of energy transmission network modelling in
the long-term investment and operation of the MCES.
Given the significance of renewable fuels as viable alter-
natives for decarbonizing hard-to-abate sectors of the econ-
omy, such as industries, transportation, power generation, and
buildings [4], substantial research attention has been drawn
towards modelling the investments and operational scheduling
of energy hubs with PtX technologies.
In [16], the techno-economic feasibility of green ammo-
nia production processes, specifically biomass and power-to-
ammonia, compared to the conventional methane-to-ammonia
process, is explored using a multi-objective techno-economic
optimization based on ASPEN. The main findings of the
analysis indicate that the system with highest efficiency is the
power-to-ammonia production system. The study presented in
[17], develops a methodology based on a techno-economic
model for the flexible production of hydrogen and ammonia
using a combination of solar and wind energy. The findings
show that the hybridization of the energy sources can reduce
the hydrogen and ammonia production costs. In [18], an
extensive investigation of the potential of onsite (ammonia
plant located at the electricity generation site) and coastal
(ammonia plant located at the nearest coast for possible export
option) Power-to-Ammonia plants is conducted with electricity
7
==PAGE:9==
sourced from hybrid solar and wind power plants. Using an
LP cost-optimization configuration, analysis is executed on a
global scale to provide a comparison of the green ammonia
production costs. In [19], a high level techno-economic com-
parison for PtX applications powered by RE is performed with
the aim to investigate their feasibility. A sensitivity analysis
on the parameters that may influence their economic viability
is carried out as well. In [20], a fast-solving LP optimization
model for dimensioning a PtX plant facilities is demonstrated
considering techno-economic data, varying power profiles, and
hourly grid prices. Furthermore, a calculation of the production
cost of e-Ammonia is performed for specific locations in Chile,
Denmark and South Australia with RE production setup, and
a comparison with the estimations from the existing literature
is presented.
Some studies delve into policy suggestions for the promo-
tion of PtX technologies using their results from the energy
hub modelling. In [21], using an integrated power-hydrogen-
emission analytical framework, a thorough analysis for the
economic feasibility and decarbonization potential of green
hydrogen in China is reported. The research concludes that
hydrogen production using wind power can be a competitive
alternative to conventional carbon-fueled hydrogen production.
Additionally, policy implications are presented to further boost
the green hydrogen competitiveness in the relevant region.
In [22], the detailed operation of an PtX energy hub is
explored with respects to its flexibility. The study includes
the production of various green fuels, combined with revenue
sources from the participation in the Day-Ahead and the
ancillary services market. A MILP model is proposed for the
maximization of profits considering market prices and techni-
cal constraints. Finally, a policy investigation is performed to
identify the premium level that will incentivize the green fuel
production in a Danish case study.
It is evident that extensive research has been done to
simulate the dynamics in a PtX hub. While some employ
a LP approach ( [18], [20]) to model the investments and
operations within a hub, taking advantage of fast results, others
rely on a MILP approach ( [15], [21] and [22]) with heavy
computational cost but higher resolution results that take into
account several technical constraints. Nevertheless, there has
not been extensive work that quantifies the differences between
the optimal investment decisions that are reached using a LP
approach and the ones from MILP approach. Furthermore,
most of the research is investigating different setups of PtX
hubs in terms of type of technologies used, location of the
hub, or choice of renewable fuel produced. However, few of
them are probing into the policy frameworks that influence the
hubs ( [21] and [22]). The RED II Delegated Acts that were
recently released, drastically influence the PtX sector in the
EU, and their ramifications for the green fuel production have
not been comprehensively examined yet. The contributions of
this study are:
• Translation of a state-of-the-art flexible modelling frame-
work for synergistic PtX energy hubs into an open source
programming language (Julia).
• Expansion of the model by accommodating investment
capabilities and its application on four ports in Europe to
investigate the production of green ammonia under the
restrictions imposed by RED II Delegated Acts.
• Analysis of potential differences in investment decisions
that may arise with the application of MILP and LP
modelling approaches.
• Scrutiny of the impact of location specific Weighted
Average Cost of Capital (WACC) and PPA prices on
the investing strategies and the production cost of green
ammonia in the selected ports.
IV. METHODOLOGY
In order to examine the impact of the EU policy on
the generation and pricing of green fuels, an optimization
model was constructed based on the EnerHub2X modelling
framework developed by Ioannis Kountouris [22]. Initially, a
MILP model was formulated. However, due to computational
limitations and the inability to reach global optimality, it was
transformed into a LP formulation. The complete MILP model
is presented in Appendix B.
The final model developed is a deterministic LP demand-
driven approach, designed to minimize the costs associated
with the investment and operation of a PtX hub that produces
30 000 tons/year of ammonia. The primary objective of this
model is to analyze investment and operation strategies based
on a specific ammonia demand in two main scenarios: 1)
”Islanded” mode, and 2) PPA with grid connection.
The model incorporates various constraints to ensure it is
demand-driven and cost-minimizing. These constraints include
technical limitations such as ramp rates, maximum and min-
imum production levels, efficiencies, storage availability, and
renewable energy profiles. Additionally, while not directly
utilized in this thesis research, the model is flexible enough
to accommodate budget constraints and spatial constraints per
unit of energy.
It is imperative to mention that, despite the model’s attempt
to optimize investments, it does not take into account the
concept of the time value of money. In any case, the model’s
robustness is leveraged to optimize for a single year and as-
sume a similar behavior for the remaining periods throughout
the project’s duration. Other authors resort to methods like
rolling horizons to avoid the computational burden associated
with this type of models, which have hourly resolution and
a duration of up to three decades [23]. Therefore, capital
investments were weighted by the annuity factor (AF) to
represent a constant cash outflow throughout the project.
A. Scenario Design
To capture the conditions imposed by the EU regulation, two
scenarios were designed to decompose the problem into spe-
cific environments. Each scenario entails different behaviors
towards electricity consumption from the grid, and investment
capabilities regarding RE. Both scenarios were constructed
with the same set of countries in mind. Countries were selected
to best represent the availability of RE and the diverse nature
8
==PAGE:10==
of the Cost of Capital landscape across the EU. Furthermore,
considering the heavy reliance of the ammonia supply chain on
a port connection, the locations were further limited to areas
within the reach of an established port facility.

[24] and [25], all the ports except the one in Greece have
announced a PtX related project in the coming years.

On the one hand, a full ”Islanded” scenario is implemented.
The idea behind this scenario is to optimize a full PtX hub

On the other hand, the second scenario requires a RE PPA

In this case, there is no requirement or possibility to invest in
RE generators. Additionally, direct consumption of electricity
from the grid without a PPA is allowed only if the Day-Ahead
(DA) spot price of electricity at any given time is below or
equal to the 20e/MWh threshold (temporal correlation).
Another important aspect to consider regarding the temporal
correlation conditions imposed by the regulation is the time
between injection and consumption of electricity from the grid.
Since the model has an hourly resolution and only optimizes
for a year, while assuming a similar behavior for the project’s
entire lifespan, it was necessary to neglect all constraints in
this regard. Regarding geographical correlation conditions, the
model complies by assuming that the RE production is on the
same location of the fuel generation hub. Likewise, in the case
of the PPA, the RE plant is located on the same bidding zone.
Since the model accounts for multiple signals, and one of
the scopes was to compare countries within each scenario, it
was decided to perform a benchmark run for each scenario. In
order to remove the WACCs’ variability from the model and
obtain a clearer picture of the drivers for the Levelized Cost of
Electricity and Ammonia (LCoE and LCoA), the benchmark
run assumed a fixed WACC, equal to the average 3.6% for
Wind Turbines and Solar PV (Photovoltaic), regardless of
the country. This approach allowed for the assessment of the
impact of different RE profiles.
Similarly, for the ”PPA + Grid” connection scenario, the
benchmark was conducted by assuming the same price for
the PPA across all countries. This price, 70e/MWh, was
sourced from [26] and represents the weighted average of the
PPA Price Index for Europe. Since the RE profiles have no
effect on this scenario, as there are no investments in RE
generators, the scope was to properly assess the effect of
the country-specific DA electricity spot prices on the overall
investment and operation optimization. To better represent the
”Additionality” requirement to be fulfilled by the RE PPA
agreement, it was decided to extract the prices from the period
2022-Q4/2023-Q1, as these were the highest available in [26].
Furthermore, a PPA profile was not established; the model
was set to optimize on a ”pay-as-consumed” basis. Provided
that the size of the Electrolyzer, Ammonia Plant, and overall
production can be deemed relatively small, it was assumed that
the hub would always be able to harvest the required amount
of energy in such manner, without impacting grid stability or
balance in its corresponding bidding zone.

B. From MILP to LP
Initially, a MILP optimization model was used to test the EU
regulation for green fuels. This model aimed to accommodate
technical constraints, such as the inability to charge and dis-
charge simultaneously, determining the minimum operational
level of a technology, and accounting for start-up costs.
However, the MILP approach posed challenges for two
reasons. Firstly, solving the model for a specific country
considering all technical constraints, possible technologies,
and given demand would exceed 24 hours, even with a MIP
gap of 0.04. Secondly, the difference in terms of investments
and capacities installed was not meaningful. The most sig-
nificant variation arose with the investment in the electricity
storage, which decreased from approximately 11 MWh to 0
9

MWh in the LP model. Lowering the MIP gap was tested
to determine if it would yield better results and decrease
the need for electricity storage. In Figure 3, runs with lower

technology.
Ultimately, the decision was made to adjust the model to
an LP formulation in order to achieve global optimality and

D. Model Validation
The validation of the model was conducted using visual
representations. Since the objective of the model is to op-
timize the fulfillment of demand while minimizing costs, the
expected outcome is to have as low as possible investments by
maximizing Full Load Hours (FLH) for green fuel generation
units, and a correlation between RE availability and green fuel
production.
Figure 9 for the ”Islanded” scenario in Denmark demon-
strates the correlation between the availability of RE in terms
of capacity factor and the production of ammonia/hydrogen.
On the other hand, Figure 34 for the ”PPA + Grid” scenario
in Denmark (Benchmark) depicts a significant increase in the
FLH for the ammonia synthesis unit and the electrolyzer.
Additionally, it exhibits a perfect negative correlation between
electricity purchased from the PPA and electricity bought from
the grid at any given timestep. The former is a result of a more
balanced investment strategy due to the non-variable nature of
a PPA, while the latter demonstrates the complementary nature
of electricity needs between the grid and PPA when prices are
low enough.
By utilizing visual representations, the model validation pro-
cess effectively highlights the correlation between renewable
energy availability, green fuel production, and optimized cost
minimization strategies.

E. Electrolysis
For the process of electrolysis, it is assumed that Alkaline
Electrolysis Cell (AEC) technology is utilized as it is the most
commonly used type of electrolyzer [33]. The electrolyzer is
fueled by electricity and water and produces hydrogen, oxygen
and heat. The resulting hydrogen is then either used as fuel
input for the production of other green fuels or pressurized in
a compressor to up to 200bar, and either exported to satisfy a
demand or stored for future use. The electrical consumption
of the electrolyzer is regarded as 53.06 MWhe/tonH2 and
that of the compressor as 4 MWhe/tonH2. The CAPEX is
taken from the Danish Energy Agency’s (DEA’s) report for
renewable fuels [33] and the OPEX is calculated as 2% of the
capital expenditure.
F. Ammonia Synthesis and Air Separation Unit
Ammonia is produced through the Haber-Bosch process
where hydrogen and nitrogen are combined to form anhydrous
liquid ammonia. The required hydrogen is supplied from an
electrolyzer while the nitrogen from an air separation unit. The
inputs and outputs of these technologies have been taken from
the DEA’s report for renewable fuels [33]. The investment cost
for both the ammonia synthesis and the air separation unit is
highly dependant on the scale of ammonia production [10],
[18]. The CAPEX for the ammonia plant and the separation
13
==PAGE:15==
unit is found from [18]. The OPEX is regarded as 2% of the
capital expenditure.
G. Storage Technologies
Three types of storage are considered in this analysis,
electric, hydrogen and ammonia storage. Electric storage is
regarded as Lithium-Ion NMC (Nickel Manganese Cobalt) bat-
tery type. This category is common for stationary applications
and its production has been developed in the last years due to
its use in the electric mobility sector [34]. It is assumed that the
battery is 1-C rate type, meaning it can charge and discharge
its whole volume within an hour. The relevant capacity and
storage CAPEX and OPEX are found from the DEA’s report
for energy storage [35].
Pressurized hydrogen storage is presently the sole storage
method implemented on a substantial global scale [36]. The
pressurized tanks serve as a means of storage primarily for
small and medium-scale applications as large-scale storage
becomes unfeasible due to limitations in material properties
and operating costs [37]. In this study, it is assumed that
hydrogen is stored in Type I tanks, which are constructed
from seamless steel or aluminium. They are made to endure
pressures of up to 250 bar and offer notable resistance against
hydrogen permeation, thereby ensuring the containment of the
stored gas. For this analysis, the storage is able to fully charge
and discharge in up to 3 hours. The relevant CAPEX and Fixed
OPEX are taken from [35].
Ammonia is usually stored in liquid form because of its
higher energy density. Typically, refrigerated carbon steel
storage tanks are utilized where ammonia is cooled to −33◦C
at standard atmospheric pressure [38]. The refrigeration pro-
cess consumes 0.0378 MWhe/tonNH3. CAPEX and Fixed
OPEX values for the refrigerated ammonia storage are taken
from [39].
H. Annuity Factor Parameters
For the calculation of the Annuity Factor, the lifetime of
the project is assumed to be 20 years. The WACC is assumed
to be 7% for the all the green fuel technologies across all
locations. The WACC for the renewable generation is taken
from the IRENA report of financing renewable production and
it differs based on the location and technology [40].
VI. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
In this section, the results of the analysis regarding the
research questions are presented. First, in section VI-A, the
results of the MILP approach are compared to the ones from
the LP approach to distinguish the differences in terms of
investments and operations. In sections VI-B and VI-D, results
for the scenarios designed based on the EU Delegation Act
on green fuels are presented and discussed. The goal is to
assess the overall performance of the LCoA and provide
insights as to whether there is a preference of location for
the production of green ammonia within the EU. To facilitate
a clear understanding of the underlying concepts, all values in
the time-based figures will be represented as 168-hour moving
averages.
A. MILP vs LP Approach
The MILP approach is considered to be closer to reality
as it accounts for technical constraints that influence the
operation of certain technologies. To investigate the impact
of these constraints on the investment decisions, the port of
Bremerhaven in Germany on a ”Islanded” mode is chosen
as an example case to conduct the MILP, with a MIP Gap of

electricity.

that may arise can be attributed to techno-economic and RE

D. ”PPA + Grid” Scenario
Investments and operations are also optimized for a ”PPA
+ Grid” scheme, and their LCoA calculated. The ”PPA +
Grid” scenario entails that investments are only to be done
on green fuels generators and purchase from the grid is
only permitted under specific conditions (subsection IV-A).
Otherwise, electricity can only be harvested from the grid
through a RE PPA with additionality. Demand is fixed to a
weekly amount, being this the decisive factor for the capacities
sizing.
As with previous scenarios, benchmark runs were performed
to have a baseline of comparison. In this case, the benchmark
was done by setting a PPA price equal to the ”Intercontinental
Averaged Market Index” [26]. With the PPA price no longer a

Figure 12 shows the decomposed LCoA across different

the use of an LP model as opposed to its MILP counterpart.

strictly dependent on maximization of FLH Table X.
The most remarkable aspect, which defines the very nature
of the ”PPA + Grid” scenario, is embedded within Figures 34-
41 and Table X. By stripping the model from the possibility to
invest in RE generators, and forcing it to rely only on the grid
for electricity sourcing, the hub is able to minimize its invested
capacities and maximize the FLH. Since the production does
not rely on RE availability, the hub buys electricity at a
constant level and does not have to oversize any technology to
fulfill the demand, because electricity will be available at all
times. This shows in a very explicit manner the huge impact
the intermittency of RE has on the overall business case of a
PtX hub and its financials.
Figure 13 shows the LCoA for all countries in the basic
and benchmark runs and the share of electricity bought from
the PPA and grid. It can be observed that while there are
significant changes in the LCoA, the grid has no considerable
weight in the overall mix. In fact, the share of the benchmark
is exactly the same as the share in the basic runs. On the one
hand, the fact that the share of the grid-to-PPA is extremely
low is a result of the frequency DA spot prices are below the
threshold. Figure 16 shows the distribution of prices. Countries
such as Denmark and Germany have a higher frequency of
price dropping below this threshold; they even have negatives
prices. Contrary to this, Spain and Greece tell a different story
in regards to the penetration of RE in the grid by the fact
negative prices are barely present, and the share below the
given threshold is even lower. On the other hand, this aligns
with the fact that the demand (driver of the model) never
changes, and that the PPA prices in both models are far away
from threshold established by the EU Regulation [6] to tilt the
balance and make the hub switch its source of electricity.
In conclusion, it is safe to establish that at present, it is
not advisable to base a business model on the possibility
of accessing the grid without the PPA intermediation as it
is unsual for prices to be below the established threshold,
although the situation may change with a higher penetration
of RE. On top of that, it is evident that the main differen-
tiator among countries is the PPA price, thus rendering its
negotiation a highly strategic task. In regards to the scope
of the analysis, it is rather hard to tell if there is a set of
countries with an advantage over others. While Denmark and
Greece exhibit high average CF and lower LCoA, Germany
lags behind with a remarkable RE availability, yet very high
PPA prices nonetheless.

VII. CONCLUSION - FUTURE WORK
Renewable fuel production with the application of Power-to-
X technologies has attracted a lot of scientific and industrial at-
tention as it seems as a promising solution to the decarboniza-
tion of challenging sectors such as transportation and industry.
The European Union has made some substantial progress
in incorporating green fuels into its environmental strategy
by issuing relevant policies and regulatory frameworks. The
recently issued Delegated Acts under the Renewable Energy
Directive greatly affect the renewable fuel generation as they
dictate the conditions under which a fuel can be regarded as
renewable.
The goal of this master thesis was to explore the ramifica-
tions of the Delegated Acts in the green fuel production and
investigate whether specific EU countries exhibit a competitive
advantage with regards to the green ammonia generation
within the PtX landscape. Through the expansion of the Ener-
Hub2X modeling framework to perform investment decisions,
the study compared capital investment selections and ammonia
production costs across various European regions to identify
the key parameters influencing the generation of green fuels.
Subsection VI-A considered the distinction between the
MILP and LP approaches through the analysis of the PtX hub
optimization for the port in Germany.
Firstly, the inclusion of a Solar PV plant and Electric
Storage among the technologies in the mix of the MILP
approach was observed. However, such additions did not
modify or significantly contribute to the overall operation of
the hub, rather they only accounted for a modest increase in
the LCoA and marginally contributed to the total electricity
generation pool. Secondly, while the LP model was unable
to capture some technical limitations in regards to minimum
activity level or non-simultaneous charge/discharge, it was
observed that the overall result in terms of operations was
almost equal. Capacities and FLH varied very slightly, around
0.2% to 2%.
Through multiple optimization runs it was proved that the
benefits of running the hub as an LP model outpace the
disadvantages. The run-time decreased drastically, offering
the possibility to deliver in-minutes results, and the technical
requirements were such that there was no need for high-end
equipment. In terms of investments and operations, it was
observed that no significant differences arise, and the strategies
remained unchanged.
In Subsection VI-B, the ”islanded” scenario was examined
where the PtX hub relied only on renewable sources to acquire
the electricity. Results concerning this scenario indicated that
the port of Esbjerg in Denmark was the cheapest option to
produce green ammonia with a LCOA of 531e/ton while the
port of Barcelona in Spain was the most expensive location
with a cost 941e/ton. Furthermore, capital investment in
renewable energy plants accounted for over 50% of the total
green ammonia cost in all cases, followed by investments
in electrolyzer technology and the ammonia synthesis plant.
Wind turbines were the predominant electricity generation
technology across all cases. The preference for wind turbine
technology over solar PV could be attributed to production
profiles and a potential overestimation of solar PV’s capital
expenditure. In addition, the differences in the investment
decisions and the sizing of PtX technologies among the
selected ports arose due to the variations of RE availability and
the WACC of the RE technologies. The findings obtained from
the benchmark run revealed that the impact of the location
specific WACCs in the green ammonia cost is not significant.
Finally, a sensitivity analysis was performed to reflect on
the influence of RE availability on the LCOA. The outcome
showcases that the ports in Denmark and Germany (Northern
European countries) tend to have less volatility with regards to
ammonia production costs. This suggests that these locations
may be more favorable for investment in PtX hubs under the
”Islanded” setup, considering their potentially more stable and
advantageous conditions for green fuel production.
Lastly, the scenario covered in subsection VI-D considered
20
==PAGE:22==
the investment and operation strategies adopted by the hubs
when no RE generator could be built. Instead, all electricity
could be sourced from the grid (under certain conditions) and
through a RE PPA.
It was shown how the PPA price is a crucial determinant
in the LCoA, with Germany exhibiting high costs (PPA
Price 80e/MWh), while Denmark benefiting from lower costs.
Furthermore, the study reveals that the main differentiating
factor among countries is the negotiation and establishment of
PPAs, emphasizing the strategic nature of these agreements.
The benchmark run allowed for the comparison between
strategies by performing a sensitivity analysis of the PPA price
and the assessment of the impact the DA prices would have on
the overall outcome. The analysis indicated that the reliance on
grid electricity without PPA intermediation was not advisable,
as prices rarely fell below the established threshold. However,
it is important to note that as RE penetration increases, the
situation may change, and grid electricity sourcing without
PPAs may become a more viable option.
The most important concept that arose from the results was
the huge impact that the intermittent nature of RE has on
the final outcome and financials of the business case. It was
showed how the elimination of the RE availability dependence
due to an established connection to the grid, allowed the hub to
invest in lower capacities for the same level of demand (around
a 30% decrease on average for the ammonia synthesis unit),
while increasing the FLH.
Overall, this scenario underscored the strategic importance
of PPA negotiations, the minimal influence of DA spot prices
on LCoA, and the impact of RE intermittency on the financial
viability of PtX hubs.
As far as future work is concerned, the following sug-
gestions can be made. These suggestions are based on a
number of limitations that were present in the context of this
study. In the present work, the RE generation technologies
included only one type of Wind Turbine and Solar PV setup
as investment options. The same holds with the electrolysis.
Further investment options should be examined, to evaluate
whether specific types of renewable energy generation and
green fuel production are more suitable in different countries,
due to their output profile and cost. Moreover, in this study
it was assumed that the PPA deriving from renewable sources
is providing the required amount of electricity without any
constraints. In reality, specific PPA production profiles should
be assumed, taking into consideration the actual production
constraints, due to the variability and stochastic nature of
renewable energy sources. Lastly, more extensive research
should be conducted on the valuation of the PPA that satisfies
the additionality criterion, to increase the accuracy of the
results regarding the green fuel cost.

B. Complete MILP Model
The MILP model presented below contains the original model that was developed in [22] and the modifications that were
employed in present study to accommodate the ”investment” layer. The modifications are highlighted with blue colour in the
equations below. The changes mostly refer to the use of the Big-M Method in order to avoid the multiplication of two variables
within some constraints that would result in non-linear problem. The issue arose due to the transformation of the capacities of
the technologies from a parameter in the original formulation to a variable in the expanded model.
