Design of an Exhibition Installation from a User Experience Perspective
Mikkel Biering Sonnenschein
==PAGE:2==
==PAGE:3==
Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to investigate how an exhibition installation can 
be designed from a user experience perspective. The case of the project 
is to design an installation for an upcoming exhibition called ‘De Hvide 
Busser’ at the National Museum of Denmark. The goal of the exhibition 
is to attract a younger audience to the museum.
Experience design theory from the museum world as well as the prod­
uct design field is presented and applied to observational field studies at 
three Museums in Copenhagen.
The project covers a design process that has been carried out in coop­
eration with the museum. The design process ends up in the develop­
ment of an installation which is made into a functional mock-up. The 
mock-up is then utilised to test visitors’ experience through use. A test 
for investigating visitors’ interpretation of two packages, to be included 
in the installation, is conducted. This is followed by a test utilising UX 
Journey for the visitors to evaluate their experience. The data is processed 
and design improvements are suggested to enhance the experience of the 
installation.

1. Introduction
1.1 Introduction of the Project
In 2015 it has been 70 years since the end of World War 2 the National Museum of Denmark (Na­
tionalmuseet) is conducting an exhibition thematising the war. The exhibition is called ‘The White 
Busses’ (De Hvide Busser) and tells the story of a rescue mission executed in the final months of the 
war. 
This thesis work is concerned with exploring how an exhibition installation can be designed from a 
user experience perspective. The context that is designed for is ‘The White Busses’ as the installation 
is to be part of this exhibition opening on the 19th of June 2015. 
1.2 Outline
The outline of this thesis is presented below
• 
Chapter 1 introduces the scope of the project as well as the historical background and 
goal of the exhibition. 
• 
Chapter 2 describes the organisational frame of the project and examines the role of a 
designer in this context.
• 
Chapter 3 examines the design discourses surrounding the project and how it influences 
the design process.
• 
Chapter 4 investigates design for experience theory from different perspectives within 
both the museological field and the product and service design field. 
• 
Chapter 5 investigates three current exhibitions around Copenhagen and the visitors’ 
interaction within the exhibitions. 
• 
Chapter 6 describes the design process from the scope of the project to the construction 
of a mock up.
• 
Chapter 7 describes conducted user tests of the mock up and presents the results. 
• 
Chapter 8 evaluates the design of the installation based on the posed specification state­
ments. In this chapter the implications of using design for experience as a design ap­
proach is also discussed. 
• 
Chapter 9 presents the results of a follow up meeting and the future of the project.
• 
Chapter 10 presents a conclusion on the work done in this thesis

1.3 Defining the Scope of the Project 
The National Museum of Denmark (NMD) was initially contacted in the fall of 2014 without 
any set scope for the project other than a desired outcome to be a design of an exhibit installation. 
The term installation is here and throughout the thesis defined as an object or number of objects 
installed in an exhibition space. 
The NMD was very open for suggestions on projects and which exhibition that should be designed 
for. They suggested ‘The White Busses’ exhibition. This exhibition had a fitting time frame as it 
was to open around the end of the project. The exhibition was to attract a younger audience. One 
of the ways in which this was to be achieved was to implement interactive content, which matched 
well with the set goal of designing an installation. Consequently it was agreed upon that the project 
should lead to a design of an installation for ‘The White Busses.’ 
While the museum’s focus was on the concrete design of an installation the goal in regards to the 
thesis was different as it was to respond to the academic requirements of The Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU). 
This among other things required a theoretical framework. Design for experience was chosen as the 
approach for the design process. It is an approach that dictates the focus on the user’s experience 
of the installation and how to design for it. The approach was chosen as it serves to give a holistic 
understanding of the aspects of designing for an exhibition. This approach is also beneficial in that 
it branches of into both engineering as well as museology, which offer an opportunity to derive un­
derstandings from diverse perspectives. The goal from the perspective of DTU was therefore that the 
project showed competence to carry out a design process in all its aspects, as described in the project 
description in appx 1.1.
Combining the two perspectives of the project the resulting main challenge of the project became: 
How to apply a design for experience framework in order to address the wishes of NMD to engage 
visitors, especially younger people. In the following section the historical background of the exhibi­
tion will be explained and the themes as well as the target audience will be outlined, forming the 
point of departure for this project.
1.4 Historical Background of the Exhibition
During the Second World War Danish and Swedish citizens were captured and deported to concen­
tration camps around Nazi Germany, namely Buchenwald, Dachau and Ravensbrück. The prisoners 
were captured due to their ethnicity or profession. Through a governmental agreement the Scandi­

navian prisoners were sent Red Cross packages throughout the war. The packages contained food 
and cigarettes and proved vital in keeping the Scandinavian prisoners alive. 
Towards the end of the war as the Nazi Germany began to collapse a deal was made in order to 
safely escort Scandinavian prisoner back to their respective countries. The agreement crafted with 
Nazi Germany was led by Count Folke Bernadotte. A convoy with Danish and Swedish volunteers 
were sent down into the war-torn Germany to collect the prisoners. 
The people behind the mission came from every part of the Scandinavian society. Danish Govern­
ment officials organised the operation and local bus drivers drove the gathered busses into Nazi Ger­
many. Nurses and Doctors took care of the medical equipment and recruits of the military helped 
building quarantine barracks.  This was done in civil busses that were painted white with a red cross 
on the side, in order to protect them from gunfire. This was however a dangerous undertaking that 
ended up causing several casualties and ended up traumatising many of the volunteers, as well as the 
prisoners who suffered gruesome conditions in the concentration camps.
Most of the busses did return home, freeing 7000 thousand Scandinavian prisoners. The exhibition 
is to cover these different stories and perspectives intertwined by this mission.
	
1.5 Themes of the Exhibition
The central theme of the exhibition is citizenship (medborgerskab) which will be explored through 
the different stories and perspectives of the people that was involved. As stated in a presentation 
folder produced as an introduction to the exhibition, the four thematic focus points of the exhibi­
tion are:
• 
The motivation - What makes people help?
• 
The demarcation - Who are your fellow citizens?
• 
The personal and moral dilemmas and the ambivalence
• 
The fragility of citizenship 
The goal of this exhibition is stated by NMD to be a ‘debating exhibition’. This is to be achieved 
through the exhibition being engaging and thought provoking for the visitors. The goal is reflected 
in the proposed themes as they each pose a question or is open for interpretation. The openness for 
interpretation is further elaborated on by NMD as they state that they do not want the exhibition 
to be one dimensional by being ideological or romantic.
‘The exhibition is to make room for the visitors to consider different dilemmas, and different perspectives 
and motivations. However, the exhibition should not be either ideological or romanticised.’ (NMoD, 
2014: 9)
1.6 Target Audience of the Exhibition
The target audience of the exhibition is young people, including the oldest classes of state school as 
well as upper secondary school, and technical schools. Children are not to be allowed in the exhi­
bition as it is considered to become too graphic. Furthermore the museum considers the Second

World War theme of the exhibition itself to be enough to draw in an older audience. 
The stated goal is to make the student relate and reflect on the notion of citizenship. While the story 
of the exhibition takes place 70 years ago the theme is argued by NMD to be highly relevant still. 
In order to achieve this, it consequently requires the exhibition to be engaging and relatable for the 
target group. 
In order to address this, a focus group has been held by NMD. The focus group session was ar­
ranged to uncover several aspects including what could make them come to the exhibition, what 
they thought was interesting about the exhibition and also what they would like to experience. This 
resulted in suggestions reflected in the previously mentioned goals of the exhibition, as well as sug­
gestions on use of social media and multimedia that could be engaged with in smaller groups. 
These three aspects: the historical content, the themes of the exhibition and the target audience each 
serves as part of the premise taken into account in designing an installation for ‘The White Busses’.

2. The Organisational Context
2.1 The National Museum of Denmark – A Museum and an Organisation
The museum where the exhibition ‘The White Busses’ is to take place is located in the centre of Copenhagen 
and called the National Museum of Denmark. The National Museum of Denmark is also however an organi­
sation that covers multiple museums around Denmark, including The Royal Danish Arsenal Museum and 
The Royal Danish Navy Museum. These two were included in the organisation recently in 2014. 
2.2 The Role of a Designer 
As experienced through the course of the project many actors are involved in the design of this exhibition. A 
network of actors affects the design process in a multitude of ways. Figure 2.1 shows the network of actors 
that affects the design process and how they have done so.
Historians – One historian have been my contact person throughout the process, which I have had multiple

Many museums in Denmark are run by the Danish state and are therefore assigned to The Danish 
Ministry of Culture, which issues guidelines for running cultural institutions including museums. 
Under Ministry is the Danish Agency for Culture focusing on running and managing cultural 
related institutions and activities.
According to the communication report published by the Danish Ministry of Culture the amount

The Danish National Museum has however experienced an increase in visitor numbers since then

3.2 The Evolution of Museums - 3 Generations
As society has changed so has the purpose and form of museums. Esben Kjeldbæk the curator The 
Museum of Danish Resistance which is also under the management of NMD, categorises muse­
ums into three generations. The first generation of museums is characterised by being a grass root 
initiative, where amateurs that have been part of a historical event puts their belongings on display. 
This type of museum often seems to celebrate a victory, e.g. a war victory, and document historical 
events.  

The second generation of museums is characterised by the involvement of professionals who is not 
directly a part of the history told in the museum and is responding to a higher instance, e.g. govern­
mental. The scope of this type of museums is more educational, they are curated such that a story is 
told. Where first generation museums solely rely on authentic historical artefacts, second generation 
museums implement models as a mean of presenting the story at hand. 
Third generation museums like The Danish National Museum are based on bigger organisations 
than the previous; part of this is also an expansion of professions that are involved in building the 
exhibitions. Communication and design of the exhibition is emphasised. Here the museum is 
==PAGE:17==

considered to compete with modern societies other cultural activities, such as cinemas and shopping 
malls. 
“The institution will generally look after its visitors, who are not only coming to reflect on history or learn. 
They are also there to be part of an event and have a good time!” (Kjeldbæk, 2009: 374)
Focus is here shifted towards media and how objects in a broader sense including film clips and 
sound bites can be communicated to the audience.  The objects used are pursued to be fewer than 
previous generations but striking and iconic.
One of the points Kjeldbæk brings up is that there seem to be a development in the roles of the 
objects displayed. Where the first generation museums where there for proof or documentation of 
history, the second generations objects served the purpose of educating and  informing the guests. 
The third generation marks a shift in that the objects on display are to fascinate the visitor. 
While the focus of the third generation museums is to fascinate people, the first generation mu­
seums had its strength in its ethos. When the objects of the museum is presented by people who 
themselves had been part of the historical events, visitors are spoken to from an even standpoint 
which consequently will facilitate discussion (Kjeldbæk, 2009).

4. Design for Experience – From the Perspective of Two Different 
Fields
Much literature written within the field of museology is concerned how a museum is experienced. 
Likewise a lot has been written within design for experience relating to design of products and 
services. Each of these fields has their strengths. The experience theory related to museums is great 
for understanding the context that the museum and the presentation of authentic historical objects. 
However these theories fall short in addressing the concrete design challenges that arises from creat­
ing an interactive installation. On the other hand the design for experience perspective from the 
product and service design field does not offer a sufficient framework for designing within the very 
specific context of museums.
Therefore in the this chapter a selection of theory and empirical studies from each field is presented 
in respectively 4.1 and 4.2 in order to represent the diversity in perspectives that can be considered 
when designing for experience within each of these two fields.   
Concluding in section 4.3 the two fields’ perspectives on designing for experience is compared and 
it is concluded what can be drawn from each field into the further design process.
4.1 Design for Experience from a Museological Perspective
Initially the concept of heritage interpretation will be introduced as well as learning in museums. 
The next paragraph will deal with how a museum can interpret difficult topics as with the case of 
‘The White Busses’. Following this is empirical study of the behaviour and motivations of museum 
visitors. This is followed by a look at how visitors engage with objects in museums. 
4.1.1 Heritage Interpretation
A big part of running museums come down to the activity of presenting history and its artefacts in 
museums, which is often referred to as heritage interpretation. Freeman Tilden defines it as such:
‘An educational activity which aims to reveal meanings and relationships through the use of original 
objects, by first-hand experience, and by illustrative media, rather than simply to communicate factual 
information.’ (Tilden, 1957: 8)
An underlying premise of the heritage interpretation is that by working with the way history is pre­
sented the meaning gathered by the audience will be affected by this interpretational work. Tilden 
distinguishes between simply communicating facts and revealing larger truths that lie behind these 
facts, with the latter being preferably in communicating history. 
==PAGE:23==
17
Tilden (1957: 9) provides six principles for correctly directed interpretation, one of them being; 
‘that an interpretation that does not relate what is being displayed to something within the visitors 
own experience will become sterile’. He goes on state that the visitor is unlikely to respond to an 
interpretation unless it touches his personal experience, thoughts, hopes, way of life, social position 
or whatever else. If you are unable to do this you have lost the visitors interest. Through this it is the 
interpreter’s job to ease the visitor’s way to create meaning of the interpretation.
The fourth of Tilden’s principles is that the aim of interpretation is not instruction but rather provo­
cation.  The visitor is not to simply be told or informed Tilden would say, but should rather provoke 
to seek out an understanding of the greater truths behind statements of facts for himself.
These two principles in particular ties well in with theories about designing for experience applied 
further down in the text. 
4.1.2 Learning in Museums
As mentioned earlier the goal of the exhibition is to be thought-provoking, debating and the visi­
tors should be confronted with dilemmas to reflect upon. In order to design how the content of the 
installation should be presented it is relevant to examine the theories of learning in museums. 
Various theories and practices of learning exist within the museum world. One is the didactic ap­
proach. The process of learning in a didactic exhibition goes through a teacher e.g. a curator who 
possesses certain amount of knowledge within a field who transmits these facts to an absorbing 
audience (Hein, 1998). Here the knowledge is broken down into smaller digestible entities. This 
ultimately leads to the didactic exhibition being rather passive from the point of view of the visitor 
(Black, 2005). 
An alternative view of the process of learning is experiential learning. This idea perceives learning as 
a life-long activity, contrary to the didactic view which is confined to a short amount of time. Here 
it is thought that visitors need to experience in order to learn, i.e. they need to see and do rather 
than to be told (Hein, 1998). The knowledge and understanding coming from experiences can then 
be processed by reflecting and applying one’s life experiences. 
Experiential learning can be described in terms of a learning cycle (fig. 4.1). Here the visitor goes 
through an experience which is then reviewed and learnt from. Finally the experience is applied to 
new or similar experiences. 
A critical aspect of applying this understanding to exhibition design is reflection is to be facilitated, 
and thereby create room for the visitor to engage in reflective observation. This is especially difficult 
since it is not possible to predict when reflection will take place. An example of this is presented by

4.1.3 Designing for a Difficult Exhibition
As stated in the presentation paper for the exhibition: ‘it is impossible to place oneself in the shoes 
of kz-prisoner’ (NMoD, 2014: 15). Designing an exhibition with this nature poses the questions of 
what should the heritage interpretation in this case strive for and what would be a desirable experi­
ence to design towards? 
Bonell and Simon (2007) define a difficult exhibition by a number of characteristics. For once 
such exhibitions have the potential to be controversial through its choice of narrative of a historical 
subject. The subject of Danish negotiation with the Nazi Government, as well as the stories of the 
privilege of the Danish kz-prisoner seems highly vulnerable to the choice of narrative. 
Furthermore the difficult exhibitions are open-ended and leave the visitor with ambiguity. ‘The 
White Busses’ seeks out this ambiguity by imposing discussion through open-ended questions and 
dilemmas. 
Thirdly the difficult exhibition will often also inflict negative emotions in the visitors. Emotions 
are bound to be evoked by e.g. the portraying of the living conditions of kz-prisoners. All this is 
suggesting De Hvide Busser to be thought of as falling under the category of a difficult exhibition. 
Bonell and Simon suggest a way to position the audience of difficult exhibitions to violence and suf­
fering of others distant in time, place or experience.  
Pitt and Britzman (2003: 759) Talks about the experience of being faced with a difficult exhibition:
 ‘This is an experience produced when one’s conceptual frameworks, emotional attachments, and 
conscious and unconscious  desires delimit one’s ability to grasp and settle the meaning of past 
events: one’s sense of mastery is undone’
This is what Bonell and Simon calls the experience of intimacy. It is an acknowledgement of the 
others experience, but also a receptivity to the details of an experience which leads to uncertainty. It 
is about coming into relation with lived lives far from one’s own (Bonell & Simon, 2007). 
The goal is to seek out an experience of intimacy instead of identification and empathy, which as­
sumedly will provoke a reflective critique of one’s grasp of what the stories and themes presented in 
==PAGE:25==
19
the exhibition. (Bonell & Simon, 2007: 70). A similar concept is discussed by Susan Sontag who 
argues that photographs have the ability to objectify people and hereby also their suffering into a 
banal feeling of sympathy (Sontag, 2003: 77)
Three structural characteristics are identified by Bonell and Simon through their investigation of 
two difficult exhibitions. One was themed around HIV/AIDS and one was telling stories from Ra­
vensbrück concentration camp. Consequently these identified structures are potentially able to serve 
as guidance in the design of similarly themed exhibitions. 
1)	 Structures that enhance the singularity of the experiences presented
In order for intimacy to be sustainable, one’s vulnerability must be combined with a re­
sponsible responsiveness, an answerability to the particularly of the traces of lives lived in 
incomparable circumstances. (Bonell & Simon, 2007: 77)
This requires for the exhibition to create the possibility to engage in the experiences separa­
tely while paying detail to the specificities. This is necessary in order to prevent the indivi­
dual experiences being collapsed into the same experience.
A characteristic of this exhibition type is that it presents statements as face value without 
commentary or explanation. In this way the immediacy is enhanced and people are forced 
into making their own reflection on the experiences. 
2)	 Structures that enhance focus and absorption in specific objects and texts
Exhibiting a small number of unique items causes visitors to be drawn into them and in­
crease focus. Few objects spaced out encourage people to approach and focus in on each 
of them. Another way this is obtained is by displaying an object with fine details which 
encourage the visitor to move close and study its particularities. The personal nature of an 
object, its connection to a lived life absorbs the visitor in a seemingly private space creating 
potential for deep emotional resonance.
4.1.4 The Interactive Experience Model 
Many distinct characteristics behaviour patterns have been identified through studies of visitors. 
Falk and Dierking suggest a model consisting of three areas that result in the interactive experience 
(Falk & Dierking, 2011). These areas are the personal, social and physical context, which all to­
gether influences the visitors’ experience (fig.4.2). First off in order to design for visitor experience it 
should be considered why people visit museums. 
Different motivations are identified: social-recreational, educational and reverential. I.e. visitors 
come to museums for different reasons ranging from socialising and having fun to with a search of 
deep personal or religious experience. The motivation of the visit will influence how the visitor per­
ceives the museum space and their behaviour within the exhibition. While ‘The White Busses’ sets 
out to be an interactive exhibition. The topic is however also connected to the suffering of Danes in 
==PAGE:26==
20
a not so far past, which might cause different motivations ranging from students browsing through 
the exhibition to reverential commemoration by older visitors. 
Another factor that differentiates the museum-goers from each other is the frequency in which they 
visit museums. The frequency of which a person visits museums can affect the approach a person 
has towards an exhibition. E.g. studies on infrequent museum-goers have shown that some feel 
physical and social uncomfortable in museum settings, because they seemed too formal or inacces­
sible to them. Another aspect influenced by visiting frequency is the way in which museum-goers 
focus within an exhibition. Infrequent museum goers have the tendency to focus intensely in the 
first part of an exhibition as they suppose is the proper behaviour. After some time this intense focus 
will transition into fatigue and the visitor focus will shift to cruising and skimming and eventually 
be more concerned with discussions of hunger and toilets (Falk & Dierking, 2011: 60).
More frequent users have shown to have another approach when visiting museums. The frequent 
visitor will acknowledge that not everything can be seen at one time, and therefore focuses in on 
what interests them, leading to a more efficient visit (Falk & Dierking, 2011: 58) It also needs to be 
considered that visitors’ frame of reference varies, and frequent visitors of museums who are gener­
ally belonging to high socioeconomic class have a reference frame closer to that of the museum staff. 
This in effect means that the communication should be able to address diverse frames of reference in 
order to engage a broad public (Falk &Dierking 2011; 74).
A pattern that has been observed amongst museum-goers is ‘modeling’ where one learns by copying 
other visitors’ behaviour. This is especially present when a visitor is insecure as to what is appropriate 
behaviour. What is appropriate behaviour is determined by the setting. While science museums gen­
erally encourages hands on experiences, art museum and historical museums are often more formal 
where one is expected to act reverentially, to look and not touch and speak in a low voice. 
This interpretation of appropriate behaviour poses a challenge in the design of an interactive instal­
lation that wants to encourage hands on engagement. This means consequently means that a design 
for guiding the visitor towards engagement should take into consideration what is perceived as ap­
propriate behaviour at this specific place at the exhibition.

4.1.6 Social objects 
Social objects are characteristic in their ability to spark social behaviour among its audience. They 
drive networked experiences among visitors by acting as a centre of conversation (Simon, 2010). 
The nature of the object directs the focus of the visitors towards the object making interpersonal 
engagement more comfortable. This happens through the process of transferring attention from 
person – person to person – object – person. 
Social objects share four qualities. They are personal, active, provocative and relational.
Personal - The personal quality of an object comes from its ability to evoke a personal response in 
the visitor relating to one’s past. An example of this could be a soup bowl in a museum setting that 
reminds a visitor of the soup bowl at his or hers grandmothers house. This quality has the potential 
to trigger sharing of amongst onlookers.
 
Active - Active objects act as a reference point in a shared discussion between strangers. When an 
active object is imposed on a situation the shared experience of the on lookers will cause the objects 
to be a natural subject for conversation. This could for instance be a visual event in an installation 
that sparks conversation discussing the object. 
Provocative - Objects that are provocative can trigger discussion amongst its on lookers. By present­
ing a provocative statement or subject visitors can be compelled to reflect collectively on it. 
Relational - Relational objects are objects that require for more people to work together in order 
to make an installation work. By inviting visitors to be simultaneously engaged in an installation a 
potential for interpersonal connection are created.
==PAGE:28==
22
These four qualities lend themselves to some of Tilden’s principles but they serve as a design guide­
line for facilitating visitor interaction and engagement in modern exhibitions. 
4.1.7 The Object knowledge framework 
Wood and Latham propose in The Objects of Experience a framework for understanding how 
people relate to objects and more specifically how visitors relate to objects on display in muse­
ums. The framework is called The Object Knowledge Framework (fig. 4.3) and is based on the 
notion of lived experiences. 
This means that e.g. when one encounters an object, one become aware of its significance. This is 
based on prior experiences, e.g. another encounter with a similar object. Though one might not 
immediately know the meaning behind the objects significance, one might react to it instantly. The 
importance of an object encounter is a kind of knowledge that is deeply rooted on an emotional, 
physical and psychological level. Meaning making of a lived experience however happens after the 
event has occurred, and furthermore:
‘The more that a person actively connects to and thinks about his or her encounter with an object the more 
he or she becomes aware of the experience.‘ (Wood & Latham, 2013)
As a consequence of this view, a person will also have his or her own perception of the world, based 
on ones lived experiences. Wood operates with this as the phenomenological term lifeworld. 
This idea can be applied to the museum context to help understand visitors’ experiences of an 
exhibition. The potential of this understanding is that it in turn can aid the designer in creating a 
meaningful encounter through the presentation of content.
The Lifeworld - The Object Knowledge Framework deals with the relationship of three worlds. 
First is the previously described lifeworld, which is what the visitor brings to an exhibition. The 
lifeworld consists of three dimensions representing different ways of knowing: individual, group and 
material.

The individual way denotes the personal significance of an object to an individual viewer.

When a visitor encounters an object and has a lived experience with it, he or she senses the object 
and thinks about it. The way in which the person is sensing of the object e.g. physical qualities will 
be based on one’s past experiences, also called the phenomenal field (Merleau-Ponty, 1962) likewise

The group dimension is much like the personal dimension in the kind of effects it has on a visitor’s 
experience. However it is the origin of these that are different. The group dimension is based on the 
part of the lifeworld that springs from cultural, social or historical meanings of objects.  
The material dimension of the lifeworld consists of the physical characteristics of an object. It in­
cludes the aesthetical aspects of the object. This is the presentation of an object in a physical space, 
including visual, audio and tactile qualities.  
The Objectworld - The objectworld consists of all of the information, actions, events and people associ­
ated with the object over its entire life, plus the way in which the museum has transformed that informa­
tion into a museum experience. (Wood & Latham, 2013)
Analogue to the Lifeworld the objectworld is built from same three dimensions, however with focus 
on the object and how it affects the human experience. 
The material dimension of the objectworld is concerned with material qualities of the object that 
can be sensed, shape, size, colour, smell and sound etc. 
The group dimension contains the way in which the object affects a person’s experience based on its 
role e.g. culturally, social and historical. This could mean themes represented by the object such as 
friendship, love or religion. 
The individual dimension deals with the personal connections that relate to an object, e.g. knowl­
edge of the previous owner of the object or the visitors having used a similar object previously. 
The Unified Experience - When the objectworld and the lifeworld come together it creates a uni­
fied experience, which can be a deeply meaningful lived experience. This is however dependent on 
how an object is transformed within the museum. 
Experiences often begin with something that catches one’s attention and often causes confusion, 
doubt or uncertainty. This can be called disequilbrium. It arises from a problem e.g. caused by an 
object at a museum. The visitor then seeks to resolve this tension of the disequilibrium by acquir­
ing knowledge that creates a new view on the world, in which the visitor obtains what can be called 
unity. This is a state of balance achieved e.g. by discussion with others or individual investigations.   
A way to facilitate a meaningful encounter could be as suggested by Wood to match up the life­
world with the objectworld. This could for instance be achieved by displaying an object that is 
somehow familiar to the visitor, hereby creating a possibility for resonance with the visitor.

4.2 Design for Experience from a Product and Service Perspective
In the following section design for experience theory from the field of product and service design is 
covered. The follows starts off with a short introduction into the field.
There have been many attempts at defining user experience, which has led to a broad range of 
definitions and perspectives and equally many concepts for understanding it (Jensen, 2013; 4). 
Experience design originally stemming from human computer interaction design is today applied in 
many different technological fields e.g. product and service design, IT and design of user interfaces. 
The footing that experience design has gained can be attributed to what have been coined as “the 
experience turn”. This is the idea that the design focus have shifted from the products to a broader 
perspective of experiences which the products construct (Oppelaar et al., 2008).
As defined by Jensen user experience design refers to IT solutions with focus on the interaction 
between user and a system or product interface. On the other hand experience design referring to a 
broader category of design with focus on the experience of the user not exclusively IT related will 
instead be used. (Jensen, 2013: 36)
The theoretical basis for the installation that this report deals with, is hereby encompassed in the 
term experience design but is also concerned with the subcategory of user experience design as the 
installation is digital and interactive. 
A model for understanding user experience design is proposed by Jensen (Jensen, 2013). It is pro­
posed that what is central to the understanding of experience is the interaction (fig. 4.4). By this 
definition it is through the interaction of a user and a product within a context that creates makes 
up the experience. This model also emphasises that its elements are interrelated and interdependent. 
Building on this model is the idea of touch points which marks the experience according to the 
interaction. As suggested by Roto (2011) the experience of a product can be momentary through 
using the objects or it can emerge from before or after the usage of the product. 
4.2.1 The Different Areas of Design for Experience
Experience design covers a big array of perspectives on the concept of experience, which also is 
reflected in the variety of focus in the theory. In designing pleasurable products Jordan breaks down 
the experience of products into four pleasures. Physical pleasure comes from sensing and object, 
social pleasure from social interaction, psychological from cognition e.g. problem solving and ideo­
logical pleasure that derives from the beliefs of the user (Jordan, 2000). 
Forlizzi breaks experience into three kinds of experience, an experience and co-experience. While 
experience is the constant stream of self-talk that happens when we interact with products, an

to integrate and balance these aspects when designing for experience. 
Six design principles is proposed by Norman in the The design of everyday things. The six principles 
serve as a guideline for designing for enhanced usability, which in turn as described above is under­
lying factor for the experience of products. The principles include: Visibility, Feedback, Constraints, 
Mapping, Consistency and Affordance, the way in which people are able to understand the poten­
tials of use of a product.

4.3 Comparing and Combining two Fields of Design for Experience  
The two fields examined have entirely different scopes of their design. One is to be applied to the 
context of a museum generally concerning the presentation of authentic historical objects. This also 
means that the museological grounded theory focus on the content to be presented. The other is 
directed towards product and service design. 
The goal of this project is to design an installation for a museum. This particular scope puts the de­
sign somewhere in between these two fields, potentially benefiting from incorporation of both. The 
premise for both design approaches is the underlying acknowledgement that you cannot design an 
experience but merely design for an experience, or for the possibility of it occurring.
The theory from both fields is concerned with the interaction of an object and a visitor/user. The 
concern for context is more pronounced in the case of the presented museological experience theory. 
The museological field applies phenomenological approaches in examining the experience, for in­
stance seen in the focus on the lived life.
Contrary the design for experience field for product design relates the experience to functional qual­
ity of the product.
It is a consequence of the field’s different means of designing for experience. It is natural since the 
focus of product and service designers is to design a product, while the museological will design an 
exhibition, including authentic objects. 
Some of the texts written on experiences within the museological fields suggest deep profound 
and sometimes reverential experiences to occur within the exhibition. This is rarely the case in the 
product and service design field where the experience is not described to such magnitude. The 
words used to describe the visitors’ experience might however be needed in order to grasp how deep 
the impact of the museum experience can be. Some of the areas that are proposed in the product 
design field are more geared towards consumer products and might be sufficient in targeting these 
profound experiences.
The further design process need for an increased intention for this specific context as it is critical 
in understanding visitors’ experience, both in regards to interactions between visitors as well be­
tween visitors and objects. The juxtaposition of the two fields facilitates to taken into account the 
profound experiences for instance described in ‘Difficult exhibitions’, while still keeping an eye on 
pragmatic attributes such as the functionality of the installation.

An observational field study at three museums in Copenhagen was conducted with special attention 
to: 
• 
Investigating the design of current historical exhibitions. 
• 
Describing the way in which visitors engage with installations within an exhibition.
• 
Describing the social interactions of visitors engaging in the exhibitions.

draw inspiration from existing solutions for installations within similar environments to that of ‘The 
White Busses’.
5.1 Methodology
The Observational studies were performed applying the AEIOU framework (Wasson, 2000). This 
method categorises the observations into five categories: Activities, Environment, Interactions, 
Objects and Users. The method is beneficial in that it helps to keep focus on different aspect when 
observing a situation, as well as it helps to understand the relation between the five elements. Fol­
lowing the observational work the findings were ordered in regards to the five elements in an affinity 
diagram (appx 5). 
The observations were then each examined. Some of the observations were applied the perspectives 
of the theoretical framework derived in chapter 4 for a better understanding.

Initially observation sessions were conducted in three different museums in Copenhagen all under 
the management of NMD: A temporary exhibition themed around fur at The National Museum 
of Denmark, An exhibition on Danish naval history at The Royal Danish Naval Museum (Orlogs­

5.3 Fur Exhibition at Nationalmuseet
The fur exhibition at Nationalmuseet sat out to display the historical fur clothing that has been 
utilized throughout history in the northern hemisphere. A declared goal of the exhibition was to 
propose the ethical dilemma related to the breeding and hunting of animals with the purpose of 
harvesting fur. 
The fur exhibition is situated in a hall at NMD, which has a high ceiling and wide open area. The 
architecture of the exhibition divides it into sections which all have different themes and media. In 
the following I will go through the different sections of the exhibition in order to present the find­
ings that were gathered in the observational studies. 
The first part of the exhibition is made up of hanging posters and videos projected on posters. The 
posters have pictures of Danish celebrities who state their opinion on the topic of fur. The video 
projections were accompanied by directional speakers hanging above. The content of the videos 
were vox pops with people on the street expressing their opinion on the topic. 
This was the first thing that visitors were faced with, and many seemed to be initially attracted 
towards the video projections. The use of directional speakers results in a small area in which the 
sound of the video can be heard. This was often misinterpreted by the on lookers who stood too far 
away from projection screen to hear the sound. This was also caused by grouping of people in front 
of the projections which caused some by standers to be left out of the area of sound. 
Seemingly there was a discrepancy from the intended use and the realised. The affordance of the 
video projection was not clear to the users who possible have thought that there was no sound. 
Family groups with kids frequented the exhibition. The kids would often seem restless and not very 
engaged in the first part of the section. While parents tried to explain the posters to the kids to little 
effect. 
This is an example of the divergent motivations that exist within groups visiting a museum. The 
parent might have an agenda of engaging in the content of the exhibition or teaching her kids about 
it. It does however conflict with the motivation of the kids which for instance could be more tied to 
the souvenir shop.

5.4 Exhibition on History of Norwegian and Danish Navy
The exhibition is situated in the Royal Danish Naval Museum in Copenhagen. The exhibition 
which opened rather recently in 2014 tells the story of the joint navy of Norway and Denmark 
starting from around the 17th century to present day. The exhibition was structured chronologically, 
with every room having a distinct theme.
 
One of the rooms was setup to mimic the atmosphere at a ship with hammocks, a wooden table 
and a gridded moving lighting pattern. The hammocks had dedicated head phones that the visitors 
could put on while laying down to hear a story. A wooden table with wooden spoons and a bowl 
stood on top of the table. Next to the room was a wardrobe with several naval uniform costumes. 
The exhibition stages the living conditions on ships in the past. Hereby it creates a connection to 
the present by allowing visitors to perceive objects and to engage in activities similar to ones used 
hundreds of years ago. 
This was also prevalent in an adjacent room where a cannonball was laid out on the floor, with a 
daring text of “try and lift the canon ball”.  This text does on one hand explain to the visitors that 
this object is to be touched but furthermore it encourages an interaction by using ‘try’ in its impera­
tive form as a command. 
In the last room themed around the contemporary Danish navy a collage hang from the wall. Here 
pictures of historical naval workers hang beside those of the present. A collage of faces that seemed 
to align with the design intend of creating a relation to the Danish navy across time.

The observations from the three museums proved useful in understanding what engages visitors.

Three different topics of the exhibition were to be selected from. The three topics were related to

One topic was to be displayed in the entrance of the beginning with the purpose of unfolding the

Another topic was the logistics of the rescue mission. This was to be situated in the scene of an

6.3 Drafting the Specification Statement
A first draft of specification statements was created in order to serve as a guideline for the design 
and a tool for evaluation. The specification statements were adjusted throughout the design pro­
cess and the final version is presented here. The specification statements are a method for creating 
looser and more flexible specifications compared to traditional requirement specifications (Thorp 
& Andreasen, 2007). One of the benefits is that it to a lesser degree confines the design process 
by having unnecessary concrete requirements. The formulations are all based on the user’s experi­
ence when using the installation.
1) The user should have an experience of connectedness with the installation.
This is the core statement for the installation, since this is the most important aspect for the visitor 
to have a meaningful experience with the installation. 
It is based off of the object knowledge framework and how users will be able feel connected 
with an objects story when encountering it, e.g. stemming from having used a similar object 
previously in his or her life. 
2) The user should be drawn to interact with the installation. 
This is the premise for making the installation work, as people need to interact with it and 
therefore need to be drawn to it. This is something that for instance could be accommodated 
by use of Nina Simon’s ideas of what makes up a social object. 
3) The user should have an experience of being able to interact with the installation and its objects 
without compromising the preservation of historical artefacts.
This statement was an addition to the original requirement statements. It came up in meet­
ings with NMD that there had been incidents where people would not pick up objects that 
were on display to be touched because they thought they were only to be observed, like most 
other museum objects. 
4) The user should have an ongoing wish to interact with the object on display while investigating 
the content of the installation.
==PAGE:45==
39
The importance of this statement comes from the idea that the visitor through haptic explo­
ration and perception of the object will achieve not only an interesting haptic sensation, but 
also greater experience of the lived life of the object as well. 
5) The user should through interaction with the installation be provoked and challenged in opin­
ions or views.
A provocation in the installation is meant to cause engagement and reflection facilitating 
discussion between the visitors. As suggested by Bonell and Simon (2007)it is also possible 
that an eyewitness account from a prisoner of a kz camp is able to leave the user in a very 
meaningful state of uncertainty in his or her view on the world.
6)Users should be able to engage with installation either passively or actively.
This statement leaves room for the visitors to be able get an outcome from the installation 
even without actively interacting with it, as there will likely be a barrier for people to interact 
with it.
7) he experience of the installation should not be influenced by the previous users’ interaction.
This meaning that the users should be guided to reset the installation after ended use. This is 
to ensure a control over the experience through a consistency in the expression of the installa­
tion.
8) The installation should appeal to all age groups, especially the young people which are the target 
group of the installation.
The exhibition is going to be visited by all age groups and the installation should therefore 
appeal to all, the focus of is though on engaging younger people.
9) he users’ experience of the installation should be that it is in coherence with the rest of the exhi­
bition.
This is in regards to both the experience and expression of the installation that should tie into 
the overall feel and aesthetic of the exhibition. This statement also is to ensure that the ac­
counts of the prisoners’ lives are presented in a way that serves them respectfully through the 
installation, as the exhibition is also a memorial exhibition

6.9 Detailing the Design and Construction of a Functional Mock-up 
Following the meeting the concept was further developed in regards to shape, size, materials, AV

functional Mock-up. The mock-up does not completely reflect the final design at the exhibition, but

6.10 Choosing a material for the Box 
Initially the material was to be chosen for the box part holding the object. Material selection was 
influenced by the architects’ aesthetic guidelines and the materials of the historic cupboard. An­
other concern was how the projection would look on the surface. Experimentation with plywood 
and particle boards showed that while the particle board had a fitting aesthetic, the texture was too 
significant behind the projection. The material of choice for the box structure was therefore made 
to be pine plywood, which had a wooden texture, resembled the historic cupboard and gave a clean 
projection surface.
The pocket of the box holding the model object was chosen to be made in frosted Poly Ethylene. 
The slight transparency allows for light to be diffused through it enabling to light up the pocket 
when the object was not present. 
6.11 Choosing a Censor for the Installation
The installation was supposed to recognise when the object was inside of it or in the hands of the 
user. When the model object was inside the installation a certain video would play and when the 
model was in the hands of the user another video would play. 
Different options for censoring were considered. The criteria for the censor were that it should 
be stable in recognising whether the object was standing inside the box or outside. Preferably the 
censor would not be visible to the user of the installation. A light censor was considered but re­
jected because it would have rely on extensive calibration depending on the light environment of 
the exhibition. Furthermore a light censor would be possible to manipulate by user for instance by 
substituting the object with a hand. A pressure censor was rejected as it would also be possible to 
manipulate and would probably require being visible.
A Near Field Communication (NFC) reader was chosen for censoring. NFC is a technology based 
on Radio Frequency Identification (RFID). The technology enables a chip to be read at close prox­
imity (4- 20cm). The NFC reader is harder to manipulate by a user since the presence of the chip 
cannot be replicated. This results in a stable outcome of the interaction, and avoids misuse of the 
installation.

7. User Testing The Model and the Installation
7.1 Testing Visitors’ Interpretation of An Oatmeal Package
In order to evaluate the installation design two user tests were conducted. This test sat out to exam­
ine how visitors of The Danish National Museum experienced a real oatmeal pack and a model of 
an oatmeal pack. The interview was conducted during opening hours at NMD. 
7.1.1 Purpose of the Test
This interview was conducted with two main purposes. One was to see whether the Ota oatmeal 
package would be recognised and what sort of associations it would trigger. The other purpose was 
to investigate the subjects’ perception of the objects and compare the difference in the visitors’ de­
scription of the two objects in order to design a model that would trigger similar associations to that 
of a real oatmeal package.
7.1.2 Method
The interviews were conducted in an open room next to the entrance hall in the Museum. While it 
was open no one else was present in the room and the room was shielded by the entrance desk from 
the busy main hall. The subjects were found in the entrance hall and guided into the interview room 
where they were seated at the end of a table with the interviewer across (fig. 7.1). 
The subject was blindfolded and presented with one of the objects, either the real oatmeal pack­
age or the model. The subjects were only presented with one object as the similarity of the objects 
would cause the associations to spill over from one to another. 
The interview was structured into three parts based on the semiotic product analysis model (Oppe­
rud, 2004)(appx 7.1). As described in section four Opperud deals with three dimensions of semiot­
fig 7.1 On the left a blindfoled subjects investigating the model. On the right the Ota Solgryn package and the model package.
==PAGE:54==
48
ics. In the first part the subjects were asked to describe the object in their own words. This was done 
in order to examine what were the first properties that the subjects noticed and talked about. After 
their initial description they were asked to elaborate on the properties of the object in regards to 
shape, feeling and sound. 
Thereafter the subjects were asked to evaluate the objects in regards to a semantic differential scale. 
The scale was a Lickert scale ranging from -3 to 3 with 0 being the middle option. The scale was 
chosen in order to offer a clearly neutral standpoint for the subjects, as well as emphasise the polar­
ity in the semantic pairs (Al-Hindawe, 1996). 
The adjectives of the scale were chosen on an assessment of characteristic words for an Ota oatmeal 
package. The adjectives were also drawn with inspiration from other semantic scales used to describe 
product design. Another consideration selecting the adjectives was that they would relate to the sto­
ry of the exhibition. This for instance meant including a pair like valuable and worthless as it would 
tie into the theme of how a simple oatmeal package can be considered worthless in one context and 
be critical for survival in another context. Hereby the semantic scale enabled the model to be judged 
on how well it would tie into the story.
The fact that it was mainly young people that were to be interviewed also affected the choice of ad­
jectives. As suggested by Al-Hindawe words were chosen that were thought to be meaningful to the 
young audience and have relevance to them. This was done in an effort for the subjects to be able to 
understand the words and thereby give a qualified evaluation.
The adjectives organised randomly on the scale so that there was no apparent pattern to what was 
valued negatively and positively. 
A part of the reasoning behind the decision to include a semantic scale in the interview was to make 
the interviewed reflect on the object. The semantic scale was to aid the subjects in talking about as­
sociations through familiarising with the object. On top of this the semantic scale itself should gave 
and understanding of what adjectives were most likely be connected to the objects and why. 
The final part of the interview was focused on the associations evoked by the interaction with pack­
age.  Here the subjects were asked to list all the associations that would come to mind. After the 
initial wave of associations they were asked about what memories handling the object elicited in 
them, e.g. persons or situations. Lastly the subjects were asked what they thought of the object and 
to sum up everything in an interpretation.

When the subjects investigated the object, they held the object in their hands weighing it. Gener­
ally this would be followed by applying pressure to surface and running their fingers over the surface 
and the corners. More rarely would the subjects shake the boxes to examine the sound, but would 
rather be presented to the rattling when they turned the objects around.
Six people were interviewed with different occupations. Two were students in the tenth grade, one 
high school student, one university student, a pensioner and on who was working. See appx 7.2 for 
the bulk data collected from the interviews.

When comparing the results for the two objects five areas stuck out (fig. 7.4 )
All the subjects answered that both objects were more simple than complex. Generally the subjects 
expressed a familiarity with the objects that could have influenced this as they would have the idea

stood. 
The subjects also generally perceived the objects to be more common than exclusive. This was most­
ly attributed to the familiarity of a rectangular shape. Another reason for this was that the subjects 
recognised the oatmeal package as a common commodity.
The subjects found both objects to be more friendly than frightening. Something that was attrib­
uted the subjects’ familiarity with the objects shape. 
In regards to the semantic pair exciting and boring there seemed to be a discrepancy. The oatmeal 
package was perceived as more boring than the model package which would score higher towards 
‘exciting’. Generally the excitement was attributed to the sound that suggested the content of the 
package. The content of the model package was harder to identify for the subjects than the oatmeal 
package, resulting in the oatmeal package scoring more towards ‘boring’ due to its familiarity.
The model package was perceived as more attractive than the oatmeal package which came out com­
pletely neutral, which was attributed to the paper type and the novel tactility.

7.1.9 Reflection on the Method and the Execution of the Interview
The semantic scale proved useful as a facilitator for the subjects to perceive the packages as they 
when rating and asked about their ratings were forced into reflection of their perception. As it is 
suggested by Al hindawe (1996). A pilot study could have been carried out prior in order to exam­
ine what adjectives does people use to describe an oatmeal package. This would possibly avoid the 
subjects rating 0 as a result of not being able to relate the adjectives to the object. This was especially 
apparent when interviewing the youngest of the group who did not quite understand the mean­
ing of some of the words in the context. It was also apparent in the case when people were asked 
about whether they perceived the object as friendly or frightening. This question often resulted in a 
slight laughter indicating that the subject s found the question a bit absurd since the connotation of 
frightening rarely stems from an object, especially an oatmeal package.
When the subjects were answering the semantic differential questions they would sometimes refer 
to a previous statement in their stream of answering. This shows how the subjects had a tendency to 
seek consistency in their answering, something that could be taken into account when constructing 
the table as the first order of the questions should be selected carefully.  
Selecting the adjectives also have an inherent critical aspect in that they can be interpreted differ­
ently across age, culture and gender (Al-Hindawe, 1996). This is something that possibly could have

been addressed through a pilot study.
Changing from the first interviews to the following was the way in which the subjects were asked 
to list their associations. When they were encouraged to ‘just shoot’ the associations came in bigger 
quantity and more spontaneous, as it seemed the subjects would otherwise be holding back a bit. 
Furthermore including pauses in the questioning seemed to encourage elaborate answers. 
At the end of the interview when the subjects were asked how they liked the object it often would 
lead to a more informal discussion of the object and peripheral aspects of it. The nature of the ques­

However when the subjects answered a casual laugh from the interviewer would sometimes halt

but not so relaxed that it ridicules the situation and becomes comical. 
7.1.10 Conclusion - What can be Brought into Further Development
This interview was conducted with two main purposes. One was to see whether the Ota oatmeal 
package would be recognised and what sort of associations it would trigger. The other purpose was 
to investigate the subjects’ perception of the objects and compare the difference in the visitors’ de­
scription of the two objects in order to design a model that would trigger similar associations to that 
of a real oatmeal package.
The interview sat out to test two things. The first was to see if any associations and memories would 
emerge from the encounter with an oatmeal package. The resulting memories were focused around 
cooking and everyday tasks for the oatmeal package, and holiday situations with families for the 
model package. 
The follow up question is then what should be designed for? The objects and their associations will 
be juxtaposed in the exhibition with the inhumane living conditions of the concentration camps. 
This will undeniable create a different experience of the objects which is not examined in this 
test. Designing for the possibilities of elicitation of memories such as Christmas and birthday has 
its qualities. The association of presents is also somehow in vein of the theme where the event of 
receiving the Red Cross packages by some prisoners was compared to Christmas. Furthermore it 
could potentially through association with family open up for a reflection on the delineation of who 
should be cared for and the reach of solidarity. 
The core purpose of the object is however to facilitate an experience of connecting with the lived 
lives connected to the historical closet and the oatmeal package. The connection between the visitor 
and the lived lives of the prisoner benefits from the resemblance of the oatmeal package. The experi­
ence of holding an object that has a direct relation to the authentic packages received by the prison­
ers will result in a stronger more meaningful visitor-object encounter. 
Simulating the authenticity of a package also establishes intimacy with the stories of the packages as 
visitors are forced into thinking of actual lived lives behind the object. 
In order to create the resemblance of the oatmeal package, one would either simply choose an actual 
package or take the characteristics found in the test into account. Descriptions such as the smooth­
ness and the flexibility of the packing should be considered. The sound should also be carefully 
simulated. As shown with the test of the package containing pearls the sound is easily recognisable 
and could easily confuse the authentic expression aimed for. The rectangular shape is also critical. 
The finish on the wrapping which should be of industrial quality is also to be taken into account as 
well as the thickness of the paper.

7.2 Testing the Mock-up of the Installation - User Experience Journey
The User experience journey test sat out to examine how visitors of The Danish National Museum 
would experience the use of a mock-up of MedborgerSkabet.
7.2.1 Purpose
The purpose of the test was to examine in what way different stages of use affected the test subjects, 
either negatively or positively. On top of that the purpose was also to investigate what specific as­
pects affected the experience of use.
7.2.2 Method
The test was divided into three phases based on the method of UX journey, before, during and after 
(Roto, 2011). These touch points were chosen as they were three distinctive stages of use, where 
only the middle one being interactive and the others being merely reflective. The three stages were 
to simulate a typical use situation where the visitors will look and observe the installation, interact 
with it and continue on. 
The UX journey method works by letting the user map out the experience according to valence and 
arousal. This allowed the user to detail his or her experience by drawing the curve in the negative 
and positive axis, and adjust the magnitude of the perceived negative or positive experience. 
A benefit of forcing the users to draw their experience is that it facilitates reflection and discussion 
of all the stages of the use as the subject explains his or her reasons for drawing the curve. A inter­
view guide was used in order to supplement with further questions ( appx 7.3)
Six subjects were tested representing the target group of the exhibition ranging from tenth grade 
to last year of high school. The subjects were tested in pairs and were found among visitors in the 
entrance hall. The interview was conducted in a meeting room located inside the museum (fig. 7.4). 
It was attempted to somehow mimic the lighting conditions from the exhibition, by covering up the 
windows and switching of the light. This was also critical in making the projection clearly visible. 
The equipment used was also somewhat limiting the setup as the projector had to be in a specific 
distance from the installation. This caused the space before the installation to be a little narrow 
possibly affecting the subjects positioning. Consequently the environment in which the test was 
conducted was very different from that of the exhibition.

7.2.4 Discussion of method
The interviews were conducted with two test subjects at a time. This was beneficial in that it showed 
how pairs of users could interact with the installation. However it could have been interesting to 
investigate how users would experience the installation alone. 
Having two subjects in at the same time, also made them influence each other. The answers were 
sometimes agreed upon in the pairs which might have caused for subjects to withhold their opinion. 
On the other hand the opinions were also discussed sometimes which required for elaboration of 
reasons. 
The diagram that the subjects drew on could have been marked with horizontal lines to mark the 
degree of arousal in the curve for instance by smiley icons. This could also possibly have made the 
graphs more comparable to each other as the scale would be less arbitrary.
The use of the UX journey method was on some occasions difficult to implement as when the 
subjects questioned how they should answer when the experience of the installation was fine but the 
story and the atmosphere caused a negative experience. 
Investigation of the after stage could have been more elaborated by for instance calling up the sub­
jects later to see if the content had resonated with them, for instance by having thought of the topic 
during the time after the test. This poses a critical aspect in applying the UX journey to the museum 
context. The definition of touch points is hard to define as the content is likely to resonate with the 
visitor long after visiting the exhibition.
7.2. 5 Findings to take on in the further development
When looking at what to derive from the results of the test usability issues become immediately 
apparent. The subjects of the test were in several cases not able to figure how to use the installation. 
The affordance of the installation was unclear due to its similarities with a button. It should be clear 
to the visitor that the object could be taken out. The subjects became confused by the chalk sounds 
that did not correspond to the video. This should be redesigned as it caused unnecessary confusion.
While it should be clear how to use the installation, it should not necessarily be obvious what the 
result of the use is. When one of the subjects stated that she expected something to jump out from 
behind the box, it indicates a build-up of expectation that can be used to benefit the experience of 
the installation. This sense of expectation can potentially help engage visitors. This however requires 
for the installation not to disappoint through not live up to the expectations.

The experience of the installation was seemingly affected by the content of the video projections.

8.1 Evaluation of the Design According to the Requirement Statements
Throughout the design process the requirement statements have been continuously consulted. 
This has helped to keep focus of the design direction and what to be designed for. In the follow­
ing section the design of the installation will be hold up against the earlier specified requirement 
statements that was setup act as a measurement tool for proposed design solutions. This is done in 
order to review and evaluate the strength of the design. The specifications are coded as to how well 
they are achieved. A green colour means that this aspect is fulfilled. A yellow colour means that the 
specification was somewhat met but that there is room for improvement, and the red colour coding 
suggests a redesign in order for this specification to be met. 
The user should have an experience of connectedness with the installation.
The conducted test of how visitors of TNMD interpreted the oatmeal package, showed 
that both the oatmeal as well as model package have the possibility to elicit memories and 
associations. Consequently this does suggest the possibility for the visitor to experience a 
connectedness with the lived life of the object. However the subjects interpreted the model 
package in an entirely different direction than what was desired in relation to the concen­

This specification statement was addressed for once by having provocative though provo­

some subjects were drawn due to curiosity of what would happen when interacting with the 
installation. Others however were passively waiting in front of it without realising how to 
interact with it
The user should have an experience of being able to interact with the installation and its

visitor to figure out whether the interaction with the object compromises its preservation,

interpretation of the object the semantic differential generally showed an evaluation of the 
objects more towards worthless than valuable.

8.2 Implications of Working with Design for Experience in a Museum Context
The choice of using design for experience as the design approach for this project initially neces­
sitated looking into two different fields, the museological field and the product and service design 
field. The two fields consist of different professions which are reflected in the literature. Where the 
museological literature is written for historians, curators, the product and service design literature is 
targeted designers and engineers. 
This discrepancy ultimately caused the need to adopt theories from both fields. As so much lit­
erature exists in either field a selection was made in order to make a reasonable representation of 
the fields. It has not come to my knowledge that a comprehensive theoretical framework exist that 
combines the two fields and addresses designing an interactive installation in a museum context. 
Applying design for experience does however arm a designer with a wide range of tools to utilise in 
the design process. Potentially applying design for experience can create the foundation for a holistic 
understanding and design for and exhibition. 
A backbone of the design process was the specification statements that helped evaluate the designs 
against. The use of specification statements that in nature is not as rigid as a requirement specifica­
tion is beneficial as the statements are hard to make concrete measurements of. This causes a free­
dom when exploring ideas and concepts. However it also somewhat complicates the decision project 
where considerations and assumptions must serve as the basis. 
For evaluating and discussing suggestions with the museum it proved very beneficial to incorporate 
mock- ups. Discussions on what would be the experience of the installation easily became vaguely 
grounded. The test of the physical mock-up and mock up in the video presentation conveyed the 
experience way better, and the resulting discussion became very concrete and useful.

10. Conclusion
This thesis project sat out to design an exhibition installation with a user experience perspective. 
The scope of the project was defined in collaboration with The National Museum of Denmark as 
to be regarding the upcoming exhibition ‘The White Busses’.  A declared goal from the museum 
was to attract young people to visit the exhibition. This goal was also identified in chapter three as 
a governing discourse for the National Museum of Denmark that as an institution must respond to 
political decisions of the Danish Ministry of Culture.  Another discourse that was identified was the 
concept of ‘the participatory museum’, a museum structure that facilitates discussion, engagement 
and participation with its visitors. These discourses have a direct impact on the direction of the 
installation design, which can be beneficial in the role as a designer to be aware of. 
Two different fields of experience theory have been explored, one stemming from the field of mu­
seology and another from the field of product and service design. Looking into literature from the 
two fields it became apparent that while they were described very differently they shared similarities 
in how the view experience. Experience was in either side of the juxtaposed theory seen as a result 
from interaction. Among the differences were the focus on the lived life and the background of the 
visitors as well as the idea of how the museum specifically as an institution is capable of creating a 
deeply profound reverential experience.
Three museums exhibition have been investigated in with focus on visitors’ interaction and engage­
ment with it. Some objects at the exhibition caused a lot more interaction than other. An explana­
tion for this was found in their qualities as ‘social objects’. 
The design process throughout the project has been in dialog with the museum. This has caused 
for continuously evaluation of the direction of the design. It was examined in chapter two how the 
organisational structure can influence the communication and negotiations of the design direction. 
The design process end through several iteration of the design in a mock –up that was tested on visi­
tors of the museum. How visitors interpreted an oatmeal package and a model package was tested. 
The packages presented to the subjects caused for a wide range of association and memories to be 
elicited, namely of oatmeal and family gatherings. It was however concluded that the interpretation 
of the constructed model diverged a lot from the real oatmeal package. 
In the other test the mock-up of the installation was tested. It was concluded through the subjects 
mapping of their experience that they were not too appealed to engage with the installation, due to 
its visual expression. Engaging with the installation also could problems that could be attributed to 
people not understanding the affordance of the installation.
The design was evaluated on the basis of specification statements and focus areas for improvements 
were identified, especially in relation to the in the area of engagement of visitors. Something that 
will be worked towards improving in the future of the project.
