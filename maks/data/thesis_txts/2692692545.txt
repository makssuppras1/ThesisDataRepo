i
Abstract
Blockage effect is the effect that a wind turbine or wind farm has on the approaching wind.
As the wind approaches, it is slowed down and, to some extent, diverted around the turbine or
farm. The magnitude of the streamwise deceleration is known as the induction. In recent years,
it has been shown that the induction caused by the blockage effect of a single turbine often leads
to a reduced efﬁciency of an entire wind farm due to an upstream interference between turbines.
This effect is called the global blockage effect. In this report, it is shown that the blockage
effect is inﬂuenced by two atmospheric conditions: the height of the atmospheric boundary
layer (ABL) and wind veer within the ABL.
Simulations are conducted with adapted RANS k-ϵ models. Stably stratiﬁed ABL’s with
ﬁnite ABL heights are modeled in steady-state simulations while neutral atmospheres with an
inﬁnitely tall ABL are modeled for reference. The effect of the ﬁnite ABL height is imple-
mented through a turbulent length scale. In an investigation of the single turbine blockage
effect for different ABL inﬂows, inductions at 2.5 rotor diameters (D) upstream are found to
be between 1.10 and 1.50 %, depending on the inﬂow. It is concluded that there is a clear
dependency between the ABL height and the magnitude of the blockage effect.
Besides the single turbine blockage effect, the effect of the ABL height is investigated for
the global blockage effect through six wind farm setups. The main wind farm investigation is
conducted for a square setup of nine turbines with a uniform spacing of 5D. For this wind farm
and the same ABL inﬂows as for the single turbine, inductions 2.5D upstream of the center front
turbine are computed in a range between 1.67 and 2.73 %. Consequently, it is shown that the
global blockage effect enhances the induction from the single turbine blockage effect, and that
the ABL height has an impact on the induction. The power loss due to global blockage effect
for the center front turbine is computed in a range between 1.5 and 3.6 %. For different wind
farm setups, it is found that the blockage effect increases when either the number of turbines is
increased or the distance between the turbines is decreased.
In the report, the variation of wind direction with height, known as veer, is also shown to
affect the single turbine blockage effect. When veer is included in the simulations, the otherwise
nearly symmetric, horizontal induction zone in front of the turbine is rotated clockwise around
the rotor center. This reduces the magnitude of the induction upstream of the turbine, and
the results therefore indicate that veer reduces the single turbine blockage effect. It is further
indicated that a larger magnitude of veer reduces the blockage effect further. The investigation
of veer in this report is relatively short, and further research should be conducted on the topic.
Finally, the report contains a brief comparison between Large Eddy Simulation (LES) results
and results obtained with the RANS model used in the report. The comparison indicates that
the RANS model overpredicts the magnitude of the blockage effect. However, the comparison
is not conclusive as the atmospheric conditions could not be completely matched between the
RANS model and the LES. The RANS model simulates a stably stratiﬁed atmosphere whereas
the LES investigates a neutrally stratiﬁed atmosphere.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the ﬁeld of wind energy, wind turbines extract kinetic energy from the wind and convert it
to electrical energy to be sold and later used by consumers. The techniques to harvest the energy
of the wind become increasingly more efﬁcient, and the science of wind energy analysis evolves
towards increasingly higher complexity and accuracy. For the wind energy industry, the source
of income is the power produced by the turbines. Consequently, the Annual Energy Production
(AEP) of a wind farm is a key number in the projecting and assessment of wind farms. A larger
AEP results in a larger portion of sustainable energy to to be used by the consumers and a larger
revenue for the company that owns the wind farm. Due to the importance of the AEP, it is
desirable to predict and calculate this number with a high accuracy.
When placing turbines together in a wind farm, the overall AEP will most likely be less
than the combined AEP of every turbine had they been operating in isolation. This reduction
is a result of several effects. For many years, the deceleration of the wind downstream of a
turbine, named the wake effect, has been known to occur and has been researched extensively.
When placing several turbines behind each other in e.g. a wind farm, the wake effect will cause
downstream turbines to operate less efﬁciently than the front turbines. As this effect has been
uncovered in more detail, and research has led to smaller uncertainties in the calculations of
AEP, another effect has been discovered to reduce the wind farm efﬁciency: the blockage effect.
The power reduction from combining turbines in a wind farm is mostly accounted for by
the downstream wake effects. However, as the wind approaches the wind farm, it will begin its
deceleration even before it reaches the farm. This is the upstream effect known as the blockage
effect. Research has shown that placing turbines closely together will result in a combined
deceleration of the wind upstream of the farm. Although single turbine blockage effect has
been known to occur for many years, the combined effect from each turbine has often been
assumed negligible due to an assumption that the inter-turbine distances were sufﬁciently large
to avoid the combined blockage effect. In 2019, the Danish energy company Ørsted announced
(Ørsted, 2019) that the often used assumption accounted for errors in AEP calculations, and that
the blockage effect should be included in calculations for higher accuracy.
Some industry models have now included the blockage effect. However, these models are
often simpliﬁed to a great extent, neglecting many potentially important parameters. One of
these is the varying wind farm inﬂow. Throughout the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL),
the free stream wind speed and direction as well as the turbulent quantities vary with the height
from the ground. These variations as well as the height of the ABL itself may cause considerable
changes to the blockage effect.

1.2
Research Questions
The purpose of this project is to investigate the inﬂuence of atmospheric conditions upon
the blockage effect. This is done through a numerical study of the blockage effect for a single
turbine and for a small wind farm with different atmospheric conditions. Below, the research
questions that are answered in the report are listed. The concepts of blockage effect, ABL
height, and veer, mentioned in the research questions, are elaborated in the remainder of this
chapter. The research questions are as follows:
1. What is the inﬂuence of the atmospheric boundary layer height on the blockage effect for
a single wind turbine?
2. What is the inﬂuence of wind veer on the blockage effect for one wind turbine?
3. What is the inﬂuence of the atmospheric boundary layer height on the blockage effect for
a wind farm?
4. How do the results from question 3 compare with results from LES investigations with a
similar setup?
The research questions are answered in Chapters 4 through 7 in the order listed above.
1.3
Atmospheric Boundary Layer
The ABL covers the surface of the Earth as sketched in Figure 1.1. The height of the ABL
is variable and depends on several parameters, e.g. the temperature differences between the
surface (ground or sea) and the air above. The height varies between less than a hundred meters
to more than two kilometers (Stull, 1988) and a large variation can occur during just a single
day. Within the ABL, the wind speed varies, starting with a wind speed of zero at the ground
and approaching the geostrophic wind speed at the top of the ABL.
The ABL can be either stable, neutral, or unstable. Veers (2020) explains that unstable
conditions are often present during the day while stable conditions are often present during the
night. The stratiﬁcation (i.e. the stability) is dependent on the temperature differences between
the land or sea and the air. During unstable conditions, thermal energy is, to a large extent,
==PAGE:17==
Chapter 1. Introduction
3
converted into turbulent kinetic energy, thus enhancing the velocity ﬂuctuations of the wind. In
comparison, the vertical velocity components in a stable ABL are nearly zero due to the much
smaller velocity ﬂuctuations.
Above the ABL, a capping inversion may be present. The capping inversion is an area
of inverted temperature variation which constrains vertical momentum ﬂuxes. It can have a
variable depth and strength where the latter is deﬁned by the gradient of temperature changes
within the capping inversion. For a high strength, the capping inversion can be considered the
”lid” of the ABL as vertical velocity components are nearly zero close to the capping inversion.
The bottom part of the ABL — approximately the ﬁrst 10 % — is called the Atmospheric
Surface Layer (ASL). Within this region, an analytical model can be used to estimate the wind
speed variation. This model is described in Section 2.1.2. If a wind turbine or farm is within
the ASL, the ABL height has a negligible inﬂuence on the performance of the turbine(s). If,
instead, the turbine reaches above the ASL and well into the rest of the ABL, the wind proﬁles
within and height of that speciﬁc ABL may have a non-negligible inﬂuence on the performance.
At least three parameters within the ABL may have an inﬂuence on the performance: ABL
height, wind shear, and wind veer, the former being a factor in the two latter parameters as
well. A direct inﬂuence from the ABL height is the ”tunnel effect”. For a low ABL with a
strong capping inversion, vertical displacements of the wind may be nearly impossible close to
the wind turbine rotor. The effect may directly slow down the approaching wind upstream of a
turbine or farm as air is restricted from going above the turbine(s).
Vertical wind shear describes the changes of the wind speed over short vertical distances
and is thus equivalent to the vertical velocity gradients. As is e.g. shown by Pedersen (2013),
the shear of velocity proﬁles throughout the ABL may vary signiﬁcantly for different ABL
conditions. The effect of shear on the power of a wind turbine is investigated by Clifton and
Wagner (2014) who conclude that power curve measurements are affected by the level of shear
across the turbine rotor.
Throughout the ABL, the wind direction may vary as well. As the Earth rotates, it creates
the Coriolis force. As e.g. Abkar and Porté-Agel (2016) explain, the Coriolis force may cause
a rotation of the wind so that the wind direction varies with height from the ground. This
phenomenon is known as wind veer. Again, turbines within the ASL may not notice the changes
in wind direction while turbines above the ASL may experience a larger veer across the rotor
area, leading to a potentially non-negligible inﬂuence on the blockage effect.
1.4
Blockage Effect
For the scope of this report, the blockage effect can be divided into three main categories:
single turbine blockage effect, global blockage effect, and lateral blockage effect. In this report,
the former two will be investigated in detail while the latter will be brieﬂy touched upon.
1.4.1
Single Turbine Blockage Effect
The single turbine blockage effect is the blockage effect observed for a single turbine operat-
ing in isolation without the inﬂuence of any neighboring turbines. As the wind approaches the
==PAGE:18==
Chapter 1. Introduction
4
turbine, it will detect the presence of the turbine due to the thrust force that the turbine exerts on
the incoming wind. Acted upon by the force from the turbine, the wind will begin decelerating.
This deceleration, called the induction, has been known for many years to occur and can be
calculated from e.g. one-dimensional momentum theory applied to actuator disks as presented
by Sørensen (2011). Theoretically, the slowdown should protrude inﬁnitely far upstream. For
practical purposes, however, it is measurable up to a certain distance.
The power loss associated with the single turbine blockage effect arises due to the deﬁnition
of the power curve. A wind turbine is manufactured to have a speciﬁc power curve, describ-
ing the amount of power produced at different inﬂow wind speeds, U∞. U∞is deﬁned as the
free stream wind speed, i.e. the velocity at hub height of the turbine without the disturbance
caused by the wind turbine. In practice, measuring U∞is difﬁcult since the disturbance of the
turbine cannot be removed during power curve measurements. To accommodate this problem,
the international standard for power performance measurements (IEC, 2017) recommends per-
forming U∞measurements 2.5 rotor diameters (D) upstream of the turbine while logging the
power production. Accordingly, the industry usually performs these measurements two to three
D upstream as mentioned by e.g. Medici et al. (2011) and Segalini and Dahlberg (2020).
In choosing this distance, an inherent error due to the induction 2.5D upstream is accepted.
When the turbine is later put in operation, it will produce less power than expected at a given,
true U∞. To illustrate this, the power curve of the DTU 10 MW wind turbine (Bak et al., 2012)
is shown as the red line in Figure 1.2. Assuming that this power curve was obtained with the
free stream wind speed measured at 2.5D, the power curve with respect to the actual free stream
wind speed would be as shown by the cyan line if the induction at 2.5D was an exaggerated,
constant 10 % (about an order of magnitude larger than the usual induction and here independent
of wind speed; used here for illustrative purposes).
Based on vortex sheet theory which will be explained in Section 2.3.1, the velocity deﬁcit (or
induction) at 2.5D upstream should be approximately 0.55 % for a turbine operating at a thrust
coefﬁcient of 0.814. Consequently, the velocity at 2.5D upstream should be U∞−0.55/100 U∞.
Medici et al. (2011) investigated the accuracy of the vortex sheet theory through wind tunnel
experiments and found that the actual induction was greater than that calculated by the theory
with 2 to 5 % induction at 2D upstream. However, numerical results obtained for the same
research showed an induction of around 1 %.
Since the work by Medici et al. (2011), several other articles have investigated the single tur-
bine blockage effect. Through Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations, Troldborg
and Meyer Forsting (2017) arrived at an analytical solution which they found to be more accu-
rate than the vortex sheet theory. Their analytical model is explained in Section 2.3.1. Again
using a thrust coefﬁcient of 0.814, the induction at 2.5D is 0.66 % calculated by their analyt-
ical model. Similarly, Segalini (2021) has developed an analytical approach to estimating the
blockage effect, resulting in an estimated induction of 0.57 %.
A similarity between the analytical models developed by Troldborg and Meyer Forsting
(2017) and Segalini (2021) is that the models are based on CFD results with uniform inﬂows.
Thus, the three ABL effects described in Section 1.3 (ABL height, shear, and veer) are ne-
glected. Furthermore, both sets of authors performed the CFD simulations using Reynolds
Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) which time averages the ﬂow (cf. Section 2.1). In contrast
to this, Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) have performed Large Eddy Simulations (LES, cf. Sec-
tion 2.1.4) with a realistic ABL inﬂow. Including a ﬁnite ABL height via a capping inversion,

Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) concluded that the blockage effect can be larger than estimated by
Medici et al. (2011), Troldborg and Meyer Forsting (2017), and Segalini (2021).
Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) investigated two different scenarios with a strong and weak
capping inversion, respectively. The ﬁrst scenario represented a strongly, stably stratiﬁed atmo-
sphere, and the second a weakly, stably stratiﬁed atmosphere. With a thrust coefﬁcient of 0.77,
the two cases resulted in inductions at 2.5D upstream of 1.18 % and 0.79 %, respectively for
the strong and weak inversions. The result for the weak capping inversion is of the same order
as the previously presented results whereas the strong capping inversion causes the blockage
effect to increase. The results by Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) thus indicate that the level of
stratiﬁcation is important for the blockage effect, and that the neglection of this in previous
studies as well as industry models result in large uncertainties.
Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) further conclude that the ﬂow for the strongly stratiﬁed ABL
is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by the presence of the wind turbine up to a distance of 5D upstream.
Similarly, Medici et al. (2011) conclude that the ﬂow is modiﬁed more than 3D upstream. These
results are important for the global blockage effect introduced in Section 1.4.2.
Another potentially interesting parameter in the ABL is the wind veer. As the wind changes
direction as the height from the ground is increased, the wind direction at the top of the rotor
plane will be skewed towards the right (in the Northern hemisphere) as seen from the perspective
of the streamwise wind while the wind at the bottom of the rotor plane will be skewed towards
the left. Effectively, this means that each streamline of the wind will encounter a differently
yawed rotor. E.g. the wind at the top of the rotor plane will encounter a rotor that is yawed
counterclockwise, introducing a local thrust force that is not aligned with the axial direction of
the rotor. Consequently, the axial thrust component is reduced as a non-zero, lateral component
is introduced. Due to the higher wind speeds of the top part of the rotor compared to those at
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 1. Introduction
6
the bottom, the resulting, effective thrust coefﬁcient of the turbine will have a non-zero, lateral
component in the direction dictated by the top half of the rotor.
As a result of the above, the wake behind the rotor is turned towards the right (in the Northern
hemisphere; seen from the perspective of the incoming wind) as explained by van der Laan and
Sørensen (2017). In the article by Abkar and Porté-Agel (2016), it is concluded that veer also
causes the wake to recover a shorter distance downstream of the turbine, leading to a potentially
smaller disturbance upon wind turbines placed further downstream. For the blockage effect, it
could be expected that the reduced axial component of the thrust force would cause a reduced
blockage effect since less force will be pushing against the incoming wind.
1.4.2
Global Blockage Effect
When turbines are placed together in a wind farm, there may be interaction between the tur-
bines. This interaction can be split into the three main categories: downstream effects (wakes),
upstream effects (blockage), and lateral effects (e.g. lateral blockage). If two turbines are placed
behind each other in the streamwise direction, the distance between them needs to be sufﬁciently
large in order for the single turbine blockage effect from the rear turbine not to interact with the
ﬂow at the front turbine. If, for instance, the distance is 3D while the single turbine blockage
effect extends more than 5D upstream, the rear turbine will affect the ﬂow approaching the
front turbine, lowering the efﬁciency of the front turbine. This upstream interference is known
as the global blockage effect. As e.g. Bleeg et al. (2018) and Nygaard et al. (2020) explain,
inter-turbine distances that were previously considered sufﬁciently large have been proven to
cause a global blockage effect in wind farms.
In practice, the global blockage effect decreases the incoming U∞, resulting in a lower power
production for at least the front row of the farm. If the wind speed 2.5D upstream of e.g. the
center, front turbine in a wind farm is measured and compared to the true U∞, an induction
would be observed which would include both the single turbine blockage effect from that turbine
as well as the global blockage effect. If the global blockage effect is not present, the observed
induction should equal that caused by the single turbine blockage effect.
Several researchers have, however, concluded that the induction observed in front of a wind
farm is greater than that from the single turbine blockage effect. Segalini and Dahlberg (2020)
have performed wind tunnel experiments with a ﬁxed front row while the rest of the wind farm
could be moved in the streamwise direction, thus increasing or decreasing the distance to the
front row. Depending on the number of turbines and distance from the front row to the rest of
the farm, their results indicated an induction from the global blockage effect (with the single
turbine blockage effect automatically subtracted) at the front row between 0.9 and 3.2 %. Larger
blockage results were observed for smaller distances. It should be mentioned here that the thrust
coefﬁcient of the turbines used in the experiments was only 0.6.
Bleeg et al. (2018) investigated the blockage effect through observations of several real wind
farms and RANS simulations. They e.g. concluded that 2D upstream of a front row turbine in
the farm, the induction is 3.4 % while it, for the same front row turbine operating in isolation,
is 1.2 %. Also Schneemann et al. (2021) has conducted blockage effect research in actual
wind farms and could conclude that the induction (measured at the top of the turbine transition
piece) was in the order of 4 %, although with a ±2 pp uncertainty (pp being percentage points).
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 1. Introduction
7
This conclusion was made for a stably stratiﬁed atmosphere and a high thrust. Conversely, no
blockage was found during unstable conditions.
In the past few years, the wind energy industry has become increasingly more aware of the
errors from neglecting the blockage effect in AEP calculations. The Danish energy company
Ørsted announced that they had realized the error (Ørsted, 2019) and have since developed a
blockage model (Nygaard et al., 2020) to be incorporated into the wake model when computing
the power production of a wind farm. In the article by Nygaard et al. (2020), the model is
presented along with some possible uncertainties and shortcomings of the model. One of these
is the neglect of ABL effects.
Segalini and Dahlberg (2020) and Schneemann et al. (2021) back up the claim that the ABL
may be a signiﬁcant factor while both Centurelli et al. (2021) and Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021)
concluded in their articles that the ABL does indeed affect the blockage effect. The stratiﬁcation
of the ABL seems to be important for the blockage effect. Schneemann et al. (2021) could not
detect blockage in unstable stratiﬁcation, indicating either that the blockage effect is not present
during these conditions or that the velocity changes due to blockage effect is unobservable due
to the larger, natural velocity ﬂuctuations in the unstable atmosphere. The stratiﬁcation has
previously been shown to inﬂuence the velocity deﬁcits in the turbine wake. Abkar and Porté-
Agel (2015) showed that an unstable atmosphere has a faster wake recovery than a neutral
atmosphere which, in turn, has a faster wake recovery than a stably stratiﬁed atmosphere. A
faster wake recovery means that the velocity deﬁcit in the wake decreases faster. The limited
amount of turbulence in the stable ABL causes the entrainment of faster moving wind outside
of the wake to occur less, resulting in the longer wake recovery. Since the blockage effect and
the wake effect are strongly coupled, this result would indicate that the blockage effect should
increase for stably stratiﬁed ABL’s.
The research by both Centurelli et al. (2021) and Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) looked into the
effect from varying the ABL inﬂow. Both sets of authors agreed that the global blockage effect
can be greatly magniﬁed by certain atmospheric conditions. However, they disagreed on the
ABL inﬂuence on the single turbine blockage effect where Centurelli et al. (2021) claimed that
the analytical models capture the true blockage reasonably well while Sanchez Gomez et al.
(2021) concluded, as described earlier, that the ABL inﬂuences the single turbine blockage
effect. Centurelli et al. (2021) used LES to investigate the blockage effect with two different
inﬂows, having ABL heights of 300 m and 500 m, respectively. In a nine turbine farm with a
square formation, Centurelli et al. (2021) computed an induction 2.5D upstream of the wind
farm of approximately 3.5 % and 2.5 % for the two inﬂow cases, respectively. Thus, Centurelli
et al. (2021) concluded that a lower ABL height results in a larger blockage effect.
Centurelli et al. (2021) modeled a neutral ABL with a strong capping inversion. As in-
troduced in Section 1.4.1, Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) modeled two different, stable ABL
inﬂows with a strong and a weak capping inversion, respectively. For the two inﬂows, the in-
duction 2.5D upstream was computed to be 4.62 and 2.93 %, respectively. In the research by
Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021), the weaker capping inversion resulted in a taller ABL than the
stronger capping inversion. The order of magnitude of the blockage effect is comparable to that
found by Centurelli et al. (2021), Bleeg et al. (2018), Segalini and Dahlberg (2020) (for the
most severe cases), and Schneemann et al. (2021). However, it is evident that the exact induc-
tion varies for the different investigations and that there are several important parameters that
should be included in the study of blockage effect. Besides the differences due to wind farm

layout, the literature indicates that the ABL inﬂows are of a large importance.
Another important conclusion from the article by Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) is about the
displacement of the capping inversion. In the weak capping inversion case, the authors found
that the capping inversion, and thus the top of the ABL, was displaced upwards as the LES was
conducted. Thus, the blockage effect resulted in a ﬂexible boundary layer that altered the ﬂow
over time. It would not be possible to capture this phenomenon in a steady state simulation as
was conducted by many of the other authors mentioned here. The strong capping inversion was
not displaced but instead maintained a constant ABL height throughout the simulation.
The global blockage effect, in interaction with the wake effect, may in some cases result in
an increased power for some turbines while the power of other turbines are decreased. Due to
decreased wake effects within the farm, caused by the presence of the global blockage effect,
some turbines may be operating more efﬁciently than if the global blockage effect had not been
present. Thus, the global blockage effect may result in a redistribution of power across the
wind farm instead of simply a reduction of power. Furthermore, the wind farm may experience
local speed-ups of the wind due to a lateral redistribution of the wind. This effect may similarly
increase the power of some turbines within the farm.
1.4.3
Lateral Blockage Effect
The lateral speed-ups of the wind is in this report called the lateral blockage effect. Besides
decelerating the incoming ﬂow, the blockage effect also displaces the wind in the lateral di-
rection towards the wind farm edges. The incoming wind, when acted upon by the thrust of
the wind farm, will, to some degree, try to avoid the farm by going either around or over the
top. The wind that is deﬂected towards the turbines at the edges of the farm may increase the
incoming wind speed at those turbines. This may, in turn, increase the power production of the
edge turbines, meaning that they will produce more power than when operated in isolation.
Several of the authors already introduced discuss this aspect of the blockage effect, including
Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021), Bleeg et al. (2018), and Segalini and Dahlberg (2020). Further-
more, both Nishino and Draper (2015) and van der Laan et al. (2019) investigated the effect and
concluded large increases in power due to the lateral blockage effect. In the article by van der
Laan et al. (2019), a multi-rotor turbine with four rotors produces up to 2 % more power than
the combined power production by four equivalent rotors operating in isolation.
1.5
Numerical Modeling
Many of the results presented in Section 1.4 were obtained using numerical modeling. Of
these, some authors performed RANS simulations while others used LES. Numerical modeling
of turbulent ﬂows — or turbulence modeling — will be introduced in more detail in Section
2.1. The main differences between RANS and LES are that:
– RANS is time-averaged while LES resolves the time-dependency of the ﬂow,
– LES resolves the large scale turbulence and models the small scale whereas RANS models
all scales,
– and RANS is generally less computationally demanding than LES.

Choosing between RANS or LES is often a compromise between accuracy and computational

The result by Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) showing that the capping inversion is ﬂexible and

capture the ﬂow. In cases where the ABL height is ﬁxed, however, the blockage effect may be

In this research, RANS is chosen as the modeling method, thus prioritizing computational

2.1
Turbulence Modeling
Fluid ﬂows have been studied for many years, and yet turbulence remains a problem which
has not been deﬁnitively solved. The governing equations of ﬂuid ﬂows are established based
on widely known and accepted principles and are therefore considered to be correct. However,
when a ﬂow experiences turbulence, these governing equations obtain a complexity, resulting
in equations to which general, analytical solutions have not been found. While the search for
an analytical solution is continued, several ways of obtaining results based on the governing
equations have been introduced. Through assumptions and simpliﬁcations, analytical solutions
to very speciﬁc problems can be obtained. To all other problems, solutions must be obtained
through either turbulence modeling or direct simulations.
In this thesis, the overall turbulence modeling method used is Reynold’s Averaged Navier-
Stokes, commonly abbreviated RANS. The RANS equations are often used in steady-state, ﬂuid
mechanical applications as they greatly simplify the complexity of the governing equations.

2.1.2
Mixing-Length Model and Log Law
The mixing-length model is an example of an algebraic turbulence model. Via this model, it
is possible to obtain an algebraic expression of the velocity within the constant stress layer of a
boundary layer. In the lower part of a boundary layer (z ≪zi where z is the vertical coordinate
from the ground and zi is the boundary layer height), it can be assumed that the shear stress, τ,
is constant and equal to the wall shear stress, τ0. This region is called the constant stress layer
and can be further divided into the viscous sublayer, the buffer layer, and the logarithmic layer.
Within the latter, which is often called the log layer, the turbulent shear stress is dominating the
overall shear stress, and the viscous shear stress can be neglected.

2.1.4
Large Eddy Simulation (LES)
In this report, no LES will be performed. However, LES results will be used for comparison,
and, therefore, a short description is provided here. LES is, in many applications, more accurate
==PAGE:28==
Chapter 2. Theory
14
than the RANS models. LES directly simulates the Navier-Stokes equations and does not use
the time-averaging simpliﬁcation that the RANS models do. Consequently, LES can be used
for transient analyses whereas RANS should only be used for steady state applications.
LES is performed by discretizing the Navier-Stokes equations in space. The turbulence in a
turbulent ﬂow consists of eddies of varying sizes. The largest eddies (up to a certain size) con-
tain the most turbulent energy while the smaller eddies are responsible for turbulent dissipation.
The spatial discretization of the LES model deﬁnes how many of the eddies can be resolved
by the simulation. Eddies that are too small will not be resolved but are instead modeled by
a sub-grid scale model. Choosing the spatial discretization is a compromise between accuracy
and computational cost as ﬁner grids will generally increase the computational cost.
2.1.5
Comparison of Model Types
The three model types (algebraic, turbulence energy, and simulation models) can all be used
to resolve some turbulent ﬂows. However, their applicability, accuracy, and computational cost
vary. In general, it can be said that the accuracy and applicability of LES are better than those
of the RANS models while the opposite is often true for the computational effort. As will be
explained in Chapter 3, different RANS models based on the k-ϵ model have been used for
the simulations conducted for this thesis. The log law velocity proﬁle is, for some reference
simulations, utilized as an inﬂow boundary condition while the turbulence throughout the wind
farm is modeled by the k-ϵ model. It is assessed that the blockage effect, for non-ﬂexible ABL
heights, mainly is a steady state phenomenon. Consequently, to reduce the computational time
substantially, RANS is preferred above LES for this research.
For the remainder of the report, the distinction between actual, mean, and ﬂuctuating quan-
tities will be ignored as all quantities will be given as the mean. Thus, e.g. U will describe the
mean velocity in the x-direction: U = u1.

Computational Setup
The tool used for obtaining the results in this report is DTU Wind Energy’s PyWakeEllipSys
(DTU Wind Energy, 2019). PyWakeEllipSys uses RANS to compute the ﬂow in a wind farm.
The model is based upon the open source AEP calculator PyWake and the general purpose ﬂow
solver EllipSys3D, the latter originally developed by Michelsen (1992) and Sørensen (1995).
This chapter describes how PyWakeEllipSys is utilized for this thesis. All PyWakeEllipSys
parameters that are discussed in the chapter are listed in Appendix A where Table A.1 summa-
rizes the chosen value for each parameter. For a more general description of PyWakeEllipSys,
the documentation (DTU Wind Energy, 2019) is recommended.
3.1
Wind Turbine
The wind turbine chosen for the simulations is the DTU 10 MW reference turbine, designed
by Bak et al. (2012). The turbine has a rotor diameter of 178.3 m, a hub height of 119.0 m, and
a rated power of 10 MW. The power curve for the turbine is shown in Figure 3.1.
PyWakeEllipSys models the wind farm ﬂow with the turbines represented by actuator disks.
An actuator disk (AD) is a permeable disk that allows the air to pass through while the AD
exerts surface forces upon the air. The surface forces imitate the actual forces exerted by a real
wind turbine upon the air. The use of actuator disks is justiﬁed by Troldborg and Meyer Forsting
(2017) who have found that the blockage effect computed by their RANS model is independent
of the geometry of the rotor at more than one rotor radius upstream of the rotor.
The AD in PyWakeEllipSys has 22 different force methods that can be utilized to imitate
the turbine forces. For this thesis, the force methods denoted ”2110” and ”2111” in the doc-
umentation (DTU Wind Energy, 2019) are used for a single turbine and interacting turbines,
respectively. The force type (set by the ﬁrst digit) is an analytical force distribution across the
AD, based on a Joukowsky rotor. The analytical distribution is described by Sørensen et al.
(2020) while van der Laan et al. (2020a) introduce some minor corrections. The force distribu-
tion ensures that the sheared inﬂow of the ABL can be properly adopted by the AD.
The second and third digits of the force method respectively mean that tangential forces and
inﬂow effects are included in the force distribution. The latter of these include the non-axi-
symmetric shear and veer from the inﬂow. The last digit of the force method indicates whether
force control is used. For a single turbine, it is not. For a wind farm with interacting turbines,
force calibration simulations are performed in order to include a force control that enables
precise calculations without knowing a free stream wind speed for each individual turbine.
The AD is divided into 64 cells along the radial direction and 64 cells along the azimuthal
direction, resulting in 4096 cells for each AD. These values are chosen based on the AD grid
study carried out by Baungaard (2019).

PyWakeEllipSys models turbulence based on the RANS equations introduced in Section 2.1.

The boundary conditions of the domain are as follows:

– Lateral planes: periodic
– Top and upstream planes: inlet

boundary condition, it is then implemented across the inlet planes.

3.3.1
Choice of Inﬂow Models
To summarize, keABLc models an idealized ABL including wind veer while keABLp ex-
cludes the wind veer. Adding fP improves the solution in the wake which further improves the
solution in the induction zone. kefP can be used as a reference for an inﬁnitely tall ABL.
In this thesis, the two models keABLcfP and keABLpfP have been used. In order to prop-
erly isolate the effect of the ABL height, wind veer must be excluded so that the solution is not
affected by the magnitude of the veer. Consequently, keABLpfP is used for the investigations
of the ABL height inﬂuence in Chapters 4 and 6. By keeping z0 and f
Ro0 constant, lmax serves
as a proxy for the ABL height and can thus be varied to investigate the effect of the ABL height.
In practice, varying lmax is done by varying the turbulence intensity at hub height.
When investigating the effect of veer in Chapter 5, both keABLpfP and keABLcfP are
utilized and the results are compared. If the proper parameters are kept constant, the only dif-
ference between the two models is the wind veer, and thus the results from this comparison is the
effect of the veer. If a simulation is conducted with keABLcfP, a simulation with keABLpfP
must be conducted with the same z0 and lmax as in the ﬁrst simulation, but with varying G
and fpg which should be determined such that both the velocity and turbulence intensity at hub
height are equal to the ones used in the keABLcfP simulation.
van der Laan et al. (2021a) describe the keABLc and keABLp models and compare them.
For further understanding of the models, this article is recommended.
For the remainder of the report, the two models keABLpfP and keABLcfP will be denoted
ABLp and ABLc, respectively.

3.5
Convergence Criterion
The single turbine induction, β, is expected to be in the order of around 1 %. Changes in
the effect due to different ABL heights and veer are expected to be of one order of magnitude
lower. In order to properly resolve these differences, it must be ensured that the result of the
simulations is properly converged towards the true result. Stern et al. (2001) explain that a CFD
result will contain two different types of errors or uncertainties: modeling errors and numerical
errors. The physical models and boundary conditions chosen in Section 3.3 as well as the AD
force method chosen in Section 3.1 will result in a certain amount of error. One can only choose
the models in a way such that the error is minimized.
The numerical accuracy can be indeﬁnitely increased by also increasing the computational
time. For a steady simulation as is conducted in this research, two major factors contribute,
according to Stern et al. (2001), most to the numerical error: the number of iterations and the
domain grid. The latter is introduced in Section 3.6, and the error is investigated in the grid
convergence study of Section 3.6.1. The number of iterations is investigated here.
In PyWakeEllipSys, the number of iterations is controlled by the magnitude limit of the
residuals, here denoted RES. The residuals are, in short, the difference between the results of
the current iteration and the previous iteration, and RES is the maximum value that all residuals
must have before ending the simulation. Setting RES to a smaller value will result in a smaller
numerical error in the results and a larger number of iterations.
Using the model settings listed in Table A.1, a study is conducted to choose a proper value
for RES. The following section, Section 3.6, will go into further detail about how the model is
set up. The study is conducted for a single wind turbine, using the ABLp model (without wind
veer). The velocity at hub height is set at 8 m/s. As explained in Section 2.2, it is expected
that the largest blockage effects will be obtained for the lowest ABL heights. Consequently, the
largest numerical errors (in absolute values) are expected to occur for a low ABL height. This
error estimation study was conducted at the beginning of the project work, and the ﬁnal ABL
parameters presented in Section 3.4 were chosen later. Consequently, the parameters used here

3.8
Changes to the Model
During the project, PyWakeEllipSys underwent several updates. Two of these have an impact
on the results presented in this report. An update was made to the PyWakeEllipSys precursor
simulation. The results obtained before this update used the k-ϵ model (not including fP) for
the precursor simulation while including the fP alteration in the 3D model. The update included
the fP alteration in the precursor simulation, and results obtained after the update thus had a
more balanced relationship between the precursor simulation and the full 3D simulation.
Another update was made to further ensure balance between the precursor and 3D simu-
lations. At the ground, different vertical grid points for the precursor and the 3D simulation
created an imbalance that forced the inﬂow proﬁle to develop downstream even when no distur-
bances were included. This resulted in an approximately 0.5 % difference in U∞from the inlet
to the farm. The update aligned the vertical grid points such that this imbalance was removed.
Both of the updates were implemented simultaneously in this project. The single turbine
simulations 4–6 as well as all wind veer simulations were performed before the update. The
differences in the results due to these changes are, in most cases, negligible but can e.g. be
observed in the results presented in Section 4.1.3.

The main purpose of this single turbine study is to gain an understanding of the bias when
performing power curve measurements. These measurements are often performed with a free
stream velocity measured at 2.5D upstream of the rotor. This generates at least two potential
problems: a power curve bias due to the existence of blockage effect and a neglect of varying
ABL proﬁles. The latter is often mentioned in relation to a power curve measurement such that
the power curve is deﬁned for speciﬁc conditions, including e.g. shear and turbulence. However,
the effect of the ABL height on the power curve measurement is not included. Neglecting the
potential difference in ABL height between the power curve measurements and the actual use
of the turbine will, based on this study, increase the bias in the power curve measurement. If,
for instance, the power curve measurement has been conducted at a time with a tall ABL and
the turbine is being used in a situation with a low ABL, the turbine will be producing less than
the expected power output. Furthermore, the existence of the turbine generates an indisputable
velocity deﬁcit at 2.5 rotor diameters upstream of the rotor, and not taking this into account
when performing AEP predictions will result in overpredictions.
4.1.1
Estimated Power Deﬁcit
For companies in the wind energy industry, the power production is often the main result
of interest. For the single turbine, no direct results of the power production at different ABL
inﬂows are obtained from the simulations. However, estimates can be made based on the veloc-
ity results. Power production generally scales with the cube of the wind speed, and thus a 1 %
decrease of the velocity would result in approximately 3 % decrease in the power. In Chapter 1,
Figure 1.2 showed the power curve error from using a biased free stream velocity with an exag-
gerated induction of 10 %. Using the logic of this ﬁgure, a power deﬁcit, P, can be deﬁned as an
estimation of the power loss when comparing the expected, biased power production based on
a false free stream wind speed (measured at 2.5D upstream) with the actual power production
at that speciﬁc free stream wind speed. Quantitatively, this power ”loss” is deﬁned by:
P = P (U∞) −P (U2.5D)
P (U∞)
(4.1)
where P is the power given by the power curve, U∞is the actual free stream velocity, and U2.5D
is the velocity measured at 2.5 rotor diameters upstream, including the blockage effect. The
power based on the latter is the power that is used to generate the power curve while the power
based on the former is the expected power production with the bias. To continue the methods
already used in this report, the velocities, U∞and U2.5D, are the AD averaged velocities.
The result from performing the operation in Equation (4.1) for every point in Figure 4.2
is shown in Figure 4.3. With this deﬁnition of the power loss, the scaling is approximately
P ≈3.06 β for all points.
In Figure 4.3, a power loss of e.g. P = 3% would mean that the power curve bias causes the
actual power production to be 3 % less than the expected power production, estimated by the
power curve and the actual free stream wind speed. Besides showing a signiﬁcant power bias
due to the blockage effect, Figure 4.3 also shows that the ABL height has a large impact on the
magnitude of the power bias — as a direct result of the impact on the velocity deﬁcit. It should
be noted that the simulations all have an actual free stream wind speed of 8 m/s.

for the with-veer case is in line with the results by Abkar and Porté-Agel (2016).

is reduced when veer is included. This is clear from the induction zones shown in Figure 5.2.

veer, the adapted surface Rossby number is almost constant with f

6.1.1
Estimated Power Loss of Center Front Turbine Due to Global Blockage
In Chapter 4, a power deﬁcit formula was introduced for the single turbine. For the wind
farm, a different approach to estimating the power loss can be used. The simulation results
include a value for the electrical power produced by each turbine. For the single turbine simu-
lations, the power results were exactly that given by the power curve of the turbine based on the
given U∞(with the inherent power curve bias from the initial power curve deﬁnition). Thus, for
those simulations, the power produced was, in all cases, 3.5063 MW. If global blockage effect
did not exist, the front row turbines in the wind farm simulations would each produce the same
amount of power as they would when operating in isolation, i.e. 3.5063 MW. Because of the
global blockage effect, however, this is not the case. Comparing the power production of the
center front turbine with that given by the power curve thus gives an estimation of the power
loss due to blockage effect. It should be noted that where the power ”loss” computed in the
single turbine case was more of an error than an actual loss, the power loss computed for the
wind farm case is an actual power loss due to global blockage effect.

Comparison with LES
This chapter aims to answer the fourth research question:
4. How do the results from question 3 compare with results from LES investigations with a
similar setup?
The answer to this research question is kept relatively brief. Accurate comparisons of RANS
and LES results can often be difﬁcult and very time consuming, and for this project, research
questions 1 and 3 have been prioritized.
The LES results used in the comparison are from the Total Control project (Andersen and
Troldborg, 2020d). The project investigates the ﬂow in a wind farm consisting of 32 wind
turbines. The results used here are for turbines represented by actuator lines. The results for
a roughness height of 2 · 10−3 m are considered in the comparison. These results are publicly
available on the project website through:
– Precursor results: DOI: 10.11583/DTU.13353161 (Andersen and Troldborg, 2020a)
– Results with 0 rotation of the wind park: DOI: 10.11583/DTU.13122224 (Andersen and
Troldborg, 2020b)
– Results with 90◦rotation of the wind park: DOI: 10.11583/DTU.13090113 (Andersen
and Troldborg, 2020c)

Conclusion of Comparison
The RANS models compute a higher blockage effect for the wind farm than the LES. As-
suming that the modeled ABL’s are comparable, and the LES results can be fully trusted, the
RANS model thus compute too high blockage effects. The LES results, however, contain large
variances making it difﬁcult to compare with the steady-state RANS results. Furthermore, the
ABL inﬂows are not completely equivalent, adding additional uncertainties to the comparison.
It is expected that a neutral ABL, as simulated by the LES, causes a lower blockage effect than
a stably stratiﬁed ABL. However, the direct effect of the ABL height should still be present for
the neutral case when a capping inversion — and therefore a ﬁnite ABL height — is included.
The LES results indicate that the capping inversion may be displaced upwards as the ﬂow is
propagating through the farm, thus indicating that steady state solutions with a non-ﬂexible
ABL height may overpredict the blockage effect.

8.1
Limitations of the Simulation Model
The simulations conducted in this report are assessed to be of sufﬁcient ﬁdelity to draw
fairly accurate conclusions based on the results. Although the simulation models do include
some simpliﬁcations and inherent errors, the overall trends presented in the result sections are
assessed to be trustworthy. However, the limitations of the models and the inherent sources of
errors following those should be discussed.
Starting with the geometrical setup of the simulations, the actuator disks introduce a source
of error due to the neglect of the actual turbine geometry. The force distribution that is applied
to the actuator disks is based on a Joukowsky rotor. With this force distribution, a constant thrust
coefﬁcient, power coefﬁcient, and rotational speed is used with a constant inﬂow wind speed.
A real turbine would have different power outputs for varying ABL inﬂows, and this physical
effect is neglected. Most of the simulation results presented in the literature described in Section
1.4 are, however, also modeled with actuator disks, and the accuracy of the approach is accepted
as sufﬁcient. The other parts of the geometrical setup includes the simulation domain and grid.
These sources of errors have already been investigated in Section 3.6.1 and are assessed to be
sufﬁciently small to still deem the results trustworthy.
The physical models used for the simulations present less known, possibly larger sources of
error. First of all, modeling highly turbulent ﬂows with steady state solvers will undoubtedly
introduce an error in the results. The RANS k-ϵ model is used in many applications and has
been thoroughly tested. Using e.g. LES to model the wind farm ﬂow would, however, highly
increase the ﬁdelity of the results due to the incorporation of time as a dimension, and RANS
results must, in comparison with the LES results, be considered an estimation. With LES, it
would also be possible to include actual turbine blades instead of actuator disks and model the
time variant rotation of the turbine.
However, many turbulent ﬂows are, overall, invariant in time. Although turbulent ﬂuctua-
tions in these ﬂows occur, the mean quantities will not develop over time. This is generally not
true for a wind farm where the turbine blades constantly change positions but introducing the
actuator disk simpliﬁcation may cause the wind farm ﬂow to become nearly time invariant. As
was shown by Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) and indicated by the comparison with LES results
in Chapter 7, the ABL may change over time due to the wind farm ﬂow. If this is the case,
the ﬂow is, again, time variant. Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021), however, showed that this time
variance is not present for strongly stable ABL’s. Consequently, inﬂow cases where the capping
inversion is naturally non-ﬂexible, may be nearly independent of the temporal dimension.
To summarize, the steady state solution may be sufﬁciently accurate in the situations inves-
tigated in this report. The comparison with LES results in Chapter 7 did show that a larger
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 8. Discussion
64
blockage effect was predicted by the RANS models than by the LES. However, these LES re-
sults were for a neutral ABL while those computed by the RANS models were for stable ABL’s.
In future work, it would be a natural step to perform comparisons with either real wind farm
ﬂows or LES results with a better comparability between inﬂows.
The kefP model used in some of the simulations is an adapted version of the k-ϵ model
where the fP multiplication factor in the eddy viscosity increases the accuracy of the results in
the turbine wake and, therefore, the induction zone. This adaptation has been tested thoroughly
for the standard k-ϵ model. However, for the ABL models, keABLcfP and keABLpfP, the
fP adaptation has not been thoroughly tested, and more validation is required. This is another
uncertainty with the physical models utilized for this report.
Yet another limitation of the physical models is the exclusion of the temperature equation in
the computations. The effects of the temperature equation are imitated through turbulent length
scales, and, accepting that the steady state solution provides reasonable results, it is assessed
that this simpliﬁcation is not a large source of error. However, the exclusion of temperature
does introduce some difﬁculties since the model cannot include an actual capping inversion and
therefore cannot model different capping inversion strengths. As was shown by Sanchez Gomez
et al. (2021), the strength of the capping inversion is very important in determining the mag-
nitude of the single turbine and global blockage effects in stably stratiﬁed atmospheres. As a
consequence, the results of this report are considered most accurate when compared to cases
with a strong capping inversion.
The idealized ABL that is generated by the ABLp and ABLc models is barotropic and com-
puted for a homogeneous, ﬂat terrain. The barotropic conditions are connected to the neglect
of the temperature equation and is therefore already discussed. The homogeneous, ﬂat terrain
is another potential source of error. The roughness height of the sea is not completely uniform,
and there will, thus, be non-uniformity of the ABL inﬂow in the streamwise direction.
Finally, the ABLp model represents a source of error as the U and V momentum equations
are decoupled, and the Coriolis force is neglected. For the single turbine blockage effect, it was
shown in Chapter 5 that neglecting wind veer resulted in up to 11 % error in the estimation of
β. Due to the limited number of simulations conducted for this research, this number should
not be considered the maximum error.

8.2.2
Single Turbine and Global Blockage Effect
All results presented for the single turbine blockage effect in this report had β values between
approximately 1.1 and 1.5 %, depending on the speciﬁc ABL inﬂow. The same ABL inﬂow
cases resulted in β values between approximately 1.7 and 2.7 % for the wind farm setup with
nine turbines and an inter-turbine distance of 5D. The ratio between β for the single turbine
and β for the wind farm case was approximately constant for all ABL inﬂow cases, when the
wind farm setup and roughness height was kept constant.
Similarly to this result, Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) reported, based on LES, that the block-
age effect was increased substantially from the one turbine simulations to the farm simulations.
In their results, the increase was from 1.18 % (0.79 %) for the single turbine to 4.62 % (2.93 %)
for the wind farm in the strongly (weakly) stable ABL. Comparing the result for the single tur-
bine blockage effect with the strong capping inversion, Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) obtained

results close to those found in this report, although in the lower end. This indicates that the
simulations conducted in this report are equivalent to ABL’s with a strong capping inversion.
This was expected as the capping inversion is here imitated by a rigid ABL height. The global
blockage effect obtained by Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) is much larger than that found in
this report. This is easily described by the much larger wind farm used in the simulations by
Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021).
The global blockage results in this report are also comparable to those presented by Cen-
turelli et al. (2021). With ABL heights of 300 and 500 m, they found inductions of 3.5 and
2.5 %, respectively. These results were obtained for a similar wind farm setup: nine turbines
in a rectangular formation with inter-turbine distances of 7D in the streamwise direction and
5D in the lateral direction. Due to the larger distance in the streamwise direction, the results in
this report indicate that the induction should be smaller in their case than what is obtained here.
This is, however, not the case. One explanation could be that the turbines in the Centurelli et al.
article have a higher thrust coefﬁcient of 0.86 which would increase the induction. The single
turbine blockage results by Centurelli et al. (2021) do not match the conclusions in this report
as they conclude that their two different ABL inﬂows do not affect the single turbine blockage,
and that analytical results capture the magnitude reasonably well.
Compared to the Sanchez Gomez et al. results, the setup by Centurelli et al. corresponds
most closely to the strongly stable ABL. Although the ABL in the Centurelli et al. (2021) case
is neutrally stratiﬁed, the capping inversion is deﬁned to be strong such that the ﬂow above the
ABL is decoupled from that below.
Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) further observed that the induction zone extended up to 5D
upstream for the single turbine case and 15D for the wind farm case. The results presented in
this report indicate similar tendencies, although without having a clear deﬁnition of the extend
of the induction zone.
8.2.3
Lateral Blockage Effect
The edge turbines were shown to experience a smaller global blockage effect than the center
turbine. However, they did still experience a velocity and power reduction. This result is in
line with that presented by Sanchez Gomez et al. (2021) where the edge turbines operated
more efﬁciently than the center turbine for the strong capping inversion while the opposite was
observed for the weak capping inversion. In the results presented by Sanchez Gomez et al.
(2021), the edge turbines produced 4 % more power than the edge turbine while the largest
difference observed for ABL Case 2a in the main wind farm setup here was 0.35 %. The large
difference may be explained by the large difference in wind farm setups.
The above result does, however, not indicate that lateral blockage effect is present. The edge
turbines produced, in all ABL cases and wind farm setups, less power than they would had they
been operating in isolation. To conﬁrm that lateral blockage effect was present, the turbines
would have had to produce more than the single turbine power. Similarly to the conclusion by
Segalini and Dahlberg (2020) about the edge turbines, it can be concluded that the edge turbines
are less affected by the global blockage effect. As Segalini and Dahlberg (2020) also explain,
the wind can move faster on e.g. the left side of the left edge turbine since there are no turbines
present there, resulting in a smaller blockage effect for the edge turbines. Furthermore, the

induction zones of downstream turbines on the other side of the farm will have less inﬂuence
of the ﬂow at the edge turbines, again resulting in a smaller blockage effect. It should be noted
that since the farm only has three front row turbines in this report, lateral blockage effect is
unlikely to occur to a noticeable degree as only the center turbine would be causing the effect.
If simulations with a larger farm were performed, the lateral blockage effect may be present.
8.3
Wind Shear
As introduced in Section 1.3, the ABL contains (at least) three parameters of interest in the
investigation of blockage effect: ABL height, wind veer, and wind shear. The former two have
been investigated in this report whereas the latter has not been directly investigated. Shear has,
however, still been a parameter in the simulations since the wind speed has changed across the
ABL. For the simulations without wind veer, both the ABL height and wind shear have been
dictating the results. A quick assessment of the inﬂuence of the wind shear can be made based
on the log law results with different roughness heights. As these simulations correspond to
inﬁnitely tall ABL’s, the effect of the ABL height is removed.
As can be seen on e.g. Figures B.3 and B.4, the shear across the rotor area is larger for the
case with a larger roughness height. Figures 4.2 and 6.2 show the β values calculated for each
of these roughness heights for the single turbine and main wind farm setup, respectively. For
the roughness heights 2 · 10−4 m and 3 · 10−2 m, the single turbine computes β values of 1.074
and 1.097 %, respectively, and the main wind farm computes values of 1.651 and 1.761 %,
respectively. This corresponds to relative increases of 2.1 and 6.6 % for the single turbine and
wind farm, respectively. Comparing these results to those of varying the ABL height indicates
that the ABL height is the most signiﬁcant factor. This investigation should be conducted in
more detail, however, in order to deﬁnitively conclude anything.
8.4
Recommendations Based on Results
This report has shown that the ABL height is an important factor in assessing the magnitude
of the blockage effect. Errors when comparing with analytical models are substantial, and even
using a log law inﬂow may result in large errors. In the wind energy industry, some companies
have started to perform AEP calculations with a blockage model included. However, the ABL
height is often neglected in these models. For stable and neutral ABL’s, the height of the ABL
has proven to be a parameter on which the blockage effect is dependent, and including this
parameter in AEP models would increase the ﬁdelity.
A speciﬁc recommendation to include the ABL height in the models cannot be made based
only on this study, however. If the inclusion of the ABL height is an expensive procedure,
a more thorough investigation should be conducted to compare with a company’s blockage
model, including actual frequencies of different types of ABL inﬂows, and assess the error
from excluding the ABL inﬂow. Generally, a wind farm produces more power during unstable
conditions for which the ABL height inﬂuence has not been investigated in this report. Con-
sequently, when yearly wind speed, wind direction, and stratiﬁcation frequencies are included,
the impact on the AEP may be less than what is otherwise concluded in this report.

8.5
Other Interesting Parameters
Wind farm ﬂows are complex and contain many parameters. In order to limit the research
conducted in this report, many of these parameters were kept constant although they may have
a large impact on the blockage effect results. Furthermore, some of the parameters that were
varied could be varied more and thus potentially produce different results.
Wind Speed and Thrust Coefﬁcient
The free stream wind speed at hub height was 8 m/s throughout the simulations described in
the report. A constant free stream wind speed corresponds to a constant thrust coefﬁcient for the
turbine, and thus only one thrust coefﬁcient has been explored. Similarly, most of the literature
studied in Section 1.4 used constant wind speeds or thrust coefﬁcients. As the analytical models
presented in Section 2.3 showed, the induction is highly dependent on the thrust coefﬁcient. In
fact, the three analytical models used only the thrust coefﬁcient as input, potentially making it
the most important parameter when assessing the single turbine blockage effect.
It must therefore be expected that using different inﬂow wind speeds would alter the observed
blockage effect. However, it is assessed that the general trends would still be the same as
obtained in this report — as long as the wind speed stays below rated wind speed. If a single
turbine is operating at above rated wind speed, a few percent reduction in wind speeds should
not affect the power production since the turbine will automatically tilt its rotor blades to make
up for the difference and produce the rated power. In a wind farm, for the blockage effect to be
completely negligible, all turbines need to be operating above rated wind speed.
Wind Direction
For a single turbine, the wind direction has no inﬂuence. Oppositely, for the wind farm
setups, the wind direction will have an inﬂuence. This would be an interesting parameter to
investigate since wind farms are often operating at different inﬂow wind directions. If the wind
farm was instead rotated 45◦, the blockage effect is expected to be different. Whether the
induction would increase or decrease depends on the wind farm setup. In the square wind farm
setups presented here, the induction would most likely decrease. Rotating the wind farm would
increase the downstream, inter-turbine distance and decrease the lateral distance, resulting in a
lower global blockage effect.
Wind Turbine
Only one wind turbine has been investigated in this report: the DTU 10 MW turbine. Vary-
ing the turbine would result in another thrust coefﬁcient of which the effect has already been
discussed. Furthermore, the power and rotational speed curves as well as the hub height and
rotor diameter would be varied.

Chapter 9
Conclusions
This report described an investigation of blockage effect for a single turbine and for wind
farms under different atmospheric conditions. The main atmospheric parameter that was inves-
tigated was the height of the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL). Furthermore, a short investi-
gation of the inﬂuence of wind veer was conducted. All results in the report were obtained with
adapted RANS k-ϵ modeling with stable or neutral ABL’s. One main result for the blockage
effect was deﬁned with the symbol β: the induction 2.5 rotor diameters (D) upstream of a single
turbine or the center front turbine in a farm. Results were obtained for an inﬂow wind speed at
hub height of 8 m/s, corresponding to a thrust coefﬁcient of 0.814 for the DTU 10 MW turbine
that was used in all simulations.
ABL Height Inﬂuence for a Single Turbine
The results in this report indicated that blockage effect can cause inductions larger than pre-
dicted by e.g. the vortex sheet theory. A main conclusion was found: a lower ABL height results
in a larger blockage effect. For the different, stable ABL inﬂows considered in this report, the
single turbine blockage effect resulted in β values between 1.10 and 1.50 %, thus indicating
considerable differences between different ABL heights. Simulations with a log law were con-
ducted to imitate an inﬁnitely tall ABL, thus removing the inﬂuence of the ABL height. It
was observed that all results with different ABL inﬂows converged towards the blockage effect
computed with the log law inﬂow as the ABL height was increased. Assuming an inﬁnitely tall
ABL would, with the results presented in this report, result in an underprediction of the β value
of up to 27 %.
The blockage effect for a single turbine affects power curve measurements. These are often
conducted with a wind speed measurement at 2.5D upstream of the rotor, with an assumption
that this measurement is the free stream wind speed. Since the single turbine induction is
present at this distance upstream, the power curve becomes biased. The resulting difference in
power curve measurements was approximately 3 times β, resulting in power overpredictions of
between 3.3 and 4.6 %.
ABL Height Inﬂuence in a Wind Farm
For the wind farm investigation, six wind farm setups were studied: three setups with nine
turbines in a square formation and another three setups with three turbines in a streamwise
line behind each other. In both sets of setups, inter-turbine distances of 3, 5, and 10 D were
considered. The wind farm with nine turbines and a 5D spacing was used as the main setup. For
this case, investigating the same ABL inﬂows as for the single turbine, β values between 1.67
and 2.73 % were found, thus conﬁrming that global blockage effect enhances the induction
caused by the single turbine blockage effect. If, in this case, a log law inﬂow is used as a
simpliﬁcation, a relative underprediction of β up to 36 % will be obtained.
In the wind farm simulations, the power loss due to global blockage was calculated for the
==PAGE:84==
Chapter 9. Conclusions
70
center front turbine as the difference between the computed turbine power and the power of the
same turbine operating in isolation. For the main wind farm case, the power loss was found
to be between 1.5 and 3.6 % for different ABL inﬂows. The difference due to different ABL
heights was, therefore, substantial.
For the different wind farm setups, it was concluded that a larger number of turbines causes
a larger blockage effect while more distance between the turbines results in a lower blockage
effect. It was found that when keeping the roughness height and wind farm setup constant,
the ratio across all ABL cases between β for the wind farm and β for the single turbine did
not vary much. Consequently, after computing β for a single turbine for all (in this case) 14
ABL inﬂows, it would only be necessary to compute β for one ABL inﬂow for the six wind
farm setups. With these 20 simulation results, the remaining 78 β values could be estimated
with a fairly high accuracy. If this relation could be proven correct, it could greatly reduce the
computational effort of global blockage investigations.
It was investigated whether the inter-turbine distance or the ABL height had the largest in-
ﬂuence on the blockage effecet. It was found that for small inter-turbine distances, changing
that distance a little caused a large change in the blockage effect whereas changes in the ABL
height were not as inﬂuential in comparison. For larger inter-turbine distances, that relation was
shown to even out such that the importance of the ABL height became larger for increasing
inter-turbine distances. Further research with more wind farm setups is recommended such that
possible quantitative relations can be found.
Besides the single turbine and global blockage effects, a lateral blockage effect has also
been shown to occur in the literature. This lateral blockage effect is supposed to increase the
efﬁciency of the edge turbines of the front row in a wind farm. The lateral blockage effect was
not shown to be present in this report. The relatively small wind farm meant that the wind was
not sufﬁciently displaced in the lateral direction for this blockage effect to occur. However, the
wind turbines on the edges of the farm did experience a smaller velocity and power reduction,
indicating that the edge turbines were less affected by the global blockage effect.
In Chapter 7, a comparison of the RANS models used in the report and results obtained with
LES was conducted. This comparison indicated that the RANS models overpredict the blockage
effect compared to the LES. However, the exact inﬂow used in the LES could not be imitated,
and therefore, the comparison was not conclusive.
The Inﬂuence of Veer for a Single Turbine
A small investigation was carried out for the inﬂuence of wind veer on the blockage effect
for a single turbine. The results from this study are based on only a few simulations, and further
research should therefore be conducted before drawing ﬁnal conclusions. The investigation
concluded that veer rotates the induction zone clockwise around the turbine. Furthermore, the
study could conclude that veer reduces the magnitude of the induction, and thus wind speed
measurements conducted at 2.5D upstream would be less affected by blockage effect when
veer is present. It was found that a larger magnitude of veer causes a smaller induction.
The investigation indicated that when veer is present, the induction zone reaches a shorter
distance upstream of the turbine. Consequently, it is expected that veer would also reduce the
global blockage effect of a wind farm since upstream interference would not reach as far. The
wind farm simulations conducted for this report did not include veer, and those results must
therefore be considered slightly exaggerated.

As has been discussed in Chapter 8, many parameters have been left unexplored in the re-
search conducted for this report. Several items would therefore be interesting to research in
future work. For more information, cf. the discussion in Chapter 8.
– Unsteady solution
It would be interesting to generate unsteady results of similar ABL cases as considered in this
report to evaluate whether the steady solution properly captures the blockage effect. With the
unsteady solver, it would further be possible to investigate whether the ABL height is displaced
due to the forces of the wind farm. The comparison that has been conducted in Chapter 7 uses
ABL inﬂows that are too different to draw direct conclusion from.
– Comparison with wind farm data
Due to a lack of data for comparison, the results of this report are difﬁcult to verify. It would
therefore be interesting to capture real wind farm data with different ABL inﬂows to compare
with the results in this report.
– Different thrust coefﬁcient, wind speeds, and wind directions
As elaborately discussed in Section 8.5, the inﬂow wind speed and wind direction were kept
constant throughout the simulations in this report. A different wind speed would result in a
different turbine thrust coefﬁcient, and a different wind direction could effectively change the
layout of the wind farm as seen by the approaching wind.
– More wind farm setups
To properly investigate the effect of the ABL height in relation to the wind farm setup,
more wind farm setups should be studied. For instance, a larger farm would be able to show
the lateral blockage effect so that an investigation of the ABL inﬂuence on this aspect of the
blockage effect could be conducted.
– More thorough veer investigation
The investigation carried out in this report for the inﬂuence of wind veer on the blockage
effect only evaluated the results from a few simulations. These results showed an arguably
non-negligible inﬂuence. Further investigation on this subject is therefore recommended.
– Investigation of wind shear
As was discussed in Section 8.3, the wind shear has been an inherent parameter in the simu-
lations conducted for this report. It would be interesting to investigate the level of inﬂuence of
wind shear compared to that of the direct ABL height inﬂuence.
