Abstract
Waste to energy facilities is a modern day technology, that oﬀers an eco friendly mean to
dispose of waste products, when other solution proves in feasible. In 2015, 3.9 mill tons of
waste was incinerated in Denmark, which otherwise should have been disposed in other
less environmental friendly ways. The waste to energy facilities furthermore contribute
signiﬁcantly to the Danish energy production, whit 25 % of the total district heating
energy to danish consumers, and 5% of the electricity produces in Denmark.
One of these waste to energy facilities is situated in Roskilde municipality, an consist of
two separate oven lines, whit oven line six established in 2013, being considered as close
to state of the art, whit wet scrubbing and ﬂue gas condensation.
This thesis has focused on daily operation of oven line six, and the possibility to increasing
the eﬃciency of energy recovery from waste incineration, in combination whit identiﬁca-
tion of the mayor energy losses.
To identify the losses, past data for the years 2016 to 2018 has been analysed through a
combination of excel spreadsheets, and MATLAB calculations. From the analysis it was
established that the energy losses from ﬂue gas leaving the boiler at elevated tempera-
tures, contributed whit more then 76% of the total energy loss. To benchmark the results,
RAMSTEAM, a Ramboll develop excel tool was used to determine the reliability of the
results, as well as to investigate the possibility to further develop a tool for the operation
managers.
To identify which variables was suitable for future operation tool, a series of statistical
analysis was performed. The analyses focused on the correlations between the overall
eﬃciency, and several variables provided by Roskilde waste to energy facility, including
concentration, temperature, ﬂow, and pressure measurements.
The analysis did however not prove successful, since a deﬁnitive result was not estab-
lished. It was established from the analysis that the data was to scattered to extract any
linear correlations. The desired product, of a tool to provide guidance to the operators
was therefor not achieved.
Even though the analysis did not provided a deﬁnitive answer, the thesis has established
the ground work for a future model, as well as enlightened which methods not to use.

1
Introduction
Today, modern day waste incineration is an eﬃcient solution to solve waste manage-
ment issues in an ecofriendly way. When recycling is not possible, or proves infeasible,
waste incineration oﬀers a solution, which combining waste disposal, with heat and power
production. The Waste to Energy facility Aﬀald-Ressourcer-Genbrug-Overskud, in short
ARGO, in the municipality of Roskilde in Denmark, has been the case study for this thesis.
The thesis aims to identify key parameters during operation of oven line six, to increase
overall eﬃciency, in regards to energy recovery from incineration of waste material, and
thereby reduce potential energy losses. Waste incineration is a mature technology, with
one of the ﬁrst incinerators build in 1870, in the UK[1]. It was however only a facility to
dispose of waste, and no energy recovery was installed. In Denmark, the ﬁrst incineration
plant was established en Frederiksberg in 1903, which also supplied the municipality whit
district heating[2]. Today waste incineration facilities supply both heat and power to the
danish heat and power grid, with eﬃciency’s above 100%, with Amager bakke waste to
energy facility in Copenhagen, as state of the art with an eﬃciency of 107% [3].
1.1
Scope and nature of the thesis
Waste as a fuel source is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from ordinary fuels, e.g. coal, natural gas,
and oil, which has an almost constant heating value. The heating value of waste can vary
depending on the composition of materials, such as plastic, organic, or others, as well as
the moister content of the waste. This aﬀects the combustion and thereby the operation
which needs to constantly balance the grate speed, and primary air supply, which aﬀects
the overall eﬃciency.
This thesis will aim to identify the energy losses, and investigate indicators, to increase
the overall eﬃciency of the waste to energy facility ARGO, by evaluating large data sets,
from paste operation through the years 2016 to 2018, and give guidance to operators to
reduce energy losses.
Since waste as a fuel source does not behave like an ordinary fuel, and operation
parameters cannot be held constant, the hypothesis is and that the overall eﬃciency can
be optimized through analysis of the historical operation data from ARGO, by establishing
which key variables leads to losses in the energy recovery process.
1.2
Waste incineration in brief
Waste incineration can be dealt with in several ways, including rotary kiln, and ﬂuidized
bed incineration. However the focus in this thesis is the incineration facility ARGO at
the municipality of Roskilde, and therefor only grate ﬁred furnaces will be covered.
Waste is collected, usually by trucks and transferred to the incineration facility, where
it is delivered to the bunker. The bunker functions as a temporary storage and mixing
platform, before the waste is further transferred into the furnace. Since waste is a com-
position of a variety of materials, spanning from plastics, to metals and organics, the
mixing of waste material makes the composition of each grab load more homogeneous.
This ensures to a certain degree, a more homogeneous heating value of each grab load,
and thereby increase the operation stability of the furnace. From the bunker, the waste is
1
==PAGE:11==
transferred through the shaft leading into the furnace, also called the waste hopper. From
the waste hopper, the waste is shifted to the grate, where to waste is slowly moved across,
while drying, ignition, combustion, and ﬁnal burning subsequently takes place, and ide-
ally in the end of the grate, only ash and material which is unable to burn remains. The
remains is called bottom ash, and remaining metals can be recycled from this. Due to
variations in waste composition, the grate pusher speed can be adjusted to ensure optimal
combustion of the waste, and ensuring an adjustable fuel supply. From below the grate,
the primary air is supplied to the furnace to ensure a continuous high temperature, and a
complete burnout of the waste. The primary air is preheated before supplied to the grate,
either from internal energy transfer by steam from the boiler, or by energy recovery from
the ﬂue gas. To account for the variation in waste load and composition, the primary
air supply is highly adjustable in regards to ﬂow and position. This is therefor a key
parameter for an optimal operation of the incinerator.
To ensure a complete combustion, secondary air is supplied above the grate. The
secondary air supply can be, but is not always preheated, and if preheated, it is usually
preheated from energy recovery from the ﬂue gas.
From the burning of waste, high
temperature ﬂue gas is created, consisting of a mix of CO2, NOx, SOx, HCl, dioxins,
dust, and water vapor. The ﬂue gas is led through the furnace, where the heat from
the ﬂue gas is transferred ﬁrst through the sides of the ﬁrst passes of the furnace, where
pressurized water evaporates. Then the ﬂue gas is further led through the furnace passes,
where heat is transferred to the high pressure steam, which then is super heated. At the
last passes of the furnace, the ﬂue gas transfer the lower temperature heat to the feed
water to preheat it, before evaporation in the ﬁrst passes of the furnace.
The ﬂue gas as mention earlier consist of a mix of toxic and acidic substances, and must
be cleaned before emitted to the environment. To remove the NOx either a non-catalytic
reduction, whereNH3 is injected into the secondary combustion chamber, or a catalytic
reduction, where NH3 is injected into a Vanadium pent-oxide catalyst, in a dedicated
tower. This process has some energy loss, since some of the thermal energy is transferred
to the evaporation of a NH3.
Dust removal can be done in several ways, including bag ﬁlters, Electrostatic precipi-
tators, or cyclones. Depending of the acid neutralization process, either wet, dry or semi
dry, the dust removal can be be before, or after acid neutralization. This process also
has some energy loss, since some of the thermal energy is stored in the hot dust, removed
from the system.
To produce power, a steam turbine is used, where the steam is expanded from high
to low pressure. The turbine can have several bled valves, to extract steam for other
purposes, such has increased heat production, or preheating of feed water or air supply.
Further more, it is possible to bypass the turbine, to only produce heat.
After the production of power, the low pressure steam leaving the turbine is lead through
heat exchangers where the thermal energy is transferred to the district heating grid. If
the plant has a ﬂue gas condensations system installed, the district heat water can be
preheated from that, signiﬁcantly increasing the overall eﬃciency.
1.3
Energy losses in waste to energy facilities
Energy losses from waste to energy facilities can generally be divided in to three categories.
Energy loss from convection and radiation to the environment, energy loss from ash in form
2
==PAGE:12==
of thermal energy, or chemical energy if the waste has not been burn out, and energy loss
from ﬂue gas leaving the stack at an elevated temperature[4]. On average 190 kg bottom
ash per ton waste is produced. The bottom ash is still signiﬁcantly hot, and usually 2%
of the energy release during incineration of waste is left in the bottom ash[4]. Due to
the high temperatures, 2 % of the energy from incineration is lost through radiation and
convection to the environment, dependent on the insulation of the incinerator[4]. The
larges contributor to energy losses is the ﬂue gas leaving the boiler. At temperatures
higher then the reference temperature, usually 25 °C, warm ﬂue gas contains thermal
energy, that if cooled to reference temperature could have released additional energy. The
energy loss from ﬂue gas can be decreased signiﬁcantly, if the ﬂue gas is used to preheat
the primary air or district heating, or if ﬂue gas condensation is installed. Further more
a reduction in ﬂue gas ﬂow would lead to lower losses, which could be accomplished by
lower excess air ratio. Waldner et al. showed that optimal mixing of ﬂue gas and primary
air can reduce the ﬂue gas volume by 19 %[5].
1.4
ARGO waste incineration facility
At ARGO waste to energy facility, two independent oven lines is establish. This thesis
focuses on line six, which is the newest of the oven lines, consisting of modern day tech-
nology, including wet scrubbing with ﬂue gas condensation, and fully automated waste
feeding systems. Line six is designed for a nominal capacity, to process 25 ton/hour waste,
with a thermal load of 292.5 GJ/hour, equal to a lower heating value of 11.7 GJ/ton[6].
During daily operation, the facility is operated from the control room, where to op-
eration manager supervises the operation. Line six can operate fully automated during
normal conditions, which includes crane operations, waste feeding to the grate, primary
air volume and zone displacement of primary air. Operation set point for steam produc-
tion is given by the operation manager. Further more heat and power production can
be controlled by the operation manager, in case of low electricity prices, or restriction of
heat production from the danish company Varmelast.

the ﬂue gas condenser, which otherwise would have left the stack and condensed in the

2.4.5
Auto-correlation analysis
Auto correlation or series correlation is a measure of how much a variable is correlated
with it self in time. Auto correlation follows the same principles as cross correlation, but
instead of calculating the correlation between to variables, the auto correlation calculate
13
==PAGE:23==
the correlation between a variable, and the variable shifted in time. If a variable is highly
auto correlated, the variable at time i, can be predicted by the value of the variable at
time i-t. The correlation is calculated, using the same principles as equation (24), with the
A term being A = xj,i, indicating variable j, at time i, and the B term being B = xj,i−t,
where t is the displacement in time. Depending on the time between each measurement,
and the type of system, time series data tent to have some degree of auto correlation,
because of the inertia in a system. As an example, weather temperature has some auto
correlation from day to day, most likely due to the "mass" of the environment, and hence
only a certain temperature change can happen over night. If systems has a high degree
of auto correlation, it could indicate a pattern, not caused by the variable. The data set
should be examined to see if data that could explain the auto correlation is missing, e.g
if some measurement could be added to the system to explain the pattern.

Discussion
The following chapter will discuss the results established from the previous chapter as
well as the methods used to accomplish this. The energy loss account and the statistical
analysis will be discussed and future prospects will be addressed as well.
4.1
Energy loss
From the energy loss calculations, it was established that energy loss from ﬂue gas was
the dominating factor. On average across all operation conﬁgurations, the ﬂue gas loss
accounted for more then 76% of the total energy loss, and whit periods of up to 85% of
the total loss. The ﬂue gas loss was highly ﬂuctuating, indication a large variety in the
input parameters. The ﬂue gas loss however was a product of several parameters, some
ﬁxed e.g the the reference temperature, and others as average values which is measured
at the same time, although the processes is not instantaneous. The real ﬂue gas loss was
the thermal energy in the ﬂue gas, which could have been released, if cooled to the ref-
erence temperature, minus the energy released during ﬂue gas condensation. This energy
released was calculated on the basis of the amount of H2O removed prom the ﬂue gas.
The measurements for the H2O was again hourly average values, which might not capture
the actual H2O changes for a given control volume, since the intial mesurement of H2O
is in the boiler.
The loss from evaporation was small compared to the ﬂue gas loss, on average around
0.7%. This energy could however partly be regenerated during ﬂue gas condensation,
since the evaporated water would pass along the system whit the hot ﬂue gas, and when
cooled to the dew point, release it energy in form of latent heat. The regenerated energy
was then counted as a reduction in ﬂue gas loss, rather then calculating the actual amount
of energy lost from evaporation.
The loss from radiation is calculated based on a loss coeﬃcient from a standard which is
a function of the fuel input. This is an average loss, so some deviation could occur. From
the results it can be seen that the loss is in the range of 1% of the energy produced, which
is expected for a well insulated plant[4].
To evaluate how well the plant was operating compared to expected values, a comparison
whit RAMSTEAM was performed. It was seen that there was a high degree of deviation
between the results calculated by the author, compared to the results from RAMSTEAM.
This was further supported from the time series of the to results, where the results cal-
culated from RAMSTEAM in general was more steady, both regarding eﬃciencies, and
energy losses. The steadiness of the results from RAMSTEAM must have been a result
of the reduced number of variables, which for further analysis should be implemented.
A further analysis regarding decisive parameters should be performed, and then imple-
mented.
It was also observed that RAMSTEAM in the majority of the data points calculated a
lower energy loss then the loss calculated by the author. From the steadiness observed
from the time series, it is likely that variables which was dynamically used in the by
the author, should have been implemented. It was however not possible, do to lag of
computational power.

4.2
Statistical analysis
The statistical analysis has been carried out as a mean to identify variables whit signiﬁcant
impact on the over all eﬃciency of ARGO waste to energy facility line 6, in combination
whit the thermodynamic theory and the energy loss calculations. As mentioned previ-
ously ﬂue gas loss was the dominant factor regarding total energy loss, and hence it was
expected that variables associated whit ﬂue gas loss could be measured and correlated
whit the overall eﬃciency. During the early phases of the analysis, the data was roughly
evaluated, and early in that stage, it was clear that diﬃculties might occur, since almost
all variable, at least to some degree, oﬀered no visual correlation to either the overall
eﬃciency, the total energy loss, or each other. As ARGO line 6 has an automated control
system, a mayor concern has been that the variables was regulated in a way that kept the
desired set point steady, e.g the steam production, and then regulated the e.g primary air
volume ﬂow rate, and through put of steam, in a patterned that was so scattered that no
correlation could be observed or calculated. This concern was emphasized when the sim-
ple correlation was calculated, and no strong correlations has found across all operation
patterns in regards to overall eﬃciency. In general almost all variables showed very little
to no correlations, and of those which showed any correlation, many lacked statistical
signiﬁcance to to be accepted.
For the total energy loss some moderate to strong correlation could be found, but not
across all operation conﬁguration. It was expected that very strong correlations between
all variables associated whit ﬂue gas loss, which however only proved true for the ﬂue gas
volume ﬂow rate, and the concentration of H2O in the ﬂue gas leaving the stack. This led
to the hypothesis that the variables might be correlated in an other way. From further
analysis, it was seen that the overall eﬃciency was to some extent correlated whit the total
energy loss, hence a reduction in total energy loss could lead to higher eﬃciency. Since
the ﬂue gas condensation can reduce the energy loss from ﬂue gas, it was investigated
how the thermal eﬀect from ﬂue gas condensation, could aﬀect the overall eﬃciency.
The total energy loss was also scale able by the thermal input, since higher input, equals
more ﬂue gas, which then leads to greater loss. An analysis of the combined eﬀect was
investigated, and it was seen that the thermal input had less eﬀect on the overall eﬃciency
then expected. The thermal eﬀect from the ﬂue gas condensation however aﬀected the
overall eﬃciency greatly, indication that the thermal load should be lowered before the
thermal eﬀect from the ﬂue gas condensation if possible.
It was further discovered, that the concentration of O2 in the ﬂue gas at low ﬂue gas
ﬂow rates, was higher, then the concentration of O2 at high ﬂue gas ﬂow rates. Since the
ﬂue gas loss accounted for the majority of the energy loss, and the ﬂue gas volume ﬂow
rate was scale able with the steam production and waste trough put, it could indicates
that the O2 concentration during operation whit low loads, could be lowered by reducing
the primary air, and then reduce the ﬂue gas loss, by lowering the ﬂue gas ﬂow rate, which
then would increase the overall eﬃciency.
To further analyses the data sets for correlations, the number of variables was reduced.
This should have been accomplished by the lasso regression which however proved un-
fruitful. The lambda variable, determining the penalty, which also is used to calculate
the reduced number of variables was very low. Therefor the reduction did not reduce the
number of variables evenly across the four operation conﬁgurations signiﬁcantly, and in
45
==PAGE:55==
general the reduced number of variables was not as low as desired. From this it could be
interpret that either all the variables was important for the system, or neither one of then
was important, or equally less important. This leaves i great amount of uncertainty, which
in future work should be more proper addressed. To decrease the number of variables,
only variables deemed important for three out of four operations patterns, and whit a
measurable correlation coeﬃcient was used for the further analysis.
The identify how the variables behaved across time, both in reference to them self and to
the overall eﬃciency, the auto correlation, and cross correlation analysis was performed.
From the auto correlation analysis it was identiﬁed that the variables was highly cor-
related to them self, which oﬀered a problem of stationary. To calculate a correlation
between variables, there must be a measurable changes. Since the auto correlation for the
variables was so high, no changes might have happened. This could have been because
of the time scale of data, which all was hour average values. For future work, the time
scale for the data should be changed and tested, to identify if the pattern for the auto
correlation changes.
To further analyse the variables, a cross correlation analyses was performed. The scope
was to identify if the variables was related in time to the overall eﬃciency. Since the
process from waste burning to ﬂue gas leaving the stack is not instantaneous, the vari-
ables was likely to have some correlation shifted in time. The analysis however proved
otherwise, whit no signiﬁcant increase in power of correlation across time. This again
might have been the result of the time scale of the data, where if the the time scale was
lowered e.g to a ﬁve minute average, the cross correlation might have been stronger.
Since all variables disregarding of time had low power of correlation, it was interesting to
see, if it at least was possible to identify if some of the variables was of greater importance
then other. From this the partial correlation analysis was performed, which showed no
signiﬁcant result. Either the variables had a power of correlation of less then 0.2, or lagged
the statistical signiﬁcance to be deemed reliable.
The statistical analysis has proven to been insuﬃcient to explain the mechanisms in the
waste to energy facility in a adequate manner. The results has proven to be in able to
determine the important factors, to establish a model. It was desirable from the author
perspective to deﬁne a model which could help the operators of the waste to energy facil-
ity to make informed decisions, which in term could lower the energy loss, and increase
the overall eﬃciency. This however was not possible, since no signiﬁcant ﬁndings was
observed.
During the analysis several problems occurred, and the burden increased, mostly from
personal lag of knowledge of proper statistical analysis by the author. Late in the project
Murat Kulahci was assigned as Co-supervisor to the thesis, which has been the main
reason of the structure and substance of the statistical analysis.

Further prospects
From the work during this thesis, several obstacles occurred, which was not possible to
be dealt whit during the time scale of the project. For future work, a list of topics to be
investigated and implemented, will be discussed.
From the interviews whit the operators it was recognised that many of these variables
was of less importance during their daily work. During operation, the operation manager
monitors the steam production, to ensure the production follows the set point. For future

5
Conclusion
This thesis has focused on the energy loss and operation of ARGO waste to energy line
six. The scope has been to identify the energy losses, how they were indicated and how
it could be represented in real time. This should combined give the operators a more
informed decision basis to maintain daily operation, whit higher performance.
It was identiﬁed that loss from ﬂue gas was the most critical factor, but also the most
ﬂuctuating, whit possibilities for improvements. The remaining energy losses was small
compared to the energy loss from ﬂue gas, and the loss from evaporation can furthermore
be regenerated. It was identiﬁed that the total energy loss to some extend was correlate
whit the overall eﬃciency.
From this it could further be included, that ﬂue gas condensation aﬀected the overall
eﬃciency more then the thermal input, which imply that the thermal eﬀect from ﬂue
gas condensation, should be kept as high as possible, and reduce the thermal load, if a
restriction on the district heating grid was in forced.
From visual analysis it what identiﬁed that the O2 concentration in the ﬂue gas was
higher during low ﬂue gas ﬂow rates. In combination whit this, the ﬂue gas ﬂow rate was
observed to be correlated whit the steam production and the waste through put.
The correlation between ﬂue gas ﬂow rate, and steam production and waste through put
was expected, but combined whit the ﬂue gas correlated to the O2 concentration, it could
be concluded that the oxygen levels during operation whit low thermal load, was higher
then best self performance. This could indicate that more primary air was supplied then
needed. From this, the energy loss could be reduced by lowering the primary air ﬂow
rate, and hence increase the overall eﬃciency. This can of cause only be accomplished
by optimal distribution of primary air at the grate, which was not analysed during this
thesis.
It was not possible to identify whit certainty which variables that could by used as
indicators for operation performance, e.g higher or lower overall eﬃciency then best self
performance. Therefor a speciﬁc algorithm was not identiﬁed. To bench mark the op-
eration, RAMSTEAM was used, which to some extent prove use full, but whit a large
potential for improvement. It was shown that actual operation performance could be
plotted side by side whit expected performance based on the RAMSTEAM calculations,
which could give a good indication on how well the plant performed. Whit further de-
velopment, this could be implemented for easy interpretation for the operators. To reach
the full potential, more variables should be implemented, since the RAMSTEAM results
still was calculated based on several ﬁxed values.

Hvad indikerer god/dårlig drift? 
Flammefronter på kameraer bruges som indikatorer på god og dårlig drift/forbrænding. 
Ved dårlig forbrænding/bunker med affald på risten, nedjusteres lasten/indfødning (set punkt på 
affalds fødning) 
 
Primær luft set punkt bliver k 
Affalds fødehastighed kan bruges som indikator for hvor godt affaldet er(brændværdi). Høj 
hastighed = lav brændværdi, lav hastighed = høj brændværdi 
 
For at øge brændværdi, såfremt der er mulighed, blandes affaldet. Dette gøres for at lufte det og 
gøre det mere homogent. 
 
• 
Hvad regulerer du efter?  
o Affald brændt? 
o Damp produceret? 
o Varme produceret? 
o El produceret? 
Som udgangspunkt kun damp mængden, som derudover oftest blot er sat på maks., med mindre der 
er restriktioner på produktionen.  Linje 6 bliver stort set drevet automatisk. 
 
Set punkt for fjernvarme temperatur fremløb kan bruges til at regulerer damp mængde for turbine. 
Ved lave ovn temperaturer og lave damp mængder, kan fremløbs temperaturen reduceres for at øge 
dampmængden.  
El og varme bliver reduceret efter elmarkedet og varmeproduktions begrænsninger fra varmelast.  
Ved for store tryk fald over posefiltre, kan luft tilførslen reduceres.

Hvad indikerer god/dårlig drift? 
God drift bliver indikeret af at damp produktionen bliver holdt omkring sæt punkt. Ydermere at der 
helst skal være et homogent bål.  
• 
Hvad regulerer du efter?  
o Affald brændt? 
o Damp produceret? 
o Varme produceret? 
o El produceret? 
Dampproduktion er vigtigste variable. 
• 
Hvilke parametre kan du regulere på? 
o Affaldsmængden? 
o Luft mængder? 
o Temperaturer? 
o Tryk? 
 
Luftmængder, Affaldsmængder. 
Hvis der skal køres i manuelt grundet dårlig drift, ved dårligt bål(ujævn forbrænding, eller lav 
temperatur, kan der regulere primærluft, specifikt i hvilke zoner.  Ydermere kan ristefødning, og riste 
hastighed ændres.  
 Ved høje mængder sekundær luft kan effektivitet et falde, ved røggassen bliver kølet.  
 
  
• 
Hvilke udfordringer oplever du ved den evt. varierende affaldssammensætning? 
Under god drift, hvor der er en stabil affaldssammensætning(jævn brændværdi), kører ovnen med 
stabile parametre. Ved pludselige skift i affaldssammensætning(brændværdi), for eksempel en grab 
flamingo eller pap som hurtigt bliver brændt af, kan ovnen have svært ved nå at regulere tilbage, da 
den kan have en tendens til at øge indfødning markant. Derefter kan den så ikke nå at brænde det 
af, hvis affaldssammensætningen går tilbage til den stabile affaldssammensætning. 
• 
Hvor går grænsen mellem de valg du skal tage, og valg der skal tages på højere niveau? 
Under begrænsninger, fra varmelast, af varme kan det være nødvendigt at reducere last. Som 
udgangspunkt kan energien flyttes over på turbinen, hvis der er kapacitet til det, maks 20-21 MW. 
Med mindre et der er negative elpriser, kan der produceres så meget el som der kan leveres. Ved 
negative priser bliver ARGO adviseret af energi net.
