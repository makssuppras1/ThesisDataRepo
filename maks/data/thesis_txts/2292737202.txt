Abstract
HLA molecules play a central role in initiating an immune response. Identifying an
individual’s HLA haplotypes therefore has a broad clinical use, e.g. in avoiding solid
organ transplant rejection. However, fast and precise identiﬁcation of the HLA types
require a reference genome, and unfortunately the present reference does not fully
represent the known HLA variation. Identiﬁcation of additional HLA haplotypes
have the potential of improve HLA typing in respect of time and accuracy.
This study entailed high depth sequencing data from two Danish families (con-
sisting of mother, farther, and child). From a high quality de novo assembly, reads
originating from each individual’s HLA region were identiﬁed. Attempts at iden-
tifying the HLA haplotypes were based on the individual’s own assembly and the
identiﬁed reads. A variant based approach were tried, where the analysis were based
on variant calling for later phasing.
To base the analysis on each individual’s own assembly would be ideal, however,
this makes us unable to compare the variation seen in individuals. To overcome this
challenge, common sequence coordinates were introduced based on the reference
genome. Unfortunately, this was unsuccessful as not all positions could be trans-
ferred. Further diﬃculties arose from the incomparable individuals before phasing
was possible. However, throughout the analysis we acquired valuable new knowledge
on how to approach the identiﬁcation of the HLA haplotypes in the future.
While this study did not yield a complete protocol for identifying HLA haplotypes, a
profound knowledge on how to proceed was obtained. The way forward would entail
analysis on a common reference within each trio, e.g. using the child’s assembly
as reference. This will eliminate the problems with incompatible positions among
individuals.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Aim
The main goal of this project is to determine the Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA)
haplotypes in individuals, using high-depth Next Generation Sequencing (NGS)
data. This is a preliminary analysis of two families (consisting of mother, farther,
and child) before the ﬁnal large scale analysis of all the 50 families in the Genome
Denmark project [1].
1.2
Motivation
The HLA region is 4Mb long and is the most gene dense region in the human genome.
The most important molecules encoded within the region are the class I and II HLA
molecules associated with the immune system. Their function is to sample intra-
and extracellular proteins and present peptides generated from these proteins on
the cell surface. Peptides presented by HLA molecules are scanned by immune cells
to determine whether the peptide originates from the host or foreign proteins. An
immune response is initiated upon recognition of foreigner peptides.
An individual carries six HLA-I and up to 12 HLA-II allotypes; all coding for peptide
presenting HLA molecules. Currently, more than 12,000 HLA-I and HLA-II alleles
have been identiﬁed [2]. This highlights the complexity of the HLA region, as each
individual carries many similar genes and each gene has a high allelic variation, for
a more detailed description see Section 2.3.2. This makes it diﬃcult, or perhaps
impossible, to ﬁnd two individuals with identical HLA haplotypes. The number of
known HLA alleles has increased dramatically during the last ﬁve years because of
NGS, see Figure 1.1, but only a minority of the alleles have been fully sequenced
and well documented, indicating a need for further investigation of this region.
The conventional approach for handling NGS data is to align reads to a reference
genome, but this introduces errors when aligning to highly polymorphic sites, like
the HLA region. To imitate the polymorphism within the population the reference
genome features alternative loci scaﬀolds. Still the reference genome only represents

Reliable typing of the HLA region is important in several areas of medicine, as

adverse drug responses, and risk of complex diseases. The introduction of NGS data
has greatly reduced time and cost of HLA typing, but it still requires a method
optimised to capture the complete complexity seen in the HLA loci. In the future,
the improvements in NGS technology will most likely entail longer reads with an in-
creased sequencing depth, hence eliminating some of the present challenges of HLA
typing from NGS data.
1.3
Previous studies
Many studies of HLA have focused on its connection to disease. Additionally, var-
ious diﬀerent HLA typing methods have been developed.
However, they are all
based on typing the HLA genes, and not the entire HLA haplotype. In the following
a few publications dealing with HLA typing in healthy individuals will be introduced.
In the study by Hajeer et al. (2013) [5], HLA alleles were investigated using reads
generated with the 454 sequencing techniques. This study investigated the frequen-
cies of HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1, and -DQB1 in a healthy Arabian population. The
analysis was done using Conexio Assign AFT 454 software, based on exons 2,

3, and 4 in HLA-I genes and exons 2 and 3 in HLA-II genes. Several other studies
have used Conexio Assign AFT 454 software to HLA genotype healthy indivi-
duals from NGS data generated with 454 sequencing techniques [6, 7]. The strength
of this method rely on the generation of long reads from the entire HLA gene ampli-
ﬁcation done with Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR). This method is not suitable
for this study due to incompatibility with our sequencing data with shorter reads.
Iqbal et al. (2012) [8] used de novo assemblies to ﬁnd the HLA-B allele present
in two individuals who previous had their alleles typed. In more detail, they con-
structed a graph containing the reference genome, all known HLA-B alleles, and
data from each individual, and from this calculated the most likely genotype for
each individual. They introduced the approach of using de novo assembly to detect
genetic variations and genotyping of NGS data [8].
Warren et al. (2012) [9] developed the method HLAminer, designed for HLA typing
from NGS data. The algorithm behind creates a hash table encompassing 15-mers
from breakdown of a HLA database. Reads are then searched for presences in these
15-mers in the the 5’-end and recruited reads are assembled. The assembled contigs
are used for a BLAST search against a genomic HLA sequence database. HLAminer
generates a score and a probability for each HLA gene identiﬁed form the assembled
contigs.
Major et al. (2013) [10] typed HLA genes from whole genome and whole exome
sequencing data, with a focus on HLA-A, -B, and -C. They found that in many
cases lack of reads make it impossible to identify all alleles. Their approach was to
align reads to the IMGT/HLA database of the coding sequences and to interpret
the results to identify the most probable allele combination for an individual. They
found that using only the coding sequences resulted in loss of important information
leading to mistyping. To deal with this, they suggest a de novo assembly approach
[10].
Bai et al. (2014) [11] introduced a method called PHLAT (Precise HLA Typing)
handling genome-wide sequencing data. The approach is to identify allele candi-
dates from read mapping to a reference with Bowtie 2, followed by likelihood
based ranking of all pairwise allele combination. PHLAT was new to integrate like-
lihood scoring and allele selection, and more important to include mapping to a
reference outside the HLA loci for genome-wide sequencing data.
A recently published paper by Huang et al. (2015) [12] introduces a new method
for conﬁdent HLA typing from short sequence reads, called HLAreporter. Their
method rely on identifying reads from the alignment to a HLA transcript database
constructed from IMGT/HLA [3]. Reads found to align to each gene are de novo
assembled and the ﬁnal assemblies are typed based on already known alleles. First,
the identiﬁcation is based on alignment to the major polymorphic exons (exons 2
and 3), followed by a second round of alignment to the minor polymorphic exons.

1.4
Project outline
High depth NGS data from 50 Danish trios (mother, farther, and child) were used
for de novo assembling. I focused on available de novo assemblies, six trios are ana-
lysed ﬁrst in a kmerﬁnder based approach and two trios in a second variant based
approach, explained in more details in Section 3.3.2 and 3.3.3, respectively. Based
on the available trios I set up an initial pipeline needing further development on how
to identify an individual’s HLA haplotype.
The general idea on how to approach the identiﬁcation of an individual’s HLA
haplotype was to identify scaﬀolds covering the HLA region and extract reads alig-
ning these scaﬀolds. The variation found in the extracted reads should be used for
identifying each individual’s HLA haplotype based on phasing.

Chapter 2
Background
In the following, a short introduction will be given to the immune system, focusing on
the adaptive immune response and the function that the Major Histocompatibility
Complex (MHC) plays in this. This is followed by a more detailed description of
the human version of MHC, the HLA. Finally, NGS technology will be introduced
along with de novo assembling and phasing.
2.1
The immune system
The immune system is crucial for survival of mammals. First line of defence against
invading microorganisms are made by physical barriers, such as the skin and epi-
thelial layers present in e.g. the intestine, respiratory tract, and genital tract. In
addition, the immune system features a cellular and a humoral protection, activated
when a pathogen is detected. Immediately upon infection, initiation of the innate
immune response seeks to ﬁght the infection, but when failing in doing so it initiates
the speciﬁc adaptive immune response.
These two arms of the immune system
cooperate in eliminating invading potential pathogens [13].
Here follows a short
introduction to the two arms of the immune system.
2.1.1
Innate immune response
The innate immune response is the ﬁrst response against invading pathogens. In-
vading pathogens are ﬁrst recognised by soluble proteins and cell-surface receptors,
to secondly recruit the various types of eﬀector cells, and the complement system
composed of serum proteins, to eradicate the pathogen. The innate immune system
is divided into a humoral and a cellular response [13].
The cellular response is initiated by pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)
recognised by innate immune cells via pattern recognition receptors (PRRs). An
example of PRRs are the Toll-like receptors (TLRs). These recognise a wide va-
riety of PAMPs, such as carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids speciﬁc
for microbes. PAMPs are evolutionary conserved patterns shared among diﬀerent
micro-organisms. As a consequence of that, most PAMPs are essential to the micro-

to a PAMP recognition, intracellular signalling and gene expression are initiated, to

The complement system contributes to the humoral response. They are proteins

not succeeded in eradicating an infection, spread of infection calls for lymphocytes
initiating the adaptive immune response [13].
2.1.2
Adaptive immune response
The essential part of the adaptive immune response comprises the lymphocytes (the
B and T cells), which recognise pathogens via cell surface receptors. The unique
property of these receptors, is that an almost inﬁnite number of diﬀerent versions
that can be made. The high diversity of the receptors are due to gene rearrangement
(somatic recombination) with further diversity achieved by imprecision in the gene
rearrangement. This leads to single lymphocytes expressing a single type of receptor,
however overall the B and T cells make millions of diﬀerent receptors, permitting
recognition of any pathogen. Only a small fraction of the lymphocytes recognises
a particular pathogen, upon recognition proliferation of the cells are stimulated to
increase their numbers.
The adaptive immune response is initiated in specialised lymphoid tissue or or-
gans. First step is the transport of the pathogen by dendritic cells (DCs) to the
nearest secondary lymphoid tissue. Second step is for the DC to meet and activate
naive T cells with receptors recognising the pathogen. This is facilitated by the DC
presenting pathogen antigen on surface receptors, followed by T cells recognising the
antigen binding it and staying in contact with the DC. Here they are activated to
divide and diﬀerentiate into eﬀector T cells.
T cells recognises pathogen derived antigens bound to MHC molecules, a mem-
brane glycoprotein whose function is to bind and present peptide antigens to T
cells on the surface of professional antigen presenting cells (professional APCs), DC,
macrophages, and B cells. There exists two types of T cells, CD8+ T cells (cyto-
toxic T cells) and CD4+ T cells (helper T cells), each performing diﬀerent tasks.
CD8+ T cells combat intracellular pathogens by killing the infected cell, preventing
it from replicating and transferring the infection. CD4+ T cells combat extracel-
lular pathogens by enhancing phagocytosis. Moreover, they help B cell becoming
antibody producing cells. When DCs travel to the secondary lymphoid tissue, they
also bring bacteria and virus particles, which can interact with the B cell receptors
of circulating B cells in the lymph node. B cells that recognise the foreigner stays
in the lymph node, take it up, degrade it, and ﬁnally present its antigen on MHC

2.2
Major Histocompatibility Complex
All vertebrates possess a MHC composed of three classes of genes. The class III
genes are not described here as they are not encoding antigen presenting genes.
Of particular interest is the MHC-I and MHC-II, which handle diﬀerent types of
pathogens but otherwise share many similarities. They perform similar tasks, such
as presenting peptides on the cell surface to CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, respectively.
They share similar overall three-dimensional structure (see Figure 2.1), genetic lo-
cation, and both possess high polymorphism within a population [15].

In case of an intracellular pathogen, antigens will be presented on MHC-I on both
the infected cells and the professional APCs. Initiation of a CD8+ T cell mediated
immune response can occur via diﬀerent routes. A DC can activate CD8+ T cells
on its own via antigen presented on MHC-I and co-stimulatory molecules. If instead
CD8+ T cells recognise antigens presented on MHC-I on an infected cell it will need
co-stimulation by CD4+ T cells, which recognise antigen on the same infected cell.
Upon activation the CD8+ T cells start synthesising cytotoxins and pack them in
granules, recognition of MHC-I bound antigen tells it to secrete its lytic granules
directly on the surface of the infected cell causing its death [13].
MHC-II on the other hand are predominantly expressed by professional APCs. As-
sembling of MHC-II occurs in the ER followed by functional maturation in endoso-
mal compartments via peptide binding. Lysosomal proteolysis in the endocytic and
phagocytic pathways prepare peptides for MHC-II binding [19]. For a more detailed
description of the MHC-II pathway see Figure 2.3.
In case of an extracellular pathogen, CD4+ T cells are activated in lymphoid tissue
upon recognition of antigens bound to MHC-II on DCs. Activated CD4+ T cells
recognise pathogen derived antigens presented on MHC-II by professional APCs.
Upon recognition, an antigen:MHC-II complex will form to ensure interaction be-
tween the T cell and the APC. This interaction mediates a T cell increased activity
of macrophage phagocytosis and activation of B cells to become antibody producing
plasma cells. In the end this ensures eradication of the extracellular pathogen via
engulfment and digestion [13].
A single MHC molecule binds peptides of a wide variety of amino acid sequences,
in contrary to B or T cell receptors which bind only one speciﬁc peptide. In the
binding groove of the MHC molecule the peptide is held tightly via non-covalent
bonds [13]. A more detailed look at the binding grooves for MHC-I and MHC-II,
also reveal diﬀerent preferences for peptides. The peptides interact at both the N-
and C-termini with MHC-I, which limits the length of the peptides to 8–10 amino
acids [19]. Whereas for the MHC-II bound peptides, no such interaction limits the
length of the peptides, therefore the length of the peptides are usually between 13–25
amino acids [13].

2.4.1
De novo assembly
Post processing of sequence reads could either be to align it to a reference genome or
assemble de novo after pre-processing. The process of de novo sequence assembly is
to merge overlapping sequence reads to form contigs and merge overlapping contigs
to form scaﬀolds. Even with a complete reference genome de novo assembly show
several advantages over the alignment approach.
Of these advantages the most
important one in our study is the ability to better capture divergent sequences such
as for the HLA region, which does not align properly to a reference genome due to
the high polymorphic nature of the genes [9, 24]. On the other hand, a de novo
assembly set higher demands for sequencing quality, such as read depth and read
length [22, 24], where the read depth is the number of reads covering a nucleotide.
Moreover, a de novo assembly fails in reconstruction of entire genomes, mainly due
to repeated regions and limited read length [24].
2.4.2
Haplotype phasing
The de novo assembled sequence will be a mosaic of the alleles present on homologous
copies of a chromosome. The detection of alleles co-locate on the same chromosome,
the haplotype, is solved by phasing the sequence reads. In short, phasing is recon-
struction of the individual’s two set of chromosomes from sequence data. The ap-
proach of phasing is to identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in sequence
reads, and use this information to bin the reads into two groups, corresponding to
the two homologous chromosomes [25].

3.2.1
De novo assembly methods
Two diﬀerent de novo assembly methods were benchmarked.
String Graph Assembler (SGA) [29], uses the string graph approach for de
novo assembly. This method rely on intact reads to create a graph based on over-
lap between those. Here the graph represents sequence information in edges, and
start or end of overlap in nodes. Longer stretches of sequences, called contigs, are
16
==PAGE:24==

assembled from the graph and these can be further assembled into scaﬀolds based
on paired-end or mate-pair data. The advantage of this approach over other graph
based methods is the use of intact reads. SGA outputs the assembled scaﬀolds to a
FASTA ﬁle.
ALLPATHS-LG [30], an improvement of ALLPATHS2 [31] able to de novo assemble
large genomes (LG) such as the human genome. ALLPATHS utilizes graph theory
for de novo assembly of NGS data with at least two libraries, one with short and
one with long insert size. A graph-based assembler like ALLPATHS-LG represents
the assembly problem as a set of contiguous sequences (k-mers) as nodes and their
relationship as edges if overlap by k-1. Reconstruction of the genome is reduced
to ﬁnding the optimal path through the graph. ALLPATHS-LG also outputs the
assembled scaﬀolds to a FASTA ﬁle.
3.2.2
Reference-base mapping of reads
Burrows-Wheeler Aligner (BWA) [32], the algorithm BWA-MEM is developed
to map low-divergent sequences of a size range from 70bp to 1Mb, to a large reference
genome such as the human genome. This renders BWA-MEM ideal for read mapping.
In short, the algorithm works by aligning seeds with supermaximal exact matches
followed by extending of seeds with the aﬃne-gap Smith-Waterman algorithm. The
BWA-MEM algorithm automatically chooses between local alignment and end-to-end
alignment, and produce diﬀerent primary alignments for diﬀerent parts of the query.
Using the option -M marks shorter hits as secondary [32].
BWA-MEM outputs the alignment in SAM (Sequence Alignment/Map) format which
is easily converted to BAM format (binary format of SAM). The format consists of
header lines starting with ’@’ and alignment lines. Each alignment line represents
an alignment and consists of at least 12 ﬁelds (depending on usage of optional ﬁelds)
[33]. Table 3.3 lists ﬁelds of interest and their associated information.

the number of kmers mapping to the two best hits, from this a unique fraction is
calculated [44]:

The variant calling done in this study is composed of two steps: First, samtools
mpileup computes the genotype likelihood from an alignment ﬁle, followed by the
actual variant call done with bcftools.
samtools mpileup [45], is a part of the SAMtools software package, made to
manipulate and parse alignments in SAM/BAM format. mpileup can generate per
base information where each line represents a genomic position. The output is in
Binary Call Format (BCF).
bcftools [46], is a set of utilities for manipulating the BCF and Variant Call
Format (VCF) [47].
The program does variant calling based on genotype likeli-
hood generated with mpileup. The output VCF format, see Figure 3.4, contains
meta-information lines (##) and a header line (#), followed by the data lines each
holding information about a position in the sequence.

3.2.6
Genomic coordinate conversion
Coordinate conversion is introduced to compare variation found in diﬀerent indivi-
duals.
liftOver [48], a tool for converting coordinates in one assembly to coordinates
in another assembly using chained and netted alignment ﬁles. Chain ﬁles contain
chains which are alignment blocks (generated with e.g. BLAT described in 3.2.3)
linked together in gapless sequences. Further linking generates a hierarchical collec-
tion of chains called net alignment.
3.3
Approach
Following the introduction of the data and programs used throughout the study,
the approaches for identifying HLA haplotypes done in this study is presented. Two
main approaches were evaluated, the kmerﬁnder based approach based on identifying
HLA alleles with KmerFinder, and the variant based approach based on identifying
HLA haplotypes from variant calling. The results are found in Chapter 4, while the
pros and cons are presented in Chapter 5.

4.2.4
Predicting HLA haplotype with KmerFinder
Predictions of HLA haplotype with KmerFinder resulted in purely homozygous
predictions for all individuals, when using a 5% cut-oﬀvalue (calculation described
in Section 3.2.4). This cut-oﬀvalue have previously shown to perform well predicting
around 11% of loci to be homozygous [44]. This could be due to the fact that the
KmerFinder predictions are based on reads found to map to the assembly, which
only represents one of the two haplotypes. Moreover, KmerFinder will only predict
known HLA alleles as the prediction is based on known alleles. Most likely not all

4.2.5
Summary of the kmerﬁnder based approach
From the results presented above, the following knowledge is used for the further
analysis:
1. The alignment of an assembly to the reference should not be done with BWA-MEM.
This method is made for aligning short sequences against a large sequence, not
two long sequences against each other, as is the case here.
2. Alignment of assemblies to the HLA region of GRCh38 identiﬁes most scaﬀolds.
3. Aligning reads to a subset of the assembly force some reads to align to regions
they do not originate from.
4. For the HLA haplotypes KmerFinder predicts all individuals homozygotic.
4.3
Variant based approach
The variant based approach is optimised based on the knowledge gained from the
initial kmerﬁnder based approach. It is based on identifying scaﬀolds aligning to the
HLA region of GRCh38 with LAST and aligning all reads to whole assembly with
BWA-MEM. Reads aligning to the selected scaﬀolds were extracted and used for vari-
ant calling. The scaﬀold coordinates were converted to chromosome 6 coordinates
and the variants in a trio merged. The merged variants should then be used for
phasing and subsequent HLA haplotyping, see workﬂow overview in Figure 3.7.

The identiﬁcation of HLA haplotypes from whole genome sequencing data can be
divided into two main parts, identifying reads originating from the HLA genes and
identifying an individual’s HLA haplotype from these reads. In the following, the
two main steps are discussed based on the variant based approach, and compared to
published HLA typing methods, introduced in Section 1.3. It is important to have
in mind that the variant based approach seeks to identify the HLA haplotype not
just typing each HLA gene.
5.1
Selecting HLA reads
In the variant based approach reads were selected based on alignment to a de novo
assembly and those aligning to the HLA region extracted. Scaﬀolds covering the
HLA region were identiﬁed from a LAST alignment to GRCh38. In the following,
parameters inﬂuencing the selection of reads are discussed and compared to how
HLA typing methods handle the identiﬁcation of reads.
Diﬀerent cut-oﬀvalues for relative alignment coverages of scaﬀolds were tried. From
these a 25% cut-oﬀwas chosen, see Section 4.3.1. Further veriﬁcation of the optimal
cut-oﬀshould include lower values, to ensure the optimum is reached. However,
this way of selecting scaﬀolds was found not to be optimal. For example very long
scaﬀolds covering the entire HLA region and continue much further will not pass the
selection criteria, since only a smaller fraction of it would align to the HLA region.
However, such scaﬀolds would in fact be optimal for HLA typing from a de novo
assembly.
Aligning reads back to the assembly relies on the assembly quality, which again
depends on the reads themself. The identiﬁed scaﬀolds were generally much smaller
than the N50 length, see Figure 4.8. This could be due to the highly similar genes
present in the region, which makes it diﬃcult to generate longer scaﬀolds.
Fur-
thermore, the scaﬀold quality is inﬂuenced by the lack of reads within these regions,
introducing N’s from gap-ﬁlling during de novo assembling. To overcome these prob-
lems require improved sequencing, with more and longer reads spanning these regions

to improve the de novo assembly, and therefore the identiﬁcation of HLA haplotypes.
Methods developed for HLA typing from NGS data take on diﬀerent approaches
for identifying reads. They are all mapping based but diﬀer in the reference they
map to. HLAminer [9], HLAreporter [12], and the approach described by Major
et al. [10], map reads to a database of known HLA alleles with only minor diffe-
rences. PHLAT, on the other hand, align reads to a complete reference [11]. One of
the advantages of using a complete reference, is that all reads can align to the site
they originate from. A database including all HLA alleles, will likewise ensure great
mapping accuracy, as shown in the study by Huang et al. [12]. Another diﬀerence
in the methods is the extend of the HLA genes mapped to the reference. It is com-
mon to only consider the main exons, as these cover the highly polymorphic regions
encoding the peptide binding part of the molecule. However, T cell activation by
HLA-peptide complexes does not only depend on the presented peptide, but does
also interact with the HLA molecule [11]. This highlights the importance of not
only typing the highly polymorphic exons, but the entire HLA gene. However, the
reference genome does not represent the true allelic diversity. Thus aligning reads to
the reference genome can cause lower mapping accuracy due to the high similarity
between diﬀerent genes. In this report, this problem is dealt with as the individual’s
own assembly is used for read mapping, to ensure reads are actually map to the
genes they originate from. And by using the entire assembly as reference mapping
competition is still ensure.
5.2
Identifying HLA haplotype from identiﬁed reads
The variant based approach used the identiﬁed reads for variant calling and further
processed before phasing. Along the way, several limitations of this approach were
identiﬁed, discussed in the following along with how other methods deal with the
actual typing.
Until this point, the variant based approach has not been biased from a reference or
already known alleles. However, comparing variation between the individuals in a
trio is impossible without a common coordinate system. To solve this, a liftOver
approach was tried, using the chromosome 6 HLA region as common reference. Un-
fortunately, not all positions could be converted, see Figure 4.11, however many of
those positions most likely corresponds to N’s in the assembly.
Phasing the called variation would (ideally) identify an individual’s haplotype. Avail-
able trio based phasing tools (e.g. PhaseByTransmission) are based on variants
called compared to one reference. This is not comparable with this study as three
diﬀerent references are used, one for each individual in the trio. Moreover, the vari-
ation needs to be merged, but again no available methods exist so a custom script
was made. However, this was still not optimal as many unknown values need to
be introduced to merge the called variation. This is a dilemma, as we want to be
unbiased from the reference genome as much as possible, but conventional methods

depend on a common reference. Hence, if we wish to beneﬁt from existing tools this

Other approaches include the published method HLAreporter [12] and HLAminer
[9], which uses a de novo assembly approach for the identiﬁed reads, as each HLA’s
main exons are assembled into contigs, followed by matching to known HLA allele ex-
ons for conﬁdent HLA typing. The introduction of de novo assembly for HLA typing
has shown improved typing ability compared to mapping based methods. However,
low coverage depth will only be suited for alignment based approaches, thus the
algorithm by Major et al. [10] is able to predict HLA types to some extend even at
very low coverage depth. Additionally, Major et al. stated that a de novo assembly
approach will improve HLA typing. In contrary, PHLAT [11] did not extract reads
for HLA typing, instead allele candidates were found from alignment of reads to a
reference and HLA typing was based on ranking likelihoods of the pair-wise allele
combinations. The most promising method is probably the HLAreporter, which
outperforms all the other methods in accuracy but also demands high coverage and
depth. However, the goal in this study was not just to type each HLA gene, but
identify an individual’s two haplotypes requiring a more extensive approach.
5.3
Future work
There is a conﬂict in the wish of being unbiased from references and still being able
to compare individuals. However, using the child as reference at trio level could solve
some of the issues. This introduces a common reference that is still similar enough
to actually capture the variation in the other individuals. From there, the approach
would still entail variant calling based on the common reference followed by phasing.
However, to compare haplotypes across trios they would need to be anchored to the
GRCh38. Furthermore, the study should be expanded to include all 50 trios which
are a part of the Genome Denmark project, enabling the identiﬁcation of 200 HLA
haplotypes in addition to the eight presently known HLA haplotypes.
5.4
Signiﬁcance of HLA haplotypes
The identiﬁcation of the 200 HLA haplotypes will contribute to the Danish reference
genome, which the Genome Denmark consortium aim to construct. Potentially, this
can make HLA typing of Danes much easier as a Danish reference genome represents
the Danish population better than GRCh38. This can contribute to faster and more
accurate HLA typing, which is important for clinical use e.g.
to avoiding organ
rejection after transplantation, and to predict adverse drug reactions.
5.5
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to develop a method for typing an individual’s HLA
haplotype from whole genome sequencing data.
