Abstract
Food waste is a critical global issue, with significant environmental, social, and economic implications.
This thesis explores data-driven strategies to address food waste in the retail sector, focusing on near-
expiry products from Denmark’s largest retail group. By employing geographical analysis and network
science techniques, our research aims to identify patterns and actionable strategies for waste reduc-
tion.
The geographical analysis revealed notable disparities in the distribution of expiring product
offers between rural and urban areas. Urban stores demonstrated greater diversity in expiring items.
Rural communes showed higher normalized levels of expiring products, possibly influenced by supply
chain dynamics and consumer behavior, emphasizing the need for region-specific interventions. Net-
work analysis demonstrated the potential of product relationships to influence purchasing behavior.
Frequently bought together items provide strategic points for nudging consumers toward purchasing
expiring products through bundling or targeted promotions. Moreover, similar products can be lever-
aged as substitutions and revealed significant price reductions. This research illustrates how applying
data and network science can create valuable insights into food waste dynamics and consumer behav-
ior. The findings support the development of targeted strategies for reducing waste while aligning with
sustainable and economic goals. Future work should address current limitations, including the lack of
fresh produce data and the length of the observation period, to extend these findings and provide a
more comprehensive understanding of food waste in the retail sector.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Food waste is a critical global challenge, with nearly one-third of all food produced for human consump-
tion lost or wasted [31]. This waste severely impacts the society, environment, and causes significant
economic losses since food production consumes vast resources, including land, water, and energy [19].
Specifically, food waste generates around 8 to 10 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions which
ultimately have a non-negligible contribution to climate change. It also highlights disparities in food
accessibility, as millions face hunger while edible resources go unused. The Food Waste Index Report
2024 shows that food wasted on a daily basis would be more than enough to feed the 783 million in-
dividuals suffering from hunger [31]. Addressing food waste is thus crucial for achieving sustainable
development goals related to climate action, resource eﬀiciency, and social equity [28].
While significant research has focused on food waste at the consumer level [30], there is a gap in
understanding the specific dynamics and drivers of waste within retail environments. Retail is a criti-
cal point where substantial food waste occurs, particularly due to expired products, overstocking, and
consumer purchasing behavior [42]. Supermarkets operate at the intersection of waste management
and profitability, where they must balance reducing food waste with maximizing revenue. It is not
just an environmental issue but also an economic challenge. In Europe, the costs of food waste are
associated with around 1.6% of net sales on average and can impact up to 4% [23]. Retailers face unique
challenges in managing perishable goods, responding to customers’ demands, and taking regional and
seasonal variations into account. Previous studies have highlighted that managing near-expiry products
effectively through dynamic pricing and increasing awareness can reduce waste and influence consumer
behavior toward sustainable purchasing [14, 43].
In Denmark, food waste at the retail level has gathered significant attention and is the second largest
area of avoidable food waste in the Danish Food Supply Chain according to the Danish Ministry of
Environment and Food [12]. Denmark’s largest retail company Salling Group was the first in Denmark
to publish their food waste figures openly in 2016. Since then, they are working towards the goal of
decreasing their food waste percentage by half until 2030. Despite ongoing sustainability efforts, food
waste in the retail sector continues to represent a substantial portion of Denmark’s total waste and de-
creasing the amount is essential for meeting the country’s climate goals and addressing food insecurity.
However, this challenge also presents an opportunity for innovation through data-driven solutions that
can improve the eﬀiciency of food distribution and reduce waste.
Using data science to approach real-world challenges has emerged to be a powerful tool for gaining in-
sights, including in the context of food waste. By analyzing patterns in consumer purchasing behavior
and forecasting demands, data science tools such as machine learning algorithms and predictive models
can help retailers reduce overstocking and better align supply with customer preferences. These meth-
ods can be applied at the level of production, distribution, and retail [2]. By tailoring interventions
to specific stages of the supply chain, data-driven approaches enable targeted and effective food waste
reduction strategies [13].
Nevertheless, current research lacks a data-driven examination of geographic patterns, such as dif-
ferences between urban and rural areas, and the application of network science to analyze product
networks including expiring products within the retail sector. Instead, existing studies predominantly
focus on demand forecasting and supply chain optimization, leaving significant gaps in understanding

1.1
Definition of Food Waste
Food waste occurs at the retail and consumption level. It is defined as food and the associated inedible
parts removed from the human food supply chain through aerobic and anaerobic digestion, land appli-
cation, controlled combustion, sewer, litter/discards/refuse, or landfill [31].
Food is defined as any substance – whether processed, semi-processed or raw – that is intended for
human consumption. This also includes drinks and any substances that were used in the manufacture,
preparation or treatment of food. Therefore, food waste also includes “inedible parts” that are not
intended to be consumed by humans. Examples of these components are bones, rinds, and pits/stones.
Food loss occurs along the food supply chain and up to, but not including, the sales level.
It is
defined as all the crop and livestock human-edible commodity quantities that, directly or indirectly,
completely exit the post-harvest/slaughter production/supply chain by being discarded, incinerated, or
otherwise and do not reenter in any other utilization (such as animal feed, industrial use, etc.), up to,
and excluding, the retail level [31].
1.2
Scope
This thesis addresses two primary research questions:
1. Are there differences in patterns of soon-to-expire products across Denmark?
2. How can network insights be used to decrease food waste?
To answer these questions, our research will focus on analyzing data obtained through the Salling Group
API, specifically concerning expiring products of all Netto, Føtex, and Bilka retail stores in Denmark,
to identify and understand regional variations in food waste patterns. Moreover, the thesis will apply
network science techniques based on purchasing data from Bilka’s online shop https://www.bilkatogo.
dk. By constructing and analyzing two networks—one based on frequently bought together items and
another on similar products—this research aims to explore how relationships between products can be
leveraged to gain insights into consumer behavior and to minimize waste. Strategies such as targeted
price reductions and product substitutions will be investigated as potential ways to decrease food waste.
The results will be evaluated to determine the potential for network insights to reduce waste at the
retail level across various locations in Denmark.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
Food waste remains a critical global challenge, as outlined in the introduction. Retailers, positioned
at the interface of supply chains and consumers, account for a significant proportion of this waste.
Advanced analytics and data-driven approaches have emerged as transformative tools for mitigating
food waste. This literature review explores the causes of food waste in retail, strategies to reduce it,
and data-driven methods employed for waste reduction.
2.1
Causes of Food Waste in Retail
Food waste in retail settings arises from a complex interplay of consumer and operational factors. This
section reviews the primary causes identified in the literature and their implications.
Slow Sales and Consumer Behavior. Slow sales due to low customer demand for specific products
can leave retailers with unsellable inventory. Consumer preferences also play a significant role in waste
generation. They often favor items with longer shelf lives or reject products with minor imperfections,
increasing the likelihood of unsold goods [37, 21, 27].
Aesthetic Standards. Fruits and vegetables that are misshapen or have minor blemishes are often
discarded, even though they remain edible. Retailers impose strict aesthetic standards, influenced by
consumer demand for visually perfect produce. These practices result in significant waste at the retail
and supply chain levels [18, 9].
Planning, Forecasting Errors, and Overstocking. Inaccurate demand forecasting is a critical con-
tributor to retail food waste. Poor prediction of customer demand leads to overstocking, since retailers
want to ensure availability, particularly for perishable items [18, 9]. Moreover, retailers often overstock
products to maintain variety [36].
Short Shelf Life and Perishability. Products with inherently short shelf lives, such as fresh produce,
meat, and dairy, are particularly vulnerable to waste. These items must be sold within narrow time
frames, and any delays or ineﬀiciencies in the supply chain can lead to spoilage before sale [27, 36].
Poor Handling Practices. Improper product handling during storage, transport, or in-store opera-
tions can lead to damage and spoilage. Examples include mishandling of vegetables/fruits, incorrect
stacking, or inadequate temperature controls [27, 18].
External Factors and Supply Chain Ineﬀiciencies. Supply chain delays, insuﬀicient real-time
monitoring, and a lack of coordinated tracking systems further exacerbate food waste. Retailers fre-
quently struggle to balance maintaining high service levels with minimizing unsold inventory. External
disruptions, such as logistics delays or seasonal variations, compound the diﬀiculty of aligning supply
and demand [21, 36, 14].
Misinterpretation of Date Labels. Supermarkets often discard products based on ”best-before”
dates rather than ”use-by” dates, contributing significantly to food waste [9, 18]. While ”best-before”
indicates peak quality, ”use-by” pertains to safety, yet the two are frequently conflated. This approach
is motivated by the desire to maintain customer satisfaction and uphold an image of providing fresh
products [18]. As a result, still-edible food is prematurely disposed of.

2.2
Strategies to Reduce Food Waste in Retail
Retailers have implemented various strategies to address food waste, ranging from operational ad-
justments to innovative pricing and product management. This section reviews key mitigation and
prevention approaches to reduce waste at the retail level.
Food Recycling, Bio-Energy, and Animal Feed. Food recycling programs and the conversion of
waste into bio-energy are effective strategies to re-purpose unsellable products while minimizing envi-
ronmental impacts. These approaches help reduce the volume of waste sent to landfills and provide
sustainable energy alternatives [14, 9]. Reusing unsellable food for animal feed is another sustainable
option that reduces waste while providing value. This approach is particularly effective for items un-
suitable for human consumption but still safe for animals [9, 37].
Food Donation and Collaborative Initiatives. Donating unsold but consumable food to charities
and non-profit organizations is a widely adopted practice. This strategy addresses food waste ethically
and provides food to those in need, though it does not eliminate the root causes of waste [14, 9]. More-
over, initiatives like Too Good To Go (TGTG) connect consumers with retailers to purchase surplus
food at discounted prices through mobile apps [17]. Such applications decrease waste, generate addi-
tional revenue, and enhance retailer reputations. In Denmark, TGTG has gained significant traction,
with thousands of supermarkets, bakeries, and restaurants actively participating. It’s success highlights
the potential of collaborative platforms to address food waste on a larger scale.
Dynamic Pricing and Discounts. Dynamic pricing strategies allow retailers to adjust prices based
on a product’s remaining shelf life. Research suggests that initiating discounts earlier at lower rates
results in less waste and higher profits compared to delayed, steep discounts. However, retailers must
carefully balance lower prices against potential profit losses [37, 13].
Assortment and Shelf Space Planning. Optimizing product assortments and shelf space allocation
can significantly reduce waste without compromising sales. Reducing the variety of slow-moving items
and prioritizing high-demand products has been shown to decrease waste by up to 8% [37, 21]. Addi-
tionally, economic penalization of overstocks would further incentivize better inventory management
[37].
Accurate Labeling and Consumer Awareness. Reformulating packaging labels to provide clearer
information on expiry dates, particularly for products with short shelf lives, can reduce confusion and
prevent premature disposal. Consumer education initiatives aimed at raising awareness about ”best-
before” versus ”use-by” dates also play a critical role in waste prevention [9, 14].
Reduced Storage Temperatures. Lowering storage temperatures can extend the shelf life of perish-
able items, thereby reducing waste. However, this strategy must balance extended freshness with the
increased energy costs associated with maintaining cooler environments [13].
Staff Training for Waste Reduction. Training retail staff to handle products correctly, monitor
inventory effectively, and implement waste reduction strategies can have an impact. Awareness pro-
grams help employees understand the environmental and financial impacts of food waste and encourage
proactive measures [9].
2.3
Data-Driven Approaches
Leveraging data-driven approaches has become increasingly important for addressing food waste in re-
tail. By integrating advanced analytics and big data technologies, retailers can enhance decision-making,
improve operational eﬀiciency, and reduce waste.
Enhanced Demand Forecasting.
Precise demand forecasting is pivotal in aligning supply with
customer demand. Predictive models leveraging historical sales data, seasonal trends, and customer
purchasing behavior enable retailers to minimize overstocking and spoilage. These models use big data
to analyze patterns and optimize inventory management, significantly improving accuracy and eﬀiciency
[9, 34, 36].
Network-Based Market Basket Analysis. Network-based approaches, such as market basket anal-
ysis, provide actionable insights into customer purchasing behavior by identifying relationships between

management, product placement, and promotional strategies. By isolating tightly connected product

Optimized Inventory and Replenishment Policies. Data-driven tools support the development

2.4
Discussion
While existing research has extensively examined the causes of food waste and proposed various mitiga-
tion strategies, there is a notable lack of approaches leveraging data and network science. Despite the
growing availability of big data tools, their application in retail food waste management and related
research remains sparse.
Dynamic pricing strategies, for instance, have shown promising results in reducing waste. Yet, studies
often fail to consider the interplay between multiple products. Research suggests that incorporating
dynamic pricing for interconnected product groups could yield significant benefits, but this area remains
largely unexplored [37]. Similarly, while network-based market basket analysis has demonstrated its
potential to uncover relationships between co-purchased products and consumer behavior [32], there is
no application to waste reduction strategies to our current knowledge.
This thesis addresses these gaps by investigating data on expiring products in Danish supermarkets
and analyzing geographical differences within the country. By combining expiring product data with
network product data, the research seeks to identify actionable insights to accelerate sales and raise
awareness. These innovative approaches aim to bridge the existing research gap and provide practical
solutions for retailers to enhance waste management and customer engagement.

Price Sensitivity and Sales Dynamics
Price elasticity was used to examine how changes in the price of products influenced their sales
velocity. Price elasticity of demand quantifies how sensitive the sales of a product are to a percentage
change in its price.
To calculate this metric, we used the following formula:
E = % Change in Quantity Sold
% Change in Price
(4.8)
where percentage change in quantity sold is the difference in units sold before and after a price change,
divided by the initial quantity sold. The percentage change in price is the difference between the new
price and the original price, divided by the original price.
In the context of our case, price elasticity was assessed for products across different communes by
tracking how their sales velocities (units sold per day) varied with price fluctuations. If price changes
lead to a significant increase or decrease in sales velocity, consumers are highly responsive which shows
in a high price elasticity. Conversely, if sales velocity remains relatively stable despite price adjustments,
the product is considered to have low price elasticity.
Most communes show an inelastic demand (E > −1 and E < 0), meaning a change in price leads
to a smaller change in quantity demanded (see Figure 4.7). For instance, a 10% price decrease leads
to only a 5% increase in quantity sold in the commune Herning with a value of E = −0.5. Communes
with an elasticity of E = 1 are unitary elastic, meaning the percentage change in price results in an
equal percentage change in quantity demanded. The total revenue remains the same, even though the
price changes. Some communes show an elastic demand, like Langeland in the second month (E < −1),
meaning consumers are sensitive to price changes which leads to more sales.

The analysis of geographical differences in expiring product offers across Denmark revealed significant

The higher prevalence of expiring product offers in rural areas, relative to population density, suggests
distinct supply chain dynamics. One plausible explanation is that rural stores maintain larger inven-

rural shopping patterns may favor bulk purchasing, since there are fewer stores within immediate reach.
This could influence stock management strategies of retailers.

5.4
Maximizing Influence
Influence maximization (IM) is the process of identifying a set of nodes in a network that can
maximize the spread of information when activated. It is a widely studied problem in social network
analysis and has applications in areas such as marketing and epidemiology. In our scenario, it is in-
triguing to investigate the speed at which soon-to-expire products can be reached within the network.
This approach could enable the identification of products that can either be discounted or strategically
positioned to enhance the sale of items nearing their expiration date.
The problem of IM is formalized as finding a subset of k nodes, called seed nodes, that maximizes
the expected influence spread under a given diffusion model. Influence spreads in discrete time steps.
Common diffusion models include the Independent Cascade (IC) and Linear Threshold (LT)
model [22, 25].
In the IC model, when a node u becomes active, it attempts to activate each of its inactive neighbors
v with a probability puv, representing the strength of the influence between u and v. If the attempt
succeeds, v becomes active in the next time step. This process continues until no further activations
occur.
In the LT model, each node v has a threshold θv, drawn from a uniform distribution in [0, 1]. Each edge
(u, v) is associated with an influence weight wuv, and the sum of weights of incoming edges for a node
is normalized to 1. A node v becomes active if the total influence from its active neighbors exceeds its
threshold:
∑
u∈A(v)
wuv ≥θv
(5.3)
where A(v) is the set of active neighbors of v.
Finding the optimal seed set is often computationally infeasible, thus approximation algorithms are
frequently used. One widely used approach is the Greedy Algorithm, which iteratively selects the node
that provides the largest marginal gain in influence spread. This algorithm guarantees an approximation
ratio of 1 −1/e (about 63%) of the optimal solution [22]. Recent advancements also include scalable
methods like the CELF (Cost-Effective Lazy Forward) optimization, which reduces computational effort
while maintaining high-quality results [25].

5.4.2
Applying the Linear Threshold Model
Due to the large size of the FBT network, sets of starting nodes have been chosen based on different
heuristics instead of applying an optimization algorithm. This approach allows for a comprehensive
analysis of the initial conditions and how they influence the progression of IM. The goal is to determine
the effectiveness of various seed sets in achieving an activation of 95% of the target nodes (expiring
products for a given month). The model runs iteratively, beginning with single-node seed sets and
==PAGE:44==
32
5 Network Analysis
incrementally increasing the size of the seed set until the activation condition is met or if no further
activations occur.
The neighbor threshold plays a critical role in shaping the dynamics of activation.
The threshold
represents the proportion of co-purchased products that must already be activated to trigger activation
of a given node.
Low thresholds (< 0.25) reflect scenarios where customers are easily influenced by a small number of
co-purchased products. In such cases, activation spreads rapidly across the network, often reaching even
less-connected items. While this simulates highly responsive customer behavior, it may overestimate the
actual influence in real-world purchasing contexts. Medium thresholds (0.25–0.5) demand a balanced
proportion of active neighbors, representing more realistic purchasing behavior where influence spreads
primarily through well-connected clusters or popular product groups. These thresholds align well with
scenarios where customers are moderately influenced by co-purchases, capturing the role of product
clusters or communities in driving activation. High thresholds (> 0.5) require a significant proportion
of co-purchased products to be activated before influence spreads. This slows the diffusion process and
limits activation to tightly interconnected clusters. Such thresholds model highly selective customer
behavior, where only strong associations between products drive co-purchases.
By analyzing activation patterns across varying thresholds, we can simulate how network structure
and customer responsiveness would influence the spread of activation in a product network. Figure 5.4
shows the amount of activated nodes per number of starting seeds with different limits. Start seeds are
high-degree nodes. The figure shows that thresholds of ≥0.3 significantly increase the amount of seeds
to reach the target nodes.
This is why we decided to set a neighbor threshold of 0.25. The following sets were selected as start
seeds:
• High-Degree Nodes: Nodes with the highest degree in the entire network.
• High-Degree Nodes from Louvain Communities:
Nodes with the highest degree within each
Louvain-detected community.
• Bridge Nodes from Louvain Communities: Nodes connecting multiple Louvain communities.
• High-Degree Nodes from Cliques: Nodes with the highest degree in densely connected subgraphs.
• Overlapping Nodes from Link Clustering: Nodes belonging to multiple clusters identified by the
link clustering algorithm.
The analysis was conducted for expiring items over a month in both København and Vejle to explore
potential regional differences. However, we observed no significant variation in activation dynamics or
the effectiveness of seed sets between the two locations. Therefore, this chapter focuses on presenting
the findings for København. The results show that high-degree nodes achieve the activation threshold
with a smaller seed set of five nodes (see Figure 5.5). These nodes often act as central hubs, facilitating
rapid diffusion. Bridge nodes perform similarly, they provide critical links between communities and
enable cross-community spread. This approach showed high eﬀiciency in the FBT network with well-
separated communities. However, the strategy of focusing on community-specific hubs (High Degree
Louvain Communities) leveraged localized influence. It required marginally larger seed sets compared
to the global high-degree strategy due to community-specific boundaries limiting spread.
As seed nodes were incrementally added, the spread of influence increases for activated nodes in the
network but not linearly for target nodes (see Figure B.10 in the Appendix). A critical tipping point
was observed, where the addition of one more seed node resulted in an explosive expansion of influence
on target nodes, reaching the majority of expiring items.
However, none of these nodes/products
consistently exhibited a direct relationship with expiring items that could be leveraged for targeted
interventions. Therefore, more localized interventions are needed as described in the next section.

5.4.3
Influence Spread vs. Buying Behavior: High-Degree Nodes and Cliques
In product networks, high-degree nodes (products frequently purchased with many others) excel at
spreading influence rapidly across the network (see Figure 5.5). However, their broad connections di-
lute their impact on specific co-purchases. A high-degree product purchase is unlikely to significantly
impact the acquisition of a connected item. For instance, a staple product like milk may activate many
products but lacks strong influence on any single one.
In contrast, products in cliques where every product is connected to each other exhibit stronger lo-
calized influence. Within these cliques, connections represent meaningful purchase dependencies. By
focusing on the nearest neighbors of expiring items in these cliques, we target products with stronger
co-purchase potential. This strategy prioritizes quality over quantity in influence spread, aiming to
activate products that are more directly linked to expiring items.

5.6
Extracting Core Relationships
After weighting the FBT network, we applied backbone extraction with the disparity filter to reduce
the number of edges while preserving its most meaningful connections [44]. This process reduces noise
and highlights meaningful structural patterns, enabling a clearer interpretation of core relationships.
The disparity filter is applied to a weighted network, where weights represent the strength of the
connections. For undirected graphs, the method evaluates each edge weight relative to a null model,
which assumes that edge weights are evenly distributed among a node’s neighbors. Each edge weight
wij is normalized relative to the total strength of the node si, which is the sum of all weights connected
to the node. The significance of an edge is determined by calculating a p-value under the assumption
that weights are uniformly distributed. The p-value for the edge between nodes i and j is given by:
pij = 1 −(1 −wij
si
)ki−1
(5.5)
where ki represents the degree of node i. This formula estimates the likelihood of observing an edge
weight wij or greater under the null hypothesis. Edges with p-values below a predefined significance
threshold, in our case p < 0.05, are retained in the backbone network, while others are removed. This
ensures that only disproportionately strong connections are preserved. For directed graphs, the method
can be adapted by differentiating between in-strength and out-strength for each node, treating the
directions of edges separately.

allowing for a focused analysis of the underlying structure. In the case of the network of FBT items,

5.7
Exchanging Products with Expiring Items
To evaluate the potential impact of the price reduction of expiring products on basket prices, we per-
formed an analysis based on the two weighted networks and expiring products data. The goal of this
exploration was to see if there are price differences in purchases using a similar expiring product as
substitute. Products are substitutable if a customer can replace one product by the other, while they
are complementary if they tend to be bought together [47].
Using the weighted SP network, all offers within a commune for the first month were identified. For
each expiring product, its most similar neighbor (substitute) was determined based on the highest edge
weight. This neighbor served as the closest alternative to the expiring item.
The identified substitute was then located in the FBT network to integrate consumer purchasing pat-
terns into the analysis. The price of the similar product and all its successors (outgoing degrees) in
the FBT network were summed to calculate the original basket price, reflecting the total cost of items
commonly purchased together. The average number of successors was 10 items. This original basket
price was then compared to a new basket price involving an expiring product instead of the substitute.
The analysis next considered the discount percentage applied to the expiring product. Using food waste
data, the average discount percentage was calculated by summing all reductions applied to the product
over the month and dividing this sum by the total number of instances, accounting for variations in
price reductions. This calculated discount percentage was deducted of the expiring product’s full price
in the network. The reduced price was then added to the cost of the substitute’s successors in the FBT
network, resulting in the new basket price. By comparing the original with the new price, the analysis
quantified the impact of discounted expiring products on the total basket value.
This analysis focused on the communes of Copenhagen and Vejle to investigate potential geographic
differences in prices. Figure 5.8 illustrates the effect of substituting a single product with its expiring
counterpart. While a more substantial price difference is observed for higher cost products, no signif-
icant geographic variation emerges. On average, consumers could save 6.29 DKK in Copenhagen and
6.42 DKK in Vejle by exchanging one product of their basket with an expiring one.

5.8
Discussion
This section synthesizes the findings from the analysis of product networks and explores their broader
implications for reducing food waste through data-driven strategies. It focuses on translating these
insights into actionable approaches, such as leveraging co-purchase patterns to design targeted promo-
tions and identifying alternative products through the SP network to influence customer behavior.
Nudging customers
The method of exchanging products with expiring ones can be used for the BilkaToGo online shop and
stores with the Scan&Go functionality.
Scan&Go is a feature that allows customers to streamline their shopping experience by using their
smartphones to scan items as they shop. As they pick items, customers scan the barcode of each prod-
uct and see the price and a running total of their purchases. Customers can bag their items immediately
after scanning, skipping the traditional checkout process where items are scanned at the register, which
reduces waiting time and contributes to digitization in retail. 93% of the stores of Netto, Føtex, and
Bilka in Denmark include this feature.
The approach described in section 5.7 can be used for self-checkout systems like Scan&Go and online
shops. When users scan or add a product to their basket, they can be nudged to buy a very similar
product that is about to expire instead, possibly saving money. Nudging is a concept in behavioral
science that involves subtly influencing people’s choices or behaviors in a predictable way without re-
stricting their freedom of choice or significantly altering economic incentives. It is based on the idea
that small changes in the way choices are presented can guide people toward decisions that are better
for themselves or society, while still allowing them to opt out or choose differently. Similar to a study
nudging people to buy more sustainable groceries [5], small notifications in the app or on the website
can help to sell expiring products faster and guide attention towards them. This can also contribute
to the exploration of new products, since customers tend to repeatedly buy the same products [35].
This behavior aligns with the concept of coherency maximization, where preferences adapt to match
past choices, making customers less likely to explore new options over time. However, certain factors
as coupons or discounts can temporarily encourage exploration [35].
Moreover, products identified as influential through clique-based or nearest-neighbor strategies (sec-
tion 5.4) are strong candidates for targeted discounts. Products often bought together are considered
complementary [47]. By simultaneously discounting expiring products and their closely associated items,
retailers can create a bundled incentive, encouraging customers to purchase both.
By combining insights from network analysis with dynamic pricing strategies, retailers can nudge cus-
tomer behavior in a way that supports sustainability goals while maintaining profitability. This method
aligns customer incentives with waste reduction efforts, creating a win-win scenario for both businesses
and the environment.
5.9
Limitations
While the analysis provides valuable insights into the dynamics of customer behavior and potential
strategies for waste reduction, there are several limitations to consider that could influence the robust-
ness and generalization of the results.
A significant challenge in this analysis is the inability to join food waste with network data based
on identifiers such as EAN and product ID. Deeper insights into the effectiveness of the strategies could
be provided if the API would also provide the EAN for product suggestion data, which is also available
on their website.
The network data lacks explicit category labels for products, making it diﬀicult to classify products into
meaningful groups based on type or other relevant characteristics. This lack of categorization limits the
==PAGE:52==
40
5 Network Analysis
ability to analyze the effectiveness of strategies across different product categories, particularly when
assessing how product characteristics influence purchase behavior.
The product networks used in this analysis are derived solely from the BilkaToGo online store. As
a result, they do not account for products purchased across other platforms or in physical stores, po-
tentially limiting the generalization of the findings to the broader retail environment. The online-only
nature of the dataset may also skew purchasing patterns compared to those observed for in-store shop-
ping.
The lack of accurate weights in the network data is another limitation. Without weighted edges repre-
senting the strength of co-purchase or similar relationships, the network’s influence dynamics may not
fully capture the actual strength of product associations. This limitation restricts the ability to precisely
model influence spread and prioritize product promotions based on the strength of their connections.
The approach in section 5.7 was based on selecting the most similar products to the expiring ones,
assuming that customers would replace items in their basket with highly similar alternatives. How-
ever, a more realistic scenario could involve selecting a random product from the basket and examining
whether it could be replaced by an expiring one. This adjustment would better reflect customer behav-
ior, where substitutions may not always prioritize the highest similarity but could instead be influenced
by availability or preferences. Moreover, this analysis was based on an average basket size of 11 given
by the data. To increase realism, multiple sizes of baskets should be considered to more accurately
reflect shopping behavior. It could be interesting to explore how factors such as basket size (e.g., bulk
shopping for families vs. single-person households), income levels, dietary preferences, and the tendency
to buy organic products or name-brands might influence substitution patterns and shopping behavior.
These limitations suggest that while the analysis provides useful insights, further research and data
improvements are needed to refine and enhance the models used to predict and reduce food waste.
Addressing these gaps would enable a more comprehensive understanding of the factors influencing
expiring items and consumer behavior in product networks.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, we examined the dynamics of food waste in the retail sector through a multifaceted analy-
sis, employing data-driven and network-based methodologies to uncover patterns and opportunities for
intervention. By focusing on expiring product data from Denmark’s largest retail group, this research of-
fers actionable insights into the factors contributing to food waste and presents strategies to mitigate it.
The findings reveal notable differences in food waste patterns between urban and rural areas.
Ru-
ral communes exhibit higher normalized levels of expiring product offers, particularly in the category
meat and fish. These patterns could be influenced by larger inventories, less frequent deliveries, and
potentially industrial factors, such as the presence of pig farms in rural areas, which could contribute
to the supply chain dynamics. In contrast, urban areas tend to have more diverse product offerings,
reflecting a broader range of consumer preferences and competitive retail environments. These regional
disparities highlight the need for tailored waste reduction strategies, such as optimizing inventory man-
agement in rural areas and promoting diverse expiring products in urban settings.
The network analysis provided a deeper understanding of consumer behavior and demonstrated how
product relationships can be leveraged to accelerate sales of expiring items. Frequently co-purchased
products emerged as strategic touchpoints for influencing consumer behavior. Bundling expiring items
with often bought together products identified in the network or offering promotional discounts could
encourage their purchase, thereby reducing waste. Similarly, the identification of substitute products
through the similar product network enables tailored suggestions for reduced-price alternatives, sup-
porting sustainable purchasing practices.
Moreover, we discovered a reduction in expiring product offers on Saturdays and Sundays.
Retail-
ers may withhold heavy discounts, anticipating higher full-price sales over the weekend, thus missing
the opportunity to promote soon-to-expire products. Given the increased consumer traﬀic on weekends,
offering more expiring products during these days could help reduce food waste.
Synthesizing these results highlights the significance of integrating data- and network-based approaches
in addressing food waste. Regional patterns provide actionable insights into localized waste dynamics,
while network approaches offer scalable, consumer-driven solutions. Together, these perspectives form
a comprehensive framework for designing targeted interventions. This thesis contributes to the growing
field of data-driven sustainability, demonstrating the potential of innovative methods to address press-
ing global issues like food waste.
However, limitations such as the exclusion of fresh produce data and the relatively short study pe-
riod promote further research. Extending the analysis to include seasonal patterns and expanding the
dataset to multiple countries could yield more generalizable conclusions.
Future work
Building on the findings of this thesis, several opportunities exist for expanding the scope and depth of
research into food waste dynamics in the retail sector.
The availability of more comprehensive data remains a critical need for advancing our research. In-
corporating sales data and analyzing a longer time frame would provide a more robust foundation for
==PAGE:54==
42

uncovering seasonal and broader patterns. Furthermore, co-purchase data from physical stores and
additional platforms beyond BilkaToGo would improve this analysis. Such data could also support
real-world testing of proposed interventions.
One promising avenue is to investigate price variations for the same products across different regions
and retail brands. This analysis could explore whether disparities are connected to local income levels
and how these variations influence consumer purchasing behavior and waste.
Additionally, exploring the behavior of distinct buyer groups offers significant potential. For instance,
families making bulk purchases, convenience-focused shoppers with smaller baskets, native versus
foreign-born individuals, name-brand versus no-name product buyers, and those prioritizing organic
items may each exhibit unique purchasing and waste behaviors. Segmenting these groups would allow
for the development of more tailored interventions.
Expanding this analysis to include the carbon footprint of expiring products could also enhance sustain-
ability efforts. Identifying which product categories contribute most to CO2 emissions would address
both waste reduction and environmental impact.
Finally, exploring the nutritional content and ingredients of expiring products could align waste re-
duction strategies with public health goals. Promoting healthy options among discounted items may
encourage consumers to make more sustainable and beneficial purchasing decisions, contributing to
both waste reduction and improved dietary habits.
These directions open up exciting opportunities for interdisciplinary and data-driven approaches, not
only deepening our understanding of food waste but also driving the development of innovative strategies
to address it.
