Master Thesis
Abstract
Cells under conditions of stress such as an injury excrete extracellular vesicles
(EVs), which have the same membrane as the cells. In the resent years these
EVs have been recognized as potential biomarkers for speciﬁc diseases. EVs
from all tissues are present in most body ﬂuids and therefore they are relatively
easy to isolate. However, although a great effort have been made previously to
characterize the EVs, the studies are lacking the systematic and standardized
methods for comparable results.
The goal of this work was to develop these standardized methods, which can
be used for systematic studies of EVs from patient samples.
To achieve this methods for characterisation of EVs were developed. This was
done by making a EV array. To make this EVs were ﬁrst produced by activat-
ing two known cell lines, Human Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells (HUVEC)
and Human T Lymphocyte Cells (Jurkat), to release EVs. These EVs were
collected and puriﬁed. The size and concentration of the EVs from both cell
lines have been characterized by nanoparticle tracking analysis and atomic force
microscopy.
The EVs were then incubated on a gold surface which was functionalized with
four antibodies, anti-CD31, anti-CD4, anti-CD146, and anti-CD42b. The HUVEC
cells are known to have the surface markers CD31 and CD146, while the Jurkat
cells are known to have the surface markers CD31 and CD4. Anti-CD42b was a
negative control.
The functionalization of the gold surface with antibodies was validated from
x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, as well as the surface markers on the cells
were veriﬁed by a immunochemistry assay.
After incubation of the EVs, the surface was studied with atomic force mi-
croscopy. Here it was found that the EVs from HUVEC cells were binding to
anti-CD31 and anti-CD146 and that there were no EVs on the 2 other antibodies.
The EVs from Jurkat cells turned out to be more difﬁcult to work with, and
therefore these EVs will have to be investigated further.
To standardize the method in which the EV array was made, a ﬂuidic device
was designed and fabricated. However, this requires further development to

Resumé
Celler, der er under stressede forhold, som eksempelvis skade ved sygdom,
udskiller ekstracellulære vesikler (EVs). Disse EVs har samme membran som
cellerne.
I de senere år er EVs blevet anerkendt som potentielle biomarkører for speci-
ﬁkke sygdomme. EVs fra alle vævstyper er til stede i de ﬂeste kropsvæsker og
er derfor relative nemme at isolere. Der er tidligere blevet gjort en stor indsats
for at karakterisere EVs, men disse undersøgelser mangler systematiske og
standardiserede metoder til kunne sammenligne resultaterne.
Målet med dette projekt var at udvikle standardiserede metoder, som kan an-
vendes til systematiske undersøgelser af EVs fra patientprøver.
For at opnå dette blev der udviklet metoder til karakterisering af EVs. Dette
blev gjort ved at lave et EV array. For at kunne lave dette blev EVs produceret
ved at aktivere to kendte cellelinier, Human Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells
(HUVEC) og Human T Lymphocyte Cells (Jurkat), til at frigive EVs. Disse
EVs blev indsamlet og renset. Størrelsen og koncentrationen af EVs fra begge
cellelinier blev målt med nanoparticle tracking analysis.
De indsamlede EVs blev derefter inkuberet på en guld overﬂade, som var funk-
tionaliseret med ﬁre antistoffer, anti-CD31, anti-CD4, anti-CD146 og anti-CD42b.
Disse antistoffer blev brugt, da HUVEC celler har overﬂademarkørerne CD31 og
CD146, mens Jurkat celler har overﬂademarkørerne CD31 og CD4. Anti-CD42b
var en negativ control.

brug af x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, og overﬂademarkørerne på cellerne
blev veriﬁceret med et immunochemistry assay.

anti-CD31 og anti-CD146, mens der ikke blev observeret nogle EVs på de 2
andre antistoffer. EVs fra Jurkat celler viste sig at være mere vanskeligt at
arbejde med, og derfor vil det være nødvendigt at undersøge disse yderligere.

1 Introduction
In the recent years extracellular vesicles (vesicles that are released from cells,
EVs) have gained a very high interest in the biomedical ﬁeld. This is because
EVs have the same membrane as the cells releasing them, and therefore they
can be potential biomarkers for diseases and can provide new understanding of
biological processes and pathogenesis.
It is therefore also possible to track an unknown EV back to the cell of orgin.
This makes it possible to track the stressed cells releasing the EVs and thereby
ﬁnd the place of the disease.
However analysis tools for EVs remain to be optimized and adjusted for their
dimensions and their complex environment.
This work is inspired by two previous studies: One study developed an EV
array [15], where they captured and identiﬁed EVs using 21 different antibodies.
The other study utilized liquid atomic force microscopy (AFM) in order to char-
acterize EV by imaging [23].
This work takes offset in these two studies and tries to combined them.
The aim of this study was to make an array of antibodies that was able to
capture speciﬁc EVs. This was done by ﬁrst producing EVs from known cell
lines, functionalize a gold surface with different antibodies and bring the EV in
contact with the antibodies.
The functionalized gold surface would then be imaged by AFM by using the
non-contact mode to verify on which spots the EV have bound. This will give
accurate information about the size of the EVs.
The work described is visualized in ﬁgure 1 to give a better overview.
The EV array will allow the capture of EVs from different types of cell accord-
ing to their surface markers. In this study two cell lines were used, Human
Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells (HUVEC) and Human T lymphocyte cells
(Jurkat). Those cell lines were used as proof of concept for this study. For the
four antibodies used in this study, it was indeed known from literature which
ones were and were not expressed on the HUVEC and Jurkat cells. As the EVs
are formed from the cell membrane of the cells of origin, they carry the same
surface markers as the cells that released them. Therefore for those EVs it was

2 Background
In this chapter the EVs, antibodies and methods used for characterisation will
be introduced.
2.1 Extracellular vesicles
EVs are a general terms for cell-derived microparticles, exosomes and apoptotic
bodies among others[34]. Since it is very difﬁcult to separate the types of
EVs[12], the term EVs will be used in this project.
EVs are spherical cell-derived vesicles enclosed by a phospholipid bilayer [2].
They have a diameter ranging from 30 nm and up to 5 µm [2, 12] and are
formed from outward blebbing of the plasma membrane of the cell [10, 7, 12].
This outward blebbing of the plasma membrane happens in the resting cells
but it can also be activated by different stimulus on the cell as seen in ﬁgure 2.
Figure 2: This ﬁgure shows the different ways that the cells are releasing EVs.

detect all chemical elements present on a surface, except hydrogen and helium,
and which chemical bonds existing between the elements [35].
A XPS measurement is performed in ultrahigh vacuum. The reason for using

2.6 Atomic Force Microscopy
AFM is a scanning probe microscope method for producing three-dimensional
(3D) images, that has the ability to produce images down to angstrom range [2].
This technique is widely used in science and engineering, because it can measure
surface topography with subnanometer resolution and high accuracy [8, 23]. It
also is able to be operated under different environmental conditions like air,
ﬂuid, gas, vacuum and both at high and low temperatures [8], which makes it
possible for instance to measure living cells in their natural environment.
AFM works by measuring the forces between a tip attached to a cantilever and
a sample immobilized on an atomically ﬂat surface for instance gold [2]. The
sample is placed on a scanner of piezoelectric ceramic that moves the sample in
the x-, y- and z-direction due to the presence of a voltage gradient.
With a very small distance between the tip and the sample attractive or repul-
sive forces will cause bending of the cantilever. These forces comes from the
interactions between the tip and the sample. At the back of the cantilever there
is a laser beam which is reﬂected and then detected by a photodiode. The
bending of the cantilever will therefore cause the reﬂection of the beam to move
and this movement is then detected by the photodiode [2]. This is then used
through a feedback control mechanism to drive the scanner [8].
The setup of the AFM can be seen in ﬁgure 8.
When using this optical beam detection method, the three main elements are
the laser, the mirror, and the photodiode. These are seen in ﬁgure 9.
The AFM can be used in different modes, contact and non-contact mode
amongst other.
In contact mode a sharp tip is scanning over the surface with a very low force.
The movement of the scanner in the z-direction creates a topography image of
the surface [8].

3.1 Introduction
To develop methods for characterisation of EVs, the ﬁrst step was the controlled
production of the EVs. The obvious approach for controlled production of EVs
is to induce apoptosis in known cells in cell culture. This will put the cells
under a lot of stress, and thereby the cells will produce EVs.
Two different cell lines were used in this work. The two cell lines were Human

be found in the supernatant[36].
Jurkat cells are T lymphocytes which are derived from the human peripheral
blood. They are suspension cells and therefore the procedures for culturing the

3.2.1 Methods for HUVEC cell culturing
Medium preparation
The complete medium consisted of F12K-medium supplemented with 10% FBS,
0.1 mg/ml heparin, 0.05 mg/ml endothelial cell growth supplement (ECGS)
and 1% Penicillin Streptomycin with a stock concentration of 10000 unit/ml
Penicillin and 10 mg/ml Streptomycin. All of the materials used for the HUVEC
cells can be seen in Appendix A, table 8.
Cell thawing
The cell culture was started by thawing a frozen cell vial in a 37◦C water bath
for 2-3 minutes. 1 ml of the cell suspension was then transferred to 5 ml of
complete F12K medium in a falcon tube.
The cells in medium were then centrifuged for 5 minutes at 150g. After the
centrifugation the cell supernatant was removed and the cells were resuspended
in 10 ml of complete medium. 5 ml of the cell suspension were transferred into
2 T25 ﬂasks and the ﬂasks were placed in a incubator at 37◦C and 5% CO2 for
24 hours.
After 24 hours it was veriﬁed that the cells had attached to the ﬂask and the
medium was changed.
Change of medium
The medium was changed every second to third day by collecting the super-
natant and centrifuge it for 5 minutes at 150g to pellet the suspension cells at
the bottom of the falcon tube. The supernatant was then removed and the cells
were resuspended in fresh medium before transferring the medium to the cell
ﬂask. For a T25 ﬂask 5 ml of medium were used, and for a T75 ﬂask 15 ml of
medium were used.
Cell passaging
When the cells were covering 80% to 90% of the cell ﬂask, they were passaged
by collecting the supernatant in a falcon tube and then wash the cell monolayer

the cell ﬂask to coat the cells. Half of the trypsin/EDTA was removed and the

The reason for adding trypsin/EDTA was, that trypsin is an enzyme, which

cells with a cooling rate of 1◦C/minute.
The freezing box was placed in a -80◦C freezer over night and then moved to a
-140◦C freezer, where they were stored until a new cell vial was needed.

3.3 Investigation of the EVs
3.3.1 Induction and collection
The above mentioned two cell lines were used for production of EVs. To produce
EVs, the cells were seeded in two T75 ﬂasks at a concentration of 5x104 cells/ml.
The two ﬂasks were prepared identically. The cells were incubated for ﬁve days.
The medium was changed at the third day. All the medium used for the EV
production were ﬁltered by a 100 µm ﬁlter before use.
At the ﬁfth day 15 ml of complete medium supplemented with 5 µM of stau-
rosporine were prepared.
The medium was then changed in the two ﬂasks. One ﬂask was incubated with
the medium with staurosporine to make EVs by inducing apoptosis in the cells,
and one was incubated with normal ﬁltered medium to have a control of healthy
EVs. The two ﬂasks were then incubated for around 24 hours. Staurosoporine
induces apoptotic cell death by inhibiting protein synthesis in the presence of
cyclohexamide [14].
After 24 hours the cell supernatant was collected in a falcon tube and cen-
trifuged at 1250g for 5 minutes to remove the larger cell debris by pelleting
them at the bottom of the falcon tube. The cell supernatant was then collected
by taking the upper 14 ml and leave the last 1 ml with the cell debris. The 14 ml
cell supernatant were dispensed in 14 eppendorf tubes with 1 ml in each, for
both the apoptotic EVs and the control EVs. The eppendorf tubes were then
centrifuged at 18800g for 30 minutes to pellet all of the EVs in the bottom of the
tubes. 950 µl of supernatant were then removed and the EVs were resuspended
in 950 µl of ﬁltered PBS. The tubes were centrifuged one more time at 18800g
for 30 minutes and 950 µl of the supernatant were removed.
At last 50 µl of PBS were added to get a total volume of 100 µl. All of the
samples with apoptotic EVs were mixed to get the same concentration in the
entire sample, and then divided in 14 cell vials in aliquots of 100 µl, before they
were frozen and stored at -20◦C. The same was done to the control EV samples.
When producing the EVs they were up-concentrated 10 times in relation to
the concentration in the cell culture supernatant, since there were 14 ml of cell
culture supernatant when starting the process and it ended by having 1.4 ml of

3.4 Discussion
3.4.1 Apoptotic process
When HUVEC cells were submitted to a treatment with 5 µM staurosporine, the
loss of attachment on the surface could be observed already after an hour. After
4 hours the cell were rounded up and had lost a lot of the adhesion area on the
surface. After 27 hours the cells or cell debris were all round and were ﬂoating
around in the medium. This process is consistent with the observations in the
literature [17, 19]. This process works by staurosporine prevent ATP binding
to kinase and thereby inhibit the protein kinase [31]. Further staurosporine is
activating caspase-3 which activates the apoptosis [17].
Staurosporine also induces focal adhesion disruption in endothelial cells. The
survival, adhesion and migration of adherent cells depend on the focal ad-
hesion which are mulicomponent juxtamembrane structures [17]. Therefore
the HUVEC cells are detached from the surface when apoptosis is induced by
staurosporine.
The apoptotic process of the Jurkat cell was not possible to image since the cells
were suspension cells and therefore the previously described loss of attachment
from the cell ﬂask could not be observed. Optical microscopy cannot be used as
easily to observe the ongoing of the apoptosis in the Jurkat cells.
3.4.2 Surface markers
The immunostaining of the HUVEC cells showed positive signals with anti-
CD31 and anti-CD146 as the primary antibodies, and no signal with anti-CD42b
as the primary antibody. The negative control with no primary antibodies
but only the secondary antibody had no signal as well, which was expected
since the secondary antibody binds the primary antibody. Without any primary
antibodies the secondary antibodies were washed away. All of these results
were expected from the literature.
It is seen that the signal from anti-CD31 is weaker compared to the signal
from anti-CD146. A reason for this can be, that anti-CD146 is more active than
Mathilde Granberg
January 2016
56
==PAGE:57==
Master Thesis
anti-CD31. Another reason can be, that the stock concentration of anti-CD31 is
unknown and therefore there can be less antibodies in the anti-CD31 immunos-
taining, which will give a weaker signal.
It can also be that the expression of CD31 on the cell membrane is weaker than
the expression of CD146 and therefore the signal appeared weaker.
The weaker green color between the nuclei and the strong green staining around
the membrane, can be due to the membrane all around the cell is stained. But
the focus is on the level of the nuclei and therefore the out-of-focus signal is
weaker.
The immunostaining of the Jurkat cells show positive signals with anti-CD31
and anti-CD4 as the primary antibodies, and no signal with anti-CD4 and

antibodies used are IgG1 antibodies and will only bind goat anti-mouse IgG1
FITC.

the anti-CD4. This can be due to the same parameters as for the results in

the AFM and the cantilever used for imaging. The imaging technique works

change in surface topography. The image will then start from where the tip
is placed as seen in ﬁgure 22. The cantilever will then follow the shape of the
vesicles and thereby make the bell shape.

4 Surface functionalization
4.1 Introduction
The aim of the surface functionalization was to bind antibodies covalently to
a gold surface and thereby make an EV array, where the EVs would bind
speciﬁcally to some antibodies and not to others.
Therefore a gold surface was ﬁrst cleaned, then functionalized with a linker,
and at last functionalized with 4 different kinds of antibodies, where 2 were
expected to bind the vesicles and 2 were used as negative controls.
The antibodies used were anti-CD31, anti-CD146, anti-CD4, and anti-CD42b.
They were bound in a monolayer in 4 different well deﬁned spots.
In this chapter the functionalization of the surface is described as well as the
testing of the functionalization. The functionalization was tested by AFM and
XPS.

4.2 Methods used for surface functionalization
The gold substrates which were functionalized were made by coating of a silicon
wafer with gold to a thickness of 10 nm. This procedure was performed in
the DTU danchip clean room facilities by Mark Holm Olsen. After the gold
deposition, the gold substrates were cut into pieces of 35x35 mm and 35x5 mm.
In the functionlization of the surface antibodies were immobilized on a gold
surface using the following procedure which took two days.
On the ﬁrst day the gold substrate was washed with ethanol and Milli-Q water.
Then it was cleaned in a UV/ozone photoreactor (PR-100, UVP) for 3x10 min-
utes and washed in Milli-Q water in between. The UV/ozone photoreactor
removes organic compounds from the surface by using UV light and having an
ozone environment [33].
Then a linker consisting of 1 mg sulfo-LC-SPDP (sulfosuccinimidyl 6-[3´ -(2-
pyridyldithio)-propionamido] hexanoate) in 500 µl of PBS was prepared. 100 µl
of the linker suspension were dispensed on the gold surface to cover all of the
surface on which the antibodies would be functionalized. This was incubated
for 20 minutes at room temperature in a humid chamber. The humid chamber
was made of three petri dishes. The bottom of one big petri dish was covered
with Milli-Q water and a smaller petri dish was placed inside the big one. The
last petri dish was used as a lid.
After incubation the surface was rinsed with ﬁltered PBS and Milli-Q water
before carefully blown dried. All the ﬁltered PBS used when working with the
EV assay were ﬁltered through a 100 µm ﬁlter.
Then the antibodies were diluted in ﬁltered PBS to get a concentration of
250 µg/ml. The proteins to immobilize were 4 antibodies; anti-CD31, anti-CD4,
anti-CD146 and anti-CD42, and 5% BSA in ﬁltered PBS as a negative control.
1.5 µl of each probe was deposited in separated spots on the gold surface.
On the gold surface there was a numbered grid of silicon seen in ﬁgure 24. The
grid consisted of 5x5 mm squares numbered with a letter and a number, as M2
in ﬁgure 24B. This was divided in smaller squares in the size of 200x200 µm also
numbered with a letter and a number. These squares were further divided into
4 squares. The location of the spots was therefore found by using a microscope

4.4 Discussion
4.4.1 AFM
The functionalized surface imaged by AFM showed that there were something
on the surface, which was around 10 nm in height. This must be expected when
functionalizing with ﬁrst a linker and then antibodies. Further some organic
contamination from the environment must be expected when working with the
sample and when imaging.
On the 2D images with enhanced color corresponding to the 3D images of the
functionalized surface, it was easier to see the height differences on the surface.
This test of the functionalization shows that there were something on the surface,
but nothing that can be mistaken for EVs. Therefore the functionalized gold
surface can be used to image the binding of the EVs by AFM.
4.4.2 XPS
The results of the black UV/ozone cleaned surface from both measurement
rounds showed a little more C and O on the surface in the second round. This
could be that the surface in the second measurement has been exposed more to
the environment than the surface from the ﬁrst measurement.
The measurements of the surface content with XPS shows that cleaning with the
UV/ozone photoreactor is the most effective cleaning compared to the RCA-1
cleaning.
When adding the linker to the UV/ozone cleaned surface, the organic content
on the surface increased. Therefore it is known that when functionalizing with
the linker, something is bound to the surface. Because of the absence of sulfur
(S) on the surface it can not be conﬁrmed if the linker is really bound onto
the surface or if the increase in C is due to some contamination during the
treatment. If S was on the surface, there would not be any doubt if the linker
was on the surface, because the linker consist of Sulfo-LC-SPDP where SPDP
are a group of amine- and sulfhydryl-reactive heterobifunctional crosslinkers
that produce disulﬁde-containing linkages [44].

5 Fluidic device
5.1 Introduction
The aim of making a ﬂuidic device was to be able to control the ﬂow when
introducing the EVs on the functionalized surface and when rinsing the surface
in order to achieve a reproducible ﬂow proﬁle over the functionalized surface.
Moreover the ﬂuidic device was thought to be used also as a measurement
chamber for liquid AFM imaging.
The device was designed so that the gold surface was ﬁxed and a channel
covered all the functionalized spots.
The channel had one inlet and one outlet placed around the functionalized
spots. This can be seen in ﬁgure 30.
Figure 30: The channel in the ﬂuidic device with an inlet, an outlet, and the functionalized
spots.
The device should enable the EV introduction, rinsing steps and the imaging
by liquid and dry AFM. This means that the channel had to be removed to
access the surface for imaging by AFM. For the liquid AFM the surface had to
be covered in liquid meaning the device should not leak.
The ﬂuidic device consisted of a frame made of Poly(methyl methacrylate)
(PMMA) and a channel made in Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). All PMMA
parts were cut by using a milling machine and a CO2 ablation laser.
In the corners of all the PMMA parts there were holes for screws. These holes
were 2 mm in diameter.
In this chapter the production and the design process are described. Two
generations of the device were made and tested for leak.

Then the mixture was degassed for approximately 1 hour and ﬁlled into a mold
made in PMMA and baked at 70◦C for 3 hours. To assure a complete curing
and hardening, it stayed in the mold for 2 more days at room temperature.

The geometry of the ﬁrst generation device was mainly focussed towards fa-

In this part the design and fabrication of two generations of a ﬂuidic device
was described. The testing of both generations of the devices was described too.
Here all of the tests for leakage in the devices were successful, and based on
that, it was decided to use the ﬂuidic device in further work.

6.2 Material and methods
The EV arrays were made both with and without the ﬂuidic device. In both of
the cases the surface was functionalized according to the procedure described
in section 4.2, before EVs was deposited on the surface.
The EVs used in this experiment were either from apoptotic HUVEC cells or
apoptotic Jurkat cells.
6.2.1 EV array with ﬂuidic device
When using the ﬂuidic device the ﬁrst step was to assemble the device. The
functionalized gold substrate was placed in the holder and the PDMS part was
placed with the channel on top of the antibody spots.
The HUVEC EVs were diluted 10 times in PBS ﬁltered through a 100 µm ﬁlter.
The diluted EVs were ﬁlled in a syringe. The syringe was connected to the inlet
of the channel by using a tube and then placed in a syringe pump. Another
tube was connected to the outlet to collect the waste. The tubes used had an
inner diameter of 0.2 mm and an outer diameter of 1.6 mm.
The ﬂow rate was set to 20 µl/min, and the pump was turned on for 5 minutes
to ﬁll the channel. Then the EVs were incubated on the surface for 30 minutes
at room temperature.
In the meantime, the syringe was exchanged with another one ﬁlled with ﬁltered
PBS. This was used to rinse the channel after the incubation. For the rinsing the
pump was turned on for 4 minutes with a ﬂow rate of 20 µl/min, and then the
ﬂow rate was turned up to 40 µl/min for 2 minutes.
The ﬂuidic device was then separated and the gold substrate was rinsed with
Milli-Q water by disposing 50 µl slowly on the surface and absorbing it with
a tissue paper whereafter the surface was blown dried. The sample was then
imaged by AFM (NX20, Park systems).
The analysis software XEI was used for ﬂatten the images by using a ﬁrst
regression order line ﬁt excluding objects higher than 10 nm.

6.4 Discussion
6.4.1 HUVEC EV array with ﬂuidic device
When using the ﬂuidic device for the HUVEC array the results were very varying
from one EV array without any EVs to another one with EVs all over the surface.
This shows that the rinsing with the ﬂuidic device was not continuous even
though the settings were kept the same throughout all measurements.
When using the second generation ﬂuidic device for ﬁlling the channel with
EVs and for rinsing it after the incubation, the device was leaking even though
the device was tested for leaks. Doing leak tests made in section 5.4.3 no leak
was determined and therefore the device was use for the EV arrays. But the
leak tests did not into take account that the gold surface would be partly wet
when assembling the device after functionalization. Therefore the sealing of the
PDMS channel onto the gold surface was less efﬁcient.
It was tried to seal the ﬂuidic device by using clamps, however without success.
Due to time restrictions, it was decided not to use the ﬂuidic device.
6.4.2 HUVEC EV array without ﬂuidic device
When making the HUVEC EV arrays without the ﬂuidic device the results were
more promising. When comparing all AFM images of the HUVEC EVs it can
be concluded that there were EVs on the spots, where a positive response was
expected, and the negative spots were free from EVs.
On the images in ﬁgure 41 and 42 from the EV array made of November 11,
2015, it can be seen that the EVs on anti-CD146 functionalized spot were smaller
and there are less compared to the EVs on the anti-CD31 functionalized spot.
A reason for this can be that the anti-CD146 functionalized spot was imaged
last, therefore the EVs could have been in dry environment for a up to 5 hours
before measured. Therefore, some of them may be collapsed, resulting in a
smaller observed size.
This also is the reason for the EVs on the EV array are smaller compared to the
visualized EVs in section 3.3.2, ﬁgure 14.
The EVs on the EV arrays also are much more stuck together than the EVs
Mathilde Granberg
January 2016
107
==PAGE:108==
Master Thesis
visualized in ﬁgure 14. This also can be due to the long waiting time in dry
environment before the imaging. Another reason can be that the EV array
of this section is made on a functionalized gold surface while the visualized
EVs are imaged on a silicon surface. It is furthermore possible that at long
incubation time, EVs exhibit a stronger adhesion to the gold surface and loose
their spherical shape.
Due to the last option the incubation time was decreased to 15 minutes when
making the EV arrays with the Jurkat EVs.
6.4.3 Jurkat EV array without ﬂuidic device
When looking at the AFM images of the EV arrays treated with gentle washing
the EVs have the same or larger size than the visualized EVs in section 3.3.2, ﬁg-
ure 15. This can be due to the decreased incubation time or the salt crystals that
were distributed over the surface, creating a more physiological environment.
In future research on EV arrays it will be advantageous to use liquid AFM which
hopefully will enable a more realistic size measurement of the EVs, because
they will be in physiologic environment as for the NTA measurements, which
gave a larger mean size of the EVs than the height measurements from AFM.
In this part the EV arrays of HUVEC EVs with and without the ﬂuidic de-
vice were made, as well as EV arrays of Jurkat EVs.
The HUVEC EV arrays with the ﬂuidic device did not give any useful results
because of leaks in the device. Therefore the HUVEC arrays were made without
the ﬂuidic device. This gave some usable results which corresponded to the
literature. The results could have been better due to the lack of consistence in
the rinsing part done by pipetting.
The Jurkat arrays were made without the ﬂuidic device and the EVs turned out
to be sensitive to the cleaning. The ﬁrst Jurkat array was made with the same
rinsing procedure as the HUVEC EV arrays, but this washed away all of the
EVs. The second EV array was cleaned very gentle compared to the ﬁrst one,
resulting in the EVs were not rinsed of any of the surface.

7 Conclusion
In this work methods for characterisation of EVs from HUVEC and Jurkat cells
have been studied.
Both cell lines were successfully cultured and EVs were isolated from both
apoptotic cells and healthy control cells. The EVs of both cell lines where
imaged by AFM, showing their surface topography, allowing an estimation of
their size.
The presence of four different surface markers on the surface of the cells was
investigated by a immunostaining assay. The HUVEC cells had the surface
markers corresponding to anti-CD31 and anti-CD146, while the Jurkat cells had
the surface markers corresponding to anti-CD31 and anti-CD4. Anti-CD42b
was shown to be negative on the surface of both types of cell and was then
further use as negative control.
The size and concentration of the EVs from both the HUVEC and Jurkat cells
were measured by NTA. The HUVEC EVs had a nicely bell shaped size dis-
tribution. The mean size of both the apoptotic and control HUVEC EVs were
around 300 nm. The mean size of the apoptotic HUVEC EVs measured by dry
AFM was around 120 nm. Therefore the size measurements of dry AFM images
has to be observed critically due to the missing natural environment of the EVs.
The concentration of the apoptotic HUVEC EVs were larger than the concen-
tration of the control HUVEC EVs, and it can therefore be concluded that the
induction of the apoptotic process in HUVEC cells lead to a stronger release of
EVs.
The size distributions of the Jurkat EVs were very random within each batch,
and the concentrations of the Jurkat EVs were also varying a lot.

the same composition of all surfaces with antibodies. The functionalized gold
surfaces were used as EV arrays.

8 Outlook
In future research on methods for characterization of EVs, the NTA measure-
ments and EV arrays of Jurkat EVs would have to more consistent for the
methods to work on EVs from all kind of cells. Further a method to control,
that the apoptotic process of the Jurkat cells works would be appreciated.
It is very importent that the characterization methods works on EVs from all
cell lines because with accurate measurements of the EVs, they may be used as
a diagnostic tool and possibly prognostic and predictive biomarkers in various
diseases [2].
But before the methods for characterization of EVs will come to that, the ﬂuidic
device will have to be redesigned to avoid leaks. Then the best rinsing ﬂow rate
will have to be found for EVs from all kind of cells. The most optimal would be
to ﬁnd a ﬂow rate that works well with all EVs.
The quality of the EV arrays would also be improved by optimizing the imaging
by liquid AFM. Then the EVs will be in physiologic environment and thereby
the images will get more precise compared to the dry AFM imaging.
When all of the above mentioned work is functioning, the next step would be to
test patient samples on the EV arrays.

9 Summary
In this project it was aimed to develop methods for investigation and characteri-
zation of EVs, because the EVs have been recognized as potential biomarkers
for speciﬁc diseases, especially for autoimmune diseases [7].
Although a great effort have been made previously to identify and characterize
these particles, the studies are lacking the systematic and standardized methods
with which the results could be compared and organized.
The goal of this work was to develop some standard procedures, which can be
used for systematic studies of EVs from patient samples.
To achive this goal two different cell lines (HUVEC and Jurkat) were chosen,
and their behavior has been investigated.
During this work:
• A standard method for reproducible production EVs from HUVEC and
Jurkat cells has been developed.
• A method for characterization of apoptotic EVs by atomic force microscopy
has been developed.
• A method for characterization of the abundance and size distribution of
the induced EVs by nanoparticle tracking analysis.
• A method for antibody functionalization of gold surfaces for speciﬁc
capturing of EVs derived from different cells has been developed.
• A method for characterization of surface functionalization by using x-ray
photoelectron spectroscopy has been developed.
• A ﬂudic device for controlled introduction of EVs to a functionalized
surface and for the rinsing procedure of the surface has been developed.
Further the device was designed for studies of the surface in liquid with
atomic force microscopy.
These methods can be used for further studies of patient samples for investiga-
tion of the correlation between diseases and the origin of the EVs.
