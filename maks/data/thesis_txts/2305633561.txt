Abstract
Managing and planning the process of baggage delivery is an important part of the airport
scheduling. This thesis is a study of how to plan a schedule for short transfer baggage
delivery in airports, that reduces the amount of bags that miss their delivery. This prob-
lem is called the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem. The problem is approached by
trying to minimize the objective through the use of metaheuristics.
Based on departure times from Copenhagen Airports homepage, a problem instance is
generated and solved using the Large Neighborhood and Tabu Search metaheuristic imple-
mented in Java.
The results obtained in this thesis are approximations to an optimal solution to the Short
Transfer Baggage Problem. The results show that the use of metaheuristics is a good way
to approximate hard problems. However, some of the results were not as expected.

Introduction
In the introduction chapter I introduce my thesis by describing the diﬀerent chapters
shortly. First I explain my motivation for working with this particular problem and there-
after describe my problem formulation. Afterwards I give a rough overview of the content
of the thesis and ﬁnally a litterature review in which existing research within this particular

1.1
Motivation
In 2015, a total of 26.610.332 passengers travelled through Copenhagen Airport. More
than 13 million of these passengers departed from the airport, while 2.966.929 of those were
transit passengers [Kastrup Airport]. According to [Turkish Airlines] a transit passenger is
a passenger who stops over for no more than 24 hours on a midpoint between the departure
and destination points and then continues on his or her journey, whether with the same
airline or a diﬀerent one. Copenhagen Airport advises a minimum of 40 minutes transfer
time both domestic and in the schengen area, while advising a minimum og 45 minutes
transfer time on intercontinental ﬂights [Kastrup Airport (2)]. Assuming that most transit
passengers prefer a shorter transfer rather than a long one, this becomes an interesting ﬁeld
to study, because of the diﬀerent uncertainties that airports are faced with, e.g. delays in
inbound arrival time or outbound departure time.
1.2
Problem Formulation
The goal of this thesis is to:
Develop metaheuristic algorithms to provide a suﬃciently good solution to dynamic baggage
scheduling at airports.
Within the subject of baggage scheduling at airports I chose to work with the problem of
scheduling short transfer baggage. Transfering baggage is baggage that arrives at the air-
port on an inbound ﬂight and departs on an outbound ﬂight within a short period of time.
This problem is called the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem. The Short Transfer
Baggage Routing Problem is a minimization problem, which seeks to minimize the amount
of short transfer bags that are not delivered to their outbound ﬂight. The Short Transfer
Baggage Routing Problem described in this thesis can be seen as a combination of these

1.4
Literature Review
As the Short Transfer Baggage Problem is little studied, there is not a lot of literature re-
garding this particular problem. Barth [Barth, T. and Pisinger, D.] studied a case where
the infrastructure and the baggage handling system consists of infeed areas where the in-
bound bags are handled, and handling facilities for the outbound bags. In this case the
airport is responsible for the short transfer baggage process, whereas Clausen and Pisinger
[Clausen, T. and Pisinger, D. 2010] studied a case where the short transfer process is an in-
dependent sub problem of the transfer baggage problem. In [Clausen, T. and Pisinger, D. 2010]
a handling company is tasked with handling the short transfer baggage.
The Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem can be treated as both a static and a dy-
namic problem. The static problem is static in the sense that all relevant data, such as the
arrival time of inbound ﬂights and departure time of outbound ﬂights, is known. In the
dynamic problem not all relevant data is available when the routing process begins and
the data can change after the initial routes are made, which means that a current solution
to the problem might turn out to be infeasible or suboptimal later on. The static Short
Transfer Baggage Routing Problem can be seen as a combination of diﬀerent variants of the
Vehicle Routing Problem, which is a very well-studied ﬁeld in Operations Research, that
tries to ﬁnd the optimal set of routes for a given set of vehicles to satisfy a set of customers.
The application of metaheuristics on diﬀerent variants of the VRP has also been proposed
in several papers over the years. Paul Shaw proposed the Large Neighborhood Search in
1998 for solving routing and scheduling problems. In [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S. 2007], an
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search heuristic was introduced as a general metaheuristic
for solving vehicle routing problems. The diﬀerent between the heuristic proposed by P.
Shaw and the one in [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S. 2007] is that the Adaptive Large Neigh-

2.1
Introduction
The problem considered in this thesis is a revised model of the Short Transfer Baggage Rout-
ing Problem proposed by Clausen and Pisinger [Clausen, T. and Pisinger, D. 2010]. This
is the problem of transporting baggage for passengers travelling with connecting ﬂights
in an airport. Passengers travelling with connecting ﬂights or transfering passengers, are
passengers that arrive at the airport with an inbound ﬂight and depart on an outbound
ﬂight shortly after. The baggage that these passengers carry is not transported to the
arrival hall like regular baggage, but instead to a diﬀerent location where it is transported
to either the outbound ﬂight or a baggage handling station. In this thesis the handling
stations are disregarded or assumed non existing and the transfer baggage can only be
delivered directly to the departing ﬂight.
The process of picking up and delivering transfer baggage depends on the infrastructure of
the airport and the problem of transporting transfer baggage can be formulated in many
diﬀerent ways depending on the airport. In this thesis the following setup is considered:
A company is explicitly assigned with handling the transfer baggage. The company has
a single depot where the baggage they need to handle is brought by an outside company
that services the inbound ﬂights. The company is then responsible for transporting the
baggage to the corresponding outbound ﬂights. The transportation of bags is done through
the use of a ﬂeet of homogeneous vehicles. These vehicles are dispatched multiple times
throughout the day and each route starts at the depot where the bags are loaded onto the
vehicle. Afterwards the vehicle delivers the bags to their corresponding ﬂights and returns
to the depot to prepare for another route. When returning to the depot the vehicle is either
given a new route immediately or instructed to wait for more bags to become available at
the depot.
The problem is to plan the routes for each vehicle such that the all of the bags are delivered

As mentioned in [Clausen, T. and Pisinger, D. 2010] this problem is NP-hard since it con-
tains the Hamiltonian Cycle Problem. A Hamiltonian cycle is a route that visits each node
exactly once. In the case where all time windows are open and only one vehicle is available
for a single route, the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem becomes a Hamiltonian
Cycle Problem. The problem of ﬁnding a Hamiltonian cycle is NP-complete as stated by
Garey, M. and Johnson, D (1979).
According to [Burke, E., and Kendall, G. 2014] a problem P is NP-hard if it is atleast
as hard as any other problem in NP. More formally, a problem is NP-hard if an NP-
complete problem P0 exists such that every instance of that problem can be reformulated
into an instance of P in deterministic polynomial time. P must be just as hard as P0
since it in some sence "includes" P0. Because the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem
"includes" the Hamiltonian Cycle Problem we know that it is NP-hard.

Metaheuristics
This chapter presents the term metaheuristics.
Metaheuristics are frameworks used to
develop heuristic optimization methods. Afterwards the two chosen metaheuristics Large
Neighborhood Search and Tabu Search are described.
3.1
Introduction
Since we do not know how to compute the solution to NP-hard problems in polynomial
time, we have to settle for approximate solutions [Burke, E., and Kendall, G. 2014]. This
means that if the problem becomes big enough it will not be possible to ﬁnd the optimal
solution within reasonable time using an optimization algorithm.
"Algorithms are conceived in analytic purity in the high citadels of aca-
demic research, heuristics are midwifed by expediency in the dark corners of
the practitioner’s lair ... and are accorded lower status" [Glover, F. 1997]
Heuristics can be used to solve problems that are somewhat impossible or impractical to
solve using mathematical models or optimization algorithms. Heuristic methods are used
to speed up the process of ﬁnding a satisfying solution to these problems, and are there-
fore ideal for solving NP-hard problems. Although an optimal solution is not guaranteed
using heuristics, it is possible to ﬁnd a suﬃcient solution within a reasonable amount of
time. These heuristics are often very speciﬁc and problem-dependant methods, while meta-
heuristics are problem-independent. In Sörensen and Glover [Sörensen, K. and Glover, F.]
metaheuristics are deﬁned as follows:
A metaheuristic is a high-level problem-independent algorithmic framework
that provides a set of guidelines or strategies to develop heuristic optimization
algorithms. The term is also used to refer to a problem-speciﬁc implementation
of a heuristic optimization algorithm according to the guidelines expressed in
such a framework.
More formally, what this means is that a metaheuristic is a master strategy that guides
and modiﬁes other heuristics to produce solutions beyond those that are normally gener-
ated when searching for local optimality [Glover, F. and Laguna, M. 1997]. Metaheuristics
generally perform better than simple heuristic algorithms.

Metaheuristics
Most metaheuristics algorithms use diversiﬁcation and intensiﬁcation to search for an op-
timal solution [Blum, C. and Roli, A. 2003]. Diversiﬁcation is meant to diversify the gen-
erated solutions to explore the solution space on a global scale rather than a local scale.
This is to try to prevent the algorithm from getting stuck on a local optimum. Intensiﬁ-
cation is meant to focus the search locally to see if a good solution is found in that part of
the solution space. Balancing the diversiﬁcation and intensiﬁcation is essential to making
global optimality achievable, since intensiﬁcation is used to narrow in on an optimum,
while diversiﬁcation allows the algorithm to escape from local optima.
An example of a local search algorithm is the hill climber, which starts with an initial
solution and then attempts to ﬁnd a better solution by changing a single element from the
neighborhood, if this element proves to provide a better solution a new solution is made
and the process is repeated untill no more improvements can be found. This is shown in
Algorithm 1.
Algorithm 1 Hill Climber Algorithm
Function HillClimber()
select initial solution s ∈solution;
while time-limit not reached do
select s∗∈N(s);
if f(s) < f(s∗) then s = s∗;
end if
end while
return s;
The hill climber algorithm does not diversity the solution, but instead only intensiﬁes it.
This means that it is a good algorithm for ﬁnding a local optimum, but not necessarily
the global optimum unless the problem is convex.
3.2
Choosing the Right Metaheuristic
The No Free Lunch Theorem of Optimization by Wolpert and Macready [Wolpert D., and Macready, W. 1997
says that all algorithms for optimization will perform equally well when averaged over all
possible functions. Looking at the hill climber mentioned in section 3 and the hill descen-
der it might seem counterintuitive that these two algorithms should perform equally well.
It is obvious that the hill climber should perform better on maximization problems and the
hill descender should perform better on minimization problems, but over all possible func-
tions they are equals. This means that there is no general-purpose universal optimization
strategy, and the only way one strategy can outperform another is if it is specialized to the
structure of the speciﬁc problem under consideration [Ho, Y. and Pepyne, D. 2001]. This
is interesting, because it means that the metaheuristic chosen to solve a given problem is
not irrelevant.
The Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem has a similar structure to the Vehicle Rout-
ing Problem with Time Windows. The Vehicle Routing Problem with Time Windows is a
well-studied problem with a lot of litterature supporting the use of diﬀerent metaheuris-

Paul Shaw (1998) and Tabu Search, which was proposed in its current form by Fred Glover

and Tabu Search was chosen because of its popularity and good results in litterature

3.3
Large Neighborhood Search
Large Neighborhood Search metaheuristic was ﬁrst proposed by Paul Shaw (1998). Unlike
Tabu Search, described in the next section 3.4, which deﬁnes the search neighborhood by
relocating part of the solution, the Large Neighborhood Search deﬁnes its neighborhood by
using destroy and repair methods to create a new solution. The idea behind this approach
is to destroy parts of the current solution and recreate it diﬀerently, to easily navigate
the solution space [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S. 2010]. The destroy method removes part of
the solution, while the repair method tries to recreate the solution. The destroy method
is often stochastic such that diﬀerent parts of the solution are destroyed in every call of
the destroy method and the repair method is often a greedy construction algorithm. The
neighborhood N(s) to the solution s is the set of solutions that can be reached by ﬁrst
applying a destroy method and then a repair method [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S. 2010].
The neighborhood N(s) is typically a very large neighborhood.
An example of the destroy and repair on a simple Vehicle Routing Problem is illustrated
in Figure 3.1. The destroy method in this example could simply remove customers from
the solution at random, while the repair method could be a greedy insertion heuristic that
reinserts the customers into the routes again. An example of a greedy heuristic, could be
one that scans all possible insertions of the unvisited customers and chooses the lowest
cost insertion, repeating this until there are no more unvisited customers.
The Large Neighborhood Search heuristic has several advantages. The large neighborhoods
and diversity of these, ensures that the heuristic explores a large part of the solution space.
This means that the heuristic can often avoid being trapped in a local optima. This makes
the Large Neighborhood Search very suited for tightly constrained optimization problems,
such as the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem, where heuristics with small neighbor-
hoods would otherwise get trapped in a local optimum [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S. 2007].
Where the Tabu Search described in next Section 3.4 only tries to exchange two variables,
the Large Neighborhood Search is able to change many variables at a time, giving it a much
bigger neighborhood of possible feasible solutions.

3.3.1
Destroy methods
For each iteration of the Large Neighborhood Search a destroy heuristic and a repair heuris-
tic is applied to the problem, thus destroying and recreating the solution anew. The de-
stroy heuristic is a very important part of deﬁning the neighborhood. As described in
[Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S 2006] the destroy and repair methods are imperative to the
performance and robustness of the Large Neighborhood Search. Many diﬀerent methods
have been proposed and used throughout the litterature. P. Shaw (1998) used the Shaw
removal heuristic that measures the relatedness of diﬀerent requests. The idea is to remove
requests that are similar in nature, in the attempt to shuﬄe these to create new solutions.
The relatedness in the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem could be deﬁned as the
diﬀerence between baggage arrival time ui and the time window overlap.
In [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S 2006] seven diﬀerent destroy heuristics are introduced. These
are random removal, worst removal, related removal, cluster removal, time-oriented re-
moval, historical node-pair removal and historical request-pair removal. This thesis uses the
random removal and worst removal method described in [Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S 2006].
3.3.2
Repair methods
Repair methods are used to reconstruct the solution after parts of it have been removed
by a destroy method. These heuritics can either reconstruct the solution sequentially or in
parallel. The sequential insertion heuristic rebuilts the solution one route at a time, while
the parallel insertion heuristic can rebuild several routes simultaneously. The insertion
heuristic presented in this thesis is a parallel.
3.4
Tabu Search
Tabu Search is a local search-based metaheuristic designed to solve combinatorial opti-
mization problems. It is designed to allow local search heuristics to be able to escape
local optima by allowing moves that does not improve the solution, thus having a higher
probability of ﬁnding a global optimum rather than a local one. The word ’taboo’ (tabu)
originates from Polynesia and it means that something is both forbidden and sacred. The
taboo part of the metaheuristic is that it uses memory to avoid cycling, i.e. revisiting the
"forbidden" solutions, by storing these in tabu lists.
There are diﬀerent strategies to avoid cycling, some of these are remembering all previously
encountered solutions (strict Tabu Search), remembering the last K solutions or the last
K moves that have been performed [Stidsen, T. 2016]. The strict Tabu Search can be very

impractical because it has a high computational cost to remember every visited solution.
Remembering the K last solutions is less costly. If a solution is visited, the tabu tenure
of this solution is then K, which means that this solution can not be revisited again for
K iterations. When the tabu list exceeds K elements, the earliest tabu is removed. The
strategy of remembering the last K moves performed is an eﬃcient way of trying to avoid-
ing cycling. An example of a move is exchanging two nodes i and j in a route. The reverse

For every iteration a tabu-based neighborhood N(s) −T is deﬁned, and the best solution
s′ in this neighborhood is chosen. The old solution s is then set to s′. If the new objective
function f(s) is better than the current best one, f(sbest), then sbest is set to s. Lastly, the
iteration count is increased and the move is added to the tabu list T. If the tabu list T
is full, the earliest added element is removed to add the latest. This is repeated until the
stopping criteria is met. The best solution sbest is returned. The stopping criteria is often
chosen to be a time limit or a limitation on the number of iterations.

Tabu Tenure
A tabu tenure is the length of the tabu list. This means that the tabu tenure is the number
of iterations that something is tabu, e.g. if the tabu tenure is 10 and a solution s′ is added
to the tabu list it will be tabu for the next 10 iterations.
The tabu tenure can be both static and dynamic. The static tabu tenure is set to a ﬁxed
value, while the dynamic tabu tenure reacts according to the search history. An example
of dynamic tabu tenure is a tenure that increases if the same solution is visited again

5.1
Generating an Initial Solution
Starting with a good initial solution s0 is important if you want to reach a good solu-
tion fast. Usually, an initial solution is either found at random or by a greedy construc-
tion heuristic. According to Lourenço, Martin and Stützle (2010) the greedy construction
heuristic has the advantage that it often yields a better initial solution and that a local
search heuristic from a greedy initial solution uses fewer iterations to get to a high-quality
solution. I decided to make a greedy construction heuristic rather than a random insertion
heuristic.
In creating my greedy construction heuristic I took inspiration from [Solomon, M. 1987]
and [Joubert, J. and Claasen, S. 2006]. According to Solomon (1987) tour-building heuris-
tics for routing problems can be divided into sequential and parallel methods. The sequen-
tial construction method construct one route at a time until every ﬂight is inserted or there
are no more feasible insertions, while the parallel construction method construct multiple
routes at a time. I decided to implement a sequential construction heuristic, based on the
results by Solomon (1987) indicating that a sequential construction heuristic proved to be
very successful in terms of ﬁnding a good initial solution.

solution, while the repair method is used to rebuild the solution diﬀerently. In this section
I will describe the destroy methods that I implemented. I used two diﬀerent destruction
heuristics in my implementation, namely random removal and worst removal.

5.2.2
Repair Heuristics
The repair heuristics can generally be considered as either sequential or parallel [Solomon, M. 1987,

rithm 6, rebuilts the problem one route at a time. A parallel repair heuristic rebuilts several
routes simultaneously. I chose to implement a parallel repair heuristic for reconstructing
the routes.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to develop metaheuristic algorithms to provide a suﬃciently
good solution to dynamic baggage scheduling at airports.
Through the study of litterature I was able to get a good insight into the importance
of metaheuristics in computationally hard optimization problems. Based on this study, I
decided to work with two speciﬁc heuristics: Large Neighborhood Search and Tabu Search.
Inspired by the work done by [Shaw, P. 1998; Pisinger, D. and Ropke, S. 2010; Glover, F.
and Laguna, M. 1997] I proposed a Large Neighborhood Search and Tabu Search heuristic
for solving the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem.
In the attempt to provide a suﬃciently good solution to the Short Transfer Baggage Rout-
ing Problem I used the Large Neighborhood Search to minimize the amount of undelivered
bags. To further improve on the solution I attempted to implement the Tabu Search to
minimize the travel time it took to service the vehicle routes.
Prior to analyzing my results I expected the Large Neighborhood Search would produce
increasingly good solutions for a good degree of destruction, q. I also expected the Tabu
Search to improve the solution in regards to wait time and travel time. After the analysis,
I found that the Large Neighborhood Search performed best at a degree of destruction of
70, corresponding to about 30% of the ﬂights, yielded the best results in correlation with
the randomness parameter of 15. The Tabu Search however, did not perform as I hoped
and did not result in any changes in the solution. Whether this is a ﬂaw in the coding
or because the problem is too tightly constraint, I do not know. Apart from that, I also
expected that removing the vehicle capacity constraint from the problem would result in
better solutions. This proved to be incorrect, as the solution got worse for capacities over
75. I suspect that this is because of the design of the insertion heuristic or because of the
structure of the problem.
Unfortunately I did not have the time to implement the dynamic part of the problem,
where the problem changes diﬀerent parameters, to simulate a real-life baggage routing
problem. Even though I did not get to implement the dynamic part of the problem, I
managed to get a good understanding of the Short Transfer Baggage Routing Problem and
got some good results showing how parameter tuning can improve a metaheuristic.

Although I am happy with my results, I believe that real-life data could have made the
