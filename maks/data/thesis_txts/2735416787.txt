Abstract
This thesis investigates the performance of Local Ancestry Inference (LAI) algo-
rithms within datasets characterized by low coverage and ancient ancestries. The
development and application of a novel pipeline, designed to emulate such con-
ditions, not only facilitates the creation of a dataset with controlled parameters
for this specific purpose but also addresses the limitations of existing simulation
tools like MsPrime. While MsPrime is adept at simulating genetic data without
the need for real-world data, it falls short in providing ancestry information for
DNA segments. This project successfully developed a methodology to overcome
this limitation, enabling a more nuanced analysis of LAI algorithms. This project
documents integration difficulties with the Ancestry HMM program, emphasizing
the importance of robust tool design and clear documentation of tool limitations.
In contrast, Recomb Mix, a tool still under peer review, exhibited promising ac-
curacy and efficiency, showcasing the potential for innovative approaches in LAI.
Despite these hurdles, this thesis lays important groundwork for future research in
population genetics, specifically in understanding the complexities of LAI in low
coverage and ancient DNA settings.

Introduction
The human genome can be viewed as a complex mosaic of genetics from varying
ancestries, hinting at a convoluted history of mixing within the our species. These
traces hidden within the DNA inside every one of us and holds the potential to help
answer a multitude of different questions, such as the impact of genetic variants
that lead to disease (Hou et al. [1]), the movements of ancient populations (Martin
et al. [2]) and the results of clashes between them by tracking certain genetic vari-
ants (Pool et al. [3]).
With high-throughput DNA sequencing technologies the amount and availability of
genetic data from diverse populations have made research in this field feasible. Local
Ancestery Inference (LAI) is an important technique for exploring the ancestry of a
species. This method enables the dissection of genomic sequences from an individual
into their constituent segments, each inherited from a specific ancestral population.
By doing so, we gain insights into the ancestral origins of different parts of our
genomes, shedding light on the interplay of migration, admixture, and evolutionary
forces that have shaped our genetic makeup.
4.1
Ancestry and Populations
In the context of population genetics, the classification of humans into distinct
populations based on genetic similarities is critical. Each population will possess a
unique composition of genetic diversity.
Genetic diversity, can be quantitatively assessed with heterozygosity rates. Het-
erozygosity in an individual means that a specific genetic position is different within
the chromosome pair, essentially giving the individual two different versions of a
single gene. High levels of heterozygosity is proportional to a wide range of genetic
traits, which enhances a population’s ability to adapt to changing environments,
resist diseases, and reduce the likelihood of inheriting detrimental genetic disorders.
From a continental perspective, the continental population within Africa exhibits
the highest level of genetic diversity (Gomez et al. [4]). This observation is in line
with the ”Out of Africa” theory, the current scientific consensus that all modern
humans originated in Africa before dispersing to other parts of the world at dif-
ferent times. The population who remained on the continent retained the original
genetic diversity, while those who migrated out of the continent experienced genetic

varying extents.
4.2
Admixed population and Local Ancestry Inference
LAI is based around the idea that our DNA is not a homogeneous blend of our
ancestors genetic material but rather very alike to a patchwork quilt, where distinct
regions of our genome bear the imprints of different ancestral populations.
An example could be the current population in South America that is made up of
genetic material from American indigenous, European, and African populations in
various levels of mixture. The map depicted in figure 1 describes the situation.
Figure 1: Three different continental populations encountering each other coupled
with a simple illustration of what genetic mixing between the three populations
looks like.
The left side of the figure contains a map that indicates via color coded arrows
the migration patterns of the different origin populations. Now as the populations
encounter each other and mix, the right side of the figure illustrates what happens
after multiple generations. The segments with a clear ancestry get mixed up into
smaller and smaller segments as a result of recombination events. This is a good
visualization of why LAI becomes an increasingly difficult task as generations in-
crease and the distance to the reference data from the ancestral populations follows
(Wilson [5]).

4.3
DNA and ancient DNA
The DNA used as the data foundation for this area of research can present some
unique challenges, especially if using DNA extracted from sediments, fossils or mu-
seum samples. The fossils and museum samples can be categorised as ancient DNA
(aDNA) samples, while the sediment DNA (sedaDNA) can be classified as both
environmental DNA (eDNA) and aDNA at the same time, thus also carrying the
challenges faced in both cases. Specifically chemical damage, contamination, and
degradation of fragment length are the main challenges, and of these, the degra-
dation should be addressed the earliest in a bioinformatics pipeline (Orlando et al.
[6]).
The degradation leads to a very short average fragment length (<50bp), where
normal reads typically exceed the read lengths of 75 to 250 base pairs (bp) found
in modern sequencing technologies. When trimming adapters from the reads it is
common for the fragments to be shorter than the read lengths resulting in adapters
being present in a significant portion of the read. Moreover when working with
paired-end reads, after adapter trimming, the forward and reverse reads should be
of identical length and represent reverse complements strands. A key bioinformatics
approach involves merging these complementary reads into one unified sequence.
This not only helps correct possible sequencing inaccuracies but also enhances the
overall data quality. If the genetic template is longer than a single read yet shorter
than the sum of paired reads, the sequences will typically overlap. By merging
these overlapping reads, one can effectively refine the sequence, especially at the
overlapping regions, to reduce errors. In the context of aDNA, this merging step
is what makes piecing together the original DNA strand possible, whether from
single-end or paired-end reads (Lien et al. [7]).
All of this makes it non trivial to obtain data that after alignment can support a
high level of coverage across the entire sequence (>25C). A high level of coverage
ensures high certainty that a reported base has been correctly identified, and in
the case of LAI where single bases can be vital to identifying origin for segments
coverage is paramount for accuracy in the predictions.

Methods
This project utilized computational simulations to investigate genetic diversity and
evolutionary dynamics within simulated populations. By employing state-of-the-
art tools in population genetics, demographic models were defined, and the genetic
makeup of synthetic populations simulated.
5.1
Population Simulation
The integration of the Demes standard for defining demographic models with MsPrime
for efficient genetic simulations was important in simulating synthetic populations
with known genetic makeup. This integration enables the specification of detailed
demographic histories and the simulation of realistic genetic data under matching
parameters.
5.1.1
Demes, demographic model
The Demes standard provides a rigorous framework for specifying demographic
models in population genetics. It allows for the precise and unambiguous definition
of populations, their sizes, growth rates, and migration patterns, thus enabling the
clear communication of complex demographic scenarios (Gower et al. [8]).
Following this standard, and with use of the examples provided on the website
hosted by the Demes paper creators, the demography used here is based on the
Out Of Affrican model proposed by Gutenkunst et al. [9] and originally contains
the current populations of: Yoruba nigerians (YRI), North and Western Europeans
(CEU), and Han Chinese form Beijing (CHB) and is here extended with a native
American example population in the form of the Karitiana (KAR) originating from
the work by Lindo et al. [10]. This outline of demographic histories for the simula-
tions should ensure reproducibility and facilitate ease of integration with simulation
tools such as MsPrime (Kelleher et al. [11]).
Demes has also developed a drawing tool to visualize the demography. Here in
figure 2 the drawing tool was used for visualization of the demography simulated
in this project.

5.1.2
MsPrime
MsPrime, a coalescent-based simulation tool, was used to simulate the genetic data
of synthetic populations based on the demographic models specified through Demes
(Kelleher et al. [11]). This tool supports the simulation of large genomic regions
across multiple populations and incorporates events such as mutations and recom-
binations at specified rates. As MsPrime simulates using tree sequences there is
not kept any actual genetic sequence information on record, so only placeholder
data on sites of interest could be extracted, thus the generated synthetic datasets
were not directly genetic sequences but rather a Variant Call Format (VCF) file
for individuals across multiple populations. This VCF file could then be used for
further analysis.
5.1.3
Generating admixed individuals with ancestry records
Just as MsPrime does not provide a real genetic sequence, nor can use one in sim-
ulations, it does not provide a ground truth in the form of ancestry for segments
of DNA. As a result, the process of admixture is performed outside MsPrime, uti-
lizing the reference population data it generates in the VCF file.
This gap has
been bridged by the work of Eriksen [12], who has developed a method to simulate

identity by descent (IBD). This technique allows for the simulation of recombina-
tion events specifically, facilitating the tracking of DNA segments back to their
origins. Eriksen’s method was adapted to serve as a ”recipe” for creating admixed
individuals from MsPrime’s VCF files.
To create the ”recipe” for the admixed population the Python scirpt ”ancestral
intervals.py” was developed. The script selects random pairs of parents from a pool
that represents the desired range and ratios of ancestral backgrounds. The ances-
tral backgrounds are, as seen from figure 2, CEU, YRI and KAR with contribution
rations of 0.17, 0.33, and 0.5 respectively. These ratios match what would be found
listed in the Demes format for the original MsPrime simulation. Once parents are
selected, the script simulates offspring. When simulating offspring genetic recombi-
nation is taken into account to mimic real life. That means the ancestry of a certain
offspring can be seen as intervals marked as the ancestral populations, even split
on maternal and paternal chromosomes as to include phasing information.

The starting point of this workflow is the VCF file from MsPrime, a simulator
that models the ancestral recombination graphs of samples based on a specified
demographic model, which is used to create a set of FASTA files. This collective of
FASTA file represents the same genomic segment across different individuals, with
both maternal and paternal DNA for all individuals. This simulated data serves as
a proxy for raw genomic data collected from biological samples.
To move from a proxy to a more realistic representation of raw gnomic data the first
step is artificially fragmenting the sequences to mimic the physical breaking that
DNA naturally undergoes. Here, a size distribution reflecting the data harvested
from a Neanderthal specimen is used to mimic the sizes found in an aDNA setting.
Once fragmented, adapters are added to each end of the DNA fragments. These
adapters are short, known DNA sequences that provide the necessary platform for
the fragments to bind to and be identified by the sequencing machine. The adapters
are typically also what enables the creation of a DNA library for sequencing. Sim-
ulation of the actual sequencing process is done using ART, a software tool that
generates synthetic Illumina reads. ART mimics the sequencing output by produc-
ing reads with error patterns and coverage levels that closely approximate those
seen in real Illumina sequencing data.
Once the Illumina reads are generated, the synthetic data now resembles normal raw
sequencing data and is handled as such. Preprocessing includes trimming (removing
adapter sequences and low-quality bases) and merging paired-end reads. One of the
challenges here in an aDNA setting is that the adapters will be part of the reads
from ART, as the DNA fragments are shorter than the read lenght of an Illumina
machine.
The reads are mapped to a reference genome using the BWA aligner. For this sim-
ulation, the reference sequence is based on a Telomere-to-Telomere (T2T) assembly
of human chromosome 1. It has been modified slightly to align with how MsPrime
simulates data.
The output of the alignment is a BAM file, which records the sequence align-
ments against the reference genome. This file undergoes further cleaning to remove
any problematic reads or artifacts, using the standard procedures outlined by the
Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK).
Each sample then has a genomic VCF (gVCF) file created for it. A gVCF compared
to a standard VCF file differs as the gVCF includes information on all the sites in
the genome, not just those with variants. It provides a complete representation of

an individual’s sample, detailing both variant and non-variant sites.
The final step in the pipeline is to combine individual gVCF files using a database
such as GenomicsDB. This integration generates a consolidated VCF file that en-
capsulates variant information across all samples.
5.2.1
Refence Sequence
For the synthetic samples in this project, the T2T-CHM13 human genome’s chro-
mosome 1 was selected as the reference DNA sequence Nurk et al. [13]. To cre-
ate a synthetic reference sequence, modifications were based on variant calls from
MsPrime simulations. Specifically, the reference (REF) column from the MsPrime
output VCF file was utilized to substitute bases at corresponding positions in the
T2T-CHM13 reference. This process resulted in a new ”fake” reference sequence,
that will allign with the information obtained via the MsPrime simulation.
5.2.2
Fragmentation length distribution
To simulate aDNA characteristics, a length distribution based on real DNA se-
quences was utilized. The data came from the Chagyrskaya8 individual, a Nean-
derthal specimen from the Chagyrskaya cave estimated to be around 80,000 years
old. The length distribution, derived from a dataset comprising 1 million lengths
from DNA libraries extracted from a bone sample, enables the simulation of aDNA
by reflecting the physical degradation observed in ancient specimens (Mafessoni et
al. [14]).

5.2.4
ART
Read data is simulated using ART by Huang et al. [16]. This generates both forward
and reverse reads on an Illumina platform, specifically the Illumina HiSeq 2500
System, with reads having a length of 150 base pairs. The setting for defining the
platform type is ‘–seqSys HS25‘.
5.2.5
leeHom
leeHom is utilized for both adapter trimming and merging of sequencing reads by
Renaud et al. [17]. This program employs a probabilistic method, which has been
proved to outperform other approaches for the same task byLien et al. [7].
5.2.6
BWA
At this stage the workflow follows standard NGS procedure, proceeding with merged
reads from different samples in the cohort. The first step for these reads is normally
to be aligned to a reference genome, in this case the previously mentioned synthetic
version is utilized as the reference instead. The alignment is performed using BWA
mem, resulting in binary alignment map (BAM) files (Li [18]).
5.2.7
GATK MarkDuplicates, Haplotypecaller, GenomicsDBImport and
GenotypeGVCFs
The BAM file obtained as the output of BWA needs processing to go from a raw
format to a more efficeint format. The BAM file is sorted to arrange sequencing
reads by their genomic coordinates. This step employs Samtools, which is integral
for manipulating alignments in the SAM/BAM format (Li et al. [19]). Following
sorting, duplicate reads are identified and marked to reduce potential bias in vari-
ant calling. The Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK) provides the MarkDuplicates
tool for this purpose, ensuring that only unique sequencing reads are considered in
downstream analyses (McKenna et al. [20]).
Post sorting and deduplication indexing of BAM files is done utilizing Samtools to
accelerate data retrieval for variant calling. Simultaneously, indexing the reference
genome, including generating a sequence dictionary and a FASTA index with GATK
and Samtools, optimizes it for alignment and variant identification.

Using GATK’s HaplotypeCaller in gVCF mode, variant calling is performed to
capture both variant and non-variant sites across the genome. This approach is
important for accurate variant discovery across a cohort, producing a gVCF file for
each sample.
A sample map correlating each sample identifier to its corresponding gVCF file
is generated. This map is essential for the batch processing of samples through
GATK’s GenomicsDBImport, which integrates the individual gVCF files into a
GenomicsDB datastore. This datastore is what allows joint genotyping via GATK’s
GenotypeGVCFs across all samples to produce a unified VCF file representing the
genetic variants within the cohort (McKenna et al. [20]).

5.4
Tools and programs for LAI
To estimate the ancestry of the synthetic samples produced in this study, two pro-
grams where used.
The programs to do the LAI on the produced samples was
AHMM, and Recomb-Mix.
5.4.1
Ancestry HMM
The AHMM program works by employing a hidden Markov model (HMM) tailored
for estimating local ancestry in admixed populations. It is designed to work with
samples of any ploidy level and can also estimate the time since admixture, however
here it was only used for LAI.
The HMM employed in AHMM is constructed with a state space S, where each state
represents a specific ancestral lineage at a particular locus across the genome. For-
mally, the state space is defined as S = {0, 1, . . . , n}, with each state corresponding
to a different ancestral population. Transitions between states model the switching
of ancestry along the genome, capturing the mosaic structure of admixed individu-
als. The emission probabilities are calculated based on sequencing reads, aligning
with the expected allele frequencies from each ancestral population (Corbett-Detig
Page 17 of 39
==PAGE:19==
Estimation of LAI estimation performance
March 2024
and Nielsen [22]).
5.4.2
Recomb-Mix
Recomb-Mix operates on a site-based approach, supported by graph optimization.
It constructs a compact population graph utilizing only population-level information
extracted from reference data. This graph is used as the basis for a cost function that
assesses the cost of each ancestral path by imposing penalties for any mismatches
and for changes across population paths and chooses the optimal path by lowest
cost (Wei et al. [23]).

7
Discussion
7.1
Clean and processed data
For this thesis the processed data acted as a stand in for real life data. The pro-
cessed data shows promising levels of information retention, at least in a scenario
with as high coverage as in the simulation shown here, and can likely facilitate
investigation on the performance of various tools and methods in aDNA scenarios
at a varied coverage levels. The high levels of information retention, especially in
non reference site data, indicates that the variance of the dataset needed for LAI is
retained within the processing pipeline, at least to the level the simulated coverage
level will allow. However, the resulting VCF file currently contains a mix of phased
and unphased sites and will likely need further processing before further use.
The use of processed data in LAI was not explored due to time constraints, coding
challenges due to unfamiliarity with workflow managers such as Snakemake, and
the initially underestimated complexity of enabling a range of tools to run on less
clean data.
As such the original goal of this project of performing LAI under aDNA scenarios is
unrealized and should be the target of further work, as the foundations for dataset
creation are in place. Further work should be able to leverage the potential to simu-
late datasets with a range of parameters such as different population demographers
and coverage levels, but still maintain an identical implementation requirements for
use in LAI tools.
7.2
AHMM
AHMM is built on a very general method and it should be a great fit for LAI.
The authors report great performance in a range of situation, this was however
not recreated here. This was due to problems with running the program, without
receiving a proportion of non numerical values in the results.
The program itself provides a supportive script that translate a VCF file into the
required input format, this required a bit of modification to work on the synthetic
dataset, and was thus also the target of a good portion of debugging, but with no
success. The VCF file itself was also debugged extensively, but proved to contain
exactly what was expected and represented in the parameters provided to the pro-
gram. Through late contact with the creator of the program AHMM it came to light
that the program was not built for the size of reference provided to it in this project,
Page 24 of 39
==PAGE:26==
Estimation of LAI estimation performance
March 2024
and the large amount of information caused an internal calculation to break. Due to
time constraints it was unfortunately not possible to reproduce a functioning run of
AHMM on the data, as such the results from the program presented here should not
be taken as an indication of the programs actual performance, but rather the result
of a partially failed run. Future work with AHMM should carefully consider the
scale of reference data to avoid this error, and potentially contact the creator of the
program to facilitate a fix for the issue, ensuring that the programs full capabilities
can be accurately represented and fully leveraged.
7.3
Recomb Mix
Recomb Mix is a new addition to the list of tools that compute LAI. The article
for this tool is currently only a preprint and has not been through a peer review
process yet, however the tool itself is freely availble on Github for use.
In this project Recomb Mix is reported to do very well, as when considering how
easily it gets punished, the TPCR is quite high. By analysing the confusion matrices
a fairer representation of its performance is represented. In the confusion matrices
it is reported that Recomb Mix never estimated a position as having the wrong
ancestry on both alleles at the same time, nor did it even once estimate that CHB
was the ancestor for a position, which is correct as CHB was not included as an
ancestor for the simulated admixed population.
The performance of the program seems very promising and could prove useful in
further work, as it would be interesting to see if the level of performance in this graph
based method is retained in an aDNA setting with low coverage and/or a setting
that employs more similar reference populations than the continental approach from
this project.
7.4
Performance and Scoring
The performance of LAI done by the tools included in this project is measured on
TPCR and the confussion matrices presented along side them. AHMM will provide
a result as a list of posterior probabilities that offers a more nuanced estimation of
ancestry, but to accommodate the scoring this will be transformed into a binary
decision based on the highest probability reported. It is essentially reduced to a
”best guess”, and ignoring further provided information.
Further, as this context is for a diploid organism the TPCR requires both the alleles
within an individual to be correct for an increase in score. There is no implemen-
Page 25 of 39
==PAGE:27==
Estimation of LAI estimation performance
March 2024
tation of partial credit scoring for getting one allele correct, such as estimating
YRI/YRI when the truth is YRI/KAR, nor is there an implementation to account
for the degree of error in an estimation, as the current scoring treats an incorrect
guess that confuses closely related reference populations (e.g., CHB for KAR) the
exact same as a guess that mistakes distantly related populations (e.g., YRI for
KAR).
A more nuanced scoring system could help highlight specific parts of the perfor-
mance and gain a more informed interpretation of that performance, however de-
veloping a scoring metric that fairly interprets the performance of a program based
on the highly varied input scenarios seems to be an entire project on its own, and
it this defently out of scope here. So in the interest of time and having an easily
comparable metric scoring on TPCR and presenting more of the nuance from the
estimation in the form of confusion matrices was chosen.
7.5
Run time comparisons
Efficient use of computational resources is a crucial aspect of evaluating LAI tools,
encompassing metrics such as CPU time, potential GPU acceleration, memory de-
mands, and the ability to parallelize tasks. However, due to time constraints this
project did not include a comparison of runtime efficiency despite its importance
as a fair comparison metric. Creation of such a metric for this project would be
unexplored territory and was thus shelved in favor of other aspects, any future work
should however prioritise implementation of this metric due to its importance.
7.6
Snakemake
Implementing a workflow into a workflow manager, notably Snakemake, was the
largest unexpected challenge for this project. The design prioritized user-friendliness
and clarity. However, the anticipated challenges were surpassed in both complexity
and quantity. It is essential to caution future users embarking on similar ventures
for the first time: while Snakemake’s exceptional flexibility and capability to in-
corporate code directly into the pipeline are advantageous, there is a significant
learning curve that can lead to encountering errors related to variable handling
within the code and workflow variables. To mitigate such conflicts, it is advised to
execute all code from scripts, enhancing workflow stability and reliability.

8
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to evaluate LAI algorithms, assessing their performance on datasets
characterized by low coverage and ancient ancestries. To do this a pipeline with the
potential to emulate datasets with those characteristics was developed, and applied
to a select number of individuals with different ancestries. The pipeline was shown
to retain information regarding non reference sites in a high coverage context, but
the retention of information remains untested in a lower coverage setting. Signifi-
cant challenges with the implementation of the program AHMM where experienced.
The reported suitability and performance of the tool for the task of LAI could not
be replicated in this project due to a misunderstanding from the documentaion of
the tool regarding its robustness to the amount of reference data. The difficulties
here underscores the importance of robust tool design and accurate descriptions of
limitations one might encounter. In contrast the implementation of Recomb Mix
as a LAI tool showed promising results. The program is currently a new addition
to the field and still under peer review, it did however demonstrate great accuracy
in estimations. Its performance in regards to avoiding a non contributing reference
population and never being simultaneously wrong on both chromosomes at a certain
position should be highlighted.
In conclusion, testing LAI algorithms under low coverage and ancient ancestry
contexts has proven to be a more complex challenge than anticipated. There seems
to be a great deal of opportunities to develop workflows and tools to improve the
current field.
The work presented in this thesis lays the groundwork for future
investigations of such algorithms but was ultimately unsuccessfully in assessing
them under varied conditions and with a wide representation of tools.
