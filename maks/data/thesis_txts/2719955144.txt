Abstract 
Stem cells can differentiate into any cell of the organism. They are currently receiving plenty of 
attention due to their great therapeutical potential to tackle various disease. Less than 15 
therapies that use stem cells are approved to date by the FDA (Food and Drug Administration) 
and EMA (European Medicine Agency). But there are close to 100 active clinical trials in phase 3 
using stem cells as the therapeutic agent.  
This situation will create in the upcoming years a big demand for stem cell therapies, that need 
to be produced at an industrial scale. In this project, a facility for producing a stem cell-based 
drug has been designed at a conceptual level. 
The designed facility has a total area of 1740 m2. It is capable of producing 30.366 doses a year. 
The project cost would be between 378 and 631 million DKK and it would take around 3 years 
and 8 months to complete it (from design to commissioning). The project cost and time can be 
reduced by using an already existing building rather than constructing a new one. However, this 
has a much bigger impact on the cost than on the schedule. 
There has been a special focus on using closed process systems for production equipment and 
having the maximum level of automation and digitalisation throughout all the facility with the 
current state of the art. It has been found that: there are no available solutions in the market to 
close and automate the first phases of production (thawing and cell expansion) and that the 
pharmaceutical sector is some years behind in automation and digitalisation compared to other 
sectors due to strong regulations and a traditional mindset across the industry. 
This project is anticipated to be a starting point for the design of future pharmaceutical 
production facilities manufacturing stem cell-based therapies with similar features to the ones 
described in this work.

1. Introduction 
Stem cells have received a lot of attention in the last years by the scientific community, but also 
by society in general. Although their potential is enormous, only few therapies are approved to 
date. This potential originates from the key properties of stem cells. 
Stem cells are unspecialized cells of the human body [1]. They have two characteristics that 
make them outstanding: 
• 
They can self-renew, creating a copy of themselves 
• 
They can differentiate into any cell of the organism 
The ability to differentiate a stem cell into – virtually – any cell in the human body, gives them a 
very interesting therapeutic potential. As a result, a non-functional tissue could be replaced by 
a new and healthy tissue produced from stem cells. Among other diseases, stem cells have the 
potential to cure or treat: Parkinson’s disease, type I diabetes, macular degeneration, retinitis 
pigmentosa, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, spinal cold injury, heart failure, cancer or corneal 
disorder [2]. 
However, this technology is still in an early development stage and currently only blood stem 
cell-based therapies are used. Bone marrow or umbilical cord blood is transplanted to patients 
to treat several blood disorders, such as lymphoma or leukaemia [3]. 
Moreover, some injuries or diseases in bones, skin or cornea can be treated by injecting tissues 
that are composed of stem cells [3]. 
Several clinical trials are ongoing now, using stem cells as the foundation for the treatment or 
cure of the disease. According to ClinicalTrial.gov (6th September 2022) – a database of privately 
and publicly funded clinical studies conducted around the world – there are more than 1000 
clinical trials involving stem cells. And almost 100 hundred of them are active and in phase 3.

The time frame of this work is not a coincidence. As mentioned before, the potential of stem 
cells is immense and there are several clinical trials for stem cell therapies that are in late clinical 
phases. It is therefore normal to expect that several of these therapies will get the approval of 
the medical agencies in the near future.  
After the approval of a drug candidate, commercialisation can start. This implies that large scale 
manufacturing will be necessary to meet worldwide demand of stem cell-based drugs.  
Sometimes large-scale manufacturing starts before phase III approval, as the time where the 
drug is protected by the patent, greatly influences the potential earnings. However, this also 
comes with a risk that needs to be evaluated for each case. 
When building a new factory, one of the first phases of the project is to make a conceptual 
design. This concept can be applied regardless of the industry, not only in pharma. In a 
conceptual design the first sketch of an idea to solve a problem is developed. For a pharma 
facility this implies understanding the business case (products to be manufactured and yearly 
capacity) to end up delivering: 
• 
A preliminary layout 
• 
Equipment and technology used to manufacture 
• 
Production process 
• 
Project cost and time estimate 
The work described in this thesis aims at developing a conceptual design for a manufacturing 
facility of a stem cell-based drug. It is an allogenic cell therapy. This means that the cell used as 
a drug does not come from the own patient, but from another person. If the cells were from the 
own patient, it would be an autologous cell therapy. This detail would have enormous 
implications in the design of a production facility. As in autologous therapies, each dose needs 
to be produced individually. Whereas in allogenic therapies, several doses can be produced 
using the same batch. 
To keep the scope of work clear at the beginning and throughout the project, a one-page 
strategy has been created and re-visited frequently with the main goals and drivers of this 
project (see Figure 2). 
As seen in Figure 2, the mission of the overall project is to design and built a production facility 
to cover potential phase III and commercial production of a mesenchymal stem cell-based drug. 
In the phase covered by this report, only the conceptual design of this overall project is covered. 
There is a strong focus on: 
• 
closed systems 
• 
automation and digitalisation 
Moreover, the aim is to use available equipment and minimise the building area to reduce the 
overall CAPEX. The project is a green field1, taking place in Denmark.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSCs) 
There are several types of stem cells. Each type has different features that define their potential 
applications. Stem cells can be classified according to their origin as [4, 5]: 
• 
Embryonic Stem Cells (ESCs): 3 to 5 days after fertilization, the embryo is formed by a 
mass of stem cells. These are Embryonic Stem Cells. These will be differentiated through 
natural processes to form all the tissues to end up becoming a human being. This means 
ESCs are pluripotent (capable of becoming any cell type in the body). These 
differentiation processes are not well known yet and still being studied. Understanding 
them and being able to replicate them in vitro would provide the ability of artificially 
generating any cell in the body. 
However, there is a bioethics conflict with these type of stem cells [6]. Due to the nature 
of ESCs, some are opposed to their use for research and medical purposes, as they 
consider embryos to have an equal moral standing to living persons. Other opponents 
of the use of ESCs consider that, even if embryos are not a human being, they have the 
potential to become one. And that it is unacceptable to use and destroy a potential 
human being. 
• 
Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs): they were recently discovered and allow to get 
almost equal properties to Embryonic Stem Cells. iPSCs are pluripotent (capable of 
becoming any cell type in the body) and created in the lab. To create them, embryonic 
genes are introduced to somatic cells. This will make them return to an ESC-like state, 
where they are capable of becoming any cell type. Some of the bioethical issues are 
overcome, but some new ones arise (e.g. being capable of creating a clone of an already 
existing human). 
• 
Adult Stem Cells (ASCs): these are undifferentiated stem cells living within a specific 
tissue. Their goal is to replace damaged or dead tissue. There is controversy whether 
ASCs are only capable of differentiating into tissue-specific cells or any type of cells. They 
can be classified - according to where they are found - into: 
o Hematopoietic Stem Cells (Blood Stem Cells) 
o Mesenchymal Stem Cells 
o Neural Stem Cells 
o Epithelial Stem Cells 
o Skin Stem Cells 
Among all these types, mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) have received a lot of attention due to 
their potential therapeutic effects: they can be used for different types of cell therapy and have 
regeneration potential [7]. 
Mesenchymal stem cells are stromal cells that can be found mainly in bone marrow, but also in 
many other different tissues (heart, umbilical cord, placenta, adipose tissue, etc.). They are 
multipotent, meaning they can convert to different kinds of cells (but not to any cell, like

Contrary to what one might think, the therapeutic effect of mesenchymal stem cells does not 
come from the injected stem cells turning into cells that are alike to the tissue. From the studies 
conducted to date, it has been proved that the real therapeutic effect comes from the immune 
and molecular response triggered by the presence of the stem cells. However, it has been 
demonstrated as well that some of the injected stem cells might turn into tissue cells. But that 
is a very small part of the dose applied [10]. 
Stem cells have the ability to migrate to the injured site, enhancing the repair process. This 
happens because of the secretions of the stem cells. These secretions consist of extracellular 
vesicles (EV)6 and molecules. The effect they have on the damaged tissues consists of: anti-
inflammatory activity, enhance migration and proliferation of other cells with a repairing 
functionality, favour vascularization7 and, thus, facilitate the arrival of nutrients [11]. All these 
effects promote the creation of an environment around the damaged tissue – mostly with 
molecules from the own body – that increases the regeneration effectivity.

Manufacturing Practices. GMPs define a set of quality standards for drug manufacturing that

agencies in the world – the FDA (The U.S. Food and Drug Administration) and the EMA (The 
European Medicines Agency) – have adopted this term to describe how drug manufacturing 
should be performed. It is mandatory – and enforced by law – that drug manufacturers follow

ensure that any drug is:

Both regulatory bodies (FDA and EMA) are used as a reference by most other medical agencies

FDA and EMA have comparable regulations. Most of the times, they are the same. In fact, it is a 
common practice that a production facility is compliant with both regulatory agencies. This is 
due to the fact that these usually express what should be done, but not how. E.g. “The ATMP

Since guidelines are so vague, decision-making is based on best industry practice, 
recommendations from medical agencies or pharmaceutical organizations (ISPE9 or ECA10), 
science and risk. In fact, most decisions regarding critical GMP systems are taken using a science 
and risk-based approach. 
As FDA and EMA have very similar requirements, this project will only look at European 
regulations to simplify the scope. Taking into account that the production facility that is 
considered in this work will be located in Denmark. If the facility had to be adapted to the 
American regulation, only minor changes would be required. 
EudraLex Volume 4 contains the set of rules that govern medicinal products in the European 
Union. There are several parts and Annexes in it, that are applicable depending on the type of 
medicine produced or activity performed.  
In this project, Part IV of EudraLex Volume 4 is applicable. Part IV are the guidelines on Good 
Manufacturing Practice specific to Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMPs)11. Stem cells 
are cell-based drug products, thus, they belong to the group of ATMPs. This guideline will be 
used for the design of the layout of a production facility. Annex 1 of EudraLex Volume 4, which 
are the guidelines for Manufacture of Sterile Products, are also used, as stem cells are a sterile 
drug.

2.3 Why is closed processing important 
Closed processing is a term that has gained a lot of attention in the last years in the 
biopharmaceutical industry. In closed processing systems that do not expose the product to the 
environment are used. This is so important because the environment - and especially the air in 
the environment, the people and the activities performed in the room – is a significant potential 
source of contamination. 
When a pharmaceutical product or an intermediate of it is exposed to non-sterile air, there is an 
inherent risk of microbial contamination. This happens because there are microorganisms 
present in the air that might end up in a drug when non-sterile air is in contact with the drug, 
thus contaminating it.  
To mitigate this risk, GMP guidelines define different types of room classifications for 
cleanrooms12 according to the number of particles present in the air (among other 
considerations). Rooms can vary from Grade A (more strict) to Grade D (less strict) [12]. There is 
also CNC, which stands for Clean Non Classified area, when a room does not meet one of the 
previously mentioned cleanliness requirements (Grade A – D), but a control of the room 
conditions is still required (e.g. warehouse).  
Stem cells cannot be terminally sterilised using heat (as this would kill the cells) nor using 
sterilising filtrations (as the cells are too big). This means that the production process for this 
type of drug should be aseptic. From the first step of the process until the drug is delivered to 
the patient, the product must be prevented from getting contaminated. It is impossible to design 
such a process where the contamination risk is 0%. However, mitigating actions and quality 
controls can be put in place, in order to manage the risk and reduce it to acceptable levels. 
A common practice – and mandatory to be compliant with GMP – is to have a Grade A 
environment when an aseptic product is exposed. However, having a room with such a Grade is 
extremely resource consuming. The ventilation system required to have a Grade A/B cleanroom 
requires a lot of energy. This results in big OPEX and a higher environmental impact than with a 
closed system. Moreover, having the product exposed in an open system – even in a Grade A/B 
environment – remarkably increases the risk of contamination.  
The alternative to having an open system and a complete room with a high classification is to 
have a closed system instead. A closed processing system will separate the product from the 
rest of the environment and keep it in an aseptic system. By doing this, the risk of contamination 
is reduced and the energy required to operate the process is also reduced significantly, as the 
room classification to operate such closed processing systems can be downgraded to C or D. 
For this reason, closed systems are usually preferred over open systems when having an aseptic 
process. For all the reasons exposed in this section and considering the benefits resulting from 
having closed systems, having a closed process is one of the main priorities of this project.

A traditional manufacturing pharma facility is viewed as a set of stainless steel equipment, with 
tanks pipes and several dedicated rooms. However, this has for a long time not been the reality 
of the pharmaceutical industry. Stainless steel dedicated facilities make sense when large 
volumes of a drug are produced. But pharma is shifting towards a more personalised approach 
and looking, not so much for the next blockbuster13, but for drugs that tackle disease for a 
reduced number of patients. This means that the volumes of product needed are smaller than 
they used to be. Thus, an alternative, and more economical, way of producing these drugs is 
needed. 
This alternative has been used for many years now, and it is known as single use. Single Use 
Systems (SUS) is a term used for equipment for pharmaceutical production meant to be used 
for one campaign/batch and then to be discarded [13]. These single use systems are commonly 
made out of plastic and they are sterilised using gamma irradiation. They present several 
advantages – and some disadvantages - over traditional stainless steel equipment, especially for 
smaller volumes. 
In fact, over 85% of preclinical and clinical14 biopharmaceutical manufacturing is currently done 
using single use systems [13]. And, SUS are also getting more and more usage for commercial 
scale manufacturing.

As it can be seen in Table 1, there are many aspects to consider when deciding between single

usually only a few days after production (if they are not cryogenized). Moreover, they allow a 
simplification of the plant operation 
 
2.5 Why is automation and digitalization important 
Automation and digitalization are almost inherent to any engineering design nowadays in most 
industries. But the biopharmaceutical industry has failed to reach the same automation level as 
other sectors such as the automotive or oil industries [18].

3. Methodology 
Although there is some general guidance, there is no fixed methodology to design a 
biopharmaceutical facility. The plant design and layout will highly depend on the process, which 
is the core of the building. Everything in the building is there to facilitate the process. And the 
process itself, is very dependent on the product(s) relevant for the project. Similar products will 
have similar principles for production processes. For instance, drugs or therapies based on stem 
cells might have similar production facilities, or at least similar principles, also depending on the 
process equipment technologies used. 
But even with similar drugs the pharmaceutical manufacturing facility can look very different. 
There is usually more than one available process to manufacture one type of product. And even 
disregarding the process, there are many other variables affecting the facility design (production 
volume, manufacturing technology, automation level, process closure, multiproduct, risk, 
location, outsourced production/support activities, etc.).  
For this reason, there are no “off-the-shelf” designs for pharmaceutical facilities. There is always 
an engineering team behind the plant concept, design and construction that creates a 
customised solution that can fulfil the facility requirements and its specific conditions.  
This project will focus on the engineering design of the production facility, but the process design 
will also be a smaller part of the project, as this is not well-established yet, since cell therapy and 
– particularly – stem cells are a newer form of therapeutics. 
Firstly, the process will be designed, taking into consideration all the steps needed to produce 
the API, formulate it with other ingredients and put into a dosage form that can be used to apply 
the drug to patients. The process scale will be defined to fit the expected demand of the – 
hypothetical – product and identify the size of the equipment needed. Specific equipment 
available in the market that can meet the process needs, scale and requirements will be 
selected. This equipment will also need to fit the automation and closed processing strategy. 
Due to the novel character of the stem cell therapy process, some process steps will not have 
an already available solution in the market that can meet the defined requirements. In these 
cases, a customised solution will be designed. 
Once all the process steps have been defined, the requirements of each of these steps will be 
assessed. This means requirements in terms of utilities need to be considered, but also in terms 
of other auxiliar and support equipment or activities. 
All the process operations will be ordered sequentially, function-relations defined and organized 
into a bubble diagram. The bubble diagram shows all the processes and activities translated into 
spaces, needed for production operations ordered sequentially, while also giving an idea of 
relevant flows through this diagram and how they interlink, in terms of product, personnel, 
material, samples and waste. 
The completed bubble diagram will give an approximate concept idea on what the 3-
dimensional disposition of the different operations is and how they will need to be with respect 
to one each other. An area list is also created before transitioning from the bubble diagram to 
the layout. With this concept principles, the layout can be created by translating all the steps 
into relative sized spaces. In this case, the actual dimensions of the equipment needed to 
perform all the operations will need to be considered, as well as the space needed for operations

4. Process design 
Typically, biological processes in the biopharmaceutical industry are divided into upstream and 
downstream. Upstream is the part of the process where the Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient 
(API)19 is produced. This typically involves a period of cell expansion and growth, followed by a 
later phase where these cells synthesize the desired product. Downstream is the phase where 
the API is purified and separated from other components to a desired extent. Downstream 
usually involves many separation steps (e.g. filtration, centrifuge, chromatography, etc.). 
Upstream and downstream are part of any bioprocess. In the case of biopharmaceutical 
processes, there are two extra steps required until the final product is reached:  
• 
Formulation: where the API is combined with other substances called excipients20 that 
are part of the final drug 
• 
Fill & finish: last step in the manufacturing process (excluding secondary packaging21 and 
other forms of packaging) where the formulated drug is put into the primary packaging22 
form 
Going back to the core biological process, stem cells differ from the “typical” bioprocess where 
the final product is a protein (e.g. monoclonal antibodies). In this case the API-product is the cell 
itself. Therefore, the downstream process looks a bit different from what is usually seen. There 
is no protein-cell separation step, and no chromatographies.

3.1 Cell expansion 
The first step in the process is the cell expansion. As already mentioned, an allogenic cell therapy 
is manufactured. There is a need to obtain a lot of cells to produce several doses that can satisfy 
the demand of all the patients. But the cells cannot be transferred directly from the cell bank23 
to a bioreactor. There needs to be an intermediate step to expand the cell population: the cell 
expansion [21]. In this first step, the cells will be transferred from a vial with the cell bank to an 
equipment that will allow the growth of the cells up to a volume that allows to put them into 
the bioreactor as inoculum. 
 
3.2 Cell culture and growth 
After the cell expansion, cells are ready to be put in a bioreactor. However, mesenchymal stem 
cells need to grow adhered to a surface. MSC cannot grow nor survive floating in the medium of 
a common bioreactor. The three main options to solve this challenge for this type of culture are: 
using microcarriers, hollow fiber bioreactor systems or fixed-bed bioreactors [22]. All systems 
have been proven to successfully allow MSC growth under GMP conditions [23, 24].  
All three systems have advantages and disadvantages when culturing MSC. The reality nowadays 
is that there is not a perfectly defined solution. When scaling up a stem cell production process, 
the culture method needs to be carefully analysed to check if the therapeutic effect of the cells 
is negatively influenced by the culture conditions. 
In fixed-bed and hollow fiber bioreactors cells are not in direct contact with the fluidic force or 
the shreds, keeping them safer from collisions; they enable a 3D growth, which replicates a 
similar environment to in vivo growth conditions. However, homogeneity in the culture is 
difficult to reach; cell density in some parts can be very high, thus hindering a correct oxygen, 
nutrient and cell waste transfer; it is furthermore also hard to dissociate clustered cells [25, 26].  
Microcarriers avoid the formation of cell clusters and enable cells to get all the nutrients and 
oxygen needed for their growth. On the other side, it is important to make sure that the material 
of the microcarrier is adequate for the cell attachment and does not have any influence on the 
effectiveness of the product, nor ends up in it. Impact between microcarriers or with the 
bioreactor impeller could potentially cause cellular damage. 
Microcarriers have been chosen among the different options, as they are easier to scale up than 
the other options, while also being well-studied for other types of culture. 
 
3.3 Detachment 
Following the cell culture, the cells will need to be detached from the microcarriers to start the 
downstream processing. The first step of this stage would be to wash the microcarriers with a 
buffer and take the culture media out. The new buffer added should be adequate for the

enzymatic treatment. The culture will be treated with trypsin or another enzyme. This enzymatic

will then be floating in the culture media. 
This enzymatic treatment is a particularly critical step in the process. If the cells are exposed for 
too long to the enzyme, they will die. The exact time that the cells can be in contact with the 
enzyme depends on the specific case. The conditions in the buffer (specially temperature and 
pH) might have to be modified to have an optimal performance of the enzyme.  
 
3.4 Tangential flow filtration and diafiltration 
Once the cells have been taken out of the bioreactor (and separated from the microcarrier), they 
still might be in an undesired medium with undesired contaminants (e.g. growth factors, trypsin, 
salts, proteins, etc.). These can be separated from the product (the stem cells) by a tangential 
flow filtration [27]. In this step, the fluid is passed through a filter, not perpendicularly, but 
flowing in parallel with the filter material surface. This filter separates the components in the 
liquid by size exclusion. By having a parallel flow, the clogging of the filter is prevented and the 
operation can be done continuously. The permeate, is the fraction that passes through the filter 
and is discarded. The retentate, is the rest of the fraction which does not pass the filter and 
contains the cells. This part is recirculated back to the feed (see Figure 5).  
This method can also be used to concentrate the product and change the medium by 
diafiltration. To do a diafiltration, the normal operation of the tangential flow filtration is 
combined with the addition of the new fresh media. The newly applied media will be combined 
later with other elements in the formulation step to get the final product (API + excipients).

3.6 Fill & Finish 
In the context of this pharmaceutical process, during fill & finish the final product is put into a 
primary packaging recipient. This step is particularly critical. Since the product needs to be 
transferred to a different recipient and will be in contact with the environment, there is a high 
risk of contamination. For this reason, it is a step that must be done in aseptic conditions. It is 
recommended that it is done in a Grade A environment with a Grade B background.  
For this project, it has been considered to disregard the fill & finish process, as the volume that 
is processed is not very big compared to the volume that a dedicated filling line can process (up 
to thousands of vials per hour). And in this case, less than a thousand (or a few thousands) vials 
need to be filled per campaign.  
Intuitively, one would ask for an externalisation of this service, as it is hard to justify an 
investment for a filling line for such a low volume. However, the nature of the product makes it 
hard to externalise this process. The short shelf life of the product makes it difficult to ship and 
perform the operation in a short enough period of time. 
Another difficulty for this step is the technology used. Fill & finish of vials is a very well-
established technology. However, not with single use systems. This means that if a traditional 
filling line is used, it will come with a big burden that stainless steel equipment is usually 
associated with. High energy costs due to cleaning operations of the equipment between 
different batches, like CIP24 and SIP25. The difficulty of operation will be higher. A solution will 
be needed that uses some single use parts.

5. Process scale 
As this design is not tailor-made for a specific drug, but for a type of drug that encompasses 
several drug candidates, the scale of the process and the production output of the facility cannot 
be set or assumed directly. Therefore, an assumption needs to be made based on the type of 
drug being addressed in this project and the data already available from already existing drugs 
with the same – or very similar – characteristics. 
The drugs being considered have the following features: 
• 
Based on allogenic stem cells 
• 
Short shelf-life after production. They need to be applied to the patient within 3 to 4 
days after production. It is more adequate to have a decentralised production, rather 
than a worldwide centralised production in order to have a fast delivery from facility to 
the end patient 
• 
No product storage. Storing the product would require a cryopreservation. This would 
mean that the cells would need to be put in a cryopreservation media before storage. 
The cells would need to be thawed, the media would need to be removed and the final 
drug formulated before shipping, which is all complicating the process. 
 
As stated before, this report does not aim to design a production facility for a stem cell-based 
drug targeted for a specific disease. However, it is hard to define the process scale with such a 
low level of detail in the definition of the product. Some drugs have millions of potential 
patients, while others can target only a few thousands worldwide. With the only purpose of 
defining the scale of this process and thus the size of the production facility in scope, a specific 
disease will be chosen that justifies the scale of the process. This disease is osteoarthritis. 
Osteoarthritis is a disease affecting the cartilage that cushions the end of the bones. It can affect 
several joints in the body, although the most common are knees, hips, hands and the spine. The 
effects are mainly pain, cartilage stiffness, flexibility loss, etc. It can ultimately lead to a disability. 
There are several factors influencing the appearance of osteoarthritis, being the main one’s age, 
obesity and gender [28].  
It is estimated that osteoarthritis affects more than 40 million people in Europe and more than 
32 million in the US [29, 30]. There are currently no drugs available in the market to specifically 
treat osteoarthritis. Anti-inflammatories are used to reduce pain, physical exercises can be done 
to strengthen the muscles around the cartilage, and ultimately a surgical joint replacement can 
be performed. However, none of these options are giving a long-term solution for people living 
with osteoarthritis for regenerating the damaged tissue. 
Mesenchymal stem cells are a promising candidate for osteoarthritis treatment. Their 
differentiation capacity and ability to trigger a response of the immune system make them a 
very good candidate. Several clinical trials have already had positive results using mesenchymal 
stem cells to treat osteoarthritis, but none of these have reached yet the approval of a medicinal 
agency [31].

6. Equipment 
Once the needed scale of the process is known, the equipment that will be used can be selected. 
For this, there needs to be a match between what is required for production and the equipment 
available in the market. As already mentioned before, it is crucial that the process is closed (or 
at least most of it) and automated to make the product more secure and have a cost-efficient 
production. Moreover, it is also adequate to use single use systems. As these allow having a 
closed process and make production easier (as cleaning activities are not required).  
For each stage, an equipment from a specific supplier will be selected and used to explain the 
different features of the process equipment. Additionally, equipment from different suppliers 
with similar features will also be included, but not described in depth.  
 
5.1 Cell expansion 
Custom-made solutions 
The cell expansion is one of the most difficult steps to automate, digitalise and close. This step 
is typically done manually in the industry. This requires highly skilled operators that can perform 
several manual operations under a biosafety cabinet. This manual operation requires a high 
room classification (Grade A and Grade B) and has an inherent high risk of product 
contamination, as the product is exposed to the environment. Following the selected production 
strategy (automated and digitalised closed process with minimal manual operations), two 
alternatives will be proposed for this step. 
Option 1 – Robotised arm inside isolator 
In this option the manual process to perform a cell expansion done by a human is mimicked by 
a robotic arm inside an isolator. This manual process includes the following steps:  
1. Thawing of the cell bank vial; 2. Transfer the cell bank to a centrifuge vial; 3. Centrifuge to 
separate the cells from the cryopreservation media; 4. Discard the cryopreservation media; 5. 
Resuspend the cells with culture media; 6. Transfer the cell to several culture T-flasks; 7. 
Incubate the T-flasks and wait until desired cell growth is achieved; 8. Add trypsin to the T-flasks 
to detach cells; 9. Transfer the liquid to a centrifuge to separate cells from trypsin and culture 
media; 10. Discard used culture media containing the trypsin; 11. Repeat steps 5 to 10 as many 
times as needed to obtain the desired number of cells that can be transferred into a bioreactor. 
As can be seen, the process is long and complex. And doing in it manually can lead to multiple 
errors and increases the risk of contamination. However, all the steps of the cell expansion only 
require moving objects in a small area, operate a centrifuge and use a pipette. These operations 
could be done by a robotic arm instead of a human.

There are several types of microcarriers available in the market. Although microcarriers coated 
by a collagen layer have been reported to be the best suited for mesenchymal stem cell cultures 
[41, 42], there is a problem with them for GMP production. Collagen is usually based on animal-

product. However, there are other xeno-free30 microcarrier alternatives available suitable for 
GMP production.  
The microcarriers are made out of cellulose and have an external coating of DEAE31 that 
enhances cell attachment. They have an average particle size of 230 µm and allow the 
attachment and growth of mesenchymal stem cells in stirred suspensions. They have a density

6.1 Strategy 
As said previously, automation has many levels. There is currently a lot of hype on what could 
be achieved through automation in pharma, but it is not that usual to see a real use of those 
technologies. Technology adoption until now has been slow if compared with other industries. 
Biophorum33 has a Technology Roadmap group that is focused on accelerating innovation and 
development of the manufacturing processes of the future [57]. This group has created the 
Digital Plant Maturity Model (DPMM) [58]. In this model, Biophorum has defined 5 levels of 
digital maturity:

The model presented above will be used to escape the broad meaning of automation and 
digitalisation in the pharmaceutical industry and have a precise definition of what is required in 
the design. 
This project will aim to have a Level 3 or Connected plant grade in the Digital Plant Maturity 
Model. This means that an ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning)34 [59], a LES (Lean Execution 
System)35 [60] and a MES (Manufacturing Execution System)36 [61] will be in place. Having these 
3 software, will enable an efficient and optimised production. Real-time process data will be 
available, allowing the plant operator to take decisions as soon as process contingencies occur, 
resource allocation will be efficient and production will be flexible and agile. 
Level 3 has been chosen as it is the highest of the digital maturity levels that can be realistically 
achieved with the currently available technology for the pharmaceutical industry. Some of the 
elements in Level 4 and Level 5 could also be implemented, but not all of them. Moreover, Level 
3 would still be ahead of most of the current pharmaceutical plants. As already mentioned 
before, pharma is very traditional and implementing changes is especially difficult because of 
the strong regulations. This has caused that – even with the available technology and proofs of 
concept throughout the industry – laboratories have not upgraded their production facilities to 
the maximum digital and automated level possible. 
As process data will be monitored in real-time, information will be gathered that allows the 
creation of batch records37. This is key for having an instant release of the product as soon as it 
is finished. Having these process data would enable a review by exception. In this type of review 
the process is validated within a certain span of specific parameters. If all the process occurred 
within the set boundaries, it can be assumed that the output is as expected and thereby the 
product is safe (quality controls would still be necessary). 
To achieve this automation and digitalization objective, following certain standards is key. The 
ISA38 has two relevant standards relevant for this level: ISA-88 and ISA-99. 
ISA-88 is a standard for the design and specification of a process control system. The process, 
the facility and the procedures are broken down into different levels of simplicity. It enables an 
automated operation of the facility (although it can also be manual) [62]. A strong use case of 
ISA-88 for the pharmaceutical industry is the recipe management during the process. The recipe 
can be simplified into several small steps and then be automized according to a set of 
parameters. 
ISA-95 is a standard to define an integrated interface between control systems and other 
business functions [63]. This standard is based on the Purdue Reference Model for Computer 
Integrated Manufacturing (see Appendix 2). ISA-95 enables the connection of levels 3 and 4 of

7. Process rooms 
The production facility will need the following rooms according to the process steps defined 
previously: 
• 
Upstream: this room includes all the process steps during the upstream phase (cell 
expansion and cell culture), where the stem cells have to be cultured, grown and 
produced 
• 
Downstream: this room includes all the process steps during the downstream phase 
(microcarrier separation, tangential flow filtration and diafiltration), where the stem 
cells have to be separated from the rest of the components/contaminants that are 
found in the culture mixture. 
• 
Formulation: this operation is very critical, as it has a big impact on the drug quality. 
Therefore, it is performed in a dedicated room. Here, the active pharmaceutical 
ingredient (stem cells), will be mixed with the excipients to create the final drug mixture 
that is delivered to the patient 
• 
Fill & Finish: this room has the filling equipment where the mixture created in the 
formulation room is put into individual vials (1 vial = 1 dose), as the primary packaging 
form. In theory, filling and formulation could happen in the same room, as they are both 
in closed systems. However, it has been decided to have them in separate rooms, as 
both steps are very critical and present a remarkable risk of contamination 
• 
Packaging and shipping preparation: in this room the vials are put into the secondary 
packaging. Secondary packaging is usually a cardboard box. In this case, more 
complexity is required as the drug product is a cell that is alive. A shipping preparation 
is needed that can maintain the vials in a cold temperature environment while they are 
transported to the patient. After this operation the product is finally completed and 
ready to be shipped and delivered 
• 
Finished product warehouse: in this warehouse area the finalised product is kept in a 
controlled environment with controlled conditions until it is picked up for shipping 
• 
Airlock: this a closed room that has two doors (or more) connecting two rooms (or 
more) that have different GMP cleanliness levels. Sometimes they can connect rooms 
with the same GMP classification to decrease the contamination risk between them. 
Not only are they a physical barrier, but they also can have a decontamination step when 
they are used for material transfer (e.g. treatment of material with oxygen peroxide). 
They also ensure the pressure difference is kept between different rooms. In rooms with 
GMO exposure, there will be a double-airlock to stay compliant with GMP and GMO. In 
some cases, when material needs to be transferred between connected rooms, a pass-
box can be used. The pass-box is a box and is smaller than a personnel airlock. There are 
two types of airlocks: 
o Personnel airlocks: for personnel pass (typically a gowning change is included) 
o Material airlocks: to transfer material in a controlled manner

9. Room classification 
In the pharmaceutical industry, a cleanroom is defined as a physically closed area where the 
conditions are controlled and the maximum number of pollutants in the air (viable and nonviable 
particles) is monitored and limited [68]. The operations in pharmaceutical production need to 
happen inside a cleanroom to limit the risk of contamination of the product and ensure patient 
safety, as stated by Good Manufacturing Practices.  
There are different types of cleanrooms, depending on the maximum number of particles per 
cubic meter (particles/m3) allowed. The EU GMPs Annex 1 distinguishes between Grades: A, B, 
C and D. Grade A is the most restrictive and clean environment and Grade D, the least. But a 
room cannot be Grade A, only a closed system (e.g. an isolator). Cleanrooms can reach up to 
Grade B This is also known as room classification.

The rooms that are exposed to GMO2 agents or were GMO2 agents are manipulated (Upstream, 
Downstream, Formulation, Fill & Finish and QC & Analysis Lab) have a double airlock to enter 
and to exit the room. Airlocks – among other functions – help maintain the pressure in rooms 
within certain levels. In this case, pressure is used to control the movement of the air in the 
rooms. But GMP and GMO requirements regarding pressure are contrary. Hence a double 
airlock is needed to live up to all the requirements  
GMPs require that the pressure is bigger in the cleanest room than in the dirtiest room, in order 
to have air moving towards the dirtiest areas (e.g. Upstream would have a bigger pressure than 
the Corridor Out, to avoid dirty air entering Upstream).  
Danish GMO regulations require air in contact with GMOs to be kept in the room to avoid the 
spread through air of the GMOs (e.g. the Corridor Out should have a bigger pressure than 
Upstream, to avoid GMO particles escaping the Upstream room). 
To solve this contradiction two airlocks are used to enter and two to exit the room. In the case 
of the Upstream room, the first PAL Out will have a smaller pressure than the Upstream room. 
The second PAL Out will have the same pressure as the Upstream room. The Corridor Out has 
the same pressure as the first PAL Out. Using this method the dirty air and the GMO particles 
are contained in the air of the first PAL Out, which can be adequately purged to avoid 
propagation (see Figure 17).

12. 
Process intensification 
Process intensification is a recently adopted term in the pharmaceutical industry, especially for 
bioprocesses. It is about increasing productivity and getting a higher output while decreasing or 
maintaining the use of resources. It can result in several positive effects to production. Like lower 
environmental impact, lower usage of materials, smaller size of production facilities or increased 
output capacity [69]. 
In the case of this project, several factors are pointing towards process intensification. This 
facility is thought for a new stem cell-based drug. The facility should be able to cover the 
production for a hypothetical phase III clinical trial, but also for the first commercial batches. 
Predicting the demand of a newly released drug is very challenging, as many factors have an 
influence on it [70, 71]. In a real case, it is likely that the project owner decides in the middle of 
the project that the production capacity needs to be increased. Process intensification could 
permit this increase in production without major changes in the facility or in the process.  
Some of the features of the facility (single use equipment, certain processes lasting for several 
days, flexibility, fermentation step, etc.) make it ideal for process intensification. There are 
several actions that can be taken in order to increase the output without the need to do changes 
to the building or increasing the operating costs of the process [72].  
In this process, the phase taking the longest by far is cell growth, which can last several days. 
The downstream operations, instead, can be done in less than 24 hours. Therefore, the major 
potential for intensification is in the upstream phase. Here are some of the strategies that could 
be implemented to intensify the process: 
• 
Perfusion in bioreactor: bioreactor intensification consists of increasing the product 
output of the bioreactor. It can be done in several ways (changing the culture conditions, 
changing the media, genetically modifying the cell, etc.). The chosen way in this case 
would be using perfusion in the bioreactor. Perfusion is a complementary process to the 
cell culture in the bioreactor, where cells are kept in the bioreactor while the media is 
continuously or periodically exchanged. This allows the addition of fresh media, while 
at the same time removing or diluting the unwanted substances in it (subproducts, 
cellular debris, etc.). This will result in an increased cellular growth. In this project, since 
the cells are the product, there would be an increased availability of the product from 
the upstream phase. 
• 
Partial batch removal: cell growth is exponential. This means that if a culture is meant 
to run for 10 days, the difference in the number of cells between two days is much 
higher in the last days of the culture than in the first days (see Appendix 8 for further 
explanation).  
This phenomenon can be used in the following way: instead of removing all the cells 
after the cell culture process is finished, only a part of it is removed (e.g. 50 - 70%) to be 
processed in the downstream. The rest is kept and re-cultured again. These cells that 
are re-cultured will already be in the log growth phase, already attached to the 
microcarriers and will be many more than if the process was re-started again. 
==PAGE:63==
Conceptual Design for a Stem Cell Therapy 
 
Nil Puig Engel 
Production Facility 
 
pg. 62 
 
This procedure done in the right way and repeated several times, allows cutting the 
main bottleneck in the process: cell growth. This phase could be reduced from 9 - 10 
days to 2 - 3 days per batch using this process intensification strategy.  
• 
Increase single use equipment volume or quantity: using single use equipment gives an 
inherent flexibility to the process and plant operation. Most of the single use equipment 
selected in this project is available in bigger sizes. This means that the batch size, can be 
changed on a constant basis according to demand, without the need to make any 
significant changes in the factory infrastructure. More pieces of equipment could also 
be added in a relatively easy way. 
Moreover, new single use equipment can be installed quickly without making any major 
changes if the required utilities / services are already in place. This means that multiple 
batches can be run at the same time using different single use equipment if a major 
increase in capacity is needed. Always, as long as the appropriate measures are taken 
to prevent cross-contamination. 
Other “more conventional” process optimization techniques – that do not require to change the 
engineering design – might also be used to increase the product output (e.g. process parameter 
fine-tuning).

14.3 Project risks 
As any other complex construction project, there are some risks to the completion of this project 
that would need to be identified before the start in order to make mitigation actions to ensure 
a successful completion. 
• 
Process uncertainty: the use of stem cells as a drug is relatively new. Only a few 
therapies (less than 15) have been approved to date by the FDA and EMA. Thus, the 
production process for this kind of therapy at an industrial scale is not well established 
yet. This implies that for therapies that will be approved in the following years, the 
facility needs to be built now without fully knowing what the exact process will be once 
operation begins. This has the risk of having a building designed – or even constructed 
– that is not fit for the final process. 
Mitigation: This risk can be mitigated by incorporating flexibility to the plant. Flexibility 
can be achieved through using single use equipment (already done); having buffer space 
in production areas – to fit in bigger equipment, if needed –; having points of use47 for 
utilities that might be used in the future. 
• 
Rely on sustained delivery of single use equipment suppliers: pharmaceutical 
production processes that rely on single use equipment have the risk that their 
production depends completely on the constant supply of those single use pieces of 
equipment. If the supplier of the equipment has delays in its deliveries or goes bankrupt, 
production can be compromised. 
Mitigation: this risk can be mitigated by having a warehouse that has capacity to store 
equipment for several batches (like the concept developed); using equipment from 
different suppliers in order to diversify the risk; the use of single use systems is also a 
mitigation strategy in itself, as it is relatively easy to change from one equipment to 
another, as they are usually “plug and play” equipment.

• 
Rely on supplier quality: single use equipment presents some risks for drug 
manufacturing. The most important ones are extractables48, leachables49 and 
equipment integrity50. Since the pieces of single use equipment are changed in every 
batch, there is a high degree of reliance on the quality of those. Thus, a lot of trust is put 
into the supplier.   
Mitigation: this risk can be mitigated by appropriately auditing the single use equipment 
suppliers, their production facilities and their QMS (Quality Management System51), 
before giving them a contract. These audits should not only be done at the beginning, 
but on an established regular basis.   
• 
Supply chain constraints [73]: due to the Covid-19 pandemic and other events, like the 
war in Ukraine and the Suez Canal obstruction, the global supply chains have been 
constrained and delayed since 2020 to date. This also impacted the supply chain of 
construction materials and pharmaceutical production equipment. It has caused a delay 
in supplies lead times and overall project completion of construction projects (including 
pharmaceutical facilities). This crisis is still on-going, and is expected to continue 
throughout 2023. Any construction project would take longer than in a “normal” supply 
chain situation if this is not taken into account.  
Mitigation: these new lead times need to be taken into account from the early phases 
of the project. Procurement activities need to be done more in advance than usually and 
a constant communication with relevant suppliers would be needed to mitigate any 
delaying actions well in advance.

Conclusion 
This project has aimed to do a conceptual design for a pharmaceutical production facility that 
manufactures a therapy based on allogenic stem cells. The scope of the design included the 
process design, the process scale, the automation and digitalization strategy, the facility layout, 
the flows (product, material/equipment, waste, GMO waste, personnel, equipment to be 
washed and samples) and process intensification strategies. Among these sections, there was a 
special focus on having a closed, automated and digitalized process. This work has also 
considered the schedule, cost and risks if the project was continued and the facility was to be 
built.  
The first step has been designing and scaling the process. Then the different steps have been 
assigned to a room, and the rooms have been connected using a bubble diagram. Afterwards, 
the layout has been created. With the experience from several SMEs and information from 
equipment supplier the project schedule, cost and risks have been defined. This methodology is 
commonly applied in the industry for this kind of project. Due to the nature of the project (doing 
an early-stage design, not designing a facility for a specific therapy and not base the project on 
a real case) there has been a certain degree of uncertainty. This uncertainty has been mitigated 
with assumptions. The bubble diagram has needed several iterations before having a final 
version. These iterations have been mainly influenced by the definition of the different flows. 
The design and construction of this kind of facility has proven to be feasible with the current 
available technology. Automation and digitalisation are still in an early- to mid-stage for the 
pharmaceutical industry. Regulations and the traditional mindset are stopping the sector from 
taking the complete advantage of the current technological possibilities – that are already being 
applied in other manufacturing industries. Closed processing has been proven to be possible 
using single use systems. This has several advantages: mitigates contamination risk, lowers 
CAPEX, avoids most of the cleaning operations and reduces the environmental impact in several 
aspects (e.g. water usage or energy consumption). However, there is still a “lack of trust” with 
closed systems from regulators. From a theoretical point of view a closed system could be 
operated in a CNC room. Nevertheless, the expectation from regulators is still to have a grade C 
room. This increases the OPEX of the facility.  
GMP regulations have been made complementary to GMO regulations. This has been possible 
due to the implementation of some elements in the design (like the double personal airlocks in 
production areas, the waste inactivation room or the effluent inactivation system). 
These elements make the project successful following the objectives from the one-pager 
strategy (see Figure 2). 
The designed facility is capable of producing an estimated 30.366 doses per year with 31 
production campaigns. The facility is 1740 m2 (excluding the elements mentioned in the Layout 
disclaimer). The cost of building this facility is 505 million DKK with a ±25% uncertainty range 
(378 to 631 million DKK). The time required for the completion of this project – including design, 
construction and commissioning – is 3 years and 8 months. The biggest share of the cost and the 
time schedule is driven by the construction of a new facility. They can both be reduced by using 
an already existing facility and revamping it for the purpose of producing such a drug. Another 
way to reduce both is placing the production area/building in or next to an already existing 
production facility. This would allow the use of some of the already existing systems, like clean 
and black utilities or offices.

Considering the relevance of stem cells, their therapeutic potential and the number of clinical 
trials using stem cells that are in late phases, the designed facility is presented as a feasible 
possibility for the production of phase 3 and early-stage stem cell-based drugs. 
As for the future steps:  
• 
there is a need to design and create a solution that allows to close and automate the 
first steps of the process where the cells are thawed, inoculated and cultured  
• 
the production process for stem cell-based drugs at an industrial scale and in a GMP and 
GMO compliant way must be further investigated and defined, as it is still very unclear 
• 
there is also a need to enhance the degree of automation and digitalization in 
pharmaceutical production 
• 
the industry needs to increase de reliance on closed systems    
In conclusion, this thesis has presented a comprehensive analysis of the steps and the outcome 
of doing a conceptual design for a stem cell production facility. Through the use of best industry 
practices and insights from industry subject matter experts, the study has provided valuable 
insights into the current situation for designing and building this kind of manufacturing facility, 
as well as its key challenges. These findings have important implications for the following years, 
where several stem cell-based therapies are expected to be approved and will need to be 
produced at an industrial scale. Overall, the study has provided a fresh perspective on stem cell 
manufacturing facilities and has opened up new avenues for future optimization of this design.

Appendix 
Appendix 1: Process scale 
There is not available data on the prevalence of osteoarthritis in Denmark. Therefore, the 
prevalence in Europe has been taken (5,24% of the population) and extrapolated to Denmark 
[29]. It is estimated that 303.665 people in Denmark have osteoarthritis. 
It has been assumed that in a plateau situation of the drug, 10% of the patients would be treated 
every year and that the treatment would consist of 1 dose containing 50.000.000 mesenchymal 
stem cells [32]. This means that 30.366 individuals would be treated each year in Denmark and 
that 1.518.324.607.330 cells are needed. 
According to [33], a cell density of 128.000 cells/mL can be obtained culturing mesenchymal 
stem cells for 11 days in a 500L bioreactor using microcarriers. This would mean each batch 
would be producing 64.000.000.000 cells. According to [27] and [34], detachment of the cells 
from the microcarriers using an enzymatic treatment with trypsin and the subsequent removal 
of the microcarriers through a filtration cause a 10% cell loss, and the tangential flow filtration 
causes and additional loss of 15% of the cells. Taking these numbers into account, after the 
upstream and downstream operations, 48.960.000.000 cells are obtained in each batch. This 
number of cells accounts for 979 doses of the stem cell-based drug. 
If each batch starting with a 500L bioreactor volume produces 979 doses, 31 production 
campaigns are necessary throughout the year to satisfy the forecasted demand.

Appendix 8: Partial batch removal 
If a cell culture is meant to run for 10 days, the difference in the number of cells between two 
days is much bigger in the last days of the culture than in the first days. This happens because 
cell growth is exponential (until the stationary and death phase are reached).  
The plot in Figure 27 represents a growth curve, I.e. the evolution of the number of cells in a cell 
culture over a period of time. As it can be seen, the change in the number of cells in the first 
days (blue circle in Figure 27) is very little, if compared to the change in the number of cells on 
a later day (black circle in Figure 27) of the log phase.  
In a case like the one foreseen in this project, where the cells are the product, they would 
typically be harvested for downstream/purification at the end of the log phase.
