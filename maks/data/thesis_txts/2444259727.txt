Abstract (English)
Periodic operation of separation units has been known since the 1960’s to enhance the efficiency of
mass transfer between phases. In cyclic distillation, the operation is split into two parts. First, only
the vapor flows through the column stages with the liquid holdups being held on the trays. After some
time, the vapor flow is turned off and the liquid is allowed to drain to the tray below. This cycle is
repeated continuously. During the vapor flow period, the equilibrium on each tray changes transiently
such that an overall higher degree of light component is rectified compared to conventional distillation.
The research in this field has mostly focused on developing mathematical models and design procedures.
Existing control studies are largely limited to PI control without mention of any tuning methodology.
Therefore, the purpose of this study is to further investigate and extend control strategies for cyclic
distillation through a modeling-simulation approach. From a vast literature study, the most important
findings are presented. In this work, an existing mass-balance model is simulated for a case of ideal and
non-ideal thermodynamics: A high purity benzene/toluene separation in a cyclic distillation column of
13 equilibrium stages, and a cyclic ethanol/water stripping column of 4 equilibrium stages based on real
plant data. For the first time, a documented tuning methodology is applied to PID control of periodic
separation. Second order plus time delay transfer functions are estimated from actuator inputs (boilup
and reflux) to their respective controlled variables (bottoms and distillate purities). Skogestad’s IMC
tuning relations are used to obtain PID tunings based on the transfer functions. The benzene/toluene
case was first studied using constant relative volatility for simplicity. The estimated transfer functions
from inputs to outputs show that the system behavior is highly non-linear with big variation in steady
state gain, time constants, and time delays depending on the magnitude and direction of the input
changes. This complicates the PID controller tuning since a particular tuning is only effective within
a small operating range.
By applying a logarithmic transform to the purities, the non-linearity of
the system could be reduced such that PID controller tunings are viable for a larger operating range.
Through a hand-tuning, it was discovered that an increase in gain, integral time constant, and derivative
time constant resulted in better disturbance rejection and mostly eliminated the oscillatory responses.
Especially the derivative action was shown to be important for this high-purity case. The controller
tuning obtained using the assumption of constant relative volatility was also shown to work well when
the equilibrium was modeled using Raoult’s law instead. The same tuning methodology was applied
to PID control of an ethanol/water stripping column based on plant data from the Lipnitsky Alcohol
Plant in Ukraine. Since this column operates without reflux, uses direct steam addition to the bottom
tray, and the required ethanol purity is only 18.25 mol%, the dynamics are faster and closer to linear.
A good PID tuning was readily obtained using the SIMC relations, effectively rejecting disturbances
and tracking set point changes. Lastly, some practical issues regarding cyclic distillation control are
discussed.

kolonnebundene, hvorpå væsken ligger stille. Efter et tidsrum stoppes gasstrømningen, og væskeind-

simulering. Fra omfattende litteraturstudie præsenteres de vigtigste resultater inden for modellering
og regulering af cyklisk destillation. I denne afhandling simuleres en eksisterende massebalancemodel

separation af benzen og toluen med høj renhed, og en cyklisk stripper med 4 ligevætrin til separation af
ethanol og vand baseret på data fra Lipnitsky Alcohol Plant i Ukraine. For første gang dokumenteres
anvendelsen af en metodik for tuning af PID-regulering af periodisk separation. Overføringsfunktioner
af anden orden med dødtid bruges til at tilnærme dynamikken fra aktuatorvariable (gasstrømning og
tilbageløb) til de kontrollerede variable (renhed af remanens og destillat). Skogestads IMC tuningre-
lationer bruges til at finde PID-tuninger baseret på overføringsfunktionerne. Benzen/toluen-kolonnen
simuleres i første omgang ved at antage konstant relativ flygtighed for at mindske beregningstiden. De
estimerede overføringsfunktioner viser, at systemets opførsel er betydeligt ulineær med store forskelle i
forstærkning, tidskonstanter og dødtid afhængig af størrelsen og retningen af inputændringen. Dette

vises det, at en øgning af regulatorforstærkning og tidskonstanter for integration og differentiering re-
sulterer i bedre afvisning af forstyrrelser, setpunktstracking og kraftig reduktion af svingninger. Særligt
differentialledet viser sig at være vigtigt for reguleringen af denne proces med høje renhedskrav. Den
samme regulatortuning kan også benyttes med god ydeevne, når benzen/toluen-systemet modeleres med
Raoult’s lov i stedet. Denne tuningmetodik bruges også på en ethanol/vand-stripperen. Denne kolonne
drives uden tilbageløb, mættet vanddamp fødes direkte til nederste bund i kolonnen, og renhedskravet
for ethanol er blot 18.25 mol% – dette fører til en proces med væsentligt hurtigere dynamik og mindre
grad af ulinearitet. En PID-tuning med god afvisning af forstyrrelser og setpunkt-tracking opnåedes
hurtigt ved anvendelse af SIMC tuningrelationerne. Til sidst diskuteres nogle praktiske omstændigheder
vedrørende styring og regulering af cykliske destillationer.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
In the face of anthropogenic climate change, efforts must be taken to mitigate this effect[1]. Air pollution
caused by particulates and greenhouse gases, among others, contribute to worsening the environment.
More than 40,000 distillation columns are operating in the USA, accounting for approximately 2.25% of
the national energy consumption, as of 2005 [2]. This widespread process with high energy requirements
is therefore an important target for efficiency improvements. Process intensification is the discipline of
adjusting the design and operation of conventional unit operations in order to reduce the equipment size
and/or increase efficiency. Process intensification has been applied to distillation processes for many
years, leading to novel designs such as reactive distillation, divided wall distillation, heat integrated
distillation columns, among others[3, 4]. Cyclic distillation was first reported by Cannon in 1961[5],
which was realized by periodically turning off the vapor flow in a sieve tray distillation column without
downcomers. The operation is thus split into separate vapor flow periods (VFPs) and liquid flow periods
(LFPs). The vapor flow is larger than the weeping limit, thus keeping liquid on each tray during the
VFP. In the LFP, the liquid holdups drain to the tray below. During the LFP, pressure builds up in the
reboiler, which causes pressure fluctuations through the column when starting the VFP again. Already
at this point, Cannon noted that a capacity increase would be possible, but the theoretical basis for
this observation was not well understood.
Based on the Murphree tray efficiency for mass transfer[6], the analytical 1936 work of W.K. Lewis Jr.
[7] investigated the tray efficiencies for three different cases of liquid and vapor flow in a trayed coloumn.
In Lewis case I, mixed vapor enters each tray. In case II, vapor with a spatial concentration profile
enters each tray, on which the liquid also has a spatial concentration gradient in the same direction
on each tray. Case III is similar to case II, except the liquid composition gradient is reversed on each
subsequent tray. Lewis noted that tray efficiencies for binary separation are ranked II > I > III. Of
these cases, conventional distillation resembles case III the most. In the mid-late 1960’s, two groups
independently showed that cyclic distillation with perfect liquid displacement is analogous to Lewis case
II by analysis of substituting the independent spatial variable of the liquid concentration gradient with
time and through analog computer simulations[8–11]. This founded the theoretical basis for achieving a
higher efficiency by controlled cyclic operation. It was noted, however, that partial mixing of the liquid
flow reduced the separation efficiency. This usually manifested itself as liquid draining from trays above
being mixed onto the tray below, which is also draining. Thus, already condensed and partially rectified
light component flowed down through the column again.

Early simulation models for cyclic distillation columns were a product of their time and severely limited
by cumbersome analog computer implementations and lack of computational power in digital comput-
ers[12]. Until the mid 1980s, most models and their solution algorithms made very strict assumptions.
Some examples are: feed only being allowed to enter the top tray, point efficiencies of 1, and linear
thermodynamics (constant relative volatility). In 1989, Toftegård and Jørgensen published a numerical
integration method for solving the nonlinear dynamic differential equations[13]. Since then, ODE solvers
have become highly accessible and available in many programming languages. Recently published mod-
els by Maleta et al., Liţǎ et al., Pǎtruţ et al., Schroll-Fleischer and Andersen are more advanced, more
accessible, and readily implementable[14–19]. Cyclic distillation is, however, not available in commer-
cial process simulators, which holds back the industrial adoption. In contrast to the older models, most
the new models include the option of applying non-linear thermodynamics, e.g. an activity coefficient
model or an equation of state. In parallel, design algorithms and basic controllers have been developed.
The work of Andersen[18, 19] extended the model of Liţǎ et al.[15] by including, among others, enthalpy
balances and feed flashing, and is the state-of-the-art model for cyclic distillation. Unfortunately, most
models for cyclic distillation assume constant pressure through the column, or a fixed pressure drop per
tray. This is a naive and simple treatment of pressure dynamics compared to conventional distillation
models. In 1987, Choe & Luyben described both a vapor mass balance model which treats pressure as
a state variable, and vapor hydraulic model, in which the vapor flow rates are dependent on pressure
differences between trays[20]. Pressure has not been treated in this way in cyclic distillation yet, but it
may be important for control studies.
While most cyclic distillation research has focused on equipment design, mathematical modeling, and
design algorithms, in-depth control studies are largely missing from modern literature[12]. The necessity
of control studies is often drawn to attention[14–16, 21, 22], partially due to industrial controllability
concerns. Wade et al. provided the first dedicated study of cyclic distillation control in 1969, focusing on
first stabilizing the operation[23]. Some advanced control studies were performed in the 1970s and 80s:
Matsubara et al. proved analytically that controlled cycling provides the optimal efficiency for distilla-
tion columns[24, 25]. Dale & Furzer demonstrated stabilizing control in simulations and experiments
with noise disturbances[26]. Later, Matsubara et al. developed a single-input-single-output (SISO) re-
lay feedback controller, which automatically ends the VFP once the product composition is reached[27]
on the top or bottom tray.
If concentrations could be measured perfectly, this would be a perfect
controller; issues with sensor accuracy and dynamics make this solution questionable. Toftegaard &
Jørgensen[28] developed an adaptive multiple-input single-output controller which automatically esti-
mates process parameters and tunes the controller accordingly. Liţă et al. (2012) used several discrete
PI controllers to control product compositions and the condenser/reboiler holdups by manipulating
reflux and product draw rates[15]. In 2014, Bîldea et al. found which tray temperatures were most
sensitive to operating condition changes and used those as controlled variables, manipulating the reflux
and vapor flow rate[21]. Still, the need for more advanced controllers is called for in literature[16, 22].
Andersen et al. (2018) propose integrating the column design and control performance in a combined
approach founded on driving force based design[19]. It was demonstrated through simulations using
a discrete PI controller presented by Matsubara[27], based on the work of Dale & Furzer [26], that a
driving force based design lends to easier control compared to other design algorithms. In addition to

To overcome the aforementioned efficiency issue of mixed liquid flow, several interesting cyclic distil-
lation designs were proposed. In their initial control study in 1969, Wade et al. did indeed mention
that the proper equipment for cyclic distillation could be quite different from conventional columns[23].
Baron-Wajcs et al.[30, 31] proposed stepwise periodic distillation where liquid flow is manipulated di-
rectly instead of relying on the interruption of the vapor flow. However, this design comes with efficiency
losses due to the tray holdups being drained simultaneously and mixed in the reboiler, while the tray
holdups are refilled from sequentially filled reservoirs of condensate. Stage-switching, developed by Ku-
rimoto et al.[32], employed a complicated set of valves and tubing to manipulate the vapor flow between
stages. Periodically, the valve positions are changed such that the stages effectively switch tray indexes,
while the liquid remains in the same physical container. Feed and products are periodically drawn. The
above are just a few example solutions of overcoming the liquid backmixing problem. The physical
realization of perfect liquid displacement in plug flow was not yet solved due to the highly impractical
designs.
A new tray design for cyclic distillation was patented by Maleta & Maleta in 2010, specifically designed
to solve the liquid backmixing issue[33]. The issue was overcome by introducing reservoirs below each
tray, which temporarily hold the falling liquid during the LFP. This is realized by a valve simultaneously
opening the tray drainage and closing the reservoir drainage during the LFP. During the VFP, the valve
is in the other position, allowing vapor flow to pass through the column. By choosing a sufficiently long
LFP, liquid plug flow can be realized. This new tray design spurred a resurgence of academic interest
in cyclic distillation — the reader may have noticed from the temporal gap of referenced works that
little research was made on cyclic distillation from c. 1990 to 2010. Toftegård et al. published another
new tray design in 2016 which allows for drainage of the liquid holdups without interrupting the vapor
flow[34]. This provides a new operating mode called sequential drainage, in contrast to the common
simultaneous drainage operation of cyclic distillation columns. In sequential drainage, a single tray is
emptied at a time, which drains onto the tray below. Next, the above tray is drained, etc. Freiesleben
showed through simulations that tray efficiencies for sequential drainage are about 10% lower compared
to simultaneous drainage[35]. It is noted, however, that the efficiency loss may possibly be worthwhile
due to the potential of avoiding the reboiler pressure buildup which normally occurs during the VFP
of simultaneous drainage operation. In this work, the focus will be put on simultaneous drainage.
Following the development of the Maleta tray, cyclic distillation finally gained a foothold in the industry.
The first industrial implementation of cyclic distillation is at the Lipnitsky Alcohol Plant in Ukraine[14].
The cyclic column produces 30 000 L per day of food grade alcohol of 13-24 mol% (typically 18.25 mol%)
ethanol from a fermentation broth of 3.29 mol%. Another cyclic distillation column for the concentra-
tion of impurities is also used at the same plant. Using only 15 trays, it is able to carry out a separation
which would require 56 trays in a conventional distillation column. Especially for such difficult separa-
tions, a remarkable reduction of equipment size is possible. This could be of interest for where space
is limited, e.g. retrofitting old equipment or maritime exhaust gas cleaning. The first dividing-wall

1.2
Thesis Statement and Project Plan
Most modern literature on cyclic distillation has focused on the development of simulation models and
design algorithms — yet frequently, the need for control studies is brought to attention. Simple SISO
controllers are commonly used in literature to serve as a demonstration of controllability instead of
developing novel and effective control strategies.
The purpose of this thesis is to develop, tune, and benchmark control structures for a set of realistic
applications of cyclic distillation units using a modeling-simulation approach.
To this end, the project is divided into the following tasks:
• Perform a literature study: Provide a historical and modern review of cyclic distillation with
emphasis on control studies.
• Establish an industrially relevant test case: A test case for controller synthesis and bench-
marking will be chosen based on industrial relevance from published literature and operating
plants. Column designs must be obtained either from published data and/or design algorithms.
• Choose a suitable model: Based on the literature review, the test cases, and dynamic modeling
requirements, a suitable simulation model must be chosen. This may either be an existing model
from literature, a modified version, or a new model. Much consideration must be put into the
required model features as this influences the controller design and relevance significantly.
• Implement simulation model: The chosen model is implemented in a dynamic simulation tool.
The model is verified by checking the steady state and dynamic behavior.
• Obtain and implement controllers: The first priority is establishing the regulatory control
using simple and published control structures.
Then, more advanced control systems will be
considered.
• Benchmark control designs: The performance of each control system is benchmarked using a
defined set of disturbances and set point tracking tests.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
This literature review draws from the most important advancements mentioned in the introduction and
presents them in a larger scope. However, the basics of vapor-liquid equilibrium will first be covered to
provide a basis for the mathematical models.
2.1
Vapor-Liquid Equilibrium
Distillation is a separation process based difference in volatilities of components in a mixture. More
volatile components will be rectified and exit the top of the column, while less volatile components are
condensed and exit the bottom of the column. This is facilitated by sequential stacking of equilibrium
stages, a so-called equilibrium cascade. It is therefore of the essence to develop a basic understanding
of vapor-liquid equilibrium (VLE); this is the most important concept in distillation theory.
Binary vapor-liquid equilibria are often illustrated as either T-xy or p-xy diagrams, see Figure 2.1.
These show the corresponding vapor and liquid molar fractions of the light and heavy components
in equilibrium.
In the T-xy plot, the lower curve is the bubble point curve of the mixture, which
is essentially the boiling point of a mixture. The upper curve is the dew point of the vapor, which
corresponds to the highest temperature where vapor starts condensing. The x,y plot typically shows
the vapor and liquid molar fraction for the lighter component in a binary mixture.
VLE data have historically been obtained experimentally. Various correlations have been constructed
to fit with the experimental phase equilibria in ideal or non-ideal situations. Another relatively recent
approach is to predict the phase equlibrium behavior based on molecular structures and interactions.

have been developed to overcome this issue, however.

2.2
The Maleta Tray Column
The main problem for the historic development of cyclic distillation was the impractical realization of
perfect liquid displacement during the vapor flow period. The 2010 patent by Maleta & Maleta [33]
solved this problem in a practical way, leading to new research interest in cyclic distillation. The oper-
ation of a cyclic distillation fitted with Maleta trays is sketched in Figure 2.2. During the VFP, only
vapor flows upwards through the column, exchanging mass with the liquid holdups on each perforated
tray. At the end of the VFP, the LFP starts. Valve positions on each tray move to the lower position,
which causes the liquid holdup on each tray to drain to the temporary reservoir below. Simultaneously,
feed and liquid reflux is allowed to flow. The LFP ends by returning the valves to their former position.
Liquid drains quickly from each reservoir to the tray below during the VFP. A computer animation and
a practial video demonstration by Maleta Cyclic Distillation are available on YouTube[39, 40]. Note
that the animation shows continuous feed flow, contrary to Figure 2.2.
Several important concepts in chemical engineering are present in this operation. During the VFP, the
holdups and compositions on each tray will change dynamically as mass is exchanged between the liquid
and vapor phases. At any moment in time, the contacting liquid and vapor cannot be in exact phase
equilibrium, which would correspond to a Murphree tray efficiency of unity:

condensation rate is also the removal rate of vapor holdup (i.e. pressure) from the column. During the
LFP, pressure will build up in the reboiler as the vaporization continues without letting the produced
vapor flow through the column. At the initiation of the next VFP, a pressure pulse may be thought to
move up through the column. In practice, many different conditions for the feed can be expected. It
could be any viable combination of composition, pressure, temperature, and liquid fraction. If the feed

the feed is above the bubble point or superheated, a flash calculation must be made to determine the

drops from tray to tray during the LFP, the liquid could momentarily be cooled since it falls to the
tray below which had operated at a slightly lower temperature during the preceding VFP. If this is
indeed a notable effect, additional heat transfer in excess of the heat of vaporization is required from
the contacting vapor.

Cyclic distillation is a complicated unit operation with both continuous and discontinuous dynamics,
as described previously. For this reason, combined with low research interest, the state of simulation

the stages will be numbered from top to bottom (1-NT). The notation will be kept consistent between

2.3.3
Combined Mass and Enthalpy Balance Model
Recent work by Andersen[18, 19] has been done to include the enthalpy balance based on the above
mass balance model of Pătruţ et al.[42] and the binary mixture enthalpy balance model of Shcroll-
Fleischer[17].
Andersen’s model uses the same mass balances as Pătruţ et al., neglecting the pressure drop and
calculating the variable vapor flow rate using a different expression, however.
The assumptions of
Andersen’s model are:
• Perfect mixing on each stage
• Vapor and liquid are always in equilibrium
• Negligible vapor holdup
• Negligible heat exchange with surroundings and column material
• Complete condensation of entering vapor – the entering enthalpy of condensation in each tray
supplies the enthalpy of vaporization for the vapor flow rate from this tray
The main advantage of including the enthalpy balance is relaxing the assumption that the liquid is
always at the bubble point and different feed conditions can be used. This could be used to introduce
feed condition disturbances. The liquid holdup temperatures can also drop below the bubble point. For
steady state design purposes, this is not important. However, for dynamics and control studies, this
phenomenon could arise due to reducing the reboiler heat duty below, which leads to bad operation.
The exact formulation of this model is quite convoluted and unclear from the referenced work; therefore
it will not be presented here.

2.4
Cyclic Distillation Control
Originally, ”controlled cyclic operation” was posed as an open-loop operating policy. The earliest control
issues of cylic distillation at the time dealt with the issue of realizing the specific lengths of VFPs and
LFPs. Control, in this thesis, is the feedback and/or feedforward control of some control objective
related to the dynamic operation. Table 2.1 shows a list of previous control studies of cyclic distillation.
The early works purely dealt with process stabilization, while the more recent controller designs ensure
the product qualities. A few of the most important works are described in the following sections.
2.4.1

strategy both in simulations and pilot-scale. Previous works had been purely conceptual and did not
specify actual control laws. The experimental setup in Figure 2.3 shows a cycled ammonia stripping
column with 5 sieve trays. Cycled operation was achieved using the 3-way valves at the feed tank and
the product tank. The feed is 0.3 m ammonia in water entering the top tray, being stripped by air
entering through the bottom tray. There is no reflux of boilup; this is a pure stripping process.

2.4.2
Matsubara et al.: Relay-Feedback Control
Following the modification to the controller of Dale and Furzer, Matsubara et al.[27] proposes that
instead of using the continuous measurement of the bottom tray composition in a discrete PI controller,
a relay-feedback controller may be used instead. Once the desired composition is reached at the bottom
tray, the controller stops the vapor flow and lets the liquid drain inside the column such that one tray
holdup drops from each tray. If the bottom tray composition is well-mixed and the measurement is
exact with instantaneous response time, this is indeed a perfect controller for composition control of one
product. Such perfect measurements are rarely, if ever, obtained – this control strategy has therefore
not been further developed or seen practical implementation in published literature. Additionally, it is
not known how this type of controller would interact when combined with other control loops necessary
for good column operation.
2.4.3
Toftegård & Jørgensen: Model Identification and Minimum Variance
Control
Previous control studies had mostly dealt with cycled stripping or other unit operations subjected to
controlled cycling. Toftegård & Jørgensen’s[41] 1986 work is general to trayed stripping, absorption, and
distillation columns. The dynamic interactions between tray compositions are mapped in a Dynamic
Quantity Interaction Diagram as in Figure 2.4 showing a 9 tray column with feed entering on tray 5
(numbered from bottom to top). Circles designate a quantity, thick arrows are mass flows, thin arrows
are other interactions, and the cut (double slash) symbols indicate a discrete time event. In the case of
cyclic distillation, the discrete time events are the liquid flows during the LFP.
On the basis of the DYQUID, a linear model with inputs xF and tvap is formulated using interaction ma-
trices. Through simulation of noisy inputs, a non-linear model for cyclic distillation is linearized using
a statistical regression model and qualitative knowledge of the interactions between tray compositions.
This could theoretically be done using real plant data instead, even at regular time intervals to obtain
an adaptive model in case of a change in model parameters. From the linearized system of equations,
the authors demonstrated the control using a minimum variance controller (MVC) tuned optimally
based on a noise disturbance. Using measurements of the composition on the feed tray (x5) and the
distillate (xd), the distillate composition is controlled by discretely manipulating the length of the VFP
from cycle to cycle. The measurement of the feed tray composition is automatically incorporated in the
controller as a feedforward element, since feed disturbances will be measurable in the feed tray before

3.1
Definition of Case Studies
Two commonly used binary systems have been chosen as test cases due to their historical and industrial
relevance in both conventional and cyclic distillation literature. The separation of benzene/toluene is a
typical example of an almost-ideal system with relative volatility at about 2.4. Thus, the system may be
simulated relatively accurately using an approximation of constant relative volatility. This speeds up
the initial system identification, controller design, and benchmarking process and allows the enhance-
ment of the methodology to be used on the more thermodynamically complex systems. Following the
simplified simulations, the benzene/toluene system will be simulated using Raoult’s law for the VLE,
which provides accurate thermodynamic predictions for this mixture.
Using the published design algorithm of Pătruţ et al. [42] and Matlab code implemented by Rasmus F.
Nielsen[44], a 14-stage design for the high purity separation of an equimolar mixture of benzene/toluene
into a 0.995 molar fraction benzene distillate and 0.995 molar fraction toluene bottoms. As the design
algorithm is only approximate, not taking the feed mixing on the feed stage into account, the column
designs are simulated until converged to a pseudo-steady state using simple discrete PI controllers to
adjust reflux and boilup rates. The code borrowed code is only used to obtain a column design, not for
dynamic simulations.
The second test case, ethanol/water, was chosen to provide an example of non-ideal thermodynamics
and because plant data from the cyclic distillation column at the Lipnitsky alcohol plant in Ukraine are
available in literature [14]. This column operates as a stripping unit taking a feed of 3.29 mol% ethanol
and using direct steam addition to produce a top product of 18.25 mol% ethanol while not exceeding
0.02 mol% ethanol in the bottoms product. As no reflux or boilup is used in this case, the dynamics
are expected to be faster than for the benzene/toluene case.
3.2
Dynamic Model for Cyclic Distillation
3.2.1
Choosing a Model
A number of mathematical models for cyclic distillation were discussed as part of the literature review.
While an effor is being made to include enthalpy balances and allow for feed conditions other than
saturated liquid, the traditional pure mass balance models have not yet been fully explored. Current
design algorithms and dynamic simulation methods are expected to work just as well, if not better,
==PAGE:36==
24
3 Methods
with a combined mass and enthalpy balance model than with only a pure mass balance model. Control
studies, however, are few and far between and have mostly been conducted using pure mass balance
models, recently being coupled to non-linear thermodynamics. A single study by Andersen et al. [18]
demonstrated some desirable controllability features of a driving force based cyclic distillation column
design utilizing a combined mass and enthalpy simulation model. The published literature on cyclic dis-
tillation control is still maturing and the pure mass balance models have not yet been fully investigated.
Therefore, for the purpose of this thesis, it is chosen to work with mass balance model of Pătruţ et al.
[45]. Due to the chosen case studies, three different kinds of thermodynamics must be implemented:
Constant relative volatility, Raoult’s law, and some type of model capable of describing the ethanol/wa-
ter system. The relatively simple Wilson activity coefficient model is sufficient for describing this system.
3.2.2
Model Implementation
The dynamic model by Pătruţ et al., outlined in Section 2.3.2, has been implemented in a Matlab
environment – all written code is available on the attached DVD. The differential equations for the
vapor flow period mass balance are integrated numerically using the ode45 solver, which is suitable
for non-stiff systems. The mass balance equations only exhibit high stiffness if any of the reboiler,
tray, or condenser holdups approach zero. The chosen model assumes constant molar holdup on trays
during the VFP. The trays in the rectifying section can, however, approach zero if the liquid reflux
approaches zero. If the mass balances for the reboiler or condenser are sufficiently negative for some
time, these holdups could eventually empty. Therefore, both for physical and numerical reasons, it will
be necessary to impose a lower limit on the liquid reflux and maintain the reboiler/condenser holdups
using additional controllers. In these case studies, perfect reboiler and condenser holdup control will
be assumed. The distillate draw rate D will control the condenser holdup, and the bottoms draw rate
B will control the reboiler holdup.
After numerically integrating the VFP differential equations for one vapor flow period of time tvap,
the liquid flow period is simulated by immediately computing the new reboiler, tray, and condenser
holdups by applying Equations (2.31) to (2.36). After the time tliq, the next VFP is again simulated by
numerical integration, using new input variables (e.g. disturbances, actuator inputs, and/or set point
changes).

3.4
System Identification and PID Controller Tuning
Traditional control theory and tuning is based on the behavior of linear systems. In such systems, the
output changes may be described as a linear combination of the input changes with fixed coefficients.
The most important consequence of this behavior is that the steady state gain of an input step change
is constant and independent of other input or state changes. The steady state gain and the dynamic
response characteristics (order, time constants) are used to tune PID controllers using various tuning
relations. For the purpose of this thesis, Skogestad’s IMC (SIMC) tuning relations will be used since they
are simple to use and founded on a good theoretical basis to achieve excellent robustness margins[48].
In order to utilize these control rules, the non-linear system must first be stabilized and reduced to a
linear model at the desired operating point.
3.4.1
System Stabilization
With a constant vapor flow rate profile through the column, the tray holdups are constant from cycle
to cycle for a constant cyclic reflux L and feed rate F. At pseudo-steady state, the tray holdups in the
rectifying section are L, while the tray holdups in the stripping section are L + F. To ensure that the
holdups are sufficiently large, a lower limit must be imposed on the cyclic reflux rate, and an upper
limit must be imposed on the boilup rate. Otherwise, the trays risk flooding or running dry; neither
the chosen model nor the simulation procedure is able to handle these scenarios.
The total reboiler and condenser holdups are integrating modes, which must be stabilized. The papers
by the group of Bîldea[12, 15, 21, 42] use the distillate draw rate to maintain the condenser level and
the bottoms draw rate to maintain the reboiler level. In the examined papers, no mention is made of
the method of level control (feedforward or feedback), it is therefore assumed to be perfect feedforward
control. The cyclic mass balances for the condenser and reboiler at pseudo-steady state can be used to
perfectly manipulate the product draw rates to maintain holdups:
Condenser:
D = V · tvap −L
(3.14)
Reboiler:

(3.15)
While this solution might be slightly unrealistic, the exact variation in holdup should not influence the
process dramatically – assuming that the condenser and reboiler residence times are sufficiently large.

For all of the transfer functions, the gain is positive, meaning an increase in reflux or boilup increases
the distillate benzene purity or the bottoms toluene purity. The gain varies up to two orders of magni-
tude, which is a severe hindrance for effective PID controller tuning. If the controllers are tuned based
on the transfer functions with the lowest gain, it is possible that the system response will oscillate
indefinitely. If the controllers are tuned based on high gains, it is possible that the overall control
performance is too conservative and cannot effectively reject disturbances. A trade-off must be made
to ensure reliable operation. For any industrial process, it is important that the products are on-spec
at all times. Minimizing operational expenditures, while important, is a secondary objective. For this
reason it is decided to tune the PID controllers based on transfer functions corresponding to the impor-
tant actuation range. For keeping products on spec, it is important to reliably increase the reflux and
boilup rates whenever the product concentrations are too low. Additionally, it is desirable to use as
little actuation as necessary. Thus, tuning the PID controllers based on the transfer functions for +5%
makes sense. To accommodate various operational scenarios, detuning by increasing τc as necessary
could be an effective solution.

4.1.3
Controller Benchmarking
Figure 4.3 show the disturbance rejection of the PID controllers tuned with τc = 1.5θ. The disturbances
are introduced after 100 cycles of pseudo-steady operation. The product purities do not deviate much
from the set point of 0.995 in any case, but the slight oscillations could probably be reduced. More

set point of 99.9% purity is too slow, while the tracking to a lower set point of 99.1% purity leads to
severe oscillations. How could this be overcome? Decreasing τc could lead to a better tracking of the
set point increase at the risk of causing instabilities with respect to disturbance rejection. On the other
hand, an increase in τc could reduce the oscillatory response of a set point decrease. These two solutions
are in direct conflict. The next section investigates a possible solution by transforming the controlled
variables.

4.1.4.1
Manual Tuning
Through trial and error and manual consideration taken to the trade-off between set-point tracking
and disturbance rejection, the following tunings were obtained for the PID controllers using logarithmic
transform on the purities:
Distillate controller: Kc = 0.1, τI = 25, τD = 5
Bottoms controller: Kc = 0.8, τI = 25, τD = 5
The disturbance rejection and set point tracking shown in Figure 4.6 is finally satisfactory. Settling
times are short, deviations are reduced, and oscillations are minimized. In addition to increasing the
gain and integral time, the derivative time constant was also significantly increased to 5 cycle times.
The performance of the same PI controllers without the derivative action is shown in Figure 4.7. The
oscillations are quite heavy, but otherwise the response is similar. The derivative action appears to be
very important for controlling a high purity cyclic distillation. Unfortunately, the derivative action of a
PID controller is highly sensitive to measurement noise. In a real application, it is therefore necessary
that the compositions are measured with both a high accuracy and precision, which is probably quite
difficult.
Inferential control coupled with state estimation and/or Kalman filtering of noise would
probably be necessary for the obtained controller tunings to be worthwhile in a practical application.
The other option would be to further detune the control loops at the cost of increasing settling times –
which is of course favored over heavy oscillations or instability.

4.1.5
Partial Conclusion and Discussion
Second order plus time delay transfer functions were estimated from the chosen actuator variables to
their controlled variables for various input magnitudes. The analysis showed high non-linearity with
respect to process gain, time constants, and time delays. This complicates PID controller tuning since
the traditional control theory is based on the behavior of linear systems. The PID controllers were tuned
based on transfer functions corresponding to +5% actuation magnitudes since it is arguably more im-
portant to be able to keep product purites at or above specification. Default tunings using the SIMC
rules lead to a slightly aggressive response with too many oscillations, especially for set-point decreases.
On the other hand, the response to increased set-points is too slow. Detuning or tightening the tuning is
an infeasible due to the system non-linearity. Using a logarithmic transform on the purities did remove
some of the non-linearity, which is a known technique from conventional high-purity distillation control.
This is evident from the more uniform transfer functions for different actuator input magnitudes. The
method of fitting to SOPTD and using the SIMC rules were not as successful using logarithmic purities
as the tunings seemed to result in low values for both Kc and τI. By manually tuning the controller
parameters, an acceptable controller performance was obtained, which relied heavily on the derivative
action of the PID controller to remove oscillations.
The necessity for increasing τI is also indicative of the high system non-linearity and interactions between
the two control loops. Thinking qualitatively about the system, it is evident that changing the reflux
L will start to have an influence on the bottom product purity after a number of cycles equal to the
number of trays caused by the liquid holdups dropping one tray every cycle. Additionally, the change
in reflux does also changes the holdup on each tray, thus gradually altering the stripping factor on every
tray in a cascade over a long amount of time. On the other hand, altering the boilup rate instantly
changes the mass transfer on all trays, rectifying the light component.

4.3
Ethanol/Water Separation
The ethanol/water separation chosen for the final case study is the beer column at Lipnitsky Alcohol
Plant, Ukraine. Plant data and a simplified sensitivity analysis was presented by Maleta et al. [14]. The
ethanol/water is a stripping column with the clarified fermentation broth entering the top stage and
saturated steam being added directly to the bottom stage. Thus, there is no boilup or liquid reflux. The
stripping column processes 30,000 L per day of fermentation broth at 3.29 mol% ethanol to food-grade
ethanol of 13-24 mol%. The typical product is 18.25 mol% ethanol. About 30 impurities constitute a
combined 0.2 mol% of the clarified fermentation broth, but these are neglected for the sake of simplicity.
4.3.1
Column Design
For a cyclic stripping beer column, Maleta et al. [14] use a linear equilibrium assumption to reach
a 5 stage column design with steam flow equal to 0.18 times the fermentation broth feed on a molar
basis. Other assumptions are perfect liquid displacement and a point efficiency of 1 (equilibrium model).
The top ethanol product has a purity of 18.25 mol%. It is important that the bottom product has a
composition of less than 0.004 mol%. The feed rate is 617 kmol/h and the steam flow is 111.111 kmol/h.
The cyclic distillation model of Pătruţ et al. [42] was modified to remove the reboiler, boilup, and liquid
reflux. Steam is added directly to the bottom tray. Top and bottom products are periodically added
to temporary storage tanks with a capacity equal to the cyclic feed rate where composition measure-
ments for control purposes may be conducted. This is essentially equal to directly measuring the top
vapor composition each period. The Wilson activity coefficient model was used for modeling the phase
equilibrium.
By simulating the proposed design, it was discovered that 4 trays is actually enough to reach both the
top and bottom specifications. This is because Maleta’s assumption of linear equilibrium was only valid
for the feed tray. As the ethanol mole fraction becomes very low in the bottom stages, the K factor for
ethanol increases significantly, which is accurately predicted by the Wilson model. The steam addition
rate was fine-tuned using a simple PI controller until the column converged to a pseudo-steady state.

circumvented by applying logarithmic transforms to the controlled variables. This is not needed to
obtain a good and robust controller performance in this low-purity ethanol stripping column.
4.4
Discussion and Future Work
Modeling and simulation of dynamic processes has advantages and disadvantages. Given an accurate
and practical process model, in-depth simulation studies may be performed at a rapid rate. Sensitivity
analyses, design, and control studies can be performed and subjected to a vast array of specifications,
parameter variations and disturbances. During this process, it is easy to become detached from the
physical reality of the process at hand. In this particular case, the cyclic distillation unit has been
modeled using molar holdups and molar flow rates. The thermodynamics are modeled based on molar
fractions. In a real plant, these entities are not measured or actuated on directly. It is simple to adjust
a molar flow rate or a holdup in a simulation study – not so in a real plant. Therefore it is important
to carefully consider the following for a physical cyclic distillation column:
• Which disturbances occur in a real plant?
• What can be measured?
• How sensitive are the measured variables to the process disturbances?
• What are the available sensors?
• Which inputs can be manipulated and controlled?
• Which actuators are used?
• What are the sensor and actuator dynamics, accuracy, and precision?
The above questions greatly advance the system complexity when compared to the current state-of-
the-art literature. Barely any experimental data are published in this field, and simulation models are
highly simplified. Consider the ethanol/water stripper simulated earlier. How could the top product
composition be measured accurately, on-line, and with fast sensor dynamics? A temperature sensor
could measure the condensate temperature, which is correlated to the composition by assuming the
condensate is at the bubble point. Pressure disturbances, heat loss, impurities, or bad mixing in the
condensate tank could influence the accuracy of this method.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis serves as an investigation and extension of the current state of cyclic distillation modeling
and control. A literature review was conducted, summarizing the most important results. Mathematical
models are mostly confined to mass balance models with few attempts at including enthalpy balances.
Recent control studies mostly rely on PI control without a specific tuning methodology. This thesis is
the first study to document the use of a tuning methodology for PID control of periodic separation. The
cyclic distillation model developed by Pătruţ et al. [42] was implemented in Matlab. Control studies
were performed on a high-purity benzene/toluene distillation column and a food-grade ethanol stripping
column based on real plant data. Skogestad’s IMC tuning relations were used to tune PID controllers
based on second order plus time delay transfer functions obtained by fitting to nonlinear model data.
The behavior of the high-purity benzene/toluene column was demonstrably nonlinear, which hinders
PID controller tuning severely. A good control performance was reached by applying a logarithmic
transform to the product purities and tuning the PID parameters manually – the SIMC tunings were
either too conservative or too aggressive due to the fixed connection between the controller gain and
integral time. The derivative action, which has not been included in previous studies, is found to be
important to reduce the oscillatory responses in this high-purity cyclic distillation. The PID tuning for
constant relative volatility could be directly transferred to the same system modeled using Raoult’s law
due to the ideality of the benzene/toluene mixture. The dynamics of the Lipnitsky ethanol stripper
were shown to be less non-ideal such that a well-performing PID tuning was easily obtained using the
SIMC tuning relations. This is both due to the lack of reflux/boilup, fewer stages, and considerably
lower purity requirements in the top product.
Major caveats of the current literature, this thesis included, is the simplistic approach taken to modeling
and control design. The models are typically mass-balance only assuming a point efficiency of unity
and perfect liquid displacement. Controller designs and tunings are based on traditional linear control
theory and manual tunings without taking robustness to process and measurement noise into account.
The next steps for this research area would be to include enthalpy balances, noise, and introduce
more advanced control strategies such as decoupling the closed-loop interactions, optimal control, and
model-predictive control. Simultaneously, it is also important to advance the practical understanding
of periodic separations. A very small amount of columns currently exist in the world, and no in-depth
experimental studies on the operation, dynamics, and control have been found in literature.
More
knowledge is required to understand this promising unit operation before widespread industry-adoption
is even a possibility.
