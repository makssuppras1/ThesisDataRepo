Abstract
Offshore wind resource assessment is a crucial task for the construction of the offshore windfarms.
Nowadays, offshore windfarms are built in shallow waters (5m-50m deep), however, the offshore wind
resources could increase by considering deeper waters.
Wind resource assessment is performed by analysing wind speed and wind directions obtained from a
meteorological masts. The met masts are equipped with several anemometers at different heights. The
main disadvantage of using offshore meteorological masts is their high costs. Therefore, an alternative is
found by using remote sensing instruments on board satellites. However, the ocean wind retrievals from
these instruments are only for 10m height. While, for a valid wind resource assessment, analysis must be
performed based on measurements obtained at turbine hub heights. In order to achieve this, vertical extrap-
olation from the remote sensing data can be applied based on Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) model.
The purpose of this project is to compare 10 years of wind resource statistics data of satellite product
from Advanced SCATterometer (ASCAT), with 10 years of output data from the NWP. Furthermore,
ASCAT is compared with 7 meteorological masts, of which 6 are located in the North Sea, and 1 is
placed in the Baltic Sea. Two products are provided from the extrapolated ASCAT, namely Usdw and
Usat. Usdw is considering long term stability correction, while Usat considers neutral proﬁles. The area
studied comprehend the North, Baltic and the Norwegian Seas. Wind proﬁles, scatterplots, and maps of
differences are shown. Results for ASCAT vs. in situ measurements, show outstanding results for FINO1
and FINO3 when considering long term stability correction, differences are below 1% and below 1.4%
correspondingly. For FINO2 is found differences above -1%, when not considering long term stability
correction. ASCAT differences with meteorological masts, that are near the coastline, show inconclusive
results whether Usat or Usdw is more accurate product. In addition, high proximity to land is found to
bias the ASCAT samples. Wind maps differences results for ASCAT vs. NWP, show that for the North,
Baltic and Norwegian Seas the usage of the long term stability correction (Usdw) provides better results.
Mean of wind speed differences at 150m is -0.3 ms−1 and standard deviation at 150m is 0.4 ms−1.

Chapter 1
Introduction
D
uring the last two decades, wind energy has been one of the main renewable energies that impacted
transition from fossil fuels to sustainable energy. According to the Global Wind Energy Council,
GWEC , the world’s cumulative installed wind capacity in year 2017 is 539,381 MW, corresponding to
approximately 22 times the capacity of year 2001 (Figure 1.1). As an example, Denmark in 2001 owned a
cumulative wind capacity of 2,489 MW [12] while in 2017 it increased more than double, reaching 5,476
MW, from which 1,271 MW [5] belongs to offshore wind power. The annual world’s cumulative wind
capacity for offshore in year 2011 correspond to 4,117 MW, increasing almost 4 times (14,384 MW) by
year 2016 [5].
Figure 1.1: Global growth of wind capacity from period 2001-2017 [5]
Nowadays, the offshore windfarms are installed in shallow waters (5m-50m deep). Offshore wind
resource could be increased by considering installations in deeper waters, where depth is below 50m [21].
Although the deeper are the waters, the higher will be the cost of the offshore construction.
Wind resource assessment is a crucial task that needs to be performed in order to obtain Annual
Energy Production (AEP) of the windfarm, the accuracy of this value is a key factor to determine project
ﬁnancial costs (Petersen & Troen, 2012). Offshore wind resource assessment is performed based on past
observations, which usually are provided by meteorological instruments installed on a mast. Currently,
meteorological masts are not available for waters deeper than 200m (Karagli et al,2013). Therefore, in
order to improve or perform the offshore wind resource assessment, other technologies could be used. This
could be for example, the usage of remote sensing techniques, such as, RAdio Detection And Ranging
(RADAR), Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR), or scatterometers and Synthetic Aperture Radars
(SAR). LIDARs installed in platforms are an alternative for replacing the met masts, however, platforms
might not be found in locations where the measurements are required Hasager et al (2013). Another
alternative for offshore wind resource assessment, could be the usage of Numerical Weather Predictions

(NWP), which is a method of weather forecasting.
Studies have been performed in order to compare accuracy between in situ measurements and
observations from scatterometers, and accuracy between in situ and SARs. Hasager et al. (2015) compared
four met masts with scaterometters on board satellites, which are, Quick scatterometter, QuickSCAT, and
the advanced scaterometter, ASCAT. In addition, this paper also used the Advanced Synthetic Aperture
Radar (ASAR) to compare with in situ measurements. Furthermore, Badger et al (2016) used Envisat
Advanced Synthetic Aperture Radar (ESAR) in combination with a NWP model and compared them with
a met mast located in the Baltic Sea.
The QuickSCAT was launched on June 19, 1999, and the mission supposed to last for a period of
36 month. However, QuickSCAT kept operative until November 23, 2009, [38] providing a maximum
of 2 samples per day for each grid cell, having an spatial resolution of 25Km and a swath of 1800Km
width. As observed in Karagli et al (2013), the samples obtained from QuickSCAT at the location of
one speciﬁc met mast, known as FINO1, correspond only to 883 samples for a duration of 10 years. It
could be argueed that a long term wind characteristic can be obtained due to the extension of its dataset.
However, it should be taken into consideration that by obtaining 2 samples per day, there might be created
a diurnal bias. Moreover, in Karagli et al (2013), state that there are lack of observations near the coast-
line where QuickSCAT grid cell might be contaminated by containing land and ocean in the same grid cell.
ASCAT is on board MetOp-A and MetOp-B satellites, which was launched in 2006 and 2012
respectively. In comparison with QuickSCAT, ASCAT can collect double the amount of samples per
grid cell and has a higher spatial resolution (12.5 km). The spatial resolution increase enables to obtain
more precise measurements. Therefore, it could provide better results than QuickSCAT. However, C.B
Hasager et al (2015), considered the synergy use combining the mentioned remote sensing instruments.
ASCAT observations were only collected for 2 years, in contrast to 10 years of data collected from
QuickSCAT and ASAR, therefore increasing the amount of available samples. Meaning that there will
be more measurements available at different timings. C.B Hasager et al (2015) states that for offshore
wind resource estimation, is more accurate to combine SAR data with scatterometer data, than combining
observations from various scatterometers, especially for the coastal regions, where SAR dominates.
As described in Badger et al.(2016), scatterometers and SARs on board satellites, are instruments
commonly used as a remote sensing technique. In addition to that, SARs are used for analysing sea
surface features, such as eddies, oil slicks, sea ice forecasting, detect and track ships [10]. This can be
achieved due to resolution of the product, from which wind maps can be retrieved at 1km grid resolution
[17]. As an advantage, remote sensing instruments can provide offshore observations for a wide extension
while a met masts only provide samples at one speciﬁc location. However, remote sensing instruments
can only provide measurements at 10m height over the sea surface, hence, wind resource assessment can
not be performed, since it must be performed based on measurements obtained at turbine hub heights.
Nowadays, offshore wind turbines have a hub heights from 80m to 130m, in addition, it is expected that
such height will increase with time [41].
Hasager et al (2015) and Badger et al (2016) state, that in order to perform a more accurate com-
parison with in situ measurements, the observations obtained from the remote sensing instruments must
be extrapolated up to the turbine operating heights. Both, C.B Hasager et al (2015) and Badger et al
(2016), have used the same extrapolation method based on logarithmic wind proﬁle equation and using an
equation from Charnock (1955). Additionally, Kelly et al (2010) explains that such extrapolation could
also be made considering a long term correction parameter. The computation of such parameter is based
on a NWP. In Badger et al.(2016), the used NWP model was the Weather Research and Forecasting model
(WRF). Furthermore, the mesoescale model WRF, it has been used for developing the New European Wind
Atlas (NEWA) (Hahmann et al.2017), the Wind Atlas of South Africa (WASA),(Hahmann et al.2015) and

ASCAT
The ASCAT is a real aperture radar that has been installed in two satellites, MetOp-A and MetOp-
B. MetOp-A and MetOp-B were launched in different periods, the 19th of October 2006 and 17th of
September 2012, respectively. The scope was to launch MetOp-B in order to substitute MetOp-A, however
MetOP-A exceeded lifetime expectancy, therefore, for a time being both satellites are functioning in
parallel. MetOp-AB are launched by the European Space Agency (ESA) and operated by the European
Organisation for the Exploitation of Meteorological Satellites (EUMETSAT). The ASCAT uses 6 vertically
polarised antennas which are transmitting pulse with Linear Frequency Modulation. It operates at C-band
(5.255 GHz) and running in sun-synchronous orbit at an approximately 837 km altitude. [8], [9].

The ASCAT methodology for obtaining wind speed and direction (at 10 meters height) consist of
processing the collected back-scattered pulses by 6 polarised antennas. These antennas can trace out two
550 Km wide swaths separated by a gap of 670 Km [7]. As stated in Martin 2014, the back-scattered
signal bounces back after hitting the ocean surface, however, in presence of low wind speed sea surface

2.2
NWP model
The NWP is a method of weather forecasting, that is performed by the usage of equations that
represent the atmospheric processes. Parameterized physical processes and equations are set into com-
puter code where it runs for speciﬁc domain and certain time [27]. Many approximations, estimations
and omissions are involved in different steps of the NWP during the process. These approximations,
estimations and omissions, can cause errors in their solutions [13]. The covered area for the NWP model,
is divided by grid cells. Weather predictions are made for each grid cell. The usage of NWP models
shows beneﬁts in the offshore wind resource assessment, as Karagali et al.2013 argues, occasionally,
due to some issues with the met mast, NWP models could run for long periods providing outputs at
different heights. These outputs could also be computed at different locations, where, for instance, there
are no meteorological masts. Furthermore, using NWP outputs is cheaper than installing a meteorological
mast. However, NWP models might not be accurate enough for wind energy applications due to some
imprecision by parameterizing the model [20]. Moreover, the spatial resolution of the model might affect
the results, especially in grids that have a combination of land and ocean. Meaning that uncertainty of
acquiring correct values at the coastline is higher than in deep waters. Certainly, such uncertainty depends
on the studied regions. A higher complexity of the studied offshore sites might increase the possibility of
obtaining less accurate values in the output model [40]. The NWP model used for this project is WRF
model, which is an open source that is widely used for mesoescale modelling systems.

2.3
In situ measurements
A method typically used for wind resource assessment is to install a meteorological mast in the
areas where a windfarm could be established. Ideally, stored measurements from the met mast should
represent an average value for a 10 minutes period. A reliable wind resource assessment would need
at least one year of measurements (Mortensen.2016), surely, the more years of data collected, the more
accurate assessment would be. In order to acquire consistent results, it is highly important that there is no
considerable missing or invalid data, otherwise, the outcome of the wind assessment might be biased by
experiencing seasonality. For instance, a continuous lack of measurements for 3 weeks of winter, due
to icing or any other anomaly in the measuring devices, will affect the results of the assessment, since
there will be missing 3 weeks of data where strong winds were expected. These years, where data is
missing from consecutive weeks, might need to be dismissed from the study. However, if the missing data
is distributed randomly over the entire year, this might not cause any seasonal bias.
Data from 7 different masts are going to be used in this project. The purpose of those particular
masts were to study whether the meteorological conditions were favourable or not for the installation of
wind farms. These mast are located in the North and Baltic Sea, the speciﬁed masts are listed below:

2.5
Wind proﬁles method
Wind proﬁles are obtained by computing the mean wind speeds at each available height for each
location of the 7 meteorological masts. For each location, the wind proﬁle will contain:
• The two ASCAT products (Usdw and Usat) of the grid cells that contain the meteorological mast.
• The WRF output from the cell that contains the met mast.
• A proﬁle from the raw in situ data will also be combined into same graph.
• If necessary, depending on the mast, the in situ data would be ﬁltered from possible wake effects
from nearby wind farms. Then, data will be ﬁltered from the date of the windfarm ﬁrst power
generation. Then the ﬁltered proﬁle will be added to the other proﬁles.
It is expected that each proﬁle will comprehend 4 or 5 wind proﬁles for each of the 7 locations.
A graph of wind proﬁles differences will be plotted for each location. This consist of computing the
difference between Usat and Usdw with the in situ data, ﬁltered in situ data and data from the mesoescale
WRF model. Furthermore, an error of the comparisons will be calculated based on equation 2.13.

Chapter 4
Discussion
The offshore wind energy industry is looking for an alternative ways for gathering data, that would
be as reliable as the conventional meteorological mast. This project has focused in comparing the accuracy
of extrapolated ASCAT winds using neutral and stability dependent proﬁles with different meteorological
mast.
From ASCAT mean wind speed maps (ﬁgure 2.4 and 2.5), can be observed that values obtained
near the coastline are overestimated due to inﬂuence of grid cells that has both-land and Sea. Land
rugosity provokes a stronger back-scattered signal, as a result, this generates an overestimation in the
samples. From the right maps from ﬁgures 2.4 and 2.5, can be interpreted that the offshore area close to
Turku, Finland, are highly biased due to the existence of abundant amount of small Islands in the area.
In addition, it is also observed that the area of Rotterdam, Netherlands, it contains highly contaminated
samples. This issue is also highlighted by C.B. Hasager et al.2015, where they argued that this might be
caused due to possible reﬂections from ships, since the area is inﬂuenced by high maritime trafﬁc through
the channels [19]. Another factor to consider, is that wind-farms can behave as a corner reﬂector where
strong back-scattered signal can be returned from the wind turbines, and as a consequence, a high mean
wind speed value.
The WRF Model has a very important role in this project, since the comparisons made between
Usdw and in situ measurements are directly depending on the long term correction values obtained from
WRF samples. Meaning that distorted WRF values will affect the Usdw values. Most of the biased results
obtained from the model are due to inaccurate parameterizations. Opposite to ASCAT, WRF tends to
underestimate the values in the coastlines as it can be observed in ﬁgure 2.7.
Conventional meteorological mast are considered as one of the most reliable instruments for mea-
suring wind speed, this is this due to their low uncertainty [6], although met mast can be inﬂuenced by
nearby windfarms and from boom mast. Observations obtained by satellites might be erroneous due
to biased back-scattered signal from the Sea. However, Barthelmie & Pryor (2003) estimates that a
reasonable mean wind speed can be obtained by using between 50 to 70 samples for an uncertainty of
±10% and conﬁdence level 90%. It can be assumed that for this project, the statistical uncertainty for
ASCAT measurements decreases due to the important amount of provided samples. For WRF at Horns
Rev, it is found by Pe˜na & Hahmann (2012) that most of the uncertainties are found in the estimation
of the heat ﬂux. As Pe˜na & Hahmann (2012) states, the uncertainty of the heat ﬂux is four orders of
magnitude larger than the uncertainty of the wind speed [36]. This increments the uncertainty of ASCAT
extrapolated winds, since as observed in equation 2.3, the extrapolation method is inﬂuenced by the heat
ﬂux. Therefore, ASCAT extrapolated data is not as reliable as the measurements obtained from the cup
anemometers.

different remote sensing instruments with measurements from met masts. C.B. Hasager et al.2015 used

C.B. Hasager et al.(2015), the reason for EZ results is an overestimation in the satellite samples due to
proximity to land.
Furthermore, Karagli et al.(2013) has compared the mean wind speed difference of FINO1 and
the co-located FINO1 from QSCAT, where studied period was from 16/02/2004 until 21/01/2007. Com-
parisons were made by extrapolating measurements from 33m to 10m. Extrapolation was made by
considering neutral (ENW) and stability dependent proﬁles (SDIS). The results showed differences of
-0.5% for ENW and 2.1% for SDIS. Although the difference provided in ENW is considered small, the
values found in this project (ﬁltered - Usdw) shows better results, 0.12% at 33m and -0.07% at 40m. It
could be argued, that results are better in this project, due to the usage of ASCAT instead of QSCAT.
ASCAT uses a higher spatial resolution (12.5Km) compared to QSCAT (25Km). Both, Karagli et al.(2013)
and this project, used the same extrapolation method.
Badger et al.(2016) used SAR data (2002-2012) and WRF Model (2011-2015). The mean wind
speed was obtained from the co-located FINO2 from SAR and WRF model. SAR values were extrap-
olated up to 100m considering neutral proﬁles (ENW100m), stability dependent proﬁles based on WRF
(SDWUST.100m) and stability dependent proﬁles based on SAR (SDWu∗.100m). All their differences with
WRF Model are between the range of -10.9% and -8.5%, while better results are found in this project.
Differences of -1.84% (10m) and -2,91% (100m) for Usdw-WRF, while for Usat-WRF results are lesser
promising, -1.66% (10m) and -6.35% (100m). Since Badger et al.(2016) and this project uses the same
extrapolation method, this might mean that the improvements on this project results with respect to Badger
et al.(2016) is due to the usage of larger amount of WRF samples and the usage of ASCAT instead of
SAR observations.
Moreover, in C.B. Hasager et al.(2015), the amount of valid samples obtained in the Baltic Sea for
ASCAT and ASAR is small. Higher number of samples are collected in this project, this can be observed
in ﬁgure 2.3, where over 5000 samples has been collected in the Baltic Sea area. This affect positively to
this project, since large amount of data reduces the statistical uncertanty.Furthermore, due to the path that
describe MetOP-AB satellites on Earth, the farther is located the mast from the equator line, it increment
the chances of collecting more valid samples. It is shown that locations above longitude 64°N have double
amount of samples than the locations in the Baltic and Mediterranean sea.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this project, it is presented comparisons between 7 meteorological masts, data obtained from a
scatterometer on board satellite (ASCAT) and WRF Model. The purpose of this project was to analyse
which of the ASCAT products (Usat and Usdw) show better results in the comparisons between ASCAT
vs. WRF and ASCAT vs. in situ. And therefore, as a result, which one could be more suitable for future
use of the offshore wind resource estimation.
ASCAT vs. in situ comparisons has shown the best results at the meteorological masts that have
least amount of missing data and are located farthest from the coastline, therefore, avoiding any biased
samples from ASCAT or WRF. This applies to FINO1, FINO2, FINO3 and IJmuiden mast. FINO1 has
differences (Usdw-ﬁltered in situ) that are below 1%. Furthermore, for FINO3, it is observed that smallest
differences (below 1.4%) are found for (Usdw-in situ). However, IJmuiden is slightly more affected from
the coastal effects than FINO1 and FINO3. It shows similar results for differences of stability dependent
proﬁles (Usdw) with in situ, and neutral proﬁles (Usat) with in situ, which is above -3%. In addition to
that, as observed from the windroses (ﬁg. 3.11), Horns Rev M7 is more affected from the coastal effects
than previous masts. M7, also shows similar results for Usat-insitu and Usdw-insitu, above -3%. On
contrary, for FINO2 better results are found in the differences (Usat-ﬁltered) when not considering long
term correction in the extrapolation, being results above -1%.
From these results, it can be interpreted that best results in the North Sea are found in the locations
that are far from the coast, and are considering the long term correction in the extrapolation method
(Usdw). Being FINO1 and FINO3 best comparisons from the North Sea. While in the Baltic Sea, in
contrary, it has been provided only one met mast (FINO2), which is located far from the coast and has
low percentage of missing data. Comparison at this met mast shows that is better not to consider the long
term stability correction for more accurate results, hence to use Usat for the Baltic Sea. For locations
that are not far from land (based from IJ and M7 comparisons), it can not be concluded if using long
term correction is better or not due to their similarity in their differences (Usat-insitu and Usdw-insitu).
However, these assumptions are not very conclusive, since they are based only on comparisons made with
one meteorological mast from the Baltic Sea, and four from the North Sea.
Comparisons performed at masts that are very close to land as EZ, are not reliable due to contami-
nated grid cells. In addition, met masts that are missing an important amount of data as GG, will not be
considered reliable due to seasonality effect.
ASCAT vs. WRF comparisons has shown that best results are found at FINO1 where differences
with Usdw-WRF and Usat-WRF are above -1%. In IJmuiden differences with Usat-WRF and Usdw-WRF
are in the range of ±1.5%. In co-located FINO3, results shows that differences are above -2.5% for
both, Usat-WRF and Usdw-WRF. Less promising results are shown for GG, where results are below 5%
for Usat-WRF and Usdw-WRF. While for M7, Usat-WRF difference is above -6% and for Usdw-WRF
it is above -4%. Furthermore, FINO2 shows results above -7.3% for Usat-WRF and above -3.3% for

Usdw-WRF. The Egmond aan Zee mast shows the worst results overall, with differences values that
are below 26% for both, Usat and Usdw. One of the main factors for these results, could be because of
contaminated grid cells that contain land and sea in the same cell.
In ASCAT vs. WRF comparison, it is observed the area that is farthest from land shows best results,
this is the case for FINO1, FINO3 and IJ. As observed, since GG, M7 and FINO2 are slightly more
surrounded by land, that can explain why their ASCAT-WRF differences are increased. Finally, the close
proximity to land of EZ, might explain why ASCAT-WRF shows such a high differences.
Despite performing comparisons between ASCAT vs. in situ, and ASCAT vs. WRF, ASCAT
vs. insitu should be considered more reliable, because results from ASCAT can be compared with real
measurements, rather than with a numerical weather prediction model (WRF). WRF Model runs are based
on approximations, estimations and omissions [13], while met mast uncertainties is considered smaller
than WRF.
As a summary, based on ASCAT vs. in situ comparisons, it can be concluded that, for North Sea
area that is far from the land, is better to consider Usdw product for possible wind resource assessment.
While Usat ﬁts better for the Baltic Sea. For areas closer to land, results can not conclude whether Usat or
Usdw suits better since comparisons with both products have equally large differences. From ASCAT vs.
WRF, it is clearly observed from the maps of differences and their histograms, that is better to use the
Usdw product in the North sea, Baltic Sea, and partially the Norwegian Sea. However, for the area around
the Norwegian ﬁords (aprox 65N,10E), Usat shows better results.

Chapter 6
Further studies
A possible improvement for further studies would be to study the masts which missing data are
below 4% and the data is not missing in continuous time. 4% represents 2 weeks for 1 year. More than
that can bring strong seasonality in the mean wind speed. In addition to that, more precise studies could
be done by considering the inﬂuenced of the boom mast in the measurements, since in this project it could
be observed the impact by looking at FINO123 wind proﬁles, where the cup anemometer is free from any
ﬂow distortion.
In this project has been concluded that grid cells that are in the coastline are contaminated and hence,
the uncertainty in the results increases in the coastline. But, as mentioned in C.B. Hasager et al.(2015),
better results are found in the coastline by combining SAR observations with scatterometers observations.
Therefore, in future studies, results of differences might improve in the coastline by considering the
combination of extrapolated ASCAT and SAR data.
In order to achieve more conclusive results, good alternative might be to perform more comparisons
considering more masts in both Seas, especially in the Baltic Sea. Ideally these mast should be placed not
too far from each other, in order to check if there is a similar pattern in the differences with Usat-in situ
and Usdw-in situ. However, this would be hard to achieve, since it is not common to ﬁnd masts that are
located not too far from each other.
Furthermore, it would be interesting to compare performances of ASCAT and WRF in other seas,
such as, the Norwegian Sea and Mediterranean Sea. It can be interesting to study other seas that are under
different climatology and geographical conditions because the conclusions obtained from there, it might
be applied to other seas that are under similar conditions.
