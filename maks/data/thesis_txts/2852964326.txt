Abstract
Integrated nonlinear photonics has become a highly active research area in recent years,
driven by the need for compact and efficient optical systems. Significant progress has been
made in developing various material platforms for nonlinear optical applications. This thesis
focuses on the linear and nonlinear characterization of microring resonators fabricated from
silicon nitride (Si3N4) and lithium niobate (LiNbO3).
Microring resonators offer a promising platform for realizing compact, efficient, and high-
performance frequency combs by leveraging Kerr nonlinearity to facilitate soliton formation.
This work aims to provide a comprehensive characterization of the linear and nonlinear
responses of microring resonators while identifying conditions critical for achieving thermal
accessibility and high output power in Kerr soliton generation.
The key contribution of this thesis is the demonstration of thermally accessible soliton
states with high output power. A soliton power of 50 mW was achieved, and a conver-
sion efficiency of about 30% was demonstrated under different operating conditions. These
advancements enable the reliable integration of microring frequency combs into practical
applications. This has significant implications for telecommunications, spectroscopy, and
metrology, simplifying system requirements for stable comb generation while maximizing
performance. For instance, in telecommunications, the findings can enable more efficient
data transmission through compact and stable frequency comb sources, improving band-
width and reducing power consumption.
Furthermore, 97.3% deterministic generation of single soliton state was achieved, repre-
senting a significant milestone in ensuring repeatable and predictable soliton formation.
Overall, the results presented here advance the development of microring resonators as a
viable technology for high-power and thermally manageable optical frequency comb gener-
ation, reducing the need for expert intervention to achieve single soliton states and making
the technology more accessible for practical deployment.

Introduction
Microring resonators are pivotal in advancing integrated optics and photonics by enabling
light manipulation at the microscale through nonlinear optical effects, such as the genera-
tion of new frequencies when intense light interacts with a medium. This thesis primarily
aims to explore and generate high-power frequency combs using microring resonators. Fre-
quency comb spectra consist of discrete and equally spaced frequency lines that can be
extensively used in applications such as precision measurements, telecommunications, and
sensing technologies.
The development of frequency combs using microring resonators has faced challenges re-
lated to low power output and conversion efficiency. Early designs suffered from high intrin-
sic losses and limited nonlinear interactions, often due to material absorption and fabrication
imperfections such as sidewall roughness and defects introduced during etching processes.
To overcome these limitations, researchers have explored alternative materials that offer
lower losses and higher nonlinear coefficients. Additionally, improvements in fabrication
techniques have boosted performance. The transition from low-quality-factor (Q-factor)
to high-Q resonators—with Q-factors exceeding 106—has been in enabling stronger light
confinement and prolonged interaction times within the resonator.
The motivation to improve microring resonators stems largely from their potential to
transform telecommunication systems by increasing data transfer rates and reducing la-
tency. Through nonlinear processes such as four-wave mixing, a single laser source can
generate multiple wavelengths, effectively forming a frequency comb. This wavelength divi-
sion multiplexing has the potential to greatly increase data transmission capacities, which
is essential in meeting the growing demand for data transfer. The rise of data-intensive
technologies, including artificial intelligence and machine learning, has led to an exponen-
tial surge in data consumption and energy usage. Data centers accounted for approximately
1% of global electricity demand in 2022, a figure that continues to rise with the expansion
of AI applications (source: International Energy Agency, 2022). Enhancing the efficiency
and capacity of microring resonator applications is therefore critical to accommodate this
demand while mitigating energy consumption.
Beyond telecommunications, microring resonators also offer promise in a range of sensing
applications, primarily utilizing their linear properties. Recent studies have demonstrated
their use in detecting chemical vapors with high sensitivity, which could be beneficial for
environmental monitoring and industrial safety [1]. Their high sensitivity to changes in
refractive index makes them suitable for detecting biological molecules, gases, and environ-
mental parameters with exceptional precision. For instance, they can be integrated into
lab-on-a-chip devices for medical diagnostics, detecting biomarkers at very low concentra-
tions. While these linear applications are noteworthy, this thesis focuses primarily on the
nonlinear properties of microring resonators and their potential to impact high-capacity
telecommunications and advanced sensing technologies through nonlinear frequency comb
generation.
This thesis aims to address the challenges in the practical implementation of high-power

Microring Resonator
In this section, the basic theory of the microring resonator will be discussed and how to
analyze the spectral characteristics of the microring resonator.
3.1
Microring Resonator
A microring resonator consists of a straight waveguide (often referred to as the bus waveg-
uide) coupled to a circular waveguide (the ring resonator). The coupling mechanism works
through evanescent coupling, where the optical fields in the bus waveguide and the ring
overlap, allowing energy to be transferred between them. This coupling is important for
controlling the resonance properties and the efficiency of the resonator, as it directly influ-
ences how much light is coupled into and out of the ring. The waveguide’s characteristics,
such as the group index, effective index, waveguide dispersion, and propagation loss, are all
factors determining the resonator’s overall performance.
• Group Index (ng): Influences the speed at which optical pulses travel through
waveguides. Defined by the equation ng = neff −λ dneff
dλ

The coupling condition between the bus waveguide and the ring resonator plays a sig-
nificant role in determining the resonance behavior of the microring resonator. There are
three distinct coupling regimes: undercoupled, critically coupled, and overcoupled. These
regimes are characterized by the amount of light coupled into the ring and how it affects
the resonance characteristics.
Figure 3.1.2 shows how the shape of the resonance and the power build-up factor changes
with addition to the three coupling conditions:
• Undercoupled Condition: (κ2 > 1 −a2 or t < a): In the undercoupled regime,
where the coupling coefficient is relatively low, meaning that only a small portion of
the incoming light is coupled into the ring. As a result, the transmission spectrum
shows minimal attenuation at resonance, and the intra-cavity power buildup is limited.
This condition is characterized by higher Q-load but lower overall circulating power
within the ring.
• Critically Coupled Condition: (κ2 = 1 −a2 or t = a): Critical coupling occurs
when the coupling losses match the internal round-trip losses in the ring.
In this
regime, all the incoming light is coupled into the ring, leading to a complete drop
in transmission at resonance.
This condition results in the most significant intra-
cavity power buildup, making it ideal for applications requiring strong light-matter
interactions, such as sensing or nonlinear optics.
• Overcoupled Condition (κ2 < 1 −a2 or t > a): In the overcoupled regime, the
coupling coefficient is higher than the internal losses of the ring. This means that the
ring loses more energy to the bus waveguide than it can retain, resulting in a broader
resonance and reduced Q-load. The transmission spectrum shows a dip that is less
pronounced compared to the critically coupled case, and the circulating power is lower
than in the critically coupled condition.

This section presents experimental measurements of group velocity dispersion (GVD) and
intrinsic quality factors (Q-factors) for lithium niobate (LiNbO3) and silicon nitride (SiN)
microring resonators. Comparing these results with theoretical simulations of GVD trends
discussed earlier highlights consistencies and deviations due to fabrication imperfections.
Understanding these linear properties allows for tailoring the resonators for efficient non-
linear processes, ultimately aiming to generate high-output-power frequency combs.
4.1
Experimental Setup
Figure 4.1.1: Sketch of the experiment setup for the linear characterization
In the setup for this experiment, the linear characterization of a microring resonator was
conducted using a setup that consists of a external cavity laser (ECL), with narrow linewidth
and high coherence, whose output is split into three different paths, each serving a distinct
function.
The first path directs the laser output to a fiber space cavity (FSC) with very low, ideally
zero, dispersion. Low dispersion are significant for minimizing distortions, ensuring precise
frequency measurement and accurate spectral analysis during calibration of frequency axis.
The second path then leads to a polarization controller, which adjusts the polarization state
to align with either the TE or TM mode depending on the measurement requirements.
After alignment, the light is fed into the device under test (DUT), which in this case is the
microring resonator under investigation. This setup allows for accurate assessment of the
resonator’s characteristics under controlled polarization conditions.
The third path is used for verification purposes by directing the laser output into a fiber
loop cavity with a known dispersion value. The known dispersion allows for a predictable re-
sponse, which can be compared against the expected behavior of the DUT, thereby verifying
the accuracy of the measurements. By comparing the response of this fiber cavity to that
of the microring resonator, the results can be cross-validated, providing greater confidence
in the accuracy of the measurements.
This three-path approach ensures comprehensive

One of the lasers (Pump) is amplified using an Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA) to
increase the power and meet the threshold conditions necessary for comb generation before
being directed into an optical circulator. From the circulator, one output is directed to a
polarization controller, which aligns the polarization before the light enters the DUT. In
this case, the DUT is a microring resonator. This ensures efficient coupling to a specific
resonant mode of the microring. The other output from the circulator is directed through a
bandpass filter to remove unwanted signals and noise, primarily due to reflections from the
chip. After filtering, the signal is sent to a photodetector (PD), and then to an oscilloscope
for analysis, specifically to observe the temporal dynamics and amplitude fluctuations of
the signal.
The second laser (Probe) also passes through an optical circulator. One of its outputs is
directed into the DUT in the reverse direction to obtain the transmission spectrum of the
device. The reverse direction measurement helps to distinguish the backward-propagating
modes and provides complementary information compared to the forward direction, allowing
for a more comprehensive understanding of the device’s transmission characteristics. This
signal then travels to the first circulator and is subsequently routed to the oscilloscope,
where it produces a beating signal. This beating allows for the measurement of the comb
detuning, which refers to the frequency offset between the generated comb lines and the
input laser frequency. This will be explained in detail later.
The remaining output from the second circulator is split into three different paths to simul-
taneously capture different aspects of the generated comb. The first path passes through
a fiber Bragg grating, which filters out the pump light, allowing the signal power of the
optical parametric oscillator (OPO) to be measured. This measurement represents the gen-
erated signal as a result of nonlinear optical processes in the resonator. This filtered signal
is then directed to an oscilloscope for visualisation. The second path goes directly to the
oscilloscope to measure the total transmission. The third path leads to an optical spectrum
analyzer (OSA) to visualize the comb spectrum.
This setup, involving multiple circulators, filters, and measurement tools, enables a com-
prehensive characterization of the microring resonator non-linear characteristics. It captures
both the power and spectral properties of the generated comb while also providing insight
into the detuning behavior. This integrated approach ensures that all relevant aspects of
the nonlinear dynamics are measured efficiently.

offset between the pump laser and the resonance of the microring mode. This detuning

To generate a frequency comb, the pump laser must be close to one of the microring’s

noisy comb generation (MI) [13].

lates inside the microring, some are absorbed by the material causing heating. This heating
changes the refractive index of the resonator due to the thermo-optic effect, which shifts
the resonance frequency. Specifically, the resonance frequency shifts to longer wavelengths
(redshifts) as the temperature increases. This thermal feedback influences the detuning and
the conditions for soliton formation.

6.1
Dispersion and Coupling Conditions for High Output Power and
Thermal Accessability
To reach high-output power combs, it is critical to understand the interplay between dis-
persion and coupling conditions within the microring resonator. Dispersion, which dictates
how different frequency components of light travel through the resonator, directly affects
phase matching and the efficiency of comb formation. Properly managed dispersion, in this
case anomalous, supports efficient comb generation by ensuring that sidebands are amplified
constructively.
Coupling conditions, on the other hand, determine how light is coupled into and out of
the resonator, influencing the balance between internal build-up of energy and extraction
of power. Overcoupling, where the coupling loss exceeds the intrinsic loss, can enhance
the output power by allowing more light to couple back to the bus waveguide, promoting
efficient parametric gain and comb generation. However, excessive overcoupling can lead
to increased energy losses, which negatively impacts the stability of the comb, making it
harder to maintain a steady and consistent output. Therefore, coupling must be carefully
balanced to avoid excessive energy losses.
The soliton power (Psol) in a microring resonator can be expressed by the following equation
[12]:

The equation for soliton power describes the balance between nonlinearity, dispersion in
the microring resonator, and the cavity parameters. The soliton power depends on the
interplay between the nonlinear refractive index (n2), effective area (Aeff), and the quality
factor (Q), as well as the group velocity dispersion (β2) and detuning (δω). Specifically, the
nonlinearity and dispersion must be carefully balanced to form a stable soliton.
The coupling efficiency (η) plays a significant role in determining the proportion of power
coupled into the resonator. A higher coupling efficiency increases the soliton power, however,
the threshold for soliton formation changes as the loaded quality factor (Q) decreases, must
also be considered. The quality factor (Q) affects how well the resonator confines light:
a higher Q results in lower energy losses per round trip, but it also leads to a decrease
in soliton power. The group velocity dispersion (β2) must be in the anomalous dispersion
regime for soliton formation, and different values of β2 can significantly influence the power
required to achieve a stable soliton.

6.4
Discussion
In comparing the results with those from other studies, it is evident that the device achieves
a significant level of output power, with around 50 mW of comb power generated in the
single soliton state. This power level is notably high relative to similar works in the field.
Most other studies have reported either high conversion efficiency or high power, but rarely
both.
For instance, while conversion efficiencies exceeding 50% have been documented,
these typically correspond to much lower comb power outputs, underscoring the trade-offs
that often characterize soliton dynamics [14].
This work demonstrates a significant advancement by generating a single soliton using a
single mode and a simple continuous wave laser. The simplicity of this approach, which
relies on manual tuning into the single soliton state with a single mode laser, contrasts
with more complex setups seen in the literature, such as those that require hybrid mode
excitation or fast frequency sweep to access single soliton, both of which involve intricate
Page 45 of 50
==PAGE:47==
Andreas Jacobsen
October 31, 2024
control over multiple parameters and increased experimental complexity [15]. A pumped
laser is also used to improve efficiency. The simplicity in this work not only emphasizes the
efficiency of the design but also its potential practicality in real-world applications, where
reducing system complexity is a key consideration.
The relatively high free spectral range (FSR) of the device, at 985 GHz, and the low
coupling Q-factor of approximately 5 × 105, both contribute to the ability to achieve this
performance. These parameters align well with the findings from Yi et al. [12]), where
high FSR and low Q-factor were found to facilitate high output power and conversion
efficiency. By optimizing the coupling conditions and dispersion characteristics, the device
achieves high output power while still retaining the ability to generate single solitons with
a straightforward tuning procedure.
While the pathway to single soliton generation requires some tuning steps and careful
control of experimental conditions, the fact that stable single solitons can be achieved with
a continuous wave laser and without reliance on higher-order mode excitation or complex
feedback mechanisms is a major step forward. This work therefore highlights an important
trade-off between system complexity and performance metrics like power and conversion
efficiency, and it provides a compelling argument for the continued optimization of simple,
effective approaches to soliton generation.

7
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the linear and nonlinear optical properties of silicon nitride and
lithium niobate microresonators to evaluate their suitability for frequency comb generation.
Linear characterization of both materials showed that silicon nitride possesses a higher Q
factor and a lower power threshold for comb generation compared to lithium niobate. Based
on these findings, silicon nitride was selected for nonlinear characterization and further
experimentation.
Nonlinear experiments explored comb dynamics in silicon nitride microresonators, focus-
ing on optical parametric oscillation (OPO) threshold, soliton existence range, and comb
detuning. Pumping three devices with varying coupling conditions and anomalous disper-
sion profiles demonstrated that higher dispersion and optimized coupling conditions lead
to higher output power combs [16]. This observation highlights the importance of disper-
sion engineering and coupling optimization in enhancing comb performance. While high
output power or high conversion efficiency can be individually attained, a trade-off is re-
quired depending on whether high conversion efficiency or high output power is prioritized.
Important to note, that for the high efficiency comb the the output power is relatively high.
A key achievement of this work is the demonstration of a deterministic pathway to single
soliton generation, simplifying access to stable soliton states. Reliable generation of single
solitons is essential for practical applications in optical communication, precision metrology,
and spectroscopy.
The demonstrated relationship between dispersion, coupling conditions, and output power
provides practical guidelines for designing microresonators with enhanced performance. The
deterministic method for single soliton generation addresses challenges in the field, although
the tuning procedure remains compound.
Future work should focus on simplifying the deterministic pathway to single soliton genera-
tion, aiming for more robust and scalable solutions. Potential techniques include optimizing
thermal tuning protocols to reduce the number of tuning steps, developing automated feed-
back control systems to maintain optimal conditions, and exploring new material platforms
with inherently favorable dispersion properties.
Addressing fabrication challenges to enable increased dispersion while maintaining high
output power is a key priority, particularly in achieving precise material control and geomet-
ric precision to ensure consistent device performance. Additionally, continued exploration
of hybrid modes and CW/CCW dynamics may yield practical methods for thermal com-
pensation, further enhancing soliton stability and accessibility. These directions are vital
for advancing the development of high-power, thermally stable Kerr solitons, with potential
applications in optical communications and beyond.
