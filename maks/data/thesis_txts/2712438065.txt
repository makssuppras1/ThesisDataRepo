ABSTRACT 
 
Enzymes are proteins with catalytic power. Their activity is very specific for determining 
substrates, they have high selectivity and additionally enzymes work under mild conditions, 
which make them have a very low environmental impact. These characteristics make enzymes of 
high interest in the industry (Sheldon & Woodley, 2018). Moreover, these catalysts have a wide 
range of products. Currently, the industry uses chemical catalysts that when compared to 
enzymes, it is clear in their drawbacks. Chemical catalysts are not as selective as enzymes, their 
reaction rates are lower and are much more toxic to the environment. Nevertheless, enzymes 
present the main disadvantage and it is their low stability in industrial processes. Enzyme stability 
still limits scale-up and industrial implementation. In enzymology, thermal stability is often 
measured, but kinetic stability (activity loss over time) is of equal importance for scale-up and 
industrial applications. It has long been known that enzyme kinetic stability can be affected by 
agitation/stirring, or what is more often referred to as ‘shear’ (Thomas & Geer, 2011). In industry, 
enzymes are therefore at risk of damage and activity loss in stirred tank operations. Nevertheless, 
the true effects of agitation remain unclear. 
 
This project focuses on the study of the stability of commercial alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) 
under industrial representative conditions. The project will apply different parameters of 
industrial interest, such as different agitation speeds, the geometry of impellers and their spacing, 
baffling of the reactor, viscosity of the fluid and air entrainment. The experiments carried out are 
all performed in a 3L laboratory-scale stirred tank reactor, with two Rushton turbines, at 25 °C 
and with a solution of PBS buffer with 0,05g/L of ADH. The stability of ADH is determined 
through the residual activity that is calculated through an enzymatic assay, and the deactivation 
constant (Kd) is also calculated. 
 
Based on the results obtained during this project, seems like agitation does affect enzyme stability 
showing a 265-fold increase of the Kd at 800 rpm with respect to the quiescent conditions. 
However, since a correlation between the activity loss and air entrainment (gas-liquid interface) 
was made, it cannot be concluded if agitation per se is the reason behind this enzymatic instability. 
Nevertheless, results show how the spacing between impellers has a negative impact on stability 
resulting in a 1,48-fold increase of the Kd from 4,5 cm to 2 cm of spacing. Furthermore, the 
addition of baffles to the reactor also increased the Kd for the two impeller types studied, by 2,6-
fold and 4,2-fold for Rushton and Pitched Blade turbines respectively. Experiments, where the 
gas dissolved in the liquid and the gas from the headspace was changed from air to nitrogen, were 
carried out, and no difference between air experiments was seen, however more experiments are 
needed to conclude the effect of the gas-liquid interface.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1. Enzymes and their relevance in the industry 
 
The chemical industry represents one of the biggest economic sectors worldwide and European 
companies play a dominant role (Wenda et al., 2011). However, this industry does not enjoy a 
reputation for being an environmentally friendly undertaking.  Many incidents around the world 
have occurred related to the release of hazardous and toxic wastes coming from the chemical 
industry. To avoid and prevent such disasters many norms and regulations have been created over 
the years, every time stricter. With these regulations, the chemical industry has shown a vast 
improvement. However, the increased focus on the environment has added pressure to the 
industries to improve or even develop new processes that are more environmentally friendly. This 
improvement challenge leads to the substitution of many chemical compounds for nature ones. 
Nature already provided the catalysts necessary to produce some of them: enzymes. Due to their 
efficiency, specificity and reaction range, enzymes are far superior catalysts for the synthesis of 
complex compounds and can be used to substitute more energy-dependent and waste-producing 
reactions. Through the years, the role of enzymes in the industry has increased, replacing many 
chemicals (Wiltschi et al., 2020). Moreover, not just more enzymes are used as a catalyst in 
different reactions but the technology of development and discovery of enzymes has progressed 
allowing us to have enzymes with higher efficiency and make improvements concerning the 
matching activities with their target substrates (Choct, 2006).  
The utilization of enzymes and protein engineering tools in the industry has extended the range 
of chemicals, pharmaceuticals and intermediates between other products that we are able to 
produce and also make possible the utilization of renewable raw materials. This has expanded the 
application of enzymes in the industry, in Table 1 (Arbige et al., 2019). An updated global market 
report for Enzymes in industrial applications states that the market should grow from 6.4 billion 
dollars in 2021 to 8.7 billion dollars by 2026 (Global Markets for Enzymes in Industrial 
Applications, 2021).

The enzyme industry as we know it now is the consequence of fast development fuelled by

Due to the well know environmental crisis the demand for safer and more sustainable processes

conditions. As result enzymatic processes are normally carried out under mild conditions, near 
ambient temperature and atmospheric pressures, this requires much less energy reducing the

1.2 Aim and Problem Statement  
 
Regardless of the numerous advantages of using biocatalysts in chemical processes, one important 
disadvantage is their poor stability in industrial operations. The utilization of enzymes in the 
industry is increasing over the years, for this reason is very important to better understand their 
activity and stability (Bulutoglu et al., 2019). 
Enzyme stability is defined by the capacity to retain its activity also as a structural conformation 
despite disruptive forces, such as increases in temperature (Yeoman et al., 2010). Enzyme stability 
is a crucial factor to determine whether the application of biocatalysts will be commercially 
successful. Catalytic proteins lose part of their activity when they are subjected to the action of 
heat, extreme pH or proteases (Longo & Combes, 1998). Moreover, it has long been known that 
enzyme stability can be affected by agitation/stirring, or what is more often referred to as “shear”. 
Every time an enzyme solution is made in industry or every time a biocatalytic reaction is run at 
a large scale, enzymes are therefore at risk of damage and activity loss. Hence, understanding this 
problem has enormous industrial relevance. 
However, it is not clear if this “shear” phenomenon per se can cause this activity damage since, 
the size of even the biggest enzyme is well beneath the Kolmogorov scale of mixing (Ghadge et 
al., 2005). 
Kolmogorov describes how energy is transferred from larger to smaller eddies, this energy 
transfer is called energy cascade. The Kolmogorov length scale defines the sizes of the eddies and 
how this energy transfer is produced. The shortest eddy scale is set by the viscosity, because the 
shorter the eddy scale, the more important the fluid's viscosity.

2. ENZYME STRUCTURE AND STABILITY 
 
The stability of enzymes is a wide and complex concept. To better understand what is behind this 
loss of activity and the causes of it, a close up of the enzyme structure is needed, and the different 
types of stability exist. An explanation of the ADH structure will also be provided as the reaction 
mechanism that follows.  
2.1. Structure 
 
Enzymes are high specific proteins whose structure determines their activity and function. A 
change in the conformation of the enzyme due to interaction with the environment the protein 
start to lose its structure; this process is defined as unfolding. Proteins are complex organic 
macromolecules of which the basic structural unit is the amino acid. There are four levels of 
protein structure that go in hierarchic order, from a linear chain amino acid sequence to a 3D 
structure.  
The primary structure of proteins is just a linear sequence of amino acids connected by a peptide 
bond. The peptide bond is formed between the C- and the N-terminal, and it is a covalent bond 
(Dulal & Swagatam, 2018). This primary structure does not convey any geometrical information.  
The secondary structure of a protein can be thought of as the local conformation of the polypeptide 
chain. This structure is formed thanks to the formation of hydrogen bonds between the amino 
acids of the backbones. There are two dominant structures formed that exhibit a high degree of 
regularity and they are connected with the rest irregular regions. These two dominant secondary 
structures are the α-helix and β-sheet, Figure 1. The α-helix is a rod-like structure whose inner 
section is formed by a tightly coiled main chain, with its side chains extending outward in a helical 
array. On the other hand, β-sheet are covalently linked chains of amino acids by hydrogen bonds, 
they can be parallel or antiparallel depending on the direction of the chains.

The tertiary structure of a protein refers to the overall three-dimensional arrangement of its 
polypeptide chain in space. It is generally stabilized by outside polar hydrophilic hydrogen and 
ionic bond interactions and internal hydrophobic interaction between non-polar amino acids side 
chains (Rédei, 2008). The tertiary structure results in one individual sub-unit. 
The quaternary structure is the stable association of multiple polypeptide chains resulting in an 
active unit. Not all proteins can exhibit a quaternary structure. However, it is found that the 
quaternary structure is stabilized mainly by the same non-covalent interactions as tertiary 
structures, such as hydrogen bonding, van der walls interactions, and ionic bonding (Dulal & 
Swagatam, 2018). The polypeptide chains can be assembled intro dimer, timers and tetramers 
depending on the number of subunits. Since the quaternary structure is of high complexity, there 
is yet a lot to be understood.

Kinetic stability on the other hand can be compromised by various factors such as the gas-liquid 
interface and mass transfer as agitation.  
 
 
Enzymes are susceptible to the gas-liquid interface, mainly due to the hydrophobic air/oxygen in 
the interface. When the interaction with the interface occurs, enzymes are absorbed on the surface 
which leads to protein deactivation, due to unfolding and aggregation (Donaldson et al., 1980). 
The conformation of protein molecules adsorbed at interfaces is determined by a balance of 
various forces, including van der Waals attraction, electrostatic interactions, hydrogen bonds and 
other complex interactions related to the hydrophobic effects (Yano, 2012).

Further from the stability loss caused by the absorption in the gas-liquid interface, another critical 
process is the mass transfer between different phases, specifically the gas-liquid mass transfer that 
occurs during aeration implementation. The transfer of oxygen in the aerobic process is a crucial 
process; however, it is limited by the low solubility of oxygen in aqueous media. 
The understanding of the behaviour of enzymes in the gas-liquid interface also as the mass transfer 
in this interface is vital to aim for an improved bioprocess design. 
Agitation is crucial to achieving a good mixing. Mixing is defined as the reduction of 
inhomogeneity to achieve the desired process result. This inhomogeneity can be of concentration, 
phase or temperature. Secondary effects, such as mass transfer, reaction, and product properties 
are usually the critical objectives. If the mixing fails, the costs of manufacturing may be increased 
significantly. 
It is a process required to ensure, in the case of the oxidative process, a proper oxygen transfer to 
the liquid. The speed of agitation affects these gas transfers since it can break bubbles resulting 
in bigger interfacial gas-liquid areas and as consequence increasing the bubbles' residence time, 
giving also a higher KLa, in the case of those reactions that have sparged air. In those processes 
where no air is being sparged, agitation is also essential since it is required to have a homogeneous 
reaction as already mentioned.  
Mixing is highly influenced by the impeller flow patterns. There are mainly two types of flow 
patterns with top-entering mixers, axial and radial, depending on the impeller type. There are four 
types of turbine impellers, which are characterized by the flow patterns and levels of shear they 
create: Axial flow, radial flow, hydrofoil and high-shear impellers. They have the widest use in 
low-medium viscosity liquid applications. Axial flow impellers generally have three or four 
blades and radial flow impellers are designed with six blades.  
This thesis will focus on Pitched Blade and Rushton turbines, Figure 2 . Pitched Blade impellers 
are a type of turbine classified under the axial flow impellers. The pitched blade turbine used to 
have 3 blades bolted and tack-welded on a hub with a blade angle of 45 degrees. There are two 
types of pitched blade impellers depending on the flow discharge. The Vortex type pumps the liquid 
“down”, creating a vortex in the liquid. The Scoping impeller is the opposite and pumps the liquid 
“up”, Figure 3. 
Rushton turbines are radial flow impellers that discharge the fluid radially outward to the vessel 
wall, Figure 3. The Rushton turbine is constructed with six vertical blades on a disk.

3. ALCOHOL DEHYDROGENASE 
 
Oxidoreductases are one class of enzyme that has become increasingly relevant in recent years. 
(Dias Gomes et al., 2019). Oxidoreductases comprise a large class of enzymes that catalyse 
biological oxidation/reduction reactions. As many chemical and biochemical transformations 
involve oxidation/reduction processes, the notion of developing practical biocatalytic applications 
of oxidoreductases is very attractive. (May, 1999). Asymmetric oxyfunctionalization of steroids 
and other pharmaceuticals, synthesis and modification of polymers, and construction of 
biosensors for analytical and clinical uses are some of the applications given to oxidoreductases. 
A type of oxidoreductases that has a particular interest, are those that carry out the oxidation of

in the oxidative mode, ADH has been more commonly used than AOX.  
This thesis will focus on Alcohol Dehydrogenase (E.C. 1.1.1.1). ADH occurs in large amounts in 
microorganisms and livers of animals. It plays a very important role in several physiological

many alcohols to ketones and vice versa. ADH can carry out this conversion thanks to a cofactor, 
NAD(P)H. NAD(P)H is an expensive cofactor and therefore needs to be regenerated to achieve 
an economically feasible process and to maintain the reaction towards the oxidative direction.

4. STATE OF THE ART 
 
Previous work has been published investigating the deactivation of enzymes triggered by a variety 
of industrial representative conditions. Several scientific works of literature are speaking of 
thermal stability and how temperature changes affect the activity of enzymatic reactions such as 
(Roca et al., 2007) and (Illanes, 1999).  However, there are not many studies done on stability, 
specifically with ADH. 
Anderson et al. 2020 studied the effect of stirring on the deactivation rate for different enzymes 
such as NADH oxidase, concluding with a large effect. Moreover, Dias Gomes et al. researched 
the kinetic stability loss of enzymes for both stirring conditions and the presence of a gas-liquid 
interface (Dias Gomes et al., 2019). The results showed that activity loss was more prominent 
when applying stirring than in those experiments where just aeration was applied.  Nevertheless, 
as reviewed by Thomas D. Geer et al 2010, hydrodynamic shear alone is rarely or never the cause 
of damage or losses.  
Thomas and Dunnill et al. carried out a study with yeast ADH on its activity loss by the effect of 
two different shear methods: cavitation, using a coaxial cylindrical viscometer and agitation, 
using a small reactor. It was concluded that the shear alone caused by agitation does not readily 
damage protein and that other effects such as air/liquid interfacial inactivation must be occurring 
in conjunction with shear (Thomas et al., 1979).  
Mohanty et al. studied the deactivation of lipases in a stirred vessel with two impellers under two 
conditions: the presence of a gas-liquid interface and the absence of it. They observed that under 
gas-liquid interface presence the enzyme showed a decrease in the activity and that the 
deactivation constant calculated was correlated with the power input of the agitation, on the other 
hand when the interface was eliminated the enzyme underwent negligible deactivation (Mohanty 
et al., 2001). 
It is well known that proteins have adsorption dynamics at gas-aqueous interfaces. These adoption 
dynamics are determined and affected by three main factors: the surface hydrophobicity, the 
structural flexibility and the electrostatic interactions between adsorbed protein and protein 
adsorbing to the surface (Li et al., 2021). This means that the behaviour with the gas-liquid 
interface is dependent on each enzyme.  
 
This thesis searches to bring some answers to this issue. The idea behind the experiments is to 
show the effect of different impellers and how the different flow patterns would affect the air 
entrainment in the liquid, resulting from or not instability loss. Additionally, different agitation

6.4.4 Impeller spacing 
 
Mahmoundi et al. showed evidence that the spacing of impellers affects the flow patterns created 
in the reactor. They reported that when the spacing between the two impellers the streams 
produced by the turbines was almost horizontal and parallel and that each flow pattern made by 
each turbine was very similar to that produced by a single Rushton turbine. On the other hand, 
when the spacing between impellers is short, the horizontal streams originated by the turbines 
acquired an inclined angle making the streams merge making part of the fluid to be trapped 
between the streams, Figure 31 (Mahmoudi & Yianneskis, 1992).

6.4.5 Viscosity    
 
It is well known that viscosity is a key factor in biocatalysis processes.  During some processes, 
such as enzyme catalysis, proteins need to go through conformation changes (Uribe & Sampedro, 
2003).  
It is known that viscosity can affect the bubble dynamics in a fluid. An increase in the viscosity 
results in an increase in the gas holdup and increased resistance to small bubbles to rise, and 
higher residence time (Ali & Solsvik, 2020) (Barros et al., 2022). During the rest of the 
experiments, a correlation between the activity loss results and the observation of bubbles’ 
presence was made.  
Since viscosity has effects on the bubble dynamics, this also might result in a change on change 
of how the enzyme interacts with the gas-liquid interface in solution. The effect of the gas-liquid 
interface on the enzyme is yet not clear, and neither is the effect of agitation, however, to take a 
step forward in trying to understand what is causing this activity loss, some experiments where 
the viscosity of the solution was increased were also carried out.

conditions studied are 17 cP f viscosity which resulted from having a solution of 0,5 wt% of CMC 
in the buffer and 80 cP which corresponds to 1 wt% of CMC in the buffer. The viscosities of the

6.4.6 Air Entrainment 
 
Some authors claim that the deactivation of some enzymes in processing can be correlated to the 
turbulent normal stress produced by agitation (Ghadge et al., 2005). However, it is well known 
that even the biggest enzyme is below the Kolmogorov length scale of turbulence, even at high 
local energy dissipation rates. Nevertheless, the true effects of agitation remain unclear.  
==PAGE:69==
 
 
55 
The effect of mixing on enzyme stability 
 
Until now in this project, it was observed a correlation between the agitation speeds and the 
deactivation of ADH. However, through the visual inspections done through the experiments, it 
is suspected that air entrainment (gas-liquid interface) might also be the cause of this activity loss 
since a correlation between the deactivation and the amount of bubbles and the vortex formed 
was also observed.  
Therefore, to step forward in determining if the agitation itself can cause enzyme instability, 2 
types of experiments were designed and run at 800 rpm, called: Surface Device experiments and 
Nitrogen Flushing experiments.  
 
Surface Device Experiments 
For these experiments, a device was designed and built (Figure 13) to eliminate the gas-liquid 
interface, by covering the surface of the liquid in the reactor.  
Since the device build failed to give the aimed effect the results obtained from these experiments 
are not shown in this project. The design of the device instead of decreasing the air entrainment 
enhanced the vortex effect and created more bubbles in the liquid, thus not eliminating the gas-
liquid interface. The results were discarded.  
Nevertheless, Patil and colleagues studied the deactivation of lipases at the gas-liquid interface in 
a stirred vessel (Patil et al., 2000). The deactivation was studied under two conditions: the absence 
of a gas-liquid interface and the presence of a gas-liquid interface. Patil and colleagues, as 
intended to do in this project, designed a lid to place on to f of the surface of the liquid in the 
reactor, which they called: a “circular baffle”. They managed to eliminate any type of gas-liquid 
interface with this circular baffle and they observed a clear difference between the presence and 
absence of a gas-liquid interface, the lipases were not deactivated when the horizontal baffle was 
used in the experiments. Even though the lipases are a type of enzyme very different from ADH, 
these results give an interesting view of the topic investigated and suggest that the gas-liquid 
interface does affect enzyme stability.  
To conclude if the agitation speed does have an effect on ADH stability per se or if the gas-liquid 
interface is the one causing the loss of activity observed, would be needed to design a better device 
to cover the surface of the liquid, as the one proposed by Patil et al., and run experiments at 
different agitation speeds. If when eliminating the gas-liquid interface it can be observed 
differences in the activity loss between agitation speeds, it would mean that the mixing does cause 
instability of the enzyme, making it lose activity.

7. CONCLUSIONS 
 
 
The experiments studying the effect of agitation show a trend that it is possible to 
correlate agitation speed with activity loss. The higher the mixing velocities the 
higher the deactivation constant. At high agitation speeds (800 rpm), the Kd increases 
265-fold with respect to the quiescent conditions. 
 
 
A visual correlation was made between the agitation velocities and the bubble and 
vortex formation in the reactor. 
 
 
The type of impellers used with a 3L scale does not seem to have a high difference 
regarding activity loss. However, Rushton turbines show a deeper and stronger vortex 
than the pitched blade turbine. 
 
 
Baffling seems to change the flow pattern of the liquid in the STR resulting in a 
significant increase of Kd, lower enzyme stability and a lower half-life when 
compared to experiments at the same agitation speed without baffles. The change of 
the Kd from non-baffled system to baffled system shows an increase of 2,55-fold and 
4,2-fold, for Rushton and Pitched Blade turbine respectively.  
 
 
The spacing between impellers in the STR was shown to have a clear effect on the 
stability of the enzyme while being stirred in the reactor. The closer the impellers the 
more instability is seen. Additionally, when reducing the number of impellers in the 
system to just one, the deactivation constant decreases, meaning higher enzyme 
stability.  
 
 
 
Viscosity effects do not show a clear trend on how it affects enzyme stability. A 
viscosity increase from 1 cP to 17 cP seems to decrease Kd by 1,4-fold while 
increasing the viscosity up to 80 cP increases the Kd just by 0,2-fold. For further 
conclusions, more experiments should be designed and performed in the future.  
 
 
No valid results were obtained from using the designed device to eliminate the gas-
liquid interface. A new device, such as the one used in (Mohanty et al., 2001) and 
(Patil et al., 2000), should be built and tested.

 
No oxygen dissolved monitoring was performed during the “Nitrogen Flushing 
experiments” so no firm conclusions could be made. Repetitions of experiments with 
oxygen monitoring are needed. Nevertheless, from other studies, it is suggested that 
oxygen might affect ADH stability. 
 
Some experiments should be repeated to have more data also as new experiments 
with new parameters should also be performed.

Is important to mention that fluids do not have a single viscosity. The viscosity changes with each 
shear rate. Generally, the viscosity decreases as the shear rate increases.  
To measure the viscosity different shear rates were applied to the fluid. The data shows lines due 
to the fact that first, the equipment measured the viscosity going from lower shear rates to higher 
and afterwards from higher to lower. This is made to make sure the data obtained is consistent 
since the first measures (at lower shear rates) could be obtained due to artifacts of the equipment.   
For this project, the maximal viscosity is the one being used from the part of the graph that is 
more constant, yellow scares in the graphs. Being the viscosities 0,017 Pa.s and 0,08 Pa.s for 0,5% 
and 1% of CMC respectively, which corresponds to 17 and 80 cP. 
The viscosity of a fluid changes the Reynolds number (Equation 14), and the power number that 
is specific for each impeller type changes when variation of the Re as seen in Figure B 3
