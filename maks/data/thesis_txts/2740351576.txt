1 Introduction 
The world is consuming more energy every day because of growing population, advancements in 
technology, and rising standards of living [1]. Buildings constitute 40% of energy consumption 
and 36% of greenhouse gas emissions based on research published by the European Commission  
[2]. Within this, around 11% originate from industrial manufacturing of building materials such as 
glass, steel, and cement. [3], [4]  
With the improvement of energy efficiency in buildings, not only the energy consumption goes 
down, but also thermal comfort and living quality of the habitant can be improved.  
Energy efficiency involves creating new products or services to enhance the effectiveness of 
building exteriors, improving the energy performance of heat, ventilation, and cooling (HVAC) 
and lighting systems, and maximizing the functionality of control systems [5]. This is particularly 
because HVAC systems stand out as the most energy-intensive elements in buildings [6]. 
Since the world has undergone a lot of changes towards sustainability, and is becoming 
increasingly eco-friendly, and energy-conserving, construction methods such as additive 
manufacturing (AM) or 3D printing present a green option instead of traditional building methods. 
In the construction field, with the use of a computer program, concrete layers are added on top of 
one another to build three-dimensional (3D) objects [7]. The 3D-printed concrete (3DPC) system 
consists of a concrete tank, a pumping system, a printing nozzle, and a motion control system [2]. 
Cementitious materials used for 3D printing typically need to possess some properties such as 
extrudability, flowability, buildability, and low shrinkage. Extrusion pressure and design of the 
nozzle can affect the extrusion process. The cementitious materials need to have sufficient 
rheological properties, meaning that they need to flow easily through the tank and then nozzle and 
applied to the printing surface. One of the factors which is important for fluidity of 3D printing 
materials is water/cement ratio. Besides, the addition of superplasticizer may improve flowability 
of the cementitious materials. When a material is buildable, it means that it can withstand pressure 
from the top layers without collapsing while maintaining its shape after deposition on the printing 
surface. Viscosity-modifying agents (VMAs), design elements (e.g., geometry and/or height of the 
layers) and optimization of mix setting time can be used to regulate the buildability of the 
materials. VMAs are often utilized to regulate water movement and porous structure in both the 
fresh and hardened states. Cellulose-ether derivatives are the most often utilized VMAs in 
==PAGE:5==
5 
 
cementitious materials. The role of VMAs is to increase the yield stress of the cementitious 
materials and efficiently minimize material deformation [8] . Shrinkage is one of the phenomena 
in cementitious materials which needs to be avoided since it has an impact on the element's 
appearance and mechanical performance. Using a lower water-to-cement ratio and or including 
mineral additives in the mixture, are practical ways for preventing the shrinkage phenomena [9]. 
Additionally, 3DPC has provided many positive aspects like minimized materials waste as well as 
labor costs, faster construction speed, and facilitating the fabrication of complicated geometries 
[10], [11]. Proper design of the wall structures can lead to reduced energy consumption in 
buildings, and this is an important factor that needs to be taken into consideration [2]. 3DPC is 
deemed to be one of the best choices when it comes to manufacturing complex wall structures and 
designs with regards to optimized thermal performance of buildings. 
One of the most common approaches in Europe to determine energy efficiency is to calculate the 
thermal transmittance value, known as U-value. Therefore, it is important to design building 
structures including walls and roofs to limit heat loss by achieving sufficient or in essence low U-
values [7]. Technological advancements attempt to increase building envelope efficiency with the 
help of thermal insulation, facades, walls, roofs, and windows which also play a significant role in 
the energy performance of buildings. Therefore, improving insulation materials is the second key 
factor in reducing buildings’ heating demands [5].  
There are some insulation materials that have been used in the building sector and construction 
industry, among which the three most important ones are phase change materials (PCMs), 
Polyurethane (PU) and geopolymer foam.  
Thermal energy storage (TES) using PCM is a promising method for enhancing building energy 
efficiency, drawing significant attention from researchers and engineers [12]. Since PCMs have a 
high latent heat storage capacity, they can both release and absorb thermal energy during the solid-
to-liquid phase transition. This feature makes it possible for PCMs to function as building HVAC 
systems. This is done in such a way that during the daytime, PCMs absorb excess thermal energy 
by melting, while at night, they solidify and release thermal energy, contributing to building 
temperature regulation [13]. As a result, due to this melting and solidification, the effective specific 
heat capacity of PCMs is substantially higher than ordinary materials and this serves as a central 
point to improving the thermal performance of building walls. 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
Geopolymer foam, known as sustainable cementitious materials, is another insulation material 
which is made of fly ash, water, and air combined with retarders, mineral additives, and a hardener 
to achieve better properties [14]. Geopolymer foam materials are inexpensive, sustainable, and 
lightweight. The thermal conductivity of geopolymer foams ranging from 0.15 to 0.25 (W/m.K). 
Geopolymer foams exhibit bulk densities that vary from 0.6 to 1.0 g/cm3 and porosities between 
55% and 75% [15] . 
As a conclusion, it is evident that proper design patterns of building structures along with right 
implementation of filling materials with higher specific heat capacity as well as low thermal 
conductivity pose to lead to a remarkable reduction in energy loss in buildings. As of now, with 
the advancement of 3D printing technology, now it is possible to build sophisticated geometries 
with targeted hollow thermo-structural performance. It is hypothesized that if we use 3D printing 
of complex geometries and fill parts of their hollow structures differently by polyurethane foam, 
geopolymer foam with and without PCM, we can reach higher levels of thermal insulation of 
buildings never achieved with regular materials or building processes.

2 Project plan 
According to [16], about 27% of the overall energy consumption in Europe is related to buildings, 
where 65% of which is dedicated to heat, ventilation and air conditioning or the so-called HVAC 
[17]. In this respect, countries need to improve the energy efficiency of buildings to lower the 
carbon dioxide footprint [18], [19]. Therefore, one of the main objectives of this project is to 
investigate the possibilities of improving the thermal efficiency or thermal insulation capacities of 
cementitious walls. Several hollow wall configurations will be designed in the very beginning 
phase of the project, and then these walls will be manufactured by means of an in-house 3D 
printing machine where the product quality needs to be adjusted by means of the addition of proper 
viscosity modifying agents in order to achieve a sound part with sufficient integrity. Next, the 
printed walls are filled with different materials having insulation properties like phase change 
materials (PCM), Polyurethane (PU) foam and foam geopolymer to evaluate which one has on 
overall lower thermal conductivity and high specific heat capacity. In the next phase, a thermal 
setup will be designed and made where the printed wall geometries will be embedded into the 
thermal setup’s chamber. A control system will be implemented to adjust the internal temperature 
or in other words, resemble the outside thermal conditions during winter and summer. In-situ 
monitoring using an infrared thermal camera will be established at the outer side of the chamber 
to evaluate the thermal efficiency of the wall. Then, a finite element (FEM)-based numerical model 
will be developed using the commercial software package COMSOL Multiphysics to simulate the 
thermal conditions of the printed wall geometries, and afterwards model will be validated against 
the experimental data from the previous phase. The model will be used to predict the impact of 
hypothetical geometries with different material properties or material compositions (e.g., addition 
or injection of materials into the wall cavities) on the thermal efficiency of the walls. 
In conclusion, the aims and objectives of the project are represented here together with a Gantt 
chart. This document is the draft of the final thesis and is submitted to DTU on June 16, 2024 while 
the complete thesis is due on September 30th 2024 and will be submitted to KTH, see Figure 1. 
1. A literature review will be carried out throughout the thesis to identify the gap in literature 
and also to find out about the importance of the subject in carbon dioxide reduction 
2. In steps 2 and 3 3D printing and preparations such as G-code writing will be done

In general, in this study, two sets of geopolymer foam samples were prepared: one without phase 
change material (PCM) and one with PCM. Initially, five samples were made without PCM, each 
differing in aluminum powder (Al) content. These included geopolymer with 0% Al, which lacks 
any foaming agent, and subsequent geopolymers foams containing 0.25%, 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1% 
Al, respectively. Subsequently, an additional set of five samples was prepared, this time 
incorporating PCM, with identical Al concentrations as the first set: these formulations included 
geopolymer PCM foam with 0%, 0.25%, 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1% Al. Among the aforementioned 
materials, two of the best ones will be chosen; one from geopolymer foam not having PCM and 
one from geopolymer foam having PCM. The criterion for the selection is optimal combination of 
the measured thermal conductivity as well as the specific heat capacity (can be simplified as 
thermal diffusivity) of the above listed set of materials. In the next step, different walls geometries 
will be studied experimentally and numerically. The experimental investigation involves 
temperature measurements using IR camera and the numerical track entails temperature prediction 
using thermal finite elements simulation.

3 Methodologies 
This chapter is divided into 6 subsections that cover a whole spectrum ranging from pre-printing 
preparation methods to 3D printing, material characterization and finally thermal performance 
analysis using simulations and experiments; material preparation, density measurements, 3D 
printing methods, material characterizations, numerical simulation and finally the experimental

3.1.1. Mix design for 3D printing (mortar) 
A combination of the raw materials including ordinary Portland cement (OPC), sand, VMA 
(Carboxymethyl cellulose known as CMC and Hydroxyethyl Cellulose known as HEC) and water 
are used in this study for 3D-printed walls, see Figure 2. OPC, sand and VMA were mixed into a 
beaker in drying state and with a spoon, until the mixture was homogenized. Then, Distilled water 
was added into the mixture, and they were stirred up to 2 minutes. The amount of the materials for 
each printing test is described in Table 1. It must be noted that the process of changing and 
modifying the mix design was iterated until the optimal mix design with desired optimal conditions 
were achieved. At the beginning of the study and experiment, OPC 2020 was mixed with sand, 
CMC when they were dry and then water was added afterwards, but due to the phase separation in 
cementitious materials for 3D printing, CMC was changed into HEC (4500-6500MPA.S,) 
produced by TOKYO Chemical Industry CO. LTD. Besides, the OPC was switched to the new 
version produced in 2023.

Since the microencapsulated PCM (MEPCM) material was used in this experiment was a slurry 
(see Error! Reference source not found.) and it was a commercial one (MikroCapsPCM25-
slurry), it needed to be dried. This MEPCM includes paraffin wax as a core and Melamine-
formaldehyde as the shell. Based on the description of the material provided by the supplier, 36% 
of the slurry has solid MEPCM and the rest of it is water. In order to make geopolymer constituting 
10 percent MEPCM of the total mass, 90 percent of the mixture needed to be fly ash. Therefore,

to achieve this, based on the calculation below (see Equation 1), approximately 278 grams of

find a common point. Based on the Table 2, the most common point between the geopolymer foam

3.2.1. Density measurement of Geopolymer foam, geopolymer PCM foam and Polyurethan 
To measure the density, three samples were prepared for each mix design, resulting in a total of 24 
samples. Initially, the empty graduated vials were weighed to determine their mass. Subsequently, 
varying concentrations of fly ash, activator, and aluminum powder were added to the vials, as 
detailed in Table 3. The mixture was stirred for 15 seconds. Foam formation occurred within 
approximately 4-5 minutes for each sample. All samples were maintained in a controlled 
laboratory environment at 22 degrees Celsius for six days to reach equilibrium. After this period, 
the samples were weighed again. The net mass of each sample was determined by subtracting the 
mass of the empty vial from the mass of the vial containing the mixture.
