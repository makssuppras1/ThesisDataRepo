A B S T R A C T
When driven at high levels, loudspeaker systems tend to produce aud-
ible non-linear distortion. This distortion can in some cases decrease
the systems audio quality, and it would therefore be an advantage to be
able to predict the perception of distortion through a model. Currently
the Total Harmonic Distortion Plus Noise (THD+N) and Total Inter-
modulation Distortion (TIM) are the standard metrics of perception
of distortion. These metrics do not show very good correlations with
subjective data. This study initially investigated three different metrics
known as the ”GedLee metric”, the "Perceptual Distortion Index" and
the ”Rnonlin metric”. The Rnonlin showed the best correlation with
subjective data, and was also directly implementable.
This study aims to investigate whether the metric known as Rnonlin
is generalizable for several types of loudspeaker distortions. This was
to be tested on both simulated and real loudspeaker systems. Through
implementation of the model used to calculate the metric in Matlab,
the metric was calculated for six loudspeakers and four samples at
several levels. The model showed increases in distortion as levels
were increased, but very different values were obtained for different
samples.
To test whether the Rnonlin could reliably predict distortion a
consensus meeting was held with assessors from SenseLabs listening
panel and a listening test was run hereafter. The consensus meeting
showed that the deﬁnition in the soundwheel for distortion was not
sufﬁcient in describing what to listen for. With a new deﬁnition,
listening tests were run on all systems and samples at four different
levels. The results showed that increased levels generally produced
higher ratings of distortion.
When the subjective results were compared with the obtained Rnon-
lin values, correlations were good for half the systems and samples
when viewed independantly. When viewed together there was no
correlation, indicating that the Rnonlin could not be used as a general
tool for measuring perceived distortion without modiﬁcations.
Modiﬁcations and a transformation were thus performed on the
metric. This improved correlations to the point where the modiﬁed
Rnonlin could be considered a general tool for measuring perceived
distortion.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
”Does this loudspeaker system sound good?”. This is a question often
asked by consumers when considering a purchase of any loudspeakers.
Of course this is easily answered if the loudspeakers are available for
the consumer to listen to. Loudspeaker manufacturers will plot the
frequency response of their devices while Hi Fi magazine journalists
will use a set of subjective words that can mean almost anything. So
how can the sound quality of a loudspeaker be assessed objectively?
Inspired by the food industry a department at the Danish company
DELTA, known as SenseLab, have been developing a so-called ”sound
wheel” (Pedersen, 2015) that can be used to objectively describe how
a speaker sounds. This sound wheel consists of a unique set of words
chosen speciﬁcally to encompass all aspects of the perception of sound
from a loudspeaker. These words were chosen to have the least amount
of bias in their meaning so that they could be used to objectively judge
and discuss a given loudspeaker.
Listening tests have been performed by SenseLab on attributes
relating to timbre. Timbral attributes concerning bass and treble have
a large inﬂuence on the sound quality and are easy to relate to for
assessors that do not necessarily have a background in engineering
acoustics or other specializations concerned with sound. These two
qualities mean listening tests on bass and treble attributes provide
meaningful results without large amounts of training.
Of course there are many more dimensions to sound quality than
just bass and treble. Attributes relating to the spatial and dynamic
aspects also relate to the overall sound quality of a given loudspeaker.
One major contributor to the degradation of sound quality in a given
system is the attribute known as "distortion".
In the context of the sound wheel distortion is considered to be non-
linear distortion; the distortion typically heard when a loudspeaker is
driven at high levels. Non-Linear distortion is a complex phenomenon
that occurs due to non-linearities present in loudspeakers Klippel
(2005) that can color sounds passed through a loudspeaker by adding
frequency content that was not present in the input. This can sound
good (for example in a distorted guitar) but is generally not considered
a preferable trait of a loudspeaker.
The problem is that determining the perception of distortion is only
currently possible through extensive and expensive listening tests.
Modelling this perception is therefore much cheaper, provided the
model is sufﬁciently accurate at estimating the perception of distortion.
To this day, no standard method of estimating perception of distortion

has been agreed upon. This is due to the fact that no model has been
considered accurate enough. This thesis attempts to identify a model
that is at least a contender for standardization.
In the initial phase of this project three different distortion metrics
were investigated: the "GedLee metric" (Geddes and Lee, 2003), the
"Perceptual Distortion Index" (Temme et al., 2013) and the "Rnonlin
metric" (Tan et al., 2004). These were also identiﬁed as being the most
relevant metrics in a previous study (Ploug et al., 2014).
The GedLee metric used a modelled transfer function of the speciﬁc
non-linear system. Although listening tests gave good correlations for
this metric, it was never tested for real systems, since modelling the
transfer function becomes very complicated.
The Perceptual Distortion Index used a model of the auditory system
and 9 model output variables to describe the distortion perceived
through a given system. These model output variables were weighted
with a neural network to assess which aspect of the distortion is most
prevalent. The problem with this system is that, at the of time of
writing, it can only be used on pure tone signals.
The metric of perception of distortion that was deemed to be the
most promising was the Rnonlin. The Rnonlin is based on another
distortion metric aptly named the Distortion Score (Tan et al., 2003).
While the Distortion Score was shown to be unsuccessful in predicting
the perception of distortion in real loudspeakers, Rnonlin provided
more satisfactory results. The Rnonlin uses a model that calculates
the mean sum of the weighted correlation between a distorted and
non-distorted signal, ﬁltered into bands using a gammatone-ﬁlterbank.
This provides a number between -1 and 1. The closer this number is
to 1, the less distorted the system was assumed to be.
Through several experiments the Rnonlin was shown to have a very
good correlation between subjective value and the metric itself. The
veriﬁcation experiment performed in Tan et al. (2004) used 10 different
real acoustic setups driven at both a low and a high voltage, and 12
artiﬁcial systems. One speech sample and one music sample were
used as stimuli. Eleven subjects with normal hearing were tested. A
curvilinear ﬁt between Rnonlin and the subjective data, with different
parameters dependant on the sample, was used to provide the best
correlation (r2 values of 0.92 for speech and 0.93 for music).
If SenseLab is to use this metric as a tool, the capability of the
metric to provide satisfactory results when implemented and used by
someone not afﬁliated with the original Rnonlin research project must
be assessed. Therefore this thesis concerns itself with investigating the
effectiveness of the Rnonlin metric when using other loudspeakers and
samples, with a different assessor panel. The thesis aims to answer
the following research question:
”Is the Rnonlin metric generalizable for several types of loudspeaker
distortions?”

”What can be done to improve the generalizability of the Rnonlin metric?”
To answer the ﬁrst question, the exact opposite question was con-

After the initial phase concluded, the model used to calculate Rnon-
lin was implemented in Matlab. Simultaneously, recordings of four
sound samples were performed. This was done through four real
loudspeaker systems and simulated through two artiﬁcial loudspeaker
systems. Several levels, with assumed different amounts of distortion,
were chosen. These different conﬁgurations were chosen speciﬁcally
to disprove the generalizability of the metric.
With the recordings and simulations in hand, a consensus meeting
with a selection of the assessors from SenseLabs expert assessor panel
was completed. The goal of the meeting was to develop a deﬁnition
of distortion that made it clear for the assessors what to listen for.
Another goal was to identify what problems listeners might encounter
in a listening test, and what could be done about these.
A listening test was then prepared and carried out with four levels
of distortion for all systems and samples. The assessors were tasked
with rating the amount of distortion for each of the conﬁgurations.
The subjective evaluations were compared with the values obtained
from the Rnonlin model. From here it was possible to determine
potential improvements to the model.

T H E O R E T I C A L B A C K G R OUND
This chapter covers the theoretical knowledge gained throughout the
thesis. It is built up to follow the signal from its source, through
the speaker, into a listeners ear, perception, evaluation and ﬁnally
prediction through the Rnonlin Metric.
The brunt of the theoretical background pertaining to this thesis was
garnered through attending courses that were a part of the Engineering
Acoustics programme at DTU. For this reason, this thesis assumes the
reader has knowledge of psycho- and electro-acoustics equivalent to
that of a student beginning his or her masters thesis in Engineering
Acoustics at DTU. Throughout this chapter there will be some overlaps
that are deemed essential to re-explain the theory of in the context of
this project.
2.1
signal processing
The entire process from loudspeaker to perception can be considered
one large chain of signal processing blocks. However, several signal
processing tools may be applied to the signal before it enters the
loudspeaker. Although many of these tools and other aspects of signal
processing could be described here, the correlation of signals was not
only very central to the thesis, but also the most difﬁcult to implement
in the several forms it can take. Therefore a brief description will be
given.
Essentially the cross-correlation measures the similarity of two sig-
nals as a function of different lag times. To explain this, consider two
signals, signal x and signal y both of length L. x is then shifted in time
along y for shift times (called lag times) of −η to η. For each lag time
the correlation coefﬁcient Rxy is then calculated according to eq. 2.1.

2.2
loudspeakers and distortion
When signals pass through any given loudspeaker the signal inevitably
changes, since no loudspeaker is able to produce a perfect copy of
its input signal in its output. These changes are collectively known
as signal distortion and are categorized into two parts: linear and
non-linear distortion. This section will go through the theory behind
these two distortion types and also describe a loudspeaker modelling
method known as the state space model. Finally, the current ways
of measuring loudspeaker distortion, and their inadequacy, will be
touched upon.
2.2.1
Linear Distortion
In general a change in linear distortion is perceptually experienced as
changes in timbral characteristics of a given sound. Linear distortion
implies that two mathematical properties are adhered to for the input-
ouput function of the system in question. These are additivity (eq. 2.4)
and homogenenity 2.5.
f(x + y) = f(x) + f(y)
(2.4)
f(a · x) = a · f(x)
(2.5)
These properties are together known as the principle of superposi-
tion. A simple example of how linear distortion can affect a signal is
through a linear ﬁlter as shown in ﬁg. 2.2. This ﬁltering process only
scales the gain of certain frequency components in accordance with
eq. 2.5 and no extra signal components are added to the signal.

2.2.2.1
Classic Distortion Metrics
The current standard metrics for measuring loudspeaker distortion are
the THD+N and the TIM, which rely on measuring the added harmonic
content of pure tone stimuli in the loudspeaker. The main problem
with both of these is precisely this reliance on pure tone stimuli
(Voishvillo, 2007). In most cases consumers of audio products do
not listen to pure tone signals, as that would become very tedious
in the long run. Most signals occurring in audio, such as music and
speech, are therefore complex. It has been shown that the pure tone
signals generate extra components and also that they interact. This
also happens in complex signals, but instead of being very localized
around speciﬁc tones, it happens across the entire frequency range.
Therefore masking effects may occur that the THD+N does not account
for.

2.3
psychoacoustics
When a signal has passed through a loudspeaker and the air surround-
ing it, it will arrive at a potential listeners ear. From the entrance
of the ear to the perception of sound three distinct psychoacoustic
phenomenons are essential with regard to the perception of distortion
(Tan et al., 2004): outer/middle ear ﬁltering, frequency selectivity and
loudness perception. These three topics will be covered in this section.
2.3.1
Outer and Middle Ear Filtering
The ﬁrst thing that happens when a sound arrives at a potential listener
is that it enters the outer and middle ear.
The outer ear consists of the pinna and the concha which can be
seen in ﬁg. 2.5. The Pinna ﬁlters the sound according to direction, and
is therefore closely related to the localization of sound. When wearing
headphones however, the effect of the pinna is negligible. The opening
in the ear, called the concha, passes the sound to the ear drum. From
the eardrum the middle ear begins. The eardrum and the ossicles
(the malleus, the incus and the stapes) transfer the vibrations to the
cochlea. The middle and outer ear together act as a broadly tuned
band-pass ﬁlter with resonances that make us more sensitive to sound
with frequencies ranging from 1kHz to 6kHz (Plack, 2005).
Therefore to mimic sound passing through the outer and middle ear,
an outer/middle ear ﬁlter must be applied to the signal. The transfer
function of such a ﬁlter can be seen in ﬁg. 2.6

2.3.3
Loudness Perception
When asking an assessor to evaluate a given attribute, removal of
any bias not related to the attribute is very desirable. One of the
biases that has been shown to have a large effect on evaluation of
different attributes is the difference in perceived loudness between
loudspeakers (Volk et al., 2015).
It comes as no surprise that loudness is the perception of how loud
a given sound is. Complicating matters is that as sound pressure
increases, so does our perception of different frequency components.
Roughly the perception of loudness follow what is known as equal
loudness contours, as can be seen in ﬁg. 2.8, and is measured in phon.
The phon is exactly equal to the sound pressure level at 1kHz and
changes at all other frequencies.

In the case of testing distortion perception, the assessor is tasked
with evaluating distortion from different speakers at different levels.
As the levels increase, so does the distortion and thus the output spec-
tra of the different systems changes. This change in output spectrum
will change loudness perception of the signal, but is a part of the
distortion attribute. Therefore only the lowest level signals (reference
signals) of each loudspeaker should be loudness normalized according
to the aforementioned method. All other samples should be adjusted
by both the gain factor calculated in the normalization process and
then be adjusted for the difference in recording sound pressure level
between them and the reference signal.
Removing the bias of the difference in loudness distances what the
assessors rate in the listening booths from reality. In the case of the
highest signal levels, the real situation may distort even further when
processed by the ear, because of the non-linearity present in the ear.
This effect is not captured when reducing the playback level for the
listeners in the test scenario.

An expert assessor is a person that has a high sensory sensitivity,
experience working with perceptual methods and is able to produce
consistent assessments in sensory evaluation of various products. To
become a part of this expert assessor panel, a potential candidate must
be between 20-60 years of age and have Danish as mother tongue. The

was presented with one or two out of three recordings reduced in

performed in the same way, in this case the potential candidate must
have a 90 % correct answers.
SenseLab maintains an expert assessor panel including 23 people of
different backgrounds. Once a part of the assessor panel training is
received through the participation of listening tests. The performance
of each assessor is reviewed by SenseLab after listening tests, and if it
is shown to be sub par on several occasions relevant action is taken.
Besides the expert panel, SenseLab also maintains another assessor
panel known as the Specialized Expert Assessor Panel (SEAP). The
difference between the members of the expert panel and SEAP, is that
SEAP members have agreed to participate in meetings and training ses-

the previous was (and still is being) developed in collaboration with

2.5
rnonlin model description
The ﬁnal part of the theoretical background chapter in this report
describes the most central aspect of this thesis; the Rnonlin metric and
the corresponding model used to estimate the amount of perceived
distortion from listeners.
Rnonlin takes into account that non-linearities inﬂuence the output
of system in different frequency regions. This is achieved by modelling
the auditory ﬁlters with a ﬁlterbank of gammatone ﬁlters that cover
the audible range. The model is based on the idea that the correlation
between the output of a given non-linear system and the input in the
matching frequency region can provide information on the amount
of distortion. With high correlation, the system is considered less
distorted than when the correlation is low.

It is worth noting that the described model does not rigidly state
whether the linear signal is simply the original signal (known hence-
forth as the "veridical reference") or a modiﬁed version of the original
signal. This is important since all real systems introduce linear distor-
tion which the correlation measurement will pick up on due to the
bandwidth of the gammatone ﬁlters. This is not desirable, and so the
inﬂuence of the linear distortion must be mitigated. This can be done
in several ways.
In Tan et al. (2004), the frequency range of the output was limited
to between 301Hz and 15900Hz to reduce the inﬂuence of the linear
distortion.
It is not directly clear whether the reference input to
the model was modiﬁed, but would be a necessity for the model to
function properly. This is only a partial solution since linear distortion
within the range will still occur.
Another solution could be recording the output of the loudspeakers
at very low levels and have that as the linear input to the model. This
would assume that no non-linear distortion occurs at those levels. This
may be true in some cases to a certain extent. However, with some
loudspeakers, the levels would have to be so low that noise becomes
an issue.

M O DE L I M P L E M E N TAT I ON
This chapter will give a brief overview of the implementation of
the algorithm used to calculate Rnonlin. While very similar to the
description given in 2.5, the code structure became slightly different
to the theoretical description of the model. At the end of this section,
Rnonlin values for all system/sample conﬁgurations used in this
project are provided.
4.1
method
The implementation of the model described in section 2.5 was per-
formed in matlab. An overview of the code structure can be seen in ﬁg.
4.1 with an example of the stages the signals pass through given in 4.2.
All signals were time-aligned before being loaded into the algorithm.
The input reference was decided to be the 70 db(A) recording for all
systems to reduce inﬂuence of linear distortion. The exceptions to this
are the hardclip system which used the veridical reference, and the
basement speaker which used the 60 dB(A) recording.
The input reference ﬁle and its sampling rate is read using matlabs
"audioread" function. The reference signal is then high-pass ﬁltered at
50 Hz with a fourth order butterworth ﬁlter using the "ﬁlter" function.
The subprogram "gtﬁlt4" calculates the fourth order gammatone
ﬁlterbank. The values for the ﬁlterbank, along with the high-pass
ﬁltered signal is fed into the "PrepSignal" subprogram. Here the
signals are ﬁltered with an outer-middle ear ﬁlter (OMEﬁlter), then
ﬁltered with the gammatone ﬁlterbank and ﬁnally reshaped into
frames of 1323 samples each.
From here, the reference signal enters a loop that loops over all
distorted signals that are to be compared with the reference signal.
The distorted signal is ﬁltered and run through "PrepSignal" in the
same way as the reference signal.
"MaxCorr" then calculates the
maximum correlation for each tempo/spectral block. The distorted
signal is also used to calculate a weigthing.
As a ﬁnal step the weighting is multiplied with the maximum
correlation values. The ﬁlters are then summed for each timestep and
an average is then calculated. When doing this for all the distorted
ﬁles, the algorithm then provides a table of Rnonlin values.

As the meeting was intended as a discussion of the distortion attribute,

A difﬁculty encountered throughout the meeting was that many of the

Since correlations are good for a small majority of the systems

7.2.1
Comparison to Original Results
In the original results r2 values of 0.85 and 0.89 were obtained, when
using a linear ﬁt, for speech and music respectively. These correlations
are far superior to the ones obtained through this project. There could
be several reasons for this. One of the main reasons could be the
difference in the listening test paradigm, more speciﬁcally what the
assessors were asked to rate.
In Tan et al. (2003) the test paradigm for Tan et al. (2004) is also
described. Here it is stated that assessors were asked ”to rate the
perceived quality on a 10-point scale where 10 indicates ”clean, com-
pletely undistorted” and 1 represents ”very distorted””. In this case,
listeners were not tasked with ignoring linear changes to the signal
or noise as they were in the listening test performed for this thesis.
This comes across as a bit counter-intuitive when Tan et al. (2004)
states that ”.. in this paper we focus on a method for predicting the
perceived effects of non-linear distortion only”. This would indicate
discrimination between the linear and non-linear part of the signal,
which the test paradigm does not include. It would seem that Rnonlin
is inﬂuenced by noise, linear and non-linear distortion, and if the
listeners are not discriminating the correlation would probably rise.
Since all this is interpreted by reading the paper, further clariﬁcation
from the researchers involved with Tan et al. (2004) to determine
exactly what was measured.
The question here is whether it is useful to separate non-linear
distortion from sound quality when judging a given system, which
is what is tested in this thesis. In the author of this thesis’ opinion
it would be useful to have several sets of attributes that together, in
some way, sum to the overall sound quality. To this end Rnonlin may
==PAGE:71==

still be useful if modiﬁed, the potential of which will be discussed and
investigated in the following sections.
7.2.2
Noise During Low Signal Amplitudes
To address the issue of noise during low signal amplitudes, one should
consider that the sine forest achieved a higher correlation than any
of the other systems. This leads to the question ”what does the sine
forest do that the other samples do not?”.
The ﬁrst thing to consider when answering this question is what
Rnonlin measures versus what the assessor assesses. The metric meas-
ures the weighted average maximum correlation between an input
reference signal and a distorted signal. This means if noise is present
in either sample, or uncorrelated noise in both, the Rnonlin value
decreases. Assessors were speciﬁcally told not to rate noise, so in that
respect they are performing a task that the Rnonlin is incapable of.
This relates to the sine forest signals high subjective rating/Rnonlin
value correlation because there is no temporal variation in the signal.
Assessors simply do not have the ability to separate noise from dis-
tortion in this sample because both are present in equal measure at
all times. This can be visualized by comparing the speech and sine
samples, both with added noise as in ﬁg. 7.10. At several times in the
speech sample the signal has values lower than the noise for extended
periods of time giving the assessor a cue. This does not happen in the
sine forest.
Figure 7.10: Veridical references of the speech (left) and sine forest (right)
samples with added noise.

performed.

7.3 modifying and transforming the rnonlin metric

7.3
modifying and transforming the rnonlin metric
In the case of the noise removal, it is assumed that the assessors are
able to distinguish between noise and distortion in situations where
the signal to noise ratio is very low. There may be several other
situations where the assessors can do this, and ﬁnding these may
prove a long and arduous undertaking. Both the increased level of
the references and the noise removal serve to decrease the offset in
Rnonlin values, but it is possible to take a different approach.
A few assumptions must be made clear for this approach. First of
all, the level with the highest Rnonlin value in a set of recordings of an
individual speaker for a speciﬁc sample, excluding the input reference,
is considered undistorted. All values below this are undistorted as
well. Second, the difference between this maximum Rnonlin value
and the reference Rnonlin value of 1 arises from noise. With these
two assumptions in hand, the following can be performed to achieve
a ”modiﬁed Rnonlin”:
• Sort Rnonlin values for a single system/sample conﬁguration
from lowest to highest input level.
• Find the maximum Rnonlin value that is not the reference, and
its corresponding index.
• Set all values below this index to this maximum level.
• Divide all Rnonlin values by this maximum value to obtain the
normalized Rnonlin.
• Repeat the process for all system/sample conﬁgurations.
This was done for all system/sample conﬁgurations with the lowest
input reference yielding the results shown in ﬁg. 7.18. This result is a
vast improvement on the other results, indicating that the modifying
Rnonlin with the above method brings the metric closer to the actual
perception
From looking at ﬁg. 7.18 it can be observed that the system that
is most unlike the others are the PC speakers. They provide high
subjective ratings, even when Rnonlin predicts low distortions. Very
likely this is because the PC speakers distort at all times, a fact noticed
during the recordings of these, meaning that even the lowest references
are distorted. If they are excluded, the results in ﬁg. 7.19 are obtained.

7.5 further work
65
7.5
further work
The Rnonlin metric, even in it’s modiﬁed state, is still not a deﬁnitive
measure of the perceived distortion present in loudspeakers. Due to
the limited time available, some ideas for further improvements of the
model were not implemented, yet could be implemented and tested if
any further work is done. These are summarized below:
• Run a validation test with other samples and/or systems to ﬁnd
out if the proposed changes to the model provide similar results.
• Identify a method that separates the linear from the non-linear
distortion in the model.
• Identify a method that reduces the inﬂuence of noise, present in
the loudspeakers, on the metric.
• Investigate how the Rnonlin can be implemented as a tool to
create value for loudspeaker system manufacturers.
In it’s current state, the improved Rnonlin model has only been
tested for 6 different systems and showed decent correlations with
subjective data. It is highly recommended that it is tested on more
speakers to see if similar results can be obtained. The transformation
eq. 7.1 describes, needs to be veriﬁed as it may only be a good ﬁt for the
systems and samples in this project. Note that the transformation step
was implemented only to test whether a ﬁt could be found that gave
a better result. The actual signiﬁcance of the speciﬁc transformation
and its relationship with what the assessors perceive is not completely
clear. What is clear is that perception of distortion seems to increase
rapidly when it becomes perceivable, and then saturates. This should
be considered for further revisions of the model.
Linear distortion has an effect on the metric which is not desirable.
It may be possible to circumvent this by using the coherence between
the input reference and the distorted signal, instead of the normalized
cross correlation. The coherence is less sensitive to linear distortion
and may therefore provide a better result. It should be stated that it
is very likely that the assessors are inﬂuenced in some way by the
linear properties of a given system, even when determining non-linear
distortion. Therefore complete removal of the linear distortion may
not provide the best correlation.
The other major issue is the presence of noise. In the ideal case
recordings void of background noise would be ideal, but this is not
possible, especially at low levels. The weighting function attemps to
remove the inﬂuence of low level noise but currently it uses limits
that were chosen somewhat arbitrarily for all frequencies. It may be
an idea to use different limits based on loudness contours for each
ﬁlter in the gammatone ﬁlterbank. It was also necessary to remove
==PAGE:82==
66
rnonlin evaluation
the frames with low signal to noise ratio. This issue could be resolved
when calculating the weighting function, and may be attributed to
the fact that only single time frames are taken into account. If more
frames were analysed and weighted at the same time, sections with
low signal to noise ratio could more easily be identiﬁed and given a
very low weighting.
With regard to the ﬁnal point, Rnonlin is only a good metric if it
can provide value to manufacturers. By providing the manufacturers
with both this metric and metrics for other attributes described in
the soundwheel, decisions based on how the speakers should sound,
and what attributes are valued, can be made quickly and efﬁciently.
Investigating how this could be implemented in SenseLab would
be very valuable.
A major problem for SenseLab with regard to
the Rnonlin is that it is patented. Investigating the oppurtunity of
obtaining or circumventing this patent would also be a part of this
implementation process.

C O N C L U S I O N
Initially, three metrics of interest were identiﬁed, that were all able
to measure perceived distortion. Through research, the most prom-
ising metric was identiﬁed as the Rnonlin metric Tan et al. (2004).
The Rnonlin became the main focus point for this thesis and two
research questions were developed to investigate it. These research
questions asked whether the Rnonlin was generalizable for several
types of loudspeaker distortion, and how the generalizability could
be improved.
Recordings at DTU were performed on four different systems, with
four samples at several levels. Two simulated systems were also used.
These recordings and simulations were then fed into the Rnonlin
model. The Rnonlin values obtained from modelling the metric in
Matlab showed promising results. As levels increased, so did the
amount of distortion detected by the model. Some issues were identi-
ﬁed, such as the presence of an offset from one that was thought to be
due to noise.
A consensus meeting with assessors was held to determine how
to describe what to listen for when assessors would arrive for the
listening test.
Here problems with the deﬁnition of distortion in
the sound wheel were identiﬁed. The deﬁnition was consequently
changed. The need to clarify the difference between noise, linear and
non-linear distortion to the assessors also became apparent. Finally
four levels for each system were chosen as indicative of increasing
perceived distortion based on the meeting.
Listening tests were then performed with 12 assessors. Results
provided a trend that showed that the assessors could identify in-
creases in distortion. Issues were also identiﬁed, as the basement and
PC speaker provided the highest amount of distortion at the lowest
levels.
The comparison between the subjective ratings and the Rnonlin
metric provided results that in fact, could hardly be any worse. The
r2 value when all systems, samples and levels were compared was
very close to zero, indicating that the Rnonlin metric could not be
considered generalizable at all. This answered the research question
regarding the generalizability of the metric but further work was done
to attempt to improve the metric as per the second question.
One of the major issues with the generalizability was assumed to be
due to the lack of correlation in background/electrical noise between
the input reference and the distorted signal when input into the model.
Slight improvements to the results were apparent when higher level

from samples, but nothing spectacular.
The major leap in correlation between subjective values and the
Rnonlin metric occurred when the metric was modiﬁed in an attempt

provided and r2 value of 0.64. From the shape of the data it seemed

• A model of the Rnonlin implemented in Matlab and ready for
