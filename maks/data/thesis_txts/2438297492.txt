Abstract
In this project, tensor decomposition methods were used to fill in missing data and
extract quantitative information from multispectral satellite imagery. The images
used in this analysis are of the Iberian peninsula, a biodiverse region considered
by many to be one of the main environmental hotspots of the 21st century [1].
The data consists of reflectivity measurements in the red and infrared channels,
which can be used to calculate the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI),
ideal for monitoring vegetation status.
The advantages of using tensor decomposition to analyze such data include
that the model can explicitly exploit its multiway structure, which is often lost
when traditional methods are used. Furthermore, terrain that share similar dy-
namics over time can be grouped together, those dynamics can be monitored
quantitatively and traced back to specific time points. Many different variants of
tensor decomposition exists. The most common ones, Tucker decomposition and
PARAFAC are discussed in this project. Tucker was found to be most appropri-
ate for this analysis as it is more flexible and allows for the extraction of more
meaningful quantitative information from the data in the form of time loadings.
Different methods to handle missing data were compared and an EM-Tucker
model was found to yield acceptable performance.
Tucker decomposition was used to analyze a subsection of the Iberian penin-
sula located in the province of Almería in the south. Roughly half of the study

region is covered with densely built greenhouses, whereas the other half is mostly
mountainous shrublands. With two ranks in the temporal mode, the model was

in the summer months, most likely caused by changes in reflectivity because of

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Iberian Peninsula is a highly biodiverse place. The peninsula harbors around
50% of plant and terrestrial vertebrate species in Europe, 30% of which can only
be found in the region [1]. However, climate models predict strong changes in
climate in the region and it has become one of the main environmental hotspots
of the twentieth century [2]. The projected warming in the next 37 years on the
peninsula varies between 1.48 - 5.18°C and a significant reduction in precipitation
is expected, increasing the already existing risk of drought and affecting the
availability of water resources. The mountain biomes are considered especially
vulnerable in this context. [2, 3].
Climate change coincides with changes in land cover and it has been estab-
lished that these phenomena are clearly interlinked [4]. As with climate change,
there have been significant changes in terms of land cover on the Iberian penin-
sula, referring to how much of the region is covered by forests, wetlands, or used
for agriculture etc. These changes include a decline in traditional agriculture,
conversion to forestry or intensive croplands, as well as human migration from
rural to urban areas and a growing tourism industry [5]. Loss of pastureland,
caused by the advance of shrublands has already been observed, as well as forest
decline, linked to aridification, droughts, and more frequent forest fires [6].
It is clearly important to be able to monitor changes such as these on a large
scale, to be able to quantify them, and detect anomalies. This can be done in a
==PAGE:12==
2
1 Introduction
cost-effective way using data collected by satellites. Multispectral satellite data is
highly dimensional, consisting of two spatial dimensions, a temporal one, as well
as an added dimension for each spectral band that is included in the analysis.
Since different channels represent different features in the land cover, one
can focus on individual channels, or combinations of specific channels to isolate
particular systems. The red and infrared channels are frequently used to monitor
vegetation status.
These are the channels that will be used in this analysis.
Examples of other interesting systems that can be monitored include deep water
imaging using the blue and green channels, or forest fires with anomaly detection
in the mid- and far- infrared channels. To obtain meaningful results analyzing
such data, it is important that the images have relatively high resolution, both
spatially and temporally, given the complex orography of the region [7]. This
however results in a very large amount of data that can be challenging to make
sense of.
The data is commonly reduced to three dimensions by calculating
the normalized difference vegetation index (NVDI) using the red and infrared
channel. The NDVI can serve as a good indicator of degradation due to climatic
and human impacts [7, 8].
The spatio-temporal dynamics of the satellite data are important when it
comes to understanding the land cover changes. The mere size of such data, as
well as the number of dimensions make this task challenging. To alleviate the
curse of dimensionality, traditional approaches to analyzing such data tend to
emphasize either time or space at the expense of the other. Highly dimensional
data is also often compressed when the data is preprocessed to make the analysis
more manageable, for example by combining many color channels into one. By
doing so, some of the information in the data will be lost. Some of these problems
can be avoided using tensor decomposition, a group of algorithms that is widely
used in data mining [9]. Tensor decomposition is also being applied to new fields
every year, and it is becoming increasingly useful as the computational power
==PAGE:13==
1 Introduction
3
and storage capabilities of modern computers increases [9].
With tensor decomposition, one can characterize spatio-temporal patterns
and trends, and identify different terrain that shares similar spatial distributions.
A great advantage of this method is that these changes can be quantified, traced
to specific time points, and anomalies are easily detected. Furthermore, the data
compression step required for many other methods to handle large scale datasets
is not necessary, which means that more of the information is retained. Some
work has already been carried out in terms of using tensor decomposition methods
to analyze spatio-temporal data quantitatively and it has been shown that such
an analysis can yield meaningful results [10].
One challenge to analyzing satellite data using tensor decomposition is the
presence of missing data caused by clouds and other artifacts. Valuable infor-
mation may be hidden behind cloud covers and therefore it is important to find
an adequate way to handle missing data. It is well documented that tensor de-
composition methods can be used to fill in missing data accurately, even when
a large proportion of the data is missing [11, 12, 13]. The amount of missing
data that can be handled while still obtaining accurate results will depend on the
tensor decomposition method that is used, as well as the structure of the tensor
that is decomposed. Although many variations of tensor decomposition exist,
most algorithms require complete information as the decomposition is based on
singular value decomposition. There are two different groups of methods used
to handle this obstacle, namely imputation and marginalization [9]. Imputation
means that initial guesses for the missing values are imputed in the tensor before
running the algorithm, whereas in marginalization, the algorithm is changed so
that it can simply ignore missing values.
One of the goals for this project is to use tensor decomposition methods to fill
in missing data caused by clouds and other artifacts in the satellite images taken
of the Iberian Peninsula. Another goal is to carry out a quantitative analysis using
==PAGE:14==

selected regions to detect patterns and anomalies. By doing so, it is possible to
separate individual land cover types and monitor changes within those regions
quantitatively. Furthermore, by extracting quantitative information using tensor
decomposition, anomaly detection within individual land cover types becomes
straightforward. Snow, for example could be considered an anomaly in terms
NDVI. To be able to achieve these goals, it is important that an appropriate tensor

sense of the results. A small region in Almería, in the southern part of Spain,
was found to be suitable and was used for the case study in this analysis.

Previous Work
The Iberian dataset has already been preprocessed and analyzed by Vicente-
Serrano et al. [14]. They gathered and processed daily AVHRR images from the
NOAA satellites with 1.1 km spatial resolution and compared them with other
already existing datasets for the same region. The time period that was analyzed
spanned over three decades, between 1981 and 2015. For their analysis, the data
was compressed into a 3 dimensional structure. This was done by calculating the
normalized difference vegetation indes (NDVI), a measurement calculated using
the red and the infra-red channels (see equation 2.1).
NDV I = NIR −RED
NIR + RED
(2.1)
NVDI always ranges from -1 to 1, although there will not be a distinct bound-
ary for each type of land cover. Highly positive values suggest there are dense
green leaves whereas highly negative values most likely represent water. Values
close to zero mean that there are no green leaves, which is often the case in for
example urban areas. Low positive values (approximately between 0.2 and 0.4)
indicate shrub and grassland [15].
Before compressing the channels, Vicente-Serrano et al. [14] carry out correc-
tions using calibration coefficients. Furthermore, images were discarded if they
were acquired before 10:00 or after 16:00, if they were full of clouds, badly af-
fected by scan lines, or damaged in any other ways. The data was then further

CHAPTER 4
Theory
4.1
Multiway Methods
Tensors, or multiway arrays are higher-order generalizations of vectors and matri-
ces. They have slightly different terminology compared to their one and two-way
counterparts where each dimension of a multiway array is called a mode. In this
analysis 3-way tensors will be represented with Xijk where i’s represent tensor
rows, j’s columns, and k’s tubes.
Analyzing tensors is not as straight forward as it can be with one or two-
dimensional data. Many existing methods to handle such data preserve its mul-
tiway nature, but some methods are based on matricizing the multiway arrays.
This process, called tensor decomposition, consists of finding a second order rep-
resentation of a higher order array, with the aim of finding a summarization of
the data by exploring the relationship between variables assumed to represent it.
This can be regarded as a generalization of the matrix singular value decomposi-
tion (SVD) to tensors, or in other words, multilinear singular decomposition. It
has become an important tool in modern data mining, enabling the analysis of
large scale tensorial structures that only a few years ago would not have been
possible [9]. Tensor decomposition methods do however impose a very restrictive
structure on the data that is being analyzed, and the geometry of the methods
is not yet fully understood [9].
==PAGE:24==
14
4 Theory
Tensor decomposition methods can be broadly divided into three different
families: PARAFAC, Tucker, and alternative methods; with the most well-known
models in the literature being PARAFAC and Tucker [16]. These methods follow
a hierarchy where PARAFAC can be considered as a constraint version of Tucker.
Any data set that can be modeled adequately with PARAFAC can theoretically
be modeled with Tucker [17]. PARAFAC however is simpler and it uses fewer
parameters. It is a well known principle in data science that the simplest adequate
model should generally be used as excess parameters tend to model only noise
[18].
4.2
PARAFAC
PARAFAC is used to decompose multiway datasets into loadings scores using
an alternating least squares algorithm (ASL) [19]. The algorithm finds common
patterns that are present in all samples, which makes it possible to fit them all
simultaneously.
PARAFAC is a model that yields a unique solution with no
rotational freedom [20].

The PARAFAC model only takes one parameter and that is the number of

variance for all three modes, latitude, longitude, and time in this case.
This
however does not match the assumptions that have been made about the Iberian
dataset, namely that it has high variance in the spatial modes, latitude and lon-
gitude, but low variance in its temporal mode. For a case such as the Iberian
dataset, one can therefore assume that high rank is needed for both spatial modes
to capture the variance in those dimensions of the data. To capture important
dynamics in the temporal mode however, a much lower rank is needed. An ex-
cessively high rank for the temporal mode would mean that many of the loadings
would simply be used to model noise. Because of this, PARAFAC will not be
considered as suitable decomposition method for the Iberian dataset.

4.4
Methods for Dealing with Missing Data
The Iberian dataset contains missing data and the SVD algorithm that PARAFAC
and Tucker are based on cannot handle missing data directly. Therefore some
adjustments need to be made. There are two approaches that can be taken to
handle this, namely imputation or marginalization [9]. The simplest way to ac-
complish this is with a single imputation, simply replacing the missing data with
sensible values before running the SVD. This method should only be used if the
amount of missing data is relatively low. Otherwise the results will most likely
be very skewed. A more comprehensive imputation approach would be to im-
pute missing values iteratively throughout the SVD, for example using column,
row, and tube means. It has been shown that such approaches can be used to
reconstruct a 3-way tensor where 20% of data is missing with high accuracy, and
that they can still yield acceptable results with 30-40% of the data missing. The
exact ratio of missing data that the algorithm will be able to handle will depend
on the individual dataset [24]. However, as the ratio of missing data is increased,
the model will become more likely to converge to a ”false” solution.
The second approach that can be taken is marginalization.
Here, missing
data is simply ignored. Marginalization methods are generally more challenging
to implement than imputation, but they have been shown to generally yield
more accurate results than imputation methods, with the difference becoming
more pronounced as the proportion of missing data increases. Such an approach
has been shown to be capable of recovering the true underlying components from
a noisy third order tensor with up to 99% data missing [9].
Various freely available tensor decomposition solution exist across different
platforms, including Python, R, and Matlab. Some of these solutions include
optimized built in features to handle missing data, with the most state of the
art ones currently being the N-way toolbox [25] and Tensorlab [26].
The N-

way toolbox includes an iterative EM-Tucker algorithm that uses column, row,
and tube means to impute missing data iteratively throughout its SVD. Tensor-
lab’s Tucker-based solution for handling missing data uses imputation as well.
However, a specialized version of Tucker is used, called low multilinear rank ap-
proximation (LMLRA). Here, the tensor is split into a set of small sub-tensors
that are then each decomposed individually. This can be useful when the tensor
that needs to be decomposed is particularly large [27]. LMLRA uses an adaptive
cross-approximation instead of the conventional SVD, which means that rows,
columns, and tubes are added to the model, step by step until a certain conver-
gence criteria is met [26]. Going into further details about how LMLRA works
requires introducing concepts that are beyond the scope of this thesis. Therefore,
LMLRA will only be used as a benchmark method to evaluate EM-Tucker.
Currently, there are no open source marginalization methods available for
Tucker models, only for PARAFAC [26]. PARAFAC is however, as previously
discussed, not an appropriate method for the Iberian dataset. Due to the project
scope, existing solutions will be used instead of developing new algorithms. This

loading matrix that correspond to the temporal mode, as described in chapter
4.3. The time loadings for a rank 1 and a rank 2 model were plotted and can
be seen in figure 5.14. The time loadings for the rank 2 model are clearly only
scaled versions of one another, with the sign flipped. Since Tucker decomposition
has rotational freedom, the loadings cannot be considered meaningfully different
when they are only scaled versions of one another. This confirms that the second
time loading describes the same dynamics in the system as the first one and can
therefore be considered redundant.
This can be further explored by plotting the time loadings against the mean
NDVI value of each frame. If an appropriate time rank was chosen for the system,
there should be a high correlation between the time loadings and the area they
are associated to, meaning that the time loadings explain a high amount of the
variance in the area. The average NDVI value per frame was plotted against
the time loadings from the rank 1 Tucker model and is shown in figure 5.15. As
the figure shows, they correlate perfectly. This is interesting because it means
that the magnitude of the changes happening in a given time point in the tensor
correlate perfectly to what shows up in the time loadings, meaning that the time
loadings convey both magnitude of changes in a given area and their exact timing.

6.3 Residual Analysis

6.3
Residual Analysis
The next question is how many time mode loadings are needed to describe the
whole region adequately. The reason why one would want to model the whole
region, as opposed to simply modeling individual parts of it with many rank
1 models, is that the exact borders of the region are not known; and even if
they were known, such borders could change to some extent between years. By
analyzing the study area as a whole, the model can identify which parts of the
frames belong to which geographical regions in the study area and then monitor
those areas quantitatively.
The whole tensor was decomposed using EM-Tucker models with various time
ranks, holding spatial ranks constant. The first step taken to estimate how many
time loadings are needed is a residual analysis, namely analyzing the difference
between the original tensor and the reconstructed one. Frame 179 and 361 are
used as examples and the results are shown in figures 6.8 and 6.9, respectively.
The idea here is that the time mode rank has been raised appropriately high
when a significant improvement in the residual plots can no longer be observed.
This is measured both by whether the values in the residual plots are generally
low enough and whether the residual plots look random enough. If they look
approximately random, that suggests that there are no dynamics in that region
that the model is not accounting for. For frame 179, there is a clear improve-
ment in the residuals when the time mode rank is increased from 1 to 2. This
is because the model can now account for the different dynamics in the upper
and lower part of the tensor. The improvement going from rank 2 to rank 3 is
however insignificant. Looking at the residual analysis plot for frame 361, further
supports the conclusion that rank 1 is not sufficient to describe the tensor. A
slight improvement can however be seen between the rank 2 and 3 model here.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Out of the two different types of tensor decompositions discussed in this project,
Tucker decomposition was found to be more appropriate to analyze the Iberian
dataset.
The main reasons are that Tucker is more flexible than PARAFAC
and allows for the extraction of more meaningful quantitative information from
the data in the form of time loadings.
A decision also had to be made with
respect to how to handle missing data. EM-Tucker significantly outperformed all
naive benchmark approaches and no other methods that were tested performed
better for the levels of missing data handled in this analysis. With respect to
data compression, it was found that calculating NDVI before running the Tucker
model (a 3-way model) resulted in a more accurate reconstruction than using
both channels (a 4-way mode) and calculating NDVI afterwards. Also, it was
shown that a higher rank in all modes resulted in a more accurate reconstruction.
To extract meaningful quantitative information in the form of time loadings and
to be able to separate phenomena that follow different dynamics, high rank is
assumed in the spatial modes, and low rank is assumed in the temporal mode.
The spatial mode ranks are fixed and the temporal one needs to be estimated. A
process to estimate the rank in the temporal mode was tested using simulated
systems were the rank was known.
The chosen methods and processes described above were used to analyze a
subsection of the Iberian peninsula located in the province of Almería in southern
==PAGE:82==
72
7 Conclusion
Spain. The study region can be broadly divided into two different terrain; moun-
tainous shrublands in the north and a region tightly packed with greenhouses
in the south. With 2 ranks in the temporal mode of the Tucker model, it was
able to reconstruct the tensor with acceptable accuracy, identify different dynam-
ics in the region and extract quantitative information about them individually.
Noticeable changes in the greenhouse area were observed in the summer months,
most likely caused by changes in reflectivity because of the greenhouse roofs being
opened in the summer for ventilation. The signal in the time loadings match the
time of year when cooling is most needed. Also, an anomaly was detected in the
north-eastern most part of the shrublands in the middle of the winter that could
not be explained with the land use map. Its dynamics appeared to be inversely
related to the dynamics in the greenhouse area. The most likely cause of this is
snow. A snow cover map of the region for the given time period confirms this
conclusion. The rank 2 model does therefore not only separate the dynamics
in the two different regions, but also picks up on a third phenomena caused by
snowfall in Sierra Gádor.
For this project, a detailed analysis was carried out for a single subsection of
the data and for only a single year. The same could however be done for a much
longer time period and the time loadings could then be used to detect seasonal
effects and find anomalies that deviate from them. The same process and methods
could also be applied to different sections of the tensor. By breaking up the tensor
into many smaller sub-tensors, each of the smaller tensors can be decomposed and
analyzed individually. The time rank approximation could be automated with
an algorithm similar to CORCONDIA, but adjusted for a Tucker model where
spatial ranks are held constant. With such a procedure, the whole peninsula
could be modeled. Another objective for potential future research involving the
same dataset would be a single-objective project were the only aim would be to
fill in missing data as accurately as possible. If a PARAFAC model were used,
==PAGE:83==
7 Conclusion
73
marginalization to handle missing values would be available, meaning that the
model could handle higher levels of missing data. There is a possibility that under
those circumstances it would be feasible to include all frames, including empty
ones. Furthermore, CORCONDIA is available to automatically estimate the rank
for PARAFAC models. As previously discussed, this method would however not
have produced meaningful results, quantitatively. This leads to a third possibility
for future research, which is to design a Tucker algorithm to handle missing data
with marginalization so that missing data can be handled in a more robust way.
