Abstract
Model Predictive Control(MPC) and methods to improve performance in estimating
and controlling systems are derived. This includes hard constraints, soft constraints
using slack variables, disturbance modelling, chance constraints, numerical optimiza-
tion of working point for stochastic variables, and on-line linearisation of non-linear
constraints.
Each methods are simulated on a single water filled tank simulation
where effects of each is shown. Different combinations of methods in controllers are
simulated on a combined realistic tank system with realistic rain-event. This has
shown how constraints and soft constraints are applicable for modelling overflow in
combined drainage systems while correct weighting of the soft constraints have shown
to be important for correct model behaviour and therefore controller performance.
Modelling unknown offsets in rain prognosis and adding margins to constraints based
on uncertainties have resulted in reduced cost and overflow in the simulated tank
system. Lastly a dual mode formulation is derived and tested and lays the ground for
infinite horizon cost optimal control and many related stochastic and robust MPC
strategies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Weather conditions today are seen to become more extreme where wet environments
get more wet and dry environments get more dry.
This is combined with rapid
expansion of urban areas resulting in large areas with impenetrable surfaces mak-
ing polluted storm water a larger problem. These facts put great demands on ur-
ban drainage systems which are often under dimensioned to handle the increasingly
extreme conditions resulting in more frequent and more severe flooding and sewer
overflow.
This has large economical and environmental consequences due to the
surface waters destructive forces and its pollution of local environment and water
bodies.([MHC19],[HVN10]). Furthermore the problem has resulted in EU directives
regarding bathing water and urban drainage ([Eur06],[Eur98]). The concept is seen
in figure 1.1 where the combined tank systems are often larger and more intertwined.
With computation speeds greatly increasing, a large variety of control tasks can now
be solved using more complex control algorithms. Model predictive control is one
of these. Model predictive control(MPC) offers a solution to control large combined
tank drainage systems. MPC can take in regards different factors and behaviour in
the combined tank systems including physical constraints, costs, and future weather
prognosis. Further improvements to the control can also be realised using knowledge
about the stochastic behaviour involved in weather prognosis.
1.2
Goal and project boundaries
The aim of this project is to investigate and compare different model predictive con-
trol strategies to identify how controller performance can be improved when applied
to an urban drainage problem. The methods investigated are the following; using
slack variables to model overflow, model non-linearities of weir overflow behaviour,
add probabilistic chance constraints in model, estimate prognosis offset and further-
more an arbitrary real cost function will be used to improve the control. Lastly to
permit further work on robust and infinite horizon methods the constrained MPC
problem is reformulated into a dual-mode formulation using LQ control. Simulation
of a single tank system as well as a realistic combined tank system will be constructed
and used for generating results. Simulations will be produced and discussed for the
methods individually to identify strengths and weaknesses. Simulations will be pro-

CHAPTER 2
Methods
Initially the basic ideas of Model predictive control and its usefulness is explained.
Thereafter it is shown how MPC is implemented using discrete time state space
modelling. Then it is shown how constraints on different variables can be defined to
fit a standard form and further how they can be softened using slack variables. Then
it is shown how quadratic cost functions on different variables are derived to fit a
standard formulation for the MPC problem. Stochastic methods are then described
using additive white Gaussian noise and disturbances. The stochastic section starts
with showing some helpful state space augmentations to include disturbance offsets
and dynamics into the model to again arrive at the standard formulation with white
Gaussian noise. Then the discrete time Kalman filter is derived and it is shown how
uncertainties develop through time which is useful for the predictive capabilities of
MPC. The uncertainties gained from the Kalman filter are also used when defining
probabilistic constraints. This is shown using chance constraints. Furthermore for a
system with non linear cost functions like a tank with cost on volume of water and on
overflow, it is shown how a numerical approach can find cost optimal working point
for the system.
2.1
Model Predictive Control
Common control tasks consists of a plant where some process is happening. The
plant has an output which we wish to follow a certain reference. The plant can be
excited by a control input which is driven by a controller. Furthermore measurement
of the plant gives knowledge used to generate the control input from the controller.
This system is illustrated in figure 2.1. The process can be described by

Where x(t) is the states of the plant, u(t) is the control signal from the controller.
d(t) is disturbance or unknown excitement on the plant. z(t) is the output of the
plant while y(t) is measurement and is not neccesarily a measurement of z(t). All
signals can be vectors of signals hence the plant can have multiple outputs and/or
inputs (SISO, MIMO, MISO, SIMO).

2 Methods
Figure 2.1: Illustration of a plant and controller.
In this work the focus is on model predictive controllers(MPC) based on discrete
time state space models. This choice is based on the strengths of MPC fitting the
problem of urban drainage well, while the discrete time description offers a framework
practical and well fitting to how a system is sampled and controlled in reality. The
state space description is an internal model(white box) meaning that the systems
internal states are modelled rather than just an input in relation to an output(black
box). This is useful for most application where some knowledge about processes are
known as with the structure, delays and tank sizes of an urban drainage system. When
modelling physical systems, uncertainties are unavoidable which can be estimated or
measured to improve modelling. Note that there is a distinction between the system
which is the real plant and the model which is the imitation of the plant. Model
predictive control is based on a receding horizon idea. Using the model the expected
system response is considered from the current time step k up until a prediction
horizon k + Hp. In this timespan the controller finds a sequence of control inputs u
that minimizes some defined cost function ŽĢ(u, ╦åxk) where ╦åxk is the knowledge of the
system at the current time step. As illustrated in figure 2.2 there is found a predicted
input sequence from time k to k + Hp denoted as uk. The predicted model output
╦åz is illustrated to show how the predicted input sequence is expected to affect the
output. This is all based on the model and the current knowledge of the plant e.g.
measurements or known disturbances. The controller applies the predicted control
input until new knowledge of the plant is gained. In the discrete controller this can
be done with a fixed sample time ts where at each sample a new measurement is

regards of robustness MPC is more prone to missing measurements since it can use a
previously calculated input sequence if a new is not available. Some disadvantages of
MPC is the requirement for processing power that quickly magnifies for larger models
and longer prediction horizons. Furthermore being model predictive means that the
performance of the controller is largely dependent on the accuracy of the model.

2.1.2
MPC constraints
The minimization used in MPC allows for adding constraints with respect to the
optimization variable. Initially the optimization variable is considered to consists only
of the vector of control inputs uk and later in this section more optimisation variables
are added using slack variables that enables to soften the constraints. Furthermore
using weights in the cost function, this can simulate a tank overflow in urban drainage
context. In this work only linear inequality constraints are considered of the form.

The constraints defined must be described by the controlled variable to enable the
controller to comply. This also means that constraints on variables not controllable
can not be formulated in the controller. Therefore constraints must always be thor-
oughly considered so they avoid defining an infeasible problem (or rules must be
defined in case of the problem being infeasible). This is especially important when
dealing with noisy or uncertain systems or even numerically uncertainties that may
give infeasible constraints. This can be handled by softening constraints as defined
later in this section.

exceeded. This may cause infeasibility when applied to variables affected by noise or
disturbances such as the states and output. Furthermore adding constraints to the
input control signal can further limit the ability to keep the output or states inside a

volume estimate would switch this around. In turn this also means that for most of

effect on the overflow but a much larger deviation is seen here both in errors figure

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The MATLAB Simulink model used is unique for this work hence direct comparison
of related work is di’¼Ćicult in terms of performance. In the context of urban drainage
control there is no widely used, unique and accessible benchmark model to use. The
Astlingen system is trying to define this although not much MPC theory is shown on
this. In this work the dimensions of the Astlingen was used with some assumptions
unique for this work to better focus on the chosen methods. The main goal of compar-
ing controllers and methods is shown and future work must be to use the controller
improvements in other used systems if performance is to be compared directly to
other work.
Some factors not implemented in the system and model which have an effect on the
behaviour are e.g. ground absorption, ground humidity, water evaporation, distance
and flow to the tank from different places in the specific sub-catchment area, complex
sewer structures and fluid dynamics through these. Furthermore in this system and
model the same intensity of rain falls in all sub-catchments. These are all factors that
are present in real life urban drainage problems. Most of them can be implemented
into the linearised model but are not likely to have an impact on how the controllers
compare to each other. Further work on modelling and calibration of parameters are
described in [Ocab] and [Ocac]. Furthermore for this system it must be noted that
the prognosis was fitted to the rain event where in real prognosis this is not possible
which may give larger uncertainties and offsets. On the other hand modern weather
prediction is well developed and advanced hence very precise prognosis are available,
especially for short term predictions.
Accurate estimates of volume in tanks and overflow are valuable because it can
identify the areas of a system that does not or will not perform satisfactory. Hence
the need for expansion or improvement of a real system can be identified simply by
simulation and not by experience during the next big storm. This is also the case for
designing new systems hence the methods that improved estimations are valuable.
Adding probabilistic constraints is in the full system implementations seen to have
very large impact on performance. The downside was that either overflow was over
or underestimated due to it being used in the model or not. A combination (e.g.
controller 3 and 4 from section 4.5) must then be argued to be a better solution. This
would result in using multiple slack variables on the same constraint. One that is
not in the model that allows for moving the constraints without affecting estimated
overflow. Another which is the estimated overflow that are in the model and activates
==PAGE:78==
70

only at the real constraint. Combining these requires more optimization variables and
more constraints to ensure the proper functionality. Therefore with more complex
systems the minimization problem would quickly become very large.
As discussed earlier, the cost optimized reference point or constraint margin, is
not the real cost optimal solution for the defined system. This is because the real costs
defined did not include a cost on deviating from a reference, and if it did the reference
was defined to empty tanks hence the optimized point would be zero for all tanks.
The optimal reference theory would be more useful in systems meant to maximize
some throughput with some cost functions(of any shape) defined. E.g a tank systems
meant to store water where there are also penalties for overflowing. In that case the
volume reference would be as high as possible without overflowing and the optimal
reference would be found using the technique presented in the theory and as shown
in the example in section 3.8. This could also be solved using chance constraints,
but in the case where the slack variable has a physical meaning(and cost) calculating
an optimal reference point would be preferable because the physical meaning of the
slack would be maintained because no constraints are moved. In other cases where
slack is used purely to penalize being close to constraints and where there may not
be a reference point, chance constraints would be the better choice. For robustness,
chance constraints are more useful since the chance of violating a constraint can be
set to zero.
Modelling offset is shown to improve the estimations in the models. But the mod-
elling must be tuned to fit the actual problem or it may result in worse performance.
For example leaving out the damping of the offset that was used in controller 5 and
7 would result in offset errors when the rain event was over. Furthermore modelling
the offset with too large or too small variance could result in worse estimations.
The dual mode description used in section 3.10 has no immediate advantages
as it is represented in this work. The formulation is the base model to use when
further work on infinite horizon minimization and robustness is used. Examples in the
literature is to impose terminal constraints [KCa] where a constraint on the end of the
prediction horizon is defined so the system is in a subspace where the unconstrained
optimal LQ controller is always feasible. This is used for different work on recursive
feasible solutions([Lor+15],[Can+12],[MDK18],[YGC18]).

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Modelling overflow into tank dynamics was realised using slack variables. Two ap-
proaches were tested where one implements the slack variable into the model dynamics
and one that does not. The resulting performance of the two controllers on a com-
bined tank simulation have shown that not modelling overflow into the model gives
errors when overflow occurs. Modelling overflow in the model dynamics gives errors
when moving the volume constraints.
It was discussed that the optimal solution
would be a combination of the two that would require more optimization variables
and more constraints.
The results have shown how non-linear dynamics such as tank overflow over a weir
can be approximated by linearising at each iteration of the MPC to better mimic real
constraints. The improvements made in the tests has shown only minimal improve-
ments because only small amounts of overflow were present compared to the overall
volume in the system.
Modelling the prognosis and prognosis error has shown to be important, and
using disturbance offset in the modelling has given large improvements in relation to
the other controllers in correctly estimating the tank volume, and from that reducing
costs. Using the uncertainty of the prognosis to add a larger margin to the constraints
has been shown to greatly improve controller performance.
This was done using
chance constraints in a single tank set-up where a specific percentage of constraint
violation could be defined. The uncertainties was also used together with a defined
cost function to numerically minimize cost. This opened the opportunity for finding
the cost optimal reference point taking in regards Gaussian uncertainty in the model
and arbitrary cost functions.
Weight manipulation of the modelled slack variable, which was used to model the
tank overflow, has been shown to improve how the model emulates the real system
overflow hence giving better estimations.
Modelling offsets, approximating non-linear overflow and manipulating weights
was combined, resulting in a controller with the combined strengths and weaknesses
of the different approaches. This controller outperformed the other implementations
in correctly estimating the overflow and volume in tanks.
Finally the problem was fitted to a dual mode formulation using a LQ controller.
This is very useful for other approaches and are commonly used in research for defining
robust infinite horizon MPC solutions.
