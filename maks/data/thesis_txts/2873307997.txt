Summary
Recent research has successfully modelled human life-courses using transformer-based
models on sequences of life-events that form a symbolic and structured language.
The life2vec model demonstrates strong predictive performance on Danish popula-
tion data, but its dense activation patterns obscure mechanistic understanding of its
reasoning pathways. In this thesis, we probe the life2vec internals by applying the
sparse dictionary learning method of sparse autoencoders in an effort to decode its
latent representations trained on population-scale registry data.
Following the framework in the seminal paper Towards Monosemanticity by An-
thropic, we train SAEs on post-MLP activations and decompose them into an over-
complete latent space with 512 dimensions, utilising 782.5 million token-activation
pairs from 3.13 million Danish life-sequences spanning four decades.
We demonstrate that SAEs extract salient features with varying levels of interpretabil-
ity. The Pregnancy feature shows clear semantic alignment between activations and
downstream effects.
The Biological Sex latent unexpectedly activates on national
origin tokens, potentially revealing how symbolic language structure affects saliency.
The High Education, High Income latent exhibits complex compositional behaviour,
activating on education and nationality tokens whilst causally influencing higher in-
come token predictions through additive steering.
Causal interventions on life2vec using the discovered SAE latents produce predictable
steering effects on next-token generation, validating its capability for enhancing inter-
pretability and showing the functional utility of the discovered features. Despite its
successful decomposition of certain transformer representations in within the registry
data, the interpretation of the discovered features requires careful consideration of
the underlying symbolic and temporal structures inherent to life-sequences.
Future work should extend this framework and consider redesigning the artificial
language to enhance interpretability, thus allowing more comprehensive analysis of
the conceptual mechanisms underlying life-course models such as life2vec and how
they drive their predictive capabilities for individual life-trajectories.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Social science has long sought to disentangle the complex forces that shape human
life. Traditional approaches often focus on broader societal or cultural structures in
isolation, as these external factors are often viewed as significant determinants of hu-
man behaviour and social outcomes. However, many different fields flourished when
they expanded their horizon by directing their attention towards each of the parts
that summarily created the whole. Following this line of thought, what if, instead of
focusing on the macroscopic notion of human life, we decided to zoom in? Peering
deeply into each individual’s journey reveals an intricate tapestry composed of count-
less events, milestones, and interactions from which rich patterns emerge that are
both uniquely personal and surprisingly universal. Studying elements on this scale
could provide critical insights with the potential to enrich or challenge our current
understanding of broader societal dynamics, underscoring the interplay between in-
dividual experiences and collective trends. The rapid digitization of the modern age
has enabled the accumulation of longitudinal data with unprecedented granularity,
presenting an extraordinary opportunity in social science: the ability to analyse the
societal DNA of human lives through novel computational frameworks that reveal the
impact of certain events on life-trajectories.
Artificial intelligence (AI) and deep learning (DL) have become inseparably linked to
the continued growing landscape of modern technology, enabling the development of
exceptionally powerful systems. Especially areas such as natural language processing
(NLP) and computer vision (CV) paved the way by showing the eﬀicacy of different
types of neural networks in tasks such as language translation, sentiment analysis,
and image classification.
However, these technologies have shifted from academic
curiosities to foundational tools that help shape our everyday lives. A prominent
example is the recent widespread adoption of generative large-scale language mod-
els (LLMs) such as OpenAI’s ChatGPT, designed to generate coherent, contextually
relevant responses across a wide range of topics [1]. Its ability to understand and gen-
erate text similar to that of humans has revolutionized how we interact with models.
The transformative impact is possibly best exemplified by the way these systems are
often fine-tuned for specific purposes in both business and education [2, 3].
A key factor behind the success of these models is the vast amount of high-quality data
==PAGE:12==
2

on which they have been trained. The ability to learn complex structures and con-
textual relationships is made possible by the availability of large and diverse datasets.
This progress can be attributed to ongoing efforts to collect and store vast amounts
of structured data. National registry data provides a unique type of dataset that
offers comprehensive, longitudinal insights into entire populations. Statistics Den-
mark maintains one of the most comprehensive national registry databases containing
records dating back to 1850, and its richness provides researchers with an opportunity
to investigate and possibly uncover some of the intricate patterns surrounding human
life with unprecedented granularity [4]. Recent work by Savcisens et al. reimagines
human life as a sequence of events, akin to words in sentences. Their proposed life2vec
model actively learns generic vector embeddings and effectively supports a wide range
of downstream tasks, including early mortality prediction [5].
Despite the remarkable advances in the capabilities of models, researchers have raised
concerns surrounding the safety, reliability, generalisability, and robustness as their
use in real-world applications continues to expand [6, 7]. A large part stems from the
limited insight into the models’ internal reasoning mechanisms, in turn making their
behaviour particularly challenging to understand. There are also worries about the
accompanying privacy issues that emerge from the personal data often included in
training [8]. Ensuring that sophisticated models operate transparently is crucial for
the future responsible development of AI, especially with the increasing influence of
decisions in finance, healthcare, and public policy [9].
In response to these challenges, a paradigm shift in AI interpretability is underway.
Traditionally, we have applied our understanding of the physical world, mathematics,
and logic to model complex systems in a predictable manner. However, the mecha-
nisms that give rise to the impressive capabilities of these AI systems elude us. This
is a direct consequence of the empirically motivated bottom-up scientific methods
originally intended to explore the nature of our surroundings. Instead, this approach
is being used to reverse engineer our own innovations in an attempt to comprehend
the underlying mechanisms that govern their behaviour. This sentiment is at the
heart of mechanistic interpretability (MI), a subfield of AI interpretability that has
gained considerable traction recently. A particularly promising approach involves the
application of sparse autoencoders (SAEs), which has shown remarkable results in
isolating interpretable, monosemantic features inherent to LLMs, and using them to
causally influence model behaviour [10, 11].
Building on the advancements mentioned earlier, this thesis aims to apply SAEs
to the life2vec model, with the goal of uncovering the internal structures that may
reveal the underlying concepts governing the trajectories of human life.

1.1
Background
The internal mechanisms underlying the capabilities of deep neural networks (DNNs)
have remained elusive to researchers since their inception [12]. Whilst humans design
the training objectives and architectures of these models, we are unable to create a
computer program that replicates their output. Extensive effort has gone towards
deciphering how these seemingly inscrutable black-box models are able to reason
and generalize about complex problems successfully, despite emerging from a series
of non-linear transformations distributed across simple interconnected computational
units known as artificial neurons [13]. These produce a series of real-valued activations
that function as detectors for features in the input based on their learned weights [14].
The large spectrum of problems addressed by DNNs has spurred the development
of diverse architectural variations tailored to specific modalities. For example, convo-
lutional neural networks (CNNs) excel in processing image data by exploiting local
spatial patterns and hierarchical feature extraction [15].
In contrast, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) were designed to handle sequential
data such as language and time series. Long short-term memory networks (LSTMs)
and gated recurrent units (GRUs) emerged as solutions aimed at mitigating the van-
ishing gradient problem and improving the retention of long-term dependencies that
were detrimental to the expressive capabilities of RNNs [16, 17]. Although the intro-
duction of residual connections in deep architectures allowed information to propagate
more effectively throughout network layers, the intrinsic recurrent nature prohibited
the ability to process input in parallel, in turn limiting its scalability. In an attempt
to overcome these challenges, a new architecture was proposed: the Transformer.
In the following sections, we present a high-level overview of the internal operations
of GPT-like transformers, providing insights designed to build an intuitive under-
standing of each component’s function. First, we describe the tokenization process
and explain how neural networks represent text using learnable embeddings, outlin-
ing their role within the transformer framework. Next, we introduce the attention
mechanism, which enables the model to selectively attend to different parts of the
input sequence, and observing some of the implications related to its structural inter-
pretability.
1.1.1
Transformer-based Language Models
The Transformer architecture is the core invention underlying the current surge in
much of modern AI research and its real-world applications.
Originally proposed
for the task of machine translation by researchers at Google, the transformer has
since been adapted for a diverse set of tasks, including end-to-end automatic speech
recognition and text-to-image synthesis [18, 19, 20].
Although some models like
==PAGE:14==
4
1 Introduction
BERT employ an encoder-only architecture for classification tasks, this thesis will
focus on the decoder-only variant exemplified by models like ChatGPT. These types
of models are trained to process a given segment of text as input and generate a
probability distribution over the vocabulary for the subsequent token. The input can
be optionally enriched with contextual cues from other modalities, such as images
or audio.
Through an iterative process of predicting and sampling tokens, these
models are capable of producing coherent and contextually relevant text, despite the
seemingly simplistic nature of the process. This is in large part due to the eﬀiciency
of the training design, namely that for every sequence of length n we get n different
predictions to train on:
p(x1), p(x2|x1), p(x3|x1, x2), . . . , p(xn|x1, ..., xn−1),
(1.1)
where we readily observe that this type of transformer has causal attention, as opposed
to bidirectional attention as seen in BERT, only allowing information to propagate
forward in the sequence. Hence, we say that the transformer is autoregressive, i.e.
predicting future words based on past data.
1.1.1.1
Tokenization and the Residual Stream
One of the most fundamental steps in NLP includes converting raw text into a nu-
merical format that models can process. But before we convert words into numbers,
we have to find a favourable representation of the basic units of language. Tokeniza-
tion is a process that divides text into smaller units, aptly called tokens. Although
tokens are sometimes simply whole words, modern approaches have found it useful to
break them into subword units. Despite sounding seemingly unintuitive, it allows the
model to generalize by generating new embeddings for unseen words by composing
them from known subword units and is commonly referred to as open-vocabulary.
Furthermore, it also improves the robustness of the models with respect to variations
in spelling or morphological changes and often reduces the overall vocabulary size.
One popular method used in transformer-based models is byte-pair encoding (BPE).
Originally developed as a data compression technique, BPE was adapted for NLP by
initially treating each character as a token and then iteratively merging the most fre-
quent adjacent pairs into new composite tokens, creating a vocabulary that includes
both full words and common subword units [21].
Once the input text is segmented into tokens, each token is mapped to a vector in the
embedding matrix WE ∈R|V|×dmodel, where V is some predefined vocabulary, where
each token is uniquely associated with a row [22]. As a result, every token is converted
into a dense vector representation. Although the convention of turning words into
vectors precedes the emergence of the transformer, it remains fundamental for the
subsequent sections to have an intuitive understanding of the related operations [23].
Conceptually, these vectors can be visualized as points in a high-dimensional space
where each dimension captures some aspect of the meaning of a word. For instance,

models often find it advantageous to learn that embeddings of words with similar

from neural networks [26].
There are two distinct notions of the LRH: the weak
version implies that a subset of input features are represented as directions, whereas
the strong version posits that all input features are represented as directions. The
former is substantiated by considerable empirical evidence, whilst the latter remains
speculative.
A consequence of the linear behaviour of embedding spaces has hinted at a broad
and foundational strategy that DNNs employ to encode information. The superposi-
tion hypothesis has garnered an increasing amount of empirical evidence regarding
how models are able to represent many more concepts than the dimensionality of
their activation spaces would conventionally allow [27, 28, 29]. Rather than assigning
a unique dimension to each concept, models seem to leverage sparsely distributed rep-
resentations, where a single neuron is able to contribute to multiple, distinct features
at the expense of a slight interference. This expressive capacity has been intuitively
compared to noisily simulating of a larger, sparse version of the network [26]. In later
sections, we will encounter models that take advantage of this inherent structure.
Lastly, whilst these output vectors are frequently referred to as embeddings, mod-
ern terminology increasingly favours the term residual stream in order to emphasize
their dynamic nature of integrating contextual information from surrounding tokens
whilst propagating throughout the layers of the transformer network [30]. Concep-
tually, the residual stream can be thought of as the model’s information highway,
continuously refined by contributions from surrounding tokens.
This dynamic ex-
change naturally raises the question: how do different layers effectively communicate
and merge their contextual contents? Intuitively, one can imagine each token asking
if there are any relevant tokens in its context window, with other tokens responding if
they contribute meaningful information. This interaction is realized by the attention
mechanism, which lies at the heart of the transformer architecture.

1.1.2
A Short Tour of Mechanistic Interpretability
As the suite of available deep learning models has become increasingly complex, efforts
aimed at disentangling their reasoning correspondingly intensified. MI represents one
of the latest branches in the quest of trying to understand these complex models.
One of the main motivations behind MI is the mitigation of AI risks such as safety
and misalignment, which is crucial if the model has access to intimate personal in-
formation, as in the case of life2vec [34, 35]. At its core, the goal is to decompose
these systems into their architectural components, i.e. neurons, attention heads, and
circuits. Formally, we define circuits as computational sub-graphs of the model where
each feature is a node and the edges are the weighted connections between them, but
several other definitions of increasing generality are prevalent in the field [29, 36, 37].
In the past, the prevalent methods for understanding models came in the form of
saliency maps and feature visualizations, both aimed at highlighting the specific parts
of the input that influenced model prediction, e.g. by probing CNNs for questions
such as ”Which part of the image led to the classification of a dog?”[38, 39, 40].
Studies of individual neurons or convolutional filters showed that these often encode
simple and interpretable features such as curves, high-low-frequency detectors, or
Gabor-like filters [41, 42, 43, 44, 45]. Beyond visualization tools, attribution methods
such as SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) or local interpretable model-agnostic
explanations (LIME) have been developed to quantify the contribution of individual
input features with respect to model output [46, 47]. Both of these explainable AI
(XAI) methods are widely used among businesses today, providing both a global and
a localized understanding of their deployed frameworks. Whilst these techniques offer
valuable insight into ”what” constitutes a model’s prediction by generating explana-
tions solely based on analysing inputs and outputs, they provide no tangible insight
into how these models reason by prying at the underlying processes and representa-
tions that govern its behaviour. In this manner, MI distinguishes itself from saliency
and related methods both in methodology, but also by turning their attention to-
wards transformer language models.
Early studies attempted to scrutinize the internals of LMs using probing methods, but
these have been criticized for being unable to provide causal explanations of model
behaviour [48, 49]. Recent work has suggested that deep models indeed contain a
compositional and interpretable internal structure [29, 30, 26]. Yet, a large collection
of features are polysemantic, meaning they often respond to multiple features that
==PAGE:19==
1.1 Background
9
are seemingly unrelated, e.g. a cat and car detector [50, 29]. Consequently, the rich
and complex feature space often hinders a straightforward human interpretation of
the computational role of each unit in the network [51].
Instead some turned their attention to circuits and found the creation of induction
heads, with the first example consisting of two attention heads able to detect and
continue repeated subsequences [30]. A notable observation of this type of in-context
learning is that induction heads consistently emerge at approximately the same point
across LMs of varying scales1, often showing as noticeable bumps or phase changes in
the training loss [36, 52]. This finding suggests that the formation of induction heads
is governed by fundamental mechanisms that are largely independent of model size,
challenging assumptions about scale-dependent complexity in these systems.
Some work has attempted grouping neurons together and disentangling their intricate
patterns using unsupervised dimensionality reduction techniques such as principal
component analysis (PCA), singular value decomposition (SVD), and non-negative
matrix factorization (NMF) [53, 54]. However, recent studies suggest that many of
these methods possibly cause interpretability illusions, and other studies indicate that
feature representations are not intrinsically bound to a single layer [55, 56, 26].
1.1.3
Sparse Autoencoders
Autoencoders (AE) have shown significant usefulness in reducing the dimensionality
of complex datasets whilst enhancing the retrieval of salient information. It does so by
learning a compact, latent representation of input data through an encoder–decoder
framework. The constraint on the latent dimension is often referred to as an under-
complete AE, which can be shown to span the same subspace as PCA under certain
conditions [57]. The inherent connection to foundational dimensionality reduction
techniques was one of the early motivations for studying AEs. Early work showed
that deep AEs could outperform PCA and the resulting learned representation was
qualitatively easier to interpret and relate to the underlying categories, with these
categories manifesting themselves as well-separated clusters [58]. Moreover, drawing
on lower-dimensional representations presents certain practical advantages, including
reduced memory usage and computational overhead.

traditional autoencoders may learn to copy input data without a mechanism promot-

a high-dimensional signal can be accurately reconstructed from a limited number of
measurements if the signal is sparse in some basis [59]. In this regard, SAEs are able
to decompose the distributed and polysemantic activations of neural networks into
a sparse, linear combination of features, some of which are humanly interpretable.
Recall, this phenomenon is explained by the superposition hypothesis.
Empirical studies using SAEs have shown that they can extract features that are
more interpretable than the original neuron activations, as the SAE latents are often
monosemantic, i.e. corresponding to single well-defined concepts [60]. Most notably,
researchers have recently identified SAE latent representations corresponding to spe-
cific semantic entities, such as the research team at Anthropic finding a Golden Gate
Bridge feature in a single layer LM, offering key insight into the internal reasoning
of large language models [10, 11]. Furthermore, interventions based on the features
derived from SAEs have allowed researchers to causally steer the model behaviour, a
capability that is of critical importance for mitigating AI risks related to safety and
misalignment. Recently, the method has also been successfully extended to vision-
language models [61, 62]. Contemporary research has explored different variants of
SAEs, how to scale them for LLMs, and automate the process of evaluating the in-
terpretability of latents [63, 64, 65, 66, 67, 51].
In summary, the integration of SAEs into MI research has provided a framework for
dissecting the internal representations of deep systems. By leveraging their ability to
transform entangled neural activations into a set of sparse, monosemantic features,
we can deepen our understanding of how models might algorithmically process infor-
mation, in turn contributing to the development of more transparent, controllable,
and ultimately safer AI systems.
1.1.4
Vectorizing the Life of Danish Residents
Recent work in modelling individual life-trajectories has demonstrated the feasibility
of generating meaningful representations from sequences of life-events using a BERT-
like transformer called life2vec [5]. Through the conception of a symbolic language, it
encodes sequences of life-events into latent embeddings using health and labour data
in conjunction with temporal embeddings, which serves as a compact summary of
an individual’s life-trajectory. However, whilst it successfully captures the semantic
content of life-events and utilizes them for prediction tasks, its use of bidirectional
attention complicates efforts to extract interpretable, monosemantic features from its
internal representations due to the lack of causal structures.
Alternative models such as the Heterogeneous Graph Transformer Masked Auto-
Encoder (HGTMAE) addressed some of the limitations of the original life2vec ap-
==PAGE:22==
12
1 Introduction
proach by incorporating social relationships into the representation [68]. Whilst the
original life2vec model focuses solely on individual life-event sequences, HGTMAE
extends this framework to capture the complex network of social interactions on an
individual’s life-trajectory. The similar predictive capabilities of both models, despite
different architectural motivations, indicate that the underlying structure is excep-
tionally rich and warrants a deeper and more meticulous exploration.
By applying SAEs to the latent representations of our GPT-like life2vec model, we
aim to decompose the complex, distributed activations into a set of monosemantic
features. These features are expected to correspond to distinct life-events or underly-
ing social dynamics, and their sparsity makes them more interpretable. In an effort
to overcome these limitations, this work pivots to an autoregressive version of life2vec.
In contrast to the BERT-based model, the GPT architecture generates embeddings
in a causal manner, making it more amenable to techniques aimed at uncovering
monosemantic features. It is our hope that the application of SAEs to the GPT-like
life2vec representations can offer a promising pathway to uncover latent features that
can yield profound insights into the rich structure of its internals.
Ultimately, this thesis aims to demonstrate that SAEs can provide a viable approach
for areas like social science to analyse the internal representations of their models,
potentially offering novel insights into the complexities of human society.
1.2
Thesis Objectives and Outline
Building on insights drawn from the field of MI, this thesis seeks to bridge two converg-
ing lines of inquiry. Firstly, the intricate representation of human life-events captured
by life2vec, and secondly, the emerging tools of interpretability that allow us to peek
into the “DNA” of these complex systems. The core objective is to apply SAE-based
interpretability techniques to the life2vec model and uncover any interpretable latent
structures within in hopes of not only enhancing our understanding of how these mod-
els capture human behaviour, but also offer a window into the fundamental patterns
that govern individual life-trajectories.
RQ1 Can an SAE trained on post-MLP activations of the life2vec GPT model uncover
monosemantic and interpretable features?
RQ2 Does causal intervention using SAE latents induce semantically coherent and

In this section, we explain the structure of the dataset we use as a foundation for the
GPT model. We do not intend to provide an in-depth review of each registry and its

Originally, the life2vec model based its filtered dataset on several records from differ-
ent Danish national registries, which contains highly detailed indicators, such as time
of birth, health, professional occupation and aﬀiliation, income level, residency, work-
ing hours, and education for approximately 3.25 million Danish citizens spanning a
10-year interval [5]. Since, work has continued by expanding the scope of the dataset
by researchers at Statistics Denmark, Danish Technical University (DTU), and the
Copenhagen University’s Center for Social Data Science (SODAS). Presently, the ex-
pansion of data has not only included more information within existing registries, as
well as other registries, but it has extended the time frame from around 2008 back
to the 1980s. As a result, the dataset now encompasses approximately 3.7 million
Danish residents across a 40-year period for most of its constituents.

identifier to every person born in or immigrating to Denmark and is the core pillar
around which each registry maintains continuously updated records on residents [69].
2.1
Labour & Income (LAB)
The LAB category is comprised of data from multiple Danish administrative registries
that collectively document labour market participation and income dynamics across
the population. Its foundation derives from Statistics Denmark’s Labour Market Ac-
==PAGE:26==

counts (Arbejdsmarkedsregnskabet, AMRUN), which provide a comprehensive and longi-
tudinal record of each resident’s occupational status, job type, and industrial sector,
without imposing a predefined concept of full-time work, which is often predefined
as 37 hours per week [70]. The Labour Market Accounts are compiled annually and
provide information on the population’s labour market status, where labour-market
related activities are given the highest priority. The system operates through a two-
stage processing framework that first creates the AMRUN (non-standardised) register,
followed by full-time standardisation to produce the final Labour Market Accounts.
Complementing AMRUN is the Employment Classification Module (Arbejdsklassifika-
tionsmodulet, AKM) and its purpose is to provide information about occupational and
employment conditions for the taxable portion of the population [71]. Through an au-
tomated process, classification of labour market status is assigned to residents through
a main activity status (BESKST), socioeconomic status (SOCIO), work function for
economically active individuals (DISCO), and industry fields (NACE/BRANCHE),
which are based on a variety of personal income information, educational data, work-
place industry classifications, unemployment insurance fund information, and com-
pany reports. AKM distinguishes between different types of employment, namely pri-
mary, wage-receivers, and self-employed individuals.
Alongside AMRUN and AKM, the Income Statistics Register (IND) aggregates the an-
nual gross and taxable income of each resident, disaggregated by source (wages, pen-
sions, unemployment benefits, disability compensation, and other transfers), as well
as associated taxes and wealth components [72]. Because Danish payroll and tax
reporting systems channel all transactions through centralized tax authorities, the
register records every salary payment, benefits ranging from child to unemployment,
educational grants, and pension disbursements. Furthermore, it contains information
on the type of company under the European System of Accounts and the individual’s
occupational code. Consequently, it is possible to determine the exact amount each
person earned in a given month back to 2008 via AMRUN, the sectoral aﬀiliation, the
designated job title, and observe whether that person received social benefits. IND
covers income since 1980 with annual resolution and covers several aspects other reg-
istries might not include such as different wealth markers. Generally, the variables
are divided into 100 bins, except for those with a narrow range of values, which in
turn are divided into 10 bins [73, 74]. By integrating all these sources, it is possible
to reconstruct the professional journey of each resident throughout their life in high
resolution.

2.3
Demographics (DEM)
Statistics Denmark derives the Population Statistics Register (BEF) from CPR and
has since its inception progressed from yearly to quarterly updates [69, 77]. BEF pro-
vides a dynamic snapshot of the entire Danish population, indicating each individual’s
municipality and parish. This geographic coverage enables longitudinal analysis of
mobility patterns, tracing how individuals and families relocate across neighbour-
hoods, municipalities, and national boundaries over time.
The CPR infrastructure also includes familial identifiers that record parenthood and
spousal relationships. Hence, family formation and dissolution processes are system-
atically tracked, reflecting marriage, divorce, and cohabitation histories throughout
individuals’ life-courses, whilst also facilitating intergenerational tracing by linking
related individuals across generations.
Supplementary substrata include Births & Deaths (LIFELINES, FTBARN), which
timestamp each birth and death event; Birth Information from various administrative
sources providing comprehensive perinatal documentation such as details regarding
parity, birth weight, and gestational age; Marital Status reflects marriage, divorce,
and cohabitation histories; and Migration (LIFELINES) records immigration and
emigration. The Household captures family composition through household identi-
fiers and relationship codes, whilst the location data (BEFBOB, BEFADR) specify
exact residential addresses.
These demographic subcategories collectively provide the structural foundation to
which all other life-events attach, including medical treatments, educational enrol-
ments, and labour-market transitions. The comprehensive integration of demographic
data ensures that subsequent analyses can account for family structure, residential his-
tory, and life-course transitions when examining health, education, and employment
outcomes across the Danish population.

as when they receive a diploma, but without quantifying the underlying activities

The primary aim of this section is to outline the set of methodological tools used
to identify features that capture different aspects of Danish residents’ life-patterns
across the domains of health, education, demographics, labour, and social factors.
We adopt a three-stage approach to investigate the internal mechanisms of a GPT-
based language model trained on tokenised Danish administrative registry data. In
the first stage, we train a SAE on the internal token activations of the GPT model
in an effort to decompose the model’s learned representations into a set of features
that may prove more interpretable to humans. We present the relevant considerations
regarding data preprocessing and the models’ architectural configurations. Secondly,
we introduce a suite of analytical methods that assists in assessing which features
demonstrate activation patterns across tokens that appear consistent with identifiable
concepts. Finally, we propose a framework for validating our hypothesised concepts
by directly intervening in the GPT model and producing causal downstream effects
that evaluate their consistency with our proposed conceptual interpretations of the
features in question.
3.1
Data Preprocessing
Section 2.6 demonstrates the severe category imbalance present in the training corpus,
most notably exemplified by the difference between the labour and social conditions
categories that span two orders of magnitude in Figure 2.2, which reflects the drastic
disparities in both the administrative reporting frequencies and the natural occurrence
rates of different life-events. Whilst conventional SAE training regimes generally pre-
serve these kinds of imbalances in order to faithfully represent the model’s learned
representations, our symbolic language introduces several times more structure both
temporally and syntactically. This presents a significant departure from the estab-
lished norm and thus demands novel considerations.
Unlike natural language, where tokens exhibit considerable positional and semantic
flexibility, the nature of our symbolic language enforces rigid constraints. For exam-

During model training, it is standard practice to monitor for so-called dead neu-
rons, which are SAE latents that remain inactive throughout the training epochs. In
an effort to remedy this, resampling procedures are introduced to reset latent weights
and gradients, thereby enabling repopulation of the dead parts of the dictionary by
emerging concepts. We implemented the resampling methodology established in the
Anthropic study, where we investigate the presence of dead neurons at 25.000 step
intervals, with up to at most 4 resampling operations during training to revive inac-
tive features [10].
The dead latent phenomenon emerges from fundamental interactions between the
L1 sparsity penalty and activation dynamics during gradient descent optimisation.
Features that exhibit infrequent activation patterns receive insuﬀicient gradient sig-
nals to overcome critical activation thresholds, creating absorbing states from which
recovery becomes increasingly improbable [26, 10]. However, our SAE did not show
any dead latent formation throughout the training process. This observation aligns
with established scaling relationships documented in recent literature, particularly
findings that demonstrate reduced rates of dead latents in smaller SAE configura-
tions [63]. The absence of significant dead latent populations could be attributable to
the GPT model’s modest residual stream dimension, since dead latent proliferation
only becomes substantially problematic in larger-scale architectures.

token windows around activating positions. We select these examples using two com-
plementary strategies: maximally activating instances reveal the strongest feature
responses, whilst uniformly spaced sub-samples across descending activation inter-
vals show how feature behaviour varies across the activation spectrum. Analysing
contexts at multiple activation levels helps distinguish between features with consis-
tent semantic interpretations and those exhibiting activation-dependent behavioural
shifts.

top-activating tokens across both maximum and mean activation sets. Features are
considered interpretable candidates if we observe substantial agreement between the
ordering and activation values of the top K maximum and mean activating tokens
across the validation set as the convergence between extreme and typical behaviours
builds confidence in our interpretations.

We apply Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) to the decoder
weight matrix columns to investigate the structure of the SAE’s learned represen-
tations [99]. Each column of the decoder represents a basis vector in the residual
stream space. The UMAP heuristic is the common choice in the field due to its ability
in preserving both local neighbourhoods and global structure when projecting high-
dimensional data to lower dimensions. Unlike linear methods such as PCA, UMAP
captures non-linear relationships and Riemannian manifold structure, making it par-
ticularly popular for analysing the geometry of SAE decoder weights. The algorithm
constructs a graph representation of local neighbourhoods in the original space, then
optimises a low-dimensional embedding that preserves these topological relationships.
The purpose of the UMAP is to uncover potential clustering patterns in its projection
that may indicate which latents capture semantically related concepts or functional
roles. Regions that appear to specialise in particular categories often cluster together,
though the clustering should be interpreted cautiously, as it can create spurious
groupings. Outlier features appearing isolated in the projection may represent highly
specialised concepts, making them candidates for detailed investigation, though iso-
lation can also result from algorithmic artifacts rather than semantic uniqueness.
Further considerations are left for Chapter 5. We use the default parameters, i.e.
n_neighbors = 15 and min_dist = 0.1, which provides a reasonable balance for
preserving local and global structures.
3.4.1
Causal Intervention
We validate our interpretations of SAE features through causal intervention experi-
ments that directly test whether identified features causally influence model behaviour
in alignment with the hypothesised concept. For select interpretable candidate fea-
tures, we perform controlled interventions by modifying the feature’s activation value
and measuring resulting changes in the model’s output logits. This approach allows
us to establish causal relationships between learned features and model predictions,
moving beyond mere correlational analysis.
==PAGE:47==
3.4 Feature Analysis and Interpretability

We employ a two-stage validation process with complementary intervention strate-
gies. First, we use activation clamping as an initial ”sledgehammer”-like approach
to test whether our conceptual interpretations produce strong, coherent effects on
downstream model behaviour. In this method, we set the feature’s activation to a
fixed value, typically twice the value of the most activating sparse latent. Whilst this
approach deliberately disrupts the model’s natural computational flow, it provides
clear evidence of whether the model responds to the feature in ways consistent with
our interpretation. When clamping demonstrates promising results, we proceed to
the more refined approach of additive steering.
For each SAE latent l interpreted as representing a specific concept C (such as
”Japanese origin” or ”high-income asthmatics”), we first process the input sequence x
through the model to obtain baseline logits pbaseline in the final layer. We then inter-
vene at the SAE’s training layer, replacing the original activation with an artificially
modified version:

predictions ∆p = pintervention −pbaseline. This approach is more forceful as it overrides
the model’s computation by replacing the original activation entirely.

not replace the original activation but instead perturbs it in the direction of the

whilst still influencing behaviour in the direction of our hypothesised concept.
For steering validation, we compare model generations against a non-intervened ver-
sion and the ground truth subsequence. Given an input sequence, we apply steering
and allow the model to generate continuations. We then assess whether the steered
generations align more closely with sequences containing concept C compared to the
baseline generations. For instance, steering with a pregnancy-related feature should
increase the probability of generating pregnancy-specific medical codes in appropriate
contexts, whilst maintaining overall sequence coherence.
We consider interventions supportive when they demonstrate specific causal effects
consistent with our hypotheses. Successful interventions must produce changes in
the output distribution, where tokens semantically related to concept C show in-
creased probability, antonyms and tokens that naturally occur at the position are
suppressed, whilst unrelated tokens remain largely unaffected. This specificity dis-

4.4
Latent z-score Heatmap
Figure 4.4 visualises the z-scores in a heatmap, illustrating the category selectivity
of each SAE latent. For each latent feature, we compute how much more or less it
activates for a given token type relative to its own average activation across all types.
Hence, the z-score can be thought of as quantifying the degree of specialisation that
a latent has for a particular semantic class (e.g., ”Demographic”, ”Labor_Income”),
measured in standard deviations from its mean. A high z-score (dark red) indicates
that the latent is significantly more active for that category than for others, whilst the
other (dark blue) represents less activity. To emphasise the distribution of category-
selective features, we sort the latents into groups according to their maximum z-
score. This visualisation strategy foregoes the ability to track individual latents across
categories but yields clearer insight into the overall specialisation patterns.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the implications of our findings and expands on the framework
considerations presented in the previous five chapters, analysing both the successes
and limitations of applying SAEs to the life2vec GPT model, such as how the unique
characteristics of the registry data reveals additional interpretability challenges. We
explore avenues where further work could enhance the interpretative capabilities for
investigating the internal structures of life-course models.
5.1
SAE Capabilities and Constraints: A Balanced
Perspective
Our experiments demonstrate the SAE is capable of extracting salient features from
the life2vec GPT model trained on population-scale registry data, albeit with varying
levels of interpretability. The Pregnancy feature provides the clearest example of se-
mantic alignment between activation patterns and downstream effects. However, we
must acknowledge that the three features we provide examples of represent carefully
selected latents from the sparse dictionary with 512 elements. This type of selection
is prevalent within the field, where critiques are directed towards presenting results
that might create misconceptions about SAE capabilities [103]. In an effort to pro-
mote transparency, we acknowledge that the majority of manually inspected features
exhibit entangled activation patterns with little to no semantic coherence. The Bi-
ological Sex and High Education, High Income latents illustrate the more common
scenario of ambiguous interpretation. The former’s unexpected activation on national
origin tokens reveals how life2vec entangles demographic characteristics in complex
ways that our framework, rooted in natural language research, does not adequately
capture. However, it is typical that a substantial minority of SAE latents (10-30%)
demonstrate clear interpretability even in natural language applications, where the
interpretation of latent behaviour is more straightforward than in our domain [10].
The findings of this thesis align with these observations, suggesting that partial inter-
pretability represents the norm rather than the exception in SAE applications.
Recent large-scale evaluations of SAE provide crucial context for understanding some
==PAGE:68==
58
5 Discussion
of their limitations. Studies have shown that SAEs systematically underperform sim-
ple baselines, such as logistic regression and naive prompting, when acting on known
concepts in tasks such as concept detection or model steering [104, 105]. The un-
derwhelming performance in these areas is attributed to fundamental limitations, as
SAE reconstructions encode strictly less information than original residual stream
representations due to optimising for sparsity rather than task-relevant information
[103]. Despite the ensuing pessimism in the realm of SAEs, most notably by the
Google DeepMind MI team, there continues to be optimism regarding their applica-
tions in hypothesis generation and circuit tracing of computational graphs in LLMs,
where the models serve as powerful tools for discovering unknown concepts [106, 107,
108]. We argue the usefulness of SAEs in terms of uncovering reasoning pathways in
black-box models is evident from our thesis. Our primary contribution with this the-
sis is enhancing our mechanistic understanding of the life2vec GPT model’s behaviour.
Causally intervening in the model confirmed that some of the discovered features in-
deed possess functional significance beyond mere correlation (Figure 4.8, Figure 4.11).
By manipulating the forward pass with the sparse coeﬀicients of the SAE decoder, we
show that it is possible to generate predictable steering effects on next-token gener-
ation, confirming that these latents represent directions in the model’s neuron basis
that are meaningful.
An explanation for why this does not apply to all features
might be the ”Hydra effect”, which according to new research demonstrates trans-
formers having robust self-repair mechanisms even when key components are ablated.
This suggests that feature-level understanding may miss important emergent proper-
ties and distributed representations across several layers that enable model resilience
[109]. For future work, we believe there is merit in considering transcoders or cross-
coders, which are able to trace conceptual representations across the model’s depth,
unlike the traditional SAEs that are confined to individual layers [67]. This could
possibly provide a more distributed insight into the reasoning of life2vec’s predictions.
5.2
Artifical Language Limitations
The feature analysis suggests that the artificial language of life2vec fundamentally
shapes and limits our ability to cleanly interpret SAE latents. Unlike natural lan-
guage, which enables us to navigate words with a degree of ambiguity given context,
life2vec operates on a rigid structural system with numerous design choices that ar-
guably oppose the linguistic principles that make it easy for humans to comprehend.
Whilst all tokens are inherently categorised in accordance with their domain-type,
some exacerbate the described problem by having a large number of similar tokens
that favour statistical distinction rather than semantic clarity. This potentially cre-
ates unwanted noise from an interpretability point of view. For example, consider
income-related tokens such as LAB_bredt_loen_beloeb, which we recall are split into
percentile bins such as Q11 and Q16. We argue that most people will not be able to
==PAGE:69==
5.3 Architectural Opportunities
59
comprehend any practical distinction in terms of conceptual understanding given the
entire spectrum of values ranging from 1 to 100. We propose that future work might
benefit from using broader terms in these instances, such as high, medium, and low.
Our experimental observations consistently demonstrate this interpretability bottle-
neck. Features frequently exhibit scattered activation patterns across binned tokens
without the monotonic or clustered structure that would suggest concepts like ”high
income” or ”economic stability.” Instead, we observe complex activation patterns that
likely either reflects that the model has discovered statistical regularities in Danish
life trajectories that transcend conventional income categorisation, or the limited la-
tent capacity of our SAE forces the model to compress multiple correlated attributes
into single features, producing activation patterns that conflate distinct concepts.
Importantly, this does not invalidate the SAE analysis for life2vec, but reframes
its objectives for future work. We propose restructuring the language with manual
interpretation in mind, or continuing with more sophisticated and novel methods. It
is always exciting to consider that the model potentially has developed an ontology
for understanding life-trajectories which has more predictive power exactly because
it escapes our conceptual limitations.
5.3
Architectural Opportunities
The field of MI applied to LLMs is rather novel and is rapidly evolving. Within the
6-month span of this thesis perspectives on their usefulness and how best to circum-
vent various limitations have shifted and continue to do so [106, 110, 111, 112]. As
previously mentioned, multiple improvements to the more baseline SAE approach em-
ployed in this thesis exist. Whilst our conservative choice serves as a baseline, there
is substantial room for improvement through the adoption of these advances.
Each of these methods could help address the limitations directly relevant to our
life2vec analysis. For instance, Gated SAEs introduce a dedicated gating mechanism
that separates the feature detection from the latent magnitude estimation, requires
fewer active features for comparable reconstruction quality [65]. We expect this might
benefit instances where binned features like income could be further distinguished be-
tween the presence of income information and specific levels of income, potentially
reducing the supposed activation noise present.
Top-K SAEs eliminate the L1 penalty entirely, replacing it with a hard sparsity
constraint that selects exactly k features per forward pass [63]. This approach removes
the shrinkage bias inherent in L1 regularisation, which likely influences our perceived
diﬀiculty in distinguishing some latents from noise.
JumpReLU SAEs introduce learnable thresholds for each feature, allowing it to
adaptively determine its activation boundary [66]. Different life-events occur at vastly
==PAGE:70==
60
5 Discussion
different frequencies, which is not accounted for with the current implementation
using a uniform sparsity penalty. The adaptive threshold could maintain dedicated
features without feature absorption by more frequent patterns, potentially revealing
features we may have missed.
Another novel variant is the Matryoshka SAE designed to address the feature
absorption problem inherently caused by the sparsity optimisation when the underly-
ing features are part of a hierarchy, in turn causing te SAE to learn gerrymandered
latents [110, 113]. In this aspect, Matryoshka SAEs aim to maintain features across
various levels of abstraction training nested, more narrow SAEs simultaneously. For
example, suppose that the SAE attempts to learn two features, i.e. one representing
”elephant” and another capturing the broader pattern ”starts with E”. Ideally, the
model would assign each concept to a distinct latent. However, since the ”elephant”
feature inherently activates for ”starts with E”, the model under its objective seeks to
reduce its L0 cost and consequently absorbs part of ”starts with E” into ”elephant”.
This compromises interpretability, activating for ”elephant” whilst partially encod-
ing the broader E-prefix pattern, consequently compromising interpretability. This
competitive dynamic becomes particularly pronounced in high-dimensional activation
spaces where the curse of dimensionality exacerbates gradient dilution effects.
Until recently, this SAE variant appeared to offer the most promising approach
for mitigating these inherent limitations. However, emerging research reveals that
narrow SAEs encounter their own challenge of feature hedging, where the model con-
flates correlated features as a result of optimising for the MSE loss [111]. Consider
two features that should ideally capture ”happiness” and ”rainy weather” separately.
When the SAE lacks suﬀicient capacity and must choose, we might expect it to priori-
tise the more frequent ”happiness” feature. Instead, the model learns a compromised
representation of ”happiness” contaminated by the negative correlation with ”rainy
weather”, producing a mixed feature that captures neither concept cleanly. There
are active research efforts aimed at addressing this problem and future work should
closely monitor these developments.
5.4
Interpretation and Resource Bottlenecks
During the process of this work the most severe limitation stems from the neces-
sity of manual feature labelling and interpretation. Natural language is able to take
advantage of existing LLMs that can help automate interpretability, however most
of the vocabulary in our domain consists of alphanumeric codes lacking an English
translated mapping. Thus, each feature often requires painstaking analysis including
cross-referencing with various Danish codes that sometimes are not centralised, e.g.
some health services depending on region, leaving no single source of truth. Without
a direct mapping some promising features end up being discarded due to the con-
straints of time, creating a cascading constraint on the research scope.
==PAGE:71==
5.5 Cautionary Notes on UMAP Visualisation
61
Consequently, our research could not explore the vast hyperparameter space that
typically accompanies modern interpretability research. We could not systematically
compare the trade-off between reconstruction and sparsity different model configura-
tions, or investigate the alternative activation functions and training regimes. Each
additional experiment would require manual interpretation of a plethora of new fea-
tures, which rapidly becomes infeasible, particularly as the overcomplete dictionary
size grows. We propose that future work prioritises establishing a complete set of
mappings from the codes used in the artificial language that converts their meaning
into natural language.
This would provide a good foundation for analysing more
features at a faster pace. We also advocate for the possibility of fine-tuning LMs
internally at Statistics Denmark on the available Danish administrative codes that
will be used in the continued expansion of the dataset. This is a necessary step for
realising the full potential of uncovering mechanistic insights into life2vec using more
advanced SAE architectures.
5.4.1
Validation Challenges
Although some domains provide ground truths that enables researchers to validate
learned feature, such as board game models like Othello-GPT where identified con-
cepts can be verified against specific board configurations and game states, most
domains lack benchmarks and the ability to verify directly [114, 115, 116]. The realm
of life-course data is no different and would be a promising avenue for future research.
The scaling of SAEs also has implications for future work, as features discovered
at one scale may not resurface at larger scales, partially due to feature splitting into
more granular sub-features, e.g. ”medical” into ”surgery” [117]. Despite not ablating
the size of the sparse dictionary, this suggests that our configuration of 512 latents
captures one of many decompositions in the life2vec representations.
5.5
Cautionary Notes on UMAP Visualisation
In this thesis, one of our results involved visualising the decoder columns of the SAE
using UMAP, a widely adopted non-linear dimensionality reduction technique. De-
spite its popularity for producing visually appealing projections, recent mathematical
analyses reveal fundamental limitations that compromise its scientific validity. Pacher
et al. demonstrate that UMAP can embed identical data into arbitrary shapes whilst
maintaining comparable distance correlations [118]. The spurious nature of the struc-
tures created under UMAP is exacerbated with increasing feature dimensionality
reduction.
Moreover, UMAP exhibits the highest parameter sensitivity among common dimen-
sionality reduction methods, with different choices of n_neighbors and min_dist
==PAGE:72==
62
5 Discussion
producing qualitatively different visualisations from identical data. Given the known
limitations of the heuristics and the absence of theoretical guarantees, growing con-
cerns within the scientific community underscore its potential unreliability [119, 120].
All dimensionality reduction techniques have inherent limitations in their ability to
visualise data faithfully, however we argue that future research efforts should carefully
consider the impact on interpretability.
5.6
Temporal Embeddings
Recall that the life2vec model incorporates temporal embeddings through time2vec.
The intermediate representations of life2vec are arguably compositionally more rich as
the semantic content carries both positional and temporal information. Our method-
ology derives from the established SAE interpretability framework and methods, but
these are rooted in natural languages and models that only carry positional embed-
ded information across sequences. Hence, our work is inherently limited as the SAE
latents capture not only the features related to reasoning mechanisms but also their
temporal relevancy. Some latents might indeed represent concepts that are sensitive
to changes time, for example ”declining medication adherence”. Currently, it is not
possible to directly assess how the timing, order, or spacing of events influences the
learned features.
Without methods that explicitly probe temporal sensitivity, we
risk misinterpreting or entirely overlooking patterns driven by time-dependent rela-
tionships. Future work should focus on developing methods that enable this type of
analysis could distinguish between which latents depend on temporal structure and
which capture semantic associations.
5.7
Ethical Considerations
The necessity of manual interpretation and analysis of SAE features for this the-
sis raises ethical concerns beyond the aforementioned technical limitations, as it in-
evitably introduces interpretative bias. Moving forward, incorporating diverse per-
spectives through collaborative efforts in the interpretation of latent features would
help mitigate individual biases projected onto the perceived model representations.
Lastly, we reiterate our position on SAEs as exploratory tools rather than instruments
for model modification. Whilst SAEs can help us better understand model reasoning
pathways, using these insights to alter their behaviour creates poorly understood and
substantial risks. The inherent complexity of interactions between features and our
incomplete grasp of their computational roles is particularly dangerous for models
like life2vec that processes extremely sensitive personal data.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, we set out to investigate whether applying SAEs to life2vec as a GPT
model gives insight into the concepts driving its internal reasoning pathways. Using a
vastly expanded dataset with several more Danish registries compared to the original
paper, we propose a framework for disentangling features based on the life-sequence
representation of life2vec by targeting a specific layer in the model. The resulting
sparse latents show promising results in the utility of SAEs on life-course models,
particularly detecting unknown concepts and in some cases provoking causal down-
stream effects on model behaviour using steering.
We organised our work around two research questions. In the following we attempt
to answer them.
First, we asked whether an SAE trained on post-MLP activations of the life2vec
GPT model is able to uncover monosemantic and interpretable features? For this pur-
pose, we trained an SAE on the second layer of the model and successfully extracted
several monosemantic features, partly illustrated by the z-score heuristic developed
to investigate category-level patterns. Several of these latents were interpretable, and
we presented latent 179 that exhibited strong activations for biological sex. We chose
this feature in hopes of demonstrating downstream effects and found it to behave
differently than anticipated. Instead, it aligned more with tokens describing national
origin in a still monosemantic way, leading us to discover unexpected nuances when
using a structured symbolic language that merits further consideration.
Third, we asked does causal intervention using SAE latents induce semantically co-
herent and predictable changes in downstream model behaviour? We introduced two
frameworks, clamping and additive steering, to determine whether our manual feature
interpretations aligned with downstream model behaviour. Clamping provided an ini-
tial indication of whether the model agreed with our hypothesised concept, which we
inferred from the increased probabilities of concept-aligned tokens. Subsequently, we
analysed how varying the steering strength influenced the frequency of expected to-
kens. We demonstrate that latent 456 underwent a significant behavioural shift as
steering strength increased, where it transitioned from favouring high-income bins to
generating none, whilst previously absent high-education tokens emerged and began
==PAGE:74==
64

to dominate the output and completely disregarding the imposed and rigid linguistic
structure of life2vec. This highlighted an additional aspect of the symbolic language
that warrants further investigation. Most importantly, we were able to induce medi-
cally impossible outputs without compromising the syntactic structure. Using a male
subject, we subtly steered the model with latent 299 signifying pregnancy, success-
fully producing several events that correspond to conceiving a child.
This thesis provided a valuable learning experience and provides a solid foundation
with several interesting avenues for future research aimed at obtaining deeper mech-
anistic insight into both life2vec and other life-course models. Although our work
initiates the progress in exploring and identifying features within the model, future
work addressing the outlined limitations of our current setup could uncover even
more compelling concepts, which may contribute in broadening our understanding of
how certain events can influence life-trajectories beyond the limited scope of human
insight.
