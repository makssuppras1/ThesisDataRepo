Abstract
Energy efficiency and indoor climate quality are critical concerns in the built environ-
ment sector. Traditional Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) design
practices, which rely on deterministic occupancy profiles and historical weather data,
often fail to accurately predict building performance. One challenge is the inadequate
consideration of fluctuations in heat gains, influenced by both climate variability and
occupant distribution. This thesis investigates the integration of stochastic occupancy
models and morphed weather data to enhance HVAC system design, aiming to ad-
dress these discrepancies and improve the sizing of HVAC systems.
A case study was conducted on a modern office building to evaluate the impact of
these advanced methodologies. The building’s HVAC systems were modeled using
both deterministic and stochastic occupancy profiles alongside historical and morphed
weather data. This approach allowed for a comprehensive assessment of the building’s
performance under various scenarios.
The study demonstrates that stochastic occupancy profiles reduce the build-up of op-
erative temperatures, thereby decreasing overheating hours and improving indoor
climate quality. This reduction aids in maintaining occupant comfort and more effi-
ciently sizing HVAC systems. Stochastic models result in fluctuating airflow and a
broader distribution of hours across different capacities at the room level, suggesting
that room-level components, such as duct capacities, should not be downsized. At the
Air Handling Unit (AHU) level, the study indicates potential for reducing the size of
fans and the heating and cooling coils, which can be downsized due to less frequent
high-capacity operation and more evenly distributed loads.
Furthermore, simulations using morphed weather data reveal that current cooling sys-
tems are inadequate for future climate scenarios. With anticipated increases in global
temperatures, the study projects higher cooling energy consumption and more over-
heating hours, underscoring the need for increased cooling capacity for future systems.
Conversely, radiator energy consumption is expected to decrease, highlighting the ne-
cessity for precise right-sizing of heating systems.
Implementing these advanced methodologies presents challenges, such as the time-
intensive process of generating and integrating stochastic profiles and interpreting re-
sults from varied future scenarios. To overcome these barriers, it is recommended to
develop standardized stochastic profiles and simulate with multiple future scenarios
to account for the uncertain climatic conditions and detailed HVAC systems.
Despite these challenges, the study concludes that the integration of stochastic occu-
pancy models and future weather data is essential for creating accurate, resilient build-
ing simulations. This approach can lead to better-sized HVAC systems and improved
indoor climate quality, ensuring that buildings are more adaptable to future climate
conditions. Continued refinement of these tools and processes is crucial for advancing
energy efficiency in the built environment sector.

1
Background
1.1
Introduction
Our population is estimated to increase by 22% from 2022 to 2050 (Pison, Couppié, and
Caporali, 2022). The latest Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change report (IPCC,
2023) states that urgent and sustained reductions in Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions
are required to reach the collective CO2 net zero by 2050 and to achieve the goal of
minimizing the temperature increase to 1.5 °C. Both the 1.5 °C and 2 °C pathways de-
mand radical changes and acceleration of transformation in all sectors the next 3 to 7
years.
The built environment sector is responsible for 37% of energy-related carbon emissions
globally and therefore has a huge responsibility for the reduction of GHG. As both
wealth and population continue to grow globally, more than ever is being built, and
the floor area is set to double by 2060 (United Nations Environment Programme, 2023).
With growth in the built environment and the need to cut carbon emissions, it is es-
sential that the sector becomes more energy efficient. These energy savings are often
achieved through simulation-based optimization. However, these building simula-
tions are currently simulated with historical weather data and occupant profiles from
building codes and standards assuming deterministic occupancy and homogeneous
spatial distribution across buildings, which does not reflect reality.
Several studies suggest that occupants and internal gains linked to their behaviour are
the most important and uncertain factors in building performance and thermal envi-
ronment (Chen, Hong, and Luo, 2018; He et al., 2022; Norouziasl, Jafari, and Chao
Wang, 2020; Chuang Wang, Yan, and Jiang, 2011). And as the standard and building
energy codes become stricter with increasing focus on tighter building envelopes and
efficient active systems, the occupancy is expected to have a rising influence on the
building energy performance (Zhan and Chong, 2021).
The building performance simulation tools used today have the flexibility and simu-
lation power to incorporate complex inputs and conduct whole-building simulations,
which will produce more realistic outputs and possibly reduce the performance gap.
Building performance simulations tools are used to estimate peaks in loads, and en-
ergy demand, allowing for proper sizing of Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning
(HVAC) systems (Page et al., 2008). However, current methods rely on deterministic
values for occupancy and internal loads, disregarding the stochastic nature of these
variables. This approach leads to significant inaccuracies in energy demand projec-
tions and, consequently, in the sizing of building systems.
The challenge lies in the inadequate consideration of fluctuations in heat gains, which
are influenced by both climate variability and occupant distribution (Page et al., 2008).
This results in systems that are either oversized, leading to unnecessary energy waste
and cost - or undersized, resulting in discomfort and inefficiencies.

1.2
State of the art
1.2.1
Indoor climate simulations
Indoor climate simulations in Denmark are a critical aspect of ensuring that buildings
meet regulatory standards for comfort and HVAC-design. These simulations are often
centered around critical rooms identified due to their high solar gains and significant
occupant-related heat gains. This focus helps understanding the worst-case scenarios
for thermal comfort and peak loads, which is crucial for designing HVAC systems that
can effectively handle these demands.
The simulations are typically conducted using standard occupancy profiles provided
by "Branchevejledning for indeklimaberegninger" - a guideline that simplifies the pro-
cess by offering predefined parameters for occupancy. In most cases the category ordi-
nary occupancy is used. In office environments, these ordinary profiles assume occu-
pancy at 80% of full capacity, while meeting rooms are assumed to be occupied at 75%,
see profiles in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Ordinary occupancy schedules for open offices and meeting rooms
This standardized approach facilitates a more streamlined and uniform assessment of
indoor climate conditions across different projects.
One of the primary reasons for adopting this highly standardized method is the time
constraints faced by indoor climate specialists. Detailed simulations that involve an in-
depth analysis of occupancy patterns and whole-building analysis require significant
time and resources. Given the limited time available for these specialists, adhering
to established standards and guidelines becomes a practical necessity. This approach
leads to compliance with regulations and meets the bare minimum requirements with-
out the need for exhaustive, time-consuming analysis.
Moreover, the reliance on standard methods also provides a level of consistency across
different projects. Consistency is beneficial not only for regulatory compliance but also
for benchmarking and comparing the performance of various buildings. However,
this approach has its limitations. While it ensures adherence to regulations, it may not
fully capture the unique occupancy patterns and specific indoor climate challenges of
individual buildings. This can lead to HVAC systems that are either over- or under-
designed for actual use conditions.

1.2.2
Danish HVAC systems
In Denmark, HVAC systems are typically divided into two separate systems: 1) a ven-
tilation system that provides fresh air and cooling, and 2) a heating system that heats
each room through radiators or floor heating.
New office buildings in Denmark often utilize Demand Controlled Ventilation (DCV)
systems. These systems are fed by a central Air Handling Unit (AHU), which provides
all rooms with air at a uniform supply temperature. The air volume flow to each room
is then regulated by dampers, which adjust according to the required flow to maintain
acceptable levels of CO2 concentration and air temperature. The AHU generally in-
cludes a heat recovery unit that transfers heat between the exhaust air and the supply
air with an efficiency of 60-95% (Mardiana-Idayu and Riffat, 2012). In Denmark, these
heat exchangers often conserve a significant amount of energy for reheating supply
air, although they can also be used to cool the supply air. Following heat recovery, the
ventilation air is heated or cooled to the desired supply temperature by a heating coil
and cooling coil.
Supply air temperature can vary significantly from project to project and depends on
several factors. Since most HVAC systems in Denmark deliver the same supply air
temperature to all rooms, it is essential to determine the appropriate supply tempera-
ture. If the temperature is too low, there is a risk of drafts, and energy may be wasted
as the radiators in each room will need to reheat the air. Conversely, if the supply
temperature is too high, the maximum supply flow might not be sufficient to cool the
room to an acceptable thermal comfort level.
Radiators or floor heating are typically installed in rooms with external building en-
velopes to maintain the temperature above the minimum requirements.
1.2.3
Traditional HVAC dimensioning
The current practice for sizing HVAC system often relies on standards, rules of thumbs
and worst case scenarios.
Heating systems are dimensioned by running a simulation with outdoor temperature
of -12 °C without any internal gains.
The dimensioning airflow for each room is usually found by assessing the needed air-
flows for critical rooms and applying the same air changes to similar rooms. The max-
imum total airflow is then used to dimension the heating coil and cooling coil.
The heating coil is dimensioned to heat the supply air from the temperature after the
heat exchanger to the supply temperature for the maximum total airflow. An oversiz-
ing capacity of approximately 20% is often added to the heating capacity.
3
==PAGE:11==
The cooling coil is often dimensioned to deliver the supply temperature at 80% of max-
imum total airflow, while the outdoor temperature is 26 °C and 60% Relative Humidity
(RH). An oversizing capacity of around 20% is usually added to the power needed to
cool the air under these conditions.
1.2.4
HVAC right sizing
HVAC right sizing entails precise estimations of design loads and appropriate sizing of
components, supported by a transition to whole-building simulations. This methodol-
ogy seeks to guarantee that HVAC systems are optimally designed to meet the specific
needs of a building, thus preventing the negative consequences linked to oversizing
(Gorter, 2010; Sherman and Walker, 2011).
Some of the main barriers to right sizing is occupancy loads and internal loads, which
are often overestimated with standard values from design guides and standards. These
inaccuracies are some of the critical reasons that commercial HVAC systems are over-
sized (Construction Innovation, 2007).
1.3
Problem statement
Buildings are designed inconveniently by dimensioning the systems based on peak
loads from simulations with historical weather data and simplified user profiles, which
do not accurately represent the complex reality of climate change and occupant be-
haviour. The fluctuations in heat gains are important to consider when calculating the
peak loads and demands that the systems need to fulfil. This leads to the research
questions that will be investigated in the study:
1. How do stochastic occupancy schedules and morphed weather data influence
the indoor climate quality and the sizing of HVAC systems?
1.1 How do stochastic occupancy schedules impact the indoor climate quality,
ventilation demands and the peak loads for heating and cooling?
1.2 How do morphed weather data affect thermal comfort and performance of
HVAC systems?
2. What are the key challenges and recommendations for adopting stochastic oc-
cupancy profiles and morphed weather data in building performance simula-
tions?
1.4
Case introduction
The case chosen for this study is Høje Taastrup City Hall. The 8700 m2 office building
opened the doors in 2023 and is the workplace for around 500 employees. The ground
floor and 1st floor mainly serve as the entry and meeting center for consultations be-
tween employees and citizens. The six middle floors are designed as open offices with
88 office desks per floor situated towards the facade, while the meeting rooms are fac-
ing the atrium, and are primarily used for internal meetings. The 8th floor is a canteen
4
==PAGE:12==
and kitchen serving lunch for the employees.
The case was chosen because of the complexity of the occupancy patterns and high so-
lar gains, while having simple and repetitive geometry of the building, which allows
the heat gains to be in the focus. The complexity of the occupancy patterns comes from
a variety in usage through the building and the fact that the meeting floors are used by
a combination of employees and citizens.
The indoor climate analysis in the original project was made by COWI A/S with the
standard approach in terms of occupancy, weather data and simulation size. The oc-
cupancy was modeled with standard occupancy profiles from "Branchevejledning for
indeklimaberegninger" and climate data from the Danish Design Reference Year (DRY)
2013. The simulations were focused on specific zones that were assessed to be critical
with large glazing area and high loads from occupants.
Notably, they are currently ventilating the building with supply air temperatures that
are regulated with the outdoor air temperature. It has been reported that the building
is experiencing overheated spaces and that the Variable Air Volume (VAV)-dampers
are fully opened a big fraction of the time during winter, because the spaces are not
cooled sufficiently with the high supply air temperatures.
2
Methodology
2.1
Stochastic occupancy schedules
An effective way to avoid the deterministic nature of standard occupancy profiles is
to create stochastic occupancy schedules. By using stochastic models to describe oc-
cupancy, the dynamic and diverse nature of human behavior is better reflected in the
new occupancy schedules. These stochastic occupancy profiles can be generated in
various ways, but one of the most recognized methods is the agent-based approach
using Markov chains (Yang, Santamouris, and Lee, 2016). This modeling approach
has been widely used over the past decades to simulate occupant movement between
spaces (Page et al., 2008; Chuang Wang, Yan, and Jiang, 2011; Chen, Hong, and Luo,
2018; Norouziasl, Jafari, and Chao Wang, 2020).
An agent-based Markov chain model simulates occupant movement by treating each
occupant as an individual agent transitioning between spaces based on a predeter-
mined probability matrix. These probabilities dictate the likelihood of moving from
one space to another based on the current location, allowing the model to capture the
dynamic behavior and interaction patterns of occupants within the simulated environ-
ment.
In this study, the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL)’s web-based stochas-
tic occupancy simulator is used, integrating previous agent-based studies to model the
presence and movement of each occupant. The first stochastic model incorporated
5
==PAGE:13==
is Reinhart’s cumulated probability statistics model (Reinhart, 2004), which describes
status transition events (e.g., arrival, departure, and short-term leave). The second
model is Wang’s homogeneous Markov chain model (Chuang Wang, Yan, and Jiang,
2011), which describes the random movement between spaces. The third model is a
new stochastic model designed for the occupancy simulator to describe meeting events
(Chen, Hong, and Luo, 2018).
This simulator is particularly suitable for models with a larger number of spaces. It
reduces the probability matrix complexity by categorizing rooms into groups: Outside
Building, Own Office, Other Offices, Meeting Room, and Auxiliary Room. It is then
assumed that the probability of moving from any space in Category A to any space in
Category B is the same. For example, the probability of a person moving from their
own office to any auxiliary room is considered the same (Chen, Hong, and Luo, 2018).
2.2
Weather data morphing
Weather data morphing is a method used to create design weather data for building
simulations that reflect future climate scenarios. It combines current observed weather
data with projections from climate models, producing time series that maintain realis-
tic sequences while representing future average conditions. This technique effectively
translates coarse climate model predictions into detailed data suitable for thermal sim-
ulations in buildings (Belcher, Hacker, and Powell, 2005).
CCWorldWeatherGen is the most commonly used weather data morphing method for
researchers studying the energy performance of buildings (Rodrigues, Fernandes, and
Carvalho, 2023). CCWorldWeartherGen is based on the third IPCC report, published
in 2001, and one of the major General Circulation Models (GCM) from Coupled Model
Intercomparison Project 2 (CMIP2), HadCM3 (Jentsch, James, Bourikas, et al., 2013).
The baseline timeframe of HadCM3 is 1961 to 1990. Morphing weather files with CC-
WorldWeatherGen from periods later than 1990 will therefore overestimate the climate
change since the HadCM3 scenarios are based on relative changes from 1970s condi-
tions (Jentsch, James, and Bahaj, 2012).
In Denmark the thermal indoor climate is usually documented using the DRY2013
weather file with data of the typical months from 2001 to 2010. Morphing with CC-
WorldWeatherGen is therefore not prefable for DRY2013, as the data is not within the
baseline period of HadCM3. Instead it is recommended to use morphing tools based
on the latest IPPC report (6th assesment) from 2023, to ensure latest climate models
with valid baseline periods.
The morphing tool implementing the latest CMIP6 climate models with the highest
amount of publications is the Future Weather Generator (Duan et al., 2025). This is an
open-source morphing tool that aligns with the 6th IPPC Assessment Report and use
the new state of the art climate models from the CMIP6. The tool morphs the latest
emission scenarios Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP): SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, SSP3-
7.0, SSP5-8.5 for both year 2050 and 2080 (Rodrigues, Fernandes, and Carvalho, 2023).
6
==PAGE:14==
In Future Weather Generator several climate models from CMIP6 can be chosen, but in
this study the GCM, EC-Earth3, will be used as it has the highest accuracy (Rodrigues,
Fernandes, and Carvalho, 2023).
Compared to the HadCM3 model used in CCWorldWeatherGen, the EC-Earth3 model
also has a significantly finer spatial resolution with more grid points. Furthermore,
the Future Weather Generator takes advantage of bilinear interpolation, allowing for
a better spatial representation of the data in the EnergyPlus Weather (EPW) location,
compared to CCWorldWeatherGen, which takes the average of the four nearest points
resulting in the same values for all locations within the cell defined by the same four
point (Rodrigues, Fernandes, and Carvalho, 2023).
The general morphing method is described by (Belcher, Hacker, and Powell, 2005) and
is based on three main statistical operations: "Shift", "Stretch" and a combination of
both.
The "shift" transformation adds the monthly projected change (∆xm) to the existing
value (x0):
x = x0 + ∆xm
(2.2.1)
The "stretch" operation scales the existing value (x0) with the fraction of the monthly
projected change (αm) by multiplication:
x = αm · x0
(2.2.2)
The combination of shift and stretch is applied when both absolute and relative monthly
mean changes are predicted for the variable (x0):
x = x0 + ∆xm + αm · (x0 −x)
(2.2.3)

2.3
Model requirements
Stochastic occupancy schedules and morphed weather data can’t be applied to any
model. It is essential to consider the implications of these changes and how they affect
the simulation model and systems. Typically, building simulations are conducted on
critical zones using idealized HVAC systems to simplify the process.
It is crucial to have a model that is affected by weather conditions and stochastic occu-
pancy, and which reflects the consequences of changing these inputs, to say something
about robustness and influence of changing the inputs.
Standard occupancy profiles from "Branchevejledning for indeklimaberegninger" are
designed to reflect occupancy in critical rooms. However, stochastic occupancy sched-
ules require whole-building simulations to account for occupants moving between
spaces. This movement causes fluctuations in heat gains from people and equipment
in individual rooms, but the total internal gains for the entire building should remain
more stable.
With idealized ventilation, the supply air temperature always matches the design sup-
ply air temperature in the simulation model, regardless of weather conditions. In con-
trast, when modeling the HVAC system in detail, the supply temperature depends on
outdoor temperature, relative humidity, and the capacity of HVAC components. For
instance, on hot and humid days, the cooling coil might not be able to cool the air to the
designed supply temperature. Transitioning from an idealized to a detailed ventilation
system helps the model more accurately reflect weather condition changes.
To effectively reflect changes in weather data and stochastic occupancy, this study will
be conducted as a whole-building simulation with detailed HVAC system modeling.

2.5
Programs
2.5.1
IES VE
Integrated Environmental Solutions Virtual Environment (IES VE) is a powerful build-
ing performance simulation software used to optimize designs and analyze indoor
climate. In this study IES VE was employed for whole-building simulation with a de-
tailed HVAC system modeled in ApacheHVAC. This approach allowed accurate sim-
ulation of the indoor climate with various occupancy schedules and weather data. The
primary focus was on assessing thermal comfort and calculating the loads necessary
for HVAC system dimensioning.
2.5.2
Occupancy Simulator
The Occupancy Simulator is a web-based app developed by LBNL, which simulates
occupants movement in office buildings using the Markov-chain model described in
Section 2.1. This tool is used to create the stochastic occupancy profiles from inputs on
occupants, spaces and events, which are later modified by python code and imported
into IES VE.
2.5.3
Future Weather Generator
Future Weather Generator is an open-source Java app for researchers and building de-
sign professionals. It morphs present-day hourly EPW files used in dynamic building
simulation to match climate change scenarios. This tool is used to morph the com-
monly used DRY2013 EPW file and project the climate change predictions for 2050
and 2080 of the four Shared Socioeconomic Pathways; SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, SSP3-7.0,
SSP5-8.5. These eight morphed EPW files can then be loaded in IES VE and used for
simulations of future climate scenarios.

The investigation of HVAC system performance reveals its sensitivity to stochastic oc-

At the room level, stochastic occupancy leads to more fluctuating airflow and a wider

creased overheating hours and considerably higher cooling energy consumption. Con-
versely, radiator energy use will decrease under future weather conditions.

5
Key challenges and recommendations
5.1
Stochastic occupancy profiles
The stochastic occupancy profiles influence indoor climate quality and system per-
formance, as demonstrated in the results. These outcomes highlight the potential for
reducing the size of several HVAC components and emphasize the importance of con-
sidering fluctuations in occupancy.
One of the key challenges is that generating and implementing project-specific agent-
based stochastic occupancy profiles is currently quite time-consuming and requires
several manual processes across different platforms. Many projects in the building sec-
tor are unable to adopt stochastic occupancy profiles due to tight budgets and sched-
ules.
Standardized profiles save consultants’ time and facilitate performance comparisons
across different projects. However, these standardized profiles could also incorporate
stochastic elements. Although this report does not propose a specific solution, it fo-
cuses on the essential elements of stochastic profiles to reflect in standardized occu-
pancy profiles.
The results indicate two main issues with standardized deterministic occupancy pro-
files, stemming from their design for analyzing critical rooms:
1. Occupancy fluctuations: This term refers to variations in occupancy within a
single room over time, as well as differences between similar rooms at the same
time-step. These fluctuations should be accurately represented at the room level
but tend to become more stable when aggregated across multiple rooms and ex-
tended periods. The findings indicate that such fluctuations reduce the number
of overheating hours and generally lead to the AHU operating below maximum
capacity in terms of airflows, cooling, and heating. Nevertheless, despite these
fluctuations, the sizing of duct branches and terminals should not be reduced, as
demand will be more evenly distributed, resulting in more frequent operation at
maximum airflows.
2. Overestimation of occupancy: Overestimation impacts comfort and system per-
formance, as seen in the results when comparing the reduced profiles with the
baseline occupancy and stochastic occupancy match. The highest overestimations
with standardized occupancy schedules happens around lunch.
39
==PAGE:47==
5.1.1
Occupancy fluctuations
As mentioned these fluctuations are important for reflecting the peak demands of in-
dividual rooms, while not over-dimensioning the whole system. The most important
factors of these fluctuations of the stochastic profiles are:
• Fluctuations across time
• Fluctuations across spaces
For a single room, the occupancy should change continuously throughout the day, fluc-
tuating within a specified range. Additionally, this pattern of fluctuation should vary
from day to day.
Different standardized profiles should be made so each room type has several stochas-
tic profiles that can be assigned. When looking at the average occupancy across several
meeting rooms, it should not be the same occupancy as looking at each room individ-
ually. Having different profiles will create fluctuations across spaces that will be aver-
aged out when including more spaces and longer time-periods.
These profiles could be the standard profiles but modified by applying a random vari-
ation to the occupancy at each time-step. By applying the random variation the oc-
cupancy will fluctuate in a specified interval and naturally create periods with higher
and lower occupancy.
Below on Figure 30 is a simplified illustration of what a standardized stochastic oc-
cupancy schedule could look like for a meeting room on a single day. This is just an
example with 75% baseline occupancy and 0.25 variation, but it could also look dif-
ferent with larger variation spans and periods where the meeting room is empty. The
orange area indicates the variation span, that profiles can vary within, while the red
line is an example of a random variation on a random day.
Figure 30: Example of standardized occupancy schedule with variation span
40
==PAGE:48==
5.1.2
Overestimation of occupancy
Addressing overestimation is more time-consuming and project-specific, than occu-
pancy fluctuations. It requires assessing the total building occupant capacity, meeting
room usage, and guest attendance at meetings. In this case, the number of meeting
rooms per employee was very low, with only one room for every 6.4 employees. Addi-
tionally, guests are a big part of the meeting occupancy, making them a crucial factor in
estimating total occupants. When reducing the number of occupants, the profiles must
be stochastic to avoid under-dimensioning systems, as deterministic profiles with low
constant occupancy will not capture peak loads accurately.
This issue cannot be solved with standardized profiles alone and requires a more spe-
cific tool. This could be a simplified agent-based simulator or a tool which projects
agent-based profiles onto specific project conditions. Despite its complexity, such a
tool could provide high value to certain projects.
However, overestimation around lunch time could be solved with standard profiles.
Many office buildings in Denmark have a canteen or an area dedicated for eating
lunch, which is often used between 11:00 and 13:00 for around 30 minuttes per em-
ployee. Currently the occupancy profiles for open offices have reduced occupancy
between 11:00 and 12:00, but only 40%. The meeting rooms have 75% occupancy in the
same time period, which means that the occupancy will be overestimated, when sim-
ulating with a canteen like in this case. Standardized profiles could include occupancy
schedules for canteens. These profiles would have matching schedules for offices and
meeting rooms. This ensures that occupants are not expected to be in both the canteen
and their office or meeting room simultaneously. It would be recommended to not re-
duce the meeting room occupancy as much as the office occupancy, as lunch meetings
can be common.
5.2
Future weather conditions
A key challenge in simulating building performance with future weather data is in-
terpreting the results accurately. Morphing weather files can generate various future
scenarios across different time periods and socioeconomic pathways. Though some
scenarios are more likely than others, predicting the exact outcome remains uncertain.
Therefore, it’s essential to simulate multiple future scenarios.
When using morphed weather files for building performance simulations, it is crucial
to consider the likelihood of scenarios and the lifespan of building components. The
exact same building won’t exist in 2080. Designing HVAC systems to meet 2050 or 2080
standards would lead to over-sizing for the current climate. Typically, HVAC systems
have a lifespan of 15-25 years and will likely be replaced with more efficient systems
in the future.
Instead, simulations should be used to assess the robustness of designs under future
weather conditions, how demands might change over time, and which system compo-
nents may lack capacity in the future. HVAC systems should be modular and adaptive,
41
==PAGE:49==
allowing demand changes to be addressed by modifying a few components instead of
the entire system, but still accounting for the carbon-cost of modularity and adapt-
ability. Flexibility can save carbon emissions, but it can certainly also be more carbon
intensive. Having a highly flexible system can cause inefficiencies and extra emissions
for materials, which in the end will exceed the emissions saved in the future.
Using detailed HVAC models for simulations with future weather conditions is rec-
ommended, as they provide a comprehensive view of how HVAC systems could be
affected by changing weather conditions. In this case study, morphed weather files
increased overheating hours, often due to insufficient cooling capacity. This lack of
cooling capacity would not have been visible with idealized ventilation, where extra
overheating hours would only be reflected by changes in solar gains and heat transfer
through the building envelope.
Future weather scenarios also indicate a decrease in radiator energy consumption,
making it relevant to ensure the right sizing of heating systems. Moving away from
conventional heating system dimensioning based on simplified simulations and extra
over-capacities is necessary.
Several studies suggest that SSP2-4.5 is the most likely scenario, consistent with cur-
rent policies (Scafetta, 2024; Fricko et al., 2017; Hausfather and Peters, 2020), and it is
described as the "middle road" in the latest IPCC report (IPCC, 2023). SSP3-7.0 and
SSP5-8.5 are considered unlikely and highly unlikely, respectively, and should be re-
garded as worst-case scenarios.
6
Discussion
The results indicate that the overheating hours are decreased by the use of stochastic
occupancy schedules, as the build-up in operative temperature is lower with the fluc-
tuations in occupancy. These fluctuations also affected the HVAC system, by having
a wider distribution of hours across different airflow capacities at the room level. The
results further indicate that duct capacities for internal meeting rooms should not be
downsized, while the fan, heating and cooling coils could be slightly reduced in size
when looking at the AHU level.
This analysis supports the theory that future weather conditions will have clear impact
on the cooling system, with the current capacity being insufficient to maintain the sup-
ply air temperature, leading to increased overheating hours and higher cooling energy
consumption.
The key challenges for implementing stochastic occupancy profiles in building perfor-
mance simulations are the time and effort that is needed to generate and implement
stochastic profiles in the current process. It is recommended to make some standadized
stochastic occupancy profiles that incorporate the fluctuations of occupancy through
42
==PAGE:50==
both time and spaces. Furthermore it would be beneficial to reduce overestimations
of occupancy, by reducing the periods with higher occupancy than is expected in the
building, like the lunch break.
For the implementation of morphed weather data it is recommended to simulate with
detailed HVAC models to see the effect of climate change on the performance of cool-
ing system. It is furthermore recommended, to simulate multiple future scenarios, as
it remains uncertain which weather conditions will be most representative. The like-
lihood of future scenarios should also be considered, where SSP2-4.5 is most likely,
while SSP3-7.0 and SSP5-8.5 are unlikely and highly unlikely and should be consid-
ered as worst case scenarios.
While this study suggest the use of stochastic occupancy schedules and morphed weather
data, some limitations of the tools, simulations, and process should be addressed.
6.1
Immature tools
6.1.1
Occupancy Simulator
The Occupancy Simulator is not mature enough yet. Even though the tool is one of the
leading in the field of agent-based simulations tools for building simulations, it still
have some bugs and limitations that influence the study.
The tool has a bug where the lunch break duration is only 10-20 minutes per occupant,
which underestimate the amount of people in the canteen, while it overestimates the
occupancy of offices and meeting room between 11:00 and 13:00. This causes inaccura-
cies in the movement of occupants and the need for a customized solution of the lunch
break in stochastic occupancy match, which resulted in lower occupancy of meetings and
conferences.
The Occupancy Simulator also shows limitations in the setup of meeting rooms. The
meeting rooms can host meetings whenever people are present in the building, which
means that the meeting rooms are often occupied already between 07:00 and 08:00 with
the stochastic occupancy match model. Usually meetings would not start before 08:00, as
also represented in the baseline occupancy profile. This extra time spread of meeting
events makes it harder to compare the stochastic occupancy match to the baseline occu-
pancy model, as their occupancy is distributed over a longer time-span.
These limitations of the Occupancy Simulator could impact the results, making it dif-
ficult to determine whether fluctuations are due to variations in occupancy profiles or
differences in distribution and slightly reduced occupant numbers.
43
==PAGE:51==
6.1.2
Future Weather Generator
The Future Weather Generator is a step in front of other morphing tools like CCWorld-
WeatherGen by implementing state of the art climate data, having a higher spatial
resolution, and more future scenarios. The main limitation of the Future Weather Gen-
erator is that it is relatively new and have been validated less than other popular mor-
phing tools, as it haven’t been used for that many research studies yet.
In this study the weather data file, DRY2013, is morphed for future scenarios, which is
constructed by the typical months between 2001 to 2010. This means that the morphing
will project the climate change onto the typical months of the DRY weather file and
create a future weather file that does not include days with extreme conditions.
6.2
Simulation accuracy
The simulation accuracy is important, as seen in the results of CO2 concentration. Here
the influence of the simulation time-step was investigated and it was revealed that
the peaks of CO2 concentration that were exceeding 1000 ppm were completely elim-
inated, when using simulation time-steps of 1 minute instead of 10 minutes. These
simulation time-steps can also have a big influence on several other results, especially
with stochastic profiles as they have more fluctuations. The stochastic profiles exhibit
a higher frequency of instantaneous changes in demand, with meeting rooms transi-
tioning from empty to occupied. Additionally, increases in occupancy can be more in-
fluential, with the possibility of meeting rooms reaching 100% occupancy. This means
that the delays of system response have a higher influence on the simulations with
stochastic occupancy.
Other variables like operative temperature can also be influenced, especially the re-
quirement R1, which is closer to the temperatures of the proportional controlled ven-
tilation. The operative temperature doesn’t increase as fast as the CO2 concentration,
but fast response time of the HVAC system means that the total amount of hours above
25 °C is reduced by 72 hours for stochastic occupancy match (2a) and 44 hours for baseline
occupancy (1c). This shows an extra point to be aware of when using stochastic profiles.
6.3
Critical rooms
With stochastic profiles, all rooms can suddenly be critical rooms. When using baseline
occupancy profiles, all rooms of the same type will have the exact same profile, which
make it easier to spot critical rooms, as the process of finding critical rooms will focus
more conditions affecting the solar gains. With stochastic occupancy profiles the occu-
pancy will fluctuate and be different from room to room at the same time-step, which
adds an extra layer of complexity to the critical rooms. The critical rooms are then a
combination of fluctuations in weather conditions and occupancy, which makes the
timing relevant suddenly.
Five similar 6-person meetings rooms facing east (R01.21-R01.25) are compared as an
example of this. With baseline profiles, R01.22 has the most hours above 25°C on the
44
==PAGE:52==
24th of June and 3rd of July, but is quite similar to the other rooms. With stochastic
occupancy profiles, R01.23 has the most hours above 25°C on the 24th of June, but has
the least hours on the 3rd of July, and the difference between the rooms are bigger. This
shows how the critical rooms with stochastic profiles vary on through time and that all
rooms can be critical in a way, if their occupancy spike at certain times.
This also means that it could matter for the results, which stochastic profile that is as-
signed to each room, as the stochastic occupancy have a big influence on the peaks in
operative temperature of each room and the critical hours change with each profile.
7
Future work
To enhance and validate the findings of this thesis, future work should consider the
following suggestions:
• Simulate with stochastic occupancy profiles that are more comparable to the base-
line occupancy to further establish if fluctuations impact indoor climate quality
and HVAC system performance. This could be done more accurately matching
the average total number of occupants presented in Table 9.
• Test the model with variations of the same stochastic occupancy model to identify
if it is the room or occupancy profile that is critical. This can be achieved by
"rotating" the occupancy profiles among similar room types, such as 6-person
meeting rooms.
• Fit the baseline model to be just below the limits for indoor climate quality,
then compare its sizing to a model adjusted similarly using the stochastic match
model.
• Simulate a typical office building with stochastic occupancy to see if the effect
remains visible with fewer meeting rooms per occupant.
• Create standardized stochastic profiles by incorporating variation into existing
standardized occupancy profiles.
• Test the impact of using standardized stochastic occupancy profiles.
• Use the Occupancy Simulator in collaboration with clients to estimate how much
occupancy is overestimated and assess its potential effect on building perfor-
mance.
• Compare weather files morphed by the Future Weather Generator with those
morphed by other popular tools to validate the Future Weather Generator.
• Use the morphed weather data to compare different climate adaptation strategies
and evaluate their resilience to climate change.

8
Conclusion
This thesis investigates the integration of stochastic occupancy models and future weather
data in designing adaptive HVAC systems to enhance energy efficiency and indoor cli-
mate quality. Given the critical need to improve energy performance in the built en-
vironment sector, the study focuses on addressing the discrepancies arising from tra-
ditional HVAC design practices, which rely on deterministic occupancy profiles and
historical weather data.
Key findings demonstrate that using stochastic occupancy profiles reduces the build-
up of operative temperatures, thereby decreasing overheating hours and improving
indoor climate quality. This reduction is essential for maintaining occupant comfort
and efficiently sizing HVAC systems. Stochastic occupancy models lead to fluctuating
airflow and a broader distribution of hours across different airflow capacities at the
room level, necessitating duct capacities that can handle these variations. At the AHU
level, there is potential for reducing the size of fans due to less frequent high-capacity
operation. Furthermore, stochastic occupancy schedules also show potential for reduc-
ing the sizing of heating and cooling coils in the AHU, as these fluctuations distribute
the load more evenly and reduce peak demands.
The study reveals that the current cooling system capacity is inadequate for future
weather scenarios. Increased overheating hours and higher cooling energy consump-
tion are projected as global temperatures rise, emphasizing the need for cooling sys-
tems capable of handling future climatic conditions. Conversely, radiator energy con-
sumption is expected to decrease, highlighting the importance of right-sizing heating
systems using detailed simulations rather than simplified methods with over-capacities.
Implementing stochastic occupancy profiles in building performance simulations presents
challenges. The process of generating and integrating stochastic profiles is time-intensive
and involves multiple manual processes across different platforms, posing a significant
barrier for projects with tight budgets and schedules. However, developing standard-
ized stochastic profiles can help overcome this obstacle by incorporating necessary
fluctuations while saving time and facilitating performance comparisons.
Accurately interpreting results can be a challenge with morphed weather files, as var-
ious future scenarios are generated across different time periods and socioeconomic
pathways, with uncertain outcomes. Therefore, it is essential to simulate multiple fu-
ture scenarios, considering the likelihood of scenarios, with SSP2-4.5 being the most
likely, while SSP3-7.0 and SSP5-8.5 are unlikely and should be viewed as worst-case
scenarios. It is furthermore recommended to use detailed HVAC models for simula-
tions with morphed weather files and consider the lifespan of building components.
Overall, the study underscores the importance of integrating stochastic occupancy
models and future weather data into HVAC system design. This approach can sub-
stantially enhance the accuracy of building simulations, leading to better-sized systems
and improved indoor climate quality.

flow by a sensor placed in each space. This control has a proportional control, with
a midband that is different for summer and winter. In the summer periode from 1st
of May to 30th of September the ventilation flow rate is set to 20% of maximum flow
rate for temperatures of 21°C or lower, while it is set to 100% when the temperature
exceeds 24°C, as seen on Figure 32. For the rest of the year it is displaced by 1 °C, as
seen on Figure 33.

Night cooling is added to further precondition the rooms during the night in hot pe-
riods. This is controlled by the night cooling controller, VF5, which runs from 01:00
to 04:00. In this time period the ventilation flow rate is set to 100% if the dry-bulb
temperature of the room is sensed to be above 24°C and the supply air temperature
is 3.5°C lower than and extract air temperature. The night cooling is then turned off
again when the dry-bulb temperature decreases to 22°C or when the extract air is less
than 1.5°C warmer than the supply air temperature.

temperature is below 19°C down to 0% at 21 °C, turning off the heater, when dry-bulb
