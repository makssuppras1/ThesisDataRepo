Abstract
X-ray diﬀraction experiments are among the most frequently used techniques for character-
izing ferroelectric materials. In certain ferroelectric materials, the microstructure strongly
inﬂuences the diﬀraction pattern in a way that complicates the interpretation.
In this thesis the diﬀraction from a material with nanometer sized twin domains is
treated theoretically, and it is showed how the diﬀraction pattern goes from reﬂecting the
average structure over multiple domains when the domains are smaller than a certain size,
to reﬂecting the local structure within one domain as the domain lengths increase. I arrive
at simple expressions to estimate the domain fractions and domain sizes from the diﬀraction
pattern.
Data from a time-resolved powder diﬀraction experiment are treated and analyzed on
the basis of the new model and compared with the usual interpretation. This shows an am-
biguity with the interpretation of a common type of experiments. We calculate the strain
and polarization of the sample from changes in the position and intensity of diﬀraction peaks.
The investigated material is a solid solution perovskite ferroelectric of the composi-
tion (BaTi03)x−1(BiZn0.5Ti0.5O3)x close to the transition between ferroelectric and relaxor
behaviour, which has high dielectric constant over a wide temperature range and is under
interest as a possible replacement for lead-containing ferroelectrics used in electronics today.

Introduction
Ferroelectrics is a type of materials with special electric properties ﬁrst discovered in the
nineteen twenties in Rochelle salt.[1] The theoretical description of this phenomenon, and
the discovery of new materials took speed after the discovery of abnormally high dielectric
constants in barium titanate and later in similar perovskite ceramics.[2]
Ferroelectric materials have a wide range of technological uses. They are used in high-
capacity capacitors for electronics and power conversion purposes. Ferroelectric materials,
that also have piezoelectric properties, are used sensors and transducers, ranging from ul-
trasound scanners for medical imaging to pickups for musical instruments. [3, 4]
In order to design better ferroelectric materials, one should strive to understand the
structure-function relationship and the structural response to external perturbations. Cur-
rently, the understanding of the structure of ferroelectric materials is lacking in some aspects.
Especially in materials that in some respect lie close to a phase transition, the structure
remains a contested subject. As many of the functional properties are optimal for these ma-
terials, a correct description of the structure in these cases could bring insight that would
enable the creation of new materials with better properties.
Frequently used techniques for characterizing the structure include microscopy tech-
niques like transmission electron microscopy and piezoresponse force microscopy, which have
very high resolution and give real space information about the structure at a wide range of
length scales, but are limited in the range of samples, that can be used as they are either
only sensitive to the surface of the sample or applicable to very thin samples.
Other frequently used methods are the scattering methods, that utilize the diﬀraction of
X-rays or neutrons. Thanks to the long penetration depths of X-rays and Neutrons, these
methods give information about the bulk of large single crystals or ceramic samples simi-
lar to the ones found in technological applications. However the measured signal contains
average information from the entire sample and do not readily give local information with
real-space resolution.
In this thesis we examine data from an in-situ experiment on samples of the solid solution
(BaTi03)x−1(BiZn0.5Ti0.5O3)x, which is one of a range of new ferroelectric materials, that
have come under interest due to concerns of the toxicity of the lead-containing ferroelectrics
an piezoelectrics, that are currently used in many applications.[5, 6]

4.1
Ferroelectric materials
This section introduces the basic concepts of ferroelectric materials as well as the current
understating of the speciﬁc group of materials, investigated in this thesis. The basic concepts
are based on textbooks by Griﬃth[7], Kittel[8] and Jaﬀe & Cook.[3] As well as review articles
by Haertling[9] and Bokov & Ye.[10]
4.1.1
Polarization
Under an electric ﬁeld, a material may respond with an electric dipole moment. An electric
dipole in its simplest form is a pair of a positively and a negatively charged particle of equal
charge q separated by a distance d. At distances much larger than the separation, (r >> d)
the electrical ﬁeld of such a conﬁguration is given by the dipole ﬁeld:

ﬁeld is exactly given by eq. (1) everywhere. This is called an ideal dipole.
Real materials consist of lots of negative and positive charges and the electric ﬁeld lo-
cally varies around these charges. When treating the electrical properties of materials, one
usually only considers the macroscopic ﬁeld, which is the ﬁeld averaged over a large enough
volume not to see eﬀects of the individual charges but small enough to describe eﬀects due to
the size and shape of the material. For the macroscopic ﬁeld, all the dipoles can be treated
as ideal dipoles and only the density of dipole moments (called the polarization) is important.
A polarization causes an electric ﬁeld similar to the dipole ﬁeld, but the exact shape of the
ﬁeld depends on the shape of the polarized material. One case to consider is an inﬁnite plate
of a uniformly polarized material with a polarization perpendicular to the plate surfaces.

4.1.2
Linear dielectrics
One very large class of materials are the linear dielectrics.
These materials are non-
conducting and have zero polarization when they are not subjected to an external ﬁeld,
but get a polarization when an external ﬁeld is applied. In general this polarization can be
any function of the applied ﬁeld strength but in many cases only the linear term is impor-
tant. When this is the case, we call the material a linear dielectric and as the name implies,
the polarization depends linearly on the electric ﬁeld:
P(E) = χϵ0E = (1 −ϵr)ϵ0E
(4)
where χ is the dielectric susceptibility and ϵr is the relative permitivity. Materials with
large positive relative permitivity will build up bound charges on the surface that reduce the
electric ﬁeld in the interior. These materials are used for capacitors with high capacitance
as the capacitance is increased by a factor of ϵr.
Beside the relative permitivity, the other important quantity of high-dielectric constant
materials is the dielectric loss tangent δ which quantiﬁes the energy dissipation of a time-
dependant electric ﬁeld in the dielectric ﬁeld.
4.1.3
Ferroelectrics
Ferroelectrics is a group of materials that can have non-zero polarization even at zero ap-
plied ﬁeld. These materials respond to an external electric ﬁeld with a large polarization
compared to dielectrics. When the external ﬁeld is removed, the remnant polarization (Pr)
remains. If an electric ﬁeld is applied in the opposite direction it will cause a small decrease
in polarization until a critical value called the coercive ﬁeld (Ec) is reached. At this point
the polarization changes sign abruptly. A plot of polarization as a function of applied ﬁeld is
called a polarization hysteresis loop. A schematic hysteresis loop of a ferroelectric is shown
in ﬁgure 1.
The polarization in ferroelectrics is non-linear in the electric ﬁeld, but for small sub-
coercive ﬁelds (E < Ec), the polarization is approximately linear and can be characterized
by a dielectric constant. The dielectric constants of ferroelectrics far exceed the dielectric
constant of most other materials and are therefore frequently used in capacitors even though
they do not utilize the ferroelectric polarization switching properties.
Ferroelectricity is typically seen in ionic solids where positively charged cations are dis-
placed relative to negatively charged anions. Ferroelectricity can thus only appear in crystals
with non-centrosymmetric unit cells and are fundamentally linked to speciﬁc crystal struc-
ture.
A common feature of ferroelectrics is a coupling between mechanical strain and polar-
ization. If a ferroelectric material is put under stress it can respond with a polarization.
This eﬀect, the piezoelectric eﬀect, is applied to to generate high voltages from a mechanical
stress to generate spark is lighters and gas stoves. Similarly, when the material is placed in
an electric ﬁeld, the material changes shape. This is characterized by the piezoelectric strain

4.1.4
Perovskite ferroelectrics
Many technologically important ferroelectric materials are metal oxide perovskites which is a
composition with a speciﬁc crystal structure. Perovskites have the chemical formula, ABO3,
where A and B are diﬀerent metal ions. The unit cell is approximately cubic with one ion
in the origin of the unit cell (the A-site), another positively charged ion in the unit cell cen-
ter (1/2, 1/2, 1/2) (B-site) and oxygen atoms occupying the three face-centered sites (1/2,
1/2, 0), (1/2, 0, 1/2) and (0, 1/2, 1/2) collectively often referred to as the oxygen octahedra.
In the real ferroelectric materials, the cubic symmetry (and therefore centrosymmetry) is
broken by a small distortion that makes the crystal symmetry either tetragonal, orthorhom-
bic or rhombohedral (or of even lower symmetry). The driving force for the ferroelectric
distortions is believed in many cases to be hybridization of oxygen and B-site ion orbitals
which works to shorten some of the B-O bond length and thereby breaks the cubic symme-
try. [11]

One widely used ferroelectric is barium titanate, BaTiO3 abbreviated BT. At low tem-

susceptibility diverges close to this temperature in a similar manner to that of ferromagnetic
materials. (In fact the name ferroelectric derives from ferromagnetism because of this close
correspondence even though ferroelectrics very rarely contain iron.)
The properties of these perovskite ferroelectrics can be tuned by doping with diﬀerent
perovskite materials making solid solutions. One important example is lead zirconate ti-
tanate (PZT) with the chemical formula Pb[ZrxTi1−x]O3, which has a large piezoelectric

4.1.5
Relaxor ferroelectrics
Another interesting type of electric behaviour is the one observed in the so-called relaxor
ferroelectrics. At elevated temperatures, relaxors behave much like the high temperature
paraelectric state of normal ferroelectrics. Upon cooling below the Burns temperature (Tb),
they transform into the erogodic relaxor state where microscopic regions of random polariza-
tion appear known as polar nanoregions (PNR), which is noticable by an increse in dielectric
constant. Close to the Burns temperature the PNRs are dynamic and will align with an
applied ﬁeld and they will return to an equilibrium conﬁguration with zero net polarization
when the ﬁeld is removed.
When the temperature decreases, the dynamics of the PNRs slow down, and at the freez-
ing temperature (Tf) the PNRs become frozen in and the relaxor enters a non-ergodic state
where a remnant polarization appears and a hysteresis loop similar to those of ferroelectric
materials appears.
The structure of relaxors has been described as a hopeless mess[14] and a number of
models of the structure exist. Both ones where the PRNs are small regions in a non-polar
matrix[10] and ones where the PNRs span the entire structure[15].
4.1.6
Microstructure in ferroelectrics
Ferroelectrics are synthesized either as single crystals, ﬁlms, or poly-crystalline ceramics. In
a single crystal, the crystal symmetry is consistent through the entire sample. In ceramics,
small grains of crystalline order are separated by grain boundaries, and the crystal lattices
of two grains separated by a grain boundary are rotated and displaced relative to each other
and thus there is no long range order beyond the length of a grain.

Ferroelectrics however rarely form large homogeneous single crystals. As the material
has a polarization ﬁxed relative to the crystal structure, a large single crystal would have a
large dipole moment, which is not energetically favourable. Therefore the crystal separates
into regions of diﬀerent polarization direction, called domains. Domain are separated by
domain walls.
Domain walls and grain boundaries are similar, as in they are interfaces
separating regions of diﬀerent crystal symmetry, but for domain walls in contrast to grain
boundaries, the crystal lattices in neighboring domains are related by speciﬁc rotations and
some kind of order persists across domain boundaries.

4.2.2
Absorption
When the energy of X-ray photons exceeds the binding energy of an electron in an atomic
orbital, it is possible for the photon to undergo photoelectric absorption. In this process the
incoming X-ray photon is annihilated and the energy goes into exciting a bound electron
from a lower lying orbital into a higher lying unoccupied orbital or into an unbound state.

4.2.3
Anomalous scattering
At energies close to the absorption edges of an atom, the atomic form factor is diﬀerent
from the Fourier transform of its electron density. This correction, called the anomalous
correction, is usually written as:
f(q, E) = f0(q) + f1(E) + if2(E)
(12)
The q dependence of the anomalous correction is usually ignored as it stems from core
electron orbitals that are small in real space and therefore is approximately constant out to
large wave numbers in reciprocal space.
The addition of an imaginary term to the otherwise real-valued form factor is signiﬁcant,
as it breaks the parity symmetry of the form factor known as Friedel’s law and allows for
diﬀerentiation between ferroelectric domains pointed in opposite directions.

4.3.1
Powder diﬀraction
Powder diﬀraction is a frequently utilized method for diﬀraction experiments, that is used
when the sample is not available as a large single crystal, when the system of interest is
inherently poly-crystalline, or simply because it is easier to carry out.
In powder-diﬀraction the sample contains small crystals with random orientation. This
removes the demand of alignment of the sample, as all possible crystal orientations are
already present in the sample. The combined conditions of energy conservation and the
reciprocal lattice then means that x-rays are scattered in cones around the incoming beam
with angles given by the Bragg equation:
2dhlk sin(θhlk) = λ
(16)
where θ is half the opening angle of the cone and d is the lattice spacing of the corre-
sponding lattice planes.
The scattered intensity is measured by scanning a point-detector over a range of angles
relative to the incoming beam or on a 2d pixel detector. On the 2d-detector the diﬀraction
pattern will be a number of rings around the direct peak which are then reduced to a 1d
spectrum by integrating over the azimuthal angle. In both cases one obtains a diﬀraction
proﬁle showing the observed intensity as a function of diﬀraction angle (see ﬁg. 10) with
characteristic peaks.
Typically a diﬀraction pattern is analyzed by calculating expected peak positions and
integrated intensities for a number of diﬀraction peaks based on an initial guess at the
crystal structure. The calculated intensities are compared with measured and the crystal

4.3.2
Texture in ferroelectrics
The form of equation (17) given in the previous section is true only for samples, where the
crystallographic orientations of grains are evenly distributed over all of orientation space.
In general this will not be true for ceramic materials due to preferred direction induced by
processing. For ferroelectrics in speciﬁc, a preferred orientation is induced by an applied
electric ﬁeld.
The distribution of crystal grains in orientation space is called the texture of the sample.
The intensity of a reﬂection is proportional to the volume of grains oriented with the [hkl]-
direction along the scattering vector. The texture can be measured by choosing a speciﬁc
crystallographic reﬂection and measuring the reﬂection intensity while the sample is rotated.
To reconstruct the full three dimensional texture of the sample, one has to measure the pole
density for two or more non-parallel crystal planes.
Furthermore, because of the piezoelectric eﬀect, the crystal structure of a ferroelectric
ceramic under an applied ﬁeld will depend on the orientation of the individual grain.
In powder diﬀraction experiments one measures simultaneously scattering from many
crystal grains.
For a given Bragg-reﬂection and detector-sample orientation only grains
with the [hkl]-direction parallel to the scattering vector diﬀract, but they can have any ori-
entation obtained by rotating around this crystal direction. Therefore grains with diﬀerent
orientation contribute to scattering in any given direction. If crystal structure varies with
orientation, (as could be the case under an applied ﬁeld) this would lead to a broadening of
reﬂections and complicated angular dependence of peak position and intensity.
For ferroelectrics it is reasonable to assume that the texture and crystal structure only
depends on the angle of the polar axis relative to the electric ﬁeld. If the experiment is
constructed so that the scattering vector is parallel to the applied ﬁeld all grains scattering
at a given reﬂection will have the same crystal structure.

5.5
Diﬀraction proﬁles non-periodic domain patters
The arrangement of the scattered intensity into superstructure peaks is a result of the exact
periodicity of the domain structures. If we allow a spread of domain lengths and construct
random 1D domain structures from these, the periodicity will be broken. It is unfortunately
not possible like before to obtain an exact result for the Φm coeﬃcients as these depend on
the domain sizes as well as the correlations of neighboring domains. Therefore we simulate
random structures by drawing domain lengths from a distribution given by a mean L1 and
a spread σ1. The domain structure is then constructed by placing these domains next to
each other and making the displacement continuous across domain boundaries.

Experiment
The experiment was conducted at ID22 at ESRF in june 2016 by Sem Gorfman from Tel
Aviv University, Hyeokmin Choe from University of Siegen, Jacob Jones from North Car-
olina State University Chris Fancher from Oak Ridge National Laboratory. Neither sample
synthesis or scattering experiments were conducted as a part of this masters thesis.
The investigated sample is a ceramic bar of (BaTi03)x−1(BiZn0.5Ti0.5O3)x of dimensions
1mm×1mm×10mm. The sample was made using conventional solid state reaction methods.
Powders of pure oxides were mixed and milled and then calcined at 950◦C.[21] The samples
used for scattering experiments, are painted with silver paste on two opposing surfaces used
as electrodes and placed in a transmission geometry with the two electrodes in the scattering
plane. The sample is rotated between each scan, to keep the electric ﬁeld parallel to the
scattering angle.
The experiment was conducted at the ID22 beamline at ESRF, which contains a high-
resolution powder diﬀraction instrument utilizing a monochromatic beam and Si 111 an-
alyzer crystals which results in an instrumental contribution to the FWHM of around
0.003◦.[43]

The experiment utilizes a custom built data acquisition system, which takes input from
photon counting detectors and bins the measured signal in ten thousand time bins, that are
synchronized to the applied electric ﬁeld.[44] This allows for a time resolved stroboscopic
data acquisition strategy, where photons are counted over many periods of the applied elec-
tric ﬁeld and binned according to their delay relative to a trigger synchronized with the
electric ﬁeld. Assuming that the structural response of the sample to the periodic ﬁeld is
also periodic, the binned counts are equivalent to the ones that would be collected during a
single cycle of electric loading but with much improved counting statistics.
The time-varying electric ﬁeld is generated with a signal generator and a high voltage
ampliﬁer. Due to experimental limitations the applied ﬁeld are not step-functions but have
a linear ramp-up of the electric ﬁeld.

7.2
Peak ﬁts
7.2.1
BT-0.08BZT
The corrected data is summed over the 36 identical scans and each time step is ﬁtted to a
peak function. The ﬁts are all made with the built in MATLAB function ”lsqcurvefit.m”.
The function minimizes the summed square diﬀerence between the measured points and a
ﬁtting object. The function uses a ”trust region reﬂective” method. [45]
Data is ﬁtted to a single asymmetric split pseudo-Voigt function with 6 free parameters
for the {111}- and {222}-scans and a triplet of symmetric Pseudo-Voigt functions with 4 free
parameters each for the {200}-scans. The initial guess for the parameters were manually
chosen for the ﬁrst time step but for all further time steps the converged parameters for the
previous time step was used.
The Voigt function ts a convolution of a Gaussian and a Lorenzian, which is the theoreti-
cally expected peak shape, when the peak width is due to a mix of Gaussian and Lorenztian
broadening. The numerical evaluation of such a convolution takes some computation time,
which in the age of slow computers was a problem for ﬁtting algorithms, therefore it has
become standard to use a Pseudo-Voigt, which is a sum of a Lorenzian and a Gaussian
with the same FWHM with relative intensities given by a shape parameter m ∈[0, 1] where
m = 0 is a Gaussian peak and m = 1 is a Lorenzian.
Parameters are constrained to be positive and the shape parameter m is constrained to
lie between 0 and 1. Furthermore for the {200}-scans the widths of the two side-peaks were

8
Results and Discussion
The behaviour of the diﬀraction pattern allows one to measure both the strain and polar-
ization of the material.
There are two ways strain appears in ferroelectric materials: Intrinsic strain is when
the lattice parameters of the crystal changes to allow for an increase of the polarization.
Extrinsic polarization is when domain walls move and domains aligned with the electric ﬁeld
grow at the cost of domains oriented diﬀerently.[20]
The intrinsic strain is evidenced by a shift of diﬀraction peak position. The peak-position
gives an apparent lattice parameter by d = λ/2 sin(θ) from which the strain can be calcu-
lated. The extrinsic strain can be seen by changes of peak intensity for split peaks eg. by a
shift of intensity from the (200) peak to the (002) peak.
In a similar way the polarization is inﬂuenced by changes of twin fractions (polarization
rotation) and changes to the local crystal structure, speciﬁcally displacement of the posi-
tively charged ions relative to negatively charged ions. (polarization extension)
In contrast to extrinsic strain, polarization is inﬂuenced not only by 90◦rotations, but
also 180◦polarization rotations, that are not associated with a strain and are more diﬃ-
cult to observe as diﬀerences between the scattered intensities from domain diﬀerent by a
180◦rotation are only due to anomalous scattering. Extension of polarization can be esti-
mated by ﬁtting the diﬀraction pattern to a structure model, but the texture induced by
the electric poling makes this treatment diﬃcult in general.
As previously observed in [24] and described in section 5, the diﬀraction patterns of the
samples in the unpoled phase are strongly inﬂuenced by the micro structure. Therefore one
has to be careful with the interpretation of the {200} reﬂection, which for all measurements
appears to be a triplet of peaks. There are two way to interpret the data:
• Two Phase model: Diﬀraction from diﬀerent domains add up incoherently. The
two side peak are the (200)- and (002)- peaks of a tetragonal phase, while the central
peak is due to a pseudocubic phase.
• Small domains: The whole diﬀraction pattern is the result of coherent diﬀraction
from a phase of nanometer sized tetragonal domains. The central peak comes from
diﬀraction from the average structure while the two side peaks are due to the local
structure.
We should note that the diﬀraction patterns reported in previous studies[21, 22, 24] do
not have the same shape as the ”zero ﬁeld” diﬀraction patterns in this experiment. While
the peak positions are similar, the side peaks have much larger intensity in the measure-
ments of the unpoled ceramic than for the measurements here at zero electric ﬁeld. This
can possibly be an eﬀect of the high frequency cycling, the high intensity x-ray beam or
a combination of both that have led to smaller domains than in the unpoled sample. The
same eﬀect has been observed in a study where the frequency dependence of the material
45

was investigated and the behaviour was attributed to the dynamics of growth and nucleation
of tetragonal domain. [26]

8.2
Strain
Figure 37 shows the strain calculated for the BT-0.08BZT sample. Both strains are strain
in the direction of the applied ﬁeld but for two diﬀerent grain orientations. Grains with
diﬀerent orientation relative to the electric ﬁeld strain diﬀerently, as they have diﬀerent
driving forces for straining. In the grains probed by the {200} reﬂection, one twin variant
has it’s polarization parallel to the electric ﬁeld while the two other variant have polariza-
tion orthogonal to the electric ﬁeld. Therefore the applied ﬁeld gives the largest possible
energetic advantage for domains to undergo 90◦rotations which gives extrinsic strain. In the
{111}-grains, all domains have an equal angle relative to the electric ﬁeld, therefore there
can be no extrinsic strain in the direction of the applied ﬁeld, and the strain is only a result
of polarization extension and elastic forces imposed by neighboring grains.

Summary and Outlook
This thesis has investigated the diﬀraction from ferroelectrics and looked at how microstruc-
ture inﬂuences the diﬀraction pattern and how the diﬀraction pattern can be used to calcu-
late structural parameters.
I have calculated the scattering proﬁles for a model of a ferroelectric with small tetrag-
onal domains and shown how for small domains, the diﬀraction pattern reﬂects the average
structure over several domains while for large domains, the diﬀraction pattern reﬂects the
local structure within one domain.
The results show, that relatively small diﬀerences in domain lengths can have a sig-
niﬁcant eﬀect on the shape of the diﬀraction pattern and therefore that the splitting of
characteristic diﬀraction peaks do not necessarily imply long range symmetry breaking but
occur continuously when correlation lengths increase beyond a critical length not much
longer than the length typically suggested for PNRs. For a range of domains sizes local and
domain averaged diﬀraction phenomena can coexist which leads to diﬀraction patterns, that
resemble the diﬀraction from a sample with diﬀerent coexisting phases.
I derive a number of simple equations to extract physical parameters from the diﬀraction
pattern and apply them to real experimental data. The data stems from a time resolved
scattering experiment of samples of the solid solution BT-BZT, that has a transition from
ferroelectric to relaxor behaviour as a function of composition. BT-BZT compositions close
to this phase transition have previously been shown to display diﬀraction patterns that are
inﬂuenced by microstructure.
The new microstructure-based interpretation give reasonable physical parameters when
applied to the experimental data, which highlights an ambiguity in the interpretation of
diﬀraction data from certain ferroelectric materials that has been discussed in the literature
for the past ﬁfteen years. Where diﬀraction pattern can either be explained by a new phase
or a complicated microstructure. The question is important because it suggests two diﬀerent
mechanisms for polarization, where the electrical response is either due to a global change
in crystal structure or by movement of domain boundaries.
The determination of microstructure is complicated because microscopy methods with
real space resolution are either limited to surfaces or very thin samples. X-ray diﬀraction
microscopy can access buried grains but have too poor resolution to resolve the small PNRs
assumed to be present in these types of materials. Single crystal studies can be made, but it
is diﬃcult to say how well results from single crystals can be generalized to polycrystalline
samples where a range of inter-grain interactions are present. It is becoming clear that it is
necessary to develop a methods of interpreting powder diﬀraction patterns, that takes into
account microstructure. New methods being developed either take the path of calculating
anisotropic PDFs or by ﬁtting a large model of correlated atomic displacements.

Interpreting in-situ x-ray diﬀraction data from ferroelectrics with nanometer-sized
twin domains.∗
Mads Carlsen† and Hugh Simons‡
Authors’ institution and/or address
This line break forced with \\
(Dated: August 1, 2018)
Nanostructured materials with nanometer sized twin domains may give rise diﬀraction phenomena
where a mix of diﬀraction from the average and local crystal structures are seen. Currently many
studies eﬀectively ignore the eﬀects of nanostructure and ﬁt a single structure model with Rietveld
reﬁnement incorporating anisotropic broadening and large Debye-Waller corrections.
Here we investigate the diﬀraction from a simple model of a nanostructured material with varying
phase fraction, domain size, and periodicity. We derive key expressions, which allow us estimate
changes to volume fractions and domain sizes, and apply them to experimental data.
We point out that there is an ambiguity in the interpretation of diﬀraction data from materials with
nanometer sized twin domains as a given diﬀraction pattern may stem from either coexisting phases
of diﬀerent symmetry or a mix of average structure and local structure diﬀraction which in turn
suggest completely diﬀerent mechanisms for polarization switching.
The understanding of x-ray diﬀraction patterns is important as nanostructured materials are abun-
dant and their response to external ﬁeld cannot readily be studies in situ by any other methods.
I.
INTRODUCTION
Nanometer sized twin domains occur in a range
of ferroelectric and ferroelastic materials.
A correct
description of the micro structure is important to
understand the function of these materials.
The structure of many nanostructured materials is a
topic of signiﬁcant debate in the scientiﬁc literature. One
example is the Morphotropic Phase Boundary (MPB) in
complex perovskite ferroelectrics close to the transition
between tetragonal and rhomohedral symmetry.
The
existence of intermediate phases is being suggested [1–3]
while others suggest that the experimental observations
are a result of the miniaturization of twin domains.
[4, 5] In relaxor ferroelectrics so called Polar Nano
Regions (PNRs) are nanometer sized domains with a
local structure and polarization diﬀerent from the long
range average.
[6] A ﬁeld induced phase transition is
sometimes observed in these materials the mechanisms
of which are not well understood. [7]
X-ray diﬀraction is a common and useful method for
investigation of these materials and allow for in-situ
investigation of the response to external perturbations.
Analysis of diﬀraction patters is complicated by the
nanostructure. [8] Many studies eﬀectively ignore these
complications and apply a single structure Rietveld
reﬁnement procedure to obtain a crystal structure from
their diﬀraction data.
∗A footnote to the article title
† madsac@fysik.dtu.dk
‡ husimo@fysik.dtu.dk
Methods sensitive to the short range structure often
report structures diﬀerent to the ones obtained from
x-ray diﬀraction experiments.
Electron diﬀraction in
electron microscopes have high spatial resolution due to
focusing of the electron beam and can directly observe
the domain structure and the local crystal structure
albeit with large restrictions on the available samples.
[9] Diﬀuse scattering PDF methods are sensitive to
the local structure but often don’t reach the long
enough ranges to see the domain sizes and analysis
of PDF data from in-situ experiments is diﬃcult be-
cause a convolution of eﬀects from grains oriented at
diﬀerent angles to the applied ﬁelds are seen at once. [10]
It is well established in the literature, that x-ray
diﬀraction gives the long-range average structure when
the size of domains become smaller than some length
but the critical length scale and the behaviour at inter-
mediate length scales is not well established. Commonly
diﬀraction data is treated by Rietveld reﬁnement with
low-symmetry phases or multiple phase models with
overlapping peaks when the eﬀect of micro structure
could equally well explain the observed data. Previously
diﬀraction from ordered striped domains has been
treated theoretically. [11, 12]
Here we present a more general treatment that allows
for calculation of both the twin volume fractions and the
size twin domains. Importantly, we emphasize that in the
common case of intermediate domain sizes, combinations
of ”average structure” and ”local structure” diﬀraction
contribute to the observed diﬀraction patterns.
This
opens up for a new way to interpret diﬀraction data in a
manner that better accommodates the range of sizes and
disorders in materials with nanoscale domain structures
and dynamics.

APPLICATION ON EXPERIMENTAL DATA
Here we apply the results of the investigation in
previous sections to in-situ X-ray diﬀraction data from a
relaxor ferroelectrix, which apparently displays an ﬁeld

to cubic symmetry is observed with x-ray diﬀraction

sample of BT-8BTZ is put under a varying electric ﬁeld.
The {200}-reﬂection shows triplet splitting at all
times. When there is no electric ﬁeld, the central peak is
dominant (>90% of intensity). When the ﬁeld exceeds
a given value, the two side peaks grow abruptly in
intensity and the position of the ”tetragonal c”-peak
changes. Changes in the ”tetragonal a” peak lags behind
the changes in the two other.
With no applied ﬁeld
the calculated phase fractions (ﬁg 7) are close to 1/3
and 2/3 which is expected for an unpoled sample. The
tetragonality ratio is c/a ≈6 · 10−3 which is close to
what has been reported previously.

DISCUSSION
For twin domains in a range of sizes given approxi-
mately by equations (8) and (9) a diﬀraction phenomena
will be observed that can be explained neither by the
diﬀraction proﬁles of the pure local structure or the
long-range average structure but may instead look like
the sum of both structures which would also be observed
for a sample containing several coexisting phases.

diﬀraction peaks coexist with signiﬁcant intensity, of less
