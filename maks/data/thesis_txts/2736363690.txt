Abstract
In our technologically driven world, where the boundaries between physical and
digital realms continue to blur, the significance of cybersecurity cannot be over­
stated. The rapid evolution of technology, coupled with our increasing reliance
on digital infrastructure, has ushered in an era where the human factor plays a
pivotal role in cybersecurity. This study delves into the core reasons behind se­
curity failures in Denmark, particularly focusing on the human element. Through
a meticulous examination of human behaviors, cognitive processes, and vulnera­
bilities, this research sheds light on the intricate interplay between human factors
and cybersecurity risks. The study employs a mixed­methods approach, combin­
ing qualitative narrative interviews and quantitative surveys to gather comprehen­
sive insights. By exploring firsthand accounts of individuals who have fallen victim
to cyberattacks, we unravel the psychological nuances and decision­making pro­
cesses underlying security breaches. Additionally, a cross­sectional survey of
diverse participants in Denmark provides quantitative data to validate hypotheses
regarding demographic, technical, and psychological factors influencing suscep­
tibility to cyber threats. Furthermore, individual factors such as age, education,
and psychological state significantly impact vulnerability to cyberattacks. This un­
derscores the critical need for tailored cybersecurity education, awareness cam­
paigns, and policy interventions to address the unique challenges faced by Danish
users. Moving forward, this study serves as a foundational step in understanding
human errors in cybersecurity.

Introduction
Technology. The ubiquitous term that defines our modern era has seamlessly
embedded itself into almost every aspect of the human life. From revolutionizing
healthcare and education to transforming banking systems, the influence of tech­
nology will never fade away. The rapid pace in which the advancement of the
cyber world, technology, and the internet is growing is both fascinating and dan­
gerous. Because regardless of its vast breakthroughs, brilliance, and innovation,
a concerning aspect emerges where our increasing reliance on technology has
propelled us into uncharted territories where the pace of development often out­
strips our understanding of its risks. As the technological advancements continue
to develop, it is crucial to acknowledge the gap between the increased human
dependence on these innovations and the ability to comprehend the dangers that
come with them. This gap between technological progress and user awareness
asserts a pressing need for enhanced education, user awareness, and security
measures in the human sector in order to avoid the plethora of threats that come
with the world­wide communication system.
Cybersecurity consists of a variety of dimensions such as information security, hu­
man factors, computer security, network security and more. While these dimen­
tions primarily focus on perfecting techniques to defend against cyber threats,
they often overlook the crucial human factor that is just as important since this
factor is considered to be the weakest link in cyber security and a main target for
attackers.[1]
1.1
Problem
Human errors are influenced by behaviors and actions and can easily be an ex­
posed target for an attacker and may also promote insecure connections and com­
promise the integrity of digital systems risking the primary principles of information
security. Ignoring the human element in cyber security not only undermines the
effectiveness of technical defenses but it also exposes vulnerabilities that can be
effortlessly exploited by malicious actors.[1]. Furthermore, even with top­notch
security measures in place, a single human error can render you vulnerable to
a cyber attack. While your credit card details may not be physically stolen, you
could inadvertently divulge them to a scammer posing as your trusted bank. This
underscores the critical importance of bolstering security against human vulner­
abilities and identifying psychological loopholes that could be exploited by cyber
attackers. Social engineering cyberattacks are sophisticated and often targeted
directly at specific groups of individuals. These attacks leverage psychological
manipulation to exploit weaknesses in human cognitive functions. Despite Den­
mark’s reputation as one of the most cyber­secure nations globally and the most
digitized in Europe, many users still lack basic security practices and awareness
of cyber threats [2].

1.2
Constributions
The objective of this thesis is to enhance current approaches to understanding
the reasons behind security failures attributable to human behavior, with a spe­
cific emphasis on users in Denmark. Additionally, this study seeks to illuminate
aspects such as human cognitive behaviors, habits, hacker techniques exploit­
ing psychological vulnerabilities, and the role of certain behaviors in facilitating
cyberattacks. It also aims to explore the association between cyberattacks and
various factors affecting their success, such as individual weaknesses, technical
or educational background, and psychological states during attacks. The central
focus lies on the human factor, which often receives less attention than technolog­
ical aspects in cybersecurity. In the information flow process, users play a critical
role and can inadvertently expose businesses to numerous cyber threats [1]. The
thesis aims to answer the follwoing research questions:
• Q1 What are the primary reasons behind security failures attributed to hu­
man behavior in the context of cybersecurity?
• Q2 How do cognitive behaviors and habits of users contribute to their vul­
nerability to cyberattacks?
• Q3 What techniques do hackers commonly employ to exploit psychological
vulnerabilities in users?
• Q4 How do individual factors such as technical background, educational
experience, and psychological state influence the success of cyberattacks?
• Q5 What are security risks imposed by the human factors?

Methodology
To begin gathering data within the Danish population, I delved into cyber security
through several approaches. First, I immersed myself in a range of literature,
spanning both popular and academic sources. These materials covered general
insights on social engineering attacks as well as specific data on human factors
and errors in cyber security. This initial research provided crucial insights, laying
the foundation for a detailed study spotlighting security shortcoming, particularly
within Denmark’s cyber security landscape. In addituion, this study employs a
mixed­methods approach, incorporating both quantitative analysis and qualitative
data collection through in­depth interviews.
2.1
Qualitative Approach:
The qualitative approach aims to delve deeply into individuals’ experiences with
previous encounters of social engineering attacks. By exploring their emotions,
behaviors, and the underlying reasons behind their decisions, the goal is to gain
comprehensive insights into why individuals made choices that ultimately resulted
in successful attacks against them.
To achieve this approach, a combination of a semi­structured interview and a nar­
rative interview was implemented. Narrative interviewing is a qualitative research
method that is used to learn about the interviewees past experiences related to
the topic being studied.
This data collection method is especially suitable for
person­centered investigations into everyday information behaviors [3]. Narra­
tive interviewing promotes storytelling, prompting interviewees to recount events
from their perspective, using their own language and terms Where narrative in­
terviews are used to collect people’s own stories about their experiences in order
to help researchers to understand people’s experiences and behaviours by ask­
ing them open ended questions regarding a certain phenomenon.[4]. To ensure
effective data collection, semi­structured interviews will complement the narrative
approach. Semi­structured interviews involve a predefined set of questions or
topics, adding structure to the interview process. This method enables focused
discussions while also allowing for exploration of related topics that emerge during
the conversation [5].
The interview begins by inviting the interviewee to share their firsthand account
of the cyber attack they encountered. Additionally, a carefully crafted set of ques­
tions is employed to ensure that the study captures all the necessary information
required.

3.1
Overview of Social engineering attacks and its
classifications
In the pool of cybercrime, social engineering attacks are the most common by
attackers. This type of attacks involves manipulating individuals into divulging
personal or confidential information from humans operating the internet­based de­
vice. Thus, unlike technical cyber attacks, that targets the system’s vulnerabilities,
social engineering attacks target the weakest link, the human factor.
The consequences of falling victim to social engineering attacks can be significant.
They can result in financial losses, data breaches, service disruptions, and harm
to an organization’s reputation. Sometimes, these attacks can persist for months
or even years before causing any notable damage [7].
Social engineering attacks revolve around understanding how people think, react,
and behave. Once attackers gather enough information about their victims, they
can easily manipulate them. Interestingly, most social engineering scams rely on
direct communication rather than technical methods like brute force attacks [8].
Many studies classify social engineering attacks into four stages: information
gathering, relationship development, exploitation, and execution or exit, as de­
picted in Figure 3.1 [9]. While [8] follows similar phases, it uses different terminol­
ogy: investigation, hook, play, and exit. Meanwhile, [10] combines both sets of
terms.

6
==PAGE:14==
Figure 3.1: Social Engineering attacks stages [9]
The information gathering phase involves the attacker conducting extensive re­
search on specific victims or groups, utilizing various resources like websites, pub­
lic records, and social media [9]. This stage is crucial as it allows the attacker to
identify potential vulnerabilities in the target’s system and select an appropriate
attack strategy [11].
During the relationship development or hooking phase, the attacker initiates
contact through methods like vishing, phishing emails, or malware attacks. Once
access is gained, the attacker focuses on building a rapport and gaining the vic­
tim’s trust [10].
In the Exploitation phase, the attacker will use manipulation and persuasion tech­
niques to force the victim to provide sensitive information or perform security mis­
takes [10], or create a security lapse which the attacker will use to execute the
attack [11].
The final stage, execution or exit, involves the attacker carrying out the attack
and withdrawing before the victim becomes aware of the breach [9].
According to [10] and [11], attacks are primarily classified as either human­based
or technical­based. Human­based attacks occur in person, such as shoulder surf­
ing, where the attacker must be physically close to the target [11]. Another exam­
ple is baiting, where a USB device may be left in a public area to entice curious
individuals to plug it into their devices, unknowingly exposing them to malware or
viruses [10]. However, [10] further refines this classification into three categories:
technical­based, social­based, and physical based. Technical attacks involve us­
ing modern tools like a USB drive, while social attacks rely on psychological ma­
nipulation, such as persuasion and coercion, to compel the victim to comply with
the attacker’s demands. Physical based attacks, require direct contact with the
victim.
[11] introduces another important classification called socio­technical based at­
tacks, which integrate both technical and social elements, combining technology
with psychological manipulation. This research focuses primarily on social­based
and socio­technical based attacks. The latter delves into sub­methods of attack:

3.1.1
In­Person Interaction
This attack type relies on establishing a trust relationship between the attacker and
the victim through various forms of contact. The primary goal is to gain the victim’s
trust, often through impersonation, where the attacker pretends to be someone
else to gain access or persuade the target to willingly disclose information [11].
Sub­methods of this attack type include quid pro quo, pretexting, tailgating, and
Reverse Social Engineering (RSE).
• Quid Pro Quo: This tactic involves offering free services or assistance in
exchange for the target’s action or information [12]. For instance, scammers
posing as IT or customer support personnel may call employees, claim to
identify an issue with their system, and offer to resolve it remotely. However,
to proceed, they often require the target to disclose sensitive information
such as passwords and credentials [8] [13].
• Pretexting: While pretexting is similar to phishing, it requires alot of re­
search on the attackers part, where the latter develops a story that would
convince the victim and justify their need of certain information, for example,
posing as a government member and claiming that the user has a problem
with their cpr number or Mit­ID (government digital authenticating system)
and offering to fix them while gaining their trust to have a full access of their
banks and governmental websites. [8] mentions that this attack can also be
used on organizations by posing as a CEO assistant from a certain branch
and would have the correct terminology and go to another branch demand­
ing classified information, some may even present a fake badge or claim to
know someone from the employees.
• Tailgating: Also known as piggybacking, is a physical method that doesn’t
rely on online communication like emails or calls. Instead, the attacker pa­
tiently waits near a person with legitimate access to a secured area and
exploits their proximity. For example, they may hold doors open for others
or discreetly slip an infected USB drive into someone’s pocket or computer
without their knowledge or consent [8] [11].
• Reverse Social Engineering: In this method, the victim is approached by
the attacker who aims to appear as a trustworthy entity with the goal of pre­
senting authority where the victim would actually seek help from the attacker.
it usually involves three main steps: sabotage, advertising and assisting.
Where first the attacker would disrupt the company’s computer system, sub­
sequently, the attacker will advertise their ability to resolve the isssue they
caused. Lastly when the victim seeks help, the social engineer(attacker)
fixes the problem created by them in the first place while requesting the vic­
tim’s password or downloading something malicious while fixing the problem
[14].
3.1.2
Communication Based Attacks
Phishing is a deceitful strategy where the attacker pretends to be someone else
to trick the victim into revealing personal information. They use various methods,

such as scams or indirect approaches, to obtain confidential details from the vic­
tim [15]. It often entails contacting recipients via calls, messages, or emails that
appear authentic, aiming to obtain personal information like credit card details or
passwords, sometimes by directing victims to fake webpages mimicking legitimate
banks. Email phishing can be subcategorized into clone­phishing, spear­phishing,
whaling, Vishing, SMiShing (SMS Phishing), and Baiting.
• Clone phishing: Clone phishing involves duplicating an email/message
that appears to be legitimate but contains fake links leading to malicious
websites. The goal is to trick the victim into believing the request is gen­
uine, potentially leading them to disclose private information.[11]
• Spear­phishing: a highly personalized form of phishing, targets specific
individuals with tailored messages. It’s challenging to detect and prevent
due to its sophistication and the extensive research required by the attacker
to gather detailed information about the target [16].
• Whaling: This type is a kind of spear phishing however, it targets high pro­
file employees and CEOs in order to gain information about a company not
an individual and the higher the position the more sensitive the obtained
information will be [17].
• Vishing: is when an attack uses a phone conversation in order to take fi­
nancial or personal information from the target [11]. It is basically a spam
where for example a caller will tell the target that they won a trip and will
need to provide information in order to get the prize. Moreover, a common
example is the hacker mimicking a Microsoft employee and will call the vic­
tim claiming that their computer is infected and will offer them guidelines or
send them a link to download the solution that is in fact a Malware. On an
organizational level, if this occurs to an employee the whole company would
be at risk of the attack [8].
• SMiShing (SMS Phishing): This attack involves sending fraudulent mes­
sages and texts via cellphones to victims to manipulate and influence their
actions [11]. While similar to phishing attacks, SMS phishing, or SMiShing,
is more efficient as people are less accustomed to receiving spam messages
on their phones compared to emails. However, the consequences can be
more severe, as attackers can gain access to various personal data stored
on their phone, including calendar, contacts, messages, photos, and notes.
This heightened risk occurs when attackers download a root kit to control
the phone [10].
• Baiting: Baiting involves sending emails containing links that entice recipi­
ents to click on them for a reward or free item [10].

Website­based attacks encompass various strategies aimed at deceiving users
and stealing their information. One common method is phishing, where attack­
ers create fake websites resembling legitimate ones to harvest sensitive data like
passwords or credit card numbers [11]. These fraudulent sites are often nearly

3.2
Human Role and Cyber Security
3.2.1
Importance of The Human Factor
The landscape of digital development and innovation in information sciences doesn’t
always guarantee high levels of security and privacy, contrary to popular belief.
Human involvement plays a significant role in the failure to protect systems, ser­
vices, information, and organizations [19]. Even the most sophisticated security
measures can be bypassed by intelligent adversaries who exploit human vulner­
abilities rather than technical weaknesses [20]. the latter also mentions how an
attacker use social engineering techniques in order to manipulate individuals into
performing actions or divulging sensitive information.
In addition to the cybersecurity vulnerabilities related to users such as risky be­
havior, lack of motivation, etc., it is important to mention those related to man­
agement like workload and inadequate staffing. While some cyber­attacks may
have minimal impacts, those targeting critical infrastructure can have significant
consequences on national security, the economy, and the safety of individuals
[19].
Moreover, attackers may take advantage of the lack of awareness or technical
skills among operators and users of Industrial Control Systems (ICS). This lack of
Why Security Fails
11
==PAGE:19==
awareness can lead individuals to overlook suspicious activities or fail to grasp the
potential consequences of their actions, inadvertently aiding attackers in achieving
their objectives [20]. Numerous international reports on cyber­attacks consistently
emphasize that human actions are the primary issue.
Furthermore, attackers may exploit the lack of awareness or technical skills among
ICS operators and users. These individuals may not recognize suspicious activ­
ities or understand the potential consequences of their actions. As a result, they
may unwittingly facilitate the attackers’ objectives [20].
It’s evident that the primary issue lies in human factors. Even the most sophisti­
cated cyber­attacks often exploit human vulnerabilities. Whether due to curiosity,
distraction, or stress, individuals may make actions without understanding the po­
tential risks. Similarly, they might inadvertently disclose sensitive information on
social media platforms, oblivious to the dangers of exploitation by cybercriminals
[21]. Despite the implementation of security technologies, many organizations re­
main vulnerable to cyberattacks [22]. Since people operate computers and other
interconnected devices, ensuring the security of these devices and environments
necessitates considering human actions [23]. To delve deeper into the signifi­
cance of the human factor, the following section provides a detailed exploration
of the security risks it introduces.
3.2.2
Human Error Types
According to [24], Human errors in cybersecurity can be classified into two main
categories: skill­based and decision­based errors. Skill­based errors typically in­
volve minor mistakes made during routine tasks, often due to factors like inatten­
tiveness, fatigue, or distractions. In contrast, decision­based errors occur when
users make flawed decisions, often stemming from a lack of understanding, ex­
pertise, or information about a particular situation.
Moreover [25] provided the same classification where the article classified human
errors to 2 categories based on the cognitive processes involved and whether
they relate to planning or executing an activity. Stating that errors stem from either
inadequate plans or deficient performance.
In the first category, it was mentioned that errors occur when individuals aim to
execute an action but either perform it incorrectly or fail to achieve the desired
outcome due to execution failures. These errors, referred to as Slips and Lapses,
manifest during the execution or storage stage of an action sequence. Slips in­
volve observable actions often linked to attentional or perceptual lapses, while

3.2.7
Risk Perception
Risk perception plays a crucial role in how individuals make decisions, especially
in the realm of information technology (IT) where threats are prevalent. However,
people often rely on shortcuts, known as biases and heuristics, to simplify their
decision­making process due to the impracticality of processing all available in­
formation. Consequently, individuals may have inaccurate perceptions of risk,
which can hinder logical decision­making. Understanding these perceptions is
essential as accurate risk perception leads to appropriate actions. Various factors
influence perceptions of security risks, including mood, recent media reports, past
experiences, technical knowledge, psychological, social, and cultural factors. Ad­
ditionally, whether a risk is voluntary, controllable, and its implications also play
significant roles [38]. [38] highlighted several factors that influences individual’s
perception of security risks including:
1. Availability Heuristic: The availability heuristic, as described by Tversky and
Kahneman in 1973, suggests that our judgment of how likely an event is can
be swayed by how easily we can recall instances of it [40]. For example, if
we hear a lot about hacking in the news, we might think it’s more common
than it actually is. But just because something gets a lot of media coverage
doesn’t mean it’s the most likely thing to happen.
2. Optimism Bias: refers to an individual’s belief that they are less likely than
others to experience negative outcomes. This type of cognitive bias can lead
people to perceive themselves as more immune or invulnerable to various
risks compared to others, which in turn can influence their behaviors and
decision­making processes.[41]
3. Level of Control: When people feel like they have control over something,
like their actions on their personal computer, they tend to underestimate the
risks associated with it. This means they might not think following security
rules is important because they don’t see the potential consequences as
serious. This attitude can lead to riskier behavior. This is similar to how
some drivers feel overly confident when they’re in control of a vehicle, even
though accidents can still happen.
4. Level of Knowledge: Not understanding information security risks can also
affect how people perceive them. If someone doesn’t know much about a
risk, they might not realize how serious it is. This lack of knowledge can lead
to poor decision­making when it comes to security measures. For example,
many people don’t know what makes a password secure, so they choose
weak passwords without realizing the risks.
5. Omission Bias: People often think it’s more acceptable to do nothing (omit)
than to take action when it comes to security breaches. So, they might ignore
security procedures, thinking it’s less risky than actively doing something
wrong.
6. Farming: How a risk is described can change how people perceive it. For
example, people might take a risk if they think about the potential gains, but
they might avoid it if they focus on possible losses.

7. Personality and Cognitive Style: People’s personalities and thinking styles
also affect how they perceive and handle risks. Some people are more
willing to take risks, while others are more cautious.
8. The Influence of Social Factors: People’s behavior is influenced by group
norms, so if everyone in a group takes security seriously, individuals are
more likely to follow security rules. Conversely, if risk­taking is common in a
group, individuals may take more risks.
Given that security breaches are infrequent, the availability heuristic often leads to
an underestimation of risk. Users are more likely to remember instances where in­

leading them to underestimate the actual risk of not following proper procedures.
Each time an individual breaches security without consequences, this risky be­

7.1
Interviews Insights
7.1.1
Interview 1
Participant A’s experience highlights the susceptibility of individuals with lower
levels of education to social engineering scams. Despite receiving a suspicious
SMS, he believed it was related to a recent update and followed the instructions,
leading to a significant financial loss. This case underscores the importance of tar­
geted cybersecurity education for vulnerable populations, emphasizing the need
to teach basic security practices and promote scepticism towards unsolicited mes­
sages or requests. Additionally, the limited recourse available to him after the
scam reflects gaps in support systems for scam victims, indicating a need for im­
proved reporting mechanisms and assistance programs.
7.1.2
Interview 2
Participant B’s encounters with scams illustrate the psychological tactics employed
by scammers to exploit individuals during moments of vulnerability or distraction.
In both instances, she fell victim to urgency and pressure, leading her to com­
ply with instructions without questioning their legitimacy. Despite her suspicions
during the second scam, the caller’s apparent professionalism and provision of
personal information heightened her anxiety and contributed to her eventual loss.
This case underscores the importance of enhancing awareness of common scam
tactics and fostering critical thinking skills to empower individuals to recognize and
resist manipulation. Additionally, the prolonged emotional impact of scams on vic­
tims, as evidenced by Participant B’s anxiety and distrust, highlights the need for
comprehensive support services to address psychological consequences.
7.1.3
Interview 3
Participant C’s experience highlights the prevalence of scams targeting routine
financial transactions, such as bill payments, and the ease with which individuals
can fall victim to familiar scenarios. Despite recognizing the scam after the fact,
Participant C chose not to report it due to the relatively small amount involved,
reflecting a common reluctance among victims to come forward. This case em­
phasizes the importance of encouraging reporting even for minor incidents, as
it helps authorities track and respond to emerging scam trends. Additionally, it
underscores the need for ongoing awareness campaigns to remind individuals
to remain vigilant and verify the authenticity of requests, especially when dealing
with financial transactions online.

Open ended Survey Answer
Participant D’s account of someone they know falling victim to a scam on Face­

ability in online transactions. The victim’s eagerness to complete the sale of her
furniture likely stemmed from a combination of factors, including the perceived

7.2
Survey Conclusions
The survey included 109 participants, a number sufficient to draw meaningful con­
clusions.
The supplementary survey was conducted to explore potential correlations be­
tween demographic factors and the number of individuals who were successfully
attacked in scams, as well as their confidence levels. This survey included 59
participants, a smaller sample size due to the limited time available and the late
realization of the need for this additional data collection. Although the number of
participants in the supplementary survey was relatively low, it still provides valu­
able preliminary insights into the research topics and hypotheses initially posited.

Key Results
The experimental analysis in the previous chapter highlighted 8 important results
that will be further discussed below.
Result 0 in section [6.1.1] concluded an important limitation of this study con­
cerns the demographics of participants.
Specifically, all participants surveyed
were younger than 44 years old, which precluded comparisons between older

The findings in Results 1, 2, 3, 4 in section [6.1.2], were aggregated due to their
common thematic focus. A mixed­methods approach, employing graphical rep­
resentations, was utilized to compare various educational fields with regard to
technical habits. These results collectively indicate that individuals with IT back­

Furthermore, it was observed that not all technical habits receive equal attention.
For instance, Result 4 revealed that a majority of individuals across all educational
backgrounds rarely update their passwords. In contrast, Result 3 demonstrated a

Result 5 in section [6.1.3] highlights the correlation between the type of device
used and the prevalent methods of cyber attacks encountered. The data reveals
distinct patterns in attack vectors based on the device utilized by the target.
The findings reveal that smartphone users were predominantly targeted through

a higher frequency of attacks via email and fraudulent websites. This aligns with
the conclusions drawn by [43] who similarly stated that email, text messages, and
phone calls as the primary communication channels for receiving scams.
The insights provided by [44] further supports the earlier finding regarding cy­
ber security vulnerabilities associated with habitual patterns. [44] discusses how
habitual behaviors, such as those related to social media usage, can lead to in­
creased susceptibility to phishing attacks. for example, individuals with strong
Facebook habits are more prone to accepting friend requests from fraudulent pro­
files and sharing personal information. Therefore validating hypothesis H5 [2.2]
(Psychological factors affect decision making).
Result 6 in section [6.1.4] The study uncovered a significant prevalence of vic­
tims who experienced a sense of urgency to respond to the scam when contacted
through their smartphones. This highlights the impact of technology, especially
smartphones, on people’s actions and habits. Where [44] explores the difference
between habits and cognitive processing in human behavior and their impact on
vulnerability to deception, like falling for phishing scams. Habits are deeply in­
grained over time, and are seen as stable aspects of our personality and self­
regulation. They’re the routines we repeat regularly, like checking messages on
our smartphones every morning. In contrast, cognitive processing depends on
immediate factors, like how important a decision feels or how much information
we have at the moment.It further states that both habits and cognitive process­
ing can make people vulnerable to deception. And in this insight, the individual
is susceptible to attacks due to both cognitive processing and habits. This re­
sult suggests that individuals with instant access to communication tools feel a
sense of responsibility and urgency to act because of built habits. Additionally,
it raises the psychological consideration that the urgency to respond may stem
from heightened stress and anxiety when having immediate access to a commu­
nication device. Consequently, how individuals cope with emergencies can either
alleviate or exacerbate stress and panic.

1. Modern scams have become highly sophisticated, often replicating legiti­

7.3
Recommendations and future works
Moving forward, this study serves as a foundational step towards understanding
the underlying causes of security failures in Denmark, particularly concerning the
human factor. While the insights gained from the survey and interviews offer valu­
able insights, there remains ample room for future exploration and expansion.
To enhance the robustness and generalizability of the findings, future studies
could be conducted on a larger scale, encompassing a more diverse range of
participants and scenarios. This would allow for a more comprehensive under­
standing of the nuanced factors contributing to security vulnerabilities and the
effectiveness of current security measures.
Furthermore, this study lays the groundwork in addressing the many factors af­
fecting human­computer interaction and can be beneficial for ongoing research
aimed at raising awareness and implementing targeted interventions to address
human error in cybersecurity. By fostering collaboration between academia, in­
dustry, and government agencies, future research endeavors can develop inno­
vative strategies to mitigate security risks and promote a culture of cyber hygiene
and vigilance. Moreover, this study would propose an aid for studies focusing on
the danish population and technological practices.
