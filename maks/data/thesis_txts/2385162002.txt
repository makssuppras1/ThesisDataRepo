II 
 
 
Abstract  
 
The increasing demand in oil and gas consumption has led to the exploration and 
development of deeper formations, which are characterized as HPHT (T > 423 K, P > 68.95 
MPa). Consequently, accurate density data are required for the corresponding HPHT 
conditions. However, density data at elevated pressures and temperatures are scarce in 
literature, either for pure compounds or mixtures. In a depletion process, density and 
isothermal compressibility are crucial properties for the estimation of hydrocarbon in place. 
In the present work, experimental density measurements have been conducted for four 
different mixtures at pressures up to 140 MPa and temperatures up to 463.15 K using a 
vibrating tube densimeter. The first system was a ternary mixture composed of n-methane, 
n-butane and n-dodecane, the second system was a multicomponent mixture composed of n-
methane, n-butane, n-octane, n-dodecane and n-hexadecane and the remaining two were 
two real reservoir fluids: one was volatile oil and the other was gas condensate which are 
bottom hole samples.  
The experimental density data was fitted to the modified form of Tait- Tammann equation, in 
order to obtain the densities as a function of temperature and pressure. Furthermore, the 
excess volumes of the ternary and the multicomponent mixtures were also calculated using 
literature density data for each pure compound and they were found to be negative for all the 
studied pressure and temperature conditions. Based on the obtained experimental density 
values, the isothermal compressibility of each of the aforementioned mixtures was also 
obtained. Finally, three different equations of state were used for both synthetic systems in 
order to predict the densities and the excess volumes. 
Furthermore, experimental heat capacity measurements have been performed for n-decane 
at pressures up to 120 MPa using a differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) apparatus Setaram 
C80 Calvet calorimeter. Finally, the obtained experimental heat capacity values combined 
with literature density data for n-decane were used for the determination of Joule-Thomson 
coefficient.

Chapter 1 
 
Introduction 
 
Petroleum is one of the primary sources of energy in human life. Due to the increasing demand 
for oil and gas, the conventional sources decline resulting the aim of development and 
exploitation of deeper reservoir formations. Such deeper reservoirs are characterized by 
extreme conditions such as high pressures and high temperatures. Oil exploitation under 
these conditions presents numerous challenges requiring the need of new technologies and 
equipment as well as knowledge of the thermophysical properties of fluids under these 
conditions. Even though computational methods for the prediction of thermodynamic 
properties have made progress, there is still lack of the experimental determination of 
thermodynamic properties in literature, especially under these extreme conditions. 
Reservoir fluids are generally composed of high molecular weight hydrocarbons in small 
proportions, which under these extreme conditions can influence significantly the fluids’ 
thermodynamic behavior.1 Conducting experiments in a laboratory is an indispensable way to 
obtain information about fluids’ thermodynamic properties. Because of their chemical 
complexity, it is preferred to emulate certain types of real reservoir fluids as synthetic 
mixtures of different hydrocarbons in order to be easier to study and to understand the 
interactions between the compounds.2 
Density and its related property isothermal compressibility are crucial properties for the 
estimation of hydrocarbons in place and recovery in a depletion process. Both properties 
contribute to the characterization of oil quality and can be used for the calculation of oil 
reserves.2 Density and isothermal compressibility are two properties that are directly related 
to temperature and pressure since as far as density is concerned, an increase on pressure 
cause an increase on density whereas an increase on temperature cause its decrease. On the 
other hand, isothermal compressibility is affected inversely proportional by pressure and 
temperature.   
Another basic thermodynamic property of reservoir fluids that is affected by pressure and 
temperature variations is heat capacity. It is a property often used in reservoir engineering in 
order to calculate calorimetric properties of liquids under pressure.1 As it is one property 
related to the second derivative of Gibbs energy, it is difficult to describe accurately and 
reliable data are required even for the modelling of this property.3 
Finally, temperature variations are strongly connected to another thermophysical property of 
particular interest in oil and gas fields, the Joule-Thomson effect. It is an effect that is mainly 
observed at large pressure drops during a depletion process.4 For HPHT reservoirs, negative 
values of Joule-Thomson coefficient indicate a temperature increase upon expansion, 
whereas at normal temperature and pressure reservoirs positive values of Joule-Thomson 
coefficient are obtained suggesting a temperature decrease.

In the present work, HPHT density measurements are performed for four mixtures using a 
vibrating tube densimeter. Based on the experimental density results obtained, their 
corresponding isothermal compressibilities are calculated. Furthermore, HPHT heat capacity 
measurements are conducted for n-decane using a differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) 
apparatus Setaram C80 calorimeter. The determination of Joule-Thomson coefficient for n- 
decane is also performed by an indirect method using experimental heat capacity and density 
measurements. In general, these properties have been studied for some pure n-alkanes and 
their binary mixtures but there is lack of literature data for high pressure and high 
temperature conditions and especially for ternary and multi-component mixtures at these 
extreme conditions.

Chapter 2 
 
Literature Review 
 
From the literature review that has been conducted, it was concluded that the available data 
for density and heat capacity measurements of n-alkanes are not adequate at high pressure 
and high temperature conditions. Furthermore, in the case of density measurements, no 
literature data found for ternary and multicomponent systems. However, in many works 
Anton Paar densimeters were used to measure densities of pure compounds. 
Quevedo-Nolasco5 used DMA-HPM densimeter in order to obtain experimental densities for 
the binary systems n-heptane+ n-decane and n-octane+ n-decane up to 25 MPa and at 
temperatures from 313 K to 363 K. Water and nitrogen were used as calibration fluids and the 
performance of DMA-HPM was checked by comparing n-heptane and n-decane experimental 
densities with literature data. Experimental n-heptane densities were compared with those 
obtained by the proposed equation by Sommer et al.6 and the deviation found to be ±0.02 % 
while densities for n-decane were compared with data obtained by the equation proposed by 
Lemmon and Span7 and the deviation was 0.2 %. Both sets of data showed deviation lower 
than 0.07 % so it was concluded that the vibrating tube densimeter measures densities 
successfully. 
Banipal et al.8 reported also the densities and heat capacities of some pure n-alkanes, 
including n-decane, at temperatures from 318.15 K to 373.15 K and pressures up to 10 MPa. 
For the density measurements a vibrating tube digital densimeter, DMA 60/512 provided also 
by Anton Paar, was used. Air and water were used as reference fluids for the calibration of the 
apparatus. The densimeter was checked by measuring the densities of cyclohexane and n-
heptane at various temperatures and the results were compared with literature data showing 
a deviation less than 0.05 %. Heat capacities were measured using a Setaram C80 calorimeter 
based on the Calvet principle. The accuracy of heat capacity measurements was checked by 
measuring the heat capacity of n-heptane taking water as reference and compare with 
literature data. The measurements showed good agreement with those of the literature 
within 0.2 % and the reproducibility of the measurements found to be 0.1 %.  
Ramos-Estrada et al.9 reported a correlation to predict liquid densities at high pressures. This 
correlation uses as variables temperature, pressure and the average molar fraction of the 
carbon number of the pure components of the mixture and is based on the principle of 
congruence combined with the Tait Equation. The average absolute deviation (AAD) of this 
equation from the literature density data found to be 0.26 %. 
Comuñas et al.10 used the DMA-HPM densimeter provided by Anton Paar in order to measure 
the density of diethyl adipate in the temperature range from 293.15 K to 403.15 K and 
pressures up to 140 MPa. A modified equation from the one Lagourette et al.11 proposed, was 
used in order to calculate the density and the uncertainty found to be 0.05 %. The apparatus 
performance was checked by comparing the experimental densities obtained for 1-butanol 
and toluene with literature values and the AAD was less than 0.08 %.  
==PAGE:18==

Ivaniš et al.12 proposed an apparatus for density measurements at temperatures from 288.15 
K to 413.15 K and pressures from 0.1 MPa up to 60 MPa that was consisted of a DMA HP 
density measuring cell and a DMA 5000 densimeter, both provided by Anton Paar. The 
apparatus was calibrated according to the method proposed by Comuñas et al.10 and for the 
validation of the apparatus experimental densities of n-hexane, toluene and dichloromethane 
were compares with the corresponding literature data. The AADs found to be 0.03- 0.10 % for 
hexane, 0.04- 0.08 % for toluene and 0.02- 0.03 % for dichloromethane. 
Segovia et al.13 also proposed an apparatus for density measurements over the temperature 
range from 283.15 K to 398.15 K and pressures up to 70 MPa. The heart of the proposed 
automated densimetric system was a DMA-HPM vibrating tube densimeter. The automatic 
assembly was calibrated based on the method proposed by Lagourette et al.11 as modified by 
Comuñas et al.10 by using vacuum, water and n-decane as reference fluids. The densities of 
toluene and n-decane were measured and they compared with literature values. For toluene, 
the density data were compared with those obtained by using Lemmon and Span7 equation 
of state as well as with those reported by Cibulka and Takagi14 and the AAD found to be 0.03 
% in both cases. As concerns n-decane, the obtained experimental values were compared with 
the values obtained by the proposed equation by Lemmon and Span7 and with the correlation 
proposed by Cibulka and Hnedkovsky15. The AAD values were 0.06 % and 0.07 %, respectively. 
The densimeter DMA 512 by Anton Paar was used by Amorim et al.16 for the density 
measurements of the binary mixture n- hexadecane – cyclohexane in the temperature range 
from 318.15 K to 413.15 K and pressures from 6.895 MPa to 62.053 MPa. Toluene, 
cyclohexane and n- heptane were used as reference fluids for the calibration procedure of the 
apparatus. The experimental error of the density measurements of the binary mixture was 0.5 
kg∙m-3 and the deviation from literature values was 0.75 kg∙m-3. All the experimental data were 
correlated with a modified Peng- Robinson EoS.17 
A vibrating-wire instrument for the density measurements of n- dodecane and n- octadecane  
was used by Caudwell et al.18 for temperatures up to 473 K and pressures up to 200 MPa. The 
calibration procedure was conducted by using vacuum, air and toluene as reference fluids at 
303.15 K. The experimental results were compared with those from Harris et al.19 and 
Kashiwagi et al.20 and the uncertainty found to be 0.2 %.  
As concerns the heat capacity of n-decane, apart from Banipal et al.8, experimental heat 
capacities for n- decane were reported by Finke et al.21 but for low temperature range (12 K 
to 300 K). In addition, González-Salgado et al.22 reported heat capacity values up to 60 MPa 
and 323.15 K as well as Regueira et al.3 reported values up to 10 MPa which were combined 
with density literature data for n-decane in order to determine the corresponding Joule- 
Thomson coefficient. 
Due to the scarcity of reported experimental heat capacity data of n- alkanes at high pressures 
and high temperatures, many authors have proposed equations to predict the heat capacity. 
Lemmon and Span7 proposed an Equation of State for the heat capacity calculation that 
requires data such as pressure, density and temperature.  
Kunz and Wagner23 also proposed an Equation of State, known as GERG 2008, that calculates 
thermodynamic properties for compounds of natural gas. GERG 2008 can be applied in two 
different ranges; the first one is for temperatures from 90 K to 450 K and pressures up to 35 
MPa and the second contains temperatures up to 700 K and pressures up to 70 MPa.

triple point to the critical for the temperatures from 243.15 K to 617.6 K.

Measurement Principles and Treatment of Data 
 
In this section the principle of measurements of density and heat capacity measurements are 
described. Density measurements were conducted by using the vibrating tube densimeter 
DMA-HPM which is provided by Anton Paar and hear capacity measurements were conducted 
using the calorimeter C80 provided by Setaram. In addition, the calibration procedure 
conducted for the densimeter is also presented as well as all the equations used for the 
treatment of data.

3.1.2 Calibration Procedure 
 
The calibration procedure is of the highest importance in every experimental apparatus. The 
accuracy and the reliability of the experimental procedure as well as of the results are directly 
related to the calibration.   
The calibration of the DMA-HPM apparatus was required in order to execute the experiments. 
The calibration of the densimeter was performed according to the procedure proposed by 
Comuñas et al.10. This process is a modification of the calibration procedure Lagourette et al.11 
previously described by Comuñas et al. In the present work, the experimental measurements 
of the oscillation period of three well-known density fluids were used, in the temperature 
range 298.15 K to 463.15 K and pressure range 0.1 MPa up to 140 MPa. The fluids used were 
the vacuum, Milli-Q water and n-dodecane. 
As it is already mentioned in section 3.1.1, the raw data of the vibrating tube densimeter is

3.2 Differential Scanning Calorimeter (DSC) 
 
3.2.1 Measurement Principle 
 
The experimental technique used for the heat capacity (Cp) measurements is a Calvet type 
Differential Scanning Calorimetry. According to this method, the deviation in the heat flow 
rate among a measured sample and a reference sample under controlled temperature 
difference is recorded.29 
For the verification of the technique as well as the determination of the experimental heat 
capacity values, Bessières et al.1 noted that three experiments should be conducted. In the 
first one, both cells should be empty at ambient pressure in order to record their difference 
heat flow rate (𝑞̇𝐸𝐸). In the second and the third experiment, the measuring cell is charged 
with water and the sample, respectively. Water is used for the calibration of the apparatus 
since water has a well-studied heat capacity over a wide range of temperatures and pressures. 
Moreover, the heat flow values (𝑞̇𝑊𝐸) between the empty and the cell filled with water are 
necessary for the determination of the volume of the cell. Finally, the values of the heat flow 
(𝑞̇𝑆𝐸) among the empty cell and the measuring cell filled with the sample, which are recorded 
in the third experiment, are needed for the heat capacity calculations at constant pressure. 
It should be noted that in the present work, the DSC apparatus was already calibrated, as a 
result no calibration procedure had to be performed. However, pressure calibration was 
required in order to convert the thermopile signal (E/V) into pressure (MPa).  The pressure 
calibration had been conducted and the results are presented in Table B- 1 in Appendix B.

4.2.2 Experimental Procedure 
 
The methods that have been conducted during the experimental procedure are presented in 
this section. Specifically, the cleaning procedure of the apparatus and the filling procedure of 
the sample into the vibrating tube densimeter. 
 
4.2.2.1 Cleaning procedure 
 
The cleaning procedure of the system is crucial between the different measurements. The 
purpose of the cleaning procedure is to ensure that the apparatus does not contain any 
residual contaminants that might influence the new sample inserted and consequently, the 
accuracy of the measurements. The cleaning procedure should be repeated after the 
completion of measurements of each sample. 
The steps followed in this process are: 
1. The sample included in the densimeter is removed from the outlet valve (valve V3 in Figure 
4.2-1) 
2. Toluene is used as solvent. It is inserted into the apparatus through a glass funnel which is 
connected to the inlet valve (valve V1 in Figure 4.2-1). The apparatus is rinsed with toluene 
several times in order to be sure that it dissolves all the impurities. Toluene is removed 
from the outlet valve. 
3. Subsequently, ethanol is used since it is a volatile fluid that can displace toluene as well as 
it can evaporate without leaving any residue. In the case of ethanol, the apparatus is rinsed 
several times too. 
4. Before the removal of ethanol from the outlet valve, the apparatus is suspected to a 
pressure test, usually at 140 MPa. This test takes place in order to be sure that there is no 
leaking in the apparatus. 
==PAGE:39==
25 
 
5. The system is dried, using pressurized air in order to ensure the removal of any residue. At 
the same time, the temperature of the system is increased up to the boiling point of 
ethanol (351.39 K39) in order to confirm the evaporation of the ethanol. 
6. Finally, the system is put under vacuum for a couple of hours using the Edwards E2M 1.5 
two-stage oil sealed rotary vacuum pump (Figure 4.2-8). The pump is connected to the 
apparatus through the valve V1 (Figure 4.2-1). 
 
4.2.2.2 Sample transfer procedure 
 
After the fulfilment of the cleaning procedure, the apparatus is charged with the sample. It is 
already mentioned that the system is under vacuum for a couple of hours.  
The steps followed for the filling procedure are: 
1. The inlet valve (valve V1 in Figure 4.2-1) is closed and the vacuum pump is disconnected. 
2. The sample cylinder is connected to Isco pump, running at pressure higher than saturation. 
3. In valve (V1) the cylinder with the sample is connected. 
4. It essential to check that the outlet valve (valve V3) to be also closed. 
5. The piston that regulates the pressure is moved to the the low end of stoke. 
6. Valve V2 is closed 
7. Valve V1 is opened slowly and the sample starts being loaded in the apparatus using ISCO 
pump. Valve V2 is opened slowly. 
8. The piston is refilled with the sample by moving it manually to the high end of stoke. 
9. When the pressure and the volume on the screen of the ISCO pump are constant, that 
means the apparatus is fully filled with the sample and as a result valve V1 is shut off.    
10. A small volume of the sample is purged from the system through valve V3 in the form of 
separator test. 
11. Using Presto A30 thermostatic bath, the desirable temperature is set as well as by moving 
the piston the desirable pressure is reached. The sample remains in these conditions until 
the oscillation period gets steady.  
 
4.2.2.3 Measurement procedure 
 
The measurements start when the pressure and the temperature are stable for at least 10 
minutes. The Microsoft Excel tool provided by Anton Paar is opened and the user has to set 
the values for the data transfer and the slope stability. Subsequently, the user has to press 
‘’Start’’ in the Recording Window (Figure 4.2-6) and the oscillation period starts being 
recorded. For each temperature and pressure, measurements are repeated three times. The 
criterion of keeping a good experimental measurement is that the deviation between the 
minimum and maximum value recorded should not exceed the difference of 0.007 μs. This 
process is conducted for all the pressure and temperature ranges of interest.

Chapter 5 
 
Models 
 
Equation of state (EoS) are widely used in order to predict or correlate thermodynamic 
properties and phase equilibria of fluid mixtures. 40 In the petroleum industry, the most widely 
used models for PVT modeling of reservoir fluids as well as for compositional reservoir 
simulation are the cubic equation of state (CEoS), such as the Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) and 
the Peng-Robinson (PR). Nowadays due to the worldwide increasing demand in oil 
consumption, deeper formations are under exploration and development. Since high 
temperatures and high pressures characterize these formations, more accurate PVT modeling 
is required. Yan et al. also proposed that the non-cubic EoS Perturbed Chain Statistical 
Associating Fluid Theory (PC-SAFT), could partially replace the cubic EoS as it can mainly 
provide better density estimations.41 
In the present work, these three models are used in order to predict the densities (ρ) of the 
two synthetic mixtures and compare them with the obtained experimental values, as well as 
for the prediction and comparison of their excess volumes (VE) in the whole temperature and 
pressure range. The program used for the modeling of the two synthetic mixtures was created 
by Farhad Varzandeh at Center for Energy Resources Engineering (CERE). The EoSs used, are 
described below.

Conclusions 
 
HPHT density measurements have been carried out for four different systems using the 
vibrating tube densimeter DMA-HPM provided by Anton Paar for pressures up to 140 MPa 
and temperatures up to 463.15 K. In addition, heat capacity measurements have been 
conducted for n-decane up to 120 MPa, using the Setaram C80 calorimeter and combined 
with literature density data in order to determine the corresponding Joule-Thomson 
coefficient.  
As concerns the density measurements, two synthetic mixtures and two real reservoir fluids 
were studied. The density values were obtained using a modified form of Lagourette et al.11 
equation proposed by Comuñas et al.10 and the obtained experimental density data were 
correlated as a function of temperature and pressure by the modified Tait-Tammann 
equation. By differentiation of the modified Tait-Tammann equation, the isothermal 
compressibility of each of the studied systems was obtained. Furthermore, the excess 
volumes of the two synthetic mixtures were calculated using bibliographic density values for 
each pure compound while three different Equation of States (EoS) were used in order to 
predict their densities and excess volumes. The predictions acquired were compared with the 
experimental data. 
Firstly, density measurements were conducted for a ternary mixture composed of n- methane, 
n- butane and n- dodecane. The experimental results were correlated with the modified Tait-
Tammann equation and the isothermal compressibility values were calculated. Subsequently, 
the excess volume of the mixture was calculated and the obtained values found to be negative 
over the whole temperature and pressure range. The studied mixture was also modeled for 
all the studied temperatures and pressures using three different EoS, two cubic (SRK and PR) 
and one non-cubic (PC-SAFT) in order to compare the experimental densities and excess 
volumes. From this comparison the absolute average deviations (AAD) for the densities were 
found to be 9.6 % for SRK, 1.7 % for PR and 3.8 % for PC-SAFT. It can be concluded that, in the 
case of the ternary mixture, PR model predicts better density values and this might be due to 
not too heavy hydrocarbons in the mixture41 while all the three models underestimate the 
volume deviation from ideality. 
Furthermore, density measurements were carried out for a multi-component mixture 
composed of methane, n- butane, n-octane, n-dodecane, n- hexadecane and n-eicosane. The 
isothermal compressibility and excess volume values were obtained as in the case of the 
ternary mixture. The aforementioned EoSs were used again for the prediction of densities and 
excess volumes for comparison purposes. From the results obtained, PC-SAFT showed better 
predictions for both properties over the whole temperature and pressure range with an AAD 
value for density equal to 0.6 %. 
A real reservoir volatile oil, which was supplied by an external laboratory, was also studied. 
Experimental density results were acquired and they were fitted by the modified Tait-
Tammann equation through which isothermal compressibility values were obtained too. The 
correlated results reported in the present work were compared with the results from the 
==PAGE:88==
74 
 
external laboratory for the temperatures T= 358.15 K and T= 432.15 K and pressures from 
41.42 MPa up to 103.81 MPa. From the comparison of density and isothermal compressibility, 
it was concluded that the results are in good agreement with a global AAD value of density 
equals to be 0.8 %. In addition, a separator test was performed and the GOR value found to 
be 220.2 sm3/sm3 while the deviation from the value of the external lab was 15 %. 
Subsequently, the experimental density results were also compared with the experimental 
values of the multi-component mixture over the whole temperature and pressure range 
studied in this work, as the former emulates a reservoir volatile oil. The differences in density 
values were big but this was expected as the real reservoir volatile oil contains hydrocarbons 
heavier than C20. 
The second real reservoir fluid examined was gas condensate. In this case a long equilibrium 
time was needed to get stability while at T=298.15 K and at pressures 60 MPa and 80 MPa, 
significant instability was observed in the oscillation period which might be due to gas 
condensation. The experimental results of density and isothermal compressibility were 
compared with those from the external lab at temperatures T= 363.15 K, T= 393.15 and T= 
423.75 and pressures from 60 MPa up to 93 MPa. Significant deviations were found for both 
properties with AAD value for density equals to 1.7 %. A separator test was also conducted 
twice and the GOR values obtained were 2459.9 sm3/sm3 for the first test and 2651 sm3/sm3 
for the second. The deviation among them found to be 7.2 % while the deviations from the 
external lab were 41 % and 37 %, respectively. The GOR values obtained in this work are in 
reasonable agreement between them but not with the external lab; however the density 
values do not disagree that much. 
To recapitulate, the vibrating tube densimeter DMA-HPM measured the high pressure 
densities of two synthetic mixtures and two reservoir fluids successfully. In addition, the trend 
of density for all the studied mixtures showed the expected dependence with temperature 
and pressure; density increases with pressure and decreases with temperature. On the other 
hand, the temperature and pressure dependences for the isothermal compressibility are just 
the opposite; κΤ increases with temperature but decreases with pressure.  
Finally, isobaric heat capacity measurements were performed for n-decane at pressures up to 
120 MPa. The validity of the Calorimeter C80 DSC apparatus was verified by comparing the 
experimental results obtained with literature data taken from REFPROP of NIST. The AAD was 
found to be 1.5 %. As concerns the Joule-Thomson coefficient negative values were calculated 
denoting a temperature rise with pressure drop which should be taken into account for high 
μJT values just for safety reasons. In addition, it was observed that the μJT increases with 
pressure and decreases with temperature. This property is much more influenced by the 
temperature than pressure.
