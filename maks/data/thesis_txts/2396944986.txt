Abstract
In ﬁre safety design procedures the intoxication of the occupants is not considered in
the design process for areas and rooms where alcohol consumption is expected, due to the
lack of data on the subject.
Recent research experiments into the inﬂuence of alcohol on evacuation have shed some
light on the topic but have also raised many new questions. They have shown evidence
that there might be a correlation between the development of drunk behaviour and the
surroundings or context in which the occupants are situated. Such a correlation could be
used to improve the reliability of further research into the subject and to develop useful
methods of crowd control for intoxicated people.
That is why the experiment in this
report investigated the development of drunk behaviour in a group of 23 people from a
mix of cultural backgrounds within a party environment, independently of alcohol, with
the use of non-alcoholic beer as a placebo.
The participants were put through 3 identical exercises - the ﬁrst one before con-
suming the beer, the second one after consuming it and the third one after the music
was stopped and the lights were made bright. The exercises were aimed at testing their
balance, reaction times, concentration, hand to eye coordination, problem solving skills,
cognitive skills and cooperation. In addition to that they performed 2 evacuations that
were used to interrupt their ’drunk behaviour’ and test their reaction,decision and overall
evacuation times.They were also analyzed for signs of drunk behaviour with video and
sound recordings.
The ﬁnal results showed clear reduction of performance in the tests for concentration,
hand to eye coordination, problem solving skills, cognitive skills and cooperation after
the consumption of the beer, but little change in the balance tests and wherever there
was change the results were inconclusive. During the evacuations there was a slight im-
provement in the performance in the second evacuation. Apart from that, some of the
participants showed signs of drunk behavior, such as reduced cooperation and increase in
noise after the alcohol was consumed. Additionally, there was no increase in the perfor-
mance of the tests after the lights were made bright and the music was stopped, instead
there was further reduction in performance, although at the same time the noise levels
decreased, cooperation improved and people became less communicative.
Based on this it was concluded that even with non-alcoholic beer some of the partici-
pants developed drunk behaviour, similar to that observed in previous experiments with
the use of alcohol. Additionally, their behaviour partially changed with the change of the
environment.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The aim of this section is to provide a brief overview of current evacuation design procedures,
background information on previous research that is relevant to the topic, and introduce the
objective and structure of the report.
The literature used is the result of bibliographic investigation through the Technical University
of Denmark’s (DTU) own library as well as research articles and reports from other institutions
on the subject of the eﬀect of alcohol on evacuation and the understanding of drunk behaviour
and its eﬀects. It is notable to point out that considerable inspiration and information was
drawn from previous research done at DTU on the topic. As it stands, at the time of the
making of this report, no other research has been found that comes close to tackling this
speciﬁc subject so directly.
On the other hand there is a large amount of literature on the topics of evacuation of buildings
and behaviour of occupants. Some of the more notable sources, which are frequently visited
by ﬁre safety engineers, have been referred to in this report. But even though there is plenty
of literature on various types of occupants, such as children, old people, handicapped people,
etc., there is little to no information or research on the evacuation capabilities and behaviour
of people under the inﬂuence of alcohol.
1.1
Fire safety and alcohol consumption.
Fires at venues where alcohol is consumed have historically had high consequences in terms
of loss of human life and some of the deadliest ones on record have occurred in recent years.
Among them are the ’Kiss nightclub ﬁre’ in Santa Maria Brazil with 242 casualties in 2013, the
Station nightclub ﬁre in W. Warwick Rhode Island USA in 2003 with a hundred casualties
and the Lame Horse Nightclub ﬁre in Perm Russia in 2009 with 154 casualties (Deadliest
public assembly and nightclub ﬁres 2017).
Large amounts of people tend to concentrate at places where alcohol is consumed and these
go beyond night clubs and bars. People number in the thousands at football stadiums, for
example, such as the Bradford City stadium where a ﬁre claimed the lives of 56 people, while
some of the intoxicated crowd were seen laughing and many moving or standing by with their
drinks in their hands (FSUK 2014).
The risks at such places are further increased due to the higher possibilities of reckless be-
haviour by the participants when they are under the inﬂuence of alcohol. Despite this, intox-
ication of the occupants is not considered in the design process for areas and rooms where
alcohol consumption is expected, mainly due to the lack of data on the subject. Consequently,
the venues that serve alcohol today might pose a greater risk than is currently expected even
if they follow current ﬁre safety regulations.
Recent research experiments into the inﬂuence of alcohol on evacuation at DTU have given
some new insights and raised many new questions (Rask 2017, Madsen and Hansen 2015).
They have shown evidence that there might be a correlation between the development of
drunk behaviour and the surroundings or context in which the occupants are situated. If
this correlation is found to be signiﬁcant enough it could be used to improve the reliability of
further research into the subject and to develop useful methods of crowd control for intoxicated
people. That is why the experiment which this report analyses investigates the development

1.2
Knowledge on the eﬀects of alcohol on human behaviour
The lack of research on the eﬀects of alcohol is not isolated to the ﬁeld of ﬁre safety. As
described in Morris 1998, research into the eﬀects of alcohol on human behavior has for a
long time had its progress impaired by the disproportionate focus on problems like violence,
aﬀecting only a small minority of drinkers, while the study of ’normal’ drinkers has been
neglected.
A large contribution to this is the fact that nations with a strong Temperance tradition
and ambivalent attitudes towards alcohol have dominated the ﬁeld of alcohol studies, while
cultures in which drinking is not perceived as a problem have seen little need to conduct
extensive research on the subject, as observed by Mäkelä 1975.
Nevertheless, there is some useful research that sheds some light on the various eﬀects alcohol
could have on people. Anthropological research is particularly helpful, since it shows that
people from diﬀerent cultures have diﬀerent drinking habits and drunk behaviour, which in
turn means that drunk behaviour is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by factors other than the alcohol
itself.
Both comparative studies and controlled experiments have demonstrated, that while ethanol
produces well-understood neuro-chemical changes, the wide variations in social and behavioural
outcomes of drinking can mainly be explained with reference to cultural factors, and to cul-
turally determined beliefs about the eﬀects of drinking (Midanik 2002; Marshall 1979; Marlatt
and Rohsenow 1980; L., L.J., and J.K. 1995; Peele 1997; D.B. and H. 1998; Heath 1998).
And as Desmond Morris eloquently states (Morris 1998):
"Alcohol is not a stimulant, but an inhibitor of inhibitions. And there is a subtle diﬀerence.
Whatever the dominant mood of the drinker, alcohol will exaggerate it by removing the usual
social restraints. If the drinker is happy he becomes happier; if he is sad he becomes sadder.
There is absolutely no truth in the idea that alcohol helps to ‘drown your sorrows’. If you are
sorrowful to start with you will only sink deeper into despair as the night wears on."
1.3
Background
1.3.1
Previous research.
Research at DTU on the inﬂuence of alcohol on evacuation is still in its early stages and
it has predominantly provided insight on the research methods to be used for organizing
experiments.
Each experiment has been building on the previous ones and expanding the knowledge on
participant behaviour. The current experiment uses insights from its predecessor "Reaction
and decision time of evacuees, A study regarding the inﬂuence of alcohol on the reaction and
decision time" by Poul Brinck Rask(Rask 2017), and both rely on data obtained from an
experiment from 2015, which compares participants performing tests under the inﬂuence of
alcohol and while wearing Fatal Vision® Impairment Goggles, which simulate drunkenness

(Madsen and Hansen 2015). Madsen and Hansen 2015 give convincing evidence that par-
ticipants behave diﬀerently when wearing the goggles compared to when they are under the
actual inﬂuence of alcohol.
That is why the experiment in Rask 2017 used alcohol to obtain its results. These results
indicate that the participants’ level of intoxication might be signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by their
surroundings, which raises the question weather a certain level of alcohol intoxication by
itself might not be enough to cause drunk behaviour. That is why this latest experiment
investigates the inﬂuence of surroundings on the development of intoxication with the use of
placebo alcohol.
All of these research experiments use testing methods that are connected to evacuation of
buildings in diﬀerent ways in order to provide further knowledge on the topic, along with
knowledge on the behaviour of people.
1.3.2
Theory
This experiment, as its predecessors, is intended to obtain data that might help improve
performance based design egress calculations for places where alcohol is consumed. More
speciﬁcally it is concerned with inspecting aspects of the Required Safety Egress Time cal-
culations or RSET for short (SFPE 2015a). The RSET is supposed to be lower than the
Available Safe Egress Time or ASET for short (1.1). The ASET is the time from the ignition
until conditions become critical in the compartment.

(Kindler 2016).
The RSET is also referred to as the evacuation time tevac and is calculated with the formula:
tevac = tpre + ttr + tq
It is the time from ignition until all the occupants have reached a safe location and it should
be noted that diﬀerent sources might use diﬀerent symbols for the diﬀerent components of
RSET than the ones used here.

As can be seen from the tables the detection time is dependent on the building category and
the detection systems installed in the building. But in reality it is also highly dependent on
the type of ﬁre and its location and consequently the time can vary from a few seconds to a
few hours. That is why there have to be prepared special ﬁre scenarios in order to determine
the most critical ﬁres that are, at the same time, realistic (SFPE 2015b).
In some ﬁre scenarios the ignition and detection happens simultaneously, but in many cases
there will be some delay. For example, if there is no detection system installed in the build-
ing, the ﬁre alarm has to be activated manually, the ﬁre starts and spreads through closed
compartments and suspended ceilings and has to work its way into a room with occupants
before being detected.
After the ﬁre has been detected it is diﬃcult to anticipate what will be the length for the reac-
tion and decision time. It is highly speciﬁc to the type of building - highly compartmentalized,
large transparent areas, etc. - and the type of occupants - whether they are familiar with the
building and its evacuation plan or not. Additionally, factors like outside weather, occupant
health, or alcohol intoxication might aﬀect their decision making and with the large amount

1.4
Objective
The focus of this project is derived from the observation in previous evacuation experiments
with alcohol consumption that the participants’ behaviour might be signiﬁcantly more inﬂu-
enced by the setting of the experiments than by the actual alcohol intoxication.
That is why the experiment from this report investigated the development of drunk behaviour
in people as a consequence of the setting and context of the situation they are in. In order
to do that it was devised in such a way as to asses how the occupants’ mental states are
changing, what makes them change and what are the consequences of that in relation to the
evacuation process. This is done by recreating a party-like environment similar to that of a
night club or bar while serving non-alcoholic beer and observing its eﬀects on volunteers and
how they are aﬀected when the environment changes.
The main diﬃculties with setting up such an experiment were in conducting it in a way that
ensures the participants do not become aware of the fact they are not consuming real alcoholic
drinks and then evaluating their behaviour in a reliable and practical way.

Methods and experiment set-up
2.1
Methods of the experiment
As described in the introduction, the experiment aimed at testing whether intoxicated be-
haviour can be caused by the surroundings and if it does, how it might aﬀect occupant
capabilities during evacuation. In order to do this the experiment environment was set up
in such a way as to facilitate an intoxicated mood for the participants with its similarity to
a party or a bar and they were served non-alcoholic beer. The participants are only brieﬂy
brought out of this environment with two evacuations. They perform exercises, chosen to
measure their coordination, concentration, reaction times, cooperation and balance and are
recorded with cameras with audio recording.
Considerable eﬀort was spent to keep the participants from noticing the hidden extra purpose
of the experiment.
They were informed that the experiment’s purpose was to test their
evacuation capabilities while under the inﬂuence of alcohol in a party-like environment, which
was intentionally as close to the actual purpose as possible in order not to arouse suspicion.
The testing methods were designed for that purpose and the information provided does not
misinform them in any way. They were informed that they would be consuming beer and were
provided with detailed information about the experiment schedule. The only thing omitted
was the beer’s alcohol content and the additional observations that were made because of
that.
The experiment was designed with several expectations, based on previous experiments, lit-
erature study and the author’s personal experience with alcohol consumption. These expec-
tations are as follows:
• Some participants develop drunk behaviour after consuming non-alcoholic beer.
• The inﬂuenced participants experience an increase in reaction and decision time after
the consumption of the beer.
• Higher noise levels after the consumption of the beer.
• Decreased participant cooperation and abilities to follow instructions after the consump-
tion of the beer.
• Reduction of drunk behaviour after interruption of party atmosphere.
The literature on which these expectations were formed is described in the introduction and
can be found in the Drunk Behaviour and The Eﬀects of Alcohol on Evacuation sections in
the Bibliography.

2.3
Experimental run
The experiment was run in the course of 2 hours on a Friday afternoon between the hours of
16:00 and 18:00. The participants were put through a series of identical exercises with each
exercise serving as a reference to the next one. Thus, the ﬁrst exercise was not intended to
be a reference to the third, but they could still be compared if needed.
Time tables of the experiment can be viewed in Appendix H.
2.3.1
Experiment phases.
The experiment is divided into 3 thematic phases, devised around each exercise (Figure 2.3).
The exercises are identical, with only the mazes being diﬀerent in each one, but still selected
in order to be as similar as possible in diﬃculty and form.
The goal of this experimental set-up is to have the participants complete the same tasks in
3 diﬀerent mental states. The ﬁrst exercise is to be a reference to the second one, the only
diﬀerence between the two is that the second one is done after the consumption of the beer
and the pizza during the scheduled 30 minute ’party time’ (’Serving of Drinks & Snacks’ on
Figure 2.3). The ﬁrst evacuation also serves as a reference to the second one and is done
before the drinking. It additionally serves as a transition to the ’party time’. The use of the
evacuations as transitions is done because they force the participants to brieﬂy move out of
the test environment in order to interrupt their mood and make them mentally distinguish
the ’party time’ from the other parts of the experiment. This is especially important for the
second evacuation, since it aims to interrupt the participants’ drunk behaviour.
The participants start by doing the ﬁrst exercise, followed by the ﬁrst evacuation. After the
evacuation the returning participants are allowed to start consuming the already poured beer
from the bar and are given the pizza. The pizza was a type of extra reward for the participants
for their participation along with the beer and it was given with the beer instead of at the
end so that it contributes to the ’party mood’.
After being left alone to consume as much as they wished for 30 minutes, the participants were
given the second exercise. In order to have them be in the "party mood" while completing the
exercise, the participants were allowed to drink and eat during it. Since all the instructions
were written on the exercise papers, everyone was simply given their exercise sheet and left
alone to complete it as if it was a party game.
Once the participants were done with the exercise the organizers waited a few minutes and
then sounded an alarm and gave a spoken message for evacuation.
The wait before the
evacuation was short but not predetermined in order to attempt to make the participants as
unprepared for the evacuation as possible under the conditions.
As the evacuation progressed, the lights were turned on and the music was shut down. The
experiment room was brightly lit and without music for the rest of the experiment, which
consisted of the last exercise.
When the participants returned, the exercise sheets were given to them and they were asked
to complete this exercise and wait until everyone is ﬁnished before leaving.
After all the participants had completed the exercise, they were informed by the organizers
that the experiment is over and they are free to leave.

2.3.2
Exercises
Each exercise contained 3 tasks. The tasks were identical for all 3 exercises. Apart from
these tasks each participant had to write down the amount of drinks they have had before
the exercise. An example of one of the exercises can be seen in Appendix D.
The ﬁrst task was to complete a printed maze of medium diﬃculty with a pen as fast as
possible, while trying to avoid touching the maze walls. The data collected from this task
was the time it took each person to complete the maze, the amount of wrong turns each
person made and the amount of times they touched the walls of the maze. This type of
task engages the participants’ patience, cognitive process, hand-eye coordination, problem
solving, concentration and, as research has shown, parts of the brain that are connected with
orientation are active while solving mazes (Kirsch et al. 2006, Skelton et al. 2006, Van Horn
et al. 1998). This task was completed by the participants in the drinking area.
The second task in each exercise was the Flamingo test. This is a standard One Leg Stand
test that requires the participants to stand on one leg for 60 seconds. Every time the person
lost their balance and needed to touch the ground to restore the balance the timer was to
be paused until the person restored the balance on one leg. The result was the number of
times the person touched the ground. Additionally, it was to be noted down if the person
sways while balancing, uses arms for balance or hops and it was encouraged to give additional
comments if needed.
The third task of each exercise was the Up & Go Test. This is a test of both reaction time and
balance. In it two people take turns to test each other - one of the participants sits on a chair
and the other stands 2.5 m from them at a marked spot with a timer, see Figure 2.4. When
the participants have assumed their positions, the standing person gives the signal ’GO’ and
starts the timer, upon which the sitting person has to get up and run as fast as possible
around the standing person and sit back on the chair. As soon as they have done that they
should yell ’STOP’. When the the signal ’STOP’ is given the standing person should stop the

• Ratio measurements - cups of beer; age; maze time, touch and wrong turns; Up and Go

The nominal measurements are descriptions and labels and are used in order to group the
rest of the data.
The ordinal measurements are values that have a meaningful order, but the intervals between
the values may not be equal. For example, the diﬀerence between ’swaying’ and ’not swaying’
could be large and the diﬀerence between ’swaying’ and swaying a lot’ could be very little.
Thus, the ordinal measurements are used to calculate frequencies of their occurrence.
Finally, there are the ratio measurements. These are the measurements that are quantiﬁable
and have an absolute zero, which allows for a wide range of both descriptive and inferential
statistics to be applied, such as mean, median and standard deviation.

statistics is the z-score). The t score formula enables you to take an individual score and
transform it into a standardized form — one which helps you to compare scores (T Score

To calculate the actual probability that there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between two sam-
ples the t-score is used in order to derive the corresponding p-value. This p-value signiﬁes
the percentage of uncertainty (or how much the distributions of the samples intersect) and,
consequently, when removed from 100% the result is the percentage of certainty.
The p-values can be determined from tables like the one in Appendix E, or through the use of

The formula was applied for a two-tailed distribution of two samples with unequal variance.
This was done because with a two tailed test the diﬀerence is checked in both directions
(positive and negative) and our population variance is not assumed to be equal. This makes
the test more critical and consequently makes the results more trustworthy.
The ﬁnal outcome of this calculation is the percentage of certainty with which it can be
claimed that the two samples are signiﬁcantly diﬀerent. When that percentage is over 95%
it is statistically safe to assume that the samples are signiﬁcantly diﬀerent.

2.6.2
Limitation due to prior knowledge
The participants had to be given a certain amount of information prior to the experiment,
which may have aﬀected their behaviour. Even though they did not know exactly when the

real evacuations.

Another source of errors could be the fact that even though the experiment environment was
designed in order to simulate a party atmosphere, the participants were nevertheless aware
that they are part of an experiment and were observed and would be analyzed, which could
have inﬂuenced their behaviour in some unforeseeable ways.

The main diﬃculty with the cameras was related to the sound recordings. Even though the
cameras chosen were the closest to the participants throughout most of the experiment, they
were nevertheless stationary, while the participants moved for some of their activities.
Additionally, the recordings still included surrounding noises like that of the music, which
slightly inﬂuences the overall noise level.

Results and discussion

3.1
Results Grouping
The results have been grouped in several diﬀerent ways, based on the participants. Firstly,
there are the results of all participants taken together for each exercise. This is the largest
grouping and the mean values calculated from it can be considered the closest to the general
population mean.
Secondly, the participants have been divided into three groups based on their beer consump-
tion - the amount of beer they drank and whether they drank it throughout the experiment
or only in the beginning of the ’drinking time’. These groups are given the names ’low beer
consumption group’, ’medium beer consumption group’ and ’high beer consumption group’,
with the low beer consumption group being 9 people and the other two consisting of 7 people
(Figure 3.1).
The low beer consumption group consists of people who drank less than one extra beer
between exercise 1 and 2 and drank in the range of 2 to 3 beers, with the exception of two
people who drank 3.5 and 3.2 beers, but since these people drank only 0.5 and 0.2 beers
extra they were also put in this group.
The people in this group are considered to have
the lowest probability to have developed drunk behaviour, since they more or less stopped
drinking after the ’drinking time’, even though they had plenty of opportunity to drink more
afterwards. These people are also the likeliest to have developed suspicions about the low
alcohol content of the beer at an early stage of the experiment. It should be noted that 4
out of the 5 female participants are within this group and the amount of beer drank by these
girls is not necessarily low for the average female, but in the end it is the early cessation of
drinking combined with the low amount that puts them in this group.
The medium beer consumption group consists of people who drank 1 extra beer and drank in
the range of 3 to 4 beers. These people did not necessarily all drink more than the low beer
consumption group, they simply had at least a full extra beer between the second and third
exercises. Based on that behaviour they are considered as people who drank a relatively low
amount but still continued to drink and, consequently, are less likely to have suspected the
nature of the experiment.
The high alcohol consumption group consists of the people who drank 1 or more extra beers
and drank more than 4 beers in total. The members of this group are least likely to have no-
ticed the nature of the experiment (until perhaps its ﬁnal minutes) and have a high probability
of exhibiting the strongest placebo reaction to the beer.

4. CONCLUSION
4
Conclusion
Anthropological research shows that there are cultural diﬀerences in the amount of alcohol
people drink and the behaviour they exhibit when drunk. Additionally, there are diﬀerences
within a single culture, based on social status, background and individual life experience. This
poses a wide variety of problems when testing the behaviour of people under the inﬂuence
of alcohol. It suggests that to get meaningful results it might be useful to focus on testing
people of the same culture, whose behaviour might be aﬀected by the country/culture they
currently reside in. On the other hand, in order to ﬁnd the most probable eﬀects of alcohol
that are most common in various cultures and peoples it is necessary to gather knowledge of
as many of them as possible.
At the same time it should be understood that the results will only be temporarily relevant
due to the changes in the culture and the drinking patterns and, in order to keep knowledge
relevant, the change and what aﬀects its speed and direction should also be investigated.
The experiment from this report has tested the development of drunk behaviour, without the
consumption of alcohol, in a group of 23 people from a mix of cultural backgrounds for the
purpose of evacuation. This was achieved by recreating a ’party-like’ environment within the
experiment area and using non-alcoholic beer in the hopes of inducing a placebo eﬀect and
then test whether this eﬀect inﬂuences the participant’s evacuation capabilities.
None of the participants showed any signs of suspicion about the alcohol content of the beer
and the majority drank continuously throughout the second two thirds of the experiment.
During that time several distinct changes were observed in them.
Firstly, the maze touch/time results of the participants showed reduced performance in each
consecutive exercise. This points to a reduction of hand to eye coordination and focus, which
could be either due to inebriation or due to a reduction in the amount of eﬀort put to complete
the task successfully. In addition to that, there was an increase in comments and notes for
all tasks, some of which were irrelevant or ’funny’, and two of the participants gave up on
completing the task during the exercise right after consuming the beer. In one of these cases
the participant scratched up the maze angrily.
Furthermore, the participants displayed increased contrast in performance, resulting in in-
creased standard deviations after the consumption of the beer and an increase in noise level,
which peaked during their drinking time and gradually decreased as the experiment was
drawing to a close.
At the same time there were things that did not change after the consumption of the beer.
The performance from the balance tasks and the evacuations showed little to no change or
even slight improvement, which was in some ways consistent with previous experiments that
used normal alcoholic drinks to conduct similar tests.
Overall the experiment results are in many ways similar to previous experiments with alcohol
and show some deﬁnite signs of the development of drunk behaviour independently of alcohol.
Additionally, there is proof that drunkenness, whether caused by alcohol or a placebo, causes
signiﬁcant changes in cooperation, willingness to follow instructions, focus and mood in some
people and it might cause the opposite changes in others. There is no proof yet that it impairs
the overall group movement capabilities, but there is evidence that individual decision making
and even that of the group might be signiﬁcantly altered.

4. CONCLUSION
4.1
Future work
The current experiment has produced results that can be used in further investigations into
the development of drunk behaviour with the use of a placebo as well as in experiments with
actual alcohol.
Even though the current experiment shows some almost certain signs that drunk behaviour
can be generated through the use of non-alcoholic beer, further research with more partici-
pants is needed in order to solidify the claims made here and there are diﬃculties with the
test methods applied here.
It has been noticed from this and previous research that the reaction and balance tests, i.e.
the Flamingo and Up & Go tasks, require a large amount of participants in order to provide
conclusive results. This makes them less practical for such experiments, due to the diﬃculty
with recruiting participants.
On the other hand, the tests that give the most clear evidence of drunkenness have been
tasks that test focus, concentration, cognitive taught process cooperation and that provide
freedom of expression such as the maze tasks and the drawing assignment from Rask 2017.
In this experiment the balance tasks provided the opportunity to write extra comments after
completing the task and these comments provided more insight than the results from them.
Additionally, more expansive research into how such tests relate to the evacuation process
can be made in order to ﬁnd out their usefulness not only in detecting drunkenness but also
in understanding how that drunkenness might impede the evacuation process.
The results from these tests point out that decision making, concentration and cooperation
are inﬂuenced by mood, which in turn is highly inﬂuenced by outside factors. This suggests,
that if outside factors are made ideal that could make the evacuation quicker, whether there
is alcohol or not. So further research on the eﬀect of diﬀerent environments on evacuation
could be made. This could include music, lights, voice recordings, spoken messages, etc.
Alongside all this it should be noted that the nationality, socio-cultural background, gender
and age of the participants in experiments inﬂuence their behaviour too. In big cities, which
are more cosmopolitan, knowledge of the behaviour of people of large variety can be useful
when designing venues where alcohol is consumed, but in venues where the occupants are of
a more homogeneous cultural makeup the evacuation process might be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent.
This is followed by the further problem with the change of cultural behaviour. Research has
shown that drinking patterns change and some cultures even adopt the drinking patterns of
others. This rate of change might be rapid enough to make research irrelevant far too quickly
and thus making it impractical to conduct. At this point this does not seem to be a problem,
but further understanding on drinking culture and its evolution, whether through analysis
of already existing research or through experiments could provide some useful insights on
evacuation of people under the inﬂuence of alcohol.

I Danmark har det videnskabsetiske komitesystem til opgave at vurdere sundhedsvidenskabelige forsk-
ningsprojekter.  
 
Ved et sundhedsvidenskabeligt forskningsprojekt forstås projekter, der indebærer forsøg på levende-
fødte menneskelige individer, menneskelige kønsceller, der agtes anvendt til befrugtning, menneske-
lige befrugtede æg, fosteranlæg og fostre, væv, celler og arvebestanddele fra mennesker, fostre og lign. 
eller afdøde. Herunder omfattes kliniske forsøg med lægemidler på mennesker og klinisk afprøvning 
af medicinsk udstyr.

den kliniske og den socialmedicinsk-epidemiologiske forskning. Begrebet omfatter, udover forskning 
af de somatiske sygdomme, tillige de psykiatriske og de klinisk-psykologiske sygdomme og tilstands-
former. Herudover inddrages tilsvarende odontologisk og farmaceutisk forskning under begrebet.

Information on participation in a research experiment 
Experiment title: Investigation Into the Influence of Surroundings on the Devel-
opment of Drunk Behaviour During Evacuation 
 
Would you like to participate in an experiment in which we examine how people who consumed alco-
hol react during an evacuation? Responsible for the experiment is Associate Professor Anne S. Dede-
richs and master student Simeon Doychinov from the Technical university of Denmark. 
 
The experiment is conducted in the Oticon Salen at DTU’s Lyngby Campus. 
 
Before you decide whether to participate or not in the trial, you must fully understand what the trial is 
about and how we implement it. Therefore we would ask you to read this participant information care-
fully. 
 
Prior to the experiment you will be provided with information about its schedule, method and purpose. 
 
If you decide to participate in the experiment we will ask you to sign a declaration of consent. Remem-
ber that you are entitled before the experiment to whether sign or not the declaration of consent. 
 
It is voluntary to participate in the trial. You may withdraw your participation at any time without giving 
any reason. 
 
Purpose of the experiment  
The aim of the experiment is to get better knowledge of people’s behaviour and abilities while they are 
under the influence of alcohol and to examine how that may affect their evacuation in case of an 
emergency. 
 
Method 
The experiment layout is running for approximately 3 and a half hours. Before starting the experiment 
each participant is going to be given a document with information about the experiment and a consent 
form, which they have to sign in order to participate. Those who agree to participate will afterwards 
commence the experiment. 
 
During the experiment the participants would be given beer and pizza, which would be purchased from 
local suppliers. Additionally, the participants will be asked to perform three exercises and two evacua-
tions. The exercises would each involve three tasks – a logical puzzle, a Flamingo Test and an Up & 
Go test.  
 
The puzzles would measure coordination, memory, cognitive taught process and concentration. The 
flamingo test investigates your balance when you are standing on one leg with the other raised around 
30 cm off the ground. The Up & Go test investigates your reaction time combined with your balance. 
To do the test you must, from a seated position, get up as fast as you can, run around an obstacle and 
sit down on the chair again. 
 
The evacuation would consist of all the participants exiting the hall to the outside upon hearing a loud 
signal. After all have been evacuated they would be called back in for the next part of the experiment. 
 
Advantages of the experiment 
By participating in the trial, you will help to create new and better knowledge in the area of building fire 
safety and more specifically in the design of emergency evacuation design for people who have con-
sumed alcohol. Also, it can be beneficial for you to see how you react and perform when you have 
consumed alcohol and have to evacuate. With the information, gathered from this experiment, it be-
==PAGE:64==
 
 
2
comes possible to better plan the fire safety of future buildings and improve the safety of current ones 
by taking into account alcohol impaired occupants. 
 
 
Side effects, risk, complications and drawbacks 
During the experiment there are different risks and side effects. In the balance and reaction tests, 
there is a risk of losing your balance and of falling. This risk is also present during the evacuation. Un-
der the influence of alcohol you will experience loss of balance, change of your eyesight and lack of 
concentration, which may cause discomfort for some people. Consuming alcohol might cause hango-
vers. You are asked to give information about your experience during the experiments. 
 
If a person is injured the experiment will be stopped or interrupted. 
 
Exclusion from the experiment 
The investigator may exclude a participant from the experiment, at any time, if their behavior is con-
sidered inappropriate towards the other participants or is somehow deemed harmful to the experiment. 
Furthermore, the experiment is interrupted if there are persons who suffered injuries.  
 
If the participant is excluded from the experiment, this person must leave the experiment area. 
 
Access to test results 
The results of the experiment would be analyzed by the organizers for research purposes in relation 
the current experiment. The participant personal data and video recordings would not be used for pur-
poses, unrelated to this experiment without the consent of the participants. 
 
When the experiment is completed and the data is analyzed you will receive information about the re-
sults. Experimental results may be published on relevant scientific journals. If you require information 
about this, please state it on the consent form. 
 
Insurance 
Technical University of Denmark has no insurance to cover any injuries of the participants in the ex-
periment. Participation is at your own risk! 
 
To learn more about the study please feel free to contact Simeon Doychinov and Anne Dederichs and 
visit the experiment Facebook event page, which can be found by the name “Experiment: The Influ-
ence of Alcohol on Evacuation”.
