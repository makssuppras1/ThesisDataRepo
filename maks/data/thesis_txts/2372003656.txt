Abstract
Calibration of distributed hydrological models has traditionally been based on discharge measure-
ments, however this approach neglects the spatial component of the hydrological model response.
Remote sensing data can be used for calibration and validation of distributed hydrological models
as they observe spatially distributed components of the hydrological cycle. The present study
aims at optimising the simulation of spatial evapotranspiration patterns of a distributed model,
the national water resources model for Denmark (DK-model) for the subdomain of Southern
Jutland, by using remote sensing data. A new parameterization of Leaf Area Index, root depth
and crop coeﬃcient based on remote sensing data of Normalized Diﬀerence Vegetation Index
and a soil property map are introduced in order to describe the spatio-temporal variability in
evapotranspiration. The calibration includes comparison of the model output with evapotranspi-
ration patterns derived from remote sensing using the Two-Layer Energy Balance model and
an innovative objective function for spatial eﬃciency. The updated model is compared to a
baseline using standard parameterization of LAI, root depth and crop coeﬃcient. The main
hypothesis of the study is that the spatial patterns of evapotranspiration in the remote sensing
based data are closer to observations than the simulation of the DK-model enabling its use for
calibration. The new parameterization and calibration procedure improves the simulated spatial
patterns of evapotranspiration considerably over the calibration period compared to the baseline.
The new parameterization of LAI, root depth and crop coeﬃcient adds spatial features to the
evapotranspiration output of the DK-model which are found in the TSEB evapotranspiration
data. The results point to a model structural deﬁciency in the two-layer method for calculating
evapotranspiration in the DK-model given there is a considerable fraction of the evapotranspira-
tion which is not governed by vegetation that the DK-model is not able to simulate. The model
performance on discharge and groundwater potential was comparable with the baseline. The
new parameterization and calibration procedure results in a redistribution of evapotranspiration
and groundwater recharge within the domain while the mean annual evapotranspiration and
groundwater recharge changed by only 4.2% and 6.2% respectively compared to the baseline.
The study illustrates that a distributed hydrological model can be constrained by remote sensing
data and that the simulated spatial patterns of evapotranspiration can be considerably improved
with a very limited trade-oﬀin discharge and groundwater potential simulations.

Introduction
1
1 Introduction
Hydrological models have become an essential tool in water resources management for improved
understanding of basin processes and quantiﬁcation and prediction of hydrological responses
under changing conditions. Hydrological models are widely used for examining the eﬀects of land
use changes, climate variability with regards to ﬂoods and droughts, sustainable groundwater
extraction, operations of irrigation, simulation of sediment and water quality processes at ﬁeld
and catchment scale among others. In order to accommodate the increasing complexity of water
management problems, more processes have been included and more interactions accounted for
in hydrological models.
Fully distributed hydrological models are spatially explicit, physically-based mathematical models
of the complete hydrological system. The principal advantage of distributed hydrological models
compared to e.g. lumped models is their ability to simulate the state variables, such as soil mois-
ture content and groundwater level, at internal points of the catchment (Beven, 1996; Butts and
Graham, 2014). According to Refsgaard (1996) there are three major problems that in many cases
are best addressed by distributed models: (i) eﬀects of changes due to human interference (e.g.
land use change and groundwater extraction), (ii) water quality and soil erosion modelling, (iii)
research on hydrological processes. There is a wide variety of applied distributed models available;
MIKE SHE (Refsgaard and Storm, 1995), HydroGeoSphere (Therrien et al., 2006) and SHETRAN
(Bathurst et al., 1995) are examples of distributed physically based models. TOPMODEL (Beven
et al., 1984), SWAT (Arnold et al., 1993) and HBV (Bergstr¨om, 1995) are more surface water
oriented and ﬁnally MODFLOW (Harbaugh, 2005) and FEFLOW (Trefry and Muﬀels, 2007) are
pure groundwater models (for full review see Refsgaard J.C. and Storm B. and Clausen T. (2010)).
The disadvantages of distributed hydrological models have been discussed excessively in literature
(Beven, 1996, 2001; Grayson et al., 2002) including problems with the representation of scale,
heterogeneity and equiﬁnality. It can not be assured that the governing equations which have
originally been designed for point-scale are physically valid on grid scale and it is diﬃcult to
estimate eﬀective model parameter values for those equations. While the models are overpa-
rameterized for the purpose of estimating discharge, the model ability to simulate internal state
variables is largely untested. Equiﬁnality refers to the problem that here will always be the
possibility of multiple descriptions that will do equally well in calibration (Beven, 1996). Finally,
if the model objective is only to reproduce catchment outﬂows, then lumped and semi-distributed
conceptual modelling approaches are more suitable (Butts and Graham, 2014). However, in spite
of the disadvantages and uncertainties related to the nature of distributed hydrological models,
the practical problems that they are applied to remain. Therefore the challenge is to strengthen
the conﬁdence of the simulations and identify the limitations of the models.
Hydrological models require calibration due to incomplete knowledge about the physical processes
of the hydrological cycle, limitations in measuring methods and heterogeneity of the hydrological
system. The traditional calibration and validation framework for hydrological models has mainly
been based on a comparison of observed versus simulated run-oﬀat the outlet of the catchment
(Refsgaard, 2000). Dependent on the type of model and the modelling objective, discharge
calibration can be a suitable type of calibration, e.g. for a rainfall-runoﬀmodel with the objective
of reproducing the hydrograph at the inlet to a hydropower plant. It has been pointed out in
literature that the traditional calibration paradigm is a weak test of the adequacy of a distributed
model that aim at simulating the internal responses of a catchment (Refsgaard, 2000; Beven,

1989; Grayson et al., 2002; Koch, 2016; Stisen et al., 2008). Traditional calibration on a limited
number of discharge stations lumps all hydrological processes together which makes it unsuitable
for laying conﬁdence on the predicted spatial variability of hydrological processes within the
catchment. In the light of inreasing awareness of the importance of spatial patterns in the
functioning of hydrological systems (Vereecken et al., 2016) and in order to have conﬁdence in the
spatially distributed simulations, it is necessary to expand the traditional calibration paradigm
for distributed models to a more adequate framework that includes spatial observational data.
As a result of the progress of Earth observation techniques, a large amount of spatial data
(including land surface temperature, land use, digital elevation models, soil and vegetation
parameters) have become readily available to the scientiﬁc community at increasing resolution.
These data sets provide independent spatial observations of hydrological variables that can
complement the temporal data traditionally used for calibration. In the past decades, signiﬁcant
progress has been made in integrating remote sensing data and hydrological modelling (review by
Xu et al. (2014)). Guzinski et al. (2015) applies satellite data for estimating evapotranspiration.
Boegh et al. (2004) and Kite and Pietroniro (1996) provide examples where remote sensing
data is used in the parameterization of hydrological models. Immerzeel and Droogers (2008)
calibrated a distributed hydrological model based on satellite evapotranspiration and Li et al.
(2009) used pattern information of remote sensing evapotranspiration data for calibration of a
groundwater model. Remote sensing data is surrogate data, meaning that the parameters are
measured indirectly and calculated based on a theoretical or empirical model. Thus using remote
sensing data for model calibration and validation is essentially a model-to-model comparison.
However in spite of the data uncertainty, satellite based evapotranspiration data contain valuable
pattern information (Mascaro et al., 2015).
1.1
Thesis Objectives
In a nutshell, this thesis aims to parameterize and calibrate a distributed hydrological model
using remote sensing data in order to optimise the spatial patterns of actual evapotranspiration
(referred to as evapotranspiration from this point on). The model applied in the study is the
national water resources model for Denmark (DK-model) developed by GEUS (Geological Survey
of Denmark and Greenland) for the sub-domain of Southern Jutland. The DK-model is a
physically-based distributed hydrological model based on the MIKE SHE code developed by DHI.
The following steps have been completed in order to to achieve this objective:
• Evaluate spatial performance of the DK-model on simulating evapotranspiration patterns
compared to remote sensing derived evapotranspiration data.
• Develop a modiﬁed DK-model which includes a spatially variable parameterization of
LAI, root depth and crop coeﬃcent in order to describe the spatio-temporal variability of
evapotranspiration.
• Calibrate the modiﬁed DK-model using an innovative objective function for pattern com-
parison against remote sensing derived evapotranspiration data in order to improve model
performance on spatial evapotranspiration patterns.
The parameterization of the modiﬁed DK-model is inspired by the method applied by Mendiguren
et al. (in review, 2017). Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017) evaluates the simulated spatial
evapotranspiration pattern of the DK-model to an evapotranspiration dataset based on the two
source energy balance model (TSEB) which is driven mainly by satellite remote sensing data.
The present study expands the methodology in two ways: 1) diﬀerent ways of parameterizing
root depth and crop coeﬃcient are investigated and 2) the model is calibrated against the TSEB

Thesis structure
The thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 describes the DK-model and the modules related
to evapotranspiration calculation along with a brief description of the study area. Chapter 3
explains the methodology applied to meet the objectives of the study. First the data applied in

The DK-model and study area

2 The DK-model and study area
The DK-model is the national water resources model for Denmark developed and maintained
by the Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS)(Højbjerg et al., 2015; Refsgaard
et al., 2011). The overall purpose of the model is to provide a basis for the assessment of
the total available drinking water resource of Denmark, including its temporal variation and
regional distribution. The DK-model has been used for investigating numerous issues including
the development of groundwater head level and long-term eﬀects of groundwater extraction
strategies, climate change impacts on the water cycle in Denmark and modelling of large scale
nitrate transport (review in GEUS (2017)). The DK-model covers the entire country and is
divided into 7 subdomains (Figure 1) that can be further downscaled for local applications.
For the present study subdomain 4 (Southern Jutland) has been selected. In this chapter
only the model components that are universal for the models developed for the purpose of the
study are explained. Modiﬁcations to the model are explained in Chapter 3. The following sec-
tions describe the model system, applications, selected model components and the case study area.

2.1
Model system
The modelling framework for the DK-model is the MIKE SHE/MIKE HYDRO code which
comprise a deterministic fully distributed and physically-based model complex for the simulation
of the freshwater cycle. The MIKE SHE/MIKE HYDRO code is considered an appropriate
tool for modelling the water cycle of Denmark because it integrates groundwater and surface
water with a direct coupling between the two systems. The model code is well-established for
applications in Denmark (GEUS, 2017; Højbjerg et al., 2015; Refsgaard J.C. and Storm B. and
Clausen T., 2010) and has been used in several cases abroad (Graham and Butts, 2005).
The MIKE SHE code was originally developed by the Danish Hydraulic Institute (DHI), the
British Institute of Hydrology and SOGREAH (France) (Abott et al., 1986).
The aim of
the model tool was to ”produce a general, physically-based, distributed modelling system for
constructing and running models of all or any part of the land phase of the hydrological cycle
for any geographical area” Butts and Graham (2014). Since then, the code has been further
developed and extended by DHI. MIKE HYDRO is also a DHI developed model tool. In the
present study, the MIKE SHE and MIKE HYDRO 2016 releases are applied. MIKE SHE covers
the major processes in the hydrological cycle and their interactions (Figure 2). In the model

2.1.1
Evapotranspiration calculation using the two-layer method
The two-layer method considers the entire unsaturated zone to consist of two layers representing
average conditions. Figure 3 illustrates the two-layer method and the processes of inﬁltration
and evapotransiration. The evapotranspiration calculation is based on a water-balance approach
presented by Yan and Smith (1994) to provide an estimate of the actual evapotranspiration
and the amount of groundwater recharge to the saturated zone. The method provides a simple
coupling between the unsaturated and saturated zone, which accounts for the variation in
evapotranspiration occurring as a result of varying depth to the groundwater table. If suﬃcient
water is available in the root zone, the water will be available for evapotranspiration. The
following description is based on DHI (2009b).
Figure 3: Schematic illustration of the 2-layer model. Inﬁltration processes are indicated with
blue arrows. Evapotranspiration processes are indicated with red arrows from the storages: 1)
canopy, 2) ponded water, 3) layer 1 of unsaturated zone, 4) saturated zone. Modiﬁed from
Refsgaard et al. (2011).
Layer 1 represents the evapotranspiration extinction depth which is the maximum depth where
evapotranspiration can be removed by the roots from the saturated zone. It extends from
the ground surface down to the total depth of the root zone and capillary fringe, which is
the subsurface layer in which groundwater seeps up by capillary action to ﬁll pores. Layer 2
appears when the groundwater table is below evapotranspiration extinction depth and there
is not evapotranspiration is taking place from this layer. The amount of water available for
evapotranspiration is determined by the soil moisture content. As the layer is assumed to be
homogeneous the soil moisture content is calculated as an average for the entire unsaturated
zone. The maximum amount of evapotranspiration ETmax that can be removed within one time
step is:
ETmax = ETrate × ∆t
(1)
where the evapotranspiration rate ETrate is equal to the crop reference evapotranspiration rate:
ETrate = ETcrop = ETref × Kc
(2)
ETref is the reference evapotranspiration and Kc is the crop coeﬃcient. The reference evapotran-
spiration represents the rate of evapotranspiration from a reference surface with an unlimited

amount of water and it is calculated from meterological data. The crop coeﬃcient is a factor
which adjusts the reference evapotranspiration for diﬀerent vegetation types (DHI, 2009b). Evap-
otranspiration in the two-layer method is removed from the diﬀerent storages in the following
order until the maximum evapotranspiration ETmax is satisﬁed or no more water is available for
evapotranspiration, see Figure 3:
1. Canopy (interception storage) (ETcanopy)
2. Ponded water (ETponded)

4. Saturated zone (ETSZ) if the plant roots can reach the water table.
The description of evapotranspiration from snow is neglected in this study as the focus is on

ETactual = ETcanopy + ETponded + ETUZ + ETSZ
(3)

present study (to be explained in Chapter 3).
1. Evapotranspiration from canopy
Evapotranspiration is removed from the canopy until the canopy storage is exhausted or ETmax
is satisﬁed:
ETcanopy = ETrate × ∆t
(4)

Interception is the process which retains

Sint,max = Cint × LAI
(5)

ETponded = ETrate × ∆t

In the case where the water table is at or above the evapotranspiration extinction depth, i.e.
the water table is in the root zone, the unsaturated zone consists of layer 1 only. The soil
moisture content is a function of depth to the groundwater table (Figure 4). Evapotranspiration
is controlled by the soil moisture, which will vary between a minimum θmin and a maximum
θmax depending on the depth to water table. θmin is the minimum water content possible when
evapotranspiration is occurring . θmax is the maximum water content that would be present
if evapotranspiration is inactive (DHI, 2009b). θmax decreases linearly with the groundwater
head level from fully saturated conditions (groundwater head is at terrain) to a water content
equivalent to the ﬁeld capacity θFC which is equivalent to a free draining soil (groundwater
head is below extinction depth and the soil is drained). θmin similarly varies linearly down to
a water content equivalent to the wilting point θWP which is the water content when plants
start to wilt as they can no longer remove water from the soils (DHI, 2009b). The diﬀerence in
water content between θmin and θmax is available for evapotranspiration from layer 1 (Figure 4).
Evapotranspiration is removed until ETmax is satisﬁed or the average water content reaches the
wilting point (DHI, 2009b).
ETUZ = ETrate × FETUZ × ∆t

plant speciﬁc water content is reached. At this point the rate is restricted as a function of the
decreasing soil moisture content i.e. FETUZ decreases below 1 to a speciﬁed value. Put diﬀerently,
the average soil moisture content of the entire soil column decreases as the water table drops
because the upper part of the column is draining to the ﬁeld capacity.

If the water table is below the extinction depth layer 2 is added to the model of the unsaturated
zone, extending from the bottom of layer 1 down to the water table (Figure 3). Evapotranspira-
tion is not removed from the second layer and thus the soil moisture is mostly equal to the ﬁeld

To summarise, evapotranspiration from agricultural lands is governed by the soil parameters
for the unsaturated zone together with root depth, crop coeﬃcient and LAI. The depth to
groundwater table controls the amount of soil moisture available for evapotranspiration.
It is important to emphasize that while the model is applying homogeneous conditions, the
physical world is characterized by a high degree of heterogeneity in e.g. ﬁeld capacity. The
two-layer method is suited for areas where the water table is shallow and where the actual
evapotranspiration rate is close to the reference rate (DHI, 2009a). The two-layer method is
chosen over the Kristensen and Jensen method which is more physically correct but which has a
signiﬁcantly higher computation time.
2.1.2
Saturated zone
The ﬁnite diﬀerence method is applied for describing the saturated ﬂow.
It gives a three-
dimensional description of a heterogeneous aquifer system with varying free and artesian condi-
tions. The groundwater ﬂow is solved by combining Darcy’s equation for ﬂow in a porous medium
with the continuity equation. The groundwater system is divided into grid cells and solved
through iteration using the PCG2 solver. Solving the groundwater ﬂow includes interaction with
groundwater and the remaining components of the cycle: overland ﬂow, root zone, rivers and lakes,
drains and groundwater extraction. The water table is adjusted after each evapotranspiration
calculation step. The hydraulic properties of the geological units are the governing parameters
for the groundwater ﬂow, in particular the hydraulic conductivity K and the thickness of the
geological layers (GEUS, 2017).
2.1.3
Overland and channel ﬂow
Overland ﬂow is generated either when precipitation exceeds inﬁltration and starts ponding on
the surface, or when the groundwater table exceeds the terrain. Once water is available for runoﬀ
the direction and volume of the ﬂow is governed by topography, surface properties and loss from
inﬁltration along the ﬂow path. When the water reaches a stream it is subsequently routed as
channel ﬂow by MIKE Hydro (Højbjerg et al., 2015). The simple hydraulic routing option is
chosen for the description of overland ﬂow in the surface water system.
2.2
Selection and description of study area
Due to limitations in time and computation power, it was decided to work on only one of the
seven subdomains of the DK-model. The objective of the study is to develop a method for
optimization of the spatial patterns of evapotranspiration of the DK-model. For that purpose
a model area that has a good performance on groundwater and discharge and where spatial
patterns are observed in the remote sensing estimates of evapotranspiration is needed. Southern
Jutland (subdomain 4) fulﬁll these requirements (GEUS, 2017).

3.2
Remote sensing derived evapotranspiration using the TSEB
Model
In the present study a remote sensing derived evapotranspiration dataset is applied for calibration.
The dataset applied was ﬁrst presented by Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017). For a detailed
explanation of the data retrieval the reader is referred to this paper.
The evapotranspiration dataset is calculated using the Two Source Energy Balance Model (TSEB)
proposed by (Norman et al., 1995) which is mainly driven by remote sensing data. The TSEB
method calculates the energy ﬂuxes for soil and vegetation separately. The ﬂuxes are estimated
based on land surface temperature, air temperature, LAI, vegetation height, albedo and fraction
of green vegetation derived from MODIS data. The climate forcing data used is horizontal
wind speed, 2m air temperature, surface solar radiation and long wave incoming radiation. The
TSEB method applies an iterative approach to satisfy the energy budget by ﬁtting the canopy
temperature and soil temperature (Norman et al., 1995). Data from three eddy covariance ﬂux
towers located in Central Jutland that represent the three main land cover types in Denmark
(agriculture, forest and meadow) are used as a reference to perform a sensitivity analysis and
calibration of four parameters in the TSEB model. The evapotranspiration estimates are instan-
taneous values that are converted to daily evapotranspiration values based on the assumption
that the evaporative fraction is constant throughout the day (Mendiguren et al., in review, 2017).
According to (Mendiguren et al., in review, 2017) the TSEB data is highly uncertain with respect
to the absolute values and is therefore only applicable for pattern comparison. The ﬁnal product
has a 1 day temporal resolution and 1km spatial resolution. The modiﬁcations made to the
original dataset presented in Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017) for the purpose of this study
are explained in the following paragraph.
The focus of the study is the growing season from April to September. The remaining months are
excluded in the calibration process due to a high degree of cloud cover and the fact that actual
evapotranspiration is very close to potential evapotranspiration during these months (energy
limited conditions and low potential evapotranspiration). The evapotranspiration dataset is
converted to mean monthly evapotranspiration maps for April-September. Ideally, only data
from cloudless conditions should be included in order to evaluate the evapotranspiration pattern.
However due to the climate in Denmark this is infeasible. Therefore a threshold of 70% cloudless
conditions was applied to the data. Diﬀerent thresholds were tested, and at 70% the spatial data
coverage for each month was suﬃciently uniform across the model domain. For each grid cell in
the mean monthly maps a second threshold is applied: there has to be at least 5 measurements
in a grid cell for it to be included.
The ﬁnal monthly mean evapotranspiration maps are shown in Figure 7. It is evident that there
is a clear East-West gradient in evapotranspiration during these months. Evapotranspiration
rates are up to three times higher in the East compared to the West. The small areas with
low evapotranspiration rates corresponds with areas with coniferous forest (see land use map in
Figure 5).

purpose of this study, a few simpliﬁcations have been introduced in order to reduce computation
time:
• The routing part of the model has been transferred from the MIKE 11 to MIKE Hydro

• The number of geological layers have been reduced from 12 to 7.

of the model have been merged, which is not expected to inﬂuence the evapotranspiration pattern.

3.4.1
Leaf Area Index (LAI)
Leaf area index, LAI (m2/m2) is a widely used parameter that describes the abundance of
vegetation. LAI cannot be measured directly from satellite remote sensing, however literature
shows that LAI and the commonly used normalised diﬀerence vegetation index (NDVI) are
strongly and non-linearly correlated (Sellers et al., 1992; Boegh et al., 2004). NDVI is a commonly
used remote sensing measure of vegetation ”greenness” or photosynthetic activity ranging between
0 and 1. It exploits the characteristics of the spectral signature for green vegetation which has
low reﬂectance in the red spectrum and high reﬂectance in the near infrared spectrum compared
to other land uses. In the study by Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017) LAI is derived from
NDVI using the empirical relationship:
LAI = 0.0633e(5.524×NDV I)
(9)
The NDVI dataset applied for calculating LAI is the MCD43B4 composite NDVI product from
the Aqua and Terra satellites with a spatial resolution of 500m and a temporal resolution of 1 day.
All data has been processed into one annual curve for each pixel in the domain. This minimizes
the noise but removes any inter-annual variability. A detailed description of the data processing is
presented in Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017). The assumption is that inter-annual variability
is similar in size to the noise and it is prioritised to keep the overall annual dynamics and the
spatial pattern in the data. The resulting mean annual dataset is repeated for each year over the
entire simulation period. The monthly mean maps of NDVI for April-September is shown in
Figure 9. The NDVI is highest during May and June. In July-September NDVI decrease in the
Eastern part of the domain. This could be due to earlier harvest, wilting and decay of plants.

Crop coefﬁcient
Spatially and temporally distributed crop coeﬃcient maps are generated based on the LAI
data presented in Section 3.4.1. The crop coeﬃcient is a bulk parameter that accounts for the
diﬀerent characteristics that distinguish actual vegetation from the reference grass assumed
for the reference evapotranspiration. Assuming that this diﬀerence can be described by LAI
alone, the crop coeﬃcient is calculated as a function of LAI following the procedure described for
natural vegetation in (Allen et al., 1998):
Kc = Kc,min + (Kc,max −Kc,min) × (1 −e−a×LAI)
(10)
where Kc,min and Kc,max are the minimum and maximum values for crop coeﬃcient and a
controls the slope.
3.4.3
Root depth
Spatially and temporally distributed maps of root depth are generated using a similar approach as
applied by Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017) and Koch (2016). The root depth of a vegetation
cover depends both on the vegetation type and the soil type, the soil type being the most
important factor (Olesen and Heidmann, 2002). One of the key variables that deﬁne soil types
is the clay content. Plant roots can penetrate more easily and thereby deeper in clayey soils
compared to soils with a higher sand content (Olesen and Heidmann, 2002). Clay dominated soils
furthermore have a higher water holding capacity compared to sandy soils. These two properties
is the basis for assuming that a high clay content is associated with vegetation with deeper root
depth and ultimately a higher evapotranspiration rate than soils with a low clay content. The
rooting depth of a crop will normally vary over the season as the crops grow and wilter. For this
reason NDVI is also included in the parameterization of root depth as a measure of the temporal
and spatial changes in vegetation.

function, the TSEB dataset is assumed to be suﬃcient at constraining it.
The root depth parameter is calculated as an eﬀective parameter and can not be considered
physical. The actual root depth of many plants such as trees do not vary over short time
scales, however the transpiration of these plants follow a seasonal variation. The root depth
parametrization here should therefore be seen as eﬀective paramter of the depth of an equivalent,
fully active root system rather than an estimation of the actual physical root depth.
3.5
Calibration scheme
The model is calibrated inversely with the software tool PEST (Doherty, J., 2005). The standard
calibration approach is a combined groundwater potential and discharge calibration. For the
modiﬁed DK-model, a new objective function is introduced in order to optimize the model against
the spatial pattern of evapotranspiration.

The most signiﬁcant advantage of the GML algorithm is that the gradient based approach makes

was prioritized to be able to run many calibrations in order to test diﬀerent modiﬁcations of the
DK-model. The DK-model has been calibrated extensively in the past and therefore the initial
parameter values provide a very good starting point for the calibration procedure. The main
disadvantage of the GML algorithm is that it is sensitive to local minima and it may not be able
to reach the global optimum of the DK-model which is a highly non-linear model. To overcome
this problem global optimization algorithms such as the shuﬄe complex evolution algorithm are
required. This was considered out of the scope of the project.

Evapotranspiration
The spatial evapotranspiration pattern is evaluated using the metric for spatial eﬃciency SPAEF.
The SPAEF metric was introduced for the ﬁrst time in the paper ”The role of spatial pattern
information in diagnosing hydrologic model behaviour and developing appropriate model cali-
bration strategy” by Demirel et al. (2017) submitted to the Water Resources Research journal
in May 2017 (manuscript ID 2016WR019683R1). SPAEF is a bias-insensitive metric which
evaluates three diﬀerent components: α, β and γ:
SPAEF = 1 −
q
(α −1)2 + (β −1)2 + (γ −1)2
(20)
In the calculation of α, β and γ, the mean monthly maps of evapotranspiration output from the
DK-model are considered simulations and the mean monthly TSEB evapotranspiration maps are
considered observations.
α is the Pearson correlation coeﬃcient between observations and simulations. It quantiﬁes the
strength and direction of the linear relationship.
β is the fraction of coeﬃcient of variations between observations and simulations which quantiﬁes
spatial variability. If β is equal to 1, the variability in relation to the mean is the same for observed
and simulated evapotranspiration. If β is bigger than 1, observed data has a higher variability
than the simulated. If β is less than 1, the simulated evapotranspiration has the higher variability.
γ is the fraction of histogram intersection which is a measure of the similarity of the z-scores of
simulations and observations. It is calculated based on histograms with 100 bins of the observed
and simulated mean monthly evapotranspiration maps that have been standardized to a mean of

0 and a standard deviation of 1.
The optimal value of SPAEF for a perfect pattern match (and the value that the calibration is
optimising towards) is 1.
The spatial eﬃciency is calculated by comparing the mean monthly evapotranspiration maps
for TSEB and the DK-model for the months April-September during the calibration period.
The mean monthly evapotranspiration maps for the DK-model are calculated only from days
that match the selected days for the TSEB data which have been ﬁltered according to the 70%
cloudless and min. 5 pixels criteria as explained in Section 3.2.
Additional to SPAEF the root mean squared error is calculated for evapotranspiration. RMSEET
was only applied to a few calibrations as it was found to be unsuitable given that the TSEB
evapotranspiration dataset is biased and should only be used for pattern calibration (Mendiguren
et al., in review, 2017).
3.5.3
Calibration strategies
The models are calibrated for the years 2000-2006 with a 10 year warm-up period for 1990-2000
in order for slow eﬀects of parameter changes to show. The calibration stopping criteria are as
follows:
• Number of maximum optimisation iterations = 15
• Relative objective function reduction triggering termination = 0.01
• Number of iterations since last drop in objective function = 3
• Maximum relative parameter change triggering termination = 0.01
The calibrated values from the original DK-model for subdomain 4 are used as initial parameter
values in this study. These parameter values are expected to be better start values and thereby
save time in the calibration process. The initial parameter values and selected upper and lower
bounds for the new parameters introduced can be seen in Appendix D.
Table 4 shows an overview of the three main calibration strategies of which the results will
be presented in Chapter 4 and the diﬀerent weights applied to the objective functions. An
overview of all calibrations carried out in the present study can be found in Appendix F. First,
the baseline model is calibrated according to the traditional calibration scheme against discharge
and groundwater potential. The baseline ET calibration is a re-calibration of the baseline
model which includes the remote sensing evapotranspiration pattern observations and SPAEF
objective function without changing the model parameterization. Finally, the modiﬁed DK-model
with the new parameterization for root depth, LAI and crop coeﬃcient is calibrated against
discharge, groundwater potential and evapotranspiration pattern with special emphasis on the
latter (SPAEF weight=12). All calibrations have equal weighting of groundwater potentials and
discharge. RMSEET was included in the baseline ET, however it was decided to exclude it in
further calibrations in order to evaluate evapotranspiration on patterns only.

Discussion

5 Discussion
This chapter is divided into ﬁve sections. First, the four main issues in relation to the study
are discussed. This includes a discussion of the validity of the TSEB evapotranspiration data
for calibration, an evaluation of the spatial patterns in evapotranspiration observed for the
diﬀerent models, the impact of spatial optimization on other model outputs and a discussion of
the advantages and limitations in the developed parameterization of the modiﬁed DK-model.
Finally, potential for future analysis is brieﬂy discussed.
5.1
Evaluation of the TSEB evapotranspiration dataset for cali-
bration
The main assumption made in the study is that evapotranspiration derived with the TSEB model
provides an independent measurement of evapotranspiration that can be used for calibration.
The TSEB evapotranspiration dataset has the advantage of being more physically-based than
e.g. the SEBAL method (Jones and Vaughan, 2010) and can be considered more closely tied
to direct observations than the water-balance approach in the DK-model. Nonetheless, it must
be acknowledged that it still is a model. In addition to this, the fact that NDVI is used as an
input to both the DK-model and the TSEB model further challenges the assumption of the
TSEB data being an independent measurement of evapotranspiration. However since the TSEB
model is more sensitive to land surface temperature than NDVI and the approach for calculating
evapotranspiration is completely diﬀerent from the DK-model, it is considered legitimate to use
the TSEB data for calibration of the DK-model.
Given the uncertainty of the TSEB data and based on the ﬁndings by Mendiguren et al. (in
review, 2017) it was decided to use the TSEB data for calibration of pattern alone, using the
bias insensitive SPAEF metric as objective function. The results show that the TSEB data has a
negative bias, i.e. it is underestimating the evapotranspiration rate, compared to the DK-model.
The main diﬀerence between the DK-model and the TSEB model is that the former uses a water
balance approach and the latter an energy-balance approach. Thus they are expected to give
diﬀerent results, especially considering the large uncertainties and assumptions associated with
the respective methods.
The parameterization of the modiﬁed DK-model rests on the assumption that vegetation in
soils with a high clay content have deeper roots than soils with a low clay content. In the
two-layer method this correlation between clay fraction and root depth is translated into higher
evapotranspiration rates for soils with high clay content (Eastern part of Southern Jutland) and
lower evapotranspiration rates for low clay content soils (Western part of Southern Jutland).
This East-West gradient in evapotranspiration rate is the most dominant pattern observed in
the TSEB evapotranspiration dataset. However the TSEB evapotranspiration data has not been
validated with regards to diﬀerences in soil types and diﬀerences between Eastern and Western
Denmark. The reference data from the eddy covariance ﬂux towers that are used for calibrating
the TSEB parameters are assumed to be representative for each land use cover (agriculture,
forest and wetland) on a national scale. This assumption is inadequate given that the ﬂux sites
do not diﬀer in terms of soil type and other variables that can aﬀect the plant response to
evapotranspiration. All ﬂux towers were placed on sandy soils in the central part of Jutland
(Mendiguren et al., in review, 2017). Additional ﬂux towers would have to be installed at sites

with diﬀerences in soil properties and preferably in East and West in order to validate the
East-West evapotranspiration gradient observed in the TSEB data.
5.2
Evaluation of spatial evapotranspiration patterns
The study shows that the simulation of spatial evapotranspiration patterns using the modiﬁed
DK-model could be optimised compared to the baseline, as the SPAEF metric increased for all
months considered (April-September). The simulation of evapotranspiration pattern improved
as a result of two things: 1) adding the SPAEF objective function and the TSEB dataset to
the calibration scheme and 2) modifying the model by replacing the standard curves for LAI,
root depth and crop coeﬃcient with spatially distributed estimates derived from remote sensing
NDVI and clay fraction data.
Under the assumption that the TSEB dataset is a true representation of the actual evapotranspi-
ration pattern it is concluded that the baseline model is incomplete with regards to simulating
the spatial distribution of evapotranspiration. The zonal approach used in the baseline model is
based on land use and crop types which has a pre-deﬁned temporal dynamics. Compared to the
TSEB evapotranspiration pattern it is evident that the baseline model is unable to simulate the
spatio-temporal complexity of the natural system.
Comparing the evapotranspiration patterns for the baseline, baseline ET and the modiﬁed
DK-model there are a number of things that stand out. The evapotranspiration rates of forests
in the baseline model are very high compared to surroundings. The TSEB data shows the exact
opposite pattern, as forests have very low evapotranspiration rates. It would be interesting to
look into why the evapotranspiration rates for forests are low in TSEB data as this contradicts
the parameterization of the DK-model. The highest SPAEF values obtained is 0.37 (1 being the
highest possible). The SPAEF objective function consists of three components, and therefore
two patterns that visually look very diﬀerent can obtain the spatial eﬃciency. In order to
estimate how diﬀerent patterns are changing from one calibration to the other, the values for the
components of the SPAEF should be analysed.
5.3
Effects of spatial evapotranspiration pattern optimization on
other model outputs
The eﬀects of introducing the spatial pattern optimization (model parameterization and calibra-
tion) on other model outputs is evaluated based on the performance on discharge and groundwater
potential simulations, annual evapotranspiration and groundwater recharge.
Introducing an additional objective function to a calibration is expected to compensate the
remaining objective functions. In spite of this, the model performance of the modiﬁed DK-model
on discharge and groundwater potential is comparable to the baseline. The NSE and water
balance errors show that there is only a minor trade-oﬀin simulating the discharge. The mean
error and RMSE show that there is no trade-oﬀin simulating the groundwater potential. The
recharge to groundwater mainly occurs during winter months and is thus not expected to change
signiﬁcantly compared to the baseline model. This explains why the trade-oﬀfor parameters
such as discharge and groundwater head are not signiﬁcantly aﬀected. This corresponds to
the ﬁndings by Demirel et al. (2017). This is also evident from the sensitivity analysis of the
modiﬁed DK-model which shows that the evapotranspiration parameters are only sensitive to

of the DK-model for nitrate modelling. The eﬀect of a certain nitrate loading on the nitrate
concentration in the groundwater is calculated based on the ﬂux to the groundwater. Thus a
change in the groundwater recharge (as shown in Figure 34) could potentially have an impact on
the estimated nitrate concentration.
5.4
Development and limitations of proposed parameterization
The ﬁnal parameterization of the modiﬁed DK-model as presented in Section 4.2 was developed
based on numerous calibrations. In this section the key ﬁndings during this iterative process are
explained and discussed along with the limitations of the parameterization and suggestions for
improvement.
The transfer function for root depth was designed to be ﬂexible regarding the shape of the
curve relating clay fraction to root depth, using the two parameters c and m. The optimal
parameter set for simulating the contrast and pattern in evapotranspiration would then be found
through calibration. Figure 36 illustrates that the function is indeed able to shift the contrast
and saturation point of evapotranspiration pattern as a result of the root depth function. Figure
36a-b illustrates a concave root depth function (c=20, m=0.01) which gives a relatively uniform
evapotranspiration pattern as it reaches the maximum root depth at a low clay fraction. Figure
36c-d illustrates an s-shaped root depth (c=40, m=0.2) which enhances the contrast in root depth.

Limitations and suggestions for improvement
In the parameterization of the modiﬁed DK-model the functions for root depth and crop coeﬃ-
cient are applied for the entire model domain. This approach neglects diﬀerences in land use
and therefore the modiﬁed DK-model is unable to simulate the low evapotranspiration rates in
areas with forest which is observed in the TSEB data. Furthermore, it is assumed that the root
depth is a function of clay fraction and NDVI alone and that they are appropriate proxies of
soil type and vegetation. The soil is characterized based solely on clay fraction and other soil
properties such as the amount of organic matter is neglected. Diﬀerences in crop types and the
impact of human activities such as harvesting, changes in irrigation and the use of fertilizers are
not taken into account. The fact that the clay fraction map and the TSEB data shows similar
patterns could also be caused by areas with sandy soils and low clay fraction coinciding with
lower vegetation cover and agricultural activity.

A separate parameterization for April-June and July-September for root depth and crop coeﬃcient
was chosen because calibrations with a common function for all months showed a trade-oﬀin
SPAEF performance between the two time periods. The reason for this is that the NDVI
pattern is reversed in July-September and thereby the opposite of the evapotranspiration pattern
observed in the TSEB data (Figure 38). Therefore the root depth in the modiﬁed DK-model is
parameterized based on NDVI and clay fraction for April-June whereas July-September is based
on clay fraction only. However the performance on spatial evapotranspiration is still better for
April-June compared to July-September.
Figure 37 shows the mean value of the monthly mean maps in Figure 38. It illustrates that the
variation of NDVI and the TSEB evapotranspiration do not follow the same temporal trend.
NDVI peaks in July as opposed to the TSEB evapotranspiration which peaks in June. This
explains why it is not possible to simulate evapotranspiration in July so that it matches the
TSEB data both in evapotranspiration pattern and magnitude relative to the other months.
It is evident that the NDVI is unsuitable for describing the evapotranspiration during July-
September, under the assumption that the East-West pattern observed throughout all months
of the TSEB data is correct. NDVI is a measure of the photosynthetic activity of plants and
therefore it can only be considered a proxy of transpiration and not evaporation. The decrease
of NDVI in the Eastern part of the domain could be a result of the wilting and decay of canopy,
an earlier harvest of crops due to climatic diﬀerences and diﬀerent crop types. Further testing of
this hypothesis should be conducted and, if validated, the weighting of clay fraction and NDVI
in the parameterization could be made season dependent.
The TSEB model calculates evapotranspiration from both plants and soils, and thus the diﬀer-
ences in pattern compared to NDVI for July-September indicate that other evaporation processes,
such as soil evaporation and evaporation from ponding, are becoming more dominant during
this period. In that case, the two-layer model does not compensate soil evaporation suﬃciently
when transpiration decreases as a result of decreasing root depth. The two-layer method is an
extremely simpliﬁed model for estimating actual evapotranspiration and groundwater recharge

based only on average conditions. It does not account for the relation between soil moisture and
unsaturated hydraulic conductivity, which means that the ability of the soil to transport water
to the roots is not accounted for (DHI, 2009b). Furthermore, the two-layer method does not
handle a large unsaturated zone well, which could be an issue in the current domain. It would
be relevant to see if the results could be improved by using the Kristensen and Jensen method
for calculating evapotranspiration. The Kristensen and Jensen method is available in the MIKE
SHE toolbox, it is based on empirically derived equations and it calculates soil evaporation and
evapotranspiration explicitly.
5.5
Future analysis
In Denmark, evapotranspiration is not the most dominant factor for the water balance and
the TSEB dataset is calculated based on cloud-free days, which is not the most representative
condition. Therefore it would be interesting to test the methodology in an area with semi-arid or
arid climate with large spatial variations in evapotranspiration. It would be relevant to look at
the impact of the spatial optimization of evapotranspiration on e.g. the summer baseﬂow.
Due to limitations in time, the present study is working only with one out of 7 subdomains
in the DK-model. The SPACE group at GEUS aims at extending the method applied in this
study to all of Denmark. Testing the methodology on a larger scale would indicate whether the
parameterization and calibration parameters are very case-dependent.
In this study it was prioritized to focus on the calibration. Due to limitations in time and lack of
proper data, validation was not prioritized. However, validation would be very relevant in order
to test whether the model has become more robust, given that it has been further constrained
with data, and to assess the model uncertainty.

Conclusions
53
6 Conclusions
The present study has investigated the potential for spatial optimization of evapotranspiration
patterns of a distributed hydrological model using remote sensing data. The study builds on the
SPACE project at GEUS (Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland) and the model applied
is the national water resources model for Denmark (DK-model) for the subdomain of Southern
Jutland.
The spatial optimization of the model consists of two parts: a modiﬁcation of the DK-model and
an extended calibration scheme which includes spatial pattern evaluation. The study features a
baseline model which includes pre-deﬁned temporal dynamics of Leaf Area Index (LAI), root
depth and crop coeﬃcient. In the modiﬁed DK-model these parameters are replaced by spatially
distributed empirically derived estimates using remote sensing data of normalized diﬀerence
vegetation index (NDVI) and a clay fraction map. The extended calibration scheme includes
a spatial eﬃciency SPAEF objective function and a remote sensing derived evapotranspiration
dataset for calibration based on the two source energy balance model (TSEB) (developed by
Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017)). Based on the calibration results the following conclusions
have been made:
• The baseline model is unable to simulate the spatial patterns observed in the TSEB
data which is dominated by an East-West gradient. The best model performance on
evapotranspiration patterns is obtained for the modiﬁed DK-model with the extended
calibration scheme, as SPAEF increases for all six months compared to the baseline and
spatial features are added to the evapotranspiration output of the DK-model which are
found in the TSEB evapotranspiration data. The new parameterization introduces three
ﬁxed and ﬁve calibration parameters.
• A linear function was found optimal for describing root depth as a function of clay fraction.
Root depth and crop coeﬃcient were parameterized separately for April-June (including
NDVI) and July-September excluding NDVI) because the reversed pattern in NDVI for July-
September does not correspond with the TSEB data. Under the assumption that the TSEB
data is an actual observation, this points to a limitation of the two-layer evapotranspiration
formulation in the DK-model. There is a considerable fraction of the evapotranspiration
which is not governed by vegetation that the DK-model is unable to simulate.
• The simulation of spatial evapotranspiration patterns was optimised without considerable
trade-oﬀs in the model performance on discharge and groundwater potential. The modiﬁed
DK-model results in a redistribution of evapotranspiration and groundwater recharge within
the domain while the annual evapotranspiration and groundwater recharge changed by
only 4.2% and 6.2% respectively.
• In accordance with Mendiguren et al. (in review, 2017) it was found that the absolute
values of the remote sensing derived evapotranspiration data was biased compared to
the simulations of the DK-model.
This emphasizes that the remote sensing derived
evapotranspiration should be used for pattern calibration only and not for constraining the
absolute values.
The study illustrates that the performance of a distributed hydrological model on simulating the
spatial patterns of evapotranspiration can be considerably improved with a very limited trade-oﬀ
in discharge and groundwater potential simulations.
