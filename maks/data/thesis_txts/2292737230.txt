Abstract
This work has investigated opportunities for Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD) modelling of particle transport in air pollution control equipment. Par-
ticularly, the modelling was conducted with focus on transport in the vicinity of
internal ﬂue gas distribution arrangements. Also, experimental investigations
were conducted for validation. The CFD model was shown to be able to predict
pressure loss and dust precipitation eﬃciency with acceptable accuracy.
Since suspended particles exchange momentum with the continuous phase,
they contribute to ﬂow patterns and turbulence, especially at high dust con-
centrations. Literature ﬁndings suggested that the dust mass loadings typically
observed in air pollution control equipment, are so high, that interaction eﬀects
cannot be neglected. Further, literature indicated that the characteristics of the
particles and the ﬂow should attenuate turbulence intensity. Correct modelling
of these interaction processes require a two-way coupling scheme. This is op-
posed to an one-way coupling scheme, in which particle trajectories are simply
calculated based on the ﬂow.
CFD modelling was initiated on a generic cut-out model of an inlet dis-
tribution screen. The modelling took an Euler-Lagrangian approach, studying
the formulation of a two-way coupled model and whether the two-way coupling
was necessary. The initial modelling suggested that the two-way coupling con-
tributed in the order of 10 % to pressure loss and turbulence intensity. CFD
modelling was then continued on a larger and more complicated geometry, which
resembled the experimental rig. Using both one-way and two-way coupling, the
CFD investigations on the experimental model focused on predicting precip-
itation eﬃciency.
Several parameters were varied to study convergence and
sensitivity. It was found that the precipitation eﬃciency was quite insensitive
to both restitution coeﬃcients and choice of particle sizes. However, modelling
of turbulent dispersion had a dramatic impact on eﬃciency prediction.
Experimental investigations were carried out while varying volume ﬂow rate,
dust loading and dust Particle Size Distribution (PSD). The pressure loss across
the experimental rig was found to be dependent on both volume ﬂow rate and
vii
==PAGE:8==
dust loading, but neither PSD nor screen surface condition. Surprisingly, the ef-
ﬁciency measurement results indicated that the eﬃciency changes insigniﬁcantly
with volume ﬂow rate, dust loading and PSD.
Validation was done by comparing CFD predictions of pressure loss and
eﬃciency to measurements. Validation indicated that the two-way coupled CFD
model was fully able to predict pressure loss at various volume ﬂow rates and
dust loadings.
Acceptable precipitation eﬃciency predictions were obtained
using one-way coupling without turbulent dispersion. Slightly worse accuracy
was obtained using two-way coupling with turbulent dispersion activated. Thus
the turbulent dispersion model was adverse in the one-way coupled models and
beneﬁcial in the two-way coupled models.
The present work was concluded by implementation of the one-way coupled
solver in a full scale CFD model of an FLSmidth Airtech product. In the full-
scale model, increased dust concentration was observed in the back-side of the
ﬁlter, away from the inlet.
This information could not have been extracted
from singlephase simulation (without dust simulation) of the model, but is very
useful for product optimization. It was therefore concluded that the formulated
model should be regarded a promising simulation tool for future design and
troubleshooting purposes at FLSmidth Airtech.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This work is comprised of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) and experi-
mental investigations of particle transport phenomena in FLSmidth Airtech air
pollution control equipment. The succeeding section introduces the topic while
chapter 2 treats the literature study conducted. Chapter 3 thoroughly examines
the governing equations of the ﬂow. Chapter 4 contains both the formulation
of the simulations and the most important results. Chapter 5 is devoted to
introducing the conducted experiments. Chapter 6 contains the comparison of
the simulation results and the experimental data for the purpose of model val-
idation. In chapter 7, the validated model is applied in a full scale fabric ﬁlter
CFD model. The conclusions are outlined in chapter 8.
1.1
Background
FLSmidth is a global engineering company with over 15000 employees world-
wide. FLSmidth provides know-how, machinery and entire factories in the ce-
ment and mineral industries.
FLSmidth Airtech is a division in FLSmidth
dedicated to emission control. Emission control, in this context, is the removal
of particles from ﬂue gasses from a variety of industrial processes. Examples
of processes include incineration of a limestone, clay and sand mixture to pro-
duce cement. The incineration process generates particulate matter which is
suspended in an air-stream. This constitutes the ﬂue gas. The ﬂue gas enters
particle removal ﬁlter equipment with gas and dust distribution arrangements
where the large and/or heavy particles precipitate. Finally the cleaned ﬂue gas
is exhausted to the atmosphere. Precipitated particles drop to the bottom of
the ﬁlter where they are collected. Figure 1.1 shows an outline of a FLSmidth
Airtech ﬁlter.

type of inlet is fundamental to the ﬂow characteristics in the entire ﬁlter and

1.2
Concept Deﬁnitions
It may be suitable to deﬁne certain technical concepts encountered frequently
through this work. Precipitation is the general removal of dust from the ﬂue gas.
Deposition can be any mechanical precipitation, though usually used for describ-
ing the removal of particles from the suspension by collision with structures such
as screens or walls. After such a collision, a particle may either stick to the object
because of adhesive forces or lose velocity due to the momentum transfer in the
collision and slide or drop to the bottom of the geometry. Turbulence modulation
is the (positive or negative) contribution to turbulence intensity from particles.
A positive contribution is turbulence augmentation or turbulence enhancement
while a negative contribution is turbulence attenuation. Filter eﬃciency is used
for describing how well, the ﬁlter is able to clean the ﬂue gas. A high eﬃciency
means that a large share of the incoming dust is precipitated in the ﬁlter.
1.3
Flow Conditions
This section is meant to clarify certain challenges, simpliﬁcations and decisions
through the work. A description of the known and unknown properties of the
ﬂow is thus suitable before any modelling is conducted.
The ﬂow consists of ﬁne particles suspended in gas. Particle diameters are
from 0.1 µm or less to above 100 µm but rarely exceed some 400 µm. Usual
mean diameters are from 1 µm to 10 µm. Two typical Particle Size Distributions
(PSDs) of cement dust can be found in ﬁgure 4.20 page 56. The chemical prop-
erties of the suspended particles are not of interest since no reactions between
the particles, the air or the structure occur. The volume fraction of particles
to gas is low, thus the best single-phase approximation to the ﬂow is a pure
Newtonian ﬂow of the gas. The suspended particles ﬂow through ducts and
chambers and is everywhere restricted by boundaries. The ﬂow is therefore in-
ternal. Fans generate a pressure diﬀerence between inlet and outlet and the ﬂow
is thus pressure driven. The ﬂow regime is fully turbulent since even conservat-
ive Reynolds number estimates exceed O(106). In the ﬁlter chambers, the ﬂow
may have three signiﬁcant velocity components and must thus be considered
three-dimensional. The ﬂow is steady upstream of the inlet screens but ﬂow
over certain internal geometries may induce transients that cannot be resolved
by a steady state solution. The velocity magnitudes typically encountered in
the ﬂow has order of magnitude O(10) m/s, hence the Mach number satisﬁes
Ma<0.2. The ﬂow is therefore weakly compressible and is thus readily assumed
incompressible. A more comprehensive analysis of the multiphase characterist-
ics of the ﬂow is necessary which is given through chapter 2.

1.4
Motivation
Further optimization of particulate control equipment require knowledge of dust
transport in both the vicinity of distribution internals and screens. As previ-
ously elaborated, the transported dust may origin from a number of industrial
processes, resulting in diﬀerent dust characteristics. Larger particles are expec-
ted to interact with both ﬂow and structures while smaller particles essentially
follow the ﬂow. Detailed knowledge of this precipitation process depending on
particle sizes is desired as the resulting change in dust characteristics after the
ﬁlter screens is crucial to the particulate control equipment optimization pro-
cess. A study of the ﬂows in distribution internals has already been conducted
at FLSmidth Airtech, but this work does not account for interactions between
the gas, the dust and the structure.
1.5
Problem Statement
This work aims for developing a CFD model for the multiphase ﬂow experienced
in gas distribution internals in particulate control equipment.
The model is
developed in order to predict ﬂow characteristics in the vicinity of ﬁlter screens
in FLSmidth Airtech products.
It should also be able to predict the extent
to which the screen is able to precipitate particulate matter (screen eﬃciency).
The quality of this work should be assessed on:
• Ability to predict ﬁlter screen eﬃciency
• Ability to predict changes to pressure loss and turbulent intensity due to
the presence of the dispersed phase
• Overall quality of the model including convergence, necessary CPU time
and sensitivity.
The model is developed using a commercial CFD code, STAR CCM+ from
CD-adapco (CD-adapco, 2014). The work investigates phenomena related to
the multiphase characteristics of the ﬂow in order to quantify the impact of the
particle modelling on the gross ﬂow characteristics. By conducting experimental
work at the FLSmidth test center Dania, the model is sought validated. The
model is then ﬁnally applied in a CFD model of a fabric ﬁlter product, and
suggestions to further model enhancements are given.

Particle-Fluid Interaction
Forces are exerted from the continuous phase (gas) on the dispersed phase
(particles). The forces accelerate the particles according to Newton’s second
law. This means that lighter particles relatively closely follow the streamlines
of the ﬂow while heavier particles, having more inertia, follow trajectories that
are diﬀerent from the streamlines. As the heavier particles travel these traject-
ories, they exert forces on the surrounding ﬂuid molecules. These forces locally
accelerate the ﬂuid, consequently changing the ﬂuid ﬂow patterns. Figure 2.1
shows a sketch of a particle with diameter D entering turbulence with small
turbulent length scales ℓ. The length scales in the particle wake are elongated
because the particle is large relatively to the turbulent length scales, D
ℓ>> 1.
This change to the turbulence characteristics induced by the dispersed phase is
known as turbulence modulation.

the ﬂow by for example shattering macro-scale ﬂuid structures, contributing
to transformation of turbulent kinetic energy, enhancing turbulence dissipation
and more. The way the particles manipulate the ﬂow depends on particle sizes,
densities, particle loading and more (Elghobashi, 1994) (Marchisio & Fox, 2013).

2.3.1
Euler-Euler
Also known as the two-ﬂuid approach (Elghobashi, 1994), (Brennen, 2005),
this model assumes the dispersed phase to behave like a continuum. Using this
method means solving a set of conservation equations for each phase. Evaluating
a coupling force between the two continua account for interchange of momentum
between the phases. Any reactions or phase changes in the ﬂow is modeled using
a source term in the continuity equation. This is not of relevance here.
The formulation of the model means solution CPU time scales with number
of phases and not with particle loading. This is a huge advantage when sim-
ulating two-phase ﬂows with a high particle loading. On the other hand, the
continuum characteristics of the model does not allow for detailed modelling
of inter-particle and particle-wall interactions. Finally, the continuum assump-
tion applied to a dispersed phase means that the phase must be homogeneous
and thus cannot consist of particles of various characteristics (diameter, dens-
ity, shape etc.), and hence a representative characteristic of the dispersed phase
must be identiﬁed which is sometimes not possible (Brennen, 2005) (Crowe,
2000).
2.3.2
Euler-Lagrange
Unlike the Euler-Euler approach, the Euler-Lagrange model ideally tracks each
particle in the ﬂow and is thus able to calculate particle interactions with the
ﬂuid and walls.
Practical ﬂows however contain so many particles that the
tracking of each particle is not feasible. Particles are therefore grouped into so-
called computational parcels, each representing a predeﬁned number of particles.
The ﬂuid is modelled using a revisited form of the usual single phase conservation
equations which include source terms. Particle properties are updated using
stepwise integration through the ﬂow ﬁeld and thus detailed modelling of ﬂuid-
particle interaction is possible (Crowe, 2005) (Marocco & Inzoli, 2009). Also
particle-particle interactions in dense ﬂows are possible using certain Euler-
Lagrange approaches known as Discrete Element Method (DEM) (Crowe, 2005).
Drawbacks to the Euler-Lagrange method applied to dilute ﬂows stem mainly
from (Crowe, 2005):
• Computer processing limitations induce the need to collect vast numbers
of particles into parcels with potential loss of information

The Turbulent Dispersion Model
The turbulent dispersion model is modelling the contributions to the particle
trajectories from small turbulent eddies. As these eddies are not resolved by
the RANS models, the turbulent dispersion model has to rely on an emprical
mathematical model which relate the perturbation magnitude to the local tur-
bulence. Even as this method is referred to as a ”random walk technique”, the
particle positions are not randomly perturbed. Instead, an extra contribution
to the velocity ﬁeld seen by the particle is added based on resolved time and
length scale of turbulence (CD-adapco, 2014) :

3.3
Coupling
For dilute suspensions, two coupling methods between the continuous phase and
the dispersed phase can be employed:
• One-way coupling
• Two-way coupling
In a one-way coupling scheme, the ﬂow ﬁeld is computed using the conservation
equations and the turbulence equations given in appendix B, but with zero
source terms.
Once the velocity ﬁeld is computed, the particle trajectories
are integrated using equation 3.59 and 3.60. Using this coupling method, the
particles does not follow the ﬂuid because they are inﬂuenced by both ﬂuid forces
and gravity forces. Particle-particle collisions cannot be computed because the
particles are assumed to be points in the domain not occupying a ﬁnite volume
(αd = 0). Particle-wall collisions can however be modelled.
In two-way coupling, the source terms in the conservation equations and the
turbulent transport equations are non-zero. Once the source terms are evalu-
ated (starting from initial conditions), the conservation equations are calculated

The forces acting on a particle must be modelled according to the application.
Generally, relevant forces could be:
• Basset force from unsteady viscous boundary layer eﬀects
• Virtual mass force which account for the extra inertia of particles because

• Viscous drag force

dispersed phase equation 3.59. Only gravity is considered in the body forces.
The virtual mass force is sometimes considered a body force because it may be
modelled as inertia to a submerged body. It could however also be modelled as
a surface force (Wallis, 2011). The choice is of little importance here since it is
small and will be neglected. Other contributions to body forces could be from
electric or magnetic forces.

accurate for dilute suspensions up to α ≈0.1 (CD-adapco, 2014).

The experimental screen is a detailed model of the real experimental set-up.

4.2.1
Geometry
The geometry is comprised of upstream piping, a diﬀuser for ﬂow distribution
and a box with the screen inserted. The layout is shown in ﬁgure 4.17. The
geometry is designed to measure the screen eﬃciency as precipitated particles
drop to a hopper. Particles precipitated on the back-side of the box drop to
another hopper. By measuring the weight of the dust from the ﬁrst hopper, the
screen eﬃciency can be readily calculated. There are several baﬄe plates inser-
ted in the upstream piping and the upstream diﬀuser. The baﬄes are included
because initial CFD investigations revealed an undesirable ﬂow distribution on
the screen. Without baﬄe plates, the ﬂow is likely to separate in the diﬀuser
eﬀectively forming a high-velocity jet onto the center part of the screen. With
baﬄe plates included, the jet is smoothed out to create a more evenly distrib-
uted velocity proﬁle. A smooth velocity proﬁle on the screen is somewhat more
realistic in a real FLSmidth Airtech product, and is probably easier to simulate
using a steady state approach.

The two-way coupled models
As explained, the parcel trajectories have to be updated after each ﬂow ﬁeld it-
eration, when using the two-way coupled model. This necessitates reducing the
number of iterations conducted before convergence. It is therefore extremely
beneﬁcial to start iterating close to a well-converged solution, obtained from
other multiphase simulations or even from single-phase simulation. Initial in-
vestigations indicated that very long simulation times still complicated the mod-

elling. Reductions were achieved by avoiding prolonged particle residence times
stemming from particles getting caught in vortices and boundary layers. During
two-way iterations, a maximum of 104 integration steps were therefore enforced.
Also, the maximum parcel time was set to 10 seconds. By observation, far the
most parcels had reached their ﬁnal destination (whether precipitation or out-
let escape) in each of these iterations. However too many parcels were aborted,
which polluted the calculated eﬃciency (since aborted parcels are regarded pre-
cipitated).
For the ﬁnal 10 iterations of each run, the solver settings were
therefore adjusted to 105 integration steps and 100 seconds maximum residence
time. After these 10 iterations, the residuals were checked to ensure conver-
gence. It was generally found that the ﬁnal 10 iterations did not change the
ﬂow ﬁeld, hence this method provides reliable results, achieved at much shorter
computation time.
As mentioned in section 4.1.3, convergence of the two-way coupled models
were obtained by reducing the under-relaxation factor signiﬁcantly. This un-
fortunately entailed longer computation time, but much better convergence was
obtained. A summery of the solver settings generally used are shown in table 4.5
page 58. Note that the under relaxation factor is extremely low. This is done in
order to obtain convergence, which is often diﬃcult to obtain at higher under-
relaxation factors. The STAR CCM+ default is 1(CD-adapco, 2014), which in
all cases for this work resulted in divergence. Much appreciation is owed to
(Svend Petersen, 2015), who recommended this solver setting.
Decreasing the under-relaxation factor to 0.005 is appropriate in order to
obtain convergence when working with the two-way coupled Lagrangian solver.

The experimental set-up was originally used for studying eﬃciency of various

could be conducted. In fact, two changes to the experimental set-up was proven
necessary:
• Changing the diﬀuser in order to improve the velocity distribution, and
the distribution of the particles in the incoming ﬂow
• Increasing bend radius and adding internal baﬄes in upstream bends.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This work has shown that dust modelling in air pollution control equipment is
both possible and necessary if key macroscopic properties need quantiﬁcation.
The work was initiated by a literature study on multiphase ﬂow modulation and
particle-structure interaction. The most necessary derivations on the continuity
equation and the Navier-Stokes equations were conducted to show the funda-
mentals of particle-ﬂuid interaction. It was found that particle-ﬂuid interaction
is both complicated and not fully understood.
CFD ﬂuid-dust modelling was carried out on a small generic model to in-
vestigate the necessity to include two-way coupling.
The analysis concluded
that the two-way coupled model showed both a signiﬁcant pressure loss and
turbulence intensity decrease. The pressure loss decrease was unexpected, but
the turbulence attenuation was in good agreement with literature ﬁndings. The
CFD modelling was then continued on a pilot scale experimental model. Mod-
elling was done using both one-way and two-way coupling. Results showed that
the choice of restitution coeﬃcient model had little impact on key macroscopic
properties. However, two-way coupling and the turbulent dispersion model both
dramatically changed the particle trajectories and the precipitation eﬃciency.
The choice of coupling scheme also signiﬁcantly changed the ﬂow ﬁeld, the pres-
sure loss and the turbulence intensity.
Experimental investigations were conducted at the FLSmidth test center
Dania. The investigations were focused on measuring precipitation eﬃciency
and pressure loss across the experimental rig. Experiments indicated that in-
creased dust load increased the pressure loss. The experiments also indicated
that the eﬃciency is independent of dust loading, gas volume ﬂow and even dust
particle size distribution. The independency of particle sizes is highly interesting
because it reveals surprising information about mechanical dust precipitation.
Validation was conducted by comparing CFD modelling to experimental
100
==PAGE:122==
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
ﬁndings. Validation of the pressure loss was highly successful, since the two-
way coupled models predicted the measured pressure loss within 5 % error.
Validation of precipitation eﬃciency showed that the one-way coupled model is
both robust and accurate. It overall performed better than the two-way coupled
model.
Analysis of the turbulent dispersion model revealed that activation
was adverse in the one-way coupled model and beneﬁcial in the the two-way
coupled model. The best eﬃciency predictions were obtained by using one-way
coupling without turbulent dispersion. Quite accurate results were, however,
also obtained by using two-way coupling with turbulent dispersion activated.
Accepting an absolute error within 10-20 %, it was concluded that the one-way

predictions.
Implementation of the Lagrangian model on a full scale CFD model provided
by FLSmidth Airtech, revealed that the one-way model was able to predict a sig-

ults also revealed interesting transport behavior of diﬀerent particles sizes, which
may be used in design purposes and when troubleshooting existing designs.
The ﬁnal Lagrangian CFD model is valid and realizable when estimating
eﬃciency. It however require extensive computer resources for accurate predic-
tions of pressure loss and turbulence modulation, because the two-way coupled
model is mandatory for this purpose.

• Validation: It was not possible with the allocated time to validate the

• Implementation: The Lagrangian model must be accompanied by a

• Simulation time: The full advantage of the two-way coupled models
is only realizable with extensive computational resources. Studies on the
necessary reduction of the under-relaxation factor must be conducted to
optimize the model.

Averaging
A.1
A Note on Averaging
The derivation of the governing equations in multiphase ﬂow often relies on
averaging techniques which will, to diﬀerent degrees of accuracy, relate the av-
erage properties of a ﬂow to its components. Mainly three procedures exist in
the literature (Crowe, 2005) (Brennen, 2005) :
• Time averaging
• Volume averaging
• Ensemble averaging
Ensemble averaging avoids many of the complications encountered in time and
volume averaging, but is not always easily implemented. Ensemble averaging is
based on probability theory and thus estimate local properties based on their
probability of being in a given state. The technique is elegant when detailed
measurements are available, since probabilities can be estimated from measured
values. However detailed measurements are not available here, and also most
commercial CFD software is based on volume discretization in which volume av-
eraging is better suited. Therefore ensemble averaging is not used for derivation
of the governing equations here, and is thus not elaborated further.
An important note should be given on the naming of the governing equations.
In single phase ﬂows, averaging the Navier-Stokes equations leads to the well-
known RANS equations (Reynolds-Averaged-Navier-Stokes equations). Recall
that the Navier-Stokes equations in this context are time-averaged to model
the larger time-scales thus avoiding the minor scale turbulent ﬂuctuations of
which knowledge is scarce. The governing equations of a multiphase ﬂow are,
on the other hand, volume-averaged to account for all modelled phases.

Appendix B
Multiphase Turbulence Modelling
The ﬂows considered through this work are fully turbulent with high Reynolds
numbers everywhere. As discussed in section 2, the impact on the turbulence
from the dispersed phase is of interest, hence the governing turbulence equations
are investigated for sources from the existence of the dispersed phase.
As with single phase ﬂow, scientists for whom DNS are unavailable must resort
to some kind of model for the turbulent scales that are not resolved. There are
a wide range of models to choose from, but perhaps the most widely used are:
• k-ϵ
• k-ω
• Menter’s Shear Stress Transport (SST)
There is a whole body of literature on recommendations when choosing the
turbulence model.
For this work, the ﬁrst two, k-ϵ and k-ω are considered.
Deriving the equations exhaustively for transport and dissipation of turbulence
kinetic energy for both models is however both undesirable to the reader and
the author. Investigating the impact of the multiphase characteristics on the
turbulence is therefore restricted to the k-ϵ model. The model comprises two
partial diﬀerential equations for transport of turbulent kinetic energy, k, and
turbulent dissipation rate, ϵ, respectively.

Appendix C
A Small Note on PSD Measurements
The mixture of particle sizes considered through this project have signiﬁcant
volume shares of very small diameters less than 1µm. Creating scientiﬁc, reliable
measurements on these small particles is a challenge. Analyzing and comparing
PSDs of very small particles is therefore subject to uncertainties. In order to
enlighten the reader with some idea of the uncertainties encountered in PSD
measurements of very small particles, consider ﬁgure C.1.
Figure C.1: Diﬀerent PSD measurements using the same sample of dust. The ﬁgure
indicate that agglomeration might occur.
The plot is created using the same sample of dust and three diﬀerent meas-
urement techniques. The analyzed dust is the coarse dust used for the ﬁrst
round of experiments. Broadly speaking, ”wet” methods distort any agglomer-
ation in the material and might thus better predict individual, larger particles.
The ”dry” methods, on the other hand, are better at capturing the smallest
particles in the dust and may or may not break up agglomeration, depending

on the supply pressure (here 3 bar). As seen in ﬁgure C.1, the dry method
ﬁnd larger particles than the two wet methods. This could indicate unbroken
agglomeration. Furthermore, increased ambient humidity promotes agglomer-

and since the experiments are run at elevated temperatures, it may be beneﬁcial
to dry the dust samples at relevant temperatures prior to PSD measurements.

The conclusion is that the true PSD of the dust remain concealed, unless
the measurement technique imitate the operating conditions used under exper-

for this work. Instead, PSDs created using similar measurement techniques are
compared in order to identify the most important trends. From acquired advice,
it was determined to use the dry method at 3 bar for all presented PSDs. It is
assumed that the method provides suﬃciently accurate results.
