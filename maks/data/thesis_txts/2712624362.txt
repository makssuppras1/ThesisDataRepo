After starting my MSc Engineering at École Centrale de Nantes with a specialisation
in Marine Engineering, I initiated a double-degree with DTU and the MSc Wind Energy

Abstract
Offshore wind energy is developing further away from the coasts where wind resources are
higher, so does the water depth. Therefore, Floating Offshore Wind Turbines (FOWT)
must be deployed in those specific sites to overcome the constraints. Works and projects
have been led to develop numerical methods to evaluate and analyse the dynamics of
floating structures. Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a powerful tool to handle
large motions of structures in complex flows, and previous projects have used CFD
methods with morphing or deforming mesh methods. In this thesis, an overset mesh is
applied to resolve the dynamic wave-response of a floating structure (TetraSpar). The
open-source CFD tool OpenFOAM (version 2106) has released a mature overset mesh
method and will be exclusively used to illustrate free and moored decay tests in heave
and pitch and non-linear interactions under regular and focused waves. The waves2Foam
toolbox develop by Niels G. Jacobsen is adopted to perform the wave generation and
absorption and for its quasi-static mooring line model. Pros and cons of the overset
method will be highlighted through the report. Finally, the experimental campaign
performed at DHI in 2017 will be the comparison point to validate and discuss the overset
method.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Onshore and Offshore Wind Energy
Through the last decades, the wind industry has experienced an extensive growth,
increasing the global installed wind capacity from 17.7 GW in 2000 to 826 GW in 2021.
The growth is even more considerable during the last 10 years in Europe, which also
observed a sixfold increase in the offshore sector (Figure 1.1). However, it is pointed out
that the recent investments in the wind energy capacity are lacking behind the target
to reach the 2030 Climate and Energy goals, meaning that more must be done in the
coming years.
Figure 1.1: Growth of total wind energy capacity in Europe between 2012 and 2021,
representing both onshore and offshore sectors, retrieved from [21]
The offshore sites are a goldmine for the industry actors as they generally yield for
stronger and steadier wind and less social reluctance. According to the graph 1.2, the
total offshore project costs have decreased significantly and is expecting to continue
on this trend, while in the mean time Vattenfall installed the world’s first subsidy-free
offshore wind farm [8]. Nonetheless, increasing the distance to the coasts while increasing
the wind energy availability often means deeper water sites, for which the levelized costs
of energy for common monopiles are unaffordable. Therefore, new types of foundations
are required to operate in deep water.

1.1.1
Floating Wind
According to the WindEurope 2017 Technical report, most of the European wind resources
are located in deep water regions (h > 60m), covering large areas as seen on Figure
1.3. Thus, floating foundations are the centrepieces in the further development towards
deeper sites, as they overcome bathymetry constraints with easier installation processes.
Figure 1.3: Mean underwater depth in Europe, generated from [7]
Floating designs and concepts are growing up with a broad diversity, but substructures
are classified into four different types (Figure 1.4): barge, semi-submersible, spar and

structure and mooring system, but is more limited by transportation process. Both barge
and semi-submersible designs are considered for their small draft and easy installation

platform is stabilised by a sophisticated taut-line system reducing its displacement. Thus,
all the classified concepts have their pros and cons, and the choice is mainly depending

1.2
Present study and objectives
With the increasing demand in energy generation, engineers have a need for robust tools
to investigate Floating Offshore Wind Turbine (FOWT) interaction under diverse load
conditions. The design operation usually includes experimental and numerical research,
from the assembly step to the decommissioning of the wind turbine. Design processes
are now using numerical models in combination of physical prototypes. Nevertheless, the
complex physics behind floating structures makes it difficult to produce accurate and
efficient numerical models.
In this way, Computation Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a powerful tool for designers
because it analyses the full physics of wave-floater interaction. This is especially good
for extreme wave events where the wave-floater interaction is complex due to highly
non-linear breaking waves.
The objective of the present study is to assess the accuracy of an open-source CFD
model in resolving the interaction with waves of a FOWT foundation, thus in simulating
==PAGE:19==
1.3 Thesis outline
5
the wave-induced floater motion. In this study, we used the open-source CFD package
OpenFOAM (Open Field Operation and Manipulation). In particular, the investigated
CFD solver employed the overset grid method. Morphing mesh is also a commonly
used technique for floating objects. More details on these two methods will be given
in the following. The capabilities of the overset grid method were investigated and the
validation of the CFD model was done against the experimental campaign on TetraSpar
[4].
1.3
Thesis outline
To meet the expectations of this project, this report is divided into the following parts:

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background
This chapter describes the theory appropriate for the application of the current project.
It defines the basis that will then be transcribed into the numerical methods presented in
Chapter 4. A first section focuses on the wave theory suitable to simulate wave-responses
of the floating body. It is followed by a presentation of the knowledge behind body
dynamics to perform the resolution of the motion.

2.2
Body Dynamics
A floating body is free to move in its six degrees of freedom, three translational and three
rotational. The six degrees of freedom are named surge, sway, heave, roll, pitch and yaw
and the reader can find a representation from the TetraSpar floater on Figure 2.5.
2.2.1

immersed into a fluid. The stable state is reached when the buoyancy force balances the

2.3
Previous studies
With the continuous development and improvement of CFD models to represent fluid-
structure interaction of FOWTs, previous works have been produced to investigate these
numerical models and validate them against experimental measurements. The main
references used in this study are:
• Niek Bruinsma [17] performed numerical fluid-structure interaction of both fixed
and moving bodies using the coupling between the fully nonlinear Navier-Stokes and
Volume of Fluid solver and the 6 Degrees of Freedom motion solver implemented
in OpenFOAM. The project work was extended with a validation of the wave
generation using the waves2Foam solvers.
• Irene Rivera Arreba [12] investigated in 2017 the capabilities of the fully nonlin-
ear Navier-Stokes/Volume of Fluid solver from OpenFOAM, and a second-order
potential flow theory solver (HydroD - Wadam softwares) to simulate the numeri-
cal responses of floating bodies. Both solvers are validated against experimental
data, among with the floating cylinder examined in the present study and the
OC5-semisubmersible floating platform.
• In 2018, Federico Mela [15] considered the OpenFOAM fully nonlinear Navier-
Stokes/Volume of Fluid and 6 Degrees of Freedom body motion solver to validate
the numerical wave-interaction of the TetraSpar floater against the experimental
campaign conducted at DHI. In particular, the CFD solver was based on a morphing
mesh technique, where mesh deformation is a result of the body motion.

CHAPTER 3
Experimental campaign
This section describes shortly the test campaign realised together with DTU Wind Energy,
Stiesdal Offshore Technologies and DHI Denmark. The experiments were made at DHI’s
deep water basin during the period May-June 2017. This chapter focuses on the main
parts of the tests and presents the useful inputs necessary to setup the numerical model.
For more information on the test campaign, the reader is redirected to the TetraSpar

composed of a floater and a counter weight, moored together with catenary lines (Figure

(three vertical cylinders each) mounted in a pyramidal shape. Finally, two heave plates, a

tubes assembled together in a triangle shape. It serves as an additional weight to the

3.3
Description of the test program
The testing program was made out of different laboratory testings, in order to simulate the
reaction of the floater to certain wave types. For each wave generation test, simulations
were made with and without wind generation, to measure the hydrodynamic response in
wave-only cases.
Moored Decay Tests
A prescribed motion is applied to the entire structure in one degree of freedom, without
generating wind or waves. In the basin, the floater is translated or rotated to a target
position, and is then released and free to oscillate. After a few damped oscillations, the
floater gets back to its equilibrium position. This process is repeated ten times with a
different initial displacement and the motion is recorded in all the degrees of freedom.
The post-processing enables the assessment of the natural undamped frequencies of the
floater.

CHAPTER 4
Numerical tools
This chapter is focused on the numerical tools used to produce and complete this
project. We used a CFD solver, called overInterDyMFoam included in the open-source
CFD package OpenFOAM v2106 to perform the numerical investigation. The applied
solver resolves the fully non-linear Navier-Stokes equations, coupled with a Volume of
Fluid for the wave-free surface, and a 6 Degrees of Freedom (6-DoF) rigid body motion
algorithm. This chapter gives an overview of the solver including the overset grid method.
Furthermore, a presentation of the numerical mooring line model, the wave generation
and absorption methods, the boundary conditions, schemes and resolution algorithm are
described here.
4.1
Fully non-linear Navier-Stokes solver and VoF method
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a powerful tool to resolve complex physical
problems involving fluid(s), for which no direct and theoretical solution exists. In this
sector, very-well known softwares are taking the leads since the last decades. OpenFOAM
is one of them and is especially famous for being open-source. That means that anyone
can request and use a free version of their solvers, but also develop and make changes to
the source code in order to come up with new features.
When two or more fluids are involved, one needs additional tools to solve the free
surface and ensure stability of the solver. One method is called Volume of Fluid (VoF)
and transcripts the fluids into a phase owned by each cell. This method is presented in
the following section.

Volume of Fluid (VoF)
Different methods have been developed to characterise the interface between two phases.
OpenFOAM uses the so-called volume of fluid (VoF) method, developed by Hirt and
Nichols [9], which is interface capturing and describes the volume fraction of each cell.
For example with an air and water interface, a cell containing only air gets a value α = 0,
a cell filled entirely with water has a value α = 1, while any cell close to the free surface
has a volume fraction in the range ]0,1[.

the field which corresponds to the equation:

between 0 and 1 for the boundedness of the field. With the new phase, the density and

4.3
Overset Grid Method
The entire goal of the present study relies on the overset mesh method. This method was
first introduced by Starius in 1980 [19] with the so-called Composite Mesh Difference
Methods (CMDM) to resolve hyperbolic equations using difference schemes on more
than one mesh. Benek, Steger and Dougherty are considered as pioneer of the overset
method with their paper in 1983 [2] on flexible grid techniques to describe the flow
around complex geometries. They were also the first to introduce the name of ’Chimera
grid’, in reference to the Greek hybrid creature formed from different animals. Since
then, multiple developments improved conservation issues related to this grid type. The
first feature related to overset grids in OpenFOAM was released with the 1706 version.
Figure 4.2: Composite mesh by Starius (1980), retrieved from [19]
The overset method consists of overlapping grids where boundary conditions in the
overlapped regions for one grid are obtained by interpolating information and fields from
==PAGE:42==
28
4 Numerical tools
the other grids. They are usually composed of one background mesh corresponding to a
block-structured mesh, and one unstructured grid including the snapped mesh around the
body. However, more than two meshes can be coupled to simulate flow around multiple
bodies.
As an alternative, morphing mesh techniques are based on a unique mesh which is
deformed or diffused according to the body motion, and the number of points or faces
does not change along the simulation. To handle the mesh deformation, an inner and
outer radius are defined into the dynamic settings, and delimit the part of the mesh
likely to be deformed. Cells laying inside the inner area (and close to the body) and
outside the outer region are preserved.
In the overset grid method, cells are displaced following the body solver (only
translations and rotations), thus ensuring more stability and robustness for the resolution.
This is especially advantageous for complex bodies with large deformations in order to
avoid instabilities in the mesh deformation such as negative volume cells.
Inside the parameters of the solid body solver, the numerical time scheme must be
specified to perform the mesh motion and advance the new position of the floating body.
The standard Newmark solver is applied in the present project, and corresponds to a
second-order implicit method.

the different mesh regions, i.e. between the background mesh and the top overset meshes,
and can take as many discrete values as the number of overlapping meshes. The allocation
is done by the user who can define the priority order from the highest value to the lowest.
After setting up the different meshes, the fluid solver must determine at each time
step how to exchange the data between them. To do so, three different types of cells are
determined:
• Donor cells : cells that provide values, from one mesh to the other
• Acceptor cells : cells whose values are computed from interpolation
• Hole cells : inactive cells, for example the background cells overlapped by the body
It must also be highlighted that the referencing depends on the point of view, either
from the background mesh or one of the overset meshes. Thus, one cell belonging to the
overset can actually be a donor for the background, and later become an acceptor from
the background cells. Furthermore in the case of a floating body which will be likely
to move inside the background, the hole cells for the background will also be updated
according to the new position of the body.
Figure 4.4 gives a representation of the cell types classification and its use during the
simulation process. On the left figure, one can see the front fringe cells laying on the
outer edge of the front mesh, and corresponding to cells requiring interpolations from
the background. Then, in the inner region of the overset mesh are back fringes (or field
cells), whose values are solved using the Navier-Stokes equations. Finally, the large dark
circles in the middle correspond to hole cells.
On the right figure, a depiction of the interpolation process between donors and
acceptors (receptor) is visible. Here, the closest donors in the background mesh take part
in the interpolation to compute the new acceptor variables.
The general overset method can be defined based on 4 successive steps applied at
each dynamic step:
1. Hole cells identification step: the overset solver points out the background cells
overlapped by the overset mesh and marks them as hole cells. No calculation is
made for those cells. This step is demanding and requires high computational time.
2. Acceptor cells identification step: the solver then discerns the cells laying on the
overset boundary, for which the solution will be determined by interpolation.
3. Donor cells identification step: the next step is to associate the background cells
required in the interpolation stencil. The stencil depends on the interpolation
method chosen.
4. Interpolation step: the acceptor cells fields are finally interpolated from the donor
cells information.

trackingInverseDistance, leastSquare and cellVolumeWeight. The open-source code also
allows the user to develop their own method. In the present project, only two methods

As stated, the overset grid technique transfers data and field values from one mesh to
the other. With a perfectly accurate overset interpolation, the quality of the final solution
would be similar to one computed with a deforming mesh technique. Unfortunately,
every interpolation induces numerical errors which impact the accuracy of the results,
leading to conservation errors or mass imbalance (what is leaving one cell is no longer
equal to what is entering other cells). This issue happening under the overset technique is
well-known and has been investigated in details by Chandar [5]. The errors concern each
interpolated field and are mainly due to the mesh quality and the choice of interpolation
method. Therefore, some parameters will be investigated later in this report in order to
reduce the conservation errors and improve the interpolation accuracy.

The user has the possibility to define restrained forces or moments applied to the body.
They can consist of a constant force, additional damping in translation or rotation, a
linear or angular spring, or even a force based on an input file. The additional damping
acts like an extra damping force opposed to the velocity of the body, in the form −αu in
order to limit the natural motion. It can be for example useful if some damping cannot be
resolved numerically. Jacobsen has developed in the waves2Foam toolbox the possibility
to simulate mooring lines defined as constrained forces. They can be modelled by three
states presented on Figure 4.5 from top to bottom: resting, simple and hanging state.

are to present large displacements during the physical tests, some differences will be

Wave generation and absorption
The main objective of the current project is to simulate numerically the wave-response of
a floating structure. Consequently, the available methods to generate and absorb waves
are described in this section.
==PAGE:47==
4.5 Wave generation and absorption
33
An option is the waves2Foam toolbox described in [13] and [3]. The toolbox offers two
different ways to generate waves. The first one is based on relaxation zones where the
velocity field and the free surface are updated at every time-step as the weighted sum of
the theoretical surface elevation and the computed elevation solved by the Navier-Stokes
equations. The weights depend on the distance to the inlet of the relaxation zone: the
wave is fully theoretical at the entry and fully resolved at the end of the relaxation zone.
The second one allows to generate and absorb waves without relaxation zones based on
the used of generating-absorbing boundary conditions. It applies a Sommerfeld-type
boundary condition combined with an optimised function to increase the efficiency of
the absorbing boundary for different wave numbers.
The dynamic wave-maker is a method imposing a displacement as a boundary
condition. This can actually be the exact numerical reproduction of a physical wave-
maker like the one used in the DHI’s basin. Therefore, the same inputs used during the
physical experiments can be applied to the numerical setup. However, it will require
longer times to update dynamically the mesh around the inlet. This method was used
by Mela [15] and will be compared to the other wave generation methods.
A third option is the wave generation and absorption library contained in the Open-
FOAM package. The velocity and phase fraction fields are defined at the inlet as a
boundary condition. The boundary conditions required are respectively named waveVel-
ocity and waveAlpha for the velocity and phase fraction. Based on the wave parameters
given by the user, the CFD solver computes the surface elevation and velocity vector
at the inlet at each step, and assigns those values to the first cells. OpenFOAM also
allows an absorption method called shallowWaterAbsorption for the phase field, and
for which the user needs to add a boundary condition for the velocity at the outlet,
fixing the velocity value. However, this method is preferred for shallow water wave tank
(h/L < 1/20) and presents limitations in deep water tank, resulting in an overestimated
reflection of the waves on the outlet boundary.
Another option is the use of a numerical porous beach, similar to the beach built in the
wave tank. In OpenFOAM, the porous beach only needs an STL file to define the shape
and position, and some absorption coefficients based on the Darcy-Forchheimer equation.
While the STL file was already furnished, a calibration of the absorption coefficients
was needed to match the reflection coefficient measured during the experiments. The
Darcy-Forchheimer acts like an added sink term Sm in the Navier-Stokes equations:

Boundary conditions
For numerical resolutions of differential equations, the user must specify boundary
conditions, either in the entire domain at the starting time, or applied to a specific region
or surface. First, all edges defined in the block mesh must be assigned to a patch type,
for example wall, symmetry, or empty useful for 2D cases. Another common option is the
generic patch type, which does not include prerequisites or topological information and is
commonly applied for inlet or outlet conditions. On the defined boundaries, it is possible
to assign boundary conditions, like a Dirichlet condition (constant value defined) or a
Neumann condition (gradient value defined). It is even possible to setup a calculated
boundary condition from other patch fields.

4.7
Discretisation and resolution of equations
Numerical Schemes
The solutions for the Navier-Stokes equations are continuous functions defined in each
physical point of the domain and for all the time range. However, solving those equations
at every couples (x, t) and finding an analytical expression is impossible. To circumvent
the problem, the domain is divided into small area (2D) or volumes (3D) with similar
physical properties, or limited changes. Then, the fluid solver would use a finite volume
method which solves the conservation laws in integral form over each of the control
volumes (cells) inside the domain. This method presents the advantage of ensuring
the global conservation over the entire domain, as opposed to finite difference methods
which can lead to large numerical errors. In the same way, the problem is only solved at
different time steps, small enough to limit large deformations from one to another. This
is done by using an Euler implicit scheme.
The continuity and momentum conservation laws from Equations 4.1 and 4.2 are
defined using several operators: a gradient for the pressure and the convective terms,
a Laplacian for the diffusive term and a divergence for the velocity. The mathematical
tools discretise all the previous terms to simplify the equations using specific schemes.

AU = DU −H = −∇P∗

U = D−1(−∇P∗+ H)

∇· U = 0 = ∇· [D−1(−∇P∗+ H)]

One can find among the available pressure-velocity coupling: PISO (Pressure Implicit
with Splitting Operators), SIMPLE (Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure-Linked Equa-
tions), SIMPLEC (SIMPLE Corrected/Consistent) and PIMPLE which is an hybrid
between SIMPLE and PISO. While PISO and PIMPLE are defined for unsteady flows,
the two others are to be chosen for steady simulations.
In this project, the PIMPLE algorithm has been preferred, for its unsteady compat-
ibility but also for its higher stability compared to PISO. When choosing PISO, the
momentum law is solved once and lead to a first estimation of the velocity. Afterwards,
the pressure equation is solved and values are available for both pressure and velocity
fields. The solver can be requested to use the computed pressure to correct the previous
velocity field from U = D−1(−∇P∗+ H), and iterate again with the pressure equation.
This is called the corrector loop, where the user specifies the number of pressure-velocity
corrections (nCorrectors).
The PIMPLE method is more advanced as it computes multiple PISO iterations.
Solving the momentum equation only once might not yield to a converged solution for
the velocity. By implementing PIMPLE, the user precises the number of momentum
resolution (nOuterCorrectors). After each resolution of the momentum equation, the
solver performs an inner-loop with nCorrectors iterations.
Increasing the number of loops inside the PIMPLE algorithm will increase stability
but also the computational cost so the two numbers of correctors must be adjusted. In
this thesis, two inner and outer correctors were implemented.

5.1.2
Decay tests
The series of decay tests presented below is often a necessary step when working with
dynamic structures. It ensures that the body can reach an equilibrium state after large
displacements, in addition to delivering structural properties like the natural period of the
decayed motion. In order to validate the overset solver, the physical and numerical results
presented by Palm et al. will be used for comparison. It represents free decay tests where
the floating cylinder is not restrained, and moored decay tests where mooring cables are
introduced. Each case will estimate the accuracy in period and motion amplitudes and
come up with potential causes and solutions when needed. For all the following tests,
the motion is normalised by the maximum displacement corresponding to the decay δ,
while the time is normalised by the experimental period.
5.1.2.1
Free Decay tests
In the heave decay test, the cylinder was moved from its equilibrium position by −0.076m
in the vertical axis, while it was rotated by 8.88 deg in the pitch decay test. For the
following pages, different cases will be compared together, mainly to judge the mesh

5.2
Water Loss Analysis
This section is a sensitivity analysis of the conservation errors due to the overset interpo-
lations that has been referenced by Chandar in [5]. This effect impacts the information
exchanges between the overlapping meshes, from acceptors to donors, and can lead overall
to significant discrepancies in the final results. The conservation error concerns every field
solved (i.e. velocity, pressure, etc.) but the volume fraction α is a simple way to record
the error accumulation through the simulation. Thus, this section is named Water Loss
as the volume fraction decreases in time, similarly to a sink or a leak. Each numerical
step will induce errors due to the overset interpolation and it is therefore necessary to
have sensible parameters to decrease them and improve the resolution of the overset grid
technique. As stated by Chandar [5], previous works already investigated the impacts
of some parameters like the mesh size. In this section, the cell size was investigated, as
well as the interpolation method and the time-step. Lastly, one case showed how the
conservation can significantly be improved.

the interpolation accuracy and reduce the differences with a morphing mesh technique.
This chapter aimed to investigate the overset grid technique available in the CFD
solver. The overset method has first been tested with a simple body to validate the
responses of free and moored decay against the floating cylinder experiments. The
overall results compared well with the physical measurements, even though limitations
from the turbulence model and the structural inputs were disclosed. Afterwards, a
deeper investigation of the conservation error resulting from the overset grid interpolation
demonstrated the influence of some numerical parameters and proposed solutions to
improve the quality of the interpolation.

Summary, Conclusions and
Further Research
In this master’s thesis, the response of the Floating Offshore Wind Turbine (FOWT)
foundation TetraSpar was investigated using the CFD solver OpenFOAM (version 2106)
against an experimental campaign led at DHI in 2017. The main numerical methods
consisted of a non-linear Navier-Stokes and Volume of Fluid solver coupled with the
6-DoF rigid body motion solver. In particular, the CFD solver employed an overset grid
method alternatively to the morphing mesh technique to handle the mesh deformation.
Summary
A first presentation of the wave theory and the dynamics of bodies in Chapter 2 was
followed by the introduction of the physical tests performed at DHI (Chapter 3). The
numerical tools available in the CFD solver required to compute the tests were described
in Chapter 4, with a major focus on the overset method, its background, setup and
operation process.
Secondly, a validation case was required to evaluate and discuss the numerical model.
Initiated in Chapter 5, the floating case cylinder presented by Palm et al. was simulated
with the overset method. Free decay tests computed using the fluid-structure interaction
solver and the overset grid technique compared well both with the physical tests and
the CFD simulations by Palm et al. based on a morphing mesh method. Afterwards,
moored decay tests showed some differences especially due to the quasi-static mooring
model. An additional analysis in this chapter concerned the overset interpolation and
the influence of key settings to decrease the conservation errors.
Lastly, Chapter 6 focused on the wave-interaction of the TetraSpar model. First, the
wave generation and absorption were validated against the experimental measurements
from the DHI’s wave tank, including a sensitivity analysis on the mesh. This was done
both for the regular wave train in 2D and 3D, and for the focused wave. The numerical
TetraSpar was then introduced and an initial investigation calibrated the lengths of the
mooring lines to validate the equilibrium case. It was then followed by two decay tests
in heave (T216) and pitch (T218). While the setup overestimated the amplitudes of the
responses, the introduction of a numerical damping in the CFD solver could reduce the
discrepancies with the experimental data. Last but not least, the two wave responses
T177 and T179 were performed and the results from the 6-DoF solver compared well
with the experimental campaign.

6 Validation of the CFD solver - Part II
Conclusions
The main outcome of the study was:
1. The CFD solver implementing the fully nonlinear Navier-Stokes and Volume of Fluid
method, coupled with the 6-DoF rigid body motion solver, showed good capabilities
in representing the decay responses and wave-interaction of floating bodies against
different experimental campaigns. The observed discrepancies between numerical
and experimental results could be improved with e.g. additional investigations on
grid refinement and turbulence modelling.
2. The applied overset grid method was found to be a reliable alternative to the
morphing mesh technique, provided that settings such as interpolation schemes
and background-overset cell ratios are carefully treated. On the other hand, no
significant advantages in using the overset grid technique were identified compared
to the morphing mesh method. In fact, overset meshes are preferred in cases
with very large motions (rotating or overturning objects), which might have less
application in FOWT foundation designs.
An additional comment can be made on the experienced large sensitivity of the
results on the floater properties such as inertia, mass and position of center of gravity.
Even if these parameters are available from laboratory tests, common uncertainties in
experimental data make necessary to calibrate the numerical setup. This calibration can
be time-consuming.
Further research
Several limitations were established along the work. To overcome them and improve the
final results, one can investigate the following points:
• other methods are available to model the mooring system. One can for example
couple OpenFOAM with the dynamic high-order finite element tool Moody. In that
way, a better representation of the stiffness and damping from the cables can be
achieved.
• an investigation of the turbulence models would yield to resolve better of the
viscous layer and forces exerted on the structure, leading to better resolution of
the dynamics.
• additional numerical models can be generated to investigate the capabilities of
the CFD model in representing the wind and wave-interactions of the turbine and
floater against the physical tests.
