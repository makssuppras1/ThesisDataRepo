Abstract
Normal hearing listeners can take full advantage of the spatially immersive audio produced by
a surround speaker setup or a pair of headphones reproducing virtual surround via binaural
technique. However, a speaker setup or pair of headphones does not produce an optimal sound
experience for hearing aid users. Binaural synthesis has been proven to work well for spatial
sound reproduction in headphones with a frequency response that covers the full audible spec-
trum. This invites the question whether it is possible to create spatially immersive sound, via
binaural synthesis, in hearing aids. Potential challenges could arise in hearing aids as they
do not provide the highest frequencies within the audible range and have ﬂuctuating receiver
frequency responses, when in use.
This thesis presents the design of a system that creates virtual surround sound for playback
in hearing aids. The developed system creates synthetic binaural room impulse responses of a
virtual 5.1 surround speaker setup by the use of head related transfer functions (HRTFs) and
simulated acoustics of an artiﬁcial listening room. The resulting binaural stimuli are equalized
for playback in hearing aids by ﬁltering with an inverse receiver transfer function that eliminates
unwanted coloration from the hearing aid electro-acoustics.
The developed system is utilized to investigate whether binaural synthesis is applicable in hear-
ing aids via binaural synthesis. This question is addressed in two listening test, where subjects
are presented with stimuli, produced by the developed system, and a stereo downmix that con-
tains no surround sound information. The tests examine the overall perceived eﬀect of sound
externalization in hearing aids and the overall preferred sound solution of the two types of
stimuli.
The results of the localization listening test showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in perceived location
between front and rear speakers when subjects were presented with the auralized sound, but
not when presented the stereo downmix. Even though the results showed signiﬁcance, it also
showed tendencies for front/back confusion that suggest some limitations of the hearing aids.
Results from the preference test showed that the stereo downmix was the preferred solution,
as the sound quality of the auralized sound was poorer in comparison. This issue has been
previously addressed by tuning of HRTFs.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since Disney Studios released the movie Fantasia in 1940, which featured musical selections
presented by a multichannel speaker setup, cinemas have lead the way in terms of immersive
surround sound for movie presentations [48, 18]. Later, companies like Dolby and DTS brought
surround sound to the living rooms by providing compression technologies that allowed multi-
channel audio streams to be stored on accessible media. Today, normal hearing listeners can
take full advantage of the spatially immersive audio produced by a surround speaker setup e.g.
a 5.1 speaker system, or a pair of headphones reproducing virtual surround sound. However, a
speaker setup or headphones are not optimal for people using hearing instruments.
Modern hearing instruments are capable of receiving and playing audio transmitted directly from
a TV, through a TV sound streamer. Modern streamers can convey sound binaurally in either
mono or stereo formats. Though stereo sound is the industry standard for sound streaming
to hearing instruments, hearing impaired people should have the opportunity to enjoy the full
auditory experience of movies and TV shows through the presentation of immersive surround
sound, similar to what one can get from a surround speaker setup.
It is well known that, aside from interaural time and level diﬀerences, the outer ear, head
and torso contribute to a coloration of the sound that helps to determine the direction of the
incoming sound, in normal listening situations. However, insert type receivers are often used
in hearing aids, which transmits the sound to the eardrum via the ear canal only. Therefore,
sound is localized within the head rather than perceived as being localized outside the head.
By convolving spatially dry sound by a so-called head related transfer functions (HRTF), it is
possible to shape the spectrum of the sound such that the spatial and ambient information of a
surround recording is preserved. This process is called binaural synthesis and makes it possible
to create the illusion of externalized surround sound, through binaural playback.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Problem statement
Binaural synthesis is not a new technology and multiple studies have been proven it to work well
for sound reproduction in headphones with full range frequency response. This has initiated the
idea to implement binaural synthesis in hearing aids to enhance the TV and movie watching
experience of hearing aid users, as they cannot take full advantage of a conventional surround
sound system. However, it is unknown whether it is possible to create a convincing, spatialized
sound environment by utilizing binaural synthesis in hearing aids. Potential problems could
arise in hearing aids, e.g. as they do not provide full audible bandwidth. Therefore, this study
sets out to investigate if/to which degree binaural synthesis can be applied in hearing aids.
1.2
Goal
The goal of the project is to develop a system that utilizes binaural synthesis with generic
HRTFs for the production of spatialized sound and use it to investigate the degree to which
binaural synthesis is suitable for creating convincing surround sound in hearing aids. As such,
the project is divided into two main parts; ﬁrst, the development of a system for binaural
playback of surround sound in hearing aids and, secondly, investigating the eﬀect of binaural
synthesis in hearing aids.
The evaluations are done through listening tests that evaluate the spatialization eﬀect on the
presented sound. As a reference, a conventional downmix solution is tested against the binau-
rally synthesized sound. This also enables a test that evaluates whether users will be prone to
use the externalized sound system over a regular stereo presentation.
1.3
Scope of the project
Many aspects have to be taken into consideration, e.g. diﬀerences in hearing aid types, re-
ceiver frequency responses and bandwidth limitations, and the acquiring and implementation
of HRTFs.
The system is made for the primary purpose of entertainment. Thus, the exact localization of
the virtual sound sources will not be of great importance in this project. If a product like this
is used in a a real world scenario, audio will be presented synchronously with a video feed that
will aid the user in terms of sound localization. In particular, the scope is to investigate if any
sound externalization is possible and if so, how it can be reﬁned. This will be done by a sound
localization experiment that compares externalized sound against conventional stereo downmix.
This project aims to investigate the eﬀect of binaural synthesis in hearing aids.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background
This chapter reviews the theory and technical aspects that are relevant to create a system that
can synthesize convincing spatial sound illusions. First, some general theory on human sound
localization will be presented. Then, theory on binaural technique will be reviewed to determine
how the processed audio is conveyed correctly to the listener in order to maintain the full spatial
information of the sound. Subsequently, a brief overview of modern hearing aids is presented.
Finally, aspects concerning spatial sound reproduction and conventional surround sound formats
is presented.
2.1
Sound localization
In order to build a system that can create a convincing spatial sound illusion, it is essential to
understand the mechanisms behind human sound localization, which is the ability to identify
the direction of and distance to a sound source.
The human auditory system is incredibly sophisticated and possesses the ability to localize sound
very accurately, which is an important ability that have given humans (and other mammals) a
distinct evolutionary advantage. Though the human eyes, with its millions of receptors, have
superior sensitivity to change, compared to the ears, they are limited in ﬁeld of view, whereas
the ears can localize in all directions and are always alert. Ears can even localize sound sources
that might be visually hidden.
When dealing with sound localization, the direction of a given source is usually deﬁned with
respect to the head of the listener. Blauert [6] described a coordinate system that deﬁnes three
planes used to specify the position direction of a sound source, relative to the listeners head. A
sketch showing the horizontal and median plane is shown in Fig. 2.1.
2.1.1

on interaural (between ears) diﬀerences only. Therefore, it is often a beneﬁt to make use of
binaural listening (meaning to listen with both ears), When trying to determine the direction

When a stimulus is of low frequency, the (horizontal) direction from where it originates will be
determined by the interaural time diﬀerence (ITD). If the source is placed at e.g. 60◦in the
horizontal plane (in correspondence with Fig. 2.1), the stimulus will arrive at the left ear before
arriving at the right ear. This is the case, as the sound wave has to travel further to reach the
right ear. Not only is the right ear placed further away in a direct line from the source, but
the sound wave must also diﬀract around the head of the listener to reach its destination. As
so, time diﬀerences can range from 0s (with the source at θ = 0◦) up to 690µs, if the source
is placed straight to the right or left of the listener (θ = ±90◦) [34]. The smallest detectable
shift in source position, using the ITD, is merely 1◦azimuth (relative to straight ahead), which
corresponds to time diﬀerence of only 10µs [41]. The ITD provides unambiguous localization
cues in the horizontal plane up to frequencies of 1.5 kHz [34].
At frequencies higher than 1.5 kHz, the sound waves will no longer be able to diﬀract around the
listeners head, without losing noticeable amounts of energy, as the wave length of the stimulus
becomes comparable to/or smaller than the size of the head. Hereby, the head will introduce a
shadowing eﬀect, that makes the stimulus louder to the ear closest to the source. The interaural
level diﬀerence (ILD) becomes signiﬁcant for signals of frequencies higher than 500Hz and can
become as large as 20 dB SPL at 6 kHz [41, 34].
The ITD and ILD provides directional cues in the horizontal plane, but are not always perfect
(on their own) at determining if a sound source is located in front or behind the listener. This
is the case as the ITD of a certain angle, relative to the ear canal (at 90◦or 270◦azimuth), will
be identical. This problem is often referred to as the cone of confusion [34], as the ITD and ILD
will be identical everywhere on the cone. This type of ambiguous spatial cues most often rises
if the listeners head is somehow locked in a ﬁxed position. When the listener has the option to
move the head, the cone of confusion is easily resolved by simply turning the head as little bit
to introduce a slight alteration of ITD and ILD [34].

2.1. Sound localization
2.1.2
Monaural spectral cues
The ITD and ILD can be used to determine the location of sound sources in the horizontal
plane, but are only parts of the complete sound localization mechanism. For example, if a
sound originates from somewhere in the median plane, the ITD and ILD would be zero, leaving
no location cues towards the sources. In this case, spectral cues, provided by the pinna1, will
help greatly in resolving the elevation of sound sources. Directional hearing can also be done
using monaural (to listen with one ear) cues. It is possible to determine the direction of a sound
source with a single ear as the pinna is asymmetrically shaped. This allows the incoming sound
to be ﬁltered diﬀerently by the pinna, as the it will distort the incoming sound ﬁeld to provide
spectral and temporal information, depending on the distance and direction of the sound source
[6]. The pinna leaves an angle dependant signature on sound entering the ear canal, that the
auditory system will recognize as a directional cue. Furthermore, the pinna also provides a
shadowing eﬀect of high frequency content that origins behind the listener head, that helps in
resolving front/back confusion.
The ridges and cavities of pinnae are quite small. Thus, it is mostly high frequency components
(above 6kHz according to Moore [34]) that are aﬀected (ﬁltered) by it, with lower frequencies
(above 500Hz) mostly being aﬀected by the head and upper torso.
2.1.3
Head related transfer functions
As stated above, the head and pinna together make a sophisticated direction dependant ﬁlter.
When an impulse is passed through this ﬁlter, the response will be a Head Related Impulse
Response (HRIR), of which the frequency domain equivalent constitutes the so-called Head
Related Transfer Functions (HRTF).
The HTRF can be obtained as a ratio of two measurements. The ﬁrst is a measurement of the
sound pressure at a reference position (located at the center position of the head, however with
the head absent). The second is a measurement of the sound pressure that occurs inside an
ear canal, when a head is placed at the reference position. The ratio of these measurements
constitutes the HRTF [32]. HRTFs are measured for each ear and as both ears are present, the
HTRF pair will (between them) contain both ITD, ILD and spectral cues. Thus, HRTFs are
commonly referred to as a pair transfer functions.
When spatially dry signals are convolved with a set of HRTFs2, the HRTFs will ﬁlter the dry
sound and make it appear as if sound is played from a certain location in a three-dimensional
space, by assigning the direction speciﬁc spatial attributes to the dry sound. Thus, the sound
has been spatialized, which potentially yields a more realistic listening experience than stereo
sound (that only varies along one horizontal axis).

When using generic HRTFs, it can become hard to distinguish between front and back, and up
and down locations [33], as the spectral cues of the HRTFs does not necessarily match those
the subjects are used to. Even though this is the case, it has been proven that the use of generic
HRTFs, in general, are quite robust [34]. This is especially the case for sound localization in the
horizontal plane, as ITDs and ILDs are well preserved even between individuals. It is therefore
possible to create a convincing spatial illusion for most of people via the use of generic HRTFs,
granted the HRTFs are obtained from a well-selected human or artiﬁcial head [31].
2.1.4
Perception of distance
The perception of distance mostly relies on sound level, but is in general not very accurate [41].
Fundamentally, it is assumed that high level sounds are close by, while low level sounds are
placed further away. Movements of the sound source or listeners will provide additional cues to
the perception of distance, as one is able to reevaluate whether the over all level has changed
[41]. An example of this could be a car appearing to move closer as the sound level increases.
Spectral cues can also be beneﬁcial for distance estimation outdoors. This is because air will
absorb some of the energy of the sound traveling through the it. It is mostly high frequency
energy that gets absorbed. This skews the frequency balance towards the low end, which is
perceived as coming from far away. An example of this, is when one can only hear the bass of
an outdoor concert playing in the neighborhood. This eﬀect is however not a very prominent
cue, as the relative attenuation is only 3-4 dB SPL per 100 meters of travel [41].
Sound reﬂections can contribute to the perception of distance when indoors or in a reverberant
environment. This is done subconsciously by judging the timing and level diﬀerence between
the direct and reﬂected sound of a source [41].
2.2
Perception of an environment
It was described above how spatialized sound can be created with the help of HRTFs. Addition-
ally, it has brieﬂy been described how the acoustics of an environment further can emphasize
the eﬀect of externalization. The addition of a listening environment, to the spatialized sound,
will create what is called auralized sound. Auralization is the process of simulating a sound ﬁeld
in a virtual room and can be achieved by adding room acoustics to spatialized sound created
by the use of HRTFs. In order to do this, a general understanding of room acoustics must be
obtained, which is presented in the following.

The direct sound is, as the name implies, the directly transmitted sound from the source to the
listener and will therefore always reach the listener ﬁrst. The direct sound is not inﬂuenced by
the geometries or surfaces of the room.
The early reﬂections are deterministic sound rays reﬂected by the walls, ﬂoor and ceiling and
will arrive slightly after the direct sound. The early reﬂections can be calculated (e.g. using
trigonometry) and thus depends on the listener’s position in the room. The early reﬂections are
perceived as being part of the direct sound, which makes the sound source appear louder and
richer, while not skewing the perceived source position [41]. This is the case as the precedence
eﬀect states that a leading sound wave will be dominating over a lagging sound wave, in terms
perceived source, even if the lagging sound is louder (yet not higher than 10-15dB [34]). The
precedence eﬀect is therefore also called ”the law of the ﬁrst wavefront”. Hence, the direct
sound will always be perceived as the origin of the sound, compared to reﬂections, which will
be perceptually fused with the direct sound, if the delay between the direct and reﬂected sound
is between 1-30ms [41]. If the delay is any longer, the reﬂections will be perceived as echos.
Due to the vast amount of reverberation in a normal listening room (e.g. a living room), one
would not be able to localize a sound source without the precedence eﬀect, as without it, sound
would appear as coming from everywhere, making it impossible to track a sound source. While
precedence eﬀect points the listeners attention towards the direct sound wave, while the early
reﬂections will provide information about the listening environment [23]. This could be size
(based on time delay between direct and reﬂected sound), shape (depending on delay between

absorb more high frequency energy, than low frequency energy, resulting in a diﬀuse sound

the late reﬂections can be approximated using statistical methods as the diﬀused sound ﬁeld
exhibits stochastic behavior [23]. The reverberation time of the late reverberations depends the
room size and absorption, and can therefore provide the listener some cues about size of the
listening room.
According to Begault et al. [5], a signiﬁcant eﬀect on externalization is found, when apply-
ing room reverberation to the spatialized stimuli.
Begault’s study showed that 79% of the
presented externalized stimuli were heard outside the head under reverberant conditions, com-
pared to only 40% for the anechoic conditions. An interesting aspect of the study by Begault
is that there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the perception of externalization using full
reverberation conditions and early reﬂection only. Therefore, the stimuli can be simulated for
maximal externalization using a minimal representation of a reverberant acoustic ﬁeld.
From the above, it is clear that the combination of direct and reﬂected sound provides spatial
cues that are essential to preserve the impression of a realistic surround sound environment.
Therefore, an artiﬁcial listening room that implements early reﬂections will be introduced to
the system to preserve these spatial cues during listening via hearing aids.
2.3
Binaural technique
Binaural technique is a term that covers a range of sound reproduction techniques, which focus
on capturing and reproducing sounds with the exact same spectral information as would be
present in the ears, in a real listening situation. The fundamental idea is, that the eardrum
is simply an input to the auditory system.
Therefore an artiﬁcially created signal will be
perceived identically to a naturally occurring one, if the two signals contain the exact same
acoustic information in the sound pressure present at the eardrum.
If the sound pressures are correctly reproduced at both ears it is possible to create an authentic
spatial sound presentation that will be perceived somewhere in the space surrounding the lis-
tener. This happens as the binaural presentation will give rise to ILDs and ITDs (and contains
spectral cues) that will trick the brain to perceive externalized sound. This phenomenon can be
described as an auditory illusion and is sometimes explained as being the equivalent to virtual
reality for audio.
There are two main ways of creating this sound illusion: binaural recordings or binaural syn-

should identify the ITD and combine it with the minimum-phase components to create a mini-

Hearing aids
For this project, it is necessary to have some basic knowledge about hearing aids, as the project
revolves around them.
There are three classes of hearing aids to consider; Behind-The-Ear
(BTE), Receiver-In-Ear (RIE) and custom devices [12]. The diﬀerent hearing aid types are
depicted in Fig. 2.7 and described in the following.
Figure 2.7: A selection of GN Hearing’s current hearing aids from the ReSound LiNX 3D series.
Three custom devices, one RIE device and one BTE device.
The custom devices are hearing aids placed in a custom molded shell, that ﬁts in the concha or
ear canal of the subjects. These hearing aids can take advantage of the pinna to obtain spatial
cues and sound ampliﬁcation. However, they are more cumbersome to produce, as they are
custom made to the individual. Furthermore, as the hearing aid blocks the ear canal (with the
exception of a small vent), the subjects will experience some degree of occlusion eﬀect5 while
wearing these. In this type of device, the receiver is close to the ear drum, as it is located
somewhere in the ear canal. The typical types of custom devices are (in descending order of
size): In-The-Ear (ITE), In-The-Canal (ITE) and Completely-In-the-Canal (CIC) devices.
The hearing aid electronics of a BTE device is placed in an elongated shell that rests behind
the ear of the listener. These devices typically have two microphones, that can be used for e.g.
beam-forming to increase SNR in noisy environments. As with the custom devices, the receiver
is ﬁxed in the plastic shell of the device itself. A sound tube that goes over the top of the
ear, connects the receiver with the ear canal. The tube is either ﬁtted in the ear by a custom,
occluding mold (hook conﬁguration) or by a simple ear tips, called a dome. The domes can
either provide a tight, occluding ﬁt or an open ﬁt that allows the listener to utilize some natural
cues.
The RIE devices are very similar to BTE devices, but usually smaller. As the receiver has been
removed from the shell and ﬁtted directly inside ear canal of the subject. The receiver is ﬁtted
using a dome, which again can be either tight or open, and connected to the devices itself via
a thin wire. In terms of electro-acoustic load, the RIE devices are conceptually equivalent to
custom devices as their receiver is located within the ear canal (an thus close to the ear drum)
unlike BTE devices.
There are many variations of hearing aids, that ﬁt many diﬀerent purposes. People with mild
to moderate hearing loses usually chooses RIE devices as these are well hidden behind the ear,

power is not to provide the full auditory bandwidth6 in the output signals from the hearing
aids. Thus, modern hearing aids are band limited to a maximum of 7-10 kHz output signals.
2.6
Surround sound
Digital television broadcasting has become today’s standard. Via digital transmission, most TV
broadcasting services oﬀer some kind of multichannel audio. This is also the case for modern
streaming services, DVDs and bluray discs. This section provides an overview of most used
surround sound formats on the market to determine which surround format should be used in
the system.
Since 2012, the Radiocommunication sector of the International telecommunication Union (ITU-
R) have recommended one universal multichannel stereophonic 3/2 sound system to enhance
the directional stability of the frontal sound image and the sensation of spatial reality, compared
to stereo sound [19, 20]. The recommended system has three front channels and two rear/side
channels in combination with an optional low frequency eﬀects (LFE) channel. Its small size
(in terms of components) have made it accessible to a broad range of people and thus become
the most common surround setup on the consumer market. A reference loudspeaker surround
setup, in accordance with ITU-R [20], is shown in Fig. 2.8. The two front loudspeakers are
placed relatively close together in order to maintain compatibility to the 2/0 format (regular
stereo). These loudspeakers should be placed in the height of the listeners head, while seated,
which is usually speciﬁed to 1.20m at an inclination angle of 0◦. The surround speakers are
likewise mounted at 1.20m or more, with an inclination angle between 0◦and 15◦.
In most surround systems, a band-limited channel that enhances the low frequencies will be
available. The Low Frequency Eﬀects (LFE) channel most often handles signals in the range
from 20-120 Hz. Furthermore, the LFE the channel should have a 10 dB higher gain compared
to the other channels. Placement of the LFE speaker is not crucial, due to the long wave lengths
of the low frequency stimuli.

2.6.1
Linear Pulse Code Modulation
Linear Pulse Code Modulation (LPCM) audio is the standard used in CDs, DVDs and Blu-
rays. This codec is an uncompressed audio format, meaning no loss in audio quality (compared
to some other codecs) but rather large consumption of storage space and transmission band-
width. LPCM supports up to eight audio channels and is therefore suitable for 7.1 soundtracks
encoding.
2.6.2
Dolby Pro logic II
Dolby Pro Logic II converts a high-quality stereo signal into ﬁve separate full frequency channels
and an optional LFE channel to achieve a full 5.1 system. Pro Logic II introduces a delay to
the surround channels, such that the sound from the front speakers arrive a minimum of 10ms
before the sound of the rear speakers. This creates a precedence eﬀect [13, 34], that ensures
dialogue and frontal sounds are actually perceived as originating on-screen.
The national Danish broadcasting corporation, called Danmarks Radio (DR), does not broadcast
TV using a surround sound format. Rather, they rely on Dolby’s Pro logic II technology to
up-convert stereo sound to 5.1 surround sound [11].

2.6.3
Dolby Digital and DTS digital surround
Dolby Digital 5.1 (also called AC3) uses six completely separated channels, with the LFE
channel having upper cutoﬀfrequency of 120Hz. The codec makes use of digital low-bit-rate
compression to encode (and decode) the audio signal. Dolby Digital uses a perceptual coding
system that divides the spectrum of each channel into frequency bands of varying size. The
bands are optimized to the frequency selectivity of the human inner ear, which makes it possible
to sharply ﬁlter out quantization noise (similar to MPEG coding). Thus, it can make compressed
audio for easy distribution, while retaining six separate channels.
Digital Theater Systems (DTS) also uses low-bit-rate coding techniques to reduce the data rate
of the audio signal. Its advantages over Dolby Digital is that it has a higher bit rate, which
entails a better sound quality.
2.6.4
Dolby Atmos and DTS:X
Dolby Atmos and DTS:X introduces object-based soundtracks, which is the newest technology
in terms of immersive movie surround sound. Dolby Atmos was the ﬁrst codec to introduce
the object-based soundtrack technology, that allows the soundtrack to have additional sound
objects which can be added to the channel-based audio. As such, sound objects can mapped
in a 3D environment, between the individual speakers, making it sound like objects can emerge
all around the listener. However, in order to make this work, the system will need a minimum
of two additional speakers in the ceiling, compared to a 5.1 system (then called a 5.1.2 system)
alongside an Atmos capable surround receiver.
DTS:X is also a version of object-based soundtracks. The main diﬀerence is that DTS:X works
on regular 5.1 or 7.1 speaker systems, unlike Dolby Atmos which requires at least two ceiling
speakers.

CHAPTER 3
Design of the auralization system
This chapter describes the functionality and structure of the hearing aid sound auralization
system, which has been created in MATLAB. A block diagram of the system is initially presented
with each block being described in sections throughout the chapter.
3.1
System overview
A system that can create auralized sound through hearing aids has been developed, based on
the theory and concepts that was reviewed in Chapter 2. Figure 3.1 shows a concept diagram of
a system that can transform a surround sound signal, in a 5.1 channel conﬁguration, to binaural
signals of auralized sound that have been equalized to play from a pair of hearing aids.
The system developed in this project is designed to generate a binaural room impulse responses
(BRIR) of virtual speakers, that are located within an artiﬁcial room. The path of the direct and
reﬂected sound rays are calculated, based on the provided room deﬁnition, for each of the ﬁve
main speakers. The incident angle of each sound ray (to the listeners head) is passed on to the
HRTF-block, where a speciﬁc pair of HRTFs (depending on the incident angle) are convolved
with the signal from a speciﬁc channel. The HRTFs separate the diotic signals into a set of
dichotic signals. The dichotic signal pairs are split into groups of direct and reﬂected sound
rays. The reﬂected rays get an added time delay due to the extended traveling distance and
are ﬁltered to simulate the energy loss of one or two wall bounces. Following this, the reﬂected
rays are summed with the direct sound rays for the right and left ear, respectively, to obtain
a binaural signal. The LFE channel is ampliﬁed and added to each channel of the binaural
signal. Thus, a binaural room impulse response is created. Finally, the BRIR is ﬁltered with a
inverse receiver transfer function (IRTF) that equalizes the frequency response of the hearing
aid receivers to produce the correct binaural signals.

3.2
Multichannel source and channel extraction
The system utilizes a digital 5.1 channel ﬁle format (e.g. Dolby Digital or DTS digital surround)
as input source, as all channels are separately available, unlike Dolby Surround and Pro Logic
which needs additional decoding to obtain 5 channels (see section 2.6). Based on how the system
is structured, it is possible to extend the system to utilize the 7.1 surround signal format or
even object-based sound formats e.g. Dolby Atmos.
The digital signal from the multichannel source is fed into the system by standard channel order
for a 5.1 surround signals [21]:
1. Lc: Left channel
2. Rc: Right channel
3. Cc: Center channel
4. LFE: Low frequency eﬀects channel
5. LSc: Left surround channel
6. RSc: Right surround channel
In a realtime system, the channel extraction/separation would be handled by either a TV, DVD-
or Bluray-player or a hearing aid streaming device. However, in this particular non-realtime
implementation, the channel extraction is handled by MATLAB that can import multichannel
audio and separate the audio channels. The multichannel audio tracks, used in the listening
tests, are either obtained by extracting sound from DVDs, using the free software DVD Audio
Extractor by Computer Application Studio or imported directly in MATLAB from video ﬁles
with multichannel tracks.
3.3
Artiﬁcial room deﬁnition
The system employs an artiﬁcial listening room to create a more realistic sound environment
for the user, following the principle described in section 2.2. The dimensions of the listening
room can be scaled to diﬀerent sizes, but must be rectangular. The implemented binaural room
impulse response (BRIR) model will only contain early reﬂections, based on the ﬁndings of
Begault et al. [5] (see section 2.2).
The listing room used throughout this project follows the recommendations from ITU-R [22] for
a reference listening room for multichannel sound system reproduction. Thus, the dimensions
of the room are 7m × 5.5m × 2.3m (W× D × H). The absorption coeﬃcient of the room is
set to be 0.3 for all surfaces of the room, which is the average coeﬃcient found for hardwood
and carpeted concrete ﬂoors. The listener is placed in the center of the width of the room and
2/5 of the depth. The speakers are placed in a 2m radius surrounding the listeners head at
the reference angles 0◦, ±30◦and ± 110◦, as deﬁned in section 2.6 and shown in Fig. 3.2. The
center and front speakers are placed in head height at 1m and the rear speakers are elevated to
1.3m.

3.3.1
Calculating sound ray paths
The system will be only be capable of presenting spatialized stimuli (meaning anechoic condi-
tions) if an artiﬁcial listening room is not implemented. Spatialization of anechoic stimuli is
not likely to be perceived as natural or convincing by a listener, since the subject is missing the
impression of a listening environment.
The artiﬁcial room is realized by introducing early reﬂections in addition to the direct sound
from the virtual speakers. This is done using the image source model (ISM) technique described
by Allen and Berkley [3]. The ISM technique is a sound propagation model that is based on
geometrical acoustics. The model relies on the geometry of the listening room to calculate the
deterministic (early) reﬂections of the sound rays from the sources.
The image source model uses a simple approach, as it assumes that the sound rays only prop-
agate in straight lines and are travelling at the speed of sound in air. For now, an assumption
is made that all sound rays are perfect specular reﬂections, but later a time delay and wall
absorption will be added to the reﬂections (as described in sections 3.3.2 and 3.3.3).
First, the listener and a single speaker is placed in a rectangular room. The distance and incident
angle from the speaker to the listener are determined using simple vector-based trigonometrical
calculations.
A reﬂected sound ray is simulated by a secondary source, that is mirrored in
surface (wall) of incident. This source is located perpendicular and equidistant to the wall as
the original source (the speaker), as illustrated in Fig. 3.3a). Due to this mirroring property, the
so-called image source has the exact same distance to the listener as the path of the reﬂected
sound ray. Knowing the location of the image source, the distance and incident angle to the
listener can be calculated.

3.4
Implementation of head related transfer functions
When listening to a reverberant sound ﬁeld in a room, the direct and reﬂection sound rays will
arrive to the listeners from diﬀerent directions. By utilizing HRTFs, it is possible to artiﬁcially
create auditory illusions of sound that appears to originate from any particular direction. This
can be achieved by convolving signals containing no spatial information, with angle-speciﬁc
HRTFs and playing the convolved stimulus through equalized headphones (or in this case,
hearing aids).
3.4.1
Generic HRTF databases
As described in section 2.1.3, it is well known that user speciﬁc HRTFs yields a more convincing
eﬀect to the speciﬁc listener. This is however not practical for a mass produced product, where
a well implemented ”one size ﬁts all”-HRTF database is a much more realistic solution. The
HRTFs used in this project are therefore generic (non-individual) HRTFs.
As described in
section 2.3, the HRTF-processed signals should be played back at the position at which the
HRTFs were measured. Therefore, it was decided to use HRTFs measured on artiﬁcial heads,
as the HRTFs are easily measured at the eardrum, using e.g.
a 711-coupler.

3.5
Creating binaural signals and adding the LFE channel
Finally, the BRIR of the artiﬁcial listening room is implemented by summing the direct sound
(from each speaker in the surround setup) received at the right and left ear of the listener,
respectively, with the ﬁltered and delayed early reﬂections of the room.
The LFE channel is simply added to the binaural signal, as this low frequency signal is assumed
to be omni-directional, meaning that it will not be perceived as coming from a certain direction.
The LFE channel gets ampliﬁed by 10 dB, in accordance with the standards speciﬁed by ITU-R
[19].
3.6
Inverse receiver transfer function
It was described in section 2.3 that a ﬂat frequency response of the transmission chain is
necessary to not introduce deterioration of the spatial cues contained in the synthesized binaural
signals [32]. Thus, it is necessary to equalize the hearing aid acoustics to reproduce the binaural
signals correctly. This is done by measuring the frequency responses of the hearing aid receivers
and creating a correction ﬁlter, called the Inverse Receiver Transfer Function (IRTF), that is
added to the playback chain to equalize the inﬂuence of the hearing aids and receivers.

sure transfer functions of the various receivers. The sound pressure output of the receivers are
captured by a 711-coupler, and the voltage across and current through the receivers are measured
by SoundCheck 15 (acoustic measurement software), via a National Instruments NI PXI-4461
2-Input/2-Output PXI Sound and Vibration Module (real-time data acquisition hardware).
The pressure and voltage transfer functions of the receivers are measured by stimulating the
receivers with a constant voltage sweep (Vg = 0.2 V, fs = 44100 Hz). The measured pressure

3.6.2
Inverse RTF ﬁlter design
With the RTFs obtained, the Inverse Receiver Transfer Functions (IRTFs) can be determined.
The IRTF will function as a equalization ﬁlter to obtain a ﬂat response from the hearing aid
receivers. The ﬁlter is simply deﬁned as:
IRTF(f) =
1
RTF(f)
(3.10)
However, it is not possible to simply invert the RTF as this may lead to an unstable system.
To ensure that equalization ﬁlter is stable, a minimum-phase component of the signal is de-
rived (explained in section 2.4). The minimum-phase components of the RTF are found using
homomorphic ﬁltering as demonstrated in Appendix B.2, following the description given in [39].
There is, however, a minor complication before the minimum-phase components can be de-
termined. As described in section B.2, the minimum-phase component is found from the real
cepstrum. This process exploits the property that the Fourier transform of a purely real signal
yields a real even and an imaginary odd part. This property is reversible, when doing an inverse
Fourier transform on the transformed signal, which is also utilized in the process. However, the
RTFs currently only consist of frequency components contained within the Nyquist frequency
(fs/2). Furthermore, the measured transfer functions are conditioned at both frequency ex-
tremes (due to AC coupling at low frequencies and anti-aliasing near the Nyquist frequency).
Therefore, the measured transfer functions are cut oﬀ(band limited) at both ends at appropri-
ate ﬂat spots, and extrapolated to reach from 0 Hz to fs/2 by the values at the band-limits.
The signals are then mirrored at fs/2 and the mirrored part is complex conjugated to create
transfer functions, going from 0 Hz to fs, with even-odd symmetry. Finally, the signals are in-
verse Fourier transformed1, yielding purely real time-domain signals (receiver impulse response)
that can be fed to the minimum-phase/excess-phase decomposition algorithm.
Fig. 3.7 shows the RTFs and the inversions of an MP receiver and BTE device using a thintube
(which is the hearing aid conﬁgurations use in the listen tests, as described in section 4.2.2).
Here, it can be seen that the IRTFs have been band limited and extrapolated from these points.
As the ﬁnal step in the surround sound reproduction chain, the generated binaural signals are
convolved with the obtained IRTF (that ﬁts the hearing aid that is going to present the stimuli)
to equalize the receivers frequency response. Thus, the spatial cues contained in the synthesized
binaural signals should be preserved.

3.7
Summary
This chapter has presented the individual blocks that makes up the surround sound reproduction
system by utilizing binaural synthesis.
The developed system extracts channel speciﬁc signals from a multichannel input and assigns
each signal to a separate virtual speaker in an artiﬁcial listening room. All sound rays (direct
and reﬂected) are convolved with optimized minimum-phase HRIRs to make sound appear as
it originates from particular directions within the room. The artiﬁcial listening room employs
early reﬂections, calculated by an image source model, to give the listener a perception of a
listening environment to enhance the spatial impression. The reﬂection rays gets appropriately
delayed and low pass ﬁltered to emulate real early reﬂections.
The direct and reﬂected rays are summed for the left and right ear, respectively, and the LFE
channel is ampliﬁed and added to each ear. Finally, the IRTF (that matches the hearing aid
used) is applied to equalize the frequency response of the receiver.
Next chapter describes how the specialized sound from the system via listening tests was com-
pared against a conventional ’downmix to stereo’-solution to investigate diﬀerent auditory at-
tributes (localization and timbre) of the surround sound in hearing aids.

CHAPTER 4
The listening tests
This chapter presents the design and results of two listening tests conducted to evaluate the eﬀect
of auralization provided in hearing aids. The listening tests intend to asses how the auralized
sound (created via processing by the developed system) performs compared to conventional stereo
downmix in terms of spatialization and user preference.
4.1
Introduction to the listening tests
The system developed in this project was made with the purpose of investigating whether
binaural synthesis yields a convincing spatial eﬀect, when presented via hearing aids.
This
chapter describes how two diﬀerent listening tests were prepared and conducted to clarify if
binaural synthesis is applicable in hearing aids, within mention limits (section 2.5). The aim
of the listening test was to evaluate if the combination of hearing aids and the system is able
to create an eﬀect of auralization and whether people are interested in using such a product in
stead of regular stereo sound.
The ﬁrst listening test is a localization task, that investigates the level of spatial impression
provided in hearing aids by the auralization system. This test should reveal if the hearing aids
manage to create an auditory illusion of sound coming from external surrounding sources. The
results will be compared to similar measurements from subjects being presented with a simple
stereo downmix. The hypothesis is that the system should provide auralization with a percept
that is diﬀerent from the downmixed stimuli, which should show no externalization eﬀect.
The second test is conducted to determine if there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in preference between
the auralized sound and downmixed sound. This test provides an overall measure of whether
auralized sound in hearing aids are of interest for people to use.
In the following, setup, execution and analysis of the outcome of these two listening tests are
presented. This includes a description of the tested hearing aids, how the stimuli were prepared,
a brief description of the test subjects and a detailed explanation of each listening test describing
the purpose, setup, execution and the results.

4.2
Conditions used in the listening tests
This section describes important conditions that were used in both listening tests. The following
sections will then focus on the test speciﬁc conditions and results.
4.2.1
Test limitations
Many diﬀerent types of hearing aids exist and each can be conﬁgured in multiple ways. Gener-
ally, it would be interesting to investigate the diﬀerence in auralization eﬀect between:
• Hearing aid types (BTE, RIE, custom devices)
• Receiver types (diﬀerent power levels)
• Bandwidth limits
• Hearing aid ﬁttings (open vs. closed)
• Wired connections and wireless streaming (to investigate if degradation happens when
streaming)
• Normal hearing subjects and hearing impaired subjects
• Generic and individual HRTFs
However, due to time constraints, the number of variables had to be cut down. It was therefore
decided to only use two types of hearing aids, each with one type of receiver. The bandwidth is
ﬁxed to that of the current generation of GN hearing aids, and all tests will be conducted with
closed ﬁtting as this will provide the best frequency response of the hearing aids. Furthermore,
it was decided to only use wired connections to the hearing aids, normal hearing subject and
generics HRTFs to keep the testing within a feasible limit.
4.2.2
Hearing aid selection
In both tests, only two types of hearing aids were tested in order to limit the test time for each
subject to an hour (at most), as the subjects otherwise would risk loosing focus if the tests were
too long. The tested devices were:
• a Behind-The-Ear (BTE) with a thin tube and closed ﬁtting.
• a Receiver-In-the-Ear (RIE) with a medium power (MP) receiver and closed ﬁtting.
Both hearing aid types had all algorithms disabled during the tests. The hearing aids were
tested with a bandwidth of 100Hz-7kHz, which is the bandwidth used in GNs current hearing
aids. These speciﬁc hearing aid types were chosen, as they together are representative of a wide
range of hearing aids available on today’s marked. As the receiver of the RIE device is located
in the ear canal it is assumed to yield comparable results to those of custom devices.

4.2.5
Subjects
In both tests, 15 normal hearing people (13 males and 2 females), between 26 and 43 years old,
were tested. As described in the very beginning of this report, the main objective in this study
is to investigate if auralization can be retained in hearing aids. The test subjects will therefore

outcome of the tests will solely depend on the eﬀect of the system and hearing aids, and not the

can beneﬁt from auralized stimuli is extremely relevant for the potential future of the project,
it is not in the scope the project.
4.3
The localization test
The intention of a potential product would be to present the spatial audio together with ac-
companying video, which would provide a bias in terms of spatial perception, as people would
be able to tie events on the screen with provided auditory stimulus. Considering this, a realistic
way to test the level of the externalization would be to present the subjects with video clips
and ask them to rate their perception of spatial immersion. Alternatively, an alternative force
choice (AFC) test between two (or more) video clips could be presented to the subjects followed
by a request for the subjects to choose. However, in both cases, it is not a certainty that the
subjects will rate their answer solely on their spatial perception. Participants might be biased
by other aspects of the presentation, e.g. timbre. Furthermore, it is interesting to examine how
well the hearing aids and developed system manages to provide a feeling of being surrounded by
sound without any form of (assisting) visual bias. This will show how well the setup performs
in a more challenging scenario, i.e. not accompanied by video.
A special test procedure was employed to collect spatially ”unbiased” data. This was done by
presenting the subjects with auditory stimuli and simply asking them where they perceived the
sound came from. The results obtained from the auralized stimuli were compared to the results
of the downmixed stimuli. The downmix served as a control condition, as it should provide no
externalization eﬀect and therefore could be used to show if the system and hearing aids provide
auralization with a signiﬁcantly diﬀerence perception.
The following sections describes how this speciﬁc test was prepared and conducted, and what
the results of it were. A description of the hearing aids, headphones and subjects used in this
test can be found in section 4.2.

The statistical tool of choice is an analysis of variance (ANOVA). The ANOVA can do data
analysis on multi-group data and answers the question whether the group means are signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent. The ANOVA is a hypothesis test (like e.g. a t-test) with a null-hypothesis, h0, that
assumes that the means of the two (or more) groups are identical within a 95% conﬁdence
interval [1]. Like the t-test, the ANOVA will provide a p-value that indicates the probability
that the group means are from the same distribution. A p-value lower than 0.05 indicates a very
low probability that the groups originate from the same distribution and the null-hypothesis
should be rejected as a result. Unlike the t-test, the ANOVA can compare data from several

4.4
The preference test
The purpose of the preference test is to investigate whether the subjects will choose the auralized
sound over the conventional downmixed sound, based on overall preference. A two alternative
forced choice (2AFC) test method is employed to determine if the auralized or downmixed sound
is preferred over the other. The auralized stimuli is compared to downmixed stimuli in both
types of hearing aids, separately. Furthermore, the auralized stimuli are also tested between the
two hearing aid types to investigate if the timbre of one hearing aid is preferred over the other.
Unlike the localization test, the preference test utilizes both audio and video, by presenting a
47
==PAGE:62==
Chapter 4. The listening tests
movie clip with two alternative sound tracks to investigate which sound treatment the subjects
prefer. The aurilization eﬀect might be more prominent in this case, compared to the unbiased
situation in the previous test, as the video will help to provide some visual context to the
auditory stimuli.
This method was chosen as it yields the most realistic use case scenario.
Hereby, the results of this test can be used as a guideline to determine whether the general
(normal hearing) population would be interested in using such a product.
4.4.1
The test setup
A custom test framework was developed for the purpose of this test. The framework was created
in MATLAB and consists of a video player in combination with a custom made GUI.
The framework utilizes a free media player called VLC by VideoLAN Organization1. This media
player features remote media controls via a HTTP connection and can therefore be controlled
by the Windows command prompt, which can be accessed directly from MATLAB. Hereby,
MATLAB can load a video-ﬁle from a directory, and play and control it using VLC. VLC also
features ”on-the-ﬂy” switching between audio tracks of a movie (e.g. changing between language
tracks on a DVD) which makes it easy for the subjects to change between audio tracks in the
listening test, thus making it easy to compare tracks. Furthermore, due to the nature of a media
player, the subjects are able to jump back and forth on a time line of the current video clip,
which enables them to re-watch speciﬁc sections of a clip over and over while switching between
audio tracks.
The GUI was created as a simple 2AFC front, using the MATLABs GUIDE (GUI development
environment). The subject was presented with a video clip and a simple GUI, that only had two
buttons saying ”Track 1” and ”Track 2”. In the test a video clip would play repetitively until
a preferred audio track was chosen by the subject. When an audio track had been selected, the
next video clip would start playing automatically.
The preference test was also conducted in a multi-purpose room at GN. The test setup consisted
of two monitors as shown in Fig. 4.8a. The top monitor was a 24 inch full HD screen placed in
eye height at a distance suitable to look like regular sized TV. This placement ensured that the
subjects kept their head oriented in a forward position, which is the optimal position to obtain
a convincing eﬀect of the HRTFs. The bottom screen contained the GUI in which the subject
entered his/her preferred sound track.

4.5
Discussion
This section provides a discussion of the observed results. First, the results of the localization
test is discussed followed by results of the preference test. The section concludes with the future
prospects of the topic.
4.5.1
The localization test
As described in section 3.3, the simulated placement of the virtual speakers, in accordance
with [19, 20], are 0, ±30 and ±110 degrees for the center, front and rear speakers, respectively.
Fig. 4.7 shows that the average perceived angular position of the auralized center and rear
speakers ﬁt this description fairly well. However, the front speakers are perceived from a wide
spread of angles that places the average perceived angle much higher than anticipated.
There can be multiple reasons for why this is happening. Based on literature, some of the
more likely hypotheses for the observed wide spread in perceived angle could be a result of
front/back confusion either due to limited monaural spectral cues as a result of the limited
bandwidth or a misﬁt between subjects and the used HRTFs, or a combination thereof. It is
suggested by Begault [4], Wenzel et al. [50] and Møller et al. [33] that front/back confusion is
often more likely to occur when utilizing non-individual HRTFs. According to these sources,
front/back confusion happens as non-individual HRTFs provide suﬃcient interaural, horizontal
cues (ITDs and ILDs), but lack proper monaural spectral cues, which diﬀer between subjects
due to physical attributes (such as ear and/or torso size and/or shape). The lack of spectral
cues would, in other words, place the perceived virtual sound source anywhere on the cone of
confusion (described in section 2.1.1).
If these spectral cues are not only an incorrect ﬁt for the subject, but are missing outright due
to the limited bandwidth, front/back confusion is very likely to happen. As seen in Fig. 4.3, the
bandwidth of the used hearing aids reaches its limit at 7 kHz, but multiple studies [36, 27, 24]
have shown that monaural spectral cues are located around and above this frequency, and that
these cues are an important help to resolve cone of confusion errors. Example-wise, the study
by Langendijk and Bronkhorst [24] suggests that the spectral front/back cues are in fact mainly
located in the 8 to 16 kHz frequency band. A similar conclusion was found by Boyd et al. [8]
who did a test on externalization in hearing-impaired listeners. The conclusion of this (Boyd
et al. [8]) study suggested that hearing impaired listeners with high frequency hearing losses
have a reduced perception of externalization compared to normal hearing listeners.
Assuming that the subjects of the localization test were heavily aﬀected by front/back confu-
sions, it would be interesting to see how the results would have looked if this was not the case.
Therefore, Fig. 4.10 shows the collected data, where all data of the front speakers that are above
90◦and of the rear speakers that are below 90◦, are mirrored in the 90◦-axis. This shows that
the overall perceived front speaker positions moves closer to the target angle of 30◦, while the
other speaker positions (especially the downmix) remain relatively unaﬀected.

introduce head tracking to the system rather than spending more resources on getting the
perfect set of generic HRTFs, as it is well known that the introduction of head movements will
greatly help resolving the front/back confusion [30, 9, 17].
4.5.2
The preference test
The results of the preference test states that the subjects clearly prefer the sound of the BTE
device to the RIE and prefer to listen to downmixed audio rather than auralized. These results
may be explained by two reasons. First, loudness biasing could have occurred and, second,
timbral ﬁdelity would seems to be of higher importance than spatial ﬁdelities.
As mentioned in section 4.4.3, the audio samples were pairwise RMS equalized in an attempt to
avoid the subjects being biased by potential loudness diﬀerences. Though the long term level
of the two presented samples were identical, large temporal and spectral variations would still
occur due to the diﬀerent pre-processing methods of the audio signals. During the preparation
of the audio samples, it was noticed how the downmixed samples in most cases sounded louder
that the auralized samples, and that the stimuli from the BTE device were perceived noticeably
louder than the RIE device.
It is generally believed that people will prefer the loudest of
53
==PAGE:68==
Chapter 4. The listening tests
two otherwise identical samples, which is consistent with the results presented here. However,
despite the results potentially being biased, the loudness diﬀerences would most likely not be
an issues for a potential product, as the users would adjust the volume (of which ever signal
processing was chosen) to a comfortable listening level. It simply means that the results of the
test may not reﬂect an entirely true picture of the situation.
It is well known, that a side eﬀect of using HRTFs is undesired timbral coloration [47, 25].
Thus, the externalized audio often becomes ”tinny” and thin (band-limited) sounding. Many
subjects reported that they chose the samples with the most complete soundscape and that this
often meant selecting the most bass rich samples. This is not because they like low frequency
content, but rather due to lack of low end frequencies in the auralized sound.
A study was conducted by Zielinski et al. [52] comparing the audio quality of band-limited multi-
channel and downmixed audio (among a group of experienced listeners) to identify the preferred
broadcasting method for band-limited transmission scenarios. The results indicated that the
choice of preference were more inﬂuenced by timbral aspects rather than spatial ﬁdelities and
that downmix would be the choice of preference, which is consistent with the ﬁndings in this
study. In other words: if the timbre of the auralized sound is not satisfying, people will not be
prone to use it over timbrally satisfying, downmixed audio. However, it has been described by
Merimaa [25, 26] and Silzle [47] that it is possible to tune the HRTFs to reduce the undesired
timbral coloration, and that this tuning can yield an improvement in sound localization. The
details behind the tuning were, however, not presented.
4.5.3
Future prospects
The results of the localization test indicates that externalization is indeed happening in the
hearing aids. However, the eﬀect seems to diﬀer among subjects and it is not clear to which
degree the auralization system delivers an immersive experience. As described in section 4.5.1,
the most obvious problem about the system is the amount of front/back confusions that seems to
occur. This is most likely due to mismatch between the HRTFs and subjects or the bandwidth
limitations of the hearing aids. Thus, the next step would be to resolve whether it is indeed
front/back confusion that disrupts the experience and if so, what causes it. As the main problem
is expected to happen due to limited bandwidth and/or the use of generic HRTFs, this is an
obvious place to start.
A new listening test could be conducted to investigate if the bandwidth of 100 Hz to 7 kHz is a
limiting factor for the auralization system. The test should be similar to the localization test,
but should instead present auralized stimuli that have been band-limited to various degrees,
in a pair of headphones with full auditory bandwidth. The hypothesis would be that the full
range signal should show no signs of front/back confusion, as the monaural spectral cues would
be preserved. The results of band-limited presentations could be used to estimate a critical
bandwidth to be used for presentation of auralized sound.
A similar test could be run to investigate the usability of generic HRTFs in comparison to indi-
vidual HRTFs. It would, however, become a cumbersome process collect the individual HRTFs
54
==PAGE:69==
4.5. Discussion
and prepare the personal stimuli for such a listening test. Furthermore, the implementation of
individualized HRTFs would not be a practical solution for a generic product, such as e.g. a
TV-streamer for hearing aids. Therefore, it might be more beneﬁcial to search for (or generate)
a well selected generic HRTF dataset suitable for general application. Additionally, this could
be combined with some degree of head tracking, which would provide the listener the option to
utilize head movements to resolve localization confusions.
A problem with the developed system is the relatively poor sound quality. The overall timbre of
the auralized sound is simply not satisfying enough to prefer it over the conventional downmixed
sound. It is a known side eﬀect in binaural synthesis that HRTFs add unwanted coloration to
the sound, even when appropriate IPTFs are applied [47]. However, it has been described by
Silzle [47] that it is possible to tune HRTFs to reduce the unwanted coloration eﬀect, such that
they overall timbre comes close to the original, unprocessed signals without losing the eﬀect of
externalization. From the results of the preference test, this type of correction seems a necessity
in order to be able to make a viable product.
Another aspect that should be investigated before a real product can become a reality is the
eﬀect of auralization when using hearing aids with an open ﬁtting, which will further reduce the
low frequency response of the presented sound. In addition, it would be interesting to investigate
if diﬀerent receiver types yields a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in perceived auralization. The stimuli
used in the listening tests were all fed to the hearing aids via a direct audio input. A necessary
step is to introduce a wireless connection between audio source and hearing aids, as a wired
connection is impractical. As such, it should be investigated if the auralization eﬀect is retained
in a wireless connection or if it might disappear due to e.g. transmission band limitations,
distortion or bit rate limitations.
Finally, it is paramount to investigate the level of perceived auralization for various kinds of
hearing impaired subjects, if a product like this should hit the market. As mentioned, Boyd
et al. [8] did a study on this question that investigated normal hearing and hearing impaired
listeners sensitivity to externalized cues, when listening directly with their ears or through BTE
devices. The results showed a decreased perception of externalization for the hearing impaired
listeners with high frequency hearing losses. However, more research is needed in this ﬁeld to
determine whether there is justiﬁcation to continue the development of a binaural hearing aid
surround sound reproduction system.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to investigate if binaural synthesis in combination with an
artiﬁcial listening room can provide a convincing auralization eﬀect in hearing aids. Therefore,
a study on human sound localization and binaural technique was conducted to obtain the
necessary knowledge to develop a system reproducing a surround sound audio track via binaural
presentation in hearing aids.
The basic principle in binaural technique states that the ear is simply an entrance to the
auditory system, and if a synthesized sound pressure is identical at the eardrum to the naturally
occurring one, in terms of spectral and temporal content, the auditory system will not be able
to discriminate between the two. A system that can generate (realistic sounding) binaural room
impulse responses of a 5.1 surround speaker setup in an artiﬁcial listening room was therefore
created. The 5.1 format was chosen, as this is the most widely used surround format. This
could however be up-scaled to a more advanced format, as the system is only limited by the
resolution of the acquired HRTF database.
The image source method is utilized in the developed system to calculate the path of the direct
and reﬂected sound rays in the listening room, from the speakers to the listeners head. Based on
the outcome of these calculations, minimum-phase HRTFs of HATS were chosen depending on
the angle of incidence of the incoming sound ray. The HRTFs that ﬁt the incident angle of the
direct rays get directly convolved with signals from the speakers, while the signals of reﬂected
rays are delayed and low-pass ﬁltered to simulate the prolonged travel and wall bounces, before
also being convolved with appropriate HRTFs. The signals are summed, separately for both
ears, to obtain the binaural signals and the LFE channel is added to each ear with a 10dB
gain. In order to make the signals suitable for playback in hearing aids, an appropriate inverse
receiver transfer function is applied to equalize the output to eliminate unwanted coloration
from the hearing aids.
The developed system was used to investigate if binaural synthesis is applicable in modern hear-
ing aids. Two listening tests were conducted to answer this question. In both test, subjects were
presented with auralized sound from the developed system and to conventional stereo downmix
which is a stereo signal that is created by downmixing a surround signal. The downmixed sig-
nals were added to the tests as reference signals, as most spatial and environmental information
of the surround signals were lost during the downmix.
57
==PAGE:72==
Chapter 5. Conclusion
The ﬁrst test was a localization test. The subjects were presented a stimulus and asked to
specify its origin in space. The subjects were not told that the stimulus was either a stereo
signal or a 5.1 surround signal, but instead that sound could be presented from all directions, in
order not to introduce a bias. The results showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in perceived location
between the front and rear speakers when the subjects were presented with the auralized sound.
This was not the case when presented with stereo downmix, as no signiﬁcance was observed.
Even though the results of the auralized sound showed signiﬁcance, it also indicated front/back
confusion, which suggests some limitations of the hearing aids. The main hypotheses to why
this is observed is the limited bandwidth used in modern hearing aids, the mismatch between
generic HRTFs and individuals or a combinations of these. The developed system lacks any
sort of head tracking, which could also be a factor that gives rise to front/back confusion.
The second listening test were conducted to investigate the overall preference between the two
types of stimuli; auralized or downmixed.
The results showed a strong preference towards
the stereo downmix. The result may be explained by the fact that the stereo stimuli were,
mistakenly, presented with an overall higher loudness level compared to the auralized stimuli,
which could have highly biased the results. Otherwise, the favoritism for the stereo stimuli
could be a result of the poorer sound quality of the auralized stimuli, in comparison to the
stereo downmix. This problem arises as the use of HRTFs introduces an unwanted coloration of
the sound, which especially makes it lack low frequency content. However, as previous research
has shown, this can be addressed by tuning the HRTFs.
The conclusion of the project is ambiguous, but the listening tests have shown that auralization
in hearing aids might be possible, especially if the user is assisted with visual spatial cues that
could be supplied from e.g. a movie. However, further investigations need to be conducted
to pinpoint the exact pitfalls of the auralization system. This thesis have in relation to the
localization test demonstrated a method for testing spatial perception that would be helpful for
future follow-up studies.
Additionally, multiple investigations needs to be conducted before a product of this kind can
become a reality. This includes the eﬀect on auralization of various hearing aid types, receiver
types and receivers ﬁttings, and if any degradation occurs to the auralization eﬀect in relation
to wireless signal transmission.

APPENDIX B
Minimum-phase/all-pass decomposition
using the real cepstrum
This appendix describes how a non-minimum-phase system, H(z), can be decomposed into a
minimum-phase component, Hmin(z), and an all-pass component, Hap(z).
A described in section 2.4, the minimum-phase component of a system is deﬁned as a system
whose poles and zeros are located inside the unit circle in the z-plane.
One method of ﬁnding the minimum-phase component is to ﬁnd the roots of the system identiﬁ-
cation polynomial that are located outside the unit circle, mirror the the roots to their reciprocal
position inside the unit circle, and then reconstructing the polynomial.
The aforementioned technique can be applied to low order systems of which the transfer function
is deﬁned.
This approach can however become cumbersome, should the system be of high
order, and automation, using an algorithm, is not straight forward to implement. Furthermore,
according to Plogsties et al. [42], this can result in signiﬁcant round oﬀerrors. Therefore, the
minimum-phase components are instead obtained through homomorphic ﬁltering of cepstra, as
described by Oppenheim and Schafer [39] and explained in section B.1 and B.2.
B.1
Deﬁnition of homomorphic systems and the complex cep-
strum
In 1967, Oppenheim [37] described a class of non-linear signal processing referred to as ho-
momorphic signal processing. This approach suggests to classify non-linear systems such that
each class satisﬁes the principles of superposition, similar to the way linear systems satisfy the
principles of superposition. Thus, signals entering the homomorphic system will be transformed
to satisfy the principal of superposition, such that they can be operated using linear (homo-
morphic) algebraic operations. As such, signals that normally cannot be represented by simple
superposition of sub-signals, such as convolution or multiplication operations, can be processed
using superposition in the so-called cepstrum.
The power cepstrum was ﬁrst described by Bogert et al. [7] and deﬁned as the spectrum of the
