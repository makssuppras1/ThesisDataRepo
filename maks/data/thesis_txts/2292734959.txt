Abstract
Regular mirrors used at normal incidence are ineﬀective in reﬂecting X-rays. On the other hand
for low grazing incidence angles, periodic multilayers use constructive interference to achieve
high reﬂectance and narrow spectral response. For the observation of high energetic phenomena
such as black holes, neutron stars and active galactic nuclei though, broad spectral response
is required. Depth-graded Ni-based multilayer coatings are a possible solution to extend the
upper energy range of hard X-ray focusing telescopes currently limited at ≈79.4 keV by the
Pt-K absorption edge. Moreover, depth-graded multilayer coatings can be designed to achieve
high reﬂectance over a wide band of X-ray energies. The challenge is to deposit smooth enough
layers to get a larger reﬂectance.
The present master’s thesis report focuses on the development of these coatings in the thin
ﬁlm deposition facility at DTU Space. It includes a description of the relevant X-ray physics of
multilayer reﬂection and a presentation of the laboratories and software used during the project.
The laboratory work was divided into two parts. First the coating methods were investigated
and optimized parameters were derived (material combination, gas composition, gas pressure
and collimation method). Then a depth-graded multilayer coating was produced and character-
ized.
In the ﬁrst part of the study, 10-bilayer multilayers with a constant bilayer thickness were
coated in the DC magnetron sputtering facility, characterized at ≈8 keV using X-ray reﬂecto-
metry and ﬁtted using the IMD software. Ni/C multilayers were found to have a mean interface
roughness ≈1.5 times lower than Ni/B4C multilayers. Reactive sputtering with ≈76% of Ar
and ≈24% of N2 reduced the mean interface roughness by a factor of ≈1.7. It also increased
the coating rate of C by a factor of ≈3.1 and led to a coating process going ≈1.6 times faster.
No conclusions were drawn on the impact of coating pressure. Honeycomb collimation proved to
limit the increase in mean interface roughness when the bilayer thickness increases at the price
of a coating process taking ≈1.9 times longer than with separator plates.
Finally using the optimized parameters derived in the ﬁrst part of the study, a Ni/C 150
bilayers depth-graded mutilayer was coated with reactive sputtering and honeycomb collimation
and then characterized from 10 keV to 150 keV. The reﬂectance is far from the theory (10-20%
far) for energies greater than 30 keV and a grazing angle of 0.10 degree, but it showed 10%
reﬂectance up to 85 keV.
Future work for further improvement is described as well and guides to run the laboratories
and use the IMD software are presented in appendices.

Terminology and deﬁnitions
Hard X-ray
Hard X-rays are deﬁned for this paper as X-ray radiation with
photon energies greater than 10 keV.
Specular reﬂection
The specular reﬂection is the reﬂection of light coming from a
single direction (a ray) from a surface into a single outgoing
direction.
The incident ray, the normal to the surface, and the
reﬂected ray are coplanar.
Reﬂectance
The reﬂectance of a surface is its eﬀectiveness in reﬂecting radiant
energy.
The radiant energy is the energy of an electromagnetic
radiation such as X-rays.
Target
In a magnetron sputtering process, a target is a source of the
material desired to be deposited.
Substrate
In a magnetron sputtering process, the substrate is the piece of
material (in our case Si) on which the material from the target is
deposited.
Grazing angle
The grazing angle is the angle between the light beam and the
surface on which the beam is incident.
Bilayer period
The bilayer period (also called d-spacing) is the thickness of a
bilayer in a multilayer coating with constant bilayer thickness.
Bilayer thickness ratio
The bilayer thickness ratio Γ is the ratio of the thickness of the
heavy material layer and the bilayer thickness.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The ﬁrst chapter introduces the background and the mainspring of the
project. It also includes the goals, the method employed and an outline
of the document.
1.1
Background
The high-energy (short wavelength) region of the electromagnetic spectrum corresponding to the
hard X-ray and soft Gamma-ray ranges presents a particular challenge to the development of
eﬃcient reﬂective optical elements such as mirrors. The reason being that at normal incidence,
the reﬂectance of any "regular" mirror is close to nothing [1]. Therefore optical instruments
such as telescopes usually operating at normal incidence do not work and alternatives are to be
developed.
On the other hand for low grazing incidence angles, multilayer coatings make use of the
principle of optical interference to reach high reﬂectance in the hard X-ray band. These reﬂect-
ances are several times larger than those possible using normal incidence mirrors and led to
remarkable advancements in solar physics and astronomy but also in a variety of scientiﬁc and
technological disciplines [1].
A periodic multilayer coating consists of a stack of identical repetitions of a bilayer. A bilayer
is made from two layers of material having diﬀerent optical properties. Periodic multilayers act
like periodic lattice planes in crystal and therefore exhibits Bragg reﬂections due to constructive
interferences. Thus, they can provide both high reﬂectance and narrow spectral response. For
many applications though (for instance X-ray astronomy and the observation of high energies
phenomena such as black holes, neutron stars, active galactic nuclei, supernovae, supernova
remnants, and Gamma ray bursts), broad spectral response is required [1].
A depth-graded multilayer coating consists of a stack of ranging layer thicknesses designed
to achieve high reﬂectance over a wide band of X-ray energies [1].
The hard X-ray focusing telescopes on the NuSTAR mission [2] and to be ﬂown on the
ASTRO-H mission [3] use depth-graded multilayer coatings to extend the energy bandwidth to
the hard X-ray range. For both missions the upper energy range is limited to ≈79.4 keV by
the platinum K absorption edge of the constituent heavy material. One possible solution for
extending the energy bandwidth into the soft gamma ray range of several hundred keV is to
use nickel (Ni) as the heavy element in the multilayer as this material does not have absorption
edges in hard X-ray/soft gamma ray range (Ni K absortion edge is at ≈8.3 keV).

Chapter 1. Introduction
The challenge is to deposit smooth enough layers to get closer to theoretical reﬂectances.
Lower roughness enables thinner layers which means better reﬂectance for higher energies. This
document reports on the development of these coatings in the direct current (DC) magnetron
sputtering facility at DTU Space including the combined methods of collimation and reactive
sputtering using molecular nitrogen (N2).
1.2
Project goals
The main goal of this project is to optimize the reﬂectance of multilayer coatings made partly
from Ni. The optimization criteria, besides the reﬂectance itself, are interfacial roughness and
coating rate. The interfacial roughness is directly linked to the reﬂectance and should be kept
as low as possible to enable the deposition of thinner and of a higher number of layers. The
coating rate is directly linked to the process time which is relevant for mass production.
1.3
Method
This study was carried out ﬁrst by investigating individually four main multilayer coating para-
meters which are the material combination, the gas composition, the gas pressure and the
collimation method. For each investigation of a parameter a multilayer coating was deposited
on a silicon (Si) substrate in the DC magnetron sputtering facility at DTU Space. Then 8.047
keV X-ray reﬂectometry (XRR) was performed, also at DTU Space. Finally the IMD software
package [4] was used to characterize the layers deposited (thicknesses and roughnesses).
Subsequently a depth-graded multilayer coating was deposited using selected values for the
parameters investigated in the ﬁrst part of the study. This depth-graded multilayer coating
was then characterized in the laboratory-based hard X-ray reﬂectometer at the Reﬂective X-ray
Optics LLC (RXO) [5] in New York, New York.
1.4
Outline
This document comprises six chapters, this one being the ﬁrst, and four appendices. Here is a
quick description of their content.
• Chapter 2 deals with the theory of X-ray physics. The nature of X-rays is speciﬁed and
relevant laws and parameters derived, including Snell’s law, the Fresnel coeﬃcients and
Bragg’s law.
• Chapter 3 describes the two laboratories used during the project. The multilayer labor-
atory where magnetron sputtering was used to deposit multilayer coatings on substrates
and the X-ray laboratory where XRR was performed on the coated substrates. It also

2.1
X-rays waves and particles
X-rays are electromagnetic waves. Consider a set of Cartesian coordinate vectors (x, y, z), a
monochromatic beam of X-rays propagating along z and an electric ﬁeld E in the (x,y) plane.
For simplicity the magnetic ﬁeld is neglected here. The spatial dependence of the electromagnetic
ﬁeld at a given instant t is characterized by the wavelength λ or the wavenumber k = 2π/λ.
The time dependence of the electromagnetic ﬁeld at a given spatial position r is characterized
by the period T or the cyclic frequency ω = 2π/T. E0x and E0y are respectively the amplitude
of the electric ﬁeld along x and along y therefore the amplitude vector of the electric ﬁeld is
E0 = E0xx + E0yy. With i the unit imaginary number and k = kz, the electric ﬁeld is given by
E(r, t) = E0 exp(i(k · r −ωt)).
(2.1)

2.8
Bragg’s law
A change in electric density results in a change in the refraction index (see Equation 2.38)
which results in greater reﬂectance.
A way to achieve that is to use bilayers made of two
diﬀerent materials with a diﬀerent atomic number Z. Moreover a multilayer with constant bilayer
thickness acts like periodic lattice planes in crystal and therefore exhibits Bragg reﬂections7
which means that for some energy and angle, constructive interferences will occur and led to
increased reﬂectance. With m ∈N Bragg’s law is given by
mλ = 2d sin θ.
(2.41)
For instance, the ﬁrst Bragg peak (m = 1) for an energy of 8.047 keV and a bilayer period
of 25 Å is at θ ≈1.77 degree. For a bilayer period of 60 Å it is at θ ≈0.74 degree.
Nevertheless the Bragg peaks exhibited in multilayer coatings with constant bilayer thick-
ness are narrow and can hardly be applied for astrophysics observations.
To reﬂect non-
monochromatic photons we want a smooth reﬂectance across a large band of energy. A way
to do that is to coat multilayer depth-graded coatings in which the thickness of the bilayers
varies with the depth in the stack.

3.1
Multilayer laboratory
The multilayer laboratory is the multilayer coating facility located in room 227 in building 328
of DTU Space. It is a clean room class 10000. Inside sits a vacuum chamber as can be seen on
Figure 3.1. This chamber is used to perform magnetron sputtering which is a technique used to
deposit thin ﬁlms (several angstroms) onto a piece of Si called substrate.
Figure 3.1: Vacuum chamber closed (left) and opened (right)
The main components inside the vacuum chamber are four magnetron cathodes attached to
DC power supplies, hence the name DC magnetron sputtering. Figure 3.2 is a top-view draw-
ing of the chamber with its four cathodes mounted vertically and sputtering outwards. The
materials that need to be deposited come in the form of bars called targets and are mounted
onto the cathodes (Figure 3.3 shows a target of Ni mounted on a cathode and the position of
25
==PAGE:26==
26
Chapter 3. Facilities and tools
Ni, C and B4C targets in the chamber can be seen in Figure 3.2). The substrates on which
the material deposition occurs are mounted vertically on the rotating outer ring. The velocity
of the outer ring and the power delivered to the cathode determine the thickness of material
deposited. Figure 3.4 shows that what is actually designated by a cathode is a set of three
permanent magnets cooled down by water inside a copper (Cu) cathode, housed in stainless
steel anode, separated by Teﬂon spacers. The magnets generate a magnetic ﬁeld in front of the
cathode (hence magnetron cathode).
Once the chamber is closed a roughing pump and then a turbo-molecular pump take the
pressure inside the chamber down to ≤2 × 10−6 Torr in ≈4 hours.
Then the chamber is
ﬁlled with a neutral gas such as argon (Ar) or a mixture of Ar and molecular nitrogen (N2).
When applying a negative voltage to the cathodes, already present free electrons (see Figure
3.4) start moving away from the target, bounce back and get trapped in the magnetic ﬁeld in
front of the target. The electrons then hit the particles of the neutral gas (see Figure 3.5) and
knocks out an electron from their outer shell. The gas particles then positively ionized (called
a plasma) are accelerated onto the target in front of the negatively charged cathode (see Figure
3.6). When the ionized gas particles smash into the target, the kinetic energy is larger than
the target material binding energy, causing target atoms to be ripped out and sputtered in the
opposite direction, towards the substrate (see Figure 3.7). During that time the additional free
electrons maintain the production of gas ions and sustain the process. Moreover some electrons
rebind to the outer shell of the gas ions, turning them back into neutral atoms or molecules. The
conservation of energy leads to a release of energy by the gas particles in the form of photons
that make the plasma glows (purple when pure Ar is present and green for a mixture of Ar and
N2). Also electrons stripped from the gas particles are captured by the magnetic ﬁeld lines from
the permanent magnets and move back and forth occasionally hitting a gas particle and ionizing
it, sustaining the plasma. The shape of the magnetic ﬁeld leaves an erosion mark into the target
as can be seen on Figure 3.3.

X-ray laboratory

The DC magnetron sputtering facility at DTU Space is a high vacuum chamber (< 10−6
Torr) in which an inert gas, argon (Ar), is used during the coating process. Moreover, a reactive
gas such as N2 can be added. The sputtering process is then called reactive sputtering. The
composition of the gas ﬁlling the chamber impacts the coating process in ways investigated here-
after. The pressure of the gas (coating pressure) during the coatings presented through out this
paper is ≈2.9 mTorr (except in Section 4.4). It was found that a coating pressure < 2.3 mTorr
is likely to lead to power dropouts during the coating. A power dropout is an interval of time
during which the plasma is not sustained and therefore during which no material is deposited.
This can lead to large errors in bilayer thicknesses and Γ values.

per unit of time. This coating rate is diﬀerent for each material. The mean roughness matters

4.3
Gas composition (reactive sputtering)
The inert gas used here was Ar and the reactive gas N2. The presence of N2 is known to lower the
roughness [9]. The process that lowers the roughness is not well understood but the reactivity
of N2 interacting on the surface at an atomic level is assumed to play a role. Moreover N2 will
be included in Ni and C deposited but in which concentration is not investigated and not taken
into account in the IMD ﬁt.
First a comparison was made between coatings performed with pure Ar and coatings per-
formed with a mixture of Ar and N2. Figure 4.4 displays the ﬁtted mean roughnesses of four
coatings as a function of their ﬁtted bilayer periods (≈25 Å, ≈50 Å, ≈80 Å and ≈120 Å)
both for a coating performed with pure Ar and with a composition of 76% Ar and 24% N2.

orr, separator plates were used as collimation and the ﬁtted Γ value is 0.41±0.05. The presence
of N2 reduced the ﬁtted mean roughness by a factor of ≈1.70. Similarly to the material com-
bination investigation, note that the mean roughness increase with the increase of bilayer period.

coating rates for C and Ni. The presence of N2 boosted the coating rate of C from ≈0.14 Å·s−1
to ≈0.43 Å·s−1 (factor of ≈3.07) while the coating rate of Ni is reduced from ≈0.30 Å·s−1
to ≈0.20 Å·s−1 (factor of ≈1.5). With Γ ≈0.41 this corresponds to a coating process going
≈1.63 times faster.

4.6
Conclusions
Assuming taking individually the best parameter values and combining them gives the best
result (which is not necessarily true but it is the approach here) the lowest roughness can be
achieved with material combination of Ni/C, a gas composition of 76% Ar and 24% N2 and
honeycomb collimation. The inconclusive results on the impact of coating pressure leads to a
choice of p ≈2.9 mTorr to avoid as much as possible power dropouts.
Let us consider Table 4.5 listing the atomic masses1 and calculated atomic radii of the ele-
ments at play here. An explanation for the C being deposited smoother than B4C could be the
size of the element. B4C being bigger than C, its deposition is not as smooth. The C being
deposited faster with reactive sputtering might come from the fact that its mass is much closer
to the mass of N2 than it is from Ar therefore if a collision happens between an atom of C and
a molecule of N2, there will be more energy transferred to the atom of C. Similarly since the
mass of the atom of Ni is closer to the mass of the atom of Ar than to the mass of the molecule
of N2, it deposits slower in the presence of N2. The reasons for a decrease in roughness when
using reactive sputtering or honeycomb collimation remain unclear.
Moreover, Figures 4.3, 4.4 and 4.7 all show an increase in roughness with bilayer thickness. It
is consistent with the possibility of roughness increasing with thickness in the depth-graded ﬁlm.
In some systems, this increase can be due to the crystallite size in the metal layers. For polycrys-
talline layers (like Ni, presumably), the rule of thumb is that the crystallite size is proportional
to the layer thickness. So thicker layers will have bigger crystallites, and bigger crystallites can
lead to increased interfacial roughness. Another possibility for increased roughness with ﬁlm
thickness is so-called "columnar growth" but we might not see its eﬀects in ﬁlms containing only
10 bilayers [11] [12] [13].

Chapter 5
Depth-graded coatings
This chapter reports the second part of the laboratory work performed
with the description of the depth-graded multilayer coating deposited and
its HXRR data.
In the light of the results obtained from the investigation of coating methods the optimized
values for the parameters were selected. The material combination chosen was Ni/C, the gas
composition was Ar and N2 and honeycomb as collimation technique was chosen over separator
plates despite the slower coating rate it implied. The pressure chosen was ≈2.9 mTorr as we
saw the eﬀect of gas pressure is unclear.
By choosing a relatively high coating pressure we
try to avoid dropouts as much as we can. The mean roughness was considered as the primary
characterization parameter and the duration estimated for this depth-graded multilayer coating
was about 6 hours which is manageable.
First the depth-graded coating was chosen to be a power-law and the coeﬃcients were derived.
Then calibration is needed with the coating chamber to achieve high precision thickness for the
deposited layers. Once the depth-graded is deposited hard X-ray reﬂectometry is performed to
assess the quality of the multilayer.

5.3
Hard X-ray reﬂectometry
After the coating process, hard X-ray reﬂectometry (HXRR) was performed at RXO at ﬁxed
grazing angles for energies ranging from 10 keV to 150 keV. The goal being to check the quality
of the multilayer by measuring its reﬂectance in a broad band of hard X-rays.
Figure 5.2 displays the measured reﬂectance from 10 keV to 150 keV for the grazing angles
0.10 degree, 0.15 degree and 0.20 degree. As expected the reﬂectance decreases when the grazing
angle increases. An important drop in reﬂectance from ≈0.95 to ≈0.20 is seen at ≈25 keV,
≈17 keV and ≈12 keV for a grazing angle of respectively 0.10 degree, 0.15 degree and 0.20
degree. For each grazing angle the other features are believed to be the consequence of several
issues. The coating process experienced several dropouts for unknown reasons leading to errors
in the layer thicknesses and possibly the number of layers. Moreover the computation of the
aimed bilayer thickness values from a reference of 30 Å might have lead to errors for the larger
bilayers. Finally the interface roughnesses are expected to be high due to the high number of
layers, especially the ones corresponding to the top layers as the investigation of coating method
revealed an increase in mean roughness with a larger bilayer thickness. Nevertheless for a graz-
ing angle of 0.10 degree the reﬂectance is at least 10% up to 85 keV.
Figure 5.3 displays both the measured and theoretical reﬂectances at a ﬁxed grazing angle
of 0.10 degree from 10 keV to 150 keV. The theory agrees with the measurement up to ≈30
keV but for greater energies there is a diﬀerence of at least 10%. Moreover, the suspicion that
dropouts resulted in fewer bilayers is consistent with the ﬁt that gives ≈140 bilayers instead
of the 150 targeted. Moreover ﬁts were tried using depth-graded roughnesses from 3 Å to 15 Å
with limited success.
XRR was also performed at DTU Space. Figure 5.4 displays both the measured and theor-
etical reﬂectances in a linear scale as a function of grazing angle from 0◦to 1.8◦. The critical
angle around ≈0.3◦is close to the one expected. As with the HXRR data, the other features
do not match.
5.4
Conclusions
The depth-graded multilayer was designed, produced and measured. The reﬂectance from 10
keV to 150 keV is low and far from the theory. Nevertheless a reﬂectance of at least 10% up
to 85 keV for a grazing angle of 0.10 degree is encouraging. One reason for the large diﬀerence
with the theory is believed to be a coating process not fully controlled as conﬁrmed by some ﬁt
trials. Another reason could be the columnar growth mentioned in Section 4.6.

Conclusion and future work
This chapter is the summary and conclusion of the document. It includes
the main results obtained throughout this project. It also contains the
future work that needs to be done to continue this project.
6.1
Summary
Investigating the coating methods lead to several results. The material combination Ni/C coat-
ings showed a ﬁtted mean roughness ≈1.5 times lower than the material combination Ni/B4C.
Reactive sputtering with a mixture of ≈76% Ar and ≈24% N2 reduced the mean interface
roughness by a factor of ≈1.7, increased the coating rate of C by a factor of ≈3.1 and shortened
the coating process by a factor of ≈1.6. Honeycomb collimation showed to limit the increase of
mean roughness with the increase of bilayer thickness while slowing down the coating process
by a factor of ≈1.9. The coating pressure inﬂuence analysis was inconclusive.
A Ni/C 150 bilayers depth-graded multilayer coating was deposited using reactive sputtering
with a mixture of ≈76% Ar and ≈24% N2 and honeycomb collimation. The process lasted
6 hours and the coatings was characterized at RXO from 10 keV to 150 keV. 10% reﬂectance
up to 85 keV was measured. Errors in layer thicknesses due to power dropouts and calibration
method and high interface roughnesses limit the measured reﬂectance. A better process control
and calibration for high bilayer thicknesses should lead to coatings closer to the theory as lower
roughness enables thinner layers which means better reﬂectance for higher energies. These results
are promising and show that Ni/C depth-graded multilayer coatings for hard X-ray telescopes
(energies greater than 80 keV) are possible by combining honeycomb collimation and reactive
sputtering.
6.2
Conclusion
To echo the project goals deﬁned in Section 1.2, the reﬂectance of Ni-based multilayer coatings
have been optimized by investigating individually the material combination, the gas composition
and the collimation method. The interfacial roughnesses have been lowered and the coating rate
evaluated. Nevertheless the challenge remains as more bilayers would be needed to reach higher
reﬂectances at high energies. The results of the depth-graded coating are clearly not good enough
for astrophysical applications but its development is still on its way and as the next section will
present, there is much more to do to keep improving it.

Investigation of gas pressure
The correct way to investigate the gas pressure eﬀects on roughness and coating rate is to ﬁx

be performed on substrates that were coated and measured with XRR. Preferably a diﬀerent

and compared with the measurement performed before the test.

During the project some coatings were made using reactive sputtering only on one layer within

6.3.5
Investigation of power dropouts
An issue when producing depth-graded multilayer is power dropouts that lead to errors in the
layer thicknesses. The reason is not clear but a longer process time and a higher power needed
for depth-graded coatings increase the number and frequency of power dropouts. Moreover, it
was noticed a slight increase of total pressure simultaneous to the power dropout. Investigation
of gas pressure around dropouts at smaller time steps (≤1 second opposed to 7 seconds now)
could shed light on the process taking place.
6.3.6
Stress investigation
During the investigation of coating methods, witness samples were coated along with substrates.
These witness samples were meant to obtained stress measurements of the coatings. The stress
is an important parameter since a high stress value can lead to the destruction of the coating
(cracks) and therefore its inability to perform its reﬂective task. The measurement of stress is
done by measuring the proﬁle on the surface of a witness sample with a stylus proﬁle before and
after coating. Unfortunately the poor quality of the data (too few measurements and misuse
of the stylus proﬁler machine) prevented any reasonable analysis. Nevertheless stress analysis
should be performed following the model of Chapter 4.

Development of Ni-based multilayers for future focusing soft
gamma ray telescopes
David A. Girou, Sonny Massahi, Erlend K. Sleire, Anders C. Jakobsen and Finn E.
Christensen
DTU Space, National Space Institute, Elektrovej, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
ABSTRACT
Ni-based multilayers are a possible solution to extend the upper energy range of hard X-ray focusing telescopes
currently limited at ≈79.4 keV by the Pt-K absorption edge.
In this study 10 bilayers multilayers with a
constant bilayer thickness were coated with the DC magnetron sputtering facility at DTU Space, characterized
at 8 keV using X-ray reﬂectometry and ﬁtted using the IMD software. Ni/C multilayers were found to have a
mean interface roughness ≈1.5 times lower than Ni/B4C multilayers. Reactive sputtering with ≈76% of Ar
and ≈24% of N2 reduced the mean interface roughness by a factor of ≈1.7. It also increased the coating rate
of C by a factor of ≈3.1 and lead to a coating process going ≈1.6 times faster. Honeycomb collimation proved
to limit the increase in mean interface roughness when the bilayer thickness increases at the price of a coating
process going ≈1.9 times longer than with separator plates. Finally a Ni/C 150 bilayers depth-graded mutilayer
was coated with reactive sputtering and honeycomb collimation and then characterized from 10 keV to 150 keV.
It showed 10% reﬂectance up to 85 keV.
Keywords: nickel, multilayer, X-ray, gamma-ray, telescope, depth-graded, reactive sputtering, collimation,
coating, magnetron sputtering
1. INTRODUCTION
The hard X-ray focusing telescopes on the NuSTAR mission1 and to be ﬂown on the ASTRO-H mission2 use
depth-graded multilayer coatings to extend the energy bandwidth to the hard X-ray range. For both missions the
upper energy range is limited to ≈79.4 keV by the platinum K absorption edge of the constituent heavy material.
One possible solution for extending the energy bandwidth into the soft gamma ray range of several hundred keV
is to use nickel (Ni) as the heavy element in the multilayer as this material does not have absorption edges in
hard X-ray/soft gamma ray range (Ni K absortion edge is at ≈8.3 keV). The challenge is to deposit smooth
enough layers to get closer to theoretical reﬂectances. This paper reports on our current status of developing
these coatings in the direct current (DC) magnetron sputtering facility at DTU Space including the combined
methods of collimation and reactive sputtering using molecular nitrogen (N2).
This study was carried out ﬁrst by investigating individually three main multilayer coating parameters which
are the material combination, the gas composition and the collimation method. For each investigation of a
parameter a multilayer coating was deposited on a silicon (Si) substrate in the DC magnetron sputtering facility
at DTU Space. Then 8.047 keV X-ray reﬂectometry (XRR) was performed, also at DTU Space. Finally the
IMD software package3 was used to characterize the layers deposited (thicknesses and roughnesses).
Subsequently a depth-graded multilayer coating was deposited using selected values for the parameters in-
vestigated in the ﬁrst part of the study. This depth-graded multilayer coating was then characterized in the
laboratory-based hard X-ray reﬂectometer at the Reﬂective X-ray Optics LLC (RXO)4 in New York City.

2. INVESTIGATION OF COATING METHODS
The multilayer coatings investigated in this section consist of 10 bilayers with a light material, carbon (C) or
boron carbide (B4C), on top of a heavy material, Ni. The substrate used is a piece of Si of thickness ≈0.8 mm
with a surface roughness of ≈2.5 ˚A. The surface coated is a rectangle of 1 × 7 cm2. Figure 1 shows one bilayer
coated on top of a Si substrate. In this section the thickness of each bilayer (also called d-spacing) is the same
for the 10 bilayers in one coating and is called multilayer period. Moreover the gamma value Γ is deﬁned here
and for this entire paper as the ratio of the thickness of the heavy material layer, zNi, and the bilayer thickness,
d, such as

The DC magnetron sputtering facility at DTU Space is a high vacuum chamber (< 10−6 Torr) in which an
inert gas, argon (Ar), is used during the coating process. Moreover a reactive gas such as N2 can be added. The
sputtering process is then called reactive sputtering. The composition of the gas ﬁlling the chamber impacts the
coating process in ways investigated hereafter. The pressure of the gas (coating pressure) during the coatings
presented through out this paper is ≈2.9 mTorr. It was found that a coating pressure < 2.3 mTorr is likely
to lead to power dropouts during the coating. A power dropout is an interval of time during which the plasma
is not sustained and therefore during which no material is deposited. This can lead to large errors in bilayer
thicknesses and Γ values.
The coating process is also impacted by the path traveled by the atoms of the sputtered material from the
target (made of the desired material) to the Si substrate.5 Collimation techniques are ways to select desired
paths. In one conﬁguration two separator plates are placed parallel to each other at a distance of 100 mm,
perpendicularly to the coated surface (one about 50 mm above the substrate and one about 50 mm below it). In
a second conﬁguration a honeycomb grid is placed parallel to the substrate at a distance of about 4 cm from the
target on one side and about 8 cm from the substrate on the other side. Figure 2a is a picture of a Si substrate
ﬁxed on a mounting plate and two separator plates separated from each other by 100 mm. Figure 2b is a picture
of a honeycomb grid placed in front of a Ni target. Note that only one of these collimations is used at once. The
inﬂuence of these collimation techniques are studied hereafter as well.
To study the impact of a parameter, for each multilayer coating carried out XRR was performed.
The
X-ray machine at DTU Space consists, among other components, of a copper rotating anode and the energy
extracted with a monochromator corresponds to the K-alpha emission line of copper (8.047 keV). Then two main
parameters were derived from an IMD ﬁt. One, directly retrieved, is the mean roughness which corresponds to
the mean value of all the interface roughnesses (for ten Ni/C or Ni/B4C bilayers these corresponds to nine
interfaces with a layer of C or B4C on top of layer of Ni, ten interfaces with a layer of Ni on top of a layer of C
or B4C and one top interface with vacuum on top of C) except for the interface with Ni on top of the Si surface
of the substrate which was set to 2.5 ˚A as speciﬁed by the manufacturer. The other, derived from the ﬁtted
bilayer period, is the coating rate or the thickness of material deposited per unit of time. This coating rate is
diﬀerent for each material. The mean roughness matters when a high number of bilayers are deposited (more
than 100) since the roughness accumulates and leads to poor reﬂectances. The coating rate is important for high
number of bilayers coatings due to time consumption (up to 12 hours for NuSTAR coatings6). Note that for
each ﬁgure and each table the number of data sets is stated, this is the number of Si substrates coated, measured

2.2 Gas composition (reactive sputtering)
The inert gas used here was Ar and the reactive gas N2. The presence of N2 is known to lower the roughness.7
The process that lowers the roughness is not well understood but the reactivity of N2 interacting on the surface
at an atomic level is assumed to play a role. Moreover N2 will be included in Ni and C deposited but in which
concentration is not investigated and not taken into account in the IMD ﬁt.
First a comparison was made between coatings performed with pure Ar and coatings performed with a mix-
ture of Ar and N2. Figure 4 displays the ﬁtted mean roughnesses of four coatings as a function of their ﬁtted
bilayer periods (≈25 ˚A, ≈50 ˚A, ≈80 ˚A and ≈120 ˚A) both for a coating performed with pure Ar and with a
composition of 76% Ar and 24% N2. Moreover the material combination used was Ni/C, the coating pressure
was ≈2.90 ± 0.03 mTorr, separator plates were used as collimation and the ﬁtted Γ value is 0.41 ± 0.05. The
presence of N2 reduced the ﬁtted mean roughness by a factor of ≈1.70. Similarly to the material combination
investigation, note that the mean roughness increase with the increase of bilayer period.

angle between the X-ray beam and the surface of the coated substrate. Also plotted is the best IMD ﬁt obtained
manually with a bilayer period of 27.90 ˚A, a mean interface roughness of 3.8 ˚A, and a Γ of 0.45 corresponding

3.2 Calibration
90% of the bilayer thickness values are comprised between 25 ˚A and 45 ˚A. Therefore a ﬁrst coating called cali-
bration run was performed with two silicon substrates to aim for depositing ten bilayers with these two bilayer
thicknesses. Moreover the mean bilayer thickness value within the 90% is d ≈30 ˚A.
The DC magnetron sputtering chamber has an outer ring on which the Si substrates are ﬁxed. This ring
rotates inside the chamber and passes by the targets (up to four) ﬁxed on an inner static ring. The outer ring
==PAGE:64==
velocity (in steps per second, one full rotation corresponds to 668,000 steps) is directly linked to the thickness of
the layer deposited on the substrate. For this depth-graded multilayer coating it was chosen to use a constant
ring velocity per bilayer (which means the ring velocity is the same for Ni and for C in a bilayer). In this
conﬁguration the ring is rotating in the same direction and one bilayer is deposited for every full rotation. This
constant ring velocity is derived from a reference ring velocity vref obtained through a linear ﬁt (ring velocity
as function of bilayer thickness) of the calibration run results. Here two targets of C and one target of Ni were
used which leads to vref = 5515 step·s−1 for a reference bilayer thickness of dref = 30 ˚A. Then for each bilayer
thickness, the ring velocity was computed by scaling the reference velocity such as
v(d) = dref
d vref.
(8)
Note that the power provided to the cathodes in which the targets sits also impacts the coating rates. Here
it was set to 1000 W for each C target and 606 W for the Ni target.
3.3 Hard X-ray reﬂectometry
After the coating process, hard X-ray reﬂectometry (HXRR) was performed at RXO at ﬁxed grazing angles for
energies ranging from 10 keV to 150 keV. Figure 7 displays the measured reﬂectance from 10 keV to 150 keV
for the grazing angles 0.10 degree, 0.15 degree and 0.20 degree. As expected the reﬂectance decreases when the
grazing angle increases. An important drop in reﬂectance from ≈0.95 to ≈0.20 is seen at ≈25 keV, ≈17 keV
and ≈12 keV for a grazing angle of respectively 0.10 degree, 0.15 degree and 0.20 degree. For each grazing
angle the other features are believed to be the consequence of several issues. The coating process experienced
several dropouts for unknown reasons leading to errors in the layer thicknesses and possibly the number of layers.
Moreover the computation of the aimed bilayer thickness values from a reference of 30 ˚A might have lead to
errors for the larger bilayers. Finally the interface roughnesses are expected to be high due to the high number
of layers, especially the ones corresponding to the top layers as the investigation of coating method revealed an
increase in mean roughness with a larger bilayer thickness. Nevertheless for a grazing angle of 0.10 degree the
reﬂectance is at least 10% up to 85 kev.
4. SUMMARY
Investigating the coating methods lead to several results. The material combination Ni/C coatings showed a
ﬁtted mean roughness ≈1.5 times lower than the material combination Ni/B4C. Reactive sputtering with a
mixture of ≈76% Ar and ≈24% N2 reduced the mean interface roughness by a factor of ≈1.7, increased the
coating rate of C by a factor of ≈3.1 and shortened the coating process by a factor of ≈1.6. Honeycomb
collimation showed to limit the increase of mean roughness with the increase of bilayer thickness while slowing
down the coating process by a factor of ≈1.9.
A Ni/C 150 bilayers depth-graded multilayer coating was deposited using reactive sputtering with a mixture
of ≈76% Ar and ≈24% N2 and honeycomb collimation. The process lasted 6 hours and the coatings was
characterized at RXO from 10 keV to 150 keV. 10% reﬂectance up to 85 keV was measured. Errors in layer
thicknesses due to power dropouts and calibration method and high interface roughnesses limit the measured
reﬂectance. A better process control and calibration for high bilayer thicknesses should lead to coatings closer
to the theory. These results are promising and show that Ni/C depth-graded multilayer coatings for hard X-
ray telescopes (energies greater than 80 keV) are possible by combining honeycomb collimation and reactive
sputtering.

At the end of the project it was found that the macro ﬁle /home/spc-mirror/specd/fl-comm.mac

values for the N2 ﬂow. These errors were spotted in time to change the analysis presented in
this document but not soon enough to re-make the missing measurements.
