Abstract
Global and national agreements have been developed to address environmental challenges,
and renewable energy penetration in the power system is increasing and will continue to in­
crease in the future in order to meet C02 emission targets. Traditional generation units, such
as thermal power plants, are heavily used in the design of power system services. Traditional
power plants can provide a variety of auxiliary services to the grid in order to improve security
and reliability of the system. Renewable energy sources, on the other hand, have limited ca­
pacity to provide those services. Renewables are connected to the power system through use
of converters, so they don’t contribute to the system inertia. Inertia is the system’s robustness to
changes. Systems with lower inertia, are more vulnerable and more likely to fail during system
disturbances. As a result, renewables should be able to participate in system inertia in order to
improve systems reliability. Fast frequency response (FFR) is one method for providing system
inertia. In order to accomplish this, kinetic energy stored in the generator rotor is extracted and
extra power is injected into the grid.
Certain precautions were necessary to ensure system reliability during the transition from tra­
ditional to renewable energy sources. As a result, several TSOs have introduced new grid
codes and technical requirements for renewable energy sources to connect to the grid in re­
cent years.The focus of this thesis is on FFR applications on floating wind turbines. As the
offshore wind market grows, new foundation technologies are being developed to meet a vari­
ety of needs. Floating wind turbines can operate effectively in deep water. The turbines are able
to harvest more stable and higher wind speeds and produce significantly more power as they
are placed further away from the shore. The IEA 15­MW reference wind turbine is one of the
most recent designs of floating wind turbines; it was chosen because it has the most capacity
and will be relevant in the future. The FFR controller is then designed in accordance with the
ENTSO­E Nordic FFR market regulations.
Both bottom­fixed and floating turbines were tested with the same configuration to see if floating
turbines have a harder time providing this support than bottom­fixed ones, and the results show
that within 4 ­ 6 m/s as well as at 10 m/s minor differences occurs, and floating turbines can
provide FFR support as well as bottom­fixed turbines. However, between 7 ­ 9 m/s Floating
turbine fails to provide the FFR support while bottom­fixed can. Wind speed, power increase
percentage, and support duration were also investigated further to determine the turbine’s lim­
itations. It demonstrates that for underrated wind speeds, duration of 5 seconds performs well
in the simulations. It is possible to increase the support percentage and duration for different
wind speed. The floating turbine’s loading is also investigated further by examining the tower
root bending moment, blade root bending moment, and shaft bearing moment. Simulations
show that changes occur during FFR support, but the turbine is capable of withstanding those
variations.
According to this thesis, providing FFR support from floating wind turbines is feasible for certain
wind speeds and promising for further investigation.

1
Introduction
A variety of national and international agreements have been developed to address environ­
mental challenges, highlighting the need to minimize greenhouse gas emissions and support
the development of renewable energy sources. Denmark has pledged to get 50% of its energy
from renewable sources by 2030 and phase out all fossil fuels by 2050, according to the re­
cent energy agreement [1]. New issues and constraints have evolved as a result of increased
rates of variable renewable energy systems (RES) in power networks. The use of renewable
energy technologies to replace conventional power plants reduces dispatchable capacity and
increases fluctuating generation, making power systems more vulnerable [2]. Moreover, most
RES sources are connected to the grid via the converter interface, contributing little to system
inertia. The significant decrease in overall system inertia poses new dangers to power system
stability and security. One of the most significant issues with low­inertia power systems is that
even little disturbances and events can result in massive frequency excursions and high rate
of change of frequency (RoCoF) values, triggering involuntary load shedding. This problem
causes power system frequency instability, which eventually leads to a blackout [3].
Concerns about supply security have grown as a result of the high levels of wind power pene­
tration into power systems. They have stimulated an increase in interest in wind turbines’ ability
to offer auxiliary services currently provided by conventional energy. Although these services
are not inherent to wind turbines, they can be stipulated by appropriate extra control actions.
Transmission system operators (TSO) are most concerned about how significant amounts of
wind energy may affect the frequency stability of the power system. In recent years, blackouts
in power systems have occurred, such as the 9th of August 2019, UK blackout, which is heavily
linked to generation from wind farms and influences public opinion on wind power plants.
The high RoCoF caused by decreasing system inertia in an interconnection drives the need
for faster energy injection to arrest declining frequency. Fast frequency response (FFR) is a
quick response to changing frequency that is becoming increasingly recognized as an important
power system reliability service.[4] Fortunately, wind turbine generators (WTG) have a unique
quality in that they can implement a fast frequency response to quickly correct for any unex­
pected frequency deviations, and so could potentially compete in this market and assist in grid
re­stabilization. Fast frequency response will necessitate wind turbines ramping up their power
output quickly, which can be challenging for the wind turbines in general, and especially for the
floating wind turbines. Wind speed can’t be increased because it’s physically impossible. Extra
power from the generator will slow down the wind turbine, reducing aerodynamic lift, which must
be avoided in order to avoid stalling. The FFR control must work in harmony with other controls
Alternative technologies with the potential to release kinetic energy quickly are currently com­
peting in the market, including, but is not limited to, batteries, supercapacitors, and other high­
dispatch­rate reserves. Hence, the focus of this thesis will be on whether it is appropriate to
expand floating turbine control by adding FFR functionality, as well as the effect of fast frequency
response on the structural loading of a floating wind turbine and the effect of wind turbine ramp
rate limits on the fast frequency response.

1.1
Motivation and Problem Description
Power system stability is the property of a power system that allows it to remain in an operating
equilibrium under normal operating conditions, and to regain an acceptable state of equilib­
rium after a disturbance [5]. In order to maintain system frequency across the entire grid and
guarantee that all linked generators rotate at the same electric speed, the physical balance
between generation and consumption should be regulated under normal power system operat­
ing conditions. The physical balance should account for instantaneously variable demand and
unpredictability of generator disturbances, which leads to frequency deviations from system fre­
quency (50 Hz in Nordic region). It is well known that in order to correct system frequency drop,
frequency control is needed [6].
Due to the inertia of their rotating masses, synchronized machines have a built­in ability to
reduce frequency deviations. Consequently, the frequency drop that results from a major de­
crease in power output is slowed. Contrarily, the majority of wind turbines (Type 3 and Type 4)
are connected to the grid via power electronic connections, which dissociate the prime mover
from the electrical system and prevent it from automatically providing frequency response [7].
In order to meet the Paris climate targets, the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA)
estimates that the worldwide wind power energy share must reach 21% by 2030 and 35% by
2050 [8]. Therefore, the integration of wind turbines into the power grid will grow over the next
few years, which will result in a decrease in inertia and a significant impact on the reliability of
the system.
The transmission system operator is in charge of ensuring the quality of supply of electric en­
ergy from generation to consumption. As a result, TSOs are developing new grid codes that re­
quire contributions from not only traditional generators but also renewable energies and power
electronics­based technologies. To address the issue, ENTSO­E published ”Fast Frequency
Reserve ­ Solution to the Nordic Inertia Challenge” [9]. Using this regulation as a guideline, this
thesis will attempt to answer the following questions:
• What are the international developments regarding FFR support?
• How should a controller action be designed to comply with the ENTSO­E regulations?
• Is it possible for Floating Wind Turbines to provide FFR support, what are the difficulties
they are encountering?
To be able to answer those questions, at first a detailed literature survey will be conducted.The
HAWC2 Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation) and reference turbine
attributes will be used to model the FFR controller utilizing the knowledge from the literature
review, which is further described in Chapters 4 and 5. Finally, it will be determined if floating
wind turbines can compete in the FFR market.

2
Literature Review
In this section, the most recent and up­to­date research will be assessed and this thesis study
will be guided by the results.
The quantity of kinetic energy present in the power system has a significant impact on how
frequency behaves. This kinetic energy is produced by all rotating devices that are directly
connected to the power system. The amount of kinetic energy in the system reduces when
renewable generation, connected to the grid through power­electronic converters, starts to re­
place conventional generation. This is also a result of the system’s increasing high voltage
direct current (HVDC) import capability. This affects the system’s capacity to withstand fre­
quency variations brought on by an imbalance between output and consumption. In order to
tackle with this issue, ”Future System Inertia phase 1” and ”Future System Inertia 2” are follow­
up projects that looked into the relationship between power system behavior and system inertia
and how to anticipate and mitigate the consequences of low­inertia scenarios [11] [12].
Nordic transmission system operators (TSOs) have integrated kinetic energy estimation into
their energy management and Supervisory Control And Data Acquisition (SCADA) systems.

Fast Frequency Response
As described in earlier chapters, FFR engages in the market to measure in scenarios with low
inertia. The power system’s frequency must stay within a specific range. Protection systems
start to function to protect machinery and keep the power system running if the frequency is not
kept close to its nominal value. When big production units or HVDC links connecting to other
synchronous systems trip, they can induce abrupt imbalances in active power being generated
and consumed, which can lead to significant frequency variations [21].
Once the frequency reaches an intolerable level, production units and loads may be discon­
nected, creating a cascading effect that could result in widespread power outages. Synchronous
generators aid the electrical grid’s ability to withstand shifts in frequency. This resistance is a
result of the kinetic energy produced by all synchronously connected rotating machinery. The
system’s capacity to withstand these shifts, however, declines as renewable generation starts
to take the place of conventional production. Modern producing facilities, like wind and solar
power plants, are made up of generating units that connect to the electrical grid via frequency
converters. These converters, on the other hand, are programmed in such a way that the oper­
ation of the generators are independent of the system frequency, resulting in reduced system
inertia. The ability of a power system to withstand changes in system frequency due to resis­
tance given by rotating masses is known as power system inertia. The system kinetic energy will
be affected by the switch from conventional to renewable generating units. Therefore, certain
measures should be taken [21] [11].
To address the technical requirements for fast frequency reserve provision in the Nordic syn­
chronous area, ENTSO­E released a report [21] in 2019. FFR providing units and FFR providing
groups, collectively referred to as FFR providing entities, must be pre­qualified in order to partic­
ipate in the Fast Frequency Reserve markets. The prequalification procedure makes sure that
all necessary technical requirements are met and that FFR providers can offer FFR in accor­
dance with the TSO’s specifications. A provider must undergo prequalification before delivering
FFR. The preliminary selection procedure entails:
1. An examination of the FFR provider’s properties
2. Successful completion of prequalification exams
3. Configuring telemetry data to be sent in real­time to the reserve connecting TSO, as well
as data logging for off­line validation.

storage resources. There are four primary features of the design that need to be taken into
account given the framework:

4.3
HAWC2
HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine Simulation Code, Second Generation) code was de­
signed to calculate the time­domain response of wind turbines. It was created between the
years of 2003 and 2006 for the Aeroelastic Design Research Program at the National Labora­
tory of Denmark in Risoe.. The code’s structural portion is built on a multibody formulation in
which each body is a combination of Timoshenko beam elements. Because the formulation is
general, it can accommodate arbitrary big rotations of the bodies as well as rather complex struc­
tural arrangements [24]. An assembly of bodies coupled with constraint equations is used to
simulate the turbine. Examples of constraints include rigid couplings, bearings, and prescribed
fixed bearing angles. The code’s aerodynamic section is based on blade element momentum
theory (BEM), but it goes beyond the traditional approach to handle dynamic inflow, dynamic
stall, skew inflow, shear effects on the induction, and effects from huge deflections. There are
numerous turbulence forms available. One or more dynamic link libraries (DLLs) are used to
control the turbine. The model’f floater properties are based on the WAMIT, radiation/diffraction
code. Added mass, radiation damping and wave load properties are calculated in frequency
domain and evaluated through WAMIT [24].
The master.htc document, which describes all the data that goes into setting up the model
for the time simulation, contains the main inputs, such as the structure of the turbine, wind,
aerodynamic and hydrodynamic properties and controller DLL. The signals of interest are also

DTU Wind Energy Controller
The Basic DTU controller is utilized to carry out the simulations on the floating turbine. The
controller is written in Fortran90 and uses the DLL interface format for HAWC2. The primary
justification for using this controller is that it was created at DTU and that the source code is read­
ily available. The controller has switching mechanisms that guarantee a smooth changeover
between the two operating modes, as well as partial and full load operation capabilities [25].
The DTU Wind Energy Controller (DTU WEC) is coupling DDLs with aero­elastic simulation tools
with InterfaceGate function. Control signals are computed per time step with updateregulation
function and control servo actions fed back to the simulation via controlaction function at each
time step [27]. Figure 4.2 displays top­level structure of DTU WEC controller
Figure 4.2: Black diagram of top­level DTU WEC [27]
DTU WEC includes fundamental control functions such collective pitch, a proportional­integral­
derivative (PID) controller for the generator’s torque, and supervisory controls (cut­in, cut­out,
emergency stop and system status monitoring). The main controller also incorporates some of
the more advanced control features, such as the curtailment feature, active drive train damper,
tower top vibration damper, and rotor­effective wind speed estimate [27].
To avoid 3­period (3P) interference with the tower/monopile natural frequency, the rotor operates
at a minimum rotational speed of 5 RPM and reaches a rated rotational speed of 7.55 RPM at
10.59 m/s, resulting in a maximum nominal tip speed of 95 m/s. The rotor has a pitch setting of
0° at the design tip­speed ratio (TSR), but it operates with positive pitch at low wind speeds to
track maximum power while maintaining the rotor speed. The rotor begins pitching at the rated
wind speed of 10.59 m/s [22].
Two active proportional integral derivative (PID) controllers are used in the controller to control
the generator torque and blade pitch angles. To keep the turbine operating within the boundaries
of the design, saturation limits on rotor speeds and blade pitch angles are put in place. The
controller operation can be divided into three regions:
• 3m/s ≤U ≤6.98m/s; minimum rotor speed : The generator torque is controlled by a
PID controller to maintain the turbine at its 5 rpm minimum rotor speed. The PID controller
works in tandem with the torque controller to control the rated wind speed. Based on the
filtered hub­height wind speed, pitch angles are calculated using a look­up table.

5.2
Second Implementation
The first implementation was able to provide the FFR support defined by ENTSO­E, but it did
not model wind shear, tower shadow, turbulence, or wave effects. A real turbine will always be
subject to these effects, which will have a significant impact on the turbine’s stability. The wave
and wind effects are especially important in the design of the floater foundation. Therefore, in
this study, the model developed in the first study is expanded to include those effects.
The first controller was designed as an external DLL; the new controller is created as an extra
subroutine in the DTU WEC’s controller turbine controller mod to allow access to important
parameters. The subroutine begins with input and local variables. Low­pass filters for generator
speed and electrical power are then introduced. Unlike the first study, this application is not
based on a baseline study. The electrical power at the precise start time of the activation is
saved and used in the same way that Pss is. The turbulent wind speed and electrical power
during the simulation are depicted in Figure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Turbulent Wind Speed VS. Electrical Power
As can be seen, when the disturbing effects are taken into account, the controller can still detect
the power at the start of the activation and provides support based on that power level. During
the support period, this implementation is running smoothly. However, one unexpected result of
this implementation is that, despite the fact that generator torque exceeds aerodynamic torque,
the generator speed is unaffected by this change. The reason behind this is still unknown.
Because the simulation detects no deceleration, there is no need for recovery. As a result,
the recovery portion of the FFR implementation cannot be studied. The code for the second
implementation can be found in the appendix A.3.2.
As second implementation fails to provide whole FFR profile, the first implementation is used to
test various cases for wind speed and support properties. The results of the first implementation
are discussed further in Chapter 6.

6.1
Effects of FFR on Bottom­Fixed and Floating Turbines
It was predicted at the start of the study that FFR would have a greater impact on the floating
turbines than the bottom fixed turbines. The main reason for this was that the floating motion of
the floating turbines makes it difficult for the turbine to return to equilibrium. To investigate this
phenomenon, simulations for bottom­fixed and floating turbines were run as part of the same
case studies. Figure 6.1 displays turbines’ responses at 6 m/s for a 2% power increase with a
support duration of 5 seconds.
Figure 6.1: Bottom­Fixed vs. Floating Turbine power and generator speed at 6m/s for a 2%
increase with a support duration of 5 seconds
When the results were compared, it was discovered that the floating turbine’s initial power before
the FFR is around 2% less than that of the bottom fixed turbine. Because the floating turbine
is tilted, it produces less aerodynamic torque and thus produces slightly less power than the
bottom­fixed turbine. When the same initial power value is used in the analysis, the floating
turbine cannot be successful in the recovery phase. Recovery is more challenging than with a
bottom­fixed turbine since the torque must be increased further in order to produce the same
amount of power. As a result, the rotor decelerates more. The difference in initial power of the
turbines is caused by differences in aerodynamic torque. The power output is determined by
the generator’s speed and torque. While the generator speed is the same for both floating and
bottom­fixed turbines, the aerodynamic torque is lower for the floating turbine, resulting a lower
power.
To address this issue, the floating turbine’s initial power was set to its value at the time of FFR
activation. Figure 6.2 shows the comparison of the turbine responses at 6 m/s for a 2% increase
with a duration of 5 seconds using this adjusted starting power for the floating turbine.

For floating turbines, the recovery period is longer. The generator speed and applied torque
have a significant impact on how long the recovery takes. During activation, the generator speed
behaves approximately identically for both the floating and bottom­fixed turbines, as shown
in Figure 6.3. However, it takes longer to accelerate the turbine during recovery. When the
generator speed drops, the bottom­fixed turbine speeds up rapidly, whereas the floating turbine
takes some time. At the same time, the floater pitch increases slightly; this increase causes a
second drop in thrust, so the generator speed remains at a lower level for a longer period of
time than the bottom­fixed case.
The IEA 15­MW RWT is a direct­drive wind turbine, which means that the rotor and generator
rotate at the same rate. When the generator speed decreases, the thrust decreases as well,
which means the floater will pitch more towards to the wind, causing the floater pitch angle to
decrease and the motion in the surge to stabilize.
At the end of the recovery period, there is a second peak in the power. This peak is one of
the concerns expressed by the TSOs when providing FFR support because it may cause a
disturbance in the power system. With an optimal, closed­loop FFR controller, this peak would
only be a minor overshoot of the steady state power.

As the support percentage increases, the support duration decreases. For example, at 5%
support, the turbine provides significantly more power to the system, causing the generator to
decelerate significantly. This increase in power causes the torque to increase further, allowing it
to decrease more quickly and in accordance with the 25 percent limit during the recovery period.
This rapid torque reduction aids in the quicker attainment of the initial level of FFR for the gen­
erator torque and aerodynamic torque balance. As a result, the recovery time tends to shorten
as support percentage rises.
However, when increasing the percentage of support, caution must be exercised. Although
higher percentages of support improved better, there is a limit to this. This limit is determined
by the turbine’s mechanical and electrical responses. Previous research has shown that when
the increase exceeds 9%, the turbine struggles to provide support and fails to complete the
recovery process [20]. As a result, when determining the percentage and duration of support,
it is critical to keep the turbine’s mechanical and electrical limits in mind.

Blade Root Bending Moment
Blade element momentum theory combines general momentum theory with a blade element
approach to calculate forces acting on the blade for a given wind turbine configuration [28].
Aerodynamic forces, lift and drag can be projected in to a component in the direction of the

6.6
Recovery Period
The results for various cases show that failure of the FFR most likely occurs during the recovery
period. This is due to the fact that the expected power level to return is generally overestimated,
and the turbine does not have enough sources to recover. To further assess the impact of
limiting power by 25% during the recovery period, the limit was increased to 100% to aid turbine
recovery.Figure 6.14 displays the response of floating wind turbine at 7 m/s with 2% increment
of the power for 5 seconds with 25% and 100% recovery limits respectively.
Figure 6.14: Response for 25% and 100% of recovery limit
As shown in the figure, the turbine can recover with a higher recovery limit. As can be seen from
the 100 percent plot, the power turbine tries to achieve is far greater than the available power
at the turbine (orange line). Even though the turbine can recover with a relaxed limit, it also
has a second peak in power that is higher than the initial FFR support, and dropping the power
completely may cause a second under­frequency event, so ENTSO­E chose a dead­band of
25%.
It is obvious that the recovery period is the most difficult part of FFR support; the best way to
deal with this issue is to track the available power. However, estimating available power is a
complex process due to changes in wake characteristics during curtailment [30].
PossPOW algorithm [31], is developed at DTU Wind Energy in order to help the estimation of
available power for down regulating the wind farm from its maximum possible power. Even
though FFR implementation attempts to do the exact opposite of down regulation, the recovery
period can be thought of as the FFR cycle’s down regulation period. PossPOW first estimates
local wind speed upstream, then uses the wake model to calculate wind speed within the wind
farm, and finally calculates production capacity. If FFR is combined with this methodology, the
chances of successful recovery and cycle completion increase.

7
Conclusions
This project has moved forward with the goal of determining whether floating turbines can pro­
vide FFR support during frequency events. The system inertia has been decreasing in recent
years, owing to the integration of renewable energy sources into the electricity grid. Because
the decrease in system inertia makes the system more susceptible to malfunctions, TSOs have
begun to take steps to increase the reliability of the electricity network, as the integration of
renewable energy sources into the system grows in the future. Obtaining FFR support from
wind turbine generators is a topic that many researchers have studied in recent years. When
the system frequency falls below an acceptable level, the wind turbines will be able to provide
extra active power to the system for a short period of time thanks to FFR technology, assisting
the system to regain balance. According to the findings of the literature review, turbines can
provide this assistance. However, there has been no research into floating turbines. As a re­
sult, in accordance with the findings of the literature review, an FFR controller was developed to
investigate receiving this support from floating turbines. This control method was developed by
paying attention to the FFR market participation requirements defined by ENTSO­E in ”Techni­
cal Requirements for Fast Frequency Reserve Provision in the Nordic Synchronous Area.” [21].
This standard has been thoroughly explained in Chapter 3, and participation with a 1.0 second
activation time at 49.6 Hz frequency is preferred over the other two options.
To create the controller, two studies were carried out. The first study was a simple application
developed by James Hakim [20], to determine whether turbines can provide this support. The
performance of this control, designed for fixed­base turbines, was tested in floating turbines,
and the behaviour during the support period was discussed in Chapter 6. These findings show
that floating turbines, can provide the FFR support in certain wind speed ranges. However
this controller ignored important effects such as turbulence, wind shear, tower shadow and
waves, and was based on preliminary measured power values, where in reality this power value
fluctuates over time. As a result, a second study was conducted to extend this controller. To
include the aforementioned effects, power values were obtained instantly in the second study.
However, this application was not successful because the change in aerodynamic torque and
generator torque had no effect on the generator speed, resulting in the generator not slowing
down and the recovery period being skipped.
The effects of FFR support on the turbine was determined by evaluating the results based on
wind speed, support duration, and support percentage. For wind speeds ranging between 7 to
9 m/s, the turbine had a difficult time recovering. According to the DTU WEC, below 6.89 m/s,
the turbine operated to maintain its minimum rotor speed of 5 RPM. Above 6.89 m/s to rated
wind speed of 10,59 m/s, the control strategy shifted to maintain the turbine at the optimal TSR.
The rotor speed was regulated using a PID controller on the generator torque to operate at the
turbine’s optimal TSR. Based on rotor speed, the controller attempted to optimize power output.
When FFR operated in this region, the turbine was unable to complete the FFR cycle and failed
at the recovery. However, at 10 m/s (almost rated wind speed) wind turbine is able to deliver a
smooth FFR support. This is an excellent example of why it is critical to consider the operational
conditions of the main controller when attempting to integrate FFR.
The recovery period shortens as the percentage of support increases. However, depending
on the wind speed, recovery may fail as the support percentage increases. According to the
ENTSO­E report, one generating entity can participate in the FFR market with a maximum active
power injection of 50 MW. For a wind farm of 66 IEA 15­MW RWTs, a 5% increase in power will
provide approximately 50 MW injection to the grid. However, this is the upper limit of FFR; wind
Thesis title
39
==PAGE:48==
farm operators may choose to deliver less than 50 MW. Therefore, wind farm operators should
carefully consider how much injection they want to deliver to the grid and bid accordingly. As
a result, depending on the size and capacity of the wind farm, the turbine support percentage
should be carefully chosen.
One of the primary goal of implementing FFR regulations on wind turbines is to determine how it
affects loads and how those affect the turbine. Tower and blade root bending moments, as well
as shaft bearing moment, were investigated. Except in cases where FFR failed, the maximum
change occurred at 5% with 5 seconds of support, where tower and blade root bending moments
decreased by 25% and 21%, respectively, and the shaft bearing moment increased by 30%.
This demonstrates that in successful FFR delivery cases, the loading does not exceed the
turbine’s maximum loading capacity.
In conclusion, floating wind turbines can provide FFR support at certain wind speed ranges.
The success of this support is determined by a variety of factors, including wind speed, support
percentage, and support duration. The best solution found in this study is a 5s of support time.
This time period is able to provide FFR support for below rated wind speeds except 7 ­ 9 m/s.
7.1
Future Work
Future work should focus on making things more realistic. First and foremost, power should be
read instantly, and available power should be taken into account during the recovery process.
While ensuring that the power at the start of the support and the power at the end of the support
are the same, the turbine’s available power should be followed, and the recovery period should
be calculated using this available power rather than the power at the start.
The future modification of the controller should have a closed loop structure because the existing
controller structure is quite simple with IF statements. The compatibility of this new control mode
with existing controllers must also be investigated further. It is critical that this new operation
mode (FFR mode) can adapt to the controller’s overall strategy. For example, for the DTU WEC
controller, FFR should be able to perform well in all three operational regions. This will most
likely allow the turbine to operate in all wind speed ranges.
Future work should also concentrate on smoother transitions between FFR regions. The current
controller has a very steep transition from buffer zone to recovery zone, resulting in a small
undershoot at the recovery. A better strategy is also needed for the implementation of the
recovery region. The current controller tries to return to initial power, but that power level may
not be present at the end of the recovery period, so it fails. To avoid this problem, tracking the
available power would be the solution.
Lastly, FFR support was required at the wind farm level, where wind profiles differed and wake
effect played an important role. The turbines on a floating wind farm, in particular, can move
in response to wave and wind loads. When compared to bottom­fixed foundations, this motion
makes calculating wakes more difficult. Wake effects should also be considered in the con­
troller’s future design, and wind farm operators should make an informed decision about how
much active power injection they want to participate in the market with.
