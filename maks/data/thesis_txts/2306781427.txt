INTRODUCTION 
 
The investigation of the technology at the root of lidar devices, based on the analysis of the 
information contained in the Doppler frequency shift, has been developed in the seventies with the 
increasing of the telecommunication industry. In the last decades lidars technology has been 
developed towards the analysis of wind resources. In fact, lidars can bypass some issues that can 
interest traditional cup anemometers, for example mechanical problems like bearing friction 
connected to the temperature change and icing, generally connected to the mobility of the cup 
anemometer components.  Also the issues connected with the installation and maintenance do not 
represent a primary problem for lidars, as they do not need to be installed on a high met mast. The 
usefulness of the remote sensing goes at the same pace as the progress in the wind turbine 
technologies, that more and more often are installed in sites that are difficult to reach. For instance, 
it is easy to understand that the growing of offshore technologies requires measurement campaign 
off the coast where, obviously, the transportation and the installation of met masts is problematic. 
(J. Cariou, M. Boquet, 2014). 
There are two main kinds of lidar devices:  the continuous wave lidars, which beams are specifically 
focused to measure the wind speed at the required distance, and the pulsed lidars, which obtain the 
value of the wind speed by averaging the information carried by several pulses. The normative   IEC 
6110-12-1 includes some guidelines for the utilization of remote sensing, but it does not include 
nacelle lidars as certificated instrument for the power curve measurement (Vignaroli, 2015). 
The power curve measured with a nacelle lidar requires the installation of a nacelle lidar (horizontal 
lidar) on the hub of the turbine. The lidar yaw inclination (around the z axis) can be easily modified 
depending on the turbine’s hub inclination, so that the lidar will always be able to measure the 
upstream wind speed. Also the roll inclination and the tilt inclination can easily be regulated 
depending on the exigence (hub level, tripods conditions etc.).  
The method for establishing the power curve is very similar to the one described in IEC 61400-12-1, 
in the sense that the needed data are the same: wind speed, wind direction, electrical power, air 
temperature, air pressure, air conditions. (Wagner, Rivera, Antoniou, Davoust, & Friis Pedersen, 
2013). 
In this study an analysis of the LEOSPHERE Iris nacelle lidar behavior is carried out by analyzing the 
sensitivity of the data collected during calibrations of 9 lidar units installed in Høvsøre site, in the 
north of Jutland, carried out between 2014 and 2015. Specifically, the lidar speed differences and 
percentage difference respect to a reference instrument – in this case a cup anemometer – have 
4 
 
==PAGE:8==
been studied as a function of environmental parameters which are expected to affect the lidar 
measurements. The environmental parameters taken in account are the atmospheric temperature, 
the pressure, the relative humidity, the wind direction, the sonic tilt angle and the CNR variation. The 
sensitivity analysis of an instrument has to be carried out after having analyzed his calibration 
characteristic which determines the uncertainty level of the measurement.  Differently from the cup 
anemometer sensitivity, which has been deeply studied throughout the years, there are not 
numerous previous studies regarding the lidar behavior. Despite lidar devices may be influenced by 
environmental parameters, at present there is still no evidence of this; specifically, according to the 
normative IEC 61400-12-1, there is the need of defining a number classification for the lidar; so far, 
it still has not been done. The sensitivity of an instrument should be studied after having analyzed its 
calibration characteristics, which give information about the uncertainty level that characterizes the 
device. Cup anemometers are generally the instruments used as a reference for the lidar sensitivity 
study, as the sensitivity behavior of the cups is easily definable during the calibration in the wind 
tunnel – a relatively small tube of a few squared meters, in which the environmental condition are 
easily applicable and modifiable. Basically, only the comparison of the lidar behavior respect to the 
one instrument which characteristics are well known can give some proper information about the 
general lidar sensitivity characteristics. The lidar sensitivity analysis could lead to several results that 
may make necessary the modification of the data filtering and, consequentially, of the lidar 
calibration results.

General principles of a nacelle pulsed lidars 
 
Phisical principle that characterizes the lidar measurement 
 
 
 
Figure 1: LEOSPHERE wind iris nacelle lidar 
 
Pulsed lidars are remote sensing devices for measuring the wind speed. The physical principles 
on which the measurements are carried out is the Doppler effect; this is a common 
characteristic also with the continuous wave lidar. In case of the Iris Leosphere pulsed nacelle 
lidars used in this study, there is the interest in measuring the wind speed components that 
contribute at the turbine power production; specifically, the tangential and the longitudinal 
components of the wind speed. This considered, the Iris nacelle lidar is provided by two 
beams, with an opening angle of 30 degrees between them. The specific principle of the 
Doppler frequency shift measurement according with each of the two beams of the IRIS are 
working is the following: a pulse of light is sent in the atmosphere at a certain frequency fi. 
The pulse of light hits particle floating at a certain distance, bouncing back to the lidar at a 
different frequency ff. The frequency gap between fi and ff depends on the wind speed at 
which the particle was floating.

Figure 2: physical principle on which the lidar measurement are based: Doppler frequency shift 
 
How is the frequency shift elaborated? 
 
Once the backscatter signal is back in the lidar device, the detector analyzes its frequency in 
comparsion with the initial frequency. Specifically, the backscatter frequency is mixed with an 
exact copy of the emitted wave; and the output of this mix is an electric current that depends 
on the frequency difference between the two waves. (Courtney M. , 2015). It is difficult to 
extract interesting information just from the current that carries information only about the 
frequency shift obtained by one pulse, as the signal is characterized by a lot of noise. Because 
of this, several pulsed are launched in the atmosphere along a pre-determined distance; and 
the collected current signals are then averaged. By carrying out this procedure, the most 
representative frequency is determined, and as a consequence the radial wind speed.   
 
Figure 3: average frequency spectrum obtained by the detector (Courtney M. , 2015)

Range gate: how different speed signals are collected along different distances?  
 
The lidar actually measures along a volume of space: the three (or two, in this case of the nacelle 
lidar) components of the wind speed – as it is going to be analyzed later (ref. figure 4) – are extracted 
by analyzing pulsed launched from the two beams. The two beams can only measure at a certain 
longitudinal distance between each other that depends on the transversal distance and the opening 
angle. Furthermore it has been said that there is the need of averaging the information carried by 
several pulses in order to minimize the noise to obtain a trustable signal. The averaged information 
comes from pulses sent at different distance, comprehended in a certain range. In the case of the 
lidars analyzed in this study, the probe length is 60 meters. So, basically, the averaged information 
to obtain characteristic Doppler frequency shift shown in figure 2 are collected along a range of 60 
meters. To carry out this process, the lidar emits a very short pulse of light, characterized by a period 
Tp . The arrival time of the pulse of light permits the determination of the distance from where the 
signal came from.  This technique is called range-gating, and the details are explained below:

Methods 
 
Calibration of the nacelle lidar 
 
The most obvious way to calibrate a nacelle lidar would be by directing his centerline towards a 
reference instrument. Both the lidar and the reference instrument have to be installed on sufficiently 
high masts, in order to avoid the affection of obstacles, terrain roughness change or orography. 
(Courtney M. , 2013).  
It has been already mentioned that one important condition for measuring with a Nacelle lidar, is the 
possibility to assume the homogeneity of the horizontal wind speed; in case of any kind of obstacles 
or terrain characteristics that can affect the flow, this condition cannot be fulfilled. (J. Cariou, M. 
Boquet, 2014) 
13 
 
==PAGE:17==
Considering that the IRIS has an opening angle of 30 degrees and a measuring range between 60 and 
400 meters, it is easy to understand that mounting it on a mast sufficiently high, which allow to avoid 
the interference of the ground and of the surrounding obstacles, is not much trustable.  
The line of sight calibration bypasses this problem: the two beams are calibrated separately, and each 
of them provides only the radial wind speed value. Basically, instead of combining the radial wind 
speeds provided by the two beams of the nacelle lidar the wind speed data collected by the reference 
instrument are projected in the lidar radial direction and compared with the radial wind speed 
recorded by one beam at a time (Courtney M. , 2013).  
 
Line-of-sight calibration procedure 
 
 
Figure 6: calibration of nacelle lidar 
 
The lidar is not actually directly measuring the horizontal wind speed:  it has been preciously explained 
that it measures two radial wind speeds instead, as it is shown in figure 6: As already explained, the 
reconstruction of the horizontal wind speed vector is made starting by some assumptions, and it is 
carried out by using a mathematical model.  During the calibration presented in this study, the lidar 
beams are calibrated one at time in different consecutive periods.  Figure 6 represents the calibration 
procedure: the pulse is directed to the cup anemometer, and from the backscatter signal the radial 
wind speed in the point where the reference instrument is located is be calculated. Anyway, the cup 
anemometer measures the horizontal wind speed, while the lidar measures the radial wind speed: 
this considered, these two different ways of measuring have to be matched for carrying out the 
calibration procedure. This considered, there is the need of projecting the wind speed measured by 
the cup anemometer in the line-of-sight direction that is the direction of the lidar beam that is being 
calibrated, depending on the wind direction 10-minutes averages provided by the sonic anemometer

(Courtney M. , 2013). Considering the small distance between the mast on which the cup anemometer 
is mounted and the one on which is mounted the sonic, in fact, the direction of the wind that strikes 
the cup anemometer should coincide with the direction of the wind measured by the sonic (this is not 
completely true, as some flow distortion are observed in correspondence of some wind directions, as 
it is going to be presented hereafter). 
 
Høvsøre test site 
 
The calibration of the 9 IRIS units took place at the Test Station for Large Wind Turbines, Høvsøre, 
Denmark. This test station comprises five test pads, each with an upstream meteorological mast. It is 
located on the west coast of Jutland at a location where the coastline runs approximately NS, 
perpendicular to the prevailing W winds. At the southern end of this line, a 116m meteorological mast 
has been placed in extension, close to six wind turbines. The Wind IRIS lidar is mounted on a platform 
at 9.5m a.g.l. in the previously mentioned 116m meteorological mast. One beam at a time 
(represented by the white line in figure 7) is trained towards masts at a distance of 267m (ref. Figure

Calibration of IRIS unit 188 
 
Before to conduct the sensitivity study, a calibration of IRIS unit 188 has been carried out, and 
then compared with the results of a previous calibration (Yankova, 2015). For the aim of this 
project, the preliminary study of one of the lidar unit calibration is important because there 
are the possibilities that the applied filters on previous calibration have to be modified 
depending on the sensitivity results. The calibration has carried out by analyzing the slope and 
intersect coefficients of the regression line which defines the relationship between the IRIS 
measurement and the cup projected wind speed has been defined, as well as the R-squared 
error.

Sensitivity analysis of the nacelle lidar units 
 
The calibration procedure for a nacelle LIDAR has been already presented; but it is not sufficient 
to give a complete overview of the instrument characteristics. In the same way as the cup 
anemometer, in order to describe properly the characteristics of a Nacelle Lidar, the analysis 
of the sensitivity to the environmental parameters has to be carried out.  
The aim of the sensitivity analysis is to study the response of the instrument at the variation of 
the environmental conditions. If a lidar is particularly sensible to the variation of a certain 
environmental parameter, e.g. the wind direction, the absolute temperature or the absolute 
pressure, the peculiarities have to be recorded and to be annotated as a characteristic of the 
instrument.  
The analysis is made by observing the lidar collected wind measurement always taking the 
corresponding cup anemometer measurement as a reference; practically, it has been analyzed 
the gap between lidar and cup collected data by varying the considered environmental 
parameter. The sensitivity analysis of a nacelle lidar is not a completely linear procedure: 
despite the fact that the analysis starts by considering calibrated data applying all the filters 
that have been applied during the calibration, it may be figured out that classifying the behavior 
of the lidar is easier by removing some filters, or by fatherly restricting the data selection.

 Sensitivity analysis of IRIS nacelle lidars 
 
In order to carry out the sensitivity analysis, the lidar behavior has to be investigated; practically, this 
means that all the data used for the sensitivity analysis must be calibrated with a traceable instrument 
as a reference. In this case, as it has been said, the reference instrument is the cup anemometer, 
which collected data are projected in the line-of-sight direction depending on the wind direction data 
collected by the sonic anemometer as a reference. The data used for the sensitivity analysis are the 
ones collected during all the calibration periods in Høvsore by the different lidar units. It would not 
make much sense to use other data collected in different periods and different sites: the sensitivity 
can obviously be influenced by the field settings, and using data collected in different field settings 
would make impossible to come out with some useful observations. Furthermore, since the sensitivity 
of the IRIS data is evaluated by considering as a reference the cup anemometer, the reference 
instrument has to be traceable and its sensitivity characteristics must be well defined. In order to 
discuss the results, in fact, it is necessary to take in account also the cup anemometer sensitivity 
characteristics in order to evaluate which sensitivity behavior is due to the cup anemometer and 
which one is due to the IRIS unit.  
Summarizing, a sensitivity analysis must consider lidar measurement in comparison with the 
measurement of well traceable reference instrument. These initial condition are very similar to the 
ones necessary for the calibration of the Iris units: it has already mentioned, in fact, that the filters 
used for the calibration and the sensitivity analysis are initially the same. The difference is that, since 
the aim is not defining the characteristic of one specific Iris unit, all the data from the different 18 
calibration periods of the lidars can be considered for the analysis. As a consequence, the available 
data are significantly more than the ones available for a singular calibration which, in case of unit 188, 
lasted only three weeks for every beam (around 13700, with all the applied filters).  The more 
numerous are the available data, the easier will be visualizing a certain data trend; this considered, 
the biggest possible number of validated data should be used. 
First of all, the wind speed absolute and percentage differences have been plotted as a function of 
the environmental parameters as a preliminary analysis. The aim is to investigate particular data trend 
in the obtained plots. The results are presented with the respective Gaussian distribution and boxplot, 
which allow a visualization of the outliers and a general overview of the data distribution.

Why is the hypothesis of a sonic tilt inclination reasonable? 
 
If the wind doesn’t flow horizontally wind turbines may be exposed to significant loads, since 
the blade travelling upwards is under different loads to the blade travelling downwards 
(Bennetts, 2011).  For this reason, the determination of the wind flow angle that characterizes 
site is really important. The inflow angle is defined as angle the off the horizontal plane at 
which the mean wind flow comes into the rotor of a wind turbine. A significantly high inflow 
inclination angle is generally found in complex terrains with steep slopes.   Sonic anemometers 
are instruments generally used for the measurement of the inflow angle (Dr. C. Meissner, 
2011) However, as it has been shown in this case, the sonic anemometer measurements not 
always provides a trustable description of the inflow angle: a tilted sonic anemometer would 
of course lead to error in the inflow angle measurement. The sonic anemometer inclination 
has been assumed as the most verisimilar hypothesis after having observed the data plot, 
which suggested a cosine-wave behavior of the sonic tilt angle as a function of the wind 
direction: it has already been explained, in fact, that it has been chosen to exclude an 
inclination of the terrain. It is natural to wonder what would happen if the hypothesis of a 
sonic anemometer inclination was incorrect, and the flow was actually inclined due to the 
terrain characteristics.

the lidar and by the sonic anemometer – or by the sonic and the cup anemometer; instead it 
has observed in the calibrations and in the sensitivity analysis that the gap between the two 
data series is generally not significant.

However, another important condition is a certain longitudinal homogeneity. The wind speed

time range, each of them characterized from a certain period Tp. Their backscatter signal

will probably lead to lower wind speed values due to the low recorded speed value. Similar 
circumstances can be the cause of significant gaps between measurements collected at few 
meters of distance.  This considered, there is the suspect that the lower values of wind speed 
recorded at the margins of the considered sector may be caused by obstacles located at a

CONCLUSIONS 
 
The sensitivity analysis of the lidar data has clearly shown a connection between the sonic tilt angle and the 
wind direction behavior. After this observation, the analysis of the tilt angle and of the wind direction has 
been deepen by plotting the tilt angle as a function of the wind direction. It has then observed that the 
relationship between the tilt angle and the wind direction has the shape of a cosine wave function. The tilt 
inclination of the flow could also depend on temporary terrain characteristics; in this case, however, the 
connection between the tilt angle variation and the wind direction looked a way too regular to consider the 
variation of the atmospheric condition as the main responsible for the tilt variation. It has then observed that 
this characteristic can be due to two factors: 
• 
The terrain is characterized by a steep slope. In this case, the incoming wind speed varies its tilt 
depending on the direction it comes from (ref. figure 71) 
This hypothesis has not been evaluated as a verisimilar condition, because the characteristics of Høvsøre 
site are fully known. Previous studies classify this site as an “A class” site and report the Høvsøre site as 
a site considerably flat, to be an onshore site.  
• 
The instrument which measures the tilt angle – that in this case is the sonic anemometer – is inclined 
respect to the z-axis. The flow, then, is actually inclined only respect to the instrument and it does 
not have particular tilt characteristics due to a steep terrain.  
This second hypothesis that has been considered as the more trustable has led to the decision of 
correcting tilt angle data in order to eliminate their dependency on the sonic anemometer inclination. 
The correction the tilt angle data leads to the modification in the calibration procedure – as the data now 
have to be filtered depending on the new values of the tilt angle. It has seen that the calibration 
characteristics do not really change by correcting the sonic tilt inclination, apart for the considered 
number of data, that change pretty much by varying the filters on the tilt angle. Even thought so as to 
calibrating a lidar unit there are no evident changes considering or not considering the correction on the 
tilt angle, it is wise to apply the tilt angle correction before to filtering the tilt angle during the calibration 
and the sensitivity analysis : observations about the sensitivity of an instrument become generally easier 
with the increasing of the amount of the available data, even if the actual sensitivity results are not 
expected to change that much by correcting the tilt. Despite the fact that in this case of unit 188 the 
calibration results don’t change, it may be that in other periods the calibration is affected by the number 
of available data. It has been impossible to determine how much old is the tilt angle inclination, since 
84 
 
==PAGE:88==
also one of the less recent calibrations showed a cosine wave tilt behavior. Probably all the calibration of 
the Iris units carried out in the Høvsøre test site were characterized by this tilt angle inclination, since it 
has been observed that also the oldest collected data are characterized by the same tilt inclination as the 
one observed for unit 188.  
It has seen that the sensitivity of the cup anemometer has widely affected the results of the lidar 
sensitivity analysis. The temperature, for instance, looks like an issue more for the cup anemometer than 
for the lidar, since the plot of the speed differences between the lidar and the cup anemometer show 
the characteristic shape of the cup anemometer sensitivity. The same impression is given by the 
turbulence intensity plot that looks close to the one obtained during the cup calibration, after the 
restriction of the wind direction sector. Other parameters such as the relative humidity and the 
atmospheric pressure seems not to affect significantly the measurements. From this study it actually 
seems that the main issues that have to be taken in account during the lidar calibration and sensitivity 
analysis, are the ones connected with the field setting. The field setting affection cannot absolutely be 
underestimated: both the lidar and the reference instrumentation are easily affected by it in different 
ways that are hard to be taken in account universally. Specifically, even if  Høvsøre is note as a flat, well 
known territory, it has noticed that also characteristics apparently not that significance like the presence 
of bushes or small obstacles can affect the measurement collection. The met masts themselves, actually, 
may represent a problem. Groups of obstacles located some meters away from the reference 
instrumentation affect significantly the analysis, absolutely determining the dependency between the 
wind direction and the turbulence intensity in the site. Similarly to what has been done by correcting the 
tilt, also the dependency between the turbulence intensity and the wind direction should be taken in 
account in the calibration of the Lidar, as the results may be falsified by some wind speed data that may 
be too much affected by the field setting. In this case it is a matter of good sense: it can be chosen to 
consider only  the data which turbulence intensity does not depend on the wind direction  by furtherly 
restricting the validated  sector (more specifically, by considering data in a range between 288 and 328, 
like it has been done in the section Horizontal turbulence intensity) limiting significantly the data 
availability and risking to compromise the reliability of the calibration, or it can be evaluated that the 
affection of the dependency between the wind direction and the turbulence intensity would probably be 
less significant than a big data reduction. A solution like this would maybe improve the sensitivity analysis 
– or at least, they would not worse it – as the data available for the sensitivity analysis from all the 9 lidar 
units are a lot; and they would verisimilarly be enough even with a further data restriction. However, 
there is the suspicion that they may affect more significantly the calibration, as the data available for a 
singular calibration of one beam are not that many - approximately 1/18 of the one available for the 
85 
 
==PAGE:89==
sensitivity analysis. However, a calibration for unit 188 with the new valid sectors (288-328 degrees) has 
been carried out and presented in the appendixes. It is interesting to notice that the R-squared coefficient 
actually improves. This improvement of the R-squared coefficient by further restricting the sector should 
not be assumed as a characteristic of all the 18 calibrations without other analysis: the different 
calibrations can in fact be affected by several factors, first and foremost the number of available data in 
each calibration. At last, it has to be considered that the best solution to improve the calibration and the 
sensitivity analysis without the need of an ulterior data filtering would be to operate directly on the field 
correcting the sonic tilt, lifting or moving the reference instrumentation to decrease the dependency 
from the obstacle and taking care of the field condition nearby the zone in general.
