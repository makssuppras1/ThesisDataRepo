Abstract 
Lignocellulosic biomass has a great potential as feedstock in anaerobic digestion, contributing 
in the renewable energy production. Nevertheless, this type of biomass also has numerous 
challenges due to its complex structure. Coffee pulp is just an example of this type of biomass 
which presents a promising option due to its composition and its availability as it is produced in 
big amounts in areas like East or West Africa. In this study the effects of bioaugmentation as a 
strategy to enhance the anaerobic digestion of coffee pulp was investigated. Experimental 
results revealed a direct correlation between substrate concentration and methane production 
rates. Bioaugmentation not only improved overall methane yields but also minimized the lag 
phase typically observed at low concentrations. At a substrate concentration of 2 gVS/L, 
methane production reached theoretical levels (around 490 mL CH4/gVS) following the addition 
of 6% bacterial culture. At 10 gVS/L, methane production increased by up to 33% compared to 
control reactors reaching 340 mL CH4/gVS. Additionally, bacteria cultivated in enriched media 
performed more efficiently than those grown in standard media at lower bacteria additions, 
although both reached similar yields at higher doses. 
The study clearly indicates that bioaugmentation is an effective approach to enhance anaerobic 
digestion of lignocellulosic biomass such as coffee pulp.

1. Introduction 
 
Anaerobic digestion is a biological process which is commonly used to treat waste and produce 
biogas. The efficiency of the process is limited by two specific stages, hydrolysis where the 
complex organic matter is broken down and methanogenesis where methane is produced. One 
way to overcome this challenge is bioaugmentation.  
Bioaugmentation involves the introduction of specific stress-resistant or efficient 
microorganisms into the underlying microbial community with the aim of improving biomethane 
production. It has been successfully applied in the degradation of lignocellulosic biomass which 
presents challenges due to its complex structure as it contains high amounts of cellulose and 
hemicellulose (Basak, 2025). 
 The addition of cellulolytic bacteria can accelerate the breakdown of cellulose, increase the 
methane yields and improve the process stability. Different bioaugmentation strategies, 
including various inoculation rates of hydrolytic bacteria have been explored to enhance 
microbial retention and activity within anaerobic digesters. The effectiveness of these 
approaches depends on factors such as inoculum selection, environmental conditions, and the 
operation of the reactor.  
Coffee processing waste represents a significant part of byproducts which are generated during 
coffee production, with high amounts produced annually in the regions with high production 
such as East or West Africa (Chala et al., 2018). The processing method determines what types 
of waste are produced. These residues are rich in organic matter and nutrients but also contain 
compounds such as caffeine, tannins, and polyphenols. For this reasons, proper handling is 
required to avoid environmental issues, including water pollution, greenhouse gas emissions 
and unpleasant odours. As they have high organic content, coffee processing waste can be 
utilised for renewable energy production through anaerobic digestion (Tsigkou et al., 2025). 
In this specific study, the aim is to evaluate the potential of selected cellulolytic bacteria to 
enhance the anaerobic digestion of coffee pulp, a lignocellulosic biomass with limited 
biodegradability. Isolated bacteria from relevant natural environments are screened based on 
their ability to degrade cellulose, which is one of the main compounds of coffee pulp. The most 
effective bacteria species has been applied in biomethane potential tests at different inoculation 
rates to assess their impact on methane yield. Additionally, the bacteria are cultivated in both 
standard and enriched media, where coffee pulp has been used as carbon source, to investigate 
how growth conditions influence their efficiency. The effect of different initial substrate 
concentrations has been also examined to understand how loading rates interact with the 
microbial community. Through this approach, the study aims to identify optimal 
bioaugmentation strategies for improving the anaerobic digestion of coffee pulp and contributing 
to more efficient and sustainable waste management processes.

2.  Background 
 
2.1 Coffee processing waste 
2.1.1 Lignocellulosic biomass 
Lignocellulosic biomass is mainly composed of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin and  
represents the most abundant renewable source for sustainable bioenergy production. These 
structural polymers are organized in the plant cell wall, where cellulose, consists of around 35–
50% of dry biomass, creates crystalline fibers that strengthen the structure. Hemicellulose is 
comprising of 20–35% and it is a branched heteropolymer of various sugars such as xylose, 
mannose and arabinose, which contributes to the flexibility and cohesion of the cell (Strang et 
al., 2017). Lignin consists of 10–25% of biomass and it is a phenolic polymer that not only 
provides rigidity and hydrophobicity but also protects the polysaccharide components from 
enzymatic and microbial attack (Sawatdeenarunat et al., 2015). 
 
Figure 1 Composition of lignocellulosic biomass 
In addition to these main components, lignocellulosic biomass contains minor components 
such as proteins, extractives and inorganic minerals that may vary depending on the biomass 
type. The specific composition significantly influences biomass conversion efficiency and its 
suitability for different bioenergy applications. For example, high cellulose and hemicellulose 
content generally is valuable for biochemical and thermochemical conversion processes, while 
high lignin and ash content are harmful due to their greater resistance (Cai et al., 2017). 
2.1.2 Coffee pulp 
Coffee pulp is the main solid waste, which is generated during the wet processing of coffee 
beans. It consists mostly of the outer layers of the coffee cherry, the skin (exocarp) and the flesh 
(mesocarp) and occasionally contains a few stray beans. It is produced in large quantities, 
especially in regions where wet processing is common and for this reason, the management of 
coffee pulp is a very crucial environmental issue. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
The pulp has high moisture and is slightly acidic, with a composition rich in carbohydrates, 
proteins, lignin and some other bioactive compounds like chlorogenic, ferulic and caffeic acids. 
These compounds give to the pulp some interesting nutritional qualities but also make it difficult 
to use it directly. As it has high caffeine and phenolic content, it can be toxic to plants and 
animals, so it can not be used as compost or livestock feed in high proportions (Tsigkou et al., 
2025). 
Despite these challenges, there’s growing interest in finding better ways to valorize coffee pulp. 
In some cases, it has been dried and brewed into drinks like cascara and kisher. These beverages 
are very beneficial for human’s health due to its antioxidant properties. These products are 
considered healthier, sustainable alternatives to typical soft drinks or teas.  
Except of the  beverages, many other uses have been explored. Coffee pulp shows huge potential 
in energy production, mainly as a raw material for biofuels such as biogas because it has high 
content of compounds that can be utilised through the anaerobic digestion. Coffee pulp can 
produce up to 244.7 L CH4/kg VS (Chala et al., 2018). Due to its high moisture is not appropriate 
choice for pyrolysis and gasification. It can also be transformed into biochar, a type of charcoal 
used to improve soil quality, or into biodegradable packaging materials. Moreover, as it is fibrous 
and rich in nutrients is a suitable ingredient for growing mushrooms, combining a waste solution 
and a food source (Tsigkou et al., 2025). 
The utilization of coffee pulp to valuable products on a large scale comes with many obstacles. 
Its composition requires pretreatment in order to remove or minimize the compounds that make 
it toxic. These extra steps can create high processing costs and complicate their implementation. 
Nevertheless, the pulp’s abundance and potential make it an important option for sustainable 
development efforts. Finding ways to reuse it effectively supports circular economy goals and 
could bring both environmental and economic benefits for the countries that produce big 
amounts (Tsigkou et al., 2025).

2.2 Anaerobic digestion 
2.2.1 Steps of the process 
AD is a complex biological process in which organic matter is broken down by microbial 
communities in the absence of oxygen, resulting in the production of biogas which is a mixture 
primarily composed of CH₄ and CO₂. This process is very unique as it has dual role in waste 
treatment and renewable energy production. The ability to convert organic waste to energy aligns 
perfectly with the global attempts to reduce the use of fossil fuels and minimize the 
environmental pollution (Dhull et al., 2024) 
AD progresses through four principal stages: hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and 
methanogenesis. During hydrolysis, complex biopolymers such as proteins, carbohydrates and 
lipids are broken down into simpler, soluble forms including amino acids, sugars and fatty acids. 
This step is usually the slowest, particularly for lignocellulosic feedstocks as they show 
resistance to enzymatic degradation (Nzila, 2017). In the subsequent acidogenesis phase, these 
monomers are fermented by acidogenic bacteria into short-chain volatile fatty acids (VFAs), 
hydrogen gas and alcohols. These products then are used as substrates in acetogenesis, where 
VFAs are further converted into acetic acid, CO₂ and hydrogen by acetogenic microorganisms 
(Pham Van et al., 2020). 
The final and most critical stage is methanogenesis, where specialized archaea convert acetate 
or hydrogen and CO₂ into methane. There are two main pathways which are involved: acetolactic 
methanogenesis, which uses acetate and hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis, which relies on H2 
and CO₂. Methanogens are very sensitive to environmental changes, especially changes in pH, 
temperature and the presence of inhibitory compounds such as ammonia or sulphides (Nzila, 
2017). The complexity of the microbial interactions makes the maintenance of a balanced 
microbial consortium very crucial, as each group depends on the metabolic output of the 
previous stage for substrate availability (Pham Van et al., 2020).

14 
 
 
Figure 3 Steps of anaerobic digestion (Nzila, 2017) 
In summary, the anaerobic digestion process is a coordinated microbial effort that transforms 
organic waste into a usable form of energy. It serves as an innovative technology in integrated 
waste management and sustainable energy practices. 
 
2.2.2 Parameters Affecting Anaerobic Digestion 
The efficiency of anaerobic digestion systems is strongly influenced by various parameters. 
These include physicochemical factors such as pH, temperature, and substrate characteristics, 
as well as operational parameters like the carbon-to-nitrogen (C/N) ratio, organic loading rate 
(OLR) and retention times. The optimization of these parameters is essential for stable reactor 
performance and high biogas yields. 
pH and Alkalinity 
The stability of the digestion process is closely depended on pH value. Each microbial group 
involved in AD has its own preferred pH range. Hydrolytic and acidogenic bacteria are more 
tolerant of a broader pH range (typically 4 – 8.5), whereas methanogens are most active near 
neutral pH, with an optimum between 6.8 and 7.2 (Dhull et al., 2024). A decline in pH, often due 
to VFA accumulation, can limit methanogenic activity. On the other hand, elevated pH may result 
in the formation of free ammonia, which is toxic to methanogens.  
 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
Temperature 
Temperature significantly affects the microbial metabolism and system kinetics. Anaerobic 
digestion can occur under psychrophilic (10 - 20 °C), mesophilic (30 - 40 °C), or thermophilic (50 
– 60 °C) conditions. While thermophilic digestion often achieves higher reaction rates and better 
biodegradation, it is also more sensitive to changes in temperature and inhibitors. Despite the 
fact that mesophilic systems are slower, they have the advantage that demand less energy 
(Pham Van et al., 2020). 
Substrate Composition and C/N Ratio 
The type and composition of the substrate have a direct impact on AD performance. Parameters 
such as TS, VS, moisture content and particle size influence microbial accessibility and 
degradation rates. An optimal C/N ratio, typically between 20:1 and 30:1, is essential for 
maintaining microbial growth without threatening the nutrient balance. (Dhull et al., 2024). Low 
C/N ratios may lead to ammonia accumulation and toxicity, while high ratios can result in 
insufficient nitrogen for microbial synthesis, which can lead to limited methane production (Li et 
al., 2018). 
Hydraulic and Solid Retention Times 
Hydraulic retention time refers to the average time the substrate remains in the digester, while 
solid retention time reflects the duration microorganisms stay in the system. These parameters 
are crucial for maintaining microbial populations and ensuring sufficient time for biochemical 
conversion. Short retention times can cause microbial washout and incomplete digestion, while 
excessively long retention times can increase operational costs without proportional gains in 
methane yield (Pham Van et al., 2020). 
Organic Loading Rate 
The organic loading rate determines the amount of organic matter introduced into the system per 
unit volume each day. An OLR that is too high can stress the microbial community, leading to VFA 
accumulation and pH decline. On the other hand, low loading rates underutilize the reactor 
capacity and lower productivity. It is vital for the maintenance of the process balance and the 
maximum biogas output to find the optimal organic load rate (Nzila, 2017). 
2.3 Bioaugmentation in anaerobic digestion 
Bioaugmentation is a microbial enhancement strategy in AD that involves the introduction of 
selected microbial strains or consortia to improve the efficiency and stability of the process 
(Peng et al., 2014). This method is particularly useful when the native microbial community is 
insufficient to break down complex substrates or when specific steps in the AD process, such as 
hydrolysis or methanogenesis, are limiting. 
 In anaerobic digestion, bioaugmentation aims to accelerate the degradation of resistant organic 
materials, such as lignocellulosic biomass, by introducing microorganisms with specialized 
enzymatic capabilities or by enhancing the methanogenic activity of the system. The introduced 
microbes can help optimize the key steps of the process, including the hydrolysis of cellulose 
and hemicellulose or the conversion of VFAs to methane (Ozbayram et al., 2018). 
The effectiveness of bioaugmentation depends on factors such as the compatibility between 
introduced and indigenous microbial populations, the environmental conditions within the

digester, and the dosing frequency and quantity of the added culture (Jiang et al., 2020). While 
the introduced microorganisms may not persist long-term, their short-term effects can 
significantly increase the overall biogas production and the process stability (Nzila, 2017).

2.4 Cellulolytic microorganisms and their role in anaerobic digestion 
AD of lignocellulosic biomass is limited from the structural rigidity and the complex composition 
of this type of biomass and hydrolysis is recognized as the main step that limits the rate 
(Ozbayram et al., 2017). Cellulolytic microorganisms which are characterized by their ability to 
degrade cellulose and hemicellulose, are fundamental to overcome this bottleneck and enable 
effective conversion to methane. 
In conventional AD systems, particularly those handling municipal or industrial waste, the 
indigenous microbial community often display limited lignocellulolytic efficiency due to 
insufficient prior adaptation to complex substrates. In contrast, microbial communities derived 
from similar environments have evolved to efficiently deconstruct lignocellulose. These 
communities are enriched into functional groups capable of producing active enzymes that 
facilitate the enzymatic breakup of complex polysaccharides into simpler compounds (Basak et 
al., 2025). 
The cellulolytic microorganisms include both free-acting enzymes and complex multi-enzyme 
structures that can operate synergistically to disrupt the bonds between cellulose, 
hemicellulose, and lignin. Their activities release soluble sugars, which are further processed 
through acidogenesis and acetogenesis, ultimately serving as substrates for methanogenic 
archaea. These complicated microbial transformations show the importance of efficient 
cellulose hydrolysis for sufficient methane production (Basak et al., 2025). 
Bioaugmentation strategies that introduce specialized cellulolytic microbiota into digesters have 
shown significant enhancement of the overall degradation of lignocellulosic materials (Sinha et 
al., 2021). These additions can improve the kinetics of hydrolysis, increase the availability of 
substrates for subsequent metabolic stages, and support a more stable and resilient microbial 
environment inside the reactor. Furthermore, they often reduce the digestion period and improve 
methane yields, even under challenging conditions such as operation in low temperatures.

There are many techniques to explain the specific roles and interactions of cellulolytic 
microorganisms within the AD microbial community. For example, metagenomics analysis has 
enabled the identification of key enzymatic functions and community dynamics, while also offers 
pathways for monitoring of bioaugmentation strategies (Ozsefil et al., 2024). Tools like this are 
important to link microbial identity with the metabolic function, supporting the development of 
robust and predictable bioaugmentation approaches. 
In conclusion, cellulolytic microorganisms constitute a crucial component of the anaerobic 
digestion ecosystem when processing lignocellulosic biomass. Their enzymatic capabilities 
directly influence the efficiency of hydrolysis and the overall performance of the AD process 
(Zhang et al., 2022). Their role can be improved through targeted bioaugmentation, and this can 
significantly advance the conversion of renewable biomass into sustainable bioenergy (Basak et 
al., 2025).

2.5 Enriched cultures 
Cultivation of microbial cultures is a critical step in bioaugmentation strategies for enhancing 
AD performance. Different microbial species often require specific growth conditions in order 
to be grown efficiently. Prior to  introduction to anaerobic digestion systems, microbial 
consortia can be acclimated to targeted conditions to improve their tolerance and functional 
efficiency. Monitoring optical density at 600 nm provides a reliable indicator of microbial 
concentration during cultivation (Zhang et al., 2022). 
In some cases, cellulolytic communities are enriched through acclimatization processes 
involving prolonged exposure to lignocellulosic substrates. Acclimatized consortia exhibit 
increased abundance and activity of hydrolytic and fermentative functional groups, improving 
their integration into existing AD systems. However, the successful establishment and 
persistence of these communities depend on their ability to compete with native microbiota, 
adapt to dynamic process conditions, and resist washout (Basak et al., 2025).

3.  Materials and Methods 
3.1 Biomass preparation and characterization 
3.1.1 Origin 
The examined biomass is coffee pulp that came from Ethiopia (East Africa) as a waste stream 
after coffee processing with the wet method. The substrate was completely dried before its use 
in the experiments. Figure 4 depicts the tested biomass. 
 
 
Figure 4 Coffee pulp 
 
3.1.2 Milling 
The coffee pulp was mechanically milled in the lab using a milling machine (IKA-MF 10-basic 
Microfine grinder)  in order to increase its surface area. The selected mesh size of the milling 
equipment was 2 mm in order  to ensure an equal particle size distribution. This step was 
essential not only for improving substrate accessibility by the bacteria, but also for minimizing 
the presence of large particles and impurities that could lead to inconsistencies or variability in 
the experimental results. The homogenization of particle size was considered critical to maintain 
reliability across all experimental treatments.

hours. Thereafter, the crucibles were relocated to an oven maintained at 550 °C for a duration of

3.2 Bacteria screening and cellulolytic activity evaluation 
3.2.1 Origin 
The inoculum came from gut content, which was acclimatized in the lab, in one case with coffee 
pulp and in the other case with sisal waste, a similar lignocellulosic biomass. After the 
acclimatization, two bacteria (I13 and I15) with high cellulolytic activity were isolated to be used 
in the experiments. The origin of the inoculum was also from Eastern Africa. The isolation process 
was not part of this study.

3.2.2 Cultivation 
The isolated bacterial strains were initially cultured in small, autoclaved serum bottles with 
working volumes of 5,10 and 50 mL, containing 80% growth media and 20% inoculation. These 
cultures were incubated at 37  oC. The bottles were degassed with nitrogen before their use to 
ensure anaerobic conditions. The cultivation step was repeated few times, each time increasing 
the working volume, to allow the gradual expansion until a sufficient amount of bacterial culture 
was obtained for use in the experiments. 
The medium used for bacterial growth was prepared in the laboratory and consisted of 29 g/L 
thioglycolate broth with resazurin (CAS: 90404), 10 mL/L Double modified Wolin΄s mineral 
solution (Table 3) and 10 mL/L of carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) as the main carbon source. For 
the preparation of the enriched medium, CMC was replaced with coffee pulp to expose the 
bacteria to experimental substrate, in this case, 10 g VS/L of coffee pulp was added. 
The growth of the bacterial cultures was monitored daily by measuring the optical density 
(OD600), until the cultures reached the late exponential phase before being used. This strategy 
was followed to ensure that the microbial population was active and in optimal condition at the 
time of inoculation.

3.2.3 Cellulolytic activity setup 
The goal of this experiment was to determine the cellulolytic activity of the isolated bacteria 
measuring the carbohydrates degradation after hydrolysis. The substrate used was coffee pulp 
while cellulose used as model compound to assess if coffee pulp contains any inhibitory

For the carbohydrate’s determination, the method phenol-sulfuric acid was used, a widely 
adopted colorimetric technique for quantifying total carbohydrates. Through this method, the 
carbohydrates were broken down by >95% sulfuric acid to pentoses and hexoses. The addition 
of phenol produce a yellow-orange colour (Nielsen et al., 2010). The intensity of the colour is 
correlated with the amount of carbohydrates. The measurement took place through the 
determination of the absorbance at 490 nm. Concentrations were calculated based on a 
standard curve derived from glucose solutions.  
For the standard curve, glucose was dissolved in water to make a standard glucose solution 
(Mohamandia et al., 2021). In test tubes, different amounts of the glucose solution and distilled 
water were added, always in a total volume of 200 μL. After that, 200 μL phenol and 1 mL sulfuric 
acid also added in the tubes. The tubes were placed in a water bath at 30  oC for 30 minutes and 
then the absorbance was measured. The standard curve is depicted in the Figure 8:

The same procedure was applied to the experimental samples. All the samples, both total and 
soluble fractions, were diluted in order to be in the range of the standard curve. The 
concentration of them was calculated multiplying with the dilution factor.

The origin of the inoculum is a biogas plant in Lille Skensved, Denmark, which is regularly fed with

3.3.2 Setup and bioaugmentation strategies 
In this specific experiment, various inoculation rates of the selected isolate were examined,

determine if they have any influence on the methane production. Cellulose was also used as the 
positive control in the experiments to test the inoculum performance. All experiments were 
conducted in duplicate, and appropriate blanks were included in each set to ensure the accuracy

3.3.3 Biogas production, composition and kinetics 
To monitor the biogas production throughout the experiment, the gas accumulated in the 
headspace of the bottles was periodically removed using a syringe and needle, typically a few 
times per week. This degassing procedure was performed consistently over the entire duration 
of the experiment to allow accurate gas volume measurements. The methane content in the 
collected biogas was quantified using gas chromatography equipped with a flame ionization 
detector (Thermoscientific-TRACE 1600-GC-FID), following the initial calibration of the 
instrument to ensure reliable measurements. 
Since bottles were regularly degassed, methane production was calculated cumulatively by 
summing the CH4 volumes at each sampling day. Each measurement reflects the gas produced 
since the previous degassing, so the total methane produced equals the sum of all methane 
volumes collected over the experimental period. 
The measured methane volumes were normalized to standard temperature conditions (0oC,1 
atm) to ensure consistency and comparability of results. This correction accounts for the 
influence of ambient temperature on gas volume measurements, allowing all data to be reported 
under the same reference conditions.

4.2 Hydrolysis experiment 
The Figure 13 shows the effect of increasing substrate concentration (g VS/L) on the degradation 
percentage of cellulose and CP for the isolated bacteria number 15. A very big drop is observed 
at lower concentrations, as the concentration increases from 1 to 5 g VS/L, the degradation drops 
sharply from approximately 90% to around 50%. This steep decline suggests a possible inhibitory 
effect occurring even at relatively low loading. 
==PAGE:28==

Between 10 and 30 g VS/L, the degradation efficiency reaches almost a plateau, with values 
fluctuating around 40%. This indicates that over a certain concentration (10 g VS/L), the 
degradation ability of the system remains relatively constant and possibly restricted by limited 
microbial activity or accumulation of inhibitory compounds. 
Overall, these results suggest that optimal degradation of cellulose for I15 occurs at low 
concentrations and further increases in substrates loading reduce the degradation efficiency 
significantly. 
The degradation trend of coffee pulp with the addition of I15 exhibits a gradual decrease in 
degradation efficiency with the increase in initial concentration. At the lowest concentration (1 g 
VS/L), degradation reaches approximately 90% indicating high biodegradability. As the 
concentration increases to 30 g VS/L, degradation steadily decreases, reaching around 35% 
which shows possible inhibition for higher concentrations.  
Both substrates show high degradation (around 90%) for low concentrations. However, cellulose 
seems to be more sensitive to initial concentration increases, with a sharp decline. On the other 
hand, coffee pulp degradation trend is steadier, suggesting that it might be more resilient to 
increasing substrate concentrations initially. The explanation for this is that coffee pulp consists 
also of hemicellulose which is easier to degrade. There is no big difference at the highest 
concentration as with cellulose is around 40% while for coffee pulp is around 35%.

4.4 BMP Results 
Figure 15 illustrates the cumulative methane production from Cellulose in two different initial 
concentrations, at 2 gVS/L and 10 gVS/L. The methane yield is expressed as mL CH4 per gram of 
volatile solids. The results represent the average of the duplicates, and the error bars indicate 
the standard deviations. The experiment ran for 36 days. 
During the initial phase, both experiments indicated an extensive lag phase until day 8. The 
possible explanation for this is the microbial activity of the inoculum, that either was taken from 
the biogas plant with an improper sampling procedure or it was not very active and needed time 
for microbial adaptation. At 2 gVS/L, there was a gradual increase, and a plateau is reached 
around day 15, followed by a sharp increase at day 32, reaching 226 mL CH4/ gVS. On the other 
hand, at 10 gVS/L, the production remains under 100 mL CH4/ gVS until day 22, when a big 
increase occurred, peaking at around 400 mL CH4/ gVS, which is in line with other studies that 
reached 410.1 mL CH4/ gVS (Manthos et al., 2024). This discrepancy shows a delayed but more 
efficient biodegradation of cellulose at higher concentrations. However, as the line at 2 gVS/L 
has not reached the steady state yet, it is expected to peak in the next days. This is also indicated 
from the Monod equation that after the calculations the μ=0.136 day-1 for 2 gVS/L and μ=0.201 
day-1 for 10 gVS/L, confirming mathematically that higher concentration presents a higher 
methane production rate. 
 
Figure 15 Cellulose controls at two different concentrations

the anaerobic digestion of wheat straw, a similar cellulosic biomass, reactors amended with 6% 
enrichment culture of cellulolytic bacteria showed 36% methane improvement (Ozbayram et al., 
2018). In another study, the 4% addition of cellulolytic bacteria showed 27% enhancement in 
methane production for wheat straw  (Ozbayram et al., 2017). These results are very similar with 
the outcomes of the current study where the same addition of bacteria with cellulolytic activity 
increased the production by 33.7% and 25.4% respectively. This correlation with the literature 
indicates the reliability of the study.

5. Conclusion 
The findings of this study can considerably contribute to the deeper understanding of 
bioaugmentation in AD of lignocellulosic biomass, like coffee pulp, offering a sustainable 
solution for the valorization of this type of waste. 
The key points from the study are summarised below: 
• 
Coffee pulp contains high amounts of cellulose and hemicellulose that can be utilised in 
anaerobic digestion. From the physicochemical characterization, CP consists of 35% 
cellulose and 15% hemicellulose in average. 
• 
The tested bacteria I13 showed high cellulolytic activity in the hydrolysis-limited step of 
AD. The selected bacteria showed that they are capable of degrading around 90% of 
cellulose at low concentrations (1 gVS/L) and 35% at high concentrations (30 gVS/L). 
• 
The used bacteria are rod-shaped and are presented in short chains, after the 
microscopic analysis. In the case where I13 grew in enriched medium, the analysis 
showed that the bacteria were captured  around the coffee particles, benefiting from the 
organic material through its consumption. 
• 
The control experiments demonstrated that there is a clear relationship between the 
substrate concentration and the methane production rate. At 10 gVS/L, the experiments 
reached the expected yields compared with the literature (250 mL CH4/ gVS for coffee 
pulp and 400 mL CH4/ gVS for cellulose). This is an indicator that there is no inhibition 
factor at high initial concentrations. However, at lower concentration (2 gVS/L), the 
production was not the expected for both control substrates. This reveals a problem in 
the inoculum quality that increases the lag phase. The Monod equation also provides a 
mathematical explanation for the phenomenon.  
• 
Finally, it is clear that bioaugmentation with cellulolytic bacteria can lead to increased 
methane production in the case of coffee pulp. At 2 gVS/L, the production after the 
addition of 6% of either bacteria grown in a typical medium or in an enriched one, reached 
the theoretical level (around 490 mL CH4/ gVS), operating at the highest capacity. At 10 
gVS/L, the methane increase reached up to 33% from the control reaching around 340 
mL CH4/ gVS, indicating that bioaugmentation works regardless of the substrate 
concentration. 
• 
Except for  the methane production, bioaugmentation clearly improves the process 
through the minimization of the lag phase compared to the controls and results to higher 
values from the low concentrations. 
• 
The bacteria grew in the enriched media where coffee pulp as organic source, shows 
higher efficiency than the bacteria grown in a typical media at lower bioaugmentation 
levels (2%,4%). While for 6%, there is not significant difference as in both cases the 
theoretical yield had been reached.  
This study is a proof that bioaugmentation is  an efficient strategy for enhancing the anaerobic 
digestion of lignocellulosic biomass by adding isolated bacteria with high cellulolytic activity. 
The application and the challenges in full-scale conditions have to be tested thoroughly in 
order to optimize the optimal bacteria addition and investigate economic feasibility.
