Abstract 
 
Rye, Camilla (2019). Vulnerability in urban design: A geospatial perspective. 
Master thesis in Architectural Engineering, 30 ECTS credits.  
Supervisor: Tessa Kate Anderson. 
Keywords: GIS, Space Syntax, Perceived safety, Spatial, Geospatial, Urban Planning, 
Viewshed, Segment maps, Field of View 
The feeling of safety is a fundamental element in people’s quality of life and is a crucial part 
of wellbeing and humans physical and psychological health. The aspect of feeling safe has 
become increasingly more important in urban planning and the way we plan, design and 
govern our urban spaces plays a vital role in urban safety. Denmark has for many years had 
a great focus on safety in our urban spaces and making them safer for its residents. One of 
this focus areas have been to make Folehaven in Copenhagen a neighbourhood that feels 
safer.  
This thesis is built around the urban planning theories and strategies that focusing on 
quantifying variables that contribute to people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban 
environment. These theories arose as a criticism towards the modernistic way of building 
cities in the 20th century with spatial separation, large scale and repetitive architecture that 
led to an increasing percentage of people feeling unsafe.   
Folehaven was given as a project from Copenhagen municipality with the request of 
analysing Folehaven to find correlations between the urban environment and the high 
percentage of residents feeling unsafe. The thesis is built around both quantitative and 
qualitative methods that is focusing on variables that contribute to people feeling vulnerable, 
safe or unsafe in an urban environment, the most important variables are listed below:  
o 
Façade Categories 
o 
Visible facades  
o 
Field of view/Viewshed 
o 
Visibility between entrances 
o 
Unclear-land use 
o 
Mixed-use 
o 
Noise pollution 
o 
Poor lighting 
o 
Indivisibility 
o 
Scale  
For analysing and identifying the variables in Folehaven, QGIS, Space Syntax and urban 
planning tools were implemented and used for this purpose. The study identifies variables in 
the urban environment of Folehaven and makes suggestion on how to improve the urban 
environment to be perceived safer for its residents.

Introduction 
1 Introduction  
 
The feeling of safety is a fundamental element in people’s quality of life, wellbeing and is a 
crucial part of humans physical and psychological health (Santana, Santos, Costa, Roque, & 
Loureiro, 2009). The feeling of unsafety can change the pattern of behaviour in how people 
live, when they feel comfortable going out, how they travel and where they go (International 
centre for the crime prevention of Crime (ICPC) , 2016 ). Residents that is feeling unsafe can 
be afraid of using public places, going out during the night and using public transportation 
during rush hours of the day. The people that are more likely to feel unsafe in an urban 
environment are often the elderly, women and children as they are more vulnerable if being 
exposed to crime (Madsen & Nykjær, 2014).  
 
Social sustainability indicators for quality of life in the last 30 years have contained 
measures for crime and personal safety. The rational is that the measures of crime and 
personal safety can be directly linked to quality of life for residents in a neighbourhood 
(Okunola & Amole, 2011). In 2016, 193 world leaders agreed on 17 Global Goals for 
sustainable development, these goals are often referred to as the millennium goals (United 
Nations Development programs, 2019). Where the pledge for Goal 11 is to Make cities and 
human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable. SDG 11 includes several 
indicators, the sub-indicator 11.7 is relevant to the perception of safety and is formulated in 
this matter “11.7 By 2030, provide universal access to safe, inclusive and accessible, green 
and public space, in particular for women and children, older persons and persons with 
disabilities” (United Nations, 2018)

The aspect of perceived safety has become more important in urban planning the recent 
years, and the way cities are planned and created have a significantly impact on the levels of 
crime and people’s perception of safety. How cities are designed and how it is organized can

help to reduce crime and increase the urban safety. Urban planners have a unique 
opportunity to influence these conditions. Many have the perception that crime prevention is

criminals and victims with measures to improve the socioeconomic conditions. But one aspect 
that might be just as important as socioeconomic conditions is the quality of the urban 
environment. The way we plan, design and govern our urban spaces plays a vital role in 
urban safety (Madsen & Nykjær, 2014). 
2 Background 
 
Folehaven is a neighbourhood located in Copenhagen, with 2186 residents. The 
neighbourhood is on Copenhagen’s municipality list over the most vulnerable 
neighbourhoods in Denmark. Where the neighbourhood is affected by many socioeconomic 
challenges. With a high percentage of uneducated, low-income, unemployed and non-Danish 
residents. The neighbourhood is also affected by being physical isolated, high levels of noise 
pollution, few mixed-use areas and vulnerable youth. Safety index surveys conducted in 
Folehaven also shows that the percentage of residents feeling unsafe in Folehaven is 
significantly higher than in the rest of Copenhagen.  
Folehaven is a neighbourhood built in the mid-20th century and is influenced by the 
modernistic urban planning from that period of time. The residential buildings in Folehaven 
are not directly connected to the villa area in the rest of Folehavekvarteret but is built as its 
own neighbourhood surrounded by institutions and large open public areas and green 
courtyards. At the time the neighbourhood was built it was seen upon as modern and 
inexpensive homes, it was a new way of constructing residential areas with large green 
facilities between the buildings, which was a direct response to the urban structure and 
density of the inner city (København Kommune, 2018).  
The project was given by Copenhagen municipality with the request of analysing the urban 
environment to find correlation between the urban environment and the perception of safety 
in Folehaven which could explain that 34,5% of the residents are feeling unsafe.

Scope 
3 Scope  
 
The scope for the thesis is to identify variables from crime preventive theories and strategies 
that contribute to people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment. The 
methods conducted will be based on the variables with the objective of being able to map and 
analyse them in the urban environment in Folehaven.  
4 Objective  
 
Objective for the thesis is firstly, to identify variables in the urban environment that 
contribute people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment from the theory 
chapter. Secondly, map Folehaven in Copenhagen using GIS, Space Syntax and urban 
analysis tools with the objective of analysing and identifying variables in the urban 
environment that contribute to people feeling vulnerable and unsafe. Then finally, make 
suggestions for measures to improve the perception of safety in Folehaven.  
o 
Identify variables from the theory chapter that contribute to people feeling 
vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment  
o 
Map variables in Folehaven using GIS, Space Syntax and relevant urban planning 
tools  
o 
Analyse the findings with urban planners at the municipality of Copenhagen to find 
correlations between variables in the urban environment and the feeling of unsafety 
o 
Make suggestions for variables and measurements for improvement that should be in 
focus for making Folehaven feel safer  
 
Research question: 
What are the variables that contribute people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in the urban 
environment of Folehaven?

Theory 
5 Theory 
 
The objective for this theory chapter is to describe the perception of safety, how the urban 
environment affects human’s perception and identify variables that contribute to people 
feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment. The theory chapter will use 
urban planning theories and strategies for identifying variables in the urban environment 
that affects the perception of safety. The variables identified in the theory chapter will the 
basis of the development of methods used in the analysis of the perception of safety in 
Folehaven.  
 
5.1 The background for urban planning theories 
and strategies with the focus of making cities 
feel safer 
 
When looking back at the history of cities there is a clear link between how urban structures 
and planning influence human behaviour and the way cities are operating. The well-
connected and compact structure of medieval cities with short walking distances, squares 
and market places which supported their function of centres, squares and marked place has 
drastically changed under the modernistic planning ideology that arose in the 20th century. 
Where the connection between invitations and behaviour were replaced with space for the 
rising tide of car traffic (Rogers & Gehl, 2010). Architect Tim Stonor from the company Space 
Syntax is talking about how the connectivity was lost in the 20th century, due to modernism 
and moving away from what we have learned about the well-connected city throughout the 
history of cities. The density grid is destroyed, the walkable, cyclable and short distance grid 
has been replaced by highways (Stonor, Connected Cities: Tim Stonor (Space syntax) , 2015). 
The city that before was planned for human interaction and connectivity were now 
transformed to a city scape filled with moving and parked vehicles (Rogers & Gehl, 2010). 
Modernism is a dominant planning ideology which were taking its departure in Le 
Corbusier’s CIAM utopia of a new and modern city, that influenced cities all over the world 
after 1945. CIAM; Le Congrès International d’Architecture moderne, was an international 
modern urban planning and architectural movement. The utopia became a real-life dystopia 
with increased problems of crime and unsafety because of spatial separation, large scale and

5.2 Perception of safety 
 
Being unsafe and feeling unsafe is two concepts that needs to be differentiated and specified. 
Being unsafe is correlated to the crime rate in an area where the main element is the 
chances of being a victim of criminalization, which makes the area unsafe for its residents. 
This concept is objective, whilst feeling unsafe is a subjective concept and is very hard to 
quantify. (Ruisjobroek, Droomers, & Groenewegen, 2015) Two types of feeling unsafe can be 
identified, one that relates to the psychological state of anxiety and one that is experienced 
based unsafety that arises from a specific incident of fear. It is assumed that the two types of 
unsafety can be treated differently, where the first type can be prevented by physical efforts, 
while the other type can be prevented with improving the socioeconomic conditions in 
residential areas (Det kriminal Præventive Råd, 2013). 
Feeling of unsafety in an area is also an individual perception that differ from person to 
person and consist of many factors and emotions. It varies with personal characteristics such 
as age, gender, work occupation and the time of day when a person is moving through an 
area. For instance, in general men feel safer than women. (Ruisjobroek, Droomers, & 
Groenewegen, 2015) 
The feeling of unsafety can have demographic, social and environmental explanations. Where 
the demographic explanations are describing that some groups of people are more unsafe 
then others. The social explanations to unsafety are that the social environment can cause 
unsafety due to changes and the collapses of social institutions. The feeling of unsafety can 
be associated with the fear of strangers both culturally and ethnically, where especially the 
lack of integration in an area with poor socioeconomic conditions can trigger feeling of 
unsafety. The environmental explanations to unsafety focus on how the design of the 
environment affects the perception of safety in an area. Where signs of disorder are perceived 
as an absence of social control. The signs may include, graffiti, waste, vandalism and noise 
pollution. Poorly designed buildings and urban space with poor visibility and lack of lighting 
can result in fewer people using the spaces because they are feeling unsafe. Unsafe variables 
in a built environment can be such as, reduced lighting, deprived permeability, vacant 
streets, hiding places for criminals and planting that deprives visibility (Det kriminal 
Præventive Råd, 2013). This thesis will focus mostly on the social and environmental causes 
of people feeling unsafe.

In the recent years there have been an increasing interest on how urban space and design 
affects residents’ quality of life. The human mind is split between genetically-structured 
neural systems, and an internally-generated worldview. (Salingaros & Madsen, 2006). Where 
the two parts elicit contradictory mental processes for our actions and how our interpretation 
of the built environment is. One part is looking for information, while the other part is 
looking for meaning. This contradiction in mental processes gives the base for philosophical, 
cultural and ideological construct. The challenges seen with human’s perception of the built 
environment is that architects have only focused on the second domain and neglecting the 
first. Where they have given artificial meaning on built environment with architecture that 
contradict physical and natural processes. Making a cityscape that is inhumane in their

Human beings connect more to a built environment embodying organized complexity than 
built environments that is too plane with monotonous facades, parking lots and closed

people because of the diminished opportunities of walkability and the conditions of the public

the opportunity to sense activity and other people. The consequences of this is that people 
rather stay indoors. Jan Gehl says the human dimension is overlooked, neglected and phased 
out (Gehl, Livet mellem husene, 2005).

5.3.3 How noise pollution in an urban environment can 
affect humans 
 
High noise levels in a built environment can have several health effects, where higher levels 
of exposure can lead to serious consequences. Noise pollution is the second biggest pollution 
in EU. (Jeong, 2018) There have been conducted a significantly amount of studies on how 
noise pollution effects humans and there is established many long-term health effects. Some 
of the effects where there is sufficient evidence for a correlation between noise pollution and 
health effects is sleep disturbance, cardiovascular disease, hypertension and hearing 
impairment. Hearing impairment occurs for LAeq, 8h > 75 dB(A) and LAeq, 24h > 70 dB (A). 
When Lden is exceeding 55 dB (A) there is a public health danger. The noise limit in 
Denmark for residential areas is established to be Lden = 64 dB for railway noise and Lden = 
58 dB for road noise (Jeong, 2018). The psychological consequences of being annoyed with 
noise is stress, fear, depression and sorrow. The behavioural consequences of being annoyed 
with noise is social isolation, aggression, alcohol and drug use etc. The figure below shows 
the health effects of noise pollution (Santurette, 2018).

5.4.2 Creating defensible space – Oscar Newman 
 
Architect Oscar Newman published the book “Creating Defensible Space” in 1996. The book 
is a further development and evolution of the book “Defensible space” Published in 1972 by 
Newman. The concern for the increased crime rates and deteriorating neighbourhoods 
revived the interest for the physical design approach to crime prevention developed in 1972 
(Newman, Creating defensible space, 1996). “Creating Defensible space” concludes on three-
case studies and several earlier works and provides an expert review on how to redesign 
neighbourhoods to be perceived safer with a set of design principles. Some of the design 
principles is listed below: (Newman, Creating defensible space, 1996). 
1. The front yards, the front of buildings and the main entrances should face the streets.  
2. Collective yard consisting of a grouping of individual rear yards should be fenced off 
to create a private zone that is only accessible from the interior unit and not from the 
street.  
3. Fencing should be minimized through the judicious placement of buildings and rear 
yards. 
4. To facilitate pedestrian circulation throughout the neighbourhood the pedestrian 
paths should be safe and convenient with direct access to each unit and should easily 
surveyed from the interior of the units.  
5. Lightning should be provided in the neighbourhood, with concentrations at 
pedestrian paths, ramps, parking lots and entrances to units.  
6. Planting should not screen the visibility to doors, windows and unit entries.  
 
5.4.3 Broken Window Theory – James Q.Wilson &

Perception of safety can also be seen in correlation with fear of crime. Where fear of crime is

5.4.4 Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design 
– 1 & 2. Generation C. Ray Jeffrey (1971) 
 
CPTED starts out from the point of view that the feeling of unsafety in a public area is not 
just the presence of criminals, but the feeling of unsafety can come from unprotected targets 
and space with lack of social control (Grönlund, 2013). CPTED is the design or redesign of a 
built environment to reduce the opportunity to commit crime and reduce the fear of crime 
(Crowe & Sorensen, 2012, s. 102). To reduce crime, violence, fear of crime and feeling of 
unsafety 1. Generation of CPTED is working with three overlapping strategies of territorial 
reinforcement, natural surveillance and natural access control.  
 
Figure 5.10 CPTED 1st generation and 2nd generation. (Love & Cozens, 2017) 
 
Territorial reinforcement is about the relationship and the connection people have to a built 
area that makes them try to keep others away or dictate the behaviour of others in their 
area. Feeling of territoriality encourages individuals to take control over an area and defend 
it against misuse, abuse or potential offending. In other words, territorial reinforcement 
improves the social control. Feeling of territoriality can be promoted by architecture and 
design that clearly identifies the individual, group or land-use activity that have ownership 
of the specific area. When the residents feel an ownership over an area they can be able to 
perform their social control role. The area needs to be defined in specific zones, private, semi-
private, semi-public and public, to make people take ownership and pride of the area. (Det 
kriminal Præventive Råd, 2013) (Love & Cozens, 2017)

Natural Surveillance can improve the social control in an area. The strategies are focusing 
on organizing physical features, activities and people to maximizing visibility in an area. One 
example is to place the windows towards the outdoor areas. It makes it easier to detect 
potential offenders and increases the feeling of safety for residents. People feel safer when 
they are visible to others and have the ability to observe others.  
CPTED emphasizes natural surveillance as the preferred approach, but mechanical and 
organized surveillance can be supplementing approaches. Mechanical surveillance can be by 
installing cameras and alarms. Lighting is also a form of mechanical surveillance, as it gives 
the opportunity for surveillance during evenings and nights. But too much light promote 
crime, by giving the criminals full vision of the street and where to commit crime. Organized 
surveillance can be conducted by the police or security guards patrolling the street. (Det 
kriminal Præventive Råd, 2013) (Love & Cozens, 2017) 
Natural Access control is reducing the opportunities for the criminal to perform criminal 
activity by making it harder to access an area a then making them aware of the risk of being 
caught in that area. It is reducing the opportunities criminals have to commit crime in an 
area by denying or restricting access to targets by design features such as entrances and 
exits, fencing, gates and lighting. Access control can also direct the movement through an 
area, which will improve the perception of safety. Mechanical and organized access control 
can also be supplemented to the natural access control. Where organized access control is 
focusing on the use of guards, whilst mechanical access control can be locks, boom gates and 
card entry, CCTV and lighting.  
In the later years concepts as maintenance, activity support and target hardening have 
emerged to function as additional supplements to CPTED.  
Maintenance gives an impression of the ownership and proprietary concerns the residents 
have to their area. If an area is perceived as poorly maintained with signs of deterioration 
offenders might see it as an opportunity to commit crime as they perceive the area to be 
poorly controlled. An area that are poorly maintained can send out the message that no one 
cares about the area, and no one will intervein if crime is committed. “The Broken Window 
theory” mentioned earlier in this chapter supports this understanding.  
Activity support encourage activities in an area by using design, signage and set up 
scheduled activities. Activity support encourages people to have a positive behaviour and 
activities by making the streets a more attractive place to stay and walk through. This can 
be done by plantings, as long it does not hinder the visibility in an area. Jane Jacobs’s “Eyes 
on the street” theory is previously mentioned in this chapter and is also talking about

Theory 
making the street attractive for people. A more attractive street with activities, there will be 
more eyes on the street that can potentially discourage some types of crime.  
Target hardening is limiting or denying access by using physical barriers such as fences,

increasing the risk of being seen and caught (Det kriminal Præventive Råd, 2013) (Love &

5.7 Space syntax in urban planning  
 
Space Syntax is a method for describing and analysing the relationships between spaces of 
urban places and buildings. The spatial layouts of buildings and urban places exerts a 
powerful influence on human behaviour. The way that places connect is directly related to 
the way that people move, interact and transact. The connectivity influences human 
behaviour. This means behaviour in terms of movement in how we flow through the city. 
Some of them that understand space best are the criminals. They make it their business to 
exploit the mistakes that are made when we design space and leave it available to crime 
(Stonor, Spatial Layout Effeciency With Time Stonor of Space Syntax , 2011). 
Space Syntax is a useful analytical and planning tool that can be used to get a better 
understanding of the spatial complexity of cities and its consequences. Space Syntax have 
the understanding that a city is developed from the notion of the “City as a spatial object” 
Built by, axial lines, isovists and convex spaces (Grönlund, 2013). Where a convex space is a 
space for which two points on its edge always can be connected with a straight line. People 
that are moving between convex spaces are choosing the shortest and most natural choice of 
straight lines. Space Syntax is a tool that uses this reasoning of pedestrian movement to 
analyse and model the pedestrian movement by connecting the convex spaces with straight 
lines and edges. This analyse is often represented in an axial map and is one of the primary 
tools of space syntax. The figure below shows an integration axial map over Copenhagen. 
Where the red line is highly integrated spatially, while blue lines are integrated segregated 
spatially (Grönlund, 2013).

By using Space Syntax tools such as axial map and segment map we can understand how 
people are moving through the city and detect islands in the city that have low permeability. 
These areas can be analysed in the context to perception of safety. Where people can feel 
unsafe if they are moving through an area with few people and therefore few eyes on the 
street. 
In the past the most common map Space Syntax have used is axial maps, as previously 
mentioned as one of the primary tools of space syntax. In the later years a new method called 
segment map have been introduced by Space Syntax. This is a new syntactic representation 
of cities that applies to both topological and geometric configuration of space, where it is 
considering the streets topological, metric and angular connections. Segment map have the 
capability to produce different types of segment analysis by using different radii. The new 
method is powerful for predicting social and economic activity. Probably the most powerful 
tool in a segment map to measure accessibility in street networks is angular depth with 
metric radius. This tool can calculate integration and choice to measure accessibility and 
compare configurational properties of space with observed urban activity. Choice and 
integration have the potential to identify the to-movement and through-movement in an 
urban area (K, B, A, & A, 2013). 
There are several powerful tools that a segment map can perform, however this thesis will 
focus on angular segment analysis with metric radius. Where the streets connectivity,

integration and choice or betweenness will be calculated. Angular segment analysis is 
instrumental in detecting major to and through routes in a street network.  
Connectivity is the cumulative turn angle to other lines. Integration analysis is a good 
predictor of the potential for each segment within a metric radius to be a highly desired 
destination. Integration forecast the to-movement potential for each segment where it 
measures the shortest angular paths in the system from all origins to all destinations. Choice 
is calculated for different radii. It shows the potential for each segment element to be 
selected by pedestrian as the shortest path. Choice is the through movement potential for a 
segment in a spatial system (K, B, A, & A, 2013). 
The object of the measurements is listed below: 
1. Connectivity (degree) measures the number of immediate neighbours that are directly 
connected to a space.  
2. Integration is a measure that describes the relativized asymmetry in the graph 
network. The global measure shows how deep or shallow a space is in relation to all 
other spaces. Using integration, spaces are ranked from the most integrated to the 
most segregated. Integration is usually indicative to how many people are likely to be 
in a space and is thought to correspond to rates of social encounter and retail 
activities  
3. Choice or in betweenness measures movement flow through spaces. Spaces that record 
high global choice are located on the shortest paths from all origins to all 
destinations. Choice is a powerful measure at forecasting pedestrian and vehicular 
movement potentials. It is usually applied to segment analysis rather than convex 
analysis, because it is descriptive of movement rather than occupation (K, B, A, & A, 
2013). 
Metric is the shortest path and metric radius is the metric distance from each segment along 
all available streets and roads from that segment up to the radius distance. Radius “n” 
calculate the distance to all segments without any restriction. If we want to analyse on a 
more local level approximately a 5-minutes’ walk a radius of 400 meters can be applied. In 
this case the map will only calculate angular turns of all nodes within 400 meters from the 
current node. This will result in the system only will identify the local relationship between 
segment elements. This radius is normally best to present the local movement through an 
area (K, B, A, & A, 2013).

5.8 GIS in urban planning  
 
Geographic Information System (GIS) is a framework for gathering, managing and analysing 
data, it is a perfect tool to understand the spatial features of a location. It analyses spatial 
location and organizes layers of datasets into maps. The software is much more than just a 
map, it can be intelligent and interactive.  
GIS is layercentric, which means that map projects are built with a layer hierarchy. Where 
each of the layer represent a specific dataset. There are many different types of data that can 
be integrated into the software and be represented as map layers. That can be data such as, 
the street network, buildings, zoning, demographics and how people are using the streets. 
This allows us to get an insight to the characteristics of an area and conduct an analyse data 
to find insights such as, patterns, relationships, and situation and correlations between the 
different layers in the map. This helps urban planners to make smarter decisions (VESTA - 
A rizing company, 2017). Figure 5.12 below shows one type of map hierarchy. 
 
Figure 5.12 GIS data layers (VESTA - A rizing company, 2017) 
 
It is GIS’s ability to map so many different layers into a common geography that gives it its 
distinctive analytic ability. By organizing so many different features in the same geography 
it gives the ability to see the relationships between otherwise separate datasets (NAPSG 
Foundation, 2014).  
GIS and Space Syntax will be used as both qualitative and quantitative methods for mapping 
and analysing Folehaven. Where the software QGIS 2.18.16 and DepthmapX 0.50 will be

The objective for the method chapter is to describe the methods used in the analysis of 
Folehaven. The method performed in this thesis will focus on variables that contribute 
people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment from the theory chapter 5. 
The first method will be a quantitative method reflecting the common variables from chapter 
5.6 and will be used as different layers and analysed in GIS. The first method will be 
supplemented with two other methods. The second method is a qualitative method made by 
Jan Gehl, where the neighbourhood will be analysed in GIS by urban criteria for a good 
public place. The third method is a quantitative method that is a counting of the people using 
the neighbourhood, the method is based on the theory of Gehl Institute.  The aim of the 
method is to identify the quality of the urban environment.  
The combination of the methods used is to provide a quantitative and a qualitative analysis 
of the area. The qualitative data provided can help to analyse and get an understanding and 
correlation of the quantitative data to help finding out why Folehaven is perceived as unsafe 
by its residents.

7.1.4 Base-map Layer 4 
 Dataset: Viewshed/field of view 
 
The viewshed is calculated by making a 5x5 meter grid with polygon observer points over the 
DHM raster map. The viewshed is calculating the area seen from each observer point. The 
DHM model have a grid of 0,4 meter that would be more preferably to use, but due to the size 
of the neighbourhood a 5x5 meter grid is more beneficial. The radius used is 100 meters, 
which is the maximum distance in our social vision. The height for the observer is 1,5 meters 
at to present the “worst case” sight for children. To make the Viewshed map the visibility 
tool in the Spatial Analyst Toolbox in QGIS 2.18.16 is used.  
The viewshed is made from a 5x5 meter grid where every non-obstacle cell is used as 
observer point and counting the number of observers points visible from each cell. The 
obstacles identified as non-transparent is listed below: 
o 
Housing 
o 
Planting 
o 
Fences 
o 
Playground objects 
o 
Highway bridge crossing 
o 
Walls 
The viewshed is used to identify which places in Folehaven have poor visibility and 
permeability. These places will often be perceived as more unsafe, especially in the night 
when it can be potential hiding places for criminals. Poor permeability can also contribute to 
less social control in an area, when areas it is not visible from the streets.

Visible entrances identify how many entrances that is visible for the neighbours. With a 
small number of entrances visible the social control is decreased.

7.3 Jan Gehl Twelve Quality Criteria method  
 
Jan Gehl’s twelve quality criteria is a tool for researching how well a urban space is 
experienced by the people using it. It is used to evaluate how the different features of a 
public space are protective, comfortable and enjoyable. The tool includes 12 design criteria’s, 
where this thesis will focus on 7 of the criteria that influence the perception of safety and 
some of the qualities that influence the quality of an urban space (Gehl Institution, u.d.): 
 
Protection against traffic and accidents. 
 
1. Do groups across age and ability experience traffic safety in the public space?  
 
Protection against harm by others. 
1. Is the public space perceived to be safe both day and night? 
2. Are there people and activities at all hours of the day because the area has, for example, 
both residents and offices? 
3. Does the lighting provide safety at night as well as a good atmosphere? 
 
Protection against unpleasant sensory experience. 
1. Are there noises, dust, smells, or other pollution? 
 
Options for mobility. 
 
1. Is this space accessible?  
 
Options to stand and linger.   
 
1. Does the place have features you can stay and lean on, like a façade that invites one to 
spend time next to it, a bus stop, a bench, a tree, or a small ledge or niche? 
Options for sitting 
 
Are there good primary seating options such as benches or chairs? Or is there only secondary 
seating such as a stair, seat wall, or the edge of a fountain? Are there adequate non-
commercial seating options so that sitting does not require spending money? 
Scale. 
1. Is the public space and the building that surrounds it at a human scale? If people are at 
the edges of the space, can we still relate to them as people or are they lost in their 
surroundings 
The criteria help planner to get an insight to the quality of a public space and which features 
is missing or contributing to unsafety.

Folehaven is challenging to improve as they are a part of the fixed residential structure. To 
improve this variable two measurement will be suggested: 
o 
Firstly, making the entrances more visible by improving the visibility in Folehaven 
with reducing the planting, walls, fences and gates that deprives the opportunity to 
see other entrances. 
o 
Secondly, improve the lighting conditions by the entrances. This will make them 
easier to observe during the dark hours.

9.9 Limitations and reflections  
 
o 
In the thesis only, the socioeconomic conditions in Folehaven from the regeneration 
reports made by the municipality is described and not the efforts made in the past or 
in the future. As the objective for the thesis was to analyse the current urban 
environment and identify variables from the theory chapter in Folehaven, the 
measurements in the regeneration reports are therefore not taken into consideration 
in the discussion chapter. The measurements suggested are based upon the findings 
in the analysis and the theory chapter.  
 
o 
The thesis has used variables from urban planning theories on crime prevention and 
qualities for good urban spaces as a base for analysing Folehaven. When Folehaven 
was built it was seen upon as modern and with inexpensive homes, it was a new way 
of constructing residential areas with large green facilities between the buildings. 
The theory based on Gehl, Jacobs and Newman are a criticism to the modern way of 
building, especially with US “Projects” in mind. Folehaven has a smaller scale 
compared to the projects and is not seen upon as a “project” or a “Ghetto”, the thesis 
is not comparing Folehaven to the “projects” but has the objective to identify 
variables from the crime preventive theories and from there analysing Folehaven to 
find if some of these variables that contribute people feeling vulnerable, safe or 
unsafe in an urban environment is present in the urban environment in Folehaven. 
 
o 
The thesis has not used crime statistics for Folehaven due to the difficulty of 
receiving such data. This would have been a long process and the short timeline of 
the thesis made it not possible. The data on crime in Folehaven could be something to 
use in the future to locate the specific areas where crime is an issue and correlate it to

10 Conclusion 
 
The feeling of safety is a fundamental element in people’s quality of life and is a crucial part 
of wellbeing and humans physical and psychological health. The aspect of feeling safe has 
become increasingly more important in urban planning and the way we plan, design and 
govern our urban spaces plays a vital role in urban safety. Denmark has for many years had 
a great focus on safety in our urban spaces and making them safer for its residents. One of 
this focus areas have been to make Folehaven a neighbourhood that feels safer. This thesis 
took on the challenge from the municipality to analyse Folehaven with the main objective to 
identify variables that contribute people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban 
environment. GIS, Space Syntax and urban planning tools were implemented and conducted 
for this purpose.   
The analysis conducted were able to identify variables in the urban environment that 
contribute people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment. Where 
quantitative and qualitative methods were used to substantiate the findings with correlation 
between the variables. The discussion chapter 9 is highlighting the variables in the urban 
environment that should be in focus for future work of making Folehaven safer and describes 
how they are affecting the perception of safety in Folehaven. The discussion chapter is 
suggestion measurements for improving the perception of safety in Folehaven. Below is the 
list of the variables the municipality should take into consideration in their future work in 
making Folehaven feel safer: 
o 
Improving socioeconomic condition  
o 
Improving the conditions in the segregated streets 
o 
Improving the visibility, indivisibility between buildings and lighting  
o 
Making the residential facades more active and friendly  
o 
Making Folehaven more mix-used and less function divided  
o 
Improving the large green areas and having more activity support in the unclear 
land-use 
o 
Decreasing the high levels of noise pollution in the outskirts of the neighbourhood. 
The ambition of analysing Folehaven with the objective of identifying variables that 
contribute people feeling vulnerable, safe or unsafe in an urban environment have been 
conducted with good results. The identified variables that contribute to the feeling of 
unsafety in the urban environment that are mentioned above should be taken into 
consideration in the future work of making Folehaven feel safer for its residents.
