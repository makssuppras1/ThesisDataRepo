Summary
Biological processes such as fermentation are utilized in various ﬁelds, helping
to discover vital compounds. For most of the experiments, ﬁnding the right
compound is like trying to ﬁnd a needle in a haystack. There is a lot of diﬀerent
parameters that inﬂuence the way fermented cells behave, how they grow, and
what they produce. To have a good understanding of what is happening, it
is important to monitor and control the environment. This project focuses on
ﬁnding an algorithm that can control the pH of a substance by dispensing a
speciﬁc volume of acid or base to the ﬂask. The algorithm is meant to be used
in a wireless shake ﬂask reactor (embedded platform), developed by atSpiro
ApS.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Biotechnology has a huge impact on our daily life. Development of new tech-
nologies and production of valuable compounds in this ﬁeld could help to heal,
fuel and feed the world. Biolabs in large companies, academia, as well as small
private laboratories are contributing to the research by discovering new com-
pounds using processes such as fermentation. During fermentation, living or-
ganisms undergo various changes which are inﬂuenced by the conditions in the
surrounding environment (fermentation media). One of the important factors
during fermentation is pH of a substance, which usually ﬂuctuates due to the
changes induced by the organisms. If this variation is not handled carefully,
essential strains might die due to the unacceptable conditions, resulting in a
failing experiments. In industry, compounds for production are fermented in
large (>1000L) stirred bioreactors which provide the required control for op-
timal fermentation.
Prior to that, a sequence of experiments are conducted
in a several-stage pipeline because in the beginning it is hard to tell at which
conditions the experiment yields the best result.
As depicted in Figure 1.1,
the process begins with many (1000s) of experiments performed simultaneously
in microwell plates. This allows to select the good performing strains (100s).
However, due to the small volume, screening throughout the experiment is not
possible and usually only end-point measurement is conducted. To get better
understanding of the process, interesting strains are moved to the next, shake
ﬂask stage, where the experiments are conducted again, but in a larger scale.
At this stage, many strains behave diﬀerently at larger volume. Again, the good
==PAGE:12==

performing strains (10s) are selected and carried on to even bigger, laboratory
bioreactors, which help to select the ﬁnal candidates.
Since the lab-bioreactors provide control, comparable to the large bioreactors,
they allow a smooth transition between them, due to the similar conditions
for the organisms. The shake ﬂask step is conducted because of its low cost,
compared to the lab bioreactors which are very expensive per experiment and
diﬃcult to setup, making it not suitable for a lot of experiments in parallel.
However, the shake ﬂasks are passive, they provide no control, and therefore the
correlation between good performing strains in shake ﬂask and in lab bioreactors,
is usually poor. The solution to this problem is introducing the control to the
fermentation process in the earlier stage (shake ﬂask).
Figure 1.1: Usual fermentation sequence
Company atSpiro ApS1 develops an automated control system device for fed-
batch cell incubation that allows to wirelessly control and monitor the fermenta-
tion process. This minimizes the contamination risk, maximizes the throughput,
allowing multiple fermentation experiments in parallel, and makes it easier for
bio-laboratory technicians to set up an experiment. Moreover, the device screws
on the top of the standard shake ﬂask, integrating into the already existing in-
frastructure. Using sensors and high-precision pumps, the aim is to monitor
and adjust pH in real-time, and provide a controlled feed of nutrition during the
fermentation. In order to achieve the goal of the device, a stable and reliable
pH control algorithm needs to be implemented.

The heart of the device is an ATmega1284P, 8-bit microcontroller unit (MCU),
that interfaces external peripherals such as a pH probe and pumps. The pH
probe is used to measure the current pH value of a substance in the ﬂask, while
the pumps can dispense precise amounts of liquid into the ﬂask to regulate the
pH value. The fermentation experiment is setup wirelessly over Bluetooth and
afterwards it runs autonomously and keeps the pH within the speciﬁed range.
The device is an embedded system that interfaces the biological part of the
experiment. The block diagram can be seen in Figure 1.3. The MCU samples
the pH using a probe, and controls the three pumps, dispensing acid, base,
or feed into the substance. If the pH is bellow the setpoint, base needs to be
dispensed to increase it. Similarly, acid is used to decrease the pH if it is above
the setpoint. However, the exact volumes of dispensed acid or base need to be
determined to prevent the system from oscillating, or unnecessary overshooting.
Designing, implementing, and testing the pH algorithm in a try-and-fail manner
directly on the real device would not be feasible due to the time and cost it would
take. Therefore, a model-based approach is used to propose and evaluate the
suitable pH algorithm. After testing, the algorithm is then implemented on the
device and run in a real-life scenario experiment.

Chapter 2
Model-Based System
Engineering
Model-Based System Engineering is a practice of creating system models that
help deﬁne and design a systems under development. By validating and testing
system characteristics early, fast feedback on requirements and design decisions
can be made. Even though models are usually never an ideal and perfect repre-
sentation of a real-life system, they provide a valuable knowledge and feedback
sooner and more cost-eﬀectively, unlike direct implementation without mod-
elling. In the following chapter, a model representing a batch experiment, al-
gorithms controlling acid/base dispensing, and a framework encompassing all
of the elements, is developed. The principle of the modelled system relies in a
theoretical control loop, presented in Figure 2.1. Plant (the fermented medium)
responds to the changes induces by the controller (the bioreactor device), and
the actual state is being sampled (pH sensor) and fed back to the controller, so
the information can be used within the algorithm.
Figure 2.1: Model - control system loop
==PAGE:16==
6
Model-Based System Engineering
In chemistry, pH represents a negative of the logarithm to base 10 of hydro-
gen ion [H+] concentration [Bur02]. This (typically 0-14) scale is widely used
to specify how acidic or basic a water-based solution is (at 25°C: neutral=7,
acidic<7, basic>7).
The pH controlling process resides in reducing the acidity or alkalinity by mix-
ing acids and bases, producing the required pH. As discussed in [RKM16] and
[AJAS15], the setup typically consists of a CSTR (Continuous Stirred Tank
Reactor) with a solution that is constantly being mixed, and two containers
containing acid and base. The pH in the CSTR is monitored using a probe,
while acid and base provides a controlled inﬂuent stream into the CSTR, chang-
ing the hydrogen ion concentration.
Even though, when adding acid or base, a pH deviation is expected, it is diﬃcult
to predict the exact pH change. Therefore, the algorithm needs to be able to
determine the volume of acid/base that should be dispensed to correct the pH
to the required level. The pH response is a non-linear process, so the way the
pH changes when acid or base is introduced, depends on the actual substance
and its titration curve. There has been many attempts to compensate for this
using a non-linear approach. For example, Jacobs et al., 1980 [JHW80], made a
ﬁrst order dynamics model with titration curve as the non-linearity. Other re-
search papers like [BS17] compares diﬀerent tuning methods for standard linear
PID controller. According to Waller and Gustafsson [GW92], the problem of
non-linearity in pH control is generally recognized, and often overemphasized.
If the non-linearity is not known, the superiority of non-linear controller is ques-
tionable. Practically, a well-tuned linear control often performs quite as well as
a nonlinear controller (Gustafsson, 1985). Moreover, the biological experiments
are usually conducted with pH buﬀer, which is an aqueous solution, containing a
mixture of a weak acid HA and its conjugate base A−, or vice versa. Their equi-
librium provides a stable pH environment for microorganisms, that dampens the
abrupt pH changes when acid or base is introduced. The buﬀer solution needs
to be chosen according to the desired pH during the experiment and the needs
of the microorganism strain. The next step while setting up the experiment con-
sists of inoculation - the microorganisms are introduced into the buﬀer solution,
forming a medium (substance) that undergoes the experiment. Depending on
the experiment, other substances are also added (e.g. substrate for the cells).
There are multiple biological reactions happening within each cell. Given that
they have suitable nutrition in form of a substrate, whole colonies reproduce
and grow. In most of the cases, the substance is becoming more acidic during
fermentation because of the cells’ metabolic products. According to [VNL11],
when pH diﬀer slightly (cca. 1pH) from the optimum, the cell activity change
is not very drastic. However, larger deviations can lead to large microbial activ-
ity reductions. Cells can regulate their own intracellular pH (e.g. by pumping
ions in/out) and can compensate for this eﬀect to some extent. However, the

Biological model

3.1
Buﬀer
In 1983, Gustafsson and Waller described a principle of fast acid-base reactions
in [GW83], which contains the theoretical background. The article was however
not well understood by the research community, so they published subsequent
papers in 1992 [GW92] and 1995 [GSSW95], explaining further details. After
thorough study of the papers, a dynamical pH model for an acid/base system was
constructed. The approach is generic, so it can be used to model any acids and
bases with diﬀerent proticity. However, the following model focuses on a buﬀer
solution containing acetic acid CH3COOH and its conjugate base CH3COO−,
inoculated with Lactococcus lactis (L. lactis) bacterium. A weak acid/base was
chosen because of its mild reaction (and change of pH), and ability to buﬀer
itself (the more is added to solution, the harder it gets [also for the cells] to
change the pH any further). Lactococcus experiment was chosen because it is
widely used in many ﬁelds. For Lactococcus fermentation, the usual substrate
contains sodium acetate, therefore acetic acid and sodium acetate was used

To construct the model, ﬁrst an underlying layer of theoretical background needs
to be understood. The words weak and strong are used to characterize the ionic
dissociation in an aqueous solution. Strong acid/base completely dissociate into
their hydrogen or hydroxyl ions, while weak reagents are only partially ionized.
Since weak acids do not completely dissociate, they stay in an equilibrium, which
can be denoted using an acid dissociation constant Ka.

It can be compared to an equilibrium constant K which describes a reaction at

but as a simpliﬁcation for this model, it is assumed that Q = K.
The model employs a reaction invariant concept. Reaction invariant is a set of
linear combinations of concentrations of chemical species that behave as con-
stants during the reaction, while reaction variant is combination of concen-
trations of species that change. Chemical reactors are usually modeled with
reaction-invariant and reaction-variant part. If all reactions are considered to
be at equilibrium at all instances (Q = K), the function is static and only
reaction-invariant part is suﬃcient. The model is designed using a set of dy-
namical equations for a set of reaction invariant variables, and a static nonlinear
relation that links the reaction invariant variables to the pH value. The static

Therefore, when adding sodium acetate, both C1 and W is incremented.
Having the concentrations and acid dissociation constants, the equation 3.6 can
be solved for [H+], which is used to derive the pH value. This is done program-
matically using the following process. When the whole simulation is started,
a row vector represented as an array data structure, is created, containing n
decimal numbers, logarithmically spaced from 10−0 to 10−14. The length of
this vector inﬂuences the calculated pH resolution. This is the span of possible
hydrogen ion concentrations [H+] which are sequentially substituted into the
equation in a loop and a result is calculated. The loop break is conditioned by
the current result being negative, while the previously calculated result being
positive. When this condition is met, [H+] used to calculate the previous result
is taken into consideration and pH is calculated −log10([H+]). This ensures
that the result is as accurate as possible, within the limits of the pH precision.
The formula for calculated pH resolution is res = 1.4 ∗10−(x−1), where x is an
exponent in element count equation n = 10x. E.g. given n = 10, 000, the model
has 0.0014 pH resolution.

Bacteria or other microorganisms (cells) such as yeast undergo various biologi-
cal processes during fermentation experiments. Even though the processes are
complex, simple approximation models are usually used to create a quantitative

exponential phase is characterized by cell doubling. Due to the growth-limiting

phase, cells start to die and their population decreases.

Chapter 5
Algorithm
There are multiple reasons scientists want to control the pH during experiments.
For example, some organisms require optimal environment to yield higher out-
put biomass concentration, whereas for others the focus might be purely on the
ﬁnal product titer. Low pH can also inﬂuence the solubility within the buﬀer,
causing some compounds to come out of solution due to precipitation. Given
that the reactor device is programmed with a pH range the substance needs to
stay in during the experiment, the system needs to actively monitor the pH and
correct it in case it shifts outside of the speciﬁed range. The goal is to ﬁnd an
algorithm that is optimized for this purpose, does not cause unnecessary oscil-
lations (overshooting), is not computationally heavy, and can be implemented
to the embedded device.
The Algorithm part of the modelling framework, that can be seen in Figure 4.1
is represented with an abstract class that contains the generic features like the
minimum and maximum of the required pH span, and method for printing the
actions to the csv ﬁle. This makes it possible to create other classes that can
extend the Algorithm class and provide the actual implementation of the logic.
When choosing the algorithm, there are several consideration that need to be
accounted for. First of all, the target platform is a battery powered embedded
device, meaning that power consumption is an issue. Secondly, the amount of
stabilizing liquids (acid and base) is limited by the container size, which is 30mL
in this device version.
==PAGE:42==
32
Algorithm
The algorithm framework is designed such that it executes the pH sampling
process every sampling time ts milliseconds, when the current pH is sampled
from the Buﬀer using a pH sensor. Afterwards, the comparison needs to be
performed to determine whether the system should react or not. The purpose
of the system is to keep the pH within the limit.
To prevent the pH going
outside the required span, it is compared to the imaginary boundaries, which
are calculated as the pH span ± the trigger range tr. By default, ts = 4s and tr =
0.02. These values seem reasonable for generic experiments. If the pH is outside
the boundaries, the algorithm proceeds to calculate the required volume dvol of
acid or base to be dispensed into the substance. However, due to the mixing
time, there needs to be a delay of at least tm between dispensings, to allow the
pH to settle. The dvol calculation depends on the concrete implementations,
proposed in the following subsections. The algorithm ﬂow chart is shown in
Figure 5.1, where the "Calculate dvol" part is dependant on the implementation.
pHL and pHH denote the lower and upper bounds of the required pH span,
respectively.

Strong acid/base model
Using the simulations from previous chapter, it was found that a weak acid/base
is not suﬃcient for pH control if the volume has to be kept below 30mL. In
this chapter, a solution is proposed by modifying the model to support a strong
acid/base reactions, so that much smaller volume can cause greater pH response.
For modeling and experiments, hydrochloric acid HCl and sodium hydroxide
NaOH were chosen for pH correction. Revisiting the electroneutrality equation
3.2, it can mostly stay the same because [H+], [Na+], and [OH−] are already
included. However, the concentration of Cl−ions also needs to be included,
since they are now present in the system (eqn. 6.1).
[H+] + [Na+] = [OH−] + [Cl−] + [CH3COO−] + [LaCOO−]
(6.1)
Regarding eqn. 3.4, C1 and C2 stay unchanged, while W now has to be updated
to keep concentration of both, sodium cation Na+ and chloride anion Cl−.
W = [Na+] −[Cl−]
(6.2)
As a consequence of this change, the electroneutrality equation 3.6 can stay
in its original form.
However, the method of updating W needs to change.

be modiﬁed when adding strong acid/base is W. When HCl is added, W is
decreased proportionally to the added enplethy.
In the same manner, W is
increased with added NaOH.

Implementation
The selected algorithm was ported to C++ language and implemented within
the system’s ﬁnite-state machine (FSM) framework. Since the microcontroller
is running in a cyclic executive manner, there is no parallelism on the chip and
all the logic is handled in the FSM, so the program is always in only one of the
deﬁned states at any given time and executes one speciﬁc operation. The im-
plemented code can be seen in Appendix 8.2. The algorithm was modiﬁed such
that instead of operating on the volume to be dispensed, it uses the number of
steps the motor needs to turn, which is (when neglecting the mechanical errors)
linearly proportional to the dispensed volume. The current pump technology is
not able to dispense less than 30µL drop because of the surface tension of the
liquid in the pump nozzle. Therefore, the minimum and maximum number of
steps were set to 20 and 50, correspondingly, calculated using measured relation
1.5µL/step. To verify if the algorithm can run even with this limitation, the
experiment simulation was executed again with updated volume constants (i.e.
dmin = 30µL and dmax = 75µL). The result of the simulation can be seen in

from the previous experiment using lower volumes. The ﬁnal biomass concen-

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Providing ideal conditions for microbial strains during biological processes is a
known challenge that is being addressed in many ﬁelds. Building a chemical
model enabled to abstract and simplify the complexity of a real biological pro-
cess, by approximating certain conditions, while maintaining the main logic of
chemical processes. The model was built in a generic fashion, such that a mul-
titude of experiment can be simulated by changing corresponding variables. A
speciﬁc, real-life fermentation experiment conducted in the laboratory was used
to ﬁt the model, so that it accurately represents the experiment. This proce-
dure allows to reuse the model for diﬀerent tests, without the need of running
a full-scale fermentation every time a new method needs to be tested.
In order to optimize the biological environment, several algorithms were tested
on the model. All the algorithms calculated the volume of liquid to be dispensed
to change the pH in case it drifts outside of the required range. Simulations
showed that a strong acid/base is needed to control the pH if the maximum
volume of available acid/base is limited to 30mL. The best performing algorithm
uses proportional logic, where the amount of liquid that needs to be dispensed
is calculated using the rate at which the previous pH change occurred. Since
fermentation is a slow reaction, it was found out that the period of 32 seconds
between individual dispensings is suﬃcient, and helps to let the strong acid/base
properly distribute.
==PAGE:62==
52
Conclusion
Selected algorithm was used both, in the simulation and in the corresponding
real fermentation experiment. According to the simulation, the ﬁnal biomass
concentration is approximately two times larger for the speciﬁc pH-controlled
experiment, in oppose to the non-controlled one. During the real experiment,
two devices were used side-by-side to monitor the fermentation process, one
with activated pH control mode. The biomass concentration was not measured
continuously during the whole experiment due to the diﬃculty and unreliability
of the measuring process. Instead, a ﬁnal CFU measurement was taken, which
showed approximately two times larger CFU for the pH-controlled experiment.
Assuming that CFU and Biomass concentration are close to being linearly de-
pendent (as discussed in Chapter 7), this is a satisfactory result, since the values
are expected to follow the same trend.
During the modelling process, several simpliﬁcations were made, which allowed
to represent a complex, real-life problem with mathematical equations. This
worked well for the given purpose, however it must not be forgotten that the
model is an approximation, rather than the exact representation of the problem.
This relates to both, the biological and chemical processes (e.g. all reactions are
assumed to happen instantaneously), and mechanical capabilities of the device
(pump, pH sensor imprecision).
Nevertheless, the simulation results showed
great consistency with real experiment, proving that the model can be used to
valuably simulate experiments.

Discussion and Future work
In the beginning of the project, an initial plan was deﬁned to estimate duration
of individual phases. The project progressed according to the schedule for the
research and problem deﬁnition part. However, building the model representa-
tion of the biological process took more time than expected. This was mainly
caused by the complexity of the theory behind the model as well as the fact
that chemistry and biology are not author’s ﬁelds of expertise. It was expected
that at least three diﬀerent algorithms will be proposed and evaluated, but it
turned out that the second one was already suﬃcient for the pH control. The
progress, results, and obtained knowledge were documented during the whole
project duration, as they appeared. This made it possible to capture details,
that otherwise would be hard to document and comment on.

that could be improved in the future. Even though the simulation framework
has a properly commented source code, it would be more user-friendly if a self-
explanatory graphical user interface (GUI) is implemented, so that it can be
used to setup diﬀerent simulations without editing the source code. Also, the
mathematical approach of solving the electroneutrality equation could be op-
timized to reduce the computation time and make simulations faster. Having
a stable, easy-to-use platform with data collection, neural network can be uti-
lized for predictive data analysis and possible strain optimization. However, the
simulation tool and the device still need to undergo various tests that assess
the functionality and reliability much more thoroughly, before the data can be
considered reputable.
