Abstract
In this project oxidation of ammonia was studied by conducting experiments with varying
residence time and modelling the experiments. All experiments were conducted in a vari-
able length flow reactor (VLF reactor). The experimental conditions studied in this project
were 720-920°C, atmospheric pressure and combustibles concentrations up to 1000ppm. The
systems CO/O2, NH3/CO/O2 and NH3/H2/O2 was studied.
The experimental results were examined using a detailed kinetic model developed by Dai et
al. [1] at the DTU research center Combustion and Harmful Emission Control (CHEC). After
all experiments were conducted, entrainment was attempted quantified. All experiments were
affected by entrainment to a certain extent.
The project concludes that Dai et al. predicts too fast conversion for the studied conditions.
The Arrhenius parameters for the mechanisms of CO + OH, NH2 + O and NH3 + OH were
addressed as subject to change and were adjusted. It was shown that the model could be
improved by changing these rate constants.

1
Introduction
This report examines oxidation of ammonia in the presence of the combustibles CO and
H2. This is done with the purpose of improving the existing models found in literature.
Laboratory experiments were performed in order to support this. The experiments were
conducted with known inlet concentrations and varying residence time in order to acquire
data regarding the reaction rate of the involved species.
The kinetic model used in this study was developed by Dai et al.[1] and was examined using
CHEMKIN PRO software [2].
The motivation for the project is that the findings from this project can contribute to improve
current models and reduce NOx releases from ammonia oxidation processes. The interest
in the project comes from ROCKWOOL’s ambitions for helping the environment. ROCK-
WOOL’s 2020 sustainability report focuses on reducing these NOx emissions as a company
goal, as they strive to fulfil UN’s Sustainability Development Goals (SDG’s) [3]. This is
formulated as: ”NOx air emissions avoided in the lifetime of building insulation sold”.
The climate and environmental crisis is a relevant and important issue to address. According
to a 2021 UN climate report ”Climate change 2021 The physical science basis” the global
greenhouse gas emissions are too great for maintaining the current climate on the globe [4].
1.1
Ammonia as a carbon free fuel
An interesting prospect of studying the oxidation of ammonia during combustion is the other
uses this information can be utilized for. In recent years, many resources have been allocated
to the research of ammonia and its potential as a green fuel for a variety of applications.
Ammonia has the potential to be used for internal combustion engines and fuel cells, among
other applications due to the combustion properties [5]. The concept of an ammonia-powered
engine is not a new concept. Ammonia engines was developed as far back as in 1937 [6].
The many resources allocated to ammonia research relates to its potential properties as a
carbon free energy carrier. Since ammonia contains no carbon atoms, this fuel should not
emit any carbon dioxide.
When ammonia is used in a combustion or fuel cell system, it should theoretically be possible
to oxidise ammonia while only producing steam, molecular nitrogen and energy that can be
2
==PAGE:9==
used to drive an engine forward as seen in reaction (1).
2 NH3 + 1.5 O2 −−→N2 + 3 H2O, ∆H= −634kJ
(1)
However, partial oxidation should be expected as well, releasing NO.
2 NH3 + 2.5 O2 −−→2 NO + 3 H2O
(2)
The chemical kinetics tends to favour nitric oxide formation, and requires a more complex
description of the process than the proposed stoichiometric equation. The nitric oxide for-
mation is also temperature dependent. This release of NOx greenhouse gasses is one of the
issues with ammonia as a green fuel.
Another downside is that it must be produced in large quantities to support the demand for
ammonia. By using the Haber-Bosch process, ammonia can be produced from nitrogen in
the air and hydrogen, which must be produced first, as seen in reaction (3).
N2 + 3 H2 ←−→2 NH3
(3)
The combustion of ammonia can be influenced by the presence of hydrogen. It is therefore
of interest to acquire knowledge of this influence in order to optimise potential ammonia
motors. This is further described in section 2.5.2.
When discussing ammonia as a carbon free fuel, it is necessary to examine the emissions from
ammonia production. Ammonia can be labeled as black, blue and green ammonia depending
on the greenhouse emission released when producing it [5]. Green ammonia is produced from
a renewable source that does not emit greenhouse gasses [5].
The challenges with producing green ammonia along with ammonia’s poor ignition qualities
and narrow flammability limits must be solved in order to have a widely commercial available
fuel that can be classified as green and efficient.

2
Literature study
The literature study describes stone wool production, the fundamentals of combustion, the
various mechanisms leading to the formation of nitrogen oxides (NOx) and models for the
oxidation of ammonia.
In the stone wool production, combustion is utilised to supply energy for conversion of the
raw material. It is necessary to have a high temperature combustion for the production,
which unavoidable will result in the emission of NOx gasses as a by-product. The source of
NOx is either from oxidation of nitrogen bound in the fuel, from molecular nitrogen in the
combustion air or from oxidation of ammonia which is released from the binder in recycled
stone wool. Additionally, the combustion can form the combustibles CO and H2, which
have an influence on the NOx formation. The formation of NOx in the presence of CO
or H2 is examined in terms of the conversion of NH3, which is released from the binder in
ROCKWOOL’s stone wool production process.
The literature study furthermore examines the modelling of nitrogen chemistry and selected
models found in the literature.
2.1
Stone wool production and recycling
In the traditional stone wool production process, the raw materials are fed to a cupola furnace,
where they are melted into a stone wool melt. An overview of the whole process used by
ROCKWOOL is seen in figure (1).
4
==PAGE:11==
Figure 1: Schematic of the ROCKWOOL process with a copula furnace [7].
The raw materials used are a mix of virgin mineral materials such as natural stone materials
and waste materials from for example steel and aluminium production. The raw mineral
materials are stacked in large blocks in the furnace along with coke fuel. Additional fuel
and oxygen are added from the bottom through nozzles. Through combustion of the fuels,
heat is generated which melts the mineral materials. Blocks of raw material are continuously
added from the top as the material sinks while it melts. The melt will leave the furnace at
the bottom [7] [8].
When leaving the furnace the stone wool melt has a temperature of approximately 1500°C
and is then spun into fibres by a cascade spinner. The cascade spinner spins fibres by letting
droplets flow from rotating wheels, where the centrifugal forces makes the droplets fly off the
wheels, dragging fibres behind them. Afterwards, the fibres are mixed with a binder, cured
in an oven and then cut into the final product [7].
It is possible to recycle stone wool waste back into the copula furnace, though it requires
that the stone wool waste is cast into briquettes, as it is comprised of fine particles. This is
necessary, as the stack in the furnace has to be permeable by gases and be self-supporting,
thus having high physical strength, until melting and combustion is almost completed [8].
In order to obtain similar properties for the recycled stone wool, the particles are cast into
cement briquettes. Portland cement is most often used for making the briquettes and it
5
==PAGE:12==
contains a binder with sulphur. This result in an effluent with a higher sulphur content and
thus more treatment of the effluent is needed. These aspects make it a costly and demanding
process to recycle stone wool in a traditional shaft furnace like a copula furnace [7] [8].
Additionally, the shaft furnace has a tendency to have reducing conditions, which results
in some of the iron in the raw materials being reduced to metallic iron. Thus making it
necessary to separate metallic iron from the stone wool melt. Furthermore, the process with
a shaft furnace does not have a high thermal efficiency. These different aspects have led
to the development of other processes, which are more flexible towards recycling, provides
flexibility in terms of fuel type and have higher thermal efficiencies. One of these processes
was developed by ROCKWOOL where a melting cyclone is used instead of a furnace and
additional pre-heater cyclones are utilised in order to further increase the thermal efficiency.
This process is called the Integrated Melting Furnace (IMF). In this process, it is easy to
recycle stone wool waste, which also lowers the thermal energy need, as it requires 23% less
energy to heat and melt stone wool waste than raw mineral material [9]. Thus, the use of
this process also fits in well in a circular economy, as material is recycled and the residual
thermal energy is utilised [10].
When recycled stone wool is mixed with raw mineral material the required energy for heating
and melting is decreased and it changes linearly with the amount of recycled material until
it reaches a maximum of 23% less energy with 100 % recycled material [9]. Additionally, the
conditions in the melting cyclone lowers the risk of reducing iron in the mineral material into
metallic iron [8] [9].
2.1.1
The Integrated Melting Furnace (IMF)
In the Integrated Melting Furnace process, the raw mineral materials for creating stone wool
are melted in a melting cyclone instead of a shaft furnace. In addition to the melting cyclone,
two pre-heater cyclones are used to utilise the thermal energy in the exhaust gas from the
melting cyclone. Thus, the hot exhaust gas is used to heat up the mineral materials in the
pre-heater cyclones before entering the melting cyclone [7] [8].
In the melting cyclone the raw materials and solid fuel, if used, need to be added as particles,
as the residence time in the cyclone is significantly lower than in a copula furnace. The
mineral material, fuel and combustion air are blown tangentially into the cyclone at or near
the top of it in order to create the swirling flow, as seen in figure (2). This flow causes the
solid material to be centrifuged towards the walls where some of the material will melt before
6
==PAGE:13==
hitting the wall, creating a liquid film at the wall. The unmelted particles will stick to the
liquid film and melt while the molten film flows downwards. At the bottom of the cyclone,
the stone wool melt is collected in a settling tank before being transferred to a spinner [7]
[8].
Figure 2: Schematic of the principles in the melting cyclone [7].
Different fuel types are available for the melting cyclone. Two of these are pulverised coal
and methane, where it is possible to use both fuel types simultaneously or methane only.
The main part of the coal, if used, is injected through burners alongside mineral material
and combustion air at the top as seen in figure (2).
Methane is injected at the bottom
through oxy-fuel gas burners in order to create a hot flame. This flame ensures that the melt
is heated to at least the required 1500°C for the spinner. Additionally, a small part of the
pulverised coal and combustion air is added in the bottom as well. An advantage of using
coal or methane instead of coke, as in the copula furnaces, is that the CO2 emissions are
reduced. In addition, the use of particulate or gaseous fuels opens op for the possibility of
using alternative fuels, such as biogas [7][8].
In figure (3), an overview of the IMF process can be seen, but there are some incongruences
7
==PAGE:14==
between the two sources [7], a PhD thesis written by V. Schultz-Falk in collaboration with
ROCKWOOL, and [8] a patent from ROCKWOOL. The main incongruence is where the
recycled wool waste is added to the system. In the PhD thesis as seen in figure (3), the wool
waste is added directly to the melting cyclone. Where in the patent the wool waste is added
right before the lower pre-heater cyclone, but after the addition of the raw mineral material
from the upper pre-heater cyclone. In order to simplify the description of the process from
the patent, what is called the first mineral material is here referred to as raw material and
what is called second mineral material is referred to as recycled material.
Figure 3: Figure of the IMF process as described in the PhD thesis by Schultz-Falk [7].
The IMF process is described by the patent[8] as: The hot exhaust gas, generated from
8
==PAGE:15==
the combustion of fuels and release of gasses from the raw material, typically have a tem-
perature of 1500 to 1750°C at the top of the melting cyclone. It is directly quenched with
air after leaving the cyclone in order to lower the temperature to around 1400°C before the
second heating of the raw material in the first conduit. This is necessary in order to prevent
agglomeration or melting of the particles, as the sintering temperature of the particles are
lower than the temperature of the hot exhaust gas. Typically, the sintering temperature of
the raw mineral material is 1200 to 1400°C [8]. After heating of the raw material and thus
cooling of the exhaust gas, the recycled material is added. The principle here is that, as the
recycled material is added to the preheating system where the exhaust gas has been cooled,
the sintering temperature of this material can be lower than for the raw mineral material.
Thus making it possible to pre-heat the recycled stone wool material as well, since the binder
in this material lowers the sintering temperature to around 900 to 1100°C. If the recycled
material agglomerates, it tends to clog the system and more energy is required for melting
agglomerated particles and thus should be avoided [8].
After heating of both mineral and recycled materials in the first conduit, they are led to the
lower pre-heater cyclone with the exhaust gas. Here gas and particles are separated and the
particles are led to the melting cyclone. The still hot exhaust gas is further utilised and led
to the second conduit, where the raw mineral material is added to the system and is heated
the first time and then led to the upper pre-heater cyclone. Here the preheated raw mineral
particles are separated and led to the first conduit to be further heated and the gas led out
of the cyclone preheating system [8].
Preferably, the raw material is heated to a temperature a bit lower than the sintering tem-
perature of the recycled material, around 850 to 950°C, in the upper preheating cyclone and
then heated to a higher temperature in the first conduit. The recycled material should ideally
be heated to temperatures of 850 to 950°C, but a minimum of 700°C is sufficient. At this
temperature it is possible to fully combust the organic binder in the recycled material, when
a sufficient amount of oxygen is present [8].
The IMF process as described by the thesis [7] is similar to that of the patent. The main
difference as mentioned is that the wool waste is added to the melting cyclone, which is
based on the same principle as for the other addition point.
It is the need for avoiding
agglomeration of the recycled mineral material and thus avoiding clogging of the system.
Additionally, the temperatures of the mineral particles in the pre-heater cyclones is a bit
different in this description. Otherwise, the two descriptions are consistent.

2.1.2
Issues regarding recycling in the IMF
An issue regarding recycling of mineral materials such as stone wool in the IMF is the organic
binder used to keep the stone wool fibres together. Most often the organic binder contains
nitrogen, usually as urea, but it can also be as ammonium compounds. The most common
organic binder in stone wool is a phenol-urea-formaldehyde resin [11].
Ammonia is formed and released when urea in the phenol-urea-formaldehyde resin thermally
decomposes under high temperatures. This happens in multiple steps and different reactions.
Urea decomposes to cyanic acid and ammonia at elevated temperatures, as seen in reaction
(4) [11]. Additionally, cyanic acid can react with intact urea and form biuret, which further
reacts with cyanic acid and forms cyanuric acid and ammonia as seen in reaction (5) [11]. In
figure (4) the structures of cyanuric acid are shown.
H2N−CO−NH2 (m) →NH4
+NCO−(m) →NH3 (g) + HNCO (g)
(4)
H2N−CO−NH−CO−NH2 (m) + HNCO (g) →C3H3N3O3 (s) + NH3 (g)
(5)
The m in the reactions is the notation for when the compound is in a melt. In addition
several other reactions also takes place in the decomposition and different intermediates are
formed, but at high temperatures all compounds either decomposes or sublimates [11].
Figure 4: The two chemical structures of cyanuric acid, which it can readily convert between [12].
When ammonia is released and thus present in an environment with high temperatures
and oxygen, it will both be oxidised to NOx as well as be reduced to N2. As a result of
the oxidation of ammonia in the IMF when stone wool is recycled, it is desired to further
investigate this. This is found in section 2.4.

2.2
Combustion
Combustion can be defined as ”rapid oxidation generating heat, or both light and heat; also,
slow oxidation accompanied by relatively little heat and no light” [13]. Combustion processes
are essential processes that can be utilised for a number of different purposes such as oxida-
tion, heat generation and/or light generation [13].
The combustion process requires a fuel and an oxidant to occur. The oxidant is typically
ambient air and the fuel type currently used by ROCKWOOL in Denmark is biogas [14],
which enters the melting cyclone alongside with the raw materials and stone wool waste [15].
However, ROCKWOOL also utilises coal combustion in other regions around the world [3].
Combustion of biogas
ROCKWOOL describes the biogas used as containing methane and carbon dioxide [14].
Methane has a unique tetrahedral structure with large C-H bond energies, for which it has
unique combustion characteristics [13]. The combustion of methane can be described as a
complex mechanism consisting of many reactions. The mechanism has different preferences
for products depending on a low temperature process (<1500 K) or a high temperature
process (>2000 K) [13]. In general, methane combustion releases energy and CO2, CO and
H2O which can influence the process [13].
Solid fuel combustion
Traditionally coal has been used as solid fuel instead of biogas by ROCKWOOL. During
combustion, all solid fuel particles decompose thermally and releases volatile components.
This can happen via pyrolysis and devolatilisation. Pyrolysis is most often associated with
gas release in an inert atmosphere. While devolatilisation, where gas is also released, takes
place during oxidising conditions. Figure (5) displays the stages in combustion of a solid fuel.
These are heating, pyrolysis (or devolatilisation), combustion of volatiles and combustion of
char [16].

First stage, heating of the process, is to dry the fuel particle and thus release any residual
water in the particles [16].
Second stage, pyrolysis, is the breaking of the weakest covalent bonds[16]. Hereby depoly-
merising the coal structure to smaller fragments and releasing light components such as
C1-C3 hydrocarbons, H2, CO, NH3 and HCN. During further pyrolysis, and thus decom-
position, methane is formed from methyl groups, HCN and NH3 from nitrogen atoms, CO
from ethers and hydrogen from ring condensation [16].
The remaining components are referred to as tar and char. Tar consists of higher hydro-
carbons which are liquid at room temperature and char is solids. The heavy hydrocarbons
from tar becomes volatile at high temperatures and decomposes into soot, H2, CO and C2H2
among other products during combustion of volatiles [16].
The ratio between how much is released as volatiles and char depends on the temperature.
Generally, higher temperatures will generate less char and more volatiles. For coal usually
20-40w% will be released as volatiles [16] and for biomass typically 75-95w% depending on
the type of biomass [17][18]. The char left after pyrolysis consists primarily of carbon and
ash, which are oxidised during char combustion leaving only ash behind.
Combustion of volatiles and char occurs simultaneously where char oxidation is the limiting
factor.
The fuel-air ratio (ϕ) is vital for a combustion process to fully combust, as the ratio between
the oxidising species and the fuel have a significant influence on the reactions occurring in the
process. If both the fuel and air available are consumed completely as stated by the chemical
equations, the mixture is stoichiometric (ϕ = 1), otherwise it can be either fuel-lean (ϕ <1)
12
==PAGE:19==
or fuel-rich (ϕ >1) [13].
The oxidant is usually oxygen from the ambient air. However pure oxygen can be used, this
is referred to as oxy-fuel combustion. Oxy-fuel burners are used to supply pure oxygen to
the process instead of air.
This process is usually utilised for carbon capture as the lack of nitrogen results in a higher
concentration of CO2 in the flue gas [19]. It can also result in cleaner combustion with less
NOx emission depending on the fuel, as there will be no oxidation of N2 from the air [20].
Oxy-fuel combustion can also reduce the energy required for heating and thus increasing the
efficiency [21], since oxygen´s specific heat capacity is lower than nitrogen’s counterpart.
Oxy-fuel combustion is utilised in the ROCKWOOL process to generate a hot flame to
increase the temperature in the melting cyclone, as a high heat transfer is necessary.
Even though pyrolysis of solid fuels or conversion of cyanide’s at high temperatures also forms
ammonia, the largest source is from the binder in the process [22][23]. The contribution of
NOx gasses from fuels are further discussed in section 2.3.5.
2.3
Nitric Oxide formation
For fuels with negligible amounts of nitrogen bound into them, the nitrogen source for NOx
formation is that of molecular nitrogen in the combustion air. For conventional fuels, natural
gas is the fuel type that contains negligible amounts of nitrogen bound into it.
NOx is
formed through either thermal or prompt mechanisms. The thermal formation of NO, called
either thermal NO or Zeldovich mechanism, is a major source of NO in a high-temperature
combustion process, that is above 1800 K [24]. Prompt NO is the formation of NO from
radicals originating from the fuel initiated by carbohydrates radicals (primarily CHi, where
the subscript i denotes different possibilities for different hydrocarbons) attacking molecular
nitrogen. These mechanisms along with N2O and NNH formation mechanisms are discussed
in this section. An overview of these mechanisms can be seen in figure (6).

When examining fuels with a non-negligible amount of nitrogen bound into them, NOx
gasses emitted from oxidation of the nitrogen bound in the fuel must be accounted for. The
chemistry is complicated since it may involve several different nitrogen compounds, which
can participate in reactions and be oxidised and reduced. The formation of NO is dependent
on the partitioning of nitrogen bound in the fuel between volatiles and char [22]. These
mechanisms are discussed in section 2.3.5.
2.3.1
Thermal NO
The thermal NO mechanism, also called the Zeldovich mechanism after the physicist who
first proposed it, is an important source of NO for high-temperature gas combustion [25].
This mechanism has been studied extensively and is well established.
The mechanism has a relative high activation energy, for which the conditions for this mech-
anism usually is high temperature, typically above 1800 K, and an excess of oxygen is also a
preferred conditions for this mechanism [24].
The thermal-NO mechanism is initiated by the triple bond of molecular nitrogen being at-
tacked by an oxygen atom forming NO and an N radical [22] as per reaction (6).
O + N2 −−⇀
↽−−NO + N
(6)
This step is the rate-determining step for the mechanism, due to the high activation energy
necessary to start the reaction.
Once this reaction has occurred, the nitrogen radical is
oxidised to NO via either reaction (7) or (8).
N + OH −−⇀
↽−−NO + H
(7)
N + O2 −−⇀
↽−−NO + O
(8)

When reaction (7) is included, the mechanism is referred to as the extended Zeldovich mech-
anism.
In lean or stoichiometric conditions, the preferred reaction is with molecular oxygen. How-
ever, if the conditions are fuel-rich, then due to the lack of oxygen, the reaction with OH will
be the dominant reaction [26]. It is therefore important to have both competing reactions in
mind when modelling the thermal NO mechanisms.
An issue when modelling thermal NO is that the rate constant for reaction (6) is too inaccu-
rate [22]. This is due to the difficulties of obtaining good results at the conditions required
to measure only thermal NO.
2.3.2
Prompt NO
Molecular nitrogen can also form NO via the prompt mechanism, or also called the mecha-
nism after the scientist who first described it [27].
Prompt NO is the dominant mechanism for NO formation in hydrocarbon and air diffusion
flames and with fuel-rich conditions at temperatures at or below 1800 K [22][28]. For tem-
peratures above 2500K, the NOx formation from the prompt mechanism is negligible [28].
For a burner with sufficient mixing, the fuel-rich zone is small, leading to the amount of
Prompt NO formed being small.
The mechanism can be described as occurring in three overall steps.
• Formation of CHi radicals where the usual hydrocarbon for prompt NO is CH
• Fixation of molecular nitrogen
• Conversion of nitrogen species
Assuming a CH radical is formed and present, then the mechanism is initiated by molecular
nitrogen being attacked by a CH radical as seen in reaction (9).
CH + N2 −−⇀
↽−−NCN + H
(9)
This mechanism is the main contributor to fixation of molecular nitrogen and contributes in
a fuel-air equivalence ratio of 1 ≤ϕ ≤1.3 [28].

2.3.4
NNH mechanism
The NNH mechanism is the formation of NO via NNH. This mechanism is preferred under
slightly reducing conditions and temperatures higher than for N2O formation. For high tem-
peratures, as found in a combustion process, a higher mole fraction of hydrogen is expected
which along with the high temperatures will promote the formation of NNH from N2 + H.
NNH has a short lifetime of 1−9 s [24], for which the dissociation of NNH to N2 + H are
expected to be rapid under most conditions. However, in the short lifetime it exists it can
react with atomic oxygen to form NH and NO [24].

2.3.5
Fuel-NO
The relative contribution to the NO formation in a combustion process depends on the
nitrogen content in the fuel, the combustion temperature and the fuel to air ratio [32]. With
some exceptions, all fuels contain some amount of nitrogen bound into them. This section
examines the oxidation of fuel containing nitrogen, fuel-N, forming NO and fuel-NO. The
mechanism leading to fuel-NO is more complex than the mechanisms for the conversion of
molecular nitrogen to NO. Even though the IMF does not use coal anymore, examination of
it is still relevant regarding ammonia, since ammonia is released from the binder and oxidised
similarly to nitrogen compounds from traditional fuels. This is due to ammonia historically
being a volatile-N from coal or biomass and therefore have been the driving force in studying
the release of ammonia in coal combustion.
The ratio of char-N/volatile-N is a competition between the solid fuel decomposition and
char forming. Studies show that the char-N/volatile-N ratio can be changed by the change in
temperature for the process [33]. Thus suggesting that different parameters, such as pressure,
temperature, particle size, and parent fuel characteristics have influence on the combustion
of coal.
In general the mass fraction of volatiles for coal is in the region of 20-40w%, while the mass
fraction of volatiles for biomass is 75-95w% [17][18].
During devolatilisation the fuel-N is split into char-N and volatile-N. This happens via ther-
mal degradation and the whole process is dependent on the fuel structure, peak tempera-
ture and particle size/heating. The char-N formed during devolatilisation follows a complex
mechanism and is therefore difficult to model. Parameters such as the volatile-N/char-N
ratio depends on the former mentioned parameters and have influence on the pathways of
nitrogen in the process. It is known that the char-N can be converted to NOx , N2O and
HCN via complex oxidation mechanisms. Generally 80-100w% of char-N will directly form
NO. When modelling it is often assumed that 100% of char-N forms NO. The volatile part
can be released as tar-N and light gasses such as methane, carbon monoxide, hydrogen and
nitrogen species.
Decomposition of tar-N in volatiles results in the formation of the nitrogenous compounds
NH3 and HCN, which are released as gasses and thus add to the volatile fraction. These
are the primary nitrogen containing intermediates of interest in the volatile-N. Modelling
of alternative present species, such as HNCO, will make the model complex, thus NH3 and
HCN are assumed being the only nitrogen containing species for the model. The reactions

2.4
Oxidation of Ammonia
Combustion of ammonia forms nitrogen oxides and N2. Thus, the oxidation of ammonia in a
combustion process has a high influence on the emission of NOx . Therefore, it is important
to outline the oxidation of ammonia in order to predict the emissions from the process. The
overall oxidation process of ammonia is: per equation (1) and (2).
2 NH3 + 1.5 O2 −−→N2 + 3 H2O
(1)
2 NH3 + 2.5 O2 −−→2 NO + 3 H2O
(2)
By controlling the conditions (concentration, temperature and pressure) of the oxidation of
ammonia, it is possible to favour the formation of N2 over NO or other nitrogen oxides.
Thus, it is important to know the kinetics and reaction mechanism of oxidation of ammonia
in order to choose the right conditions [22].

2.4.2
Thermal DeNOx
Selective non-catalytic reduction (SNCR) is a technology for control of NOx emissions post-
combustion.
SNCR with NH3, also known as Thermal DeNOx , aims at reducing NO by taking advantage
of ammonia as a reducing agent. This process has been studied extensively, for which a
number of characteristic features are known: [24]
1.
The process is restrained to function in the temperature range of 1100-1400 K. The
reaction becomes too slow under 1100 K and for temperatures above 1400 K ammonia will
be oxidised to NO rather than N2. If kept in the temperature range, the reaction will be
self-sustaining and does therefore not require addition of other fuel components [24].
2. When kept below 1400 K, the reaction requires oxygen to proceed. An increase in oxygen

concentration correlates with a shift towards lower temperatures and widens the available
temperature range [24].
3. The presence of combustibles CO and H2, which are covered in section 2.5, will shift the
temperature range for NO removal towards lower temperatures due to an increased radical
pool. High concentrations of combustibles will narrow the temperature range [24].
4. Thermal DeNOx is not an explosive process and takes approximately 0.1 s during ideal
mixing [24].

N2 +H2O or NNH+OH are formed. The latter initiates the dissociation of NNH as described

K for DeNOx is estimated. The lower limit is due to the reaction reducing ammonia to N2
becoming too slow and ammonia instead being oxidised to NO.
The challenge in understanding the chemistry of the Thermal DeNOx process in details, is
the reactivity and lifetime of NNH. Klippenstein et al. [24] estimates that the lifetime of
NNH is 10−9. It should be kept in mind, that the lifetime of NNH is an estimate which
makes sense for modelling, and have yet to be accurately determined through experiments
to a precise value, that will allow for accurate modelling of the reactions [22].
2.4.3
Surface reactivity of ammonia
Ammonia is known to decompose on quartz surfaces [37], such as the quartz reactor used for
the experiments. Studies show that ammonia’s surface sensitivity is important for reactors
with high surface/volume ratio [38].
A high surface/volume ratio for quartz surfaces is
expected to widen the temperature window of the ammonia oxidation [22].
The surface reactions occurring can be both thermal decomposition and ammonia oxidation
reactions on the surface [38].
It is expected that the influence of the surface on ammonia is dependent on the concentrations
of water vapour in the system [22]. This is due to the presence of water vapour being suspected
to alter the surface conditions. It is reported that the altered surface conditions can inhibit
the oxidation of carbon monoxide on a quartz reactor surface [39].
The induction time for oxidation of ammonia has been observed to be affected by ammonia’s
surface reactions[37] such as heterogeneous reactions [37]. This influence means that the
residence time for flow reactor experiments may not match the residence time when modelling
the same experiments. The induction time is outside of the scope of this project, which focuses
on the models ability to predict the conversion rate. Thus allowing to shift the residence time
axis to match the predicted curve of composition change in order to verify and optimise the
model.

==PAGE:35==
Glarborg et al. [22] concludes that loss of radicals on the surface of quartz reactors generally
is insignificant at temperatures great enough for combustion.
It is noted that oxidation
of reactive species that are sensitive to initiation, such as ammonia oxidation, could be
influenced by the radical loss on a quartz surface.
2.5
Ammonia oxidation in the presence of combustibles
In combustion, oxidation of ammonia is seldom done in an environment with only ammo-
nia present. Often combustibles are present, which can have influence on the oxidation of
ammonia. The influence of the combustibles carbon monoxide and molecular hydrogen is
investigated in order to understand and model the behaviour occurring in the process. These
species are usually present in combustion of biogas and thus expected to be part of ROCK-
WOOL’s combustion conditions.
Their presence replenishes the radical pool available and thereby combustibles are able to
shift the temperature range for NH3 oxidation towards lower temperatures [22].

2.6
Modelling of nitrogen chemistry
The modelling of ammonia oxidation is a complex matter and many articles have proposed
chemical kinetic models for predicting the outcome. It is desired to have a model that is
not complex and is able to accurately predict as many properties as possible. These two
objectives are difficult to achieve.
Regarding the complexity of the models an issue is, that nowadays, due to the complexity of
the chemical and physical processes involved, computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is used
for simulating various combustion processes [43]. In industrial ovens the local temperature
differences can vary greatly, for which CFD modelling is necessary. NOx kinetics is slow
and the system is not in equilibrium, for which it depends on the local conditions inside the
reactor. For the complexity of such processes the calculations requires lots of computational
power and time to obtain a result. Thus it is desirable to have a simple model in order to be
able to simulate results within a reasonable time frame.
Regarding the ability to accurately predict the properties of oxidation of ammonia no com-
plete model have yet been created, but several chemical kinetic models exists for predicting
specific outcomes at specific conditions for the oxidation of ammonia.
In general three different model types exist.
1) Detailed kinetic models:
As the naming implies, these are detailed and they consist of several thousands elementary
33
==PAGE:40==
reaction. An example of this is the model developed by Glarborg et al. [22].
2) Skeleton models:
These are a simplified versions of the detailed kinetic model. In the skeleton model, any
non-important species are not taken into consideration and the rate constants have been
optimised to fit the desired conditions (concentration, temperature and pressure).
3) Global models:
These model types consist of a couple of reactions. An example is the reactions for Selective
Non-Catalytic Reduction.
NH3 + O2 −−→NO + H2O + H
NH3 + NO −−→N2 + H2O + H
As the complexity, with great temperature gradients and the system not being in equilibrium
is not present in the experiments conducted as part of this project, CFD is not used. Instead
a detailed kinetic model have been used in conjunction with the experimental results in order
to improve a already existing model.
The model used throughout this project utilises the modified Arrhenius expression seen in
equation (86) [13].

2.7
Summary
In the literature study, it is described that, ammonia is released from the binder in the
ROCKWOOL stone wool production process. This ammonia will be released in the melting
cyclone, where the conditions will lead to the oxidation of ammonia. The main products of
the oxidation of ammonia is N2 and NO.
When N2 and oxygen are present during a high temperature combustion process, some of
it will be oxidised into NO or other nitrogen oxides. The formation of NO from N2 is well
understood. The mechanism for thermal-NO and prompt-NO are well established. Since
35
==PAGE:42==
the amount of prompt-NO is insignificant compared to the total amount of NO formed from
these processes prompt-NO is sometimes not included in models. Whereof thermal NO must
be included.
During the oxidation of ammonia, it will be involved in different reactions depending on the
conditions (concentration, temperature and pressure). As a result of these conditions leading
to different reactions, it will influence the products of the oxidation. The oxidation of NH3
without the presence of combustibles is well researched due to its potential as a carbon free
energy carrier.
Literature on the influence of CO and H2 on ammonia oxidation was studied. Reactions with
CO are known, but an accurate model for the NH3/CO/O2 system is not established. The
oxidation of ammonia in the presence of CO and its influence should be examined further in
order to accurately predict the mechanism. This project examines the NH3/CO/O2 system
further by obtaining and analysing experimental data from this system.
H2 is capable of lowering the onset temperature for the NH3/H2/O2 system. The mechanism
for the high temperature process in the NH3/H2/O2 system is also not accurately known.
This project aims at obtaining and analysing data from this system.
Both systems will
be affected by the conditions (concentration, temperature and pressure).
It is therefore
important to acquire further knowledge about the influence of these conditions on the systems
through experiments.
The chemical kinetic model used in this study is Dai et al.[1]. The model is used since it
is developed to predict nitrogen chemistry systems with the relevant species, concentrations,
pressure and temperature for this project.

3.1.4
FTIR spectroscopy
In FTIR spectroscopy the light absorption of the gasses in the infrared spectrum is measured
[46][47].
A broad spectre of light is used, which is reflected with mirrors where specific
wavelengths are blocked due to wave interference. Thus depending on the mirror position,
different wavelengths are blocked and a spectre can be formed.
The raw data is converted from light absorption for each mirror position into light absorption
per wavelength, using Fourier transformation. This is done by a computer which furthermore
is able to convert the data into the concentration for each species [46][47].
3.1.5
Flue gas analyser
The flue gas analyser analyses different gasses with different methods. The analysis of CO
and CO2 is performed by a non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) sensor. The sensor can detect
samples passing through a sample chamber using an infrared light source. By comparing
the sample gas to a reference gas, in this case nitrogen, the absorption can be measured for
specific wavelengths. The flue gas analyser can then convert the measured absorption to
concentrations of different gas molecules. The flue gas analyser has been calibrated against
known gas concentrations of CO, CO2 and O2, as well as against pure nitrogen as reference
point for when no CO, CO2 or O2 is present.
3.1.6
Gas chromatography
The principle of gas chromatography (GC) is that different volatile molecules have unique
partitioning characteristics between the column substrate and the carrier gas. The charac-
teristics allows for separation and detection of the various components. The variables that
needs to be understood for the components includes: Choice and thickness of column coating,
column length and diameter, choice of carrier gas and flow rate and the oven temperature
[48].
The GC unit used analyse in three major steps. Injection, separation and detection [48].
A gas sample is injected automatically into the GC unit, which then regulates the sample
temperature. The different components in the sample is separated in the column and the
detection is performed through a Thermal Conductivity Detector (TCD) [48].
The separated components and carrier gas are led into the TCD passing over different hot
42
==PAGE:49==
filaments. The varying measured thermal conductivity of the molecules influences the elec-
trical resistance which is directly compared to a reference and the signal is hereby measured.
This signal can then be converted into a concentration using the calibration data [48].
During the experiments the GC analyses the gas five times for better accuracy. The average
measured concentration is used, while the deviation in the measurements is used as error
function for the experiments.

Preparation of setup
Before the start of each experiment, a specific start-up procedure for the setup must be

First the FTIR needs to warm up to the required 191°C with a flow of nitrogen. Additionally,

to the required temperature, liquid nitrogen has to be added to the cooling system which
ensures cooling of the electronics in the FTIR. When the FTIR is ready for use the signal of
the FTIR is checked with an Signal-to-Noise-Ratio (SNR)-test on the computer.
SNR is a measure of the signal quality. It is a comparison between the signal and the noise.
By comparing SNR values to calibrated and previous values, the measuring accuracy of the
FTIR can be validated as a part of the start up procedure for each experiment.
The gas flows to be used in the experiment need to be set to the former calibrated values. The
description of how the gas flows are calibrated can be found in section 3.2.3. The calibration
of the flows are then checked by reading the inlet values from the FTIR and any deviations
can be adjusted accordingly.

temperature takes approximately one hour.

3.2.5
Reactor pressure
The pressure inside the reactor can be slightly above or below atmospheric pressure due
to the flow of the gasses and reactions inside the reactor.
Despite the possible pressure
differences inside the reactor during the experiment, these variations are so small, that the
pressure inside the reactor is assumed to be atmospheric. The reactor is fragile. A increased
or decreased pressure can damage the reactor and the experiments.
The pressure difference between inside the system and outside the system is relevant to know
in order to troubleshoot the system. Lower pressure inside the reactor is likely to cause
entrainment of oxygen in the system, if any defects are present. Higher pressure inside the
reactor is likely to cause in leakage out of the system, if any defects are present.
3.2.6
Testing for leakages
Another important part of preparing the setup for experiments is the test for leakages. For
this purpose a gas leak detection spray is used with a high flow of nitrogen. It is used to
check all fittings which have been loosened and tightened again. If there is a leakage it would
be visible as the bubbles generated from the detection spray would increase in size. This
test is performed every time a fitting has been loosened and regularly on fittings and valves
which are moved when the setup is used for experiments.
It should be noted that this method is not sufficient in order to find entrainments into the
system.

measurements. If the experiment shows full conversion, then the model predictions time

fitted to the point next to the longest residence time, in order to limit possible influences of

4.6
Summary and discussion
Entrainment was present in all of the experiments. The exact influence varied but the rate
of conversion was deemed reliable, since the rate of conversion seemed unaffected.
The
uncertainty from the known entrainment should still be taken into account when examining
the experiments. All conclusions drawn could be affected, as the actual initial concentrations,
which are important for the modelling, are affected.
The CO/NH3/O2 experiments showed that the rate of conversion is temperature dependent.
The conversion of NH3 is faster than of CO, as CO is not fully converted before NH3 is. The
two dominant reactions for converting NH3 and CO, compete for the same radicals. Even
though, the conversion of ammonia is dominant, the pathway for CO oxidation is likely to be
the one which drives the reactions forward due to it being overall chain branching and thus
replenishes the radical pool. This trend is also seen when varying the ratio between CO and
NH3, as more CO increases the rate of conversion significantly for both species. This also
means that the temperature can be lowered in order to obtain the same rate of conversion,
when the amount of CO is increased.
Thus the addition of CO effectively increases the
conversion of ammonia.
The H2/NH3/O2 experiments showed that the rate of conversion for the system is temper-
ature dependent. The conversion rate for H2 is faster than for NH3. Oxidation of ammonia
occurs at lower temperatures with the presence of hydrogen, but the conversion stagnates
at the examined temperatures. The observed stagnation is a result of terminating reactions
being more dominant than chain-branching reactions at specific conditions. The replenishing
of the radical pool is therefore insufficient to maintain conversion. This effect is likely due to
78
==PAGE:85==
NH3 consuming OH radicals faster than the H2 reaction pathways produces them. However,
loss of radicals to the reactor surface could also interfere with the reaction mechanisms. Fur-
ther experiments are necessary in order to establish which exact mechanisms results in the
observed stagnation. Additionally, further experiments could be useful in order to establish
at what ratios and temperatures this stagnation is occurring.
The model by Dai et al. predicts faster conversion than observed for all experiments con-
ducted. The model predictions improve as the temperature increases. This could be due to
several reasons.
It must be expected that radicals are lost on the surface of the reactor. However, this influ-
ence is neither quantified nor included in the model. Loss of radicals could partly explain the
discrepancy between experimental results and model predictions. Furthermore, inaccurate
predictions of the reaction rate for the dominating reactions, chain branching reactions and
chain terminating reactions could have a big influence on the model predictions. Addition-
ally, the rate constants are likely optimised with experimental data for temperatures higher
than examined in this project. It is likely that the models temperature dependency could be
optimised to improve model predictions for 720-920°C.

5
Model optimisation
For this project it is chosen to base modelling on the model by Dai et al. [1]. Dai et al. has
been examined at the experimental conditions used for the experiments in this project. That
is, the temperature range 720°C-920°C, atmospheric pressure and combustibles concentration
up to 1000ppm.
This section describes the adjustments to the model by Dai et al. [1] in order to match the
results obtained by the experiments. The adjusted model is referred to as Optimised Dai
et al. The optimised Dai et al. predictions is furthermore compared to Dai et al. and the
experimental data and the benefits are discussed.
Stagni et al.[55] concludes that a comprehensive understanding of the kinetic behaviour of
ammonia combustion at low temperatures (<1200 K) is still a challenge [55]. This may partly
explain that Dai et al. consistently predicting faster conversion rates than observed.
The adjustments to the model were made for reaction rates which have a significant influence
on model predictions. Thus making it possible to adjust the model in the temperature range
720-920°C. Since the model predictions are consistently faster than observed, it is complex to
improve the model to better reflect the experiments. This is due to the complex mechanisms
of ammonia chemistry and that adjustments have to be in agreement with experimental data.
Thus the adjustments to the model are based on which mechanisms can be improved. The
mechanisms which have been adjusted are for reactions with CO + OH, NH2 + O and NH3 +
OH.

5.4
Summary and discussion
The model was attempted optimised for the experiments conducted throughout this project.
The optimisation yields model predictions in better agreement with the measured data than
Dai et al. The optimised model is significantly better at predicting the NH3/CO/O2-systems.
It is insufficient at predicting the NH3/H2/O2-systems and the CO/O2-system. More opti-
misation is therefore needed for CO oxidation and NH3 oxidation with H2.
It should be noted that the optimised model’s accuracy is compared to the experiments for
which it was improved. Thus, it should be verified against other experiments in order to be
validated.
The experiments suggested that the optimised model´s accuracy increased at higher temper-
atures, but the optimised model was not tested against higher or lower temperatures than
100
==PAGE:107==
for the experiments. The model was furthermore fitted in the temperature range 720-920°C
by changing the A-parameters. The model parameters should be further optimised for a
reasonable temperature dependent reaction rate.
Additionally, the possible radical loss at the surface was not taken into account in the op-
timised model. As the influence of this effect is unknown it is possible that it should be
included in in the model in order to obtain more accurate predictions.

6
Future work
While working on the project it was evident that there is still information to be revealed
about ammonia oxidation. This section reflects upon what could be improved in the cur-
rent experiments, which experiments could be useful for further investigation and further
improvements for optimising the detailed kinetic model.
For future work is is recommended to improve the experimental setup in order to minimise
defects causing entrainment. It could be relevant to go through different sealings in the setup
and checking if the current tubing makes diffusion of oxygen possible. This could be tested by
doing experiments with different kinds of tubing. As it is probably not possible to completely
avoid entrainment it is also recommended to quantify entrainment more systematically than
what has been done in this project. Additionally, it is recommended to improve the movable
parts in the setup to counter the fragility of the VLF reactor, as problems with cracking of
the reactor was experienced in the current project.
It is furthermore recommended to conduct experiments for quantifying the surface sensitivity
of the experiments. The uncertainty regarding loss of radicals on the surface must be deter-
mined in order to remove the uncertainties of the experiments. Experiments with varying
surface to volume ratios would be relevant.
It is recommended to collect more experimental data to be used for optimising of the model.
Additional data for both the CO/NH3/O2 system and the H2/NH3/O2 system would be
useful, especially at varying ratios. Investigating the oxidation without water vapour in the
H2/NH3/O2 system might also be beneficial. With more experiments in the H2/NH3/O2
it could be possible to establish when stagnation occurs and establish reaction rates for the
mechanisms responsible for the stagnation. It could be relevant to investigate the reaction
NH3 + H −−⇀
↽−−NH2 + H2 and establish its influence on stagnation.
The optimised model seems promising for estimating the NH3/CO/O2 experiments. It is rec-
ommended to validate the optimised model against other experimental data. Generally, the
Arrhenius parameters found in this project needs improvement in order to adjust for temper-
ature dependency. Additionally, the mechanisms with NH2 + O needs further investigation
in order to establish the influence of the different reactions.
It is also recommended to obtain more sufficient data for NOx formation at the investigated
temperatures. It is relevant to investigate the NOx producing pathways to improve the model
and accurately predict the NOx formation.

7
Conclusion
The project aimed at exploring oxidation of ammonia as a function of residence time through
oxidation experiments and kinetic modelling. Several experiments were conducted. Oxida-
tion of CO, oxidation of ammonia with either CO or H2 and water vapour, and oxidation
of ammonia and H2 without water vapour. The reproducibility of the experiments was ac-
ceptable. All experiments were conducted at low concentration and controlled temperatures.
The experiments all showed a temperature dependency. This was observed as increased con-
version at increased temperatures. The VLF reactor allows for changing the residence time
with movable parts. It is concluded that points of defect in the reactor or movable parts
must be improved in order to reduce entrainment.
The mechanism for CO oxidation is concluded to be inaccurate at the studied temperature
interval (720-920°C). It is determined that the reaction rate from Dai et al.
for CO +
OH −−⇀
↽−−CO2 + H needs improvement in this temperature interval. 2 For the CO/NH3/O2
it is concluded that the system gains plenty of OH radicals from CO oxidation mechanisms.
It is shown that an increased CO:NH3 ratio increases the overall conversion.
From the H2/NH3/O2 experiments it is concluded that that conversion of ammonia in the
presence of hydrogen occurs at low combustion temperatures. It was furthermore observed
that conversion of hydrogen and ammonia stagnated and the concentrations became constant
given sufficient time. The stagnation is credited to depletion of the radical pool needed to
oxidise the species. It is concluded that oxidation of ammonia and hydrogen at concentra-
tions <1000ppm reaches steady state without full conversion at low temperatures.
It was furthermore proven that water vapour in the system would influence the conversion.
The influence was credited to water vapour adding more OH radicals to the system. Ex-
periments showed that ammonia conversion increased while hydrogen conversion decreased
without water vapour.
It is concluded that possible radical loss on the reactor surface must be quantified in order
to improve the model and to further understand the mechanisms behind the experimental
data obtained.
The experiments showed that the model developed by Dai et al. consistently predicted faster
conversion than observed. It was found that the model by Dai et al. is not able to predict
the experimental results at the studied conditions. It is concluded that the model is not
optimised for the temperature range studied in this project. It is furthermore concluded that
103
==PAGE:110==
the model is inaccurate when predicting the oxidation of ammonia with the presence of either
CO or H2 at the studied conditions.
Sensitivity analysis and literature showed that the Arrhenius parameters for the mechanisms
of CO + OH, NH2 + O and NH3 + OH should be optimised to improve predictions for the
experiments conducted in this project. It is concluded that these parameters need to be
further optimised in order to obtain accurate temperature dependency. It is shown that the
optimised parameters in this project is an improvement when comparing the predicted data
with the obtained experimental results, compared to Dai et al. However, the optimised values
need to be validated against more experimental data in order to determine the accuracy of
the optimised parameters.

Searching for leakages and entrainments of oxygen
Several methods were used in order to search for leakages out of the system or entrainments
of oxygen into the setup.
The most obvious and easy approach to check for leakages was by using gas leak detection
spray to search for leakages at fittings and joints as described in section 3.2.6, but none were
found.
After running an experiment the oxygen flow was stopped in order to see if it was possible to
recover the initial concentration of CO when the oven was hot. This test revealed that the
concentration of CO would slowly increase, but not reach the initial value when adjusting for
the lower flow. This along with the results from the flue gas analyser further indicated that
an oxygen entrainment into the system was present.
Another approach to entrainment detection was to switch the inlet and bulk flow. The idea
being, that the pattern of CO concentrations increasing at the three longest residence times
could be due to entrainments at specific locations along the reactor or reactor shield. Such
an entrainment could influence the oxidation of CO before expected. The test showed the
same pattern at a variety of temperatures regardless of the oxygen being in the bulk gas or
the movable inlet tube.
In order to limit any potential entrainment in the reactor, where switching the inlet flows
would not influence the reaction, and to eliminate that there could be a potential problem
with surface reactivity, the reactor was changed.
This did not change the pattern with
increasing concentration at the longest residence times either.
The pattern of the CO concentration with residence time did not change in any of the different
approaches used. Therefore, it was suspected that the entrainment of oxygen into the setup
might be into the CO flow before the reactor. Inspection revealed a small fracture on the
soft tube leading to the movable inlet tube. The fracture was responsible for entrainment of
a few hundred ppm of oxygen. This small entrainment was not noticeable on the flue gas
analyser as the concentration of oxygen is measured in percentage. Additionally, the small
entrainment was not enough to noticeable shift the concentrations and flows of the other
species measured on the FTIR. Though the entrainment had an influence on the reaction
since the reaction would be initiated already in the movable inlet tube. The soft tube was
111
==PAGE:118==
then replaced by a hard tube and the movable inlet tube was replaced with a tube with a
bent in the top so it was possible to attach the hard tube.

O2 entraining into the setup a flue gas analyser was used. This analyser was chosen since it,

centrations. The FTIR is not capable of accurately measuring CO2 at small concentrations.
