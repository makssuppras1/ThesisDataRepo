Summary (English)
Handling imbalanced data sets is a hurdle many classiﬁers struggle with because
they e.g try to maximize the accuracy or rely on the fraction between the diﬀer-
ent classes.
In this thesis we explore how three well-known classiﬁers: Random Forest, Neural
Network and Logistic Regression behave on diﬀerent data sets when we reduce
the positive/negative ratio of observations.
We do this by tuning the classiﬁers hyper-parameters and/or combine it with
four re-sampling techniques; SMOTE, Random Oversampling, Random Under-
sampling and Condensed Nearest Neighbours and aim to ﬁnd if one or more
classiﬁer/re-sampling generally performs better.
We will be testing the classiﬁers/re-sampling on four datasets, three rather small
data sets with diﬀerent amount of noise and one large data set.
The thesis can be divided into two sections:
Section one:
This section consists of the theory. The theory covers measure(s) used to evaluate
a classiﬁer’s performance. Furthermore the section contains the theory behind
each re-sampling method and each classiﬁer.
Section two:
Since our results consists of more than 250 ROC-curves, we present here a sum-
mary of the results, which should give an overview of how each classiﬁer/re-
sampling combination performs.
==PAGE:4==
ii
Each ROC-curve can be found in Appendix B,C,D and E, if one wants to dig
into the detail of each classiﬁer/re-sampling combination.
Unfortunately we were not able to conclude that one classiﬁer and/or re-sampling
method was better generally, and the (potential) reason behind that is discussed
at the end.

Chapter 1
Abstract
Dealing with imbalanced data sets is a general problem many classiﬁers have,
since it often leads to heavy over ﬁtting of the majority class.
This thesis explores how Random Forest, Neural Network and Logistic Regression
perform on increasingly imbalanced data sets.
The classiﬁers are optimized either by tweaking the classiﬁer by it self and/or
apply diﬀerent re-sampling methods. The performance is tested on four data sets:
three rather small with increasing noise and ratio of imbalance, and one large data
set, also with increasing ratio of imbalance. Using the results obtained we end
up concluding that not one classiﬁer/re-sampling method complete outperforms
others, but that the classiﬁer of choice should be the personally most well-known.

Chapter 2
Introduction
Correct classiﬁcation of data is crucial in many cases. When detecting fraud or
rare diseases, just to mention two ﬁelds, it is important that the results from a
data analysis are trustworthy and precise. Therefore it is important to have the
optimal tools for working with the data. There are several critical point of risks
in data analysis, one being imbalanced data sets.
One of the problems regarding imbalanced data is the paradox of accuracy: Hav-
ing a data set where 99% of the data consists of the negative class (e.g. non-
fraudulent customers) would lead to an accuracy of 0.99 if an algorithm classify
all data as non-fraudulent, ignoring the actual problem of being able to detect
the fraud.
One way of dealing with imbalanced data is to modify or tweak the classiﬁer
used, such that it is able to adapt to the imbalanced data e.g [6] describes how to
tune a neural network by introducing a new loss-function and [4] describes how
to create one meta-classiﬁer by stacking three classiﬁers.
Another method which have been used when dealing with imbalanced data is
simply to make it less imbalanced, this can be done by either oversampling [2],
undersampling [10] or combining them.
==PAGE:18==
4
Introduction
When researching this ﬁeld, we found that the majority of the literature have a
speciﬁc pattern in common; most of them focus on one classiﬁer, which makes it
hard to decide a classiﬁer of choice, since there might be no comparison between
other classiﬁers. Furthermore having to read and understand several diﬀerent pa-
pers regarding tuning diﬀerent classiﬁers can be very time consuming, and since
there, to our knowledge, not is one classiﬁer or re-sampling technique which has
been proven superior when dealing with imbalanced data, we try to solve that
problem here.
With this thesis we aim to enlighten how three individual well-known classiﬁers,
Random Forest, Neural Network and Logistic Regression, behave on increasingly
imbalanced data and how they can be improved by tweaking them or/and by
applying re-sampling methods to increase their performance - more speciﬁc we
try to shed some light on
• How do Random Forest, Neural Network and Logistic Regression behave
on diﬀerent scales of imbalanced data set?
• Is it possible to tweak these classiﬁers to increase their performance on
imbalanced data?
• Is there a classiﬁer/re-sampling technique which generally is superior to the
other?

4.1
Random Forest
A Random Forest is a bagging algorithm where we train several decision trees on
diﬀerent subsets of the data in order to create one classiﬁer, based on majority
voting.

4.1.2
Creating a forest
A forest is a bagging of multiple trees [8]. To classify a new observation, x, we
pass it to all the trees and the prediction is a majority voting amongst the trees.
When we grow a tree, we ﬁrst pick a random subset of size N of the training data
and in each split, instead of considering all k features, we only consider a random
subset of features - often the amount of features in the subsets are between 1 and
√
k, [8].
The reasoning behind creating a random forest is that a single decision tree
often has a low bias but a high variance ([8], [3]) and by averaging the trees we
can reduce the variance while still keeping the bias low.

This chapter describes how we can modify (or use) the hyper-parameters for the
classiﬁers in Chapter 4 to, hopefully, enhance the performance on imbalanced
data sets.
When tweaking there are three scenarios to keep in mind:
1. False positives are worse than false negatives
2. False positives and false negatives are equally bad
3. False negatives are worse than false positives
If one is dealing with fraud detection it is often worse for the company, e.g a
bank, not detecting a fraudulent transaction than ﬂagging a non-fraudulent as
fraud, and then discover that it was a valid transaction (scenario 3).
On the other hand, a judge in a court might have it the other way round: it
is better letting a person go, who actually has committed a crime rather than
sentence an innocent man to jail (scenario 1).

The optimal point on a ROC-curve for a classiﬁer, f, is the upper-left corner.

The same goes for all other fractions, thus the line tpr = fpr corresponds to f
making random guesses.

9.4
Neural Network
The network is created in Python using the torch module (also known as pytorch).
Several diﬀerent depths and lengths of the network was tried with inspiration from
[5] and [6] ending up using a two-layer network with length of (200,50) in the ﬁrst
and second layer, respectively.
Several drop-out rates were tried ending up with a drop-out rate of 0.5 in all
of the layers. It was also increased to 0.75, 0.8 and 0.9, which did not have a
signiﬁcantly (positive) eﬀect.
As activation function we used the ReLU function, deﬁned as
ReLU(x) = max(0, x), for the hidden layers and the sigmoid-function (4.15) for
the output layer.
The number of epochs, the amount of which the entire training set is passed
through the network, is set to 100 and for each test we create two networks: one
using the cross-entropy as loss-function and one using the MSFE.
9.4.1
Ensuring samples from each class in each batch
A problem when using the MSFE is that we need observations from each class in
every batch. This problem also arises if we want to calculate the training AUC.
To overcome this we split the training set x into two sets x1 and x0 containing
the positive and negative samples, respectively. We then calculate how many of
each samples, from each class, there has to be in each batch and create a batch
xbatch by concatenate the required observations from x0 and x1.
The last batch might end up having fewer observations than the other batches,
since it is created from the remaining observations in x0 and x1.

To answer the three questions in the introduction we look at

2. How often is each re-sampling superior?
3. Is re-sampling always superior to not re-sample?
This is done by the following:
1) How often is each classiﬁer superior to the other
We count how many times each classiﬁer has been the best (based on the AUC),
across each data set for each number of positives i.e we start by looking at the
number of positives, NP = 45, for the BC data set, ﬁnd the classiﬁer which
produces the highest AUC and increment that classiﬁers counter by one. We
here only keep track of the classiﬁer so say Random Forest(RF)+SMOTE has
produced the highest AUC for BC with NP = 45, we increment the RF counter
for BC by one. If multiple classiﬁers produce the same AUC, they are both in-
cremented by one.

Chapter 12
Discussion
The main goal(s) for this thesis was to investigate how three well-known clas-
siﬁers behave when applied to imbalanced data set, how they can be combined
with re-sampling methods and be tuned to increase their performance. Also we
wanted to test if we could ﬁnd patterns in the classiﬁers/re-sampling on increas-
ingly imbalanced data.
12.1
Classiﬁers
We tested three classiﬁers: Random Forest, Neural Network and Logistic Regres-
sion.
The Random Forest was clearly the worst performing classiﬁer, as seen in Fig-
ure 11.1. One of the reasons that Random Forest might perform poorly on the
original data (i.e with no re-sampling) is, as brieﬂy mentioned in the theory, the
splitting criteria. Having very imbalanced data (or even worse: imbalanced and
few data points) really works against the splitting criteria when we use e.g gini
impurity, or any measure in general which compares fractions of the data. One
==PAGE:66==
52
Discussion
way to overcome this could be a weighted impurity measure when calculating the
splits.
Another problem with the Random Forest is that when we down sample we,
naturally, reduce the amount of data points, which can lead to zero-impurity
leafs very fast, e.g having just one point from each class would lead to two zero-
impurity leafs in the ﬁrst split.
We tried to vary the bias/variance trade-oﬀby testing diﬀerent numbers of trees
in the forest and maximum number of features in each split. The depth of the
tree naturally also might have an eﬀect, since very deep trees would have a low
bias but most likely a high variance, making it a solid choice to include in further
work.
Generally the Neural Network performed quite well. As the theory in [6] pro-
posed we did see an improvement in performance when using the MSFE as
a loss-function, compared to the cross-entropy, when the proportion of posi-
tive/negative was low (Figure B.6 and B.11). On the other hand when we started
re-sampling the cross-entropy starts performing just as well and some times bet-
ter than MSFE.
One of the things which might have decreased the performance of the network is
the fact that the structure of the networks were exactly the same, and it might
be that diﬀerent loss-functions or imbalance ratios beneﬁt diﬀerently regarding
the depth, length, number of epochs, learning rate, weight decay etc. Due to the
extensive parameter grid-space, we ended up using one structure which (gener-
ally) worked well, which leaves room for further investigation.
The Logistic Regression turned out to be a really good choice as well. One thing
we did not tune in the Logistic Regression is the feature space meaning we did
not perform any transformation of x. Having a good domain-knowledge makes
it possible to choose the correct functions, φ, and since we used φ(x) = x this
could be a subject for further optimization. Furthermore increasing the number
of diﬀerent shrinkage values to be considered, could potentially lead to a better
ﬁt.
==PAGE:67==
12.2 Re-sampling
53
12.2
Re-sampling
SMOTE generally did a really good job in the BC data sets. In Figure 11.1b we
see that SMOTE produced the highest AUC for all fractions in BC and BC20. We
do, on the other hand, also see ROS beating SMOTE when looking at NP = 2 for
the SUSY data E.1. The reason could be, that since SMOTE linear interpolates
two points, we do end up with all the points being on a straight line, when we
only have two points. This is, probably, not close to the true distribution. Also
we applied SMOTE to the input-space; possibly it would have a better eﬀect ap-
plying SMOTE in the feature-space since points that are close in the input-space
might be far away in the feature-space.
The Random Oversampling did a better job than expected. The expectation
was that it would perform poorly since we have rather few instances from the
minority class. When looking at Figure 11.5, we see that the classiﬁers beneﬁt
more from using ROS than SMOTE when using the Neural Network.
Since ROS does not create new samples, but only adds the same sample again,
we are certain that the samples have the correct label. The SMOTE can end
up producing false positives, which clearly makes it diﬃcult for any classiﬁer to
generalize.
The CNN clearly did a inadequate job - often it made the performance of the
classiﬁers worse.
With the same argument as for the SMOTE, CNN might perform better when
it is applied in the feature space. Since we applied CNN to the input-space, it
might remove points which should not have been removed.
Since a decision tree does not create a decision boundary as the Logistic Re-
gression, but creates "boxes" with "sides" orthogonal to the features, it was not
expected that it would beneﬁt greatly from the CNN, especially not when we re-
sample from both of the classes. If we just re-sample from the majority class, it
could be a better down sampling method than Random Undersampling, since one
would assume that we do not risk losing as much information as with Random
Undersampling.
The Random Undersampling was a "passive" method. Generally it did neither
worse nor (signiﬁcantly) better. However, we do see that it is the re-sampling
==PAGE:68==
54
Discussion
method which gave the biggest beneﬁt for the SUSY data with NP = 2 when using
Neural Network with cross-entropy and combined with MSFE as loss-function it
yielded the highest AUC.
12.3
General remarks
It is worth noticing that the results shown in e.g Figure 11.1 are created once.
To provide more robust results, since some of the classiﬁers/re-sampling relies on
randomness, running the tests several times and average them could be a solution
to consider.
Furthermore, each classiﬁer has probably been sub-optimal, i.e they are tuned
to what time, computational power and the implementation allowed. Being able
to run the tests several times on completely optimized classiﬁers, could lead to
more general and robust results.

Chapter 13
Conclusion
We have classiﬁed four diﬀerent data sets. Three rather small with increasingly
imbalanced data and one large. We used the three classiﬁers: Random Forest,
Neural Network (with two diﬀerent loss-functions) and Logistic Regression. The
classiﬁers were combined with diﬀerent over-/undersampling techniques aiming
to improve their performances.
From the results there did not seem to be a clear pattern on when each classiﬁer
performs better.
On the other hand the Logistic Regression and Neural Network seem generally to
be a good choice, no matter the positive/negative ratio or the increasing noise.
The Random Forest performed worst of the classiﬁers and will not be the recom-
mended choice when dealing with imbalanced data, if the splitting criteria is not
modiﬁed speciﬁc for imbalanced data.
Regarding the re-sampling, SMOTE was the optimal re-sampling technique for
all numbers of positives in two of the smaller data sets. SMOTE does seem to
be, generally, a good choice. When we compare the beneﬁt of using re-sampling
to not re-sample (Figure 11.2, 11.3, 11.4) we do see that no classiﬁer beneﬁts
greatly from SMOTE, thus if computational power is a very important factor,
one might as well consider not re-sampling and just optimize the classiﬁer.
When we look at the CNN it often dramatically reduced the performance of the
==PAGE:70==
56
Conclusion
classiﬁers.
The Logistic Regression was the best classiﬁer 3/4 times in the large data set and
also performed very well on the smaller, thus seems to be a very robust choice
(at least for a benchmark).
From the experiments and results it seems like that the best classiﬁer/re-sampling
for imbalanced data sets most likely depend on the data, on that much on the
classiﬁer. With that in mind, simply choosing the personally most well-known
classiﬁer, might be the best approach to cope with imbalanced data.
