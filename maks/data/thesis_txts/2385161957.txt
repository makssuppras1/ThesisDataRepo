Abstract
The reactivity and activity of two Sn-substituted T-sites in Sn-beta (Sn-sites) have been
investigated in terms of the adsorption strength, the height of the reaction barrier, and a
microkinetic model. This was carried out for both the closed and the open conﬁguration
of both Sn-sites. The Meerwein-Ponndo↵-Verley-Oppenauer (MPVO) redox reaction of
acetone and isopropyl alcohol (i-PrOH) was used as the probe reaction for the deter-
mination of the reactivity. The two Sn-sites were selected based on their stability, thus
the most stable and unstable Sn-sites were chosen, which were the T5 and T8 positioned
Sn-sites (the T5 and T8 sites), respectively. The open sites were generated via hydrolysis
of the closed sites in which the T5 site was more stabilised by the hydrolysis than the
T8 site. The adsorption energies and reaction barriers for the MPVO reaction showed
that there was an insigniﬁcant di↵erence in the reactivity of the two Sn-sites. This was
furthermore emphasised by the reaction rates obtained from the microkinetic model in
which it was concluded that the reaction rates did not di↵er signiﬁcantly. The reactivity
of the closed sites was also investigated in which it was concluded that the closed Sn-sites
could not participate in the MPVO reaction within the obtained conﬁgurations in this
thesis.

Problem formulation
The interest in developing zeolites with Lewis acid characters has been growing in the past
decades. Consequently, di↵erent zeolite structures like Mordenite Framework Inverted
(MFI) and Beta (*BEA) have been used as framework for zeotype catalysts containing
Ti, Zr and Sn. Especially, the interest in incorporating Sn into these structures has gained
a lot of attention in the last couple of years due to its usage within biomass conversion.
As a result, zeotype catalysts such as TS-1 and Sn-beta have been developed.
Sn-beta is a highly relevant catalyst within biomass conversion. It is capable of catalysing
the Baeyer-Villiger (BV) reaction, the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley Oppenauer (MPVO)
redox reaction and the isomerisation of monosaccharides with a higher yield and selectivity
than any of the other zeotype catalysts. Especially, the fact that Sn-beta is able to convert
biomass derivatives from C5 and C6 compounds makes it highly relevant. This selective
and very active zeotype catalyst has been in focus within the research ﬁeld due to the
interest in the location of the active sites and the e↵ect of the Lewis acidity.
Using quantum mechanics, it is possible to map the reaction mechanism for a given
reaction. Therefore, in order to understand the reactivity of Sn-beta, a study is conducted
in which dispersion and steric e↵ects are taken into account properly.
In this thesis,
7
==PAGE:8==
the MPVO reaction between isopropyl alcohol (i-PrOH) and acetone is used as a probe
reaction to determine the catalytic reactivity of Sn-beta using density functional theory
(DFT) methods. Correction for the e↵ect of dispersion is included in the calculations
using the BEEF-vdW functional and the steric aspects are covered by accounting for the
entire unit cell of Sn-beta. The reactivity of Sn-beta is determined by monitoring the
MPVO reaction between i-PrOH and acetone. Based on the obtained electronic energies,
the activation barrier for each of the reaction steps is calculated.
By comparing the
di↵erent energy barriers for each Sn-site, the di↵erence within the reactivity of the nine
di↵erent Sn-substituted possible active sites can be identiﬁed if present.
Based on the energies obtained from the DFT study, an analysis of the activities can be
obtained using statistical mechanics and generating a micro kinetic model. Therefore, the
main questions of this thesis are if the reactivity and activity di↵er between the di↵erent
Sn-sites within the Sn-beta framework and if so, which Sn-site is then the most reactive
and active.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Today’s modern society is heavily dependent on fossil fuels such as crude oil, coal, and
natural gasses. They are used for a wide range of chemicals and fuels [1]. About 85 %
of crude oil is used for the production of transportation fuels and 10 % is used for the
production of industrial chemicals [2]. The sources, however, are not sustainable and
therefore alternatives are needed.
Most of the transportation fuels can be replaced by alternative, non-carbon rich sources
such as fuel cells, solar, water, and wind power [1]. But for the production of industrial
chemicals, a carbon source is required and for this crude oil is presently utilised. Biomass
is currently the only alternative non-fossil carbon source.
Biomass is all biologically
produced matter e.g. plants [2, 1]. In contrast to fossil fuels, biomass is renewable [1].
Unlike fossil sources, it can be produced in most parts of the world, whereas fossil fuels
can be found in only limited areas [2].
Compared to the aforementioned alternative
energy sources (wind power, etc.) biomass is a carbon source [1]. One of the considerable
challenges for chemists today, however, is to convert biomass into useful chemicals for the
industry in an economical way.
The renewable carbon resources in biomass are carbohydrates such as starch, cellulose,
sucrose, and hemi-cellulose. From the raw biomass the intermediate building platforms;
ethanol, glycerol, xylose, fructose, and glucose can be extracted [2].
These can then
furthermore be transformed into renewable carbon building blocks C2, C3, C4, C5, and
C6. A full description of the di↵erent applications can be found in the report by US
Department of Energy, 2004 [3].
The conversion of biomass building platforms should be done in an energy eﬃcient way.
1
==PAGE:14==
The transformation of these into renewables is of great interest to obtain a sustainable
future. Often a catalyst is used to make the reaction more eﬃcient. A catalyst is known
to be able to lower the required activation energy of a reaction leading to a faster re-
action rate. Di↵erent types of catalysts have been developed for biomass conversion in
which especially catalysts with a Lewis acidic character have shown great potential within
biomass [2]. One group of catalysts which have gained special attention during the past
decades is zeotype catalysts, which have a Lewis acidic active site.
A zeotype catalyst is a modiﬁed zeolite [4]. Zeolites are Brønsted acids and they are
made up by di↵erent well-known building blocks. The structure can di↵er from zeolite to
zeolite, and even the Brønsted acidic character can be changed into a Lewis acidic. If the
acidity is changed, the zeolite is called a zeotype catalyst [4, 5]. Di↵erent structures like
Mordenite Framework Inverted (MFI) and beta (*BEA) have been used as frameworks
for zeotype catalysts containing e.g. Ti, Zr and Sn. The interest in incorporating Sn into
these structures has gained a lot of attention in the last couple of years. As a result,
zeotype catalysts such as TS-1 and Sn-beta have been developed.
The relevancy of Sn-beta is based on its ability to convert biomass building platforms
such as fructose and glucose into C5 and C6 building blocks. This is done by catalysing
the Baeyer-Villiger (BV) reaction, the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley Oppenauer (MPVO)
reaction [6], and the isomerisation of monosaccharides. The reactions are performed with
higher yield and selectivity using Sn-beta than for any other catalyst. The interest of
the location and the di↵erences of the active sites within this selective and very active
zeotype catalyst have gained much attention the past years [13]-[39].
To outline these di↵erences, the reactivity and activity of Sn-beta should be investigated.
The reactivity of a catalyst can be described by the adsorption energies and reaction
barriers of a reaction, whereas the activity can be described by reaction kinetics [7, 8].
Both measures can be determined using quantum mechanics such as density functional
theory (DFT). The reactivity can be determined by mapping the reaction mechanism for
a probe reaction and using statistical mechanics, transition state theory, and the ﬁndings
from the DFT calculations, it is possible to determine the activity of the di↵erent sites
within Sn-beta.
In this thesis, a study of the reactivity and activity is conducted in which dispersion and
steric e↵ects are taken into account. Dispersion correction is included in the calculations
using the BEEF-vdW functional. The steric e↵ects are attended to using the unit cell of
Sn-beta. The MPVO reaction between isopropyl alcohol (i-PrOH) and acetone is used

as the probe reaction. The main objectives for this thesis are to determine the reactivity
and activity of two chosen Sn-substituted T-sites using DFT to determine the reaction
barriers and adsorption energies. In addition, the binding behaviour and Lewis acidity
of the two sites are also to be determined since it has been suggested that fructose and
gluctose either binds bi- og tridentat. The reaction barriers and adsorption energies can
be calculated from the electronic energies of the reactants, intermediates, products, and
transition states for each step in the reaction. By a comparison of the energy barriers
for each Sn-site, it will be possible to identify a di↵erence within the reactivity of the
di↵erent Sn-substituted T-sites. Thus, the most reactive site can be determined. The
activation energies of the transition states can be used to determine rate constants for
the elementary reaction steps. Using the di↵erence in energy between the intermediate
steps, the equilibrium constants can be calculated. As a probe reaction, the Meerwein-
Ponndorf-Verley-Oppenauer redox reaction has been used.
To give a proper exposition of the reactivity and activity of Sn-beta, the following subjects
will be covered.
• Chapter 2 - Sn-beta gives a short introduction to zeolites in general, which is given
as a basis for a more thorough exposition of Sn-beta and its properties. Previous
and current ﬁndings by other research groups will also be presented in this chapter

• Chapter 3 - Methodology describes the basic theory and methods behind the
DFT calculations carried out in this thesis. It also includes the method behind the

• Chapter 4 - Structures of Sn-beta is about the studies made before the deter-

• Chapter 5 - Reactivity of Sn-beta outlines all the results found in this thesis.
This covers adsorption of the probe molecules on both the ’closed’ and the ’open’ Sn-
sites an the determination of the activation energies required for each of the reaction
steps for the MPVO reaction, as explained in Section 2.4. This will culminate in a
representation of the full reaction path at the end of this chapter.
• Chapter 6 - Catalytic Activity of Sn-beta is based on the results found in
Chapter 5. In this Chapter, the activity of Sn-beta is determined using statistical

Chapter 2
Sn-beta
In the 1950s, zeolites became industrially important [9]. Zeolites are solid catalysts, which
have been widely used in the industry - especially in the ﬁeld of reﬁnery [7] and for the
conversion of gases such as methanol to formaldehyde. E.g. in the reﬁnery industry, the
zeolite is used to remove SOX and NOX [9].
The framework structures of zeolites are crystalline with a three-dimensional polyanionic
network consisting of periodic building units (PBU) such as SiO4 and AlO4 and secondary
building units (SBU) such as ring structures. Si and Al are both tetrahedrally coordinated,
see Figure 2.1. The hydrated aluminosilicates are linked through tetrahedrally bonded
oxygen-atoms, which form the porous structure with regular channels and interlinked
voids [9], thus, creating a large surface area.

Consequently, the diameter of the
channels is quite small and found in the micropore range, which is less than 7 ˚A. By
repeating the SBUs the periodic structure of zeolites is obtained [9, 11], see Figure 2.2.
The well-deﬁned pore dimensions allow selectivity based on shape and size of reactants
entering and products leaving the zeolite. Therefore, zeolites are also called molecular
sieves [4, 12].

As explained earlier, the active site was found within the channels of the zeolite. The

Zeolites are solid acid-base catalysts [4], which is why one the most interesting properties
of zeolites are that they can act as a very strong Brønsted acid [12]. The strong acid can
be obtained by exchanging the cation Si4+ with Al3+, whereupon a net charge of -1 is
achieved. This leads to the fact that positive ions, such as H+, can be used to neutralise
the charge. As a result, an active site is generated.
The Brønsted acidity is one of the characteristics of zeolites, however, by substituting
the aluminium-atoms with atoms such as Sn, Ti, or Zr, Lewis acidity is obtained. These
types of zeolites are called zeotype catalysts. Taramasso et al. [18] was able to substitute
a small amount of aluminium in the MFI structure with Ti-atoms. This zeotype catalyst
was named titanium silicate-1 (TS-1). The interest in this zeotype catalyst grew due
to the Lewis acidic character of the active sites, which were active in the epoxidation of
oleﬁns and alcohols to aldehydes and ketones, reactions that can be used in the production
of e.g. Nylon-6 [4].
TS-1 can catalyse the Baeyer-Villiger (BV) and Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley-Oppenauer
(MPVO) redox reactions. Both reactions are important within biomass conversion [4]. In
the BV reaction a ketone is converted into an ester using hydrogen peroxide H2O2 [19]
and the MPVO reaction involves the oxidation of alcohols and the reduction of aldehydes
and ketones [6, 20]. The narrow channels in TS-1 showed reactant selectivity towards
small substrates. In 1993, the group of Corma [17] introduced Ti-beta, a zeotype catalyst
with larger channels than TS-1. Even though the channels only di↵ered by ⇠1 ˚A, it had
a large inﬂuence on the reactivity and selectivity compared to TS-1 [4, 17].
The interest in developing zeolites with Lewis acidic character has been growing ever since
the discovery of TS-1. Therefore, di↵erent zeolite structures like MFI and *BEA have
been used as the framework for zeotype catalysts containing Ti, Zr and Sn. Especially,
interest in incorporating Sn in these structures has gained great attention in the last
couple of years.
To create an eﬃcient catalyst, the group of Corma [19, 21, 22] showed that by synthesising
an Al-free Sn-beta, a very active and selective catalyst could be obtained. The catalyst
was able to catalyse the same reactions as Ti-beta. That being the BV, the MPVO redox

of Sn-beta.
2.1
Nine crystallographically di↵erent T-sites
Corma et al. [19] was able to incorporate 1.6 wt% tetrahedrally coordinated Sn into the
*BEA framework, which was conﬁrmed by 119Sn Magic-Angle-Spinning (MAS) NMR [19]
and later utilising Extended X-ray Adsorption Fine Structure (EXAFS) by the group of
Bare [14]. Thus, Sn is in oxidation state four and tetrahedrally coordinated to oxygen
atoms [14, 15, 19].
It is well-established that *BEA has nine crystallographically di↵erent positions, which
are named T-sites.
It is possible to substitute Sn-atoms into all of these T-sites and
hence Sn-beta is obtained. This entails that the environment surrounding the Sn-atoms
at di↵erent T-sites di↵ers from each other [4, 15, 24, 23], which means that they have
di↵erent electronic properties, site accessibilities, and possibly di↵erent reactivity and
activity [23]. The Sn-substituted T-site will in rest of this thesis be denoted Sn-site.
The unit cell of Sn-beta includes 192 atoms from which 64 are silicon, 128 are oxygens,
and one is Sn-atom [25]. The size of the unit cell is 12.6 ˚A x 12.6 ˚A x 26.6 ˚A within a
tetragonal lattice system [26], see Figure 2.5.
The nine T-sites can be divided into three main groups, viz group I, group II, and group
III. Group I includes T1, T2, T3, and T4.
These sites occupy a vertex of two four-
membered rings.
Group II, however, consists of T5 and T6, which are linked to the
vertex of a four- and a ﬁve-membered ring. Lastly, the members of group III are T7, T8,
and T9, which share ﬁve- and six-membered rings [14].

2.2
Lewis acidity
Sn-beta is able to catalyse redox reactions such as BV and MPVO due to its Lewis acidity.
As a result, the catalytic activity of the Sn-beta zeolite is proportional to its Lewis acidity,
which is characterised as the ability to accept electrons. The strength is, therefore, related
to the energy di↵erence and the degree of overlap between the lowest unoccupied molecular
orbital (LUMO) of the Lewis acid and the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of
the Lewis base. If the LUMO is very stable the T-site is much more acidic. The LUMO
in Sn-beta is a linear combination of orbitals, thus obtaining σ*Sn-O. The σ*Sn-Os are the
acting orbitals. When an acid-base adduct is formed, the orbital is contributes to the
electron density, which leads to a lengthening in the four Sn-O bonds. By lengthening the
Sn-O bonds, the framework gains ﬂexibility, which also contributes to the stabilisation of
LUMO [27]. Thus, a strong Lewis acid is obtained.
To test whether or not Lewis acid sites are present in Sn-beta, probe molecules such as
pyridine have been adsorbed and desorbed at 150 ◦C whilst monitoring the process using
IR spectroscopy [28]. If a Lewis acid is present in the *BEA framework, an IR band will
appear at 1452 cm−1 when pyridine is desorbed from the Sn-site. The group of Corma [28]
saw a clear band at 1452 cm−1, and concluded that Lewis acids are present in Sn-beta.

properties will be elucidated

2.3. THE ACTIVE SITE
2.3
The active site
The active site is where the Sn-atom is substituted. A grown interest in the precise location
and the electronic environment of the active site of Sn-beta has evolved due to its e↵ect
on the catalytic activity [14]. Therefore, many approaches toward ﬁnding a correlation
between the activity and structure have been carried out. The structural environment
around each Sn-atom has been investigated using Fourier transformed infrared (FTIR)
[29, 30, 31], ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) [32], and NMR spectroscopy [23, 21]. These have
often been combined with the use of DFT calculations [23, 15].
The group of Corma has conducted several studies on Sn-beta [19, 22, 28]. In one study,
the group investigated the ability of Sn-beta to polarise a carbonyl group. For the BV
reaction, the group found that Sn-beta was able to polarise the keto-group instead of the
H2O2 or any other functional group present [19]. Thus, Sn-beta was selective towards
the keto-group. It was proven that Sn was better in polarising the carbonyl group than
Ti [22], which was attributed to the higher electronegativity of Sn. These ﬁndings raised
a lot of questions, which led to another study performed by Corma et al. [22]. In this
study it was proven that Sn-beta was still catalytic active when water was present in
the reaction media. Sn-beta is a hydrophobic zeolite due to the silicious cavities in the
framework [33]. This means that it has a low water adsorption and does not deactivate
due to the water. In fact, Sn-beta gave a higher turn-over number (TON) [7] compared to
Ti-beta, which means that it was able to run more cycles than Ti-beta before deactivating
[28]. Based on these observations, Boronat et al. [30] performed DFT studies on Sn-beta
using cluster models. The group claimed that the ﬁndings of Corma et al. [19, 22, 28]
could be explained by the fact that the Sn-site in Sn-beta was partially hydrolysed [30].
The Sn in Sn-beta is tetrahedrally bound to four oxygen atoms (Sn(OSi)4) and it has
four ’gaps’ opposite to the oxygen atoms [15]. By partially hydrolysing the Sn-site in Sn-
beta during calcination of the zeolite, one of the Sn-O-Si bonds is broken and hydrolysed
to obtain a hydroxyl-group, i.e. Sn(OSi)3OH is formed. This leads to an increase in
the ﬂexibility of the framework and the Lewis acidity of the site is increased [30, 27].
Therefore, several studies on both hydrolysed and unhydrolysed sites have been performed.
The names of these two sites are ’open’ and ’closed’ site, respectively, illustrated in Figure
2.6.

Methodology
The reaction mechanism of Sn-beta has been investigated using multiple spectroscopic
methods. A conclusion on the reactivity and activity has not yet been reached. Beside
experimental attempts, quantum mechanical methods have also been used to search for
a possible reaction path. The reason for doing these calculations is to obtain the energy
of the system of interest. To do so the Schr¨odinger equation, given in Equation 3.1, must
be solved.
ˆH n = En n
(3.1)
In Equation 3.1, ˆH is the Hamiltonian, En is the n-th energy of the system, which is given
as an eigenvalue, and  n is the wave function. The Hamiltonian describes the system’s
kinetic energy and potential energy.
The latter stems from repulsive nucleus-nucleus,
electron-electron and attractive nuclei-electron interaction [42]. Until now, it has only
been possible to analytically solve the Sch¨odinger equation for very simple systems con-
taining one electron, e.g. the hydrogen-atom and the H+

the Schr¨odinger equation is currently impossible to solve exactly for complex systems e.g.
those considered in this thesis given the current tools e.g. Turing-based computers [43].
Therefore, approximations have to be made.
One of the most integrated approximations is the Born-Oppenheimer approximation,
which allows a separation of the Schr¨odinger equation into two individual parts describ-
ing the nuclei and the electrons, respectively [44]. This allows to solve the electronic
Schr¨odinger equation for a given nuclear conﬁguration:

3.1. EXCHANGE-CORRELATION FUNCTIONALS
The exchange-correlation term, ˜EXC[⇢], is made up of the exchange-correlation term,
EXC[⇢], consisting of the terms explained earlier, and the kinetic energy di↵erence between
the interacting, T[⇢], and non-interacting system, Tni[⇢]. It should be noted that the
exchange term is in fact not changed due to the introduction of the non-interacting system,
consequently, it is only the correlation term, which is a↵ected.
If ˜EXC[⇢] was known the Kohn-Sham equation would lead to an exact energy of the
system. Thus, the Schr¨odinger equation would be solved exactly. The interactions of
the electrons with an external ﬁeld, Vext, and the Hartree term, VHartree, are both known
quantities. The kinetic energy and the exchange term, EX, are both known for the non-
interacting system. The remaining correlation term including correlation from the kinetic
energy, ˜EC, is the unknown quantity. EX can be determined exactly using the Kohn-Sham
orbitals. The correlation term, ˜EC however, cannot be determined exactly. If EX and
˜EC are determined at the same level of accuracy, then EX will be able to compensate for
the error introduced by the approximation of ˜EC. This is why most exchange-correlation
functionals use approximations to determine both terms [42]. The approximations are
only presented when the unknown functional for the exchange-correlation term has to
be determined. The goal of DFT is to determine a functional that connect the electron
density with the corresponding ground-state energy [46].
Therefore, in the following
section di↵erent approaches towards the determination of the exchange-correlation term
will be presented.
3.1
Exchange-correlation Functionals
In this thesis, the exchange-correlation functional Bayesian error estimation functional
with van der Waals correlation (BEEF-vdW) has been used. Two di↵erent approaches
towards the approximation of the EXC term will be presented very shortly, namely the
Local-Density Approximation (LDA) and Generalised-Gradient Approximation (GGA).
In LDA, which is the lowest level of theory with respect to Jacob’s Ladder, it is assumed
that the density can be seen as a uniform electron gas locally [42, 46]. This means that each
point in space is treated as a homogeneous gas whereupon a summation of the individual
contributions are made. This functional, even though very crude assumptions are made,
works very well for solid metals, but lacks of performance with respect to molecules [45].
LDA does not correctly describe bond energies nor Van der Waals forces, which are crucial
for understanding a zeolite system such as Sn-beta [47]. LDA was, therefore, not chosen
as functional for the calculations carried out in this thesis.

GGA is a signiﬁcant improvement over LDA. In this method, the gradient of the density,
r⇢(r), is also taken in account for the exchange-correlation energy, EXC[r, r⇢(r)] [47].
By doing so, inhomogeneities of the actual electron density is accounted for [45]. Some of
the well-known GGA functionals are PW91 [48] and PBE [49] just to mention a few [47].
The methods presented in this section are only two types of functionals. Other more
challenging, accurate, and computational expensive functionals have also been developed.
There are two main types of functionals. One which is constrained to fulﬁl the physical
laws and one which is semi-empirical. When choosing a functional for the calculations
to be carried out, one has to analyse the system of interest to see which functional will
describe it most accurately compared to computational cost and time. The functional,
BEEF-vdW, used in this thesis is a semi-empirical functional accounting for van der Waals
forces. The functional originates from GGA and it has shown to describe accurately long-
ranging dispersion forces in zeolites [50].

3.2
Practical DFT
The solution of the Kohn-Sham equations is done in an iterative process. An initial guess
of the electron density is given as an input to calculate the e↵ective potential, vR, which is
then used to solve the single-particle Equation 3.5. As an output, a new electron density,
⇢, is obtained. This process is continued until convergence [54]. An illustration of the
process is given in Figure 3.1. If the initial guess of the electron density is far from the
actual density, the process will be prolonged due to many iterations. Di↵erent codes have
been developed to carry out this process. In this thesis the code of grid-based projector
augmented wave (GPAW) method is used. The method was developed by Mortensen
et al. [55] in 2005 and reviewed by Enkovaara et al. in 2010 [56]. The PAW method
is a generalisation of the pseudopotential and augmented-plane-wave methods [57]. A
thorough description of these two methods can be found in the book of Jorge Kohano↵
[42], but they are beyond the scope of this thesis. In the following, the main points of the
PAW method will be given.

3.3
Transition state search
The reaction between reactants to form products always proceeds through one or several
transition states. A transition state is a high-energy intermediate conﬁguration between
the reactants and products, and it is assumed that it is located at a saddle point within
the minimum energy path (MEP). The location can be a rather diﬃcult task to determine
[44] and di↵erent methods can be used. In this thesis, two methods have been applied.
The ﬁrst method is the Nudged Elastic Band (NEB) and the second is the method of
ﬁxed bond length.
The latter is a rather simple method to determine the transition state, and it should only
be used in cases in which the transition state is simple. E.g. the geometrical conﬁguration
should only involve a breakage and/or formation of one bond length. Starting from the
geometry of the reactants, the bond length between the participating atoms are varied.
Each time the bond length is varied by the length, L, the structure is optimised while
ﬁxing the new bond length. The optimised structure is subsequently used upon variation
of the bond length once a again. The structure is subsequently optimised while ﬁxing the
bond length. The procedure is carried out until the product of the reaction is obtained.
An advantage of the method is that the calculation time is signiﬁcantly lower compared
to the NEB method. One considerable pitfall, however, is that it is very easy to slight
the actual transition state of the reaction, thus, not following the MEP [60].

3.4. MICROKINETIC MODELLING
A method to avoid this kind of error has been developed to ensure that the a saddle point
is determined. The method will be explained very brieﬂy. An initial and ﬁnal state of
a reaction are used to construct the MEP in which set of images are generated between
the two states with equal spacing. The images holds the positions of the atoms. To
ensure that the structures do not relax into the stable initial and ﬁnal state, a spring is
introduced between the initial and ﬁnal state. The spring adds a force to an atom, which
is done by comparing the position of the same atom for the previous and next structure.
A small modiﬁcation has been applied to the NEB method, which is the ’climbing image’.
It ensures that the image closest to the saddle point reaches for the maximum energy.
Using the NEB method along with the method of climbing image, the true saddle point
for the reaction is determined [61].

In the study conducted by Ivanova et al. [35], it was determined that a strong Lewis
acid would be hexa-coordinated, whereas a weak Lewis acid would be penta-coordinated.
Since the single and co-adsorptions indicated that the T5 site was a strong Lewis acid, it
was expected based on the study by Ivanova that it would prefer to have six ligands. The
signiﬁcant di↵erence between the reaction barriers for the penta- and hexa-coordinated
T5 site may therefore be addressed to the fulﬁlment of the urge to be six coordinated. It
would therefore also be interesting to investigate if a change in the coordination of the T8
site, thus going from six to ﬁve ligands since the T8 site was a weaker Lewis acid than the
T5 site, it could have a correspondingly inﬂuence on the reactivity of the hydride shift.
In the investigation of the deprotonation of i-PrOH, it was seen that the ﬁnal state for
the hexa-coordinated T5 site was higher in energy than the T8 and penta-coordinated
T5 site. This meant that this conﬁguration was more unstable than for the two other
conﬁgurations. It could therefore be argued if the most stable conﬁguration of the hexa-
coordinated T5 site was obtained after the deprotonation. If the most stable conﬁguration
was not obtained, this would explain the signiﬁcant di↵erence in the reaction barrier for
the hydride shift.

explain some part of the energy di↵erence between this thesis and the study conducted
by Boronat et al.. It would therefore be interesting to conduct a study using the same

and i-PrOH individually. The initial single adsorptions for the closed Sn-sites showed
that the T5 and T8 sites had a preference towards acetone and i-PrOH, respectively.
This could be explained by the fact that the T5 site was located on the rim of one
12-membered ring, whereas the T8 site was positioned in the interface between two 12-
membered ring structures. Therefore, it could be argued that the di↵erence in adsorbate
preference could be seen within the di↵erent electronic structures surrounding the two
Sn-sites. The closed T8 site was more opened and exposed due to its location, which
indicated that the ﬂexibility was higher for this Sn-site compared to the closed T5 site.
In Chapter 2, the Lewis acidity was deﬁned as the ﬂexibility of the Sn-site, which meant
that the closed T8 site eventually would be a stronger Lewis acid than the closed T5 site.
This meant that the preference at the T8 site towards the stronger Lewis base, i-PrOH,
was in agreement with the strength of the Lewis acidity of the Sn-site. The ﬂexibility
of the T8 site resulted in a larger overlap between the LUMO of the Sn atom and the
HOMO of i-PrOH. This justiﬁed that the closed T8 site was a stronger Lewis acid than
the closed T5 site, which was the opposite for the open site.
Furthermore, the single adsorptions showed that the adsorption strength of the acetone
and i-PrOH at the closed T8 site did not get weaker than the single adsorptions on the
open site, whereas the adsorption strength on the closed T5 site changed signiﬁcantly. This
indicated that the Lewis acidity of the T8 site did not get stronger due to the hydrolysis
of the site, which was expected since this have been reported by several research groups
[15, 23, 24, 27]. The Lewis acidity did, however change at the T5 site in which the site
became a stronger Lewis acid due to hydrolysis. This change in the Lewis acidity of the
T5 site could be explained by the gain of ﬂexibility, which was not seen at the T8 site.
For the co-adsorptions on the closed Sn-sites, it was observed that neither the closed T5
nor T8 sites were able to accommodate both probe molecules at the same time. The probe
molecule with the weakest adsorption in the single adsorption calculations was, therefore,
repelled from the Sn-site. At the T5 site, the atomic environment was one of the limiting
factors for the co-adsorption since the T5 site only had one side at which acetone and i-
PrOH could be adsorbed, whereas T8 had two exposed sites in which acetone and i-PrOH
could adsorb on each site separately. The inﬂexibility of the surroundings was the main
reason for the absent co-adsorption for both Sn-sites. The geometry of the Sn-sites was
not able to change during the co-adsorption, which meant that an energetically favourable
geometry was not obtainable for the two sites. According to Boronat et al. [24], the initial
step in the reaction mechanism for the MPVO reaction was the co-adsorption of the probe
molecules. Thus, without changing the geometry of the closed Sn-site, this would not be

possible for this conﬁguration. The lack of co-adsorption for the closed Sn-site furthermore
indicated that the reactivity of these sites were less than for the open sites.
Therefore, to obtain a more ﬂexible geometry, the adjacent oxygens were protonated
during the deprotonation of i-PrOH in which T8 was chosen as the probe site. When the
hydroxyl group on Si was pointing towards the Sn-site, acetone preferred to adsorb on
the hydroxyl group except for O2 in which acetone was repelled from the Sn-site. This
could be explained by the electronic structure surrounding the Sn atom. Even though
the adjacent oxygens were protonated by the alcohol, the position of the Sn atom did not
change, which meant that the location of the orbitals did not change either. Therefore,
it was not possible for the Sn-site to obtain another geometry such as the octahedral.
The adjacent SiOH ensured that trigonal bipyramidal geometry was maintained and the
inﬂexibility of the Sn-site was retained when only i-PrOH was adsorbed. This meant
that the MPVO reaction would not take place on the closed site as long as the SiOH was
pointing towards the Sn atom, since the carbonyl group had to be polarised by the Sn
atom for it to undergo the MPVO reaction [24].
The adjacent hydroxyl group was, therefore, moved to the opposite site of the Si atom.
This was carried out for O2 and O3, which showed that for O2 it was possible to co-
adsorb the probe molecules, which was energetically unfavourable, and that O3 acetone
was repelled from the Sn-site. The fact that acetone was repelled from the Sn-site when
O3 was protonated and that it was possible to adsorb acetone and i-PrOH simultaneously
for the protonation of O2 indicated that the reason for the unsuccessful co-adsorptions
was not due to steric e↵ects, but geometric e↵ects. When O3 and O2 were protonated
a tetrahedral and trigonal bipyramidal geometry were obtained, respectively. This could
indicate that the Sn-site had obtained some ﬂexibility when protonating O3, thus pushing
the Sn atom into the 12-membered ring. The protonation of O2 did, however, not allow
for any ﬂexibility, which meant that the Sn-site was not able to move in the direction of
O2 to obtain the more stable tetrahedral geometry. Thus, the MPVO reaction did not
take place at the closed Sn-sites.

Since the MPVO reaction did not proceed in Sn-beta at 100◦C, an analysis of how much
the temperature had to be increased for the reaction to take place. The investigation of
the required temperature showed that the reaction temperature had to be increased by
170◦C for the MPVO reaction to take place at both the T5 site with these conﬁgurations.
Furthermore, it was seen that the height of the reaction barrier had an importance for
the reaction rate, which was this was also investigated. For the MPVO reaction to take
place at 100◦C, the reaction barrier for the hydride shift had to be reduced by 41 % for
the T5 sites, respectively. This showed that either the temperature had to be increased
signiﬁcantly or that the reaction barrier should be reduced. A way to reduce the reaction
barrier could be by using a better catalyst for the MPVO reaction between acetone and
i-PrOH.
Normally, the MPVO reaction takes place in a liquid phase, but this was not possible
to model within this thesis. Therefore, the reaction was modelled in gas phase, which
meant that the probe molecules were stabilised signiﬁcantly when migrating from outside
to inside the zeolite, resulting in large barriers for this migration. For the liquid phase
MPVO reaction, it is not expected that such a stabilisation of the system will take place
since the probe molecules already will be stabilised by the liquid medium.
The error caused by this crude simpliﬁcation was, however, negligible since the barrier for
the migration in and out of the zeolite did not inﬂuence the reaction rates. By reducing
the barrier for the migration in and out of the zeolite by 1.6 eV, the reaction rate was
unchanged. In fact, the size of the barrier would not inﬂuence the reaction rate unless the
energy of the probe molecules outside the zeolite got below the energy of the co-adsorption.
Therefore, the model in gas phase is assumed to be a valid for this study.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
From the investigation of the hydrolysis of the Sn-sites, it was concluded that the open
Sn-sites were more stable than the closed sites. The T5 and T8 sites were lowered by
-0.53 eV and -0.31 eV, respectively by the hydrolysis. But when the energy of the closed
site with H2O adsorbed and the open site were compared there was a clear di↵erence
between the two Sn-sites. The T5 site was equally as stable as the closed site with H2O
adsorbed while the T8 site was higher in energy. This meant that the T8 site was diﬃcult
to hydrolyse. It was therefore concluded that the hydrolysis could take place at both
Sn-sites.
The single and co-adsorptions indicated that there was a di↵erence in the Lewis acidity
of the T5 and T8 site, which was furthermore emphasised by the single adsorptions on
the closed sites. This was seen in the adsorption strengths and adsorbate preferences.
Both the closed and open T8 sites preferred i-PrOH. The closed T5 site preferred to
have acetone adsorbed, whereas the open T5 site preferred i-PrOH. This indicated that
the T5 site did change in Lewis acidity due to the hydrolysis, whereas the T8 site did
not. Furthermore, the adsorption strength of i-PrOH on the open T5 site was in general
signiﬁcantly stronger than the ones seen for the T8 site. This combined with the previous
observation showed that the acidity of the T5 site had changed from a weaker to a stronger
Lewis acid. This resulted in an observed di↵erence in the reactivity of the two Sn-sites
with respect to the adsorption strengths. The strength of the co-adsorption at the T8 and
penta- and hexa-coordinated T5 site was -1.62 eV, -1.77 eV, and -1.74 eV, respectively.
Thus, it was concluded that the T5 site was the strongest Lewis acid and the most reactive
Sn-site.
The co-adsorptions also showed that both the T5 and T8 sites were able to coordinate
89
==PAGE:102==
six ligands, but for the T5 site, it was found that the site could penta-coordinate as well.
Both the penta- and hexa-coordinated Sn-sites were equally stable, which meant that
both conﬁgurations were possible. The T8 site was only found in a hexa-coordinated con-
ﬁguration. These ﬁndings were crucial for the biomass conversion of fructose or glucose,
since it was expected that these two molecules would bind either bi- or tridendate.
From the study of the transition states, the reaction barriers for the deprotonation of
i-PrOH, the hydride shift, and the deprotonation of H2O showed that there was not a
signiﬁcant di↵erence between the two Sn-sites. The reaction barriers for the T8 and penta-
and hexa-coordinated T5 site for the ﬁrst reaction was 0.62 eV, 0.63 eV, and 0.65 eV,
respectively. Thus, there was no di↵erence in the reactivity.
For the hydride shift a di↵erence in the reaction barrier was observed in which the reaction
barriers were 1.39 eV, 1.33 eV, and 1.05 eV for the T8, penta-, and hexa-coordinated T5
site, respectively. The di↵erence in the reaction barrier for the T8 and penta-coordinated
T5 site was explained by the uncertainty within DFT calculations, whereas the latter
was explained by the higher energy of the initial state for the hydride shift. The more
unstable conﬁguration could be due to the hexa-coordinated Sn-site or that the most
stable conﬁguration was not obtained.
The reaction barriers for the deprotonation of H2O were also signiﬁcantly di↵erent. The
barrier for the T8 site was only 0.07 eV whereas it was 0.47 eV for the T5 site. This
was explained by the movement of H2O from underneath the Sn-site to i-PrO⇤−, which
resulted in a large reaction barrier. Thus, the Sn-sites di↵ered in reactivity with respect to
this transition state. In general, it was concluded that a di↵erence in reactivity between
the T5 and T8 site was only seen for the adsorption strengths. It was therefore concluded
that the only di↵erence in reactivity was seen for the co-adsorption of the two probe
molecules.
From the study of the closed site, it was seen that it was diﬃcult to co-adsorb the two
probe molecules, which meant that the closed site was not as reactive with respect to the
co-adsorptions as the open site. Furthermore, it was also found that the closed site was
not able to catalyse the deprotonation of the alcohol. It was, therefore concluded that
the closed site was not active in the MPVO reaction for the conﬁgurations presented in
this thesis.
From the investigation of the activities, it was seen that reaction rates of 1.64·10−9 s−1
and 2.06·10−10 s−1 were obtained for the MPVO reaction between acetone and i-PrOH at
100◦C at the T8 and penta-coordinated T5 site, respectively. Thus, the two reaction rates
90 of 157

resembled each other, which was in correspondence with the energy diagram obtained from
the reactivity study. The low reaction rates led to the conclusion that the MPVO reaction
determined in this thesis would not occur under reaction conditions. The analysis of the
temperature showed that the it should be increased with 170◦C for the MPVO reaction
to take place within these conﬁgurations. Furthermore, the investigation of the reduction
of the reaction barrier for the hydride shift led to the conclusion that the reaction barrier
should be reduced by 41 % for the MPVO reaction to take place at 100◦C.

Outlook
For the future work, it would be interesting to investigate the electronic environment
surrounding the other seven Sn-sites using the MPVO reaction between acetone and i-
PrOH. This would be interesting since T1, T3, T3, T4, T6, T7, and T8 did not di↵er
signiﬁcantly in energy, but the electronic environment could have an inﬂuence on the
reactivity even though this was not seen for the ones investigated in this thesis. By using
the same reaction as in this thesis, the reactivity and activity of these Sn-sites could be
obtained and compared directly.
In this thesis only the open and exposed hydrolysed Sn-sites were used for the investiga-
tion, however, it would be useful to determine if the non-exposed Sn-sites were more or
less reactive than the T5 and T8 sites. In this way, it could be established if the Sn-site
had to change geometry to catalyse e.g. the MPVO reaction.
The reaction mechanism of the MPVO reaction in Sn-beta was presented by Boronat
et al. in which the reaction between 2-butanol and cyclohexanone was used. It would,
therefore, be interesting to carry out the same reaction using PBCs and BEEF-vdW, and
in this way see if similar results would be obtained for the reaction barriers. Furthermore,
it would also be interesting since it is possible to experimentally determine the reaction
rates for this reaction and thus it could be combined with the theoretical work.
The ability of the Sn-site to have both ﬁve and six ligands indicated that it would be able
catalyse the MPVO reaction for either fructose or glucose. Therefore, for the future work
it would be of high value to determine if the Sn-site can accommodate the sugars.
An investigation of a further hydrolysis of the Sn-site should be carried out to see if a
more stable conﬁguration could be obtained. And furthermore to see if these hydrolysed
93
==PAGE:106==
Sn-compounds would catalyse e.g. the MPVO reaction.
In this thesis, it was seen that the T5 site was able to coordinate with both ﬁve and
six ligands. Ivanova et al. [35] suggested that a strong Lewis acid was found in a hexa-
coordinated complex and weak in a penta-coordinated complex. In this thesis, it was
concluded that the T5 site was a stronger Lewis acid than the T8 site. Therefore since
the reaction barrier for the hydride shift at the hexa-coordinated T5 site was signiﬁcantly
smaller than the one obtained for the penta-coordinated T5 site, an investigation of a
penta-coordinated T8 site would be interesting.

B.1
Convergence test
In this section, all preliminary studies will be presented. These studies include a conver-
gence test of the grid-spacing, h, the optimisation of the geometry of Sn-beta with Sn
substituted at each of the nine di↵erent T-sites. The convergence test was carried out
to determine the most optimal grid-spacing for the adsorption calculations. Using the
optimal grid-spacing from the convergence test, the geometry optimisation of the nine
crystallographically di↵erent Sn-sites will be calculated to be able to select three repre-
sentative Sn-sites for further investigation. From an investigation of the adsorption of
H2O on the three Sn-sites, it was possible to reduce the number of Sn-sites necessary to
investigate further from three to two.
In Section 3.2.1, the theory behind PAW was introduced in which the wave functions
were represented on a uniform grid where the grid-spacing was the spacing between the
grid-points. Hence, if a ﬁner grid-spacing is applied, the grid resolution is higher. As a
consequence, the applied Central Processing Unit (CPU) time is raised. Therefore when
choosing the grid-spacing, it should be a compromise between precission and CPU time
[66].
The convergence test was performed for the adsorption of i-PrOH on Sn-beta in which
the T-site, T8, was substituted with Sn. T8 was chosen arbitrarily for this purpose. To
determine the optimal grid-spacing, the adsorption energies of i-PrOH in Sn-beta were
calculated for grid-spacings from 0.14 ˚A to 0.22 ˚A. Therefore, three di↵erent calculations
had to be carried out for each of the grid-spacings: one for the optimisation of pure Sn-
beta, a second for i-PrOH in gas phase, and a third for the adsorption of i-PrOH on T8,
see Equation B.1.
Eads = Eoptimised −(ESn−beta + Emolecule)
(B.1)
Eads was the adsorption energy of i-PrOH on T8 in Sn-beta. Eoptimised was the energy of
the optimised structure of Sn-beta with i-PrOH adsorbed, ESn−beta was the energy of pure
Sn-beta, and Emolecule was the energy of i-PrOH in gas phase. The optimisation of i-PrOH
was conducted without PBCs. The results from the convergence test are summarised in
Table B.1 in which it can be seen that the adsorption energies vary when changing the
grid-spacing. The variation in the adsorption energies were, however, not large. The
variations did not show a clear trend. Therefore, based on a comparison between the
quality, CPU time, and the limited time within this thesis, a grid-spacing equal to 0.20
˚A was chosen.

one of the criteria for the MPVO reaction to proceed was that the double bond in the
carbonyl group in acetone had to be polarised by Sn, which was only possible when the
carbonyl group was directly coordinated to the Sn atom, see Section 2.4. Based on these
observations, this type of adsorption was not used for the further investigations.

Appendix H
Reactivity of the Closed site
The MPVO reaction between i-PrOH and acetone was as presented earlier chosen as the
probe reaction for this thesis. The MPVO reaction includes a deprotonation of i-PrOH,
a hydride shift between i-PrOH and acetone, and lastly a hydrogen transfer from the
produced water molecule to the newly formed alkoxide. To be able to model the reaction
between the two molecules, a prior investigation of the behaviour of i-PrOH and acetone
on the Sn-site was performed. Initially, single adsorptions of the two probe molecules on
the closed T5 and T8 were performed. Based on these, the co-adsorption of i-PrOH and
acetone on the two Sn-sites were made.
H.1
Single and co-adsorptions of probe molecules on T5
The adsorption energies of acetone and i-PrOH were determined for the closed T5. This
was done by placing both molecules separately on the Sn-site whereupon an optimisation
of the structure was performed.
The T5 positioned Sn-site was placed in the rim of one 12-membered ring. Therefore, the
oxygen atom in acetone was adsorbed on the centre of the Sn atom. The optimised bond
distance between Sn and the oxygen atom was 2.56 ˚A. For i-PrOH, the bond distance
between Sn and the oxygen in the alcohol was 2.64 ˚A. Using Equation B.1, the adsorption
energies were calculated. For acetone, the adsorption energy was -0.83 eV, whereas the
adsorption energy of i-PrOH was -0.77 eV. Thus, the calculations indicated a preference
towards acetone. In Table H.1, all bond lengths and adsorption energies are listed.
Based on these initial calculations, it was decided to co-adsorb acetone and i-PrOH on the

H.2
Single and co-adsorption of probe molecules on T8
In the preliminary studies, it was explained how T8 was placed in the interface between
two 12-membered ring structures, see Figure 4.4. This meant the Sn-site had two freely
exposed sites at which H2O could adsorb. This was still applicable for the adsorption of the
two probe molecules, i-PrOH and acetone. Therefore, four calculations were performed to
determine the adsorption energies of acetone and i-PrOH. A strong adsorption indicated
that the molecule had a preference with respect to the two freely exposed sites of Sn,
namely the up- and down-site.
The ﬁrst calculation was the adsorption of acetone on the down-site of the Sn atom. The
calculation showed that acetone was placed inside one of the 12-membered ring structure.
The optimal structure was obtain with a bond length of 2.53 ˚A between the Sn-site and
acetone. No sterical overlaps were seen, which indicated that the size of the 12-membered
ring was large enough to accommodate acetone. The optimised structure is shown in
Figure H.2 a. The adsorption energy was -0.75 eV, which meant that the adsorption of
acetone on T8 inside the ring structure had a stabilising e↵ect.
For the adsorption of acetone on the up-site, no sterical hindrance was observed and the
molecule was easily ﬁtted into the ring structure of Sn-beta. The optimised structure
showed that the bond length of Sn-OAc was 2.46 ˚A with an adsorption energy of -0.62 eV.
