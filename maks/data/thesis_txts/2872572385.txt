Abstract
Intron retention (IR) is an increasingly recognized form of alternative splicing that can di-
versify the transcriptome and, when translated, alter the proteome. This thesis investigates IR
in the context of neurodegenerative diseases, Parkinson’s disease (PD), progressive supranu-
clear palsy (PSP), and multiple system atrophy (MSA), with a focus on identifying retained in-
trons that give rise to novel peptides. Using mass spectrometry data from postmortem brain tis-
sue, intron-derived peptides were identified and it was assessed whether these retained introns
could be distinguished from non-retained ones using only sequence-derived features. Two ma-
chine learning approaches were explored: a random forest (RF) model trained on engineered
features (e.g., GC content, k-mers, intron length), and a feed-forward neural network (FFNN)
trained directly on raw DNA sequences.
While both models performed modestly (AUC ≈0.57–0.59), they captured complementary
aspectsoftheIRsignal,andanensemblemodelslightlyimprovedpredictivepower. Intronposi-
tion and GC content emerged as the most informative features, whereas other expected signals,
such as intron length or polypyrimidine tract strength, showed limited predictive value. These
outcomes likely reflect substantial data limitations, including the small number of confirmed
translated IR events (n=241) and the absence of broader genomic and transcriptomic variation
data. The exclusive reliance on peptide-level evidence may obscure regulatory signals and con-
strain model performance.
Importantly, this study identified translated IR events in brain tissue that may generate
novel peptides, raising the possibility of disease-specific regulatory mechanisms or immune-
related responses in neurodegeneration. Overall, the findings highlight both the potential and
the current limitations of sequence-based IR prediction and emphasize the need for future in-
tegrative approaches that bring together splicing regulation, proteomic evidence, and disease
biology.

Introduction
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disease, affecting
more than six million people worldwide [1]. It is primarily characterized by motor symptoms
such as tremors, rigidity, and impaired movement and coordination [2]. PD is caused by the
degeneration of nerve cells in certain parts of the brain, most notably the substansia nigra. A
pathological hallmark is the formation α-synuclein aggreagates in dopamine neurons, forming
Lewy bodies. In addition to neurodegeneration, PD has also been associated with autoimmune
processes, in which the body mistakenly identifies its own cells or tissues as foreign and attacks
them. Evidence suggests that the production of antibodies against neuronal proteins can con-
tribute to chronic inflammation and progressive loss of dopamine-producing neurons [1].
Multiple system autrophy is a rarer and more agressive disorder that is part of a family
known as atypical parkinsonian disorders, sharing many of the same symptoms as PD [3]. Like
PD, MSA involves neurodegeneration and abnormal protein aggregation, but with some key dif-
ferences. In MSA, α-synuclein accumulates in glial cells rather than neurons, and degeneration
affects multiple brain regions, including the basal ganglia, cerebellum, and brainstem, disrupt-
ing both motor -and autonomic functions [4]. Progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP), another
atypical parkinsonian disorder, shares overlapping symptoms with PD and MSA but is distin-
guished by the aggregation of tau protein, rather than α-synuclein, in affected brain cells. This
tauopathy contributes to neurodegeneration and distinct clinical symptoms, such as early pos-
tural instability and gaze palsy [5].
The brain regions examined in this study each play distinct roles in neural function and are
differentially affected across neurodegenerative disorders. In this study, five different brain re-
gions are included, namely the substantia nigra (SN), putamen (PUT), frontal cortex (FC), cere-
bellum (CBX) and medulla (MED). The substantia nigra (SN) is central to motor control and is
particularly susceptible to degeneration in PD and MSA [6]. The putamen also supports motor
function and learning, and is disrupted in both PD and MSA [7]. The cerebellum helps coordi-
nate balance and precise movement, and damage here, common in MSA and PSP, can lead to
unsteady gait and lack of motor coordination [8]. The frontal cortex, especially its prefrontal
regions, is important for thinking, planning, and social behavior, and is often affected in PSP
and MSA, contributing to cognitive changes [9]. Finally, the medulla oblongata helps control
vital autonomic functions like breathing and blood pressure, and its degeneration in MSA may
underlie symptoms of dysautonomia [10].

1.1
Alternative splicing and the splicosome
Alternative splicing, particularly intron retention (IR), is increasingly recognized as a reg-
ulated mechanism that expands transcriptomic and proteomic diversity. Once dismissed as
splicing noise, IR has since been linked to gene regulation, novel protein isoform generation,
and disease processes. In the brain, where transcriptional plasticity is especially high, IR plays
a dynamic role in neuronal function and has been implicated in neurodegenerative diseases.
While most research has focused on cancer and Alzheimer’s disease, emerging evidence sug-
gests that IR may also contribute to the molecular pathology of Parkinson’s disease (PD), multi-
ple system atrophy (MSA), and progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP), highlighting the need for
further investigation in these contexts. [11, 12, 13]
1.1
Alternative splicing and the splicosome
Splicing is the process in which a pre-mRNA, transcribed from DNA, is edited by the spliceo-
some to remove non-coding regions known as introns and join together coding regions known
as exons, producing a contiguous mature mRNA transcript. The mature mRNA is then exported
from the nucleus and translated to a protein. However, splicing is a complex process that not
only excises introns and ligates exons but also regulates exon inclusion or skipping through al-
ternative splicing. Alternative splicing is therefore responsible for a lot of the transcriptome
complexity in mammals, where a single gene can create multiple different RNA transcripts, and
hence different protein isoforms. Studies estimate that 94% of human protein-coding genes are
alternatively spliced and that the central nervous system is one of the main sites of this [14].
Alternative splicing plays a critical role in various biological processes, including development,
differentiation, and response to environmental signals. Dysregulation of splicing can lead to a
range of different diseases, highlighting its essential role in maintaining normal cellular func-
tion and genome integrity [15]. The main types of alternative splicing are illustrated in Figure 1.

splice site, while U2 binds near the branch point upstream of the 3’ splice site. This recognition
sets the stage for recruitment of the U4/U6-U5 tri-snRNP complex, resulting in a catalytically ac-
tive spliceosome that performs two transesterification reactions to remove the intron and ligate
the flanking exons (Figure 2). [17, 18]

1.1.1
Alternative splicing in Neurodegenerative diseases
Alternative splicing plays a central role in the nervous system, shaping gene expression
during development, synapse formation, and cellular responses to external signals. In neurons,
where precise regulation and adaptability are crucial, alternative splicing generates a range of
distinct isoforms that are critical for processes like differentiation, synaptic function, and even
programmed cell death. The nervous system is a known "hotspot" for alternative splicing, and
manysplicesitesareevolutionarilyconserved, reflectingtheiressentialroleinmaintainingneu-
ronal identity and function. When these finely tuned splicing events are disrupted, the conse-
quences can be profound. It is estimated that 15–50% of human genetic diseases involve splic-
ing defects, and growing evidence links aberrant splicing to the onset and progression of neu-
rodegenerative diseases, where small changes in transcript variants can contribute to progres-
sive neuronal dysfunction. [22]
In Parkinson’s disease, the best characterized splicing target is the SNCA gene, which en-
codes α-synuclein, the main component of Lewy bodies. Alternative SNCA splicing produces
severalisoforms,includingSNCA140(full-length), SNCA112(lackingexon5), andSNCA126(lack-
ingexon3), eachwithdifferingaggregationproperties. TheregulationofSNCAsplicingisfurther
influenced by SNPs and microsatellite variants that affect enhancer elements and splicing mo-
tifs, contributing to altered isoform ratios in PD and MSA. Beyond SNCA, genes such as LRRK2
and PARK2 also exhibit disease-relevant splicing changes, including transcript variants with dis-

1.2
Intron retention
Intron retention (IR) refers to the process by which a full, unspliced intron remains within
thematuremessengerRNA(mRNA)transcript. Onceconsideredtranscriptionalnoiseorabyprod-
uct of incomplete splicing, IR has now emerged as a widespread and regulated form of alterna-
tive splicing of biological significance. Historically, IR was observed most frequently in plants
and viruses, where it was recognized as a functional component of gene expression regulation.
However, technological advancements, particularly in RNA sequencing, have revealed that IR is
common in mammalian systems as well. Recent large-scale studies suggest that IR affects more
than 80% of protein-coding genes in humans, highlighting its role in shaping transcriptomic
complexity [23]. Beyond simply expanding the transcript repertoire, IR contributes to gene ex-
pression control and can lead to the production of novel protein isoforms or degradation-prone
transcripts. [24, 25]
The occurrence of IR is influenced by a combination of intrinsic and extrinsic regulatory
factors. Intrinsic characteristics such as high GC-content, short length and weak splice sites are
features commonly associated with retained introns [26]. Extrinsic factors that might influence
retention include RNA binding proteins such as SR proteins or hnRNPs. These proteins might ei-
ther promote or inhibit intron excision. Moreover, RNA structure and epigenetic modifications
can influence the selection and accessibility of the splice sites, and the recognition of branch
points [25].
Figure 3 depicts the diverse fates of an intron-retained transcript. Many IR transcripts are
detained in the nucleus, where they may be spliced post-transcriptionally in response to spe-
cific stimuli. Others are exported to the cytoplasm and degraded via nonsense-mediated decay
(NMD) if the retained intron introduces a premature termination codon. Some IR transcripts,
however, escape NMD and are translated into novel protein isoforms, contributing to proteomic
diversity. This complexity is particularly evident in cell types with high transcriptional plastic-
ity, such as neurons, where IR has been implicated in rapid transcriptome remodeling during
synaptic activity. [24, 25]

1.2.1
Current Perspectives and Progress in Intron Retention Research
A central challenge in IR research has been its reliable detection. The low abundance of re-
tainedintronsandtheirsequencecomplexitymakethemdifficulttocapturewithstandardRNA-
seq pipelines. Short-read RNA sequencing remains the most widely used approach, although
long-read sequencing technologies such as PacBio and Oxford Nanopore offer improved res-
olution of full-length IR isoforms. Bioinformatic tools such as IRFinder and SUPPA2 can detect
and quantify IR events, but analyzes must account for biases in GC content, repetitive elements,
and RNA degradation [23]. Experimentally, IR can be validated using RT-PCR or polysome pro-
filing, and proteomics approaches such as mass spectrometry can confirm whether IR isoforms
are translated. However, detecting peptides from IR transcripts remains technically challenging
and relatively rare [24].
A study by Liu et al. [3] combined RNA-Seq with mass spectrometry (SWATH-MS) to deter-
minehowIRaffectstheproteome. Theyshowedthattranscriptswithretainedintronsaregener-
ally associated with decreased protein abundance, suggesting that IR predominantly functions
as a gene-silencing mechanism or a form of regulated unproductive splicing and translation
(RUST). However, under certain conditions, such retained introns may undergo delayed splic-
ing and contribute to novel protein isoforms.
IR has also been implicated in disease contexts. In cancer, studies have shown that in-
creased IR diversifies the transcriptome and disrupts tumor suppressor gene expression, and
can generate neoepitopes [25]. A review by Zhang et al. [18] described IR as one of several
alternative splicing modes altered in cancer, potentially producing aberrant proteins that fuel
oncogenesis. Insupportofthis, acomprehensivestudybyDvingeandBradley[27]revealedthat
widespread intron retention was common in both solid and liquid cancer types. Their analysis
of 805 matched tumor and normal samples from The Cancer Genome Atlas demonstrated that
many cancers have increased retention of both alternative and constitutive introns, indepen-
dent of direct mutations in the splicing machinery. Here, it was also found that retained in-
trons often were present in the cytoplasmic fraction of cancer cell lines, suggesting that intron-
containing mRNAs may contribute to the cancer proteome.
A more recent study by Li et al. explored IR in the context of Alzheimer’s disease by in-
tegrating RNA sequencing and mass spectrometry data from human and mouse brains. They
found widespread IR in the Alzheimer’s cortex and demonstrated that some of these transcripts
were indeed translated. Retained introns were particularly enriched in genes involved in RNA
metabolism, immune processes, and proteostasis. Notably, only a few IR events were found at

Although intron retention has been increasingly studied in cancer and Alzheimer’s disease,
itsroleinParkinson’sdiseaseremainslargelyunexplored. AnotableexceptionisastudybyKoks

focus on IR or its translation into the proteome. Thus, while evidence points to the presence of
widespread IR in human tissues, there remains a significant gap in understanding the role and
consequences of intron retention in Parkinson’s disease, as well as MSA and PSP. This under-
lines the importance of further research to characterize IR at the transcript and protein level in
PD and related neurodegenerative disorders.
This thesis aims to address this gap by studying intron retention in the brains of patients
with Parkinson’s disease, multiple system atrophy (MSA), and progressive supranuclear palsy
(PSP), using peptide evidence from mass spectrometry. By focusing on IR events that are not
only retained but also translated, this work aims to identify which peptides stem from intron
retention and investigate which sequence features may predict these events.

1.3.1
Random forest
Random forest is an ensemble method that creates multiple decision trees based on ran-
dom subsets of the data, and aggregates their results either through averaging (regression) or
majority voting (classification). RF employs two main sources of randomness: the selection of
data samples (via bootstrapping) and the random subset of features used for each tree split,
thereby promoting model diversity and reducing overfitting. Unlike single decision trees, which
may be highly sensitive to noise and prone to overfitting, RF mitigates these risks through ag-
gregation, resulting in more robust and generalizable models. Furthermore, RF models are non-
parametric, can handle mixed data types, and offer built-in mechanisms to assess feature im-
portance, making them particularly well-suited for complex classification tasks. [28, 29]
In this study, the key challenge lies in the severe class imbalance between retained introns

datasets [30]. This makes RF an ideal choice for this study, where the goal is to accurately detect
retained introns despite their rarity.
Random Forest has been widely used in bioinformatics due to its high predictive accuracy,
scalability, and robustness to noisy, high-dimensional data, which are traits commonly found in
biological datasets. It has been applied in various tasks such as gene expression classification,
disease gene prioritization, SNP interaction detection in GWAS, and protein function prediction.
In particular, RF is effective in cases where interpretability is crucial along with accuracy, as it
provides feature importance scores that help uncover biologically meaningful patterns. In ge-
nomicstudies, includinganalysesofalternativesplicingandintronretention, RFhasbeenlever-
aged to rank sequence features, model complex regulatory relationships, and integrate diverse
omics data. [29]
1.3.2
Feed Forward Neural Network
Artificial neural networks (ANN) are computational models inspired by the structure and
function of the human brain. Among the simplest and most widely used forms is the feedfor-
ward neural network (FNN), in which information flows in one direction, from input to output,
through layers of interconnected neurons. Each neuron performs a weighted sum of its inputs
followed by a non-linear activation function, enabling the model to learn complex relationships
in the data. FNNs are typically trained using labeled data, optimizing a loss function via gradient
descent to adjust weights and minimize prediction error. This architecture is particularly useful
for classification tasks, where each input is assigned to a predefined category based on learned
patterns. [31, 32]
In the context of this project, a feedforward neural network is employed to explore whether
raw DNA sequences can be directly used to classify sequence elements without prior feature
engineering. This approach simplifies the input representation and allows the model to au-
tonomously learn relevant features from the nucleotide sequence itself. While more complex
architectures like convolutional or recurrent neural networks are often used for sequence anal-
ysis, a feedforward design provides a minimal yet informative baseline for evaluating how much

information can be extracted from sequence composition alone. Jurtz et al. [33] demonstrated
that even simple dense neural networks can perform competitively on biological tasks, such
as predicting subcellular localization, when properly tuned and trained on suitable encodings,
like one-hot vectors. This highlights the relevance of FFNNs as an accessible starting point for
biological sequence classification.
1.4
Research objectives
The aim of this project is to predict whether an intron is likely to be retained based solely
on its DNA sequence or sequence-derived features. To address this, two main approaches were
explored: (1) training a random forest model on engineered features such as GC-content, intron
length, and k-mer frequencies, and (2) training a feed-forward neural network directly on raw
DNA sequences to assess whether sequence patterns alone can distinguish retained from non-
retained introns.
This analysis is performed in the context of neurodegenerative diseases, including Parkin-
son’s disease (PD), progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP), and multiple system atrophy (MSA),
using mass spectrometry data derived from postmortem brain samples. By linking retained
introns to detected peptides, the study aims to identify intron retention events that give rise
to novel protein products. These peptides may reflect disease-specific regulatory patterns or
serve as neoantigens, potentially contributing to the autoimmune responses observed in some
PD cases. Ultimately, this project integrates computational prediction with proteomic evidence
to explore intron retention as a functionally and clinically relevant mechanism in neurodegen-
eration.

2.3
Intron mapping
The mapping described in this section was performed in Python, and an overview of the
scripts used is given in Table B.12 in Appendix B.
Due to inconsistencies in the intron annotations from the original mass spectrometry data,
the initial phase of the project involved remapping all peptides to ensure accurate identification
of retained and non-retained introns for downstream analysis.
Remapping was performed using the same reference database of GenBank files described
in Section 2.2. The main issue observed in the original data was the incorrect labeling of pep-
tides as intron-derived, when in fact many matched exonic regions. To address this, all candi-
date intron-derived peptides were extracted by parsing the raw TSV output from Spectronaut,
keeping only entries flagged as potential intron matches. Each peptide sequence was then
cleaned by removing charge states and amino acid modifications, resulting in pure amino acid
sequences.
To determine whether a peptide could originate from a normally spliced coding region,
a reference coding sequence (CDS) database was created by parsing the GenBank files for the
2,909 blood -and brain-expressed genes. Each cleaned peptide was then compared to this CDS
database, and peptides matching a coding region were flagged and excluded as they could not
be considered intron-derived.
To detect peptides originating from retained introns, a custom translation database was
generated. For each GenBank entry, all three possible reading frames of the full nucleotide se-
quence were translated, producing one FASTA file per gene. Protein accessions were mapped
to their corresponding GenBank file names using the UniProt API [35], with manual corrections
applied as needed.
CandidatepeptidesthatdidnotaligntoanyCDSregionweresubsequentlysearchedagainst
the three-frame translations. For each cleaned peptide, an exact match was searched in the
translated FASTA file corresponding to its gene. When a match was found, its amino acid posi-
tionandreadingframewererecorded,andnucleotidecoordinateswerecomputedas: AA_position×
3+frame offset. Results were cached to avoid repeated peptide searches and the outputs were
separated into two files: one for peptides that successfully mapped to a translation and one for
those that did not.

2.3
Intron mapping
To ensure accurate region annotation, exon and intron boundaries from all GenBank files
were extracted using a custom script. This script was created to merge overlapping CDS features
across isoforms and defines introns as intervening regions. The resulting file contained sequen-
tial exon and intron coordinates per gene. This approach was necessary because GenBank an-
notations often include multiple isoforms with variable intron and exon counts. By treating all
annotated exons, including those not part of the final CDS, as true exons, the method prevents
peptides from being mistakenly classified as intronic when they are in fact exonic in alternative
isoforms.
Finally, each peptide was assigned to a region (exon or intron) by comparing its nucleotide
coordinates to the exon–intron boundaries. Peptides mapped exclusively to exon regions were
removed from the final dataset. Additionally, peptides that did not fully map to the three-frame
translations were manually reviewed. Those found to partially overlap with intronic regions
were manually added to the final data set.

where both models were correct, both were incorrect, or only one was correct. The resulting
p-value from McNemar’s test indicated whether the models’ performance differences were sta-
tistically significant.
To evaluate whether model complementarity could improve classification performance,
predictions from the previously trained random forest and feedforward neural network (FFNN)
ensembles were averaged to form a joint ensemble. The final probabilities for each sample in

3.1
Mapping
Peptide mapping revealed 13,727 peptides mapped to introns across all samples, repre-
senting 248 unique peptides that map to 241 distinct introns in 221 genes. This indicates that
some introns are covered by multiple peptides and some genes harbor more than one retained
intron when considering all samples together. In comparison, the original mapping from a pre-
vious student project identified 100,939 intron peptides [34], a substantial difference from the
current results. This highlights the importance of performing the updated mapping to ensure
that all introns classified as retained are supported by at least one peptide mapped directly to
the intronic sequence.
A small subset of peptides, eight in total, could not be assigned to either the annotated
coding sequences (CDS) or the full translated genome. Closer inspection revealed that these
peptides corresponded to partial matches, where different segments of the peptide could be
alignedtoseparateregionsofthegenome,butnotasacontinuousmatch. Outofthese,fivepep-
tides partially aligned to intronic regions and were therefore kept in the final intron-retention
dataset. These cases were particularly interesting, as one part of the peptide mapped to an in-

3.8
Branch point and Polypyrimidine tract scoring with BPP
To assess whether differences in splicing-related motifs might contribute to intron reten-
tion, we used the Branch Point Predictor (BPP) tool [41] to evaluate branch point strength and
polypyrimidine tract (PPT) quality. For each intron, BPP analyzes the last 100 bp upstream of
the 3’ splice site (or the full sequence if shorter), scoring candidate branch point motifs based
on a position weight matrix and scanning for downstream PPTs. Each intron receives a branch
point score (sc_bps), a PPT score (sc_ppt), and a combined score (sc), reflecting the likelihood
of proper splice site recognition. The tool also reports the predicted position of the optimal
branch point relative to the splice site.

4.1
Methodological Limitations and Biological Considerations in Detect-
ing Intron Retention
While this study presents novel insights into translated intron-retained peptides in human
brain tissue, several limitations of the methodology and dataset must be considered when in-
terpreting the results.
First,thepeptide-baseddetectionmethodusedherereliesonmappingmassspectrometry-
derived peptides back to a reference genome, meaning that cis-acting splicing mutations, such
as point mutations in canonical splice sites, branch points, splice enhancers, or silencers, can-
not be directly detected. These types of mutations, described in detail by Abramowicz and Gos,
can disrupt or create splice sites, activate cryptic splice sites, or interfere with exon recogni-
tion, leading to exon skipping or abnormal intron inclusion [48]. Because we are working from
peptide sequences rather than genomic or transcriptomic reads, only amino acid-altering mu-
tations may appear in the peptide data, and even then, such variants may lead to mismapping
or exclusion during the alignment step. As a result, this approach captures only a subset of the
molecular mechanisms underlying aberrant splicing.
Second, the current analysis treats intron retention as a binary event, either an intron is
fully retained or entirely spliced out. In reality, partial intron retention is biologically common
and often results from the use of alternative splice sites, which can include portions of an intron
or exclude portions of an exon. Such events may lead to transcripts that contain only fragments
of intronic sequence. Studies in various organisms have shown that fully retained introns rep-
resent only a subset of intron-related splicing events, with a proportion arising from alternative
3’ and 5’ splice site usage [21] ˙Because this analysis lacks transcript-level resolution, it cannot
capture these more nuanced forms of intron processing, potentially oversimplifying splicing
complexity.
Third, a major biological limitation is that most intron-retaining transcripts are not trans-
lated. IR transcripts are typically detained in the nucleus and degraded or processed post-trans-
criptionally before export. Only a minority of IR-containing transcripts escape the nucleus and
reach the ribosome for translation. Even when export occurs, transcripts containing prema-
ture stop codons due to retained introns are often recognized and targeted by the nonsense-
mediated decay (NMD) pathway before translation can take place. [21, 25, 24] As such, the
Page 57 of 70
==PAGE:65==
4.2
Intron retention mapping findings
dataset used here, derived from proteomic evidence, only captures the translated subset of IR
transcripts. While this aligns well with the study’s aim of exploring potential immune-relevant
peptides, it excludes a broader population of retained introns that may play important regula-
tory roles. Complementary transcriptomic data would allow for a more comprehensive view of
intron retention, including non-translated or detained transcripts.
In summary, while proteomics enables direct detection of translated IR peptides, it pro-
vides only a narrow window into the complex mechanisms of alternative splicing. Integrating
transcriptomic data, especially long-read sequencing, would improve our ability to distinguish
between full and partial intron retention, detect splicing-affecting mutations, and assess nu-
clearexportversustranscriptdegradation. Theseinsightswouldbevaluableforclarifyingwhich
splicing events are biologically meaningful and which may contribute to disease processes, par-
ticularly in the context of neurodegeneration.
4.2
Intron retention mapping findings
Several specific intron retention and alternative splicing events observed in this study may
hold important biological relevance for neurodegenerative disease pathology. Notably, a sub-
set of peptides matched canonical exon regions with only a single-nucleotide deviation from
thecodingsequence, suggestingpotentialpointmutations. Amongthese, threepeptidesexhib-
ited a Serine (S) to Arginine (R) substitution and were mapped to the proteins TBCD_HUMAN,
RIMS1_HUMAN, and ALS2_HUMAN - all of which are implicated in neuronal function and dis-
ease. Mutations in TBCD have been linked to neurodegenerative encephalopathy [49], while
ALS2 is associated with juvenile-onset neurodegenerative disorders such as amyotrophic lat-
eral sclerosis (ALS) and primary lateral sclerosis (PLS) [50]. RIMS1, a key component of synaptic
vesicleexocytosis, playsacriticalroleinsynapticsignaling[51]. Althoughthedetectedsubstitu-

relevant to neurodegeneration warrants further investigation.
In addition to point mutations, evidence of complex alternative splicing events was also
observed. In particular, one peptide was manually mapped and confirmed to span intron 2
and exon 5 of the SNCB gene, which encodes β-synuclein. This suggests a splicing event in
which exons 3 and 4 are skipped, resulting in a non-canonical transcript that retains an in-
tronic sequence. While the role of α-synuclein (SNCA) in Parkinson’s disease is well-established,
β-synuclein has been less extensively studied. Recent literature, however, indicates that β-
synuclein may act as a molecular chaperone that inhibits α-synuclein aggregation, potentially
having a protective effect [52]. Intron-retaining or alternatively spliced non-canonical variants

4.4
Gene length and exon number
In this study, we observed that genes containing retained introns tend to be longer and
have a greater number of exons compared to genes without intron retention (Section 3.3). This
isperhapsunsurprising, aslongergenesnaturallyharbormoreintrons, increasingthestatistical

association reflects a biologically meaningful pattern or is simply a byproduct of gene architec-
ture. Longer genes are also more prone to alternative splicing, which may further explain the
association with intron retention. This relationship has been well-documented in comparative
genomic studies, which suggest that genes with greater transcript length tend to generate more

4.5
Intron-Level Features Associated with Retention
This study examined several intron-specific properties such as length, GC content, and ge-
nomic position to identify potential sequence-level predictors of intron retention in protein iso-
forms. Together, the analyses provide a composed view into the structural features associated
with retained introns in translated transcripts.
Page 60 of 70
==PAGE:68==
4.5
Intron-Level Features Associated with Retention
Contrary to findings from several transcriptomics-based studies, we found no statistically
significant difference in intron length between retained and non-retained introns when control-
ling for gene-level context (Section 3.4). This result diverges from prior work, including Mon-
teuuis et al., who highlight that retained introns are generally shorter across a range of organ-
isms including vertebrates and plants [25]. Similar results were also observed by Li et al. in an
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) dataset, where retained introns were significantly shorter than non-
retained ones [12]. One possible explanation for this discrepancy is the data modality: while
most previous studies use RNA-seq and measure all retained introns regardless of their transla-
tion status, our approach only captures those that are translated into peptides. This could bias
our dataset toward longer retained introns, since longer introns may be more likely to contain
an open reading frame or produce stable peptides detectable via mass spectrometry.
In agreement with previous literature, we observed a small but statistically significant in-
crease in GC content in retained introns compared to non-retained ones. Similar results were
reported by both Monteuuis et al. and Li et al. in human and model organisms [25, 12]. One pos-
sible explanation is that higher GC content can lead to the formation of stable RNA structures,
which might make it harder for the spliceosome to recognize and remove the intron. Monteuuis
et al. also describe how GC-rich repeat regions near splice sites can promote intron retention
by interfering with the binding of trans-acting splicing factors [25]. The modest increase in GC
content we observed, especially around splice sites, might reflect a similar effect in our data.
We found that retained introns were slightly more common near the 3’ end of genes (Sec-
tion 3.5). This trend fits with what others have observed, that introns close to the transcript’s
end, especially the last few, are more likely to show up in translated proteins. Monteuuis et
al. also report a trend for retained introns to cluster toward the 3’ end of genes, particularly in
brain-specific transcripts [25]. One reason for this could be that introns near the 5’ end are more
likely to introduce early stop codons, which can trigger degradation of the transcript through
nonsense-mediated decay. In contrast, introns near the 3’ end are less likely to interfere with
translation, so they might be more frequently tolerated or selectively retained.
In summary, our findings align partially with previous studies, particularly regarding GC
content and positional bias. However, the lack of difference in intron length contrasts with ear-
lier work and may reflect the proteomics-based nature of our dataset. Together, these obser-
vations underscore the importance of considering both biological and technical context when
interpreting sequence features associated with intron retention.

To explore the sequence characteristics that may contribute to intron retention, we investi-

Our splice site analysis revealed that retained and non-retained introns differ more sub-

sites [25, 54], but no clear consensus exists on whether the 5’ or 3’ splice site is more predictive.
Our findings may reflect broader differences in local sequence context rather than changes to
the canonical GT-AG dinucleotides alone. Moreover, the position-by-position nature of DiffL-
ogo highlights specific base-level differences but may fail to capture more complex regulatory
motifs that are flexible in position. This suggests that complementary analyses such as motif
discovery or structural predictions, may be needed to identify distributed or shifting sequence
elements involved in intron retention.

While this study focused on key sequence features such as splice site composition, intron
entropy, and branch point signals, several other biologically relevant aspects could further en-

the binding of regulatory proteins like hnRNPs and SR proteins, has been linked to intron reten-

retained introns occur in compact “exon–intron–exon” units under 400 nt, potentially altering
the exon definition dynamics. [54]

with ROC AUCs ranging from 0.6617 to 0.7261 across validation folds. This considerable vari-
ability suggests that certain data subsets are inherently more challenging to classify, likely due
to biological heterogeneity in intron retention. In particular, the fact that different hyperpa-
rameter configurations were optimal for each fold implies that the discriminative features and
their distribution vary substantially across the dataset. These differences may stem from dis-
tinct sequence-level characteristics which influence model learning. Pooled predictions across
folds yielded a ROC AUC of 0.642, which dropped to 0.593 on the unseen hold-out set. This drop
isexpected, asthemodelsweretunedindependentlyforeachfoldandmayhaveoverfittothose
subsets. Applying a shared set of average hyperparameters across all folds might improve gen-
eralization and yield more stable performance.
Features
Feature-wise, absoluteintronposition, GC-content, andShannonentropywerecon-
sistently ranked as the most informative, reinforcing trends observed in the exploratory analy-
sis. In contrast, intron length showed minimal predictive value, also consistent with what was
observed from the feature analysis. Interestingly, the inclusion of 3-mer frequencies and branch
point predictions degraded performance, suggesting that expanding the feature set with noisy
or redundant variables can impair model effectiveness. The simpler feature set not only main-
tained performance but also reduced complexity and training time.
Sampling strategies
The evaluation of sampling strategies revealed that no method was uni-
versally optimal across all folds. However, balanced subsampling offered the most robust and
consistent performance. Synthetic oversamplers such as SMOTE and ADASYN underperformed
across folds, and in one case, simple undersampling of the majority class provided the best
results - implying that reducing the number of non-retained introns, rather than synthetically
inflating the minority class, can help the model focus on relevant patterns.

The two models differ fundamentally in how they represent and process the data. The RF

4.10
Final Remarks
Despite careful tuning and evaluation, both the RF and FFNN models demonstrated lim-
ited predictive performance. This is unlikely to be due to suboptimal model design but rather
reflects the inherent limitations of the available data. Most notably, the dataset included only
241 unique retained introns, which is a small number to train classifiers on, especially given the
complexity of DNA sequence input. The severe class imbalance, while biologically realistic, fur-
ther complicates classification by making it harder for models to learn discriminative features.
In addition, our approach lacks genomic and transcriptomic variation data, such as expression
levels, splicing factor activity, or chromatin state, all of which likely influence intron retention.
These omissions restrict the kinds of features we can extract and limit the model’s ability to cap-
ture the full biological context. While the methodology is appropriate for the aim of identifying
retainedintronswithtranslationalpotential, aricherandmorebalanceddatasetwouldlikelybe
necessary to substantially improve predictive performance. We acknowledge these constraints
and consider them key directions for future improvement.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to identify sequence-based features predictive of intron retention by
leveraging peptide-level evidence and machine learning models. The goal was to understand
which retained introns are not only present but also translated - providing insight into the sub-
set of intron-retaining transcripts that produce novel peptides, which could potentially act as
neoantigens and trigger immune responses. Although the methodology aligns with this trans-
lationalfocus, theabsenceofgenomicandtranscriptomicvariationdatalikelylimitedthesignal
we could capture, narrowing the scope of detectable predictive features.
Throughprecisepeptidemapping,wegainedinsightintowhichintron-retainingtranscripts
aretranslatedand, indirectly, whichproteinsmightbeaffected. Althoughthiswasnotaprimary
aim, it opens avenues for further investigation, such as gene set enrichment analysis, to explore
functional implications. Among the tested features, intron position and GC content emerged
as the most informative, supporting previous findings in the field. Conversely, some expected
differences, such as intron length or PPT signals, did not emerge as significant, likely due to lim-
itations in sample size and data completeness.
Both the random forest and feed-forward neural network models showed only modest pre-
dictive performance. This limited performance is unlikely to be solely due to model design or
tuning, and may instead reflect constraints in the dataset: the number of retained introns avail-
able for training was small (only 241 unique sequences), and the extreme class imbalance re-
flected biological reality but added further complexity to the task. With more data, particularly
more confirmed retained introns and access to broader genomic and transcriptomic features,
future models would likely have greater predictive power.
Ultimately, the challenges encountered in this project reflect a broader issue in the field:
intron retention remains a poorly understood and highly context-dependent phenomenon. It
is likely driven by a complex combination of sequence signals, splicing regulation, chromatin
structure, and cellular context, factors that were not fully captured in this study. Gaining a
deeper understanding will require integrative approaches that combine data from multiple lay-
ers of regulation, alongside larger and more diverse datasets. Despite its limitations, this work
contributes to the growing effort to make sense of which retained introns are translated and
why, and lays a foundation for more comprehensive modeling in the future.

Description
