Abstract
This thesis documents the design and development of a test setup for investigating defor-
mation of natural asperities on sheet metal surfaces under subsurface straining of which
the aim is to simplify the testing procedure compared to an existing setup. The test setup
is based on the principles of a Marciniak test with the addition of combined normal pres-
sure. A first iteration of the setup is manufactured.
The existing test setup with similar function is described and evaluated in terms of usabil-
ity. User needs and requirements of the new setup are identified based on discussions and
interviews with the user of the existing setup and presented in a design and development
chapter. A list of specifications and requirements are presented accompanied by additional
design considerations. The main problem is decomposed into subproblems to which solu-
tions are generated by brainstorming and discussions. Considering the solutions, selected
design proposals are presented on a system and subsystem level and based on discussion,
a system design and subsystem designs are chosen. Concluding the design phase the iter-
ative design on component level is presented for selected components. In parallel a plastic
finite element analysis software iForm2D is utilized to simulate the forming process from
which results are used to assist the design and development phase as well as dimensioning
of the active tool parts.
Selected components exposed to high loads are dimensioned using analytical methods dur-
ing the design phase. The final design is documented by a number of technical drawings
used for manufacturing of parts comprising the setup.
Using the commercial finite element analysis software Ansys, more complicated compo-
nents are validated while parts are manufactured. Proposals for design changes are made
where necessary. Simultaneous iForm2D is used to validate the final design.
Once all parts are manufactured the setup is assembled and evaluated in terms of design.
As proof of concept and to evaluate the functionality a number of experiments are per-
formed.
Lastly proposals for future work is given in terms of the possible research and in terms of
improvements and add-ons expanding the capabilities of the new test setup.

1 Introduction
Sheet metal forming is an essential part of modern production technology of which the
processes are numerous. The main objective for a successful sheet metal forming process
is clamping the sheet while forming to control local flow and stress conditions. It is desir-
able to retard the flow of material such that straining occurs in predefined areas without
causing instability and fracture. To control the process it is important to have a good
understanding of friction in the tool-sheet contact interface. The friction is highly depen-
dent on the surface topography and how it develops throughout the process. Thus a lot of
research is conducted in this area including at DTU where a new method for testing sheet
metal asperity flattening has been developed [1]. The setup deployed is primarily designed
to investigate flattening of model asperities during subsurface straining. To accommodate
sheets with model asperities the setup is large and bulky and thus unnecessarily large for
investigating natural asperities. Thus the metal forming group at DTU desire a new and
more compact setup for investigating the deformation of natural asperities during subsur-
face straining.
The new test method is based on a Marciniak test with applied normal loading. A conven-
tional Marciniak test, named after the inventor Z. Marciniak, is performed by stretching
two clamped sheets over a cylindrical punch [2]. Figure 1.1 displays the working principle
of the Marciniak test. The lower sheet with a central hole is called ’driving sheet’ and
the upper sheet is called ’test sheet’. The driving sheet is essential for the Marciniak test
as it prevents a circumferential fracture in the test sheet at the punch periphery which
would otherwise occur early in the process. Furthermore the material in the driving sheet
flow easy compared to the test sheet due to the hole. This helps localize the strain to the
central part of the test sheet due to friction between the sheets. This implies that high
friction between the sheets is desired and low friction in the interference of driving sheet
and punch.

An advantage of the Marciniak test is the continuously flat center of the test sheet during
stretching. Furthermore formability is not influenced by friction due to the unsupported
free center of the test sheet.
This make the Marciniak test common for determining
forming limit diagrams (FLD), also known as a forming limit curve (FLC), which graphi-
cally illustrate the instability boundary for a sheet material. Figure 1.2 display a generic
forming limit diagram with examples of linear strain paths; uniaxial tension, plane strain
and balanced biaxial stretching. By default the Marciniak test produce balanced biaxial
stretching, however by using test sheets with cutouts as seen in figure 1.3 the complete
range of strain ratios in the forming limit diagram is achievable.
This thesis presents the design and development of a new and more compact test setup
based on the Marciniak test principle enabling the metal forming group at DTU to test
deformation of natural asperities in various multi-axial strain states, ranging from uniaxial
tension to balanced biaxial stretching.

2 Project statement
The metal forming group at DTU desires a new and compact setup for testing asperity
flattening on natural surfaces, hence the problem statement:
’Design a new compact tool for asperity flattening under subsurface straining based on
the Marciniak test principle, which allows variations in the ratio between in-plane
strains. The goal is to manufacture an experimental setup and proof of concept.’
The aim of the project is developing a compact setup compatible with the servo presses
located in Building 427 at DTU Lyngby.
Manufacturing a one-off specialized test setup is time consuming and thus it is important to
start the manufacturing process early if the setup is to be assembled and tested within the
time frame of this project. To achieve this goal the design is based partly on fundamental
dimensioning and partly on previous experience building test setups.
While parts are
manufactured in the workshop the design is verified in more detail and suggestions for

3 Background
Modeling of friction in metal forming processes have always been of interest as it is of
large impact on material flow, tool life and surface quality of the final part. Theoretical
models are based on analysis of the real contact area in tool-workpiece interface during
forming processes. Asperity flattening have been studied and modeled since the 1940s and
continues to this day.
3.1
Previous modeling and testing
A brief description of the most important contributions to the topic of asperity flattening
and real contact area in sheet metal forming is presented below. A more detailed review
is available in [3].
• In 1942 Bowden and Tabor proposed a theory based on individual plastic deformation
of contacting asperities between two metal surfaces. It was observed that for large
values of normal pressure the well known linear Coulomb friction model was no
longer valid. This point was independently discovered by other researchers around
the same time.
• A year later in 1943 Orowan proposed using the shear flow stress as an upper limit.
• In 1959 Tabor proposed that relative motion between the surfaces result in further
flattening of asperities due to locally induced shear stresses emerging in the contact
regions, reducing the required yield pressure.
• In 1967 and 1968 Fogg conducted experiments with stretch forming and normal
pressure and found that constant normal pressure resulted in larger contact area
when stretching as previously described by Tabor. In Fogg’s experiments the applied
normal pressure did not result in full contact area, indicating the use of relatively
low normal pressure.
• In 1971 Kasuga proved that friction is proportional to the real contact area at both
high and low normal pressure.
• His discovery was supported by Wanheim et al. in 1974 and Bay and Wanheim in
1976.
• So far most research have been focused on dry friction where asperities can flatten
freely which is not the case if liquid lubrication is trapped in the contact interface.
The lubricant carries part of the load and thereby increase the pressure required to
flatten asperities. This effect was studied by Nellemann et al. in 1977.
Since then theoretical models have been refined and experimentally verified by many
independent researchers. The use of FEM have increased since the 1980s and play a large
role in the research done today.

The existence of this setup begs the question: Why design a new one? There are three
main reasons.
1. Size and weight. To accommodate sheets with model asperities the existing setup
is designed large making it bulky and heavy and thus unnecessarily large for testing
sheets with natural surfaces. For size reference see figure 3.2 displaying the sheet size.
2. Lack of control and data gathering. The press used with this setup is old and
manually operated, i.e. a handle is actuated for the press to move resulting in imprecise
motion both in terms of distance and velocity. Furthermore there are limited data
gathering during the process making analysis of tests limited.
3. Complexity. There are a lot of steps in the process of using the setup whereof some
have a risk of errors and thus making the results of poor quality or non reproducible. A
description of each step is presented in the following to better understand the process
and a visual representation of the process is displayed in figure 3.3 by a process diagram.
Prep.
Before initiating a test, solid steel blocks are placed below the lower clamping
unit and a dummy sheet and test sheet are placed on top of the lower clamping
unit. The sheets are radially aligned with small wooden sticks though holes
in the sheets displayed in figure 3.2 and lower clamping unit.
Step 1
The upper clamping unit is lowered to just short of contact with the sheets.
Guide pins are inserted through the upper clamping unit into the lower clamp-
ing unit to ensure radial alignment.
Step 2
The drawbead is formed with maximum force from the press. The two clamp-
ing units are bolted together using eight M8 bolts.
Step 3
The clamping unit is lifted to allow extraction of the solid steel blocks which
are replaced by 3D printed plastic blocks with a height that ensures correct
vertical alignment between the normal pressure area on the lower punch and
the test sheet. This is to enable normal pressure be exerted without deforming
the sheets.

3.3
Application of research
The main beneficiary of a new test setup is the metal forming group at DTU. The setup is
intended for easy, fast and consistent testing of stretching with combined normal loading of
natural surfaces and hereby allow the metal forming group to publish papers on the subject
based on high quality test results. In the longer term the sheet metal forming community
in general and the industry benefits as more results and knowledge are published.

can simulate tribology, i.e. friction, lubrication and wear in the forming process, hence
the name TriboForm. Figure 3.4, 3.5, 3.6 and 3.7 is an example of how Triboform work.

panel for a Mercedes-Benz C-class Coupé [4].

4.1
Workings of the setup
The setup utilizes vertical motion of the servo press to form the drawbead, draw the
Marciniak test and to compress a spring which apply normal pressure to flatten asperities.
The stroke of the Marciniak test is defined by stacking solid spacers to determine the
press displacement. A union clamp the dies in position to prevent material flow through
the drawbead and ensure straining in the center of the test sheet. Figure 4.2 display the
principle workings of the new test setup.

4.6
Operating the setup
The actions required for conducting a test cycle is described below. The square boxes
represent the input parameters available in the servo press user interface. Figure 4.22
display a visual representation of the testing procedure.
1. Preparation
Raise servo press to maximum height. Position test sheet and driving sheet in the
setup. Insert 40 mm aluminium spacer below the lower drawbead die. Assemble normal
pressure spacer stack according to desired normal pressure, and position spacer stack
in the setup. Each shim is equivalent to 7.2 MPa. CAUTION: Maximum 12 shims
corresponding 86 MPa normal pressure.
2. Drawbead forming
Adjust parameters Position to 555 mm, Velocity to 0.5 mm/s and Force depending
on material and press cycle start.
3. Securing
Tighten locking nut either by hand or by using the wrench to lock normal pressure
(only if normal pressure is applied). Lift the union and rotate counter clockwise to
clamp the sheets in the drawbead dies.
4. Adjustments
Adjust parameters Position to current position + 37 mm and Velocity to 2 mm/s and
press cycle start.
5. Extracting
Remove normal pressure spacer stack and 40 mm aluminum spacer. Insert one or more
thinner aluminum spacers to limit Marciniak travel if desired - if no spacers are inserted
maximum Marciniak travel is 40 mm. CAUTION: Do not interrupt the cycle as the
lower drawbead die needs support before union is operable!
6. Forming
Adjust parameters Position to 555 mm and Velocity to 0.5 mm/s and press cycle start.
7. Releasing
Loosen locking nut using the wrench handle on the wrench to release normal pressure.
CAUTION: Make sure the locking nut is completely out of the thread! Release the
union by inserting the stud on the wrench handle into the holes and rotate clockwise
until the union drops onto the lower drawbead die.
8. Retrieving
Adjust parameters Position to maximum value and Velocity to 5 mm/s and press cycle
start. The test is now finished and the test specimen can be retrieved.

5.3
The path to a final design
By interpreting the user needs and product requirements in section 5.1 through 5.2 the
actual design process is initiated. Firstly the geometric shape of the system is determined
by evaluating multiple system setups. Within the system are subsystems which secondly
are designed to fit the desired system. Each subsystem comprise of components which
are iterated multiple times until the setup design is satisfactory thus concluding the third
and final step of the design process. This process require meticulous brainstorming and
formulation of ideas. Fast formulation of ideas are important and thus a considerable
amount of hand sketching is required.
5.4
System design
Requirements from section 5.1.2 define the geometrical boundaries of the setup to fit within
the limits of the press available to the project. Therefore the design process initiates by
investigating geometrical shapes to comprise the system setup. Generally two types of
system shapes are considered; axisymmetric and rectangular.
Axisymmetric design without columns
Initial system shapes revolve around a small compact axisym-
metric design. The idea of which is to contain the test within a
solid tubular shaped setup. The setup benefit from self aligning
geometry about the Marciniak punch and a small outer radius.
Test adjustments such as normal force and marciniak stroke is ad-
justable by rotating the tubular parts relative to one another on
vertically displaced levels. This ensures rigid structure and simul-
taneously creates solid vertical positioning. The system rely on
constant downward motion of the press. Figure 5.1 display a con-
ceptual sketch of a small axisymmetric system without columns.
Figure 5.1: Axisymmetric
design without columns.
Axisymmetric design with columns
Further development of the axisymmetric design leads to increase
in height of the system as it allows for introduction of additional
components. This is to circumvent the unidirectional press mo-
tion required by the compact design. Increase in height leads to
columns being the main alignment part of the system. Figure
5.2 display a conceptual sketch of a axisymmetric system de-
sign comprising of three vertical columns separating two circular
plates. Aligning of test sheets rely on intricate designing of the
dies. Load from the press need to circumvent the top surface of
the structure by perforating the plate and accepting the resulting
decrease in rigidity. Normal pressure is applied by a component
located on the lower side of the plate comprising the top struc-
ture. Adjustability of the normal pressure is hampered by the
structure preventing access.
Figure 5.2: Axisymmetric
design with columns.

Axisymmetric design with locking mechanism
Combining the principles of figure 5.1 and 5.2 aims at utilizing
axisymmetric geometry to create easy securing by rotating latch-

onto levels of the column structure. Figure 5.3 display a concep-

The test setup requires total reversibility and thus any latching
mechanism must be capable of both locking and unlocking ap-
plied load. A latch system is not capable of unlocking if any load
is applied without compromising safety for the user. Latching
mechanisms are required for locking normal pressure and clamp-

Normal pressure actuation
Applying normal pressure while stretching the sheet over a punch requires a constant
normal force during the process. Several methods of applying force are described in section
5.2. Three methods are evaluated.
Threaded spindle
A threaded spindle powered by an electric motor mounted to
the system is capable of supplying normal load. The challenge
is to apply a constant load. This requires a clutch system and a
control loop with feedback to the motor. Many configurations
of system control and motor with clutch are able to approxi-
mate a constant load but the configuration is cumbersome and
expensive. The spindle application if assembled as displayed
in figure 5.5 is spacious and hard to fit within the geometric
boundaries.

Hydraulic actuator
Hydraulic actuators are easy to control and capable of supply-
ing large forces. Applying a constant force demands a constant
flow of hydraulic fluid which is controlled by a relief valve. This
implies pairing the setup with a hydraulic supply of desired di-
mensions. High pressure hoses supplies the fluid to the system
and thus requires that the safety shield of the servo press is
bypassed.

force. The challenge is containing the spring to avoid compro-

pressure as required from section 5.1.2 is capable of high force
exertion per compressed unit length and thus requires high

stretched but comprise a simple system. The spring is chosen as normal force actuator.
The compression of the spring is done by inserting spacers and compressing the spring by

Sustaining normal pressure
Utilizing a spring to supply normal force to the test sheets rely on the ability to lock and
unlock the stored energy. Two methods of locking and unlocking are evaluated.
Lever action
A lever action is a mechanical system which comprise of self
locking geometric shapes. The lever is able to compress the
spring as the geometric shapes of the lever action mechanisms
gear the supplied force through the motion. An example of a
commonly known lever action mechanism is a cam lobe. Figure
5.8 display sketches of lever action methods evaluated during
this process.
Figure 5.8:
Lever action
mechanisms.
Thread
Utilizing threaded fastners as a means for sustaining normal
pressure is a simple method. Types of threaded fastners are
various and the application of which makes complete control
of the spring possible.
The application of threaded fastners
demand the process being simplified to avoid a cumbersome
process similar to the one described in section 3.2 where many
bolts are used to clamp and unclamp the drawbead dies. Figure
5.9 displays sketches of methods involving threads.
Figure
5.9:
Threaded
mechanism.
Sustaining normal pressure conclusion
Two main challenges are safety and integrity of the test sheet while compressing and
unloading the spring. The lever action system enables locking and unloading of energy but
compromises on safety when unloading due to the same geometric conditions ensuring self
locking. The shape intended to lock in one direction will inevitably accelerate motion and
magnify forces in the opposite direction. An example is a cam lobe compressing a spring
only to have the rotation accelerate preceding maximum compression. A configuration of
threads to contain the spring is a simple solution. If dimensioned accordingly the thread
is capable of withstanding the forces with an acceptable degree of safety.
A threaded
solution is chosen.

Timing system
It is previously determined to use the press motion to compress the normal pressure spring
and a threaded configuration to sustain the pressure. The servo press available to the
project contain only one actuator and thus is limited to one vertical motion. To simplify
the testing process it is thus necessary to design a mechanism which enables timing of part
interactions while the press is in continuous downwards motion. To simplify the testing
procedure it is decided that forming of drawbead and compression of normal pressure
spring is done simultaneously. Two methods of timing vertical motion is evaluated.
Sleeve timing system
A configuration of vertically positioned sleeves interlocking by
flanges is able to account for radial alignment and guiding
vertical motion while simultaneously introduce timing.
The
sleeve configuration is suitable for enclosing the normal pres-
sure spring as it contain the spring and radially align the mech-
anism. A sleeve configuration is simple and versatile. Figure
5.10 display sketches of sleeve timing systems.
Figure 5.10: Sleeves.
Telescopic timing system
Sleeves are not meant to exude axial loading and thus the form-
ing motion is not an ideal application for sleeves as a means
of timing. Rods dimensioned accordingly are capable of with-
standing significant loading and thus a system of telescopic rods
are a simple and durable solution to alter the timing of the
applied load from the press. Figure 5.11 display sketches of
telescopic rods.
Figure 5.11:
Telescopic
rods.
Timing system conclusion
Both configurations are chosen and further developed as timing is necessary throughout
the setup. The application of timing yields multiple purposes. Timing of applied load is
by use of telescopic rods and timing of vertical motion for the normal pressure mechanism
is by use of a sleeve configuration.

Union mechanism
Clamping and unclamping of the drawbead dies described in section 3.2 includes loosening
a number of bolts. It is an effective but cumbersome method for preventing separation

effectiveness of the clamping.
Threaded locking
Threaded fasteners are a simple and effective method for clamp-

Threads are strong connectors and thus capable of
withstanding loads from the forming process. Threaded fas-

A latch mechanism utilizes geometric spacing of a rigid struc-

accurately dimensioned parts to avoid failure due to excessive

Threaded fasteners is a simple solution but does not comply with the needs of section

option but the risk of damage to the thread is not acceptable. The latch mechanism is
chosen to improve consistency of testing and simplify the process.

5.5.5
Restricting vertical motion
Vertical motion of the servo press is the core of the forming process. The press is operated
via input to the control system. Three parameters are available to the user. Position,
motion velocity and maximum force. To simplify using the setup it is intended to use rigid
structures to restrict the motion of the press. This implies controlling the process by the
input maximum force. The rigid structures is intended as gauges for spring compression
and stroke length when performing the Marciniak test.
Notches
Rigid structures containing notches or shelves yields the op-
portunity to have one structure with multiple notches and/or
shelves to restrict motion to multiple levels. Such structures
require a rigid design and manufacturing from materials capa-
ble of withstanding loads applied to small areas. Figure 5.14
display sketches of notches and shelves.
Figure 5.14: Notches and
shelves.
Threads
Threads provide easy adjustments in length and thus require
few parts to cover the entire span of motion. Consistency in
height adjustments rely upon the configuration as visual inspec-
tion either by a nonius scale or other measures are required to
dial in the height. Motion restriction by threads demands ac-
curate dimensioning with regards to safety against component
failure. Figure 5.15 display sketches of threads.
Figure 5.15: Threads.
Spacers
The simplest solution is rigid spacers manufactured to specific
dimensions. The spacers need dimensioning to withstand ap-
plied pressure and needs consideration to user safety. The ap-
plication of spacers requires a set of spacers manufactured to
cover the span of motion within an acceptable resolution such
that a stack of spacers restrict motion to a desired level. Figure
5.16 display sketches of spacers.
Figure 5.16: Spacers.
Restricting vertical motion conclusion
As the tests are conducted with loads of high magnitude safety is an important issue.
Notches and shelves meant for connecting to opposing parts subjected to high levels of
load does not meet the requirements to safety. Utilizing threads to restrict motion implies
dimensioning threads to withstand the load causing the physical dimensions of the thread
to be large. The simplest solution is to use spacers manufactured to comprise sets with
a desired stacking resolution. This is a reliable consistent system and thus spacers are
chosen.

5.6.4
Wrench
As described in section 5.5.2 it is decided to lock the normal pressure using threads. A
large locking nut in combination with a wrench is designed to lock normal pressure. At
maximum normal pressure the locking nut has to rotate approximately eight times to
completely disengage the thread. Due to the load carrier rods restricting angular motion
to approximately 90◦a conventional spanner needs engaging and disengaging a minimum
of 32 times which is both time consuming and uncomfortable for the user. Thus a low
profile ratchet wrench is designed to ease use. Figure 5.20 display the workings of the
wrench. The design is heavily inspired by conventional ratchet wrenches with a central
sprocket matching the hexagonal interface on the locking nut. The shifter and leaver is
used to select which spring loaded pin is engaged and thus in which direction torque is
applied. All parts are placed in a three plate sandwich construction bolted together. A
400 mm handle is used to increase the maximum applicable torque. Due to the standard
hexagonal interface of the locking nut a conventional 55 mm spanner can be used if the
ratchet wrench is to fail.
A stud at the end of the handle is used to loosen the union as described in section 5.6.1.

1:
The main system of the setup is a rectangular column system.
2:
A compressed spring apply normal pressure.
3:
A threaded mechanism contain the spring while compressed. The pressure is

4:
Sleeves and telescopic rods are introduced for timing of applied force.
5:
An axisymmetric union mechanism based on a latching system lock the dies

6:
Restriction of vertical motion during drawbead forming, Marciniak stroke and

validated force ratio distribution among forming parts.

simulation lay base for finite element analysis of selected components. The analysis of

Two categories of research are possible with the new test setup; basic and advanced re-

• Marciniak stroke and thus strain value.
• Normal pressure.
• Strain path.
• Strain rate.

changing tool surface or applying lubrication to the asperity flattening process. Advanced
research parameters are listed below.
• Tool surface.
• Lubrication type and amount.

Camera module
A camera module interchangeable with the normal pressure assembly enables high quality
video of pre-embossed grids on the test sheet being stretched during a Marciniak test.

opposed to just at the end of the test.
Friction modeling during Marciniak forming
A Marciniak punch with a rotating circular ring contact area supported by opposite force
of the normal pressure enables introduction of relative velocity between test sheet and
tool surface. A small powerful electric motor connected to interchangeable wear parts in
the punch generate the necessary torque for constant rotational speed during a test. By
measuring and logging the required torque the equivalent dynamic coefficient of friction is
determined during the forming process.

Needle roller and cage thrust assembly
Needle roller and cage thrust assemblies are designed to accommodate heavy
axial loads and peak loads in one direction. They provide a high degree of stiffness
within a minimum axial space. In applications where the faces of adjacent
machine components serve as raceways, needle roller thrust bearings take up no
more space than a conventional thrust washer. The bearings can also be
combined with bearing washers to create complete bearings.
• High axial load carrying capacity
• High stiffness
• Long service life
• Require minimal axial space
• Can be combined with bearing washers

LS 4565
LS universal washer for cylindrical and needle roller thrust
bearings
Universal washers for cylindrical and needle roller thrust bearings are supplied as
separate components. They are made of hardened carbon chromium bearing
steel and their raceway surface is ground, while all other surfaces are turned. The
washers are suitable for applications where accurate centring of the washers is
not necessary or where low speeds are involved. They can be used as shaft or
housing washers for AXK series bearings, and as shaft washers for AXW series
bearings.
• Enable full interchangeability of washers
• Suitable where accurate centring of washers is not necessary
• Suitable for low-speed applications
