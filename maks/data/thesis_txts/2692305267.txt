Abstract
Lactococcus lactis(L. lactis) is a genus of Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB), which is a
gram-positive bacterium. It ferments carbohydrates to produce large amounts of
lactic acid. It has the following characteristics: catalase-negative, anaerobic or
facultatively anaerobic, cytochrome free, spore free, etc. Lactococcus lactis is widely
used in the production of cheese and other dairy products. In order to make better
use of Lactococcus lactis, and providing a more clear understanding of the metabolic
mechanism of
Lactococcus lactis. L.lactis MG3163 was used for an adaptive
laboratory evolution(ALE) experiment. After 850 generations of isolation, a mutant
strain CS3155 was screened out for further study.
To figure out what factors limit the growth of Lactococcus lactis, a growth experiment
was set up by growing L. lactis MG1363 and other mutant strains obtained from the
ALE experiment. The medium used in the experiment is SALN
medium with
0.2%(w/v) sugar(maltose, galactose and glucose) as a single carbon source. It was
found that among these three sugars, L.lactis strains ferment galactose slowest while
glucose is the fastest fermented one. And mutant strain CS3155 was found to
consume sugar faster than the wild type(L. lactis MG1363). The results showed that
the growth rate of CS3155 on SALN medium containing maltose was 30% higher
than that of MG1363. There is also a 23% and 7% increase on the medium
containing galactose and glucose respectively. By comparing the metabolites
produced by glycolysis, it was found that the content of lactic acid significantly
increased when fermenting maltose. By calculating the sugar consumption fluxes of
different strains, compared with MG3163, the value of CS3155 was increased by
25% on maltose. Galactose and glucose fluxes decreased by 35% and 6.7%,
respectively. And the final fermentation product conformed to the composition of
homolactic fermentation in terms of ferment maltose.

1 Introduction
1.1 An overview of the project
Lactococcus lactis(L. lactis) MG1363 was founded that ferment maltose slowly
compared with glucose. To find out the factors that limit the Lactococcus lactis
ferment different sugars, an adaptive laboratory evolution(ALE) experiment was
introduced. By growing L. lactis MG1363 on SALN medium with 0.2%(w/v) maltose
and adjusting osmotic stress, after several months, those mutants that ferment
maltose faster than L. lactis MG1363 were isolated. And CS3155 is one of them and
it is the strain obtained after 850 generations.
To provide a better and wider use of L.lactis, the factors that limit sugar
metabolization on Lactococcus lactis have to be researched. This is also the aim of
this project. To achieve this goal, a growth experiment was set up to figure out the
growth rate of L. lactis MG1363 and mutant strains got from the ALE experiment by
growing them on SALN medium with 0.2%(w/v) maltose, galactose, and glucose.
And then to find out whether there are differences in the metabolic pathways, or are
there any changes in terms of main metabolites between different ALE generations
when ferment sugars.
The outline of the thesis is listed as followed:
Chapter 1 is the literature review part. It presents the background of this project,
including the introduction of Lactic acid bacteria and the relevant metabolic pathways.
Chapter 2 describes the materials and methods applied in this project.
Chapter 3 is the results and some discussion related to the results of this project.
Chapter 4 is the conclusion about the results obtained in the project and the outlook.

1.2.2 The application of Lactic acid bacteria in the food industry
Because of the various biological functions, lactic acid bacteria are widely used in the
food industry, agriculture, medicine and health fields. Lactic acid bacteria fermented
food is a kind of functional health food recognized by the world, it could provide the
food with sour taste and aroma, also helps to improve the nutritional value of food.[3]
On the other hand, lactic acid bacteria and their metabolites are also natural
preservatives[4] with no toxic adverse effects, which can inhibit or kill the growth or
reproduction of pathogenic bacteria in food, greatly prolongs the shelflife of food.[5]
1.2.2.1 Application of lactic acid bacteria in the dairy products
The technology of producing dairy products by fermentation with lactic acid bacteria
is very mature now. It can not only improve the nutritional value, but also produce
lactic acid, break down protein, form flavor substances, and produce antimicrobial
substances. At present, dairy products have become an important component of food.
Yogurt is a kind of fermented dairy product made from animal milk such as cow's milk
and goat's milk by lactic acid bacteria. Because the nutrients such as lactose and
casein in animal milk are easily absorbed by the human body after the decomposition
of lactic acid bacteria, it can help alleviate the lactose intolerance common among
Asian people.[6][7] The fermentation of lactic acid bacteria can also be used to
produce cream. The lactic acid produced in the fermentation can inhibit the
reproduction of spoilage bacteria to some extent and improve the stability of the
cream. The other function is to provide aroma, so sour cream produced by
fermentation has a stronger aroma than sweet cream.[8]

1.4.1 Homolactic Fermentation
Homolactic fermentation is a process in which microorganisms degrade glucose as a
substrate to pyruvate through the glycolytic pathway (Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas
pathway, EMP pathway)(figure 2), and pyruvate is reduced to lactic acid catalyzed by
lactate dehydrogenase.[33][34] In homolactic fermentation, one molecule of glucose
can be converted into two molecules of lactate, and this chemical reaction is used for
the performance of substrate-level phosphorylation to make two ATP molecules.[35]
The reaction is as follows:
Glucose + 2 ADP + 2Pi →2 lactate + 2 ATP
During the homolactic fermentation process, pyruvate undergoes reduction to lactate
or lactic acid in the presence of lactate dehydrogenase enzyme. This process is
named “homo-“ because it produces a single acid as the end product.[36]
In the process of homolactic acid fermentation, microorganisms can use 1 mol of
glucose to produce 2 mol of lactic acid, and the theoretical conversion rate is 100%.
However, due to the existence of other physiological activities of microorganisms in
the fermentation process, the actual conversion rate cannot reach 100%.[37] The
process of homolactic fermentation is elaborate as followed.

1.5 Microbial growth
Bacteria population follows a sequential series of growth phases and the microbial
growth can be divided into four phases, including lag phase, log phase, stationary
phase and death phase.
figure 4. Microbial growth phase
During the lag phase, there is an intense activity that prepares for population
increase, but there is no increase in population. In the log phase, there is an
exponential increase in population. As for the stationary phase, it is a period of
equilibrium, which means microbial deaths balance the production of new cells. The
death phase is the opposite of the logarithmic phase, in this period, the population of
bacteria is decreasing at a logarithmic rate.[44]
1.4.1 Factors affecting microbial growth and/or survival in foods
The factors that affect microbial growth could be divided into four aspects. The first
one is the kinetics of microbial growth, that is the growth phases (lag, exponential,
stationary, death). The second factor is the Intrinsic factors, that is the environment in
the food, for example, nutrient content, pH and buffering capacity, redox (Reduction-
Oxidation) potential (Eh), antimicrobial barriers and compounds, water activity,
extrinsic factors (surrounding environment), relative humidity, temperature and
gaseous atmosphere. The other one is extrinsic factors (Surrounding environment),
including relative humidity, temperature and gaseous atmosphere. Implicit factors
(Factors relating to the microorganism) are another aspect, including the specific
==PAGE:14==
10
growth rate (µ), mutualism, antagonism, commensalism and communication, stress
responses.[45].
1.4.2 Growth conditions for L.lactis
The lactic acid bacteria grow slowly and have smaller colonies on a solid medium. It
can grow quickly in a liquid medium. Generally, all Lactococcus species can grow in
4% NaCl solution. Lactococcus lactis can grow between 10 ℃to 45 ℃, and the
optimal temperature is 30℃. The optimal pH for growing L. lactis is in the range of
6.3 to 6.9, it is also reported that the lower limit for growth is within the range of 4.0 to
5.0.[46]

1.7 HPLC
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is an analytical technique for
separating, identifying, and quantifying the components in a mixture using a
chromatographic column, which is then identified and quantified by the spectroscopic
method. In the 1960s, column LC using low-pressure glass columns was further
developed to high-performance liquid chromatography HPLC using high-pressure
metal columns. Therefore, high-performance liquid chromatography is a highly
improved column liquid chromatography. Solvents are not passed through the
column under gravity but are fed into the column using infusion pumps at pressures
of up to 400 atmospheres.[65]
1.7.1 The separation principle of HPLC
HPLC involves a stationary phase (a solid, or a liquid supported on a solid) and a
mobile phase (a liquid or a gas). The mobile phase flows through the stationary
phase and carries the components of the mixture with it. Sample components that
display stronger interactions with the stationary phase will move more slowly through
the column than components with weaker interactions.[66]
The stationary phase is a very small porous granular material that exists in a column,
and the mobile phase is a solvent or a mixture of solvents that is forced through the
column under high pressure. Injection samples with a syringe, connected via a
sample loop of the valve, make the samples mixed with the mobile phase, then
components in the sample at different rates through a chromatographic column, due
to the various components and the stationary phase, the adsorption capacity of
differences between each component in turn out chromatographic column, through
the appropriate detector converts component concentration into an electrical signal is
passed on to the computer software for the HPLC. At the end of the run, the
chromatogram can be obtained in the HPLC software.[67]

(2) Injector
The injector is located next to the pump. The simplest method is to use a syringe to
inject the sample into the liquid phase system. Six-way injection valves and
automatic injectors are now commonly used. Among them, the automatic sampler
can repeat the injection within a set time.
(3) Column
Chromatographic columns are used primarily to separate components in mixtures.
Stainless steel columns, rather than glass ones, are now commonly used. Commonly
used fillers are silicon dioxide or polymer gels. The eluents used for LC can be either
acidic or alkaline, and most cylindrical shells are made of stainless steel because of
their resistance to a variety of solvents.
(4) Detector
The detector detects the components separated from the column. In the absence of
the substance to be measured, the composition of the eluent is consistent, and the
addition of the substance to be measured changes the composition of the eluent, and
the detector can measure this difference and convert it into an electrical signal that is
transmitted to the recorder. There are a variety of detectors, such as ultraviolet and
visible light detectors, fluorescence detectors, electrochemical detectors, and so on.
==PAGE:21==
17
(5) Data recorder
The changes in eluent detected by the detector are presented in the form of electrical
signals, so our eyes cannot see them, and we need the help of the computer's data
processor (integrator). There are many types of data processors, such as a simple
system consisting of a built-in printer and word processor, or a software system
designed specifically for LC systems with not only data acquisition capabilities, but
also peak fitting, baseline correction, automatic concentration calculation, molecular
weight determination, and more.
(6) Degasser
There may be some invisible gases in the HPLC system, which will lead to increased
noise and an unstable baseline. The degassing machine uses a special polymer
membrane tube to remove gas. Many very small holes on the surface of the polymer
membrane tube allow air to pass through while preventing any liquid from passing
through.
(7) Column heater
Liquid chromatographic separation is usually greatly affected by column temperature.
To get consistent results, it is important to keep the temperature consistent. For some
analyses, such as sugar and organic acids, better results can be obtained at 50 to
80°C. For this reason, chromatographic columns are usually placed in cylindrical
heaters.

2.4 Growth experiments
The first step for the growth experiment is to prepare needed mediums. M17 broth
and M17 agar plates are sterilized at 121℃for 15min and then for store strains. The
L.lactis strains can live on M17 agar plates for almost 2 weeks at -4℃. The SALN
medium is prepared for growing strains. In general, a batch of 5L SALN will be
prepared, before using, 0.2%(w/v) single carbon source should be added, and adjust
the pH to 7. Because there are some substances in the medium that are easily
decomposed by heat，the 0.2μm filter membrane will be applied for SALN medium
sterilization.
The strains were firstly grown on M17 agar plates, and single colonies were picked
into a tube with 5ml SALN medium with 0.2%(w/v) carbon source(maltose, galactose
or glucose). Generally, 3 to 4 tubes were prepared previously, to ensure all the
strains stay at the log phase before scale-up, the culture in tubes is diluted one by
one. After 8 to 12 hours of incubation at 30℃, the culture will be inoculated in a bottle
with a 50ml fresh SALN medium with the same carbon source. Since L.lactis
MG3163 grows slowly, the third tube is used for scale-up while the others take the
last tube. There will be a magnet in each bottle to prevent the lactococcus lactis
strains from settling to the bottom of the bottle. The bottles will be put into a water
bath with a temperature of 30℃, and then begin to take samples every 20 to 45
minutes to test OD at 600nm.

The metabolites of L.lactis glycolysis are analyzed by HPLC. Samples were collected

by interacting with the stationary phase and mobile phase. In this general way, the
metabolites can be qualitatively and quantitatively analyzed.

4 Conclusion and outlook
To figure out the factors that limit the growth of Lactococcus lactis, a growth
experiment was introduced by growing L. lactis MG1363 and other mutant strains
obtained from the previous ALE experiment.
The result revealed that L.lactis strains grow fast on the SALN medium with glucose
while growing slowly on the medium with galactose. And the mutant strain CS3155
was found to consume sugar faster than the wild type(L. lactis MG1363). The results
showed that the growth rate of CS3155 on SALN medium containing maltose was
30% higher than that of MG1363. There is also a 23% and 7% increase on the
medium
containing
galactose
and
glucose
respectively.
By
comparing
the
metabolites produced by glycolysis, it was found that the content of lactic acid
significantly increased when fermenting maltose. The sugar consumption fluxes of
different strains were also compared, compared with the value of L.lactis MG3163,
the value of CS3155 was increased by 25% on maltose. Galactose and glucose
fluxes decreased by 35% and 6.7%, respectively. And the final fermentation product
is more related to the composition of homolactic fermentation in terms of ferment
maltose. It is successful isolation for obtaining the mutant strain CS3155, but the
reason why it can produce more lactic acid when fermenting maltose instead of
glucose and galactose could be researched in the next stage.
