Abstract
This thesis investigates the capability of the MIKE21/3 Wave Model FM to predict wave run-up and overtop-
ping. The model consists of a 2D version, MIKE21W, governed by Boussinesq-type equations, and a 3D version,
MIKE3W, based on a Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) solver. The study benchmarks both the newly
implemented MIKE21W (with unstructured meshes and wetting–drying) and MIKE3W against established ref-
erence data. While a validation report for MIKE3W already exists, this work builds upon it by extending and
further testing the models’ capabilities.
Validation is performed against laboratory and field experiments compiled in the CLASH database (De Rouck
et al. (2004)), as well as the empirical design formulations of EurOtop (2018). A structured series of numerical
experiments is conducted: solitary-wave and irregular-wave run-up cases evaluate the models’ ability to repro-
duce extreme uprush statistics (Rmax and Ru2%), while a broad selection of overtopping cases from CLASH
provides mean overtopping discharges (q) across a wide range of freeboards, slopes, and wave climates, serving
as a reference for overtopping accuracy.
Model performance is quantified using standard skill metrics (RMSD, scatter index, and relative bias), alongside
sensitivity analyses of numerical parameters (mesh resolution, time step, and random-phase seed) and physical
descriptors (wave steepness and breaker parameter ξm−1,0). Results show that MIKE21W accurately captures
the general trends of run-up and overtopping, although with a slight tendency to underpredict in non-breaking
wave conditions. MIKE3W generally yields improved predictions and performs very well overall, although
with minor overpredictions in cases where breakers transition from plunging to spilling. This behaviour is
linked to the absence of a turbulence closure model: while turbulence schemes such as k–ϵ were tested, they
introduced excessive dissipation and were deemed unsuitable for this type of assessment. Both models demon-
strate improved predictive skill at lower relative freeboards, whereas increasing freeboard is associated with
greater scatter in the results. At larger freeboards, overtopping is increasingly governed by splash and spray
processes, which are not explicitly represented in either model due to their underlying formulations.
The thesis concludes with practical guidance for coastal engineering design. MIKE21W is recommended for
preliminary design and large-scale assessments, provided that mesh resolution and numerical settings are care-
fully controlled, as it offers substantial computational savings compared to MIKE3W. MIKE3W, in turn, is better
suited for design-critical applications involving waves with higher nonlinearity or strong vertical bathymetric
variations that violate the mild-slope assumptions of MIKE21W. Finally, recommendations are given for future
model development and targeted experimental validation to reduce remaining uncertainties and strengthen the
reliability of overtopping predictions in engineering practice.

Introduction
In recent decades, climate change and associated sea level rise have intensified the urgency of protecting low-
lying coastal areas from flooding and storm impacts. Coastal structures such as dikes represent vital defences,
but their performance depends critically on wave run-up and overtopping processes. Run-up determines whether
waves exceed structural crests, while overtopping directly affects flooding hazards and structural safety. Ac-
curate prediction of these processes is therefore essential for designing resilient coastal infrastructure and for
managing flood risk in the face of increasingly extreme conditions.
A wide spectrum of modelling approaches exists for simulating wave–structure interactions. At one end, high-
fidelity Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) models based on the Navier–Stokes equations can resolve de-
tailed hydrodynamics but are computationally demanding and often impractical for large-scale or routine engi-
neering applications. At the other end, depth-integrated models such as Boussinesq-type formulations provide a
balanced alternative, capturing key nonlinear and dispersive effects with much greater efficiency. This trade-off
between accuracy and computational feasibility makes them particularly attractive for engineering practice.
Within this framework, the MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM, developed by DHI, has been enhanced with features
such as unstructured mesh capability and wetting-and-drying functionality. These developments increase its
potential for simulating wave run-up and overtopping over complex geometries and thus for application in design
and assessment. While earlier validation efforts have examined the ability of MIKE3W to simulate overtopping
for a limited set of cases, systematic benchmarking of the MIKE21W implementation is still lacking.
This thesis, therefore, undertakes a comprehensive validation of both MIKE21W and MIKE3W with respect to
wave run-up and overtopping. The models are tested against the extensive experimental data available in the
CLASH database (De Rouck et al. (2004)) and against the empirical formulations of EurOtop (2018), both of
which are internationally recognised references in coastal engineering. The CLASH dataset provides a robust
experimental foundation across a wide range of structures and conditions, while the EurOtop (2018) manual
offers practical guidance derived from long-standing research and testing. Using both ensures a rigorous and
multifaceted validation.
By combining validation against established benchmarks with a critical assessment of computational and prac-
tical aspects, the study aims to investigate the suitability and limitations of MIKE21W for engineering use.
In doing so, it complements earlier MIKE3W validation work and contributes to improving the reliability of
numerical overtopping assessments in design practice.
This thesis, therefore, aims to answer the following research questions:
• Under which conditions is MIKE21W suitable for accurately simulating wave run-up and overtopping,
and when are more advanced models like MIKE3W required?
• How well do the MIKE21W and MIKE3W models reproduce benchmark data from the CLASH database
and empirical formulations from EurOtop (2018), in terms of predicting wave run-up and overtopping?
• What are the computational and practical trade-offs between MIKE21W and MIKE3W in engineering
applications, particularly with regard to setup complexity, simulation time, and accuracy?

These questions are explored through a structured program of numerical simulations, benchmark comparisons,
and critical analyses of model applicability.
Chapter 2 establishes the theoretical foundation for the work. It introduces the governing principles of wave
run-up and overtopping, defines the key physical and statistical parameters, and outlines the modelling frame-
work used in the study. The chapter also provides detailed descriptions of the MIKE21W and MIKE3W models,
highlighting their governing equations, numerical implementations, and turbulence closures. Furthermore, the
performance evaluation metrics adopted in the thesis, such as RMSD, scatter index, and relative bias, are pre-
sented, together with a discussion of the reference datasets and empirical formulations used for benchmarking.
Chapter 3 focuses on the validation of run-up predictions under solitary wave conditions. This controlled
setting allows for a systematic assessment of the models’ ability to capture fundamental processes of wave
uprush and rundown without the complexities of irregular wave fields. The simulations are compared against
established experimental and numerical benchmarks, enabling a clear evaluation of how accurately MIKE21W
and MIKE3W reproduce extreme run-up metrics, specifically Rmax.
Chapter 4 extends the analysis to irregular wave conditions, which are more representative of real coastal
environments. The chapter investigates the models’ ability to reproduce statistical run-up measures under a

2.1
Run-up
Wave run-up is generally defined as the maximum vertical extent of wave uprush above the still water level
(SWL) in coastal areas. It consists of two components: swash oscillations and wave-induced setup (Holman
(1986)). Accurate estimation of wave run-up is crucial in coastal engineering to ensure the safety, stability, and
performance of both coastal and offshore structures.
Run-up plays a key role in flood protection design, as well as in the prevention of shoreline instabilities caused
by scour and erosion, providing essential input for the planning, design, and management of waterfront infras-
tructure.
Run-up is generated when incident waves, whether wind-generated, infragravity, or long-period events, propa-
gate into the nearshore zone and undergo transformation through shoaling, breaking, and bore formation in the
surf zone. Upon reaching the beach face, the bore collapses and initiates an uprush of water, commonly called
swash, that propagates shoreward as a thin, shallow supercritical sheet flow.
Following the definition provided by Christensen et al. (2002), the surf zone can be divided into four distinct
horizontal regions, as illustrated in Figure 2.1. In the shoaling or deformation zone, waves increase in steepness
and their profiles deform into an asymmetric shape, leaning forward. At the breaking point in the outer zone,
waves begin to overturn, forming a jet whose strength depends on the breaker type, i.e., spilling or plunging. In
the inner zone, the wave front can be described as a moving bore propagating toward the shoreline. Finally, the
swash zone is defined as “the part of the beach that is alternately dry and wet due to the wave run-up” (Petti &
Longo (2001)).
Figure 2.1: The surf zone dived into the shoaling/deformation zone, outer breaking zone, inner breaking zone,
swash zone (from Christensen et al. (2002)).
The dynamics of the uprush are governed by a combination of factors, including the initial momentum of the
bore, gravitational deceleration along the beach slope, bed friction, percolation losses in permeable substrates,
and lateral divergence of the swash lens. Under these conditions, the run-up height can exceed the local water
depth by several times, thereby violating the assumptions of linear wave theory and rendering depth-averaged

equilibrium concepts inapplicable (Svendsen (2006)). Moreover, the rapid variation of water depth over short
horizontal scales prevents the use of locally constant depth approximations.
Hydrodynamically, the process is strongly dependent on the surf similarity parameter, ξ, which dictates whether
swash motion is dominated by short-wave bores (reflective and intermediate beaches) or long-period infragrav-
ity waves (dissipative beaches).
From a hydrodynamic perspective, the characteristics of swash motion are strongly influenced by the surf simi-
larity parameter, ξ. This parameter determines whether the uprush is dominated by short-wave bores, as typically
observed on reflective and intermediate beaches, or by long-period infragravity waves, which are more relevant
on dissipative beaches. Therefore, the surf similarity parameter provides a critical framework for predicting
run-up behaviour across different coastal morphologies.

2.1.1
Definition of run-up
Traditionally, empirical formulas are used to estimate run-up elevation as a function of wave characteristics (e.g.,
significant wave height and wavelength) and the foreshore slope. However, Atkinson et al. (2017) evaluated
the performance of 11 run-up models against field observations and found significant variability among them.
The models with the best accuracy had an RMSE of 25%. No single model consistently outperformed the
others, highlighting the importance of site-specific calibration. In general, models derived from field data tended
to yield more accurate estimates than those based on laboratory experiments, which often overpredict run-up
heights.
Run-up can be quantified in different ways. The most direct method is through the maximum observed run-up
elevation, Rmax, which depends on the observation duration. Alternatively, a more statistically robust measure
is the run-up level exceeded by 2% of the incident waves over a given period, denoted Ru2%.

2.1.3
Statistical robustness of Ru2%
The run-up height is commonly quantified using the parameter Ru2%, as previously introduced. This definition,
representing the run-up level exceeded by only 2% of the incoming waves, is widely adopted in coastal engi-
neering practice. Nevertheless, its application in numerical modelling may be problematic when simulations
cover relatively short durations. In such cases, the limited number of incident waves provides an insufficient
statistical basis for the 2%-exceedance estimate. Moreover, the waves contributing to the 2% largest run-ups are
typically the largest non-breaking waves, which are also the steepest and therefore among the most challenging
for the model to resolve reliably.
As a more statistically robust alternative, the use of Ru10% can be considered. This parameter corresponds to
the run-up level exceeded by 10% of the waves, thereby relying on a larger portion of the run-up distribution.
The 10%-exceedance run-up thus provides a valuable complementary metric in situations where the simulation
length does not permit a stable estimate of Ru2%.
According to EurOtop (2018), several exceedance levels, including Ru1%, Ru2%, Ru20%, and Ru50%, have
historically been reported for completeness in run-up experiments carried out at Delft Hydraulics (now Deltares),
such as Report M422 (1953), Report M500 (1956), and Report M544 (1957). These values provide a broader
statistical description of the run-up process, which is particularly valuable when comparing numerical model
predictions with experimental or field data of different record lengths.
However, in the present study, these additional exceedance levels were not considered in the analysis. Despite
efforts to retrieve the historical experimental data, the relevant values could not be accessed in a reliable and
complete form through publicly available online sources or archives. Consequently, the comparison of numer-
ical results with experimental reference data is limited to the 2% exceedance levels, reported in the EurOtop
database.

2.2
Overtopping
Wave overtopping is defined as the average discharge of water passing over the crest of a coastal or hydraulic
structure per unit width, denoted q, and typically expressed in either m3/s per m or in l/s per m (EurOtop
(2018)).
The most common form, known as green water overtopping, occurs when the crest level of the structure is lower
than the maximum wave run-up Rmax, allowing a continuous sheet of water to pass over the crest. A second
form arises when breaking waves on the seaward slope generate splashes, which may be carried over the crest
either by their own momentum or by onshore winds. A third, less significant mechanism is spray, produced by
wind acting on offshore wave crests; this contributes negligible volumes to the total discharge.
Empirical prediction formulae for overtopping generally account for both green water and splash, as these were
measured during the model tests on which the methods are based. Wind effects are not explicitly included: while
onshore winds have little influence on large green water events, they can increase discharges below 1 l/s per m
by blowing spray droplets landward.
Understanding and accurately quantifying wave overtopping is of fundamental importance in coastal and hy-
draulic engineering, as it directly affects the design, safety, and functionality of coastal structures. Applications
include:
• Designing and assessing the structural stability of dikes, seawalls, breakwaters, and revetments;
• Evaluating flood protection systems and risk to infrastructure located behind coastal defences;
• Ensuring the safe operation of coastal roads, railways, and promenades exposed to wave action;
• Managing erosion, scouring, and water intrusion that may affect materials or vegetation on the landward
side of structures.
Wave overtopping is inherently stochastic in nature—varying randomly in time, space, and volume. Typically,
only the largest waves contribute significantly to overtopping, carrying large volumes of water over the crest in
short bursts, while smaller waves may not result in any overtopping. As a result, reliable overtopping estimates
depend on adequate sampling durations and proper spatial averaging across the structure width.
EurOtop (2018) provides a widely adopted classification of the average overtopping discharge in terms of its
potential consequences:
• q < 0.1 l/s per m: Negligible effect on structural strength of the crest and rear slope;
• q = 1 l/s per m: Poor-quality grass covers or clay may begin to erode; no damage expected for rubble
mound structures;
• q = 10 l/s per m: Significant overtopping for dikes and embankments; potential for severe erosion on the
harbour side of rubble mound breakwaters under large wave conditions;
• q = 100 l/s per m: Crest and landward slopes require protection (e.g., asphalt or concrete); rubble mound
breakwaters may generate transmitted waves and require armour coverage on the landward side.
While the mean overtopping discharge provides a useful first estimate of overtopping severity, it is not suffi-
cient on its own to fully characterise risk or design requirements. In particular, the wave height that causes
Validation of MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM for Wave Run-up and Overtopping

==PAGE:19==
overtopping plays a critical role: for a given overtopping discharge, larger incident waves imply more energetic
and potentially damaging overtopping events.
EurOtop (2018) therefore emphasises the need to consider not just the average discharge but also the incident
wave conditions, the duration of overtopping, and the intended function of the structure. Acceptable overtopping
limits vary significantly depending on the application—whether for structural integrity, asset protection, or the
safety of pedestrians, vehicles, or operational facilities—and are discussed in detail in §3 of the EurOtop manual.

From a practical perspective, it may seem desirable to design a structure such that no water passes over the crest.
However, in design practice, the concept of zero overtopping cannot be taken literally, as prediction methods
never return a true value of zero discharge. Instead, very small mean discharges may be predicted that, in reality,
correspond either to negligible amounts of water or to only one or two overtopping waves within an hour.

2.2.1
Overtopping reduction measures
EurOtop (2018) emphasizes that increasing the crest freeboard is the most effective means of reducing overtop-
ping, as discharges decrease exponentially with increasing freeboard. However, when raising the crest is not
feasible, several supplementary measures can be considered.
One option is to increase permeability, for example by using porous armour layers or foreshores, which dissipate
wave energy and thereby reduce run-up and overtopping. Another approach is the construction of a submerged
breaker bar, which triggers wave breaking offshore and lowers wave heights at the structure toe. Finally, mod-
ifications to the crest geometry, such as the installation of a storm wall or a bullnose, can deflect or dissipate
overtopping flows, particularly for steep or vertical structures.
Although these measures are important tools in practice, the design and optimisation of overtopping reduction
strategies are beyond the scope of this thesis.
2.3
Wave theories
The representation of water waves is of primary importance in coastal and ocean engineering, as wave forcing
governs run-up, overtopping, and the loading of coastal and offshore structures. Depending on the water depth,
wave steepness, and the degree of nonlinearity, different theoretical descriptions have been developed to ap-
proximate wave motion. These theories can be broadly classified into regular wave theories, which describe
deterministic, periodic or non-periodic (solitary) waveforms, and irregular wave theories, which represent the
random nature of natural seas through spectral methods. In fact, real sea states are rarely regular. Instead,

they consist of a broad spectrum of wave components with varying frequencies and directions, driven by wind
forcing and modified by wave–wave interactions. To account for this randomness, irregular wave theories are
formulated in the spectral domain. One of the most widely used spectral models is the JONSWAP spectrum,
which has become a standard representation of wind-sea conditions in engineering practice.
The following subsections present the main theoretical descriptions of regular and irregular waves employed in
this study: solitary wave theory, cnoidal wave theory, and the JONSWAP spectrum for irregular waves.

equation and are applicable in shallow-water environments where dispersion is weak compared to nonlinearity.

2.3.2
Irregular wave
Natural sea states are inherently random, as they result from the superposition of a large number of wave com-
ponents with different frequencies and directions. This stochastic nature of the ocean surface can be described
completely and compactly using the wave energy density spectrum. The spectrum characterises the distribu-
tion of wave energy as a function of frequency, and, when combined with an appropriate directional spreading
function, it fully defines the sea state.
Several spectral formulations have been developed over the years, with their parameters typically expressed in
terms of the significant wave height, Hs, and the spectral peak period, Tp. Among the most widely used are the
Pierson–Moskowitz spectrum Pierson Jr & Moskowitz (1964), which describes fully developed seas, and the
JONSWAP spectrum Hasselmann et al. (1973), which accounts for the growth stage of wind-generated seas in
fetch-limited conditions.
To extend the spectral description to directional seas, a directional spreading function is introduced. This func-
tion distributes the spectral energy across different propagation directions. Several forms exist, including the
trivial case of a unidirectional distribution, the Gaussian distribution, and the commonly used cos2s distribution
Longuet-Higgins (1963).
In the present work, the JONSWAP spectrum is adopted in combination with a unidirectional distribution.

significant wave height at the slope toe, Hm0 toe. To ensure direct comparability between the model results and

originally introduced by Zelt & Skjelbreia (1993), allows the separation of one-dimensional wave fields into
incident and reflected components by analysing surface elevation time series recorded at multiple wave gauges.
The method relies on the known spatial distribution of wave gauges and uses the linear superposition of wave
components to estimate incoming and reflected wave signals. For reliable decomposition, the spacing between
the gauges must be selected to resolve the range of wavelengths containing most of the wave energy. In this
study, gauge locations are chosen following the criteria outlined by Zelt & Skjelbreia (1993), ensuring that
both the minimum and maximum spacing capture the shortest and longest wavelengths of interest, respectively.
Intermediate gauges are placed using a geometric telescoping distribution to provide a smooth transition in
spacing. An example of wave gauge spacing and placement is shown in Figure 5.5.
The decomposition procedure is implemented in Python (Jensen & Carstensen (2014) and Kramer et al. (2023)),
where the surface elevation time series from the model output, combined with the known gauge positions, are
used to calculate the incident and reflected wave signal. An example of decomposition of the wave time series
is illustrated in Figure 4.4. From this, the incident significant wave height is derived and subsequently adopted
as the representative significant wave height at the base of the slope.
Finally, to ensure consistency with the experimental database, an automated correction procedure was estab-
lished. Cases in which the decomposed incident wave height deviated from the target database value were
re-run iteratively with adjusted input wave conditions until convergence was achieved. The resulting matched
wave heights were then used as the basis for all further analysis.

2.5.4
Flood and dry
To accurately simulate wave run-up and overtopping processes, the Flood and Dry option is enabled in the
MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM graphical user interface. This option manages the treatment of moving boundaries
by reformulating the governing equations when the local water depth falls below the predefined wetting depth,
hwet. Specifically, when the water depth is less than hwet, the momentum fluxes are set to zero, and only mass
fluxes are considered in the computation. If the depth further decreases below the drying threshold, hdry, the
corresponding element or cell is excluded from the active simulation domain.
This numerical approach to handling wetting and drying fronts is based on the finite volume formulations pre-
sented by Zhao et al. (1994) and Sleigh et al. (1998). Further technical details can be found in Section 6.3.1

2.5.5
Wave breaking
In MIKE21W, wave breaking is incorporated through the surface roller concept introduced by Svendsen (1984).
Breaking is assumed to initiate when the local water surface slope exceeds a predefined critical angle. At
this point, a surface roller is formed and modelled as a passive bulk of water that is detached from the main
wave motion and advected with the wave celerity. The roller’s presence introduces an additional convective
momentum term, accounting for the vertical non-uniformity of the horizontal velocity distribution.
This approach is illustrated in Figure 2.7. A detailed description of the roller formulation and its implementation
can be found in Madsen et al. (1997) and Sørensen et al. (2004).
Figure 2.7: Cross-section of a breaking wave and the assumed vertical profile of the horizontal velocity compo-
nents (from Schäffer et al. (1993)).
Although the model is primarily developed for spilling breakers, it has shown satisfactory performance in cap-
turing wave height decay even in the presence of plunging breakers.
The main parameters governing the roller model and their default values are summarised below:
• Roller form factor: Defines the geometry of the roller. A default value of 1.5 is used for spilling breakers.
For plunging breakers, a value of approximately 2.0 is recommended, as suggested by Ozanne et al.
(2000).
• Roller celerity factor: The roller is assumed to travel at a celerity equal to the wave celerity multiplied by
this factor. During the transition from initial breaking to the bore-like regime in the inner surf zone, the
factor increases gradually from unity to the specified roller celerity factor. The default value is 1.3.
Validation of MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM for Wave Run-up and Overtopping
20
==PAGE:31==
• Initial breaking angle: Defines the local surface slope threshold for breaking onset. A default value of
20° is applied for spilling breakers. For plunging breakers, Madsen et al. (1997) recommends increasing
this value to 25° to improve accuracy.
• Final breaking angle: Represents the terminal critical slope during the transition to the bore regime. The
default value is 10°.
• Half-time for roller cut-off: Governs the transition from the initial to the final breaking angle. It is typi-
cally set to Tp/5, where Tp is the peak wave period. For plunging breakers, a shorter time scale of Tp/10
has been found to yield better results (Madsen et al. (1997)).
This parametrisation enables a dynamic and physically informed representation of wave breaking behaviour,
contributing to the accurate simulation of energy dissipation in the surf zone.
In MIKE3W, no explicit physical model for wave breaking is implemented. Instead, the dissipation of wave en-
ergy during breaking is implicitly handled by the numerical scheme. When the wave front steepens sufficiently,
the higher-order harmonics are not adequately resolved on the computational mesh, leading to their numerical
damping.
2.5.6
Type of outputs and their usage
To analyse the simulation results, several types of output files generated by the software are utilised. Each
output format serves a specific purpose—whether for result verification, data extraction for further analysis, or
purely for visualisation.
The following output types are employed:
• Point output: This format provides time series of selected variables at one or more specified locations
throughout the simulation. It is particularly useful for extracting surface elevation data, which is used in
the wave decomposition process described in section 2.4.
• Line output: This output type captures the spatial variation of variables along a defined transect within
the domain. It is used to estimate the time series of run-up by identifying the furthest landward position
reached by water during the simulation. Additionally, it can be used to visualise surface elevation profiles
at specific time steps.
• Discharge output: This format is designed for calculating wave overtopping across a defined cross-
section. It provides both the instantaneous discharge time series and the accumulated discharge volume.
These outputs are essential for quantifying the total overtopping per simulation and for analysing the
temporal evolution of overtopping. They also help identify potential overestimations due to numerical
artefacts.

Solitary wave run-up
The solitary wave run-up scenario represents one of the most fundamental benchmark cases in coastal engineer-
ing. Its simplicity and well-defined physics make it an ideal starting point for understanding more complex
interactions between waves and coastal structures.
This case is chosen specifically for its clarity in setup and interpretation. Compared to irregular or random wave
scenarios, the solitary wave problem involves fewer variables and is well-supported by theoretical and empirical
literature. Thus, it provides a solid foundation for validating numerical models and gaining initial insights into
run-up behaviour, which will inform and contextualise more advanced cases later in this thesis.
In this chapter, the solitary wave run-up is modelled with MIKE21W and expressed using the maximum run-up
Rmax, since solitary waves represent single-wave events.
3.1

Solitary wave interaction with coastal topographies and structures has been the focus of extensive research. Two
primary configurations have garnered attention: vertical walls and sloped shores or structures. These serve as
standard test cases for model calibration and validation due to their frequent use in analytical, numerical, and
laboratory studies.

Model setup
The model domain is constructed as a one-dimensional (1D) configuration by constraining the mesh to a single
cell in the cross-shore direction. This effectively eliminates transverse variation and ensures that the problem
remains strictly 1D. The domain length is set to 1000 m, with the bathymetry consisting of a 600 m flat region
followed by either a vertical wall (in the flat-bed test case) or a sloped beach (in the inclined-bed test case).
The computational mesh is quadrangular, with varying spatial resolution to optimise simulation accuracy and
computational efficiency. In the flat portion of the domain, the mesh cell size is uniform with an edge length
determined by the number of elements per wavelength, n/L, the tested cases are shown in Table 3.2. To increase
resolution in the region of interest—i.e., the sloped section where run-up occurs—the longitudinal cell size is
refined to one-fifth the size of the cells of the flat zone. A transitional zone of 200 m is implemented at the
interface between the flat and sloped regions to gradually reduce the cell length. This smooth refinement strategy
avoids sudden changes in resolution, thereby preventing numerical instabilities while improving the accuracy
of wave transformation and run-up prediction.

Wave generation
At the offshore boundary, waves are generated using either solitary or cnoidal wave formulations, depending
on the case. In both configurations, the wave direction is set perpendicular to the shoreline to maintain a purely
1D propagation.
Solitary wave
The solitary wave implementation in MIKE21W is a non-published feature (not directly accessible through the
graphical user interface (GUI)), but embedded within the software’s code. The formulation used follows the
theoretical background presented in Section 2.3.1, which, given the water depth, defines the solitary wave solely
by its wave height.
For simulations involving the vertical wall, the solitary wave height is specified in non-dimensional form as a
ratio to the water depth, H/h, ranging from 0.10 to 0.68. For the sloped beach cases, the wave height is similarly
normalised, with H/h ranging from 0.12 to 0.32. These ranges were chosen to ensure wave nonlinearity while
avoiding wave breaking, which is not activated in the simulations.
Cnoidal wave
As said, the solitary wave formulation is not available directly in the GUI; therefore, as a tentative to model
similar cases with one of the built-in formulations, the cnoidal wave formulation is tested. The cnoidal wave is
generated by specifying both wave height and wave period. To simulate an effectively non-periodic, isolated
wave event (similar to a solitary wave), the period was set to a large value, T = 400 s. This allows the wave
to be treated as quasi-solitary in behaviour. The dimensionless wave height, H/h, for the cnoidal wave cases

4
Irregular wave run-up
In the previous chapter, the focus was on solitary waves, which represent an idealised and relatively simple case
for investigating wave run-up processes. Solitary waves are useful for developing fundamental insight because
they are characterised by a single crest, allowing for controlled studies of wave-structure interaction. However,
solitary waves are rarely representative of natural sea states, which are inherently irregular and governed by a
spectrum of wave components with varying frequencies, amplitudes, and phases.
The transition from solitary to irregular waves marks a significant increase in complexity. Irregular waves
are composed of a superposition of individual wave components, typically described by statistical or spectral
representations. This introduces phenomena such as wave–wave interaction, reflection from coastal structures,
and constructive or destructive interference, all of which strongly influence the magnitude and variability of
wave run-up. From an engineering perspective, it is precisely these irregular sea states that must be considered
for reliable design and assessment of coastal and offshore structures, as they correspond to the most common
and realistic loading scenarios encountered in practice.
The present chapter, therefore, extends the analysis to irregular waves, with the aim of bridging the gap between
idealised wave conditions and realistic environmental forcing. Building on the convergence studies and sensi-
tivity analyses performed for solitary waves, the parameter settings that provided the most accurate and stable
results in the simpler case are adopted as a baseline for the irregular wave simulations. This ensures that the
numerical framework is both robust and consistent, while also allowing the study to focus on the additional
challenges introduced by the stochastic and spectral nature of irregular waves.
4.1
Literature review and experimental comparison
Historical development of wave run-up studies
The study of wave run-up has undergone a long evolution, moving from early qualitative observations to ad-
vanced numerical and probabilistic formulations. The development reflects broader progress in coastal engi-
neering, experimental hydrodynamics, and computational modelling.
Early interest in wave run-up was motivated by practical concerns in harbour and coastal protection works.
Initial descriptions were primarily observational, with measurements often taken in the field to guide design.
A key breakthrough came with the work of Iribarren (1965), who introduced what later became widely known
as the Iribarren number (also called the surf similarity parameter). This dimensionless quantity links wave
steepness and slope angle, and remains a fundamental scaling parameter for run-up studies:

breaker wave type (spilling, plunging, or surging).
In the mid-20th century, systematic laboratory experiments provided the first empirical formulations of wave
run-up. Saville Jr (1957) and Hunt Jr (1959) performed early flume studies, establishing relationships between

run-up and slope steepness. With Asbeck et al. (1953), for the first time in an international paper, a probabilistic
description of run-up exceedance levels is introduced, such as Ru2%, the run-up height exceeded by only 2% of
incident waves. This probabilistic perspective was a turning point, recognising that design against maximum
run-up requires statistical treatment of irregular wave trains.
The 1970s also marked the publication of the Shore Protection Manual (US), which consolidated empirical
datasets into design charts and formulas. The SPM formulations expressed run-up as a function of the surf sim-
ilarity parameter, explicitly accounting for breaker type. These semi-empirical relations were quickly adopted
in engineering practice and became a standard reference for decades.
While laboratory studies dominated early research, the 1980s and 1990s saw a growing recognition that field
conditions could differ substantially from laboratory flume results. Holman (1986), through video monitoring
of natural beaches, provided important datasets on swash and run-up statistics. Similarly, Nielsen & Hanslow
(1991) studied run-up variability on natural beaches, highlighting tidal influence and wave group effects. These
works underlined the complexity of natural run-up processes, which are influenced not only by slope and wave
steepness but also by beach morphology, infragravity waves, and wave groups.
During this period, probabilistic formulations became increasingly central. Design practice shifted towards
quantiles of the run-up distribution, with Ru2% becoming widely adopted as a design parameter for coastal
structures.
The need for broadly applicable predictive formulas for natural beaches led to the development of more refined
empirical models. Among these, the widely cited work of Stockdon et al. (2006) proposed a predictive parame-
terisation of Ru2% based on a large dataset of field observations. This formula remains one of the most widely
used in coastal hazard assessment, particularly in beach environments.
With increasing computational power, numerical models emerged as powerful tools for run-up prediction. Boussinesq-
type models and nonlinear shallow water models allowed detailed simulation of wave shoaling, breaking, and
swash dynamics. Later, fully three-dimensional Navier–Stokes solvers with Volume-of-Fluid (VOF) techniques
enabled further study of turbulence and porous media interaction.
Modern studies increasingly combine empirical, numerical, and probabilistic approaches. EurOtop (2018), for
instance, integrates laboratory data, field measurements, and numerical simulations to provide updated overtop-
ping and run-up guidance for coastal engineers.
4.1.1
Current state of the art in wave run-up prediction
As mentioned, wave run-up is inherently a stochastic phenomenon, driven by the irregular and random nature
of sea states. It is important to emphasise that the maximum run-up height is not necessarily associated with
the absolute highest incoming wave, but rather with the highest non-breaking wave capable of propagating up
the slope without significant energy dissipation. This implies that neglecting the probability of wave breaking
can lead to systematic overprediction of run-up levels, particularly in sea states where breaking is expected to
occur frequently.
In practice, the prediction of wave run-up on coastal structures is typically approached through empirical and
semi-empirical relationships derived from physical model tests. The most widely applied formulation in Europe
is presented in EurOtop (2018), where the run-up exceeded by 2% of the incoming waves, Ru2%, is described
by two main equations. The mean value approach is given in eq. (4.2), while a limiting maximum condition is
presented in eq. (4.3).

reduces uncertainty by avoiding the additional complexities introduced by scale-dependent roughness and wind
effects, thus isolating the core hydrodynamic processes governing the wave run-up.

4.4.3
k–ϵ model in MIKE3W
The slight overprediction of run-up in MIKE3W without the k–ϵ model, for values of ξm−1,0 ≲1.4, compared
with the underprediction obtained when the k–ϵ model is included, suggests that the turbulence model may be
generating excessive dissipation.
The issue of turbulence overproduction in RANS models is well-documented in the literature (see Mayer &
Madsen (2001)). This tendency is partly attributed to the conditional instability of turbulence models in regions
that are close to potential flow. To address this, Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) proposed an extended formulation
of the k–ϵ model. Kærgaard et al. (2019) investigated its application in MIKE3W and demonstrated that the
modified formulation improves the representation of turbulence outside the surf zone and, as a result, yields a
better reproduction of inner surf zone dynamics when compared with the classic observations by Ting & Kirby
(1994). Nevertheless, the problem of excessive turbulent kinetic energy (TKE) production was still observed.

Concluding remarks of the k −ϵ model
The suspicion of turbulent kinetic energy (TKE) overprediction, inferred from the underestimation of wave
run-up when applying the k–ϵ model, has been further investigated. A comparison of the time-averaged, non-
dimensionalised turbulence levels in the inner surf zone with the experimental measurements of Ting & Kirby
(1994) revealed an overprediction of approximately 40%. This magnitude of overprediction is consistent with
previous findings, where Lemos (1992) and Lin & Liu (1998) reported values in the range of 25–50%. The
turbulence level in the swash zone was seemingly comparable to the levels found by Petti & Longo (2001).
Several potential sources of the discrepancy were examined, including the number of sigma layers, sigma pa-
rameter settings, bed resistance, and scaling effects. However, none of these adjustments produced significant
changes in the predicted TKE levels. It is therefore concluded that the overprediction of TKE cannot be at-
tributed to inappropriate parameter choices, but rather originates from inherent limitations of the turbulence
closure itself. This conclusion is in agreement with the broader literature, which highlights the challenges of
accurately modelling turbulence in breaking waves and the general tendency of RANS models to overpredict
TKE.

One contributing factor may be the neglect of air entrainment in the roller. The mixing of air and water within
the roller reduces the effective density, which limits the downward penetration of turbulence. Consequently, a
substantial portion of the turbulence is dissipated directly within the roller, rather than being transferred to the
underlying layers. This mechanism is discussed in further detail by Christensen et al. (2002). Since the modifi-
cation of turbulence models lies beyond the scope of this thesis, no further attempts to address this shortcoming
are undertaken here.
4.4.4
Assessment of model applicability for engineering design
Both MIKE21W and MIKE3W demonstrate good capability in predicting wave run-up, with MIKE3W gener-
ally providing greater accuracy. MIKE21W exhibits a tendency to slightly underpredict run-up. However, in
the plunging breaker regime, its results align closely with observations. As discussed in Section 4.2.3, this align-
ment is likely due to competing model behaviors: the artificial suppression of wave breaking, which increases
run-up, offsets the model’s general tendency to underpredict. Consequently, the good fit in this specific regime
may not indicate a physically consistent representation, and its robustness under a wider range of conditions
requires further investigation.
MIKE3W shows a slight tendency to overpredict run-up in the lower range of the plunging regime, approaching
the spilling breaker regime. For both models, the inclusion of advanced physics, specifically the surface roller
formulation in MIKE21W and the k–ϵ turbulence closure in MIKE3W, led to a decrease in agreement with the
measurements. Therefore, the use of these specific options is not advised for run-up estimation in engineering
design applications.
The strong performance of both models should be interpreted in the context of the relatively steep slopes used in
this study. MIKE21W, which is based on Boussinesq-type equations optimized for milder slopes, demonstrated
very good predictive capability. Its results suggest that the model’s application can be extended to steeper slopes
than those for which it was originally developed. Nonetheless, MIKE3W, with its ability to more fully resolve
vertical processes, achieved a higher degree of accuracy under these challenging conditions.
This makes MIKE21W an excellent tool for preliminary studies and scenarios where computational efficiency
is key, providing reliable estimates with low cost. MIKE3W, in turn, is the preferred choice for final, detailed
designs where maximum accuracy is required. Its slight tendency to overpredict in some regimes is a beneficial
trait for engineering, as it inherently falls on the side of safety.

5.1
Literature review and experimental comparison
Early overtopping research, dating back to studies such as Tominaga et al. (1967), relied primarily on physical
modelling and laboratory experiments on simple structures such as seawalls, breakwaters, and dikes. Founda-
tional guidance documents emerged in the late 20th century, including the UK Environment Agency Manual on
Overtopping Besley (1999), the Dutch TAW Technical Report on wave run-up and overtopping at dikes TAW
(2002), and the German guidelines für Küstenschutzwerke (2002).
A major milestone was the publication of the first EurOtop manual in 2007 (EurOtop (2007)), which consoli-
dated and expanded on earlier national guidelines. It incorporated significant new experimental data, notably
from the CLASH project (De Rouck et al. (2004)), which compiled an extensive database of overtopping tests
from different structure types and environmental conditions.
The second edition, EurOtop (2018), further refined prediction methods, introducing updated formulae that im-
proved accuracy for very low and zero freeboard conditions, steep slopes, and vertical structures with complex
geometries (e.g., foreshores, storm walls). It also provided enhanced treatment of individual overtopping wave
volumes and tolerable overtopping limits, along with multimedia resources (e.g., videos) on its dedicated web-
site. The continuous increase in overtopping-related publications reflects growing awareness of the problem,
especially in relation to extreme storms and climate resilience (Romão et al. (2024)).
Several approaches are used to study and predict wave overtopping, each with advantages, limitations, and
typical application contexts. The most widely applied in engineering practice are empirical models. They
relate dimensionless mean overtopping discharge to relative crest freeboard using exponential-type relationships,
originally proposed by Owen (1980) and later refined in EurOtop (2007, 2018).
EurOtop (2018) manual provides empirical formulations for the prediction of mean wave overtopping discharge
at coastal structures. Two sets of equations are proposed: a mean value approach, representing a best fit to ex-
perimental data, and a design and assessment approach, which incorporates a degree of conservatism to account
Validation of MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM for Wave Run-up and Overtopping
56
==PAGE:67==
for uncertainties. In this work, the mean value approach is adopted, as it offers a more accurate representation
of observed data and is thus more suitable for validation purposes.
The wave overtopping discharge is described using two separate formulae: one applicable to breaking waves
and one for non-breaking waves. These are presented in eq. (5.1) and eq. (5.2), respectively.
The general structure of the equations is derived from the original work by Owen (1980), in which overtopping
discharge was shown to decrease exponentially with increasing freeboard Rc. This formulation was further
developed in EurOtop (2007). However, subsequent studies, particularly van der Meer & Bruce (2014), demon-
strated that the 2007 formulae tended to overpredict overtopping discharges in cases of low or zero freeboard, re-
vealing the need for more flexible formulations. This led to the introduction of additional coefficients, ultimately
resulting in the updated equations published in EurOtop (2018), which are now considered state-of-the-art.

Recent developments have focused on using Machine Learning (ML) algorithms for overtopping prediction,

their use are discussed in section 5.1.2.
Physical models remain the benchmark for overtopping research, especially for complex geometries or com-
bined hydrodynamic and structural processes. Wave flume (2D) and basin (3D) experiments typically follow
Froude scaling, with scales ranging from 1 : 20 to 1 : 60.
Advantages include direct measurement of mean discharge, wave-by-wave volumes, and flow velocities, while

accounting for real fluid–structure interactions. On the other hand, challenges include scale effects, such as
those due to surface tension, viscosity, and Reynolds number that can distort results if not properly addressed,
and model effects, such as limitations due to flume width, absence of wind, or boundary reflections. A deeper
description of these phenomena is given in Frostick et al. (2011).
The usage of empirical models, usually referred to as overtopping prediction formulae, is constrained to the
experimental range of their underlying datasets and simplified structure geometries. Extrapolation towards out-
of-range parameter values or other structure types than those used for the fitting may result in inaccurate and
even invalid overtopping predictions.
Numerical modelling constitutes a powerful approach for predicting wave overtopping, providing a flexible
and detailed alternative to empirical formulations and physical model testing. By solving the governing fluid
dynamic equations, most commonly Boussinesq-type or Navier–Stokes equations, through numerical discreti-
sation techniques, these models enable the simulation of a wide range of hydrodynamic processes and structural
configurations. Their capacity to resolve time-dependent flow fields, pressure distributions, and overtopping
volumes makes them an indispensable tool for assessing overtopping under varying sea states and for structures
of increasing geometric complexity.
A fundamental distinction in numerical modelling of hydrodynamics and coastal processes lies between depth-
integrated and depth-resolving approaches. Depth-integrated models, such as the Nonlinear Shallow Water
(NLSW) equations or Boussinesq-type solvers, simplify the vertical structure of the flow by integrating the gov-
erning equations over the water column. This reduces computational complexity and allows for efficient larger-
scale simulations, at the cost of resolving detailed vertical dynamics. In contrast, depth-resolving models—
typically based on the Reynolds-Averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) equations and closed with turbulence models—
resolve the full vertical structure of the flow. These are more computationally demanding but are essential for
capturing vertical gradients in velocity, turbulence, and mixing, particularly in complex nearshore or structure-
interaction environments. Both modelling types are employed in this thesis (see Section 2.5).
Other types of models not used in this thesis include extensions of the OpenFOAM CFD code, which are general-
purpose, open-source computational fluid dynamics (CFD) tools capable of solving the full Navier–Stokes equa-
tions in 3D. These allow high-fidelity simulations of complex free-surface flows, turbulence, and wave-structure
interaction, but typically require significant computational resources and user expertise.
5.1.1
CLASH database
As described in van der Meer et al. (2009), the CLASH (Crest Level Assessment of Coastal Structures by
full-scale monitoring, neural network prediction and hazard analysis on permissible wave overtopping) project,
funded by the European Union under the Fifth Framework Programme, developed one of the most extensive
and homogeneous databases on wave overtopping ever assembled. The database was compiled between 2002
and 2004 and includes 10532 overtopping tests from 163 independent test series, sourced from more than 160
laboratory and field campaigns conducted worldwide.
The data incorporated into the CLASH database originates from a broad range of sources, such as:
• Historical overtopping tests from universities, research institutes, and engineering firms;
• Newly conducted tests under the CLASH project, specifically designed to fill ”white spots” in the existing
knowledge;
• Prototype measurements at full-scale coastal structures (e.g., at Ostia, Samphire Hoe, and Zeebrugge);
Validation of MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM for Wave Run-up and Overtopping
58
==PAGE:69==
• Supplementary tests conducted to explore specific phenomena, such as the influence of oblique wave
attack and the roughness of armour layers.
About 80% of the data comes from CLASH project partners, while the remaining data comes from other inter-
national contributors. Both public and confidential data are included, but no identifiable project-specific details
are retained, ensuring general applicability.
Each test in the database is described using 31 parameters, which are grouped as follows:
• General parameters: Test ID, reliability factor (RF), complexity factor (CF);
• Hydraulic parameters (11 total)
• Structural parameters (17 total)
Reliability and Complexity Factors
To evaluate the quality of each test, two subjective but systematically assigned indices are included. The Re-
liability Factor (RF) ranges from 1 (very reliable) to 4 (unreliable), based on measurement quality and data
completeness. The Complexity Factor (CF): Ranges from 1 (simple structure) to 4 (very complex), based on
how well the structure can be represented with the selected parameters.
These factors help users assess which tests are most suitable for specific research or design purposes.
Hydraulic parameters
Among these parameters, some describes deep water conditions (Hm0,deep, Tp deep, Tm deep, Tm−1,0 deep), some
toe conditions (Hm0,toe, Tp toe, Tm toe, Tm−1,0 toe). It is also specified the angle of wave attack relative to the
normal on the structure (β), the mean overtopping discharge q and the overtopping probability Pow.
Whenever some parameters were missing, because not measured or not noted during the tests, an acceptable
value was searched for. This has been done by assumptions based on previous study or further calculations. To
account to the introduced uncertainty of these values, a minimum RF of two was assigned.
Structural parameters
They include: the water depth at deep water hdeep, the measure of the foreshore slope m, the water depth in
front of the toe of the structure h, the toe width Bt, the toe depth ht and the roughness/permeability factor of
the structure γf. The slope of the structure below and above the berm is indicated respectively by cot αd and
cot αu. The mean slope of the structure, excluding and including the berm, is indicated respectively by cot αexcl
and cot αincl.
The berm is described by its slope tan αB, the width B, the water depth on the berm hb and the width of the
horizontally schematised berm Bh.
Finally, the crest freeboard of the structure Rc, the armour crest freeboard Ac and the crest width of the structure
Gc.

In addition to the available datasets such as the CLASH database, and the empirical formulations commonly
found in the literature (e.g., EurOtop (2018)), predictive methods based on Artificial Neural Network (ANNs)
modelling have been developed for wave overtopping estimation. Neural networks are data-driven models
widely used as generalised regression tools for capturing complex cause–and–effect relationships. In prac-

main phases: a training (learning) phase, during which the model identifies relationships from known data, and
a testing/validation phase, where the model’s predictive performance is assessed using an independent subset
of data.
The value of such tools becomes especially apparent when estimating overtopping in complex cases—for ex-

to fail entirely. In other instances, the model remained technically operational but produced unphysically high
overtopping rates due to isolated, unrealistically large discharge events triggered by singular extreme waves.
The minimum time step played a stabilising role and allowed for the use of lower Riemann numbers; however,
this effect was limited, as the model still exhibited instability beyond a certain threshold despite the reduced
time step.

number influences the random phase distribution of the wave components and, consequently, the timing and
magnitude of individual waves. This was particularly impactful in low-discharge conditions, where overtop-

mitigate this variability, longer simulation durations could be employed to capture a more representative statis-

Case selection from CLASH database

additional influence of berm geometry. The selection process involved a series of filtering steps, aimed not only

the timeframe of this thesis.

Wave height matching procedure
Both in MIKE21W and MIKE3W, the model was calibrated to reproduce the target incoming wave height at the
toe of the structure, Hm0, toe, as reported in the CLASH database. To achieve this, the modelled wave field was
decomposed into incident (right-going) and reflected (left-going) components, as described in Section 2.4. The
ratio between the desired and simulated incoming wave heights was computed and used to adjust the boundary
wave height iteratively. The simulation was repeated with the corrected input until the modelled incoming wave
height fell within ±1% of the target value.
This calibration procedure ensured that the numerical model accurately reproduced the experimental wave forc-
ing conditions, thereby enabling meaningful comparison of the resulting cumulative overtopping discharges.

5.7
Assessment of model applicability for engineering design
Both MIKE3W and MIKE21W demonstrate strong capabilities in predicting overtopping, particularly in situ-
ations where the discharge is influenced by multiple overtopping waves and the relative freeboard is not ex-
cessively large. Under such conditions, the agreement with experimental results is excellent, underlining their
applicability for most practical engineering cases.
At larger relative freeboards, overtopping increasingly occurs through splash and spray processes, where wa-
ter passes the crest as droplets rather than as coherent wave volumes. Neither MIKE3W nor MIKE21W can
explicitly resolve these processes due to their underlying formulations. Capturing such effects would likely
require higher-fidelity CFD approaches specifically designed for free-surface fragmentation. This, however, is
very computationally demanding, and the level of detail provided by such models is typically unnecessary for
practical engineering design.
From a design perspective, it is important to note that MIKE3W generally exhibits a positive relative bias,
leading to conservative predictions, while MIKE21W shows a slight negative relative bias, which may result in
underprediction. This systematic difference should be taken into account when selecting the most appropriate
tool for engineering assessments.
When the models are applied to zero-overtopping evaluations, as described in Section 2.2, longer simulation
durations are required. This is not a shortcoming of the models themselves but rather a consequence of the
stochastic nature of wave generation, where rare overtopping events demand sufficient simulation time for
statistical convergence.
Another practical consideration is computational demand. For the relatively simple geometries used in this
study, a typical MIKE21W simulation required about 3–4 minutes, whereas MIKE3W required approximately
Validation of MIKE 21/3 Wave Model FM for Wave Run-up and Overtopping

==PAGE:90==
15 minutes, i.e. four to five times longer. While this difference may appear modest for small domains, it be-
comes significant when modelling larger geometries, including effects such as wave spreading or more complex
boundary conditions.
Ease of use is an important factor for practical applicability. The MIKE interface is structured such that transi-
tioning from a two-dimensional to a three-dimensional configuration requires only minor modifications. This
design facilitates the transfer of results from preliminary design phases, typically performed in MIKE21W, to
more detailed analyses in MIKE3W with minimal additional effort.
Overall, both models perform very well in cases with significant overtopping discharges, making them suitable
and reliable tools for engineering design. Based on SCI and relative bias, approximate applicability ranges are
Rc/Hm0 ≲1.7 for MIKE21W and Rc/Hm0 ≲2.3 for MIKE3W. Beyond these thresholds, seed dependence
becomes increasingly important and should be examined more carefully. It remains the user’s responsibility
to judge when the limitations of Boussinesq-type models are critical, such as under highly nonlinear wave
conditions or steeply varying bathymetries. In many practical settings, the comparatively low computational
cost of MIKE21W makes it an efficient and accurate option, especially in early design phases.

Conclusion
This thesis has evaluated the capability of the MIKE21W and MIKE3W numerical models in reproducing wave
run-up and overtopping processes, with validation against experimental benchmark datasets. The results provide
insight into the relative strengths, weaknesses, and applicability of the two modelling approaches in coastal
engineering practice. The following addresses the research questions posed at the outset, followed by practical
design considerations.
Under which conditions is MIKE21W suitable for accurately simulating wave run-up and overtopping,
and when are more advanced models like MIKE3W required?
MIKE21W is appropriate when a computationally efficient, early-phase assessment is needed, particularly for
moderate wave conditions and overtopping regimes where multiple waves contribute to the mean discharge. It
captures the dominant physical mechanisms qualitatively and provides sufficiently accurate average overtop-
ping estimates for moderate relative freeboards, making it useful for preliminary design and screening studies.
MIKE21W tends to slightly underestimate run-up for non-breaking waves while showing close agreement in
the plunging breaker regime. However, the model’s alignment in the plunging breaker regime is likely due
to suppressed breaking compensating for a general underprediction bias. Whether this agreement holds under
different conditions is a key question for future work.
MIKE3W becomes necessary when higher fidelity is required across a broader range of conditions, such as in
detailed final design, safety-critical assessments, or situations involving complex three-dimensional effects and
rapidly varying bathymetries. The model produced robust run-up predictions for ξm−1,0 > 1.4 and demon-
strated more consistent reliability overall, making it the preferred choice when accuracy outweighs computa-
tional efficiency.
How well do the MIKE21W and MIKE3W models reproduce benchmark data from the CLASH database
and empirical formulations from EurOtop (2018), in terms of predicting wave run-up and overtopping?
Both models successfully capture the main trends observed in the benchmark data, providing a solid basis
for engineering use. However, systematic, model-dependent biases remain. For run-up, MIKE21W generally
yielded slight underestimations. Its closer agreement in the plunging breaker regime should be interpreted
cautiously, as it likely results from a compensating effect between two opposing tendencies, one leading to
overprediction and the other to underprediction (as discussed previously). These tendencies may not consistently
balance in other cases.
MIKE3W achieved good agreement for larger surf similarity parameters (ξm−1,0 > 1.4) but tended to overpre-
dict run-up at smaller ξm−1,0, owing to the absence of turbulence-induced dissipation in the tested configura-
tions. Incorporation of the k–ϵ turbulence closure introduced excessive dissipation, thereby reducing accuracy.
Analysis of the turbulent kinetic energy (TKE) further confirmed the well-documented RANS tendency to over-
produce turbulence in breaking waves.
For overtopping, both models reproduced mean discharges well at moderate relative freeboards, showing excel-
lent agreement with experimental data. Predictive skill declined at higher freeboards, where splash and spray
processes, not explicitly resolved in both models, become increasingly important. A slight systematic bias was
evident, with MIKE3W tending towards overprediction and MIKE21W towards underprediction. Differences

in predictive accuracy were also reflected in the scatter index (SCI), which exceeded unity at a relative freeboard,
Rc/Hm0, of 1.7 for MIKE21W and 2.3 for MIKE3W.
Furthermore, cases with very low overtopping rates (q ≲10−3 m3/s/m) proved sensitive to wave-phase ran-
domness (random seed), requiring long-duration simulations to ensure statistical convergence. Overall, the
results show good agreement with CLASH and EurOtop (2018) trends in regimes where the dominant physical
processes are represented by the models, while discrepancies arise in regimes controlled by breaking turbulence,
splash/spray processes, or stochastic variability.
What are the computational and practical trade-offs between MIKE21W and MIKE3W in engineering
applications (setup complexity, simulation time, and accuracy)?
The trade-offs between the two models are clear and context-dependent. MIKE21W offers substantially faster
runtimes: in this study, typical simulations required only ≈3–4 minutes, compared with ≈15 minutes for
MIKE3W with 10 sigma layers (a factor of 4–5). This computational efficiency makes MIKE21W attractive
for parametric studies, early-phase design, or large-domain applications where many runs are needed.
By contrast, MIKE3W, although more computationally demanding, delivers higher fidelity and more reliable
predictions across a broader set of conditions. The model’s modest systematic overprediction can be conserva-
tive from a design safety perspective.
Practically, the GUI support for transitioning from 2D (MIKE21W) to 3D (MIKE3W) streamlines workflows;
early MIKE21W results can be carried into MIKE3W for detailed follow-up with limited extra setup effort.
However, the added setup complexity and run time of MIKE3W become important considerations for large and
complex domains.
Practical recommendations for engineering design
• MIKE21W is recommended for preliminary design and situations where computational speed is important
and the targeted wave/run-up regime is moderate.
• MIKE3W is recommended for final design, safety-critical assessments, or when three-dimensional effects,
or higher-fidelity run-up/overtopping estimates are required.
• Avoid relying on the inclusion of MIKE21W’s breaking treatment or on the k–ϵ closure model in MIKE3W
for run-up and overtopping design without careful validation, since both can introduce excessive dissipa-
tion and reduce accuracy.
• For overtopping at very low discharges (q ≲10−3 m3/s/m), plan longer simulations to overcome seed
sensitivity and ensure statistical convergence. It should also be noted that splash and spray processes are
not explicitly modelled.
• Approximate applicability thresholds are Rc/Hm0 ≲1.7 for MIKE21W and Rc/Hm0 ≲2.3 for MIKE3W,
beyond which seed dependence becomes critical. Model selection, however, should not rely on a single
freeboard or wave parameter. It should instead be guided by the design stage, problem complexity, avail-
able computational resources, and the required accuracy.
In summary, both MIKE21W and MIKE3W are valuable and defensible tools for coastal engineering when
applied with an awareness of their respective limitations. MIKE21W offers an efficient and practical solution
for early-stage assessments, while MIKE3W provides the fidelity required for detailed design and safety-critical

guide targeted three-dimensional refinement where higher accuracy is needed.
6.1
Future work
The present study has established a foundation for the systematic validation of MIKE21W and MIKE3W in
predicting wave run-up and overtopping, using both the CLASH database and the empirical formulations that
underpin EurOtop (2018). Building on this work, several directions for future research can be identified.
A natural next step is to expand the validation effort by parametrically reproducing the full range of cases con-
tained in the CLASH database. Automating the generation of computational meshes through a parametric mesh
generator would make it possible to systematically test the models across all available structure geometries,
slopes, and wave conditions. Such a framework would reduce human bias in case selection and provide a more
comprehensive picture of model performance. Furthermore, coupling these systematic validation results with
machine learning techniques could help to identify recurring patterns of model bias, quantify relative shortcom-
ings, and propose correction factors or safety coefficients for engineering design.
Another promising line of investigation is the inclusion of structural porosity and permeability in the modelling
framework. Many coastal defenses, such as rubble-mound breakwaters, dissipate wave energy through inter-
nal flow and turbulence within their porous cores. Accounting for this effect is essential for a more realistic
representation of wave transformation and overtopping processes.
In addition, the present study has focused primarily on normally incident waves. In practice, wave obliquity
and directional spreading play a significant role in governing both run-up and overtopping. Future model devel-
opment and validation should therefore address multidirectional wave conditions, both to increase realism and
to align more closely with design practice in coastal engineering.
Finally, as shown in Section 2.5.3, turbulence closure remains a key challenge. The standard k–ϵ model tends to
overestimate turbulence in certain conditions, leading to discrepancies in overtopping predictions. Future work
should explore improvement of the turbulence closure schemes. Although this extends beyond the specific scope
of MIKE21W and MIKE3W, improvements in turbulence modelling would benefit a wide range of coastal and
hydraulic applications where accurate overtopping prediction is critical.
