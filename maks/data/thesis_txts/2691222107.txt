Abstract
Germanium-Vacancy (GeV) colour centres in diamond are a promising platform for quantum optics and
quantum information processing. They are highly ямВuorescent via a zero-phonon transition, which makes
them exceptionally coherent quantum emitters. In this work, a set-up has been build to study the resonance
ямВuorescence of single GeV colour centres at a temperature of 4K. Single emitters are addressed using a
confocal arrangement. The set-up uses cross-polarization rejection to reject excitation light with an optical
density over 6. This allows the collection of resonance ямВuorescence emitted through the zero-phonon line.
The power dependence of the emission is studied, measuring decoherence limited linewidths as low as
90 ┬▒ 2MHz. The transition frequency of some GeV centres has been observed to hop between diямАerent
values. This is attributed to second degree Stark eямАect due to charge ямВuctuations in the surroundings in the
neighbourhood of the emitter. All emitters measured stop their resonance ямВuorescence during periods of
time in the order of milliseconds, this is attributed to the emitter shelving to a long-lived state. By studying
the resonance ямВuorescence over time, the transition to this state and the relaxation out of this state has
proved to be driven by the excitation laser. The success of this set-up opens the door to investigations of
the nature of the resonance ямВuorescence, such as its spectrum, photon-photon correlations and presence of
quadrature squeezing.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Quantum optics describes the quantum mechanics of light, this includes the rich behavior of light when
it interacts with atom-like systems. This phenomena ends up explaining the everyday light-matter in-
teraction where light interacts with large numbers of atoms at room temperature. In this case, the light
has been devoid of its most quantum-mechanical properties. Behaviours such as the anti-bunching of the
emitted photons [1] or the squeezing of the quantum ямВuctuations of the ямБeld [2] cannot be observed when
experimenting with a collection of atoms. Ion traps and laser-cooling were the beginning of quantum op-
tics experiments with single atoms [3]. This allowed for the ямБrst measurement of photon anti-bunching
in the resonance ямВuorescence of a sodium ion as early as 1977 [4]. In contrast, measuring the quadrature
squeezing in resonance ямВuorescence happened recently, thirty years after its prediction, in 2015 [5]. The
key to this experiment was using solid-state quantum emitters. A part from bypassing the complications of
trapping and cooling single atoms, solid-state emitters such as quantum dots have higher dipole moments
that allow for more ямВuorescence and easier collection.
Good platforms for quantum optics experiments also make good platforms for quantum information
processing (QIP). In particular, establishing large quantum networks requires ямБne control of the coupling
of light to matter [6]. Light is the best platform for transporting quantum information, qubits can be en-
coded in single photons or as continuous-variables in the quadratures of the light ямБeld and then transferred
through optical ямБbers. Single quantum emitters can be used as a single photon source and, most critically,
quantum memories, which repeaters in quantum networks should have in order to overcome the problem
of attenuation in optical ямБbers.
The interest in developing QIP technologies has fueled the search for easily addressable single quantum
emitters. As an alternative to trapped ions and nanofabricated artiямБcial atoms, colour centres in diamond
have proven to be an outstanding platform for quantum optics and QIP.
1.1
Colour centres in diamond
Colour centres are crystallographic defects that give coloration to transparent large band-gap crystals [7].
These defects usually consist in vacancies and/or substitutional atoms. Diamond, with its large 5.47 eV
bandgap [8], can host a diversity of colour centres. These defects have an electronic structure within the
band-gap of the diamond and include long-lived spin-states and highly coherent optical transitions. This
defects can be synthesised with densities low enough so that a single emitter can be addressed with a fo-
cused laser beam. This makes them highly interesting for quantum optics experiments and for applications
in QIP[9].

1.1.3
The Germanium-Vacancy colour centre
The remarkable properties of the SiV centre prompted a search for other group-IV defects. The large
interstitial space in the split-vacancy structure allows for heavier group-IV atoms to be implanted [16]. This
includes germanium (GeV centres) [21][22], tin (SNV centres) [23][24] and lead (PbV centers) [25][26]. The
Germanium-Vacancy center, and other group-IV defects, share most of its properties with the SiV centre.
It may be neutrally (GeV0) or negatively (GeVтИТ) charged, which determines its electronic spin to S = 1
or S = 1/2 respectively. As the other group-IV defects, it is usually found in the GeVтИТstate. Most of its
ямВuorescence is emitted through the ZPL at 602nm [21][22]. The level structure of the GeVтИТcan be seen
in ямБgure 1.2. The ground state sub-levels are labeled 1 and 2 and excited sub-levels are labeled 3 and 4.
Allowed transitions are labeled A, B, C and D, the majority of the ZPL emission comes from the C transition
[22].
GeVтИТcenters have been implanted in nanophotonic waveguides and demonstrated high quantum ef-
ямБciency without the need of cavities [28]. Optical and microwave spin control of a GeVтИТcenter has been
achieved by applying a strong magnetic-ямБeld to create Zeeman splitting in the four levels. At 2K, the Zee-
man sub-levels are driven by GHz microwaves and the spin relaxation is measured to be over 20┬╡s and a
spin coherence time of about 19ns is measured [29]. The strain dependence of the energy diямАerence be-
tween levels has been used to tune two GeVтИТcenters at the same frequency [30]. This is an important step
to establish QIP protocols that require indistinguishable photons.

4.1
Spectral jumps
In the process of measuring the RF as function of frequency detuning, the majority of the emitters have
been observed to suddenly change their transition frequency. For a given emitter, these jumps in frequency
are restricted to a certain set of frequencies. Usually the emitters hop between two frequencies, although
emitters switching between up to six frequencies have been observed. RF scans of 4 emitters are shown in
ямБgure 4.1, where it can be seen that the spacing between these frequencies is varied, the smallest jumps
recorded are around tenths of GHz and the largest multiple GHz. This phenomenon cannot be caused by
sudden changes in the excitation laser frequency because the structure of the frequency modes is unique
for each emitter and constant over time.
Although spectral jumps in GeV centers have previously been reported [30], there are no published
in-depth studies of this phenomenon. The proposed cause for the spectral jumps is that charge ямВuctuations
in the neighbourhood of the emitter can induce second-order Stark eямАect. Adding small amounts of non-
resonant 532nm light does not prevent the spectral jumps.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
This project has been successful in its goal to build a set-up capable of exciting single GeV centres and
collecting their RF through the ZPL.
The cross-polarization rejection set-up that has been built is able to reject resonant excitation light with
an OD up to 6.6. Although this value is lower than ODs reported in similar set-ups, it is still higher than
the intrinsic extinction rates of the polarizer and PBSs, which has been achieved by taking advantage of the
Imbert-Federov eямАect. Achieving this cross-polarization rejection eямГcacy has required selecting polarizing
elements with minimal eямАect on the beam proямБle, ямБnding a lens and a support mechanism for it that could
cool to cryogenic temperatures without aямАecting the polarization of the beam and careful alignment and
collimation of the beam in order to use a SMF to spatially ямБlter cross-polarized light. Furthermore, precise
orientation of the polarizer and the correcting QWP have proved critical in achieving high OD. This has
been done using motorized rotation stages which are programmatically oriented to maximize the OD. All
this conditions make the OD highly sensitive to mechanical disturbances in the set-up, which are constant
due to the oscillations created by the cryostat pumps. The set-up has allowed collection of RF with SNRs of
up to 10. The SNR has been observed to decrease for lower excitation powers due to the limited RF emitted
and collected in comparison to the dark count-rate of the APD used as detectors. The SNR also decreases
for higher-powers due to the increasing fraction of the excitation light that does not get rejected.
The RF emission of the C transition of GeV centres has been studied as a function of the excitation
power and detuning from the transition frequency. Sudden changes in transition frequency have been
observed, emitters switching between up to six frequencies have been observed. This is attributed to charge
ямВuctuations in the vicinity of the center that cause second-order Stark eямАect. Two emitters without spectral
jumps have been studied by measuring their RF and scanning the detuning of the excitation light, this is
done for a range of excitation powers. The RF as a function of detuning has been observed to follow the
Lorentzian proямБle that is expected from the theory of two- and three-level systems. The intensity of the
RF at zero detuning shows a saturation behavior which has also been predicted for two- and three-level
systems. Non-power broadened linewidths are measured by extrapolating the linewidth of the Lorentzian
proямБle to zero excitation power. The linewidths measured are 90 ┬▒ 2MHz for one emitter and 97 ┬▒ 3MHz
for the other. This values are higher than what is reported for similar temperatures, this is caused by the
Lorentzian proямБle being broadened by spectral diямАusion, but still close to the life-time limited linewidth.
Under resonant excitation, emitters have been observed to stop emitting RF temporarily. This is inter-
preted as the emitter being shelved in a third long-lived state. Time-traces of the RF of one emitter has
been measured and analyzed to obtain statistics of the duration of RF emitting periods and dark periods as
a function of power. The duration of RF emission follows an exponential distribution with a rate param-
eter that increases quadratically with power until it starts to saturate. This indicates transitions involving
double-photon absorption. The rate parameter of the dark periods also increases with power, thus the
relaxation out of the shelf state is also driven by the excitation power.
Concluding, this project has built the base for further study of the optical characteristics of the RF of
GeV centres.
