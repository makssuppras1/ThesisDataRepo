Abstract 
Historically, energy access has been excluded from the humanitarian response, 
especially regarding clean cooking interventions. This situation has brought 90% of the 
world's forcibly displaced population to levels of energy poverty, with its associated well-
known negative impacts. Most of the solutions designed by humanitarian programmes are 
conditioned by their usual budget constraints and short-term mandates, leading to 
inefficient and unsustainable free-distribution models that are not aligned with the long-
term needs of the displaced population. To deal with these limitations, market-based 
approaches, where the provision of clean cooking services is led by the private sector, are 
being increasingly promoted.  
This research aims to investigate the role of a Global LPG Fund to support the clean 
cooking transition in displacement settings by providing LPG solutions - a cleaner, safer, 
and lower-carbon option than the primary fuels currently used. Additionally, this research 
explores how the fund can contribute to creating an LPG market in contexts of displacement 
and what financial mechanisms can the fund use to engage the private sector in market-
based interventions. Finally, some of the key considerations needed to set up the Global LPG 
Fund are analysed, and a set of recommendations for the fund’s future steps are suggested. 
To do so, a qualitative research method is applied, based on a review of the existing 
literature and interviews with relevant stakeholders in the field. The research concludes 
that a Global LPG Fund could positively accelerate the transition to clean cooking systems 
in displacement settings and play an enabling role in attracting the private sector.

1. Introduction  
More than 1% of the world's population is forcibly displaced and approximately 90% 
of them live in energy poverty, subject to all the well-known negative impacts this creates 
(UNHCR, 2021a). Energy access has historically been excluded from the humanitarian 
response in displacement settings, especially when referred to modern and clean energy 
cooking services (Whitehouse, 2019). 
In recent years the issue has gained traction and remarkable initiatives have been 
put in place. The UN's “Clean Energy Challenge” campaign, launched at the 2019 Global 
Refugee Forum, aims to achieve the SDG7 target 7.1. “Ensure universal access to affordable, 
reliable and modern energy services by 2030” for displaced persons and host communities, 
and directly targets “Energy for cleaner cooking” as one of the key development areas. 
Aligned with the sought progress on the SDG7 indicator 7.1.2 "Proportion of population with 
primary reliance on clean fuels and technology" (UN, 2022; UNHCR, 2021a). The global 
commitment to this issue was reaffirmed in September 2021, at the UN's High-Level 
Dialogue on Energy with the “leave no one behind” principle on SDG7. At the country level, 
progressive host governments have expressed a desire to see investment in and around 
displaced communities, also to deliver development co-benefits for their own populations 
living in energy poverty (SEforALL, 2021; UN, 2021). 
As for the technology able to provide this transition, there are few options for clean 
and modern cooking energy, with feasible business and financing models, especially for 
low-income households. While reliable data on this matter is still limited, some studies 
evidence the high impact of cooking fuel purchase/collection on the refugee’s household 
economies (Tran et al., 2021). Therefore, it is fair to assume that there is latent demand, 
ability, and willingness to pay for modern technologies, as are Liquified Petroleum Gas 
(LPG) cooking systems (Haselip et al., 2022). The LPG is a cleaner, safer, and lower-carbon 
option than most of the primary fuels currently used in displacement settings, such as 
firewood or charcoal. It is a mature technology that has been chosen in many countries to 
promote the clean cooking transition and presents a well-established supply chain, market 
competition and suppliers worldwide (Puzzolo et al., 2019).  
Despite the global commitment and the evidenced demand for clean cooking 
solutions among the displaced population, the transition remains hampered by the inherent 
challenging conditions in the displacement contexts and the inefficient solutions the 
humanitarian sector has been offering. The short-term and limited funding of humanitarian 
programmes leads to free handouts of energy technologies for cooking, which are 
inadequate and ill-suited solutions for meeting the long-term needs in protracted crises 
(Bisaga & To, 2021). 
Targeting this problem, the Global LPG Partnership (GLPGP), together with the 
United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR), developed a concept note for a 
Global LPG Fund, which is currently under consideration. The fund aims to address the LPG 
potential in displacement settings while financially supporting interventions following

market-based approaches which, led by the private sector, intend to ensure durable 
solutions (Haselip et al., 2022).  
This research aims to investigate the role of this fund in the clean cooking transition 
in displacement settings, as well as to explore the fund’s considerations when creating an 
LPG market, using financial mechanisms to engage the private sector, and setting up the 
financing facility. Finally, a set of key recommendations for the next steps of the fund are 
suggested. 
1.1 Learning objectives 
This research is motivated by the humanitarian need for clean energy access for 
cooking. The harmful impacts of the current cooking systems in displacement settings are a 
serious issue that must be tackled. From this starting point and given the needs of the 
research, different learning objectives are expected to be integrated: 
▪ 
Analyse the current situation in displacement settings in the Global South with 
regards to energy access for cooking and the different approaches that are being 
applied to promote a clean transition. 
▪ 
Identify the main barriers to achieving a durable transition to clean energy 
cooking systems in displacement settings. 
▪ 
Design a suitable methodology for a qualitative research aligned with the project 
needs. 
▪ 
Develop an interview questionnaire to extract valuable insights from experts in 
the field. 
▪ 
Analyse the benefits of creating a Global LPG Fund to accelerate the clean cooking 
transition. 
▪ 
Summarise the key results and suggest relevant recommendations for the Global 
LPG Fund. 
1.2 Research questions and goals 
To shed some light on the clean cooking energy services gap in displacement settings, 
the present research has been driven by the research question “how can the Global LPG 
Fund contribute to the transition to clean cooking systems in displacement settings 
in the Global South?”. 
In order to be able to answer the question, research sub-questions were designed to 
cover the different areas of action of the fund. The research sub-questions are: 
▪ 
How the Global LPG Fund can overcome market barriers for LPG cooking 
solutions in contexts of displacement, creating a resilient and durable market? 
▪ 
What are the financial mechanisms that the Global LPG Fund can use to ensure a 
greater engagement of the private sector in sustainable market-based energy 
interventions? 
▪ 
What are the key considerations for setting up an LPG financing facility?

2. Literature review 
The following section explores three of the major topics closely related to the 
research question: the energy access for displaced populations, the transition to clean and 
modern cooking systems and the use of LPG as a suitable solution for that purpose. These 
are essential topics to understand the context where this research takes place. 
2.1 Energy access for displaced population 
The UNHCR (2021a) estimated that the number of forced displacement persons 
worldwide surpassed 84 million by mid-2021, being over 85% of them displaced within or 
into developing countries. This means that more than 1% of the total world population is 
forcibly displaced (UNFPA, 2022). In addition, the number of people displaced has been 
growing steadily every year, as shown in Figure 1. In consequence, the phenomenon has 
become a permanent global concern, particularly for sending, transit, and hosting states, 
that has influenced international geopolitics (Cedillo & Espín, 2021; UNHCR, 2021a). 
 
Figure 1: Overview of the number of forcibly displaced persons in the recent years 
Source: UNHCR, 2021c 
Despite energy being generally recognised as a basic human right (GPA, 2021), its 
access has historically been excluded from the humanitarian assistance programmes 
(Whitehouse, 2019). Consequently, the majority of the displaced people lack access to clean, 
safe and secure energy services, limiting significantly their quality of life (Lehne et al., 2016). 
2.1.1 Forced displacement in the Global South

Displaced Persons (IDPs) (Tran et al., 2021). As defined by the UNHCR (2011),  a refugee is 
someone unable or unwilling to return to their country of origin owing to a well-founded 
fear of being persecuted for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular 
social group, or political opinion. Thus, refugees are people who have crossed international 
borders to seek safety and are no longer living in their country of origin. If the claim for the 
refugee status has not yet been determined, then that person is known as an asylum seeker

Besides the type of displacement, there are other factors to consider when describing 
these contexts, such as the scale and location of the settings and the longevity of the crisis. 
The scale accounts for the size of the setting in terms of people living in it, which can go from 
a few thousand to several hundred of thousand people. As for the location, settings can be 
considered rural camps, peri-urban or urban settlements. Rural camps are typically located 
in remote areas and are very dependent on humanitarian support given the lack of services 
nearby. Urban settings are close to populated centres and accommodate large shares of 
displaced population (Tran et al., 2021). Longevity refers to the time the displacement crisis 
has been ongoing. If it remains for less than 5 years it is described as a humanitarian 
emergency, while a protracted crisis affects >25,000 people and persists for >5 years (Tran 
et al., 2021; UNHCR, 2019). Vianello (2016) reports that the average length of time spent as 
a refugee is 17 years and the UNHCR (2019) estimated that, by the end of 2020, 15.7 million 
refugees (76% of the total) were experiencing a protracted situation.  
There are only three possible durable solutions to situations of displacement, beyond 
the immediate humanitarian assistance: voluntary repatriation to the home country, 
resettlement to a third country and local integration in the host country. (UNHCR, 2021b). 
Zetter (2014a) underlines the complexity of finding the most appropriate long-term 
solution for displaced persons. All of them require from high cooperation and support 
among international organisations and NGOs, and a decided intention from the different 
authorities and stakeholders involved. Nowadays, the national agendas are especially 
doomed to the inclusion of the migratory crises among their priorities, especially after the 
adoption of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (Cedillo & Espín, 2021; UN, 2022).  
Among all the SDGs, the present research focuses on the SDG7, and how affordable 
and clean energy can be provided to persons living in a context of forced displacement. 
However, as presented later, by tackling this issue there is a shared benefit across several 
other SDGs (Tran et al., 2021).  
2.1.2 Affordable and clean energy (SDG7) in contexts of displacement 
Energy access has historically been excluded from the humanitarian response in 
displacement settings (Whitehouse, 2019). In contrast, other basic needs, such as food, 
water, education, shelter or health, have been the target of humanitarian interventions for 
decades. Thomas et al. (2021) hold the Inter-Agency Standing Committee (IASC) Cluster 
System as responsible (Figure 3). The cluster, adopted in 2005, was designed to improve 
the effectiveness of emergency humanitarian response and coordination among 
organisations. It includes the types of humanitarian interventions split into sectors, which 
are led by different agencies. For instance, education is led by UNICEF and Save the Children, 
while health is led by WHO. However, the cluster does not include energy (Rosenberg-
Jansen, 2020; Thomas et al., 2021).  
The absence of governance and prioritisation of energy interventions has led to a 
severe lack of access to clean and sustainable energy among the displaced population. In 
refugee camps, 97% of the people have limited or no access to electricity and 85% are forced 
to burn biomass like firewood for cooking (Lahn & Grafham, 2015). Responding to this issue, 
==PAGE:14==
 
7 
emerges the humanitarian energy sector, which aims to provide energy access and cover 
the energy needs of persons suffering war, famine, violence and persecution, climate 
change, or natural disasters, and who, consequently, are living in emergency situations that 
may, or not, lead to protracted crises (Rosenberg-Jansen, 2020).  
 
Figure 3: IASC Cluster System  
Source: Thomas et al., 2021 
With the current trends, the achievement of SDG7 can be compromised in the context 
of forced displacement. Consequently, practitioners and organisations within the 
humanitarian energy sector have made repeated international commitments to raise 
awareness and bring the topic to the table in recent years (GPA, 2021). In 2018 was 
established the UN-led Global Platform for Action on Sustainable Energy in Displacement 
Settings (GPA), a global initiative to promote actions that enable sustainable energy access 
in displacement settings. In addition, in the 2019 Global Refugee Forum, the UNHCR 
launched the Clean Energy Challenge (CEC) (UNITAR, 2018).  
The CEC aims that settlements of forcibly displaced people and nearby host 
communities (HCs) will have access to affordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy 
by 2030. This is a clear commitment to pursue the SDG7 target 7.1 within contexts of 
displacement. One of the CEC’s key areas of development is “Energy for cleaner cooking”, 
which seeks to make progress on the indicator 7.1.2 of the SDG7, “Proportion of population 
with primary reliance on clean fuels and technology” (UN, 2022; UNHCR, 2021a). 
Furthermore, in September 2021, at the UN High-level Dialogue on Energy, the global 
commitment to “leave no one behind” on SDG7 was endorsed by world leaders, donors, 
companies, and the international community (SEforALL, 2021; UN, 2021). 
==PAGE:15==
 
8 
While progress is happening at the global and political levels, and progressively host 
countries have expressed a desire to see investment in and around displaced communities, 
changes on the ground are required to ensure real improvement in displaced people’s lives. 
The free handouts of energy services have dominated the space, resulting in inadequate and 
inefficient strategies. These ill-suited practices, together with the contextual and structural 
barriers in the humanitarian energy space are challenging the transition to sustainable 
energy access solutions, more particularly when targeting clean and modern energy 
solutions for cooking (Grafham, 2020; Vianello, 2016; Whitehouse, 2019).  
2.2 Transition to clean and modern cooking systems 
The cooking systems in displacement settings have not been a priority among 
humanitarian organisations for a long time, leading people to source their own fuels and 
cook the food within the available means (Bisaga & To, 2021; Vianello, 2016). The process 
of cooking presents a large number of negative impacts on the displaced population, from 
health risks to environmental degradation. Fortunately, in recent years, the topic is gaining 
traction and humanitarian agencies are looking for solutions to accelerate the transition to 
cleaner cooking systems (Tran et al., 2021).  
Nevertheless, the transition presents complicated challenges due to the uncertain 
conditions on the ground and the limited capacity of action of humanitarian agencies (Lahn 
& Grafham, 2015). Market-based solutions, led by the private sector have been promoted to 
overcome these barriers. Consequently, measures must be adopted to create a secure 
environment for private service providers, including the adoption of financial instruments 
that contribute to creating sustainable delivery models (Cohen & Patel, 2019). 
2.2.1 Cooking systems in contexts of displacement 
It is estimated that 85% of the refugees living in UNHCR managed camps rely on solid 
biomass fuels, such as firewood and charcoal for cooking (Tran et al., 2021). Figure 4 
presents the distribution of the primary fuel choice in refugees' households from a sample 
of 111 rural refugee settlements, mostly located across Africa (Haselip et al., 2022).  
As for the cookstoves used, Rivoal and Haselip (2017) observed that 79% of the 
families living in the Nyarugusu refugee camp in Tanzania were cooking on mud-stoves, 
while three-stone fires were used by 20% of the households (see Figure 5). Besides, 85% of 
the households reported that the cooking was done indoors, and 88% stated their 
ventilation system was based on a hole in the wall (or similar) to allow the smoke to leave.

2.2.2 Key challenges for the transition 
Despite all the proven negative impacts of traditional cooking systems, the transition 
to clean cooking remains hampered by several structural and contextual challenges (Cohen 
& Patel, 2019). The main challenges are presented divided into four categories, primarily 
extracted from the combined findings of Bisaga & To (2021), Cohen & Patel (2019), and 
Lahn and Grafham (2015). First, challenges originated in the own humanitarian energy 
institutional space. Second, challenges derived from the continuous funding gap in the 
humanitarian energy sector6. Third, challenges linked to the country where the 
displacement setting is located. And, finally, challenges associated with the local context. 
▪ 
Institutional challenges 
As mentioned in Section 2.1.2, there is a lack of leadership at the institutional level 
when dealing with the provision of energy services in humanitarian crises. Therefore, there 
is no actor strongly motivated to advocate for funds, coordinate initiatives or take 
responsibility for the performance of the sector (Bellanca, 2014; Thomas et al., 2021).  
Furthermore, there has been a lack of strategic prioritisation of clean cooking 
solutions in initiatives such as the CEC or the Global Strategy for Sustainable Energy 
(UNHCR, 2019), which leave the door open to low-tier biomass-based improved 
cookstoves7 (ICS) (Haselip et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2021). Consequently, up to date, only 20 
modern energy cooking programmes have been implemented, while the number of ICS 
biomass-based interventions already reached 132 in 2015. In addition, only 4 out of the 20 
clean cooking programmes implemented did not involve free-distribution models (Bisaga 
& To, 2021). As Lahn and Grafham (2015) point out, this approach (sometimes forced by 
country-specific regulations) aims to quickly address the basic needs resulting from a crisis.

However, it is financially unsustainable and can disrupt the conditions to create markets in 
displacement settings. For instance, it can reduce the willingness to pay, arise resentful

participation of private service providers (Cohen & Patel, 2019; Tran et al., 2021).

Funding challenges 
Humanitarian funding is often politically motivated and short-term. Therefore, 
sudden changes in mandate priorities and amounts are relatively common, leading to 
uncertain funding cycles and a high risk of discontinuity in programmes (Cohen & Patel, 
2019). In addition, as observed in Figure 7, the humanitarian space suffers from chronic 
budget constraints. Those are particularly present in the humanitarian energy sector, as 
funds are prioritised for life-saving interventions and not energy programmes that require 
longer-term thinking (Lahn & Grafham, 2015; Tran et al., 2021).

Country-specific challenges 
The uncertainty around a displacement setting is highly influenced by the 
government's decisions and applicable regulations. For instance, the recognition or not of 
the semi-permanent status of displacement settings in protracted emergencies, and the 
limited rights of refugees. Without a politically guaranteed stability or rights that allow the 
generation of income among the displaced, larger investments in energy access that require 
longer payback periods may be discouraged (Bisaga & To, 2021; Cohen & Patel, 2019; Lahn 
& Grafham, 2015). 
Besides, the complex and slow bureaucracy in developing countries and the 
reluctance of governments to transfer certain functions to humanitarian agencies can 
present barriers to energy interventions (Lahn & Grafham, 2015). 
▪ 
Local challenges 
Many sites hosting displaced populations are in rural border areas with harsh 
conditions of poverty, where the lack of infrastructure and the inadequate supply chain can 
hinder the “last-mile” delivery of energy services. Furthermore, these locations are often 
politically unstable and conflict-affected, creating insecurity among potential stakeholders 
(Lahn & Grafham, 2015).  
Within this group are also included the social, cultural and economic barriers created 
by the livelihoods of the displaced population (Lahn & Grafham, 2015). These include lack 
of awareness, perception of convenience, safety and reliability of new technologies, as well 
as cooking habits, traditions and gender roles (ESMAP, 2022; Tran et al., 2021). The main 
economic barrier is affordability, as modern cooking solutions may become too costly for 
people with limited or inexistent income generation (Corbyn & Vianello, 2018). 
2.2.3 Sustainable delivery models 
As Tran et al. (2021) remark, it requires multi-pronged and multi-sectoral 
approaches to create delivery models capable to overcome the challenges the humanitarian 
energy space is facing. A delivery model is the set of actions and instruments used to provide 
energy access services taking into consideration the needs, capacities and preferences of 
the users, and the context that governs the environment where the provision of services 
takes place (Bellanca & Garside, 2013). Traditionally, humanitarian agencies have delivered 
energy access solutions through inefficient and unsustainable models, heavily reliant on 
grant-based funding and using free-distribution methods (Tran et al., 2021). 
Revealed the failure of traditional approaches, market-based interventions, where 
the provision of energy solutions is led by a private service provider, have been increasingly 
promoted (Bisaga & To, 2021). Market-based aim to establish sustainable delivery models, 
removing the reliance on grants over the long-term. Besides, the participation of private

2.3 LPG as a solution 
LPG is a widely used, clean-burning and easily portable fuel, consisting of a blend of 
propane and butane. It is a naturally occurring and unavoidable by‐product of oil and 
natural gas refining and extraction. LPG is considered a clean cooking solution and thus, a 
key element in the pursuit of SDG7 (Puzzolo et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2021).  
The International Energy Agency (IEA) (2021) includes a significant share of LPG 
(including bioLPG8) in its projection of cooking fuels in developing markets until 2050, as 
observed in Figure 8. However, the adoption of LPG for cooking in displacement contexts 
remains challenged, especially in long-term interventions (Vianello, 2016). The next 
sections present the main pros and cons of adopting LPG in these contexts and introduce 
the Global LPG Fund for Refugees, a financing facility conceptualised to support market-
based interventions addressing LPG potentials (Haselip et al., 2022).

2.3.1 Pros and cons of LPG 
Cooking solutions based on LPG have been successfully used in humanitarian 
emergencies as a viable short-/medium-term solution to reduce the use of biomass cooking. 
UNHCR is currently supplying LPG to refugees in Bangladesh and Rwanda, and is supporting 
pilot programmes in Niger and Tanzania. The main drivers for these interventions are two: 
(i) security and safety, as people do not need to leave the settings to collect firewood and 
expose themselves to potential violent events, including SGBV; and (ii) environment

16 
protection, as the no need for firewood and charcoal for cooking reduces environmental 
degradation9 (Haselip et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2021). 
Cooking with LPG has other significant benefits compared to traditional low-tier 
cooking systems. The efficiency during combustion is 10 times greater, reducing the 
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 3.7tCO2 eq./stove per year. Besides, the combustion 
does not produce smoke and the amount of black carbon10 released is minimum. These 
features reduce the risk of suffering respiratory illness and improve the cooking experience 
(GLPGP & WLPGA, 2019; Rivoal & Haselip, 2017). Vianello (2016) also underlines the 
capacity of LPG stoves to heat instantly and be easily adjusted. 
Time savings in cooking and collecting the fuel, especially if the LPG distribution 
point is located in or nearby the camps, can be used to pursue other activities such as 
education, leisure and livelihood opportunities. Furthermore, LPG can be transported by 
ship inside tanks and, once it is discharged in a terminal and sent to filling stations, 
transferred to cylinders for a road or rail distribution, which is particularly helpful when 
having to supply remote rural areas (GLPGP & WLPGA, 2019; Puzzolo et al., 2019).  
All these benefits obtained from switching from traditional biomass-fired to LPG 
cooking systems contribute indirectly to all SDGs, but more particularly to the SDGs 
included in Figure 9 (GLPGP & WLPGA, 2019; Haselip et al., 2022).

Despite all the great benefits, the traditionally used free-distribution models by

economic burden that it implies, Tran et al. (2021) consider as major long-term constraints 
for widespread of LPG in displacement settings the lack of established distribution 
networks and the increased risk of supply chain disruptions during times of global crises.  
The adoption of LPG in displacement settings can also be highly affected by the 
national regulatory environment and the supply-demand of the international market, as 
LPG is generally imported to developing countries. In addition, affordability, habits, 
traditions, and safety concerns of the displaced population may hamper the deployment of 
LPG (Bisaga & To, 2021; Puzzolo et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2021).

2.3.2 The Global LPG Fund 
Given the highlighted benefits that LPG can provide in contexts of displacement and 
their contribution to the SDGs, the GLPGP, in collaboration with UNHCR, developed a 
concept note for a Global LPG Fund for Refugees (Global LPG Fund) (Haselip et al., 2022). 
The idea, still under consideration, was announced in the UNHCR 2019 Global Report 
(2019). The Global LPG Fund aims to address and satisfy identified potential for LPG as a 
viable cooking fuel solution in contexts of displacement. The fund is conceptualised to have 
the mandate to serve both the displaced population and the HC, who usually rely on the 
same low-tier cooking systems (Haselip et al., 2022). 
The fund rationale is based on the assumed existence of latent LPG market demand 
in contexts of displacement. Despite the lack of detailed data, the experience in active LPG 
programmes and pilots reveals that there are significant opportunities. On its mission to 
address LPG potential, the financing facility is meant to provide properly structured and 
supervised support to maximise the achievement of such potential. The overarching goal 
would be to maximise the effectiveness of public funds and to support the supply and 
demand of the market to create a commercially viable environment for the private sector 
(Haselip et al., 2022). 
To date, there have been a very small number of funds with a mandate to operate in 
the humanitarian energy space (Cohen & Patel, 2019). Some of the most noted are the USAID 
Smart Communities Coalition Innovation Fund (SCCIF), which recently awarded USD 
465,000 to de-risk a PAYG solar home systems in Ugandan refugee settlements (Shell et al., 
2020), and the Kakuma Kalobeyei Challenge Fund (KKCF), created by World Banks’ member 
IFC in partnership with AECF. KKCF operates in northern Kenya and targets both displaced 
populations and their HC (KKCF, 2019).  
These existing financing facilities also aim to enable better economic integration and 
self-reliance through the support of commercially viable and sustainable businesses 
however, their mandates still lack a longer-term view. For instance, the SCCIF was launched 
in 2020 and its implementation period finishes in 2023 (USAID, 2022). Besides, most of the 
existing entities are pure grant enablers for the private sector and do not consider 
innovative financial mechanisms (Bisaga & To, 2021; Cohen & Patel, 2019). 
With this operational and funding gap in mind, and considering the benefits LPG 
provides as a clean cooking solution in displacement contexts, the Global LPG Fund could 
be an enabler instrument to achieve SDG7 for the world’s forcibly displaced populations 
(Haselip et al., 2022).

3. Conceptual framework 
This section compiles the three key concepts that will support the themes analysed. 
Those are the gap between humanitarian and development aid, the use of the sustainable 
humanitarianism framework to bridge it, and the relevance of social finance approaches 
when modelling investments aiming for social and environmental impact. 
3.1 Humanitarian aid versus development aid 
Humanitarian actions are designed to provide assistance, protection and solutions to 
individuals or communities in response to human needs that can arise from different types 
of emergencies, such as natural disasters or armed conflicts. Depending on the context of 
the emergency, people may be forced to flee their homes, facing then greater threats 
(Barnett, 2011; Zetter, 2014a).  
Humanitarian programmes mandates are tied to specific short-term quick-impact 
actions within the emergency context they support (Crisp, 2001). Unlike humanitarian 
relief, development aid responds to persistent structural problems. Its objective is to 
provide solutions that can ensure a sustainable livelihood and resilient communities, 
abandoning systematic poverty (Humanitarian Coalition, 2021). Development aid is framed 
within a neoliberal approach, which posits economic welfare development as a long-term 
solution to the conflicts (Décobert, 2020). 
Resources and funding for humanitarian actions mainly come from outside of the 
affected country, while development aid is organised around certain governing 
partnerships between local and external institutions. This leads to two very different 
spaces, with separate structures, decision-making processes and funding cycles (Hinds, 
2015; Lie, 2020). Delivering a successful and effective response to an emergency and 
providing people with tools to become crisis-resilient requires actions covering both 
instantaneous humanitarian needs and long-term development. However, the complexity 
and unpredictability of the conflicts addressed can make it very difficult to determine when 
to stop providing humanitarian relief and start a development programme. Besides, the 
frequently rigid mandates of development programmes may limit their capacity to adapt to 
changing realities on the ground, forcing humanitarian interventions to deal with structural 
issues, a purpose for which they are not designed that can generate dependencies and 
hamper progress to sustainable solutions (Fabre et al., 2017). 
The protracted nature of several existing humanitarian crises together with the 
blurred transition point between models translates to inefficient use of resources. This 
situation has brought into debate the separation of humanitarian and development aid and 
the need for a transition path from life-saving humanitarian relief to long-term development 
programmes. The humanitarian-development nexus is being discussed as a concept to 
bridge the gap and promote synergies between both approaches (Lie, 2020; Strand, 2020; 
Weishaupt, 2020). 
==PAGE:26==
 
19 
3.2 Sustainable humanitarianism to strengthen the nexus 
The transitional space between the provision of humanitarian assistance and long-
term development support is known as humanitarian-development nexus. The concept has 
gained momentum in recent years, and in the 2016 World Humanitarian Summit the 
strengthening of the nexus was recognised as a priority to achieve more effective and 
efficient assistance in fragile contexts (OCHA, 2017; Strand, 2020).  
The link between humanitarian and development aid is challenged by differences in 
operating values, mandates and institutional requirements, funding cycles, and notions. 
However, the nexus can be reinforced by tackling shared objectives and promoting 
humanitarian actions that can lay the foundations for sustainable development (Hinds, 
2015). Audet (2015) describes sustainable humanitarianism as an approach that attempts 
to provide crisis relief but whose impacts extend beyond the humanitarian action itself. 
Hence, it is an intervention model that integrates the nexus concept by addressing 
humanitarian needs with a commitment to long-term impact. 
Cohen and Patel (2019) argue that the humanitarian energy space is placed at the 
intersection between humanitarian and development aid, as it aims to cover basic needs 
and promote economic and social development in contexts of displacement through energy 
interventions (Rosenberg-Jansen, 2020). However, these interventions are often not seen 
as a priority for the humanitarian community because they are perceived as long-term 
investments (Lehne et al., 2016). 
Sustainable humanitarianism suggests actions to strengthen the nexus that are being 
considered in the humanitarian energy sector, such as market-led interventions. Market-
based approaches have been recognised as one of the best ways to deal with the worst 
effects of protracted conflicts. Additionally, operating in the nexus requires a deep 
knowledge of the context and a fluid relationship with both humanitarian and development 
stakeholders. Therefore, activities that seek a better understanding of the environment can 
help reinforce interventions in the nexus (Betts & Omata, 2015; Hinds, 2015; Zetter, 2014b). 
Rethinking the role of the private sector is also important to bridge the gap between 
humanitarian and development aid. Sustainable humanitarianism opens the door to the 
business sector willing to participate in the space. If the framework is suitable, the private 
sector can lead market-based initiatives, contribute to expanding the funding channels, 
overcome humanitarian and development programmes' rigidness and improve their 
efficiency (Ahmed, 2021; Betts & Omata, 2015; Zetter, 2014b). 
Ahmed (2021) evaluates different innovative humanitarian financing mechanisms 
that can be used to boost the funding for programmes designed to strengthen the 
humanitarian-development nexus. From a social finance perspective, with human-centric 
investment decisions, impact investing emerges as a strategic tool to support the 
sustainable humanitarianism theories (Ahmed, 2021; Rizzi et al., 2018).

3.3 Social finance as a framework for impact investing 
Social finance is the economic discipline that goes beyond lending and investment 
approaches and intends to redefine how and why money is raised and used, trying to link 
two completely different capital sources (donor and commercial capital) to achieve positive 
social and environmental impact (Nicholls et al., 2015; Walker et al., 2021). 
This discipline emerges as a critique of the existent traditional financial system and 
its failure to provide social and environmental welfare. Governments are responsible to 
address these market failures and can do it by monetising and integrating into the market 
those impacts that are not properly captured in traditional investments. Environmental 
taxes, emission fees, and subsidisation of social enterprises are some examples. However, 
in some cases, non-governmental agencies are needed to ensure sustainable and effective 
markets (Addis, 2015; Andrikopoulos & Triantafillou, 2021).  
Social finance possesses its own tools to achieve social and environmental welfare, 
such as impact investing. Through impact investing, capital is allocated aiming to create a 
measurable positive social and environmental impact while obtaining a certain economic 
return adjusted or not to the market conditions. Therefore, it sits between the commercial 
“financial-only” and the donor “impact-only” capital (Hangl, 2014; Walker et al., 2021; 
Wilson et al., 2015). An impact investment must go along with the investor's commitment 
to measure and report the performance of the underlying investments towards the social 
and environmental objectives pursued (Weber, 2017).  
The decision-making process for an impact investment is based on the return-risk-
impact triangle. Based on this triangle, the social finance discipline manages to attract 
investors with different risk-reward and impact objectives. For instance, public or 
philanthropic investors, who usually have strong social/environmental impact targets, can 
take equal or more risk than other investors and are much more tolerant to receive lower 
returns compared to the risk taken. By blending the financing, a transaction can be de-risked 
and made more appealing to more conservative investors. Therefore, blending capital can 
be used to catalyse private sector investments into sustainable development interventions 
(Andrikopoulos & Triantafillou, 2021; Convergence, 2021; Nicholls et al., 2015). 
Despite the socio-economic advantages of this approach and the growing number of 
organisations following this discipline, there are markets where impact investing and the 
use of blended structures is yet very limited. Various reasons prevent a wider deployment 
and most of them are present in the markets located in displacement settings, such as 
cultural and behavioural barriers, lack of transparency and cooperation among 
stakeholders, absence of reliable data or lack of commercial skills and investable deals 
(Andrikopoulos & Triantafillou, 2021; Nicholls et al., 2015).

4. Methodology 
The present study is approached through a qualitative research design. Qualitative 
research is chosen due to its capacity to provide in-depth exploration when the researcher 
is investigating an innovative topic that has been little addressed (Morse, 1991). Applying 
this research method is, thus, well justified since several experts in the field claimed the 
existence of a knowledge gap about financial mechanisms to support the clean cooking 
transition in displacement settings (Bisaga & To, 2021; Rosenberg-Jansen & Haselip, 2021). 
The research design consists of a combination of two qualitative research 
methodologies: document analysis and semi-structured problem-centred expert 
interviews. This research approach is the most appropriate strategy to acquire information 
about how a humanitarian energy fund (Global LPG Fund) can better support a clean and 
modern transition in cooking systems through LPG solutions in displacement contexts in 
the Global South. The main reasons for choosing this combined research design are stated 
below. 
First, document analysis is a strong method to gain information about what has been 
studied in the field. This method is especially relevant given the current situation in 
displacement settings concerning the Covid-19 crisis. Due to these extraordinary 
circumstances, displacement settings have become more inaccessible for humanitarian 
agencies and supply chains have been disrupted (DI & IRC, 2021; OCHA, 2021). Shupler et 
al. (2021) state that inequities in clean cooking fuel access may have been exacerbated by 
Covid-19 lockdown. In consequence, reports, articles and other related sources discussing 
energy access interventions in displacement settings are crucial to understanding the 
nowadays context. The document analysis used in the present study follows the analysis 
parameters of Bowen (2009) and the classification parameters in terms of accessibility of 
Scott (1990).  
Second, the interview guidelines are designed in a semi-structured format to bring 
flexibility and adaptability to the process (Galletta & Cross, 2013). The methodology used 
to conduct the interviews follows a combination of approaches, as applied by Döringer 
(2021). The approaches applied are the problem-centred interview (PCI) presented by 
Witzel (2000) and the expert interview presented by Bogner et al. (2009). With this 
combination, it is possible to acquire expert knowledge while narrowing the discussion 
down to a “problem”. In this case, the negative impacts traditional cooking systems have on 
displaced populations and the lack of financially sustainable delivery models to accelerate 
a transition to clean and modern solutions. These two approaches aim to complement each 
other by aligning and focusing the interview around a particular “problem”, whose solution 
can deeply be discussed by an expert on the matter (Döringer, 2021).  
Finally, the findings resulting from the document analysis and the problem-centred 
expert interviews will be analysed and discussed under a thematic analysis following the 
criteria of Braun and Clarke (2006). Thematic analysis is a flexible research tool which 
entails screening all the gathered relevant information and extracting key themes that can 
==PAGE:29==
 
22 
be linked and shared across different sources. This allows the researcher to synthesise ideas 
and topics discussed, materialising the meaning of the data (Braun & Clarke, 2006). 
4.1 Document analysis 
Document analysis is a qualitative research technique very useful to support other 
forms of research, such as problem-centred expert interviews. This support is made by 
adding data and relevant information to the research from previously documented studies, 
plus it can also contribute to generating new/improved questions for the interview process. 
Therefore, this methodology can then be differentiated from the literature review, whose 
mission is to provide the researcher with the fundamentals and context of the topic studied, 
rather than adding data to the research enquiry (Bowen, 2009; Flick, 2018). 
The process of reviewing documents is very useful within the investigation since it is 
a way of validating information obtained through alternative sources, which in this case are 
the inputs from the problem-centred expert interviews (Bowen, 2009). Besides, document 
analysis becomes very relevant given the difficulty to obtain recent empirical data regarding 
energy access and clean cooking in displacement settings. The global crisis brought by the 
Covid-19 pandemic has led to a redirection of humanitarian efforts to prevent the virus 
propagation and provide medical assistance to these settings (DI & IRC, 2021; OCHA, 2021). 
Furthermore, supply chain disruption and lockdowns due to the pandemic have hampered 
interventions targeting the clean cooking transition (Shupler et al., 2021). 
Publications from academic journals as well as grey literature (reports, white papers, 
abstracts, and websites containing relevant information) that focused on the topic studied 
are considered in the document review. The main source of literature is the Humanitarian 
Energy Research Library (HERL), an access restricted online library managed by Sarah 
Rosenberg-Jansen, research lead at the GPA. The library is shared among practitioners in 
the field of humanitarian energy and includes the most relevant documents in the field. The 
content is well organised and divided by themes, which facilitated the filtering process and 
the selection of the most relevant documents. The main themes considered when navigating 
through the library were: “Household Cooking”, “Delivery Models” and “Finance”. The 
library also allows for direct keyword searching, so complementary searches were made for 
“LPG”, “clean cooking” and “financial mechanisms”. The access to the library was granted by 
Sarah Rosenberg-Jansen, after meeting her in a call coordinated by James Haselip, senior 
researcher at UNEP Copenhagen Climate Centre (former UNEP DTU Partnership).  
Additionally, along with the research, several professionals in the field, such as James 
Haselip, Sarah Rosenberg-Jansen, and some of the experts interviewed, have provided 
recommendations regarding supplementary readings. Furthermore, the information was 
complemented through searches on online databases. ResearchGate, Google Scholar, 
Science Direct, Det Kgl. Bibliotek, and DTU Orbit were consulted. The primary research 
queries consisted of the combination of keywords such as "LPG”, “displacement 
settlements", “refugee”, “energy access”, “delivery models”, “innovative finance”, "social 
finance”, “humanitarian-development nexus”, etc.  
==PAGE:30==
 
23 
An exhaustive selection of the information obtained during the search was carried 
out to identify the relevant literature. From this point, search rounds were carried out 
divided into three areas: (i) market conditions for the adoption of LPG for cooking in 
contexts of displacement, (ii) mechanisms to support financially sustainable delivery 
models and (iii) considerations for the creation of a humanitarian energy fund dedicated to 
LPG. Given the scope and objectives of the research, publications that do not include specific 
inquiries about cooking systems in displacement contexts were disregarded. 
The documents reviewed to support the analysis section follow the classification 
parameters in terms of accessibility described by Scott (1990): closed, restricted accesses 
and open to all public. Most of the documents analysed were open to the public and a few 
were restricted. As for the sorting criteria among all the consulted documents, the principles 
of authenticity, credibility, representativeness and meaning of Scott (1990) are used. 
Institutional documents and frameworks of the organisations that work around the 
displacement settlements (both NGOs and intra-governmental agencies) are also 
considered since these are intended to record and legitimise how the actions and 
implementations are carried out (Flick, 2018). 
The review of the relevant literature sheds light on the need for accelerating the clean 
cooking transition in displacement settings in the Global South. However, it also pointed to 
a knowledge gap affecting the financing of long-term clean cooking interventions in 
displacement settings. The lack of reliable data and indicators to guide humanitarian energy 
interventions on this matter can lead to inefficient policymaking decisions and unsuccessful 
humanitarian interventions (Fabre et al., 2017; Rosenberg-Jansen & Haselip, 2021). Past 
studies already established that policies are only successful if policymakers understand the 
whole environment affected by them and know what are the mechanisms that need to be 
enabled to achieve the desired results (Sanderson, 2002).  
However, it becomes very challenging to capture all the factors affecting a 
humanitarian energy intervention. The energy needs of displaced people may be 
overlooked in the planning and provision of services due to the in-depth knowledge missing 
and the over-challenging country-specific regulations (A. Scott et al., 2018). Furthermore, 
programme planning and implementation are not free of inconsistencies. Unintended gaps 
between theory and practice may appear, leading to an inefficient allocation of resources 
and an unsatisfactory outcome for the programme itself (Mosse, 2004). 
Reading and analysing the selected documents allowed the extraction of initial 
shared understandings, such as: (i) growing awareness of the negative impacts of 
traditional cooking systems in displacement contexts and the need for humanitarian energy 
interventions providing clean cooking solutions; (ii) interventions’ need to switch from 
free-distribution approaches to alternative sustainable market-led delivery models; (iii) the 
complexity of market creation in displacement settings, which requires further 
investigation; and, (iv) an existing research gap about concrete actions to bring financially 
sustainable long-term clean cooking solutions.

4.2 Problem-centred expert interviews  
Along with the document analysis practice, the qualitative research is complemented 
by problem-centred expert interviews. The approach taken to carry out the interviews 
follows the notions presented by Döringer (2021), who merges different methods from 
recognised authors. On one hand, she includes the theory-generating expert interview from 
Bogner et al. (2009). On the other hand, complements the latter with a problem-centred 
interview (PCI) from Witzel (2000), who empathises the importance of individual 
experiences and opinions towards a specific interview topic or problem. 
▪ 
Designing expert interviews 
Meuser and Nagel (2009) highlight expert interviews as a qualitative empirical 
research method that allows focusing on the specific knowledge of the expert in a certain 
field of action. According to the authors, "experts" can be defined as people with privileged 
access to decision-making processes, or people responsible for the development, 
implementation or control of specific activities through a certain level of knowledge or 
exposition of the context. Therefore, by interviewing relevant stakeholders in the field, it is 
expected to obtain unique and insightful data about the issue from different perspectives, 
which later on will be scrutinised and analysed to obtain conclusions. In this case, the 
interview is designed knowing in advance which target groups of experts would be part of 
the sample of interviewees, given the literature review carried out to set the basis for the 
research. 
Debates have been raised regarding the elements that constitute an expert or the 
different forms of expert interviews (Döringer, 2021). Bogner et al. (2009) extend the 
discussion to different approaches for qualitative research based on expert interviews, 
distinguishing three types depending on the purpose of the research: (i) explanatory expert 
interviews, to be used only to obtain orientation on a particular topic; (ii) the systematic 
expert interview, which targets regular retrieval of information; and (iii) the theory-
generating expert interview, aimed for the reconstruction of interpretative patterns and 
subjective action orientation criteria.  
Theory-generating expert interview is the most appropriate approach for the present 
research given the need to analyse and extract common ideas among the interviewees and 
to create a set of criteria useful to manage and operate a Global LPG Fund. However, in any 
case, Bogner et al. (2009) point out that findings may be conditioned by the interests of the 
person carrying out the interview, meaning the researcher. 
▪ 
Classification of the experts

Questionnaire design 
To avoid loss of fluency in the dialogue and a more complete understanding of the 
interview context, all participants receive a brief introduction of the research objectives 
together with the questionnaire. The questionnaire is a tool designed to support the

narrative sequence in the dialogue and can be used by the interviewees to prepare 
themselves. Furthermore, it helps the researcher to summarise and condensate in some 
questions the most relevant topics for the interview (Flick, 2018).  
The interview questionnaires were designed following the guidance of Bogner et al. 
(2009), who recommend to freely design the interview according to the needs of the 
research. Thus, a semi-structured interview model with open responses is chosen. This 
structure provides remarkable flexibility, both to the interviewer and the interviewee. It is 
sufficiently structured to address specific details of the research question while at the same 
time giving room to the respondents for argumentation or to address other thematic 
approaches if necessary (Galletta & Cross, 2013). Besides, as Flick (2018) points out, 
interviewees' thoughts are more likely to be better captured in a semi-structured interview, 
rather than in a closed interview or standardised questionnaire. In addition, this format of 
interviews offers greater adaptability to the researcher during the interview process 
compared to other types of structures (Döringer, 2021). 
Keeping in mind the different backgrounds of both groups of experts (EIE and HEE), 
two sets of questionnaires were formulated. It is worth mentioning that there has been a 
refining process for both questionnaires. They were initially tested with a few experts of 
each group and then adapted to better capture the key observations for future interviews. 
Both questionnaires were refined twice during the research. The final version of both 
questionnaires is presented in the Appendix.

Designing problem-centred interviews 
The present qualitative research is not using interviews with experts stand-alone but 
combines them with a problem-centred approach. This method is characterised by its 
practical relevance and its transdisciplinary nature. It is used to direct the questions in 
order to obtain the most precise information in the answers. Furthermore, since this 
approach sets the framework of the interview around a “problem” it also helps to establish 
a clearer definition of expert, complementing, thus the expert interview approach (Bogner 
et al., 2009). Additionally, focusing the discussion around a topic or “problem” prevents 
from deviations along with the interview process (Döringer, 2021). In the case of study, the 
“problem” is meant to be the negative impacts traditional cooking systems have on 
displaced populations and the lack of financially sustainable delivery models to accelerate 
a transition to clean and modern solutions. 
==PAGE:35==
 
28 
Approaching the interviews under the combination of two methodologies (expert 
interview and PCI) makes it easier to obtain insights from the interviewees that would not 
have been possible otherwise (Döringer, 2021). The dialogue in the interviews between the 
researcher and the expert is intended to be egalitarian, a trust-based conversation in which 
the meaning attributed to the “problem” is collaboratively reconstructed (Murray, 2016).  
▪ 
Interview strategy 
When conducting the interviews, initial priority was given to experts belonging to 
the HEE group. This strategy allowed obtaining first-hand information about the market 
environment in contexts of displacement given experts’ experience in the field. Additionally, 
strategies to overcome the inherent barriers to the creation of a market were discussed.  
Once a certain number of HEE have been interviewed, the priority was shifted 
towards the EIE group. Understanding the main market barriers and how to overcome 
them, it was possible to generate a debate about how to reach certain engaging market 
conditions for the private sector, including financial de-risking mechanisms. Besides, this 
group of experts was well prepared to provide insightful information about relevant actions 
to be taken when setting up and operating an LPG fund. 
As for the mean to conduct the interviews, different geographical locations between 
the interviewer and interviewee, and the sanitary measures consequence of the Covid-19 
pandemic, forced to use mostly online approaches. So, even though face-to-face interviews 
allow a more versatile communication, that can be reinforced with gestures and expressions 
(Witzel, 2000), most of the interviews were conducted through video calls. The platforms 
used are Microsoft Teams, WhatsApp and Zoom. These systems offer the possibility to easily 
record the interviews. Hence, always the interviewee agreed with that, the interviews were 
recorded for a better analysis.  
4.3 Thematic analysis 
To organise the information obtained during the research process a thematic analysis 
is used. This method allows a detailed association and description of the data while at the 
same time it is analysed and reported thematically (Braun & Clarke, 2006; Sandelowski & 
Barroso, 2003). It is a very flexible tool frequently used among researchers because it 
provides a rich and detailed, yet complex, account of the data.  
Additionally, when dealing with different sources of data, as is the case with several 
interviews and documents analysed, allows the search and identification of common 
threads or trends that extend across the data set. Furthermore, this analysis method does 
not require specific theoretical knowledge from the investigator (Braun & Clarke, 2006; 
Vaismoradi et al., 2013). The process to carry out the analysis follows the guidelines of 
Braun and Clarke (2006), who propose and describe a six phases design step by step.  
i. 
Familiarisation with the data and repeated reading of the same. 
ii. 
Organising the data into different groups by the similarity of the content. 
==PAGE:36==
 
29 
iii. 
Generation of labels/names to form thematical groups, which can also be 
classified into subcategories, redefined, discarded or divided.  
iv. 
Review and refining of the themes extracted in the previous phase. 
v. 
Themes must be clearly defined and named. 
vi. 
Generating the report. 
As stated by Braun and Clarke (2006), the researcher is responsible to determine 
which themes capture best the relevant information that must be analysed in relation to the 
research question. However, it is important to remark that these themes are not selected 
from a pre-research point of view, but are revealed from shared conclusions of the data 
analysed (Braun & Clarke, 2006; Sandelowski & Barroso, 2003). In this case, the sources of 
information are the documents analysed and problem-centred expert interviews.  
As there are no set parameters defining the amount of coincidence necessary to show 
evidence of shared conclusions within the data analysed, Braun and Clarke (2006) 
underline that it is the researcher's judgment which has to legitimate such findings through 
justification. Besides, the themes chosen might not necessarily be the most predominant 
ones across the data, but, as a whole, they capture key elements to answer the research 
question. The three main themes identified for the analysis are: (i) the creation of an LPG 
market in displacement settings; (ii) the financial mechanism needed to engage the private 
sector; and (iii) considerations for setting up an LPG financing facility for displacement 
settings 
Finally, it is important to remark, as Sandelowski and Barroso (2003) highlight, that 
research is not a static process. In consequence, and even though there is a knowledge gap 
on the project’s topic, the traction that SDGs are bringing to the field, especially by the SDG7 
indicator 7.1.2, may lead to new publications while this research is carried out.

5. Analysis 
As has been already demonstrated in this research, people living in a situation of 
displacement have limited access to basic energy needs. The cooking systems they are 
currently using are harmful to them and the environment (Lahn & Grafham, 2015). To avoid 
these vulnerable groups falling behind on the achievement of SDG7 different initiatives have 
taken place in recent years.  
All humanitarian energy experts (HEE) interviewed agree that clean cooking has 
gained traction among governments and humanitarian agencies. In words of I. Bisaga, the 
GPA and the Moving Energy Initiative (MEI)11 have helped to stimulate and coordinate the 
sector. Yet, despite this is getting the attention of more stakeholders beyond just the 
traditional humanitarian partners, there is still a lack of interest from development partners 
limiting the implementation of long-term interventions, she adds. L. Patel reaffirms the idea 
that the humanitarian sector lies at the nexus between the humanitarian and development 
space and, as such, it should have instruments to bridge them. However, energy access 
interventions in displacement settings so far have been dominated by short-term funding 
mandates and delivery models based on the free-distribution, declares L. Clarke. This 
situation leads to unsustainable interventions, particularly if the crises become protracted. 
With these operational and funding constraints in mind, and considering the benefits 
LPG provides as a clean cooking solution to displaced people and HCs, the creation of a 
financing facility dedicated to addressing LPG potential and supporting financially market-
based interventions can be a step towards the achievement of SDG7. Consequently, this 
section presents and analyses the key findings obtained from document analysis and expert 
interviews in relation to three main themes that can shed some light on what role should 
the Global LPG Fund take to accelerate the clean cooking transition in contexts of 
displacement. 
Firstly, there are presented the different factors that influence the creation of an LPG 
market in contexts of displacement. Secondly, financial mechanisms that can contribute to 
engaging the private sector on LPG cooking interventions are analysed. And finally, the 
section concludes with some key elements to be thoughtfully addressed when setting up an 
LPG financing facility.

5.1 Creating an LPG market in displacement settings 
In order to accelerate a sustainable transition to clean cooking solutions in 
displacement settings, the humanitarian energy sector must take actions that bridge the 
humanitarian and development assistance gap. The promotion of market-based 
interventions instead of inefficient and unsustainable free-distribution models can 
contribute to strengthening the nexus (Betts & Omata, 2015; Tran et al., 2021). To do so, 
through an LPG cooking intervention, it is fundamental to consider the key elements that 
contribute to the creation of a resilient market in displacement settings. These are: (i) the 
regulatory environment, (ii) the importance of reaching economies of scale, (iii) the 
affordability, (iv) the awareness, behaviour and safety conditions, and (v) the development 
of the market. 
▪ 
Regulatory environment 
Not all displacement settings in the Global South are suitable when planning an 
energy access intervention for cooking LPG. There are several factors to be considered, even 
if there is evidence of an existent demand, declares A. G. Egziabher. When studying a clean 
cooking project in these contexts, E. Puzzolo and L. Clarke coincide that the existence of an 
enabling regulatory environment is key. 
If refugees are not allowed to work, they won’t be able to generate income and thus, 
there is not going to be money to purchase LPG or any other clean cooking product. When 
that happens, people are reliant on public donations or free self-collected firewood (if 
available nearby), alleges L. Patel. Therefore, if the humanitarian energy sector wants to 
shift from the free-distribution delivery models to market-based approaches, it is important 
to engage the government of the host country.  
However, as Lahn and Grafham (2015) underline, defending the interests of refugees 
while trying to maintain good relations with host governments can be challenging and 
negotiations to secure even limited rights to work for refugees can take years. Bisaga and 
Huber (2020) claim the critical importance of strong relationships among agencies and 
governments, so the policymaking process can be influenced. Additionally, I. Bisaga and E. 
Puzzolo agree on the need to bring political stability to the displacement settlements. This 
is, ensuring certain longevity of the settlement, which is important not only to support 
market-based interventions but also to bring dignity and protection to displaced people. 
L. Patel brings the example of the clean cooking programme in Cox’s Bazar 
(Bangladesh) as a case of great cooperation among stakeholders. There, under a very 
adverse regulatory environment, humanitarian agencies managed to freely distribute LPG 
kits to almost 100,000 people (including Rohingya refugees and the HC) (Rafa et al., 2022). 
Another learning from this case is the importance of broadening the scope of the 
programme and including HCs, which often experience the same levels of energy poverty. If 
the local population benefits from a clean cooking intervention, besides reducing tensions 
between displaced and host populations, governments will be more supportive, adds L. 
Patel. Exemplified by the Bangladeshi authorities when they did not permit solar PV 
solutions for refugees because most HCs did not have that technology (Tran et al., 2021). 
==PAGE:39==
 
32 
On the other hand, market-based interventions must be designed not only in 
coordination with policymakers but also in coordination with them and their national plans. 
To illustrate that, A. G. Egziabher and L. Clarke bring the example of Rwanda, where the 
government, due to the concerning levels of deforestation, banned firewood and charcoal 
for cooking in 2019 (Nkurunziza, 2020). However, as A. G. Egziabher underlines, when the 
prohibition took place, the system didn’t have the supply capacity to provide alternative 
clean cooking solutions to refugees. In consequence, cash transfers provided by 
humanitarian agencies ended up spent in informal markets to buy those same fuels that 
were banned, says L. Clarke. 
National LPG quality standards in the country where the intervention is planned 
should be well developed and enforced to allow viable and safe LPG practices, remarks J. 
Rockall in line with the Clean Cooking Alliance (CCA) (2019). This includes the cylinder 
distribution system models, which are typically decided between two options: the 
Consumer Controlled Cylinder Model (CCCM)12 and the Branded Cylinder Recirculation 
Model (BCRM)13 (NORAD, 2020). E. Puzzolo remarks on the importance of targeting 
countries whose model is based on the BCRM, which has been proved more successful, 
especially in early-stage markets. This distribution model ensures better levels of 
availability, safety and maintenance of the LPG cylinders and, thus, of the market itself (CCA, 
2019). Contrary, E. Puzzolo claims that in the few countries with the CCCM, such as Ghana 
or Guatemala, there is a higher risk of black-market for LPG equipment and unlicensed and 
uncertified refilling businesses, exposing users to safety hazards. 
M. Pal claims that many developing countries in Africa are nowadays promoting LPG 
in their national plans. In consequence, programmes aiming to address LPG potential are 
likely to receive support especially if they bring external funding to reinforce the value chain 
of LPG across the country. Additionally, improvements in country-level infrastructure can 
improve the procurement efficiency of LPG, which translates into lower fuel prices. This is 
exemplified by K. Chen with the import terminal capacities in African countries, which are 
dominated by LPG pressurised cargoes (GLPGP, 2022). Investments in larger storage 
facilities with capacity for refrigerated LPG cargoes could significantly reduce the price per 
ton of LPG, he continues. 
▪

The creation of an LPG market can be, nonetheless, limited to certain geographical 
areas. Even with an upgrade of the value chain and infrastructure, rural displaced 
settlements are logistically challenging to supply, coincide J. Kavilu and V. Sabula. And, if 
remote areas are connected, it comes with an added cost, adds J. Kavilu. To create a market 
in isolated areas, it is necessary to offer certain levels of economies of scale to the LPG

Companies can achieve cost and operational efficiencies only by operating at scale

economies of scale is the lack of reliable data about displacement settings. The private 
sector needs to understand the market in order to offer well-suited solutions to the needs 
and practices of the target customers (Bellanca, 2014). What is the ability to pay among the 
displaced population? How many potential customers are there? What are their energy 
needs? These are basic questions any LPG provider might be wondering when looking at 
these markets, claims K. Chen. However, answers to these questions are usually unknown 
(Scott et al., 2018). Consequently, expenditure on gathering data to provide reliable 
information is crucial. 
At the same time, I. Bisaga introduces the concept of anchoring customers (business, 
hospitals, NGOs, etc.), which can be used to attract providers. Finally, to facilitate economies 
of scale, V. Sabula suggests partnerships with multiple well-established local LPG providers 
to reduce capacity risk and amplify network reach, while K. Chen mentions the importance 
of creating a solid market starting with (peri-)urban settlements, which are more accessible. 
▪ 
Affordability 
Subsidies on LPG prices can be used to protect service providers' interests while 
tackling affordability barriers among displaced populations (CCA, 2019). L. Patel and M. Pal 
point out that there are many countries in West Africa where the cost of LPG has already 
been subsidised by the government. However, if that subsidy is not in place or is not enough, 
someone else must ensure an accessible retail price, especially in remote locations and low-
income communities. This could be considered under the Global LPG Fund mandate, suggest 
V. Sabula and L. Patel.    
To consider such a thing, we must not forget that most of the LPG consumed in Africa 
is imported, remarks K. Chen. Consequently, LPG price is impacted by the international 
market and currency exchange rates, making it volatile. While some costs won’t change 
much, such as the local transport and distribution once the supply chain is established, the 
wholesale price of the fuel can suffer significant hikes. Then, if the subsidy can’t absorb the 
increase and there aren’t other governmental mechanisms to do so, such as tax reductions 
(which are typically very low on LPG), consumer ability to afford LPG can be seriously 
hampered, emphasises J. Rockall. Indian low-income households recently experienced these 
difficulties, as an unprecedented heatwave combined with the world geopolitical situation 
raised LPG prices, preventing fuel purchase even with a well-established national subsidy 
plan (Hrishikesh, 2022). 
Aside from subsidies meant to regulate the LPG price, consumer finance solutions 
and smart business models should also be considered as additional measures to overcome 
affordability barriers (Bisaga & To, 2021; NORAD, 2020). Cash transfer is a relief instrument 
that can give freedom of choice and dignity to the displaced person, particularly if the 
regulatory framework prevents any income-generation activity, says S. Huber. Cash-based 
==PAGE:41==
 
34 
distributions are very important because provide a foundation or a guaranteed level of 
income, adds I. Bisaga. Unfortunately, over the last couple of years, cash transfers have been 
cut. Funds have been channelled to support other crises, such as the Covid-19 pandemic or, 
now, the conflict in Ukraine she adds. Additionally, more and more criticism has appeared 
around this mechanism due to the lack of control over the purchases done with the 
transferred money, aggregates L. Clarke. Cash transfers meant to support a clean cooking 
intervention need to be accompanied by available and affordable solutions, declares A. G. 
Egziabher. 
E. Puzzolo points out an additional challenge for the LPG market creation concerning 
affordability: the high upfront cost of the cookstove or the refill of the cylinders. Refugees' 
and IDPs' economy is so frugal and often limited to day-per-day expenses even when there 
are cash transfer interventions in place. Hence, consumer-focused microfinancing 
instruments should be available to enable access to LPG cooking (FINDEV, 2019). The main 
microfinancing instruments used in displacement settings are microcredits, revolving funds 
and saving groups, declare V. Corno and J. Kavilu. 
Microcredits include interest on the principal and are obtained from microfinance 
institutions (MFIs) (Energypedia, 2014), which are not always available in contexts of 
displacement, as J. Kavilu points out. Whereas, revolving funds, typically grant-funded, and 
saving group funds, funded through the combination of individuals’/households’ savings, 
do not necessarily ask for interest, and are directly managed by organisations or groups of 
individuals, says V. Corno. Their operational system is cyclic, meaning that the repayment 
is needed to provide further loans to more people, which can limit the scaling up of the 
market, according to V. Corno. A successful example of an implemented revolving fund is 
the one set up by Practical Action (2007) in Kassala state, East Sudan. It was designed to 
support an LPG cooking intervention by providing the upfront costs of the cooking 
equipment. The fund was managed by a women association within the community and 
reached 7,000 households. 
On the other hand, there are the PAYG models which substitute high upfront 
expenses (upfront cylinder refill) for real LPG consumptions costs, read by smart meters 
installed on the cylinders (Figure 11) and paid through mobile money (FINDEV, 2019). 
However, I. Bisaga recognises PAYG models for LPG are very limited. According to E. 
Puzzolo, the main reason is the considerable cost of LPG meters, which is higher than the 
cylinder itself. Additionally, one of the few existing solutions, from the Tanzanian company 
KopaGas only applies to big LPG cylinders, used in Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) 
but not much at household level (Rivoal & Haselip, 2018).  
Several experts agree that accessible PAYG models provide more facilities for the 
consumer than microfinancing approaches, as proved in electrification interventions says 
V. Corno. With PAYG the user only needs to interact with the LPG provider, while models 
based on microfinance require close coordination between the borrower and the LPG 
provider (FINDEV, 2019). Therefore, to make the market more accessible to displaced 
people, the Global LPG Fund could consider establishing partnerships or providing financial 
support to any of the few companies that are currently developing PAYG technologies, such 
as KopaGas, Envirofit, BBOXX, PayGo Energy, or Proxygaz (CCA, 2019).

Awareness, behaviour and safety 
Even with an affordable LPG cooking solution the demand must be incentivised, 
declares S. Huber. And continues, “why people who are collecting firewood for free in the 
forest would want to participate in the market?” L. Patel underlines that any clean cooking 
intervention must be demand-driven, otherwise will be inefficient and impossible to 
sustain. 
Modes of demand creation include general awareness-raising activities about the 
benefits of LPG (e.g., through media campaigns) and personal contact through 
organisations' representatives (NORAD, 2020). V. Corno and I. Bisaga coincide in the 
importance of involving women in these activities. As has been presented in this research, 
cultural dynamics in most developing countries generate unequal gender norms regarding 
the cooking process. Since women are mainly responsible for cooking and collecting 
firewood, they are also the most affected collective by the negative impacts of the traditional 
cooking systems (ESMAP, 22). 
S. Huber firmly claims that users are aware of the harmful traditional cooking 
systems. Women suffer from itchy eyes and cough when cooking due to the smoke, spend 
long hours carrying firewood, and expose themselves to the risk of suffering SGBV, she 
continues. However, the benefits are difficult to understand if not experienced, declares L. 
Patel and I. Bisaga. Product demonstrations and word-of-mouth advertising appear to be 
the most important general drivers to create demand (NORAD, 2020). And only if there is a 
full understanding of the benefits of LPG, significant behaviour change can be achieved, 
concludes S. Huber. 
As L. Clarke points out, behaviour change requires time. In consequence, any 
intervention in clean cooking should target behavioural barriers at early stages to ensure 
==PAGE:43==
 
36 
acceptance before scaling up the project. Within this group of barriers there are some which 
aren’t real but perceived by the displaced people, aggregates L. Clarke. Some commonly 
perceived barriers are the higher value of money when spent on small daily purchases, 
faster food cooking on traditional cookstoves or the better taste of the food cooked on three-
stone fires (Nerini et al., 2017; Quinn et al., 2018). However, as I. Bisaga underlines, most of 
them have been proved fictitious. She brings the example of the taste of the food cooked 
using LPG cookstoves, which, as demonstrated by Perros et al. (2021) under a blind test, 
couldn’t be differentiated from food cooked in traditional cookstoves. 
Misinformation is tackled with education, says V. Corno. As Galimberti (2021) points 
out, the more educated people are, the more they strive to improve all aspects of their life, 
and this includes re-considering preconceived barriers. Consequently, as E. Puzzolo 
suggests, the Global LPG Fund should consider spending resources on educating displaced 
people about the benefits of cooking with LPG but also on offering understandable answers 
to their perceived barriers regarding this solution. Additionally, L. Clarke sees educational 
activities as a way to reduce fuel stacking14, a common praxis in displacement settings that 
prevents users from experiencing the full benefits of the clean cooking transition (Quinn et 
al., 2018). 
Surveys conducted in two urban refugee camps in Kachin State, Myanmar revealed 
that safety is seen as the main concern among refugees (Tran et al., 2021). To this matter, I. 
Bisaga underlines the difficulty of learning how to cook with a whole new system, especially 
if you are used to cooking in three-stone fires or mud-stoves. There is a learning curve 
among users, she adds. To accelerate that process and reduce safety risks it is necessary to 
train the users. Typically, the service provider is responsible for the training (Figure 12), 
says L. Clarke, but if they don’t have the capacity to do it, the Global LPG Fund should 
prioritise demonstrations and training sessions for displaced and also LPG distributors.

Market development 
Achieved optimal market conditions, the LPG market can scale up very quickly, says 
K. Chen. Examples are the LPG markets in countries like Indonesia, China, Brazil and India, 
where the consumption increased from 28.5% in 2011 to 71% by 2020 (Mani et al., 2020). 
Nevertheless, given the particularities of displacement settings, S. Huber remarks on 
the importance of carrying out pilot projects before designing a scale intervention. 
Supporting financing needs of any pilot activities should be considered so that the funded 
activities have the best chance of sustainability (Cohen & Patel, 2019). Pilot interventions 
are the best method to reveal weaknesses in the market system, adds S. Huber. Besides, she 
agrees with I. Bisaga on alert about the risk of not properly addressing any of the challenges 
for the creation of the LPG market. If that happens, displaced people will shift back to 
firewood and charcoal cooking systems, leading to market failure. A failure in this context, 
meaning an unsatisfactory fulfilment of demand or supply needs, would risk the 
achievement of the SDG7 in displacement settings and put at stake the global commitment 
to “leave no one behind”. 
As I. Bisaga claims, despite the efforts we are already certainly not on track to reach 
SDG7. It is important to start thinking long-term and providing sustainable solutions, and 
for that we need to attract private sector investment and participation (Bisaga & To, 2021). 
Therefore, as shared by several experts, innovative financing mechanisms are urgently 
needed, and the Global LPG Fund could play a crucial role there. 
5.2 Applying financial mechanisms to engage the private sector 
There is evidence that in order to achieve universal energy access for cooking private 
sector must take a significant role in the transition (ESMAP, 2020). Private participation can 
greatly boost the humanitarian energy sector in its mission to provide clean cooking 
solutions in contexts of displacement. As revealed in the literature review and discussed in 
the previous section, the engagement of the private sector is decisive for the success of any 
market-based intervention. Service providers and manufacturers can contribute to 
narrowing down the funding gap, providing the missing technical and commercial skills and 
building durable capacity to sustain supply chains in the long-term (Vianello, 2016). 
Consequently, the Global LPG Fund, under a social finance framework, should de-risk 
private investors' participation in investments that have positive social and environmental 
impacts. To do so, the fund must first understand the private sector and then, consider the 
use of financial mechanisms to enable private participation, such as: facilitating grants, 
concessions, Results-Based-Financing instruments including carbon credits and impact 
bonds, and risk guarantees.  
▪ 
Understanding the private sector 
A study carried out by Norwegian Agency for Development Cooperation (NORAD) 
(2020) identified the main reasons why some of the major oil & gas companies, like Shell, 
BP, Exxon, and Chevron refuse to participate in LPG business in developing markets. These 
==PAGE:45==
 
38 
were: (i) negligible impact of LPG activities on group profit, (ii) low upside compared to 
other uses of capital, and (iii) high risk regarding reputation and liability spilling over from 
non-compliant and/or unsafe practices by local LPG sector competitors in developing 
countries. While the third argument can be tackled by promoting high-standard policies and 
enforcing the regulation around the LPG in a country, as already discussed in the previous 
section, the first two reasons are directly linked to the difficulty to meet private sector 
commercial objectives.  
Therefore, to engage the private sector is important to understand first what are the 
required returns or hurdle rates15 for the players that could potentially participate in the 
LPG intervention, suggests H. Mahbouli. These rates are often influenced by macroeconomic 
and industry trends within the country where the investment takes place. This information 
should be used to understand the LPG business expected returns. However, if the fund aims 
to engage pure private commercial investors into investments carried out in contexts of 
displacement, the financial return will need to be risk-adjusted, explains H. Mahbouli. 
The risk premium on the return is going to be dependent on the private investor's 
confidence in the market. The CIP director illustrates that with a practical example: “for the 
same type of investment, one to be made in a stable region with reliable market forecasts 
and a second one to be made in a region at risk of armed conflicts; any investor will require 
a much higher return in the second investment”. The higher the risk assumed by the private 
sector, the higher is going to be the premium on the return (Cohen & Patel, 2019). 
Consequently, the first step to engage private companies is to provide reliable information 
about the market. That will reduce risks and thus, reduce the financial requirements needed 
to support their participation, says V. Sabula. Additionally, the financial support required is 
also very dependent on the existing national subsidies, coincide M. Pal and J. Rockall. There 
is a clear example in India, where through the Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana scheme 
millions of LPG connections and cylinders have been greatly subsidised, and thus the risk 
assumed by private LPG providers has been significantly reduced (Hrishikesh, 2022; Mani 
et al., 2020). 
When screening the market for potential private stakeholders, it is important to 
analyse companies' interests beyond financial returns, states H. Mahbouli. Under the social 
finance framework, the Global LPG Fund should be able to attract impact-first actors, which 
are nowadays more present in the financial ecosystem. Impact investors can help the fund 
leverage its participation and blend financial returns depending on the investors’ objectives, 
he continues. Once the market for potential stakeholders has been examined and the 
required financial returns revealed, it is time to design the financial support structure that 
better suits the interests of all parties. L. Patel highlights the importance of understanding 
that the financial security required from the private sector will be higher during the first 
interventions of the fund. Referring to past success stories is always a good way to attract 
more stakeholders, she adds. 
 
15A hurdle rate is the minimum rate of return required on a project or investment (Kenton, 2021).

On the other hand, it is important not to financially overprotect service providers, 
says the CIP director. The fund should be there the absorb, to a certain extent, the main 
potential downsides of the intervention, protecting the commercial interests of the private 
sector. However, it is important that they have “skin in the game” too, she adds. This 
figurative speech refers to the fact that, if the private companies involved are financially 
exposed to the performance of the intervention they are going to be more compromised to 
achieve successful results. 
▪ 
Grant funding to ignite the engagement 
Several experts agree that grants are not only helpful but usually a necessary 
financial instrument to support private sector providers to start operations in fragile 
markets like refugee camps. The AECF has vast experience providing loans and grants to 
incentivise business in rural and marginalised communities, points out the CEO V. Sabula. 
She recognises that local private companies targeting this kind of markets usually struggle 
to access capital from traditional financial institutions, making grants from the AECF or 
other development institutions their only option to finance. 
Service providers can use direct grand funding for several capital-intensive upfront 
expenses (Bisaga & To, 2021). For instance, to establish a reliable and secure supply chain, 
manufacture a certain number of LPG cylinders, invest in a refilling LPG plant, etc., However, 
the amount and the conditions of the grants can influence a lot on the capacity to build a 
sustainable market environment, underlines M. Pal. Bisaga and Huber (2020) present the 
example of BBOXX, a Kenyan company that in early 2018 received a grant from the MEI to 
de-risk its participation in a pilot project for the sale of Solar Home Systems (SHS) in 
Kakuma camp. The grant covered the acquisition of an initial 75 units, rent, staff and 
marketing costs for an initial six-month period, plus some additional logistics costs due to 
the remoteness of the camp’s location. Despite the encouraging results, after six months the 
financial support ceased, leaving the company with the difficult choice of continuing 
operations, which weren't yet at a commercially viable scale, or withdrawing from Kakuma 
(Bisaga & Huber, 2020; Cohen & Patel, 2019). 
The exit strategy is one of the main challenges when providing financial support, 
indicates L. Patel. Grant funding is limited and has a certain mandate, after which the project 
should be capable of continuing operations without significant support, adds V. Sabula. 
Therefore, as J. Kavilu remarks, the grantor must define a rigorous grant-awarding process 
to ensure that the grantee invests the money in sustainable business models meant to 
smooth the market operation and create self-sufficient structures, so reliance on grants is 
removed over the long-term (Bisaga & To, 2021). 
Bisaga and Huber (2020), aligned with V. Sabula’s opinion, underline the importance 
of providing flexible financial assistance to the private sector based on the context and 
stakeholder’s needs. MECS and Energy 4 Impact (2022), in a report reviewing the funding 
landscape of modern energy cooking, introduce the concept of scale-up grant funding. It 
consists of relatively small first grants for pilot projects and follow-on grants to scale-up 
proven interventions. As the report mentions, this concept is yet not very extended in clean 
==PAGE:47==
 
40 
cooking interventions. However, it could be considered for the Global LPG Fund, as it 
guarantees certain continuity and scale to successful pilot projects. 
On the other hand, there is a general consensus among interviewees that financial 
support mechanisms must go beyond pure direct grants. The CEC also advocates for a 
greater engagement of the private sector in the design of sustainable delivery models based 
on market approaches and lowering the use of grant-based funding (Bisaga & To, 2021). As 
several experts agree, diversifying financial instruments is key to achieve sustainable 
delivery models and build resilient market capacities. Nevertheless, it has been also pointed 
out that there is no silver bullet that can suit all the different potential market-based 
interventions because displacement settings present very singular characteristics from one 
to another. As claimed by K. Chen, there is a global need, but solutions must be context-
specific.  
▪ 
Implementing concessions 
L. Patel introduces the concession concept, developed by the MEI, as a helpful 
mechanism to engage the private sector by improving the long-term sustainability of their 
operations. It consists of a competitive tender process where private companies bid a price 
to supply a clean cooking solution to a displacement setting and surrounding HC for a 
certain time. The tender is based on a retail fuel price that has been capped at an affordable 
level for the majority of the consumers. Then, the supplier’s bid that better fulfils the 
conditions of the tender is awarded the concession to sell at that price, while the concession 
funder absorbs the difference with the bid price - which corresponds to the true cost of the 
solution (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2021b).  
This financial instrument has, thus, a double impact. On one hand, it guarantees clean 
cooking solutions at affordable retail prices for the displaced population, while on the other 
hand, it reduces the supplier’s sales risk by subsidising part of the fuel cost. As E. Puzzolo 
underlines, protection against sales risk is particularly relevant in LPG interventions 
because service providers do business through the fuel supply, i.e. from the LPG cylinder 
refilling, and not from selling/renting the initial equipment (cookstove and cylinder). 
There are several key factors to consider if planning to support an LPG intervention 
through a concession model. First of all, there must be clean cooking providers in the region 
that are interested in participating in the tender so there is market competition (MECS & 
Energy 4 Impact, 2021b; Tran et al., 2021). Second, the market needs to be located in a 
densely populated area to generate economies of scale and be accessible enough so the 
supplier can establish a reliable supply chain. Third, regulations must be attractive for a 
market-based intervention. And last but not least, it is necessary to have available 
information about fuel prices deemed affordable by a majority of the displaced population, 
so the price cap can be calculated (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2021b; Patel & Gross, 2019).  
Additionally, as M. Pal underlines, the economic benefits for the concession funder 
when the financial mechanism is applied in environments where there is already a firm 
ability to pay among consumers, coming from income-generating activities or subsidies, like 
cash transfers. This may increase the price cap of the concession and, thus, reduce the 
concessionary subsidy (i.e. the difference between price cap and price bid). 
==PAGE:48==
 
41 
Unlike other financial mechanisms, concessions provide continuous financial 
support to private companies (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2021b). Yet, concession 
agreements have a defined length, which should be sufficiently long (minimum 3-5 years) 
to allow the private sector to build a sustainable business model before the subsidy is 
discontinued, declares M. Pal. At that point, the provider should be able to maintain the 
offered cap prices without a subsidy thanks to the economies of scale, coincides L. Patel with 
Bisaga and To (2021). 
The idea of the concession was initially conceptualized in 2016 to create market 
opportunities for the sale and distribution of non-wood-based fuels in and around the 
Kakuma refugee camp (and nearby Kalobeyei settlement) in Kenya (Patel & Gross, 2019). 
Despite the potential it presents, the Kakuma concession is yet to be implemented16 due to 
time and funding constraints, remarks L. Patel. Funding constraints were accentuated by 
the private sector requests for further de-risking mechanisms, such as guarantees. Those 
requests were justified by the novelty of the delivery model and the inherent risks in the 
displacement settings (Bisaga & To, 2021).

Countries in Subsaharan Africa and Asia, like Rwanda, Uganda, Tanzania, Cambodia, 
China, Indonesia and particularly Kenya, have received RBF interventions (Bisaga & To,

now some programmes targeting high-tier cooking solutions, e.g., KOSAP (Kenya) and 
BRILHO (Mozambique) (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2022). 
Compared to conventional upfront grant financing, RBFs have a significant impact on 
risk allocation (Stritzke et al., 2021). The development and execution risks are largely 
shifted from the funder to the project implementer, making RBF approaches very appealing 
for funders, says V. Sabula. Yet, she also points out the reputational risk of non-performance 
borne by the funder, which is described by Stritzke et al. (2021). To avoid that it is important 
to define ambitious but achievable targets and design an adequate verification process, she 
adds. However, several experts agree that those are very challenging tasks. RBF targets have 
been typically based on outputs (e.g. number of sales of stoves or cylinder refills), instead 
of outcomes (e.g. health improvements, gender equality, or emission reductions), 
underlines I. Bisaga. While good performance in outcomes guarantees a positive social and 
environmental impact on the displaced population, good results on output can be 
misleading. E. Puzzolo and L. Patel exemplify that with the practice of fuel staking among 
the displaced, who may have received/bought a clean cooking solution but still use low-tier 
systems.  
Therefore, from the fund perspective, promoting RBF interventions that target socio-
environmental impactful outcomes could be a good strategy since under the umbrella of 
social finance it could unlock additional capital from impact investors, being thus, a good 
fundraising strategy for the Global LPG Fund. On the other hand, not well-defined or not 
enough ambitious RBF targets can be seen as a regular form of subsidy for the service 
providers, declares L. Patel. Consequently, as suggested by Bisaga and To (2021), this may 
develop a heavy reliance on support and translate into similar challenges to grant funding. 
The verification process is the foundation of RBF approaches. Hence, implementing 
monitoring and evaluation systems that are accessible, objective and adequate for the 
intended targets is key for the successful implementation of an RBF intervention (Stritzke 
et al., 2021). Unfortunately, as A. G. Egziabher and I. Bisaga declare, it can be a very difficult 
task when targeting outcomes and not outputs, especially if the intervention is carried out 
in remote displacement settings. Under these circumstances the measurement becomes 
more expensive to manage and takes more time to verify, which leads to a longer waiting 
time until beneficiary companies can receive the reward, creating potential cash flow 
challenges (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2021a). I. Bisaga brings the example of the clean 
cooking health benefits, which are impossible to be instantly quantified and can only be 
effectively measured through the counting of health services provided, illnesses, or deaths 
related to respiratory problems in a certain period of time compared to a business-as-usual 
scenario. 
==PAGE:50==
 
43 
A noted benefit of RBF approaches is that they typically give service providers 
freedom to develop their own strategy, says V. Sabula. By allowing them to operate however 
they feel more comfortable, it can engage a larger number of private companies, so 
increasing market competitivity. This is one of the reasons why RBF programmes have been 
transitioning from a fixed incentive reward system (e.g. per stove sold) to reverse 
auctions17, which in case of competition may lead to financial savings for the funder. In any 
case, the fact that RBF approaches are based on pay-per-success can challenge the start of 
operations due to the already described high upfront cost of clean cooking interventions 
(Stritzke et al., 2021). Therefore, despite the attractiveness of the RBF mechanism it can 
hardly be applied alone, and that is something the Global LPG Fund should take into 
consideration. 
▪ 
Carbon financing 
Carbon finance is an innovative funding mechanism that puts a financial value on the 
carbon emissions avoided (carbon credits). It is a form of RBF because it requires a strict 
process of verification (Hamerkop, 2022). Given the large amount of GHG emissions that 
can be reduced by transitioning to clean cooking systems in displacement settings, it is a 
financial mechanism that is gaining a lot of interest in the humanitarian energy space, as 
several experts agree.  
Bisaga and To (2021) report that in 2014 the amount of solid biomass burnt by 
refugees reached 3.9 million tons of oil equivalent, realising 14.3 million tons of CO2 to the 
atmosphere. UNHCR’s first carbon financing agreement was done in a refugee camp in 
Rwanda in 2014 (Tran et al., 2021). Since then, other interventions have benefited from 
carbon credits. For instance, an ongoing solar cookers intervention in Chad, which was 
initially supported by grant funding, switched to carbon financing in 2019 upon reaching a 
level of scale (Bisaga & To, 2021). 
To be able to claim carbon credits, a project must ensure additionality, says O. 
Levallois, expert in carbon finance. This means to demonstrate that the project will result in 
emission reductions that would not occur under a business-as-usual scenario, which added 
to the continuous emission monitoring can translate to be a very significant cost 
(Hamerkop, 2018). Consequently, combining carbon finance with standard RBF funding 
approaches can be a good option to dissolve results-verification costs among different 
financial support schemes. 
When a project is certified following any of the multiple existing standards, each of 
the tons of CO2 not emitted will generate one carbon credit. Then, these credits can be sold 
to companies that want to offset their emissions (Hamerkop, 2022). Therefore, as Bisaga 
and To (2021) remark, carbon financing can increase the financial viability of a project by 
creating an additional revenue stream. This additional revenue can be used to subsidise part 
of the fuel cost, reducing affordability barriers and boosting the market, which can be very

appealing when trying to engage private companies to participate in interventions in 
displacement settings, agree K. Chen and O. Levallois. 
 Nevertheless, the process of certification of carbon credits is very time consuming 
and can extend for several years until the credits are obtained and are ready to be traded, 
declares O. Levallois. Furthermore, the ownership of the credits could be disputed between 
different stakeholders, like the government of the host country (Hamerkop, 2018). In 
developed markets, there are regulations about the ownership of the credits but in 
developing markets, where these approaches are still very new, it is something that a 
project must clarify before moving forward with the carbon financing, adds O. Levallois. 
Among the barriers, there is also the instability of carbon credit markets, which can 
impact the long-term planning of energy projects and the availability of future funding (Tran 
et al., 2021). Despite in the last two years carbon credit prices for clean cooking projects 
have risen from around USD 3-4 to 6-9 per ton CO2eq and some studies expect a further 
increase (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2022), it is convenient that interventions willing to 
benefit from this mechanism design protection measures from undesired price volatility. 
From the perspective of the Global LPG Fund, that could be addressed by supporting 
interventions that follow the most popularly accepted certification standards, such as the 
Gold Standard or the Clean Development Mechanism (Hamerkop, 2022). That could ensure 
a larger number of potential buyers and liquidity in the carbon market, says O. Levallois. 
Additionally, the fund could replicate the initiative taken by the Fair Climate Fund and offer 
a fixed price for carbon offsetting (Tran et al., 2021). 
▪ 
Development impact bonds (DIBs) 
Impact bonds are also RBF mechanisms in which the upfront capital is provided by a 
private impact investor, under a contract agreement with a funder, for a project whose goal 
is delivering social/environmental positive outcomes (see Figure 14). Achieved those goals, 
the funder repays the investor with an interest, whereas a failure implies that the impact 
investor loses part of the capital borrowed, including the interest (Bisaga & To, 2021; Cohen 
& Patel, 2019). Consequently, DIB address one of the main challenges of RBF approaches; 
the access to upfront capital. 
As happens with carbon financing, the impact certification is time and resource 
consuming, declares H. Mahbouli. However, unlikely the previous financial instrument, DIBs 
can add value to positive impacts in different spaces. The report published by MECS and 
Energy 4 Impact (2022) includes the example of Cardano Development, a company that has 
created a DIB to finance companies bringing clean cooking solutions to market in Sub-
Saharan Africa. The DIB combines several impact measures using the Gold Standard multi-
SDG impact methodology (Shell Foundation, 2019) and aims to certify the impact of projects 
on different particular SDGs of interest to donors. 
Therefore, through DIBs, the Global LPG Fund could potentially monetise LPG 
benefits, such as improved health, gender impacts, livelihood and environmental benefits 
and create additional revenue sources. However, as H. Mahbouli and O. Levallois coincide, 
DIBs are still at a very early stage and there is not much interest in SDGs certificates aside 
from carbon credits.

Risk guarantees 
Guarantees can mitigate risks associated with the displacement setting market, such 
as uncertain longevity and unstable demand (Cohen & Patel, 2019). According to V. Corno, 
these guarantees comfort private service providers because any potential negative event 
that compromises the commercial viability of the project (e.g. unexpected cease of 
operations of the refugee camp) would imply the execution of the guarantee. Furthermore, 
risk guarantees can ease private actors’ access to traditional financial instruments, such as 
commercial loans (Cohen & Patel, 2019; Van Landeghem, 2016). This mechanism is 
illustrated in Figure 15. 
I. Bisaga suggest that the Global LPG Fund could play a guarantor role for private 
investors and service providers willing to collaborate in LPG interventions in displacement 
settings. However, as L. Patel claims, the private sector is likely to request higher guarantees 
in the first LPG interventions with more protection during the first years of the intervention 
given the novelty of the fund and the lack of knowledge of the market. Additionally, V. Corno 
underlines that setting up a risk guarantee is not straightforward as it requires several 
related activities, such as the development of a legal and regulatory framework aligned with 
the applicable laws in the country, which might not be cost-efficient depending on the size 
of the intervention.

5.3 Setting up an LPG financing facility 
The process of setting up a financing facility must be carried out with extreme 
diligence and professionalism, says H. Mahbouli. Given the challenging environment where 
the fund plans to operate, all the details must be well captured before approaching funders, 
he continues. This includes key elements such as the mission of the fund, the considerations 
to obtain international support, and the operation and management strategy. 
▪ 
Defining the mission of the fund 
The statement of financing facility mission must be clear and concise, claims H. 
Mahbouli. Furthermore, as Cohen and Patel (2019) indicate, the mission should frame the 
scope of the fund, in terms of the type of interventions and the focus market it is going to 
support. Following these guidelines, the fund’s goal is well defined by Haselip et al. (2022): 
Address and satisfy identified potential for LPG as a viable cooking fuel solution and 
to provide properly structured and supervised financial support to maximize the 
achievement of that potential. The overarching goal would be to maximize the 
effectiveness of public sector funds and to support supply and demand that will 
evolve to commercially viable private sector activity (Haselip et al., 2022: 4). 
Regarding the focus market, and despite the concept note developed by the GLPG and 
UNHCR named the fund “Global LPG Fund for Refugees”, there is a common agreement 
among experts that the target market should be broader than just refugees. In fact, Haselip 
et al. (2022) already point out that the fund should have the mandate to serve surrounding 
HCs. Following this line of argument, I. Bisaga emphasises that access to clean cooking 
solutions is a worldwide issue that is particularly exacerbated in fragile contexts in the 
Global South, so if we aim to universally achieve the SDG7 the scope of this fund should be 
broadened. 
==PAGE:54==
 
47 
Additionally, V. Sabula underlines the importance of supporting the mission with a 
strong rationale. This can be done by highlighting the gap addressed with the creation of the 
fund, i.e. the energy access gap for clean cooking solutions due to the lack of longer-term 
funding and sustainable market-based approaches. Finally, finding the differentiator 
element of the fund is key (Mahbouli, 2017). As has been reported in this research, a few 
existing funds are targeting similar opportunities but none of them is dedicated exclusively 
to LPG solutions, claims E. Puzzolo. Therefore, referring to the special benefits of using LPG 
for cooking and its contributions to the SDGs is fundamental. 
▪ 
Seeking international support 
Having key stakeholders on board is essential for the success of the Global LPG Fund, 
starting with the UNHCR, as claimed by all the humanitarian energy experts interviewed. 
The UNHCR is the organisation with better oversight and understanding of the 
displacement population needs, adds E. Puzzolo. However, the UNHCR in its 2019 Global 
Report (2019) also underlined the importance that the fund receives international 
community support. 
The main challenge to overcome when seeking international support for the Global 
LPG Fund is the political risk, states K. Chen. Despite the positive effects LPG can have on 
health, protection of forest resources, climate change, poverty, etc., it has been a challenge 
to mobilise funding to support interventions due to its fossil-fuel nature (NORAD, 2020). 
This reluctance has been revealed by some interviewees: the IFU manager and the CIP 
director coincide in refusing the support to fossil-fuel linked projects, and L. Clarke 
indicated that the IKEA Foundation, funder of the Renewable Energy 4 Refugees 
programme, would not fund LPG for the same reason. 
Although there is clear evidence of LPG support among developing countries and 
governments hosting displacement settings in the recent years18 (Chen et al., 2021; GLPGP, 
2022), the Global LPG Fund needs to first address the international political objection, 
especially from developed countries, to have real opportunities, declares K. Chen. This 
aversion should be battled with clear information that highlights and proves the benefits 
over the long-term of transitioning to LPG in displacement settings, he continues. The 
international community must be aware of the strong base of evidence regarding the 
significant health and environmental benefits of LPG as a transitional cooking fuel, despite 
its being a fossil fuel (CCA, 2019). 
To overcome this barrier, the Global LPG Fund should consider including references 
to bioLPG, suggest E. Puzzolo and K. Chen. Even though this technology is still in an early 
stage of development19, various publications agree that, once fully developed, bioLPG 
production could greatly reduce the public and private sector investment required to 
significantly increase clean cooking capacity (K. Chen et al., 2021; GLPGP, 2020; IEA, 2021).

BioLPG can be produced from municipal solid waste and other renewable feedstocks, which

Finally, efforts should be put into building competitive cost analysis between 
alternative existing high-tier solutions which might provide similar benefits in 
displacement contexts, so the international community can see the advantages of LPG 
versus other technologies, suggests the CIP director. For instance, rural or peri-urban 
displacement settings are often off-grid, meaning that the provision of electric cooking 
would require a considerable investment to interconnect the households, making the 
technology much more expensive (Howells et al., 2006; Puzzolo et al., 2019). As for solar-
powered cooking systems, Vianello (2016) highlights the main disadvantages: the need to 
cook outside during daylight hours; cannot fry or roast; requires continual realignment; and 
the need for extensive awareness-raising. 
▪ 
Operation and management of the fund 
K. Chen claims that it should be the responsibility of the UNHCR to assess the 
potential of LPG in displacement settings. On the other hand, the GLPGP should administer 
the fund and provide the technical and commercial knowledge to execute the interventions 
if a potential for LPG is revealed.  
While the technical and commercial know-how brought by the GLPGP can contribute 
to reducing the lack of operational experience of the fund, which is the fifth-biggest barrier 
for clean cooking funders (see Figure 16), it is particularly relevant the impact of UNHCR by 
mapping LPG potential in contexts of displacement, as the lack of an investable pipeline 
ranks the second main barrier in the statistics (MECS & Energy 4 Impact, 2022). As Cohen 
and Patel (2019) mention, a well-developed pipeline together with prospects of scalable 
and bigger-ticket investments tend to attract larger, more serious and established players.

Mention apart requires the barrier of lack of profitability from Figure 16. As it is in 
the interest of the humanitarian energy sector to narrow down the funding gap in clean 
cooking space, the fund should ideally approach both public and private funders when 
raising capital. To attract private capital, H. Mahbouli underlines that the strategy of the 
fund must make commercial sense, while Cohen and Patel (2019) indicate that evidence of 
underlying returns and/or cash generation capability is going to be required by commercial 
investors. Consequently, the fund’s operation should be between a grant-making facility and 
a commercial investment vehicle, aligned with K. Chen’s opinion. 
Under these circumstances, funders may be reluctant to provide pure grant funding, 
especially for market-based interventions because it will be deploying the fund over time. 
To ensure the sustainability of the facility, the fund should, whenever the conditions of the 
market allow it, prioritise returnable grant instruments that can then be recycled into new 
projects. This is, following a revolving fund concept, remarks M. Pal. On the other hand, 
traditional humanitarian partners might be unwilling to participate in a fund that follows a 
neoliberal approach, turning a profit from business activities that target impoverished and 
vulnerable communities, as V. Corno and Cohen and Patel (2019) coincide. 
To address these differences, H. Mahbouli appeals to the social finance framework 
and the use of blended financial structures where, besides traditional public/donor and 
commercial funding, social-impact capital is considered. Impact investors can contribute to 
balancing funders' interests as they sit between financing-only and impact-only positions. 
However, they would probably require a strong commitment from the fund towards the 
monitoring and verification of impacts, reducing the amount of capital available for 
investments. Besides, the monitoring and verification process, if not aligned with an 
internationally accepted system for measuring and managing impacts for clean energy 
access investments, like the IRIS+ (Bass et al., 2019), can generate discrepancies among 
participants. Proof of that is the different perceptions of impact between some of the experts 
interviewed, like the IFU manager and H. Mahbouli.

6. Conclusions 
This project has been driven by the research question “how can the Global LPG Fund 
contribute to the transition to clean cooking systems in displacement settings in the Global 
South?”. To shed some light on the subject, research sub-questions were designed to cover 
the different areas involving the Global LPG Fund's contribution. Those focused on revealing 
the considerations that the Global LPG Fund should have when creating an LPG market in 
displacement settings; applying financial mechanisms to engage the private sector; and 
setting up its financing facility. 
The literature review was key to contextualise the research. It provided a 
comprehensive understanding of the energy access situation in displacement settings, the 
status of the transition to clean and modern cooking systems, and the use of LPG as a 
suitable solution for that purpose. At the same time, the literature evidenced an existing gap 
in the provision of clean energy access services in displacement contexts, particularly for 
cooking solutions. 
The gap has its roots in a number of challenges the humanitarian energy sector faces 
nowadays, such as the lack of long-term thinking from humanitarian agencies, the absence 
of technical skills to support energy interventions, or the lack of sustained funding. To 
address this gap, the research has taken into consideration three different perspectives: the 
humanitarian and development aid divergences, the use of sustainable humanitarianism to 
bridge these spaces, and the relevance of social finance discipline to frame impactful social 
and environmental investments. 
In order to narrow down the existing gap, there is a widespread consensus among 
experts that points out the necessity to provide sustainable long-term solutions that bridge 
humanitarian and development aid approaches. This means shifting away from inefficient 
free-distribution 
models, 
traditionally 
used 
in 
the 
humanitarian 
sector 
and 
counterproductive for development aims, to market-based initiatives. This opens the door 
to private sector participation, which must be attracted to participate. Under a social finance 
framework, it is expected that market-based initiatives led by the private sector can 
optimise resources and obtain positive impacts for the displaced population while 
generating commercial profit. 
The Global LPG Fund, still under consideration, could act as an enabler vehicle for 
this process to happen, positively contributing to the clean cooking transition in 
displacement settings. Aiming to address and satisfy LPG cooking potential, the fund would 
tackle the clean energy access gap while financially supporting private sector activity to 
become commercially viable. This support cannot be done without the proper LPG market 
conditions, which the fund could contribute to creating as part of its overarching goal of 
supporting the supply and demand. After an extensive analysis, five key elements for the 
market creation stood out: (i) the regulatory environment, (ii) the importance of reaching 
economies of scale, (iii) the affordability, (iv) the awareness, behaviour, and safety 
conditions, and (v) the market capacity of development. 
==PAGE:58==
 
51 
Established the market conditions, the Global LPG Fund could work to maximise the 
effectiveness of the currently existing public funding in the humanitarian energy space and 
support financial mechanisms that engage the private sector. These financial mechanisms 
should comfort private actors by de-risking their participation in LPG interventions and 
securing certain economic returns.  
Beyond pure grant funding, this research has analysed other instruments that can 
contribute to designing financially sustainable interventions, such as concessions, Results-
Based-Financing (RBF) approaches (including carbon financing and Development Impact 
Bonds (DIB)) and guarantees. The analysis showed that given the inherent complexity of 
displacement settings, clean cooking interventions, including the financial mechanisms that 
support them, must be context-specific. Furthermore, it has been highlighted the 
importance of diversifying instruments to achieve sustainable delivery models and build 
resilient market capacities. 
Despite the potential advantages of considering the Global LPG Fund as a tool to 
reduce the clean energy access gap in displacement settings, the fund will not be able to 
operate if does not receive support from the international community. Nevertheless, 
achieving this support can be challenging given the existing reticence to support a fossil-
fuel derived product, especially among the developed countries. 
To conclude, with the support of the main insights extracted from the thematic 
analysis, a series of recommendations for the next steps of the Global LPG Fund are 
presented. 
6.1 Further recommendations 
Recommendation I 
The Global LPG Fund should coordinate and cooperate actions with humanitarian 
and development agencies to influence host countries' policymaking. Achieving strong 
relationships among stakeholders may lead to political stability and favourable conditions 
for displacement settings. 
Recommendation II 
The Global LPG Fund should work to reveal the latent LPG demand in displacement 
settings by gathering reliable data and implementing pilot projects. Additionally, demand 
can be boosted through in-person demonstrations of the LPG cooking and involving 
displaced communities, and particularly women, moving towards people-centred designs 
tailored to the local needs.  
Recommendation III 
The Global LPG Fund should ensure affordability levels in displacement settings. This 
could be achieved by partnering with PAYG technology developers or MFIs, so instruments 
designed to overcome the high upfront costs of LPG are made accessible to the displaced 
population. 
==PAGE:59==
 
52 
Recommendation IV 
The Global LPG Fund should prioritise the support to market-based interventions 
through a diversified portfolio of financial mechanisms. Besides, interventions should keep 
a strong commitment to measure and verify impacts, so they could potentially benefit from 
carbon financing and SDGs credits, and attract additional capital from impact investors.  
Recommendation V 
The Global LPG Fund should seek support from the international community by 
presenting reliable information about the benefits of the transition to LPG in displacement 
settings, cost-competitive analysis regarding alternative clean cooking technologies, and 
the upcoming potential of bioLPG.
