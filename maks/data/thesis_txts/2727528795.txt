Abstract
Autonomous driving of outdoor robots on unstructured roads is a complex task due to
the lack of high-precision maps and distinct road features. The primary objective of this
project is to propose a data fusion-based solution for outdoor path navigation that allows
robots to effectively perceive the environment and perform path planning on unstructured
roads, ultimately resulting in autonomous navigation.
A deep neural network was trained using image data captured from the spring and summer
seasons. The network successfully performed semantic segmentation of the environment,
separating the images into road and background segments.
Based on the segmented
images, a path planning algorithm combined with a trajectory optimization algorithm is
proposed to generate the final navigation instructions.
Through testing, the proposed solution was able to meet the objectives of this project.
Also, suggestions for specific problems are presented at the end of the thesis.

Introduction
The initial chapter, covered in Section 1.1, introduces the topic of autonomous naviga-
tion in natural environments for mobile robots and presents key research questions and
objectives in Section 1.2. The latter includes designing effective navigation systems and
enhancing robot perception adaptability and further delineates the project’s precise goals,
such as outdoor road detection, optimal path planning, obstacle avoidance, and ensuring
autonomous driving. Lastly, Section 1.3 provides an overview of the entire thesis struc-
ture, bridging current knowledge analysis to extensive system testing and implications for
future research.
1.1
Context and Motivation
The development of mobile robots with full self-service mobility has been a hot topic of
research in the robotics community in recent decades. With advances in computer science
and energy technology, our lives have the potential to change dramatically as a result of
autonomous driving.
A key challenge in autonomous robots is navigation in natural environments such as
forests [1, 2]. While robots have been successfully deployed in controlled environments
such as factories, navigating in natural environments is much more challenging due to
the presence of obstacles, uneven terrain, and unpredictable weather conditions [3, 4,
5]. Effective robot perception and navigation strategies are required to enable robots
to navigate in these environments without human intervention [6]. In this context, the
data fusion of multi-sensors has emerged as a promising approach for improving robot
navigation in natural environments [7, 8]. For example, through the integration of visual
information with radar data, robots can enhance their perception and responsiveness to
the surrounding environment, resulting in improved navigation accuracy and reliability.
By utilizing a blend of sensors, mobile robots can accurately identify drivable areas,
enabling them to achieve autonomous driving that is both safe and reliable.
The motivation for this thesis stems from DTU’s automation and control department’s
outdoor robot navigation project. The project focuses on developing a comprehensive sys-
tem to address the challenges of mobile robot perception and navigation in unstructured
outdoor environments, including agriculture and forests. The goal of the master’s thesis
is to develop a vision-based and depth-informed navigation system that empowers robots
with the capability to autonomously and safely navigate specific outdoor roads through
data fusion.

2.3
Related Work
Autonomous navigation technology has been the subject of extensive research in the
robotics community in these decades, and traversable region detection is one of the funda-
mental problems for autonomous navigation systems. This section will introduce recent
studies in the field, across various scenarios and diverse solutions. The objective is to
gather insights and valuable references that will guide the design and implementation of
the path navigation system for this project.
The vehicle’s drivable terrain are structured roads, semi-structured roads, and unstruc-
tured paths. Structured roads typically feature edge lines and uniform pavement struc-
tures, common in urban settings, whereas unstructured paths lack a structural layer,
mostly embodying natural landscapes found in agricultural fields or wilderness areas. Our
analysis mainly focuses on path detection strategies rooted in visual techniques, some of
which integrate LiDAR to assist in detecting navigable regions.
For autonomous driving systems, the process of identifying traversable roads is considered
an image segmentation problem. The fundamental objective is to further identify safely
driving areas, based on the features of the road depicted in various scenarios within the
image. For structured and semi-structured pavements, color characteristics are distinctive
and can be utilized for identification purposes, Xiaowen Wu et al. [9] presented a three-
level data fusion based on Dempster-Shafer’s decision theory to robustly analyze road
area images in front of the car from a single color camera. Fig.2.4 shows the results of
road segmentation in different scenarios output as a bird’s eye view.
In addition to color features, the texture of the road surface, clear road edge demarca-
tions, and the vanishing point play crucial roles in road area segmentation within images.
Shihavuddin et al. [10] propose a path map generation approach for autonomous vehicles

2.4
Problem analysis
A comprehensive and specific problem analysis will be conducted in this section based
on the specific hardware and testing environment, preceding system design. The system
incorporates a depth camera D455, mounted at the front top of the vehicle terrain hopper.
From the previous section, we know that the irregularity and diversity of unstructured
roads in the field environment are key factors to consider when building the robot nav-
igation system for this project. While the Terrain Hopper already had a laser scanner
mounted on the front bottom, this sensor was excluded from the solution due to the lack
of reliable features on the running trail around the DTU, unlike city roads. Furthermore,
satellite positioning in agricultural and field environments is often compromised by tree
and vegetation shading, leading to the decision not to incorporate GPS sensors in our
system. As most of the work in the field of robot navigation is around computer vision,
this project’s solution focuses on data fusion based on visual information.
Traditional image processing methods are heavily influenced by light and terrain, which
must be considered for autonomous navigation tasks in field environments. Consequently,
this project primarily employs image segmentation using neural networks as its main
solution. In section 2.3, CNN has proven to be a highly effective and popular approach for
road segmentation, particularly when working with small datasets. The U-Net structure,
previously utilized in a DTU project, is one such network. The Vision Transformer (ViT)
structure has demonstrated superior performance over CNN structures in many vision
tasks due to its capacity to capture global information from a single image. However,
ViT demands more data and requires substantial datasets to outperform CNNs. For the
purpose of road segmentation in this project, the U-Net++ structure will be employed,
with U-Net serving as the baseline.
First, we use the deep learning framework of U-Net++ to segment the path area. The
training samples are mainly collected from the typical scenarios of DTU running trail
including straight road, curve road and fork road. Sunlight and shaded roads are also
considered.
Based on visual detection, our system used a computer vision approach
to the image segmentation result to extract the path edge, which will be used for path
planning. In unstructured scenarios, local path planning based on local path perception is
important for autonomous navigation, and two different local path planners were utilized
in this project. From those path planners, a line can be generated which will be sent to
the robot via the mobotware platform.
Furthermore, as outlined in Section 2.1 regarding the route, this project must account for
the robot’s navigation through urban roads that intersect the running path. Implementing
a pre-programmed solution is not feasible without a GPS sensor.
To concentrate on
autonomous navigation in unstructured environments, the system will be designed to
enable the robot to stop safely when approaching an urban road, at which point manual
control can be employed to cross the street.
Finally, the robot must possess an emergency stop and obstacle avoidance capability to
rectify potential errors in image segmentation. Utilizing the depth data returned by the
camera, the system will continuously monitor the sector located 1-4 meters in front of the
robot. This real-time detection will enable timely warnings to prevent collisions.
The proposed solution enables the implementation of an outdoor robot path navigation

System Overview
Fig.2.8 shows the diagram of our outdoor robot local path navigation system. The first
part is the road detection in a single image frame.
A convolutional Neural Network
based model is trained to segment the road area in the image frame. After homography

Summary
In this chapter, a comprehensive and detailed analysis of the proposed autonomous nav-
igation system solution for this project is presented. First, based on the objectives and
execution requirements of this project, the runway of DTU Lyngby campus is used as the

The overall solution will be applied to TerrainHopper, a cross-country robot equipped with

In addition, depth information is used to identify obstacles in the real-time environment
to assist the robot in obstacle avoidance, and two different local path planning algorithms
will be applied to plan the actual path of the robot.

tonomous navigation system will be described in detail.

Calibration
In computer vision, establishing a camera’s geometric model is crucial to determine the
correlation between 3D positions on an object and its image counterparts. This involves
camera calibration, often using the Zhang Zhengyou algorithm[15]. It captures checker-
board images at various angles to calculate the camera’s intrinsic and extrinsic parameters.
With the self-calibration feature available in the Intel RealSense SDK 2.0, calibrating the
D455 stereo camera has become more eﬀicient. It allows for a calibration process in under
15 seconds, eliminating the need for a dedicated checkerboard picture.

training a fully convolutional network, like U-Net++. High-resolution images, such as

offer substantial detail, potentially enhancing model performance. However, this benefit
comes with a trade-off, as higher resolution necessitates increased computational resources
and memory, thereby prolonging the training process.
The UNet++ architecture, by design, is capable of accommodating variable image input
sizes. For the image semantic segmentation task in question, which requires differentiating
the path region from the background, it may be beneficial to explore downsampling the
image to a lower resolution.
Experimenting with different resolutions will help find an optimal balance between re-
source usage and performance, as presented in Figure 3.7, which depicts the sunlight spot
path at varied resolutions. Features and boundaries of the path retain their clarity at
256x256 and 128 x 128 resolutions. However, a noticeable loss of detail occurs as resolu-
tion drops to 64 x 64 and further, complicates the accurate labeling of roads. To put it
into perspective, an image of size 256 x 256 comprises 65,536 pixels, whereas an image of
size 128 x 128 has 16,384 pixels. This means that using 128 x 128 reduces the number of
pixels by a factor of four, which allows for a speedup of nearly four times when performing
convolutional layer calculations, with fewer parameters and memory usage. Therefore, a
128x128 image optimally preserves vital details while ensuring eﬀicient model training
and inference.

4.1.1

U-Net is a deep learning network based on encoder-decoder structure, its most important
feature is the left-right symmetric structure and skip connection, seeing in Figure.4.2a.
U-Net is capable of accommodating input and output images of varying sizes. The U-Net
architecture primarily consists of two parts: the left side, known as the encoder, which
is responsible for downsampling, and the right side, termed the decoder, which performs
upsampling. Together, these components give the network its distinctive ’U’ shape.

Perspective Transformation
Perspective transformation, or homography, is a commonly employed technique in com-
puter vision. It enables the alteration of the viewpoint from which an image or 3D scene
is seen, making it appear as though viewed from a different position. In robotics, a typical
application involves transforming a 3D scene into a 2D image, such as creating a bird’s
eye view or a top-down view. This transformation can provide a comprehensive perspec-
tive of the environment, proving valuable in various tasks including navigation and object
detection.
The bird’s-eye perspective significantly simplifies the robot’s tasks related to path plan-
ning and obstacle avoidance. With this overhead view, a direct correlation can be estab-
lished between the image coordinate system and the robot’s own coordinate system. As
a result, the robot can utilize a flat 2D map for path planning and navigation tasks.

In computer vision, contour detection is commonly used for identifying and localizing
objects in an image. Contours primarily signify the boundaries of distinct objects present
within the image. The semantic segmentation result is a binary image. Hence, the road
region, denoted by pixel value 1, is identified as the path contour within this binary image.
The method proposed by [25] follows the following steps:
• Contour Search: Using the OpenCV[26] library’s find Contour() function to
obtain the external contours of two path areas. The findContours() function is used
to detect the contours in a binary image, and the location of the key pixels that
make up the contour are returned as an array.

• Closest Point Matching: For each point in the first contour, solve to find its

• Mutual Pair Identification: Check the nearest point matching result, whether
there are pairs of points that are nearest to each other. The contour merging is
performed in the next step if valid, otherwise, the largest contour set is returned.
• Contour Merge: Find the leftmost and rightmost point pairs based on horizontal

5.1
Mobotware
As mentioned in Section2.5 for system overview, the robot’s navigation command execu-
tion is performed through the mobotware operating platform, and the control commands
need to adhere to a specific programming language, Small Mobile Robot Control Lan-
guage (SMR-CL)[27]. SMR-CL is a simple interpreted language intended for the control
of mobile robots. The two most simple commands are ’fwd d @v0.5’ and ’turnr r b’, which
control the robot to move forward a given distance ’d’ at a speed of 0.5m/s or turn ’b’
degrees with radius ’r’ m, respectively. The two basic commands can drive the robot but
it is diﬀicult to achieve continuous smooth driving.
Figure 5.1: An illustrate of Mobotware drive command:’drive 1.5 1.0 45’
Therefore, another more general command ’drive [x,y,th]’ is chosen as the control com-
mand. As illustrated in Figure.5.1, this command takes the target point and angle as
inputs within the robot’s odometer coordinate system (with positive x denoting forward
and positive y to the left). It navigates the robot to align its trajectory with the line
defined by that target point and angle. Moreover, this command ensures a consistent
velocity when executed multiple times in quick succession, e.g. 8-10 times per second.

5.3
Middle-Line Path planner
Ensuring vehicle safety is the most important factor in path planning, thus placing the
robot at the road’s center is optimal considering hardware constraints and steering speed.
By maintaining the robot in the middle line of the road, the risk of colliding with curbs
or other monitored obstacles is minimized.
Moreover, this approach aligns the path
closely with the actual trend of the road, providing a smoother trajectory without frequent
directional changes.
As discussed in Chapter 4, perspective transformation converts the original image into
a bird’s eye view to generate a local path representation. The Middle-Line Path(MLP)
algorithm proposed here transforms road edges from this bird’s eye view into a 2D grid
map in the robot’s coordinate system. Path centers, identified as key points at 0.5m
intervals along these edges, are linked together to create the final planned paths. A visual
illustration is provided in Figure 5.4.
Figure 5.4: An example of Middle-Line path planning algorithm
The Middle-Line Path (MLP) algorithm is very simple and intuitive. It does not accom-
modate the robot’s dynamics like speed, turn radius, and other physical constraints. This
can impact the robot’s capability to drive on the planned path. Moreover, if the robot
strictly maintains a central position, effective obstacle avoidance could be compromised.
Thus, the path derived from MLP requires further optimization to make it applicable
in real-world scenarios and incorporate aspects like obstacle avoidance and trajectory
optimization, which we will discuss in the next section.

Future work
In this chapter, a selection of suggestions for future work based on the solution and test
result of this project, will be presented.
• Semantic segmentation of color and depth images: From the related work in
Chapter 2 and the test results in Chapter 4, it is clear that segmentation based on
visual images only is susceptible to brightness variations and blurred images. There-
fore, integrating depth images can offer a more effective approach. Neural networks
are used to simultaneously learn color and depth images to achieve more accurate
semantic segmentation of the surrounding environment. Moreover, enhancing the
segmentation with additional semantic classes can improve the model’s understand-
ing of the environment, thereby augmenting the robot’s perception capabilities.
• Add more sensors: In the complex and changing outdoor environment, the camera
is easily disturbed by external objects, such as leaves and sand, which is diﬀicult
to cope with only a monocular or depth camera. Therefore, introducing a diverse
range of sensors can be beneficial in building a more robust system. For example, the
introduction of LiDAR can obtain more accurate environmental information in 3D
space and realize different perception modules, such as simultaneous localization
and mapping. In addition, there are GPS, multiple depth cameras and distance
sensors that can be added to the system.
• Create a well-built Map: With access to more precise maps and environmental
information, a robot is better equipped to drive autonomously. The construction
of global and local maps facilitates more eﬀicient path-planning tasks, enhances
the accuracy of robot trajectory prediction, and minimizes unnecessary energy con-
sumption.
• Improves adaptability to complex terrain and weather: The solution pro-
posed in this project supports the robot’s autonomous navigation in the outdoor
environment during the spring and summer seasons. Hence, to handle winter con-
ditions like snow or navigating dirt roads, it’s necessary to augment our dataset
with relevant scenarios and retrain the neural network to accommodate these new
conditions.
Moreover, due to the limitations of perspective transformation, the vehicle may
miscalculate distances on terrains with substantial undulations or slopes, which can
lead to instability in autonomous navigation. Therefore, investigating autonomous
navigation across a variety of terrains would be a valuable avenue for future research.

Conclusion
A path navigation system is implemented in this project for driving a robot autonomously
on an unstructured road——DTU running trail. Terrain Hopper, a mobile robot equipped
with an onboard computer and an RGB-D camera, was used for this project.
The system is composed of three main modules: the perception module, the path planning
module, and the robot control module. Robot control is managed by the Mobotware
operating platform already equipped in the robot.
To begin, the neural network ”U-Net++” is used as an image semantic segmentation mod-
ule to aid the robot in identifying drivable unstructured path areas. Utilizing the seman-
tic segmentation information, the local path representation is accomplished by extracting
road edge information through perspective transformation and boundary extraction, and
other image analysis methods.
Subsequently, the path planning module is equipped with two different path planners:
A* path planner and Middle Line Path(MLP) planner. Furthermore, to optimize the
robot’s navigation decisions for the reference paths generated by the MLP, a multiple path
generation algorithm based on lateral isometric transformation and a pure path tracking
algorithm are implemented. In addition, the module incorporates collision analysis and
multi-frame data fusion to enhance the stability of robot control.
The system has undergone extensive testing and analysis on both individual modules and
the system as a whole. The test results emphatically demonstrate the system’s ability
to stably control the robot’s autonomous navigation under a variety of road types and
environmental conditions. Finally, based on the system’s performance and test results,
potential future research directions are proposed. These suggestions could help improve
the system’s robustness, enabling it to tackle more complex outdoor environments.
