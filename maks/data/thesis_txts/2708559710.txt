Abstract
Offshore wind development in Europe has already exceeded 25 GW by the end of 2021,
while the first large­scale commercial projects will begin construction in the U.S. in 2022.
The potential for U.S. offshore wind is significant, and the current pipeline includes 35.5
GW of development on the North Atlantic East Coast. While U.S. offshore wind will ben­
efit from developing a local supply chain related to manufacturing and logistics, the first
wind farms will depend on the already well­established European supply chain. This work
explores the potential benefits for U.S. offshore wind supply chain development and the
associated opportunities for reducing the levelized cost of energy and subsequently in­
creasing the economic competitiveness of offshore wind in the U.S.

Introduction
Wind energy has a key role in accelerating the global energy transition [1]. Over the
past decades, it has become an integral part of the electricity system due to its evolution
and development in capacity, cost reduction, deployment, and scale. For 2021 total wind
energy capacity additions are estimated to reach 90 GW, where 11 GW corresponds to
offshore wind [2]. In total, IEA’s forecast indicates that global offshore wind cumulative
capacity could reach 117 GW by 2026.
Figure 1.1: Wind Energy Global Cumulative Capacity Forecast, IEA 2021 [2]
In connection to technological trends enhancing energy yields from increasing turbines
size and plant capacity, reducing the Levelized cost of energy (LCOE) is one of the main
elements driving the sector’s growth. The LCOE is the primary tool for approximating the
plant­level unit costs of diverse generation technologies over their operational lifetimes.
Hence, LCOE displays a technology’s economic costs in terms of capital expenditures
(CAPEX), cost of financing (WACC), energy production (AEP), operating costs (OPEX),
and the lifetime of the technology [1]. The LCOE reductions for offshore wind have made
this technology a leading competitor among renewable energy technologies.
Global offshore wind energy trends exhibit LCOE reductions by more than 40% between
2014 and 2019 [3]. LCOE for offshore wind projects commissioned in 2020 ranged within
$78/MWh and $125/MWh (USD2020/MWh) with a global average below $95/MWh [4].
With an approximate 66% share in the total cost for offshore wind energy production as­
sets [1], CAPEX is a fundamental element for targeting future LCOE reductions. Capital
expenditures include the wind turbine generators, the balance of the system (BOS; foun­
dation, electrical infrastructure, and access infrastructure), soft costs, and development
costs [5]. The comprehensive assessment of supply chain and infrastructure impacts in­
volving its main contributors, such as component manufacturing, vessel operations, and
port infrastructure planning, is essential for identifying where to achieve CAPEX reduc­
tions that can support large­scale offshore wind energy deployment [3].
Although significant cost reductions occur in mature markets, such as the North Sea, from
Belgium to Denmark, and the U.K.’s territorial waters [6]; several countries have ambitious

1.1
Motivation
As depicted in Figure 1.2, the U.S. expects to integrate offshore wind power equivalent
to 5.3 GW towards 2025. This forecast is part of the national target for achieving 30
GW of offshore wind installed capacity by 2030 [8]. This ambitious deployment plan is
a suitable scenario to analyze how the U.S. can leverage its manufacturing and infras­
tructure strengths under the current industry practice, where LCOE declines are steered
by CAPEX reductions. Especially considering that, until now, all significant offshore wind
experienced companies and specialized infrastructure remain concentrated in Europe.
1.2
Research Question
The project focus is to provide insightful answers on:
What is the potential for local supply chain development to reduce the LCOE of offshore
wind in the U.S. and thus increase the economic competitiveness of U.S. offshore wind?
The below subset of questions aims to support and expand on this project’s research:
• What CAPEX elements does the supply chain localization most significantly impact?
• How do BOS components costs fluctuate due to project design considerations and
domestic supply chain availability?
• How do the estimated CAPEX and LCOE vary as a function of OWE industry trends
projections within the U.S. environment? E.g., turbine rating increase, grid connec­
tion, or localization.
1.3
Scope
This research work will focus on assessing impacts in offshore wind energy development
costs arising from the early­stage commitments in the U.S. East Coast. Although this

approach applies to multiple locations, this project utilizes the New Jersey Board of Public
Utilities (NJBPU) state­driven offshore wind target for reaching 7.5 GW by 2035 [4] as a
case study. The selection of NJ concentrates on three considerations. First, the projected
early maturation of in­state manufacturing facilities and infrastructure specialized in large­
scale offshore wind projects [9]. Second, the advanced development of Lease Area OCS­
A 0498 [10]. Third, the availability of supply chain data and accessibility to industry project
design parameters [11].
The analysis’ objective is to capture the significance of the local supply chain and in­
frastructure development as an opportunity for future LCOE reduction. Hence, sizing the
research into the NJ case study aims to understand which particular cost categories have
the most significant influence on LCOE within a reasonable spectrum. Therefore, CAPEX
is the main scope for this end. CAPEX conventionally encompasses four dimensions:
BOS, WTG costs, softs costs, and project development. This report only encompass re­
search on BOS components, the remaining dimensions are directly sourced and selected
from the literature examination.
1.4
Approach
The project performs a techno­economic analysis constructed from a wind energy devel­
oper perspective. The modeling of a representative offshore wind farm (ROWF1) in Lease
Area OCS­A 0498 will quantify the impacts of the New Jersey offshore wind supply chain

2.1
Offshore Wind Energy Costs
OWE has been established around several international markets as a key source of low­
cost and clean energy [14]. Historically, LCOE stands out as one of the metric steering
OWE cost analysis [15]. Among several reasons, this metric enables a straightforward
comparison of projects with different cash flow profiles and capital recovery periods [16].
Thus, it stands out as a valuable metric to forecast feasible future cost reductions in the
development of a new OWE market [17]. LCOE indicates a technology’s economic costs
in terms of capital expenditures (CAPEX), cost of financing (WACC), energy production
(AEP), operating costs (OPEX), and the lifetime of the technology [1].
The consolidation of offshore wind as the leading renewable energy generation tech­
nol ogy depends on the ability to identify cost reductions opportunities and knowledge
on how society can maximize the value of wind energy in the electricity and energy sec­
tors [14]. For now, this project only targets one of these aspects, cost reductions from an
LCOE­driven perspective, without considering the value of OWE to the electricity system
and society.

2.1.4
OPEX
Operating costs include those expenditures associated with the management, mainte­
nance, and repair of an OWF [1]. They also include incurred costs with insurance and
lease agreements to utilize the ocean areas where offshore wind assets are located.
Hence, OPEX is equivalent to all costs incurred after the OWF’s operations start, but
before its decommissioning stage, and necessary to operate the asset and maintain its
availability to generate power [4]. These expenditures commonly contribute to an ap­
proximate 34% of the total life cycle cost for OWE projects [1]. At the same time, BVG
associates estimate a share distribution of 28.2% for OPEX [22].

2.1.5
AEP
The Annual Energy Production (AEP) of an OWF is the representation of the total amount
of power generated by each WTG over one year delivered in kilowatt­hour [24]. The wake
loss and energy production of an OWF are significantly dependent on the wind resource,
and the layout of the individual WTGs [3].
In this report, this metric is evaluated as a function of wind speed, wind direction, layout,
and WTG characteristics [12]. The selected model and tool utilized for calculating the
AEP of ROWF1 are described in Section 4.2 and Chapter 5. AEP optimization processes
are disregarded due to this project’s consideration to assume a fixed WTG type, layout
and wind resource.
2.2
Offshore Wind Cost Modelling
Due to the fast­paced development of OWE technology, forecasting its costs evolution is a
challenging task. The available costs forecasts over the last five years exhibit trends that
go beyond the published estimates in 2015 [25]. Hence, the retrospective assessment on
the development of these estimates reveals an underestimated perspective from multiple
stakeholders in the sector. In general, the cost of OWE is highly variable across time
and projects; and there is a predominant uncertainty linked to how technological and
commercial developments would influence future costs [26].
The main statements that relate to this trend could relate to four main categories [16].
Firstly, the interconnection across cost steering elements and their effect on LCOE are
complex to capture in available bottom­up engineering models. Secondly, detailed antici­
pation of technological innovation, selection of available technologies, regulatory environ­
ment, and specific site assessments are uncertain. Thirdly, macroeconomic implications
and policies strongly influence OWE cost development. Lastly, those in possession of the
most reliable knowledge on the updated market conform costing trends hold that informa­
tion confidential due to the high exposure to commercial sensitiveness.
Although these sector trends limit the standardization of cost estimations, there is a con­
stant aim from several stakeholders for elaborating reliable and specialized analysis on
future cost development. These analyses circulate four principal methodologies [16]:
• Expert elicitation

• Analysis or replication of auction bids
• Analysis and extrapolation of historical trends
• Bottom­up engineering analysis
2.2.1
Expert Elicitation
Expert elicitation is a methodology utilizing the knowledge of subject matter experts for the
development of estimates of unknown or uncertain quantities [25]. This method stands
out as a helpful tool when there is a high likelihood that future conditions would differ from
previous events or the availability of historical data is scarce. The main contribution of
this approach is to enable future uncertainty analyses [27]. The advantage of this method
is its flexibility for accepting variations and uncertainties.
Although expert elicitation is a widely used approach for forecasting cost trajectories for
various technologies, certain limitations are constraining its performance as an accurate
methodology. First, the process should clearly state the motivational or cognitive bias
steering the consulted individuals’ perspectives to increase accuracy. Second, the result
might contain a high concentration of heuristics conclusions. Lastly, due to the niche
profile of the industry, consulted individuals could be highly dependent on a homogeneous
set of principles and knowledge sources [25].
2.2.2
Analysis and extrapolation of historical trends
The OWE sector has also relied on analyzing historical cost trends. These mathematical
formulations are mainly known as learning rates or experience curves [28]. The subse­
quent extrapolation of those rates represents a strategy for estimating future cost reduc­
tions [16]. Although learning curves explain previous OWE cost trends, when deployed
for future cost reductions, its main limitation is to speculate future trends following past
events. Additionally, they have a considerable influence based on geographic location,
analyzed period, specification of the learning curve, and primary target, e.g., CAPEX or
LCOE based [28].
The learning curves are integrated into energy systems from a perspective where em­
pirical data is combined in mathematical functions to relate cumulative experiences to
the cost decrease of technologies [29]. Thus, two data sets are needed, the cumulative
production of the specific technology over a substantial period and technology develop­
ment production costs [28]. The experience curves will result in the reduction of the total
product cost as a function of cumulative production [30].

2.2.3
Bottom­up modeling
Future costs characterization is also obtained from bottom­up engineering modeling. This
methodology can evaluate the potential impact of specific technological innovations, such
as WTG OWF design, manufacturing components, installation strategies, and O&M ac­
tivities. This method involves the development of engineering design models and cost
estimates for specific OWE system components. Further, the bottom­up methodology is
used to estimate costs for an overall project and consequently evaluate the life­cycle costs
for a bottom­fixed OWF [13].
The main features of bottom­up models are their ability to estimate costs from a high­
resolution perspective and their capacity to couple novel technology innovations into energy­
systems modeling. Plus, they can capture how these innovations could steer a project’s
development. However, the deployment of these models also has certain drawbacks.
First, the possibility of including avoidable levels of complexity due to its high resolu­
tion. Second, susceptibility for inaccuracy and errors concerning its extensive calibration.
Third, the amount of required data [13]. Fourth, the uncertainty of characterizing technol­
ogy concepts that are still not entirely available. Lastly, challenging to capture the impact
of technology innovations on the whole energy system.
2.2.4
Replication of auction bids
This method is utilized for deriving LCOE or cost components from wind procurement
activities [16]. In practice, the analysis of prices resulting from the competitive procure­
ment of renewable energy sources is employed to compare financial and technological
performance. Nevertheless, comparing prices from auctions or PPAs at face value might
is complex [33].
Differences arise from regulatory environment, tax schemes, support regimes, and market
conditions. In specific analyses, direct comparisons are executed between procurement
prices and LCOE estimates with the assumption that these two metrics are synonymous
[33]. However, the direct comparison of procurement prices with LCOE estimates can
not be performed without an adjustment to fit revenue sources, market conditions, and
financial parameters.
The auction bids replication approach is utilized for its simplicity and suitability for con­
trasting projects with unrelated cash flow profiles and capital­recovery periods. Another

procurement prices from competitive processes are commonly accessible through public

NREL ORBIT
BOS costs have been addressed as all the capital expenditures required to build an OWF
apart from the cost of WTGs (Section 2.1.3). Thus, BOS cost modeling is essential for
project developers to optimize solutions to future cost reductions. The enhancement of
BOS CAPEX is fundamental as the industry evolves from novel installation strategies,
innovative technologies, and project execution conditions.
ORBIT (Offshore Renewable Balance­of­system and Installation Tool) is a model to cal­
culate BOS CAPEX based on specific component cost data, detailed vessel and compo­
nent characteristics, installation processes based on project geospatial data, and industry
standards [13]. The tool delivers the cost outputs and installation times based on OWF
design characteristics. BOS components in ORBIT are defined as substructure, scour
protection, cables (array system and export system), offshore substation, and the instal­
lation processes for each of these components, including WTG installation.
ORBIT is a process­based model that tackles the development of a project from two
fronts—first, the engineered design and procurement of BOS components for an OWF.

supply chain. In the U.S., the Jones Act regulates these operations. The unique char­

activities of merchandise and passengers between two U.S. points, to be U.S. built and
registered (flagged), as well as owned and crewed by U.S. citizens or residents [4]. This
law is under the enforcement of the U.S. Customs And Border Protection (CPB) author­
ities [43]. There are currently no U.S. flagged wind turbine installation vessels (WTIV)
available in the market.

4.1
Offshore Wind Energy Systems
The basic definition of an OWF can be defined as a number of wind turbines, arranged in
a group at a given site [24]. However, offshore wind energy systems do not only represent
the physical composition of a group of wind turbines. OWFs are complex energy systems
embodying multiple disciplines, e.g., aerodynamics, structural engineering, or electrical
design. They involve several stakeholders, such as project developers, suppliers, financ­
ing institutions, population, or governmental organisms. In long operational life cycles,
the performance of the assets extends over significant periods. Lastly, OWFs contain
significant interaction within each system component at various levels, e.g., components
to WTG, WTG to plant, and plant to the grid. Hence, OWE systems are commonly as­
sessed from a systems engineering perspective [65].
4.1.1
Systems Engineering
Systems engineering can be defined as a methodical, multi­disciplinary approach for
the design, realization, technical management, operations, and retirement of a system
[66]. Systems engineering balances organizational, cost, and technological interactions
in complex systems. From this perspective, this project is focused on modeling BOS com­
ponents and their interaction with an OWF economic profile, evaluated as a metric from
an LCOE function. The following sections include the technical characterization of BOS
components of an offshore wind farm.

4.3.1
Substructure (FOU)
4.3.1.1
Technical Characteristics
The elementary purpose of a monopile (MP) is to support the static and dynamic loads
of the WTG. The MP anchors the WTG firmly to the sea bed using the embedded part of

the monopile [22]. Typically, this substructure includes a transition piece (TP). The TP is
a structure between the monopile and the WTG tower. It functions as a support structure
for diverse operations, including boat landing, cables, and personnel access.

4.3.1.2
Installation Fundamentals
MPs with TPS installation is a sequential process executed by the same vessel. MPs are
hammered into the seabed, including drilling activities when required based on seabed
conditions. Afterward, the TPs are lifted and then grouted or bolted into position [9]. The

the U.S. market, as the Jones Act [43] limits the capacity for global WTIVs to be loaded
at U.S. ports. Thus, using U.S. flagged feeder barges to load MPs and TPs in domestic
ports is critical for successfully executing OWE projects.

4.3.2
WTG
4.3.2.1
Technical Characteristics
Even though WTG is not considered a part of the BOS elements, its technical description
is required to understand its interaction with the remaining BOS components.
From an essential perspective, offshore wind turbines are conventionally designed with a
horizontal rotor axis [11]. A typical turbine has three blades connected to a central hub,
forming a rotor that turns a shaft connected to the generator. The generator is located
within the nacelle, which sits at the top of the tower structure and can rotate to face the
oncoming wind direction [59]. The tower is the fastening element to a TP or FOU.
As of today, commercially available technology for offshore WTGs is deployed on rated
capacities between 8MW to 10MW, with rotors up to 170 meters in diameter [5]. Original
equipment manufacturers and developers continue to announce their plans to introduce
operational WTGs from 12 MW up to 15 MW by 2024 [69].
4.3.2.2
Installation Fundamentals
In the first stage, nacelle, hub, and generator assembly are co­located in one site. Due
to the size and weight of components and the final assembled nacelle – too large to be
transportable by road or rail – the assembly plants need to be located at a port facility
equipped with the infrastructure to trans­ship completed components to a marshaling fa­
cility. From this point, the turbine installation process is executed from a jack­up vessel
through lifting and the one­by­one offshore assembly of tower, nacelle, and hub, and each
of the three blades.
Solicitations in the U.S. to start project execution in the short­term with 8­10 MW WTGs,
would not require a WTIV with a capacity above 1,200 tonnes [9]. However, the lifting
cranes for future developments should contemplate a hub height beyond 130 m. As a
part of the limitations originated from the Jones Act [43], the successful deployment of
available WTIVs in the U.S. is correlated to the integration of feeders to the installation
strategy.

ORBIT ­ Installation Module

nical description above, where turbine components are assembled one by one. This
method executes at least five lifts to hub height. These lifts correspond to the tower,
nacelle and hub, and three individual blades [13].

4.3.3
Scour Protection
4.3.3.1
Technical characteristics
As observed in Figure 4.2, the scour protection purpose is to limit the interaction of sea
bed sediments with the MP [22]. This intrusion effect is originated from the shifting flaws
and water speeds around the MP. Crushed rock is the most used scour protection for
bottom­fixed OWE assets. Lastly, scour standard levels depth oscillate between 1 m to 2
m [70].
4.3.3.2
Installation fundamentals
Scour protection is installed by dumping crushed rocks, bags of stones, or the design
selected material around the base of the MP. In the case of crushed rock dumping, the
works are performed with the equipment of fall­pipe vessels, which is a significantly avail­
able type of vessel among the dredging industry [22]. In comparison, bags of stones are
installed through a lowering process executed by positing a standard offshore construction
vessel next to the substructures [5].
4.3.3.3
ORBIT ­ Design Module
This module employs a series of standard design procedures established by DNV GL [13].
ORBIT produces the scour protection parameters based on the MP diameter (Table C.3),
and the user’s selected input for scour depth. ORBIT will calculate the volume of material
required for each position on the OWF. The volume will interact with the input cost of the
material for providing a final result on CAPEX.
4.3.3.4
ORBIT ­ Installation Module
The module provides an approximation of the time and cost associated with the installation
as a function of the amount of material, distance to the port, plant capacity, and vessel
characteristics. This module’s process consist of dumping a layer of crushed rock large
enough not to be removed by hydrodynamic forces [13].

Supply Chain & ROwF1 Selection Criteria

4.3.4
Array System
4.3.4.1
Technical characteristics
The essential purpose of subsea cables is to deliver the power from the WTGs to the
onshore grid [9]. For the case of the array cables, its core function is to connect the OWF
turbines and the offshore substation (OSS).
The predominant technical standard, seen in the first generation of OWFs, has shifted
from the medium voltage, 33 kV rated cables with copper or aluminum cores towards
66kV rated cables [22]. The cables are integrated by five main elements: optical fiber
cable, insulation, conductor, electrical screen, and mechanical and chemical protection.
4.3.4.2
Installation fundamentals
Subsea cable installation can be executed by a single cable installation vessel (CLV)
performing a combined lay and burial task. As observed in Figure C.1, this task consists of
two main scopes. First, cable lay­down is done with the deployment of a plough. Second,
the burial is executed by a ROV, and the utilization of a jetting tool [9].

Conclusions
The target supporting the energy transition in the U.S. stipulates 30 GW of offshore wind
power by 2030. This objective is driven by the commitments of the northeast Atlantic
states, where New Jersey stands out with a goal of 7.5 GW installed capacity in 2030.
However, one of the main challenges for achieving that level of deployment is the de­
velopment of a robust supply chain that can meet the specifications of the offshore wind
industry. This thesis evaluated the impacts of integrating a potential local supply chain
and infrastructure in terms of CAPEX and LCOE for a bottom­fixed offshore wind farm in
Lease Area OCS­A 0498.
The presented analysis translates the regional manufacturing and infrastructure sectors of
New Jersey into a cost model capable of identifying costs variations for BOS components.
The supply chain evaluation compares the trade­off between foreign­sourced and locally
produced components. Further, the infrastructure examination focal point is the availabil­
ity of vessels and the adequacy of ports required to develop large­scale offshore wind
assets. The computation for translating the supply chain localization executed a bottom­
up engineering approach with the support of the Offshore Renewables Balance­of­system
and Installation Tool (ORBIT) and PyWake.
Furthermore, the built model provided insights on the impacts of the offshore wind supply
chain localization in CAPEX and LCOE. This function utilized multiple project configura­
tions that emulate the gradual development of a specialized supply chain. The first project
configuration reached CAPEX of $3,558/kW and LCOE of $109/MWh with the simulation
of the current industry practices for developing an OWF in the U.S. In line with the ex­
pected 2024 COD for the project in Lease Area OCS­A, the obtained LCOE approximates
the global average LCOE of $111/MWh for bottom­fixed offshore wind farms collected by
NREL for the same period [84]. In contrast, the estimated CAPEX falls significantly outside
the projected capacity­weighted average CAPEX of $3,078/kW [84]. However, this differ­
ence is not material as CAPEX ranges between projects due to specific considerations.
The sourced LCOE and CAPEX estimations served their purpose as a rough benchmark
for analyzing the first project configuration.
The first set of extended configurations from the base case delivered meaningful find­
ings on the supply chain localization. First, the instauration of the domestic production
of BOS components is a reliable solution for reducing the high costs of transportation
and mobilization of global vessels. The LCOE declined 7% against the base case, and
CAPEX decreased %3. The added value of this configuration is its capability for balanc­
ing the augmented markup cost for locally produced components. The second extended
configuration characterized the critical role of having access to specialized infrastructure.
The inclusion of the NJWP lowered LCOE by 2% and decreased CAPEX by only 1%. De­
spite not showing immediate cost reductions, the configuration helps the reduction of total
installation times, which could drive costs down in combination with other localization con­
ditions. The deployment of the third configuration included the integration of a Jones Act
compliant installation fleet. The advantages of this configuration translated into enhanced
vessel efficiency, elimination of mobilization rates, and feeder barge independent instal­
lation processes. This configuration captured a 2% LCOE reduction and a 3% CAPEX
decrease. Lastly, the 14% LR in the last project configuration produced the highest LCOE

The configurations captured offshore wind industry trends, design considerations, and
required execution parameters.
The first configuration includes upsizing the selected
WTGs, which is one of the most exploited industry trends. The integration of 15 MW WTG
while maintaining the original plant capacity immediately shifted the LCOE to $78/MWh
equivalent to a %20 reduction. However, CAPEX did not show considerable improve­
ments. The obtained reduction is marginal. This indicates that when keeping the same
plant capacity, the WTG upsizing strategy does not offer a CAPEX reduction even in the
presence of a localized BOS supply chain. This is attributable to the enlargement of the
BOS components ignited by the size of the 15 MW WTGs. The results confirm that this
configuration is somewhat dependent on the capacity of the WTGs for yielding higher
AEP and NCF. From a supply chain perspective, it remains complicated to follow the im­
plications of utilizing larger WTGs currently. Thus, choosing WTGs with a higher rating to
significantly decrease the LCOE should be carefully supported by the analysis of supply
chain and infrastructure constraints associated with its deployment.
The second influential configuration that questions the model configuration is the reallo­
cation of the POI. The simple selection of a POI at a larger distance steered the LCOE to
$103/MWh (+5%) and the CAPEX to $3,532/kW (+6%). This exemplifies that even in the
presence of an entirely localized supply chain, the sudden design update of one of the crit­
ical system components could lead to significant economic impacts. This is important as
such decisions sometimes fall outside the developer’s control. Thus, the thoughtful opti­
mization of all system components should accompany every step during the development
of an OWF.
The last deployed configurations did not significantly differ from the local supply chain
integration. First, the weather configuration only shows a 0.25% LCOE reduction. The
enhanced installation process mostly drove it. Even if the structure utilized the entire
available weather data set, different strategies could provide a better approach to a more
detailed examination of weather profiles. Second, the hypothetical maturation of the ves­
sels rates only lowered LCOE by a 1%, and CAPEX reduction is marginal. These results
indicate that vessel rates variations do not pose a critical problem under a mature market.
However, the maturation rates do not account for potential bottlenecks and scarce spe­
cialized vessels. Lastly, the LR application to the localized supply chain results in a 2%
LCOE reduction and a $3,246 CAPEX (­3%). This result supports the thesis’s approach
to cost reductions through specific supply chain considerations over the deployment of

7.2
Future Work
The results in this research work adequately reflected the deployment of a bottom­up engi­
neering methodology and the utilization of the LCOE as the metric steering its evaluation.
However, as the offshore wind sector continues to evolve, there are three fields where the
findings of this thesis can continue to expand and enhance the included analysis.
The first field corresponds to the incorporation of a broader systems engineering approach
due to the extensive scope of the bottom­up engineering model and the constraints arising
from the commercial sensitivity of the cost data. The further optimization of specific sys­
tems within the design process was disregarded. Therefore, future research work could
include:
• Electrical system optimization. The model showed a significant exposure to the
modification of electrical design parameters. Thus, an in­depth optimization of the
electrical design considerations could provide higher resolution on the LCOE and
CAPEX variations.
• Layout optimization. The approach defining the initial layout utilized a sourced con­
figuration. Afterward, when the layout was updated to fit different WTGs, the pro­
cess did not incorporate a proper optimization method. The inclusion of tools such
as TOPFARM could support an improved method for layout design.
The second field, corresponds to the further extension on additional supply chain matters.
• Interaction with a larger group of stakeholders. One of the main challenges was the
scarcity of cost data for BOS components. Even do the likelihood to obtain precise
figures is low, the engagement of a wider group of industry­related individuals could
enhance the calibration of the cost assumptions.
• WTG CAPEX. The single item with the most significant contribution to the total life
cycle cost of an OWF was only evaluated as a fixed parameter. Thus, a detailed
analysis of the supply chain of this crucial system component could add significant
value to further research.
Lastly, the evaluation of the impacts of the supply chain on a context beyond LCOE could
represent another extension to the wind farm design criteria in this report.
• System Value Evaluation. The utilized LCOE metric applied only to the level of
the individual plant and did not address the value that the generation asset adds
to the electricity system. Thus, a system value metric could enable a more robust
understanding of the active participation of OWFs in electricity markets. Especially
when being deployed in a nascent environment such as the U.S.

A.2.1
WTG Upsizing ­ Layout ROWF2
The three main parameters for allowing wind farm simulations in PyWake are wind re­
source, WTGs, and layout. Thus, to generate a 15 MW WTG layout, the resizing process
is executed as follows.
First, WRA remains consistent with the initial coordinates for ROWF1 and further updated
to a 150 m height. Second, a 15 MW turbine object is created in PyWake with the re­
sources available in IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine [83] and PyWake EllipSys [85].
Third, the simple modification of the layout assumes the elimination of 1 WTG for every
three positions.
The wind farm simulation results for the selected layout of 66 generators with a 15 MW
rating in Lease Area OCS­A 0498 (Lat 39.095937°, Lon 74.1444669°), deliver a total AEP
of 4274 GWh including wake losses of 2.9%. The layout at the top right side of Figure A.1
is set in an Easting, and Northing coordinates configuration. NCF factor is 47.6 %.

Bottom­fixed OWF Technical
Background
C.1
Substructure FOU
C.1.0.1
Technical Characteristics
MP functions INCLUDE wave load balance on the substructure itself and the capability of
allowing cable connections. Currently, MPs stand out as the most utilized substructure for
water depths below 60 m [60]. A monopile is a cylindrical steel tube usually driven into the
sea bed. The embedded section of the MP is of constant diameter to allow entry into the
sea bed. Roughly, the estimated dimensions of a substructure for a 10 MW WTG, would
range on the diameter of 10 m, a 120 m length, and up to 2,000 tonnes [22]. The TP is
a structure between the monopile and the WTG tower. It functions as a support structure
for diverse operations, including boat landing, cables, and personnel access.
C.1.0.2
Installation Fundamentals
The jack­up vessel performing this work is multifunctional, and it is likely to be the same
vessel performing the WTG installation works. This vessel is a Wind Turbine Installation
Vessel (WTIV) [13].
The New Jersey market perspectives point out that OWFs will utilize MPs with a mass
greater than 1,000 tonnes [9]. Thus, WTIVs with crane lifting capacities of 1,500 tonnes
are required to execute this task. Vessel lifting range is dependent on the crane’s power,
the height of the lift, and the horizontal distance from the hook to the base of the crane
[5].
C.1.0.3
ORBIT ­ Design Module
ORBIT incorporates a 10­step module with the focus of providing the financial outcome
of the initial design process of an OWF substructure [6].
C.1.0.4
ORBIT ­ Installation Module
This module collects the MP and TP characteristics, distance to operations port, and
vessel specifications to estimate the total cost and time associated with the installation
process. To achieve this, ORBIT executes a series of subprocesses dynamically. Diagram
process in Figure 4.3, gives a detailed step­by­step description.

C.2.1

C.2.1.1
Technical Characteristics
A WTG encompasses electrical and mechanical components and systems that constitute
the elements mentioned above: turbine nacelle, rotor, and tower. Nacelle components
include: auxiliary systems, bedplate, control system, drivetrain, frame and cover, fasten­
ers and conditioning monitoring systems, power take­off, yaw system, and yaw bearing
[9]. Rotor components include: blades, hub casting, blade bearings, blade pitch system,
spinner (hub cover), auxiliary systems, and fabricated steel components and fasteners
[9]. Lastly, tower components include: steel plate, personnel access and survival equip­
ment, the electric system including switch­gear, and tower internal lighting and fasteners
[9].

C.7.1
Pywake
PyWake is an open source wind farm simulation tool, implemented in Python, with the ca­
pability to calculate wind farm flow fields, power production and annual energy production
(AEP) of wind farms [86]. Further, as observed in the center block in Figure C.3, PyWake

wind farm model is then initialized with two main inputs, site and wind turbines.
Figure C.3: PYWake Process Overview. DTU Wind Energy [86]
PyWake is an open­source wind farm simulation tool, implemented in Python, with the
capability to calculate wind farm flow fields, power production, and annual energy pro­
duction (AEP) of wind farms [86]. Further, as observed in the center block in Figure C.3,
PyWake core is to provide a unified interface to wind farm models of different fidelities [86].
PyWake wind farm model is initialized with two primary inputs, site and wind turbines.
The object site is referenced to a given position, reference wind speed, and wind direction
profiles. Additionally, the object collects the specific site conditions in terms of wind speed,
wind direction, and turbulence intensity, plus the probability of each combination [86].
The wind turbines object is configured for a given turbine type, and effective windspeed
[86]. The input wind turbines object should deliver the power and thrust coefficient, as
well as the specifications of the WTG in terms of hub height and diameter. Lastly, as a
result, Pywake provides a wind farm model simulation. This object contains the calculated
effective wind speed, power production, and elements to obtain an OWF’s AEP and its
respective flow maps [87].
The result of the wind farm object is the combination of one of the integrated engineering
wind farm models and a wake deficit model, superposition model, and optional blockage
and turbulence models. The model utilized for this section is IEA37 Simple Bastankhah
Gaussian [78]. The specifications on the deployment of this model are included in Chap­
ter 5. Hence, the PyWake deployment will adhere to the already stated site conditions in
Lease Area OCS­A 0498 and a plant configuration of 1 GW powered by the DTU 10 MW
WTG.
