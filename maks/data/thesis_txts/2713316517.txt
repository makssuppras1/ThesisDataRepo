Abstract
DTU Wind and Energy Systems
Master of Science in Wind Energy
Automated damage modelling in composite laminates based on
non-destructive test images
by Asier Barbeito Albizu
Wrinkle defects are commonly induced during the thermal-mechanical manufacturing
process
of
laminated
composite
structures
or
components.
The
resulting
fibre
misalignment originating from the ply wrinkles will compromise the structural integrity
of a composite structure.
This damage criticality can be assessed numerically but the
geometrical modelling process is typically very time-consuming if complex wrinkle defects
have to be incorporated into the finite element model. This study presents an automated
non-destructive test image-based geometric reconstruction method for thick laminates
with complex ply wrinkles. The geometry of a thick laminate is reconstructed based on
the limited inspected data from the laminate side images, where some distinguished
composite plies can be readily detected. The two-dimensional geometrical reconstruction
of the detected wrinkled plies from each image is performed using a multiple Gaussian
functions model or linear interpolation, while the undetected intermediate plies are
defined by distance-based interpolation.
Based on the obtained two-dimensional
geometries, the three-dimensional reconstruction is performed via an inverse distance
weighting interpolation method.
A thick glass fibre laminate with artificially induced
wrinkles is manufactured for model verification.
The geometries of cross-sections from
the same locations of the reconstructed model and the realistic laminate are compared.
The reconstructed model shows a good agreement with the realistic laminate. Progressive
failure analysis of a two-dimensional and a three-dimensional reconstructed model are
compared in terms of damage progression and load versus nominal strain response to
highlight the relevance of modelling three-dimensional wrinkles to obtain more accurate
damage criticality assessments.
Keywords: Composite, wrinkle defect, geometry reconstruction, finite element modelling,
Non-destructive test

1 Introduction
Composite materials are becoming more popular in many sectors due to their high
strength-to-weight ratio. In the wind energy sector, components like blades and nacelles are
typically composite based. A wind turbine blade consists of an aerodynamic shell stiffened
by several integral shear webs and load-carrying laminates.
Each of these components
comprising the blade has a different load carrying purpose. The aerodynamic shells, which
are made of sandwich structures, are mainly designed to withstand elastic buckling. The
flapwise bending from wind pressure loads is resisted by the shear webs and the flapwise
load-carrying laminates (spar caps), while the edgewise bending from gravitational loads
is withstood by the edgewise load-carrying laminates [2].
Wind turbine manufacturers are nowadays aiming for higher power generation per
turbine by lengthening the blade dimensions. The longer the wind turbine blades are, the
higher the overall shear webs’ and spar caps’ thickness needs to be to stand higher loading
conditions. For instance, the DTU 10 MW Reference wind turbine’s spar caps reach a
laminate thickness of 83 mm in the mid-span [3]. These are considered thick laminates,
and considering the increasing tendency of these components’ thicknesses, this study will
focus on thick composite laminates.
Multiple
defects
can
be
induced
to
the
laminated
components
during
their
manufacturing process, which will threaten the load-bearing capacity of the structure as
these defects tend to propagate as the laminate is loaded.
Some examples of those
manufacturing-induced
defects
are
misalignment
of
fibres
(wrinkles),
non-uniform
distribution of the fibres in the matrix, voids in fibres and matrix, delaminated regions,
and initial stress in the lamina as a result of its manufacture and further treatment [4].
Among those defects ply wrinkles are considered one of the most significant effects that
occur in composite materials [5]. Figure 1.1 shows an example of a failed blade during
operation, where the component’s structural integrity was affected by wrinkles in the
blade’s spar cap.
(a) Wind turbine generator with the failed
blade.
(b) Wrinkle in the blade spar cap.
Figure 1.1: Blade failure during wind turbine operation due to a wrinkle in the
spar cap [1].

These structural failures can be prevented if accurate and realistic damage criticality
assessments of the defective components are done. For that finite element (FE) analysis can
be applied, where the component is modelled under multiple loading scenarios. However,
one of the main challenges for the damage modelling of wrinkled composite laminates is
the time-consuming process for the FE model generation. To overcome this challenge, this
study will perform the geometrical reconstruction of the laminates based on non-destructive
test (NDT) images.
1.1
Theoretical Background
During the thermal-mechanical manufacturing process of composite laminates,
wrinkle defects are commonly induced to the laminated composite structures or
components. Severe wrinkles will compromise the structural integrity of the composite
components due to the fibre misalignment [1, 6].
Fibre misalignment, i.e.
fibres with
orientation angles larger than zero relative to the loading direction,
will reduce
mechanical properties such as stiffness, strength and fatigue [7].
This mechanical
properties decrement has been the general outcome of multiple experimental studies
where wrinkled composite laminates have been tested under tensile, compressive or cyclic
loads [8–11].
The identification and modelling of these damages in large composite
structures such as wind turbine blades, is time-consuming, where the operators must
identify and locate the defects in the component and assess their criticality.
Multiple
studies have been conducted to investigate how to accurately detect these defects’
characteristics
by
non-destructive
testing
[12–15].
On
the
other
hand,
many
investigations on how to perform a reliable damage assessment have been carried out
based on either two- or three-dimensional models [6, 9, 16–22]. Previous studies suggest
that the damage criticality can be assessed numerically allowing to incorporate complex
damages into the finite element model [23–26].
Nevertheless, the modelling of such
complex wrinkle geometries is not that commonly used as it implies large modelling
efforts [23].
The actual wrinkle geometry is commonly simplified to a half-sine or cosine wave
shape.
The
criticality
is
assessed
through
either
empirical
equations
or
FE
simulations [27].
The simplified wrinkle geometries are usually characterised by three
parameters:
wavelength (λ), amplitude (δ) and a maximum angle of the wrinkle
steepness (θ) [8, 23].
This simplification will result in lower modelling efforts at the
expense of poorer quality wrinkle representation, which will lead to less accurate damage
criticality assessments. Despite its inability to characterise complex wrinkle geometries,
this method is still widely used in industry and research due to its simplicity.
The ongoing investigation and development of NDT techniques is improving their
accuracy in wrinkle geometry characterisation. However, detailed inspection data cannot
be utilised if the simplified sinusoidal wave method is applied when modelling the wrinkle
defects. Xie N. et al. [6] proposed a method to generate finite element models based on
NDT information, by modelling symmetrical and single wrinkle defects with a single
Gaussian function model.
Chen X. et al. [23] automated the FE model generation of
asymmetric and multiple wave ply wrinkles based on a multiple Gaussian functions
model. Nikishkov G. et al. [28] also combined finite element (FE) modelling with NDT
data and developed a novel algorithm for finite element mesh generation from X-ray CT
images for unidirectional composites with fibre waviness. However, the X-ray processing
time for the NDT data gathering can lead up to a couple of hours [29]. Xie et al. [26]
developed
an
algorithm
to
bridge
the
NDT
data
with
mechanical
properties
characterisation of wrinkled laminates.
One of the proposed methods generated a

These wrinkles are commonly
induced in wind turbine spar-caps, shear webs or/and aerodynamic shells, which make
the blade prone to an early failure on its life cycle [1].
Herein lies the relevance of
modelling realistic 3D wrinkle defects for a more accurate damage assessment, 3D
measurement or reconstruction methods are required to convert the wrinkle geometry
data into finite element models, which will also provide higher reliability damage
assessments. Zhou E. et al. [24] worked on a 3D reconstruction model of composite plies
based on 2D images. The main objectives of this study were to visualise, manipulate, and
reconstruct fibre/resin internal arrangements to perform macro-mechanical property
analysis from the generated model.
Smith R. A. et al. [25] successfully developed a
method that utilises ultrasonic (UT) data to obtain 3D fibre orientation maps, which
were afterwards used to build the FE models of the laminate.
However, UT scanning
techniques have some disadvantages concerning inspection speed (point scan) and the
requirement of a coupling liquid [30]. Therefore, the presented alternative method for the
3D wrinkle reconstruction in this work will be based on NDT specimen images.
In
contrast to X-ray or UT scans, the processing time to obtain laminate images is minimal
which will speed up the geometrical reconstruction of the laminate.

1.2
Contribution of the study
An automated image-based geometric modelling method for thick laminates with 3D
ply wrinkles is proposed. The wrinkle images will be processed and their geometry will
be captured automatically, avoiding any time-consuming manual data gathering and
observational biases [31], and facilitating an automated geometric reconstruction of
complex wrinkles.
The following work presents an extension of the 2D geometric
modelling of thick laminates proposed by Chen X. et al. [23] with the following new
contributions:
1. Automated image processing for characterisation of wrinkle geometries from NDT
images to facilitate realistic geometric reconstruction of complex wrinkles in thick
==PAGE:18==
Chapter 1. Introduction
4
laminates.
2. Automated geometric reconstruction and FE meshing of 3D ply wrinkles for efficient
damage criticality assessment.
This work aims to automatise the full data processing from images to accurate
damage criticality assessments of complex 3D ply wrinkles in thick laminates. This way,
the previously mentioned downsides of modelling complex defects i.e.
large modelling
efforts and manual data gathering from thick laminate images will be avoided.
Firstly, the wrinkled specimen images will be processed, and the geometries of some
distinguished composite plies will be captured by an image processing tool.
The
non-detected plies are later included using distance-based interpolation method.
Two
methods are provided to characterise the wrinkle geometries from the input images:
interpolation and multiple Gaussian curve fitting. The 3D geometric reconstruction will
be based on the 2D wrinkle images from all four specimen sides, where the data points in
the 3D space will be obtained via inverse distance weighting interpolation. Once all the
nodes for the FE model are defined, input files containing mesh, material properties,
loading and boundary conditions, will be generated automatically for FE modelling. In
this work, the input files for the commercial software Abaqus [32] are provided as an
example.
1.3
Overview of the thesis
The study initially presents the followed methodology to bridge the input NDT
specimen images with the final FE model and its damage assessment in Section 2. Here,
the proposed image processing tool that automatically characterises the geometry of
some distinguished plies from a 2D wrinkle image is presented.
Subsequently, the
geometric reconstruction method based upon the obtained 2D wrinkle geometries is
explained.
Next, the followed process to manufacture a thick wrinkled glass fibre
laminate for the model verification is presented in Section 3. Section 4 defines the FE
model set-up.
Thereupon, the results for the 3D geometrical reconstruction model
verification are gathered, and discussed, in Section 5.1. The relevance of modelling 3D
wrinkles over 2D ones is also discussed in Section 5.2.
Finally, main conclusions and
limitations of the proposed methods and possible future work are stated in Section 6.
This thesis work led to the development of a manuscript, see Appendix D.

2 Methodology
A method combining an image processing tool that detects wrinkle geometry from a
specimen image, and a geometric reconstruction is described in this section. The geometry
of some distinguished composite plies is captured from image processing. Two methods are
provided to characterise the wrinkle geometry from the input images: interpolation and
curve fitting. The undetected plies are included by applying a distance-based interpolation.
An inverse distance weighting interpolation based on the four-face image wrinkle geometries
is performed to obtain the 3D geometric reconstruction. Figure 2.1 presents the flow chart
of the method, which is further discussed in Sections 2.1 and 2.2.

obtains the geometrical data from NDT images based on their colour contrast is
proposed. This tool allows to capture more realistic wrinkle shapes and dimensions, as
well as avoid time-consuming manual geometrical data gathering. The flow chart of the
image processing from the specimen image to the scaled wrinkle geometry can be seen in

images and their captured wrinkle geometries can be found in Appendix A. The following
method to automatically model asymmetric with multiple wave ply wrinkles has been
implemented in the commercial software package MATLAB [33]. The pseudocode of the
algorithm is presented in Appendix C, Algorithm 2.

the black pixels represent mainly the wrinkled plies while some represent noise pixels.

2.2
Geometric reconstruction
The main idea of the geometric reconstruction is to obtain a 3D model of a wrinkled
thick laminate based on multiple specimen side face NDT images as shown in Figure 2.10.
Here, four images respectively representing each side of the specimen have been used to
generate the 3D model.
Figure 2.10: Exemplary 3D reconstruction based on four wrinkle images. Input
images obtained from Hallander P. et al. [11]
Prior to performing the 3D reconstruction, the 2D reconstruction of each inputted
image is done. For that, the developed method by Chen X. et al. [23] is followed with
some slight modifications. The image processing tool provides coordinate data points for
each wrinkle’s geometry. However, in order to ensure that the wrinkle orientation in the
3D space is properly captured, the 2D wrinkle height data points and their peaks and
valleys will be considered for the mesh discretisation of the X and Z axes. Chen X. et
al. [23] proposed a multiple Gaussian functions model to obtain the wrinkle heights at the
discretised coordinates due to its robustness and a lower amount of available coordinate
data points.
Nonetheless, the developed model will also include a linear interpolation
method for this purpose considering the high number of data points per wrinkle obtained
from the image processing tool.
The geometric reconstruction is divided into two sections, the 2D reconstruction of
the detected geometry from the NDT images, Section 2.2.1, and the 3D reconstruction
based on the reconstructed laminate side face wrinkles, Section 2.2.2. As aforementioned,
the coordinate data of some distinguished composite plies are obtained by processing the
wrinkle images based on their colour contrast. In order to determine the geometry of the
remaining undetected plies, the following assumptions have been made [23]:
(a) The thickness of the composite plies is assumed to be constant. The interface layers’
thickness varies representing resin-rich or heavily-packed layer regions.
(b) The bottom composite layer is assumed to be completely flat which is considered
valid as it is the layer placed against the mould during the manufacturing process.
(c) The composite layers and the interface are perfectly bonded which will not always
be true but an acceptable assumption for the purpose of this study.

6 Concluding remarks
An automated image-based geometric modelling method is proposed to reconstruct
thick laminates with three-dimensional ply wrinkles.
An image processing tool and a
geometric reconstruction method are combined. The image processing tool detects some
distinguished geometric details of the laminates. The detected coordinate data points from
the NDT images can be processed by either interpolation methods or multiple Gaussian
functions fitting.
The two-dimensional geometric reconstruction of the image plane is
first conducted by interpolating the undetected layers based on the captured layers. The
geometry of the entire laminate is then reconstructed based on these two-dimensional
reconstruction planes via an inverse distance weighting interpolation. Finally, finite element
meshes are generated for finite element modelling.
Experimental verification of the model has been conducted.
The geometries of the
cross-sections in several positions of the laminate from geometric reconstruction show a
good agreement with the actual geometries of these cross-sections from the laminate slices.
The deviations in the reconstructed geometry are given due to the high dependency of the
reconstruction method on the image processing tool. Any deviation in the 2D reconstructed
face images induces deviations in the geometry of the laminate.
An advantage of the
proposed 3D reconstruction method is the possibility of adjusting the model to accept
more input geometric data. The inverse distance weighting interpolation method allows
the three-dimensional reconstruction of the undefined points while preserving the locations
of the inspected data points i.e. processed wrinkle NDT images or even manually measured
point coordinates.
The finite element modelling of progressive failure of two laminates with different
wrinkles has been compared. Here, the damage evolution for the laminate with the 3D
wrinkle showed a lower degradation in global stiffness. This highlights the relevance of
modelling realistic wrinkle geometries, over commonly modelled defects with simplified
geometries, to obtain more accurate damage criticality assessments.
The damage
evolution across the laminate resulted in being highly dependent on the wrinkle geometry.
Overall, the presented method facilitates the automation of the full process from
geometry characterisation based on wrinkle NDT images to finite element meshing,
allowing more accurate and efficient damage criticality assessment of thick laminates with
complex three-dimensional wrinkles. This work has covered the scope of the project, yet
future work concerning the developed method could include:
(i) Improvement of the image processing tool to avoid deviations in the 3D reconstructed
geometry induced by the captured wrinkle geometry from the NDT images. The
proposed tool is highly dependent on the input image’s resolution and colour contrast.
(ii) Local mesh refinement for the resin-rich regions should also be included aiming for
a more accurate progressive failure analysis of the cohesive elements. The proposed
model can only refine the mesh for each material layer, but not user-defined localised
areas.

(iii) Incorporate the possibility of modelling severe wrinkles in the form of fabric folding
with reverse curvatures.
(iv) Investigate the model’s capability to reconstruct laminates with curved wrinkle
orientations. The geometric reconstruction verification in this study only focused
on a straight wrinkle orientation specimen.
(v) Extension to modelling of sandwich structures.
The proposed model focuses on
composite laminates, however, wrinkle defects (face wrinkling) are also common in
sandwich structures like wind turbine aerodynamic shells.
(vi) Coupling the wrinkled laminate models with spar cap, shear web or aerodynamic
shell models, to obtain a full-component damage criticality assessment.

Abstract
Ply wrinkles are commonly induced during thermal-mechanical manufacturing process of laminated
composite structures or components.
Fibre misalignment due to ply wrinkles compromises the
structural integrity of a composite structure. The damage criticality can be assessed numerically
based on non-destructive testing (NDT) results. Geometrical modelling is usually required and it is
time-consuming if complex wrinkles have to be incorporated into the finite element model. This
study presents an automated image-based three-dimensional geometric reconstruction method for
thick laminates with complex ply wrinkles. The geometry of a thick laminate is reconstructed based
on the limited inspection images from the sides of laminates. The two-dimensional geometrical
reconstruction of the detected wrinkled plies is done using a multiple Gaussian functions model or
linear interpolation, while the intermediate plies are defined by distance-based interpolation. Based
on the obtained two-dimensional geometries, the three-dimensional reconstruction is done via an
inverse distance weighting interpolation method. A thick glass fibre laminate with artificially induced
wrinkles is manufactured for model verification. The geometries of cross-sections from the same
locations of the reconstructed model and the realistic laminate are compared. The reconstructed model
shows a good agreement with the realistic laminate. Progressive failure analysis of a two-dimensional
and a three-dimensional reconstructed model are compared in terms of damage progression and load
versus nominal strain response to highlight the relevance of modelling three-dimensional wrinkles to
obtain more accurate and efficient damage criticality assessments.
1. Introduction
Laminated composite materials are widely used in wind
energy, aerospace, sports equipment, and many other
sectors.
During their thermal-mechanical manufacturing
process, wrinkle defects are commonly induced to the
laminated composite structures or components.
Severe
wrinkles will compromise the structural integrity of the
composite components due to the fibre misalignment [1, 2].
Fibre misalignment, i.e.
fibres with orientation angles
larger than zero relative to the loading direction, will
reduce mechanical properties such as stiffness, strength and
fatigue [3]. This mechanical properties decrement has been
the general outcome of multiple experimental studies
where wrinkled composite laminates have been tested
under tensile, compressive or cyclic loads [4–7].
The
identification and modelling of these damages in large
composite structures such as wind turbine blades, is
time-consuming, where the operators must identify and
locate the defects at the component and assess their
criticality.
Multiple studies have been conducted to
investigate
how
to
accurately
detect
these
defects’
characteristics by non-destructive testing (NDT) [8–11].
On the other hand, many investigations on how to perform
a reliable damage assessment have been carried out based
on either two- or three-dimensional models [1, 5, 12–18].
Previous studies suggest that the damage criticality can be
assessed numerically allowing to incorporate complex
damages into the finite element (FE) model [19–22].

Nevertheless, the modelling of such complex wrinkle

The actual wrinkle geometry is commonly simplified to

The simplified wrinkle geometries are
usually characterised by three parameters: wavelength (𝜆),
amplitude (𝛿) and a maximum angle of the wrinkle
steepness (𝜃) [4, 19].
This simplification will result in
lower modelling efforts at the expense of poorer quality
wrinkle representation, which will lead to less accurate
damage criticality assessments.
Despite its inability to
characterise complex wrinkle geometries, this method is
still widely used in industry and research due to its
simplicity.
The ongoing investigation and development of NDT
techniques is improving their accuracy in wrinkle geometry
characterisation. However, detailed inspection data cannot
be utilised if the simplified sinusoidal wave method is
applied when modelling the wrinkle defects. Xie N. et al.
[1] proposed a method to generate finite element models
based on NDT information, by modelling symmetrical and
single wrinkle defects with a single Gaussian function

Chen X. et al.
[19] automated the FE model

multiple Gaussian functions model.
Nikishkov G. et al.

The presented

three-dimensional wrinkles.
Experimental verification is
conducted. The geometries of the cross sections in several
positions of the laminate from geometric reconstruction
show a good agreement with the actual geometries of these
cross sections from the laminate slices.
It should be noted that the accuracy of the geometric
reconstruction is highly dependent on the captured wrinkle
geometries from the images. Therefore, future work should
consider the improvement of the image processing tool.
Furthermore, local mesh refinement for resin-rich region
should also be included in the presented geometric
modelling framework. Moreover, the presented method has
limitations to model severe wrinkles in the form of fabric
folding with reverse curvatures. Further investigations on
its capability of reconstructing curved wrinkle orientations
should be conducted.
Nevertheless,
the geometric
modelling method presented in this work can facilitate
efficient
geometric
modelling
of
complex
three
dimensional ply wrinkles in thick laminates for more
accurate and efficient damage criticality assessments.
