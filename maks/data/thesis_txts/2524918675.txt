Abstract
Industrial bio-processes are mostly run under aerobic conditions. Often, they have a high
productivity, but are limited by the oxygen transfer rate, due to its low solubility in water.
Oxygen makes aerobic processes more energetic, when used as electron acceptor, resulting in
more biomass and a higher output of heat. The presence of oxygen fully oxidise the electron
donor into CO2, generating the extra energy, but reduces the amount of carbon used for
product formation.
Although anaerobic bio-processes are slower, they have the potential for higher yields with
less unwanted side product formation. The production costs and energy usage are lower, as
requirements for heat removal, aeration and mixing are greatly reduced. They are further
easier to design, due to product formation being coupled to growth, resulting in a linear
relation for qp(µ).
The biggest obstacle when designing anaerobic bio-processes is the regeneration of NAD+.
If this is not considered, side products will be formed to restore the redox balance of the cell
(mixed acid fermentation), decreasing the potential of the overall process. When designing
the bio-process it is important to regenerate the redox balance via product formation.
In this project, a straightforward method to derive black box stoichiometry is described. One
via thermodynamic generalisation and another by balancing ATP, resulting in a grey box
model. The metabolism obtained from grey box has a high potential as it uses information
regarding ATP production/consumption to estimate the stoichiometry. This requires a more
detailed overview of the process as a whole allowing for rational decisions and metabolic
targets for an improved process to be considered before an experiment is even carried out.
Heijnen J. J et all. demonstrated that many products can be theoretically produced under
anaerobic conditions, assessing the feasibility of the theoretical substrate to product forma-
tion [6]. In this study, we’ll go into depth with the suggested methods making four rational
designs of anaerobic cell factories based on thermodynamic calculations and the Herbert
Pirt relation with a focus on utilizing glycerol as a substrate.

Background
The article, ”Thermodynamics-based design of microbial cell factories for anaerobic product for-
mation” by Heijnen et al. (2015) gave the inspiration for the project. They state the possibility
to predict the feasibility of anaerobic processes by utilising simple thermodynamic generalisa-
tions, see Figure 2.
Under anaerobic conditions, microorganisms acquire their energy from the conversion of sub-
strate to product. The article presents a general approach to check the feasibility of an anaerobic
bio-process under the assumption that all electrons from the substrate is found in the product
[6], providing the energy for growth, thereby coupling growth and product formation. [6]
The best known anaerobic bio-process is the production of ethanol by Saccharomyces cerevisiae.
See Figure 1.

1.1
Motivation
For the last century, petroleum has been the go-to raw-material for most chemical products.
The last decade has experienced an increased interest in the transition from a petroleum- to a
bio-based economy. The US department of energy (DOE) published a list of chemicals relevant
for the transitions to a bio-based economy [3, 6, 24].
The coupling of a low value substrate to a high value product can be challenging [3]. It is
often hard to compete with the production price of existing bulk chemicals and products with
commercial value are already being produced chemically. Another problem is it it can be hard
to ﬁnd proper conversion methods, making it hard to enter the market, while it is challenging
to predict the next potential target. The methods provided can easen this. [3]
Bio-based production can provide new and better methods of manufacturing [32]. They have
a better conversion of carbon and the ability to utilise renewable feedstocks. Another aspect
is use of microbes with the potential to use non-conventional feed-stocks, some which could be
considered as industrial waste products.

Noorman et al. (2019) goes through the steps, which should be taken for a viable bio-based
economy. The three main concerns to be considered when designing future bio-processes are.
• Reduction of water usage in the bio-process as a whole.
• Zero oxygen consumption. Anaerobic process for highest substrate to product conversion.
• Zero carbon ”waste”: Minimize production of CO2 and unwanted ”bi-products” utilising
the elements which could have been used for product formation.
Here, the focus will be on anaerobic bio-processes, as opposite to most processes being aerobic
nowadays. The addition of oxygen makes sense, as oxygen utilisation is the most eﬃcient way of
generating energy currently known. Most of the time, the extra energy is not necessary, leading
to more biomass and thereby is less carbon used for product formation. An exception is when
biomass is the product, here aerobic is the best option. [3]
Beneﬁts of anaerobic bio-process: Higher substrate to product carbon conversion (higher
yield), less heat generated, bigger bioreactors, lower mechanic energy usage, oxygen transfer is
no longer a problem, which also reduce heterogeneity. [6, 13]

conversion of these resources and waste streams into value added products, such as
food, feed, bio-based products and bioenergy”

Thermodynamic analysis of product reaction
Here, a short review of the article ”Thermodynamics-based design of microbial cell factories for
anaerobic product formation” by Heijnen et al. (2015) is presented.
All non-spontaneous reactions inside the cell either consume or produce Gibbs free energy. The
energy arise from degrading a substrate (e.g. oxidation of sugars) and transfer of electrons from
an electron donor to an electron acceptor. [6]
The Gibbs free energy released should be enough to be converted into useful energy and run the
three main reactions of the cell: catabolism (energy producing), anabolism (energy consuming)
and maintenance of the cell. [6]

A generalized way to predict black box stoichiometry
Chemotrophic microorganisms are energetically driven by the transfer of electrons from an
electron donor to an electron acceptor (redox reactions) [18]. More than a thousand of reactions
is happening inside the cell [8], which can be divided into a network of energy producing
(catabolic) and energy consuming (anabolic) reactions. The sum of all reactions is deﬁned as the
metabolism [8, 18], and a schematic overview of the electron transfer inside the cell is shown in
ﬁgure 3. Here, the substrate has a higher degree of reduction than biomass (γCs/Ns > γBiomass),
resulting in the release of electron from biomass formation, making the substrate e-acceptor in
the anabolic half reaction. The substrate is being reduced when donating electrons, oxidising
the electron acceptor. If electrons are required for biomass formation (γCs/Ns < γBiomass) an
electron acceptor is needed in the anabolic reaction [18] (See Appendix C.1).
Electrons are acquired from the substrate (electron donor) and used for maintenance, biomass-,
and product formation in the industrial setting.
Figure 3: Flow of electrons in the metabolism. An electron is transferred from a reduced donor (being
oxidised) to an oxidised electron acceptor (being reduced) [18]. If substrate is more reduced than biomass,
electrons will be released upon biomass formation. Picture from Van Loosdrecht et al. (2010) [18]
These reactions give a simpliﬁed approach to a black box stoichometric model, which can be
further expanded to create an simple kinetic model [6, 8, 18] (beyond the current scope of
the project - See appendix A.2). A notable simpliﬁcation is the formation of biomass. Here,
many individual building blocks need to be synthesized for diﬀerent polymerization reactions,
for the cell to grow. By reducing the overall balance to utilisation of diﬀerent elements, the
reaction network can be simpliﬁed to a single growth equation summarizing the total amount
of elements used.
The black box model lump these reaction together into the metabolism,
summarizing inputs and outputs of the cell as a single stoichometric equation.
In the following chapters, two ways to predict black box stoichiometry are presented. First
via thermodynamic generalisations. The only criteria here is information regarding the redox
couple to derive the metabolism from the catabolic and anabolic reactions. The second method
utilise information regarding the biochemical pathway and an ATP steady state assumption.
Both methods allow for easy calculation of the stoichiometry if the yield of biomass on the given
substrate (Yx/s) is known [18]. If YS/P is not known, the stoichometry can still be estimated.

biological process, knowing only the electron-donor (D) and -acceptor (A) redox couple of the

Deriving the individual half reaction is essential for both methods. The catabolic reaction is
the energy generating and most important process for the model, and will be covered ﬁrst.

5.2.2
Available energy
The Gibbs free energy generated should be coupled to biochemically available energy in the
form of ATP production. This can either be via proton motive force (PMF) or substrate level
phosphorylation (SLP). The thresholds for industrial growing organisms are 27 kJ/molP for
organisms utilising proton motive force as the main energy source, while organism utilising
substrate-level phosphorylation requires 84 kJ/molP. The most common way of producing en-
ergy for fermenting microbes, is via SLP. It is important to balance ATP as insuﬃcient amounts
might result in a non working process. A common mistake is forgetting to take transport over
the membrane into account. [6]
Heijnen et al.

yields more Gibbs free energy than the suggested limitation results in a high chance of a viable
bio-process. [6]

Thermodynamic prediction of the Herbert Pirt substrate
distribution
Process design requires information regarding consumption (C-, N-source) and production
(product, CO2) of elements during the metabolism [13]. This information is directly coupled
to the speciﬁc uptake rates (q-rates) describing cell activity via the transfer of compounds over
the cell membrane [13].
A black box model deﬁnes the relationship between the stoichometry of the metabolism and the
q-rates resulting in the process reaction (Section 7). It lumps all inputs and outputs in relation
to the metabolism coupled by intra-cellular steady state properties. [13, 15]
Assuming pseudy steady state inside the cell, elemental balance follows from the speciﬁc q-rates
as consumed substrates should be equivalent to produced products. [13, 15]
6.1
Herbert pirt substrate distribution under single substrate limita-
tion
Parallel to the black box stoichometric model, substrate uptake is used in three independent
reactions: growth, product formation and maintenance. Each described by their biomass speciﬁc
uptake rates, describing the transport of compounds (S, P, CO2, NH+
4 ) over the cell membrane
(moli/h/CmolX). This describes the behaviour of the microbe, for instance is qh correlated with
amount of titrant to be added and qs is substrate demand. The q-rates are determined by the
microorganism, environmental parameters (pressure, temperature and pH in the bioreactor)
and changes in extracellular concentrations. Speciﬁc uptake rates are normally deduced via
experiment, but estimation allow assessment of the process beforehand. [13, 14]
Herbert-Pirts realised substrate uptake can be reduced to growth, product formation and main-
tenance, which lead to Herbert Pirt substrate distribution under single nutrient limitation
(Equation 38). [13, 14, 15]
qs = as · µ + bs · qp + ms
(38)
Where a is mol substrate used per CmolX produced, b is mol substrate used per mol product
produced and ms is mol substrate used per hour to maintain 1 CmolX in the bioreactor [15]. In
the case of a catabolic product formation coupled to growth, the coeﬃcient b is neglected as no
extra carbon is energy are needed for product formation [15]. This is the case with anaerobic
bio-processes as product formation is a result of the substrate being catabolised and thereby
used to drive growth and maintenance [13].
qs = as · µ + ms
(39)
The HP substrate distribution is only valid under single substrate limitation. This is to ensure
constant q-rates if the limiting substrate is kept constant.
If the concentration of a given
compound is high enough it will be in excess and a change would not inﬂuence the q-rate as
the transport system is fully loaded (see Figure 4). [15]

By keeping one substrate limited (cs) and the rest in excess, the substrate becomes the limiting
factor, and a change in cs (or µ) will alter the behaviour of the system as a whole. Assuming
pseudy steady state across the cell membrane the rest of the q-rates will follow when a change
in the limiting substrate is made. If more compounds were limiting, the degree of freedom of
the system would increase, as more factors would have a impact on the system.

L-valine is a essential branched chained amino acid (BCAA), used as a supplement for the food,
feedstock and cosmetic industries [16, 25]. It is an essential amino acid adding long lasting
industrial value to it [16].
8.1
Organism
Corynebacterium glutamicum is widely used for production of amino-acids [9, 16], due to its
high availability of L-glutamate, making it an ideal candidate for the production of L-valine.
Other notable candidates include: Brevibacterium, Escherichia and Serratia, as they have all

C. glutamicum was considered strict aerobic, until Yukawa et al. (2007) demonstrated that it

Now, C. glutamicum is known to produce lactic acid
from glucose under oxygen deprivation conditions to re-oxidise NAD+ and produce acetate to
generate ATP [12]. Optimal conditions for C. glutamicum is pH 7 and 30 degree Celsius, which
are the conditions chosen for the thermodynamic calculations.

8.3.1
Initial considerations
When combining the two reactions (Figure 6 and 7) it is noted that it is not in redox balance.
NAD+ is not re-oxidised and the demand for NADPH is higher than expected. One NADPH
is consumed in the conversion of (S)-2-acetolactate to (2R)-2,3-dihydroxy-3-metylbutanoate
(catalysed by AHAIR) and one is used in the restoration of the nitrogen donor L-glutamate
from 2-oxoglutarate (Table 7). Two NADPH is needed per L-valine, but only one is produced
natively in the degrading pathway, resulting in 0.5 L-valine per glycerol.
Resulting in the
constraint that the catabolic pathway should run at least twice to drive product formation
(hence λCat ≥2).
Balancing the redox potential is the main problem when designing an
anaerobic process, and should be the ﬁrst problem addressed when creating a viable process.
NADPH is produced in the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), entered from glucose-6-phosphate
(G6P). When using non glucose substrate G6P has to be reached via glyconegenesis, which can
be energetically expensive for the cell. It is desirable to use a NADPH independent gene if
possible or alter the enzyme to utilise NADH instead - restoring the NAD+ balance of the
cell via product formation. This problem has been addressed by previous studies, suggesting
changing the genes, engineering the enzymes or up-regulate of the PP pathway [26, 40]. Anaer-
obic growth will further limit the ﬂow toward the TCA-cycle and thereby the availability of
2-oxyglytarate, limiting the availability of the N-donor L-glutamate. When C. glutamicum is
grown anaerobic with glucose as sole substrate, NAD+ is regenerated by producing lactate
from pyruvate [12, 40]. Under oxygen deprivation a mixed acid fermentation producing lactate,
acetate and succinate was achieved [20]. Instead of NAD+ being restored via ”bi-product”
formation it is desirable to have NAD+ regenerated in the product pathway. If NAD+ could
be re-oxidied in the product pathway, the yield would increased as less carbon is funneled into
the production of organic acids.

Itaconic acid
Itaconic acid is within the top 12 value adding chemical for a bio-sustainable future [24]. It is
used as a building block for polymers and has use as a bioactive compound [17, 24, 31]. Karaﬀa
and Kubicek (2019) consider itaconic acids role as a selective advance (acidify the environment)
and as a survival mechanism for Aspergillus terreus by inhibiting the enzyme isocitrate lyase in
some bacteria and other pathogens [17].
Itaconic acid is currently produced via chemical synthesis and aerobic bio-processes utilising
Aspergillus terreus [17, 24, 31]. The US department of energy explored the technical barriers in
regard to a microbial process and what should be established before the bio-process is econom-
ically viable. They suggest the production of co-acids should be reduced and the overall yield
and productivity should be increased [24]. This should be the focus for a new bio-process.
9.1
Biosynthesis of itaconic acid
Itaconic acid is produced similarly to citric acid in Aspergilus niger. The diﬀerence between
citric acid production and itoconic acid is the three genes: cadA, mfsA and mtta. The gene
cadA encodes lysine decarboxylase, converting citric acid into itaconic acid, while mfsA and
mtta encode a mitochondrial and membrane transport protein respectively. [17, 31]
Pyruvate is decarboxylized to acetyl-CoA. Co-enzyme A then delivers the acetyl-group to ox-
aloacetate from the Krebs cycle.
The two carbon acetyl and four carbon oxaloacetate are

Organism
Aspergillus terreus is already used industrially, but it has been postulated, that utilising the high

One of the biggest obstacles is, that fungi do not grow well in absence of oxygen and Aspergillus,
speciﬁcally, is obligate aerobic. Another big barrier is the low activity of TCA cycle during
anaerobic conditions as it is only utilised to produce metabolites essential for growth.
It would not make sense to use A. niger or terrues for an anaerobic itaconic acid production
platform. Diﬀerent yeasts and bacterial strains have been suggested for itaconinc acid produc-
tion, but none have shown the same results as A. niger under aerobic conditions [17].
Bacillus licheniformis is used to produce industrial enzymes for detergents and would be the
best candidate for an anaerobic bio-process to my knowledge [29]. B. licheniformis can grow
anaerobically [29] and has previously been used to produce citric acid [29, 33]. The biggest
downside being itaconic acids hypothesised anti-microbial eﬀect, resulting in zero growth of B.
licheniformis.
The chance of having an working anaerobic process (lack of anaerobic production strain for this
compound) at the given time is very low. It will still be assumed, that A. niger is used to
design a black box anaerobic process with glycerol as substrate. One main problem with citric
acid production is the high water usage. Utilising glycerol as carbon and energy source instead
of glucose, sorbitol or molasses [17, 29, 31] could decrease the water usage [6].

Karaﬀa and Kubieck argue that citric acid and itaconic acid processes are virtually the same,

• No regeneration of NAD+ in product pathway. Electron transport chain does the job

Acetoin
Acetoin is a food and fragrance additive, frequently used in ﬂavoring such as vanilla, strawberry,
butter and caramel [30, 36]. Additionally, it is used as a precursor for a variety of organic
products [36]. Its commercial potential, coupled with some bacteria’s native ability to ferment
acetoin, has made it a promising bio-based platform chemical, resulting in a classiﬁcation as
one of the 30 platform chemicals by the US department of Energy [19, 24, 36].
Microbes, such as lactic acid bacteria, Saccharomyces, Bacillus and Klebsiella utilise acetoin
as a storage compound produced during aerobic carbon overﬂow and to prevent acidiﬁcation
during anaerobic growth due to its neutral properties [36, 37].
Acetoin is currently produced via chemical synthesis [24, 36], though Heijen et al. (2015) (Figure
2) suggests it is theoretically possible to produce anaerobically utilising diﬀerent substrates and
some organism already ferment acetoin [37, 38].
10.1
Organisms
Xioa et al. (2014) state, that bacterial systems have the most robust production of acetoin via
fermentation. This allows for usage of cheap substrates, mild processes and a minor environ-
mental footprint compared to the petrochemical industry. [36]
B. subtilis is a facultative anaerobic gram positive model organism, found in soil and the human
intestines. B. subtilis can undergo anaerobic respiration in the presence of nitrate as external
electron acceptor [21] and is known to create mixed acid fermentation in absence of both oxygen
and nitrate [11, 21]. B. subtilis, when undergoing mixed acid fermentation, produce lactate,
acetate, 2,3-butanediol and acetoin when utilising glucose as electron- donor and acceptor [11,
21], making it a suitable production host. Native B. subtilis strains can produce 50 g/L with
the proper fermentation conditions [36].
Lactate is the major side product used to restore the redox balance in B. subtilis (encoded by
idh), while ATP is mainly co-produced with acetate (encoded by pta and ackA) when grown
anaerobic using glucose [11, 37, 38]. See Figure 12 for a schematic overview.

10.5
Potential processes
There is still a couple of obstacles before an acetoin bio-process is industrial viable. For all
three processes pathway optimization has to be done as none are redox balanced. Using xylose
further requires extra ATP input.
NAD+ is not regenerated in the product pathway, making the process anaerobic unfeasible as
accumulation of NADH will prevent growth or result in formation of ”side-products” to restore
the redox balance. The main focus for all three substrates is to design a redox balanced process,
which further generate enough ATP to be viable. Here, it is suggested that it would make more
sense to produce 2,3-butanediol or in the xylose case make an acetate co-fermentation. As high
oxygen concentration (100ppm) favor acetoin production, it might make more sense to design
an aerobic process. Excess carbon has also demonstrated to increase secretion of organic acid
[1].
Glucose: Degrading glucose via the Enter-Doudoroﬀ(ED) pathway yield 1 ATP 1 NADH
and one NADPH instead of 2 ATP and 2 NADH (EMP-pathway). Producing 2,3-butanediol
instead would result in a redox and energy balanced process. ED-pathway should either be
inserted in B. subtilis or an organism already utilising it to degrade glucose should be used as
production host. This approach would most likely be the least labour intensive of the three.
Glycerol: Restoring of NAD+ is a problem. This could be prevented by utilising nitrate as
electron acceptor and glycerol as c-sources. See Yukawa et al. (2006) for more infomation.
Xylose: Co-production of acetate. Degradation of xylose to pyruvate cost 1 ATP. As the main
source of usable energy comes from SLP in anaerobic processes, this could be ﬁxed by producing
acetate as a secondary product. Here, one ATP is produced, see Figure 12.

Final remark
The methods described provides a quick vector based method to estimate the stoichometry of
a bio-process only knowing the electron donor and acceptor. The dissipation method requires
information regarding Gibbs free energy produced/consumed in the catabolic (∆GCat) and
anabolic (∆GAn∗) reactions, while the energy dissipated for growth in the metabolism can be
estimated via an empirical equation (∆GDiss). This information together with the stoichometry
of the two half reactions (derived to calculate ∆GR) is enough to estimate the metabolism. The
metabolism can then be used to derive the Herbert Pirt substrate distribution and process
reaction.
The method can be further reduced to only knowing the theoretical product reaction (∆GCat =
∆GTheor) and the degree of reduction (γ) of substrate and product to derive the HB parameters
from the Gibson parameters.

This is to my knowledge the quickest method to derive a black box model and process reaction.
When using the diﬀerent methods, it was quickly noted that the results are quite similar. For
instance in the valine case as was calculated to −0.581 from the Gibson parameters and −0.587
via the dissipation method, while the ATP methods was higher than the two, being −0.965.
This is due to the limitation in ATP, where this value probably is closer to real life.
The second method used is the ATP method. This is described as a grey box model, as it
utilises information regarding the biochemical pathway in which product is formed and substrate
degraded. The main advantage of the ATP method is that it provides inside in the metabolic
pathway allowing design decisions to be evaluated while estimating the stoichometry. Opening
the black box by taking the metabolic pathway into account provides information regarding
potential targets for engineering. It further allows for the comparison of diﬀerent pathways for
production of the same compound.
The methods provides a general approach to evaluate the viability of a bio-process by estimating
the stoichometry, q-rates and yields. This information can be used to make a kinetic model
or experimental/process design and provide a good ﬁrst ”guess”. NB. the methods can also be
used to estimate aerobic bio-processes with some alterations.
Both methods serves as a ﬁrst approach to evaluate a potential process. They make it easier
to explore the next potential market competitive product, by knowing if it will be commercial
feasible (Equation 49) and the chance for it to work.
Industrial relevance: Anaerobic bio-processes provides a better carbon conversion, resulting
in higher yields. It is often cheaper to run and set up compared to aerobic bio-processes. They
generate less heat, allowing for bigger reactor sizes and reduced heterogeneity due to dissolved
oxygen not being a problem.
Hopefully, more anaerobic bio-processes will be designed in the further, and the methods de-
scribed give an easy way of estimating the metabolism and predicting the cost of the process.

mediates in TCA will be very challenging to produce without oxygen and making an anaerobic
process would properly be way to slow for the industry to be interested. The same applies
to product where fungi is the best production candidate. Fungi grows best in the present of
oxygen.
The upside being, that the dissipation and Herbert Pirt methods are still viable for aerobic
process (more tricky to ﬁnd qp, but the rest is almost the same) as most of the calculations can
be carried out int the same manor. The Herbert Pirt substrate distribution would then contain
the variable |b|, being mol substrate used per mol product produced.
