Abstract
The temporal and spacial distribution of spawning ﬁsh is a key point in ﬁsheries
management. Detailed information about the spawning grounds are vital for man-
aging and protecting essential habitats. For the North sea, there is a general lack of
comprehensive information about spawning sites, in particularly during summer.
In august 2018 and 2019 a series of standard hauls for ﬁsh larvae were made
to investigate the distributional patterns of individual species, and the inter-species
distribution diﬀerences and their linkages to measured hydrographical characteris-
tics.
It was found that re-occurring areas of high abundance was seen for many of the
sampled species. The larvae were patchily distributed across the North Sea, with
indications of spawning sites being linked to recurrent hydrographic features. For
many of the larvae species a concentration of individuals was seen in the vicinity of
hydrographic fronts, being either a coastal or an oﬀshore frontal zone system.
The individuals of two species (Trachurus trachurus and Scomber scombrus) were
measured, and indications for spawning grounds was seen. For T. trachurus, there
were indications of a southern spawning ground in the shallow nutrient rich waters
of the Southern Bight.
For S. scombrus, there were indications of two separate
spawning grounds; one oﬀthe Scottish coast in the northwesterly North Sea, and
another oﬀthe Danish coast near Skagerrak.

Introduction
2.1
The project
This report is the result of a sub-project under a larger initiative aiming to produce
a recruitment estimate for the North Sea sprat stock. The sprat project investigates
the total abundance of sprat larvae during a given year by catching late-stage sprat
larvae.
The overall aim is to add to existing knowledge of spawning grounds and larval
drift in the North Sea. During August in 2018 and 2019 a series of standard hauls
for ﬁsh larvae were made from the Danish research vessel Dana. All caught ﬁsh
larvae from the late summer period were preserved from the two cruises.
Detailed information on larval ﬁsh in the North Sea, at this time of the year,
is, at present, very scarce. For many species spawning areas and nursery grounds
are not fully covered, and there is a general lack of comprehensive information
about spawning sites.
Furthermore, information concerning the linkage between
oceanographic features and spawning sites are needed (Munk; Fox; et al. 2009)
The temporal and spacial distribution of spawning ﬁsh is a key point in ﬁsheries
biology. Detailed information about the spawning grounds are vital for managing
and protecting essential habitats. The two ways of identifying spawning areas are,
1) investigating the presence of mature ﬁsh in trawl surveys, or 2) by looking at
the presence of eggs and larvae (Munk; Fox; et al. 2009) In this case the latter
approach has been utilized.
Besides the sprat larvae, a number of ecologically and commercially important
larval ﬁsh species were sampled. This project focuses on investigating the distri-
butional patterns of the individual species and the composition of the entire ﬁsh
larvae community. The methodology employed will be described and the prelimi-
nary results that are based on the collected samples, will be presented. In total, 150
samples have been analysed from which diﬀerent ﬁsh taxa have been identiﬁed.
A spatial analysis of the data will provide a picture of the abundance of the
18 most abundant taxa in late summer for 2018 and 2019. Furthermore, a statis-
tical analysis will inspect the inter-species distribution diﬀerences and linkages to
measured hydrographical characteristics.
The larval stage of ﬁshes shows of rapid development with substantial changes
in size, structure, physiology and behavior. For the majority of ﬁsh larvae this stage
is planktonic and therefore highly inﬂuenced by hydrographical and trophodynamic
processes (Munk and Nielsen; 2005)

2.2
The study area
2.2.1
The Greater North Sea Region
The North Sea is an area of economically and environmental importance. Being one
of the most productive seas on earth, the North Sea is also surrounded by densely
populated and heavily industrialized regions (De Wilde; Jenness; and Duineveld 1992)
The Greater North Sea region persists of a relatively shallow sea on the European
continental shelf (with the exception of the Norwegian Trench). The Region is char-
acterized as a temperate coastal shelf with a deep channel in the north west, a
permanently thermally mixed water column in the east and south, and a seasonal
stratiﬁcation in the north. (ICES. 2018)
An overview of the North Sea ﬁsheries
In the North Sea, more than 140 diﬀerent ﬁsh species have been described. The
most economically important ﬁsh species account for less than 10 percent of the
total number of species, comprising around 80 percent of the total North Sea ﬁsh
catch in terms of biomass (Mateos-Rivera et al. 2019)
Approximately 6600 ﬁshing vessels are active in the Greater North Sea region.
Total landings peaked in the 1970s at about 4 million tonnes. It has since declined
to about 2 million tonnes. Since 2003, total ﬁshing eﬀort has declined substantially.
Pelagic ﬁsh landings are greater than demersal ﬁsh landings. Herring and mackerel
account for the largest part of the pelagic landings. Sand-eel and haddock account
for the largest part of the demersal landings (ICES 2019)
In terms of tonnage of catch, most of the ﬁsh stocks harvested from the North Sea
are being ﬁshed at levels consistent with achieving good environmental status (GES)
under the EU’s Marine Strategy Framework Directive. However, the reproductive
capacity of the stocks has not generally reached this level. Almost all the ﬁsheries
in the North Sea catch more than one species. To control ﬁshing on one species
therefore aﬀects other species as well. Furthermore, biological interactions occur
between species and ﬁshing on one stock may aﬀect the population dynamics of
another (ICES 2019)
Hydrography
The surface area of the North Sea stretches approx.
525,000 km2 (De Wilde;
Jenness; and Duineveld 1992) Due to it’s shallow depths the North Sea belongs
within the group of shelf seas. The hydrographical structure is complicated, with an
inﬂow of Atlantic water, strong westerly winds, and distinct tidal currents. Many
smaller local bodies of water, currents and frontal systems, play important local
roles. They are created by the local geographical, hydrographical and climatological
circumstances.

The general circulation pattern in the North Sea moves in a counter-clockwise di-
rection. The residual currents in the eastern part of the North Sea are predominantly
directed to the north-east. The primary outﬂow occurs in the north-eastern region,
through the Norwegian Channel (De Wilde; Jenness; and Duineveld 1992)
The Stratiﬁcation of water masses is of major hydrographical signiﬁcance in the
North Sea. It occurs either by thermostratiﬁcation and/or halostratiﬁcation. In the
shallow southern part of the North Sea, high turbulence prevents stratiﬁcation. In
the deep central and northern areas, seasonal or permanent thermostratiﬁcation is
common (De Wilde; Jenness; and Duineveld 1992)

Subregions
Included in the Greater North Sea region are the North Sea, Skagerrak, Kattegat
and the English Channel (See Figure 1).

Northern North Sea
The northern North Sea has a depth of 0–500 meters. It is heavily inﬂuenced by
oceanic inﬂow and has a deep channel in the east.
The majority of the area is

stratiﬁed in summer. The dominant human activities are ﬁshing, and oil and gas
production(ICES. 2018)
The northern North Sea is more strongly inﬂuenced by Atlantic inﬂow via the
Faroe–Shetland gap. The fresh water input originates from Norwegian and Scottish
land-masses. In the summer, thermal stratiﬁcation leads to development of tidal
mixing fronts, but during the late winter and spring, thermal stratiﬁcation is minimal
and salinity-based fronts are more important (Munk; Fox; et al. 2009)
Oﬀthe west coast of Denmark are an area of sand banks referred to as the
Fisher banks. Historically this was en important ﬁshing ground, but has, due to
over-ﬁshing, declined in importance.
Oﬀof the southwest coast of Norway lies the Norwegian Trench. It is approx.
100 km in width, with a depth typically about 300 meters, increasing to a 700
meters maximum in Skagerrak. The Norwegian Trench is important to the cyclonic
circulation associated with westerly and northerly winds, where it allows a return
outﬂow in deeper water. (Huthnance; 1991)
Southern North Sea
The southern North Sea is tidally energetic and strongly inﬂuenced by terrestrial-
runoﬀ. (Munk et al; 2009) It has a depth of 0–50 meters and is characterized by
major river inputs and heavily mixed water. The dominant human activities are ﬁsh-
ing, shipping, ports, gas production, wind farms, and sand extraction(ICES. 2018)
The Wadden Sea streches along the coast of northwest continental Europe. It
consist of tidal ﬂats and wetlands. Both the Wadden Sea and the German Bight are
important nursing areas for early stages of many marine ﬁsh species (Bils et al;
2012)
Dogger bank is a large sandbank in shallow waters in the midst of the North
Sea. The sandbank rises to within 20 meters of the surface (Huthnance; 1991)
The Southern bight likewise is an area of shallow waters. The area is highly
inﬂuenced by tidal currents, and the sandbanks typically rises to within 10 meters
of the sea surface(Huthnance; 1991)
Skagerrak and Kattegat
Skagerrak and Kattegat creates the link to the Baltic Sea.
It is less saline and
less tidal than the other parts of the Greater North Sea region. The water column
is usually mixed. The dominant human activities are ﬁshing, shipping, and wind
farms.Skagerrak and Kattegat have high hydrographical and ecological diversity.
They sustain important nursery grounds of many ﬁsh species (Munk; Cardinale;
et al. 2014)
The English Channel
The English Channel creates the link from the southern North Sea to the Atlantic.
It is generally mixed and strongly inﬂuenced by wind events. The dominant human
activities are ﬁshing, shipping, and aggregate (gravel) extraction(ICES. 2018)

2.2.3
Biota
The North Sea is characterized by episodic changes in productivity of the key com-
ponents of the ecosystem. Phytoplankton, zooplankton, demersal and pelagic ﬁsh
show such cycles of variability.
As a whole the pelagic habitat of the North Sea is characterized by the diﬀerence
between the mixed waters in the southern and eastern parts of the region, and
the stratiﬁed waters in the north. Pelagic organisms are inﬂuenced by the frontal
dynamics of the sea, many of which are semi-permanent (ICES. 2018)
Organisms are also inﬂuenced by the dominating benthic substrate. The benthic
substrate of the Greater North Sea is predominantly characterized by soft sediments
(ranging from muds to gravel beds). The gravel beds are mainly distributed in the
English Channel and the Southern North Sea. The shores consist mainly of mobile
types of sediment (mud, sand, and boulder beaches). Rocky shores are found in the
Northern North Sea and the English Channel (ICES. 2018)
The food web of the Greater North Sea region is characterized by high production
in autotrophic organisms. Primary production is highest in the coastal regions due to
nutrient inputs from the rivers and mixed water columns. Phytoplankon is consumed
by zooplankton and benthos, which in turn are eaten by ﬁsh, seabirds and mammals.
Many of the larger ﬁsh have been reduced in numbers and the North Sea food web
can now be considered to be in a state of perturbation (ICES. 2018)

Methods
The method for the research conducted has been divided into four sections; hy-
drography, ichthyplankton sampling and treatment at sea, lab processing and data
analysis.
3.1
Hydrography
Hydrographic descriptions are based on standard CTD casts carried out by all par-
ticipants in the IBTS Q3 from late July to late August 2018 and 2019 (from ICES
database "ICES.dk").
3.2
Ichthyoplankton sampling and treatment at sea
Sampling procedures followed the ICES Manual for the Mid-water Ring Net sam-
pling during IBTS Q1 (ICES. 2013) Fish larvae and juveniles were sampled in the
Skagerrak and North Sea area during the Q3 IBTS (International Bottom Trawl
Survey) on board the Danish research vessel Dana. The samples were collected over
two survey periods in the month of August, two years in succession. The survey
periods were the 30/7-16/8 2018 and the 30/7-16/8 2019. 82 hauls were completed
successfully in 2018. Of those 2 were with a diﬀerent net (Bongo-net) and 9 were
done during daytime. 68 hauls were completed in 2019. of those, 2 were labeled as
fails due to sampling diﬃculties, 1 was a repetition of a previously sampled station,
and 5 were done as a comparison between diﬀerent nets. In total, samples from 131
hauls/stations were used in further analysis (See Figure 2).

Throughout the survey, ﬁsh larvae and juveniles were sampled at night (After
22:00). Ichthyoplankton hauls should only be conducted at night during the period
between 30 minutes past sunset to 30 minutes before. If there is no cloud cover,
meaning that the daylight period has been extended, ﬁshing should not begin until
60 minutes after sunset and cease 60 minutes before sunrise (ICES. 2013) The
haul-locations for the ichthyoplankton sampling varied between the two years. Each
year the locations were placed, where possible, as to comply with the day-time hauls.
A reasonable coverage of the North Sea area were considered.
The hauls were carried out using a MIK net (See Figure 3). The MIK net or The
Mid-water Ring Net is at present the standard gear for sampling ﬁsh larvae during
the North Sea IBTS in the ﬁrst quarter. The main target of MIK-sampling is larval
herring between 2-3 cm length, which at that size are of low densities. Therefore,
the MIK net has a relatively large opening of 2 metre in diameter (ICES. 2013)
The length of the net is 13 metre, with a mesh size of 1.0 mm. Furthermore, a small
MIKey M net was attached to the MIK ring. This device was added to sample eggs
and exceedingly small larvae. The MIKey M samples are not considered in the data.
The gear was equipped with a depth sensor and a ﬂowmeter. The depth sensor
was mounted on the ring. It monitored the real time depth of the MIK, and distance
to the bottom was calculated. Fishing speed was 3 knots through the water, and
the wire was paid out at a speed of 25 metres per minute (0.4 m/s) and retrieved
at 15 metres per minute (0.25 m/s).
The maximum tow depth was 100 metre,
measured from the lower part of the ring. At shallower depths the tow should be to
5 metre above the bottom, likewise measured from the lower part of the ring. If the
duration of a single haul was less than 10 minutes, a double haul was conducted.
The net would go to above the bottom a second time without the net breaking the

The MIK samples were processed on-site. Immediately after the haul had been
completed the sample was sorted. All ﬁsh larvae were collected from the sample.
At sites where large amounts of ﬁsh larvae were collected, the sample was divided
into a smaller sub-sample. The sorting of the fresh material was carried out whilst
the sample was kept cool on ice and the sorting time as short as possible. The
death of a larvae cause osmotic loss of water, muscle contraction and degeneration
of the tissue by proteolytic enzymes (munk and Nielsen; 2005) A fast ﬁxation
is therefore essential. Clupeid (Sprat, Anchovy, Sardine) larvae were identiﬁed and
kept separate from the rest of the ﬁsh larvae. These were stored in a container
labeled "Other larvae". All ﬁsh larvae were preserved in 96 percent ethanol.
As a part of the on-site treatment, the larvae ware photographed (See Appendix
A.1). Representative sizes for all species were selected, as best possible. This was
done to illustrate larval development through imagery and to be used in later species
identiﬁcation. The selected specimens were set aside in the sorting process and kept
on ice. The larval morphology and morphometry (standard length) was captured in
images and measured using a stereo-microscope with video equipment attached to
it.

3.3
Laboratory processing
Following the two survey periods further processing of the samples were conducted in
a land-based laboratory. Essentially, the "Other Larvae" containers were sorted into
smaller vials, each containing grouped individuals (at the taxonomic level species,
genus or family). It was decided that more details of Mackerel and Horsemackerel
were wanted, and length-measurements of each individual within these two species
were carried out.

(b) Gobys

3.3.1
Sorting of species
Each sample underwent the same sorting process. Firstly the larvae were removed
from the conservation containers, rinsed from ethanol and placed in water-ﬁlled trays
(See Figure 4). The larvae were identiﬁed and sorted into diﬀerent taxa. The main
characteristics used in identiﬁcation of ﬁsh larvae are the body shape, pigmentation
pattern and meristic and morphometric characters. In the identiﬁcation process a
"look alike" approach was used. The larvae were identiﬁed to at least the family level
by matching characteristics of individuals and observing general shape and striking
features. The aim was a division into the highest taxonomic separation. However,
certain individuals were grouped in families (Gobiidae, Trachinidae, Triglidae and
Soleidae) and some were left Unidentiﬁed.

mm, using a ruler. It should be noted that measurements of preserved larvae deviate
substantially from from live-length and shrinkage will be in the order of 5-15 percent
(Munk and Nielsen; 2005) This was not taken into account and the lengths and
mackerel and horse-mackerel are the true length after being preserved in ethanol.

3.4
Data analyses
3.4.1
Abundance estimation
The abundance of each species for each station was calculated as number of indi-
viduals per m2. 18 taxa was treated in abundance estimation.
Equation for abundance estimation:
Individuals per m2 = (catch/(flow ∗opening area)) ∗depth of tow
where ﬂow and depth of tow are in m, opening area in m2.
3.4.2
Analysis of bio-physical linkages (CCA)
An analysis of species distribution explained by environmental variables, was carried
out using a CCA (canonical correspondence analysis). 15 taxa was treated in the
statistical analyses.
Logtransformation of abundance data was done in order to correct for possible
statistical errors associated to rare or very common species. To check for co-linearity

Analysis of length diﬀerences (kolmogorov–Smirnov test)
The lengths of mackerel and horse-mackerel larvae were compared between years by
using a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test. The Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic (the D-value)
quantiﬁes a distance between the empirical distribution functions of two samples.
The null hypothesis is that the samples are drawn from the same distribution. The
test is signiﬁcant with a p-value under 0.05.

General
The Mullidae family comprises six genera, with a total of 62 species, two of which

Horse-mackerel (Trachurus trachurus)
Figure 9: The Area of colored circles illustrates abundance of Horse-mackerel larvae
at sample stations in 2018 and 2019. The circle in bottom left corner illustrates the
area when abundance is 2 larvae per m2
General
Horse-mackerel is a southern species. It reaches it’s northerly distribution limit in
the northern North Sea. It reaches 60 cm and approx. 2 kilos (Munk and Nielsen;
2005) It is ﬁshed and landed mainly for human consumption. In autumn, horse-
mackerel leave the North Sea to return later in the spring. It is widely distributed
pelagically, and it’s habitat preferences are poorly understood. A variety of hydro-
graphical features may be expected to aﬀect it’s distribution, temperature being a
signiﬁcant one (Fish-Map 2006a)
Spawning season and sites
Horse mackerel is a batch spawner. Females release 5-16 batches of eggs over a
period of several months. Peak spawning season in the North Sea falls in May and
June, and spawning has been reported oﬀthe coasts of Belgium, the Netherlands,
Germany and Denmark (Fish-Map 2006a)
Survey data (spatial distribution)
Horse-mackerel is most abundant in the southern part of the North Sea region, with
peaks in the Southern Bight. It is also present along the east boarder of the North
sea and north of Dogger Bank (See Figure 9).

The species Turbot and Brill are diﬃcult to identify, and are therefore grouped in

4.4.3
Comparison of larval lengths between years
Information can be extracted by comparing the length of the individuals in one year,
with those of the next. The two surveys were conveyed during the same weeks both
years. Therefore one would assume that the length of the larvae would be approx.
the same. If one year is signiﬁcantly larger than the other, was there then favorable
environmental conditions that year?
Even though sampling took place in the same weeks, they were not carried out at
the exact same location. Comparing all collected individuals in 2018 with 2019 is not
a fair comparison. Stations were therefore paired up, meaning that a 2018-station
was paired with the closest 2019-station, with a maximum distance of 1 nautical
mile (See Appendix A.2.1 for map of paired stations). The stations that were not
paired, were left out of the analysis.
The lengths were compared by using a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test.
The Kol-
mogorov–Smirnov statistic (the D-value) quantiﬁes a distance between the empirical
distribution functions of two samples. The null hypothesis is that the samples are
drawn from the same distribution. If the test is signiﬁcant with a p-value under
0.05, it means that year-0 larvae in 2018 is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent in size than year-0
in 2019.

Distribution and abundance of ﬁsh larvae are impacted by a highly complex
system in which variables aﬀect one another. It is not only hydrographical features,
such as temperature and salinity, but also the marine organisms responding to these,
not to speak of anthropological disturbance, pollutants and climate change.
In general, it should be stated that both spawning and juvenile sites can not be
considered as static, permanently placed areas. There can be great variability in the
location and distribution of the larvae nursery areas. This is primarily due to wind
velocity and direction, and the resulting changes in the direction of drift patterns of
eggs and larvae (Warnar et al; 2012)
The taxa are grouped simply by the one environmental parameter, within the
CCA, that aﬀect the distribution the most.
The species grouped together may
therefore not reside in the exact same areas, giving that other factors may pull the
distribution in diﬀerent directions. However, by grouping them in these simpliﬁed
assemblages, and comparing the observed abundances with existing knowledge of
the adult stock, assumptions of the placement of the important key areas may be
made.

5.2
A regime shift?
The ecosystems of the ocean exists in multiple stable states (Kaiser et al. 2011)
A regime shift is deﬁned as a conspicuous and rapid jump from one stable state to
another.
Looking at the past, it is evident that changing environmental parameters, must
be considered when managing future ﬁsheries. The North Sea has previously expe-
rienced a regime shift. In 1988 a regime shift is assumed to have been caused by
an inﬂux of nutrient-rich Atlantic water (Kaiser et al. 2011) This change had a
profound inﬂuence on e.g. the recruitment of cod (Gadus marhua) , that has been in
decline since the 1980’s. It is assumed that this regime shift had a negative impact
on the food supply to juvenile cod.
An increase in temperature in the Northern part of the Atlantic Ocean is re-
ported over the last few decades. This is likely to aﬀect the distribution and abun-
dance patterns of some ﬁsh species within the North Sea.
Consequently, these
changes will alter the traditional preferred areas for feeding and spawning grounds
(Mateos-Rivera et al. 2019) The CCA analysis of this report shows that the
environmental conditions in the North Sea has a signiﬁcant impact on distribution
and abundance of ﬁsh larvae. A rise in temperature is therefore likely to alter the
general hydrographical features in the system and cause changes in ﬁsh stocks.
One southerly species that has previously been linked to the eﬀect of global
warming is the striped red mullet (Heessen; Daan; and Ellis 2015) It is referred
to as the newcomer to the North Sea. Since a study like this has not previously been
conducted in the North Sea at this time of year, it can not be stated whether Striped
red mullet was present in the same numbers in the surveys of the previous years.
However, the species is not present in literature or atlases over North Sea ﬁshes,
and has previously not been considered as such. The northerly distribution of the
Striped red mullet could therefore be seen as an indication of a system undergoing
changes.
It should be mentioned that some historic sources mention the presence of Striped
red mullet in the Irish Sea, Celtic Sea, the Channel and the North Sea. This in-
formation underlines that striped red mullet is not in fact a newcomer(Heessen;
Daan; and Ellis 2015) In this case the species long-time absence might be more
interesting than it’s present revival.
Through the nursery ground analysis it also became evident that oceanic fronts
are important for ﬁsh stocks. The exact location of salinity fronts are inﬂuenced
by meteorological parameters such as precipitation, wind and pressure-driven water
currents. Even so they are likely to re-occur with suﬃcient spatial and temporal
predictability. These long-term features allow spawning site selection (Munk; Fox;
et al. 2009) A reoccurring pattern was observed in the community analysis. Reoc-
curring high abundances was found in the vicinity of coastal and mid-ocean frontal
features in 2018 and 2019.
These frontal features could be susceptible to climate change. Climate change
will lead to altered precipitation patterns, wind velocity and higher temperatures.
Such changes could very well aﬀect survival during the vulnerable early stage of
North Sea ﬁsh, and aﬀect the subsequent recruitment to the stocks.

Conclusion
Distribution an abundance of ﬁsh larvae in the North Sea are highly dynamic, and
spawning and juvenile sites for the diﬀerent species are not within permanently
placed areas. In spite of this variability, re-occurring areas of high abundance is
seen for many of the sampled species.
The larvae were patchily distributed across the North Sea, with indications of
spawning sites being linked to recurrent hydrographic features. For many of the
larvae species a concentration of individuals was seen in the vicinity of hydrographic
fronts, being either a coastal or an oﬀshore frontal zone system. It seems that the
physical processes of frontal zones provide a favorable reproductive habitat.
These areas of high abundances would be likely to re-occur with some spatial and
temporal predictability. If length measurements were added to all sampled species,
abundance maps could indicate locations of spawning and nursery grounds.
For horse-mackerel larvae there were indications of a southern spawning ground
in the shallow nutrient rich waters of the Southern Bight. The larvae grew in size
the further north they were sampled, and it is assumed that horse-mackerel larvae
are carried northwards with currents and spread in the North Sea.
For mackerel larvae it seemed that nursing grounds could exists oﬀthe Scottish
coast in the northwesterly North Sea. High egg abundances was sampled in this
area along with small larvae. There were indications of another spawning site oﬀ
the Danish coast near Skagerrak. In this area the smallest mackerel larvae were
most abundant.
To better understand North Sea larval distribution in the summer, a longer
time-series of measured larvae identiﬁed to species-level is necessary.
