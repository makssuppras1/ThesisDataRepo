Summary (English) 
One of the most widespread classes of marine organisms is alpha-proteobacteria (Jensen & Fenical 
1996; Ladau et al. 2013). Strains of the alpha-proteobacterium Ruegeria mobilis with unusually high 
genetic similarity have been collected from marine surface waters and aquaculture settings across the 
world (Sonnenschein et al. 2016). This indicates an unknown niche or subtle genetic and phenotypic 
differences within the species. 
The purpose of this thesis is to explore the potential differences of 45 strains of R. mobilis and their 
four subsequent phylogenetic sub-clusters to determine if any genetic or phenotypic traits reflect 
niche specialisation. R. mobilis produce the antibacterial compound, tropodithietic acid, and a second 
purpose is therefore to determine if particular sub-groups have a so far overlooked biotechnological 
potential (Sonnenschein et al. 2017).  
Using phenotypic and genomic analyses, specific similarities and differences of the strains were 
mapped. All strains were bioactive against the fish pathogen Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287, formed 
biofilm and grew at pH 6-10 and salinity of 0.5-16% sea salt. Genomic analysis indicated that all 
strains produced the osmolyte ectoine. The possible influence of salinity on ecotine production is 
currently being investigated by chemical analyses. All strains were motile and able to utilize glucose, 
D-xylose and arginine, while not utilizing lysine and L-arabinose, contradicting genomic indications 
concerning substrate utilisation and motility. Phenotypic patterns corresponded to those of the 
phylogenetic sub-clustering, with groups combining different sub-clusters depending on growth 
temperature ranges, iron-chelating activity and metabolism of dimethylsulphoniopropionate (DMSP).  
The inhibitory and potential probiotic properties of the R. mobilis-sub-clusters against V. anguillarum 
was examined in co-culture with the microalgae Tetraselmis suecica CCAP 66/4, an aquaculture feed 
organism. All tested strains were inhibitory and co-culture caused a 100-fold reduction of V. 
anguillarum in the cultures.   
In conclusion, we found no correlation between specific phenotypic or genotypic traits and global 
versus local occurrence. The species is well adapted to the marine environment and holds potential 
as probionts in marine aquaculture.

1. Introduction 
With the ocean covering approximately 70% of the surface of the Earth, it is the largest ecosystem 
on the planet (Joint et al. 2010). It contains an unsurpassed diversity of microbial life and only a small 
part of the marine microbiota has been studied. The ocean, therefore, has the potential to harbour 
many microorganisms and associated properties that have yet to be discovered (Jensen & Fenical 
1996).  
Studies about the diversity within the marine microbial life indicate that there are distinct phylum-
specific diversity patterns (Ladau et al. 2013).  Some of the most abundant bacterial classes are alpha-
proteobacteria, gamma-proteobacteria, cyanobacteria and actinobacteria with alpha-proteobacteria 
being the most widespread class (Jensen & Fenical 1996; Ladau et al. 2013) 
Strains of the alpha-proteobacterium Ruegeria mobilis collected from marine surface waters and 
aquaculture settings across the world show unusually high genetic similarity that points towards an 
unknown niche or subtle genetic and phenotypic differences within the species that have yet to be 
identified (Sonnenschein et al. 2017). Exploring the potential differences could provide clearer 
understanding of the marine environment and the life that it holds.  
Additionally, an increasing number of species of pathogenic bacteria are becoming multiresistant to 
conventional antibiotics and hence are very difficult to combat (Dennesen et al. 1998). Antibiotics 
are currently used as the main means to restrain bacterial disease outbreaks in aquaculture (Cabello 
2006). Alternatives to classic antibiotics are needed due to this increasing resistance development as 
well as the daunting possibility of resistance transferring to human pathogens (Verschuere et al. 
2000).  
A search for methods to control pathogens that do not involve conventional antibiotics is therefore 
ongoing. Probiotic bacteria have been considered a promising alternative. Bacteria capable of 
inhibition of several fish pathogens like Vibrio anguillarum are naturally present in the marine 
environment. For members of the Rhodobacterales family, Liang et al. (2003) has hypothesised that 
this activity can be attributed to the active compound tropodithietic acid (TDA). TDA is thought to 
have multiple target sites, resulting in a lower risk for the pathogens developing resistance 
(Rasmussen et al. 2016; Porsby et al. 2011; Wilson et al. 2016). This makes Ruegeria mobilis and 
TDA especially interesting for use as a probiotic in aqua control and as an antibiotic.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Marine environment 
The ocean is covering 70% of the surface of the Earth and is the largest ecosystem on the planet (Joint 
et al. 2010). The geochemical properties of seawater are not uniformly distributed throughout the 
world and comprise many niches for its inhabitants. To explore and exploit microbial capabilities, the 
parameters characterizing these niches need to be considered as they will shape the microbial pheno- 
and genotype and are introduced in this section. 
The ocean has a slightly basic pH typically ranging between 7.5 and 9.0 (Smith 2017). However, the 
pH of the ocean is changing. Early Archean values were significantly lower compared to now, ranging 
between 6.5 and 7.0. This increase is mainly a consequence of solar brightening and decreasing 
interaction between seawater and oceanic crust (Smith 2017). With recent environmental impacts of 
ocean acidification, a decrease in the pH of the ocean is starting to become apparent. The acidification 
is a result of carbon dioxide uptake by the ocean from the atmosphere and has resulted in an average 
drop of pH in the ocean from 8.25 to 8.14 between 1751 and 2004. The pH of ocean water is predicted 
to reach 7.85 in 2100 (Jacobson 2005).  
The change in pH is suggested to affect the microbial community composition as well as individual 
behaviors of marine microorganisms (Krause et al. 2012; Das & Mangwani 2015). Ocean 
acidification might influence not only marine nutrient biogeochemistry, but also microbe mediated 
nutrient transformations (Hutchins et al. 2009). Key processes mediated by marine microorganisms 
like primary productivity, bacterial respiration, calcification, carbonate dissolution, trace gas 
production, trace metal availability, nitrogen cycling and phytoplankton biochemical composition 
could be affected (Joint et al. 2011).  
The Earth is heated by the sun and the ocean water plays a crucial part in capturing and retaining the 
heat. This is due to the large surface and the high heat capacity of water. The Earth’s atmosphere is 
contributing to the retention of heat as it acts as a green house, holding in the heat, that would 
otherwise quickly be released into space once the sun had set. This enables the ocean temperature to 
stay fairly constant with the warmest temperatures being found close to the equator and the coldest 
towards the poles (Fig. 1) (NOAA 2013). The specific temperatures and temperature range are 
however controlled by an array of several factors; ocean currents being the most important.  Without

2.2 Thriving in the marine environment 
As the largest ecosystem on the planet, the ocean is the largest source of microbial life. In a study by 
Ladau et al. (2013) different phylum-specific diversity patterns were found using statistical 
modelling. Some of the most abundant bacterial classes are alpha-proteobacteria, gamma-
proteobacteria, cyanobacteria and actinobacteria with alpha-proteobacteria being the most 
widespread class. It was indicated that  alpha-proteobacteria richness patterns are qualitatively similar 
to the aggregate richness pattern of all bacteria, while those of other classes are unique (Fig. 6) (Ladau 
et al. 2013).

2.2.1 Free-living and attached bacteria 
Marine bacteria predominantly have two “life forms”; either single free-living cells in the water or 
attached to surfaces or aggregates. Surfaces can fix bacteria to a specific location or serve as a travel 
vehicle or nutrient source, as for the case of attachment to particles like phytoplankton. Both states 
provide their own specific niche with different advantages and difficulties, though only a small 
fraction of bacteria exist solely in one form. However, the environment does play a role in the relative 
abundance and the differences in the communal structures both in richness and diversity (Rieck et al. 
2015). 
Higher diversity and richness is found in particle-attached communities compared to the free-living 
bacteria and contribute greatly to the overall diversity in marine waters (Rieck et al. 2015). The 
particle-attached community is impacted by seasonal changes that occur, while free-living bacterial 
community tend to stay more constant. During spring and summer, the abundance of particles 
increases in response to the phytoplankton bloom. This increase impacts the abundance of certain 
bacterial classes that thrive attached to algae.   
Alpha-proteobacteria are found in the upper layer of the oceans and abundance decrease with water 
depth. This in addition to an overall abundance increase seen in correlation with algae blooms 
suggests that alphaproteobacterial prefer living attached to surfaces (Porsby 2010).

2.2.2 Marine biofilm and quorum sensing 
As mentioned, bacteria can form aggregates either on their own or on biotic or abiotic surfaces. These 
dense aggregates are called biofilms. Biofilm formation is a common trait for many marine bacteria 
and a key characteristic for the Roseobacter group (Slightom & Buchan 2009). Biofilms can form 
from monocultures. This is however very uncommon. In nature, several types of microorganisms 
beside bacteria congregate to form biofilms. Microorganisms like archaea, protozoa, fungi, yeast and 
algae are almost always part of a biofilm found in nature (Hall-Stoodley et al. 2004). The bacterial 
cells are embedded in an adhesive matrix of extracellular polymeric substance (EPS), which is 
produced by the cells themselves. Biofilms typically provide the bacteria with greater access to 
nutrients, and increased bacterial defences against eukaryotic hosts, competing bacteria, 
physiochemical disturbances and environmental stresses. This is in part due to the physical protection 
of the dense aggregate and in part due to the act of quorum sensing (QS) (Davey & O’toole 2000).  
QS occurs when bacterial concentrations reach a high cell density such as in biofilms (Miller & 
Bassler 2001). The bacteria can communicate by producing and secreting signalling molecules called 
autoinducers or pheromones, which can bind to receptors found on other bacteria. It can occur 
between a single species of bacteria or between different species (Bassler 1999; Bassler et al. 1997; 
Surette et al. 1999). The binding of the signalling molecule activates transcription of genes, including 
genes for autoinducer synthesis that take part in coordinating certain behaviours (Miller & Bassler 
2001). 
The concentration of autoinducers increases as a function of cell density. When cell density is very 
low, diffusion reduces the concentration of inducers to almost zero, and the inducers will not be able 
to bind to the receptors. As the population grows, and concentration exceeds a certain threshold, this 
makes for a positive feedback loop. When the stimulatory concentration threshold is exceeded, an 
alteration in gene expression is triggered. QS is used to regulate symbiosis, virulence, competence, 
conjugation, antibiotic production, motility, sporulation, and biofilm formation, among other things 
(Miller & Bassler 2001; Kjelleberg & Molin 2002). 
Gram-negative bacteria communicate using signalling molecules such as Acylated Homoserine 
Lactones (AHLs) (Fig. 7). AHLs mediate population density-dependent gene expression in several 
Gram-negative bacteria (McClean et al. 1997). Understanding of QS mechanisms in bacteria living 
in the marine environment is limited (Cicirelli et al. 2008), and only few systems are known. In a 
roseobacterial marine sponge symbiont, a complex LuxR-LuxI type QS network have been identified.

2.2.3 Dealing with osmotic pressure 
Osmolytes are electrolytes that accumulate in the cytoplasm to balance external osmotic pressure. In 
some extremely halotolerant bacteria, strong electrolytes are needed and salts are accumulated. Salt 
can however be toxic to the enzyme proteins within the cytoplasm, whereas perturbation from weaker 
electrolytes are less damaging (Arakawa & Timasheff 1985). Neutral substances like sugars, polyols, 
betaines and amino acids are examples of weaker electrolytes and are called “compatible solutes” 
(Peters et al. 1990; Arakawa & Timasheff 1985).  
Osmolytes are necessary for organisms to survive in the marine environment and they are synthesised 
by a wide range of bacteria. Though common ancestors may not have possessed osmolytes 
evolutionary pressure have resulted in the gains and losses of a variety of solutions for handling 
extreme osmotic stresses over time (Simon et al. 2017).  
Ectoine is a common osmolyte and functions as a “compatible solute” to maintain osmotic balance 
(Peters et al. 1990). It is synthesized by a wide range of bacteria, including members of the 
Roseobacter group (Simon et al. 2017; Ventosa et al. 1998; Trotsenko et al. 2007; Reshetnikov et al. 
2004). Ectoine is a heterocyclic amino acid and a partially hydrogenated pyrimidine derivative (Fig. 
3), produced via a biosynthetic pathway that is a branch of aspartate family amino acid synthesis 
(Peters et al. 1990). Three specific enzymes are responsible for the conversion of diaminobutytic acid 
into ectoine. The pathway starts with aspartate semialdehyde (ASA) being converted to 
diaminobutyric acid (DABA) catalysed by DABA aminotransferase. DABA is then converted into 
Ng-acetyl-DABA by acetylation by DABA acetyltransferase. Finally, Ng-acetyl-DABA is converted 
to ectoine by ectoine synthase. Most halophilic bacteria have these enzymes encoded in the 
chromosome in the cluster ectABC (Gö ller et al. 1998; Kuhlmann & Bremer 2002; Louis & Galinski 
1997; Cánovas et al. 1999). Evolutionary conservation of the ectoine biosynthesis pathway is

2.2.4 DMSP and iron  
Many marine bacteria, including members of the Roseobacter group have been characterized as 
ecological generalists (Moran et al. 2004; Moran et al. 2007; Polz et al. 2006). They thrive in a wide 
variety of environmental conditions and utilise a variety of sources of energy, carbon, nitrogen, iron 
and other essential nutrients. These nutrients can be scarce in the marine environment making this a 
useful trait for bacteria. Two known key characteristics are now presented: DMSP metabolism and 
iron chelation.  
The total amount of organic material is named dissolved organic matter (DOM), where 30% consists 
of amino acids, carbohydrates and lipids. Amino acids make up 1-3% of total dissolved organic 
carbon (DOC), the majority being fucose, rhamnose, arabinose, xylose, mannose, galactose, glucose 
and inositol. Natural sugars make up 2-4%, the majority being glycerine, alanine, aspartic acid and 
serine (Riemann & Azam 2002; Ogawa & Tanoue 2003). The largest contributor of high molecular 
weight DOC is acyl polysaccharide (APS) accounting for between 50% and 80% in surface samples 
(Aluwihare et al. 2002).  The largest supplier of available carbon is however, the primary production 
by phytoplankton (Ogawa & Tanoue 2003). In algae, between 1 and 10% of carbon, is bound in 
dimethylsulphoniopropionate (DMSP). When released DMSP provides substantially to the amount 
of DOC in the ocean (Matrai & Keller 1994; Yoch 2002), primarily concentrated in surface waters 
(Andreae 1990).  
DMSP is an organosulfur compound (Fig. 4), that serves as an osmolyte in marine phytoplankton, 
seaweeds and some species of terrestrial and aquatic vascular, where it functions as “compatible 
solute” (Andreae 1990). Additionally, it functions as a chemoattractant, for motile bacteria of the 
Roseobacter group (Miller et al. 2004)

DMSP can be broken down by several species of bacteria via two different pathways; the 
demethylation pathway or the cleavage pathway (Fig. 5). In the demethylation pathway DMSP is 
catalysed by the DMSP demethylase (DmdA), MMPA-CoA ligase (DmdB), MMPA-CoA 
dehydrogenase (DmdC), and either the MTA-CoA hydratase (DmdD) or acrylate utilization hydratase 
(AcuH). In the cleavage pathway DMSP is catalysed by a DMSP lyase (DddP, DddW, DddY, DddQ, 
DddL or DddK), an acrylate-CoA ligase (PrpE), and an acryloyl-CoA reductase (AcuI). AcuH 
catalyses a side reaction forming 3-hydroxypropionyl-CoA in the cleavage pathway (Bullock et al.  
2017).  
DMSP remains intracellular throughout the growth cycle of most phytoplankton. Due to the size of 
the compound and polarity it cannot pass through intact membranes, but is released via cell lysis or 
breakdown of the compound (Turner et al. 1988).

DMSP is primarily broken down by bacteria for carbon or sulphur. DMSP broken down by the 
cleavage pathway is primarily used as a source of carbon, through entering the methylmalonyl-CoA 
pathway (Bullock et al. 2017). While methanethiol resulting from the demethylation pathway of 
DMSP is the biggest source of sulphur for planktonic Roseobacter species, supplying ≥ 95% of their 
sulphur needs (Zubkov et al. 2001; Kiene et al. 1999). This even despite the considerable amounts of 
sulphate are available in natural waters (Kiene et al. 1999).  
Apart from the active functions of DMSP, some breakdown products have their own functions. It has 
been suggested that marine phytoplankton breaks down DMSP, by producing the enzyme Alma1, and 
use the breakdown product acrylate as a poisonous defence against predators such as protozoa. When 
attacked, DMSP lyase breaks down DMSP though a grazing-activated mechanism (Keller et al. 1989; 
Wolfe et al. 1997). Furthermore, DMS is released alongside acrylate and provides a link between the 
aquatic and terrestrial environment in the global sulphur cycle as well as having an impact on the 
global climate. (Lovelock et al. 1972) 
Sulphate is taken up by algae and plants and then reduced and incorporated into organosulfur 
compounds like DMSP. DMSP is then cleaved by bacteria and algae, resulting in DMS being released 
into the atmosphere via diffusion. The flux of sulphur being released to the atmosphere in this way 
exceeds any other source by a factor of more than 10 (Yoch 2002). Once in the atmosphere, DMS is 
oxidised, mostly to aerosol sulphate, where it is able to nucleate cloud droplets influencing the 
reflectivity and stability of clouds (Andreae 1990). It has been proposed (the CLAW hypothesis) that 
increased cloud formation would reduce the amount of sunlight that reaches the Earth’s surface, 
decreasing the global mean temperature. Such a decrease in sunlight and temperature would slow the 
algal growth, decreasing the release of DMS, thus creating a negative feedback loop (Charlson et al. 
1987).  
Iron is essential for most organisms, due to its unique chemical properties. Though iron is abundant, 
it is largely unavailable in the aerobic environments as it is bound in insoluble complexes of Fe3+. 
The insoluble ferric dominates over the bivalent Fe2+, which is toxic to aquatic animals (Davidson 
1993). Fe2+ bind to alkaline gill surfaces and oxidise into insoluble Fe3+. The insoluble substances 
cover the gills and cause epithelial damage and respiratory disruption (Teien et al. 2008). Strategies 
for scavenging and acquestering iron from the environment has therefore been developed. 
Siderophores are compounds produced by bacteria, fungi and graminaceous plants to do exactly that 
(Hider & Kong 2010).   
==PAGE:25==

Siderophores are low-molecular-weight iron chelating agents that can dissolve complex bound iron, 
thus making it bioavailable for microorganisms by up-take of the Fe-siderophore complex via specific 
receptors. Siderophore production can support probiotic action by the siderophores’ inhibitory 
mechanism-of-action scavenging iron from the environment and thereby limiting  growth of 
pathogenic bacteria (Gatesoupe & J 1997), as well as being a virulence factor for pathogenic bacteria. 
Environmental factors are determinants for the diversity of a microbial consortium. One would 
therefore expect marine bacteria to have genetic and phenotypic traits that reflect this and make them 
thrive under the conditions mentioned.  
2.3 Ruegeria mobilis 
The species Ruegeria mobilis is an aerobic, Gram-negative bacterium of the class alpha-
proteobacteria in the Roseobacter group. Most members of this group are strictly marine or 
hypersaline and contain genes involved in utilizing of sodium gradients and osmoregulation (Moran 
et al. 2007). R. mobilis is found both as free-living and particle-associated with higher abundance in 
particle-associated samples due to a well-adapted lifestyle (Sonnenschein et al. 2017). They can shift 
between the two lifestyles as needed: presumably, attached to surfaces when nutrient supply is 
sufficient and constant and planktonic when dispersal is favourable. Given their efficiency in relation 
to surface-attachment, Ruegeria could play an important role in determining biofilm composition by 
being first-colonisers on solid surfaces and helping or preventing other marine organisms from 
colonizing these surfaces (D’Alvise et al. 2010; Rao et al. 2006; Dang & Lovell 2000).     
Strains of R. mobilis produce the secondary metabolite tropodithietic acid (TDA), which acts as an 
antimicrobial [see section 2.3.1]. Production of TDA is only seen when R. mobilis grows at stagnant 
conditions, where biofilm is formed (Porsby et al. 2008; D’Alvise et al. 2014).  Though biofilm 
formation is not a prerequisite for TDA production, the conditions, for which biofilm formation 
occurs, are favourable for TDA biosynthesis (Prol García et al. 2014). In addition, biofilm formation 
and TDA production have been linked to intracellular concentrations of the signal compound cyclic 
dimeric guanosinmonophosphate (c-di-GMP) (D’Alvise et al. 2014). All the strains of R. mobilis 
analysed in this thesis are TDA-producing and therefore likely antagonistic against a range of other 
bacteria.  
Quorum sensing plays a big role in biofilm formation and regulate TDA production in certain 
Rhodobacteraceae (Berger 2011). Sonnenschein et al.  (2017) searched for the AHL-inducing TDA

Sonnenschein et al.  (2017) studied R. mobilis F1926 as a representative for this clade and found it to 
be highly versatile and capable of growth at a wide range of temperatures, pH values and salinity

On a genomic level, strains of this species are surprisingly similar given the differences in the

sequence analysis (MLSA) of 16S rRNA, gapA, gyrB, ftsZ, mreB, pyrH, recA, rpoA, and topA gene 
sequences, that showed the strains could be differentiated into four sub-clusters (Fig. 8) 
(Sonnenschein et al. 2017).

niche has been identified and no correlation has been established between the parameters measured 
during sampling (depth, temperature, salinity, oxygen, nutrient concentration), the distance to the 
shore (as categorized by coastal, open ocean and high seas) or the biomes associated with the 
sampling sites (Sonnenschein et al. 2017).

2.3.1 Tropodithietic acid (TDA) 
TDA is an antimicrobial compound produced by several species of bacteria. TDA is effective against 
several different Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria as well as fungi and microalgae (Liang 
2003). TDA is particularly effective against V. anguillarum (Porsby et al. 2011). It was first isolated 
and identified in 2003 from a Rugeria sp. isolated from a water sample collected from the surface 
near Neuharlingersiel in the German North Sea, and is mainly found in marine bacteria including all 
strains studied for this thesis (Liang 2003; Sonnenschein et al. 2017). TDA is an organic compound 
with a seven-membered ring structure, a carbonyl, and an acid group. It holds two sulphide atoms 
that form a disulphide bridge. Furthermore, it exists in equilibrium with a tautomeric form, 
thiotropocin (Greer et al. 2008). The two structures are of similar energy and easily interconvert via 
a 1,5-hydrogen shift (Fig. 9) (Thiel et al. 2010).  
 
Fig. 9 - Tropodithietic acid; TDA (a) and the tautomeric form thiotropocin (b).  
Modified from Thiel et al.  (2010). 
Available iron is required for production of bioactive TDA. Low iron results in the production of a 
non-inhibitory TDA analogue (pre-TDA) (D’Alvise et al. 2016a).    
TDA is a secondary metabolite, meaning it is not directly involved in the normal growth, 
development, or reproduction of the bacterium (Thiel et al. 2010). TDA is produced by several genera 
of bacteria under different conditions.  TDA production has been detected in Phaeobacter spp., 
Silicibacter strain TM1040 and Ruegeria spp. (Brinkhoff et al. 2004; Bruhn et al. 2005; Geng et al. 
2008; Porsby 2010). It has been suggested that the antimicrobial attribute enables bacteria to establish 
surface attachments (Geng et al. 2008).  
TDA is active against both growing and non-growing bacterial cells. The mode of action of TDA was 
recently shown to be disruption of the proton motive force (Wilson et al. 2016), which explains the 
activity towards a broad range of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria (Porsby et al. 2011). 
==PAGE:29==

The antimicrobial activity of TDA is pH-dependent and low pH increases the impact (Kintaka et al. 
1984; Cane et al. 1992).  
Utilisation of TDA producing strains as a probiotic in aquaculture is promising. It has been found that 
continuous exposure of the fish pathogen V. anguillarum to sub-lethal concentrations of TDA did not 
result in development of resistance (Rasmussen et al. 2016; Porsby et al. 2011), nor did it affect 
virulence in this pathogen (Wilson et al. 2016).   
Geng et al. (2008) suggested that members of the Roseobacter group use a common pathway for 
TDA biosynthesis, that goes through thiotropocin (Cane et al. 1992). The biosynthesis of thiotropocin 
is, however, only partly understood. The proposed biosynthesis begins from phenylacetate formed 
via the Shikimate pathway (Cane et al. 1992). Shikimate is converted into phenylacetic acid via the 
shikimate-chorismate pathway, and phenylacetic acid then undergoes oxidative ring expansion to 
thiotropocin (Cane et al. 1992). This proposal has been backed up by Thiel et al. (2010) and Brock 
et al. (2014), though they propose that a bound phenylacetyl-CoA substrate is more likely than a free 
phenylacetic acid. A gene cluster for the biosynthesis of TDA has been identified in that members of 
the Roseobacter group (Fig. 10) (Brock et al. 2014; Geng & Belas 2011). TdaA is a positive regulator 
of the cluster (Geng & Belas 2011), while the functions of the remaining encoded enzymes are 
unknown. 
 
Figure 10 – TDA biosynthetic gene cluster of P. inhibens DSM 17395 (Brock et al. 2014).  
Parts of the synthesis are, however, still un-known, e.g. how the two sulphur molecules are added to 
TDA. It has been hypothesised that DMSP could serve as a possible source of sulphur in bacteria, 
that contain the DMSP cleavage pathway. However, since TDA production still appears in strains 
lacking this pathway, more research is still needed on this subject (Bruhn et al. 2005; Newton et al. 
2010; Geng et al. 2008; Porsby 2010).

2.4 Seafood production  
The human population is increasing in size every year and is estimated to reach 1010 individuals in 
the span of the coming 30 years (United Nations, Department of Economic and Social Affairs, 
Population Division, 2015). Thus, the demand for a sustainable steady supply of high-quality protein 
is set to be increasing as well. Currently, this demand is partially being met by the supply of shell- 
and finfish meat, but the increasing need cannot be met by capture fisheries alone due to the depletions 
of several fish stocks (FAO 2014). 
Estimations within sustainable aquaculture yields show that more than 90% of all fisheries are 
currently maximally exploited, or overexploited (FAO 2014). Production of seafood through 
increased capture fisheries is therefore limited and unsustainable. A push towards continued increase 
in production of seafood obtained from cultured species is therefore needed.  
The aquaculture industry already contributes with an increasingly large proportion of seafood 
production with more than half of the seafood produced in 2013 and 2014 supplied by the aquaculture 
industry (Fig. 11) (FAO 2014).  
 
Fig. 11 - The relative contribution from aquaculture and wild catches to the total increase in seafood production

2.4.1 Control of fish pathogens 
With the increase in aquaculture as well as fish being reared at high densities at large scale, the need 
for adequate pathogen control is becoming more apparent. The spread of infectious diseases may have 
large economic consequences for individual fish farmers, and sometimes for entire subsectors 
==PAGE:31==
  
2. Theoretical background 
 
29 
 
(Verschuere et al. 2000). In this regard, bacterial fish pathogens are in general considered the most 
important infectious microbes in aquaculture (Meyer 1991).  
The industry aims to reduce the number of pathogenic bacteria in their facilities. Using disinfectants 
and biocides is not always enough, and antimicrobials have therefore become a necessary evil being 
used not only to treat infected fish, but also as a prophylactic measure (Cabello 2006). Vaccines have 
been developed, and have proven to be highly effective (NORM/NORM-VET 2013). Vaccines can, 
however, not replace antibiotics in its entirety, since larvae and bivalve molluscs have no adaptive 
immune system and therefore cannot be vaccinated (Cabello 2006). 
Prophylactic administration of antibiotics can pose a severe risk to human health due to the spread of 
microbial antibiotic resistance (Cabello 2006). A sustainable alternative is therefore desirable.  
2.4.2 Probiotic bacteria in aquaculture 
Probiotics are “live micro-organisms which, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health 
benefit on the host” (FAO and WHO 2001) and are being considered as a promising alternative to 
antibiotics in aquaculture. Most probiotic strains belong to the genus Lactobacillus and could at first 
glance be considered a genus to explore for use in the aquaculture, since they have proven effective 
against pathogens in the gut of humans and terrestrial animals (Ringø & Gatesoupe 1998). They 
produce bacteriocins that exhibit inhibitory properties against mostly, but not exclusively, Gram-
positive bacteria. Since, most pathogens in aquaculture are Gram-negative, this genus might not be 
the ideal candidate (Verschuere et al. 2000).  
Furthermore, lactic acid bacteria are not dominant in the normal intestinal microbiota of larval or 
growing fish (Ringø & Gatesoupe 1998), and high doses of bacteria must be added for them to 
establish themselves in the unfamiliar environment. Furthermore, since the intestinal microbiota of 
fish are established at the juvenile stage or after metamorphosis (Ringø & Gatesoupe 1998; Strøm & 
Ringø 1993; Joborn et al. 1997; Gildberg & Mikkelsen 1998; Gildberg et al. 1995), the addition of 
probiont must take place before the fish reaches this stage. When dominance is established after this 
stage, continuous addition of the probiont is required to retain this microbiotic composition artificially 
(Verschuere et al. 2000). Under dominant conditions, lactic acid bacteria can be effective in both fish 
and fish larvae, but is not optimal. Since, the larval stage is the most vulnerable, it is also important 
to mention that lactic acid bacteria solely colonise the intestinal mucus, which is not optimal for 
==PAGE:32==
  
2. Theoretical background 
 
30 
 
larvae, that rely more on their surrounding environment (Verschuere et al. 2000). Probiotic bacteria 
that colonise gills, skin, feed, water or abiotic surfaces would therefore be preferred (Hai 2015).  
Members of the Rhodobacteraceae family have also been explored as potential probionts, due to their 
production of TDA and ability to colonize outside of the gut (Porsby et al. 2008; Gram et al. 2015). 
A member of this family, Phaeobacter has been intensely investigated. Phaeobacter is naturally 
occurring in many aquaculture settings and is thought to be a viable candidate for potential probiotics 
(Porsby et al. 2008; Hjelm et al. 2004; Ruiz-Ponte et al. 1998). Concerns regarding this genus has 
arisen as it was discovered that many Phaeobacter strains produce algaecides. Even, they do so in 
very small quantities when induced in bacterial monoculture (pers. comm. Eva Sonnenschein), this 
could affect their use in aquaculture. It is important that the bacteria do not induce any damage to the 
host or surrounding environment. For aquaculture, this encompasses toxicity to not only the fish and 
fish larvae, but also to the algae which are used as feed for the larvae.  
R. mobilis, a species closely related to Phaeobacter, does however not produce any algaecides and 
could potentially present as a better alternative for the use in aquaculture (pers. comm. Eva 
Sonnenschein).  
2.5 Purpose and hypotheses  
The purpose of this thesis is to explore the potential differences of 45 strains of Ruegeria mobilis and 
their four subsequent phylogenetic sub-clusters to determine if any genetic or phenotypic traits reflect 
niche specialisation, as well as determining if particular sub-groups have a so far overlooked 
biotechnological potential.  
It was hypothesised that R. mobilis holds biotechnological usage and potential as a probiotic in 
aquaculture, due to production of TDA. As well as the genetic similarity observed within the species 
could be due to an unknown niche or be a result of subtle genetic and phenotypic differences hiding 
within the strains, that are yet unknown.

3.2 Genomic analysis 
3.2.1 Traitar 
Using the online version of the microbial analyser, Traitar, 42 out of the total 47 strains were analysed 
according to 67 phenotypic traits. The phenotypic prediction is based on two different classification 
models. The first having been trained by using the protein and phenotype presence and absence labels 
from 234 bacterial species (phypat classifier). The second having additional information on 
evolutionary protein family and phenotype gains and losses (phypat+PGL classifier) (Weimann et al. 
2016). 
3.2.2 antiSMASH 
Using the online tool for the automatic genomic identification and analysis of biosynthetic gene 
clusters, antiSMASH 3.0, all 47 strains were analysed. The tool detects putative gene clusters of 
unknown types as well as detection and identification of 1172 clusters with known end products 
(Weber et al. 2015).  
3.2.3 DMSP metabolism 
All strains were screened for known genes encoding the enzymes involved in the metabolism of 
DMSP. For the demethylation pathway, the DmdA gene originated from Ruegeria mobilis S1611 
(NCBI accession number NZ_LNXC01000002.1), DmdB from Rhodobacteraceae bacterium HL-91 
(NCBI accession number LJSG01000013.1), DmdC from Sphingomonas paucimobilis (NCBI 
accession number AB035677.1), DmdD from Ruegeria pomeroyi DSS-3 (NCBI accession number 
CP000031.2) and AcuH from Emericella nidulans (NCBI accession number AJ011563.1). For the

3.3 Phenotypic analysis  
3.3.1 Growth rates 
Strains to be tested were precultured in 100 µL half strength YTSS (½YTSS) (2 g Bacto Yeast extract, 
1.25 g Bacto Tryptone, 20 g Sigma Sea Salts, 1 L de-ionised water) (Sobecky et al. 1997) in 
polystyrene 96-well round-bottomed microtiter plates (Thermo Scientific) in duplicate and incubated 
for 24 hours at 25°C. 
The precultured strains were then transferred into a new microtiter plate using a replicater. OD600 was 
measured every 60 minutes for 48 hours to evaluate the growth rate of the individual strains in relation 
to each other.  
3.3.2 pH range 
Strains to be tested were precultured in 100 µL ½YTSS in a 96-well microtiter plate in duplicate and 
incubated for 24 hours at 25°C. 
Initial testing was performed on solid ½YTSS media with buffers added at a final concentration of 
50 mM. Sodium acetate was used for pH 5.0-6.5, Tris HCl for pH 7.0-9.0 and CAPS for pH 9.5-11.0. 
Media were adjusted to differing pH levels by addition of hydrogen chloride or natrium hydroxide 
before autoclaving. pH levels were measured following autoclaving. 90 mL polystyrene omnitrays 
(Thermo Scientific) were prepared each containing 40 mL media. The precultured strains were 
stamped from the liquid culture onto the surface of the agar using a replicater. The plates were 
incubated at 25°C and growth visually evaluated after 24 hours, 48 hours and 1 week.   
Further testing was performed in liquid ½YTSS media with added buffers with a final concentration 
of 50 mM. Sodium acetate was used for pH 5.0-6.5, Tris HCl for pH 7.0-9.0 and CAPS for pH 9.5-
11.0. Media were adjusted to differing pH levels by addition of hydrogen chloride or natrium 
hydroxide before autoclaving. pH levels were measured following autoclaving. Polystyrene flat-
bottomed 96-well microtiter plates (Thermo Scientific) were prepared each well containing 100 µL 
media. The precultured strains were stamped from the liquid culture into the wells using a replicater. 
OD600 was measured following inoculation and the plates were incubated at 25°C and OD600 was 
measured after 24 hours, 48 hours and 1 week to evaluate growth.

Testing was performed in liquid defined minimal media for P. inhibens (Zech et al. 2009). Glucose,

plates were prepared with each well containing 100 µL media. The precultured strains were stamped

measurements after 24 hours, 48 hours and 1 week to evaluate utilization.  
Testing was re-done with better aeration for selected strains representing the four sub-clusters. Nine 
strains were selected; R. mobilis F1926, S1923, S1424, S2144, NBRC101030, S1611 and M41-2.2,

2 mL were then withdrawn and the cells washed two times. Washing was performed by spinning 
down the cells at 10000 x g for 1 minute, removing the supernatant and resuspending the cells in 
defined mineral medium without any carbon source added. 
Defined mineral media was then prepared with the addition of either glucose, arginine, lysine, D-
xylose or L-arabinose to a final concentration of 5.5 mM. For each strain, 100 µL washed pre-culture 
was added to 1 mL media in 15 mL Falcon tubes and incubated at 25°C at 250 rpm. Growth was 
visually evaluated after 24 hours, 48 hours and 1 week to evaluate utilization.

3.3.7 Motility  
Strains to be tested were inoculated in 100 µL ½YTSS in a 96-well microtiter plate in duplicate and

Testing was performed on soft ½YTSS media containing 5 g/L agar, allowing movement. Omnitray

3.3.8 Biofilm formation 
Strains to be tested were inoculated in 100 µL ½YTSS in a 96-well microtiter plate in duplicate and 
incubated for 24 hours at 25°C. 
Crystal violet staining of formed biofilms was performed according to the crystal violet assay to 
assess for biofilm formation by O'Toole and Kolter, modified for marine bacteria (O’Toole & Kolter 
1998).  
OD600 of the preculture was measured and strains were stamped from the liquid culture into a new 
microtiter plate the wells using a replicater. OD600 was measured following inoculation and the plate 
was incubated at 25°C.   
After 48 hours OD600 was measured. The culture medium and non-adherent bacteria were removed 
by inverting the plate and taping on a paper towel. The wells were washed by adding 200 μL 
demineralized water (milliQ) to each well and inverting the plate and taping on a paper towel. The 
plate was then dried for 5 minutes in a flow bench.  
Using gloves,125 μL 1% Crystal violet (diluted in water (w/v)) was added to each well and left for 
15 minutes to stain. The crystal violet was removed and the wells were washed three times by adding 
200 µL demineralized water (milliQ) to each well and inverting the plate and taping on a paper towel. 
The plate was then dried for 15 minutes in the flow bench.  
200 μL of 95% EtOH was added to each well and mixed with the crystal violet in the wells. After 30 
minutes 100 μL of the mixture was transferred to a new microtiter plate. The intensity of crystal violet 
is measured at OD590.

3.3.9 Bioactivity 
Strains to be tested were inoculated in 100 µL ½YTSS in a 96-well microtiter plate in duplicate and 
incubated for 24 hours at 25°C.  
Bioactivity was tested against Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 (Skov et al., 1995) and Staphylococcus 
aureus 8325 (Novick, 1967) (Hjelm et al. 2004).  V. anguillarum 90-11-287 was inoculated in 5 mL 
Marine Broth (MB, Difco 2216, Zobell, 1941) and incubated (stagnant) at 25 °C for 2 days. S. aureus 
8325 was inoculated in 5 mL Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) and incubated (stagnant) at 25 °C for 2 days. 
Initial testing was performed by stamping the precultured strains from the liquid culture onto the 
surface of the agar using a replicater.  
The solid media consisted of agar, Instant Ocean® salts (IO; Aquarium Systems, Inc., Sarrebourg, 
France), casamino acids as well as peptone when S. aureus is embedded.   
The agar was melted and adjusted to 44-45°C. Glucose was added to a final concentration of 0.4%. 1 
µL of outgrown culture per mL agar was added and the mixture was mixed carefully by swirling the 
bottle. The plates were poured containing 40 mL per Omnitray and left to solidify for 30 minutes.   
The precultured strains were stamped from the liquid culture onto the surface of the agar using a 
replicater. The plates were incubated at 25°C and inhibition zones were evaluated after 24 and 48 
hours.  
Further testing was performed against V. anguillarum using a well diffusion assay to obtain a higher 
inoculate volume. Nine strains were selected and precultured in ½YTSS at 25°C for 24 hours. The 
precultured strains were inoculated into fresh ½YTSS without iron and ½YTSS containing 0.1 g/L 
iron citrate.  
Solid IO media was used with 0.4% added glucose. This time 0.5 or 1 µL of outgrown culture per 
mL agar was added and the mixture. The plates were cast in large petri dishes containing 60 mL per 
dish and left to dry for 30 minutes. Wells were punched and the second round of precultures strains 
were sterile filtered using a 0.2 µm filter and 50 µL of filtrates were added to each well. The plates 
were incubated at 25°C and inhibition zones were evaluated after 24 and 48 hours.

3.3.10 Inhibition of Vibrio anguillarum in axenic algae co-culture  
Inhibitory and thus potential probiotic properties of the representatives for R. mobilis-sub-clusters 
against V. anguillarum 90-11-287 were examined in co-culture with axenic microalgae Tetraselmis 
suecica CCAP 66/4 (Erner, K. E., pers. comm.).  
To achieve the axenic condition, the algae culture was treated with five antibiotics (gentamicin, 
tetracycline, chloramphenicol, streptomycin and penicillin G) at 50 mg/L each for 48 h at 18°C 
(Grotkjær et al. 2016). The antibiotic treated T. suecica culture was centrifuged at 10000 x g and 
washed with sterile 3% IO three times to remove residual antibiotics and resuspended in f/2-Si 
medium (Guillard & Ryther 1962). Bacterial concentration of the algal culture was then determined 
by plating 100 µL on a MA plate.  
The axenic algae culture was grown in f/2-Si medium at 18°C with fluorescent white light at 1500 lx 
until the algae reached a concentration of approximately 105 cells/mL before introduction of any 
bacteria. The concentration was estimated from manual cell count under microscope.  
One representative from each sub-cluster was selected as well as the two Phaeobacter strains 
(probiont) and the V. anguillarum strain (pathogen) were precultured in 5 mL ½YTSS at 25°C and 
250 rpm for 24 hours. OD600 of the cultures were measured to ensure comparable cell concentrations. 
A 3-fold dilution of V. anguillarum was performed in 3% IO.  
50 mL Falcon tubes were then prepared containing 20 mL f/2-Si medium each. Co-cultures were 
prepared by adding 200 µL probiont and 200 µL pathogen to the medium. Mono-cultures were 
prepared by adding 200 µL of either probiont or pathogen and 200 µL ½YTSS to a final concentration 
of 107 and 104 CFU/mL respectively. A negative control was prepared by adding 400 µL ½YTSS to 
the medium. The cultures were then incubated at 18°C, stagnant with light. At the time points 0, 24, 
48 and 72 hours, 300 µL was taken out from each culture, dilution series were made in 3% IO and 
plated on MA and TSA plates. Co-cultures were plated in dilutions of 10-5-10-7 on MA and 10-1-10-6 
on TSA. Probiont mono-cultures were plated in dilutions of 10-5-10-7 on MA and TSA. Mono-culture 
of the pathogen was plated in dilutions of 10-1-10-6 on MA and TSA. The negative control was plated 
in dilutions of 10-1-10-2 on MA and TSA. CFUs on the plates were counted after 2-3 days incubation 
at 25°C.

4.1.3 DMSP metabolism  
Using BLAST, it was indicated that all strains, apart from 27-4, were able to produce DmdA, DddP 
and PrpE, while sub-cluster 2 and 3 were able to produce AcuI as well and thus strains from these 
sub-clusters are the only ones that are able to fully utilize DMSP. Strain 27-4 was indicated to produce 
DmdB, DmdD and PrpE, and therefore not be able to break down DMSP (Appendix II, table 2 and 
3).    
Since not all strains would be able to break down DMSP fully, but still contain some of the enzymes 
from the pathway, KEGG was utilized to explore the possible pathways that the different end products 
could potentially enter. The products explored are MMPA which could be produced for all R. mobilis 
strains as well as Acryloyl-CoA for sub-cluster 1 and 4.   
MMPA and acryloyl-CoA were not found to be part of any known pathway, that could result in it 
being broken down in any other way than what was examined in the DMSP pathway.

5. Discussion 
The purpose of this thesis is to explore the potential differences of 45 strains of Ruegeria mobilis and 
their four subsequent phylogenetic sub-clusters to determine if any genetic or phenotypic traits reflect 
niche specialisation, as well as determining if particular sub-groups have a so far overlooked 
biotechnological potential. To achieve this purpose a combination of genomic and phenotypic 
analyses was carried out. 
Members of the Roseobacter group are considered to be generalists. They thrive in a wide variety of 
environmental conditions and utilise a variety of sources of nutrients (Moran et al. 2004; Moran et 
al. 2007; Polz et al. 2006). This was expected to be the case for the member of the Roseobacter group, 
R. mobilis. In addition, it was expected that the strains of that species grew at the same or similar 
conditions, due to the unusually high similarity shown by Sonnenschein et al. (2017).  
Phenotypic analysis of growth parameters for the 45 strains showed that all strains grew at wide 
ranges of pH and salinity; also, the results demonstrated that they can utilise several substrates. From 
this, it is reasoned that the species is a generalist and that these traits are common for the entire 
species.      
The average pH of the ocean varies between 7.5 and 9.0 (Smith 2017). The pH ranges supporting 
growth were therefore expected to encompass these values. Also, most non-extremophilic bacteria 
grow over a broad range of external pH values, from 5.5–9.0 (Padan et al. 2005). All strains grew at 
pH between 6 and 10; likewise, Sonnenschein et al. (pers. comm.) observed growth at pH between 6 
and 9 for a selected strain of R. mobilis. Therefore, this phenotypic trait does not differentiate the 
strains.  
Members of the Rhodobacteraceae group are susceptible to changes in pH, though their specific 
response to changes are often context-dependent Changes in pH does not affect bacterial abundance, 
but have the potential to cause compositional shifts, depending on the community assembly and 
environmental factors (Krause et al. 2012).   
Given the growth seen at relatively high pH levels, it was assumed that the strains are able to maintain 
a cytoplasmic neutral pH by acidifying or alkalinizing the cytoplasm relative to the external pH, or 
releasing buffers to the external environment (Booth 1985). Buffers were added in the experimental 
setup to avoid the pH of the growth media changing, but pH was however not measured post 
incubation.

5. Discussion

The ocean is a saline environment (Turekian 1968). Therefore, it was expected that the strains would 
all withstand a salinity of at least 3.5%. Even higher salt tolerance was however expected due to the 
nature of the environment of surface seawater. Due to high fluctuations in salinity at water depth 
above 1000 m caused by evaporation and precipitation as well as freshwater input from rivers in 
coastal regions, a broader tolerance range was expected (Peters et al. 1990). Many marine bacteria 
have a broad salt tolerance, unrelated to their origin (Ventosa et al. 1998). Furthermore, Sonnenschein 
et al. (pers. comm.) found that R. mobilis grew at all tested salinities above zero (maximum tested 
concentration of 12% sea salt). All analysed strains grew at salinities between 0.5% and 16% sea salt, 
proving the species to be highly halotolerant.   
Utilisation of specific carbon sources to fulfil oceanic bacterial demands are not well defined, though 
glucose have shown to support growth in the majority of marine bacteria including R. mobilis (Rich 
et al. 1996; Church et al. 2000). Several sources of carbon exist in the ocean including carbohydrates, 
amino acids, fatty acids and CO2. Differentiation of the strains was indicated in relation to substrate 
utilisation. Traitar analysis predicted utilisation of arginine for sub-cluster 1 and 2, utilization of D-
xylose for sub-cluster 3 and 4, utilization of indole for sub-cluster 1 and 2, utilization of L-arabinose 
for sub-cluster 1, and production of lysine decarboxylase for sub-cluster 3. Utilisation of arginine, D-
xylose, L-arabinose and lysine were experimentally tested and these predictions turned out to be 
wrong. All strains were identical in their utilisation of the substrates and could grow in defined 
mineral media containing D-xylose and arginine, while none were able to utilize lysine or L-
arabinose. All tested substrates are known to be present in marine environments (Aluwihare et al. 
2002; Sommerville & Preston 2001; Schell 1974). The classification of substrate utilisation was based 
on the most important proteins of the metabolism pathways. Given the genomic misclassification and 
phenotypic results, R. mobilis shows utilisation of several substrates as well as indicating a sub-
cluster-specific difference in metabolism. Further research is, however, needed to confirm this.   
Given the geographical location of the collection sites as well as the findings of Sonnenschein et al. 
(2017) it was expected that the strains would be able to grow at a wide range of temperatures reflecting 
this geographical distribution. Indeed, the strains grew at a wide range of temperatures. However, all 
strains shared similar ranges.  Given the limited number of samples belonging to sub-cluster 3 and 4, 
no definitive connections could be made between the natural temperature of the sample location and 
the growth range. All strains belonging to sub-cluster 1 and 2 grew between 10 and 37°C, while 
strains belonging to sub-cluster 3 and 4 grew between 15 and 37°C in the tested timeframe of one 
==PAGE:57==

week. Strain 270-3 showed slight growth at 10°C. Genomic analysis using Traitar indicated the ability 
to grow at 42°C, which was not demonstrated by the phenotypic analysis. This may be due to the 
limited incubation time of 1 week; also, growth may have remained undetected given the slow growth 
rate under these conditions. Bacterial metabolism and growth is observed in environments with 
temperatures ranging from below 0°C to over 100°C. Yet, individual strains are generally adapted to 
much narrower temperature ranges (Isaksen et al. 1994). Marine bacteria are, however, versatile and 
do often grow at relatively broad ranges (Johnson et al. 1968; Isaksen et al. 1994). No temperature 
optimum was determined for the strains. However, it is common that the temperature optimum of 
bacteria in pure culture does not reflects the temperature of the environment from which they were 
isolated (Isaksen et al. 1994). 
Production of osmolytes is a way for organisms to cope with the external osmotic pressure. Ectoine 
is a common osmolyte, produced by a many marine bacteria (Simon et al. 2017; Ventosa et al. 1998; 
Trotsenko et al. 2007; Reshetnikov et al. 2004), including members of the Roseobacter group such 
as Ruegeria spp. (Simon et al. 2017). Production of the osmolyte ectoine was predicted for all strains 
by genomic screening for biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) using antiSMASH, and was 
hypothesised to be a contributor to the high salt tolerance. It has yet to be confirmed, since this is 
currently being investigated by chemical analyses.  
Another common trait for marine bacteria are the metabolism of the algal osmolyte DMSP. It is 
particularly common in algae-associated species like those of the Roseobacter group. Many members 
of this clade can utilise DMSP as their primary a source of sulphur. DMSP functions as a 
chemoattractant for these motile bacteria that then swim towards the algae (Miller et al. 2004). 
Motility is, therefore, a key characteristic for the bacteria, giving them an advantage for establishing 
themselves on algal surfaces (Slightom & Buchan 2009). Motility was indicated for sub-clusters 2, 3 
and 4, which conflicted with the generally accepted knowledge about R. mobilis being motile by polar 
flagella. All strains were motile when tested experimentally. The classification of motility was based 
on the most important proteins of the flagellar apparatus and chemotaxis-related proteins. Strains of 
sub-cluster 1 are likely to possess proteins that are not considered by the model, but which hold 
similar functions, as to retain motility. The recurrent motility trait supports their ability to swim 
towards nutrient sources like DMSP.     
Phaeobacter is a genus belonging to the Roseobacter group that has been intensively studied as a 
potential probiont in aquaculture. Many of these can however produce algaecides potentially affecting

56 
 
the algae and feed on it for sulphur (pers. comm. Eva Sonnenschein). This could be a problem for use 
in aquaculture feed cultures consisting of algae. Like for R. mobilis, the algae provide an attachment 
surface and food in the form of DMSP, which the bacteria can use as a source of carbon and sulphur 
(González et al. 1999). In return, the bacteria produce the growth promotor phenylacetic acid and the 
antibiotic TDA. This nurture and protect the host (Wilson et al. 2016). A mutualist-to-parasite switch 
does, however, occurs when the algae senesce. Under these conditions, algae release the 
phenylpropanoid, p-coumaric acid, triggering production of potent algaecides (Wilson et al. 2016). 
This mutualist-to-parasite switch does not exist in R. mobilis. Sonnenschein et al. (pers. comm.) have 
found that no algaecides are produced by the species, potentially making them a better option for use 
in algal cultures.    
Degradation of DMSP was analysed to determine if the lack of algaecides would translate to a lack 
of the DMSP metabolism. DMSP is widely available due to the rapid cycling and long chemical half-
life in natural environments. Its utilization even without algaecides is therefore still likely to provide 
nutritional benefit (Stefels et al. 2007; Dacey & Blough 1987).    
Bacteria within the Roseobacter group have been shown to possess enzymes that specifically and 
efficiently catalyse reactions of the demethylation pathway,  while only possessing a few enzymes of 
the acting in the cleavage pathway (Bullock et al. 2017). 
Using analyses with BLAST and KEGG it was indicated that none of the strains could break down 
DMSP via the demethylation pathway, meaning that none could use DMSP as a source of sulphur. 
Meanwhile, only strains from sub-cluster 2 and 3 could break down DMSP via the cleavage pathway, 
using DMSP as a source of carbon. The remaining strains could only partially break down the 
compound into MMPA and Acryloyl-CoA. These two compounds were not found to be part of any 
known pathway and would therefore likely accumulate or be taken up and utilised by other organisms 
in a natural environment. This is quite unusual for members of the Roseobacter group, where 
degradation of DMSP via the methylation pathway appears to be dominant (Bullock et al. 2017). As 
no experimental work was performed to confirm the utilisation of DMSP, unknown enzymes could 
exist enabling further breakdown of MMPA and acryloyl-CoA.    
Several other phenotypes and secondary metabolites were indicated by antiSMASH and Traitar 
analysis, highlighting some of the similarities the strains share. The genomic screening for BGCs 
showed that all strains of R. mobilis have predicted production of homoserine lactone(s), a bacteriocin

and a type 1 PKS. The Traitar analysis revealed that the strains shared several phenotypes that were 
consistently positive across all strains of R. mobilis; growth on MacConkey agar, growth in KCN, 
bile-susceptibility, growth on ordinary blood agar, ability to oxidize glucose, utilize acetate, citrate, 
D-mannitol, glycerol, myo-inositol and malonate, hydrolyse urea (not DSM17395), produce lipase, 
oxidase (not 27-4), DNase (not DSM17395 and 27-4) and catalase (not S1847) as well as enzymes 
that can convert nitrate to gas and nitrate to nitrile.  
Furthermore, it was hypothesised that R. mobilis holds biotechnological potential as a probiotic in 
aquaculture, due to the production of TDA. Bioactivity against the fish pathogen V. anguillarum was 
confirmed by both spot assays as well as well diffusion assays. Iron was revealed to be crucial to this 
activity as well as provoking the production of a brown pigment indicating that TDA is the main 
contributor to this trait (Geng et al. 2008; D’Alvise et al. 2016b). 
In aquaculture, the probiont would be utilized in algal cultures that are to be used as feed for the fish 
larvae. It is not enough that they do not kill the algae, by not producing algaecides, but it is just as 
important that they keep their antimicrobial properties. When grown together with axenic algae, all 
tested strains of R. mobilis induced a 100-fold reduction of the CFU count of V. anguillarum. This is 
the same reduction seen for co-cultures using Phaeobacter spp. as probionts, suggesting that R. 
mobilis could be just as good at inhibiting the pathogen.  
Whether a 100-fold reduction of the pathogen is enough to prevent infection in larvae and vibriosis 
is unknown and may differ between fish species. However, if such a reduction could be reproduced 
in natural systems, it could serve as a potent biocontrol agent in aquaculture.     
Increasing the production of TDA, could provide a stronger inhibition of the pathogens. Production 
can be enhanced by certain environmental factors. Increasing the concentration of iron and lowering 
the pH of the water in fish farms would increases iron availability and could potentially increase TDA 
production. This is, however, not a viable option, since both low pH and high iron levels can be 
damaging for both algae or fish (Slaninova et al. 2014).  
Iron is important for both pathogens and probionts. As a natural non-specific defence mechanism, 
many fish use iron-binding proteins rendering iron unavailable to pathogenic micro-organisms. Fish 
pathogens like V. anguillarum, can circumvent this defence mechanism by producing siderophores 
(Frans et al. 2011). The siderophores secreted by non-pathogenic strains can, however, compete for 
iron with pathogenic bacteria to inhibit their growth. This has been observed in several siderophore-

producing bacteria such as Bacillus spp., Escherichia coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Zhu & 
Yang 2015; Patel et al. 2009).  
Given the importance of iron and its essential role in production of the antibiotic TDA, it was expected 
that iron chelation by the probionts could result in stronger inhibition of the pathogen; this aspect was 
examined. BGCs for iron chelating siderophores were predicted for sub-clusters 1 and 2, as well as 
for strain 270-3, with a similarity to known genomic clusters between 22-33%, while it was not 
predicted for strains from sub-cluster 3 and 4. This finding was supported by the CAS assay indicating 
iron-chelation. Siderophore production was also suggested for Phaeobacter strain DSM17395, which 
is supported by Collins et al. (2015). This could, however, not be confirmed by the CAS assay.  
Iron chelation is not a prerequisite for TDA production, but could potentially provide an advantage 
to strains living in iron depleted environments, making them better suited for use as probionts in 
aquaculture. No significant differences were, however, observed when comparing the inhibition by 
iron-chelating strains to the non-chelating strains. Further investigations are required to determined 
how the iron concentration could influence the production of siderophores and whether this would be 
impacted by the production of TDA. (Collins et al. 2015) 
In conclusion, strains belonging to phylogenetic sub-cluster 2 could prove to be the most promising 
candidate for probiotic use in aquaculture. Further research is, however, needed to support this 
hypothesis.

6. Future perspectives 
 
59 
 
6. Future perspectives 
To strengthen the evaluation of R. mobilis as probiont and further understand its environmental role, 
several more analyses should be conducted. For example, some phenotypes were indicated by the 
genomic analyses, but not yet confirmed by laboratory experiments and thus, should be tested 
experimentally. Extractions and identification of ectoine and the siderophore could be done as well 
as growth assays to support the findings regarding DMSP utilisation.   
Another phenotype that could be tested is the utilisation of the marine polymer chitin. The turnover 
of chitin is crucial for recycling of carbon and nitrogen in marine ecosystems and as the most abundant 
polymer in the ocean and in the exoskeletons of arthropods such as crustaceans. It often serves as a 
source of carbon and nitrogen for marine microbes. Given the fact that R. mobilis has been found in 
biofilms on the surface of crustaceans like copepods, makes it interesting to determine, whether these 
organisms serve as a nutrient source or solely as a surface for attachment.    
The conditions in which the probiotic abilities of the R. mobilis strains were tested, were far from 
reality, and can therefore not be taken as more than an indicator of the potential probiotic properties. 
Furthermore, as all tested strains showed similar reduction of the pathogen growth, no conclusions 
could be made regarding the ranking of potential as a probiont for use in aquaculture. Further research 
is therefore needed for determining the probionts effectiveness in large-scale natural non-axenic 
systems. 
It was speculated that siderophore-producing strains could have a probiotic advantage over those that 
do not produce siderophore, since this compound could contribute to the iron depletion of the water 
that is part of the natural defence of fish. This was however not shown under the tested conditions. 
Further research is therefore needed to explore the inhibitory effect of the siderophores themselves 
as well as for strains against other fish pathogens not capable of producing competing siderophores. 
This could be done by creating TDA knockouts of the strains to see if the siderophores themselves 
hold inhibitory properties. Additionally, siderophore knockouts could be created and compared to the 
original strains to see if the siderophores provide additional or synergistic inhibition, as well as to 
identify the siderophore.   
The concentration and availability of iron is likely to play a role in both the production of siderophores 
and TDA, but also the virulence of pathogens. Higher and lower iron concentrations should therefore 
be investigated to see how the potential probiotic effects change when iron is sparse or abundant.
