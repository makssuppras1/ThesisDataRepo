Abstract
To precisely estimate the wind resource of a potential wind energy site, input of high quality wind
speed and direction measurements is essential. However, wind data is often imperfect, making the
work of sitting engineers demanding, analyzing extensive datasets with erroneous wind data. Sev-
eral data ﬁltering techniques have already been developed, but most of them operate in a black-box
and not reliable way, while a common practice in the industry is manual data handling.
In this thesis project, six datasets with observations from meteorological masts provided by En-
vision Energy are investigated. The datasets are mainly based on cup anemometer observations
while they cover a variety of climatological conditions. The work presented here aims at investig-
ating new ﬁltering algorithms, such that the erroneous data can be automatically removed from
the datasets. In order to correctly set the ﬁltering criteria, it is necessary to understand why the
cup anemometers fail. The identiﬁed errors can be associated with mast shadow and other non-
instrument related eﬀects, temperature dependency and icing. The eﬀectiveness of these criteria is
tested and compared with other data handling tools and procedures. The proposed criteria eﬀect-
ively remove the faulty wind observations, and they are easy to program and fast to implement.
Naturally, the procedure guarantees a repeatable result, which is an advantage compared to the
manual screening approach. The implementation of the proposed criteria results in a mean wind
speed increase between 2 and 9 %, for most of the datasets. This development in mean wind speed
is found to be, in most cases, greater than in the alternatives. The eﬀect of gap ﬁlling, after data
handling, in both wind statistics as well as annual energy production (AEP) is also investigated
in this project. For gap ﬁlling, a heated sonic anemometer, that shows a very good availability
despite harsh winter weather conditions, as well as data from two mesoscale models, are used.
The mesoscale datasets are ERA5, a commonly used and globally available reanalysis dataset, and
new high-resolution simulations from the New European Wind Atlas (NEWA) project. Finally,
diﬀerent anemometers are compared and evaluated in terms of their ability to accurately measure
the mean wind speed and wind speed standard deviation in diﬀerent environmental conditions. In
general, the diﬀerent instruments show a rather good agreement, with the deviations mostly seen
in low temperature periods; although several conclusions on the sensor’s behavior can be drawn.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Wind data is one of the most critical parameters for the wind energy industry. A great variety of
wind energy applications require information of wind speed and direction. Such applications are
wind resource assessment, wind farm planning, annual energy yield estimates, wind turbine load
calculations and control of wind farms. The vast development of the wind energy industry the last
years demands this information to be as accurate as possible so that miscalculations of the annual
energy production (AEP), loads, wind farm losses, etc, can be minimized.
However, wind measurements are often imperfect, making the mission of siting engineers challen-
ging for the identiﬁcation of potential good sites for wind farm development, the determination
of precise energy yield estimates and eﬃcient wind farm layouts. An example showing how poor
the quality of measured wind data can be is seen in Figure 1.1, where wind speed comparison of
two cup anemometers mounted on the same meteorological mast is presented. Such imperfections
are usually a result of the failure of measuring devices, due to icing and other environmental ef-
fects, sensor’s inaccuracy, ﬂow distortion caused by meteorological mast shadow, as well as poorly
designed measuring campaigns that do not comply with the recommended procedures and best
practices.
Considering the growing need of the industry for more precise wind data measurements, ﬁnding
eﬃcient ways to ﬂag these incorrect values is a matter of a high importance. Many wind data
screening algorithms have been already developed. An example of a standard tool used for wind
data ﬁltering, is Windographer (AWS Truepower.
Inc, UL, New York, USA), where the user
can specify the ﬁltering criteria and have a good overview of the data after screening. However,
most of the available algorithms operate in a black-box way, such as the user has no clear idea
on how the data is treated and whether good quality data is also removed or not.
To assure
that all faulty values have been removed from the data-sets and no correct data points have been
accidentally deleted, many siting engineers prefer to carry out data handling manually, examining

The imperfection of wind data together with the lack of well documented and high quality screening

shortcomings from the data-sets, saving time for the siting engineers, has been the main motivation
for the present study.
1.2
Goal of Study
In this study, the main focus is given on met mast based wind data measurements and more
precisely on measurements performed by cup anemometers. To that end, the predominant cup
anemometers in the market and their technical speciﬁcations are examined and documented. The
main errors in wind speed measurements associated with cup anemometer working principles, such
as overspeeding, friction dependency on the air temperature and asymmetrical response on diﬀerent
tilt angles, etc., are presented.
In addition, several industrial data-sets used for wind resource assessment campaigns, provided by
Envision Energy and use cup anemometers for measuring wind speed, mainly in cold and icing
climates are characterized and documented. The available data-sets are thoroughly examined and
the main shortcomings found in wind data, mainly associated with ﬂow distortion due to mast
shadow and environmental eﬀects are shown. Based on these data-sets and the shortcomings that
are found, ﬁltering criteria that remove, at ﬁrst the obviously faulty data points, and later the
less evident errors are established and their eﬀect on the data-sets is examined. Two existing data
ﬁltering algorithms as well as the ﬁltering criteria recommended in the wind resource assessment
handbook [3], are tested.

Furthermore, diﬀerent types of cup anemometers are compared and evaluated after removing most
of the shortcomings from the wind speed measurements. The last stage of the project deals with
the eﬀect of the diﬀerent data ﬁlters on the the wind statistics and the AEP. To more realistically
estimate the wind speed and AEP, the gaps in the datasets need to be ﬁlled in, especially in sites
where data ﬁltering removes a great amount of wind data. This is done here using three sources: (1)
ERA5, the standard mesoscale reanalysis dataset used in wind resource assessment applications,
(2) NEWA, a brand new high resolution mesoscale dataset that is based on ERA5 and (3) wind
data from heated sonic anemometers in met masts where they are available.
Considering the imperfection of wind data measurements, the inadequacy of the existing data ﬁl-
tering algorithms and the increasing need of wind energy industry for more accurate measurements
as the wind power is proportional to the third power of the wind speed, the present study aims to
come up with useful recommendations for siting engineers. These recommendations correspond to
the following 4 categories:
1. Main sources of shortcomings in wind speed measurements based on cup anemometers
2. Eﬀective data ﬁltering criteria that can be used to resolve erroneous wind speed data
3. Performance evaluation of the diﬀerent types of cup anemometers based on sensor-to-sensor
comparisons
4. Eﬀect of the ﬁltering criteria on the AEP calculations and the wind speed statistics

Chapter 2
Wind Resource Assessment (WRA)
Wind Resource Assessment (WRA) is one of the main application of wind data measurements.
To precisely estimate the wind resource and the potential wind energy production at a given site,
wind data of good quality is required. As discussed previously, one of the goals of this study is the
establishment of ﬁltering criteria that can eﬀectively sift wind speed data. The main motivation
behind wind data cleaning, at Envision Energy, is the estimation of the energy yield for sites that
are under wind farm development. Such energy yield estimates are the main goal of WRA and
this is the reason why a description of WRA, stages and best practices is given here.
2.1
WRA Goal and Methodology
The main goal of a complete WRA program is the precise estimation of the AEP, for a given wind
energy site. The estimated AEP is the basis for the later ﬁnancial assessment of a certain project
and it is the main driver of the decision whether a wind energy project is feasible or not.
The stages of WRA are seen in Figure 2.1. The ﬁrst stage deals with the identiﬁcation of potential
windy sites, the inspection and ranking of them, and the selection of locations in the selected sites
for wind measurement, where met mast will be later placed on [3]. After the selection of a suitable
site, the next step is to design and initiate a measurement campaign that will produce information
regarding the site-speciﬁc wind conditions. In this stage, the meteorological mast(s) are installed,
wind speed data, including wind direction, mean wind speed and standard deviation, temperature,
etc, is measured and analyzed in a way that can be later used as an input for AEP estimates and
wind turbine suitability studies. Being the basis of the AEP calculation and the selection of a suit-
able wind turbine generator (WTG), the monitoring campaign is one of the most signiﬁcant parts
of WRA. The processed data acquired through stage 2, is then used for gross AEP calculations
that are carried out using relevant software (Stage 3). For this stage a Long-Term extrapolation of
5
==PAGE:28==
wind speed using Measure-Correlate-Predict methods is necessary for the ﬁnal calculation of the
energy yield.
Moreover, a wind farm shows a great number of technical and environment losses related to elec-
trical eﬃciency, noise curtailment, etc, which should be estimated. Along with the losses a degree
of uncertainty, among others, associated with wind data measurements and the ﬂow model used for
the extrapolation of wind conditions from met mast to WTGs locations and hub heights, should
be also considered. The analysis of losses and uncertainties on the AEP calculation is carried out
in Stage 4, and it is then combined with the initial AEP calculation forming a bankable energy
yield report, which is used for ﬁnancing the project. In the present study, main focus is given on
stages 2 and 3 of WRA, where wind data is collected and analyzed and estimation of the gross
AEP is curried out. It is also mentioned that WRA campaigns that use met mast based wind data
only, are investigated here.

2.2
Wind Monitoring Campaign
Information regarding the second stage of WRA, where on-site wind data measurements are per-
formed, is presented, here.
2.2.1
Best Practices for WRA
As mentioned above, wind measurements are an essential part of WRA, as input for the AEP
calculations and the load assessment is generated here. To successfully carry out a monitoring
campaign, several requirements suggested by AWS Scientiﬁc, Inc., for the National Renewable
Energy Laboratory, in Wind Resource Assessment Handbook (1997) [3] should be fulﬁlled.
To assure that the measurement campaign is long enough to capture the variability of wind speed
and that the wind data measured is representative of the area, the following guidelines shall be
followed:
1. Monitoring duration shall be minimum one year so that the diurnal and seasonal variability
of the wind can be determined. A good practice is usually to extend the duration to 2 or
more years ensuring that the data recovery is at least 12 months after data handling
6
==PAGE:29==
2. Gaps in wind data should be limited to one week
3. Areas such as ridges oriented perpendicular to the prevailing wind direction, locations with
the highest elevation on site and areas where the local winds can funnel should be avoided,
as wind speed can be greater than in the surroundings
4. The met mast tower shall be placed as far as possible from local obstructions. As a rule of
thumb all sensors must be located at a horizontal distance that is 10 times the height of the
obstruction in the prevailing wind direction
5. The met mast should be located close enough to all the wind turbines of the wind farm
so that wind conditions can be extrapolated with tolerable uncertainty to the wind turbine
side. All turbines shall be placed inside a radius of 10 km for ﬂat terrain sites and 2 km for
complex terrain locations [32]
6. The selected met mast location should be representative of the majority of the site
The main parameters that shall be measured in every monitoring campaign are the wind speed,
wind direction, air temperature and pressure. Average, standard deviation, minimum and max-
imum values of all these parameters should be recorded, sampled at a frequency of 1 or 2 s and
stored as 10-min averages. For assuring quality of the measurements the following recommenda-
tions shall be considered[3]:
1. Wind speed measurements should be taken at multiple heights with 20 m diﬀerence to de-
termine the wind shear and to simulate wind turbines with diﬀerent hub heights.
2. Primary anemometers must have a backup sensor at the same height allowing in-situ com-
parisons and gap ﬁlling. Ideally sensors should be redundant at all heights.
3. All anemometers and vanes should be calibrated by an accredited institute. All anemometers
need to be re-calibrated and reconditioned after two years of operation.
4. The anemometer booms should be oriented in the same direction and have a length of at
least 6 times the mast width. The booms are mounted with a 45◦and 90◦oﬀset from the
prevailing wind direction for tubular and lattice masts, respectively. The upper-level sensors
should be mounted 0.3 m or more above the tower top minimizing tower shadow eﬀects.
5. If there is a lightning arrester the boom should have at least 50 times the diameter of the
arrester.
6. Wind direction should be measured at several heights so that the wind farm layout can be
optimized on the least possible wake losses within the wind turbines.
7. The wind vane should be oriented such as its dead-band position is not directed towards the
predominant wind direction.

2.2.2
Requirements for Cup Anemometers
As stated above, in this study, WRA programs that are based on wind data measured by met masts
are investigated. The main instrument used for this kind of wind measurements is the cup anem-
ometer and the quality of wind speed measurements taken by this device should be ensured. At
ﬁrst, all used cup anemometers should be calibrated using the calibration procedure, as described
in IEC 61400-12-1 Edition 2 [39], by a MEASNET (International Network for Harmonized and
Recognized Measurements in Wind Energy[13]) accredited institution [32]. The cup anemometers
need to be re-calibraded after two years years of operation, to ensure the precision of wind speed
measurements. Furthermore, the existing brands of cup anemometers, show a big variation in the
ability to measure wind speed, in terms of their working eﬃciency in diﬀerent environmental con-
ditions. Therefore, depending on the application, the location and the environmental conditions,
cup anemometers able to correctly measure the wind speed should be chosen.
Since diﬀerent cup anemometers. as mentioned above, often show big deviations in wind speed
measurements, a classiﬁcation of the several cup anemometers, based on their dynamic response
to diﬀerent external environmental conditions is done in ACCUWIND - Methods for classiﬁcation
of cup anememeters[28]. In this study, two diﬀerent models are used to simulate the dynamic
response of cup anemometers under diﬀerent external conditions, such as tilt angle, turbulence
intensity and temperature.
Two classes, A and B, corresponding to ﬂat and complex terrain,
respectively are used for the classiﬁcation. The output of this classiﬁcation is a value, that is
connected to the uncertainty of the measurements, for each class. The higher the number, the
higher the uncertainty of the instrument in wind speed measurements is. For instance, the results
of the classiﬁcation of Wind Sensor P2546-OPR, as calculated in [35], are: 1.32A and 3.71B, for
class A and B, respectively. It is mentioned that, according to IEC 61400-12-1 Edition 2 [39],
3 additional classes related to terrain and environmental conditions have been included. Class
C, that refers to ﬂat terrain and temperatures down to −20oC, Class D, that refers to complex
terrain and temperature down to −20oC and Class S that refers to special environmental and
terrain condition that do not comply with the other four classes. A description of the working
principles of cup anemometers and a presentation of the sensors available in the market is carried
out in Chapter 4.
2.3
Wind Data Imperfections: Need for Data Filtering
As already discussed, wind data is often imperfect and many shortcomings associated with wind
speed and direction measurements can be found. Therefore, an important step of a successful
monitoring campaign is the validation and ﬁltering of wind data. This indicates that an eﬀective
data screening process of a high quality, which can ﬁlter out the shortcomings and result to a
precise AEP estimate, is required. For this reason, several ﬁltering criteria and algorithms have
been developed. Some examples of the existing screening conditions and tools, that are used later
for data handling, are shown in this section.

2.3.1

Resource Assessment Handbook (1997) [3], several tests between measured wind data parameters

be identiﬁed and a decision whether they are faulty values or not can be made. Range, relational
and trend test criteria as found in [3], are presented in Tables 2.1, 2.2 and 2.3, respectively. Even

and its ﬁrst and second derivatives are plotted against each other. The points are enclosed outside

2.3.4
Manual Data Handling
As mentioned previously, a common way for data screening at Envision Energy is the manual
inspection of the whole time series of the data. This approach often results to a good quality data
ﬁltering as the responsible engineer examines every single point of the dataset and makes a decision
whether a wind data point should be removed or not. However, this manual procedure, has certain
disadvantages as it is strongly depended on the person that does the ﬁltering, as well as it can be
very time consuming as a detailed screening of a big dataset could take up to a working day. Some
guidelines of the procedure used for manual data screening in this study are listed below:
1. Wind direction sensors are inspected for consecutive zero values and for out of range values
(higher than 360o or lower than 0o), indicating that the sensors are frozen or out of order.
When all direction sensors are out of order the wind data from all anemometers are deleted
2. Each one of the available wind speed sensors is checked for out of range (zero values or
extremely high mean wind speeds), sudden picks in mean wind speed or consecutive values
pointing the oﬀset of each instrument. This indicates sensor failure and the faulty values of
each sensor are removed
3. The mean wind speed data is then inspected for big deviations between the diﬀerent an-
emometers. When a signiﬁcant deference in wind speed between the sensors is found, the
anemometers are compared and a decision on which sensor measure incorrectly is made
4. The pattern of the mean wind speed is also checked. For time periods when a sensor shows a
diﬀerent pattern in mean wind speed, compared to the others, the data from this instrument
is removed from the dataset
2.4
From Wind Speed to AEP
As mentioned above, the step that follows a wind measurement campaign in a WRA is the es-
timation of the energy production. The measured and analyzed wind data is used as an input for
AEP calculations. Before the AEP calculation the mean wind speed data need to be extrapolated
it to long-term periods, so that the gaps on wind measurements and the long-term interannual
variations can be considered. This is usually done by means of mesoscale datasets where wind
data is available a few decades. This extrapolation, so-called long-term correction, is done using
measure-correlate-predict (MCP) methods [32]. In the present study, long-term correction is not
carried out for the calculation of the AEP. The AEP is calculated only to test the eﬀect of gap
ﬁlling on the datasets. To ﬁll the gaps in the wind data, two mesoscale datasets, ERA5 [37] and
NEWA [11], and when available sonic anemometers that show a very good data recovery, are used
for gap ﬁlling in this study. The two mesoscale datasets are presented below.
ERA5
ERA5 is one of the most commonly used mesoscale datasets for long-term correction in WRA ap-
plications is ERA5. ERA5 is the ﬁfth generation of ECWMF (European Centre for Medium-Range
11
==PAGE:34==
Weather Forecasts) atmospheric reanalysis of the global climate, where model data and observa-
tions are combined into complete data-set that is available globally. In the reanalysis a previous
forecast is combined with new observations every 12 hours in an optimal way to estimate the state
of the atmosphere and produce a new forecast. Even though this reanalysis procedure considers
data in a window of 12 hours, ERA5 provides estimated data for each hour of a day, while it is
currently available worldwide from 1979 and on-wards. The reanalysis horizontal resolution is 31
km x 31 km and, mean wind speed at multiple heights, mean wind direction, mean temperature
and pressure are some of the available, among others, parameters [37], [23].
NEWA
New European Wind Atlas (NEWA) is a brand new European scale dataset that aims to be the
standard wind atlas in site assessment applications. When completed, NEWA will provide a high
resolution and freely available data-set for wind energy resources in Europe. NEWA is developed
using the Weather Research and Forecasting model (WRF). NEWA dataset is made with ERA5
datasets as starting point; hence, it represents a reﬁnement. The resolution of the mesoscale sim-
ulations is 3 km x 3 km[4],[2].
AEP Calculation
After the long-term correction, or in the present study, the gap ﬁlling using mesoscale data and/or
sonic anemometers, the AEP for given wind turbine generators (WTGs) can be calculated. The
standard tool used for such calculations is WindPro (EMD International A/S, Aalborg, Denmark)
and it is the software used for all AEP calculations presented in Chapter 8. WindPro is a software
suitable for the design, development and assessment of wind energy projects.
It consists of a
number of objects and modules, for noise and shadow ﬂicker calculations, measure-correlate-predict
methods as well as energy yield calculations [25].
In this study, the software is used only for
manual data handling, AEP calculations, and to download ERA5 datasets. The AEP in WindPro
is calculated using the WAsP engine, the selected WTG power curve and a Weibull distribution
based on the measured wind statistics. For all AEP calculations that are carried out in this report,
a single WTG placed at met mast position is used, while the hub height is, each time, equal to
the top measurements height. This is done to minimize the uncertainty of the ﬂow model and the
quality of the elevation and roughness maps used.

Chapter 3
Wind Resource Assessment in Cold
Climates
Many sites in Cold Climate (CC) oﬀer huge wind energy potential for future development of wind
farms. This fact has already driven the wind farm developers to develop projects in such areas.
However, despite the high wind speeds a lot of problems are encountered during the development
stage as well as the operational stage of the wind farms due to icing. Therefore, to successfully
carry out a WRA in a CC site some guidelines stated here need to be followed.
First of all the conditions characterizing a CC need to be stated. According to IEA Wind Task 19,
a low temperature climate (LTC) is deﬁned as a site where the temperature is lower than -20 ◦C
for more than 9 days a year and the annual mean temperature is less than 0 ◦C. An icing climate
(IC), on the other hand, is a site where instrumental icing, i.e period when icing is visible on struc-
tures, occurs for more than 1% of a year and meteorological icing, i.e period when meteorological
conditions allow ice accretion, is found for more than 0.5% of a year. A CC site is a LTC or IC, or
a combination of both, as it is schematically shown in Figure 3.1 [18].
Figure 3.1: Presentation of CC, LTC and IC deﬁnitions (Source: Available Technologies of Wind
Energy in Cold Climates, IEA wind, 2006, Figure 1 [42])

A WRA program for such areas with extremely low temperature diﬀers from a regular WRA
mainly in the wind measurement phase. Ice growth on sensors and met structure can lead to
instrument and met mast failures, and thus, to measurement errors and loss of data. Ice built-up
on the cups of cup anemometers and on the wind vanes can slow down or even stop the instruments
leading to mistaken calculations of wind speed and direction. Similarly, ice accretion on the booms,
met mast and lightning rod can increase the ﬂow distortion and lead to erroneous measurements.
To minimize such problems, related to wind speed measurements the following recommendations,
proposed by IEA in Available Technologies of Wind Energy in Cold Climates [18] and in Wind
Enrgy Projects in Cold Climates [42], shall be followed.
1. Cup anemometers that are equipped with shaft and/or cups, minimizing the problems due
to icing
2. Heated wind vanes should be used, preventing icing issues
3. Ultrasonic anemometers that have no moving parts and they are not vulnerable to icing shall
be used along with the cup anemometers and wind vanes ensuring suﬃcient data availability
in low temperature periods
4. Booms and all the neighboring objects close to the sensors on the met mast should be heated
so that ice build-up can be avoided and, thus the ﬂow distortion can be minimized
5. Remote sensing techniques such as LiDARs and SoDARs could also supplement the meas-
urement campaign
6. Even if all the above mentioned guidelines are followed, extra attention should be paid in wind
data cleaning, since even heated sensors can face many problems in very low temperatures
7. Measurement mast shall be designed taking into account the ice build-up that can lead to
failure of the construction
8. All used devices should be designed and installed in a way that allows service during cold
weather
9. Power supply should be ensured for the whole wintertime
10. Good quality equipment and strong materials able to cope the CC diﬃculties should be used
11. One year of ice measurements at hub height or higher should at least be performed. These
measurements include meteorological and instrumental icing and they should be long-term
corrected capturing the correctly the diurnal and interannual wind variability
12. Communication with the nearby wind farm operators and owners should be established en-
suring they are getting knowledge and experience with CC in this particular region
Most of the industrial datasets that are presented in Chapter 5 are measured in Norway and Sweden
and very low temperatures are often observed. Most of wind data are quite aﬀected by icing issues
with many gaps, periods where sensors are oﬀ, etc.

Chapter 4
Cup Anemometers and Other
Instruments
As already discussed, the main focus of the study is given to wind speed measurements performed
by cup anemometers. Hence, a brief history, the working principles and the basic errors associated
with wind speed measurements by cup anemometers, are presented here. In addition, the cup
anemometers dominating the market as well as a description of sonic anemometers used in two of
the met masts are presented.
4.1
Cup Anemometer
The cup anemometer has been the most commonly used instrument for measuring the wind speed
for the past 100 years and is still the most common instrument for wind speed measurements be-
cause of the advantages it has compared to alternative devices [31]. It is used in various applications,
among others, in meteorology, in airports wind speed measurements, air pollution measurements
and lately in the wind energy industry. Some of the reasons why the cup anemometer has been the
predominant instrument for wind speed measuring applications are its reliable and robust design
enabling the long-term operation in the ﬁeld even under extreme weather conditions. It is easy
to install because of the omnidirectional design; meaning the installation is not depended on the
prevailing wind direction. Another asset of the cup anemometer is that the rotor rotation rate is
almost proportional to the wind speed indicating that an accurate calibration can be applied [15].
Finally, another beneﬁt of this device, especially for the wind energy industry, is the very low price
compared to alternatives, such us sonic anemometer, LiDAR and SoDAR [31].

4.1.1
Brief History of Cup Anemometer
A brief review on cup anemometer’s history as found in The Cup Anemometer, a Fundamental
Meteorological Instrument for the Wind Energy Industry [31], indicates that it was invented by
the Irish astronomer John Thomas Romney Robinson at Armagh Observatory in 1846, as a 4-cup
anemometer [16] (see Figure 4.1). In 1926, the Canadian meteorologist John Patterson proposed a
3-cup anemometer since he found out that it is superior to the 4-cup anemometer, mainly due to
its higher aerodynamic torque, the quicker response and the more uniform design [36]. Afterwards
a very thorough research on the instrument started worldwide. The ﬁrst research eﬀorts focused
on the optimal number of cups, the aerodynamic performance, the frequency recording system and
errors due to ﬂuctuating wind speeds [22],[6],[33]. Later on, due to the development of the wind
energy industry, demanding accurate wind speed measurements, a deeper look was given into the
response of the instrument in turbulent wind, since an asymmetric rotor response to changes in the
wind speed was observed, leading to the phenomenon known as overspeeding. The increasing need
for more precise measurements driven by the fast-developing wind energy industry, due to the fact
that wind power is proportional to the third power of the wind speed, led the research into optimal
calibration methods, the possibility of ﬁeld calibration, the impact of the environmental conditions,
the eﬀect of aging, etc [31],[26],[30],[17]. Finally, recent studies focused on the classiﬁcation of the
cup anemometers dominating the market, based on systematic errors of the instruments applying
general external climatic conditions [28],[27].
4.1.2

around a vertical shaft that drives a signal generator. The air ﬂow reaching the cup anemometer

4.1.3
Errors Associated with Cup Anemometer Wind Speed Measurements
The main sources of errors in cup anemometry, that can result in miscalculations of wind speed,
include biases caused by turbulence, tower and boom ﬂow distortion, errors in calibration and
other biases associated with the environmental conditions. Some of the typical sources of biases
are presented here.
18
==PAGE:41==
4.1.3.1
Errors Related to Turbulence
As discussed by L. Kristensen (1999) [14], four types of biases associated with the response of the
cup anemometer in turbulent wind ﬁelds can be identiﬁed [24]. The ﬁrst is the so-called overspeed-
ing, or u-error, as introduced by MacCready (1965)[20] caused by the asymmetrical response of
the instrument in changes of wind speed. The anemometer responds faster in sudden increase of
wind speed rather than it breaks, in a decrease of wind speed. The lower the distance constant of
a cup anemometer, i.e the length of the column of air that has to blow through the anemometer
in order to reach 63% of its total reaction to a change [15], the lower this bias is.
The second error is known as data processing (DP error) or v-bias; it is triggered by wind direction
ﬂuctuations and thus the fact that the cup anemometer measures the mean of the total horizontal
wind speed. The third error, w-bias, is caused by the non-horizontal wind component, w. If the
wind diﬀers from the horizontal projection by a certain angle, the sensor measures a wind speed
higher than the true value [21]. Finally, the fourth bias, called stress bias, corresponds to the fact
that cup anemometers do not, in reality, show a cosine response to changes of the ﬂow angle. In
case of an ideal anemometer with a cosine response, this bias would be zero[24].
Comparing cup anemometers with other type of instruments, such as sonic anemometers, these type
of turbulence related biases of cup anemometers can be seen. Figure 4.3 illustrates a comparison
between wind speed and wind speed standard deviation of a Thies cup and a sonic anemometer.
The diﬀerence in symbols represents the mean wind speed and standard deviation is related to the
diﬀerent way that each sensor measures the wind speed. Cup anemometers measure the length of
the wind vector, while sonic anemometers the magnitude of wind vector in the mean wind direction
[5].

standard deviation (right) (Source: Wind Power in forests Winds and eﬀects on loads,

based on these biases.
4.1.3.2
Other Errors
Here, several errors related to temperature dependency of the cup anemometers and other non-
instrument related errors, are introduced.
Friction Errors due to Temperature
Another error associated with wind speed measurements by cup anemometer is the diﬀerence
in the bearing friction of the anemometers in respect to temperature.
In general the friction
becomes higher as the temperature decreases and lower for high temperatures. This means that
cup anemometers slow down in low temperatures as friction in bearings increases, and overestimate
the wind speed when air temperature is very high [29].
Errors due to Icing
Icing is one of the most frequent sources of errors in cup anemometer wind speed measurements,
especially on CC sites. Cup anemometers can often freeze completely measuring no wind speed or
their cups can be partly frozen aﬀecting the aerodynamic performance of the instrument. Figure
4.4 shows the signal of 5 cup anemometers and a heated sonic anemometer during wintertime. For
a long period, the sonic anemometer measures higher wind speed than the cup anemometers at a
higher level, which is an indication of partly frozen cups. The eﬀect of icing can be also observed
from the ﬂat signals of several sensors. This kind of errors are analyzed in Chapter 5 and removed
from the data in Chapter 6.
Met Mast Tower and Boom Errors
Some wind speed measurement errors are associated with ﬂow distortion because of poorly installed
booms, or booms with dimensions diﬀerent than the recommended best practices. A signiﬁcant
source of error is also the mast shadow eﬀect in the cases where the predominant wind directions
has not been accounted when boom directions are decided. Finally, some damage in the sensor can
be caused by inexperienced installation personnel during mounting. Errors associated with met
mast shadow, are later accounted and removed. An example of the inﬂuence of the met mast on
wind speed measurements is seen in Figure 4.5. High wind speed and temperature data has been
directionally binned and the bias due to mast shadow eﬀect can be clearly seen.

Chapter 6
Data Filtering
A presentation of the ﬁltering criteria, tools and algorithms that are used in the current study can be
found in this section. Basic ﬁltering criteria, aiming to remove obviously erroneous data, and more
advanced conditions that remove most of the remaining outliers are introduced. Moreover, a de-
spiking method, manual screening, handbook ﬁltering criteria and Greenwich screening algorithm
that are discussed in 2, are now implemented.
6.1
Basic Filtering Criteria: Obviously Faulty Values
Here, some basic ﬁltering criteria that are used to mark the obvious shortcomings associated with
points where one or multiple sensors do not work properly, are presented. This includes cases when
a sensor is frozen due to icing, when the measured wind speed and standard deviation values are
outside a speciﬁc range, i.e lower than the calibration oﬀset, negative, etc. and other kinds of basic
errors. The performance of these conditions and the data recovery after their use for each of the
met masts are shown and compared to the results. In addition, results from phase-space de-spiking
method, and the eﬀect of using the recommended practices for data handling, described in 2, are
shown.
6.1.1
Criteria
The basic ﬁltering criteria, introduced in Table 6.1, aim to remove all the points where the sensors
are obviously malfunctioning as mentioned previously. Conditions 1 and 2 are used to correct the
points where a cup anemometer is completely out of order. In these cases the wind speed does not
deviate at all during the 10-min period and the data logger records a standard deviation of 0 ms−1.
During calm wind periods, the wind speed normally changes slightly and thus the standard devi-
ation is greater than 0. In addition, consecutive points of no change in mean wind speed indicate

7.2
Thies vs Sonic
Similarly to Wind Sensor-Thies comparisons, the wind speed measured by the available sonic sensor
is extrapolated to the height of Thies, using the shear estimated with the four Wind Sensor cups,
so that comparisons between Thies at 136.5 m and Sonic at 130.3 m can be made. As illustrated
in the top sub-ﬁgures of Figure 7.4, Thies and sonic show very good agreement when measuring
wind speed on high temperature periods. In low temperature periods cup anemometer faces icing
problems opposite to sonic anemometer, which being non-mechanical operates well in low temper-
atures. This is an expected observation, and it is associated with the way the two sensors measure
the wind speed. As discussed in Chapter 4. Cup anemometers measure the magnitude of wind
vector and then the mean over 10-min is calculated, while sonic anemometers measure the mag-
nitude of wind speed in the mean wind direction and then the 10-min mean is taken. However, in
low temperature periods the sonic anemometer measures much higher wind speed than the Thies,
due to the strong dependency of the cup anemometer on temperature. As discussed in Chapter
4, cup anemometers experience icing and friction issues in low temperatures, while the available
heated sonic can still operate properly.
The middle part of the ﬁgure shows again the above mentioned fact. Thies anemometers experi-
ences many problems due to low temperature and icing compared to sonic, and hence, the outliers
seen are associated with low temperatures. In addition, in the middle right ﬁgure, it is seen that
the outliers are mainly related to low standard deviation values. This can be explained due to
the lower ability of Thies to measure wind speed variation compared to sonic in low temperatures.
Moreover, bottom ﬁgures, illustrate wind speed standard deviation comparisons. Based on the dis-
cussion in Chapter 4, it is expected that Sonic anemometer is able to measure a higher frequency
wind variability compared to Thies. However, the comparison shows a diﬀerent result, with Thies
measuring higher standard deviation that the sonic. This could be due to the low accuracy of
the sonic anemometer used, because of its geometry. Another explanation could be the way that
the wind speed measured by the sonic is sampled, which in this case is unknown. Finally, as it is
displayed in bottom right ﬁgure, the deviations are found in low temperatures. Bearing friction
of cup anemometers becomes higher in low temperatures which slows them down and makes them
less responsive to wind speed changes.

8.1
Gap Filling using Mesoscale and Sonic Anemometer Data
The motivation of gap ﬁlling, as described previously, lays on the fact that in many CC sites wind
data, show a poor recovery after data handling. The remaining data is often less than a year
long, and thus gap ﬁlling is essential such that the seasonal wind viariations can be captured and
miscalculations of the wind conditions can be avoided. As already mentioned, for the ﬁnal AEP
calculation, gap ﬁlling is handled together with the long-term correction which is the common
practice in the industry. Here, for simpliﬁcation, gap ﬁlling is done only for the short-term meas-
urement period, using two mesoscale datasets and sonic anemometers when available, as presented
below.
ERA5, as described in Chapter 2, is the most commonly used mesoscale data-set for long-term cor-
rection in energy yield estimates. The most representative ERA5 data-sets, in terms of correlation
and distance between the met masts, are downloaded using WindPro and used for the analysis.

8.3
Sensitivity Analysis
Several AEP and wind speed comparisons using diﬀerent ﬁlters are carried out in this section.
Figure 8.4 illustrates the development of the merged top sensors mean wind speed using basic,
advanced, Greenwich ﬁlters and manual data handling for all 6 data-sets. It is seen that removing
the obviously faulty, using basic ﬁltering, an increase of approximately 2-3% on mean wind speed is
achieved for all met masts except met mast 6 where very few shortcomings are observed. Removing
the remaining shortcomings using advanced ﬁlter results to a higher rise of mean wind speed in all
cases, a fact that can be also seen when using Greenwich and manual data screening. The highest
increase in wind speed is found in met mast 3 which is aﬀected by icing to a great extend. In
general, for sites that are aﬀected by icing the use removing basic shortcomings is not suﬃcient.
For instance met mast 3 and 5 are the ones with the biggest icing problems and an increase of 6
and 3% in mean wind speed, between basic and advanced ﬁlter. Therefore, it is essential to use
some detailed screening, able to screen the majority of the erroneous data. On the other hand,
in sites with mild climate, the use of advanced ﬁlter shows a lower increase in the mean wind speed.
Looking now in Figure 8.5, the development of the AEP using the available ﬁlters can be found.
In most of the cases the change in AEP does not correspond the change in mean wind speed. The
AEP is calculated by multiplication of the Weibull distribution and the power curve and not using
the mean wind speed value. Therefore, an increase in mean wind speed does not necessarily mean
a shift of the distribution towards the high wind speed values. This is the reason why in some
cases where the increase in mean wind speed is higher, for instance in manual screening of met
mast 1 compared to basic ﬁlter, the increase in the AEP is lower than the corresponding to basic
ﬁlter value. It can be concluded that especially for CC sites removing basic shortcomings is not
enough, since a big increase in AEP is seen when using advanced ﬁlter, and thus a more detailed
screening is necessary.

Chapter 9
Conclusions, Practical
Recommendations and Future Work
9.1
Conclusions and Recommendations
The main motivation for this study was the lack of a transparent tool for ﬁltering out faulty cup
anemometer observations from extensive datasets used for wind resource assessment. Data ﬁltering
is however not a simple task, since faulty observations are caused by a wide variety of reasons.
These wind data imperfections, are not only related to cup anemometer response to turbulent wind
ﬁeld, the eﬀect of which can be minimized in modern design cup anemometers, but also on there
dependency on environmental, temperature and other non instrument related issues such as mast
shadow eﬀects, wind vane failures, etc. To precisely estimate the wind resource, as well as the po-
tential loads on the WTGs, of a wind energy site these wind data imperfections need to be resolved,
in a fast but accurate and correct way. Therefore, the main aim of the project was to come up
with some recommendations that can be useful for siting engineers when handling wind speed data
measured by cup anemometers. These recommendations deal with the sources of cup anemometer
biases, possible ways to correct them and the eﬀect of these corrections on wind statistics and the
AEP, and ﬁnally, statements related to the performance of the diﬀerent cup anemometers based on
comparisons among the available instruments. These practical recommendations are listed below:
1. For the investigated sites, eﬀects of temperature and mast shadow on wind speed were evident.
For cold climate, some of the temperature related errors, mainly associated with partly frozen
cup anemometers, are not evident to the eye and will not be detected unless sensor-to-
sensor comparison is made. Hence, all the possible sources of errors should be thoroughly
investigated, to correctly handle wind data, especially in cold climate sites.
2. As shown in Chapter 5 a big variety of shortcomings is found in all the industrial datasets
used for WRA. Typically, after data cleaning, data recovery in the studied datasets can
be decreased between 5 to 20 %, depending on the climatological conditions of the area.
73
==PAGE:96==
If not considered, these errors result to underestimation of the wind statistics, and thus
to misleading conclusions regarding to the potential AEP of the prospective wind farm, as
presented in Chapter 8. Thus, data handling is a very important part of WRA and it should
be treated carefully by siting engineers.
3. Detailed wind data handling is necessary for any dataset used for WRA, independently of
the climatological conditions in the area. Icing issues on cup anemometers and wind vanes
can be also seen in mild climates, while met mast ﬂow distortion can be seen in any mast-
based wind measurements, as already mentioned. This can be done using the basic ﬁltering
conditions and the start/stop and wind vane corrections of the advanced ﬁltering criteria, as
discussed in Chapter 6.
4. In cold climate, wind data ﬁltering should be done in a more detailed manner, as icing on
cup anemometers can aﬀect the aerodynamics and the friction of the instruments resulting
to miscalculations of wind speed. This, as shown in Chapter 6, can be done using a reference
anemometer from the same mast. This reference instrument should ideally be a heated sonic
anemometer, or a heated cup anemometer, as both sensors showed a very low temperature
dependency.
5. Combining basic and advanced ﬁlters results to a data handling of a good quality, especially
in cases where a third sensor can be used as reference, while these conditions are very easy and
fast to implement. Manual screening, on the contrary, is person dependent, time consuming
and not repeatable. Taking into account the development of wind energy and the increasing
number of potential wind farm sites that need to be analyzed, manual screening approach
becomes even more risky for accurate wind resource estimates. Therefore, it is suggested
that the recommended advanced ﬁltering criteria are preferred over manual screening for
wind data handling.
6. The diﬀerent types of cup anemometers showed small deviations in mean wind speed and
standard deviation data after removing most of the shortcomings. However, in a few cases the
diﬀerences could be considered as important. For instance, cup anemometers calculate higher
wind speed compared to sonic anemometers, possibly because of the diﬀerent way the wind
speed is measured by the instruments, as discussed in Chapters 4 and 7. In addition, Wind
Sensor seems responding better to wind ﬂuctuations compared to Thies, due to their lower
distance constant, and could be used in sites where turbulence is an issue. Therefore, when
designing a measurement campaign siting engineers should be aware of the performance of
each instrument depending on the climate conditions, temperature and turbulence intensity,
of the area, so that accurate wind resource estimates can be held.
9.2
Future Work
The study has showed that wind speed data can be eﬀectively ﬁltered using some rather simple
criteria. The extent to which the proposed criteria function should however, be further evaluated.
This can be done using for instance, Windographer, where the user can set the ﬁltering conditions.
In this manner, their eﬀectiveness can be tested, while more criteria able to remove additional
shortcomings can be identiﬁed.
74
==PAGE:97==
The project focused on wind data measured. exclusively by cup anemometers and a 2D heated sonic
anemometer. However, a big number of instruments contributes to the wind data measurements,
the data of which is often problematic and it needs some type of data handling. Mast-based wind
data measurements require more instruments apart from cup anemometers, such as wind vanes,
temperature and pressure sensor and sonic anemometers, similar criteria for automatically ﬁltering
out erroneous data measured by these sensors should be found.
Moreover, as remote sensing
technologies for wind data measurements, such as LiDARs and SoDARs have been developed and
an increasing number of WRA programs use them to supplement the met mast measurements,
similar criteria able to clean LiDAR and SoDAR data should be developed.
