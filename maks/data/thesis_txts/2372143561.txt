Summary
The purpose of this thesis has been to establish a link between advanced propulsion systems
on ships and route guidance. Advanced propulsion systems are characterized by their many
operation possibilities due to the their complex system construction, which leaves room for
optimization. Route guidance is deﬁned as tools advising navigators about the optimal route
with regard to fuel minimization and safety. The thesis is focusing on the DFDS ferry King
Seaways, as the ship is a good case for the purpose of the thesis, and as it was possible to con-
duct a visit on the ship.
The advanced propulsion system on King Seaways has been examined by investigating
all the relevant components related to propulsion. The most important components are the
four main engines and the two propellers. The main engines are described by an averaged
speciﬁc fuel oil consumption curve and an estimated lubrication oil consumption rate. The
Kappel propellers are modeled by use of the Wageningen B-series due to lack of propeller per-
formance data. Other components such as diesel generators, auxiliary systems, ﬁn stabilizers
and rudders have also been investigated in order to assess their importance for the propul-
sion.
To determine the thrust requirement of the ship all the relevant ship resistance contribu-
tors in ship propulsion theory have been considered. Both the still water resistance and the
combined air and wind resistance have been estimated by use of model test results. The wave
and swell added resistances are estimated with a Design Tool developed at DTU especially for
the purpose. The effect from shallow water has been evaluated by use of a newly developed
method based on CFD calculations. To describe added resistance due to activity by rudders
and ﬁn stabilizers simpliﬁed models have been outlined. Other resistance contributors like
trim variation and fouling are not considered in this study. The total ship resistance is con-
verted to a thrust requirement by use of model test data.
The informations gathered within the advanced propulsion system and the models out-
lined within ship propulsion theory have been implemented in a calculation program. The
calculation program is able to calculate the fuel and lubrication oil consumption on the con-
3
==PAGE:8==
sidered route between Amsterdam and Newcastle for two different strategies; a constant speed
strategy and a optimal power strategy, that respectively represents a single speed and dual
speed strategy. The program is furthermore providing route guidance for the two strategies,
stating number of engaged main engines, corresponding engine load and engagement times
for each route leg. The optimal power strategy has undergone two differently designed opti-
mization procedures to assess the inﬂuence of this on the results, but no clear tendency could
be observed in that respect.
The calculation program has been tested on two voyages and compared to fuel reports pro-
vided by the ship. The calculation results proved to be signiﬁcantly lower. This is explained by
disregarded maneuvering costs, limitations within the calculation program and model inac-
curacies due to necessary simpliﬁcations.
Several optimization parameters in the calculation program have also been varied in order
to study their inﬂuence on the fuel oil consumption and the implemented strategies. Param-
eters like 15 minutes earlier departure and lower propeller revolutions proved to reduce the
fuel oil consumption for both strategies. A change in maximum main engine loading from 80
% to 100 % was very beneﬁcial in some cases for especially the constant speed strategy and
without signiﬁcant inﬂuence in other cases. Lowering the propeller diameter introduced also
notable savings, but this is of course a unrealistic change in an operational situation. By hav-
ing 15 minutes earlier departure and a lowering of propeller rate of revolutions to 175.5 rpm,
the savings of respectively the constant speed and optimal power strategy were 3.8 % and 4.3
% compared to a base case representing the real operation conditions.
In the study it was not possible to prove a clear advantage of using the optimal power strat-
egy as expected. This is mainly due to theoretical conditions concerning the shapes of the
speed-power curves together with the fundamental difference between SFOC and FOC, and
limitations in the optimization procedure. However under the right conditions and settings
of optimization parameters, the optimal power strategy has demonstrated its potential by giv-
ing fuel savings. More accurate models of the advanced propulsion system and the resistance
contributors together with an improved optimization procedure for the optimal power strat-
egy are expected to give more accurate results and thereby a more reliable assessment of the
optimal power strategy.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Background of the Project
In the recent decades the fuel consumption of ships has been in focus globally due to the in-
creasing focus on climate changes around the world. The burning of fuel emits not only CO2,
that contributes to global warming, but also the so-called SOx, NOx and particles, which are
unhealthy for both nature and humans. The fuel consumption is also an important factor for
the shipowners, because the fuel costs are a very big part of the total costs of operating a con-
ventional merchant ship. Due to these factors any possible reduction of the fuel consumption
of ships is encouraged by shipowners, governments and international organizations.
Many companies within the maritime industry are engaged with the topic of reduction of
fuel consumption. One of them is the maritime division of the company Force Technology
located in Lyngby, Copenhagen, that among other things tests model scale ships in towing
tank and wind tunnels and provide route guidance software to ships in operation. This project
is made in collaboration with this division and is a part of a bigger project called Performance
Evaluation of Ships with Focus on Energy Efﬁciency and Safety. Hence this master’s thesis
contributes to a sub-project to this project called Route Guidance for Ships with Advanced
Propulsion Systems, which also is the reason for the title.
1.2
Route Guidance and Advanced Propulsion Systems
The ﬁrst challenge of the project is to make an appropriate interpretation of the title, which
contains the two terms; Route Guidance and Advanced Propulsion Systems.
1.2.1
Route Guidance
Route guidance for ships ranges from simple effective rules and guidelines known by experi-
enced seaman to advanced software tools, that calculates the optimal route for ships based
on theoretical models and weather information. This master’s thesis is meant to contribute
11
==PAGE:16==

mainly to the latter. However the project will make use of the former in order to combine
these different branches of route guidance, but also because many of these simple rules and
guidelines are very effective and useful. Two examples of computer tools for route guidance
are given below:
SeaPlanner
Together with DMI (Danish Meteorological Institute), Force Technology have developed the
decision support system called SeaPlanner. The main objective of SeaPlanner is to assist nav-
igators in voyage planning by providing estimates of the optimal route and speed, selection
of propulsion plant operation and safe weather routeing. By using SeaPlanner the navigators
are able to reduce the fuel consumption on the voyage and to avoid unacceptable weather
conditions. SeaPlanner is build on a propulsion model taking into account all parameters
affecting a vessel during a voyage including still water resistance, added resistance in waves,
shallow water, wind, propeller characteristics and engine conﬁguration. These parameters
are addressed by use of semi-empirically methods, model test results and sea trial data. More
information about SeaPlanner can be found in the user manual, [5].
GreenSteam
The danish company GreenSteam is providing a real-time decision support system to ships
with main purpose of reducing the fuel consumption. The GreenSteam system is collecting
real-time data about the ship’s operating conditions. This includes propeller pitch and rev-
olutions, rudder angle, shaft torque, speed log, fuel consumption, wind speed and draught
information. By use of machine learning and mathematical optimization the software gives
an estimate of the most optimal trim of the vessel. This is given on a screen on the bridge,
that also shows the real-time performance of the ship. According to their homepage typical
fuel savings ranges from 3 to 5 %. More information about GreenSteam can be found on their
homepage, [6].
1.2.2
Advanced Propulsion Systems
An advanced propulsion system can be many things, and therefore the deﬁnition of this term
has been very important in the preparation of the project formulation of this master’s thesis.
Basically an advanced propulsion system can be everything that is different from a conven-
tional propulsion system. Here a conventional propulsion system is considered as a machin-
ery conﬁguration with one main engine connected to a ﬁxed pitch propeller and three diesel
generators to produce electricity. A key characteristic of a conventional propulsion system is
that the system can be described by a single speciﬁc fuel oil consumption (SFOC) curve. This
machinery conﬁguration is found on many tankers and bulk carriers, and the possibilities of
varying the operation of this conﬁguration are limited in terms of reducing the fuel consump-
tion.
12
==PAGE:17==
1.2. ROUTE GUIDANCE AND ADVANCED PROPULSION SYSTEMS
An advanced propulsion system is on the other hand characterized by the many operation
alternatives and can be described by several SFOC curves depending on the conﬁguration.
Therefore possibilities for optimizing the operation of the system are more likely to exist. In
the following some examples of advanced propulsion systems are given in order to outline the
wide range of the term and the opportunities within the project.
Shaft Generator
The above mentioned conventional propulsion system can be turned into a advanced propul-
sion system by adding a shaft generator (PTO - Power Take Off) to the system. The purpose
of a shaft generator is to produce electricity by use of the main engine as a supplement to
the diesel generators. Large two-stroke main engines generally have a signiﬁcant lower SFOC
than diesel generators used for electricity production, and therefore a fuel reduction can be
obtained by producing the electricity through shaft generators.
In the sortiment of MAN Diesel & Turbo three types of shaft generators are found [1], which
can be mounted several ways in the engine room depending on type. The three different types
are:
- Gear Constant Ratio (GCR): This is the simplest and cheapest solution, that only works at
maximum two speciﬁed engine speeds depending on the chosen gear solution. The GCR
is therefore usually used with a CP-propeller installation. The electricity is produced by
an alternator connected to the main engine through a gear with constant gear ratio. The
total efﬁciency of the shaft generator is about 92 % divided between 2 % loss on the gear
and 6 % loss in the alternator.
- RENK Constant Frequency (RCF): The RENK planetary gear unit ensures that a constant
electrical frequency can be delivered for a engine speed between 70 % and 105 % of the
NCR (nominal continuous rating), but the principle is otherwise the same as the GCR.
Therefore the solution is suitable for a FP-propeller installation. The efﬁciency of the
shaft generator is between 88 % and 91 %.
- Constant Frequency Electrical (CFE) : This design comprises of rotor running with the
same revolutions as the propeller shaft and thereby generating electricity at this fre-
quency. The electricity is subsequently converted into the frequency of the system,
which means that this type of shaft generator has the greatest operational range of the
three types. The CFE shaft generator can either be mounted at the front-end of the en-
gine or on the propeller shaft. The latter is a very popular solution for large FP-propeller
ships, for example the Mærsk E-class ships build between 2006 - 2008, due to its mount-
ing simplicity and low maintenance costs. The CFE efﬁciency is between 89 % and 91
%.

Combined shaft generator/motor
Occasionally the shaft generator is combined with a shaft motor (PTI - Power Take In), where
electricity produced by auxiliary engines or others can be used for propulsion of the ship. The
electricity is simply converted into rotational energy through the shaft motor. The shaft motor
is installed if the ship needs some extra propulsive power in addition to the main engine(s) or
if the ship is equipped with energy saving systems that produce electricity from e.i. waste heat
(see below).
Waste Heat Recovery Systems
Waste Heat Recovery Systems (WHRS) have become very popular the recent decades due to
their ability to utilize the heat in the main engine exhaust gas. For the biggest systems it is
possible to recover up to 11 % of the energy in the fuel, that otherwise would be lost. MAN
Diesel & Turbo describes in their publications (see [2]) three types of WHRS:
- Power Turbine and Generator (PTG): The exhaust gas from the main engine(s) is led
through a power turbine, and the produced rotational energy is thereafter converted
to electricity in a generator. This system will recover 3-5 % of the total energy and is
recommended for ships with a main engine power below 15 MW.
- Steam Turbine and Generator (STG): Steam is generated in a boiler by use of the exhaust
gas from the main engine(s). The steam is afterwards led through a steam turbine con-
nected to an electricity producing generator. Around 5-8 % of the total energy can be
recovered, and the system is recommended for ships with main engine power below 25
MW.
- Steam Turbine, Power Turbine and Generator (ST-PT): This WHRS is a combination of
the PTG and STG systems and can recover 8-11 % of the total energy. The combined
system is recommended for ships with above 25 MW main engine power.
The produced electricity by WHRS is normally used to cover the electricity need of the
ship partly or fully. This can save a lot of fuel for the auxiliary engines, and it is sometimes
even possible to install one diesel generator less on the ship. The WHRS-electricity can also
be used for propulsion by utilizing a shaft motor as indicated above.
The aforementioned Mærsk E-class vessels are equipped with a ST-PT WHRS and two com-
bined shaft generators/motors. This makes the class a good example of an advanced propul-
sion system, because there exists many possibilities for operating the system and therefore
many different SFOC-curves.
Another variant of WHRS is waste heat utilization by use of an Organic Rankine Cycle unit,
that currently is under development by a collaboration between Mærsk, DTU MEK, Calnetix

Battery Packages
A new addition to the concept of advanced propulsion systems is the various battery ferries
put into service the recent years. The batteries are used for both propulsion and to cover the
electrical needs of the ships, and they are either charged by shore connections or engines on-
board. The former are found mainly on ferries operating on short crossings. A good example
on the latter is the Scandlines Ferry Hybrid Project, see [3], where one diesel generator has been
replaced by a battery package on several Scandlines vessels. This allows the remaining diesel
generators to run at their optimal load all the time and thereby avoiding loads with higher
SFOC. Thus, when the ship’s electricity demand is lower than the electricity production by
the diesel generators, the batteries are charged, and when the demand is higher than the pro-
duction, the stored energy in the batteries is used for propulsion or other needs. Scandlines
estimates that the fuel consumption can be reduced to one third per car per crossing on the
Gedser-Rostock route. One of the challenges with this system consist in assessing the optimal
point in time to charge and discharge the batteries in order to minimize the fuel consumption.
1.3
Purpose of the Project
The purpose of this master’s thesis is to investigate the advanced propulsion system on the
DFDS ferry King Seaways in relation to route guidance. The goal is to achieve a reduction in
fuel consumption by applying an operation strategy, where the engines are operating at their
optimal load all time instead of part loads in order to obtain an average route speed. In other
words the ship should operate at a Dual Speed Strategy (optimal power strategy) instead of a
single speed strategy (constant speed strategy). The speed in the former is then adjusted by
engagement or disengagement of main engines.
During the project a calculation program will be developed, that is able to estimate the fuel
oil consumption of the two different strategies on the considered route. It will be achieved by
utilizing a combination of knowledge of the advanced propulsion system and ship propulsion
theory. The program will be able to state all the relevant route guidance parameters that is
necessary to operate the ship by the two strategies. The fuel oil consumption results will be
bench-marked against real fuel consumption reports, and different parameters will be varied
in order to optimize the usage of the propulsion system.

The visit on King Seaways revealed two load limits of the main engines, which will be ac-

25 % minimum load limit
In the MaK manual for the main engines it is stated that continu-
ous operation below 25 % power load is not recommended. Operation below this limit results
in a bad fuel combustion and thereby increased soot deposits in the engines. According to the
manual this can be avoided by limited operation time below 25 % or a subsequent high load
operation to blow out the soot. However these solutions are not consistent with the scope of

this project, and therefore the 25 % minimum load limit has been chosen to be permanent.
80 % maximum load limit
According to the engine crew on King Seaways all ships belong-
ing to DFDS are restricted to operate below a maximum limit of 80 % load, which corresponds
to the CSR of these engines. This is also complied with on King Seaways. Exceptions are sit-
uations where extra power is needed such as maneuvering in port in strong wind. As will be
seen later this restriction agrees more or less with the minimum of the SFOC curve. The limit
is imposed due to maintenance reasons as the maintenance costs of the main engines will
increase signiﬁcantly for higher loads.
On King Seaways the limit is maintained by controlling the charge air pressure and the fuel
pump index. The charge air is the compressed air after the turbochargers, that is fed into the
combustion chambers. It is strived to keep this pressure at 1.5 bar, which corresponds to 80
% load. The fuel pump index is a measure for the useful stroke (measured in mm) of the fuel
pump plunger and is therefore a measure of the amount of injected fuel. At full load the fuel
index is 46mm, but for 80 % load the index is to be kept at approximately 37 mm. Practically
these parameters are controlled by the Electronic Remote Control System (see [9]), that has a
load point setter, where the load of the main engines can be controlled.
In the calculations this maximum limit will be respected, but it will also be investigated if
fuel consumption reductions can be achieved by increasing the maximum load limit.

2.1.3
Deviations of SFOC
In this study three different kinds of possible deviations of the SFOC have been identiﬁed:
Temperature and Pressure Deviation
The SFOC of marine combustion engines is varying with the ambient air temperature
and pressure and scavenge air coolant temperature. Generally the SFOC will rise with
increasing temperatures and decrease with higher pressure.
Deviation from Change in Lower Caloriﬁc Value
If the engines are running on fuel with a higher LCV than the fuel used during the shop
tests, the SFOC will be lowered, because the fuel contains more energy.
Deviation due to Variation of Propeller Revolutions
Variations of the propeller revolutions for the same engine load can occur in for instance
heavy seas, because the resistance on the ship is changing continuously. The engine
rate of revolutions will thereby also change, which will lead to a change in SFOC of the
engines.

2.1.4
Current Operation Philosophy
Generally a merchant vessel can be in three overall conditions: steaming at sea, maneuver-
ing and cargo handling condition. The main engines are not running during the latter as the
main engines are used for propulsion only. In the two ﬁrst conditions the main engines are
operating in two different ways:
In the maneuvering condition the main engines are governed by the captain, that controls
the engine revolutions by the engine telegraph. In this study this condition is disregarded,
because it only make up a small part of the total voyage. Furthermore it is very difﬁcult to
optimize the engine operation in this condition, because the focus of the captain is to put the
ship safely to berth, and therefore he uses the engines to the extent the situation requires.
The steaming at sea condition makes up the majority of the voyage and is therefore the
most contributing condition to the fuel consumption. The operation philosophy currently
practiced at King Seaways is simple and consequent, but rather effective. When steaming at
sea the engine telegraph is consequently put at maximum, and the engines are therefore al-
ways operating at their maximum load. As described in section 2.1.1 this corresponds to 80
% of the MCR. When comparing with ﬁgure 2.2 the engines are in that way always operating
close to the minimum SFOC.
This philosophy is complied with regardless the number of engaged main engines. Con-
sequently the propulsion system is only able to deliver three different powers on the voyages.
This results in three possible speeds depending on weather and ship condition among other
things. The navigating ofﬁcers have therefore limited options for fuel saving route planning
as it can be difﬁcult to know when to switch between the three powers/speeds. However the
ofﬁcers strives to use less engines on the shallow water of the ﬁrst half of the voyage between
IJM and NCL in order not to waste fuel. The present study will address this challenge and in-
vestigate the optimal engage/disengage times of the main engines.

2.1.5
Main Engine Operation Strategies
Based on the average interpolated SFOC curve, several main engine operation strategies have
been created in order to illustrate the consumption of the engines at different power demands.
Three relevant strategies have been investigated in terms of SFOC and FOC and is bench-
marked against equal load curves of 2, 3 and 4 main engines. The calculation procedures can
be seen in appendix B.1, B.2, B.3 and B.4, but are described shortly below:
Start at Minimum SFOC
The strategy starts out with running two ME’s on equal load. When reaching the point
of minimum SFOC for the given load limits, a third main engine is started. The load on
the third ME is then increased up to its minimum SFOC, while the load on the two ﬁrst
ME’s is kept at the minimum SFOC. The same procedure is undergone with the fourth
ME. When all four ME’s are on the minimum SFOC point, they will jointly run up to the
maximum load.
2 and 4 Main Engine
This strategy follows the same principle as the ﬁrst strategy, but here running with three
main engines is disregarded. The reason for this strategy is the special running condi-
tion for 3 ME’s explained later in section 2.6. Two ME’s are running up to the point of
minimum SFOC within the load limits, and here the two remaining ME’s are started.
They will now run up to the point of minimum SFOC, and thereafter all four ME’s will
run jointly up to their maximum load.
Equal Load
In this strategy the ME’s are running at equal load all the time. Two ME’s are running
equally up to the point, where it becomes more efﬁcient in terms of SFOC to switch to
three equally loaded ME’s. This switch means that the individual load at a engine will
decrease in order to fulﬁll the same power demand. The same procedure is followed for
the 4ME condition.
In ﬁgure 2.3 the SFOC is shown as a function of the total power demand for a maximum
load of 80 % (ﬁgure 2.3a) and 100 % (ﬁgure 2.3b). Notice that the SFOC is normalized by the
number of running engines, and the minimum load is set to 25 % cf. section 2.1.1. Generally it
is seen that running the main engines on equal load is most fuel efﬁcient. At a maximum load
of 100 % the equal loading strategy is always most efﬁcient. For 80 % maximum load (ﬁgure
2.3a) the other strategies are only beneﬁcial when main engines are started. However as ex-
plained in section 2.1.1 operation below 25 % load is undesirable, which creates useless zones
for the two ﬁrst strategies when engines are started (represented by upward oblique lines). In
that way the equal loading will also be the best strategy for 80 % max. load.

Combinator Curves
A combinator curve is the combination of rate of revolutions and pitch angles that controls
the performance of a CP-propeller. On King Seaways two combinator curves are in use de-
pending on the number of engaged main engines on each propeller shaft. Both combinator
curves uses 142 rpm’s for smaller engine loads and 181 rpm’s for higher engine loads, while
the pitch settings are different in order to take up the bigger torque. However it has not been
possible to utilize these combinator curves in combination with the Wageningen geometry,
because they were made for the Kappel propellers.

gen geometry, but they will still be based on 181 rpm’s in order to match the rate of revolu-

will give a required thrust coefﬁcient on the propellers. Together with the advance number

3.1.3
Wave and Swell Added Resistance by use of Design Tool
Added resistance due to wave and swell interaction is a complicated subject, and it requires ei-
ther model test data or extensive theoretical calculations to be addressed. In this study no test
data or lines drawings were available, which means that this resistance has to be estimated
another way. In the SeaTrend PAM manual [13] empirical methods are used to estimate wave
added resistance, but this is not considered to be suitable for this study. The PAM calculations
are based on old model tests on the extended Series 60 hull form with no bulb and a hull shape
very unlike King Seaways.
In the project it is therefore chosen to use the Design Tool for Estimating Wave Added Resis-
tance developed during two master theses projects by Christian S. Nielsen (2015) and Mads
A. Martinsen (2016) described in [14] and [15]. The design tool consists of a big database
containing wave added resistance transfer functions valid for ship types like tankers, con-
tainer ships and RoRo ferries. The transfer functions are calculated by use of strip theory and
asymptotic limit theory. The tool requires four ship input including length, breadth, draught
and block coefﬁcient, and three sea state input including signiﬁcant wave height, zero-up-
crossing period and wave direction. By use of these inputs the tool interpolates in its database
and provides the wave added resistance. Validation of the design tool shows generally good
agreement with both direct theoretical calculations and model test results.
In terms of this project several advantages by using this Design Tool has been identiﬁed.
First of all the limited amount of input makes the estimation of wave added resistance rela-
tively easy, which is beneﬁcial as this estimation is not the purpose of the study. Secondly the
Design Tool is available both in Excel and MatLab format, which makes it easy integrable with
the developed programs in this study.
The tool has however also some limitations that will affect the study. The tool is only capable
to provide wave added resistance from beam to head sea (90◦−180◦), which means that it is
not possible to consider sea states, where the waves direction relative to the ship comes from
0◦−90◦. Besides that, the tool can only be used for Froude numbers (based on ship length)
up to 0.25, which will delimit the use for this case to a maximum speed of 18.3 knots. Lastly
it should be remembered that the results are based on theoretical calculations on other ships,
which means that inaccuracies should be expected.
The Design Tool will in this study also be used to evaluate the added resistance due to
swells. The results in this connection will probably be inaccurate compared to the wave re-
sults, because the energy spectra used in the Design Tool are only valid for wind generated
waves. Therefore some difference must be expected. However the ﬁnal results will most likely
be more realistic by applying the design tool than neglecting the inﬂuence from swells, which

3.1.7
Other Resistance Contributors
In theory other types of resistance will contribute to the total resistance on the ship. However
they will not be included in this study, because they either are insigniﬁcant, too time consum-
ing to estimate or have equal signiﬁcance for each operating condition.
Fouling
Biological material like algae, barnacles and small animals will over time attach to the hull of
the ship. This will increase the resistance of the ship hull and in the end the fuel consumption.
Fouling will however not be considered in this study, because there exists no simple method
to estimate the inﬂuence of fouling accurately. Furthermore the inﬂuence will be equal for
each operating condition and will in that way only be a factor multiplied on the ﬁnal results.
Lastly King Seaways is operating in relatively cold water, which slow down the development
of fouling.
Trim
The trim of the vessel can inﬂuence the fuel consumption of the ship typically with a couple
of percent. King Seaways is usually trimming forward with a difference between the trim fore
and aft of 0.1m-0.2m. However due to the same reasons as for fouling the inﬂuence of trim
will not be considered, as it will be too time consuming for this study, and it is not expected to
inﬂuence the conclusions.

4.1
Overview of Calculation Program
4.1.1
Purpose
The purpose of the calculation program is to calculate the minimum fuel oil consumption on
the route for the implemented operation strategies. In this respect the program should be able
to ﬁnd the best usage of the advanced propulsion system on the speciﬁed route. Speciﬁcally
for this case it is the number of engaged main engines and how long time they should be
engaged. In that way the program should be able to ﬁnd the approximate optimum instants
to switch between machinery conﬁgurations depending on weather, depth, etc.
4.1.2
Implemented Strategies
Two different strategies are evaluated in the program:
Constant Speed
The speed on each route leg is equivalent to the average speed on the whole route. The
average speed is deﬁned as the speed found by dividing the route length with the re-
quired passage time. This strategy implies different engine power along the route to
compensate for changes in weather, etc.
Optimal Main Engine Load
In this strategy the main engines are forced always to operate on their optimal loads de-
ﬁned by the minimum of the SFOC curve. This means that only three different speeds
will be available on each route leg, because only three different machinery conﬁgura-
tions are available. For this strategy the calculation program needs to ﬁnd the number
of engaged main engines on each route leg, that will give the minimum fuel oil con-
sumption on the whole route and still ensures compliance with the time schedule.
Common for the two strategies is that all the route legs have the same length in order to
simplify the programming procedure.

4.2.1
Inputs of Program, (1)
Most of the inputs to the calculations are given in the ﬁrst part of the main script of the cal-
culation program in order to enhance the clarity of the program. However where inputs are
independent of the situation, they can be given in the individual scripts and functions. By ap-
plying this structure the program can be executed by only changing parameters in the initial
part of the program.
In the ﬁrst part of the input section all necessary data are loaded and the most impor-
tant constants are speciﬁed. The data includes main engine and propeller characteristics and
weather data, which are easier to have as data ﬁles instead of calculating them within the
program. Then all ship particulars including different lengths and coefﬁcients are speciﬁed
together with the constant main engine and propeller characteristics.
Subsequently, all the route characteristics are given and weather data are processed accord-

among others, while the weather data includes signiﬁcant wave/swell heights, zero-up-crossing
periods, headings and wind speed and direction. The weather characteristics are processed
by use of a dedicated function shown in appendix F.2, that requires the weather data and route
characteristics as inputs.
In the end some preliminary calculations are performed including the investigated speed
range and the corresponding wake and thrust deduction coefﬁcients, wake velocities and ad-
vance numbers. The minimum speed is set to 10 knots, because speeds below are not inter-
esting for this speciﬁc case. The maximum speeds is determined from the Froude number
limitation in the Design Tool of Fn = 0.250, which for this case corresponds to 18.3 knots.
This means that the calculated brake power curves will be extrapolated for higher speeds.

get the most reliable weather data. For each voyage the following parameters were noted for
approximately 16 waypoints along the route:

4.2.6
Optimization of Machinery Conﬁguration, (13)-(17)
Optimization Method 1
In order to ﬁnd the optimal machinery conﬁguration on each route leg all possible combina-
tions will be investigated in the program. As it is possible to use three conﬁgurations on each
leg, the total number of combinations is equal to 3 raised to the power of the number of route
legs. A combination matrix is created with all possible combinations in it, where the number
of rows and columns are equal to respectively the number of combinations and the number
of route legs (see example in appendix F.5). Each row thereby represents the applied machin-
ery conﬁgurations on the whole route. For each combination (matrix row) the total time and
fuel oil consumption can now be calculated by summing up the individual times and FOC’s
corresponding to the number of engaged main engines.

Optimization Method 2
Another combination matrix is also created, as method 1 has the limitation that changes
of machinery conﬁguration are geographically locked, because all route legs have the same
length. This combination matrix will not contain combinations of machinery conﬁgurations,
where changes between number of engaged main engines happens more than one time. On
the contrary, the route will be divided into many more route legs in order to ﬁnd the optimal
time instant to change machinery conﬁguration (see example in appendix F.6). The total time
and FOC for each combination are calculated the same way as for the ﬁrst combination ma-
trix.
For both methods the combinations with a total time outside a speciﬁed route duration
range will be discarded, and the combination with the minimum total FOC will be picked
out to be the result of the optimal power strategy. The lubrication oil consumption (LOC) is
now calculated for this machinery combination by use of the individual times and number of
engaged main engines on each route leg. Lastly the FOC and LOC costs are calculated and
summed in order to estimate the propulsion costs of the two strategies1.

6.1
Discussion of Results
6.1.1
Comments on Resistance Components
Some general remarks can be given about the size and variation of the considered resistance
components. This can help to identify possible inaccuracies in the calculation model and the
explanations hereof. The still water resistance make up the major part of the total resistance
as expected. This underlines the importance of using the most accurate still water resistance
curve with the associated challenges discussed in section 3.1.1.
The added resistances due to wind waves and swells are minor parts of the total resistance,
which can be explained by the fact that the wave and swell heights are relatively small for the
two considered voyages. Only shortly the waves and swells reach heights at about 1 meter.
The air and wind seems to cause considerable more resistance compared to waves and swells,
when the wind direction is against the ship. This can be ascribed to the large frontal area of
the ship.
Fin stabilizers are also seen to cause signiﬁcant resistance often in the range between
wave/swell and air/wind resistance, which underlines the importance of including the sta-
bilizers in the resistance assessment although the model is very simpliﬁed. Note that all cal-
culations are made with two ﬁn stabilizers as this is the most common operation case. On the
contrary the rudder resistance is at all speeds insigniﬁcant compared to all other resistance
components. This was not expected as the observed rudder corrections during the visit were
relative intensive. This suggests that the outlined model for the added rudder resistance can
be insufﬁcient. Lastly it is also observed that shallow water can cause a signiﬁcantly resis-
tance increase (up to 25 %) depending on water depth, which emphasizes the importance of
considering shallow water for the case of King Seaways.

6.1. DISCUSSION OF RESULTS
6.1.2
Deviation from Reported FOC’s
As mentioned in section 5.2.1 the calculated FOC’s are signiﬁcantly lower than the reported.
This can be explained by the following points:
- Propeller Characterization
The propellers used in the calculations deviates a lot from the actual propellers on King
Seaways, which is expected to have a certain inﬂuence on the calculated FOC’s. How-
ever as the calculated FOC’s are signiﬁcantly lower than the reported, this would im-
ply that the propellers in the calculation program are much more efﬁcient than the real
propellers. This is hardly correct as the Kappel-propellers are optimized for usage on
King Seaways. Besides that the pitch-to-diameter ratios of the calculations propellers
are found from the thrust requirement of the ship, which means that they run at the
required efﬁciency and should therefore not perform much different than the Kappel-
propellers. The inﬂuence of the propeller characterization will be noticeable, but the
total FOC difference must be explained together with the following points.
- Forecast based weather data
This introduces inaccuracies as the forecasts are not a picture of the actual conditions,
but does as the name suggests only make qualiﬁed estimations about future weather
conditions. However for a route guidance tool forecasts are the only available weather
data.
- Maneuvering Costs
The FOC values given in 2.3 also include the fuel used during maneuvering in the two
ports, which is not accounted for in the calculations. The ship is in maneuvering con-
dition for about one hour in total at each voyage, where the engine loads are varying
a lot and in average run at part loads. This results in higher SFOC in general, but fur-
thermore in transient enlargement of the SFOC due to accelerations etc. As a result the
FOC reports compared to the calculation results will be signiﬁcantly higher alone due to
maneuvering.
- Inﬂuence of Sea Current
Sea current is not accounted for in the calculations, but in reality current can change the
effective length of the voyage. This means that the ship should sail at different speeds
in reality and will therefore affect the FOC in the reports. In SeaPlanner the route length
is 260 nautical miles, while the reports (see table 2.3) states lengths between 270 and
283 nautical miles, which of course also includes distances in port. It is therefore also
likely that the current contributes to the higher FOC’s in the reports as the maneuvering
distances are not that long.

6.1. DISCUSSION OF RESULTS
- Wave and swell model
Even though that the design tool for estimating the wave and swell added resistance is
well validated in the reports [14] and [15], some uncertainty in the calculations of the
added resistance due to waves and swells should still be expected. This is due to the
complexity of the subject and the relative insufﬁciency of the used weather data.
6.1.3
Discussion of Optimization Results
It was expected that the use of optimization method 2 would improve the performance of the
optimal power strategy, because the change of machinery conﬁguration could be done more
accurately in the time frame. However as described in section 5.3 the opposite was actually
the case, which was not expected. Though this can be explained by the structure of the two
combination matrices, which is discussed further in section 6.2.4.
In that connection it was also observed in section 5.3, that none of the two considered
operation strategies proved to be superior to the other, even though it was expected that the
optimal power strategy would be the best. It rather seems to depend on the operation and
weather conditions, which strategy gives the minimum FOC. First of all it can be explained
by the fact that the optimal power strategy always will have a route duration equal or smaller
than the constant power strategy due to the constraint that the engines always will run at
optimal load. As the earlier departure will extend the route duration for both strategies, this
optimization parameter is not capable to change the difference between the two strategies
signiﬁcantly. Secondly the optimization matrices are suspected not to reveal the fully poten-
tial of the optimal power strategy, which will be discussed further in section 6.2.4.
The conceptual difference between FOC and SFOC should also be considered in this re-
gard, which is illustrated in ﬁgures 2.3 and 2.4. The optimal power strategy is deﬁned such that
the engine always will run at the minimum SFOC. However the corresponding FOC curves
(equal loading) are increasing steadily and will not have a minimum like this as shown in ﬁg-
ure 2.4, because the FOC is found as a product between the SFOC and the power. As a result
the loss when running at non-optimal loads is not that signiﬁcant when FOC is considered
compared to SFOC. This mean that the optimal power strategy may not be that advantageous
as expected.
The last hinder of the optimal power strategy to be permanently superior to the constant
speed strategy is the shape of the speed-power curves. The increasing gradient of the curves
means that equal sized increments in the exerted engine power will convert into smaller and
smaller speed increments, which can be interpreted as a high speed penalty. The phenomena
is inﬂuencing the dual speed (optimal power) strategy much more than the constant speed
strategy, because this strategy will always imply higher ship speeds at parts of the route. Due
77
==PAGE:82==
6.2. DISCUSSION OF METHODS
to the shape of the speed-power curve this will not be counter-balanced by the use of a lower
speed. The phenomena is regarded to be the main reason for that the dual speed strategy not
always is superior to a single speed strategy. On the other hand if the speed-power curves
were linear, the dual speed strategy would prove superior at any times.
Despite the above mentioned conditions the optimization cases and the results proved
that it is possible to minimize the fuel oil consumption by varying several parameters. Intro-
ducing an earlier departure and lowering the rate of propeller revolutions will for this ship
reduce the FOC. Concerning the latter it should however be remembered that the SFOC at
the same load will be slightly different for different engine speed (rpm’s) as explained in sec-
tion 2.1.3, which will be discussed further in section 6.2.1. Therefore it cannot undoubtedly
be concluded that 175.5 propeller rpm’s is better than 181.0 rpm’s, but the study suggests that
using lower rpm’s in the combinator curves most likely would be beneﬁcial.
Changing the maximum main engine load proved to be very advantageous in some cases,
but was without notable inﬂuence in other cases. This applies especially for the constant
speed strategy. The reason is that the load increase can result in situations, where ship speeds
can be achieved with two engines instead of three, and this results in a signiﬁcant decrease
in FOC rate. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.4, where the vertical ’jumps’ on the FOC curves are
much less notable for 100 % maximum engine load compared to 80 % load. However as noted
in section 2.1.1 a maximum main engine load of 100 % means a signiﬁcant increase in main
engine maintenance costs, which should be considered when changing this parameter.
Lastly it should also be noted that the lubrication oil consumption proved nearly always
to be lower for the optimal power strategy compared to the constant speed strategy (see e.g.
ﬁgures 5.8 and 5.9). The reason is that the constant speed strategy in average seems to have
more engaged main engines on a voyage, which accordingly to the deﬁnition in equation 2.2
gives higher LOC. This study therefore suggests that operating the engines by a optimal power
strategy will lower the consumption of lubrication oil. However it should be remembered that
the deﬁnition of the LOC rate was quite simpliﬁed in this study and implied some consider-
able assumptions. Further investigations should therefore be made of the LOC rate in order
to verify this conclusion.
6.2
Discussion of Methods
6.2.1
Main Engine Description
The main engine speciﬁc fuel oil consumption (SFOC) was in this study only described by
a single curve as described in section 2.1.2. However this is a rather simpliﬁed description
as several deviations of the SFOC exist as mentioned in section 2.1.3. The description of the
78
==PAGE:83==
6.2. DISCUSSION OF METHODS
SFOC could be made much more complete by using a so-called SFOC engine map, where the
SFOC is contour plotted as a function of engine rate of revolutions and a performance related
parameter such as the brake mean effective pressure or the torque. In that way it could be
possible to ﬁnd the SFOC dependency of engine rate of revolutions and not only load, which
would be relevant for changes of the propeller rate of revolutions in the combinator curve.
This engine map was however not obtainable for these engines, which is the reason for not
using it in this study.
The start-up of an engine also requires burning of fuel in order to warm up the engine. The
amount of fuel used for the warming up condition is neglected in this study as it is difﬁcult to
estimate according to the engineers at King Seaways. However ideally it should be included,
because it most likely contribute on a smaller scale to the FOC on the whole voyage. The more
changes of machinery conﬁguration on a voyage, the more fuel will be used for start-ups and
thereby increase the overall FOC.
Similarly, load changes increase the rate of FOC transiently, and this should ideally also be
considered in a complete main engine description. However as this is assumed to be just
as difﬁcult as the start-up consumption to estimate, it is also neglected in the calculations.
Besides that, it is expected to be relatively small compared to the start-up consumption and
especially to the FOC in steady engine condition.
6.2.2
Ship Resistance Estimation
The estimation of the total ship resistance is maybe the most inﬂuencing part on the FOC
calculation results. Therefore a big effort should also be made in order to estimate all the
resistance components as accurate as possible. In this study all relevant resistance contrib-
utors were implemented successfully in the calculations, but inaccuracies are expected to be
present for especially the still water resistance and the wave and swell added resistance. The
reason for the uncertainty of the still water resistance was explained in section 3.1.1. To avoid
this uncertainty new model tests could be performed to validate one of the resistance curves
shown in ﬁgure 3.1 or simply replace the used curve.
The wave and swell added resistances were estimated with the Design Tool developed in
[14] and [15] in order to simplify this part of the project. In section 6.2.5 in [15] the tool was
compared with strip theory for a 109 meter Ro-Pax ferry. Generally the design tool proved to
perform well, but however under-estimated the peak amplitude of the added resistance and
over-estimated at higher wave frequencies. A similar behavior must be expected for King Sea-
ways. Therefore it could improve inaccuracy of the wave and swell added resistances if these
were based directly on strip-theory. However this was not done in this study due to time limi-
tations and lack of the exact hull geometry of the ship.
The separation of the wave and swell added resistance is probably also source of some inac-
79
==PAGE:84==
6.2. DISCUSSION OF METHODS
curacy, because couplings between these are most likely to exist. In the separation these cou-
plings are neglected, which can result in both over- and under estimation of the total added
resistance from waves and swells.
6.2.3
Propeller Characterization
One of the reasons for the FOC calculations were off compared to the fuel reports was in
section 6.1.1 explained to be the propeller characterization. Even though King Seaways is
equipped with Kappel propellers, it was chosen to model the propeller by use of the Wagenin-
gen B-series. This decision was mainly a consequence of the lack of data of the Kappel pro-
pellers. The results would undoubtedly be more accurate if Kappel propeller characteristics
were used due to the fundamental differences between these and the Wageningen propellers.
However this would require much more work and help from the propeller designer, because
the lack of data means that the propeller characteristics should be obtained by software pro-
grams developed and owned by MAN. Within the time limitation of this project, the chosen
solution is therefore considered to be adequate. However as the propeller characterization
also applies for both strategies, the relative difference between these will not be inﬂuenced
signiﬁcantly.
6.2.4
Optimization Procedure
As explained in section 4.2.6 the minimization of the fuel oil consumption is done by use of
two matrices, which rows represent different voyages and the number of engaged main en-
gines on each route leg are given in the columns. The row within an acceptable time window
and with the smallest FOC is in this study regarded as the optimal combination of engaged
main engines. This minimization procedure has the advantages that it is very clear for under-
standing and also rather easy to implement in the calculation program, which was needed for
this study in order to delimit the time usage of implementation.
However this matrix method also restricts the solution frame substantially, when only these
two combination matrices are considered. For the ﬁrst matrix the number of route legs cannot
be higher than approximately 8 as the main engine engagement times would bee too short.
As the waypoints between route legs are ﬁxed geographically, this means that the route is dis-
cretized quiet roughly and the found combination of main engine conﬁgurations is often not
optimal.
The usage of the second matrix breaks partly with this problem, but however introduces the
disadvantage that machinery conﬁguration only can be changed on time with the present
design of the combination matrix. Thereby combinations with more than one change are
not considered, which will delimit the analysis signiﬁcantly. However the route can now be
divided into much smaller legs, so the optimal time for changing conﬁguration can be calcu-
lated much more accurately.
80
==PAGE:85==
6.3. DISCUSSION OF CONCEPT
Based on these considerations it can be argued that the results of the study could have
been improved by applying a more comprehensive optimization procedure, that would have
the ability to combine the principles behind the two combination matrices. This could have
been achieved by for example expanding the second combination matrix with combinations
allowing two or more conﬁgurations changes. A better optimization procedure would also
include a possibility to vary the individual length of the legs. This would remove the geo-
graphical restriction of the optimization and result in a much bigger solution frame.
In this context a totally different approach could also been taken by applying optimization
algorithms. Here the objective would be speciﬁed to be a minimization of the overall FOC by
varying the parameters investigated in chapter 5 in a much more extended solution frame.
Optimization algorithms like gradient-descent or particle swarm optimization could be ap-
plied in this connection.
6.2.5
Other Calculation Program Limitations
Besides the optimization procedure the calculation program also contains other limitations,
that affect the ﬁnal FOC results. First of all it must be repeated that sea current is not included
in the calculations. This means that the effective length and average speed of the route will
be different in reality, which will inﬂuence the FOC positively or negatively. However the in-
ﬂuence will be the same for both operation strategies, and it is therefore not expected to have
high importance for the comparison of these.
Another limitation of the program is the inability for the optimal power strategy to obtain
the exact speciﬁed route duration time. As a consequence of this the FOC for this strategy will
often be higher than necessary, because the ship is forced to arrive before schedule. Thereby
the optimal speed strategy will perform more poor than necessary. This is partly dealt with by
allowing earlier departure as investigated in section 5.2.5. However another solution could be
to enforce that the main engines were allowed to run with variable loading in one of the route
legs, so the ship speed could be adjusted in order to get the speciﬁed arrival time. In that way
the two strategies could be compared on the same terms.
6.3
Discussion of Concept
Generally the concept of using a Dual Speed strategy (optimal power strategy) instead of a
single speed strategy has proven to be beneﬁcial under the right circumstances for the case of
King Seaways. As seen from table 5.8 the advantage by using the strategy is determined by the
weather conditions of the different voyages and the variation of optimization related param-
eters. To achieve reductions of fuel oil consumption by use of the Dual Speed strategy, these
should therefore be considered beforehand in order to be able to plan the optimal voyage.
81
==PAGE:86==
6.3. DISCUSSION OF CONCEPT
As discussed in section 6.1.3 the greatest hinders of the dual speed (optimal power) strat-
egy to be superior to a single speed strategy are the fundamental difference between SFOC
and FOC together with the shape of the speed-power curves. Optimizing with the basis of
minimum SFOC as done in the optimal power strategy is maybe not the most optimal ap-
proach due to the shape of the speed-power curves and thereby the speed-FOC curves. A
strategy with focus on minimizing the FOC instead of the SFOC is therefore assumed to be
able to improve the Dual Speed Strategy concept further.
The concept development must also be said to depend much of the considered ship. Ap-
plying the Dual Speed Strategy for King Seaways was largely a question of ﬁnding the correct
utilization of the four main engines. The procedure can be transferred more or less unchanged
to many other ferries, because the machinery conﬁguration of King Seaways is quite common
for this ship type. However for other ship types the procedure would probably be different due
to the difference in machinery conﬁguration. This is illustrated by considering the Mærsk E-
class ships mentioned in the introduction, which are equipped with only one large two-stroke
engine, four auxiliary engines and a Waste Heat Recovery System. As the advanced propul-
sion systems of King Seaways and the E-class are so different, it will therefore also require a
different approach in order to establish the link between the advanced propulsion system and
route guidance.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion and Recommendations
7.1
Conclusion
In the project the advanced propulsion system of King Seaways was investigated and all the
relevant components were identiﬁed and described. In that connection a visit on King Sea-
ways were conducted with purpose of familiarizing with the operation of the ship and obtain
information, that is not available from shore. In order to estimate the thrust requirement of
the ship all the relevant resistance contributors were examined, and the calculation methods
were established.
A calculation program was successfully produced by use of the knowledge about the ad-
vanced propulsion system and resistance contributors. The two considered strategies, Opti-
mal Power Strategy and Constant Speed Strategy, representing respectively a dual speed and
a single speed approach, were implemented in the program. Route guidance information
and the corresponding fuel and lubrication oil consumption and propulsion costs are given
as outputs of the program. Two optimization procedures were tested for the optimal power
strategy, but none proved to be superior to the other.
The calculation program was used to calculate the fuel oil consumption on two of the voy-
ages the author participated in. The calculated fuel oil consumption turned out to be signiﬁ-
cant smaller than the fuel reports obtained from the ship. This is explained by the disregarded
maneuvering costs and current inﬂuence, the propeller characterization and the simpliﬁed
resistance model and weather data.
For the two considered voyages attempts to minimize the fuel oil consumption were car-
ried out by combining parameters such as earlier departure, maximum main engine load,
propeller rate of revolutions and propeller diameter. A realistic earlier departure on 15 min-
utes proved to be very beneﬁcial for the minimization of fuel oil consumption, and the crew
83
==PAGE:88==
7.1. CONCLUSION
should deﬁnitely aim at departing before if cargo loading allows it. Increasing the maximum
main engine load from 80 % to 100 % proved to be very beneﬁcial in some cases for especially
the constant speed strategy and to be without inﬂuence in other cases. The decisive differ-
ence here was if the load increase make it possible to achieve the speed with one engine less
engaged. Lastly decreases in propeller rate of revolutions and especially diameter proved to
be very advantageous in a fuel reduction perspective. While the latter is unrealistic in an op-
erational situation, the former can quite easily be achieved by lowering the main engine rate
of revolutions. Here it should be noted that the SFOC is assumed unchanged for different rate
of revolutions.
Fuel reductions compared to a base case on respectively 3.8 % and 4.3 % were achieved
by having 15 minutes earlier departure and 175.5 propeller rpm’s instead of 181 rpm’s. Fur-
thermore it seems that the use of the optimal power strategy is lowering the lubrication oil
consumption in most cases, because the average number of engaged main engines is smaller
for this strategy.
The results showed generally no clear tendency of which operation strategy is superior to
the other. The performance of the strategies is depending much on weather and operation
conditions together with the variation of the aforementioned operational parameters. The
optimal power strategy were expected to perform best at all times, because the engines al-
ways are operating at their optimal load. But due to especially the shape of the speed-power
curves and the insufﬁcient optimization procedure, the optimal power strategy showed not
always to be the best performing operational strategy. However for the most realistic combi-
nations of operational parameters, the optimal power strategy proved to be signiﬁcant better
than the constant speed strategy. In that way the dual speed approach demonstrated its po-
tential, and the project succeeded with creating a link between advanced propulsion system
and route guidance.

7.2. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE WORK
7.2
Recommendations for Future Work
Making a calculation program for use in route guidance like the one presented in this thesis
requires coverage of many different subjects. The two main subjects here were the advanced
propulsion system of King Seaways and general ship propulsion theory. Separately these are
in themselves large topics, and it requires much time to investigate them properly. It has
therefore also been necessary to conﬁne the study and make simpliﬁcations in the calculation
program. This means that there is room for future work and investigations, which will be
summarized in the following within three categories. Some of the points have already been
indicated in the discussion chapter.
Advanced Propulsion System
- The main engine consumption is only described by a single SFOC curve applying for all
engines as described in section 2.1.2. To make a more detailed description the individual
SFOC curve could be used. Furthermore in order to address the SFOC deviation due to
variation of rpm’s an engine consumption diagram could be used.
- The ﬁn stabilizers were modeled as simple wing proﬁles ﬁxed in the same angular posi-
tion at the ship’s side. This is a very simpliﬁed model, because the angle of inclination
are varying continuously and the geometry was assumed. Further investigations should
be made within this as the ﬁn stabilizers were observed to cause a notable amount of
resistance.
- As indicated before the propeller characterization in the study is realistic, but do not
represent the actual conditions due to the fundamental differences between Wagenin-
gen and Kappel propellers. The accuracy of the fuel oil consumption calculations could
be improved by utilizing correct data of the propellers.
Ship Resistance
- A validation or change of the still water resistance curve would remove much of the un-
certainty in the estimation of the total ship resistance c.f. the discussion in section 3.1.1.
- The wave and swell added resistance estimated with the Design Tool is considered to be
reliable for this purpose, but the accuracy of the model could be improved by basing the
wave and swell added resistance estimation on direct strip theory calculations or model
test results.
- Other resistance contributors such as the variation of the trim and fouling on the hull
and propeller could make the resistance estimation more detailed and accurate. The
inﬂuence of trim could be assessed by use of model tests.
85
==PAGE:90==
7.2. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE WORK
Calculation Program
- The calculation program does not include the effects of sea current. As explained before
sea current will affect the effective route distance and thereby the ship speed, which
in the end will inﬂuence the fuel oil consumption. The accuracy of the program will
therefore increase with an implementation of the effect of sea current.
- The weather data from SeaPlanner was provided to the calculation program quiet roughly
discretized in 16 way points along the route. Some of the ﬁgures in the study showed that
this inﬂuences the accuracy of the results. By increasing the resolution of the weather
data, a higher accuracy in the results could be achieved.
- The effectiveness of the optimization methods (combination matrices) were discussed
in section 6.2.4, and there is clearly room for more comprehensive procedures within
this ﬁeld. The study could beneﬁt from a research within optimization theory and algo-
rithms with purpose of a later implementation of a optimization algorithm in the calcu-
lation program.
As a last remark this study has showed that it is possible to combine knowledge of ad-
vanced propulsion systems with the concept of route guidance. An immediate continuation
hereof would be to implement knowledge about these systems in weather routeing software
programs, which would give these many more possibilities to plan the optimal voyage by min-
imizing the fuel oil consumption as much as possible.

function PowerCorr = ShallowWaterCorr(Lpp,B,T,Cb,Cw,V,h)
% Data
rvisc = 0.75;
g = 9.81;
% Viscous resistance correction
fv = 1.0 + 0.57*(T/h)^1.79;
% Wave resistance correction
fw = 1.0;
% Resistance increasse without sinkage
f0 = rvisc*fv + (1-rvisc)*fw;
% Additional sinkage estimation
disp = Lpp*B*T*Cb; % Displacement
Fnh = V/sqrt(g*h); % Froude number based on depth
Fnhd = V/sqrt(0.3*g*Lpp); % Froude number length
dsink = max(1.46*disp/Lpp^3*(Fnh^2/sqrt(1-Fnh^2) ...
                    -Fnhd^2/sqrt(1-Fnhd^2)),0)*Lpp;
% Additional displacement
Aw = Cw*Lpp*B; % Waterplane area
deltadisp = dsink*Aw/disp;
% Resistance increase factor due to sinkage
fsink = (1+deltadisp)^(2/3);
% Total power increase due to shallow water
ft = f0*fsink;
PowerCorr = ft;
end

 dependent on

% assumed not to be influenced by the rpm's and pitch of the
 propellers.
