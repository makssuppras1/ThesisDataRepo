Abstract
After several fires in open car parks, where many cars have been engulfed by flames, there is an increasing
interest in understanding and modeling possible fire spread among cars. In general, car park fires do not
cause fatalities, but they often have a great cost in relation to damaged cars and structural repair. It is
therefore very relevant to improve their design and safety levels.
Statistical research and test from the European commission of steel structures states that in an open car
park at most 3-4 vehicles can be expected to be on fire at the same time. Besides, the tests showed that
the fire spread to the adjacent cars after 12 minutes. However, recent and past cases of fires in open and
semi-open car-parks clearly showed the shortcoming of this assumption.
This study focuses on hydrogen fuel cell vehicle (HFV) and is aimed at investigating the fire spread from
a hydrogen-fueled car to adjacent cars and comparing with regular cars.
The investigation is carried out by means of computational fluid dynamics (CFD) investigations on numer-
ical models implemented in Fire Dynamic Simulator (FDS). Of the investigated outputs, the most relevant
are validated on simplified models against analytic solutions.
A model of an open car park in Denmark has been used as case-study and a simplified model of a car is
used. Three scenarios is set up to investigate the fire spread.
Scenario 1 is a reference scenario with one car in a simple room. The fire spread within one car is inves-
tigated and compared to the reference curve from the European commission. Two different methods is
developed; one with ignition of car parts controlled by timers, and one with ignition of car parts controlled
by ignition temperature of the materials.
Scenario 2 is investigation of the fire spread from car to car. The method with ignition controlled by timers
is used for the ignition car and the method with ignition controlled by ignition temperature is used for
the adjacent car. The time to ignition of the adjacent car is of interest. The simulation is carried out for a
distance between the cars of 60cm and 80cm.
Scenario 3 is the same set up as for scenario 2 except a hydrogen release is implemented. The hydrogen
is modelled as a pool fire underneath the car and is ignited after 227 seconds in reference to full scale test
results. The simulation is carried out for a distance between the cars of 60cm and 80cm.
The results of scenario 2 shows that the fire spreads after 423 seconds for the distance 60cm between the
cars and after 464 seconds for 80cm. For scenario 3 the fire spreads after 262 seconds for 60cm and after
264 seconds for 80cm. The results show that the distance between the cars doesn’t have a big impact on

the fire spread and none in scenario 3.
The fire spread in scenario 3 is depending on the hydrogen release as the adjoining car is ignited by the
heat from the hydrogen flame. The scenario is set up to be worst case and if the hydrogen is released later
than 500 seconds it will not have an impact on ignition time of the adjoining car. The hydrogen release
is modelled simplified and doesn’t reach the HRR that is used as input value. The modelling of hydrogen
release needs to be further studied to be able to compare the simulations with real cases of fire.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Fire spread in open car parks is a very hot topic at the moment. After several fires in open car parks where
between 10 and 1400 cars have been damaged there is a focus on the topic.
In general no fatalities are recorded in car park fires but often it has a great cost in relation to damaged
cars and structures and it is therefore relevant to look at safety [25].
There are three different definitions of car parks.
Closed car park is a closed off building with no natural ventilation. Often closed car parks are under-
ground.
Open car park is a natural ventilated car park. It can be a parking lot or a garage, but there is no walls.
Semi-open car park is mix of the others. It has openings in the walls to get natural ventilation. Often
the walls are 1 meter high and the rest is open. Semi-open car parks are often multistory buildings.
Statistical research and test from the European commission of steel structures (2002) [20] states that in an
open car park at most 3-4 vehicles are on fire at the same time. Besides, the test shows that the fire spread
to the adjacent cars after 12 minutes. These values have been used as a design criteria. The statistics is
based on 78 reports from 1995-1997 from the Fire brigade of Paris. The conclusion of the study is that no
protection of the steel structure is needed in these cases.
Today, 20 years later, there are reported a number of large car park fires like the multistory car park fire
in Liverpool on December 31st, 2017. Here 1400 cars were destroyed by the fire and part of the building
collapsed [21]. In 2002 a car park near Schiphol airport burned and 30 cars were on fire at the same time
[14].
These events indicate that the design of an open car park without any additional fire protection such as
insulation of the structure and sprinklers is not good enough to limit the structural damage and spread of
a fire.
The requirements for ventilation and sprinkler systems are different from country to country. In Denmark
a separate ventilation system is required for closed and semi-open car parks above 150 m2 to remove toxic
gases from the cars. A sprinkler system is required for closed and semi-open car parks above 2000 m2 [42].
In many of the severe car park fires, these systems are either not present, badly designed or dysfunctional
[21].
One of the reasons for the nowadays more severe fires could be the materials in the cars. In newer cars
there are more combustible materials like plastic and synthetic materials compared to older cars. This will

have an influence on the fuel load. A study by the European commission [19] have shown that cars from
the 90’s compared to cars from the 80’s have twice as much fuel load. If this development has continued
it could indicate that the design criteria is outdated.
With the increased focus on global warming it means changes in fuel for cars. Two types of vehicles with
a clean power source are on the market right now; battery electrical vehicles and fuel cell electric vehi-
cles. At the moment battery electric vehicles are most common but companies like Hyundai, Toyota, and
Honda are focusing a lot on developing fuel cell electric cars [46, 1]. Fuel cell electric cars run on hydrogen
produced by electricity. The only leftover product from the car is water vapour.
Hydrogen-fueled vehicles (HFV) are on the market worldwide. In the end of 2016 there were 68 HFV in
Denmark and 10 hydrogen refuelling stations. The goal is that there will be more than 500 HFV in 2018 in
Denmark [5, 1].
Some investigations on firespread have been made on gasoline vehicles in open car park fires but only a few
on hydrogen-fueled vehicles [32]. Hydrogen has a low ignition energy and has a high flame temperature.
It is therefore important to investigate whether this will create a more severe fire in an open car park in
relation to the structural elements and the firespread. HFV will therefore be the focus in this report.
1.2
Object and aim of the project
The aim of the project is to investigate the fire spread from a hydrogen-fueled car to an adjacent car by
looking at the parking distance between the cars and comparing with regular cars. The temperature and
energy will be examined in relation to the structural elements.
• Is there a difference in comparison to fire spread from a gasoline car?
• Is there a need for specific rules for parking hydrogen-fueled cars?
• Can a Computational fluid dynamic (CFD) model simulate a real case of fire for HFV?
• What modelling issues are to be faced in working with CFD?
1.3
Methodology
The investigation is carried out with numerical assessment with the use of CFD. The program Fire Dynamic
Simulator 6 (FDS) will be used. A model of an open car park in Denmark will be used for the simulation.
The results from the simulation will be validated with analytic solutions for a simplified case.

Statistical studies on car fires
Not many statistical studies on car parks exist and especially not on hydrogen fueled vehicles since they
are not that widespread yet.
A statistical study from 1995-1997 by the European Commission of steel structures [20] investigates fires
in car parks in France. The statistics are based on 327 reports on fire in underground car parks in 1997 and
78 reports on fire in upper-ground open car parks in 1995-1997 from the Fire Brigade of Paris. The study
showed that 97.7% of the fires included 4 or less cars in underground car parks and 100% of the fires in
open car parks included 3 or less cars.
In 32% of the cases in underground car parks, the fire was not due to cars, this was 30% for open car parks.
The study concludes that fires in underground car parks are more severe than open car parks and they
should therefore still be treated differently.
In New Zealand a statistical analysis was conducted in 2007 looking at car park fires from 1995-2003. There
were on average 3370 vehicle fires per year in the period. 12 of these were in parking buildings and 3% of
the incidents involved multiple vehicles. The study does not differ underground car parks and open car
parks. The four leading reasons for fire were arson, electrical fault, mechanical failure, and carelessness.

Literature on state of research on car fires
Study on fire spread from adjoining vehicles to a HFV
A study on fire spread from a HFV to adjoining vehicles was made in 2014 by Yohsuke Tamura et al.[32].
Two tests were conducted; one with a fire in a HFV with an adjacent gasoline vehicle in an outdoor parking
lot, and one with three adjoining HFV assuming transportation in a carrier ship. Both tests showed that
it was the flames from the interior and exterior materials that lead to fire spread and not the hydrogen
flames because of the short duration of the hydrogen burning. For test 1 the thermal pressure relief device
(TPRD) was activated after 30min, and the fire spread to car 2 after 60min. For test 2 it takes 109 min
before the fire spreads to an adjoining vehicle and after 117min TPRD is activated for the ignition car. For
these tests the fire was started in the rear right tire.
Study on hydrogen gas cylinders for evaluating the safety of HFV
A study on hydrogen gas cylinders was made in 2004 by Yohsuke Tamura et al. [31]. They both did tests on
hydrogen gas cylinders alone and on hydrogen gas tanks in vehicles. The test on hydrogen gas cylinders
showed that for upward venting the flame height could be up to 10 meters and the diameter up to 2.6
meters. For downward venting the flame height could be up to 2.7 meters and the diameter could be up
to 6 meters. The TPRD was activated after around 60 seconds and the burning of the hydrogen started
when released and ended after 1 min.
The vehicle fire test was made for two ignition places. The first ignition of the vehicle was in the ashtray
inside the car and was done for both upward and downward venting. For both upward and downward
venting the TPRD was activated after 15-17 min. The flame height and width were very similar to the ones
in the test of the hydrogen gas cylinders alone.
The second vehicle test was ignition by a gasoline pool fire underneath the trunk room where the hydro-
gen gas cylinder is placed. In this case the TPRD was activated after 3 min and 47 seconds after ignition.
The hydrogen was released inside the trunk room and created a fire ball more than 10 meters in diameter.
Study on jet flame from hydrogen car in road tunnels
In 2007 a CFD study on jet flame from hydrogen cars in road tunnels was published by Vladimir Molkov
and Jean-Bernard Saffers [48]. It examined the effect of ventilation in road tunnels if there was a leak in
the hydrogen tank. Because hydrogen has a low ignition energy a leak therefore has a high probability of
ignition. The lower flammability level for hydrogen is 4% and the upper level is 75%. The heat release rate
of the fire depends on the release rate of the hydrogen. Two scenarios were examined; one with a 6 MW
hydrogen fire and one with a 30 MW hydrogen fire. The study showed that the 6 MW flame length was
short and the fire was controlled by the ventilation. For the 30 MW the flame length reached the ceiling
and spread under the ceiling for 45 meters. The high temperature of hydrogen could therefore cause dam-
age to the structure. The fire was controlled by the ventilation since the supply of oxygen was limited in

2.3.1
How hydrogen fuel cell works
It is an electrochemical reaction that happens in a fuel cell. A fuel cell consists of three parts, as seen on
fig. 2.6; an anode (red on figure), a cathode (blue on figure), and a membrane/electrolyte (yellow on figure)
in between the two. To produce enough energy the fuel cells are stacked. [47]
The fuel cell work as described in the following [47]:
1. Hydrogen gas (brown blobs) from a tank is forced into the fuel cell at the anode
2. Oxygen (turquoise blobs) is forced into the fuel cell at the cathode
3. The anode is made of platinum working as a catalyst. It breaks up the hydrogen atoms into hydrogen
ions and electrons (small black blobs)
4. The hydrogen ions/protons are attracted to the cathode and they pass through the membrane. It is
made of a special polymer that only protons can travel through
5. The electrons meanwhile pass through an outer circle
6. The electrons power the electric motor as they move to the cathode
7. At the cathode the hydrogen ions and electrons recombine with the oxygen and produce water (H2O)
and heat
8. The water is led out through an exhaust pipe as water vapour or steam

2.4
CFD in fire engineering
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is the analysis of fluid flow, heat transfer, and chemical reactions
by computer simulation. It is based on the conservation equations for mass, energy, and momentum [45].
CFD techniques were first used in the late 1970’s and from there it has developed considerably. In the
beginning CFD modelling was used to investigate the cause of previous fires and now it is used to predict
fire scenarios to make buildings safer and to prevent fires.
To verify results from CFD models comparison with physical tests and analytic models is to be performed
[49].
There are different turbulence models for modelling the smoke development and spread. The models can
in general be divided into those who use the Reynolds Average Navier Stokes simulations (RANS) models,
and those who use the Large Eddy Simulations (LES) and at last the Direct Numerical simulation (DNS)
model [3].
The RANS models are based on the Navier-Stokes equations. These equations have a closure problem
meaning they have 2 more variables than equations and can therefore not be solved without some approx-
imations. A turbulence sub-model is introduced with 2 equations and no new variables. The most often
used sub-model is the k-ϵ model. It works by dividing the turbulence into an average value and a varying
value. [3, 8, 10].
The LES model solves for the large scale eddys while a subgrid scale model describes the small scale ed-
dys, the ones smaller than the grid. The eddys are the motions of flow. To get a precise result using this
method a fine mesh is needed. The most common used subgrid scale model for solving the small eddys is
the Smagorinsky model [10, 3].
The DNS model is also based on the Navier-Stokes equations and solves the equations for all turbulent
flows without averaging or approximation other than numerical discretizations. Using the DNS model it
takes a lot of computational power and simple geometry [8, 10].
There are many different CFD programs and some of them are made for fire modelling. FDS (NIST),
OpenFOAM (ESI), SmartFire (FSEG), SOFIE (CU), Jasmine (FRS), and Solvent (for tunnel fires). FDS is the
most commonly used program by fire engineers and the municipalities in the world mostly because it is
open source [3].

2.5
FDS - models and limitations
Fire dynamic simulator is used in this project. The program is developed by NIST in 2000. To visualize the
results simulated in FDS, Smokeview can be used. It renders the model in 3D with animations of the fire
and smoke and is very good to visualize the results from FDS.
2.5.1
Features of FDS
Hydrodynamic model
The turbulence model is the Smagorinsky form of Large Eddy simulation (LES). The program uses a form
of the navier stokes equations that works for low speed and thermally driven flow for smoke and heat
transfer from fires.
Combustion model
FDS uses a single step chemical reaction via a two-parameter mixture fraction model. Two components
of the mixture fraction model is computed by default. The first is mass fraction of unburned fuel and the
second is the mass fraction of burned fuel [8]. A two step chemical reaction can also be used. The first step
is oxidation of fuel to carbon monoxide and the second step is oxidation of carbon monoxide to carbon
dioxide [8].
Radiant transport
The radiative heat transfer is modelled with the solution of the radiation transport equation for a gray
gas. The equation is solved using the Finite Volume Method (FVM).
2.5.2
Geometry and mesh
The program is based on a rectangular mesh therefore the geometry needs to be modelled in rectangular
obstructions [8]. The obstructions will be moved by the program so they match the size of the cells.
The best way to find the appropriate mesh size for a model is to start with a coarse mesh and then refine
until no change is seen in the results. For buoyant plumes a formula has been derived to describe how
well the flow field is resolved, D∗/δx where D∗is found by eq. (1) [8].

2.5.3
Limitations of FDS
There are some limitations to the program that are stated below.
Low speed flow assumption
FDS can only solve for low speed flows for smoke and heat transfer. This means than the program can not
simulate explosions and jet flames [8].
Rectangular geometry
The rectangular grid is a limitation for some types of geometry. There are functions in the program that
help with this like SAWTOOTH but they are not expected to give good results. With a fine mesh a curved
geometry can be modelled almost precise but it will increase the calculation time [8].
Fire growth and spread
The program was initially made for industrial fires. It is very reliable when the heat release rate is specified,
the temperature and flow velocities can be predicted with 10-20% difference from experimental studies. If
the heat release rate is then predicted rather than specified the uncertainty is increased. This is due to the
fact that the input values on the fuel materials are unknown or imprecise and that the combustion and
radiation is more complex in reality [8].
Combustion
The combustion model is based on the fact that the reaction of the fuel and oxygen is infinitely fast, no
matter the temperature. This is a good assumption for a well-ventilated fire. But if the fire is in a under-
ventilated room FDS can not model the reaction precisely [8].
Radiation
The radiation model also has some limitations. Because of the simplified models for combustion the
chemical combustion of the smokey gas can effect the absorption coefficient and the emission of thermal
radiation. Also the radiation transport can be effected if the fire is a growing fire. It can lead to a non-
uniform radiant energy, which is called the ”Ray effect”. It can be solved by using more solid angels than
the default of 100 solid angels [8].

Case study
Three scenarios are investigated
• Scenario 1 is a reference scenario with one car in a simple room. The fire starts in the back right tire
and spreads to the whole the car
• Scenario 2 is two cars simulating a fire starting in car one and investigating the fire spread to the

• Scenario 3 is the same as scenario 2 but car 1 is a hydrogen fuel cell vehicle in a semi-open car park.
The fire spread to car 2 is investigated
Scenario 2 and 3 are compared to find the differences between regular cars and hydrogen cars in relation
to fire spread and temperatures.

3.8
Discussion
Scenario 1
Method 2 in scenario 1 is a good reproduction of the experimental curve for method 1. The method is good
for the ignition car since it is controlled all the way trough by time controllers. The disadvantages of the
method is that it is manually controlled and the fire spread within the car is therefore not natural. Also it
can not be affected by the heat flux of the second car.
The best result would be if the experimental curve could be reproduced by method 3. The method lets the
car burn by ignition temperature as it would in reality. To get the HRR curve as in method 1 the right HRR
curves for the materials must be used and all materials that contribute to the fire must be incorporated.
In this thesis that was not achieved. The HRR curve has the same peak value as the experimental curve
but the fire spread happens too fast. The ignition temperatures were used for the materials in the car but
the metal on the outside of the car was not taken into account. The metal protects the interior of the
car and would slow down the fire spread, since it will take longer before the interior reaches its ignition
temperature.
The choices of HRR curves for the materials were very simplified. The data on these values was very lim-
ited and a simplification was therefore made. For the tires a study on fires in automotive tires [13] was
used. The study showed that there was a big difference in where the tire was ignited in relation to the
HRR curve. The results of the simulation are therefore highly depending on the assumptions that have
been made.
Using method 3 it limits the firespread investigation to only one car. Since the heat release rate curve for
method 3 is too fast, the next car would ignite much faster than if method 1 or 2 was used. It is valid to
use method 3 for car 2 since the ignition part of the HRR curve is very close to the experimental in method
1 and the ignition time of the car would therefore be unaltered.
Scenario 2
The fire propagation happens after 7 minutes for a distance of 60cm between the cars. This is 5 min
shorter than the tests from the European commission [19]. The ignition of car 2 is depending on the
ignition temperature of the materials. The back part of car 2 ignites first since it has the lowest ignition
temperature.
The total fuel load for the burning of the two cars is 7048.77 kJ. The fuel load for the burning of the cars
individually added together is 9235 kJ which could indicate that not all parts of the second car is ignited.
In Smokeview it can be seen that the side of the car away from the fire on the second car is not ignited.
This is due to the heat not reaching the ignition temperature on those surfaces. The difference in the fire
development, compared to scenario 1, is the ignition place. In scenario 1 the car is ignited in the tire on all

sides. This way the fire spreads to both the side and the bottom of the car, and to the rest of the car. In
scenario 2 car 2 is ignited on the side of the coachwork and spreads to the whole side and top of the car,
but not the bottom and the other side. The development of the fire also means that no more cars would
ignite if placed next to car 2. This is in line with the statement that method 3 can not be used for other
than the time to ignition.
For the distance of 80cm the time to ignition is increased. That is expected since it takes longer before the
heat reaches the car. The difference is only 40 seconds though and therefore having wider parking spaces
will not make a great difference on the fire spread.
Scenario 3
The firespread in scenario 3 is happening faster than for scenario 2. It is very depending on the time of the
hydrogen release. For this scenario the worst case was investigated to see the effect a hydrogen release
could have. The adjoining car is ignited after 262 sec for a distance of 60cm and is ignited by the heat,
created by the hydrogen flame. For the distance of 80cm the adjoining car is ignited after 264 sec. That
shows that the bigger distance does not affect the fire spread in this case. The heat from the hydrogen is
high enough to reach the second car even though the distance is 20cm longer. As for scenario 2 the total
fuel load is lower than it should. In Smokeview it can be seen that the same part of the car as in scenario
2 is not burning.
The faster the hydrogen is released the faster the fire will spread to the adjoining car. As studies have
showed the time to activation is depending on where in the car it is ignited and this is therefore also a
factor of interest for further studies of the hydrogen part.
The hydrogen is modelled as a burner as big as the car in this scenario. This is a very simplified model of
the hydrogen release and should be examined further for making the best model of it. The input value for
the HRR for the hydrogen burner is set to 64500 kW. The HRR curve for scenario 3 shows that the hydro-
gen gives a peak of 12000 kW. To work correctly the HRR should have been even higher. FDS though have
limitations on creating a jet flame as hydrogen creates in reality. The lower heat release rate can also be
caused by lack of oxygen when the hydrogen is released. It creates a pressure that prevents new air from
coming in.
The room temperature reaches a maximum of 770◦C in both scenarios. This means that the hydrogen does
not affect the temperature more than the burning of the cars already do. This could also indicate that it will
not affect the building structure further. This is primarily due to the short duration of the hydrogen flame.

4. CONCLUSION
4
Conclusion
Three scenarios have been set up to investigate fire spread between cars. Scenario 1 is a reference study
to investigate the fire spread within one car with different methods. Scenario 2 is a study of fire spread
between two regular cars and scenario 3 is a study of fire spread between a HFV and a regular car.
Is there a difference in comparison to fire spread from a normal car?
The fire spread from a HFV to an adjacent car have been investigated. The results from CFD modelling
show that fire spread from a HFV is faster than for regular cars if the hydrogen is released within 500
seconds from the start of the fire. The result show that the firespread for regular cars happens after 423
seconds. For the investigated case the hydrogen is released after 227 seconds and the fire spreads to the
adjacent car after 262 seconds. This is the worst case scenario and if the hydrogen is released later than
423 sec after the fire starts the effect of the hydrogen will be different since the adjacent car is already on
fire.
Is there a need for specific rules for parking hydrogen-fueled cars?
It is too early to establish whether specific rules are needed for HFV. Though the study shows that if the
hydrogen is released early in the fire duration it can have an impact on the fire spread. The study also
showed that the gas temperature was not affected further by the hydrogen. It reaches the same maximum
temperature as for the burning of the cars. The study also shows that changing the distance between the
cars does not have an impact on the fire spread when the hydrogen is released.
Can a CFD model simulate a real case of fire for HFV?
This study shows that modelling the release of hydrogen from a HFV is complicated. The hydrogen will
be released trough an outlet with a small diameter and when ignited create a jet flame. This cannot be
modelled in FDS and a simplification is therefore needed. In this study the hydrogen was modelled as a
pool fire as big as the car assigned with a HRR curve [26] with a maximum of 64500 kW. The total HRR
curve from FDS shows that HRR peaks when the hydrogen is released to 12000 kW. Compared to the input
value it should be much higher. The modelling of a hydrogen release should therefore be studied further
to be able to use it to compare with real cases of fire.
What modelling issues are to be faced in working with CFD?
There are some limitations in working with FDS that have an influence on the validity of the results. For
the simulations to be useful the model has to be validated against full scale tests and analytic models.
Beside that there are some other limitations in the set up in FDS which are:
• the geometry can only be in rectangular forms which limits the detailing of the model.
• the openings to the rest of the car park is modelled as open but in reality the FDS model is only a
part of the car park which means that the floor and ceiling will limit the flow outside the modelled

area.
• the material choices and parameters for HRR are very simplified in the model and limits the total
fuel load compared to tests and experiments.
• the hydrogen can not be modelled correctly since FDS cannot model a jet flame.
4.1
Further studies
The results of this thesis leads to some further studies than can be made.
For further studies the temperature in the structural elements would be of interest both for scenario 2 and
3. Especially the hydrogen could have an effect on the structural elements.
The design of the car park can be further investigated. Variation of the height of the car park would be an
interesting aspect of the fire spread and the temperature of the concrete elements. Beside that a better
radiation model should be used to simulate the radiation from the ceiling to the car. The structure of the
ceiling with TT beams have an influence on the heat distribution and the radiation.
In this study the fire spread was limited to two cars. For further investigation a study of fire spread to
several cars should be carried out. For that study to be possible and reliable a better model must be made
for the second car. In this thesis method 3 was used but it did not fit the experimental curve and was too
fast. A better model for method 3 is therefore needed. This can be done by optimizing the materials in the
car model. The fuel load is too low and the fire spread within the car is too fast.
In the thesis the gasoline car was considered empty. For further studies the effects of gasoline in the tank
would be of interest for both scenarios as it would increase the fuel load.
