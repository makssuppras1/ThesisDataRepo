Abstract
Process mining techniques provide powerful insights into how business processes be­
have in reality, but most existing tools assume centralized event logs and retrospective
analysis. The Distributed Architecture for Process Mining (DAPM) was conceived to sup­
port streaming process mining across autonomous organizations, yet previous theses on
security, streaming execution, and data ingestion resulted in isolated prototypes that could
not jointly deliver an operational, user­accessible platform.
This thesis integrates and extends these subsystems into a single cohesive architecture
for secure, decentralized streaming process mining. Conceptually, it introduces a unified
backend per organization that combines role­based access control, inter­organizational
trust establishment, dynamic management of processing elements, and a fully integrated
pipeline lifecycle. Technically, the work realizes this architecture as a modular mono­
lith deployed per organization, with cryptographic, token­based authentication between
peers, subscription­ and voucher­based access control, runtime upload and catalogue ex­
change of processing elements, and ingestion and anonymization embedded directly into
source components. On top of this backend, a graphical user interface is provided as the
single entry point for managing organizations and projects, designing cross­organizational
streaming pipelines, and monitoring their execution without manual API interaction.
The resulting artefact is evaluated through a realistic hospital–research scenario, a requirements­
based assessment, and a usability study. The scenario shows that anonymized patient
event streams can be processed across two organizations while preserving local control
over data and algorithms. The requirements evaluation indicates that all functional and
non­functional requirements derived from the motivating problem are met or strongly met.
A usability survey with eleven participants reports consistently high scores for learnabil­
ity, efficiency, feedback, control, and satisfaction, suggesting that the interface effectively
abstracts the complexity of distributed streaming process mining. Together, these results
demonstrate that the integrated DAPM platform addresses the identified gaps and pro­
vides a robust foundation for future research and practical adoption.

Introduction
Modern companies and organizations rely heavily on well defined structured processes to
meet both their operational and strategic objectives. These processes consist of a series
of coordinated activities designed to achieve specific outcomes. With increasing digital­
ization, organizations now have the capability to record the execution of these processes
in the form of event logs. Each event typically captures information such as the activ­
ity performed, the timestamp, the responsible entity (such as a user), and other relevant
metadata. These logged events reflect the actual behavior of the business processes in
execution and form a rich source of data that can be systematically analyzed.
This is where process mining plays a vital role. Process mining is a research and tech­
nology field that bridges the gap between traditional model­based process analysis and
data­driven techniques. By analyzing event logs, process mining allows organizations to
gain insight into how their processes operate in reality, highlighting deviations, inefficien­
cies, and improvement opportunities. Specifically, process mining encompasses three
core techniques: discovery, where a process model is automatically generated based
on event data; conformance checking, which assesses how well the actual execution
of processes aligns with predefined models or rules; and enhancement, which involves
improving existing models by incorporating additional data insights. By applying these
techniques, organizations can monitor performance, detect inefficiencies or deviations,
and continuously refine their processes for better results.
Traditional process mining techniques rely on stored event logs, which are typically col­
lected and exported from information systems after process activities have been executed.
While the process itself may still be ongoing, the analysis is inherently retrospective as
it depends on data that has already been persisted. This lag makes it difficult to detect
and respond to process deviations or performance issues in real time. For example, in
a hospital setting, if a patient treatment deviates from clinical protocols, such as a de­
lay in administering medication or a missed diagnostic test, traditional process mining
would only reveal this issue after the fact, potentially too late to take corrective action.
This limitation highlights the need for real­time, streaming­based approaches that allow
organizations to monitor, analyze, and intervene in process execution as it unfolds.
Streaming process mining addresses the limitations of traditional approaches by enabling
real­time analysis of ongoing process executions as events arrive continuously. Instead of
waiting for complete logs to be stored and exported, streaming techniques operate directly
on live event streams, allowing systems to detect deviations, bottlenecks, or compliance
violations the moment they occur. This real­time capability enables organizations to react
as issues emerge, such as when delays, unexpected workflow patterns, or emerging
bottlenecks appear during process execution. By enabling proactive rather than reactive
interventions, streaming process mining enhances operational agility and supports time­
sensitive decision­making.
However, implementing streaming process mining in practice introduces significant tech­
nical and organizational challenges. Handling continuous data flows across distributed
systems requires scalable, fault­tolerant architectures and secure collaboration mecha­
nisms. In many cases, the data, algorithms, and processing resources involved in pro­
cess mining are owned by different organizations, making integration and cooperation
complex. To address these challenges, Burattin et al. [1] proposed the Distributed Archi­

tecture for Process Mining (DAPM), which introduced the concept of distributed process
mining pipelines. DAPM provides a framework for defining, executing, and managing
pipelines that can span multiple organizations while allowing each participant to maintain
control over its data and algorithms. This architecture plays a crucial role in enabling col­
laborative and privacy­preserving process analysis, especially in scenarios where data
and computational responsibilities must be shared across organizational boundaries.
While previous research theses developed individual subsystems covering aspects such
as streaming data ingestion and anonymization Sarker and Zaman [2], distributed execu­
tion Becke and Klaassen [3], and security and access control Dirani [4], these components
have remained isolated and limited in scope. This thesis focuses on integrating and en­
hancing these backend subsystems into a unified architecture that supports the creation
and execution of distributed streaming pipelines. The integration ensures that all com­
ponents operate cohesively as part of a complete end­to­end backend system, while the
enhancements improve reliability, and interoperability among the modules, resulting in a
more stable and extensible foundation for distributed streaming process mining.
To realize this goal, the thesis introduces an integrated backend architecture that unifies
the previously separate subsystems of DAPM: security, execution, and data ingestion and
anonymization into a single modular monolith per organization. Each organization oper­
ates its own self­contained backend, maintaining autonomy over its data and algorithms
while interacting securely with peers through standardized APIs. This architectural con­
solidation eliminates the fragmentation of earlier prototypes, simplifies deployment, and
provides a stable foundation for continuous collaboration and scalability. By integrating all
critical services within a cohesive backend, the platform achieves consistency in lifecycle
management, fault isolation, and extensibility across organizational boundaries.
Building on this integrated backend, the thesis introduces a graphical user interface (GUI)
that provides a central point of access to the system. The GUI allows users to visually
construct, configure, and execute streaming pipelines, as well as manage organizations,
users, and projects, without requiring programming expertise. It interacts directly with
the integrated backend to expose its functionalities in an accessible and user­friendly
manner. Through this combination of backend integration, system enhancement, and
user­centered interface design, the thesis advances DAPM toward practical deployment,
making distributed streaming process mining both technically cohesive and accessible to
a broader range of users.
This thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 reviews the state of the art in process

the core DAPM concepts used throughout the thesis. Chapter 4 presents the motivating
scenario and derives the system requirements. Chapter 5 outlines the Design Science

presents the technical implementation of the integrated system, including backend, se­
curity, ingestion, and the graphical interface. Chapter 8 contains the results and eval­
uation: a scenario­based demonstration, requirements­based evaluation, and usability
study. Chapter 9 discusses the findings in relation to the research questions and outlines
limitations and future work. Finally, Chapter 10 concludes the work done in this thesis.

2
State of the Art
This section provides background on process mining and the Distributed Architecture for
Process Mining (DAPM), outlining key concepts, capabilities, and existing solutions. It
also reviews the state of the art and highlights current limitations that motivate the research
problem addressed in this thesis.
2.1
Process Mining Tools and Frameworks
Process mining has evolved through numerous tools and frameworks, ranging from aca­
demic prototypes to commercial products. These solutions enable the analysis of event
logs to discover, monitor, and improve business processes. However, most existing tools
assume a centralized environment where data, algorithms, and execution resources be­
long to a single organization. This limits their applicability to distributed or collaborative
settings
2.1.1
Traditional (Offline) Tools
• Prom [5] is the foundational open­source framework for process mining, offering an
extensive ecosystem of plug­ins for discovery and conformance checking. Despite
its versatility, it assumes centralized log access and static execution, with no support
for streaming or distributed workflows.
• RapidProM [6] extends ProM within the RapidMiner platform, allowing visual work­
flow construction, but still processes static event logs and lacks cross­organizational
capabilities.
• PM4Py [7] and PM4KNIME [8] make process mining accessible to developers and
analysts through Python and KNIME integration. While flexible, both operate in
batch mode and rely on complete event logs, offering no native support for streaming
or secure collaboration.
2.1.2
Streaming and Cloud­Oriented Tools
• The Beamline [9] Framework introduced one of the first approaches for streaming
process mining. Built on Apache Flink and PyBeamline, it allows real­time analysis
and simulation of event streams. However, Beamline assumes a trusted, single­
domain environment and does not address access control, privacy, or collaboration
across organizations.
• ClowdFlows [10] provides a browser­based platform for building and executing data­
mining workflows collaboratively. While it supports streaming data and integration
with external services, it is not tailored to process mining and lacks mechanisms for
secure, selective sharing of algorithms or data.
Across existing tools, most process mining frameworks remain centralized and depend
on full visibility of event data within a single organization. Even recent streaming­oriented
frameworks lack built­in mechanisms for access control, trust management, or distributed
execution across independent entities. These limitations restrict their applicability to col­
laborative or privacy­sensitive scenarios. The Distributed Architecture for Process Mining
(DAPM) was proposed to address these challenges, enabling secure and decentralized
streaming pipelines.

2.2
Early DAPM and Static Process Mining
The Process Dry Lab (PDL) [11] introduced a peer­to­peer architecture to share process
mining algorithms without exposing source code. It did not support real­time or streaming
data. However, it laid the groundwork for the Distributed Architecture for Process Mining
(DAPM), where cross­organizational mining pipelines could be executed securely and
collaboratively.
The main DAPM architecture was formalized in “Process Mining Pipelines with Controlled
Sharing of Data and Algorithms” by Burattin et al. [1]. It addressed key challenges in
enabling process mining pipelines across organizational boundaries — notably balancing
algorithm reuse, data privacy, and pipeline orchestration. While the design handled static
logs well, it did not yet support real­time or streaming data.
The thesis Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: Designing and Validating Process
Mining Pipelines by Møller and Barak [12] focused on improving usability in building DAPM
pipelines. It introduced a graphical user interface that allows users to construct pipelines
visually through drag­and­drop interactions. A key feature was a validation framework
based on a type system that ensured only compatible components could be connected.
This significantly lowered the technical barrier for non­programmers. However, the work
was limited to static event logs and did not support streaming process mining scenarios.
The thesis A Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: Deploying and Executing Pro­
cess Mining Pipelines Doce and Tounsi [13] expanded on the earlier pipeline design by
implementing the actual orchestration and execution of distributed pipelines. It adopted
a peer­to­peer architecture where each organization (or peer) retained control over its
data and computation. Each peer followed a microservice design, and the execution of
pipelines was broken down into asynchronous steps like data transmission and algorithm
invocation. However, this implementation remained tailored to static data and lacked
streaming capabilities.
Complementing this, Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: User Management and
Security by Xiao [14] introduced a security and access control framework. It provided
fine­grained permissions for pipeline elements and ensured organizations could securely
collaborate without exposing sensitive assets. The system was integrated into the DAPM
prototypes developed in the earlier theses, supporting cross­organizational control over
data usage and algorithm access. Like the others, it was designed with static process
data in mind.
Despite the valuable contributions of these earlier efforts, the DAPM framework remains
incomplete, with critical limitations, lack of loose coupling due to centralized orchestration,
and most notably the absence of support for streaming data
2.3
Toward Streaming: Recent DAPM Theses
Building on the early, static­log foundations of DAPM, a new generation of theses was
developed in parallel to extend the framework toward real­time, streaming process min­
ing and fully decentralized execution. Conducted as a coordinated effort, these three
works collectively aimed to transform DAPM from a conceptual architecture into a func­
tioning platform capable of operating over continuous event streams across independent
organizations.
• Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: Security and Access Control Across
Organizations by Dirani [4]: This thesis contributed to DAPM by introducing a trust­
aware security framework that defines how independent organizations can collab­

It proposed a
role­based access control (RBAC) model to manage permissions within and across
organizations, ensuring that only authorized users could access or share process

protocol to establish secure communication between peers. The work also intro­

• Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: Conceptualization and Performance
Analysis of an Online Data Streaming Pipeline by Becke and Klaassen [3]: This the­

the conceptual and technical foundations for pipelines that operate on continuous
event data. It formalized the structure of a distributed streaming pipeline, defining
stages, processing elements, and communication patterns between organizations.
The work implemented a fully distributed architecture capable of validating, building,
and executing pipelines across peers in real time. It also introduced mechanisms for
pipeline validation and performance benchmarking, demonstrating that the system
could sustain high throughput while maintaining correctness. Through these contri­
butions, the thesis established the architectural and performance basis for enabling
continuous, real­time process analysis within DAPM’s distributed framework.
• Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: Ingesting and Filtering Online Stream­
ing Data by Sarker and Zaman [2]: This thesis focused on extending DAPM’s data
ingestion layer to support real­time event streaming and privacy­aware preprocess­
ing. It introduced a configurable ingestion service capable of connecting to live data
sources, such as Wikipedia edits, and transforming incoming events into formats
suitable for process mining. Using JSON­based configuration files, users could de­
fine filtering rules and mapping schemas without modifying code, enabling flexible
and dynamic data preparation. The thesis also implemented anonymization mech­
anisms through pseudonymization and suppression to protect sensitive information
when data crossed organizational boundaries.
2.4
Identified Limitations and Open Challenges
Although the three most recent DAPM theses expanded the framework toward streaming,
security, and data ingestion, they remained isolated prototypes that could not jointly realize
the intended goal of a unified, secure, and configurable platform for distributed streaming
process mining.
The thesis on Security and Access Control Across Organizations by Dirani [4] introduced
a role­based access model and inter­organizational trust mechanisms, but it was never
applied to actual streaming process mining scenarios. Despite its title, the system oper­
ated independently of any streaming or execution layer, and its access control features
were limited to managing users and partner organizations. In addition, it lacked a graphi­
cal interface to configure or monitor these elements, which made it impractical for broader
integration into DAPM.
The thesis on Conceptualization and Performance Analysis of an Online Data Streaming
Pipeline by Becke and Klaassen [3] implemented the first operational streaming back­

end, but it lacked integration with the security and ingestion subsystems. The pipelines
were static and could not be validated, built, or managed through a consistent lifecycle,
and there was no user interface to control or observe pipeline execution. As a result, it
demonstrated technical feasibility but did not enable practical use in multi­organizational
environments.
The thesis on Ingesting and Filtering Online Streaming Data by Sarker and Zaman [2]
provided real­time data ingestion and anonymization mechanisms, but it operated inde­
pendently from the other DAPM components. Its configuration relied on manual file edit­
ing, it offered only basic visualization of results, and it had no integration with the security

Across all three works, the lack of architectural integration and user interface support pre­
vented DAPM from functioning as a cohesive and usable system. Addressing these gaps
by combining and extending the previous subsystems into a unified end­to­end architec­
ture forms the main objective of this thesis.
2.5
Research Questions
The previous section identified several open challenges within the Distributed Architecture
for Process Mining (DAPM). Although earlier theses successfully introduced components
for security, streaming pipeline execution, and real­time data ingestion, these subsys­
tems remain isolated and cannot jointly support end­to­end distributed streaming process
mining. To address these gaps, this thesis investigates how these components can be
integrated into a single cohesive architecture and how the overall system can be made
more accessible through a graphical user interface.
The goal of this thesis is therefore twofold: first, to integrate and enhance the three pre­
viously separate DAPM subsystems into a single, conceptually and technically unified
architecture; and second, to introduce a graphical user interface that represents the sin­
gle entry point to this architecture, providing users with an intuitive environment to design,
configure, and execute streaming pipelines.
This leads to the following research questions:
• RQ1. Can the three previously developed DAPM subsystems be integrated into a
single cohesive platform that supports streaming process mining end to end?
• RQ2. Does the integrated architecture preserve DAPM’s decentralized model and
allow organizations to maintain control over their data and processing elements
through standardized, loosely coupled communication mechanisms?
• RQ3. How usable and effective is the integrated DAPM graphical interface in sup­
porting users across the main stages of streaming process mining?

DAPM Framework Concepts
This chapter provides the necessary background for understanding the Distributed Archi­
tecture for Streaming Process Mining (DAPM) as used in this thesis. It first summarizes
the core DAPM concepts and the pipeline lifecycle to establish a common foundation.
The conceptual description of the DAPM pipeline presented in this section is based on the
thesis Distributed Architecture for Process Mining: Conceptualization and Performance
Analysis of an Online Data Streaming Pipeline by Becke and Klaassen [3], which intro­
duced the DAPM pipeline model. Their work defined the structure and semantics of the
main pipeline components.
3.1
Users
In DAPM, each organization is composed of users who hold different access roles and
permissions. Depending on their role, users can perform various actions such as assem­
bling, configuring, or executing process mining pipelines.
3.2
Pipeline Definition
A pipeline in DAPM is defined as a distributed network of processing steps that perform
tasks such as data transformation, process mining, or result storage. Each step operates
within an independent environment managed by its hosting organization, ensuring that
algorithm implementations remain within secure boundaries. In this setup, a user can
design a pipeline that includes processing steps provided by external organizations, such
as a heuristics miner operator. Access to these external components is regulated through
explicit permission requests exchanged between collaborating parties.
3.3
Pipeline Components
A DAPM pipeline is built from two main components: Processing Elements (PEs) and
Channels. Processing Elements represent the functional building blocks of the pipeline,
each responsible for performing a specific operation such as generating, transforming,
or storing data. Channels serve as the communication links between these elements,
carrying data in a unidirectional flow from one step to the next. Together, they define both
the structure and behavior of a pipeline.

Figure 3.1: Symbols used to represent Components
Processing Elements are categorized into three main types according to their role within
the pipeline:
• Source: A source element is responsible for introducing data into the pipeline. It
typically retrieves information from external systems or data streams, such as APIs,
databases, or event sources. Sources act as the entry points for process mining
data.
• Operator: Operators receive input from one or more upstream elements, apply
transformations or analyses, and produce new output data. Examples include pro­
cess discovery operators (e.g., heuristics miners), conformance checkers that com­
pare observed events against predefined models, and filters that remove or modify
events based on certain conditions. Operators form the computational core of a
pipeline, where most of the analytical logic is executed.
• Sink: A sink represents the endpoint of a pipeline, where processed data is delivered
to an external system or stored for further use. Common examples include writing
results to databases, exporting models, or forwarding event streams to visualization
dashboards. Sinks therefore act as the bridge between the pipeline’s analytical
process and external consumers of its results.

3.4
Organizations
In DAPM, each organization represents an independent entity that manages its own data
and processing elements. Organizations provide Processing Element Templates (PE­
Templates) that define reusable algorithms, which can be shared securely with others
through permission requests. When access is granted, a separate instance of the pro­
cessing element is created and executed within the owning organization’s environment,
preserving privacy and ensuring that no internal implementation details are exposed.
3.5
Stages of the Pipeline Lifecycle
The lifecycle of a DAPM pipeline defines the main stages a pipeline passes through. It
consists of three key stages: Assembly, Configuration, and Execution.
Figure 3.3: Pipeline Life Cycle
Assembly: In this stage, the user designs the pipeline by selecting, organizing, and con­
necting the required processing elements into the desired structure. This step determines
the functional behavior of the pipeline and implicitly specifies which organizations’ re­
sources are involved.
Configuration: Once the pipeline structure is validated, the system requests usage per­
missions from every organization whose resources are included. This ensures that each
organization maintains control over how its algorithms and data are accessed and used.
Execution: After all required permissions have been granted, the pipeline is ready to run.
A user with the appropriate privileges in the owning organization can start or terminate the
execution as needed, initiating the data flow through the connected processing elements.

6.1.2
Architectural Rationale
Each organization’s backend is designed as a modular monolith, integrating the previously
separate subsystems into one cohesive unit. This architectural choice provides clear mod­

microservices within the same organizational node. Empirical studies have shown that

systems Taibi and Lenarduzzi [16] and Zhou et al. [17]. The modular monolith enables
internal cohesion, simpler maintenance, and faster integration cycles while retaining the
flexibility to evolve into finer­grained services in future iterations [17].
This architectural decision helps satisfy NFR2 – Integration and Maintainability by con­
solidating components into a single backend per organization, which reduces deployment
and coordination overhead and stabilizes internal interfaces.
6.1.3
Decentralization and Interaction Model
Each organization in the DAPM ecosystem operates independently, maintaining full con­
trol over its local data, algorithms, and user management. Communication between or­
ganizations occurs exclusively through secure peer APIs, which handle authorization and
data exchange without exposing internal resources or relying on shared databases. Real­
time event data is exchanged asynchronously through middleware such as Apache Kafka,
enabling privacy­preserving collaboration without central coordination.
This model supports NFR1 – Security and Confidentiality and NFR4 – Decentralization
and Autonomy by enforcing clear security boundaries and separation of ownership, and
by avoiding centralized control points.
6.1.4

A unified web frontend provides a single interaction layer for the integrated DAPM sys­
tem, removing the need to maintain separate interfaces for each organization. It can be
hosted independently, for example via GitHub Pages or a similar static hosting service,
and connects to organizational backends through standardized client APIs. While this
design allows each organization to develop its own interface if desired, it also supports
shared or centrally accessible GUIs that interact with distributed backends in a consistent
manner, while keeping all backends fully decentralized and independently operated by
each organization.
This approach ensures common accessibility without centralizing computation or data,
enabling users from multiple organizations to authenticate, design, and execute pipelines
through a coherent interface. It directly supports NFR3 – Usability and Accessibility by
simplifying interaction for technical and non­technical users alike; the effectiveness of this
interface will be evaluated in Section 8.3.
In summary, the proposed architecture defines a modular monolithic backend for each
organization, interconnected through secure peer APIs and supported by a unified fron­
tend layer. This structure establishes a coherent foundation for integrating the previously
independent DAPM subsystems: execution, security, and ingestion, while preserving de­
centralization, autonomy, and usability across the system.

Cross­Organizational Trust and Collaboration
Cross­organizational collaboration in DAPM requires secure identification of partners, au­
thenticated communication, and controlled authorization without relying on any central
authority. While Dirani [4] introduced the initial concept of a cryptographic handshake be­

based authentication, decentralized key distribution, and policy­driven authorization.
The enhanced approach replaces static key configurations and manual approval steps
with an automated trust mechanism, where each organization can independently ver­
ify the authenticity of others through signed tokens and standardized discovery of public
keys. This design achieves continuous verification of organizational identity, scalable ac­
cess governance, and seamless collaboration between autonomous peers. The following
subsections describe the main components of this cross­organizational security layer.
Conceptual Identity Proof and Trust Handshake
Establishing secure communication between independent organizations in DAPM begins
with a trust handshake that verifies organizational identity and establishes a foundation of
trust between peers. At a conceptual level, this handshake consists of one organization
presenting a short­lived, verifiable identity proof to another, allowing the receiver to confirm
the authenticity and freshness of the request before any collaboration can proceed.
Upon receiving such a request, the peer organization validates the identity proof by check­
ing its origin, integrity, and validity period against a trusted representation of the sender’s
public identity. If the verification succeeds, the receiver records the requester as a trusted
organization, establishing the initial trust anchor for all subsequent interactions. The con­
ceptual sequence of this exchange is shown in Figure 6.3.

Once inter­organizational trust is established through the handshake, each subsequent
request continues to undergo cryptographic verification using short­lived identity tokens.
This ensures continuous authentication throughout all cross­organizational interactions.
Authorization, however, is governed by a subscription­based model that defines which
Processing Elements (PEs) and services each partner may access.
This mechanism replaces the static, manually maintained visibility lists from previous

illustrated in the access control model (Figure 6.2). In this context:
• PE Owner refers to the organization that provides or exposes one or more Process­

• PE Subscriber denotes the organization that consumes or invokes PEs owned by
another organization under the terms of a subscription tier.
Access between a PE Owner and its Subscribers is regulated through a tier­based model,
where each tier defines a specific level of authorization and service quality.
• Free: Grants access to public or educational PEs with limited resources.
• Basic: Enables access to a standard set of PEs for general use and testing.
• Premium: Provides high­performance or proprietary PEs for advanced use.

6.3.1
Runtime Upload of Processing Elements
Earlier conceptual work by Becke and Klaassen [3] defined Processing Elements as static
templates registered during system initialization. While this approach provided a clear
structural foundation for pipeline composition, it limited the ability to evolve or extend
available processing elements without redeployment. In the integrated architecture, this
constraint is removed: each organization can now upload new Processing Element (PE)
templates dynamically through a dedicated management interface.
Once uploaded, the element is compiled, validated, and registered in the local Template
Repository without interrupting ongoing operations. This mechanism transforms DAPM
from a fixed prototype into a living platform that organizations can continuously expand
according to their analytical and operational needs.

ceptual contribution illustrating the internal lifecycle of Processing Element registration
in DAPM, from upload and validation to tier assignment and availability for internal and

Each uploaded PE is associated with two essential descriptors:
• A subscription tier (Free, Basic, Premium, or Private) that determines who may
access it across organizations, as described in Section 6.2.3.
• A configuration schema that specifies the parameters required for its initialization
and validation. This schema is later used during pipeline validation to ensure correct
configuration of processing elements(see Section 6.4.1).

coupling between peers. It also increases transparency: even if a particular PE belongs to
a higher subscription tier, its presence in the catalog informs users that the resource exists

persistent lifecycle states, token­based security, and automated cross­organizational au­

artifacts directly consumed by the next, ensuring determinism, auditability, and secure
collaboration between independent organizations.

6.4.1
Assembly and Validation
In the assembly stage, the pipeline begins in the Draft state (as shown in Figure 6.8),
where users connect sources, operators, and sinks to define their intended data flow.
Once the structure is complete, the pipeline must be validated to ensure that it satisfies
both structural and semantic requirements before advancing to configuration. The valida­

graph is acyclic, connected, and consistent in terms of message types and component
roles.
Within the integrated DAPM framework, this stage has been extended through the dy­
namic exchange of configuration schemas. In earlier work, configuration schemas were
on static, pre­distributed artifacts or out­of­band sharing, which limited validation to static
Processing Elements (PEs). The unified framework now enables these schemas to be
shared dynamically between organizations as part of the cross­organizational exchange
model described in Section 6.3.2.
This allows, for example, OrgA—in the motivating scenario—to validate a pipeline that
includes a heuristic miner offered by OrgB prior to any cross­organizational execution or
data exchange. The configuration schema ensures that each PE’s parameters, expected
inputs, and constraints (such as required attributes or field types) are verified according
to the provider’s definition.
By supporting schema­driven early validation, the system preserves pipeline correctness
across organizational boundaries while reducing coordination overhead. Validated pipelines
are then persisted in the ValidatedRepository, providing traceability and a reliable transi­

This conceptual enhancement supports FR5 – Distributed Pipeline Execution and Feed­
back by strengthening the foundation for distributed pipeline execution, ensuring that only
structurally verified pipelines progress to later stages . It also strongly supports NFR2 –
Integration and Maintainability by providing a consistent and reusable validation artifact
that improves integration and maintainability across lifecycle transitions. Furthermore, this
stage also supports FR8 – Pipeline Lifecycle Management, as robust early validation con­
tributes to a consistent, traceable, and securely linked pipeline lifecycle, spanning design
and validation, configuration, build, execution, and termination.
6.4.2
Configuration
After a pipeline has been validated, the configuration phase ensures that all required
security and authorization conditions are fulfilled before moving to the next stage. This
phase is particularly crucial when a pipeline contains Processing Elements (PEs) owned
by external organizations, as each external component requires explicit approval from its
provider.
Earlier work by Dirani [4] introduced the conceptual process for configuration, distinguish­
ing between internal permission checks and cross­organizational access requests. In that
model, however, approval decisions were handled entirely manually, and each request
returned only a simple status (APPROVED or REJECTED). Such a binary, human­driven
process proved unsuitable for larger, dynamic settings involving multiple organizations
and frequent requests. Furthermore, the absence of a verifiable authorization artifact
made it impossible to audit or automate later stages in the pipeline lifecycle.

In the integrated framework, configuration is redefined as a dynamic authorization phase
that leverages the subscription and policy mechanisms presented in Section 6.2.3. When
a validated pipeline contains external PEs, the system automatically detects them and
prompts the user to initiate an access request to the owning organization. Once the re­
quest is submitted, the provider organization evaluates it against its tier­based policy. If
the requester’s active subscription tier does not meet the minimum tier required by the

satisfies the tier requirement and all defined constraints (e.g., maximum allowed duration
or number of concurrent executions), the request is approved automatically. Requests
that exceed these constraints but otherwise meet the tier conditions are flagged for man­

transparency while providing scalable, policy­driven authorization that replaces the static,
manually managed approach of the earlier DAPM iteration.

6.4.3
Build Phase
Once a pipeline has been fully configured and all required authorization tokens are avail­
able, the build phase is initiated.
In this phase, the logical structure of the validated
pipeline is transformed into a distributed, executable configuration, where each Process­
ing Element (PE) instance is created and linked according to the defined data flow. Con­
ceptually, this phase marks the transition from the design and configuration stages to the
execution­ready state of the pipeline lifecycle.
The fundamental orchestration process for building pipelines was first introduced by Becke
and Klaassen [3], who designed the component responsible for translating validated pipeline
graphs into instantiated, connected elements.
Their design defined how source, op­
erator, and sink elements are sequentially created, configured, and connected using
inter­organizational communication channels, ensuring correct data routing and synchro­
nization between participants. This conceptual foundation is preserved in the integrated
framework to maintain functional continuity with earlier DAPM developments.
However, the earlier model lacked any integrated security measures for verifying or au­
thorizing these inter­organizational build requests. In the unified architecture, the build
phase now operates under the token­based trust mechanism introduced during the con­
figuration stage (Section 6.4.2). Each organization verifies every incoming build request
using the capability token that was issued as part of the access request process. The
token represents explicit consent from the provider organization and is required for each
external PE used in the pipeline.
When a pipeline includes a PE owned by another organization, the actual instance is
created within the provider’s own DAPM environment—ensuring that both processing el­
ements and execution remain under the provider’s control. The requesting organization
transmits the build request together with the signed token, while the provider verifies the
requestor’s identity and the validity of the token before instantiation. Only requests ac­
companied by valid, non­expired, and unrevoked tokens can trigger the creation of a new
PE instance, preventing unauthorized or outdated pipelines from being executed.

the PipelineBuildService and PipelineRepository—but now operates under explicit trust
verification. This enhancement transforms the build phase from a procedural step into a
verifiable security checkpoint. Tokens act as cryptographic contracts between provider
and consumer organizations, binding the build operation to the consent issued during
configuration.

The execution phase represents the final stage of the pipeline lifecycle, where a config­

overall orchestration logic for execution was originally defined by Becke and Klaassen

Elements (PEs) exchange data through streaming channels. In this stage, sources emit
events, operators analyze, or discover, or filter events, and sinks consume or store the
results according to the pipeline design.

While the execution process itself was conceptually established in earlier work, its security
aspects were not addressed. The security framework proposed by Dirani [4] focused pri­
marily on authentication and authorization during configuration and cross­organizational
communication, but did not extend these protections to the execution stage. As a result,
prior models offered no mechanism for verifying that an execution request truly originated

In the integrated framework, this stage is preserved conceptually but extended with stronger

any PE instances. This step ensures that no unauthorized or spoofed execution requests
can trigger remote activity, thereby extending the zero­trust model to runtime operations.
Once verified, all authorized PEs are activated and data begins to flow across the pipeline’s
channels. As in previous work, termination remains a deliberate and complete shutdown:
sources cease emission, operators stop processing, and all channels and instances are
removed.
Within the integrated lifecycle, termination is explicitly modeled as a state
transition—from EXECUTING to TERMINATED—and all such transitions are logged and
auditable to preserve traceability.
By introducing authenticated execution triggers and identity­based verification, this phase
reinforces the security model established in earlier stages. It supports FR1 – Continuous
Trust Verification by guaranteeing that all runtime actions are backed by continuous trust
verification between organizations, and it strongly supports NFR1 – Security and Confi­

Implementation Overview and System Setup
The integrated DAPM system was implemented following a modular monolith design, as
outlined conceptually in Chapter 6. Each organization operates a self­contained back­

where organizational separation was achieved through distinct backends, but extends it
by incorporating all additional modules developed in later theses and providing unified,
well­defined interfaces between them.
The modular monolith design was selected deliberately to separate internal concerns
through cohesive modules while avoiding the deployment overhead and fragmentation
associated with premature microservice decomposition. Each module (e.g., the Pipeline
Lifecycle Manager, Subscription and Voucher Service, or Dynamic Processing Element
Loader) is isolated at the package level and interacts with others only through stable ser­
vice contracts, ensuring maintainability and future scalability.
Every organizational backend runs inside its own Docker container, encapsulating its run­
time dependencies and configuration. This containerization ensures deployment repro­
ducibility and operational isolation across organizations. Each container integrates:
• a PostgreSQL database for structured state such as users, projects, roles, subscrip­
tions, pipelines, and lifecycle metadata;
• a MongoDB instance used for anonymization­state management (pseudonym map­
pings, lookup tables, and raw/anonymized event documents);
• dedicated Docker volumes for runtime­compiled PE templates, configuration schemas,
logs, and cryptographic key material.
This dual­database design reflects the two fundamentally different categories of data han­
dled by DAPM. PostgreSQL manages strongly structured, transactional, administrative
state, while MongoDB manages flexible, document­oriented ingestion and anonymization
data including pseudonymization mappings. During anonymization the backend checks

7.3.2
Cross­Organizational Authentication and Trust Establishment
This section operationalizes the solution presented in Section 6.2.3, implementing token­
based handshake, decentralized key discovery, and subscription governance.
Handshake and Identity Verification.
In the earlier prototype by Dirani [4], organiza­
tional identity was represented by static RSA key pairs manually stored in application
configuration files. This design proved brittle: every organization had to embed its part­
ner’s public key into its own configuration, which prevented key rotation and created a
single point of failure during setup. Any change in key material required coordinated re­
deployment across all organizations, limiting scalability and flexibility.
The new implementation replaces this static setup with a dynamic handshake protocol
built on short­lived JSON Web Tokens (JWTs). When an organization initiates collabo­
ration, it issues a signed identity token using its private RSA key stored securely in a
PKCS#12 keystore. This format was chosen because it provides strong encryption for
private keys, integrates natively with Java’s security libraries, and supports certificate­
based trust anchors—ensuring that each organization can safeguard its cryptographic
identity independently.

During the build operation, the backend retrieves the corresponding ValidatedPipelineConfig
and invokes the PipelineBuilder, which instantiates and connects the required Process­
ing Elements (PEs). Communication between these elements is facilitated through Kafka
topics, which serve as dedicated, asynchronous data channels. This mechanism was
inherited from Becke and Klaassen [3] implementations and provides reliable and decou­

When a pipeline includes PEs owned by external organizations, the build request includes
a wrapped approval token. The requesting organization (OrgA) embeds the approval
token previously issued by the provider (OrgB) inside a new identity token signed with its
private RSA key using the signExistingToken() method. This produces a nested token
pair:
• the inner token confirms that OrgB authorized access to the PE, and
• the outer token proves that the request currently being made originates from that
subscriber organization (OrgA).
Upon receiving the request, OrgB first verifies the outer wrapper token using OrgA’s public
key, authenticating the caller. It then extracts and verifies the inner approval token using
its own key, ensuring that the token was in fact issued by OrgB. Next, OrgB checks that
the approval token’s audience matches the wrapper token’s issuer, that the peTemplateId
matches the requested Processing Element, and that the token’s jti has not been marked
as used or revoked. Only when all these checks succeed is the remote PE instantiated
within OrgB’s environment.
By combining this double­verification model with Kafka­based orchestration, the build
phase ensures that cross­organizational execution is initiated only under authenticated

8.1
Scenario­Based Demonstration
This section demonstrates how the integrated DAPM artefact can be used to address
the motivating scenario introduced in Section 4.1. The underlying architecture and im­
plementation are not tailored to this specific case—organizations, processing elements,
and pipelines are configured dynamically through the backend and GUI—but the two­
organization hospital–research collaboration between OrgA (hospital) and OrgB (research
partner), shown in Figure 4.2, is used as a concrete instantiation. The goal is not to re­
peat the implementation details from Chapter 7, but to show, at a higher level, how the
deployed system supports a cross­organizational streaming pipeline in a realistic setting.
8.1.1
Case Setup
For the demonstration, the final prototype was deployed on three independent virtual ma­
chines: OrgA’s backend on DAPM3, OrgB’s backend on DAPM2, and the React GUI on
DAPM1. Each backend runs in its own Docker container with a local PostgreSQL and
MongoDB instance, as described in Figure 7.1.
The case follows the motivational scenario: OrgA owns live patient event data and wishes
to apply a Heuristics Miner algorithm provided by OrgB, while ensuring that raw patient
data does not leave OrgA before being anonymized and that OrgB’s algorithm remains
under OrgB’s control. Both organizations interact solely through the DAPM GUI and the
integrated backend. This case was chosen because it: captures the core problem of
secure, decentralized streaming process mining across organizations and exercises all
major components of the integrated system. It therefore provides an appropriate test bed
for demonstrating the feasibility of the integrated DAPM system.
8.1.2
Applying the Artefact to the Scenario
1. Establishing Trust Between Organizations.
Through the GUI, an administrator
at OrgA initiates a collaboration with OrgB. The handshake workflow is executed: OrgA
sends a signed identity token, OrgB verifies it using OrgA’s JWKS endpoint, and both sides
store each other as PE Owner / PE Subscriber in their local databases. This demonstrates
that cross­organizational trust can be established without manual key exchange or central
coordination.
2. Uploading and Discovering Processing Elements.
Both organizations can upload
new Processing Elements (PEs) at runtime via the GUI. For the scenario, OrgB uploads
a Heuristics Miner PE, assigns it to the Basic subscription tier, and publishes it in its local
catalogue together with its configuration schema and I/O types. OrgA then retrieves this
catalogue through an authenticated peer request and stores the metadata locally. This
shows that organizations can dynamically contribute and discover PEs without redeploy­
ment.

3. Designing the Cross­Organizational Pipeline.
Using the GUI, a user at OrgA cre­
ates a new project and assembles the distributed pipeline:
• HospitalEventSource and DepartmentFilter at OrgA,
• Heuristics Miner at OrgB,
• PetriNetSink at OrgA.
Configuration panels are populated automatically from the stored schemas, and the pipeline
is validated structurally before it can progress. This demonstrates that a cross­organizational
pipeline can be designed visually without manual JSON or API interaction.
4. Tier Upgrade and Permission Configuration.
When OrgA inspects the retrieved PE
catalogue, the Heuristics Miner is marked as requiring the Basic tier, while OrgA initially
holds the Free tier. OrgA’s administrator redeems a signed voucher that upgrades the
organization to the Basic tier. During the pipeline configuration phase, OrgA’s backend
detects that the pipeline uses an external PE and a leader sends an access request to
OrgB. OrgB evaluates the request against the subscriber’s tier and usage constraints and
returns an approval token if the policy is satisfied. This shows that subscription­based,
decentralized authorization governs access to external PEs without manual negotiation.
5) Building and Executing the Pipeline.
Once all required approvals have been ob­
tained, OrgA builds the pipeline. For the external Heuristics Miner, OrgA wraps the ap­
proval token inside a new identity token and sends it to OrgB, which verifies both layers
before instantiating the PE in its own runtime. Data then flows as follows:
1. OrgA’s source PE ingests live patient events and applies anonymization rules so
that only de­identified data leaves OrgA.
2. The anonymized stream is sent to OrgB, where the Heuristics Miner continuously
discovers and updates a Petri net.
3. During execution, the GUI’s pipeline view displays the evolving Petri net in the re­
sults panel. The user can observe how the model changes as more events arrive,
matching the scenario’s need for continuous monitoring of patient flows. Execution
can be stopped directly from the GUI, which transitions the pipeline to Terminated
and preserves the final model for later inspection.
8.1.3
Outcome of the Demonstration
The demonstration shows that the integrated artefact:
• establishes cryptographic trust and subscription relations between autonomous or­
ganizations;
• allows both organizations to upload PEs at runtime and to discover external PEs via
authenticated catalogue requests;
• enforces tier­based access control using vouchers and automated approval deci­
sions;
• executes a distributed streaming pipeline in which anonymized data is processed
locally in each domain and feedback is returned in real time; and
• enables all required actions to be carried out through the graphical interface, without
manual API calls.

Discussion and Future Work
In this chapter, we address the research questions and evaluate to what extent they have
been answered.
In addition to answering RQ1–RQ3 based on the results presented earlier, this chapter
summarizes how the evaluation validates the proposed solution and highlights the main
threats to validity that may affect the interpretation and generalizability of the findings.
These threats are discussed in relation to each research question and motivate the future
work directions outlined at the end of the chapter.
9.1
Discussion of RQ1: Integration of the DAPM Subsystems
RQ1 asked: Can the three previously developed DAPM subsystems be integrated into a
single cohesive platform that supports streaming process mining end to end?
The results presented in Chapter 7 and the scenario demonstration confirm that the inte­
gration goal has been achieved. The formerly disconnected subsystems— security and
trust establishment, ingestion and anonymization, and distributed pipeline execution—are
now part of one unified backend per organization, communicating through stable internal
interfaces and standardized peer APIs. The modular monolith architecture proved an
effective choice: it preserved internal separation of concerns while enabling rapid inte­
gration and consistent interaction between previously incompatible components.
The scenario execution shows that all stages of an end­to­end streaming pipeline (data
ingestion, anonymization, structural validation, configuration, build, and execution) can
now be performed without manual intervention or subsystem­specific configurations. This
demonstrates a cohesive platform where the lifecycle of streaming pipelines is uniformly
managed across organizations.
This conclusion is supported by the requirements­based evaluation in Section 8.2. Func­
tional requirements FR3 – Dynamic Discovery and Publishing of Processing Elements ,
FR5 – Distributed Pipeline Execution and Feedback , and FR8 – Pipeline Lifecycle Man­
agement are all fully met, while NFR2 – Integration and Maintainability is strongly met.
Together, these requirements operationalize what it means for DAPM to function as an
end­to­end streaming process mining platform, providing structured evidence for an affir­
mative answer to RQ1. The graphical interface itself, specified in FR6 – Graphical User
Interface (GUI) and evaluated in Section 8.3, builds on this integrated backend and is
discussed further under RQ3.
A limitation, however, is that the evaluation instantiated the platform with only two organi­
zations in the hospital–research scenario. The underlying architecture and implementa­
tion are generic and allow additional organizations, processing elements, and pipelines to
be added through configuration, but larger deployments with more diverse organizations
and heavier workloads were not empirically examined. Such deployments would provide
stronger evidence regarding scalability and integration robustness. Nonetheless, within
the scope of this thesis, RQ1 can be considered positively answered.

9.4
Future Work
Based on the results and limitations discussed above, several directions for future work
emerge, addressing both further validation of the proposed solution and functional exten­
sions towards broader real­world adoption.
Scalability and Performance.
While the current evaluation demonstrates feasibility for
a two­organization setting, systematic benchmarking under higher event rates, more or­
ganizations, and larger catalogues of processing elements would provide stronger ev­
idence regarding performance and scalability. This includes measuring end­to­end la­
tency, throughput, and the behavior of the subscription and authorization mechanisms
under load.
Pipeline Monitoring and Observability.
The existing lifecycle and GUI could be ex­
tended with dedicated monitoring views for running pipelines, including metrics such as
per­PE throughput, lag, and error rates. Such dashboards would support operational
monitoring and troubleshooting in real deployments.
Economic and Governance Extensions.
The voucher­based subscription mechanism
implemented in this thesis demonstrates how decentralized access control can be auto­
mated, but it does not yet cover real­world billing or usage reporting. Integrating payment
systems, fine­grained quotas, and provider dashboards represents a natural next step
towards a production­ready platform.
Security Hardening and Sandboxing.
Runtime upload of processing elements increases
flexibility but also raises security concerns. Future work should investigate stronger sand­
boxing techniques for uploaded code, as well as enhanced audit logging and revocation
mechanisms for inter­organizational tokens.
Broader Usability Studies.
Finally, the usability evaluation should be replicated with
larger and more diverse user groups, including practitioners from different domains and
organizations. This would help validate the generality of the usability findings and refine
the interface for broader adoption.
GUI Feedback and Interaction.
The usability study indicated that some participants oc­
casionally missed validation feedback in the pipeline editor. Future work could strengthen
error visibility through direct visual signalling. For instance, when validation detects a
structural issue, the affected node or connection could be highlighted in red and com­
plemented with inline warning icons and concise explanations of the required corrective
action. Such cues would make configuration and permission issues more immediately un­
derstandable for non­technical users and improve the overall efficiency of pipeline design
and troubleshooting.

Conclusion
This thesis addressed the lack of an integrated, practical platform for secure, distributed
streaming process mining within the Distributed Architecture for Process Mining (DAPM).
Previous work produced separate prototypes for security and trust establishment, stream­
ing execution, and ingestion and anonymization, but these were not yet combined into a
cohesive, user­accessible system.
Beyond simply combining these subsystems, this work introduced several key enhance­
ments. A unified architecture was designed and deployed as a modular monolith per orga­
nization, combining cryptographic trust establishment, subscription­based access control,
dynamic management of processing elements, and a pipeline lifecycle that spans design,
validation, configuration, build, execution, and termination. On top of this backend, a
graphical user interface was developed that allows users to manage organizations and
projects, assemble cross­organizational pipelines visually, and monitor their execution
without manual API calls or low­level configuration.
Through a scenario­based demonstration, a requirements­based evaluation, and a us­
ability study, the thesis showed that the integrated platform can support an end­to­end
streaming pipeline across organizations while preserving local control over data and pro­
cessing elements, and that the graphical interface is perceived as intuitive and effective
by its users. Taken together, these results indicate that the research questions posed at
the beginning of the thesis can be answered positively: the previously separate DAPM
subsystems can be integrated into a cohesive platform, decentralization and organiza­
tional autonomy are preserved, and the graphical interface successfully supports the main
stages of streaming process mining.
At the same time, the empirical evaluation of the running system was carried out in a two­
organization deployment and did not include systematic performance benchmarking or
very large­scale deployments. While the requirements­based assessment shows that the
implemented architecture meets the specified functional and non­functional requirements,
questions of scalability and robustness under higher loads and in larger, more heteroge­
neous settings remain open. Future work should therefore investigate these aspects in
more demanding scenarios, strengthen security and sandboxing for runtime­uploaded
components, and broaden usability studies to more diverse user groups. Within these
boundaries, the thesis demonstrates that it is both feasible and beneficial to move from
fragmented DAPM prototypes to an enhanced, decentralized, and user­oriented architec­
ture for end­to­end streaming process mining.

This thesis was written by Mahdi El Dirani in 2025 as part of the requirements for the Master of
Science in Computer Science and Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It
builds upon and integrates several previous works on the Distributed Architecture for Process Min­
ing (DAPM) by combining the execution, security, and ingestion subsystems into a single, unified
platform. The work further introduces a graphical interface and several architectural enhancements
that make streaming process mining pipelines usable end­to­end across organizations. It has been
a challenging yet highly rewarding experience, bringing together research, design, and engineering
into one cohesive system.
