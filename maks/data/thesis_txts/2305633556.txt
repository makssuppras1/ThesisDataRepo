Abstract 
The generation and transportation of pollutants in sewer system are very complex phenomena. In 
literature we find a huge amount of model used to model those processe: from very detailed 
deterministic models to stochastic differential ones that describe only the main processes 
occurring in the system under study. 
Most studies recognise the rain as origin of the problems occurring in a sewer system; the rain has 
a stochastic trend and the description of the system is affected by uncertainties so the stochastic 
differential approach has been chosen in this work to describe the dynamic of TSS in a sewer 
system. 
In particular we will implemented the so called “grey-box” model developed by H. Bechmann 
(1999) regarding the prediction of the loads of pollutants in a sewer system. 
The model will be used with high time resolution measurements, coming from the AMOK project. 
This type of data were not available when the model was developed. 
The tool use to implement the model is CTSM (Continuous Time Stochastic Modelling) that is a 
package of R used to develop stochastic differential models. 
The aim of the thesis is to verify if and when the forecast obtained by the model can reproduce 
the trend of the observations for what concern the TSS concentration in a CSO structure. 
Forecasting concentrations of pollutants can be useful in the field of decision making: knowing in 
advance these concentrations can be useful to protect the surrounding environment from the 
discharge of high polluted wastewater.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
In the last few years monitoring of water quality is becoming one of the most important and 
helpful tool to understand the dynamics of wastewater and sewer systems, further, to evaluate 
the impact that these systems can have when discharging diluted untreated wastewater in the 
environment (J. Alferes 2014).  
The structures driving those discharges are the Combined Sewage Overflow (CSO) that are built to 
protect the Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) during events of high flow. 
The European Water Framework (2000/60/CE) committed all the Member States of the European 
Union (EU) to achieve by 2015, a good ecological quality of water bodies. The pollution coming 
from CSOs falls within this objective, but not every EU country has national directive about 
pollutant loads. Most of existing legislations refer just to hydraulic parameters but not to water 
quality parameters to regulate the discharges of CSOs in the environment (G. Dirckx 2011). 
Generation and transportation of pollutants in sewer systems are very complex phenomena, but 
in the last years, the understanding of solids processes is improved considerably (G. Mouri 2010). 
This improvement has allowed a more accurate control of sewer solids and of pollution generated 
by solids in sewer. In this context, the development of new technologies for the evaluation and the 
prediction of water quality is fundamental (G. Mouri 2010). 
As Mouri and Oki state (2010), the problems related with sediments in sewer are now widely 
known:  
 concentration of pollutants, 
 loss of hydraulic capacity,  
 production of corrosive acids and gas,  
 generation of infected water,  
 discharge of pollutants to the environment that can cause serious problems to the 
receiving water bodies (i.e. eutrophication).  
 
Sediments deposit during dry weather period and they are resuspended and transported during 
rainy events. Usually, attached to these suspended sediments we find pollutants that strongly 
affect the quality of the wastewater discharged through the CSOs, such as heavy metals and 
organic micro-pollutants. Hence, the prediction of the quality of the discharged wastewater is 
important for taking suitable measures to reduce the impact on the surrounding environment (G. 
Mouri 2010). For example, by knowing the load of pollutants in the near future, it can be possible 
to decide if the wastewater can be directly discharged to a river or if it needs to be treated before. 
The processes and the dynamics occurring in a sewer system are very complex and 
interconnected, so discrete sampling and laboratory analysis are not able to give a full description 
of the phenomena occurring in the sewer. In order to fully grasp the variability of flow and 
concentrations during overflows events, the measurements needed to quantify the pollution have 
to be in continuous time. In fact, the traditional schemes to monitor the water quality, such as 
==PAGE:19==
2 
 
instantaneous sampling, are able to detect long-term impact but they cannot detect the dynamic 
behaviour of the pollutants. 
To overcome this problem there is a transition to an acquisition of data through the usage of 
sensors that can obtain high time resolution measurements of a large variety of physical and 
chemical variables (J. Alferes 2014). Not even these methods are capable to give a complete view 
of the processes that take place in a sewer system, but they help to have a more comprehensive 
view. Furthermore, we are able to visualize and analyse the variations of quality of the water at a 
high temporal resolution, describing the dynamics of the pollution and trying to take some actions 
to avoid bad ecological effects. 
The data coming from high temporal resolution monitoring can, after being processed and/or 
screened to detect erroneous data, provide the basis for the development and calibration of water 
quality models (W. Boenne 2014). 
At the present time, because of the complexity of the phenomena occurring in the sewer, the 
existing models are simple. This leads to uncertainties, which can be accounted by using stochastic 
approaches (H. Garsdal 1994). 
 
The purpose of this thesis is (i) to implement an existing stochastic model able to evaluate and 
predict pollutant concentrations, (ii) by using a new set of data not available when the model was 
developed in the late ‘90s. These data come from high time resolution measurements in the inlet 
of a wastewater treatment plant, and they were collected during the AMOK project in the Viby 
catchment. The measurements include: flow, ammonia and total suspended solid (TSS) 
concentrations. These data have already been verified and calibrated in previous student projects. 
 
To implement the stochastic model, CTSM-R is the package of R used. By using this software, it is 
possible to run the so-called grey-box models, which are based on stochastic differential 
equations. The model used in this study simulates the mass of sediments in the sewer and 
provides as output the predicted concentrations of pollutants (H. M. H. Bechmann 2000).  
 
The hypotheses of this project are the following: 
 the deposition of pollutants in sewer systems is strongly linked to previous rain events 
and flows: pollutants deposits gradually build up in the system during dry weather 
periods and are then flushed out during rain events; 
 the dry weather flux of pollutants is described by a fixed diurnal profile describing the 
pollutants entering into the sewer system minus a contribution to the deposition; 
 the rate of deposition of the pollutants can be described by first order linear 
differential equations. 
 
The main objectives and the research questions that we pose are: 
 Implement an existing model (H. Bechmann 1999) to forecast the load of pollutants in a 
CSO structure;

2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
This section regards the theory needed to develop the model implemented in this thesis. Starting 
from a state of the art about the existing model used in the field of wastewater quality, arriving to 
the description of the specific model used in this thesis. 
2.1 Water quality modelling in sewer systems 
A model is not the reality but a limited and simplified representation of it: it is a tool to simplify 
the complexity of a real system. Even if models are intrinsically false, they have many advantages: 
they are simple, they can simulate and reproduce observations, they return helpful results 
(Bertrand-Krajewski 2007). 
Construction of municipal drainage systems has historically two main objectives: public hygiene 
and prevention of flooding. The aspect of pollution monitoring and control has become important 
later. The development of mathematical models, in this field, followed this trend (W. Rauch s.d.). 
From the middle ‘80s researchers and practitioners have widely used models for hydraulic and 
hydrological purposes, such as investigation on flow propagation, rainfall-runoff processes, 
overflows. For what concern pollutant concentrations and loads we have to wait the middle of the 
‘90s (Bertrand-Krajewski 2007). 
The sewer system is the part of an integrated urban drainage system in which the wastewater is 
transported to the wastewater treatment plant from the catchment area. If the hydraulic capacity 
is exceeded, the water is discharged directly in the receiving water body. The receiving water 
bodies could be rivers, lakes, sea, groundwater, but models focus mostly on rivers (W. Rauch s.d.). 
A schematic representation of an integrated urban drainage system is shown in Figure 1, the 
arrows represent water and mass flows.

Nouvel (Nouvel 2002) states, we can’t have a complete view of the system without neglecting 
something: to obtain an understandable representation we have to look just at some aspects

ecosystems are strongly effected by individual rain events. The source of these environmental 
impacts, the rainfall, is irregular as its negative effects in magnitude, duration and occurrence (W. 
Rauch s.d.). 
The models used to formulate the complexity of the real world are made by means of 
mathematical language aimed to model every physical processes occurring. At the same time lack 
of knowledge and the complexity make these physical models arduous to design, use and interpret 
(A. Carrascal 2010). 
Usually, in water quality models it is not possible to estimate concentrations and loads of 
pollutants without an estimation of the flow: most water quality models have a hydraulic or 
hydrologic part. Another reason for this coupling is that quality and quantity mitigations are often 
complementary (Zoppou 2001). 
Models regarding water quality have developed as sets of equations describing each process of 
the system. Further improvements, since 1970s, have just added more and detailed equations to 
reproduce more observations or to describe other sub-systems of the model (Bertrand-Krajewski 
2007). This brought to an increase of complexity and difficulties. Models based on the knowledge 
of the processes at small scale are not able to represent successfully complex systems, such as 
sewer systems (Bertrand-Krajewski 2007). 
A huge amount of different models exists to describe behaviour of water quality and quantity, 
from simple conceptual models to complex physical ones. A distinction is between stochastic and 
deterministic models. 
 
2.1.1 Deterministic models 
In deterministic models, processes are described by differential equations, there are not stochastic 
elements and the relationships between inputs and outputs are completely determined: in linear 
models a single input is linked to a single output through differential equations; in non-linear 
models a single input can be linked to multiple outputs. The solution could be computed using 
different numerical methods: path simulation, finite difference or finite element (P. Erdi 1989). 
These models include both dynamic and static models. They can describe processes with different 
temporal variations: 
 Steady state: no temporal variation; 
 Time series of steady states in which the input of the steady state model is a time series; 
 Dynamic: to describe both temporal and spatial variations of an event (P. Erdi 1989). 
These kind of models are based on well-known physical laws. The uncertainties, related to a not 
perfect knowledge of the system under study, are unlikely to be included (P. Erdi 1989).

For example, L. Heip et al. (1997), in the context of SPRINT-project, develop a very detailed 
deterministic model to investigate the sewer flow quality in a typical rural catchment. A measuring 
campaign of 9 months was undertaken and, during this period, the hydraulic behaviour was 
monitored in continuous time. Samples were taken during both wet and dry weather flow to 
analyse COD, BOD, TP,TSS and TKN. These data were used to calibrate and verify the model. This 
model takes into account a large variety of processes occurring in the sewer and in the catchment 
area, so, as the authors observe, with the knowledge of that time (1997), a deterministic model 
cannot predict absolute values of loads or concentrations of pollutants. Anyway this model can be 
useful to compare the impact of different design options (L. Heip 1997). 
Another example is the model developed by H. Garsdal et al. (1994). This model aimed to describe 
the water quality processes occurring in a sewer system both to dissolved and sediment attached 
pollutants. The result is a very complex mathematical formulation, requiring huge computational 
efforts. Furthermore, the authors conclude that this model is not capable to predict the behaviour 
of pollutants without an adjustment of the parameters (Henrik Garsdal 1994). 
We can find a different approach in the work of P. Dempsey et al. (1997). They start from the idea 
that deterministic models are too complex, hence too slow to run, to give useful results for 
operational options. They proposed a simplified model, SIMPOL, that represents only the main 
processes that occur in the system. The various parts of the system are schematised with tanks 
connected to build the structure of the system. Simulations consider one pollutant at time (P. 
Dampsey 1997). 
 
To evaluate pollution linked to rain events, total suspended solids (TSS) are one of the most 
important parameter. In fact the impact on the receiving water bodies is determined by the 
pollutants attached and absorbed in TSS, such as heavy metals, phosphorous, organic compounds, 
polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. Various studies recognise the importance of the combined 
sewer overflows (CSOs) discharges in the impact on receiving water bodies during intense rain 
events, especially if they go directly into small rivers (L. Rossi 2005).

Several deterministic models have been used to estimate loads of TSS and pollutants attached to

2.1.2 Stochastic models 
Most of the processes linked to urban drainage systems have stochastic characteristics: behaviour 
of rain events, rainfall-runoff processes, behaviour of flow in sewer systems, and particularly the 
trend of suspended solids in the sewers. The behaviour of sewer sediments is, in fact, random and 
inhomogeneous (M. Ruan 1997). Due to these stochastic characteristics, stochastic models can 
better describe the processes occurring in these systems.   
The structure of the stochastic model comprises one or more stochastic elements. The spatial-
temporal behaviour of a process, or a system, is modelled with random components that linked a 
single input to different outputs. By running multiple times, a stochastic model is able to estimate 
probability distributions. Taking into account also the uncertainties, a stochastic model can give 
more information about the system under study, respect to a deterministic model (P. Erdi 1989). A 
schematic representation of a stochastic process is give in Figure 3:

weather pollution, the importance of the TSS accumulated in the system during dry periods is 
pointed out. This amount can reach, during a rain event, the 80% of the total mass (L. Rossi 2005). 
The accumulated sediments will be then washed out during a rain event. 
Another advantage of the stochastic approach, according to L. Rossi et al., is that it takes into

2.2 Stochastic differential approach 
Often the sewer systems are modelled using, as configuration, a succession of storage volumes 
and pipes. With this approach, the resulting model has many parameters that cannot be 
determined due to lack of data or information (H. M. H. Bechmann 2000). 
A model that allow us to reduce the number of parameters to estimate is the stochastic 
differential model, the so called “grey-box model”. It describes only the most important physical 
processes occurring in the sewer system, hence the parameters and the states to estimate are 
few. Due to this small amount of parameters to be estimated, the computational efforts needed 
are also few and so the model can be used for on-line purposes (Jacob Carstensen 1996).  
 
Grey-box models combine the characteristics of white-box and black-box models (Figure 4) 
comprising equations of the known physical processes to describe the model and statistical 
techniques and experimental data to model the lesser understood parts of the system (S. Estrada-

2.2.3 Wiener process 
The standard Wiener process is the first construction of the Brownian motion. The Brownian 
motion describes the random movements of a particle in a d-dimensional space, it depicts a 
macroscopic picture of a huge amount of microscopic effects that randomly affect the movement 
of the particle (P. Morters 2008). If the position of the particle at time 0 is S0, its position at time t 
is the result of a number of random displacements due to external forces or to the contact with 
other particles:

where Xi is the vector of displacements assumed to be independent and identically distributed in 
Rd (P. Morters 2008). The displacements are the microscopic inputs that contribute to create the 
macroscopic effect, but probably not all of them contribute to the final movement, hence we can 
use the mean and the covariance of the microscopic inputs. Poorly speaking all microscopic events 
with same mean and covariance, give as a result the same macroscopic process, known as 
universal object (P. Morters 2008). This property is called universality. The universal objects are 
useful to study various phenomena in the field of statistic (P. Morters 2008). 
The features of any continuous stochastic process describing a macroscopic event are: 
 the process must have independent increments; 
 the process must have stationary increments; 
 the process must have a continuous path; 
 the increment is multivariate normally distributed.

2.3 CTSM-R 
Continuous Time Stochastic Modelling (CTSM) is a R package that provides a useful tool that uses 
real time series data to identify physical models. 
This tool was developed during the EU PASSYS project (phase I: 1986-1989). The aim of this 
European project was to characterize the thermal properties of building components, providing 
reproducible data test and carrying out experimental and theoretical research on several methods 
of measurement (Wouters P e Vandaele L 1994). The first applications of this tool cover a wide 
range of topics: building of photovoltaic systems, heat dynamics of buildings, solar thermal 
collectors, dynamics of heat exchangers and many more.  
In general, using CTSM-R it is possible to manage stochastic differential models; it can model both 
non-stationary and non-linear systems. This program is able to fill the gap between statistical and 
physical models by the means of a continuous time formulation of the system under study and 
using discrete time measurements (Team 2015). 
This tool has the capacity to join the statistical features and the capacity of handling data of R, 
with the power of a continuous time stochastic model. 
It can perform maximum likelihood and maximum a posteriori estimation of unknown parameters. 
It can handle outliers and missing observations. For the estimation, numerous independent data 
set can be used. 
 
The next section deals with the description of the model structure, of the methods used and of the 
statistical background.

2.3.3 Filtering method 
The role of a filter is to obtain estimations of the states of a system that optimize a particular 
criteria, starting from available information such as inputs and observations (M.I. Ribeiro 2004). 
The aim of any kind of filter is to evaluate the optimal estimation of the quantity under study, the 
states of a given system; usually the data come from a noisy environment. The optimal estimate is 
reached through the minimization of the error of the estimation respect a defined condition. In 
practice, the filter evaluates and propagates the conditional probability density function 
conditioned on the actual data, coming from the measurements (M.I. Ribeiro 2004). 
Figure 5 depicts a schematic representation of a typical application of a Kalman Filter. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5 Kalman filter application (M.I. Ribeiro 2004) 
The dynamic of the Kalman Filter results from repeated cycles of prediction and filtering 
processes. This filtering dynamics are derived under linearity and Gaussian conditions.  
Under conditions of non-linearity, the conditional probability density functions are no more 
Gaussian: in this condition the non-linear filter evaluates the mean of the non-Gaussian functions 
and propagates them. This process need a huge computational effort. To overcome this problem 
we can use an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) that is the result of the linearization of the non-linear 
dynamics around the previous state estimates (M.I. Ribeiro 2004).

measurements (𝑌1

2.3.4.1 Checking estimation results 
The optimization process has to end at a global optimum. To verify this point, CTSM-R returns a 
set of statistical parameters useful also for a physical interpretation of the estimated parameters 
(Rune Juhl 2013). 
To evaluate the quality of the parameter estimates, CTSM-R returns some statistic results: 
 t value: value of the t statistic. A t-test (or Student’s T test) is a test in which two means are 
compared to understand if they are different and how significant is this difference; 
practically it lets us understand if this difference occurs by chance or not. A t-value, 
resulting from t-test, is the ratio between the difference between two groups and the 
difference within the groups. To smaller values correspond a similarity between the 
groups, to higher values correspond more difference between these groups 
(http://www.statisticshowto.com/t-test/ 2013). 
 Pr(>|t|): p-value of the t-tests; it is the probability that the initial sate or parameter is not 
significant; if this value is > 0.05 the model could be over-parametrized. This value is always 
associated to the t-value (Rune Juhl 2013). 
 dF/dPar: derivative of the objective function with respect to the initial state or parameter; 
if this value is significantly higher then 0, the solution could not be the true optimum and 
we should change something for the optimization and so repeating the computation (Rune 
Juhl 2013). 
 dPen/dPar: derivative of the penalty function with respect to the initial state or parameter; 
if this is significant respect to dF/dPar, the initial value of the state or the parameter could 
be close to one of its limits (Rune Juhl 2013). 
 correlation matrix: it is built with the correlation coefficients between each variables and 
the others, it is an indication of the dependence between multiple variables at the same 
time; if the off-diagonal values are close to 1 or -1, the model could be over-parametrized 
and we could delete some of the parameters considered (Rune Juhl 2013).

In this section the implemented model is described and also the steps followed to structure it in 
the CTSM-R package, together with a brief explanation of the processes of estimation, filtration 
and simulation. then the process of parameter calibration is shown. 
 The methods used to evaluate the model and its results are explained. Finally the data used are

3.1 The Bechmann “grey-box” model  
The model used to obtain forecast of TSS concentrations at the inlet of a WWTP is a very simple 
model, developed by H. Bechmann (1999), that does not take into account all the processes of 
transportation and transformation that the TSS is subject to. It takes into account just the dynamic 
of deposition and resuspension at which the suspended solids undergo respectively during dry and 
wet weather periods. 
The reasons of this simplicity  are basically two: the first one is the fact that it is impossible, and 
probably useless, to describe all the processes the TSS undergoes; the second reason is the fact 
that this model is supposed to run with on-line measurements, so the computational efforts have 
to be reduced as much as possible. 
For this specific work a set of off-line measurements, previously collected and verified, has been 
used for the impossibility to use the model directly with on-line measurements. These data set will 
be briefly described  in Paragraph 3.2. The advantage is that we can compare the simulations 
made by the model and the observed measurements to evaluate the quality of these results. 
 
The model is constructed as a stochastic differential model. It is composed of a stochastic 
differential equation describing the physical behaviour of the system, an observation equation for 
the output and the variance of the measurement error. In addition we have to specify the inputs 
to the model, Qin and T in our specific case, and the initial values, with lower and upper bounds, of 
the parameters involved. 
In the next part, the model structure is defined and the steps followed to structure it in the CTSM 
field are explained.

Now the “grey-box” model has all the needed information to start the estimation of the optimum

estimation is the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) explained in Section 2.3.2.1.

5) PARAMETERS CALIBRATION: 
It is impossible to find the exact parameters to use for each event, because the model is a 
simplification of the system and the parameters are affected by environmental factors. Anyway, 
making a calibration can help to find the set of parameters that better approximate the real 
behaviour of the system.  
Checking the values of the derivative of the objective function respect to the initial state or 
parameter, it is possible to evaluate if the parameters found are the true optimum or not. 
In fact if the value of this function is different from zero, it means that the particular initial state or 
parameter is not the optimum, hence some setting has to be changed. 
After having changed the value of some parameters, the estimation is repeated until the value of 
dF/dPar is as close as possible to zero.

estimate, so it is clear that the results temporally nearest to the starting points are nearest to the

both the initial value and a range of variation (upper and lower limits). 
Some of these parameters are computed through an estimation in MATLAB (for the script see 
Appendix B), others are just decided by the modeller. 
The parameters estimated in MATLAB regard the dry weather flow (DWF) pattern (equation 36). 
This equation describes the diurnal variation of the wastewater flow. 
The program gives an estimation of the parameters of the harmonic function describing the 
entering amount of pollutants and a plot representing the measured (red) and the simulated 
(blue) states in the corresponding time window (Figure 9).

For the manual calibration, the derivative of the objective function with respect to the particular 
parameter or initial state has been taken into account. This value is given by the model after the 
estimation of the parameters. Changing the value of a parameter and checking the variation of

3.1.2 Model evaluation 
Objective of any kind of model is to reproduce reality. The calculated state variables have to be as 
close as possible to the measured ones. 
A great number of methods exist to assess the goodness of a model, classified as follow: 
 Statistical tests; 
 Visual techniques; 
 Measurement of deviation. 
In this work, statistical tests have been used to calibrate the parameters, instead visual techniques 
have been used to evaluate the results obtained. First of all, we have compared the simulated 
values with the observations, then we have calculated the percentage of exceeding values with 
==PAGE:43==
26 
 
respect to a threshold for both the observed values and the simulated ones, to see if the model is 
able to detect peaks of concentration of pollutants. Lastly, scatter plots have been used to 
coupling and compare observed and measured values. All these methods are described in the 
following sections. 
 
3.1.2.1 Threshold 
The scenarios analysed are important events that can occur in a sewer system (rain events, non-
calibrated sensors). In these cases a method to coupling observed and modelled values is the 
exceeding of a specified threshold (alarm or trigger) (N.D. Bennett 2013).  
To quantify the behaviour of the model, a contingency table can be built. This is a table in which 
there are: 
 Hits: both observations and simulations are above the threshold; 
 Correct negative: both values below the threshold; 
 Misses: number of values missed by the model; 
 False alarms. 
An ideal model would fill only the diagonal of this table. The other two values are useful to 
evaluate the model: high-misses means underestimation, while high-false alarms overestimation 
(N.D. Bennett 2013).

The ARIL is a measure introduced for the first time by X. Jin et al. (2010) to make comparisons of different 
forecast for the whole time period.

where n is the number of time steps, Qobs,t (l/s) is the observed value at time t, limitlower,t and limitupper,t are 
the lower (5%) and upper (95%) confidence bands calculated at time t. The term of the summation is called 
Relative Interval Length (X. Jin 2010). 
This parameter is not a measure to evaluate the goodness of the model or the outputs of the model, but it 
gives the percentages of observations falling into the calculated confidence bands. Hence to smaller values 
correspond a larger percentage – and vice versa – and a better performance (X. Jin 2010).

the observed peaks of concentration of TSS. 
We will discuss in this section only the scenario of the rain events, because is in this scenario that 
we found the highest peaks that we want to know in advance.

5. CONCLUSIONS 
 
The aim of this thesis was to implement a model developed by H. Bechmann (1999) able to make 
forecast of the loads of pollutants in a sewer system, and to test its performance by using a new 
dataset of high-resolution measurements. Among the data come collected during the AMOK 
project we have focused on TSS. This is because this water quality parameter can affect the 
environmental status of the receiving water body. Also, the behaviour of TSS is more difficult to 
predict then other pollutants, such as ammonia. 
The implemented model, the so-called “grey-box” model developed by H. Bechmann (1999), was 
tested on its ability to make short-term predictions making it suitable for on-line purposes. 
The periods selected to test the model included 10 rain events and 6 periods when the sensors 
were not calibrated. The rain events were selected based on the fact that during these events the 
TSS accumulated in the sewer system was flushed out and a peak of concentration of pollutants 
occurred. 
While analysing the periods of calibration we wanted to understand if the model was able to 
detect this malfunctioning or if the outputs would follow the observations. 
Various inflow scenarios have been analysed to simulate different knowledge about the model 
input:  
 the future inflow is known; 
 the future inflow is unknown and it is estimated by a model; 
 the future inflow is unknown, so it is set to a constant value equal to the last known value. 
To investigate the behaviour of the model and to understand its limitations, different time 
horizons have been considered, in the specific 5, 10 and 20 minutes and also different numbers of 
scenarios to be simulated by the model (1000, 100, 10). 
 
The main conclusions of this study are: 
1. How many scenarios are needed to estimate the uncertainty of the output? 
The number of scenario is a parameter that can be decided by the modeller. This 
parameter influences  the computation of the probability distribution of the simulations: 
having more scenarios, the model has more values with which calculate the pdf, so this will 
be nearest to the real one. However, we have seen that changing the number of scenarios 
the behaviour of the model still the same: the highest values are underestimated, while the 
smallest are overestimated in the scenario of the rain events. For the other scenario, the 
forecast follow quite well the observations. 
2. How good are the model forecast? And can the model identify sensors malfunctioning? 
 For the rain event the highest values are underestimated, while the smallest are 
overestimated; this behaviour is observed for all the three cases of the different inflow. 
Among the 10 events analysed, only one does not follow the trend of the observations 
(08-09/04/2014). However we can consider this as a good result. 
==PAGE:96==
79 
 
 For the scenario of the sensors calibration the under- and over-estimation of the values 
is not observed, at least it is much less. The forecast follow the trend of the 
observations in all the three cases of the different inflow, but 2 among the 6 events 
analysed do not follow this trend (19-20/03/2014 and 18-19/06/2014). This result is not 
so good, because the model is not able to detect the failure of the sensors. 
3. For how long can we trust in the forecast? 
Analysing three different time horizons (5, 10, 20 minutes) we have seen that moving in 
time from the known starting points, the quality of the results decreases: the values of the 
forecast are more and more different from the observations. This is true for both the 
scenario and for the three different types of inflow utilised. 
This result is the consequence of the decreasing influence of the EKF, so the prediction 
power of the model is low. 
4. How good is the model to predict peaks in concentrations? 
The analysis of the ability of the model to predict peaks in concentrations shows that, for 
the case of known inflow and unknown and constant inflow, the peaks are detected but 
increasing the time horizon the amount of the false alarms also increases and there are no 
missed values. For the case of unknown and simulated inflow the situation is different: 
there are no false alarms but the misses increase increasing the time horizon. So the 
reliability of the forecast is limited in time. 
5. Weaknesses of the model. 
We have found that the response of the model is highly sensitive to some parameters 
(precisely σ1 and σh). However, a more precise quantification of this influence should be 
carried out through a sensitivity analysis. 
We have also found that some of the forecast, that represent concentrations of TSS, have 
negative values: this is probably due to the values of the parameters and also to the 
formulation of the model. However, this would require a re-formulation of the structure of 
the model. 
 
Overall, the results of this study suggest that the model can be used to obtained forecasts of 
pollutant concentrations in the near future, but it is not able to detect sensors malfunctioning. 
However, knowing the pollutants concentrations as short time predictions can be useful to protect 
the environment from the discharge of high polluted wastewater.

6. 
FURTHER DEVELOPMENTS 
 
The model implemented in this work was developed in the late ‘90s (H. Bechmann 1999) and then 
it has been utilized in few applications, so possible future developments and outlooks can be 
various, respect the little work conducted in this thesis. 
A first, and probably most evident, observation is that for some of the events under study the 
bands of confidence have some negative values. So fully understanding the reasons of such 
negative values and reformulate the model has to be the first further step. 
Another observation can be made regarding the values of the parameters used. In fact, some of 
them are evaluated with a script in MATLAB – based on Fourier’s description of the daily variations 
in the pollutant load – while the others (a, b, xm, Qm, x0, σ1, σh) are decided by the modeller and 
there are no literature that can help to make this decision.  Hence, a sensitivity analysis on the 
parameters can be useful to understand which parameter influence more the results and how. 
The water quality parameter used with the model was only one: the TSS concentration. It could be 
interesting to reformulate the equations to test the model also with other type of pollutants, such 
as NH4 or COD, and see if the outputs follow the same dynamics of the present work. 
Last, but not least, the set-up used in this work was off-line, while the model should be tested in 
an on-line context to allow its use for real time control.
