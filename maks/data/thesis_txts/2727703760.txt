Abstract: 
Recombinant protein production has been studied and used by many around the world to 
produce enzymes, pharmaceuticals and other molecules for various purposes. In the explosive 
expansion of the field of synthetic biology scientist failed to establish standards that help in 
exchange and comparison of knowledge and data. Many have attempted to standardise the field 
by producing modular genes and vectors that is accessible to everyone. They still lack user due 
to the need of unconventional cloning procedures.  
Surface display platform LppOmpA-NB is an expression system derived from gram- negative 
bacteria that displays a protein of interest on the surface of the cells tagged with a nanobody 
for easy quantification by GFP binding assays. The SEVA is a vector architecture that builds 
standardised vectors that contain modular segments a user can choose from. SEGA is a genome 
architecture that follows the same philosophy as SEVA. In this research, the expression of 
surface display platform is studied in the two standardised platforms. By performing GFP:NB 
assays the efficiencies of the two standards were tested with the surface display platform. I 
found that the two standards were not expressing the surface display platform. The standard 
vector architecture and the genome architecture are modular by design which can be used to 
find the right configuration for the most efficient expression of the surface display platform. 
Moreover the surface display platform can be added to the database for users around the globe 
to produce functional protein.

1. Introduction: 
 
Recombinant DNA technology was just a theory few years ago. Manipulating genomes by 
cutting or inserting DNA segments with the help of enzymes and laboratory techniques to 
produce recombinant proteins is called recombinant DNA technology. Today this genetic 
engineering is being used in multiple sectors of the world to sustain life and economy.  In 
agriculture genetic engineering is used to transfer desire genes to the other species instead of 
the conventional method that transfers both specific and non- specific genes 1. Not only plants 
but human lives have also been improved with recombinant DNA technology. Vaccines and 
pharmaceuticals like Recombivax HB that vaccinated against Hepatitis B saved the lives of 
many humans. Genetically modified bacteria was used to produce recombinant human insulin2.  
 
Use of fossil fuels to produce enzymes and chemicals for industrial and pharmaceutical use 
reduced after the use of microbial cell factories. Cell factories contain genetically modified 
organisms usually by recombinant DNA technology to produce desired recombinant proteins 
which are usually enzymes or chemicals. The new age of this technology comprise of synthetic 
biology, systems biology and evolutionary engineering. The process simply entails altering the 
genome or the plasmid DNA of a microorganism using genetic engineering tools and methods. 
The newly constructed DNA is transferred into an organism that produces the recombinant 
protein that can later be isolated and purified in downstream processing.  
 
The commonly used cell factories are Escherichia coli and Saccharomyces cerevisiae 3. E. coli 
has a well-established genome and the cost of production is lower while keeping the yields 
high. They are used in both industries and laboratories. The two strains of E .coli we will talk 
about are the cloning strain DH5 and the protein production strain BL21 (DE3). E. coli DH5 
is derived from a rod shaped  K-12 strain. It is used in the laboratory at end of a cloning 
procedure where the assembled DNA is transformed into competent DH5 cells4 . The BL21 
cells are preferred hosts for protein production. The strain does not have a flagella due to 
deletions in fli genes. This makes it non- motile, thus driving energy into fast cell growth where 
the cells double in numbers every 20 minutes. With the help of the T7 promoter and the inducer 
IPTG the strain can produce proteins at a much higher capacity that the K-12 derived strains5.

Proteins and enzymes are usually produced intracellularly and isolated and purified in the

1.1.  Surface display platform: 
 
1.1.1.Surface display in gram- negative bacteria: 
 
Displaying proteins on the surface of the cell membrane is inherent to multiple microorganisms 
like yeast, gram positive and gram negative bacteria. E.coli is no exception especially with high 
yield recombinant protein production. E.coli genome has been studied extensively and is easy 
to genetically manipulate. The proteins are displayed on the surface by a simple mechanism 
yet to be fully understood. The protein produced is simply carried by a carrier to the cell surface 
through the inner membrane and then the periplasm. Once it reaches the outer membrane an 
anchor holds the protein and binds itself to the outer membrane thus displaying the protein.  
 
Although small proteins get expressed on the cell surface all the time larger proteins do not 
pass through the cell wall of the cell easily. Large proteins need help from the carrier proteins. 
There are multiple carrier proteins described in previous research but there are two main 
systems that have proven to be efficient in carrying large molecules across the inner membrane 
and through the periplasm to the outer membrane. Autotransporter proteins and ice nuclease 
proteins are the most studied types of carrier proteins.  
 
Surface display in gram negative bacteria is facilitated by proteins present in the outer 
membrane of the cell, outer membrane proteins (OMPs). There are two types of OMPs namely 
- barrel OMPs and Lipoproteins.  - barrel OMPs are proteins on the transmembrane that are 
 sheets rolled into a cylinder6 . Porins are OMPs that are protease resistant. The well-known 
porins are OmpA, OmpC and OmpX. This type of system usually facilitates display of small 
peptides but there are novel systems that can display larger proteins7.

Lipoproteins are outer membrane proteins that contain lipid moieties and are found on the 
underside of the outer membrane. They do not travel through the outer membrane 6. The N-
terminus in the protein anchors the protein to the outer membrane. A few lipoproteins have 
been used to display proteins on the cell surface. They are Trat, PAL and Lpp. TraT can 
transport passenger proteins attached to the middle and C-terminus while PAL can transport 
proteins attached to the N-terminus8 . Lpp facilitate transport of larger proteins like gfp when 
combined with a truncated OmpA. There are more surface display systems dependent on 
virulence factors, Tat (twin-arginine protein translocation) and surface appendages.

Proteins produced on the surface of the cells has apparent benefits compared to industrial 
enzymes that are hard to purify and can only be used once. The surface display of proteins 
eliminates the need for transportation of substrate and products in and out of the cell while 
protecting the cell against any toxicity by the substrates and products 9. Surface display of 
proteins has been done in many hosts like yeast, phage and bacteria. Moreover recent 
technologies have made it possible to use bacterial surface display in biosensor applications10. 
One of the best methods developed to quantify and qualify the surface display is to bind 
fluorescent antibodies to it that help in flowcytometric and microscopic analysis 9. 
Fluorescence assisted cell sorting (FACS) a flowcytometry based analytical method is 
combined with surface display on E.coli to screen larger libraries. This was not possible before 
due to less accessibility to the substrate.

1.1.2. LppOmpA-NB 
As discussed above surface displaying of proteins is achieved by the passenger protein binding 
to the carrier protein to pass through the cell membrane and being displayed by an anchor. In 
a study done by Heyde. S et all.,9 the authors use one such system that has been exploited in 
this research. The LppOmpA-NB or the Surface Display Platform (SDP) as referred to  in this 
report, contains three components, a fusion of Lpp lipoprotein and the outer membrane protein 
OpmA that uses a single chain antibody called a nanobody (NB) as a protein tag.  
 
The first 9 amino acids of E.coli Lpp bound to amino acids 46-159 of OmpA forms the Lpp-
OmpA fusion. It was first used to surface display large molecules like - lactamase, a cellulose 
binding protein and alkaline phosphatase11 Lpp-OpmA as seen in Fig 1 passes through the 
outer membrane 5 times. The barrel loops of the - sheets in OmpA facilitates the translocation 
of the passenger protein across the outer membrane which is initially bound to the C- terminus 
of the Lpp-OmpA fusion. Although efficient in displaying large bacterial proteins the fusion is 
not efficient in displaying chimeral proteins. This might be due to steric hindrance on the outer 
membrane12.  
 
Nanobodies are small recombinant single domain antibodies that were discovered in serum of 
camelids in the 1990s13 They belong to the group of antibodies called Heavy Chain only 
antibodies (HCAbs). Nanobodies have adapted to be functional and soluble without a light 
chain variable domain. The evolution of long complementary- determining region allows them 
to adopt novel confirmation for antigen recognition and subtle amino acid variations14 . Besides 
the small size and stability they can be easily produced in E.coli. The small size of the nanobody 
allows for the binding of affinity tags. This makes nanobodies a candidate for flowcytometry. 
Study by Salema V et al., shows that nanobodies can be bound with different labels like biotin 
for magnetic cell sorting (MACS) which used along with gfp for fluorescence activated cell 
sorting (FACS) could be beneficial as an analytical methods.  
 
In the study done by Heyde. S. et all.,9 fluorescence based detection of surface display was 
done by fusing nanobody to GFP. There remain to be an issue with GFP which is that there is 
no way to differentiate between intracellular GFP and the surface displayed protein as both 
cells glow. For this reason the authors devolved a system where the surface displayed protein 
is detected by binding the GFP to the nanobody outside the cell. This means the purified GFP

1.2. SEVA 
 
1.2.1.Standardization in synthetic biology 
 
Scientists came together to assign a uniform nomenclature for recombinant DNA technology. 
They decided a plasmid should be called a “plasmid” , and much more15 . However they failed 
to implement standards for assembly of said plasmids and for the tools used to assemble them. 
Individual laboratories have their own standards, but there is a necessity for standards since 
there is exchange of knowledge and resources between different laboratories. Assembly of 
complex genes and engineering whole genomes require characterization of biological functions 
and devices that make them usable16 . Impediment in data comparison due to the lack of format 
in tools and devices built in one lab requires a scientist in another lab to re-clone the same 
sequences into their own vectors. This limits the opportunity to engineer devices with parts 
from different laboratories. Moreover addition of genes to wild type vectors with unnecessary 
sequences increases the burden on the cells17 . 
 
1.2.2.SEVA standard 
As discussed above, the need for standardisation led the authors of the study17 to create a 
database that succeed in dealing with said problems. The database is easy to navigate, minimal 
and the tools are compatible with old and new genetic engineering methods. The database 
consists exchangeable modules like the origin of replication, antibiotic marker, expression 
systems and reporter genes that are covered with restriction sites that can is used also to 
exchange the modules. The database (http://seva.cnb.csic.es) allows the user to configure their 
own preferred construct (Fig 4).  
 
The authors built the SEVA standard with a minimalist to offer easy user experience. They 
focused on three main components, origin of replication, antibiotic marker and the cargo 
segment. Naturally occurring sequences were torn down to minimal DNA segment that still 
retain function. To maintain the design they removed all restriction sites present in pUC18 and 
additionally Sfil, AvrII and NotI. The  amino acid sequence was retained  by mutating a codon.

Antibiotic Markers: 
The SEVA standard was built with six major antibiotic selection markers namely, Ampicillin, 
Kanamycin, Chloramphenicol, Spectinomycin/ Streptomycin, Tetracycline and Gentamycin. 
These antibiotic resistance markers were combined with the five origin of replications to give 
thirty different constructs ensuring compatibility with each other.  
 
The SEVA standard although well designed lacks replication termination signals and active 
segregation systems which keeps the low-copy plasmid stable in E.coli 19. In that case the 
authors expect the housekeeping topoisomerases to resolve the replication intermediates and to 
keep the plasmid stable through antibiotic selection.

The SEVA database contains all the existing plasmids and new plasmids submitted by 
researchers. The new plasmids submitted by researchers must follow the SEVA standard which 
means i) all the restriction sites need to be removed and replaced with SEVA restriction sites 
and ii) the plasmid name must follow the SEVA nomenclature as described above. The SEVA

1.3.  SEGA 
Recombinant DNA technology and the its standardization has always been focused on plasmid 
DNA. Chromosomal recombinant gene expression has become increasingly prevalent with the 
advancement of biology. Unlike plasmids chromosomes do not rely on copy number and can 
host larger genetic elements 20. The cells that carry plasmids may lose the gene of interest due 
to structural instability by mutations and loss of plasmid due to segregational instability21 . The 
requirement of a selective pressure such as antibiotic selection because newly divided cell do 
not always end up with the plasmid due to the above mentioned reasons. This not only increases 
the cost but also increases the risk of rise multi drug resistant strains22 .  
 
Integration of genes into the prokaryote chromosome are usually done by homologous 
recombination by λ-Red system or by RecET system and site- specific recombination23–25 . 
Homologous recombination takes place when the gene of interest containing a sequence that is 
homologous to the sequence in the chromosome binds to the specific site. The down side is 
that only a few kilobases of DNA can be integrated and said integration would be affected if 
there are more than one homologous sites. Site specific recombination requires the bacterial 
chromosomal attachment (attB) but allows for freedom in the insert.  
 
Keeping these reasons and the above mentioned need for standardization in mind the authors 
from26 created a standardized landing pad that allows for genome integration of a gene of 
interest and grants predictable expression across the genome. 
 
1.3.1. SEGA construct 
The Standardised Genome Architecture also called as SEGA was designed by the authors to 
integrate DNA fragments into the genome via λ-Red recombineering and a landing pad that 
was constructed with a few features. According to the authors the landing pad i) enables

insertion of genes, ii) provides functional gadgets that are well- characterised and iii) “provides 
insulation against genome- context dependent effects”. The two parts to focus on are the λ-Red 
recombineering and the SEGA landing pad. 
 
The λ-Red system is derived from the bacteriophage λ phage. The λ induces in bacteria  state 
of efficient genetic exchange. This state, ‘hyper-rec’ state is the work of three proteins which 
make up the λ-Red system27 .  Without getting too deep into the molecular biology of the λ-
Red system, the mechanism of the recombineering can be simply explained (Fig 6). The λ-Red 
system contains exo,  and gam proteins. These proteins have specific functions that make the 
λ-Red system so efficient. The exo protein is an exonuclease that starts degrading dsDNA from 
the 5’ end resulting in a 3’ overhang. The  protein is a monomer that binds to ssDNA and its 
nucleolytic and recombination properties promotes annealing to a complementary single 
strand. The gam protein prevent nuclease activity by nucleases like RecBCD by binding to it27.

SEGA Landing pad: 
The authors of the SEGA landing pad was designed with inspiration from the SEVA standard. 
The landing pad contains two control elements (Fig 7.b). The control element C1 provides 
transcriptional control and the control element C2 provides translational control. An upstream 
gadget galK (Fig 7.a) was added that makes the cells sensitive to 2- deoxy- galactose so that 
the control elements can be integrated. Followed by the “control bricks” before the “cargo 
brick” is the middle gadget (Fig 7.c) that contains features like bicistronic gene expression 
buffer28, peptide tags or signal peptides. They are useful for  downstream processing of 
proteins. The middle gadget also houses the standard recombineering site RS2 which is one of 
the sites for integration of gene of interest.

1.4. Project Aim 
Genetically engineered platforms that can express functional proteins like enzymes and 
pharmaceuticals are revolutionising the industry and the world of synthetic biology. These 
platforms are designed to give control over the whole process of protein expression. Moreover 
the standardised design with newly implemented long awaited synthetic biology standards 
make these platforms accessible to all around the world. Gone are the days where a scientist is 
burdened by designing vectors adding and removing genes to get a specific function. The new 
age databases with standardised platforms and vectors allow a user to select any vector with 
any features required.  
 
The Surface display platform enables display of functional proteins on the surface of the cell 
and providing easy quantification with the help of GFP binding assay. Moreover the scientist 
that developed the Surface display platform also show highly efficient expression of functional 
proteins that can be used for industrial and ecological applications. The Standardised Genome 
Architecture developed by the same scientists provide yet another standardised platform with 
features that a user can choose form. These platforms not only efficient but is also speaks to 
the sustainable biotechnology by eliminating the need for chemical produced from fossil fuels 
to produce and purify industrial enzymes.  
 
In this research Surface display platform will be into the Standardised European Vector 
Architecture (SEVA), a database of vectors designed to implement standards to the field of 
synthetic biology and bring accessibility to researchers across the globe. This provides an 
opportunity to add my construct containing the Surface display platform to the SEVA database 
thus enabling the distribution of the platform to better applications. Moreover the expression 
of the platform in a standardized vector will be studied in order to optimize the performance of 
the platform. 
 
The Surface display platform will also be added to the SEGA platform to study the expression 
of the two expression systems and adding this multimodule expression platform to the SEGA 
collection.

2.1.4.M9 minimal media: 
 
1 L M9 minimal media was made by mixing 100 μL CaCl2, 100 mL 10x M9 salts, 1 mL 2M 
MgSO4, 100 μL thiamine, 500 μL trace elements (see below), and 10 mL 20% carbon source, 
before adding MQ water to 1 L.  
The trace element solution was made by mixing 10 g FeCl3 · 6 H2O, 2g ZnSO4 · 7 H2O, 0.4g 
CuCl2 ·2H2O,1gMnSO4 ·H2O,0.6gCoCl2 ·6H2O, and 3.2mL 0.5M Na2EDTA pH8, before 
adding water to 1 L.  
 
2.1.5.LB Media: 
1L LB media was made by mixing 10g Tryptone , 10g NaCl, 5g yeast extract and 900ml 
distilled water. pH adjusted to 7.0 using NaOH (1N).  
 
 
2.2. Methods: 
 
2.2.1.Cell cultivation: 
 
The E. coli cells were cultivated in LB medium with 1% agar for solid media.  
LB media was used for liquid cultivation also. The cultivations were performed overnight at 
37oC at 250 revolutions per minute (RPM) unless mentioned otherwise.  
The LB media was supplemented with antibiotics, Ampicillin (100g/ml), Kanamycin 
(50g/ml), Chloramphenicol (25g/ml), Spectinomycin (50g/ml), Tetracycline (10g/ml), 
Gentamycin (10g/ml).  
  
2.2.2.Plasmid isolation: 
 
Plasmids were isolated and purified from E. coli  cell using the NicleoSpin® Plas- mid kit 
(Macherey-Nagel) following the instructions provided by the manufacturer. The purified 
plasmids were eluted in 50ml MilliQ water for storage and sequencing.

2.2.3.Agarose gel electrophoresis: 
 
The agarose gel was cast by adding 3l Sybr Safe DNA gel stain to 1% agarose. The gel was 
run using 2.5 l GeneRuler 1kB Ladder and 5l TriTrack as the loading dye. The gel was run 
for 35minutes at 90V.  
 
2.2.4.Gel purification and PCR clean-up: 
 
DNA from PCRs and agarose gels were isolated and purified using the NicleoSpin® Plas- mid 
kit (Macherey-Nagel) following the instructions provided by the manufacturer. The purified 
plasmids were eluted in 50ml MilliQ water for storage and sequencing.  
 
2.2.5.PCR: 
 
For colony PCR One Taq mastermix (NEB) was used and instruction from the manufacturer 
was used. For USER fragment PCR Phusion U Hot Start DNA Polymerase (Thermo Scientific) 
was used following instructions from the manufacturer.  
 
2.2.6.USER Cloning: 
 
The USER assembly was performed by adding 0.5l USER enzyme and 0.5l DpnI with 1 l 
T4 Ligase buffer, DNA fragments (250ng DNA per fragment) and dH2O to make 10l reaction 
to a PCR tube. The thermocycling process involved incubation at 37oC for 30 minutes and then 
32 oC for 3 minutes to 20 oC for 3 minutes by 1 oC decrements. This is followed by incubation 
at 16 oC for 10 mins. The assembled DNA is transformed into E. coli DH5 cells.  
 
 
2.2.7.Restriction Cloning:

The restriction digestion of the plasmids were done using FastDigest BoxI and FastDigest SmiI 
(Thermo Scientific) with 10x FastDigest Green Buffer (Thermo Scientific) following 
manufacturer’s instructions. The digested fragments were separated on agarose gel. The gel 
was purified with NicleoSpin® Plas- mid kit (Macherey-Nagel) gel purification kit. The

manufacturer’s instructions and transformed into E. coli DH5 cells. 
 
2.2.8.TSS Transformation: 
 
To transform USER product into E. coli DH5 cells 25l of TSS competent cells prepared in 
Morten Nørholm Lab were used per transformation. KCM buffer was prepared with 5l 5x 
KCM, 10l USER and dH2O to bring the total volume to 25l. The KCM buffer was added to 
the TSS competent cells and incubated on ice for 30 minutes. 1ml of LB was added to the cell 
mixture and recovery growth was performed by incubation in 37oC at 250rpm. The cells were 
spun down at 5000 rpm for 2 minutes and resuspended in left over liquid after discarding 
supernatant. The cells were plated on LB agar supplemented with antibiotics. 
 
2.2.9.Heat shock transformation: 
To transform plasmids into BL21 (DE3) cells 50l of BL21 (DE3) competent cells prepared in 
Morten Nørholm lab was used per transformation. 10l of purified plasmid was added to the 
competent cells thawed on ice. The cells were incubated on ice for 20 minutes. DNA- cell 
mixture was heat shocked at 42oC for 45 seconds. The mixture was placed back on ice for 5 
minutes. 1mL LB is added to the mixture and incubated at 37oC for 1 hour. The recovered cells 
are centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 2 minutes and re resuspended in left over liquid after discarding 
supernatant. The cells were plated on LB agar supplemented with antibiotics.

2.2.11. 
GFP-Assay: 
 
All BL21 (DE3) cells were cultivated overnight. The overnight culture was diluted into fresh 
LB liquid media supplemented with antibiotics to and OD of 0.1. The cultures are incubated in 
37o C at 250 rpm till OD is measured to be 0.3- 0.5. 5mM rhamnose is added to the culture and 
incubated again in 30oC at 250 rpm for 3 hours. The OD was measured and the protein 
production was stopped by pelleting cells via centrifugation for 4 minutes at 2272g in a Thermo 
Scientific Multifuge X3R centrifuge. Cells were resuspended in 50l of 50mM PBS solution. 
50 l of GFP (0.12mg/ml) was mixed with the cells. The mixture was incubated at 30oC for 20 
mins at 250rpm. The GFP bound cells were centrifuged at 2272g for 4 minutes. The pellet was 
washed twice with 300 l 50mM PBS solution and resuspended in 220l 50mM PBS solution.  
 
2.2.12. 
GFP fluorescence and OD measurement: 
 
200l cell suspension was loaded on dark 96 well F bottom plate  (Greiner Bio-one). The 
fluorescence was measured in a Synergy Mx plate reader (BioTek) at gain 80. Cells were then 
transferred to a 96 well F bottom plate  (Greiner Bio-one) and OD at 600nm was measured.

3.1. pSEVA- Surface display platform 
 
3.1.1.Assembly of construct pSEVA-SDP 
 
The plasmid of choice for this research is pSEVA121. The SEVA standard plasmid as 
discussed above contains three gadgets. The origin of replication in pSEVA121 is an RK2. 
RK2 is a low copy number origin of replication. While being a low copy plasmid the choice 
was made keeping in mind that RK2 is broad host range plasmid. The broad host range is 
necessary when the goal is to create a standardised construct to be included in the SEVA 
database. The SEVA plasmid contains an AmpR gene that confers to resistance toward 
ampicillin providing selection pressure. The pSEVA121, in factory setting comes with a 
multiple cloning site but the plasmid pSEVA121 plasmid used in this research contained a 
SEGA platform. This was a result of previous experiments performed in the Genome 
engineering group.  
 
I wanted the Surface display platform (SDP) consisting of LppOmpA-NB cloned into the 
SEVA standard. The SDP as we will call it is a surface display platform. So upon induction the 
platform expresses the LppOmpA anchor that displays nanobody (NB) on the surface of the 
cell. The placement of SDP to be next to the SEGA platform. With a pRha promoter the 
expression of the SDP can be controlled in the cell. A plasmid containing two expression 
systems with two different promoters hold many opportunities. 
 
All cloning performed in this research was done with the help of USER cloning. One of the 
objectives in this research was to optimize the expression of SDP in pSEVA121 plasmid. USER 
cloning makes cloning a promoter, two genes and a TEV site easier. It also allows for inclusion 
of a TIR library to optimize the expression of the SDP in pSEVA121. Since the expression of 
SDP is assayed with fluorescent tag a TEV site is needed to remove any affinity purification 
tags that could affect fluorescent protein binding to the nanobody.

3.1.2.GFP- NB Assay with pSEVA-SDP 
 
To study the expression levels of the SDP authors from performed what I call the GFP:NB 
assay to measure fluorescence in a microplate reader. The assay is done by first growing the 
cells to a mid-exponential growth phase and inducing expression with 5mM rhamnose. The 
induced cells are then harvest and bound with gfp. The cells are then washed twice to remove 
unbound gfp and then fluorescence of the gfp is measure in a microplate reader. BL21 (DE3) 
cells are used for its high protein production and easy induction as discussed in the introduction. 
So I transferred  pSEVA-SDP into BL21 (DE3) cells. The expression of BL21 (DE3) with and 
without SDP (negative control) was measured by GFP:NB assay. During the GFP binding 
process in the experiment GFP was added in 10-fold dilutions i.e., 1x, 1/10x, 1/100x to see the 
difference in fluorescence. The fluorescence and OD 600 were read in a microplate reader.

3.1.3.Assembly of pKA-SDP 
 
The results observed above proved the procedure to be flawless but still failed to compare the 
expression of  SDP in two different constructs. SB13+Lip Ab contains a functional protein 
Lip+ Ab attached to the C terminus of the NB in LppOmpA-NB. As previously mentioned the 
expression of the SDP when a protein is bound to the C-terminus of the nanobody is higher 
than SDP without any functional protein Heyde. S. et all,. 9 
 
In order to get a measurement unaffected by a functional protein I created a plasmid derived 
from SB13+ Lip Ab. New primers were designed and using USER cloning method I removed 
the functional gene Lip Ab from the plasmid. The resulting plasmid pKA:LppOmpA-NB 
(pKA-SDP) contains a high- copy pKA with a KanR marker and the SDP and pRha promoter 
inducible by rhamnose. The plasmid was transformed into DH5 cells and plated on LB plated 
supplemented with Kanamycin. A few transformants were selected  and the sequence was 
verified whole plasmid sequencing (Unveil.bio), and the plasmid was transferred into BL21 
(DE3) strain for expression study.

4 DISCUSSION

4. Discussion: 
 
4.1. SEVA 
 
The SEVA plasmid pSEVA121 used in this research contains three main gadgets. The origin 
of replication, antibiotic selection marker and the cargo. The simple and standardised design 
makes it easier to add any gene of interest and express the added gene. This also means that 
analysing the expression results is made easier due to the lack of unnecessary DNA in the 
plasmid. The pSEVA121 vector was used in this research to study the expression of the surface 
display platform.  
 
The Surface display platform (SDP) is an expression platform consisting of the bacterial 
surface anchor fusion LppOmpA that is N- terminally bound to a small antibody called a 
nanobody. The LppOmpA fusion simply carries the passenger protein to the surface of the cell 
and displays it. The nanobody acts as an antibody tag that can bind to GFP for quantitative 
analysis.  
 
The base objective in the beginning of the research was to study the expression of SDP in a 
SEVA plasmid and also to optimize the translational capacity. So the SDP was cloned into the 
pSEVA121 plasmid by USER cloning.  However, the pSEVA vector used in the cloning 
contained a SEGA landing pad. Nevertheless, the SDP was inserted next to the SEGA landing 
pad. The SDP was inserted with a pRha promoter that drives expression upon induction with 
L- rhamnose and a TEV site at the C- terminal of the nanobody. The TEV site was added to 
assist the GFP binding by getting rid of other protein tags. Initially the idea of a standardised 
plasmid vector that contains two expression systems that can be individually expressed by their 
specific promoters was interesting to work on.  
 
But there was a problem, see the SEGA landing pad in the plasmid contains a constitutive 
promoter J23100. This promoter drives the expression of the GFP encoding gene present in the 
cargo gadget of the SEGA landing pad. That means the cells are always fluorescent 
intracellularly. However the expression of the SDP is quantified by washing the induced cells 
==PAGE:54==
4 DISSCUSION 
43
with pure GFP and measuring its fluorescence. This means that the two fluorescent signals 
intracellular and extracellular will not provide any information on the expression of SDP in the 
SEVA plasmid. For this reason the SEGA landing pad was removed using USER cloning. 
 
4.2. pSEVA-SDP 
 
The SDP was inserted into pSEVA121 giving pSEVA-SDP. The plasmid was transformed into 
DH5 and selected on ampicillin. The select transformants were sequenced and the sequence 
alignment showed two single deletion likely caused due to read error during nanopore 
sequencing. There were no mismatches observed in the SDP sequence. The plasmid was 
transferred to BL21(DE3) cells to quantify the expression of the SDP.  
 
The GFP:NB assay is performed by growing cells to a mid- exponential growth and induced 
with 5mM rhamnose. The cells are harvested after incubation and washed with GFP. Unbound 
GFP is washed away. These cells are loaded on both dark and clear microplate reader to get 
fluorescence and OD 600. The dark is better suited for fluorescence measurements because the 
it prevents against the lose fluorescence signal from neighbouring wells.  
 
The first GFP:NB assay was performed with BL21(DE3) cells containing pSEVA-SDP and 
BL21(DE3) WT cells without any plasmids. The BL21(DE3) WT cells were used to observe 
base level fluorescence to compare the results. The values obtained were plotted into a graph 
(Graph 1). The cell cultures were quantified under different conditions. The cells were both 
induced with 5mM rhamnose and uninduced. The induced cells were washed with 10- fold 
decreasing concentration of pure GFP. The decreasing concentration of GFP was supposed to 
provide a gradient level to compare other fluorescence to. The induced cells with pSEVA-SDP 
did have decreasing fluorescence with decreasing concentration of GFP (Graph 1. Blue, 
orange, grey). But the induced cells that did not have any GFP (Graph 1. yellow) showed more 
fluorescence than the cells with 1/100x GFP. This could mean a few things. Either the cells 
have a basal level of fluorescence that is picked up by the microplate reader or the cells require 
more washing steps. But if the issue was the washing process to remove unbound GFP the cells 
that were uninduced and without GFP (purple) should have a much lower fluorescence which 
they do not have, in fact having a similar fluorescence to other cell cultures. Another 
speculation could be that there is something the plasmid that is expressed on the surface 
==PAGE:55==

affecting the fluorescence measurement. However that is not the case because the BL21 (DE3) 
WT cells (green) that have no plasmid also show fluorescence similar other cells.  
 
The results  of the previous experiment was not enough to provide any conclusive theories as 
to why the fluorescence of the cells was in such a manner. More GFP:NB assays had to be done 
to gain more data. One way to do that is to compare the SDP in pSEVA-SDP to a SDP in 
another plasmid that is already known to work (positive control). The SB13+ Lip Ab, a high 
copy pKA plasmid containing the SDP along with Lip Ab encoding gene. GFP:NB assay was 
performed again (Graph 2). And this time the cells containing pSEVA-SDP were measured 
with different induction and GFP conditions against the SB13 cells. The cells containing 
pSEVA-SDP in all four condition, induced: GFP+ (blue), induced: GFP- (orange), uninduced: 
GFP+ (grey) and uninduced: GFP- (yellow) were all found to have low yet similar 
fluorescence. The SB13 (red) cells however, when induced with 5mM rhamnose and washed 
with GFP, showed almost 10 times more fluorescence than the other cells. Firstly, the results 
show that the GFP:NB assay works perfectly and the protocol does not have any flaws. 
Secondly, the expression of SDP is not the problem. In order to check for flaws in the construct 
the pRha-SDP-TEV sequence from the SEVA-SDP sequencing result was aligned to the 
SB13+ Lip Ab sequencing results and found perfect alignment. So, possibility of  flaws in the 
pSEVA-SDP sequence is also eliminated.  
 
The SB13 +Lip Ab contains a functional protein sequence attached to the C- terminal of the 
nanobody sequence. The expression of SDP is increased when a functional protein is bound to 
the C- terminus of LppOmpA-NB9 . The fluorescent measured from SB13 cells would hence 
be influenced by the protein and cannot be compared to pSEVA-SDP. Therefore the Lip Ab 
sequence was removed from the pKA plasmid using USER cloning. The new plasmid was 
transformed into DH5 cells and selected on kanamycin. The select transformants were 
sequenced and the sequence alignment showed no mismatches. The pKA-SDP plasmid was 
transformed into the BL12(DE3) cells and was used for the next GFP:NB assay.  
 
In the third attempt at this assay (Graph 3) the fluorescence of cells containing pSEVA-SDP 
were measured against both SB13+ Lip Ab and pKA-SDP. The plotted results show that the 
cells containing pSEVA- SDP show the same fluorescence levels as before independent of

4.3. AbR gadgets: 
 
The collection of pSEVA-SDP plasmids with different antibiotic markers were created to add 
the SDP to the SEVA standard. It also gave me an opportunity to eliminate antibiotic resistance 
mechanism in pSEVA-SDP as a possible reason for its minimal expression. The pSEVA121-
SDP contains an ampicillin resistance gene. So the cultures used for the GFP:NB assay are also 
supplemented with ampicillin. The pKA-SDP and SB13+ Lip Ab constructs contain resistance 
to kanamycin so the cell cultures containing these plasmids were supplemented with 
kanamycin. Ampicillin being a - lactam the antibiotic resistance to it is provided by - 
lactamase produced by the gram- negative bacteria32 . The kills bacteria by destroying the cell 
wall. Upon speculation I performed another GFP:NB assay. This time with pSEVA-SDP 
constructs with different antibiotic resistance. The resistance markers are gadget offered by the 
SEVA database. They were exchanged by restriction digestion of the plasmid at standard 
restriction sites flanking antibiotic selection marker gadget in a SEVA plasmid. By ligating 
new antibiotic selection markers into the pSEVA- SDP construct a new library was quickly 
made. The new library contained pSEVA-SDP with CmpR, SpecR and GenR. I was unable to 
exchange AmpR with KanR and TetR. The new plasmids were transferred into DH5  cells 
==PAGE:57==

and selected with specific antibiotics. The transformants were sequenced and verified with 
sequence alignment.  
 
The plasmids were transferred into BL21(DE3) and used for the GFP:NB assay. The results 
from the assay were plotted into a graph. (Graph 6). The results show the same fluorescence 
pattern as the previous results. All cells containing pSEVA-SDP regardless of the antibiotic 
resistance marker and regardless of induction show similar fluorescence at same level as the 
previous GFP:NB assays. The pKA-SDP when induced with 5mM rhamnose showed 35 times 
more expression than the other constructs. Interestingly, the cells containing pKA-SDP that 
were uninduced (Graph 6, brown) also show high fluorescence. But that might be due to 
human error.  
 
Assessing all the results from multiple GFP:NB assays with different assay conditions we know 
that the SDP sequence present in the pSEVA-SDP construct does not have any mutations, the 
expression does not depend on antibiotic selection markers and the SDP expresses at a higher 
level in a different plasmid with a high copy number. This leads to the low copy RK2 origin of 
replication as a suspect for minimal expression of the SDP.  
 
RK2 in SEVA was derived from pJB785TT33 . The origin of replication allows for a plasmid 
construct with a broad host range. It is known to be conserved and stable in a variety of different 
hosts18 . One possibility that explains low expression levels is that characteristic feature of RK2 
to stabilize during replication actually drives resources toward replication rather than gene 
expression. The graph 5 showing the growth of the BL21(DE3) cells containing pSEVA-SDP, 
pKA-SDP and SB13+ Lip Ab after induction from mid- exponential growth. The cells 
containing pSEVA-SDP grow steadily more than tripling in population while the cells 
containing the high copy pKA only doubles in population. The phenomenon of reduced growth 
rate is observed during protein production due to resources being driven towards translation 
after induction34 . The fast growth of pSEVA-SDP cells might mean that the cells focus their 
energy on growth rather than protein production.

4.4. Future studies with pSEVA-SDP 
 
The first interesting step would be to make use of the SEVA standard and exchange the origin 
of replication gadget to both medium copy and high copy origins of replication. If RK2 of the 
plasmid is the issue using a origin of replication should express the SDP at a much different

Moreover SEVA standard being used by many researchers allows the library to grow. Addition 
of a surface display platform that can display any functional protein at a high efficiency could 
bring major influence in the industrial and pharmaceutical industry.

J21300 promoter was unsuccessful with only green colonies. The M9+ NiCl2 plates containing

cells were transferred to LB plates supplemented with 5mM rhamnose. The LB+ Rha plates 
had some green colonies a some white colonies indicating loss of GFP cargo. To confirm

4.6. Future studies with SEGA-SDP 
Due to time constraints of the research the SEGA+SDP from colony A could not be sequenced

assays to study the performance of SDP in the SEGA landing pad.  The SEGA landing pad is 
a standardised platform which can be distributed around the world for scientists to study and 
use for functional applications. Having the SDP on a chromosomal landing pad could possibly 
produce industrial enzymes that otherwise puts too much burden on the cell if present in the 
plasmid. So the SEGA landing pad with SDP can be optimised to express bigger enzymes at a 
higher rate. The surface display platform just has too much potential applications for “green” 
protein production to sustain the industry.

5 CONCLUSION

5. Conclusion 
 
In this research, an expression platform, Surface display platform that consist of an LppOmpA 
fusion and a nanobody was inserted into a SEVA plasmid. The platform expresses a protein on 
the surface of the cell and use the nanobody as to bind free GFP for easy quantification. I 
inserted this platform in the standardised vector to study the expression of the platform on a 
simple vector with a low- copy number.  
 
The expression of the surface display in the SEVA vector was observed to be minimal. 
However this does not mean the surface display platform does not get expressed. The controls 
used to compare the expression levels show that the surface display platform expresses just 
fine. The issue falls on the SEVA vector. The advantage in this situation is that the origin of 
replication can easily be switched in a SEVA construct. This research subtly shows the need 
for a standardised vector that has exchangeable gadgets. The surface display expression once 
optimised for SEVA vectors can be added to the collection making it accessible to scientists 
around the world.  
 
The SEGA platform built with inspiration from SEVA is chromosomal landing pad. The 
surface display platform was integrated in SEGA as well. Although the expression of surface 
display platform was minimal in SEGA just like SEVA the issues are different. The surface 
display platform can be fully functional and optimised in both SEVA vectors and the SEGA 
landing pad offering multi-level applicational opportunities from laboratory experiments to 
production of industrial enzymes.
