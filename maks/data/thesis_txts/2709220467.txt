Abstract
Msc Business Analytics
Understanding gender gaps in employment and mobility using large-scale behavioral data
by Silvia DE SOJO CASO
Recent studies have unveiled the gender gap in human mobility, with women day-to-day
travel behaviour differing from that of men. However, the scientiﬁc understanding of the factors
driving gender differences in travel patterns is limited due to the lack of suitable data. The gender
division of labour and household responsibilities have been proposed as a possible explanation
for the observed gap in mobility. Nevertheless, these hypotheses have not been investigated at
scale.
This work will quantify and describe gender differences in mobility behaviour and test if the
division of labour can partly explain the gender disparities. To achieve this goal, we split the
analysis into (i) Identify how employment inﬂuences the gender gap in mobility, (ii) Determine
how demographic features explain individuals’ entropy.
We use a novel pseudo-anonymized and GDPR-compliant dataset containing multiyear GPS
traces of half a million individuals and demographics. The gender differences in mobility are
studied by computing the number of visits, the duration of stops, and predictability. To study the
division of labour’s effect, we ﬁrst estimate the individual employment status from the mobility
data using heuristic algorithms.
Our ﬁndings indicate that the gender gap in mobility appears regardless of the employment
status. However, an individual’s employment status strongly affects their mobility behaviour
and impacts the observed gender differences in travel patterns. Furthermore, we ﬁnd a complex
interrelation between demographic features and conclude that it is not possible to explain the
gender gap in mobility with a single demographic feature.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The sustained increase in women’s participation in formal labour markets is possibly the most sig-
niﬁcant economic transformation in the last century. As women entered the labour market, cities
became more productive, and incomes soared (Black et al., 2014). Gender studies in economics
identiﬁed that for every percentage increase in women’s employment, the median of real wages
(for both men and women) and the GDP increased (Weinstein, 2017).
The International Monetary Fund endorsed that gender equality promotes economic growth
and results in better development outcomes. Furthermore, it helps reduce income inequality and
increase economic diversiﬁcation, which supports economic resilience (Policy et al., 2018). For
this reason, empowering women in the economy and closing gender gaps in the world of work is
critical to attaining the United Nations 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development and achieving
the Sustainable Development Goals (UN, 2017).
Although the female labour force participation rate has risen steadily, it has not done so evenly
across cities. Researchers identiﬁed various factors that may affect female labour force participa-
tion rates and wage growth; among these factors, we ﬁnd regional differences in gender roles
(Weinstein, 2017), industry concentration and housing prices (Patrick et al., 2016), and commute
times (Black et al., 2014). Therefore, the economic and social need for women’s economic empow-
erment is clear, but for women to work, the city needs to support it.
The United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE) found ’a male bias’ in trans-
port planning and a failure to address gender ‘in system conﬁguration’ (UNECE, 2009). Gender-
insensitive design and biased urbanisation policies reinforce geographical segregation, increase
inequalities and limits the mobility of minorities (Chant, 2013). Therefore, it is critical to investi-
gate the role of gender in urban mobility to eliminate such segregation.
Scholars began the study of gendered mobilities by surveying and analysing how mobility
and gender intersect. Cresswell et al., 2016 disclosed that how people move (where, how fast,
how often) is demonstrably gendered and reproduces gendered power hierarchies. Madariaga
et al., 2019 identiﬁed that the inﬂuence of socio-demographic factors over individuals’ roles and
responsibilities materialises in their relation to the surroundings. When desegregating the data by
gender, they identiﬁed that employment trips had a more substantial effect on males mobilities’
than females. Instead, female mobility was more conditioned by domestic and care-work related
trips.
However, surveys are limited by their small sample size, resolution, and potential reporting
biases (Llorente et al., 2015). The introduction of new technologies allowed to study new data

sources. Nevertheless, while individual mobility was studied extensively, only a handful of stud-
ies examined gender-disaggregated large-scale data (Gauvin et al., 2020, Alessandretti et al., 2020).
Gauvin et al., 2020 mapped the differences between genders in mobility using call records and
identiﬁed that women travel to fewer unique locations, and their movements tend to be more
spatially localised than men. Furthermore, they disclosed a signiﬁcant correlation between the
gender gap in mobility and the gender gap in employment, suggesting that employment status
may be a plausible reason behind the differences in mobility behaviour (Gauvin et al., 2020). Yet,
call traces remain coarse, both in space and in time, and are unsuitable for investigating mobility
as a continuous state (Llorente et al., 2015).
Alessandretti et al., 2020 used GPS traces to identify spatial containers, i.e. scales, that restrict
mobility behaviour. In their studies, they found that in some countries, the movement of females
was more contained than men, with the presence of more scale levels. They linked the gender
differences in mobility to the countries’ Gender Inequality Index (GII). However, they identiﬁed
the need to continue to explore the characteristics of containers by evaluating demographics and
contextual features.
Quantifying the differences in mobility between genders is indispensable to determine the ex-
tent of the gender gap. However, understanding the reasons behind these differences will provide
a framework for designing gender-oriented and evidence-based policies.
In this work, we are interested in understanding the relationship between mobility, gender
and employment. Our central hypothesis is that the effect of gender on mobility is to a large
extent indirect because it is mediated by other factors, including division of household and labour
responsibilities. By studying high-resolution mobility data over a large population, our main
goal is to advance the state-of-the-art by quantifying the effect of employment on mobility and
understanding the driving mechanisms of the observed differences in mobility across genders.
We use a novel data set containing multiyear GPS traces of half a million individuals and
demographics from the United Kingdom and Spain. Analysing location traces collected by smart
devices provides an unprecedented opportunity to study the reasons behind the gender gap in
mobility.
We employ state-of-the-art time-space heuristics (Lucchini et al., 2021) to identify the home
and employment location of the user. These locations allow us to categorise users as commuters
or non-commuters; then, we use these categories as a proxy of their employment status. Finally, to
evaluate the appropriateness of the method, we will analyse the stability of the results and perform
a complimentary validation against census data of the unemployment distribution across regions.
We want to contribute to this body of knowledge by looking at the inﬂuence of employment
and other demographic factors on the gender gap in mobility. Using Econometrics and Machine
Learning models, we quantify the extent to which gender impacts mobility, taking into account
factors such as age, country of residence, urbanisation and employment status.

1.1
Scope
This study integrates research on human mobility behaviour with studies on gender differences.
Our objective is to quantify and describe gender differences in mobility behaviour and determine
the inﬂuence of employment status. To achieve this goal, we divide the research as follows:
• In chapter 5, we explore the demographic characteristics and the inherent gender gaps in
the users’ mobility. We use statistical analysis to examine the gender differences in mobility
metrics distributions.
• In chapter 6, we describe the spatio-temporal heuristics, HDA and EDA, used and explore
how variations on the model’s parameters affect the stability of the results. Ultimately, the
results of this exercise allowed us to estimate the employment status of the users.
• Chapter 7 is dedicated to exploring the interrelation between mobility, gender, and employ-
ment using statistical analysis. Furthermore, we expand the study to other demographic
features and compare the relative differences between demographic groups.
• Chapter 8 aims at broadening the understanding of how socio-demographic characteristics
inﬂuence mobility. We model the mobility entropy using gender, age, country of residence,
urbanisation and employment status.
We will limit the mobility study to three mobility metrics that describe how much user moves
and how long and heterogeneous the movement is.
Finally, since the gender gap and unemployment levels are deeply rooted in each country’s
economy and cultural attributes, the analysis of the gender differences in employment and mo-
bility is constrained to Spain and the UK. This scope limitation will allow us to compare two
European countries with different employment rates (Economics, 2021) and gender gap in min-
utes of unpaid work per day (detailed in section 2.2). Furthermore, this will allow for a simpliﬁed
analysis that brings a deeper understanding of the potential differences between countries. This
work will provide a baseline for future studies that include additional countries and build up over
our conclusions.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This chapter will review the empirical ﬁndings surrounding human mobility, focusing on the
modelling of trajectories with probabilistic distributions (section 2.1). We will deepen this knowl-
edge by exploring gender differences in mobility patterns (section 2.2). After presenting the the-
oretical components, we explain the different mobility data types (section 2.3), discuss their pros
and cons, and summarize their applicable HDA and EDA algorithms (sections 2.4, 2.5).
2.1
Empirical ﬁndings on Human Mobility
The study of human mobility currently spans multiple disciplines and encompasses a variety
of topics. This review will present the progression of the ﬁeld, focusing on the characterization
of human trajectories at an individual level. For this reason, we will review some of the most
inﬂuential papers that discuss the probabilistic distributions of travelling distance and duration;
within large scale data-driven human mobility research.
First and foremost, it is necessary to remark on the differences between spatio-temporal scales.
We can study human mobility on different levels: From a population scale, with analysis on mi-
gration or travel demand, to an individual scale, where we aim to understand the mobility of
patterns of single subjects. Although the ﬁrst papers presented will depart from population-level
analysis, they mark the foundation of individual mobility research development. This project will
centre on the study of individual trajectories.

2.1.2
Evolution of the Empirical Findings
The ﬁrst ﬁeld to study mobility data was Geography. Researchers studied census data to quantify
migrations within the UK and made the ﬁrst attempts to explain and predict migration patterns
(Ravenstein, 1885). These pioneer studies identiﬁed that the main indicators determining move-
ment and interactions between places were distance constraints and socioeconomic factors. These
ﬁndings laid the groundwork for later studies on individual movement as well as the study of
differences in movement based on demographic aggregations.
In the late 20th century, measurements on albatrosses (Viswanathan et al., 1996) suggested
that diffusion processes characterize animals’ trajectories. In particular, they approximated their
displacements with the Lévy ﬂight.
Lévy ﬂights are a class of random walks whose step size, ∆r, is not constant. In general terms,
we can describe Lévy ﬂights’ path as a series of small steps with occasional large displacements.
The formulation of this stochastic process comes from choosing the step size, ∆r, from a probabil-
ity distribution with a power-law tail
P(∆r) ≃(β −1)∆r−β,
(2.5)
with ∆r > 1; β > 1 (Shlesinger et al., 1993).
More recent ﬁndings on monkeys and marine predators (Ramos-Fernández et al., 2004, Sims
et al., 2008) reafﬁrmed the Lévy ﬂight characterization for animals’ trajectories. Moreover, the
knowledge of the uncertainties surrounding human mobility patterns, such as multiple means of
transportation or imposed social restrictions, allowed researchers to hypothesize that human tra-
jectories could be approximated with random walks or diffusion models (Baronchelli et al., 2013).
Brockmann et al., 2006, quantitatively assessed if humans did indeed disperse following dif-
fusion processes at a national scale. Given the difﬁculty to obtain data about human mobility,
banknote displacements were used as a proxy for human travel. The study is considered the ﬁrst
empirical test, and it was performed by analyzing the circulation of 464,670 dollar bills in the
United States obtained from the bill tracking system whereisgeroge.com. The analysis concluded
that the distribution of travelling distances, ∆r, decays as a power law, indicating that trajectories
of banknotes, and by inference human behaviour, can be described by scale-free random walks,
Lévy ﬂights (Fig 2.1).
Although providing the ﬁrst large scale evidence of a Lévy behaviour in human mobility, the
study can not be used to assess movement at an individual level. After all, banknotes are carried
by multiple individuals and can not account for their individual trajectories.
Thanks to the widespread adoption of mobile phones in early 2000, the number of papers on
human mobility and mobility patterns displayed an almost exponential increase (Barbosa et al.,
2018).

2.2
Gender Differences in Mobility
As discussed in section 2.1.2, multiple studies have analyzed human mobility patterns with CDRs,
GPS or other sources. However, in most of the studies, there is a lack of gender-disaggregated
data, and when the data is available, it is treated as a peripheral topic (Gonzalez et al., 2008, Song
et al., 2010).
Feminist scholars performed surveys on the importance of gender in mobility and disclosed
that how people move (where, how fast, how often) is demonstrably gendered (Cresswell et al., 2016).
Considering that daily individual mobility is subjected to each individual’s roles and responsi-
bilities, it is not unexpected that demographic and socioeconomic factors will inﬂuence mobility
behaviours.
Multiple studies have reported that women continue to be the main responsible for the tasks
associated with the reproductive roles, such as the family care-work and household management
(Punt-6, 2005; Miranda, 2011; WEF, 2019). To quantify the gap between genders, economists have
aggregated some of these tasks within the term unpaid work1.
OECD studies on the gender gap in unpaid work showed that women dedicate on average
4.7 hours to unpaid work, 2.5 hours more than men (for a population aged 15-64 over the period
1998-2009) (Miranda, 2011). Furthermore, when evaluating differences by country, a negative
correlation between women’s employment rate and women’s average unpaid working time was
revealed (Figure 2.6). However, when assessing the average unpaid working time per gender by
country, it was identiﬁed that even in the country where men dedicate the highest time on unpaid
work, Denmark, the average time spent was still lower than the dedicated time by women in the
country with the lowest women unpaid working time, Norway (Miranda, 2011).
The observed connection between female employment and care-work highlight how every-
day life might differ between genders. Therefore, studying how mobility patterns differ between
gender and demographics can bring new knowledge on gender gaps behaviours.
Namely, collected unpaid work-related travel data in Madrid (Madariaga et al., 2019), found
that the number of trips made for unpaid work-related activities almost equalled those made for
employment purposes. But, when desegregating the data by gender, they identiﬁed that "care
work is the single and foremost purpose of travel for women, in much the same way as employment is the
main purpose of men’s travel" (Madariaga et al., 2019).
Moreover, several empirical studies identiﬁed that women generally travel shorter distances,
spend more time travelling, connect different trips - trip chaining - and prefer public transport and
taxi services than private cars more than men (Ng et al., 2018).
Recent studies using survey data from three major metropolitan areas in South America inves-
tigated the differences in genders, socioeconomic levels, and travel purpose. They discovered that
gender and socioeconomic status factors had a signiﬁcant impact on mobility. Although socioeco-
nomic status had a higher inﬂuence than gender, the gender gap persisted across all socioeconomic
levels (Macedo et al., 2021).

2.3
Data sources for Human Mobility
The increase of literature on human mobility during the latest decades has been a direct conse-
quence of the widespread adoption of mobile phones. The devices brought new data sources
suitable to be used as a proxy for human trajectories. Nowadays, sources such as public trans-
portation, taxis and surveys (Liang et al., 2013), Wireless RFID sensors data (Atzmueller et al.,
2016), mobile phone call records (Gonzalez et al., 2008), location-based social networks informa-
tion (Noulas et al., 2011, Jurdak et al., 2015) and data collected from GPS devices (Alessandretti
et al., 2020) can be used to infer human trajectories. This section will summarize the main char-
acteristics of the most inﬂuential used data sources in human mobility studies: surveys, mobile
phone call records, and GPS.
Surveys are perhaps the most traditional sources and can provide a detailed description of
individual attitudes and behaviours. They can provide more detailed results than other sources,
but their acquisition is generally costly in terms of ﬁnancial and human resources. Furthermore,
surveys are limited by their small sample size, resolution, and potential reporting biases. Besides
these limitations, surveys are hard to conduct regularly. Having surveys "out-of-sync" has been
a tendency in countries going through economic recession, which either decided to discontinue
them or altered their update rate (Llorente et al., 2015). Yet, surveys deﬁne a reliable framework
and can be used as a validation set for other techniques.
The introduction of mobile phones ameliorated this situation. In a few years, mobile phone
subscriptions’ world coverage rose from 12% of the world population in 2000 (ICT, 2014), to 108%
in 2019, reaching 8.3 billion subscriptions (ICT, 2019). The Call Data Records (CDRs) are collected,
for billing purposes, by mobile phone operators. They contain an enormous amount of informa-
tion on how, when, and with whom we communicate. In the past decade, mobile phone traces
have been demonstrated to provide valuable, timely, ﬁne-grained information on human mobility
(Blondel et al., 2015; Barbosa et al., 2018). Together with the progress in computing power and
data-mining methods, the data obtained from CDRs enabled to capture movement at the hour’s
scale from individuals to nation-wide spatial scales. However, it has been argued that mobile
phone traces remain too coarse, both in space and in time, and are unsuitable for investigating de-
tails such as human choices (Lima et al., 2016). For this reason, mobile phone data is categorized
within the non-continuous location traces (Vanhoof et al., 2018).
On the other hand, the adoption rate of devices equipped with location sensors offers un-
precedented possibilities to study individual mobility at a ﬁner-grained level. Global Positioning
System (GPS) data is recorded with a high frequency such that they can be treated as continuous
trajectories (Lima et al., 2016). The positioning with GPS is obtained by measuring the arrival time
of signals transmitted from multiple satellites. The accuracy of GPS is susceptible to the presence
of buildings and other obstacles that disturb satellites signals. Thus the accuracy can vary from
2m in rural areas to 20m in urban areas (Renso et al., 2008). Differently from GPS, in cellular ar-
chitecture for mobile data, an area is covered by several antennas. Each area is partitioned in a
number of, possibly overlapping, cells, uniquely identiﬁed by the antenna. Multiple methods can
be used to calculate the approximate position of the mobile device with CDR data. However, the
accuracy level is maintained lower than the accuracy obtained with GPS traces for both rural and
==PAGE:24==

urban areas (Renso et al., 2008), which highlights the beneﬁts of using a GPS based data set.
2.4
Home Detection Algorithms
The emergence of new data sources boosted the development of new models. However, along
with the novel opportunities, new challenges appeared. Now, the latest data sources require addi-
tional pre-processing techniques before obtaining clear information. Perhaps the most important
step for the analysis of individual location traces is identifying where someone lives, in other
words, detecting their home location (Vanhoof et al., 2018). Being able to detect a user’s residence
allows the study of mobility at the individual level and, at the same time, it enables the combina-
tion of other data sources, such as census data.
Home Detection Algorithms (HDAs) are the different methods used to attribute a potential
home location to every user in the database from the geographical metadata. The characteristics
of the data sources condition the HDA techniques to employ. Following Vanhoof et al., 2018
characterization, we will split the methods into non-continuous location traces, i.e. mobile phone
data, online social networks, and continuous location traces, i.e. GPS, Wi-Fi positioning.
A larger body of literature evaluates the quality of HDAs for non-continuous location traces.
In Vanhoof et al., 2018, the quality of the home detection from CDRs is assessed. It differentiates
models from being single-step or two-step approaches. Single-step methods use a decision rule
based on an a priori deﬁnition of home. Instead, the two-step methods cluster individual cell tow-
ers together as meaningful places before annotating them using a decision rule. The paper focuses
on the comparison between different single-step algorithms and concludes that algorithms incor-
porating time-constrains outperform several alternatives when tested at a national level ground
truth data. Nonetheless, the paper highlights that the magnitude of mismatch, a maintained 35◦
gap, is indicative of the severity of the home location problem and challenges the validity of single-
step approaches in the literature.
Following the call for further HDA validation from Vanhoof et al., 2018, the paper Pappalardo
et al., 2020 provides a dataset that allows the validation of HDAs on individual-level ground truth
data. The paper analyses multiple HDAs in three streams of mobile phone records and calculates
their corresponding accuracy. Ultimately, the time-constrained algorithm with decision rule home
locations is the most active towers during night time (between 7 pm and 7 am, inclusive); achieves the
best overall accuracy. Moreover, the paper studies how the accuracy of detecting home locations
changes with the number of records per user. The paper concludes that just a small fraction of the
records is enough to accurately estimate an individual’s home location.
However, for continuous traces, the literature is limited. Vanhoof et al., 2018, propose that
after identifying the stops, the annotation of the meaningful places (such as home or work) can
be done through interpretation, by surveying or employing time-space heuristics. On the latter,
the author cites Nurmi et al., 2008, which proposes a fully automated, non-parametric Bayesian
approach for identifying places from GPS data. However, the algorithm is not tested at scale and
lacks the ﬁnal annotation step. Ergo, the algorithm can identify stops with meaningful location
clusters, but does not label them into a speciﬁc category, i.e. home, work.

continuous traces. Validation methods are not presented in the latest studies, which highlights
the acceptance of the method but at the same time opens up the door for new studies to deﬁne
assessment procedures.
2.5
Employment Detection Algorithms
Employment Detection Algorithms (EDA) are the counterpart of HDAs that identify a potential
employment location to every user in the data. Lucchini et al., 2021 perform an analogous exercise
to the proposed HDAs sliding window. First, they identify as potential employment location, the
most visited location during weekdays, from 9 am to 5 pm, which lasts at least 30 minutes and
have a frequency of 5 visits per week. Then, aggregates by user and stop in a given day, the time
spent in [t-14, t+14] and marks as Employment the stop that has the highest potential employment
time within the window and is not a Home location.
The methods for HDA and EDA applied by Lucchini et al., 2021, present the limitation ex-
tracting home and work locations for night and weekend jobs. For individuals with night jobs,
the algorithm will result in work times complementary to the residential times of daily jobs. How-
ever, weekend jobs are characterized by few work hours that might make the work detection noisy
when considered. The author describes that this limitation is well known by existing literature.
To the best of our knowledge, there are no high-quality data sets of home and work locations to
validate existing algorithms systematically.
It is important to remark that the primary goal of an EDA is to detect an employment location.
However, differently from the HDA, the cases where a an employment location is not identiﬁed
are of particular interest since they will highlight unemployed users. Therefore, EDAs will allow
us to study the differences in mobility between employment statuses.
Although literature concerning EDAs is not extensive, other sources explore how can regional
unemployment be reconstructed from mobility traces. In particular, Llorente et al., 2015 study
how social-media data can be used to reveal information of the city-level socio-economical status.
The study is built using geo-located Twitter messages in Spain and evaluates how human activity,
mobility, and interpersonal communication correlate with municipalities unemployment levels.
They identify that regions exhibiting more diverse mobility ﬂuxes display lower unemployment
rates. The diversity of movement is measured using the entropy of the number of different areas

unemployment. The regions with lower unemployment display a strong peak of activity in the
morning and lower activity levels during the rest of the day.
Even though the paper does not deal with the detection of the employment location, its con-
clusions are relevant to consider the diversity of movement as a potential indicator in enhanced
EDAs.
2.6
Summary
The stat-of-the-art deﬁnes a set of fundamental metrics that describe mobility behaviours. Al-
though previous researchers have already identiﬁed a gender gap between mobility metrics, there
is still the need to explore why these differences appear. Previous results suggested that this gap
could be partially explained by the gendered division of work in households, but these hypotheses
have not been tested at scale.
High-resolution mobility data can be used for this purpose. GPS data has outperformed other
sources’ accuracy and is suitable for studying individuals’ mobility. In particular, the data used
in Alessandretti et al., 2020 paper shows strong potential and demonstrates that powerful results
can be obtained from this dataset.
Nevertheless, before analyzing the data, it is necessary to identify each user’s home location
and employment status. Although the literature on HDAs with GPS data is limited, Lucchini et al.,
2021, propose an heuristic algorithm that ﬁts the need of our study. Consequently, both HDA and
EDA will follow the approach proposed by Lucchini et al., 2021. We will apply a time-constrained
decision rule calculated over a sliding window. Yet, the single-step method employed in the paper
can be complemented with an evaluation of the sliding window results.

3.2.3
Mobility Data
The original data set consisted of a series of GPS coordinates (latitudes and longitudes) and the
time of each recording. However, previous to this project, the data set was treated, and users’ GPS
traces were transformed to stops using the scalable stop-location detection algorithm InfoStop
(Aslak et al., 2020). The algorithm was designed to detect stops from sequences of spatio-temporal
data, leveraging the ﬂow-based network community detection algorithm Infomap (Rosvall et al.,
2009). The algorithm reduces the raw data to stays and destinations where an individual was
stationary. 4 parameters characterise it: the maximum roaming distance allowed for two points
within the same stay (r1), the minimum duration of a stay (tmin), the maximum time difference
between two consecutive records for them to be considered within the same stay (tmax) and the
typical distance between two stays in the same destination (r2). In the given data set, the param-
eters were set to: r1 = r2 = 20m, which represents that if an individual was moving within this
radius, the movement was aggregated as one location, this is aligned with the accuracy level of
GPS signals in urban areas. The temporal parameters were set to tmin = 10 minutes if a user is in a
position for at least 10 min a stop is detected and tmax = 24 hours, which represents that if there is
not a new GPS trace in the data, the maximum duration for a stop will be one day.
Therefore, the data set that will be employed throughout the study is based on a collection of

3.3.2
Daily Trajectories
Before being able to infer home or employment locations, it is necessary to transform the list of
stops per users into daily trajectories (Fig. 3.5).
The current time-scale has information at the seconds level, but to be able to interpret indi-
vidual trajectories it is necessary to perform an aggregation at a higher resolution. We consider
it acceptable to aggregate the stops at an hourly level since both home and employment can be
considered long-stay stops, compared to the other stops that may appear each day.
To obtain the hourly sequence per day and user, we need to to take in to account two chal-
lenges that difﬁcult the implementation: Some stops may cover more than one day, and some
may overlap at the start or end hours.
FIGURE 3.5: Schema of HDA and EDA pre-processing step. Description of the
transformation from a list of user stops to individual daily trajectories, with locations
at hourly level
The ﬁrst goal of the implementation is to detect the stops that cover more than one day and
split them.
First of all, we calculated the duration of a stop outside the ﬁrst day. When a stop only hap-
pened over one day this duration is set to zero, otherwise, it provides the excess hours which
indicate how many additional days the stop comprises. To perform the stop split per day, the
stops are divided into sub-data-frames based on the number of additional days needed. This
allowed to accordingly update all the impacted parameters such as stop_duration, start_day,
end_hour, etc. Lastly, the data-frames were merged back together.
With the stops split by day, it was possible to include all the hours of the day as columns in
the data frame and ﬁll the location_id in the corresponding stop range. The ﬁnal transformation
consisted of aggregating all stops daily. In case of conﬂict, with two stops falling in the same
hour bucket, the logic applied prioritised the stop with the longest duration. This is aligned with
our goal to detect long-stay stops. The implementation was performed taking advantage of the
pyspark Window and UDFs functionalities.

4.2
Statistical Methods
4.2.1
Central tendency indicator
To study the central or typical value for a probability distribution, it is necessary to select a mea-
sure aligned with the distribution of the study’s data.
The most common central measures used in statistics are the arithmetic mean, median, and
mode. As observed in section 5.2, the stop duration and the number of stops are not normally
distributed and instead present a highly skewed tail. Using the mean in these circumstances
would not be the most appropriate indicator since the mean is very sensible over extreme points
and would be impacted by the tail outliers. However, the median, being the middle value of the
distribution, is not affected by outliers in the same way and provides a better approximation of
the "typical" value in skewed distributions. Therefore, the median is used as a central tendency
indicator.
Likewise, in skewed distributions, the non-symmetric spread of the data can not be accurately
described with the standard deviation. Alternatively, we use the IQR (Q3 −Q1) and the range of
the extreme values to depict the spread of the data.
4.2.2
Bootstrap Sampling
The main goal of bootstrap sampling is to estimate the distribution of a statistical property (e.g.,
mean, median) based on a given sample (Conejo et al., 2010). It is an alternative approach to
hypothesis testing and allows comparing statistical parameters and visualising their distributions.
The method takes the original sample data of the study and resamples it to create simulated
samples. The resampling is performed with replacement, maintaining the probability of the draws
independent of each other. Consequently, the method considers the original sample as a proxy for
the entire population and then draws random samples from it. The principal assumption of the
method is that the original sample accurately represents the actual population. As discussed in
section 3.4, studies on smartphone penetration suggest that the results obtained from smartphone
data can reveal the population-level behaviour.
After drawing the different samples, we can compute their statistical properties and observe
their sampling distribution. For instance, we can plot the distribution of the median of all the
samples (Fig. 4.1).

4.2.3
Test signiﬁcance
Since our objective is to compare the distributions of the mobility indicators between genders, we
can validate the signiﬁcant difference of the distributions with hypothesis testing.
The signiﬁcance level has been deﬁned to 0.05 for all evaluations, and the following statistical
tests have been used:
• Mann-Whitney U test, also known as Wilcoxon rank-sum test, is a non-parametric test (it
does not assume the form of the underlying distribution). The test is used for comparing in-
dependent groups of continuous data. Therefore, the test will be used to examine differences
across groups of users with independent observations when no subject can be found in both
groups. The null hypothesis is that two samples originate from the same distribution. On
the other hand, Low p-values will rule out the null hypothesis, implying that the two distri-
butions are signiﬁcantly different. Because the test is based on ranking the observations, it
focuses on the difference in means (Hodges Jr et al., 1963).
• Kolmogorov–Smirnov test is a non-parametric statistical test that is used to evaluate the
statistical difference of two distributions. The maximum distance between the two empiri-
cal cumulative distributions is the statistical value employed. As a result, the KS takes the
whole distribution into account, while the Mann-Whitey test is focused on the ranking of
observations. Low p-values will reject the null hypothesis and indicate a signiﬁcant sta-
tistical difference between the two distributions (Chakravarti et al., 1967). However, one
important limitation is that it only applies to continuous distributions. As a result, the KS
test is applied in this study for all continuous distributions, whereas the Mann–Whitney U
test is used only on the few occasions when the distributions are discrete. The KS test can
be applied for one sample against parametric distributions or between two samples to test
their statistical difference. Both forms are used during the analysis.
• Levene test is available in a parametric and non-parametric form and evaluates the equal-
ity of variances of two samples. The null hypothesis is that the population variances are
equal (homogeneity of variance or homoscedasticity). If the resulting p-value is less than
the signiﬁcance level, then the obtained differences in sample variances are unlikely to have
occurred based on random sampling from a population with equal variances (Derrick et al.,
2018). The test is only employed to evaluate if the results obtained from the N-way ANOVA
are relevant for the case.
• Shapiro test tests the null hypothesis that a sample came from a normally distributed pop-
ulation. However, when the sample size is sufﬁciently large, the test may detect even trivial
departures from the null hypothesis, impacting the ﬁnal result of the p-value. However, the
test also provides the W statistic, which continues to be accurate in large samples. The W
statistic measures how well the ordered and standardised sample quantiles ﬁt the standard
normal quantiles. The statistic ranges from 0 to 1, with 1 being a perfect match (Shapiro
et al., 1965). We will use the test to evaluate the normality of the mobility metrics and linear
model errors.

Sliding Window Technique
A sliding window problem is a subset of dynamic programming problems. They can be identiﬁed
as an optimisation exercise where we need to run a sub-list over a collection of data iteratively. It
is a standard method that reduces the time complexity of calculations when we need to resolve
overlapping sub-problems (Piterman, 2018).
The sliding window problem we have at hand is to identify the home and employment lo-
cations of an individual, considering not only the daily activity (row-level calculation) but the
activities performed on the contiguous days (Fig. 4.2). A sliding window aggregates the activi-
ties within the window range and selects the "most common location" to solve this problem. The
sliding window is introduced within the HDA and EDA algorithms (sections 6.1, 6.2). The imple-
mentation details, such as selecting the window range and the "most common" optimisation, are
explained within the different algorithm versions described.

Machine Learning Modelling
Machine learning is the ﬁeld of research that seeks to transform empirical data into usable models
by using computational algorithms. The models use observations to learn patterns and the possi-
ble interactions of the available features. The models employ the obtained knowledge to predict
the desired output in supervised learning.
Inspecting how the distributions behave when controlling different features can highlight com-
pelling behaviours; however, when the variables present complex inter-relations, our ability to
understand and read these relations is limited. Having interrelated variables is common when
studying human behaviours, where variables are likely to interact. For instance, we could hy-
pothesise that the gender gap in mobility might interact with the urbanisation level. The way we
move in cities is different from the countryside, but are the differences between genders the same
in both areas? Perhaps the differences widen, or maybe only in a speciﬁc age range. To broaden
our understanding of the patterns and interactions of the features, we complemented our analy-
sis with machine learning models. Understanding how each feature contributes to explaining the
outcome variance showcases the relative importance of each feature and allows us to comprehend
which factors are impacting mobility the most.

4.4.2
Model Training
Because we are primarily interested in analysing the features’ relative importance on the predicted
variable, we ran the linear regression on the entire data set without regularisation. However, for
the gradient boosting model, we performed cross-validation.
First, we split the data into train and test, keeping 15% of the observations for the ﬁnal test
evaluation. XGBoost implementation presents a broad number of parameters that can be adjusted
(XGBoost, 2014). To ensure we obtain the best possible results, it is necessary to understand how
the different parameters impact the results. We have focused on the following tree booster param-
eters:
• n_estimators The number of trees in the forest. If the n_estimators is higher than one, we
have multiple trees and the algorithm will predict the results of each tree and calculate the
result for the ensemble sequentially.
• learning_rate The step size shrinking during the update stage to prevent overﬁtting. It
ranges from 0 to 1, and the higher the learning rate, the higher the shrink of the feature
weights, resulting in a more conservative boosting process.
• gamma The minimum loss reduction required to make a new partition on a leaf node of the
tree. The larger gamma is, the more conservative the algorithm will be. It ranges from 0 to ∞.
• max_depth Maximum depth of a tree. Increasing this value will make the model more com-
plex and more likely to overﬁt. It ranges from 1 to ∞.
• min_child_weight In a child, the minimum sum of instance weight required. The build-
ing process will stop partitioning if the tree partition step leads in a leaf node with a sum
of instance weight less than the minimum set. The larger min_child_weight is, the more
conservative the algorithm will be.
• subsample The training instances’ sub-sample ratio. Setting it to 0.5 implies that XGBoost
will randomly sample half of the training data before building trees. Overﬁtting will be
avoided as a result of this. Every boosting iteration will include one sub-sampling.
==PAGE:44==
Chapter 4. Methods
35
• reg_lambda The L2 regularisation term on the weights, which is the sum of the squared
coefﬁcients, shrinks the regression coefﬁcients so that variables, with a minor contribution
to the outcome, get their coefﬁcients close to zero. The increase of lambda translates into a
more conservative model.
• reg_alpha The L1 regularisation term on the weights, which is the sum of the absolute coef-
ﬁcients. The penalty has the effect of forcing some of the coefﬁcient estimates, with a minor
contribution to the model, to be exactly equal to zero.
To improve the model’s accuracy, we have deﬁned a space of possible values that the param-
eters can be set to and performed a randomised search to ﬁnd the most optimal combination of
hyperparameters. Random search is a cost-effective (in computational power) and time-effective
(in computational time) model training technique, that randomly selects multiple hyperparam-
eter sets from the possible space and compares the results of each model with cross-validation
(Bergstra et al., 2012). Cross-validation is a resampling procedure for evaluating machine learning
models using limited data samples. The train data set is divided in k f olds, and the model itera-
tively picks k f olds −1 parts to train the model and one to evaluate it. Considering the available
data, we used 5-fold cross-validation and kept the model’s hyperparameters that resulted in the
best average score over the data splits.
4.4.3
Results evaluation
This exercise aims ﬁrst to compare the different versions of the models and second, evaluate, for
the selected model, how the features explain the outcome and understand their possible interac-
tions.
We calculated the R2 mean and standard deviation of the cross-validation folds to evaluate the
model results. However, when using the same data for evaluation and hyperparameter tuning,
the test data in each fold of the cross-validation procedure is also used to choose the features.
Therefore, this evaluation could lead to biases in the performance analysis. To avoid that, we
compare the cross-validation results against the test set. Ultimately, using the isolated test will
allow evaluating the out-of-model errors.
We use different methods to assess the feature’s importance depending on the model.
In the linear regression, we can directly use the coefﬁcients βi value to understand the degree
of importance of the coefﬁcients and their sign to understand the direction of their contribution.
However, we complement the coefﬁcients assessment with the features’ relative importance. The
relative importance is calculated by partitioning the models’ R2 over the coefﬁcients. The relative
importance is irrelevant when the predictors are uncorrelated since it would translate into the
sum of each uni-variate regression R2 value. However, with correlated features, as expected in
our case, the model’s total R2 of the model is partitioned by averaging over multiple regression
models with different combinations of the predictors (Grömping, 2007).
==PAGE:45==
Chapter 4. Methods
36
We test the signiﬁcant difference between the categorical values in the ANOVA analysis. Two
null hypotheses are tested simultaneously, the within and between hypotheses. The within hy-
pothesis tests if there is no difference in the group means for each independent variable. In con-
trast, the between hypothesis tests if there are no interactions between variables, in other words,
if the effect of one independent variable does not depend on the effect of another independent
variable. The signiﬁcance tests are performed using the F statistic, a group-wise comparison. It
measures the variance in each group’s mean to the dependent variable’s overall variance. The F
value will be higher if the variance within groups is smaller than the variance between groups,
indicating a higher likelihood that the difference observed is real and not due to chance. The
dependence on the variance comparisons highlights the importance of the equal variances as-
sumptions. We can use the F statics and their respective p-values values to evaluate the feature
importance.
Gradient boosting is also an explainable model because it employs decision trees as weak
predictors. Nevertheless, when increasing the depth of the trees, the complexity increases and
understanding how the features contribute can become challenging. However, some tools pro-
vide new approaches to assess the model feature importance. In particular, we use Shapley values
with Shap library. Game theory is the central concept underlying the Shapley value’s applications
in machine learning models. The model’s input features are linked with players in a game, and
the model function with the game’s rules. Using fair allocation from cooperative game theory, the
credit for a model’s output, the game score, is divided among its input features, the players (Lund-
berg et al., 2017). Shapley values explain feature contributions reasonably since they satisfy the
symmetry, additive and efﬁciency properties. They are considered an excellent interpretation tool
that allows us to understand which variables are used in most splits and give the most signiﬁcant
gain in impurity.

The metrics selected for the mobility studies are the monthly number of stops, the stop dura-

Longer durations and fewer stops indicate that females’ movement is more contained, travel-

The study of temporal patterns highlighted a difference in behaviours between males and fe-

Chapter 6
Algorithms Implementation
This chapter presents the developed algorithms for home and employment detection. Both algo-
rithms present parameters that can be adjusted after implementation based on the observed data
behaviours. A set of metrics is deﬁned and evaluated to identify the best parameter combination.
In particular, we are interested in maximizing the stability of the results. During the HDA eval-
uation, we will ensure that the ﬁnal version minimizes the ﬂuctuation of the residence selected.
Similarly, during the EDA evaluation, we will ensure that the employment status only swaps on
sharp mobility changes.
Both sections, HDA and EDA, are subdivided by the method behind the algorithm implemen-
tation, the metrics deﬁned for the evaluation, the ﬁnal stability characterization of the versions
and ﬁnal version selection.
Furthermore, the ﬁnal version of the algorithm is validated against census data. The employ-
ment algorithm will enable the study of the differences in mobility based on employment status.
6.1
Home Detection Algorithms
This section aims to adapt the HDA methods described in the literature review, section 2.4, to our
data characteristics. We will explain the research process followed and present the implementation
of the most relevant algorithms tested, HDA-A and HDA-B.
The two methods studied contain an iterative process that allows us to adjust the algorithm’s
inner parameters and the sliding window range. For this reason, we can obtain different versions
of the same algorithm. To compare the versions, we will introduce a set of metrics that will allow
us to perform a qualitative assessment. The objective is to assess the method’s stability to select
the most robust solution.
6.1.1
Algorithm Description
The algorithm will study the user’s daily trajectories, obtained after pre-processing the stops,
detailed in section 3.3.2. The ﬁrst step is to identify the Potential Residences.
Lucchini et al., 2021 identiﬁed the home location on the hourly range from 8 pm to 4 am for
weekdays and weekends. To validate the range’s suitability with our data set, we study how
the stop duration evolves hourly (Fig.5.6). We conﬁrm the range’s appropriateness within the
observed longer stays (details in section 5.2.1). Moving forward, we will denominate this range
home-hours range.
==PAGE:57==
Chapter 6. Algorithms Implementation
48
From this point, our objective is to deﬁne an algorithm that evaluates the list of potential loca-
tions, one user at a time during the home-hours range. Then, from the list of possible residences,
the algorithms need to select one location for a given day, considering the activity on that day and
the activities performed on the contiguous days. To achieve it, we need ﬁrst to deﬁne the opti-
mization objective function, the operation that will allow us to select the most common location.
Second, since we need to aggregate the information of the contiguous days iteratively, we need to
deﬁne an aggregation technique.
The ﬁrst method tested was the HDA-M, where we aggregated the daily potential residences
monthly and identiﬁed as home the residence that appeared more times within the given month.
The HDA-M algorithm allowed us to get familiarized with the data and the implementation meth-
ods. However, this method was very rigid, and the aggregation performed did not allow us to
study the changes at a daily level. For this reason, we discarded this method and solved the
problem with the sliding windows technique (section 4.3).
As discussed in the literature review, section 2.4, Lucchini et al., 2021 deﬁnes a sliding window
of 28 days and does not explain or validate the selected window range. In this study, we iterate
through multiple time windows and study the effects of increasing the window range. The ranges
are deﬁned as [t −X, t + X] days to consider the impact of both past and future behaviours. For
instance, with this range, an actual change of residence (moving-out) would be reﬂected earlier
than considering only past data. The selection process of the most appropriate window range,
will be detailed in section 6.1.2.
When it comes to deﬁning the objective functions, we can aggregate the different versions in
two main procedures:
HDA-A
In version HDA-A, we fractured the algorithms into a set of steps. First, we assessed each user’s
locations within the home-hours range and selected a Potential Daily Residence as the location with
the highest number of visits on the given day. Second, we aggregated the Potential Daily Residences
within the evaluated window range and selected the location with the highest number of visits in
the window range as the ﬁnal Day Residence (Fig.6.1).
FIGURE 6.1: HDA-A. Schema of the algorithm logic and example of the implemen-

==PAGE:58==

This method was based on the count of locations’ appearances and did not include a minimum
number of visits for a location to be considered home. This represents that for all the days in the
data set a location was identiﬁed as home regardless of the number of times the locations was
visited.
The division into sub-problems simpliﬁed the implementation of the method but created a loss
of data. Once the location was selected at a daily level as Daily Potential Residence, all the other
locations in the home-hour range were dropped. As a result, the sliding window aggregation only
considered the most common locations of the days in the range.
We run the algorithms with multiple windows ranging from 14 to 56 days with bi-weekly
intervals.
HDA-B
To resolve the issues detected with HDA-A, we designed a new algorithm. The objective was
two-fold, ﬁrst to aggregate the sub-steps deﬁned in HDA-A into a single iteration to avoid data
loss. Second, to create an additional layer of rules that would allow us to ﬁlter out days when we
don’t have enough data to detect a location.
The primary difference with HDA-A comes from the objective function deﬁned. Instead of
maximizing the visits count, we maximize the visit frequency over the available daily data. We
calculated the number of hours a location appeared for each day against the number of available
hours on the given day. This procedure allowed us to compare the days against each other without
being inﬂuenced by potential hours without data. Furthermore, since all frequencies were kept on
the same scale, it was possible to introduce ﬁlters on the minimum frequency for a location to be
considered home.
Another essential modiﬁcation was to keep the daily dictionary of locations’ frequencies in-
stead of selecting one location as the potential daily residence. With this modiﬁcation, we avoided
data loss at a daily level.
Before performing the aggregation with the sliding window technique, ﬁrst, we evaluated the
amount of available data within the home-hours range. Then, we introduced a ﬁlter to ensure a
minimum number of hours of data, the minimum hours’ boundary. For each day on the data set, if
we did not have enough stops in the home-hours range, we did not consider the information of the
given day. The home-hour range comprised 9 hours, and we deﬁned the boundary at a minimum
of 3 hours of data. At this stage, 9% of the days did not have enough data points in the home
hours range. The heuristic decision to set the boundary at 3 hours was taken after evaluating the
distribution of hours with data displayed in ﬁgure 6.2.

6.1.2
Metrics for HDA quality assessment
To study the stability of the different ranges and versions explored, we deﬁned a the following
metrics:
• Nº of days without a residence detected. Count the number of days when no locations
exceeded the boundaries deﬁned. We aim at maximizing the number of days with residences
detected to avoid data loss.
• Nº of different residences detected. Count the number of distinct residences detected for
each user. We aim at minimizing this number and observing more than one residence in
only a limited number of users.
• Nº of backwards changes. Flag every time a residence re-appears as home and count the
number of incidences. The metric highlighted the cases where a non-deﬁnitive move oc-
curred, indicating potential algorithm errors.
• Nº of days per residence. For each distinct residence, count the number of days that the
residence appeared per user.
We ﬁrst evaluated the distribution of the selected metrics. Second, to compare algorithms, we
computed the density and proportions of the desired levels, i.e., the fraction of users with one
residence detected.

6.2
Employment Detection Algorithms
This section presents the steps performed during the EDA implementation and details the heuris-
tics that characterize the selected EDA.
The logic behind the EDA implementation departs from the stat-of-the-art heuristics presented
by Lucchini et al., 2021. The authors identiﬁed the workplace as the most visited location during
weekdays, from 9 am to 5 pm, which lasts at least 30 minutes and have a frequency of 5 visits per
week. We have adapted the three pillars described in our study by considering the data quality
and the project’s ﬁnal objective.
First, we have included the work-hours range and the focus on weekdays. Although this can
be considered the most common work-week schedule in the countries explored (both with a more
substantial presence of urban areas), we will not be able to detect night or weekend shifts. Further-
more, when it comes to partial shifts, we will employ ﬁlters that allow certain ﬂexibility. Second,
we introduced the duration and weekly frequency heuristics as a set of ﬁlters that can be adjusted
based on the results obtained from the evaluation of the algorithm metrics.
Ultimately, the EDA will identify a location as employment for each window range that will
indicate if a user is a commuter or non-commuter. Before the start of the COVID-19 pandemic,
the vast majority of employed persons in the EU-27 mainly worked at their employer’s or their
own premises (79.0% in 2019). Only 2.9% of employed persons worked mainly at home (Eurostat,
2020). For this reason, we used the commuter categories as a proxy of their employment status.
6.2.1
Algorithm implementation
The ﬁrst step was to apply the HDA. If the HDA did not identify any residence for a user, the user
was removed from the data-set before implementing the EDA.
The implementation of the EDA shared the same structure and logic as the presented HDA-
B. The objective function of the EDA maximized the visit frequency while keeping the deﬁned
constraints. Therefore, the ﬁrst step of the algorithm was to obtain a daily level dictionary of
potential workplaces with their hourly frequency over the work-hours range.
At a daily level, we applied the minimum hours’ boundary to ensure a minimum number of
hours of data. We discarded the day’s information if it did not have enough data in the work-
hours range. We set the boundary to a minimum of 3 hours of data, following the same logic
detailed for the HDA-B, which ﬁltered out 19.5% of the days (Fig.6.15). Since the weekends were
not part of the employment evaluation, we discarded their daily data.
Another constraint was to discard the set of locations previously identiﬁed as home for the
user from the list of potential workplaces. This constraint represented a signiﬁcant drop of 26%
days. The fraction of days dropped by the two constraints is depicted in ﬁgure 6.16.

Lastly, since our objective is to differentiate users by their employment status, we deﬁned a

pass the frequency ﬁlters or were identiﬁed as home. We deﬁned the days that did not pass the
data quality ﬁlters as null.
6.2.2
Metrics for EDA quality assessment
To study the stability of the different ranges and versions explored, we deﬁned a the following
metrics:
• Nº of days without employment status. Count the number of days that did not pass the
data-quality ﬁlters. We aim at maximizing the number of days with employment status, to
avoid data loss, while keeping reasonable boundaries.
• Nº of different employment locations detected. Count the number of distinct locations de-
tected as employment for each user. We aim at minimizing this number and identifying
more than one highly frequent daily location in a limited number of users.
• Nº of employment location backwards changes. Flag every time a employment location
re-appears and count the number of incidences. The metric highlighted the cases where a
non-deﬁnitive change of employment occurred, indicating potential algorithm errors.
• Nº of status changes per user. For each user, count the number of swaps between the daily
employment status.
• Nº of unemployed users. For each user aggregated the status at monthly level and count the
number of unemployed users per month.
We ﬁrst evaluated the distribution of the selected metrics. Second, to compare algorithms, we
computed the density and proportions of the desired levels, i.e., the fraction of users with one
employment location detected.

Entropy
According to the gender differences discovered in the exploratory analysis (section 5), female
visits are more heterogeneous than males. The entropy distribution between employment status
shows that employed and unemployed users have signiﬁcantly different behaviours. What is
more, the deviation between employed and unemployed is stronger than the deviation identiﬁed
between genders (see Appendix C.3.1, Fig.C.30).
The distribution peak for unemployed users falls on higher entropy levels, and the boot-
strapped samples show a higher median for the unemployed than the employed. The identi-
ﬁed lower entropy for the employed matches our initial assumptions; more routinary behaviours
translate into lower movement diversity, thus lower entropies. In the UK, the mean and standard
error of the medians for unemployed was 3.36 ± 0.017 while for employed was 2.65 ± 0.064. Sim-
ilarly, in Spain, we identiﬁed 3.51 ± 0.025 for the unemployed and 2.96 ± 0.009 for the employed.
These results can be found in Appendix C.3.1, Fig.C.30.
Nonetheless, when stratifying the employment statuses per gender, we detected signiﬁcant
differences between males and females in both employment situations. The KS test conﬁrmed
different distributions, but the gender gap in employed and unemployed users had notable dis-
parities.
The difference between genders for the employed followed the same behaviour as the over-
all population (Fig.5.8 in section 5); females presented a higher peak shifted towards higher en-
tropy values (lower variance and higher mean than males). For unemployed users, although
both genders displayed higher entropy than their employed counterparts, the difference between
employed and unemployed males was higher than the difference between females. These dif-
ferences resulted in unemployed females presenting a peak in lower entropy values than males
(lower variance and lower mean). The detected swap on the gender gap highlights that although
employment does not explain the gender gap on its own, it has a substantial impact on mobility
and this impact is different by gender. We identiﬁed the same behaviour for the UK (Fig.7.8) and
Spain (results detailed in the Appendix, Fig.C.37).
Reﬂecting on the EDA implementation and the known differences between unemployed males
and females, we hypothesise a possible explanation.
We identiﬁed as unemployed, the users who did not have a persistent location, different from
home, during traditional ofﬁce hours. Given the broad usage of these methods in the literature, it
is fair to assume that the algorithm can correctly detect unemployment. Furthermore, the studied
data set only included users within employment ages.
However, a study performed by the Ofﬁce of National Statistics in the UK surveyed 6,713
mothers and 5,447 fathers and identiﬁed that from April to June 2019, 17.5% of mothers and 1.8%
of fathers were economically inactive and looking after their family home (National Statistics,
2019). Then again, we did not consider the stay-at-home parents during the algorithm implemen-
tation. The relatively high fraction of stay-at-home mothers suggests that some females identiﬁed
with the EDA as unemployed will be stay-at-home parents who don’t do and don’t look for paid
work. Furthermore, we expect that the inherent differences between unemployed and stay-at-
home parents will be reﬂected in mobility behaviours. In particular, we suspect that stay-at-home

7.4
Summary
We initiate the analysis of the gender differences in mobility by testing whether gender differences
are still present when ﬁltering per demographic characteristics. First, we investigate the dispari-
ties between genders for employed and unemployed users separately. In all metrics, the genders
display different distributions in each group. Then, we extend the same analysis for all the de-
mographic features and reach the same conclusion: A single demographic feature can not explain
alone the gender differences in the mobility metrics.
Nevertheless, it was still possible to detect interesting behaviours. The gender gap in entropy
changed its behaviour for the unemployed. While for all the other features, females had higher
entropy levels than males, males presented a higher entropy than females for unemployed users.
We hypothesise that the presence of stay-at-home mothers might be inﬂuencing these results.
Lastly, we study the differences in the medians for each demographic subgroup by bootstrap-
ping the relative difference (Mg −Fg)/Fg. The differences between subgroups sample sizes, high-
lighted the data imbalance present in the data set. Although the results for the number of stops
were inconclusive, the stop duration and entropy highlighted the importance of the employment
status and the urbanisation level.
The relative difference in the stop duration increased for unemployed users and users in rural
areas similarly. Regarding entropy, we identiﬁed the swap mentioned above for the unemployed
and detected a potential interaction between employment and urbanisation. In particular, the
relative difference for the unemployed increased for rural areas, but the employed increased for
urban areas. Nevertheless, the relative differences for the employed are less substantial than the
differences between unemployed, indicating that the gender gap is larger between unemployed
users.

Modelling the mobility entropy using
socio-demographic features
With statistical analysis, we evaluated several hypotheses and identiﬁed changes in behaviour.
One of the most interesting insights detected for the uncorrelated entropy was the relationship
between gender and employment status. The statistical analysis identiﬁed differences in the distri-
butions and potential correlations; however, the study of the relative differences by demographic
trait was limited to the comparisons of medians.
In this chapter, we depart from the knowledge gained from the previous analysis and inves-
tigate the extent to which gender inﬂuences mobility while accounting for socio-demographic
characteristics. We implement different regression models to deepen our understanding of the
uncorrelated entropy behaviour, following the methodology described in section 4.4. The models
helped us uncover interactions between the demographic variables, evaluate the feature impor-
tance, and validate the previous insights about the behaviour of the gender gap.
8.1
General Linear Model
After performing the necessary data transformations, we started the modelling exercises by im-
plementing a linear regression with the uncorrelated entropy, aggregated at the user level, as the
target. The uncorrelated entropy characterises the travel patterns’ heterogeneity (equation 2.3).
We selected it for this study because of its broad use in research, and the noteworthy behaviours
and normal distribution identiﬁed in sections 5.2.3 and 7.3. Moreover, we used the categorical
variables country, gender, and employment status, and the continuous variables urbanisation and
age, as predictors.
Table 8.1 consolidates the results of the features’ contributions. The model identiﬁes the em-
ployment status as the feature with the highest relative importance, 75.83%. Its negative contri-
bution to the intercept indicates that the entropy lowers for employed users, which aligns with
the observed behaviours in the statistical analysis. Additionally, the model identiﬁes the country
as a feature that explains 20.32% of the variability in the model, highlighting the recognised dif-
ferences between the two countries studied. Gender explains 2.34% of the variability, a relatively
low fraction compared to employment. The positive coefﬁcient of the gender indicates that the
entropy increases for females. This behaviour is consistent with what was seen in the statistical
study for employed users, but not for the unemployed. The linear regression can not detect this
behaviour since feature interactions were not included.

Discussion
In this chapter, we will summarise and contextualise the main ﬁndings of this study. We will
begin by contextualising the behaviours observed during the algorithm implementation and the
results from the validation exercise. Afterwards, we will discuss the implications of the results
from the statistical analysis and the entropy model. Moreover, we will compare the results of the
two studies and detail their differences.
Finally, we will discuss the limitations of this study and propose possible improvements and
potential future research on the topic.
9.1
Interpretation of the results
Before interpreting the results, it is essential to remember that the data set studied was biased
towards younger adults. Previous studies indicated that younger adults present higher activities
at night (Cuttone et al., 2018), which might inﬂuence not only the results from the mobility metrics
but also the HDA implementation.
In particular, the HDA identiﬁed more than one residence for 20% of users. We hypothesise
that the reason behind this high proportion is that younger users might have more than one ordi-
nary residence than older adults; for example, their parents’ and signiﬁcant other’s residences.
Similarly, the EDA detected multiple employment locations for 28% of the employed users.
However, when a change of location occurred, the algorithm kept its decision and did not change
back to previously identiﬁed employment locations.
Nevertheless, we decided to constrain the study to users with a maximum of three homes
and two employment locations detected to ensure that we perform an analysis over unambiguous
results.
The limited number of users and the known biases in the data impacted the robustness of the
validation conclusions. However, we detected a correlation between the census data and the EDA
that indicated the success of the algorithm implementation.
During the exploratory analysis, we recognised different distributions per gender in all mobil-
ity metrics. The presence of the gender gap in mobility was aligned with the research on the ﬁeld
(Gauvin et al., 2020, Alessandretti et al., 2020) and exhibited the need to understand the reasons
behind gender differences. Our study found that females’ movement was more contained, travel-
ling less and staying in their stops longer, but their travel patterns were more heterogeneous. The

gender differences in the entropy did not align with Gauvin et al., 2020 ﬁndings. Still, we estab-
lished that the inherent differences between our datasets, CDR and GPS data, were the source of
our discrepancies.
In Spain, users presented shorter and more heterogeneous stops than in the UK. Following
Brum-Bastos et al., 2018, we hypothesise that the two countries’ differences could be linked to
environmental factors such as weather. However, in both countries, the differences between sum-
mer and winter months were not as abrupt as expected, indicating that other socio-economic fac-
tors relative to the country, such as cultural differences or the gross domestic product per capita
(GDPC), may also have an inﬂuence. Nevertheless, the inherent differences between the countries
did not rescind the differences between genders since we detected a gender gap for all mobility
metrics in Spain and the UK.
The statistical analysis of the gender differences by demographic suggests that it is not pos-
sible to explain the gender gap in mobility with a single feature. Gender differences in the dis-
tributions appeared in all demographic subgroups, including the employment status. Therefore,
we concluded that the labour market differences can not directly explain the differences between
genders. Instead, a thorough analysis that considers the interaction between socio-demographic
features is necessary to understand the complexity of the gender gap.
However, it was still possible to detect gender differences between the employed and unem-
ployed. In particular, while females had higher entropy than males for all the other features,
males presented a higher entropy than females for unemployed users. We hypothesise that the
presence of stay-at-home mothers within the unemployed female subgroup might be inﬂuencing
these results.
The study of the relative differences between genders highlighted the effects of the data imbal-
ance. Although no robust conclusions could be taken from these exercises, the stop duration and
entropy studies highlighted the importance of the employment status and the urbanisation level.
We complemented these ﬁndings with a study of temporal patterns that identiﬁed differences
in routines between males and females and between countries. In particular, the analysis of the
stop duration highlighted that females arrived home earlier than males in the UK. This behaviour
was not identiﬁed for Spain, but we hypothesised that cultural differences, such as the traditional
dinner time, might affect these results. When testing employed and unemployed users separately,
this difference stayed in the UK and appeared for unemployed users in Spain. This ﬁnding sug-
gests that mobility metrics, particularly the distribution of stop durations over the day, can be
used to study the gender differences within and between countries. Furthermore, studying the
stop duration temporal patterns could help researchers and policymakers understand why we
continue to detect gender gaps in mobility.
Finally, we implemented different regression models to deepen our understanding of the mo-

count factors such as age, country of residence, urbanisation and employment status. All the
entropy models identiﬁed the employment status as the feature with the highest relative impor-

9.2
Limitations
During the development of this study, and thanks to the methodologies followed, we identiﬁed a
set of limitations.
First, the in-depth data-quality analysis performed during the algorithm implementation high-
lighted that fewer days in Spain reached the imposed data quality boundaries, i.e. Spanish users
had fewer daily registered stops. However, when inspecting the HDA results, Spain’s stability
levels were not far from the United Kingdom’s, indicating that the limited number of day stops
did not restrict the algorithm’s stability.
Second, we could not obtain ﬁrm conclusions during the algorithms’ validation, given the
reduced number of users available in Spain. However, this limitation should not restrict the study
conclusions since previous literature did not validate the algorithm results nor assess the stability
of the algorithms.
During the exploratory analysis, we observed that the data in our study was more representa-
tive of younger generations. Moreover, the fraction of users per age group and gender indicated
that females have a higher fraction than males in the younger age groups. This behaviour suggests
that the results of our study present a bias, and the gender gap analysis compared the population
of males against a slightly younger population of females.
Furthermore, we identiﬁed signiﬁcant sample size imbalances between most demographic
traits, and gender, country and employment status were characterized as heteroscedastic. There-
fore, the differences between subgroups could impact the results from the signiﬁcance test since
the number of samples might impact the sample variance and indicate an erroneous difference in
the distributions.
During the statistical analysis, we complemented the distributions signiﬁcance tests with an
evaluation of the relative gender differences per subgroup. However, since we compared the
medians and the samples were limited for some subgroups, no robust conclusions could be drawn
from this exercise. Therefore, the results were considered only as behaviour indications.
Finally, the entropy models used the same data as the one used to estimate the employment
status. We applied this methodology to maintain a high volume of users during the modelling
exercises. However, the R2 score obtained might have been inﬂuenced by the method applied. If
more data had been incorporated, we could have split the EDA and modelling exercises and used
a set of months to estimate the employment status and another set to study the entropy.

9.3
Future Improvements
The limitations identiﬁed highlighted the need for further validations and tests to increase the ro-
bustness of our conclusions. However, we can perform a collection of improvements to overcome
them.
First, although the state-of-the-art does not require validations for the HDA and EDA, the ﬁrst
improvement for the algorithms would be extending the validation analysis to the UK. Addition-
ally, we could include more in-depth validations on the number of residences and employment
locations detected. For example, studying the proximity between locations identiﬁed as home
or employment would help us ensure that the error does not come from the stop identiﬁcation
algorithm.
Furthermore, it would be interesting to design non-generic HDAs that could switch between
decision rules based on individual traces’ characteristics instead of being generically applied to all
users. The availability of individual-level ground truth could shed light on the structural effects
that currently obscure high-level validation practices, such as the change of residence.
The metrics selected for the mobility studies are the monthly number of stops, the stop du-
ration, and the monthly uncorrelated entropy. We used the number of stops to proxy how much
the user moves by counting the stops identiﬁed by the InfoStop algorithm. Although the stop
detection algorithm has been extensively validated in the research ﬁeld, a better way to estimate
the amount of movement would be to include the number of steps in the analysis and calculate
the radius of gyration for each user.
Similarly, we calculated the uncorrelated entropy to measure the heterogeneity in the user
movement. However, the uncorrelated entropy is measured with the historical visitation prob-
ability and thus is based on the frequencies. Using the real entropy, we would be considering
the order and the time spent in each location by including the probability of ﬁnding a particular
sequence in the trajectory.
Therefore, expanding the analysis with the metrics mentioned above would bring additional
insights and align the study with the state-of-the-art metrics used in the ﬁeld.
An important limitation was the data imbalance. Introducing methods that deal with data het-
erogeneity and avoid within-group noise, such as Welch ANOVA, robust regression and Kruskal-
Wallis test or even extending the bootstrap sampling to the distributions comparisons, would lead
to more robust results. In addition, studying a more extensive and balanced data set would allow
a deeper understanding of the data heterogeneities.
Another way to equalise the differences between demographic levels is to perform match-
ing experiments. In particular, we could replace the evaluation of the relative differences with a
matching experiment. Matching enables a comparison of outcomes between groups to evaluate
the group’s inﬂuence, limiting confounding bias.
Nevertheless, the logical next step is to expand all the analysis to more countries. Unfortu-
nately, the time available during the master thesis dissertation did not allow for this expansion;

answer: Are stops still shorter for females when looking at not-home stops during the work-day?
Another interesting element would be to study how the presence of stay-at-home mothers
might be inﬂuencing our results. A ﬁrst approach would be to look at the mobility of unemployed
females in countries with different ratios of stay-at-home mothers. Then, we could compare the
mobility of females between different age ranges: below the average age of attaining ﬁrst-time
motherhood, ages with offspring under care-age, and ages with offspring outside care-age. Other
options would be to include application data to estimate parenthood and compare unemployed
mothers’ and non-mothers behaviours.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
Over the last decade, the number of studies on human mobility has increased due to technological
advancements and data availability (Lima et al., 2016). However, while individual mobility has
been studied extensively, only a handful of studies examined the role of gender using large-scale
data (Gauvin et al., 2020, Alessandretti et al., 2020). In addition, the gender division of labour and
domestic responsibilities has been suggested as a potential explanation for the observed gap in
mobility. Still, these hypotheses were not tested at scale.
This study used large-scale behavioural data to quantify and describe gender differences in
mobility behaviour. Our objective was to understand the relationship between mobility, gender
and employment and to quantify the extent to which gender impacts mobility; this study included
the analysis of factors such as age, country of residence, urbanisation and employment status.
The ﬁrst step was to identify with time-space heuristics users as commuters or non-commuters;
then, use these categories as a proxy of their employment status. Next, we performed a statistical
analysis to test if the users’ employment situation explained the gender differences. We observed
that, although the differences in employment status partially explain the differences in travel pat-
terns, the gender gap was still present within each employment group. The statistical analysis
was extended to the other socio-demographic features, and we concluded that it is not possible
to explain the gender gap in mobility with a single factor. We complemented these ﬁndings with
a study of temporal patterns and identiﬁed differences in routines, with females arriving home
earlier than males in the UK. In Spain, the same behaviour was observed in unemployed females.
We modelled the mobility entropy, which describes the heterogeneity in the travel patterns,
using different regression models to broaden the understanding of how socio-demographic char-
acteristics inﬂuence mobility. All models identiﬁed the employment status as the feature with the
highest relative importance, followed by country and gender with substantially lower effects. Us-
ing only demographic features, we explained almost one-sixth of the users’ entropy. Finally, we
studied the interactions between employment status and gender. We concluded that the relative
impact of the employment status over the entropy was stronger for males, conﬁrming their corre-
lation and indicating that the weight of employment is more important for males than females.
A bias towards younger generations and limitations on the number of uses and the demo-
graphic imbalances inﬂuenced our results. However, expanding the analysis to more countries
and introducing models that deal with data heterogeneity would improve the robustness of our
conclusions. Nevertheless, this study presents improved HDA and EDA algorithms accounting
for stability, and novel ﬁndings that describe behavioural patterns capturing individual behaviour
based on gender, urbanisation level, age and the estimated employment status.

Entropy
The entropy distribution is tested for normality using the Shapiro and the one sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov
tests. Given the volume of observations used for testing, the results of the p-values are unreliable
(Shapiro et al., 1965). For this reason, we evaluate normality with the W-statistic in the Shapiro test
and F-statistic in KS. The W-statistic has a value of 0.997 indicating that the sample came from a
normally distributed population. Similarly, the one sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests, evaluates
the statistical difference of the sample with the normal distribution. The F-statistic has a value of
0.923 conﬁrming the good match between distributions.
Lastly we visualize the results with a quantile-quantile plot (qq-plot) and conﬁrm that the
observations fall within the normal ﬁt with only light tails (Fig.C.27).
FIGURE C.27: Monthly Entropy Normal Q-Q Plot. The entropy observations lie

sume the uncorrelated entropy follows a normal distribution.

Linear regression assumptions
We test if the residuals are normally distributed using a Shapiro test. The p-values provided
by the test are unreliable due to the large number of data used for testing (Shapiro et al., 1965).
Accordingly, we use the W-statistic to assess normality. With a value of 0.995, the W-statistic
indicates that the errors are normaly distributed. However, we complement the results with a
quantile-quantile plot (qq-plot) and conﬁrm that the observations fall within the normal ﬁt with
slight tails. Nonetheless, these deviations indicate a certain skewness of the data that could be
corrected with a squared-root transformation.
FIGURE D.1: Linear Regression residuals Q-Q plot. The residuals lie along the
normal ﬁt, with only slight deviations on the tails. Therefore, we can conﬁrm the
normality assumption of the regression error.
D.2
Height and weight interactions in Gradient Boosting model
We looked at how height and weight interacted with each other and with gender and employment
status. While weight has a more complicated relationship with gender, height serves as a proxy
for gender. The detected users with a high impact on the entropy and low weights were identiﬁed
as outliers. In fact, it users with large weights also showed a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the entropy.
Furthermore, people of tall stature also acted as outliers. We should eliminate outliers from the
model to improve performance.
