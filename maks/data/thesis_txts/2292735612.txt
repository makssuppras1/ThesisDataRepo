CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In order to reach an energy matrix build on respect for the environment, energy policies are
a determinant factor for a sustainable development since the energy, as a basic resource for the
mankind, determines the economic and social development at the same time that determines the
different stages of the energy cycle (mining extraction, fuel transportation, generation and utilization
of energy and waste assessment), which produce a harmful impact to the environment, which
signiﬁcance it is not sanitary, inﬂuencing the quality of life, but also because of the environmental
costs (climate change, extinguishing natural resources, acid rain, radioactive pollution...).
During the last years the growing tendency of energy consumption from fuel resources(except
nuclear energy) was below the average rates, this includes energy production from natural gas,
oil and coal. On the other hand, last year renewable power generation sources (hydroelectric,
solar, wind...) reached record shares of global primary energy consumption, having also the
highest growth tendency among all the other energy resources [1]. From this statistics it can be
inferred that the energy utilization tendency is slightly changing and countries are starting to wage
on new renewable energy sources. However, the transition from the current energy matrix to a
sustainable matrix is very slow and needs a social energy consensus around the sustainable energy
source. Even though the goth tendency might have changed, current data still shows a very high
worldwide dependency on energy production from the conventional energy sources, which leads
to high CO2 emissions, and thus an increase of the pollution related problems widely known.
For that reason, research on renewable energy sources from all perspectives (energy production,
storage, transportation...) has to be continue, and also with a growing rate and quality increase, so
sustainable technologies can become more costly attractive for the main energy producer companies
and countries.
One of the main problems of renewable energy sources is the production irregularity and
unpredictability, strictly related to seasonal, weather and localization factors. However, the behave
of the consumers it is neither regular, but is much more predictable. A potential solution for this
problem relies on large scale storage of intermittent energy production to improve grid system
reliability when supplied from renewable sources [2]. Smart grid technology seems to be the way to
follow for the future electric grid. Drawbacks of the conventional grid, for instance the dependency
on continuous power generation from fuel sources, can be overcome with the smart grid by the

Energy storage systems is a fundamental part of the smart-grids, since their expected beneﬁts

cells.
1.1
Thesis Objectives
This project is established with the main goal of researching on a new proposed power supply
unit (PSU) architecture for grid-connected large power capability SOEC/SOFC systems. The
proposed PSU architecture aims to greatly reduce the losses of the entire system (not considering
it unit by unit, but considering the PSU as a single block) and reducing the energy production
and storage costs, so an efﬁcient and costly-effective high power capability storage system for
renewable energy resources can be achieved.
The main project objectives are:
• Perform a theoretical study of the proposed PSU unit for SOEC/SOFC systems in comparison
with the traditional PSU, analyzing the possible ways of optimization for higher efﬁciency
and energy storage cost reduction.
• Design and implement a suitable DC/DC converter for the PSU considering the necessities of
fuel cells (i.e. very low current ripple). Converter has to reach an acceptable efﬁciency, with-
out the need of reaching extremely high efﬁciency, since the system efﬁciency improvement
is supposed to be obtained due to the PSU architecture and not the converter optimal design.

2.1
What are the regenerative fuel cells?
Many of the current ways of generating electricity, including coal-ﬁred power stations and
nuclear power stations, convert the energy in a fuel to electricity by ﬁrst converting the energy to
heat which drives an engine that produces the electricity. This way of producing energy is not very
efﬁcient, since many losses occur during the different energy conversion processes.
A Fuel Cell is an electrochemical device that uses a chemical reaction to produce electricity
directly from the fuel, which is a much more efﬁcient way to generate electricity. The classic
example of a Fuel Cell is the hydrogen Fuel Cell, which takes advantage of the fact that when
hydrogen and oxygen react to produce water, energy is released.
In a Fuel Cell it is possible to break the reaction into two halves by using an electrolyte layer,
ﬁgure 2.1 shows a diagram of the fuel cell operation. When using hydrogen as a fuel, on one
side hydrogen molecules loses electrons to the anode forming H+ ions which diffuse through the
electrolyte at the center of the cell. On the other side the H+ ions react with oxygen (O2) and gain
electrons from the cathode to produce water (H2O)[5, 7]. In order for this to happen, electrons ﬂow
from the anode to the cathode through an external electric circuit in which voltage is generated,
that when connected to a load electric current is produced [5, 7]. So Fuel Cells operate as a battery
with a much higher energy capacity as can be recharged by adding more fuel. In this case the solid
oxide stacks are operating in Solid Oxide Fuel Cell mode (SOFC).
On the other hand, recent studies [REFs: Haldor documentation] on Solid Oxide Cells, have
proved that fuel cells can also operate in the reversible mode, called Solid Oxide Electrolyser
Cells mode (SOEC), so hydrogen can be created from an external electrical energy source. If the
electrons are forced to ﬂow through the opposite direction, from cathode to anode, by applying a
voltage, water can be separated back to hydrogen and oxygen again, which is called the electrolysis
operation [5, 7].

where the green square refers to the anode of the solid oxide stack and the blue square the cathode.

Renewable energy system: from production to storage
The end-use of the SOEC/SOFC system studied in this thesis is the construction of a pilot
plant for synthetic natural gas production and storage to the natural gas grid from methanation

SOEC/SOFC electrical characteristics
Research performed in [8, 9] show that the electrical characteristics of SOCs are mainly
dependent on the cells operating temperature, but also on the fuel composition and pressure. This
means that is not possible to ﬁnd a singular I-V curve that describe the electrical characteristic
of SOCs. However, in order to design the PSU the electrical operating conditions of the SOCs
are required, in [8, 9] a general I-V curve for the SOEC/SOFC from Haldor Topsoe has been
considered under certain experimental conditions as developed in [REF: Annabelle]. Figure 2.4
shows the I-V characteristic of a single SOC, which will be the starting point for the PSU design.
Figure 2.4: I-V characteristic of a single Solid Oxide Cell (SOC) at SOEC mode (left, negative
current) and SOFC mode (righ, positive current).
Even though the mechanical processes of SOEC and SOFC are founded on the same basis, due
to variation in the internal resistance and the current direction, the operating voltage in SOFC mode
is lower than in SOEC mode [REF: Haldor]. In addition, tests demonstrated that the maximum
safe current capability in SOEC 60 A while in SOFC mode is 30 A. This results in a much higher
power capability in SOEC mode than in SOFC mode. For instance, if a stack with 75 cells is used,
the maximum power rating in SOFC mode would be ∼1.5kW while in SOEC mode would be
∼5kW. Therefore, it can be expected a higher cost-efﬁciency with a SOEC/SOFC system, making
it more attractive.

2.4
Traditional bidirectional PSU based on SOEC/SOFC system
There is not a singular PSU topology for SOEC/SOFC, but different architectures can be found
depending on the end-up application, the system requirements and the SOEC/SOFC stacks structure.
However, a general topology, presented in ﬁgure 2.6, can be deﬁned as the root of all the other
emerging sub-topologies for grid-tie energy storage SOEC/SOFC systems.
Figure 2.6: Traditional PSU architecture for SOEC/SOFC systems.
These approach is based on a parallel connection of an AC/DC converter with a low-pass ﬁlter
to reduce the ripple from the rectiﬁed voltage, a DC/DC converter and the SOEC/SOFC stacks.
Power ﬂows from the AC side to the SOC stacks when operating in SOEC mode (energy storage)
and from the SOC stacks to the AC side when operating in SOFC mode. Therefore, the use of
bidirectional power converters is required to allow power transfer in both directions. The DC/DC
converter allows the SOEC/SOFC power ﬂow regulation and step-up the voltage when of the SOCs
since usually their rated voltage is lower to the DC-link voltage.
Different studies performed on PSU for SOEC/SOFC system show different approaches in
order, for instance, to reduce the number of components used, reduce costs, improve the SOCs
stacks performance, reduce the number of cells per stack or prolonging the SOCs lifetime. Some of
these approaches are the implementation of different arrangements paralleled DC/DC converters or
paralleled DC/DC and DC/AC converters [10], the implementation of complex DC/DC converters

[11, 12], the utilization of low losses semiconductors (as SiC power devices) [13], the optimization
of the inductors [14] or the inclusion of auxilary power sources (as supercapacitors and batteries)
to compensate the slow dynamics of fuel cells [15, 16]. However, all these solutions and sub-
topologies have a common drawback which is inherent to the general PSU topology in ﬁgure
2.6.
The main drawback of the traditional PSU architecture consists of its intrinsic power rating
symmetry. Power rating symmetry implies that the power rating of the system is the same regardless
the power ﬂow direction. As explained in the previous section, the power capability of the SOEC
stacks is greater than the power capability of the SOFC, so the power rating of the SOEC would
determine the power of the system. For instance, considering a SOCs arrangement consisting of 4
stacks in series with 75 cells per stack capable of operating up to 450 V 60 A in SOEC mode and
up to to 210 V 30 A in SOFC moden then, PSOEC =∼27kW and PSOFC =∼6.3kW, according
to the I-V characteristics of a single SOC (ﬁgure 2.4). Therefore, the power rating of the DC/DC
converter should be at least of Pconv =∼27kW. This would lead to an oversized converter when
operating in SOFC mode and, adding the design difﬁculties due to the wide voltage range of the
SOC, achieving high efﬁciency for both operating modes with the traditional PSU architecture is
signiﬁcantly challenging.
Therefore, the research on an asymmetrical PSU architecture that decreases the costs and
increases the efﬁciency of the system is pivotal in order to make this a feasible product.

Another factor to consider is the tight relation, regarding efﬁciency and costs, between the fuel
cells and the characteristics of the power supply unit.
It is estimated by [hydrogennet.dk..] that in 2020 the efﬁciency of an alkaline electrolysis
system will be around 98%. But nowadays the losses of power system units are higher than a 2%.
Therefore, it is evident the necessity of improving the efﬁciency of the PSU, since the PSU would
decrease the efﬁciency of the entire system even if the fuel cells have very low losses.
In 2020 the costs of an alkaline electrolysis system are expected to be by [hydrogennet.dk..]
10.000dkk/Nm3/h. Assuming the lower heating value of hydrogen (3.3kWh/Nm3), in other
words the lower value of heat released by the hydrogen, the cost of the PSU would be 3300dkk/Nm3,
which is the 33% of system costs. So, also the necessity of decreasing PSU costs is more than
evident.
Another factor that determines the efﬁciency and costs of energy storage applications is the
lifetime of the system. Fuel cells degradation are estimated to have in 2020 ratios of 0.025%
[hydrogennet.dk..]. If a maximum degradation of 10% is allowed, the lifetime of the system would
be more than 40 years. For proﬁtable energy storage system the PSU should match this durability.

The proposed system is composed by the same elements as the traditional PSU system pre-
viously presented (AC/DC converter, LC ﬁlter, DC/DC converter and SOCs), but its difference
remains in the system connection and power ﬂow. In order to achieve an asymmetrical power rated
architecture the PSU connections varies depending on the operating mode of the system by using
two switched represented by S1 and S2 in ﬁgure 2.7.
• SOFC mode (ﬁgure 2.7a): SOCs are operating in SOFC mode and thus, supplying energy.
Energy is transferred from the SOCs to the DC/DC converter which controls the power ﬂow
by regulating the current supplied by the SOCs and steps-up the voltage to an appropriate
level for the DC-link bus. Then, energy in the DC-link can be transferred to the grid by the
same AC/DC converter, if a bidirectional inverter is used, or can be used for other applications
if required. SOCs and DC/DC converter can be electrically isolated from the grid with the
AC/DC converter or an isolation transformer. In this scenario the SOCs are connected in
parallel to the DC/DC converter and thus, Pconv = PSOFC.
• SOEC mode (ﬁgure 2.7b): SOCs are operating in SOEC mode and thus,energy is stored
by the generation of hydrogen with the steam from the methanator using the surplus energy
from the renewable energy sources. As in SOFC mode, the DC/DC converter controls the
power ﬂow regulating the current through the SOCs and steps-up the voltage to the DC-link

lower cost design.
Some other considerations with the proposed PSU architecture can be listed:
• Power ﬂow though the DC/DC converter has always the same direction, so it is possible to
utilize a unidirectional DC/DC converter which permits the design of a simple converter with
less components and a simpler controller as well.
• Converter has to be controlled in current mode control in order to regulate the current ﬂowing
through the SOCs.
• Even though the power rating range is minimized, the converter input voltage range is still
large, which might be a design challenge for high performance operation.

3.1
Solutions analyzed
Single-stage boost converter
The most straight solution is to chose the basic boost converter composed by one single
switching device. Boost converter has been widely implemented in many applications for low and
mid-power [REFERENCE] which makes it, a priori, suitable for this application. However, this
converter topology generates a high input ripple to the source, which can be very detrimental for the
solid oxide stacks. In order to reduce this ripple a large inductor could be implemented, resulting in
a larger converter size, costs increase and larger inductor ac and core losses. Furthermore, due to
19
==PAGE:38==
20
CHAPTER 3. CONVERTER DESIGN
the high current provided by the SOCs, which maximum rated current is 60 A, current stresses at
the power semiconductors would be to high.
In addition the digital control implementation for this converter may become complex since
the current through the inductor contains high frequency ripple that makes difﬁcult the complete
attenuation of it by using low cost signal conditioning circuits.
Parallel connection of boost converters
The parallel boost converter is based on the connection of different symmetrical boost stages,
as shown in ﬁgure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Parallel connection of boost converters diagram.
With the parallel connection the losses at each switch and the thermal dissipation is distributed
among the different semiconductors, since the total power rating is ideally equally distributed to
each stage. The switching frequency can be larger than the switching frequency with a single stage
boos converter, since the switching devices are faster with lower power.
Implementing a converter based on the parallel connection of different converters also allows
a modular design, which provides larger ﬂexibility for the design, since the power stage can be
expanded to obtain wider voltage and current ranges.
On the other hand, by using this converter topology the current ripple cannot be reduced but
the opposite effect can occur, since the PWM are in the same phase the ripple tends to be added up.
[REFERENCE OR TEST IT].
Interleaved boost converter
Interleaved converters are a variation from the parallel connected converters. The interleaving
technique refers to the strategy of connecting different converters in parallel with the same switching
frequency but the PWM signal of each stage with a phase shift of 360 ◦/N, where N is the number
of stages.
By interleaving N converter modules the frequency f of the total output current ripple is N
times the frequency of the ripple of the input and output currents of each converter module [17],
which allows a reduction of the overall converter frequency and thus, the input and output ﬁlter
components. By means of interleaving technique, the inductors current ripple is phase shifted, so
the input current ripple amplitude decreases due to the inductors current cancellation.
==PAGE:39==

Therefore, an interleaved converter results in a reduced ﬁltering and energy storing requirements,
so the inductors and capacitors can be smaller without compromising the efﬁciency and power
density of the converter. Current stresses of the semiconductors will decrease, but, however,
by increasing the number of semiconductors, losses may increase if an appropriate selection of
semiconductors and a proper design are not performed.
Summary
A step-up converter topology is required for the system, however, high step-up ratios and not
necessary and thus, topologies based on the traditional boost converter are suitable. The interleaved
boost converter, which is a topology derived from the parallel-connected boost converters, offer
many advantages such as input current ripple cancellation, modular design, higher power density
and electrical and thermal stresses reduction on semiconductors.
A three-stage interleaved boost converter has been chosen for the DC/DC converter stage. This
allows a current reduction of three times the total input current for each converter stage. Accounting
the maximum current from the SOEC of 60 A, the maximum current ﬂowing through each converter
stage will be 20 A, assuming an ideal power distribution. From ﬁgure [Power curve SOECSOFC],
can be inferred that when the SOECs supply maximum current the voltage across the converter is
110 V, this leads to a power rating of 2.2 kW.
In order to design and perform the components selection for the converter, ﬁrst the single-stage
boost converter is analyzed and the main design parameters are derived. The interleaved boost
converter will be subsequently designed according to the power requirements for each stage and

ﬁxed duty cycle are performed to verify the design.

The boost converter can operate in three modes according to the current ﬂowing through
the inductor: continuous conduction mode (CCM), discontinuous conduction mode (DCM) and
boundary conduction mode (BCM). Depending on which is the operation mode of the converter,
different design procedures have to be carried out.
In DCM the energy stored in the inductor reaches zero during the off-state of Q, which requires
less energy storage and thus, smaller energy storage components can be used. The frequency of
operation is constant. However, in DCM the highest peak current is obtained, which makes the
operation less convenient than the BCM.
In the BCM the converter operation is controlled to remain in between the CCM and DCM,
therefore the switching frequency changes in order to maintain the boundary operation. Since the
switch Q turn-on occurs at zero current, the turn-on losses at the switch are eliminated. At the same
time, the diode D turn-off occurs at zero current the reverse recovery losses are eliminate too. On
the other hand the inductor current ripple is twice the average current, which causes an increase of
the rms current across the semiconductors and also higher core losses and winding losses in the
inductor.
CCM requires a simpler control of the converter, since operates at a ﬁxed frequency. The
inductor requires a larger inductance, since it needs more energy storage to avoid reaching zero
current operation. The main advantages is the reduced current ripple, which reduces the peak
current as well, and thus, the turn-off losses are lower. However, in CCM transistor Q turn-on
losses are present, differently from the other two conduction modes, which can be increased by the
diode reverse recovery charge Qrr. For that reason, when operating in CCM, diodes with very low
Qrr are recommended, as Silicon Carbide diodes, which Qrr is almost ineffective.
To summarize, in CCM and BCM less energy storage is required, so are optimal for high power
density applications. However, in high current applications, as the present application, the high
peak currents that this operating modes present would become a great drawback. For that reason,
CCM has been chosen for the current design.

3.4.1
Magnetics design
The inductor is designed to operate at the switching frequency fs = 25 kHz. In mid/high power
applications a suitable ripple current is between 20% to 40% of the nominal inductor current [18].
However, SOEC/SOFC stacks demand a low AC ripple since its lifetime and performance are
related to the current ripple. The maximum input current ripple will be limited to 2 A (<4% ).
The inductance for each stage is calculated with equation 3.18. Since it is desirable to keep
the operating point of the SOEC/SOFC stacks at its maximum power capability, it would not be
pragmatic to choose an inductance to keep the current ripple within the desired range for low
currents. By an iterative calculations it has been found that with an inductance of 1 mH the ripple

3.4.3
Capacitor selection
The output capacitor, placed across the load, ﬁlters the switching frequency ripple out of the
output current. This is required in order to keep an acceptable power quality at the DC link. From
equation 3.24, the minimum capacitance required is calculated to be 20 µF.
During the operation of power semiconductors, there are situations during commutations where
the peak voltage can reach very large values if there necessary precautions are not taken. This is
mainly due to the presence of parasitic inductances distributed all around the conducting paths of
the board and the components.
One of the most critical situations occurs at the turn-off of the IGBT, where the large current
ﬂowing through it will be forced to zero causing a large di/dt. If the parasitic inductances Lp are
too large, this situation will lead to a large overvoltage (recalling Faraday equation for inductances
v = Lpdi/dt). This overvoltage will stress the semiconductors as well as the output capacitor,
and after a long time operation under these conditions it will result in a poor performance of the
converter.
To solve this problem , the capacitor must be placed as close as possible to the switching
devices in order to minimize the parasitic inductances. Furthermore, the capacitor should be able to
store this extra amount of energy, so an oversize of the capacitance and the voltage rating may be
done. However, due to the converter design it is difﬁcult to place the output capacitor close to the
output of each stage of the interleaved converter. Therefore, small ﬁlm capacitors of 12 nF have
been placed very close to the output of each stage, so the aforementioned effects due to the stray
inductance are minimized.
Losses in the capacitor due to the equivalent series resistance (ESR) also needs to be considered.
Capacitors with low ESR should be chosen whenever possible. Connecting several capacitors
in parallel reduces the net ESR. The parallel connection of two Metallized Polypropylene ﬁlm
capacitors EXPE1B206MTA from Panasonic has been chosen as a solution. Main speciﬁcations
are listed in table 3.5.

CHAPTER 4
Digital control design
The control stage of the converter is necessary in order to reach and keep the power requirements
of the load. Furthermore, the control stage has to be capable of keeping the input power inside the
allowed limits of the SOC stacks. In other words, the control must prevent of overtaking the power
capacity of the SOC stacks.
For long time power converters have been controlled by analogue circuit. The main drawbacks
for using an analogue control are the low ﬂexibility in order to realize changes in the control
parameters, the high sensibility to the noise, large components variations due to the switching
noise and the circuit complexity. For these reasons, a digital control will be implemented for
this system. One of the main problems of digital controls are the delays generated due to the
algorithms calculations of the processor. However, slow dynamics of the SOC stacks allow larger
time response of the closed loop converter, which eases the design of the digital control.
A DSP-based PI control is implemented using the TMS320f20837 microcontroller from Texas
Instruments.
4.1
Control strategy
The proposed PSU architecture, allows a unidirectional power converter and thus, the current
ﬂow through the converter has the same direction independently of the operation mode SOEC or
SOFC. However, the electrical characteristics of the SOCs are different for each operating mode,
and therefore, to assure that the current limitations of the SOCs are not exceeded it is desired to
have an inner current control loop. In addition, the energy from the SOCs is transferred to the DC
link at the output of the DC/DC converter, which also needs to be controlled to a certain value
with high precision and small oscillations. In this ﬁrst PSU prototype the system is simpliﬁed by
using an output resistive load that sets the input current reference according to the DC link voltage.
Therefore, the control loop is composed by an inner current control loop and an outer voltage
control loop with the desired output voltage as a reference. Figure 4.1 shows the control scheme.
The interleaved boost converter show asymmetries in the power sharing between each stage,
therefore it would be recommended to implement an independent control strategy for each stage,
so different duty cycles would be generated for each converter stage. This could be done by

4.2
Delay effect
The delay effect in digital control always needs to be taken into account because it will add
some difﬁculties to the system. The delay introduces a phase shift to the signals which can result
into system instability [26]. The delay can be originated by different sources [26]:
• Sampling time
• Filter delays
• Algorithms execution and interruptions
When operating with discrete systems, as digital controllers, it is required to ﬁnd a continuous
time model of the sampled data system in order to use it in the continuous time model for calculating
the PI parameters that make the system stable. So afterwards, the PI controller in continuous domain
can be transferred into an equivalent discrete model that keeps the closed loop properties determined
in the continuous time design and thus, the system stability [26]. The common methodology to do
that is considering a model of the sampling system, typically represented by a sample and hold, and
ﬁnding an equivalent continuous model in S domain to connect it in cascade with the controllers
[26].
There can be found different approaches and models to converter the Sample and hold delay
of a digital controller to the continuous domain, as explained in [26]. The most common used for
Digital PWM controllers is the Pade approximation, where the delay effect is considered to be half
of the switching period. The delay effect using Pade approximation is shown in equation 4.1.

4.3
Signal measurement and conditioning circuit
Figure 4.2 shows a simpliﬁed diagram of the signal conditioning circuit for output voltage and
input current signals measurement.
Current sensing is performed the current transducer CASR 15, which provides galvanic isolation
between the primary circuit (high power) and the secondary circuit (low power). From the current
ﬂowing through the sensor, a voltage signal proportional to the current is obtained. Then this signal
is properly attenuated according to the 3.3 V reference of the ADC integrated in the DSP.
From a voltage divider connected at the output of the converter a voltage analog signal is
obtained within the sensor range, with a relation of 0.134 V for every 50 V sensed. The scaled
down voltage is ﬁltered by an anti-aliasing ﬁlter with a large corner frequency and then is sensed
by the isolation ampliﬁer, which isolates the power stage ground from the control board ground.
Input and output voltage from the optocoupler are proportional. The differential signal from the
optocoupler is converted to a single-ended signal, with the proper gain and ﬁltering, by a differential
ampliﬁer.

and experimentation that has been carried out. The success of the obtained results, and also the

The paramount objective of this project is the experimental research on a novel PSU architecture
for a doubly-fed SOEC/SOFC system with the end-up application of the development of a synthetic
natural gas generation plant based on a SOEC hydrogen generation from the surplus energy from
renewable sources. Grid-tie PSU are mainly composed by an AC/DC converter, LC ﬁlter and a

DC/DC converter designed speciﬁcally for the application under study.

6.1
Conclusion
Second chapter synthesized the actual state of PSUs for bidirectional SOEC/SOFC systems
and the different approaches done for improving system performance and efﬁciency. However, it is
concluded that all the solutions proposed in the literature have a common drawback, which is the
symmetry of PSU for both operating modes. This results into an oversized converter design when
operating in SOFC mode, which causes a challenging design of a high efﬁcient DC/DC converter
and being further stressed due to the wide current and voltage operating ranges of the SOEC/SOFC
stacks.
A novel PSU is proposed which allows an asymmetric design of the converter, so the converter
operating points are adjusted to a tighter range by signiﬁcantly reducing the converter power rating
under SOEC mode, while the total PSU power rating is kept constant. The operating range and
power rating reduction to a similar power level for both operating modes (SOEC and SOFC modes)
eases the design of a high efﬁcient DC/DC converter. Furthermore, the proposed power supply unit
allows the utilization of a unidirectional power converter giving the possibility of implementing a
less sophisticated converter which, therefore, will ease a high efﬁcient and low cost design.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE DEVELOPMENTS
Chapter 3 is destined to the DC/DC converter design. Converter topology selection is a critical
step when designing efﬁcient converters for a speciﬁc application and there not exist a dictated
general methodology for it, but an analysis of the application requirements has to be properly done.
For a grid-tie SOEC/SOFC applications, low input and output ac components are demanded, since
large ac components can dramatically reduce SOEC/SOFC stacks lifetime and performance. The
converter is supplying energy to the DC-link bus which requires low ac component for a proper
power factor quality and not compromising electrolytic capacitors lifetime, usually used in the
DC-link. Interleaved boost converter topology is chosen due to input and output current ripple
reduction thanks to the interleaving technique.
The design of a converter and its optimization is a complex problem with multiple solutions
where different factors have to be considered as the application, the aimed efﬁciency and the power
rating. It has been executed as a design from the basic equations of the DC/DC boost converter and
a components selection which takes into account the maxim of minimizing the power losses. The
losses characterization is performed based on the current-voltage characteristics of the SOEC/SOFC
stacks which determine the operating points of the converter and thus, directly affect the efﬁciency
curve. It has been obtained an efﬁciency model for the whole operating range of the converter
which matches with the experimental results with a maximum variation of 1 %. The implemented
converter is proved to be very efﬁcient, reaching an efﬁciency of 97 % at SOEC mode with half load
and a maximum efﬁciency of 93.3 % at SOFC mode at 40 % load. Losses sources are identiﬁed
with the losses characterization model and corroborated with an experimental thermal analysis.
Maximum power losses are due to the IGBTs and more precisely due to the switching energies.
Chapter 4 deals with the digital control design. In order to obtain a robust and fast dynamic
response, the control loop has been designed using two loops, the fast inner current loop and a slower
outer voltage loop. The system controls the output voltage according to a given voltage reference by
regulating the average input current. The inclusion of the inner current loop with a larger bandwidth
aims to achieve a faster control response. In order to calculate the PI parameters for the controllers,
ﬁrst the system transfer functions have been derived from the using the state-space modeling. The
control loop design has been veriﬁed with MATLAB/Simulink step response simulations using the
calculated transfer functions and PLECS based simulations using the interleaved boost converter
power stage.
Experimental results show, ﬁrst of all, a converter response of maximum 2.8 seconds with
input voltage steps up to 100 V. Due to the large voltage steps applied, a large settling time is
expected. Moreover, the very slow dynamics of the SOEC/SOFC stacks makes this settling times
as assumable. Due to laboratory equipment limitations high power tests could not be performed,
however, results obtained under SOEC operation mode verify the feasibility and potential of the
proposed PSU architecture. Operating with a rectiﬁed voltage of 450 V and a SOEC current of
15.4 A, the converter operates at 2.3 kW power rated while the SOEC stack operates at 4.6 kW
power rated, which corresponds to two times the converter power rating. In conclusion, energy
consumption during the hydrogen generation from the SOEC would be less affected by the converter
losses and thus, the energy production cost would decrease.
