Summary
In order to facilitate renewable sources integration in the energy landscape, the feasi-
ble power generation outcomes need to be predicted in advance. This thesis aims at
developing an Open Source platform for generating scenarios of wind and solar power
production, considering spatio-temporal dependencies. Building upon the existing RES-
Gen code, methods include statistical and data based approaches for the enhancement of
point forecasts and of the relying arrangement of trajectories in space and time. Geosta-
tistical models are considered in order to extrapolate and generalize these dependency
structures to higher dimensionality. The different methods implemented were tested
and applied on two main datasets, one in the United States and one in Europe.
Results show substantial improvements in the prediction of first lead times and in the
generalization of a correlation system from a small training subset in space and time.
A final application study analyzes the accuracy of what has been implemented and can
serve as a documentation for the use of RESGen. In addition to analysis in this report,
the different test cases brought to the development of the code’s organization. While
the existing platform was specific to one dataset and non-optimized, this project led to
the creation of a structured flexible code. It can be adapted more easily to different
database and modules can be added for improvement models.
An open release of the code is available on GitHub [1] for the RE-Europe dataset [2]. A
corresponding documentation can be found in appendix C. This master thesis project
was performed along with the contribution to an article [3] and to a presentation in the
conference Stochastic Weather Generator 2016 in Vannes [4].
Keywords: energy markets, forecasts, wind and solar power, space-time dependency.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Increased concerns are addressed to climate change in the current social and economical
context. In order to restrain global warming under the 2oC level, a restricting agreement
has been signed and agreed on climate by 175 countries during COP21 in Paris. The
energy sector is one of the most impacted field with the necessary evolution of produc-
tion mix. Renewable sources are and will be playing a key role in a green transition and
generation of electricity. Wind and solar energy are the most preeminent technologies,
after a rapid growth in the last decades. In order to benefit from their full potential, one
should be able to handle their variability and make it a more secure resource, reducing
losses from limited predictability. Producing these renewable energies is one thing, inte-
grating them into power grids and electric utility systems is another one. Their capacity
limitations and the necessity to be entirely reliable to consumers make the planing of
these sources critical.
The stability of the power grid must be ensured at any time by maintaining the balance
between electricity production and consumption. There are several solutions for accom-
modating larger shares of renewable into future power grids. Energy storage equipments
(such as batteries, pumped-hydro, etc.) are so far uneconomical and makes flexibility
very reduced. The expansion of (cross-boarder) transmission capacities and demand-side
consumption management techniques are other possibilities in development. Predicting
in advance the amount and units that will generate the future electricity remains the
essential solution among all.
Different markets are accessible for producers to bid in advance their energy as explained
in [6]. The day-ahead market is the main one for trading power, and maybe the more
important one. Sellers and buyers agree on the delivery of energy and price for the
following day [7]. Follows an adjustment phase with the intra-day market, which allows
sellers to correct their trading. These transactions help secure the necessary balance
between supply and demand. This is a continuous platform where trading is possible
until one hour before delivery.
Any imbalance, defined as the deviation between actual generation and production bid,
will then be charged by the Transmission System Operator (TSO). The market value of
unpredictable renewable energies is thus reduced to a cost for regulation. Point forecasts
are the most common form of projection for wind or solar participations. They provide
estimate of the power productions with a forecast length of up to 2–3 days ahead and
with a forecast resolution ranging from 15 min to 1 h [8]. An overview of the current
forecasting challenges for wind energy operational management can be found in [9]. Al-
though forecasting techniques are constantly improving, predictions are never perfect
and resulting forecast errors can induced costs at different levels.
==PAGE:18==
2
1 Introduction
For a wide range of decision making problems, it is important to account for spatio-
temporal dependencies between the different renewable production units. It gives in-
formation on possible interactions and compensations. Space-time trajectories are post-
processed point forecasts with consideration of that dependency structure. They have
increased interest in the study of different schemes and uncertainties. If appropriately
incorporated in planning studies, they could significantly increase the value of wind and
solar power. They can for example be used on power markets to minimize the imbal-
ance costs of trading renewable power as studied in [10] and [11]. In this context, an
open source platform for Renewable Energy Scenarios Generation (RESGen) was imple-
mented [3].
1.2
Perspective of the study
RESGen was initially created in collaboration with EPRI and applied to data from the
United States, more specifically within the Western Interconnection with a total of 39
load regions. The purpose of this thesis is to adapt it to the RE-Europe data set, which
models the continental European electricity system, including renewable energy inflows
for the period 2012-2014. This data set consists of 1494 location and up to 96 lead times,
resulting in a more complex data set to handle, with a larger dimensionality. Consider-
ing the problem in its full dimension would require too much past data. Therefore, an
alternative modeling procedures to extrapolate and extend the spatio-temporal depen-
dency has to be found.
1.3
Project organization
Three research question where addressed to pursue this study:
• What are the characteristics of RESGen as a scenarios generator?
• Can it be improved with alternative models?
• How is it possible to extend the application of RESGen to high dimensionality
problems and data sets?
To have a global understanding of the platform, some sensitivity analysis will be con-
ducted with the purpose to improve scenarios accuracy. In order to do so, the study
was started from the root, looking at the potential enhancement of input point forecasts.
While the idea is not to implement a new generator of point predictions, their quality
could be improved by considering past observations and different statistical models. In
that way it is expected to improve the sharpness of predictive densities and thus make
==PAGE:19==

scenarios more accurate. The models that will be studied include a very common im-
plementation through persistence and more theoretical, statistical based models with a
weighted regression and a recursive least-square filter. Quality tools will help define the
best method.
The two main statistical techniques employed in RESGen for generating scenarios are:
conditional quantile regression density estimations and a copula approach. While several
methods have already been tested in the literature concerning different copulas, empha-
size will be made on making the quantile regressions more flexible in order to account
for potential nonlinear dependencies between observations and forecasts. This is done
by adding a spline basis to the quantile regressions, compared to the original linear ones.
The purpose of this analysis is to suggest an alternative in the model, considering its
advantages and drawbacks.
The implementation of a higher dimensionality with the RE-Europe data set implies
the necessity to extrapolate the dependency structure. For a high number of locations
and lead times, the necessary amount of past observations and predictions needed to
compute an accurate covariance structure would be enormous. A subset of limited loca-
tions should be used to define a statistical model in continuous space and time. A final
application case in France will try quantify the approach results and demonstrate the
possible outputs.

CHAPTER 2
RESGen: Renewable Energy
Scenario Generation
platform
In the past years, several suggestions appeared in the literature to tackle the issue of
scenarios generation. Forecasting issues are often seen as individual problems, consider-
ing each site and lead time separately. The overall picture and thus dependency is not
always considered. Recent works showed the apparition of tools for the generation of
these space-time trajectories [12] [13]. However, RESGen seems to be the only one so
far considering both a conditional quantile regression model for the determination of ob-
servations distributions and a copula approach for the space-time dependency structure
estimation [3].
This platform was created in collaboration with EPRI in order to generate space-time
wind and solar scenarios for the Western Interconnection in the United States (Figure
2.1a). In this area, a subset of 39 loads regions will be used for wind and solar at different
locations, represented in Figure 2.1b.
(a) Interconnections, United States
(b) Load Regions, Western Interconnection
Figure 2.1: RESGen data setup [3].
In order to model a dependency structure in space and time, the necessary inputs are
past observations and point forecasts for locations and lead times considered. The year
data available in the US data set contains day-ahead forecasts, with an hourly time
resolution one day in advance, and intra-day forecasts, with a 15-min time resolution for
the next 5 hours, generated every 15-min. While the day-ahead market will thus divide
the day in 24 time steps, the intra-day one will be available for 4 × 24 = 96 time steps
in a day.

6
2 RESGen: Renewable Energy Scenario Generation platform
2.1
Climatology time of day transformation
Through seasons and time, meteorological phenomenons such as sunrise, sunset or wind
streams evolve. While things are known to be more steady in term of wind distributions
over a year, solar emissions will be really affected. In order to get rid of these seasonality
effects and work in an uniform environment, climatology transformations are necessary.
Whether day-ahead or intra-day markets are considered, time horizon is divided in time
steps (24 and 4×24 = 96 respectively). For each of these time steps, a profile of observed
power productions over the years is made. A probability and thus cumulative density
function can be obtained.
The hourly power distribution for location SCE in the intra-day and day-ahead markets
are represented in Figure 2.2 for a solar generation system and in Figure 2.3 for a wind
power plant respectively. The market parameter is not influent on these transforma-
tions, its only purpose is to show the different day distributions. The renewable type of
generation is much more relevant on differences of behavior.
Figure 2.2: Cumulative distribution functions timely distributed of a solar power
system SCE in the intra-day market.
Wind production is more evenly distributed than solar. This is due to the fact that it has
no critical condition such as the diurnal change of day and night. While seasons effects
are however hard to notice from Figure 2.3, it can be pointed out that night distributions
show a higher proportion to high generations than day. This could be translated as a
tendency to produce more at night than during day hours. While this is very specific to

2.2
Determination of space-time dependencies
RESGen’s particularity in the determination of space-time dependencies is the use of
both conditional quantile regressions and a Gaussian copula approach. The first one
will help estimating predictive densities conditionally to forecasts while the other will
determine correlations between resulting stochastic variables representing one lead time
and location each. This part will develop the mathematical scope and layout of these
two estimations in the code.
==PAGE:24==
8
2 RESGen: Renewable Energy Scenario Generation platform
Let’s define the set up of the environment. A market of K lead times and N locations
will be considered for wind or solar production. The number of lead times is for exam-
ple defined as every hour in a day (K = 24) in the day-ahead market and as every 15
minutes of the next 5 hours (K = 20) in the intra-day market. At every time step t, the
K next lead times t + 1, t + 2, ... and t + K are considered.
2.2.1
Getting the marginal predictive cdf

The generation of scenarios is highly dependent of input point forecasts. In order to
enhance trajectories quality, first step is thus to correct initial predictions. Physical
models can allow to estimate relevant patterns that influence wind and solar power.
Also, statistical concepts are used to model the conversion from meteorological param-

3.3
A weighted regression-based framework
The idea of a weighted regression came first from the willingness to see the correlation
between forecasts and observations of the training period for each lead time. To get
rid of seasonality effects, the study was made in the uniform domain. Data were trans-
formed using climatology distribution functions. A linear regression was initially tested
but gave lower quality forecasts because of the lack of flexibility, especially for further
lead times. A more robust method was then implemented, as a locally weighted regres-
sion. The theory behind it was discussed by Cleveland [21].
A locally weighted regression will iterate several time on data in order to find the op-
timal weight of each point to the general trend. The model starts on a simple linear
regression and evolves toward a more realistic trend. The further points are from the
model, the lower weights will be attributed to them. The main advantage of this method
is robustness and low sensitivity to outliers. More over, data are analyzed span by span
to really get the locally tendency. Considering N points and a fraction n ∈[0, 1] for
example, the algorithm will look for the true yi by taking n×N closest points to (xi, yi).
The number of iterations is a flexible parameter of this method too. Further weighted
local linear regressions can be performed. However, each iteration takes approximately
the same amount of time as the original fit, which makes it time consuming. For this
study, lowess function was used in Python with its default parameters (a fraction of 2/3
and 3 iterations).
Locally weighted regressions are plotted for each lead time on Figure 3.4 in green for
the training period of test case APS in the intra-day market. As a reference, linear re-
gressions were also plotted in red. Local effects can especially be noticed on further lead
times where data are more spread out and may need a more flexible parametrization.
From that graphic, it seems that a simple linear regression will tend to overestimate
lower power generations and underestimate higher ones.
The quality of this transformation was evaluated and plotted in Figure B.8 that can be
found in Appendix B.3. This transformation is not changing significantly the forecast
quality. It only seems to improve slightly predictions for further lead times. As differ-
ences were not significant enough, the method was not studied further. However, as
mentioned earlier, only default values of lowess function were used. Sensitivities could
be made on the fraction of data and number of iterations.
Even though improvements were not very significant, a combination of these transformed
data instead of original point forecasts in the weighted persistence model was tested.
Results in terms of coefficient and quality can be found in Appendix B.3 on Figures
B.9 and B.10 respectively. Estimated parameters are very similar to Figure 3.2, even
for further lead times where some improvements were expected. The overall quality
even seems a little lower than the first model, especially in term of MAE. A detailed
comparison should be made to quantify the slight differences.

RESGen’s particularity in the determination of space-time dependencies is the use of
both conditional quantile regressions and a Gaussian copula approach. The first one
will help estimating the predictive density conditionally to forecasts while the other
will determine the correlation between the resulting stochastic variables. As mentioned
earlier in this report, the Gaussian copula is the most convenient one to use in renewable
energy applications. In this chapter, focus will be made on the first decisive step in the
determination of the dependency structure: conditional quantile regressions. The type
of regressions that can be chosen is very specific to cases and data. Few literature was
available on the comparison between different models of conditional quantile regressions.
This section will study the comparison between linear and spline quantile regressions.
4.1
Spline conditional quantile regressions
As exposed in Chapter 1, the initial conditional quantile regressions implemented in
RESGen are linear ones. Regarding the overall trend, there is no particular reason that
the relationship between observations and predictions should be different than simply
linear. This comes from the simple fact that in the optimal case the correlation will be a
linear function in the form ax+b with a slope of 1 and intercept 0. Therefore, the trend
is expected to simply vary around this perfect case. While this will concern the majority
of quantiles, the extreme ones needmore flexibility due to numerous outliers. In order
to identify a more accurate trend of theses extremes, spline quantiles were thought to
be implemented. In mathematics, a spline is a function composed of piecewise-defined
polynomial estimations. While Python has no library developed to generate spline quan-
tiles, R was used for this functionality and interfaced via Python.
R’s quantiles functions were implemented with the help of Roger Koencker’s article [26].
bs function was used as a B-spline generator for polynomial splines. As there was no
particular reason for a change of trend along predicted values, the case was consider as
one big ”piecewise” fragment. Resulting quantiles will thus be continuous polynomial
functions of degree fixed to three. Another possibility was the choice to force or not the
presence of an intercept in the polynomial estimation. In the logic of what has been seen
in last chapter and the possibility of an induced bias along lead times, this intercept was
included in quantiles calculation. Sensitivities about these parameters will be discussed
in section 4.3.

Environmental and geophysical processes such as wind and solar distributions are char-
acterized by spatial and temporal variability. Considering the number of locations and
lead times, the necessary amount of past observations and predictions needed to com-
pute an accurate covariance structure of the European dataset will exceed from far the
3 available years. A subset of limited locations should be used to define a continuous
statistical model in space and time. In order to assess Ireland’s wind power resources,
Haslett already tried to define estimators of space-time modeling in [29]. A good review
of geospatial space-time models was also found in [30]. More recently, Gneiting detailed
some practical simplifying assumptions in [5] to model the structure of covariance be-
tween two space-time coordinates. However, it has to be reminded that the covariance
matrix needs to stay semidefinite positive. The enunciation and demonstration of this
property can be found in Appendix A, extracted from [31]. The model that will be
implemented in this chapter should not modify this assumption. The study bellow will
be based on the assumption of stationarity and full symmetry of the structure while a
discussion will be made on the separability property.
5.1
Geostatistical space-time models
Facing a limited number of spatially and temporally dispersed observations, geostatisti-
cal approaches have been developed to fit random function models in continuous space
and time. The notions of stationarity, symmetry and separability, defined in [5] are
going to be exposed in this section. Let’s consider the spatio-temporal random function:
Z(s, t), (s, t) ∈Rd × R
(5.1)
indexed in space by s ∈Rd and in time by t ∈R.
The random field Z is said to have full symmetric covariance if for all space-time coor-
dinates (s1, t1) and (s2, t2) in Rd × R:
cov{Z(s1, t1), Z(s2, t2)} = cov{Z(s1, t2), Z(s2, t1)}
(5.2)
This assumption could be discussed in the case of renewable sources as wind and solar
generations are under the influence of air, temperature and other physical flows that can
be asymmetric. However, this information is already considered and included in predic-
tions themselves. The estimation of the covariance matrix is symmetric by construction
in RESGen, as expressed through Equation (2.13).
The second assumption made for this study is stationarity of the covariance matrix. It
means that the structure only relies on spatial separation s1−s2 and temporal lag t1−t2
for observations sites s1, s2 and times t1, t2. This spatially and temporally stationarity
is characterized by a space-time covariance function C, defined on Rd × R such that for
all space-time coordinates (s1, t1) and (s2, t2):
cov{Z(s1, t1), Z(s2, t2)} = C(s1 −s2, t1 −t2)
(5.3)
==PAGE:53==
5.2 Separability assumption

The random field Z is said to have separable covariance if there exist purely spatial
and purely temporal covariance functions covS and covT, respectively, such that for all
space-time coordinates (s1, t1) and (s2, t2) in Rd × R:
cov{Z(s1, t1), Z(s2, t2)} = covS(s1, s2) · covT(t1, t2)
(5.4)
Let’s notice that non symmetric covariance structures are not separable. This separabil-
ity property is what will be discussed in this chapter.

are positive definite. Therefore, whether the covariance matrix is separable or not, a
combination of these functions will preserve the positive definiteness. They constitute
possible candidates to fit the dependency structure of RESGen.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
7.1
Overview of work
RESGen was implemented to facilitate the generation of renewable power trajectories
considering time and space dependencies. It involves the determination of marginals
predicted cdf with conditional quantile regressions and the implementation of a spatio-
temporal dependency structure with a copula. Different alternatives in the model were
conducted. Input point forecasts were seen to be improved with a simple model of per-
sistence. Spline quantile regressions showed their limits with too few robustness and are
not recommended for the determination of marginal predictive cdf.
Facing computational and data limitations, this report conducted a thorough analysis
on the possibility to extend the dependency structure in order to implement higher di-
mensionality cases with a relevant accuracy. A geostatistical approach, considering both
time and space coordinates coupled, was successfully designed through a combination
of exponential and Cauchy models. Final French test case showed satisfying results and
the emerging of possible studies to be followed in the estimation of covariance function
parameters. From the method exposed, high dimensional dependency structure in space
and time can be estimated.
The new structure of RESGen is emphasized with the documentation available in Ap-
pendix C. The code constitutes a great satisfaction in its own. The different studies
conducted implied the necessity to transform the whole framework. It was implemented
in order to release an open source version and thus allow an easy access and understand-
ing to users. The class structure enables possible adjustments and improvements.
7.2
Perspectives
Space for enhancement is left from this work. Main issue encountered with RESGen was
the difficulty to represent and certify the quality of trajectories, which are outputs of
the platform. While the general trend can visually be verified, it is difficult to quantify
the veracity of space and time dependency structure. The reduction of these scenarios is
a module that could be added to the code and would simplify the interpretation. More
technical aspects could be approached in order to make the code more efficient and less
time consuming. The use of Python as a coding language is thought to be a clever
choice. It allows a class-based approach that is necessary with locations and lead times
data structure. Even if the code organization has been improved during this work, par-
allelization aspects would be necessary to increase calculations efficiency. The creation
of cdf or climatology databases as mentioned earlier would need such improvements to
be compiled in a reasonable time scale.
==PAGE:70==
54
7 Conclusion
Spatio-temporal aspects of forecast uncertainty, which are relevant for decision-making
processes or probabilistic load flows, are well integrated in the scenarios generated by
RESGen for wind and solar power productions. They can be used for the study of renew-
able sources impacts in power systems as in [33]. Costs and welfare in energy markets
can be optimized with these scenarios as inputs of a stochastic optimization problem
[10][11]. The generalized construction of the platform makes it available for any stochas-
tic process that involves spatial and/or temporal dependencies.
Future of the electricity market is made of uncertainties. Distributed energy could be
one possibility, allowing every individual to produce its own electricity (solar panels,
small wind turbines) and store it thanks to different technologies (batteries, electrical
vehicles). Another option would tend to the ”European copper plate”, where all coun-
tries are interconnected and using each others electricity when needed. The idea behind
this internal energy market is to harmonize European electricity markets and strengthen
competition [6]. Also, it could allow to mitigate the potential price impacts of unpre-
dictable renewable sources [34]. A combination of both approaches (distributed energy
and generalized deregulated market) is in my opinion the most feasible near future of
electricity power system. In this context, RESGen would be extremely relevant and
useful in the production of scenarios, considering all nodes in Europe and different lead
times.
