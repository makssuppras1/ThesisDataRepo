Abstract
The rapid development of high-throughput sequencing techniques pro-
duces an abundance of examinable data, thus novel methods for an-
alyzing data of distinct scientific fields, omics, are highly warranted.
In this master’s thesis, a framework has been built and evaluated for
integrating data from different omics to account for the underlying
biology, the purpose being an aid to generate hypotheses for further
research.
To investigate the proposed scientific aim, data from two PhD stud-
ies, both containing distinct data, were utilized. Data from the first
PhD study were used to build the framework, while validation of the
framework was done using data from the second PhD study. By em-
ploying a mixture of PERMANOVA and network analysis, coupled
with independent computations, examination of the two-dimensional
data structures was performed.
PERMANOVA showed that percentage-based normalization accord-
ing to the peak area of metabolomic data was superior to mean- and
median-based normalization. Network analysis presented to be biolog-
ically sound, when compared to known biological connections. As an
example of validation of extensive hypothesis testing, an association
between the bacteria Cohaesibacter and a metabolite with a mass of
257.1857486g/mol was found to be statistically significant in the pres-
ence of a TDA-producing Phaeobacter.
The data framework and all its associated components have proven
capable of performing initial data handling of both pre-processed and
raw data. Furthermore, it is shown to be semi-automatic with filter-
ing being data-driven, and only the data given needing to be in the
appropriate format. However, there are optimizations to be made to
the framework. With more time and a higher data availability, optimal
benchmarking of the correlation- and network-based computations can
be made, to further improve the functionality of the framework.

Introduction
To couple multiple data sets, originating from different scientific fields, with different omics,
is of interest to many different stakeholders within and around the scientific world[1]. In
the past ten years, there has been an explosion in the amount of biological data worldwide,
partly due to an increase in the number of methods and techniques available in the labora-
tory. Data science is necessary to gain a better and deeper understanding of how to analyze
the abundance of data[2, 3]. This brings with it an opportunity to expand the knowledge
within each field, as well as lead to new and interesting findings.
By furthering our knowledge of data science within the scope of cross-omics analyses, we
can help in various fields, e.g., the field of precision medicine and molecular diagnostics[4].
Precision medicine emphasizes tailoring medical care to individuals, largely based on infor-
mation acquired from molecular diagnostic testing. Molecular diagnostics centers around a
collection of techniques used to analyze e.g. the genome and the proteome. Knowledge of
molecular biology and medical testing are utilized for the diagnosis. Furthermore, it uses
risk detection, disease monitoring, and individual therapy selection.
By combining different aspects of the human and veterinary biology, additional information
and knowledge can be learned, potentially leading to the discovery of new therapeutic tar-
gets, new biomarkers, and other applications that may be used to either diagnose or monitor
activity of both disease and therapeutics, thus expanding the field of medicine[5].
However, to fully embrace the possibilities, frameworks and statistical guidance tools need
to be developed, thus providing operational aid to e.g. clinicians.
The scope of this project is two-folded; Firstly, address the issue of dealing with multiple
datasets of different omics and variables, thus attempting to find and implement a systematic
method or approach of combining two matrices using multivariate statistics and mathemat-
ical modeling.
Secondly, use the systematic approach to examine the genomic qualities of bacteria samples
and find the chemical usage by looking at both metataxonomic and metabolomic data.
It was quickly discovered, that it would not be possible to set a hypothesis for this project,
seeing as it is rather exploratory. Instead, a scientific aim of this project is proposed:
To build and evaluate a framework for integrating data from different omics in order to
generate hypotheses for further research

Biological theory
This section covers the biological knowledge needed to process and understand the results

1.1.1
Introduction to the omics
Omics is a term describing a field of study in biological sciences. The word encapsulates the
methodology, terminology, and focus inherent to the specific field[6].
There exist numerous different omics, such as genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and
metabolomics, with the possibility of further fields of study coming to life, as technology
improves and human knowledge expands.
In this project, data from two different omics are used and examined: metataxonomics and
metabolomics, along with qPCR data. To aid in understanding this project’s purpose and the
related possibilities, an introduction to the different omics, what is related to each field, what
information can be found from each field, and how that information can be used will be given.
Metataxonomics
Metataxonomy is the field used in research of the microbiota of virtually all naturally oc-
curring organisms. Metataxonomics can be defined as the high-throughput process used to
3
==PAGE:12==
characterize the microbiota, thus creating a metataxonomic tree, showing the relationships
between all sequences obtained[7]. In its essence, metataxonomic analysis is used to profile
the microbiome of whatever biological area one wishes to examine[8].
As the microbiota encompasses both the bacterial entities and their related secondary ge-
netic elements, metataxonomy couples well with e.g. metagenomics.
There are different ways to examine the microbiota, where 16s rRNA and 18s rRNA se-
quencing are commonly used. The key difference between the two types of rRNA sequencing
is the specific sequence targeted and the organism in question. The 16s rRNA sequence is
a component in the prokaryotic small ribosomal subunit, also known as the 30S subunit.
This is, as the name suggests, found in prokaryotic ribosomes. On the contrary, the 18s
rRNA sequence is a component inherent to the eukaryotic small ribosomal subunit, the 40S
subunit[9]. Thus, 16s rRNA sequencing is used for bacterial metataxonomic examination,
and 18s rRNA sequencing is the equivalent for examining human cells.
The 16s rRNA sequence is a highly conserved and specific bacterial genome region that is
present in all bacteria[10]. The examination itself is largely similar, however[9].
Metabolomics
The scientific field that is metabolomics is relatively new compared to more established fields.
While scientists have been discovering the world of genomics since the early 1900s, it was not
until 1998 that the term ’metabolome’ was introduced to the scientific literature[11, 12]. It is
widely regarded as a highly emerging tool and field, which holds high potential for precision
medicine, especially in discovering biomarkers[10, 5].
The metabolome is quantified as a patchwork, build from results of various analytical
methods[5].
This is characterized by dividing the study of the metabolome into subsets
of metabolites, of which the various analytical methods are based[5]. The analytical meth-
ods include, but are not limited to, liquid- and gas chromatography mass spectrometry, and
high-resolution mass spectrometry. These methods offer perspectives related to biomarkers
of effect and exposure in biological samples[13].
Important terms to know in metabolomics include the mass of a metabolite, retention time,
the length of time it takes each compound to travel through or fall off the column in the
LC-MS, and the signal itself, the metabolite peak area. Peaks refer to the mass to charge
ratio, and have the unit of m/z. It is used to distinguish the mass from the baseline[13].
Peak can be measured both in peak height and in peak area. Peak height is the actual peak
4
==PAGE:13==
identification as measured from the MS, while peak area is measuring the surrounding mass
to charge ratio of the peak[14]. Peak area is determined to be superior in the context of
this project, as it is a better indicator of differences between samples, while also ensuring no
issues due to saturation[15].
qPCR
qPCR, or quantitative polymerase chain reaction, is a well-established method, used for
detection, quantification and typing in molecular diagnostics. It is proven to be easy to per-
form, minimizes contamination risks, quantitative in determining e.g. viral copy numbers,
and versatile[16].
The principles of a representative qPCR can be described in the following steps[17].
1. Denaturation, at high temperatures, causing the double-stranded DNA to melt
2. Annealing, at a lower temperature, connecting the single-stranded primers
3. Extension, increasing the temperature slightly, DNA polymerase attaches to the primer
and incorporate the complementary nucleotides to the DNA template
qPCR provides details about gene abundance in the samples given, allowing quantitative
information on a genomic level[16].
1.2
Computational theory
This section will go into detail of the different computational tools and statistical analyses
that are used to aptly examine the multifaceted data, and to generate statistically relevant
results of the biology expressed in the data.
1.2.1
Correlation analysis
Correlation analysis is a cornerstone in statistical analysis; In general, it can be said that
it analyses how different variables are related. A correlation is measured by the correlation
coefficient, which is always between -1 and 1. There are three correlation scenarios; No cor-
relation, positive correlation, and negative correlation. If the correlation coefficient is zero,
there is no relationship between the variables. A negative correlation coefficient means there
is a negative correlation and a positive correlation variable indicates a positive correlation
5
==PAGE:14==
between the variables. Where -1 and 1 are a perfect negative or positive correlation, respec-
tively.
Spearman’s Rank correlation
Spearman’s Rank correlation is a non-parametric method that measures the correlation us-
ing rank values to evaluate the monotonic relationship between two variables. The method
numerically ranks the variables from lowest to highest with 1, 2, 3, up to the n’th rank,
respectively, where the lowest value gets the highest rank value. The difference between the
rankings is found and used to calculate the coefficient of determination Rs[18].
Bray-Curtis
The Bray-Curtis method is actually a dissimilarity It is often times used for analyzing the
abundance of bacterial diversity, and have shown effective at analyzing multivariate ecological
data[19]. Thus, it is appropriate for microbiome and metabolome analyses.
Bray-Curtis works by finding the lower value for each species, summing the found values
and dividing by the total counts for all species[19]. It can be understood as how much one
sample ’undershoots’ the other samples.
1.2.2
Network analysis
Network analysis, in the simplest terms, is a set of techniques used to illustrate relations
among the features added.
It is used to analyze the structures that emerge from these
relations[20]. In microbiome analysis, networks are implemented to show how microbial re-
lations vary, typically between two or more conditions, e.g. where treatment has been given
and where it has not. Furthermore, it is used to display interactions between both commu-
nity members and communities as a whole[21].
Network analysis builds upon correlation analyses, by using the e.g pairwise correlation to
determine the correlation coefficient and combining these into a network, where nodes and
edges are selected by a given threshold for the correlation[22].
Networks and their related data structures are, in their own way, complex designs. To gain
a better understanding of how to operate within network analysis, a description of the basic
concepts and terminology will be given.
Creating a network essentially means visualizing all that can be found in your data, by link-
ing all the variables in the data set(s) to each other. Every unique biological variable, e.g.
a specific microbial taxa or metabolite, in a network will be declared a node. The nodes
are connected via edges, the lines between the nodes. These edges signify the association
6
==PAGE:15==
between the nodes, and may be based on different dependencies[21]. In the network anal-
ysis performed in this project, the association of the edges between the nodes is based on
correlations, specifically Spearman’s rank correlation, where the edges increase in width in
response to the absolute value of the correlation. Furthermore, the edges in the networks
created in this project were colored green to signify positive correlation and red to signify
negative correlation. This means, that if a line between two nodes was found wide and green,
there were most likely a positive correlation between the two variables.
A network can also have direction. This is indicated by having edges with arrows, pointing
from one node to another, and means that the node, where the edge ’originates’ from causes
a change in the connected node, i.e. where the arrow attaches. However, the networks in
this project will be undirected.
Once a network has been generated from the data, multiple different metrics can be quantified
from the network to summarize the structure of the network. One of these metrics is known
as the degree. The degree, also known as strength, is the number of edges linked to the node
and describes the node’s involvement in the network. Nodes with a high degree of centrality
often have high centrality by other measures and are more likely to be strongly connected
in networks from various domains[23].
1.2.3
PERMANOVA
PERMANOVA, or permutational multivariate analysis of variance, is a method used for
testing multivariate data, with additional option for permutations. Originally proposed by
Marti J. Anderson in 2001, the method utilizes non-parametric permutation tests to compare
groups of objects[24]. Specifically, the theory is used for testing the null hypothesis that the
center and dispersion of groups differ. While a multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA)
tests for the similarity of the multivariate sample averages, PERMANOVA tests for similarity
concerning a chosen distance measure, e.g. the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity[25].
The R-package Vegan and its inherent function Adonis2 provides PERMANOVA analysis
for any given data with classes[26].

2.3
Normalization
As mentioned in section 1.3.2 about the NCLTEE data, the metabolomic data used in this
study came from LC-MS analysis. It is high-throughput data. However, this type of data is
prone to contain non-biological signals, potentially leading to systematic bias[31]. Systematic
bias occurs from numerous causes: instrument calibrations, changes in temperatures during
the experiment, different people working on the experiment etc.[32, 31]. Bias is an inherent
side product when working with mass spectrometry-based data, and they can be found in
e.g. the accuracy of the mass measurement, the liquid chromatography retention times, or in
13
==PAGE:22==
the peak intensities[32]. If not addressed, it may lead to faulty results, consequently ending
in wrong conclusions on your data.
There are many ways to normalize the data and appliances for the different needs. However,
the aim should always be the same: to ensure consistent and accurate interpretation of re-
sults in data workflow and analyses by returning the data to a normal condition or state, by
trying to account for bias, thus making the samples more comparable[33].
Examples of normalization in omics data are linear regression normalization, variance sta-
bilization normalization, median normalization, and quantile normalization. V¨alikangas et
al[34] covers and systematically evaluates different normalization methods used in proteomics
data also from mass spectrometry, which should theoretically also apply to the data used in
this project.
This project tried and evaluated different ways of normalizing the data. The first normal-
ization performed on the metabolomic LC-MS data was a percentage-based normalization.
This was in relation to the highest peak found in each feature/mass, resulting in the highest
peak per feature being set to 100, and then the rest of the peaks in the feature being a
percentage of that, accordingly.
The second normalization method utilized and evaluated was a median-based normaliza-
tion. To be precise, both a standard median-based normalization and a median absolute
deviation-based (MAD) normalization were tested. In a median-based normalization, each
peak area is divided by the median of its feature, that is, the middle value in a sorted data
set. MAD-based normalization divides each peak area by the median absolute deviation.
This is used in statistics to measure variability, also known as dispersion. It is defined as
the median of the absolute deviations from the median of the data, thus:
MAD = median(|Xi −˜X|),
(1)
wherein Xi is the univariate data within a feature and ˜X is the median of Xi[35]. The reason
for using median absolute deviation is the robustness in relation to the standard deviation.
Furthermore, Chawade et al. found it to be a valid option for the normalization of high-
throughput mass spectrometry data[31], as is the case for the metabolomics data used in
this project. When using the ordinary median instead of e.g. the mean, outliers and extreme
values are not regarded in the same manner. It is not necessary to know the value of the
minimum- and maximum values in a data set.
Lastly, normalization by dividing all peak areas by the mean of their respective feature was
performed and evaluated.
14
==PAGE:23==
Some of these systematic bias’ were seen clearly in the metabolomic data set, in the fea-
tures (the masses of the metabolites). At first glance, there were a lot of indistinguishable
masses located around 132.xxxx m/z (mass over charge), with the ’xxxx’ being arbitrary
additional numbers. This appeared confusing, leading to believe that these artifacts were
due to technical noise from the laboratory equipment, during the LC-MS. To tackle this,
a user-chosen cut-off were implemented to the function for extracting the data, which were
set to 200 m/z per default, as there appeared a big clump of masses below the thresh-
old of 200 m/z, indicating that these were all inherent noise. Thus, all metabolite features
with a mass below 200 m/z, or whichever cutoff were chosen, were removed from the data set.
2.4
Data filtering
From the extraction of the data, appropriate formatted data sets, comprised of data from
two different omics, were now generated. However, prior to the actual data analysis, filtering
of the data had to be accomplished to improve the quality of the data.
The function for filtering data takes as input the pre-processed data from the data-extraction
function, as well as an indicator of which data set is relevant, be it the RWA set, denoted as
genom, or the NCLTEE set, denoted as metab.
When performing data filtering, the first mode of action were to remove features that have
a variance equal to zero. If a feature has a variance equal to zero, then that means that the
data values of that feature are constant, i.e. they are all the equal. This may be in form of
all zeros or all of any arbitrary number, although that is unlikely given the type of data.
As introduced in the section 1.2.1, the main method for analysis in this project is based on
correlation analysis, why variables with higher variance is preferred. Otherwise, the variables
will not be able to provide information to us, and can curve the correlation leading to biased
results.
Addressing possible features with a variance of zero were a main priority. Following, an
examination of different attributes found within the data was performed, to get an overview
of the next approach. The different attributes were the mean, the standard deviation, the
variance, the coefficient of variation, i.e. the standard deviation divided by the mean, also
known as the relative standard deviation, and finally, the ratio of zeros within each feature.
All of these metadata attributes were plotted, shown in the following figure, to be used as
an intermediate result, used for developing the method.

increased levels of depth. Furthermore, it would handle the risks of singleton or doubleton
occurrence. In microbiome analysis, a singleton means an operational taxonomic unit (OTU),
with only one sequence. Thus, the sequence cannot be matched to its counter strand[37].

However, in layman’s terms, and for use in this project, singletons and doubletons are defined
as the situation where all but one or two samples are equal to zero. This is a known issue,
frequently occurring when operating within microbiome analysis[37]. Single- or doubleton
occurence is often times caused by either OTUs or amplicon sequence variants (ASVs)[37],
and can be considered as noise, that are typically removed. An excess amount of zeros in
the data can be a major challenge, especially when analyzing microbiome data[23], because
models, be it parametric or non-parametric, can become invalid.
To rightfully implement an automatic and data driven cutoff of the zero ratio metadata, the
data was fit to a non-linear function, in the form of a Michaelis-Menten, expressed using the
formula:

found, which were all potential knee points. Then, a linear fit going from origo to the point
of the asymptote was created using linear regression. These bits of information would be

2.5
Network analysis
Network analysis is pivotal in this project. In section 1.2.2, the computational theory be-
hind network analysis, what it entails and its usage, was described. Now, it was time to
implement it in R-studio, and begin visualizing the data as per the steps 3 and 3a, shown
in the data workflow in figure 2.
There are a multitude of different ways to do network analysis. The method used in this
study is proposed by Peschel et al[23]. They have developed an R-package, called NetCoMi,
which is an acronym for ”Network Construction and Comparison for Microbiome data. It is
freely available at the GitHub associated with their study[41].
The R-package NetCoMi comes with 3 main components: netConstruct, netAnalyze, and
netCompare.
netConstruct builds microbial association networks, from compositional count data[23, 42].
netConstruct takes as input the data, and various other settings can be chosen, such as
the association measure and appropriate threshold to be used. netAnalyze determines the
properties of the network built, using netConstruct[43]. Here, the clustering measurement is
chosen, along with other determinants of how the network should present visually. Further-
more, netAnalyze gives a summary of the top variables in various network metrics such as
the degree, betweenness centrality, eigenvector centrality and more. Lastly, netCompare dif-
ferentiates network properties for microbial networks using a variation of permutation tests,
Rand index and more[44]. The information gained from netCompare compiles into global
network properties, such as modularity, edge density, average path length and more.
When using NetCoMi, a limit of variables was specified. Only the 76 variables showing the
highest variance were included in the network. Showing associations between every variable
in the data produced noise and clouded the networks, effectively decreasing the analytic
quality of the networks.

4
Discussion
Through this thesis, I have attempted at optimizing correlation analysis, when using multi-
omics data sets, thus coupling of omics data. Throughout my studies, I have balanced a
biological mindset, with an computationally analytical focus. This leads to the results, and
the discussion of them, being dual edged; on one side, I wish to fairly evaluate the com-
putational methods used to explore the biological systems, their uses and limitations; on
the other side, I wish to understand in detail, what happens bio- and ecologically in the
systems presented. Consequently, qualitative biological benchmarking upon the networks,
and quantitative statistical benchmarking on the normalization approaches will be included
in this discussion.
As the aim of this project is a part of the pioneering trope venturing into the topic of multi-
omics analysis, a noticeable part of this discussion will be based on internal results and
methodology, as external research is sparse.
As previously mentioned, the framework for the data pipeline is based on the RWA data
set. By using the original data format in place from when the RWA data set was received,
this is affecting the necessary pre-handling of any future input data. A clear example of this
is seen, when I began examining the NCLTEE data set, using the data framework. There,
format changes needed to be executed, e.g. changing from phyloseq-objects to the desired
data format, for the system to work. Although this is not uncommon in data frameworks,
such as R-packages, it still implies, that our system is not optimized to any and all data
formats, meaning the user has to do some data-handling, before employing the functions
expressed in the framework.
However, it needs to be emphasized, that once the correct data format is in order, the frame-
work operates both automatically and data-driven, as the filtering of the data is based upon
the metadata found in the data. This makes it amazingly easy to employ two independent
data sets and perform thorough analyses upon it. Underlining the number two, as it is cur-
rently not possible to use the framework with more than two sets of data.
Another important point to make, is that the data framework operates on pre-processed
data, i.e. if needed, the user is responsible for normalizing or scaling the data prior to filter-
ing and analyzing. However, as this was the case for us, when proceeding with the NCLTEE
data set, with raw metabolomic data in need of pre-processing, there are different available
functions in place for handling this. As mentioned previously in section 2.3 normalization,
these functions are used to normalize by either the peak area, the mean value, the median,
33
==PAGE:42==
or the median absolute deviation. Thus, the computations needed to perform these specific
normalization methods are already in place, and need only tweaking to function on new data
input.
4.1
Evaluation of the data filtering
I was successful in implementing a filtering of the excess number zeros displayed in especially
the metataxonomic 16s rRNA sequencing data. By utilizing the nature of how the metadata
for the ratio of zeros presented, I fitted a Michaelis-Menten equation to the metadata, and
used that to introduce automatic filtering, derived from the actual data. If we observe the
detailed depiction of the method developed in figure 4, we see an example of the procedure,
in which a threshold of approximately 0.73 is obtained, meaning that any variables that have
a higher ratio of zeros, i.e. the number of zeros divided by the total number of sample will
be removed from the data set. This is included in an attempt to improve the data quality,
without introducing user bias. This is determined to be a valid option, however, there are
pitfalls present, which needs to be addressed.
The dotted red line in figure 4 displays the linear regression, going from Origo till the be-
ginning of the asymptote.
This is used to compute the longest distance from the fitted
curve to the straight line, to find the knee-point. However, the secondary coordinate, placed
in the upper part of the Michaelis-Menten curve, used to fit the linear regression, is not
the actual asymptote. The secondary coordinate was set to 90% of the maximum value
of the zero-ratio metadata, in an attempt to avoid the procedure being affected by a right
skewed distribution, i.e. the high amount of variables situated in the vicinity of the asymp-
tote. Theoretically speaking, this decision should yield a preferable threshold, though it does
bring with it an aspect of bias, by not having the filtering being completely based on the data.
Another point of interest, in relation to the filtering of the data, is the risk of not having
zero ratio-metadata displayed in the same fashion. The system is built around the formula
of a Michaelis-Menten equation, but if the distribution of the metadata was e.g. sigmoid, the
system would not be able to handle it, leading to an error and subsequently a crash in the
program. In order to make the framework valid for any input, further work will be needed to
improve the error handling along with the ability to operate with data of a different fashion.
Furthermore, there is a potential loss of information associated with the data filtering as
is. When the data is filtered upon the zero ratio, the different treatment groups within are
34
==PAGE:43==
not accounted for. Instead, it looks at the data as a whole. So, in a situation where there
is a high number of zeros in a variable, e.g. if treatment has not been administered, and
a low(er) number of zeros in the same variable, where treatment has been administered,
there is a risk of loosing that variable based on the total number of zeros.
Antibiotics
kills bacteria, thus this situation could occur, where there is higher expression of a certain
variable, due to the presence or absence of antibiotics. This notion of loss via zero filtering
is supported by the findings of Peschel et al[23]. Optimally, there should be some cross-
validation, when performing e.g. zero ratio filtering, where the treatment groups within the
data are considered.
4.2
The optimal normalization
The metabolomic data used in this project were raw and needed pre-processing prior to
analyses. Therefore, I seized this opportunity to assess and evaluate the optimal practice of
normalizing data in relation to correlation-based network analysis. We used PERMANOVA
to determine the effect of the four different normalization techniques on the metabolomic
data, the results of which is shown in table 1.
The results of the PERMANOVA clearly show peak normalization to be superior. In rela-
tion to the model displayed in equation 4, it contributes to the smallest error of the fit, as
found by the sum of squares. Furthermore, peak normalization is able to best explain the
variance within the data, as informed by the R2-value, while also having a remarkably higher
F-statistics than the other three forms of normalization. In other words, the significance of
normalizing the data by the peak area appears remarkably more likely that the null hypoth-
esis (H0) of no difference among the group centroids to be false[24].
This is somewhat contradictory to findings in the current state of research, where methods
such as median, variance stabilization normalization, and quantile-based normalization’s are
highly regarded in relation to especially high-throughput mass spectrometry data[31, 45].
This is interesting, seeing as median-based normalization scores the lowest in the PER-
MANOVA results. There, the least degree of variance was found to be explained by using
median as base.
When examining the results of the mean-based normalization, I find what can be catego-
rized as the ’middle-of-the-pack’ solution. It shows a higher R2-value than both the median
and the MAD approach, but falls short of the peak-based approach, especially in relation
to their F-statistics. According to the PERMANOVA, it could be a valid solution for when
an additional point of view is wanted on data already analyzed via peak-based normalization.
35
==PAGE:44==
An important thing to note, whenever normalization and pre-processing of data becomes
relevant, is that the quality of different normalization methods is determined by the data.
Thus, substantial assessment of the different methods is an important and valid step to do,
prior to choosing a normalization method[31].
4.3
The analytical effect of normalization
Now that the quantitative benchmarking of the normalization methods has been covered,
it would be interesting to examine whether the same pattern occurs, when observing the
different networks presented in section 3 Results.
Figures 8-13 displays networks, where the metabolomic part in the NCLTEE data set is
normalized by the highest peak area. We see an overall inclination of high correlation, both
within data from same origin, and between the individual data sets. This is illustrated by
the abundance of notably wide green edges connecting the nodes. When looking at network
based upon the mean approach, namely figure 14-16, I get results in similar fashion, with
generally high correlations throughout. However, I find a lower degree in the networks, as
seen in e.g. figure 14 compared to figure 9. A lower degree would mean that the number
of edges per node is comparatively small in relation to the networks shown in figures 10-13
using peak. This can be good and bad; it can indicate an increased level of noise within the
peak normalized data, leading to excessive, maybe even false-positive, correlations. However,
it could also signify that the overall variance found within the mean-based normalized data
is low compared to that of the peak-based normalized data, which would be in line with the
results seen in table 1 from the PERMANOVA.
When observing the networks based on median normalization, figures 17-18, I see a theme of
significantly lower correlations, i.e. no wide green lines connecting the 16s rRNA sequencing
with the LC-MS data stand out. Although I can observe very high correlation, in the form
of clusters of metabolites strongly linked, as seen in e.g. figure 18, this phenomenon does
not contribute greatly to the scope of this project, of doing cross-omics analyses.
Importantly, I see a distinct difference in the balance of the number of taxonomic features
and metabolite features present, in relation to the method of normalization. As described
in section 2.5, the networks presented have been simplified, to only include the 76 variables
with the highest variance. When examining figures 9 and 11, which are networks generated
36
==PAGE:45==
upon peak-based metabolomic data, both with the control group and the treatment groups
of no TDA-producing Phaeobacter and TDA-producing Phaeobacter, I only observe three
and zero metabolites respectively in each network. This leads to the conclusion that the
data of the control group in the context of peak-based normalized data exhibit very low
variance, seeing as it was the common denominator in the figures. This is a problem, when
the goal of the network analyses is to examine the connections between the metabolites and
the bacteria.
So while peak-based normalization appears superior on paper, especially in relation to the
R2-value according to the PERMANOVA analysis, it does have its disadvantages in certain
situations, as above mentioned.
4.4
Qualitative benchmarking on networks
We have now discussed the path leading to the networks, both the pre-processing and the
filtering of the data. Furthermore, I have examined the effect that different methods of
normalization have on the correlation-based networks. But how well do the networks them-
selves function, both in relation to NetCoMi as a network-generating tool, and in the aspect
of their ability to help with biological findings?
I begin by looking at how the R-package NetCoMi and its options fit our needs.
When using NetCoMi, fortunately it is possible to select reasonably the association measure
of choice, which is beneficial. We used Spearman’s Rank correlation, as it was deemed the
most stable when dealing with non-parametric microbiome data. Additionally, it provides an
option to set a threshold for the selected association measure, thus enabling the user control
over what is displayed. We have examples of the utility of the threshold shown in figures 5-7.
These network are based on the RWA data set, and are divided into whether the samples
came from pigs treated with antibiotics or not. Figure 5 is displayed with a threshold of 0.2,
meaning that every line displayed, whether green or red, signifies an absolute correlation
between the nodes of at least 0.2, thus increasing the minimum absolute correlation among
nodes in figures 6 and 7. While it may be tempting to use a lower threshold, to increase the
number of connections, I observe that it creates an overly noisy plot, making it difficult to
understand what is going on. Consequently, it is clear that to optimally utilize a network and
its visual aspect is a balancing act. This is further exemplified in figure 7, with a threshold
of 0.7, leading to very few and far between connections. We find a more satisfying balance
in figure 6, where the threshold is set to 0.45. This yields a manageable overview of the
37
==PAGE:46==
network, while also illustrating the magnitude of the correlations reasonably.
It is important to note, that there is not a clear-cut threshold which works for all data com-
positions. As with the normalization, it is dependent on the data.
To assess the biological capacity of the networks, I compare findings with known biological
connections, thus benchmarking the usage in a qualitative manner.
If we begin by examining the coupling between the bacterial abundance from the 16s rRNA
sequencing and the antibiotic resistance genes from the qPCR, making up the RWA data
set, there are several outcomes of notable character. Firstly, when examining figure 7, we
observe in the right hand side of both groups that the gene TolC1 and the bacteria Es-
cherichia Shigella is closely correlated, with a correlation of 0.7 or higher. TolC1 is known
to be a housekeeping gene for E. coli, making it a gene constantly present, as it is required
for multiple cellular functions[46]. This outcome is perfectly in line with the findings from
Tams et al[27]. Furthermore, in figure 6, in the upper-left side of the treated population,
we observe close relations between Increp64, Sul2, tetA, BlaTEM, and StrB. It is known
that the latter four genes, which are all resistance genes, are placed on Increp64, which is
a replication initiator[27]. As there appears a strong association in the treated population,
but not in the untreated, we can deduce that Increp64 associates with the resistance genes
as a cause of treatment, meaning there is increased antibiotic resistance.
Lastly, Increp64 is found on Shigella [27].
We see generally more connections after given treatment, shown also in the number of
clusters. This is somewhat to be expected, as it is an indication of antibiotic resistance
formation[47].
4.5
Biological discoveries and hypothesis generation
Now that I have partly validated the prowess of the networks in a biological and ecological
sense, I can examine the networks created with the NCLTEE data set, in which I examine
the metataxonomic and metabolomic traits more deeply. In this examination I will hone in
on results of peak-based normalization, as I have established that it, in theory, should be
able to provide us with the most statistically sound results.
When looking at the populations where a TDA-producing Phaeobacter is present and com-
paring it to one where it isn’t, as in figure 12, I observe multiple interesting findings. The first
one is the cluster of metabolites found in upper right corner, showing overall high correlation.
When TDA is not deliberately present, I find outgoing associations from the cluster with
38
==PAGE:47==
e.g. the bacteria Marinobacter. However, when TDA is present, these associations seize. We
know that TDA is an antimicrobial secondary metabolite, why it may be the cause.
Another interesting observation lies in the connection between Cohaesibacter and a metabo-
lite with a mass of approximately 257m/z, which can be viewed in the lower right hand side.
There, I see a strong association when TDA is present and a weak association when TDA
is knocked out. Ecologically speaking, this may occur because Cohaesibacter feels a threat
from the secondary metabolite, TDA, and therefore produces or employs this other relatively
small metabolite. However, it can also be the result of co-production in an ecology where
Cohaesibacter is closely related to other bacteria surrounding it. This association appear
strong, visualized by having an association of at least 0.7, as seen from figure 13 in the center.
From figure 19 and table 2, I find that Cohaesibacter and the metabolite now known as
’257.1857486’ has a Spearman’s Rank correlation of 0.36 when the TDA-producing Phaeobac-
ter has been knocked out, and a correlation of 0.84 when TDA is present in the system. Fur-
thermore, when TDA is present, there is a significant association between the two variables,
while also a significantly superior fit of the model, compared to when TDA is absent.
Based on this, there appears to be a high chance that TDA influences the dynamics of
association between Cohaesibacter and metabolite 257.1857486. This link has been found
statistically significant and makes a valid foundation for extensive hypothesis testing, thus
completing the scientific aim of this thesis.
These findings have contributed to increasing our biological knowledge, by proposing vital
associations that lead to new hypotheses. By using the framework created in this project,
I am able to quickly go from raw data into potential new topics of analysis, to be explored
further in the both the laboratory and the literature.
4.6
Strength and weaknesses of the framework
There are a few notable limitations to cover, that became clear while doing this project.
Firstly, the RWA data set consisted of an abundance of data from both the 16s rRNA se-
quencing and the qPCR. Meanwhile, the obstacle of having to use the same samples in both
data sets, meant that I was left with relatively few samples in the NCLTEE data set, as
there simply wasn’t generated more metabolomic results. Consequently, I had to pool the
samples across time stamps together, effectively ignoring the time series aspect of the data
set. Optimally, it would have been relevant to have had more samples per group from the
39
==PAGE:48==
NCLTEE study to enhance the power of the statistical testing, but sometimes the circum-
stances of a project results in compromises being made.
On the other hand, due to the plentiful amount of data from the RWA project, I was able
to both build the framework around, and conduct analysis upon a single week of data. The
reasoning for using week two in the RWA data set, was that most pigs are treated in that
week, hence increasing the chance of seeing the effects of the antibiotics. Because of this,
there is a big element of further testing and examinations of the framework to be done, by
including the data from the additional weeks from RWA study.
As regards the topic of testing, I was not able to perform optimal benchmarking of the
correlation- and network-based computations.
Ideally, I would have preferred to do the
benchmarking quantitatively, by statistically comparing all of the different correlations across
the data sets, however that proved to not be possible within the scope of this project. There-
fore, I settled for a qualitative benchmarking.
The framework developed in this project is not perfect. It cannot handle every kind of input,
there are various additional components that could be added to both the pre-processing
and the filtering of the data given, along with numerous optimizations that could be done
throughout the framework, in terms of speed, memory handling, automation, and overall
function.
However, with just a few modifications, to suit the data in question, it can go from raw data
to graphically appealing and informative analyses, showing the structure within the data,
and leading to new hypotheses and discoveries.

Perspective & further work
The data framework created in this thesis is relevant for use primarily in a research setting,
but it also has potential to be valued in a clinical setting. As a device, it could potentially
expand both the rate and the depth at which new uncoverings occur. The principal reason-
ing for utilizing computations in biology lies in their ability to introduce novel theoretical
knowledge, while reducing the amount of resources needed. A framework like this is neces-
sary to combat the vast amount of data generated in the scientific world.
With further studies into the topic of coupling omics data, another dimension into the under-
standing of microbiomes, metabolomes, and more may be acquired. It may show significance
in the field of precision medicine, by the discovery of new biomarkers and tailored medical
care to individuals.
With more time and resources, the future of this framework can develop in the path of
more concise analyses and multiple omics representation. This could be both in involving
additional omics, not included in this thesis, but also in doing cross-omics analyses of more
than two omics simultaneously. That would in theory add yet another dimension to the
understanding of the underlying processes in organisms.
However, when venturing into pioneering grounds, it may be the case, that this is not possi-
ble at the current state of research. There may be limitations on the biological knowledge,
or a lack in the generation of data from the analyses in the laboratory, or in the possibilities
of computations. Or it may be a combination of all three options. Likewise, an additional
point of interest is whether the potential knowledge gained has relevance, or whether it may
be unsustainable in a skewed cost of effectiveness.
Interprofessional collaboration is a necessity, if progress is to be made in the field of multi-
omics analyses.
The programs created and data used in this thesis is freely available on GitHub. This is
done in accordance with the ongoing movement of data sharing, enabling sustainable use of
knowledge and contributing to the open source scientific world.
Lastly, just as the individual user of this framework has to evaluate their data in relation
to pre-processing, it is also acknowledged that the individual researcher must evaluate the
ethics behind the retrieval of the data.

6
Conclusion
To summarize, and in accordance with the objectives presented in the introduction, I have
created a data framework to perform initial correlation analysis of data with highly differ-
entiated features. The framework has proven capable of filtering empty columns, filtering
upon the zero ratios in an automatic and data-driven fashion, and filtering upon the vari-
ance. Furthermore, it has presented biologically sound networks based on Spearman’s rank
correlation, providing good visual representation, used to find levels of connections between
feature.
By using PERMANOVA, I have established that a percentage-based normalization accord-
ing to the peak area is statistically superior when operating with metabolomic data. Lastly,
I have benchmarked the usability and the validity of the framework on real data, exhibiting
clear applications of the framework.
Finally, I have exemplified how the path of the functions in the framework can lead to new
hypotheses generation for further research. How to go from raw data to statistically sound
associations, and gain an overview of the data.
The above mentioned should be regarded in light of the context of this master’s thesis. It
was not within the scope of the time frame and the resources given to construct a universal,
fully optimized, and complete design, usable by all working with omics.
In conclusion, while optimizations are needed, the framework constructed in this master’s
thesis can be used to combat the issue of data analysis of multiple omics, and may be an aid
to expand our biological understanding.
