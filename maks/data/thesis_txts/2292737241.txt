Abstract
This thesis concerns the investigation into using electric servo motors as haptic sensors for an autonomous
six-legged inspection robot to detect obstacles. Design and implementation of modiﬁcations on electric
servo motors is carried out, allowing for sampling of necessary parameters in order for these to function as
haptic sensors.
Two techniques for obstacle detection are proposed: acceleration detection and current integration. Both of
these techniques are tested and evaluated along with the requirements for communication with a high level
control computer, in order achieve functional obstacle detection.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
As modern society is hugely dependent on oil, immense amounts of effort are involved in its extraction and
processing. Since the majority of the Earth’s surface - that is 70.8 % - is covered by water [1], some of the
extraction of crude oil is bound to take place offshore. So called oil rigs1 are mainly constructed from steel
and placed at sea, where they drill, extract and store oil and natural gas.
The design of these offshore oil rigs vary, but the majority have load bearing constructions and moorings
made from steel. This creates a hazard as the rough offshore environment catalyses metal fatigue, with the
risks of oil spills or even entire rigs collapsing and/or sinking.
DONG Energy’s Siri platform is of the jack-up type - meaning that the entire platform rests on three huge
steel beams (legs) that can be lowered into place - which will have to cope with the enormous forces from
the sea. This hazard also applies to the main storage tank, which at DONG Energy’s Siri platform, is located
on the seabed [2].
Every day millions of DKK worth of crude oil is extracted from the Siri ﬁeld, and the cost of down-
time due to cracks caused by metal fatigue and other abnormalities is enormous. Therefore it is of great
interest if oil rig operators would have a way to be able to detect such abnormalities in an early state.
In August 2009, during a routine inspection at DONG Energy’s Siri platform, cracks were found in the
storage tank underneath the platform. The repairs have been costly and time consuming, with operations
resuming to normal as late as September 2014 [3] [4].
Currently, inspections are carried out by special diving teams and ROVs2. This method has certain draw-
backs, such as the size of the ROVs that at the same time rely on human control, based on visual feedback.
Furthermore, these kind of inspections are time consuming and expensive.
This underlines the desire for an alternative method of carrying out the inspections, that would take humans
out of the loop and reach otherwise inaccessible areas.
This thesis is concerned with the development of an autonomous robot for the underwater inspection
for cracks in oil rig legs and storage tanks. The advantage of using an autonomous robot is that - unlike
human beings - can be sent into oil tanks, that for practical reasons cannot be emptied, it does not get tired
and is much smaller than an ROV. Furthermore, it can be deployed to check the structural integrity of the
legs after storms, before returning the crew and sending in divers.
The environment in which the robot has to operate, consists of three-dimensional steel structures. In order

1.1
Project Objectives and Scope
There are many aspects to creating a complete inspection robot, capable of inspecting steel structures for
cracks, far more than can be covered in this thesis. Therefore, in order to limit the scope of this thesis, the
emphasis is put on the aspect of localization, a cornerstone of autonomy.
In order to successfully move on vertical and inverted steel surfaces, magnetic feet are used. The design of a
practical magnetic foot is based on the research carried out by Jesper Astrup Jørgensen in his bachelor’s
thesis. [5] The magnetic foot has already been produced and thus will not be treated in this thesis. The
magnetic feet can be seen mounted on the legs of the robot in ﬁgure 1.1.
The development and implementation of a technique for detecting cracks in steel, as well as waterprooﬁng
of electrical components and power consumption considerations, will not be covered in this thesis.
With these limitations in mind, the project objectives are stated as:
• The design and implementation of modiﬁcations on electric servo motor that allows for the usage as
haptic sensor.
• Analysis of leg performance when acting as haptic sensor.

CHAPTER 2

2.1
Background
The development of the robot was initiated by Per Dalgas Jakobsen (Software Arkitektur), who works as an
external consultant for DONG Energy A/S. As a consultant, Jakobsen has ﬁrst hand experience with the
problems of inspecting for metal fatigue on the Siri offshore platform in The North Sea.
The Siri platform is of the jack-up type, situated about 220 km west of the coast of Jutland, at 60 me-
ters of water [2]. Siri’s jack-up construction consists of three legs made from steel pipes of various diameters,
large enough to accommodate the robot between them. Other parts of the construction consists of ﬂat
steel surfaces with various obstacles, such as steel bars and beams, treated by Jan Lorenz Svensen in his
Bachelor’s Thesis [6].
At the bottom of the sea, a storage tank is located, which holds extracted crude oil, waiting to be transferred
to tankers. The storage tank measures (W x D x H) 50 by 60 by 17.5 meters, with a capacity of 50,000 cubic
meters [2] and is one of the main challanges when it comes to inspection for metal fatigue. Due to the fact
that the tank cannot be emptied for inspection in combination with the poor lighting and visibility conditions
and the strong currents and turbulence, divers and ROVs are unable to carry out inspections inside. This
again makes a desirable application for an autonomous inspection robot.
In order for the robot to successfully navigating this environment, checking for cracks in the steel structures,
it will be necessary for the robot to localize itself.
2.2
Operating Environment
Due to the nature of the environment where the robot will be operating, there are certain limitations for the
exteroceptive sensory that can be used for localization:
Vision
A vision based approach would not be beneﬁcial due to poor lighting conditions at the bottom of the sea.
This is especially true when operating at night and inside storage tanks. Also, the opacity of oil makes a
vision based approach virtually impossible.

CHAPTER 2. ANALYSIS
Laser Scanner
Laser scanning would seem as an ideal solution as this does not depend on lighting conditions. But the
refraction of light in water and oil makes this very difﬁcult. However, there do exist laser scanners designed
for underwater operations [7] [8], but these are too large and bulky for this purpose and do not solve the
problem of localizing in oil.
Global Positioning System
GPS would also be a desirable means of localizing, but does not work underwater, as the radio waves from
the GPS satellites do not propagate well through water.
SONAR
A logical and well developed alternative would be sonar,1 as this technology is well matured. Unfortunately,
the propagation speed of sound varies with both pressure, composition and viscosity of the medium. Sonar
equipment is also too large and impractical for this scenario.
Odometry
There are however, still options. Odometry is advantageous in the sense that it can be used with complete
disregard for lighting conditions and visibility. Odometry, however, is somewhat imprecise and error
accumulates over time2, which is why this option needs to be used in conjunction with other localization
methods.
Gyroscope and Compass
A gyroscope is a good option for determining orientation, as this works just as well underwater as above
water. Further, a three dimensional compass can be used underwater, as the Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld is also
present here. A common advantage for gyroscopes and compasses, is that they are relatively inexpensive
and come in small size ICs.3
As the operating environment limits the exteroceptive sensory that can be used for localization, an al-
ternative way of sensing the environment, is to use the legs as haptic sensors, detecting when an obstacle has
been hit. By comparing this information with a predeﬁned map, localization can be achieved.
Detecting obstacles can also be relevant to make sure that a leg has actually touched down on the surface
that the robot is walking on, before moving other legs. The implication of not making sure that a leg is on
ﬁrm ground before proceeding, could be falling over an edge, possibly resulting in the loss of the robot.

2.3.1
Current Integration
When power is applied to the servo, the armature current, IA, will increase until the resulting magnetic ﬁeld
generates enough torque to turn the armature (that is, given that the servo is not driving too heavy a load or
the applied voltage is not too small). As the armature accelerates, the current draw decreases until a constant
rotational velocity is achieved. Because the armature rotates relative to the magnetic ﬁeld of the stator, a
voltage is induced across the armature. This induced voltage is known as counter-EMF5 or back-EMF, and
is given by:

3.1
Analysis
In order to implement the desired obstacle detection scheme and to make the robot walk, it is necessary to be
able to control the servo motors of the robot’s legs.
For the purpose of locomotion, the Hitec HS-646WP waterproof servos are used, which are based on a
three-pole ferrite (permanent magnet) electric motor.1 As these servo motors do not provide any feedback of
the back-EMF2 and armature current by default, they are augmented with the OpenServo PCB.3
The OpenServo PCB is an open-source design and ﬁrmware, based on the Atmel ATmega 168 micro-
controller, freely available to anybody [12]. The OpenServo PCB, along with schematics can be seen in
Appendix A.2, ﬁgures A.2 and A.3, respectively.
The OpenServo ﬁrmware will need to be modiﬁed in order to implement measuring of servo motor current
along with position, velocity, supply voltage and back-EMF. Knowing the back-EMF is necessary as we are
interested in knowing the rotational acceleration of the servo motor armature, which would otherwise be
based on the second derivative of the position. Differentiating the position twice would be very sensitive to
noise, which is why it is desirable to avoid. By instead using the back-EMF, the rotational velocity can be
measured and calculated directly, making it necessary to only differentiate once.
The ATmega 168 micro-controller will have to be programmed using the C programming language to
implement the above mentioned features. As mentioned in the introduction, the robot has 24 joints meaning
that 24 HS-646WP electric servo motors have been bought, needing to be augmented with an OpenServo
PCB. Due to lack of supply, it has been necessary to have these boards manufactured.

is bearable to humans.

3.2.2
Sampling of Back-EMF
Sampling the back-EMF has proved to be a problematic topic. Figure 3.3 shows measurements of current
and back-EMF during a run with the servo motor. The ﬂat section of the back-EMF measurements occurring
around t = 17.65 s, is a by-product of the reconﬁguration of the H-bridge that occurs when the servo motor
changes direction.
After a closer investigation it was realized that the magnitude of the ﬂat section is equivalent to approximately
0.7 V, which corresponds to the voltage drop across a diode. This makes sense, as the two MOSFETs9
located at the bottom of the H-bridge (components Q1A and Q4A), have built-in diodes which come into
effect when the MOSFETs are open (operating in the cutoff region).10
When the servo motor overshoots its reference position, the direction of rotation is changed by switching
which motor terminal is connected to electrical ground. This is done by opening (cutoff) one MOSFET and
then closing (saturating) the other. When the ground connection is switched, the induced back-EMF (having
a polarity opposite to the supply) draws current from ground through the diode of the MOSFET operating in
the cutoff region, resulting in a voltage of −0.7 V. Because the back-EMF samples are signed depending on
the direction of rotation, the values end up having the same sign as before the change of direction.
A similar effect is seen occurring in the beginning of the run, where the back-EMF drops to −0.7 V. This is
caused by the large PWM signal sent through the servo motor during acceleration, driving a large current
through the armature coil. When the MOSFET controlling the PWM signal (components Q1B and Q4B)
cuts off, the armature coil draws current through the diode at the bottom of the H-bridge, resulting in a
negative voltage (because the diode is connected to electric ground). This effect is also seen in ﬁgure 3.1 as
the negative voltage occurring after each PWM pulse.

3.4
Modeling
A SIMULINK model of the servo motor including the controller was created in order to simulate the behavior
of the servo motor, and to help estimate the armature inductance, LA. Block diagrams of the model can be
seen in Appendix B, ﬁgures B.1 and B.2, respectively.
The model is designed to take into account the dynamic friction that occurs when the servo motor is
rotating, but not the static friction. The loss of output due to wear and tear of bearings is accounted for when
estimating the rotational friction constant, B, as worn bearings also mean an increase in dynamic friction.
Simulating an obstacle was implemented as a sudden drop in output velocity (to zero) at a predetermined
time, controlled by a timer within the model. This was done because simulating an obstacle as an inﬁnite
amount of load torque would make the armature position assume negative values, indicating that it rotates
backwards.
A PWM controller imitating the actual PWM control in the software had to be designed, as the PWM signal
gives different motor dynamics than a constant voltage applied to the servo motor. Furthermore, saturation
blocks were added to represent diodes, such that currents could not become negative (i.e. ﬂow backwards).

Analysis
In general when using sensory, a higher sample rate is desirable over a lower sample rate, as this yields better
resolution. In this case, due to the use of the legs as haptic sensors and because the robot will be moving
rather slowly, the necessity of a high sample rate is limited. It is estimated that a sample rate of at least 30 Hz
should be sufﬁcient, if the servo motors are to act as haptic sensors, in this scenario. Later testing will prove
whether this assumption holds. As the robot has a total of 24 servos, the time it takes to receive feedback
from each servo becomes critical. Thus the communication time with each servo must not exceed Tmax,
where:
Tmax =
1 s
24 × 30 = 1.388 × 10−3 s ≈1.38 ms
(4.1)
As the internal sample rate of the servos are 500 Hz (Ts = 2.0 ms), it will not be beneﬁcial to have an
external sample rate faster than 500 Hz.
The communication with the servos will be made as a plugin to DTU Automation’s RHD1 framework. The
RHD is a hardware abstraction layer (HAL) framework, that allows for easy real-time interaction between
high-level control and hardware.2 The RHD plugin has already been written and implements communication
with a servo motor using a USB 3 to I2C 4 communications adapter, the USB-ISS.5 Because of the limited
space and low power requirements on board the robot, the Beaglebone Black is chosen as the robots main
computer. Furthermore, the Beaglebone Black features a low price and a wide array of features, making
it an ideal choice. Therefore, the RHD plugin will have to be modiﬁed to use the Beaglebone Black’s I2C
bus to communicate directly with the servo motors, without the use of the USB-ISS. Furthermore, the RHD
plugin will have to be modiﬁed to read out extra data such as back-EMF and implement address changing
over I2C. The latter is much desirable as each of the 24 servo motors will need their own unique address and
will remove the necessity of reprogramming each servo motor with its own address.

Naturally, it is desirable to be able to change the I2C slave addresses of the servo motors using the I2C

of the servo) to/from an EEPROM.12 The special commands are summarized in table 4.1.
The RHD plugin was modiﬁed by adding an extra variable, saddr. This variable is set to the value of the
address that the user wants to assign to a speciﬁc servo motor, using rhdtest. Furthermore, a condition in

of the OpenServo ﬁrmware. First the saddr variable containing the new address is written to register 0x20
(the address register), then command 0x86 is sent and ﬁnally command 0x80.

be as short as possible to avoid data loss. The length of the two problematic communications paths were
approximately 1 m and were then shortened to approximately 0.7 m (including connectors), solving the
problem.

5.4
Summary
In this chapter, the testing of the two proposed obstacle detection techniques has been tested in practice
using a single robot leg mounted on a test stand. While performing tests, it was noted that the teeth of the
plastic wheels mounted on the output axle of each servo motor, had a tendency to wear, generating back lash.
Ironically the servo motors are shipped with these plastic wheels, despite having a pure metal gear train. This
primarily was a problem with the knee joint, as this was exposed to the larges torques. It is recommended
that the plastic wheels be changed to metal ones, for durability.
==PAGE:54==
38
CHAPTER 5. OBSTACLE DETECTION
First, the method of detecting obstacles by change in acceleration was investigated. The results proved
that this method is much more reliable than calculating the expected back-EMF. In all scenarios, a clear
indication of striking an obstacle was seen as spikes in acceleration, carrying opposite sign to that of the
initial acceleration, independent of the effect of gravity. Despite the ’jumps’ in back-EMF when overshooting
the reference, all results showed that it should be statistically possible to distinguish between these and
striking an obstacle.
However, further testing will have to be conducted to investigate the effects of sampling times longer than
16 ms, as the best obtained sampling time when simulating 24 servo motors was 23.01 ms.
Calculating the expected back-EMF by integrating armature current proved much more difﬁcult than antici-
pated. As a result, the expected back-EMF, EA,exp, deviated from EA in a manner that made it impossible
to tell whether an obstacle had been hit or not. In an attempt to compensate for this, interpolation of the
armature current data was carried out, trying to reconstruct the shape of the actual armature current. This
attempt however, proved futile as the interpolation did not lead to reliable results. Furthermore, it was seen
that the effect of gravity inﬂuences the result in an undesirable way, making this method very undesirable for
obstacle detection.
It was seen that the P-controller used, might not be precise enough when the load on the servo motor
is substantial. This was seen as the large steady state errors occurring when rotating the knee joint upwards,
working against gravity. Since this effect is only substantial on the knee joint, this is not considered a priority.
However, it might be worth investigating the effects of adding a PI-controller, in order to minimize steady
state errors.
As noticed when evaluating the results of the current integration method, the armature current, IA, will,
given the right circumstances, deviate from zero when an obstacle has been struck. Ideally, this phenomenon
can be used in conjunction with the acceleration detection method to achieve a more robust detection of
obstacles.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the ground work for using the electric servo motors as haptic sensors, creating a proof of
concept, has been carried out and documented.
First the HS-646WP electric servo motor was augmented with the OpenServo PCB, enabling the op-
tion to measure necessary parameters. The ATmega 168 microcontroller was then programmed in order to
implement the desired functionality of sampling back-EMF, current draw, position and voltage.
Then, a plugin for DTU automation’s RHD software framework was modiﬁed in order to run on the
Beaglebone Black and use its I2C bus to communicate with the HS-646WP electric servo motor. The
communication performance was analyzed, achieving a sample rate of up to 43.46 Hz when simulating
communication with six legs, surpassing the initial goal of 30 Hz.
Lastly, the two proposed obstacle detection methods were then tested on a custom build test stand. The
method of calculating the expected back-EMF by integrating the armature current proved difﬁcult and
unreliable, yielding no usable results.
The method of acceleration detection showed promising results, despite the recurring problem with ’jumps’ in
back-EMF when overshooting the reference. It was seen that a potential basis for statistically distinguishing
between the ’jumps’ and actual obstacles exists, given the right circumstances. When evaluating this method,
a concern for the inﬂuence of sampling frequency was raised and commented, as the maximum obtainable
sampling frequency using the Beaglebone Black alone, might not be high enough.
6.1
Future Work
The work carried out in this thesis will need further development, in order to achieve a robust method of
detecting obstacles.
First and foremost, the possibility of statistically distinguishing between striking an obstacle and over-
shooting will have to be investigated for the acceleration detection method to be useful. Further, it will have
to be investigated which sampling speeds are ideal for achieving possibly ideal measurements for statistical
analysis.
Using a development board like the Teensy 3.1 as a dedicated communications module, should be considered
if a desirable sampling frequency cannot be achieved using the Beaglebone Black’s I2C bus alone.

A.4.1
Conﬁguration of I2C Bus
Implementing the RHD plugin on the Beaglebone Black required reconﬁguration of the its I2C bus. As the
documentation and development of the Beaglebone Black primarily community based, at times it proved
difﬁcult to ﬁnd relevant information about the I2C buses.
Despite being documented as having two I2C buses, investigation proved that the Beaglebone Black actually
has three: one reserved for communications between the CPU and the power management IC, one disabled
by default and one ready and multiplexed to header P9, pins 19 and 20. Further investigation showed that the
third I2C bus, enumerated i2c-1 within Linux, was conﬁgured to run at 100 kHz by default. Increasing the
speed of the I2C bus to the desired 400 kHz, proved difﬁcult as the Beaglebone Black used here, is running
