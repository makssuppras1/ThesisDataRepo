Summary
This thesis investigates, applies, and compares methods for forecasting inflation in Denmark.
More specifically, forecasts are desired for the future development of the overall Danish con-
sumer price index, which is calculated and published monthly. As the best possible measure
of inflation in Denmark and thus the purchasing power at a given time, the index is vital
to many different stakeholders. That is why knowing the future price development is also
of great value. It applies to the future in both the short and long term. The problem that
forms the basis for the thesis is whether it is possible to forecast inflation in Denmark with
the help of mathematical models.
Several different forecasting methods have been compared and used to forecast inflation
in Denmark. The forecasting methods that have been used can be divided into four groups:
multiple regression models, exponential smoothing models, ARIMA models, and neural net-
works. To examine the applicability of the methods, observations of inflation from 2018
onwards are reserved for testing. Inflation observations from 2013 to 2017 have been used
for a so-called time series cross-validation, based on which decisions regarding model specifi-
cations have been made.
The results show that it is diﬀicult to outperform a univariate benchmark consistently. How-
ever, there are indications that it is possible to do as well as or even better than a univariate
benchmark when making forecasts with a time horizon of up to 6 months. It can, for exam-
ple, be done by constructing models that incorporate information from selected predictors.
Furthermore, in many cases, it is helpful to combine several models. It can be, for example,
an exponential smoothing model, a vector ARIMA model, and a multivariate neural network.
The performance of the models depends very much on the period in which the models are
used. Therefore, this thesis’ results are strongly influenced by the abnormally high inflation
in 2021 and 2022.
This thesis contributes first and foremost to academia with knowledge of inflation forecasts
in a Danish context. In addition, the thesis quantifies the quality of a range of different
predictor variables. The thesis also states that univariate models are very competitive re-
garding forecasts of future inflation in Denmark. Finally, this thesis emphasizes that the
predictability of inflation is highly dependent on the state, which is why future model-based
forecasts should perhaps, to an even greater extent, reflect the current state.

Preface
This master thesis was prepared at the Department of Technology, Management and Eco-
nomics at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements for acquiring
a M.Sc. degree in Mathematical Modelling and Computation. The work related to the thesis
preparation has been carried out in the period from 24 January to 24 June 2022 and corre-
sponds to a total of 30 ECTS credits.
The thesis has been completed in collaboration with Statistics Denmark, which, among
other things, has provided data and access to their systems. At Statistics Denmark, there
has been cooperation with the oﬀice for Prices and Consumption, which is an oﬀice in the
department of Economic Statistics. The work has been supervised by Assistant Professor at
DTU Nina Lange and Professor at DTU Pierre Pinson.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the reader to the thesis’ topic and problem formulation. The thesis is
proposed by Statistics Denmark. The aim, therefore, mainly follows what Statistics Denmark
has aimed at with the thesis.
1.1
Inflation
Whether it is past, present, or future, inflation is an important concept that affects several
parts of the development of society. The concept of inflation is a macroeconomic phenomenon
that covers an ongoing general price increase. In other words, inflation describes how prices
of goods or services increase during a period and thus how the purchasing power in general
changes over time.
It is not a given that there is inflation everywhere at all times. Prices of goods and ser-
vices may be unchanged. They can also fall. This phenomenon is called deflation. Prices
can also experience extreme increases. This phenomenon is called hyperinflation. However,
the most common is that prices rise to some moderate extent. Therefore, the term inflation
is used in this thesis in general, although price developments would have been more accurate.
There is generally broad agreement that low but present inflation is optimal [Nat18]. There-
fore, governments and central banks worldwide use various tools to keep inflation at a de-
sirable level. It is possible to pursue an expansive monetary policy where interest rates are
kept low. This will typically boost the economy but will also increase inflation. On the other
hand, it is possible to pursue a contractual monetary policy where interest rates rise. This
will make it more attractive to save money and thus help to keep inflation calm.
As the title reveals, this thesis is about predicting or, more accurately, forecasting future
inflation. Before starting this work, however, several things are important to keep in mind.
First of all, the development to be predicted is governed and influenced by the central bank
in the individual countries. Inflation in a country for a given period is a result of many
things. These include the precautions taken by the central bank in a country. This fact
does not make it impossible to work with inflation and its forecasts. It is just worth noting
that central banks’ decisions play a role. In addition, some factors are important concerning
inflation for different countries. This thesis is limited to primarily working with price devel-
opment in Denmark. In Denmark, there are extraordinary conditions that are crucial for
price development. The fixed exchange rate policy ensures the maintenance of a central rate
of DKK 746.038 per 100 euros on a narrow fluctuation band of +/- 2.25 percent [Nat18]. In
Denmark, interest rates are reserved for controlling the exchange rate and can thus not be
used at the same time to influence inflation. Denmark also does not have an actual inflation
target, as in many other countries.
However, due to the fixed exchange rate policy, the
development in prices and thus inflation in the euro area and Denmark are closely connected.
The inflation target of the euro area is to keep inflation below but close to 2 percent in the
==PAGE:17==
1.2 Motivation
2
medium term [Nat18]. In this way, the fixed exchange rate policy forms the basis for a stable
price development in Denmark. Denmark has pursued a fixed exchange rate policy since the
1980s. In the beginning, against the German D-mark and afterward against the euro.
In a Danish context, inflation is described by the annual change in a consumer price in-
dex. In addition, there are two closely related indices. It is the net price index that does
not include indirect taxes on goods and services. Moreover, it is the Harmonized Index of
Consumer Prices (HICP), which shows the development of consumer prices in the different
EU countries calculated in the same way. The HICP makes it easy to compare developments
in consumer prices across the EU countries.
Historically, inflation in Denmark has been higher than in recent decades. In the 70s and 80s,
it was not uncommon for inflation to be at 10% or above [Den]. In the last three decades,
inflation has not been that high. It is usually attributed to the fixed exchange rate against
the euro [Nat18]. Although inflation in the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s has been relatively mod-
erate, it is not a given that this will also be the case in the future. For example, inflation
in 2021 and the beginning of 2022 has risen considerably again. The reasons for this are
complicated to summarize. Possible explanations include the reopening after the pandemic
and the outbreak of war [Ban22]. These are influences that are extremely diﬀicult to forecast.
It is particularly challenging with a mathematical model.
It is obvious that consumer prices and increases in consumer prices are of great importance
to citizens. They risk that their money and savings lose value and that they are not able to
buy what they expected. In addition, sharp price increases are not desirable for society as
a whole, among other things, because they create unrest in society and damage competitive-
ness. Overall, it can be said that inflation is good because the alternative, namely deflation,
is far worse. However, excessive inflation is not desirable.
1.2
Motivation
The development in consumer prices is not random. This fact, of course, leads to the disci-
pline of predicting future price developments in the most appropriate way. This also includes
determining when one method is better than another. The two terms forecasting and pre-
dicting are often used interchangeably. However, there is one nuance difference that is worth
paying attention to. When predicting results, estimates are made for unseen data. Forecast-
ing is the sub-discipline that deals with predicting the future. This thesis is about forecasting,
as it is the future development that is interesting.
Clearly, it is of great value to many stakeholders to have access to quality forecasts of future
inflation. Future inflation is essential when it comes to decision-making in governments, cen-
tral banks, and other authorities. If there is a prospect that prices will develop undesirably
in 6, 12, or 18 months, central banks can take action [Nat18]. It also has great value for a
large part of the private business. When it comes to planning and budgeting, it is vital for
companies to know the price development in both the near and slightly distant future. In
other words, it does matter whether prices increase by 0.5% or 5% next year. In addition to
this, future inflation or expected future inflation is also crucial for, among other things, the
determination of salaries, contracts between companies, and contracts between companies
and individuals. It is also nice to know how much the money is worth next year for the
average consumer, as this can affect decision-making in the private economy.

Problem Statement
Overall, it can be said that it is desirable to know future inflation both because it allows
private stakeholders to act on it and because it allows central banks and other authorities to
act on it and try to influence it if necessary. The objective that benefits the stakeholders can
be inscribed in a problem statement that briefly and concisely forms the overall framework
for the project.
How is it possible, with the help of mathematical models, to forecast inflation in Denmark?
What methods can be used, and how effective are they in terms of forecasting future inflation
based on historical data?
The overall purpose of the project is thus to forecast inflation in Denmark.
Written in
other words, the problem to be solved is that one does not know how much money is worth
in the future. The second part of the problem statement proposes exploring and using dif-
ferent methods to solve the problem. Finally, it is also described in the problem statement
that mathematical models are preferably used. This is in contrast to macroeconomic models,
which often use numerous equations to model economic development, where inflation is a
quantity among many quantities. Such models are not implemented and tested in this thesis
but can be useful for comparison purposes and valuable insight knowledge into mechanisms
that help to influence inflation. In addition, the investigated methods should also be com-
pared with each other, and a sort of assessment will be made of how good the different
methods are. Various methods of forecasting the future are suitable for different situations.
By testing the methods, this suitability can be assessed.
It is crucial to establish one or more so-called Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to eval-
uate the methods.
It is central to this thesis and projects that deal with forecasting in
general. Initially, it can be mentioned that it is, of course, optimal to predict as close to
the correct value as possible while optimally also establishing some form of quantification of
the uncertainty of a given forecast. In addition, an essential part of evaluating a method is
also to establish a benchmark that can be used for comparison. If applied methods do not
outperform the benchmark method, which is relatively simple, the forecast cannot provide
the desired knowledge about the future.
Although this thesis deals with forecasting inflation, the applicability of the outcome is
not necessarily limited to this. Many other relevant macroeconomic variables, which are also
valuable to know future values of, are also diﬀicult to predict. If it turns out that there are
methods that can successfully forecast inflation, then it will be nearby to adjust, test, and
apply these methods to other variables.

2.1
Consumer Price Index
The purpose of the consumer price index is to show the average price development for goods
and services included in private household consumption in Denmark. The prices are collected
from the 7th to the 15th of the month [Den20]. The index is based on samples of thousands
of prices collected in more than a thousand stores. The consumer price index describes the
prices that consumers pay. It means that indirect taxes such as VAT and other taxes are
included. In addition, general subsidies to reduce prices are deducted. These general sub-
sidies have not been adjusted for in the net price index. In the same way, VAT and other
taxes are also not included in the net price index. The only real difference between the
consumer price index and the HICP is that the calculated rental value of housing is not in-
cluded. Apart from these nuances, the three indices are otherwise calculated in the same way.
In this thesis, the focus will primarily be on the consumer price index and its forecast.
This index is the key indicator that is of greatest interest. It has the most significant impact
on consumers and is the index that has been published for the longest time. In other words,
inflation is perceived as the increase in the consumer price index. However, both the net
price index and the HICP are still interesting. The net price index because it describes the
actual prices set by the stores and HICP due to comparability with other EU countries.
The consumer price index, which is published once a month, is based on a product grouping
of household consumption. The grouping of goods and services is in accordance with the Eu-
ropean International Classification of Individual Consumption by Purpose, ECOICOP. The
grouping is assigned weights corresponding to households’ expenditure on the consumption
of goods and services in Denmark [Den22]. The weights that are updated annually are used
to calculate the index. In addition to the overall consumer price index, there are five levels in
the hierarchical classification, where the lowest level is called the elementary aggregate index.
There are three necessary steps to calculate the individual elementary aggregate prices based

Equations 2.1 to 2.7 set out the main formulas for calculating the consumer price index and
the two closely related indices. In addition, several equations and calculation methods are
relevant in Statistics Denmark’s work with the indices. These are, for example, methods
that relate to the replacement of goods and services or change of reference period. The index
is currently published with 2015 as the reference period.
Although the methodology for calculating the consumer price index and the other indices
may not be used directly for forecasting, it is relevant to know when working with the time
series. It is noted, for example, that the geometric average is used in the calculation of
shop prices, product prices, and elementary aggregate prices for the elementary aggregate
indices. In contrast, arithmetic averages are used in the calculation of aggregate indices. The
geometric average also called Jevon’s index, has the advantage that price changes weigh the
same regardless of the price level [Den20]. When calculating aggregate price indices, only
the arithmetic mean is used. Here are no problems with different price levels. In addition,
it helps to make the index more transparent.
The details of the calculations underlying the indices, including the use of geometric and
arithmetic means, have been determined at the European level. Elementary aggregate in-
dices are not published. All aggregate indices are published to a single decimal place.
2.2
Econometric Forecast of Inflation
Before and during a project that deals with inflation and its forecast, it is relevant to inves-
tigate the field’s trends and development. Since reasonable accurate projections of inflation
are of great benefit to many people, this is, of course, something that is of interest. Therefore,
work is being done globally to model inflation and other variables. In Denmark, systematic
modeling of inflation and other macroeconomic variables is primarily done with so-called
econometric models. It is primarily public and financial institutions that, for many years,
have built and developed econometric models to model the entire economy or parts of it.
These models can then be used for different purposes, including forecasting the future or
modeling different scenarios.
One of the institutions that systematically model inflation and other economic variables
is Danmarks Nationalbank. It is done with the quarterly economic model MONA, which has
been developed continuously by Danmarks Nationalbank over the last decades. According
to the most recent description, the model currently consists of 336 equations, of which 42
are estimated behavioral relationships [Nat03]. The other 294 equations are divided into
identities, so-called technical relations, and non-estimated behavioral relations. According
to the description, MONA is a synthesis between the pure short-term model, where prices
never change, and the pure long-term model, where volumes are always in equilibrium be-
cause prices clear the market.
There are 336 endogenous variables corresponding to the
number of equations in the model. In addition, there are 401 exogenous variables, which can
nevertheless influence each other.
Central to Danmarks Nationalbank’s model is the many variables and equations that describe
the relationships between them. A simplified overview diagram shows many of MONA’s vari-
ables and interactions between these. It is included in Figure A.1. It gives an overview of
how variables interact with each other. In addition, essential exogenous variables are high-
lighted. The diagram shows that wage level and foreign price level affect the price level in
Denmark, while prices in Denmark affect other variables such as imports and exports [Nat03].
==PAGE:22==
2.2 Econometric Forecast of Inflation
7
Danmarks Nationalbank uses the model to give their best estimate of values of, among
other things, GDP, employment, unemployment, and inflation.
Danmarks Nationalbank
publishes these regularly as ”Outlook for the Danish economy” [Nat22]. These are published
2-4 times a year. A prediction is published for the current and the next two years. However,
only one value is published for the overall annual increase in consumer prices. There is no
monthly specification. This is a different way of forecasting inflation compared to the one
pursued in this thesis.
A model that is somewhat reminiscent of MONA is the SMEC. SMEC is short for Simu-
lation Model of the Economic Council and is a macro-econometric model used by the Danish
Economic Council.
The model is more than 30 years old and has been continuously de-
veloped [GS06]. The model consists of about 600 endogenous variables, of which about 30
are actual estimated behavioral relationships. The model makes use of approximately 500
exogenous variables. According to the Danish Economic Council, the model describes an
open economy in which inflation in the long term is similar to inflation in other countries.
The model is used in the Danish Economic Council’s work for, among other things, the
preparation of forecasts and impact calculations. The Danish Economic Council publishes
the so-called wisemen’s reports twice a year, which are general reports on the state of the
Danish economy. A forecast of inflation in Denmark is not published, but the European
Central Bank’s inflation target is used as a basis for other calculations. As for MONA, a
chart showing fundamental interactions has been released for SMEC. It is shown in Figure
A.2. Here it is again seen that the wage level and the foreign price and wage level affect the
price level in Denmark [GS06].
A third model is ADAM, which can be traced back to the 1970s. ADAM is short for Annual
Danish Aggregate Model and is used by several different public authorities, including the
Ministry of Finance and other ministries. Among other things, it is used to assess the effects
of various interventions and to make economic projections. The maintenance of the model is
today handled by Statistics Denmark, which has published a book that describes the model
[Den12]. The book describes the model as a synthesis between Keynesian and Neoclassical
theories. The model is more comprehensive than the two aforementioned. It has about 2500
endogenous and 1000 exogenous variables.
Out of 2500 equations, approximately 90 are
estimated behavioral equations. For these equations and several of the equations in the two
aforementioned models, it applies that they are estimated based on historical data and then
used in the model.
As is the case for the other models, an arrow diagram has been published for ADAM, show-
ing selected interactions. It is shown in Figures A.3 and A.4. The diagram shows that the
domestic price level is affected by wage rates, foreign price level, the so-called factor produc-
tivity, as well as tax rates and interest rates [Den12].
The three models described are probably the most widely used for public authorities and
institutions. In addition, some financial institutions are likely to have their own methods
of modeling inflation and other variables. Although the econometric models are not made
to forecast inflation in Denmark in the same way as is the intention here and, as such, are
outside this project’s scope, the models provide valuable knowledge about the interaction
between inflation and other factors. According to the models, inflation can be influenced
and, at the same time, help to influence various variables.

2.3
Literature Review
This section aims to provide a brief state-of-the-art overview of the relevant field of research.
It should help create a theoretical foundation, clarify the focus, and justify the project’s
relevance. The discipline of forecasting future inflation has existed for several decades. Over
time, several more or less mathematically based methods have been used.
2.3.1
Phillips Curve
One of the oldest and perhaps most famous methods is the Phillips curve, proposed by the
New Zealand economist William Phillips in 1958 [Phi58]. Initially, Phillips proposed the
inversely proportional relationship between unemployment and wage growth. It later turned
into inversely proportional unemployment and inflation. The Phillips curve is not perfect.
For example, very low unemployment does not mean infinitely high inflation.
However,
it should be mentioned that William Phillips made his empirical observations at a time
very different from today. The original Phillips curve from Phillips’ paper entitled ”The
Relation Between Unemployment and the Rate of Change of Money Wage Rates in the
United Kingdom, 1861-1957” is included in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Phillips Curve. 1861-1913 [Phi58]
Philips fitted the first curve on data points from 1861 to 1913 and compared the curve
with newer data points. In the following time, however, large parts of the Western world
experienced a situation that was not possible according to the curve. It was a period of high
unemployment and high inflation, sometimes called stagflation. Recent analyzes of, among
other things, American data points show that there is no longer necessarily the same strong
inverse proportionality as Phillips described in 1958 [SW08]. The Phillips curve is used today
by many as a theory rather than as a usable model. That is, further developments of the
initially proposed curve are most commonly used.
2.3.2
Key Review Articles
One of the most elaborate articles dealing with forecasting inflation is the 2008 ”Phillips
Curve Inflation Forecasts” by James H. Stock and Mark W. Watson [SW08]. It is based
on the attempts that have been made over the past two decades to forecast inflation in the
United States. Stock and Watson examine models that have their origins in the Phillips

2.3.3
Autoregressive Forecasting
Perhaps the most widely used approach to forecasting inflation is using autoregressive meth-
ods. These are pure autoregressive models, usually referred to as AR and extensions. Be it
the moving average part, which is called MA for itself and in conjunction with the autoregres-
sive part ARMA. Alternatively, the autoregressive integrated moving average model, where
the relevant time series is differenced one or more times. Finally, the so-called VARIMA
models or variations thereof, which are multivariate models, are also widely used. The afore-
mentioned models are univariate.

3.1.5
Partial Least Squares
In addition to the presented regularization methods, there are also additional dimension-
ality reduction tools. That is, tools used when there are many variables compared to the
number of observations, and some of the variables may be correlated with each other. A
very well-known and widely used method is principal component analysis and, in addition,
principal component regression, where data is projected to a lower-dimensional space by
including only the principal components that explain the majority of the variance in the
data. A possible problem with PCA and PCR is that the selection of principal components
is unsupervised. There is no guarantee that the principal components, which explain the in-
dependent variables the best, can explain the dependent variable, which is often the objective.
A possible way of working around this possible problem is to incorporate information from
the dependent variable when reducing the dimensionality. This is the purpose of partial least
squares, which is often referred to as PLS. PLS creates linear combinations of the inputs for
regression. The linear combinations are uncorrelated and are the components used for the
subsequent least squares regression.
PLS can also be used for two or more dependent variables, but one univariate dependent
variable is often used. PLS is a method that is not scale-invariant, so to avoid distorted
results, it is necessary to standardize the data to mean 0 and variance 1 [HTF17].

3.2
Exponential Smoothing
Exponential smoothing is a group of simple and more complicated methods that are used,
among other things, to smooth out time series.
In addition, exponential smoothing can
also be used to forecast future values of time series. The remarkable thing about exponen-
tial smoothing is that the most recent observations have the greatest weight and thus have
the greatest significance for the generated forecast. It contrasts the methods and models
described in the previous section, where the primary purpose is to describe a relationship
between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. In other words, mul-
tivariate methods, where the methods in question in this section are univariate.
3.2.1
Decomposition of Time Series
Before the actual methods related to exponential smoothing are presented, the focus is on a
concept relevant to understanding a specific time series and exponential smoothing in gen-
eral. It is the concept of decomposition of time series.
The concept is about decomposing a time series to consist of several components. Typi-
cally, there is a trend component, a seasonal component, and a residual component. There
are many different methods to achieve this decomposition. The most basic is called classical
decomposition. The classical decomposition exists in both a multiplicative and an additive
version, as stated in Equations 3.18 and 3.19 [HA21]. There are situations where it is more
advantageous to use one over the other. If the magnitude of the seasonal component remains
relatively stable over time, for example, additive decomposition should be used.
Yt = Tt × St × εt
(3.18)
Yt = Tt + St + εt
(3.19)
For the additive decomposition, the trend component is calculated as a moving average where
at least one entire season is included. After calculating the trend component, the detrended
series is calculated by subtracting the trend component from the original series [HA21]. The
seasonal component is obtained by stringing together the averages of the detrended values
for each particular season. In addition, the component is adjusted to sum up to zero. The
residual component is calculated by subtracting the two estimated components from the
original series.

3.3
ARIMA Models
ARIMA, an acronym for autoregressive integrated moving average, covers a wide range of
models and methods suitable for fitting and possibly forecasting time series. The approach
differs from other methods such as regression models and exponential smoothing in some
ways. One of the characteristics of ARIMA models is that they take advantage of the corre-
lation between an observation and one or more lagged observations.
This section and ARIMA models are based on the concept of stochastic processes. A stochas-
tic process is defined as a family of random variables {Xt} where t belongs to an index set
[Mad07]. It means that a time series can be perceived as a realization of a stochastic process.
Stochastic processes are defined in both continuous and discrete time, although time series
models, as in this case, are usually discrete.

make predictions of future values. For both univariate and multivariate ARIMA models,
the prediction methodology is similar to that used for, for example, exponential smoothing.

left-hand side so that it is expressed in terms of the current and previous errors and obser-

observations and k is the desired forecast horizon. Finally, the known observations and errors
are inserted. For longer forecast horizons, the expression will typically contain unobserved
values. Forecast values are inserted here. Future errors are expected to be zero.

3.4
Neural Networks
Another group of methods that in some areas are similar and in others different from both
ARIMA models, exponential smoothing and regression models, is neural networks. Neural
networks are machine learning algorithms that have a variety of applications. In addition to
classification, forecasting, which is the focus here, is one of the possible applications. Neural
networks are also sometimes called artificial neural networks so as not to confuse them with
biological neural networks that the artificial networks are inspired by. There are dozens of
different types of neural networks. Here the focus is on one of the more straightforward types.
It is the feedforward neural network with a single layer.

without the nonlinear sigmoid function, the neural network would be nothing more than a
linear model based on a weighted linear combination of the values in the input layer.
It must be considered an advantage associated with neural networks that it is possible to
describe nonlinear relationships. On the other hand, it is a disadvantage that a neural net-
work is a kind of black box. There is no interpretation of the values in the hidden layer. It
also applies to the weights multiplied by the inputs to each node. It contrasts with the lin-
ear regression model, where the estimated coeﬀicients allow an interpretation that provides
increased insight into the present problem.

values corresponding to the desired prediction are added as input. Using the weights, the
prediction can be calculated relatively simply with Equations 3.68 and 3.69. In the case of a
neural network autoregression, the historical values are used as input. For forecast horizons

are tests for level and trend stationarity, respectively. The tests have been performed using
adf.test and kpss.test in version 0.10-51 of tseries in R [Hor22]. They have been performed

words, the ADF test rejects the hypothesis that the series has a unit root, which is a cause
for non-stationarity. On the other hand, the KPSS tests reject the hypotheses that the series
is level and trend stationary.

value three times. Thus, the increase is assumed to be evenly distributed over the entire
quarter.

Denmark quarterly since 2010 [Den]. In the same way, as for the change in average earn-

CHAPTER 5
Methodology
This chapter on methodology aims to overview how the various forecasts have come about.
It includes how the different methods have been used, what choices have been made, and
based on what these choices have been made.
Before the forecasting methods are used,
a benchmark is established, which is later used for comparison with the other methods. A
measure of error is also chosen, which is used to assess the accuracy of the forecasts. It is also
explained how cross-validation is used for model selection. The end of the chapter examines
whether it is useful to combine forecasts obtained in different ways. It is also examined
whether the chosen methods make forecasting future core inflation easier than forecasting
overall inflation. Finally, it is presented how prediction intervals can be constructed using
empirical errors.
5.1
Establishment of Benchmarks
Two different benchmarks are established. The first is a random walk, which means that
the first and all subsequent predictions are the same as the most recent observation. The
random walk, which is equivalent to an ARIMA(0, 1, 0), is stated in Equation 5.1.
∇Yt = εt or Yt = Yt−1 + εt

As can be seen from the two equations, both similarities and differences exist between the
two metrics. Both metrics calculate an average error in units of the variable of interest. Both
are, of course, also negatively oriented. MAE calculates the mean of the magnitudes of the
errors. Here the sign of the error means nothing, and every error is weighted equally. For
the RMSE, on the other hand, the errors are squared. The error sign still does not matter,
but large errors are given a relatively high weight. One result of the equations is that the
root mean square will always be equal to or greater than the mean absolute error. It also
means that the difference between the two metrics can provide valuable information about
the distribution of the errors. If the difference is large, so is the variance of the errors. On
the other hand, if the difference between the two is minimal, so is the variance of the errors.
The sum sign in the equations is to be understood in the sense that a calculation is made
of the error for a given forecast horizon for all values of t. Working with several different
horizons means that a metric is calculated for each forecast horizon. That way, situations
can arise where different model specifications rank differently for different horizons.
5.3
Time Series Cross-validation
Overall, different methods are used to forecast inflation in Denmark. For many of these
methods, some choices have to be made regarding the specification of a model. For mul-
tiple regression models, it could be which and how many variables to include. For sparse
regression methods, it could be determining the value of a hyperparameter. For exponential
smoothing methods, it could be the choice between additive or multiplicative error models
or the choice between different options for the trend and seasonal components. For ARIMA
models, it could be the choice between different orders. For multivariate ARIMA models,
it could be whether a regressor should be included or not. Finally, for neural networks, it
could be the determination of architecture.
All of these different choices must be made on an informed basis. It would be invalid to
make the choices based on an in-sample error. This will lead to overfitting. Cross-validation
is used to select the optimal specifications. Based on the cross-validation, the estimated pre-
diction error can be calculated. The choices regarding model specification are made based on
the desire for as low an estimated prediction error as possible. After deciding on the model
specification, the model’s performance is tested. This is done with a test set, based on which
no decisions are made other than the usual estimation of coeﬀicients.
In the present case, it is decided that no decisions regarding model specification are made
based on data from January 2018 onwards. Therefore, values of inflation and other rele-
vant time series up to and including December 2017 are used to select the optimal model
specifications. In addition to cross-validation, it is also done based on information criteria,
correlations, autocorrelations, and relevant plots. The advantage of cross-validation com-
pared to information criteria is that the calculated errors are comparable, which is not the
case with the information criteria.
There are different versions of cross-validation. However, approaches such as k-fold cross-
validation and leave-one-out cross-validation are not viable for time series.
It would be
problematic to use future observations to estimate a prediction error. To avoid this, time
series cross-validation, which is a variation proposed by several references, is used [HA21].
This variation, which ensures that only previous observations are used to make a prediction,
is outlined in Figure 5.1, where orange cells should represent data used to train a model and
red cells should represent data used to test a model and thus calculate an error.

One thing that is important to note is that this approach, unlike later methods, is not based
on lagged values of the predictors. That is, future values of the predictors must be forecasted
in order to be used to forecast inflation. It is done with a simple AR(1) model for all predic-
tors. This is a weakness of this method. There is undoubtedly uncertainty associated with
the forecasts of the predictors. This fact also degrades the quality of the calculated predic-
tion intervals. For the shortest horizons, the proportion of predictions that fall within the
interval is not even close to the 95% it should be. For longer horizons, it is even worse. This
is because the interval calculation assumes that the predictors’ forecasted values are correct,
which is obviously not the case. Therefore, the prediction intervals become erroneous and
unusable.
Considering the two measures of error, it is seen that the conclusions are the same whether
evaluating RMSE or MAE. The performance is similar for several of the models. It is seen
that LRM1 and LRM3 perform largely similarly in the cross-validation. The Wheat term
in LRM1 that is mostly insignificant is the only difference between the two models.
In
general, it is also seen that both RMSE and MAE, as expected, become larger the longer
the horizon. When inspecting the table, it is obvious that the model based on backward
stepwise regression performs better than the other models, which is why this model must be
considered usable. It has EuroHICP and Earnings in common with the other models in the
table but also includes variables, which were deselected in Section 4.3. These include Supply
and Vacancies.

5.5
Comparison of Exponential Smoothing Models
In Section 3.2, various models within the exponential smoothing framework are presented.
With the introduction of the state space notation, the models are classified according to
the included components. The possible models are stated in Tables B.1 and B.2. Of the
eighteen models in the tables, the three additive error models with a multiplicative seasonal
component are excluded in advance. In addition, the nine multiplicative error models are
excluded. It is done to allow values that are not strictly positive. According to Hyndman,
negative and zero values will lead to numerically unstable multiplicative errors [HA21]. It is
certainly possible that consumer prices are unchanged or slightly declining in the short term.
In addition, none of the findings in Section 4.1 indicate that the seasonal variation increases
over time. It means that there are six possible specifications left.
The further model selection is somewhat similar to the one used for the linear models. Since
all exponential smoothing models are univariate, nothing prevents the entire time series dat-
ing back to 1991 from being used. For each of the six models, the smoothing parameters are
estimated using the inflation series from 1991 to 2017. In addition, the information criteria,
AIC, AICc, and BIC, are calculated. To make the estimates and calculate the information
criteria, ets in version 8.16 of forecast in R has been applied [Hyn22]. The model specifi-
cations and the resulting information criteria are shown in Table 5.7. For all three criteria,
it is clear that there is a big difference between the models with and without a seasonal
component. The seasonal component is penalized but does not add value to the model. This
is consistent with the finding in Section 4.1 that the impact of the seasonal component is
minimal.

the predicted intervals is acceptable.

regressors. Lagged values of the regressor are used instead to avoid this. A fifth ARIMAX

is also low for short horizons. However, it is particularly interesting that the predictions
appear to be more accurate for the long horizons. Here, both RMSE and MAE are smaller
than for the other methods.
A sixth model is constructed, which, like ARIMAX4 and ARIMAX5, uses Earnings and
Supply as regressors. The model differs by forecasting future values of the regressors with a
random walk. In other words, the most recently observed value is repeated 18 times and is
thus used to make predictions. From the cross-validated RMSE and MAE in Figure 5.14, it
is seen that it has been possible to combine good results for short horizons from ARIMAX4
and good results for long horizons from ARIMAX5. Also, the proportion of correct predic-

the regressors are generated. Overall, it can be concluded that the chosen seasonal ARIMA
model was successfully improved by including exogenous regressors. The chosen ARIMAX
model is the first, where the variables selected by the backward stepwise regression are used
as regressors.

5.7.2
VARIMA
The cross-validation of the ARIMAX models shows that it may be advantageous to intro-
duce an exogenous input in several cases. However, the ARIMAX models do not take into
account that the exogenous variables are also affected by the other variables in the model.
That is why four VARIMA models are formulated and cross-validated similarly to previous
model specifications. The formulation of the four models is based on the formulation of the
ARIMAX models and thus also on Section 4.3 on variable selection. All VARIMA models are
estimated using marima in version 2.2 of marima in R [Spl17]. Before the model parameters
are estimated, the model order is specified. It is done using define.model in version 2.2 of
marima [Spl17]. All VARIMA models are defined to generate autoregression at lag 1 and
seasonal moving average terms at lag 12. Before estimating the parameters, all variables are
also differenced using define.dif in marima in R. Thus, the specification is similar to that
established for the univariate ARIMA models. In general, there are different designations
for VARIMA models in the literature. Even though models elsewhere are called multivariate
ARIMA models or MARIMA models, they may be the same type of VARIMA models used
here.
The first VARIMA model is constructed by including the variables selected by the backward
stepwise regression and inflation as endogenous variables. The model variables are listed in
Table 5.15, where the other three VARIMA models are also described. For the model, the
beginning of the training period is identical to that for ARIMAX1. No information criteria
are calculated for the various models estimated on all available data until December 2017.
These would not be comparable. In return, the Akaike information criterion or a variation
thereof is used to estimate the model parameters in the R function called marima [Spl17].
The parameters are estimated in a maximum of 50 iterations. In R, marima performs a
stepwise model reduction using a penalty that must be specified. Initially, no penalty is
applied. The cross-validated RMSE and MAE are calculated. Both measures of error and
the proportion of predictions that fall within the prediction intervals are shown in Table
5.16. It is seen that the errors are relatively small for long horizons when compared to, for

5.8
Building Feedforward Neural Networks
The last new method used to forecast inflation is neural networks. Specifically, the type of
neural network called feedforward neural network is described in Section 3.4. The remark-
able thing about this method is that it does allow non-linear relationships between input
and output variables. The advantage of using neural networks in this context is thus that
it is uncovered how a non-linear model performs compared to the linear ones. On the other
hand, it is a disadvantage that no further interpretation of the estimated weights is made.
As described in Section 3.4, only networks with a single hidden layer are constructed. This
is chosen to limit the number of weights to be estimated. Already, there are only a few
hundred or fewer observations available. With an approximately equal number of weights to
be estimated, it is important to be aware of overfitting. A total of four different models are
investigated - one univariate and three multivariates, where additional variables are used as
exogenous input.
The first univariate neural network can be constructed with many different architectures.
It is not known in advance which architecture is the best. The number of input nodes and
the number of nodes in the hidden layers must be determined. However, the number of
nodes in the hidden layers is determined as half the number of input nodes plus one. This is
a default choice [HA21]. The number of non-seasonal lags and seasonal lags must be deter-
mined. For all models, a seasonal lag is included. This is decided based on Section 4.1, which
clearly shows seasonality. For the non-seasonal layers, seven options are examined. For each
of the numbers of non-seasonal lags, the cross-validated RMSE, MAE, and the proportion
of correct predictions are calculated and reported in Table 5.18. All weights in all neural
networks are estimated using nnetar in version 8.16 of forecast in R [Hyn22]. The predictions
and the 95% prediction intervals are calculated using forecast in the same version forecast
and are in accordance with the description in Section 3.4. Of course, when the number of
non-seasonal lags exceeds 12, this lag is not included twice. It means that the number of
input nodes for the seven different specifications is 6, 8, 10, 12, 13, 15, and 17.
From Table 5.18, it is seen that the results are not impressive compared to other mod-
els. Nevertheless, the model specification with 13 non-seasonal lags performs better than
the others. Another interesting observation is that the proportion of predictions within the
intervals is tiny. This is especially pronounced for long horizons. These intervals are practi-
cally useless, suggesting that there is something wrong with the method by which they are
constructed. The intervals are constructed with simulations as described in Section 3.4.3. It
is done using normally distributed errors, which are too small compared to realistic residuals
in an out-of-sample context. Therefore, for further use of neural networks, a different method
of calculating prediction intervals is necessary. It could, for example, be an approach based
on previous errors.

5.9
Combining Forecasts
With the neural networks, the investigation of different methods for forecasting inflation
in Denmark is completed. In addition to the univariate and multivariate neural networks,
univariate and multivariate ARIMA models, exponential smoothing, and regularized and
unregularized linear models have been investigated. Nevertheless, another approach is immi-
nent to examine now that such different methods have been used. It is to use combinations
of forecasts produced with different approaches. Armstrong discusses just this and, among
other things, key principles for combining forecasts and useful applications [Arm01]. In ad-
dition to this, Armstrong examines the improvement by combining forecasts for 30 different
studies that include vastly different methods, data, measures of error, and horizons. The
number of components also varies but is typically between two and six.
The desire that underlies combining forecasts is that the average of several forecasts is
better than a single forecast. The approach assumes that each method adds knowledge that
is not contained in the other methods. Each method is used separately and then constitutes
a so-called component. Here, two combinations of forecasts are constructed - a univariate
and a multivariate. Both combinations contain three components. The three components
are used to construct the combined forecast using equal weights. Even though it is possible,
varying weights between the components and thus the three methods are not used here. No
domain knowledge or in-depth analysis indicates that it is advantageous to weigh the meth-
ods differently.
The first combination combines forecasts obtained with an exponential smoothing model,
a univariate ARIMA model, and a univariate neural network. The exponential smoothing
model is the specification with additive errors and a damped additive trend. The ARIMA
model is specified as (1, 1, 0)×(0, 0, 1)12. In the neural network, 13 non-seasonal input nodes
are included. The motivation behind this combination is that all three models are univariate.
Thus, the performance of this combination indicates whether it is possible to combine and
obtain better performance in the univariate case. For the combination, the cross-validated

5.10
Forecasting Core Inflation
When making forecasts of inflation and forecasts in general, it is essential to be clear about
what time series are forecasted and what the purpose of this is. So far, only forecasts of the
change in the overall consumer price index compared to the previous year have been made.
This is because this index is often considered the actual inflation in Denmark. However,
many other indices describe the price development for various products and services. Every
month, Statistics Denmark publishes 384 elementary indices and aggregate indices. Each of
these could, in principle, be forecasted according to the same principles as is done with the
overall consumer price index. Perhaps the second most interesting aggregate index is the
one that includes the entire consumer price index except energy and unprocessed food. In
2022, this index comprises 87,79% of the total consumer price index [Den22]. The change in
the index is often referred to as the core inflation.
Energy and unprocessed food have in common that both have historically been subject
to unstable price changes [Spa11]. One of the purposes of core inflation is to describe the
underlying price development. It is interesting from a forecaster’s point of view. It is un-
doubtedly helpful if it is possible to forecast the underlying price development. In addition,
the supposedly most volatile elements are not included. One can imagine that core inflation
is, therefore, easier to predict.

using exponential smoothing, the measures of errors are small in both cases. Thus, there
are no clear indications that it should be particularly advantageous and easy to forecast
core inflation from a forecaster’s point of view.
However, the conclusion is not quite as
unambiguous as in Bermingham’s dissertation, as, for example, lower measures of error are
achieved with the benchmark models when forecasting core inflation.
5.11
Constructing Prediction Intervals
So far, forecasts of future inflation have been made in various ways. The primary focus has
been on the so-called point forecast. The performance of this point forecast has been used
in the selection of methods. Prediction intervals have also been calculated for many of the
methods. It is initially done as described in Chapter 3. However, there are some methods
where there is an obvious need for an alternative approach to constructing the intervals.
It can be seen, for example, in the fact that the proportion of correct predictions in the
cross-validation is either way too large or way too small when keeping in mind that these
are intervals that should include 95% of the observations. It may be because underlying
assumptions are not met, which is the case in, for example, Section 5.4.2. The fact that the
intervals are unreliable is problematic. The intervals or another measure of uncertainty is
==PAGE:91==
5.11 Constructing Prediction Intervals
76
perhaps as important a part of a forecast as the point forecast itself.
Instead of using unreliable in-sample estimates for the uncertainty, it is obvious to use
past out-of-sample forecast errors to construct future prediction intervals. It can be done
in several different ways. One of them is using so-called parametric assumptions, the most
common of which is the assumption that the forecast errors are normally distributed [HA21].
With an estimate of the two parameters of a normal distribution, it will be possible to con-
struct intervals that include a desired part of the probability distribution. However, there
is no guarantee that the forecast errors are normally distributed. Therefore, normality tests
are performed to test the null hypothesis that the forecast errors come from normal distribu-
tions. So-called Lilliefors tests are conducted using lillie.test in version 1.0-4 of nortest in R
[Lig15]. The resulting p-values from the tests are listed in Table 5.24. p-values that give rise
to rejecting the null hypothesis at a 5% significance level are marked in bold. From the table,
it is seen that the results of the normality tests are diverse. For most tests, the null hypoth-
esis cannot be rejected. It suggests that it is possible to apply a parametric assumption of
normality. On the other hand, there are several tests where the null hypothesis is rejected
at a 5% significance level, which means that the forecast errors are not normally distributed.
This is especially the case for the long forecast horizons. It is further seen that the conclu-
sion is different depending on which forecasting method is used. It is kept in mind that a
uniform way of constructing prediction intervals regardless of method is desired. Because
the normality tests do not unequivocally indicate normality for all horizons, the parametric
assumption must therefore be considered violated.

Rather than a parametric approach, prediction intervals are constructed using an empirical
error distribution. This approach no longer assumes normally distributed errors [HA21]. The
idea is again that past errors provide information about a future forecast distribution. When
working with forecasts a unit of time in the future, the error related to the point forecast
can be written as et = yt −ˆyt|t−1. When rearranging and changing the indices, the term for
the error can be used to express the forecast as in Equation 5.5, where eT+1 is an unknown
error and ˆyT+1|T is the point forecast [HA21].
yT+1 = ˆyT+1|T + eT+1
(5.5)
Instead of the unknown error, a previous error is inserted. It can be done an arbitrary num-
ber of times. The errors to be inserted are the previous errors. In this way, many suggestions
for the future forecast are obtained. The prediction intervals can then be relatively simply
==PAGE:92==
5.11 Constructing Prediction Intervals
77
determined as quantiles among the distribution of forecasts. For example, a 95% prediction
interval is determined as the 2.5% and 97.5% quantiles.
Prediction intervals for longer horizons are constructed in the same way.
The error re-
lated to forecasts h steps ahead can be written as et = yt −ˆyt|t−h. The only difference is that
previous errors observed for that particular horizon are used. This is stated in Equation 5.6,
where eT+h is the unknown error to be replaced with the previous h-step errors.
yT+h = ˆyT+h|T + eT+h
(5.6)
The previous errors must represent the distribution of future errors. Therefore, no in-sample
errors are used for this purpose. Instead, out-of-sample errors calculated in the validation
and test periods are used. In the present case, cross-validation takes place from 2013 through
2017. That is 60 months in total. The approach does not make sense when it is performed
with only a few errors. Therefore, no empirical prediction intervals are constructed until the
end of 2017. It is assumed that the distribution of previous errors at this point resembles
reality. For each prediction interval, all observed errors since the beginning of the validation
period are inserted. In R, quantile in version 3.6.2 in stats is used to determine the quantiles.
The advantage of the described approach to the construction of intervals is that it is empir-
ical. It assumes that future forecast errors are distributed in the same way as the previous
errors. The downside is that an empirical error distribution is not as transparent as a the-
oretical method. Furthermore, the intervals are most often not symmetrical. Finally, with
the approach used, it is theoretically possible that the point forecast is outside the interval if,
for example, all previous errors have the same sign. However, this is extremely unlikely. In
that case, there is clearly something wrong with the point forecast. This option is possible
because empirical errors are only used to construct prediction intervals and not the point
forecast. Overall, it is decided that the advantages outweigh the primarily minor disadvan-
tages.
To illustrate how prediction intervals are constructed using empirical errors and the differ-
ence compared to theoretical calculation, intervals are constructed for some selected methods.
Only 95% prediction intervals are constructed. The starting point is the information from
December 2017 and earlier. Intervals are constructed for all horizons of 1, 3, 6, 9, 12, and

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
This chapter discusses topics relevant to the thesis in one way or another. The chapter builds
on all previous chapters and will evaluate the thesis critically. It is done, among other things,
by interpreting the results, reflecting on the methods used and decisions made, and putting
the thesis into context.
7.1
Availability of Data
The first chapter mentions the difference between the concepts of forecasting and predicting.
The terms are used interchangeably both in this thesis and elsewhere. When it comes to
forecasts, however, there is a time dimension that forms the basis for the work of predicting
future values. It is crucial when a forecast is made. It is also crucial for what point in time
the forecast is made. Common to all the methods used is that they create forecasts using
historical data. Therefore, the exact time the data used is available is very important. The
data used can be divided into inflation itself and predictor variables.
Through Chapters 5 and 6, there is no distinct focus on exactly when a forecast can be
created. The reason for that was to keep the focus on the application of methods. It is
said that a forecast is created with data from a particular month and earlier. In practice,
however, not all data from January are available by the end of 31 January. Therefore, it is
not practically possible to make a 1-month forecast for inflation in February at the end of
January.
The consumer price index in Denmark is published on the 10th of each month.
The in-
dex is published the next working day if the 10th is a weekend or public holiday. It means
that 10 days into each month, it is possible to make a 1-month forecast for the current month
if one of the univariate methods is used. If a forecast is desired earlier, it is always possible
to make a forecast with a longer time horizon. However, it is common knowledge that the
longer the horizon, the larger the uncertainty. It is shown through Chapters 5 and 6, largely
without exception.
The data availability becomes even more complicated when it comes to the predictor vari-
ables. Some of the variables described in Section 4.2 are available immediately at the end of
the month. These are the data provided by FRED and Danmarks Nationalbank’s Statbank.
Other data are available at about the same time as the consumer price index. Finally, some
data are calculated by Statistics Denmark and published quarterly. It means that the timing
of when a value for a given month is available varies. A practical solution to this problem
is to use the last available value and then apply a RW to forecast values that have not been
calculated or published yet.
A relevant aspect worth considering is who uses what data for forecasting. Statistics Denmark
has often calculated the indices days or weeks before publication. Therefore, the situation

regarding data availability will, of course, be different for Statistics Denmark since they will
have access to some data before others.
7.2
Influence of Decisions
Several decisions must be made during the preparation of forecasts and the selection of
forecasting methods. All other things being equal, these decisions will affect which models
are selected and, ultimately, the results. It can not be different, but it is vital to keep in mind.
An important decision is the one that relates to the split of data into training, validation,
and test periods. The start of the training period is not the same for all ways of forecasting
inflation. To use as much data as possible, it has been decided to set the beginning of the
training period to the point when all used variables are available; however, in such a way
that no data from 1990 or earlier have been used.
No investigation of different training
periods has been performed. It is thus not known with certainty whether this is the optimal
way to do it. For many models, a relatively large number of parameters must be determined.
Therefore, as long a time series as possible is needed.
The time series cross-validation used to estimate the performance of a model specification
includes the period from January 2013 to December 2017. This period was initially two years
shorter and thus ended in December 2015, but has been extended to make this performance
estimate more reliable. The validation period thus includes both periods when inflation is
unchanged and periods when inflation is declining and rising slightly. However, inflation in
the validation period is generally stable compared with, for example, the 1980s.
The test period starts in January 2018 and ends in April 2022. Although inflation at the
beginning of the period is stable, the period is characterized by very high inflation in 2021
and the beginning of 2022. Compared to the level of inflation during the training period and
the validation period, this is a problem. There is thus a low degree of similarity between
the periods, which is problematic. By looking at Figure 4.1, it is seen that it is not readily
possible to choose training, validation, and test set, so this is not the case. Put another way,
it is diﬀicult to predict a large increase in inflation in 2021 and 2022 when the models are
not trained to predict such. It is also diﬀicult to estimate the uncertainty when inflation
during the validation period behaves differently. By choosing different training, validation,
and test periods, the results would probably be different. However, this would not change
the challenge of a large difference in inflation across the periods. This challenge is part of
predicting something at a specific time. It is not possible to generate new data. When the
scope is limited to predicting inflation in Denmark, there will only be a time series of limited
length to predict.
Another decision that significantly influences the results is the one about the evaluation
of both predictions and intervals being based on a different number of observations depend-
ing on the horizon in question. Initially, the generation of new predictions was stopped 18
months before, for example, the end of the test period. In that case, the number of errors
on which the two measures of error are based, and the number of intervals were the same
for all horizons. It has been changed so as not to miss potentially valuable information. In
general, it can be mentioned that the division into training, validation, and test periods
has been based on when the forecasts are made. Theoretically, it would also be possible
to work with sliding periods. In that case, the test period for the 18-month forecast would
start 17 months before the test period for the 1-month forecast. This would ensure that the
==PAGE:109==

number of observations is the same for all intervals. However, it would diminish the intuitive
understanding of the split in periods and lead to a major change in the test setup.
7.3
Iterative Approach to Forecasting
In Chapter 3, where the methods are presented, an important aspect is only superficially
discussed. It is the fact that the forecasts are primarily generated iteratively. It is to be un-
derstood in the sense that the models are trained on the data available at a given time. Then
a 1-month forecast is made with the estimated model. Subsequently, the 2-months forecast
is generated using the 1-month forecast as the most recent observation. The procedure is
repeated for the 3-months forecast and so on. This iterative procedure is used in forecasting
with exponential smoothing models, univariate ARIMA models, univariate neural networks,
and VARIMA models. For forecasting with ARIMAX models and multivariate neural net-
works, the iterative approach is also used for the response variable.
The iterative approach is not applied directly for forecasting with the multiple regression
models. Here, the 1-month forecast for inflation is not used for the subsequent forecasts.
Nevertheless, there is an iterative element for the multiple regression models since the future
values of the predictors are generated iteratively by an AR(1) model.
This way of generating forecasts assumes that the 1-month forecasts are of good quality. In
practice, a forecast will always be subject to uncertainty. One can imagine that an uncertain
1-month forecast makes the 2- and 3-months forecasts even more uncertain. The uncertainty
accumulates the more iterations are used to generate the forecast. The 18-month forecast,
for example, is preceded by 17 iterations, where one forecast value is used to forecast the
next. The performance of all models examined in this thesis shows that the greater the hori-
zon, the greater the error. This is true in virtually all cases. It is unknown how much of the
difference in performance for different horizons is due to uncertainty accumulated iteratively.
It can be discussed whether it is an alternative option to train the models differently so
that they are designed to forecast inflation a certain number of months into the future. It
is possible to work either with one frequency or several frequencies corresponding to the
desired horizons. In this way, many iterations are avoided, where potential uncertainty can
accumulate. No investigation has been made of the possible gain by training models using
other than monthly data, where the models are subsequently used iteratively.
There are, however, not only benefits associated with avoiding the iterative way of gen-
erating forecasts.
First, the number of models would potentially multiply.
For example,
parameters would have to be estimated for each of the six models. Model specifications
would also have to be chosen. In addition, the number of observations to determine the pa-
rameters of the models would be much smaller if, for example, only quarterly data were used.
It also intuitively does not make sense not to use available information. Finally, a model for
forecasting each number of time units forward in time would complicate the understanding
and interpretation of the models. Even if the models’ parameters are updated each time a
new observation becomes available and therefore are not constant, it will still not be possible
with an overall model to interpret the parameters in some cases. Finally, situations may
also arise where the X and Y month forecasts are not consistent with each other based on an
intuitive understanding. Without an empirical basis, it is concluded that the most optimal
is to use one model for forecasting inflation regardless of the horizon.

7.4
Evaluation of Results
The problem statement formulated in Section 1.3 asks whether it is possible to forecast in-
flation in Denmark with the help of mathematical models. It also asks what methods can be
used and how effective they are in terms of forecasting future inflation. The first sub-question
has already been answered in that different forecasting methods have been presented and
applied. The second sub-question is more complicated to answer, but the best bid for an
answer is obtained by evaluating the results presented in this thesis.
As mentioned in Section 6.1, the ranking of the models depends on which horizon and which
measure of error is most emphasized. If the emphasis is on RMSE performance, there are no
better models than the AR(1) benchmark for both the 9 and 18 months horizons. According
to Table 6.1, more models are better than the benchmark for the shortest horizons. During
the test period, ETS, ARIMAX, and the combination of forecasts are the best of these. If,
on the other hand, most emphasis is on MAE performance, the ARIMA model without ex-
ogenous input stands out as the best. RMSE is generally considered more important than
MAE to penalize large errors. Thus, an initial conclusion is that it has not been possible to
construct models that perform better than a simple benchmark for long forecast horizons.
On the other hand, it is possible to construct models that perform better than both bench-
marks used for comparison for the short horizons.
It is indicated above and in Section 6.1, the results are not entirely unambiguous. There is
not a significant difference between the performance of the models and the performance of
the two benchmarks. There is thus no guarantee that these figures for performance will be
repeated in a future period. Instead, there is a guarantee that they will change because infla-
tion and its development differ from period to period. It is also seen from the considerable
difference in performance during the test period compared to the cross-validation. In this
context, it is also noted that the RW benchmark performs exceptionally well in the validation
period. It is not surprising because inflation during the validation period has been relatively
stable despite small fluctuations.
One of the results that are, after all, relatively clear is that the multiple linear models
do not perform as well as several of the other models. The same is true for the model that
forecasts inflation using partial least squares. In both cases, this applies primarily to the
shortest horizons. In both cases, this is seen in the test period and in the time series cross-
validation. It is thus a clear indication that forecasts made without using previous inflation
values for anything other than estimating parameters are of inferior quality. Apart from
this, none of the models are particularly good or particularly bad. It applies both to the
constructed model and the benchmarks. It is not surprising since some of the models use
the same data. Past values of inflation itself, as well as some descriptive variables. Some of
the models are even closely related.
The forecasts, prediction intervals, and inflation are visualized for selected models to show
how different models forecast over time and how often they forecast correctly. Here, the
above observation is confirmed. Namely, that the forecasts are not decisively different and
that none of them manage to predict the large increase in inflation in 2021 and 2022. This
sharp rise in inflation is central to why the errors are of such a significant magnitude. Per-
haps it should be mentioned that there has not been a focus in advance on predicting a
sharp rise in inflation. There has only been a focus on predicting future inflation. It would
not be credible to design a model that would perform well in a predetermined period. An
alternative starting point could be to have the desire to predict an increase in inflation. How-

ever, based on a mathematical approach in a Danish context, it would be diﬀicult to find
data that could then be used to examine warnings that indicate a future increase in inflation.
The inability to forecast the rise in inflation in 2021 and 2022 also greatly affects the relia-
bility of the probabilistic forecasts constructed using empirical distributions of errors. The
reliability diagrams in Figure 6.4 reveal that the empirical error distributions do not contain
enough large positive errors. It is to be expected that the low reliability is primarily due to

the probabilistic forecast to suggest that it would lead to higher reliability to assume that
the errors are normally distributed. An insuﬀicient proportion of observations within the
prediction intervals, as seen here, should always give cause for concern. In a situation with
volatile price developments, there is, therefore, a need for a probabilistic forecast that takes
this into account. An obvious option is to give more weight to newly observed errors. In this
way, several-year-old errors are prevented from affecting the current prediction intervals, as
is the case with the empirical prediction intervals in this thesis.
When evaluating the results listed in Table 6.1 in the most compressed way, it is also worth
paying attention to what is forecasted and what these forecasts are compared to. A forecast
is made for inflation. It is then compared with the actual inflation calculated based on the
consumer price index calculated to the fifth decimal place by Statistics Denmark. However,
the index and the YoY increase are published with only a single decimal place.
This is
because the index itself is merely an estimate of the overall price level. In that light, a differ-

conclude that the results do not indicate any significant difference between the benchmarks
and the models that are not BSR or PLS.
At the end of Chapter 5, it is examined whether it is advantageous to combine different
forecasts. Here, the combination is better than the best forecast for some horizons. During
the test period, this is also the case for the short horizons when it comes to MAE. However,
there are no clear indications in the results that combining the forecasts, in general, should
be particularly advantageous. It has also been examined whether it is more advantageous
or easier to forecast core inflation compared to overall inflation. The conclusion is the same
as the one Bermingham draws in his dissertation [Ber11]. Based on the cross-validation,
there are no indications that future core inflation in Denmark should be particularly easy
to predict. Finally, prediction intervals have also been constructed using empirical errors.
The proportion of correct predictions is too low during the test period. However, this must
first and foremost be attributed to the period’s exceptional development in inflation. Apart
from this, there are no indications that it is not valid to construct prediction intervals with
empirical errors.
7.5
Hypothetico-Deductive Method
The project work that forms the basis of the thesis is, of course, an engineering project and
thus a scientific project. The use has primarily been made of engineering methods within
forecasting, statistics, and mathematics in general. These are combined with introductory
domain knowledge about inflation, price indices, monetary policy, and economics in general.
At the beginning of the project period, a project definition report has been prepared, which
has formed the basis for the project work. The intentional approach presented in the project
definition report has roughly been implemented. However, there have been some adjust-
ments. For example, the list of forecasting methods has been changed to include exponential
==PAGE:112==
7.5 Hypothetico-Deductive Method
97
smoothing. This is done to investigate exponential smoothing in conjunction with the other
methods.
Overall, it can be said that the scientific method has been used in the project work that
forms the basis of the thesis. A widely used interpretation of the scientific method is the
hypothetical-deductive method, which is also a focal point in this thesis. The method, which
consists of different phases, exists in various variations. One variation is shown in Figure
7.1. In a way, the phases reflect the project work. On the other hand, it can not be sharply
divided. In practice, several things are done simultaneously and in a way that does not fully
obey the phases.
Figure 7.1: Hypothetico-deductive method [Hel21]
The first phase, omitted in some versions, is the observation phase. In this thesis, the phase
is slightly different from the ordinary observation phase, where observations are made in
nature in a broad sense.
In this thesis, the observation phase includes the observations
of price indices and their development over time. Sometimes merged with the observation
phase is the next phase, which is about defining a question or problem. In the present case,
the question is whether it is possible to forecast inflation in Denmark. It corresponds to a
problem that would be highly beneficial to society if it had a solution. It is typically also
in this phase that narrowing begins regarding what to work with and, not least, what not
to work with. In this thesis, no work is done to forecast inflation in countries other than
Denmark. In addition, no work is done to forecast elementary aggregate indices.
The next phase is the research phase, where information is collected. It aims to become
wiser on the things that can help answer questions or solve the problem. For this thesis, this
phase has been about acquiring new knowledge within the relevant subject areas. The phase
==PAGE:113==
7.6 Comparison and Contribution to the Literature on Inflation Forecasting
98
has also been about exploring which different solutions could be used and which solutions
others have used to solve a similar problem.
The next stage is the one in which a hypothesis is formulated. In this phase, the information
phase is used to formulate a solution that presumably solves the previously formulated prob-
lem. For example, in other cases, a hypothesis would be a presumed mathematical context
or a specific causal explanation. In the present case, however, the hypothesis is not specific.
Here, the hypothesis may be that a particular way of forecasting inflation is better than a
simple benchmark. The hypothesis is vague because inflation is affected by many different
things. For example, business and corporate choices cannot be put into a formula. In the
experimental phase that succeeds the hypothesis phase, a way of testing the hypothesis is
being designed. In the present case, this is the test of a specific model specification from
2018 to 2022.
After tests or experiments have been performed, the results are analyzed.
An example
of just this in this thesis is Chapter 6. Here, results for different models are evaluated. Vi-
sualizations are also made to help discover the advantages and disadvantages of the tested
models. Based on the results, it is possible to confirm or disconfirm the previously formu-
lated hypothesis. It is noted that it is often not possible to prove the hypothesis definitively.
It is also impossible here. In the present case, the performance of a given model is compared
with that of the benchmark models.
If the hypothesis is confirmed, it is possible to conduct several experiments that further
support the hypothesis. If the hypothesis is not confirmed, the assumptions underlying the
hypothesis must be re-evaluated. In other words, the hypothesis should be reformulated.
This hypothesis adjustment often takes place several times as an iterative process. In a way,
this is also the case in this thesis. Many different methods for forecasting inflation have been
experimented with. On the other hand, still not quite. In practice, many models are tested
against each other and thus not generated in an iterative process.
The last of the phases is the documentation phase. Regardless of conclusions, it is essential
to report the results obtained and thus share the findings of the work performed with others.
This is, of course, done with this thesis. Although not all parts of the work process can be
placed entirely in the phases mentioned, the scientific method is good for understanding how
the work is performed.
7.6
Comparison and Contribution to the Literature on
Inflation Forecasting
As described in Section 2.3, this thesis is far from the only study that deals with inflation
and its forecast. One of the things that makes this thesis special is that it deals with inflation
in Denmark. In addition, other theses, dissertations, and articles are, of course, based on
older data. This is far from irrelevant, as inflation behaves differently from period to period.
Thus, all the references examined here do not have results that incorporate the sharp rise in
inflation in 2021 and 2022.
One of the similarities with other relevant literature in the field is the methods used to
forecast inflation. A large part of the methods used here is thus also part of the toolbox
from which other relevant literature has chosen methods. When it comes to results and
==PAGE:114==
7.7 Future Work
99
conclusions, the situation is somewhat ambiguous. Stock and Watson, who review a wide
range of methods, arrive at a conclusion roughly in line with the results of this thesis [SW08].
The simple univariate benchmark is not outperformed consistently. Similarly, the outcome
of this thesis is consistent with that of Faust and Wright, who conclude that procedures with
few or no parameters appear to work shockingly well [FW13].
It also means that the results of this thesis differ from the theses indicating that it is possible
to achieve a significant reduction in the errors associated with forecasts of inflation. It ap-
plies, among others, to Lindholm et al., who forecast inflation in Sweden using BVAR models
[LMS18]. It also applies to Aanes & Gullien, who use deep neural networks to forecast in-
flation in Norway [AG18]. Finally, this also applies to Kop, who concludes that some recent
models can significantly improve inflation forecasts [Kop18]. Compared to these results, the
results of this thesis are therefore not encouraging. It is noted that the methods, models,
and predictor variables in this thesis are not the same as in the three mentioned studies.
Therefore, it can not be ruled out that an adjustment of approach in this thesis would lead
to lower errors than the selected benchmarks. However, there is nothing in the results of
this thesis to suggest that the conclusion should be any different from the reviews of Stock
and Watson as well as Faust and Wright, both of which point to low predictability of inflation.
Overall, it can be said that this thesis contributes knowledge about the performance of
the models used in the period in question. The results also emphasize that the univariate
benchmarks are diﬀicult to outperform. In addition, the results in this thesis also emphasize
that the development of inflation and thus the optimal way of working with forecasts is very
different from period to period.
7.7
Future Work
If the scope of the project work had been more than 30 ECTS credits and there had thus
been more time, it is clear that some things would be obvious to investigate further. One of
them would be to focus on a refinement of the models. It applies both in the identification
of and selection of models. A characteristic of this thesis is perhaps that too many methods
have been studied. It is very much a horse race between models, which also has its advan-
tages. However, another and perhaps better approach would be to put more effort into the
data analysis to get even wiser before selecting models. In that case, other and non-classical
methods of forecasting might be obvious choices. Another apparent refinement of models
would be to dive deeper into what approach other references that report results better than
benchmarks have used. For example, BVAR models that Lindholm et al. considers extremely
useful have only been investigated superficially [LMS18]. An in-depth study would reveal
whether it is possible to replicate the results in a Danish context. The same is true with the
deep neural networks that Aanes and Gullien conclude are helpful in specific periods [AG18].
Instead of just building and testing feedforward neural networks, which are among the most
simple neural networks, it would be relevant to dive deeper into, for example, the types that
Aanes and Gullien suggest. It would be most obvious and correct to examine in advance
which neural network is the optimal one instead of simply choosing the simplest, which is
done in this thesis.
The amount of data is relevant for many models, especially for neural networks.
It has
been characteristic of the modeling in this thesis that the amount of data may have been too
small to apply some of the methods in a meaningful way. The time series to be forecasted
is only a few hundred observations long. Many of the predictor variables used have even
==PAGE:115==

100
fewer observations. Therefore, it will no doubt be necessary to look at being able to use
more observations to train and test models. It is not easy in a Danish context. One option
would be to look more closely at foreign data. It applies to both price indices and possible
predictors.
In the literature and this thesis, it is seen that the predictability of inflation and the re-
sults, in general, depends extremely much on the period in question. This thesis, therefore,
has probably not studied the significance of this enough and how it can be used to improve
forecasts. In Section 6.2, it is seen that the forecast error is exceptionally high in 2021 and
the beginning of 2022. In the period up to and including 2020, virtually all forecasts work
as they should. It is possible to look at the period before and after this shift as two different
regimes, where forecasts should be made differently. Therefore, a Markov chain that takes
these transitions between states or regimes into account would be an obvious approach to ex-
amine. Concerning the definition of regimes, domain knowledge in economics is paramount.
Although it is impossible to predict a major change in inflation a long time in advance, it is
crucial to take into account as soon as possible that the situation has changed. This is now
done only to a certain extent for the models worked on in this thesis.
7.8
Applications of Inflation Forecasts
Prior to work related to this thesis, a number of research goals have been formulated, the
purpose of which is to be operational and guiding. A goal that has only been addressed to
a limited extent is considering who can benefit from inflation forecasts and how these are
made accessible. In Section 1.2, authorities, businesses, and private consumers are identified
as stakeholders who may benefit from good quality inflation forecasts.
The inflation forecasts presented in this thesis are all made with the statistical program-

forecasts in a statistics program yourself. One way to meet this low level of accessibility
could be to present the forecasts with a data visualization tool.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this thesis, a number of different forecasting methods have been compared and used to
forecast inflation in Denmark. The forecasting methods that have been used can be divided
into four groups: multiple regression models, exponential smoothing models, ARIMA mod-
els, and neural networks. The development in the total Danish consumer price index from
1991 to 2022 and values of predictor variables from all or parts of the same period have been
used to train, validate, and test the selected models.
Results show that it is diﬀicult to outperform the two univariate benchmark models, random
walk and AR(1). However, there are indications that it is possible to perform as well as or
even outperform the univariate benchmarks when making forecasts 1, 3, or 6 months ahead.
It can be done by adding an exogenous input to a seasonal ARIMA model. It can also be
done by combining, for example, an exponential smoothing model, a vector ARIMA model,
and a multivariate neural network.
In general, the results also show that the performance of the model types depends very
much on the period in question.
This is also why the results are strongly influenced by
the fact that inflation in Denmark in 2021 and the beginning of 2022 has been abnormally
high. A notable observation in this context is that the random walk benchmark performs
exceptionally well compared to the AR(1) benchmark in the validation period from 2013 to
2017. The reverse is the case in the test period from 2018 to 2022.
Overall, it can be concluded that the predictability of inflation is relatively low. It is also
concluded that the longer the horizon, the less predictable inflation will be. However, the
usefulness of good inflation forecasts means that model-based forecasting of inflation in Den-
mark can be a helpful supplement to other methods that have the same purpose.

APPENDIX F
R Code
This thesis builds beyond a theoretical foundation largely on findings made through practical
investigations and tests. These have been made with the programming language R. The R
code is structured as ten files. The files are listed below this description. All files are created
so they can stand alone and thus do not need any other R files. Some of the files load external
data from XLSX files, which are therefore also necessary to run the code. Likewise, all files
use an RDA file containing previous values of relevant indices. At the beginning of the R
files, the required packages are loaded. It may be necessary to install certain R packages to
run the code. The R files have been submitted as part of the thesis. With the R files, it is
possible to reproduce all results and figures in this thesis.
