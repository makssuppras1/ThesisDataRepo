Abstract
Per­ and Poly­ fluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) concentrations in environmental matrices
has been growing due to the large group of substances and industrial uses, leading to
the direct or indirect release into the environment. These concentrations then build up in
plants and animals, termed as bioconcentration or bioaccumulation within a body. Due
to the challenge of carrying out huge sampling campaigns to cover a diverse amount of
species, and hence difficulty in determining the risk of bioaccumulation, efforts are re­
quired to set up empirical or mechanistic models for predicting bioaccumulation potential.
This thesis analysed the relationship of a variety of physicochemical properties of PFAS,
and its relationship with bioconcentration and bioaccumulation factors. The database of
bioconcentration factors (BCF) are laboratory based values and bioaccumulation factors
(BAF) are from field measurements. Physical properties include molecular weight, mo­
lar volume, vapor pressure, surface tension (γ), parachor (Pr), total polar surface area
(TPSA), polarizability, number of hydrogen bond acceptors, number of rotatable bonds,
melting and boiling points. Chemical properties include solubility (Sw), air­water parti­
tioning constant or Henry’s law constant (Kaw), acid dissociation constant (pKa), organic
carbon­water partition coefficient (Koc), octanol­water partition coefficient (Kow or P), ap­
parent octanol­water partition coefficient (D), binding coefficient to human serum albumin
protein KHSA. Quantitative structure–activity relationship (QSAR) model, ACD/Percepta
was used to predict the parameters. In addition, a mix of experimental and modelled
data collated by Interstate Technology and Regulatory Council (ITRC) was also included.
Relationships are established against the standardised logarithmic values.
Firstly, distributions of each property are analysed.
Then, correlation matrices within
physicochemical properties and to the database of bioconcentration and bioaccumula­
tion factors. Subsequently, linear regression of multiple subsets are carried out and dis­
cussed. Finally, predictions are attempted on test sets and PFAS with less available data.
There is a large variance in the data and the strength of the linear relationship is improved
by identifying the individual subsets. Stratification of data include PFAS groups, environ­
ment where the species is found such as marine, brackish or freshwater, trophic levels of
species, and tissue of the organism tested. As a standalone descriptor, it is generally the
molecular weight or molar volume that has the strongest linear correlations to the biocon­
centration or bioaccumulation factors. Depending on the group, logD is still a moderate to
strong descriptor for bioconcentration or bioaccumulation, and support the phospholipid
mechanism even though PFAS is not considered lipophilic.
The relationships identified from this thesis is the first step to identifying relevant param­
eters for further work in developing a more robust model for predicting bioaccumulation.

Per­ and Poly­ Fluorinated Alkyl Substances (PFASs), hereon referred to as PFAS or
PFASs, are a large group of thousands of substances gaining attention more than ever
for its toxicity and persistence in the environment [1]. Since its discovery, PFAS have
been in the industry for more than 50 years, and some of which are restricted in Den­
mark and international agreements [2]. PFAS has been used due to their highly stable
and surfactant properties, making them ideal materials as water and grease repellents, in
textiles, food contact materials, firefighting and many more industries [3]. A chemical with
a fluoro­carbon segment is classfied as PFAS. Per­fluorinated substances have a fully
fluorinated carbon chain, also referred to as ”terminal PFAS”, while poly­fluorinated sub­
stances are partially fluorinated, where the non­fluorinated part can degrade to terminal
PFAS and other by­products.
Human exposure to PFAS are recognised to be widespread and economic pressures re­
lated to health and environment would increase if PFAS is left without remediation [4]. For
majority of the PFAS, critical gaps in understanding the risks exist, specifically a need to
develop more comprehensive monitoring programmes, predictive models for bioaccumu­
lation amongst others [5].
The Stockholm Convention [6] had listed well­known long­chain PFAS Perfluorooctanoic
acid (PFOA) in Annex A for elimination and Perfluorooctane sulfonic acid (PFOS) in Annex
B for restriction, and most recently Perfluorohexane sulfonic acid (PFHxS) to Annex A.
Priority organic pollutants (POPs) in the convention are evaluated against persistence (P),
bioaccumulation (B), and toxicity (T), and must present evidence of long­range transport
e.g. detected in the Artic, demonstrating the need to scrutinise the behaviour of similar
chemicals in the environment.
1.1
Environmental Implications of PFAS
PFAS is found in all environmental media, across water, soil and air [7, 8]. As a conse­
quence, it enters the food chain and has also been detected in human blood and milk
[9, 10]. Persistent, often lipophilic (fat­loving) chemicals accumulate in the food chain,
some example routes being from algae to Daphnia magna (small planktonic crustacean)
to small fish to predatory fish and to predatory bird, another example would be vegeta­
tion to cow to milk to mother (and breastmilk) to baby [11]. Exposures to direct sources
may also exist, such as a fire­fighting site contaminated with perfluoroctane sulfonic acid
(PFOS) in Korsør, Denmark [12].
Although increasing amounts of bans, restrictions has brought down the levels present in
humans over the years, the vast amount of fully florinated compounds and its precursors
released continue to pose risks to our health and ecological systems. Furthermore, PFAS
has shown bioaccumulative properties through a variety of laboratory and field measure­
ments [13].
There still remains a gap in knowledge about their bioaccumulation and biomagnification
potential in marine ecosystems, including their indirect impact on seafood, highlighting the
necessity for additional research in this field [14, 15]. The Zurich statement [16] also iden­
tified the need for understanding the behaviour of PFAS in various environmental media,
especially its prolonged effects. Therefore, this study would investigate the bioaccumula­
tion, B, potential of PFAS with reference to existing models.

1.2
Challenge of determining bioaccumulation of PFAS
Bioaccumulation could be used as a generic term to describe the accumulation of a chem­
ical substance inside a living body and across food chains. It encompasses bioconcentra­
tion factors (BCF) often determined in laboratory settings, environmental bioaccumulation
factors (BAF) and biomagnification factors (BMF). In scientific literature, and herein this
study, BCF refers to measurements in the laboratory following standardised protocol, and
the term BAF would be referring to field measurements. BMF is the factor for accumu­
lation across trophic levels (or food chains) but is excluded from the scope of this study.
BAF includes every exposure route (i.e., dietary, water, and contact with sediments via
skin and ingestion), and could be based on residues in tissues (muscle, liver etc.) or the
whole organism [17]. The mechanics of bioaccumulation would be discussed further in
Chapter 2.
PFAS are amphiphilic, has both hydrophobic ”tail” and hydrophilic ”head” as shown in the
figure Figure 1.1. These substances display characteristics of adhering to interfaces more
often than a specific media (i.e. water or oil).
Figure 1.1: General structure of non­polymeric, perfluorinated substances from Panieri et
al. (2022) [18]
Information on bioaccumulation potential are typically derived from octanol–water partition
coefﬁcient (KOW), bioconcentration factor (BCF), bioaccumulation factor (BAF), and biota–
sediment accumulation factor (BSAF) parameters that could be obtained from empirical
measurements or models ranging from simple equilibrium partitioning to detailed kinetic
models of an organism [19].
PFAS are not lipophilic, thus may not accumulate in fatty tissues with the same mecha­
nism as most neutral organic pollutants. Without a mechanistic model, empirical measure­
ments of BAFs/BCFs, biomagniﬁcation factors in ﬁsh or elimination rates in mammalian
species [20, 21] have to be used, which are tedious and often insufficiently extensive to
cover inter­species variability in bioaccumulation potential [22]. Given its amphiphilic na­
ture, different PFAS chain lengths and types of polar head groups would potentially affect
the rate of bioaccumulation. It is important to understand the underlying mechanisms of
ionisable and fluorinated substances and develop bioaccumulation models [20].

1.3
Study Objectives
This study would aim to analyse and establish a statistical relationship between physico­
chemical properties with bioaccumulation via measurements taken in air, water, sedimen­
t/soil, and biota from various literature, modelled and empirical studies.
• Compile a comprehensive database of physico­chemical properties, and biocon­
centration and biomagnification factors of PFAS. Include uptake rates, elimination
or detoxification rates.
• Compile a database of PFAS measurements in water, sediment/soil, air, surrounding
different environmental media, contaminated sites.
• Define the mechanisms of bioaccumulation and how bioconcentration factors (BCF)
and bioaccumulation factors (BAF) are established.
• Characterise statistical relationships by means of transformation, correlation etc.
between the different properties across databases, with the bioconcentration (BCF)
and bioaccumulation factors (BAF), and select predictors that are relevant to ob­
served or simulated concentrations.
• With the characterisation, attempt to estimate the bioconcentration (BCF) and bioac­
cumulation (BAF) factors of other groups of PFAS not often captured in bioaccu­
mulation studies e.g. fluorotelomer alcohol (FTOH), perfluoroalkane sulfonamide
(FASA), and therefore prediction of PFAS bioaccumulation in other study areas and
other types of PFAS.

Properties from the dataset that would be parameterised are listed below:
• Bioconcentration factors (BCF): BCF is the ratio of the concentration of a sub­
stance in an organism to its concentration in the surrounding environment (usually
water). In Burkhard’s database, it refers to measurements performed in the labora­
tory with a water only exposure route, or only source of chemical to the organism is
dissolved in the aqueous phase (i.e., no sediment and/or algal food present in the
test).
• Bioaccumulation Factors (BAF): BAF is similar to BCF but includes all exposure
routes, such as food. Both are used to assess the potential for substances to ac­
cumulate in living organisms. In Burkhard’s database, it refers to measurements
performed in the field and BAF subsequently calculated.

• Metabolic rates (kU): The rate at which an organism converts substances into

• Elimination rates (kE): The rate at which an organism neutralizes or eliminates
toxic substances. This can involve various biochemical processes, such as metabolism,

2.3
Data on Physical and Chemical Properties
The Interstate Technology and Regulatory Council (ITRC) collated literature on density,
melting point, boiling point, solubility, vapor pressure (VP), Henry’s Constant (KH), Crit­
ical Micelle Concentration (CMC) Log Koc and pKa, a mix of modelled and measured
(field or laboratory) values. Data was summarised into Section 4 Physical and Chemi­
cal Properties Table 4­1 in the ITRC online document [23]. Prediction from models were
based on established Quantitative structure–activity relationship (QSAR) models, which
are related to the chemical structure of these PFAS. In this study, Advanced Chemistry
Development, Inc. (ACD/Labs) Percepta­PhysChem Suite [24], public chemical database
PubChem from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) in United States
[25] were used as sources of information. Physico­chemical properties of PFAS were col­
lected from these three sources for a mixture of measured and modelled quantities.
From ACD/Labs, the following parameters are estimated: vapor pressure at 20°C, molec­
ular weight, molar volume, number of rotatable bonds, number of hydrogen bond accep­
tors, parachor, surface tension, polarizability, molar volume, octanol­water partition coef­
ficient (LogP), apparent octanol­water partition coefficient (LogD) at pH=7, pKa, solubility
(Sw), logKa(HSA). From PubChem, structures as well as predicted octanol­water partition
coefficients (LogP) from XLOGP3 [26] in the Chemical and Physical Properties section of
each PFAS were used. Refer to the appendix for the list of PFAS and individual citations.
The list below briefly explains the physical properties that would be part of the analysis:
• Molecular weight (MW): Mass of a molecule, typically measured in atomic mass
units (amu) or grams per mole (g/mol). It is calculated as the sum of the atomic
weights of all atoms in the molecule.
• Molar Volume (MV): Volume occupied by one mole of a substance typically in cubic
centimeters per mole (cm3/mol).
• Melting point (MP): Temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid. This is a spe­
cific physical property of a substance and indicates the point where the molecular
structure breaks down to form a liquid.
• Boiling point (BP): Temperature at which a liquid becomes a gas (vapor). At this
temperature, the vapor pressure of the liquid equals the external pressure surround­
ing the liquid.
• Vapor pressure (VP): The pressure exerted by a vapor in equilibrium with its liquid
or solid phase at a given temperature. It indicates how readily a substance evapo­
rates.
• Surface Tension, γ: Describes the elastic tendency of a liquid’s surface or the
cohensive forces between molecules at the surface. Can be expressed in dyne per
centimeter (dyne/cm) or millinewton per meter (mN/m).

The list below briefly explains the chemical properties that would be part of the analysis:
• Solubility (Sw): The ability of a substance (solute) to dissolve in a solvent to form
a homogeneous solution. It is often expressed in terms of concentration, such as
grams of solute per liter of solvent.
• Henry’s law constant (KH or Kaw): Air­water partitioning constant that relates the
solubility of a gas in a liquid to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid. Ratio
of a substance’s concentration in air to water at equilibrium.
• Octanol­water partition coefficient, Kow or P: The ratio of a substance’s concen­
tration in octanol to its concentration in water at equilibrium (L water / L octanol). It
indicates the substance’s hydrophobicity and is used to predict its distribution in the
environment and within organisms.
• Apparent octanol­water partition coefficient, D: D is similar to P (the octanol­
water partition coefficient), but it takes the pH of the environment into account. While
P measures the distribution of a substance between octanol and water at a specific
pH (usually neutral pH of 7), D considers the ionization state of the substance at
different pH levels.
• Acid dissociation constant (pKa): A measure of the strength of an acid in solu­
tion. It is the negative logarithm of the equilibrium constant for the dissociation of a
weak acid into its conjugate base and a proton (H+). A lower pKa value indicates a
stronger acid.
• Soil­water partitioning coefficient, Kd: The ratio of the concentration of a sub­
stance adsorbed to soil particles to its concentration in water at equilibrium. It helps
predict the mobility and persistence of chemicals in the soil environment.
• Organic carbon­water partition coefficient, Koc: Similar to Kd, but normalized
to the organic carbon content of the soil. It provides a measure of a substance’s
tendency to bind to soil organic matter.
• Binding coefficient to Human Serum Albumin (KHSA): represents the chemical’s
affinity constant to human serum albumin – the major carrier protein in plasma. log

Efforts are geared to create mechanistic models, or formulating equations for the mass
balance, for ionogenic organic chemicals in fish, as the bioaccumulation mechanism does
not occur in the same way as neutral compounds accumulating in the lipids. Armitage

Empirical Relationships and Statistical Tests
Firstly, the distributions, means, standard deviations of all the databases would be pre­
sented and analysed. Histograms and scatter plots of the data would provide insight into
the distribution e.g. skewed, symmetric or biomodal, while a Kernel Density Estimate
(KDE) is a non­parametric way to estimate the probability density function (PDF).
For BCF/BAF, the bioaccumulative properties of each PFAS and PFAS group would also
be briefly discussed. The Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chem­
icals (REACH) of European Union (EU) sets a criteria of BCF > 2000 (logBCF > 3.30) as
bioaccumulative (B) and BCF > 5000 logBCF > 3.70) as very bioaccumulative (vB).
Then, statistical tests would be carried out to determine the relationships first between
each physicochemical parameter, then between physicochemical parameter and BCF/BAF.
There exist strong correlations between some chemical parameters, for example molec­
ular weight with chain length, and it would be important to identify these prior to matching
it up with BCF/BAF.
Referring to the study objectives, there would be attempts to test the hypothesis of the
phospholipid or protein mechanisms by relating BCF/BAF to logKow, number of perfluori­
nated carbons, and identifying correlations to other physicochemical properties within the
whole dataset as well as subsets into species, tissues and other relevant parameters.

2.6
Processing of Databases
Burkhard bioconcentration factors (BCF) and bioaccumulation factors (BAF) database
contains information from 183 studies for 83 PFAS including isomers. The database re­
quired pre­processing, checking for values below detection limits, and filling in of missing
Chemical Abstracts Service Registry Number (CAS RN) where possible. Data on sums
of isomers and sums of PFAS are not directly relevant in the relationship analysis as it is
not related to one particular physical or chemical property. Labels branched perfluorooc­
tanoic acid (br­PFOA), branched perfluorooctance sulfornic acid (br­PFOS) and branched
perflurohexane sulfonic acid (br­PFHxS) does not have distinct physicochemical proper­
ties as it is assumed to be sums or consolidation of the isomers. 13 of the PFAS had
values below detection limits, or ”n.a.” values, 7 of which are aryl fluoroalkyl ethers from
one study, but this group is excluded due to lack of information obtained on its physico­
chemical properties. 63 PFAS remains, and 57 available for analysis after consolidating
the physicochemical databases.
The Burkhard dataset is first analysed to pick out unique PFAS and its CAS RN. If a CAS
RN is not present, a Distributed Structure­Searchable Toxicity Substance Identification
Number (DSSTox Substance ID) is provided where possible. The substances are then
matched with simplified molecular­input line­entry system (SMILES) collated from various
sources including NORMAN Suspect List Exchange [33, 34], PubChem [25], dataset from
Glüge et al. [35] and EPA Comptox Dashboard [36, 37]. SMILES of each PFAS was the
input into the ACD/Percepta tool.
Note that the CAS RN for PFAS is different for its acid and dissociated form (e.g. per­
fluorooctanoic acid 335­67­1 and perfluorooctanoate 45285­51­6), however it would be
treated with the same SMILES notation as input to ACD/Percepta as the charges and
hydrogen atoms are not reflected. On the other hand, it may make a difference in the
chemical names, CAS RN and other properties retrieved from PubChem.
The ITRC database would be matched with Burkhard’s database mainly with the acronyms
or abbreviations of PFAS chemical names.

3
Results and Analysis
In this section, distributions and statistical analysis of within the datasets (bioaccumu­
lation data from Burkhard, physicochemical data from Interstate Interstate Technology
Regulatory Council (ITRC) and QSAR estimations from ACD/Labs) will be presented and
evaluated, followed by their correlation and relationships with bioaccumulation data.
3.1
Distribution of Data
Distributions are important to understand the spread and tendencies of the data, for exam­
ple if it is normally distributed, and if the mean could be a good representation of the center
of each subset considered. Thus knowing distribution profiles would help to interpret any
underlying patterns, identifying outliers, and may support the conclusions drawn from re­
lationships established with the method. Bioconcentration and bioaccumulation factors
will be first discussed, then the property estimations from ACD/Percepta, PubChem and
finally the ITRC database.
3.1.1
Distribution of bioaccumulation factors from Burkhard’s database
As Burkhard has already done an extensive review of the bioaccumulation factors by
grouping the data into PFAS groups, species, tissues and study quality, the focus here
would be to present a general overview of the dataset and evaluating subsets to utilise in
characterising the relationships with the physicochemical properties later in this section.
The database for bioconcentration (BCF) and bioaccumulation factors (BAF) is found in
the original author’s work [17]. Results across literature had been standardised to logarith­
mic form with units of liter per kilogram wet weight (log L/kg­ww) for both bioconcentration
and bioaccumulation factors, and referred to as logBCF and logBAF respectively for this
project. This section would also analyse the differences for logBCF and logBAF values
for the qualities of studies defined in Chapter 2 (”high”, ”medium” and ”low”).
The mean, median, standard deviations, 2.5 and 97.5 percentiles (95% confidence in­
tervals) of finalised logBCF and logBAF in Burkhard’s database are calculated for each
PFAS. Statistics for the entire database as well as PFAS groups were calculated as well.
These values are summarised in the Appendix (Table A.4). The spread of data for each
PFAS is illustrated by boxplots Figure 3.1, with the median, minimum and maximum indi­
cated.

The variation within each PFAS can be due to a variety of factors. For laboratory based
bioconcentration factors (logBCF), experimental conditions such as the concentration of
exposure, duration of exposure, species and tissue analysed will influence the results.
Similarly, for field derived measurements of bioaccumulation factors (logBAF), sampling
locations, species, tissue would cause the results to be different. The ”Study Quality”
introduced in Burkhard’s paper may help to reduce the variance and eliminate some ex­
treme values, but does not remove the inherent factors that affect the bioconcentration
of PFAS in a body. This project aims to perform linear regression analysis and deter­
mine how much of the variance can be explained just by the chemical itself. It will take
place in the form of correlation coefficient R for the strength of the linear relationship, and
coefficient of determination (R2) for explained variance.
The mean logBCF 1.86 ± 2.50 L/kg­ww and mean logBAF 1.50 ± 2.32 L/kg­ww for all
PFAS compounds were calculated from the entire database, of which it can be seen that
standard deviations of 2.50 and 2.32 are very large. Long chain perfluorophosphinic acids
(PFPiA) have the highest bioconcentration factors. Flurotelomer sulfonic acids (FTSA)
have low and even negative logBCF values, which indicate bioconcentration factor less
than 1 and no accumulation. A number of carboxylic acids (PFCA) tend to have lower
bioaccumulation factors (logBAF) as compared to sulfonic acids (PFSA).
Carboxylic acids (PFCA) and sulfonic acids (PFSA) have the most data, with PFSA with
BCF values 2.09 ± 1.21 L/kg­ww and BAF 2.58 ± 1.20 L/kg­ww and PFCA with BCF
values 2.24 ± 1.58 L/kg­ww and BAF 2.03 ± 1.60 L/kg­ww. According to REACH reg­
ulations, both groups PFCA and PFSA are bordering or already at the bioaccumulative
criteria (logBCF/BAF > 3.30). The long chain perfluorophosphinic acid (PFPiA) group has
the highest mean logBCF at 7.49 ± 1.57 L/kg­ww. It is important to note that the mean
and standard deviations are only meaningful representations if the data is normally dis­
tributed. As a quick analysis, the difference between the mean and median is generally
more pronounced for logBAF than logBCF, indicating that the distribution may be slightly
skewed from normal.

In ITRC’s database, there is a mix of experimental (both laboratory and field), modelled
values and extrapolated values from field or measured values. A larger variance is hence
expected from these datasets. Each individual PFAS may have multiple studies and a
large range of data points. In order to match the physicochemical properties by PFAS
to the BCF and BAF factors, one value to represent one PFAS has to be derived for
subsequent use in regression. ACD/Percepta and PubChem predictions does not have
a range for individual PFAS since its outputs from an algorithm. The distributions shown
here are all datapoints available in ITRC’s database, as well as the means calculated for
each PFAS.
It should also be noted that pH was not collated for most of the studies. For ionisable com­
pounds, distribution between different phases are influenced by the ionisation state, can
be influenced by the ionization state, which is affected by the pH. pKa, LogKoc, solubility,
critical micelle concentrations (CMC) and LogKaw can all be influenced by pH, depending
on the experimental conditions or the algorithm.
Specific to organic carbon­water partition coefficient (Log Koc), the matrix in which it is
assessed (e.g. soils or sediments) affects the data measured as well. Regardless, LogKoc
has a normal distribution when all data is put together (Figure 3.23). The mean of each
PFAS, however, does not reflect the same distribution.
Boiling point, density and vapor pressure are skewed­left distributions from Figures 3.19
to 3.21. Critical micelle concentration, CMC (Figure 3.22) and LogKoc (Figure 3.23) has

two peaks are observed . Similarly, melting point has some higher frequencies at the
extreme ends (Figure 3.26). Compared to ACD/Percepta, the collated pKa values from

also has the higher frequencies observed at the extreme ends as the above­mentioned
properties.

As explained in the earlier section on distributions of data, there are large variations in
bioconcentration (logBCF) and bioaccumulation (logBAF) factors as expected due to the
wide variety of studies, types of PFAS, tissue of the body tested and many more factors.
As there are different ways to classify and divide the dataset, the aim is to determine
if a general case can be applied. Every PFAS would have one set of physicochemical
parameters and multiple logBCF and logBAF datapoints.
The database of bioconcentration factors (logBCF) and bioaccumulation factors (log­
BAF) is subset by firstly the environmental matrix (marine, freshwater and brackish), then
trophic levels, then PFAS groups (e.g. perfluorocarboxylic acids (PFCA), perfluorosulfonic
acids (PFSA)) and the tissues (e.g. blood, liver, whole body). The correlation of each
physicochemical property with the entire database as well as each subset is tabulated. to
The purpose is to illustrate if stratification could change the correlations significantly.
T­tests were carried out by pairs of groups of PFAS as well as tissues. It was found that the
means are statistically most similar between the following pairs: (1) blood and whole body,
(2) brain and embryo, (3) gill and muscle, (4) intestine and remainder parts, (5) liver and
gonad, (6) muscle and whole body without liver, (7) ovary and gonad (Appendix Table A.6).
In the correlation matrices, tissues that are deemed to have statistically similar means are
grouped together. Then, blood, brain, muscle, gonad, liver, whole body individual subsets
were considered as well, because they have the more than 100 datapoints.

in the Appendix. Table B.1 summarises the correlation coefficients of ACD estimated pa­

3.3
Linear Regression
Apart from the correlation coefficient (R), the regression analysis includes determining
constants for slope, intercept, coefficient of determination (R2). Correlation coefficient (R)
describes the strength of the linear relationship, while the coefficient of determination (R2),
can quantify the variance explained by PFAS chemicals. Linear regression is carried out
on the entire dataset for selected parameters with bioconcentration and bioaccumulation
factors, then Cyprinus carpio (Common carp) and Danio rerio (Zebrafish). Only ”high”
quality studies for bioconcentration and bioaccumulation factors are included for linear
regression analysis.
The relationship of number of perfluorinated carbons with Log BCF and Log BAF for the
entire dataset is first analysed. Without narrowing into species specific datasets, we will
investigate the relationship of different subsets with number of perfluorinated carbons,
molar volume and apparent octanol­water coefficient (LogD).
For bioconcentration factors (logBCF) of perfluorocarboxylic acids (PFCA) with number
of perfluorinated carbons, the correlation coefficient, R is 0.652, and coefficient of de­
termination, R2=0.426 (n=228). As for bioaccumulation factors (logBAF), R=0.752 and
R2=0.566 for (n=852). Perfluorosulfonic acids (PFSA) displayed similar strengths with
R=0.638, R2=0.407 for logBCF (n=97) and R=0.665, R2=0.442 for logBAF (n=365). The
correlation coefficients were stronger for logBAF than logBCF with number of perfluori­
nated carbons in the entire dataset. Slopes of regression are similar between PFCA and
PFSA, but differs for Perfluoroalkyl Ether Sulfonic Acids (PFESA), Perfluorophosphonic
acid (PFPA), Perfluorophosphinic acid (PFPiA), Fluorotelomer sulfonic acids (FTSA) for
the available data.
When separated by environments, marine and brackish environments have poorer lin­
ear relationships to number of perfluorinated carbons, while freshwater has moderate
to strong correlations for both logBCF (R=0.723) and logBAF (R=0.571). In freshwater
systems, the number of perfluorinated carbons of a PFAS can explain up to 50% of the
variance in logBCF. The correlation coefficient for marine environment is not significant
for logBCF or logBAF. In brackish environments, the correlation is weak (R=0.455 for
logBCF, R=0.259 for logBAF).
The estimated trophic levels in the species database give an indication of the species
location in a food chain, and it is likely that the size of the body is larger the higher the
trophic level [40]. In this report, the trophic levels were separated into bins to create
subsets of data. Moderate to strong linear relationships were observed for trophic level
subsets with perfluorinated carbons, with R values from 0.545 to 0.770. Trophic level
2.5­3 group of logBAF has a weak correlation 0.296 is the exception. With apparent
octanol­water coefficient (LogD), stronger correlations were observed in trophic levels 1­
2.5 (R=0.843) and trophic levels 3.5­5 (R=0.803).

Discussion
4.1
General considerations
Brusseau (2024) highlighted the critical need for efficient and simple methods of predicting
physical–chemical properties for the thousands of existing PFAS of all structure types
[41]. He summarised a framework which includes (1) Aggregate relevant measured data
sets for the selected property or parameter. (2) Curate the compiled database based on
what the data represents, highlight significant differences, limitations and applicability. (3)
Identify data gaps. (4) Fill in the data gaps with validated predicted values. (5) Develop
PFAS specific models. The present report had followed the framework, from studying
individual datasets, to analysing its gaps and studying individual relationships as much as
possible.
Assessing and quantifying PFAS uptake and accumulation in plants and animals is chal­
lenging due to several factors of uncertainty, and the magnitude of bioconcentration fac­
tors. It complicates the development of predictive tools, and has to be very species or site
specific. In animal studies, it could be further complicated as concentrations are mea­
sured in various tissues, such as muscle, blood, liver, kidney, and other organs, or whole
body samples, and it would be difficult to quantify a specific value that could encompass
all components.
PFAS accumulation is influenced by specific biological constituents such as phospholipid
membranes and proteins. Many studies have shown that PFAS tend to associate primarily
with proteins and cell membranes, with less interaction with lipids compared to typical
hydrophobic contaminants.
When the mean BCF or BAF of each PFAS is calculated, then regression attempted, it
would result in a stronger correlation. The relationships established here could serve as a
good predictor for species specific accumulation and its mean bioconcentration or bioac­
cumulation factors (BCF or BAF), thus mean equilibrium concentrations. The relationship
of each parameter with bioconcentration factors also vary depending on the PFAS group.
It is difficult to resolve the variance of bioconcentration factors (BCF) or bioaccumulation
factors (BAF) as each PFAS only has one value of a particular physico­chemical property.
Nevertheless, the regression attempts to make a best estimate of the mean value of each
PFAS. Separating by tissues, trophic levels or species will give generally better outcomes
than PFAS groups. From the regression plots in the results section, it was noticed that
further stratification will alter the relationships observed.
4.2
Predictions
Prediction using species specific linear regressions will always outperform the regression
from the entire dataset. However, it is a good middle ground to have the whole regression
as a starting point for the bioconcentration or bioaccumulation estimations. Therefore, a
linear equation was derived from the entire dataset and it will be used for comparison in
the subsets.

rotelomer alcohols (FTOH), fluorotelomer sulfonic acids (FTSA) perfluoroalkane sulfon­
amido acetic acids (PFSAA), the linear equations derived from the subsets and the entire
datasets would be applied. In other words, there will be two test sets, one is 20% of the

4.3
Physical Properties
Several studies observed that bioconcentration factors exhibit correlations to PFAS chain
length [42, 43, 29]. Most of the experiments are conducted in controlled laboratory settings
and produce high correlations for perfluorinated carboxylic acids (PFCA) and perfluori­
nated sulfonic acids (PFSA). Many perfluoroalkyl acids (PFAA) were positively correlated
with increasing chain length, and absorption was higher for PFAS with sulfonate groups
compared to carboxylate groups [44].
On the other hand, Yoshiyuki et al. (2012) [43] found that Cyprinus carpio L. (Common
Carp ) exposed to perfluorinated carboxylic compounds (PFCA) displayed a curvilinear
relationship between the BCFs and the number of carbon atoms, with r squared value
of 0.984, with the BCF decreasing after 14 carbon atoms. The equation is shown in
Equation (4.1).
log BCF = −0.104( number of carbons )2 + 2.87( number of carbons ) −15.5
(4.1)
This tallies with the relationship shown in Figure 3.32 for common carp, where the dataset
deviates from the linear line. There is a curvilinear relationship between number of per­
fluorinated carbons with bioconcentration factor (logBCF), with a poor R2 of 0.096. On
the other hand, the relationship is not as apparent for bioaccumulation factor (logBAF),
with a stronger linear relationship indicated by correlation coefficient R of 0.879 and R2 of
0.773.
In general, the strength of the relationship and RMSE of perfluorinated carbons to logBCF
or logBAF for a given subset would be would be similar to molar volume. This means that
the distinction between each parameter is not as essential as finding the right subset to
establish the empirical relationship.
Molar volume was found to be an important description of air­water interfacial adsorption
coefficients (Kaw) for PFAS [45], which in turn influence fluid­fluid interfacial adsorption on
the transport of transport of PFAS. However, Kaw had low correlation strengths (0.205­
0.378) when used for predictions.
4.4
Chemical Properties
Martin et al. (2003) found [42] strong linear relationship between alkyl chain and uptake
rates. This related to the moderately strong correlations observed with bioconcentration
factors. In this dataset, uptake rates also have high correlations with number of perfluori­
nated carbons.
Apparent octanol­water LogD estimated from ACD could be used as a predictor for log­
BCF in Danio rerio, as well as albumin binding constant LogKa(HSA). The strength of
correlation was 0.884 and 0.875 respectively. In Cyprinus carpio (Common carp), the
albumin binding constant LogKa(HSA) was a better predictor than LogD.
Organic carbon­water partition coefficient (LogKoc) has the lowest RMSE values when
used for predictions for the entire dataset, Danio rerio and Cyprinus carpio. It also had
good correlations with bioconcentration and bioaccumulation factors, although the rela­
tionship with bioaccumulation factors are more indirect. Octanol­water partition coeffi­
cient, LogKow or LogP has traditionally been used to estimate LogKoc as there have been
empirical relationships estabilished [46]. In our database, while LogKoc is compiled across

4.5
Uncertainties and Limitations
It is beyond the scope and resources of this report to review all published literature on
the chemical and physical properties of PFAS. References would also be made to other
sources where relevant. Toxikinetics and the mechanistic mass balance models are not
considered and thus the biological omplexities in each plant or animal is simplified.
Unmeasured or unknown precursor PFAS in mixtures may accumulate in organisms and
be biotransformed into persistent PFAS. This transformation can skew the measured bio­
concentration factors (BAFs). Improved understanding of these precursors and their bio­
transformation is essential for more accurate assessments.
Ultimately, the user should pick the most fitting subset and perform a regression suitable
for their own applications.

5
Conclusion
There are a myriad of physicochemical parameters for PFAS. It is important to highlight
that many physicochemical parameters are highly correlated, especially when using a pre­
dictive QSAR tool such as ACD/Percepta. This gives rise to the possibility that prediction
of bioconcentration factors could be simplified into utilising a handful of physicochemical
parameters instead of using many parameters to create a complex model. That being
said, using other statistical tools such as principal component analysis (PCA) may be
useful to group elements and explain some variance in data. Multivariate regressions or
machine learning could be the next steps.
The database is extensive and thus the analysis of relationships has a lot of potential,
and many more permutations can be tested. The empirical relationships established in
this study is able to predict the bioconcentration factor (BCF) and bioaccumulation factor
(BAF) with the correct subsets extracted and related to a QSAR estimation tool. Drawing
back to the study objectives, the following conclusions can be drawn from the project:
1. Database compilation and review: Compilation of a comprehensive database on
physicochemical properties from ACD/Percepta (QSAR tool), ITRC database, Pub­
Chem and combined it with the bioconcentration factors (BCF) and bioaccumulation
factors (BAF) from Burkhard’s database.
2. Mechanisms of bioaccumulation: Mechanisms of phospholipid and protein­binding,
as well as interfacial behaviour of PFAS has been described. These also serve as
explanations for the relationships observed with apparent octanol water coefficient
(LogD) and albumin binding coefficients. LogKa(HSA).
3. Statistical relationships: Distributions of each parameter and correlation matrices
in the physicochemical properties, bioconcentration and bioaccumulation factors
(BCF/BAF) were presented and discussed. Statistical relationships of properties
within and across databases were analysed for its normal distribution, outliers, and
data reliability. Specific predictors were selected ultimately for regression against
bioconcentration factors (BCF) and bioaccumulation factors (BAF).
4. Estimation of bioconcentration and bioaccumulation factors: Linear regres­
sions were examined and its equation used to predict PFAS with limited data and
compared actual to predicted values. The estimates were extended to test sets as
well as PFAS that had less data available, to characterise the potential errors and
suitability of the empirical model for other uses. Ultimately, dividing into subsets
firstly by tissues, then trophic levels (or species specific when possible) will be more
effective.
5. Future prospects: This project serves as the first step to break down which physic­
ochemical properties estimated from a QSAR tool could be useful. The methods
here could be employed as the database for PFAS properties expands. Dataset
can also be used for other predictive models.
