Abstract
Fatigue in welded steel connections of offshore structures domains their service lives. Fatigue
cracks commonly occur at or in the vicinity of the weld toe or weld root. After welding, the heat-
affected zone (HAZ) is formed between the base metal and the weld metal. Weld toe cracking
initiates in the HAZ and propagates into the base metal. In practice, welded connections may
experience multi-axial fatigue loading, while limited research is carried out in that field. The
primary objective of this thesis is to investigate the fatigue life of the HAZ in butt-welded steel
connections. To achieve this, specimens were fabricated using S355J2 grade structural steel.
The specimens were created by welding two 10mm thick plates with a double bevel groove
weld. The flat surface of one of the plates was maintained to ensure a uniform HAZ through
thickness. After welding, the plates were cut into smaller pieces and machined into a round
cross-section with a V-notch in the HAZ. The notch radius was designed to be 0.3mm, with an
opening angle of 60◦. Cutting the plates into smaller pieces released residual stress that had
developed during the welding. A total of 22 v-notched specimens were experimentally tested
using a servo-hydraulic fatigue testing machine. Two bi-axial loading conditions, in-phase 0◦
and out-of-phase 90◦, with the stress ratio R = 0.1 and biaxiality ratio λ = 1.0 are studied,
in addition, the specimens were also tested under uniaxial loading condition in pure tension
and pure torsion. The nominal stress approach and effective notch stress approach are used
to evaluate the fatigue of the HAZ. The nominal stress was hand calculated, which was used
mainly to design fatigue tests. The FEA software Abaqus was used to calculate the effective
notch stress. The data results of the uniaxial tension and torsion fatigue test demonstrated that
the nominal and shear stress ranges were reasonable and could be used as a baseline for the
biaxial fatigue tests. The biaxial fatigue tests demonstrated that the 90◦out-of-phase loading
conditions caused lower fatigue life compared to 0◦in-phase, which were in agreement with the
findings in the literature [1, 2]. For biaxial loading conditions in-phase Φ = 0◦and out-of-phase
Φ = 90◦, the majority of maximum principal stress data results were below the FAT340 design
curve, which was an indication that the effective notch stress approach predicted lower fatigue
life for the majority of the specimens subjected to biaxial loading condition compared to the
FAT340 design curve.

Introduction
Offshore structures are exposed to a large amount of repeated cycles of stress such as waves and
wind. As time progresses, the cyclic stresses can lead to crack initiation and propagation and
result in structural damage [3]. This process is defined as fatigue. Fatigue occurs at critical
locations where the structure experiences high stress concentrations, such as in welded con-
nections, at geometric changes, or at notches [4]. Fatigue is one of the most critical causes of
structural damage and failures, and therefore an important area to address [5].
The knowledge of fatigue dates back to the 19th century where repeated railway accidents
occurred. This led a railway engineer, August W¨ohler, to start an investigation. W¨ohler’s in-
vestigations into railway axles led to the observation, that failure was not due to stresses above
the ultimate tensile yield strength of the material however, due to cyclic tensile stresses below
the yield strength [6]. At the time, fatigue not a researched area, and with more and more fail-
ure of structures happening without warning, research on fatigue began. W¨ohler’s research in
fatigue, led to more advanced investigations of stress states and material behaviors. Over time
when more investigations were made, it became clear that fatigue could occur under complex
loading conditions.
Studies of uniaxial fatigue, where stress is applied in one direction, are well understood, and
different methods have been made to predict the fatigue life of welded steel structures under
these loading conditions. Yet, in practice, offshore structures are subjected to more complex
loading conditions which cause biaxial or multiaxial stress states in the material. Biaxial fatigue
is more complex and is where stresses are applied in two different directions. Biaxial fatigue in
the heat-affected zone is even more critical. The HAZ is the region between the weld and the
base material, and this zone experiences high temperatures but below the metal’s melting point
[7]. The HAZ is considered as the most critical and complicated region, because it influence the
microstructure and mechanical properties of the overall welded connection [8]. The HAZ is the
main reason for the degradation of strength in welded steel connections [9].
Both global and local approaches have been developed to assess the fatigue of welded steel
connections.
The nominal stress approach in the current Eurocode 3 standard for fatigue
assessment is a global stress approach, which does not account for local stress concentrations
in the HAZ and welding-induced notches.
This approach is easy and time efficient to use,
however it can be conservative. Therefore local approaches have been made, such as the effective
notch stress approach, the hot-spot approach, and the fracture mechanics approach. The local
approaches accounts for the local geometric features of the notch such as the opening angle and
the radius of the notch. This makes the approach more precise in predicting the fatigue life of
welded connections because it accounts for the stress concentrations at notches. The effective
notch stress approach is implemented through the development of a finite element model (FEM)
and is a useful tool for predicting the fatigue life of complex structures with complex loading
conditions. Although research on fatigue has advanced, there is still no consensus on which
approaches are the most accurate for predicting the fatigue life of welded connections under
biaxial loading conditions. There is still need for research within biaxial fatigue to improve
methods and predict precise fatigue life of welded connections.

1.1
Problem statement and motivation
Biaxial fatigue in welded steel connections of offshore structures, induced by wind and waves,
critically impacts structural safety. Cracks commonly initiate in the HAZ and although studies
have examined the fatigue life of the HAZ under uniaxial loading conditions, there remains a
considerable gap in research on biaxial fatigue loading under in-phase and out-of-phase con-
ditions of the HAZ. A global and local fatigue assessment approach is implemented to assess
the fatigue life of the HAZ under biaxial loading conditions, which is the nominal stress ap-
proach provided by Eurocode 3, and the local effective notch stress approach provided by the
International Institute of Welding (IIW).
1.2
Objectives of the thesis
The objective of the thesis is to study the fatigue life of butt-welded steel connections in the
HAZ under biaxial loading conditions and include the following:
• Apply the nominal stress approach provided by Eurocode 3 standard to calculate the
initial stress levels for the experimental uniaxial fatigue tests, which will be used as a
baseline for the biaxial fatigue tests.
• Perform the uniaxial and biaxial fatigue tests on the notched specimens, which are ex-
tracted from the heat-affected zone (HAZ) of butt-welded steel connections to evaluate
their fatigue life.
• The uniaxial tests will be performed with a stress ratio R=0.1 under constant amplitude.
• The biaxial fatigue test will be performed under in-phase δ = 0◦and out-of-phase δ = 90◦
loading conditions with the stress ratio R=0.1 and under constant amplitude.
• Apply the local effective notch stress approach provided by the IIW by developing a finite
element model of the notched specimen and determine the local stresses.
1.3
Limitations
The project has limitations. The project has limitations which are that only specimens of one
material quality which is mild steel S355J2 is fatigue tested. The fatigue tests are also limited
to one stress ratio which is R=0.1. For biaxial out-of-phase loading, the tests are limited to
only a 90◦phase difference and other phase differences are not tested. The specimens are only
tested with constant amplitude loading and the influence of temperature and corrosion, which
can be critical in offshore structures, is not considered. Additionally, the assessment methods
are restricted to the nominal stress approach and the local effective notch stress approach. For
the finite element model analysis, a simplified model is developed, and microstructural changes
in the heat-affected zone are neglected and only one set of material properties is used, which
can affect the fatigue life predictions.
1.4
Assumptions
The assumptions made is that the results obtained from the HAZ, can be compared to other
studies, where test on materials have not been conducted in the HAZ. Residual stresses induced
by welding, which could influence the fatigue life of the butt-welded steel connections, are
assumed to be neglected. For the butt-welded steel connection, the location of HAZ has only

2.1
Fatigue in metals
Fatigue in metals occurs when a material is subjected to repeated cyclic loading. These repeated
loading will, over time, initiate and propagation cracks and eventually cause the material to
fail. The material first undergoes crack initiation at a microscopic level. As the cyclic loading
continues, the cracks grow and transition into a macroscopic level. This is where the crack is in
the progression phase and continues in this phase until failure occurs in the material [3]. The
fatigue life of metals can be categorized into two phases; initiation period and crack growth
period, which is illustrated in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Fatigue life phases of materials [3].
Crack opening in materials can be classified into three modes. Mode I, which is the opening
mode and is characterized by the crack opening normal to the plane of the crack surface due
to tensile forces. Mode II is the sliding mode, which is where the crack surfaces slide past each
other in-plane due to shear forces. Mode III is the shearing mode and is where the crack surfaces
displace laterally relative to one another due to out-of-plane shear force [10]. The three crack
opening modes is depicted in Figure 2. Crack opening mode I and III are the primary focus in
this project, as they are the most relevant in biaxial fatigue in welded steel connections.
Figure 2: Crack opening modes [11].
Fatigue cracks will propagate through paths of least resistance and microscopic stresses will
concentrate at the crack tip until another slip band is activated. This is observed as a non-
linear path resembling a zigzag pattern, which is observed at a microscopic level [3]. Mode
I, or pure tension loading, leads to macroscopic crack growth which is perpendicular to the
loading direction, corresponding to the first principle stress. The microscopic crack is initiated
at the surface of the material with a 45◦inclination from the loading axis. Under Mode III,
pure torsional loading, the same crack phenomena occur in the microscopic plane [12]. The
macroscopic and microscopic crack behavior for modes I and II is illustrated in Figure 3.

2.2
Cyclic stresses
Cyclic stresses occur when a material is subjected to repeated loading over time, which for off-
shore structures are due to wind and waves. Stress can be applied to a material in different ways,
including tension, compression, bending, or torsion. Furthermore, the stress can be applied with
a constant or variable amplitude, which is illustrated in Figure 4. In this project, pure tension,
pure torsion, and combined tension-torsion stresses are applied to the test specimens with a
constant amplitude. When a structure is subjected to cyclic loading with a constant amplitude,
the mean, minimum, and maximum stress are constant throughout the cycles. Fatigue assess-
ment of welded connections subjected to constant amplitude loading conditions can be simpler
to perform due to the uniform stress pattern, which makes the fatigue life easier to predict
[13]. The other loading scenario is the variable amplitude loading condition. In this case, the
amplitude varies throughout the cycle, and the mean, minimum, and maximum stress are also
varying. Analyzing this type of stress pattern can be difficult because it is unpredictable. This
type of cyclic stress is best at representing a realistic scenario that offshore structures encounter
in natural environments [13].
Figure 4: Constant and variable amplitude loading [13].

the mean stress is zero, the material undergoes equal cycling in both tension and compression
and the load ratio R=-1 due to the minimum and maximum stresses being the same magnitude
but has the opposite sign. Figure 5(b) illustrates a loading condition with positive mean stress,
and the minimum stress is 0, in this loading condition the ratio is always 0. Figure 5(c) has a
higher positive mean stress, and the minimum stress is positive as well. The cycle remains in

2.3
Biaxial loading
Structures assembled using trusses, frames, and stiffener plates are primarily subjected to uni-
axial stresses [17]. When a material is subjected to stresses along a single axis either σx, σy or
σz, it is called uniaxial loading. Where in biaxial loading structures are subjected to stresses
in two directions, which can be tension, bending, or torsion. Uniaxial and biaxial loading are
illustrated in figure Figure 7.
Figure 7: Uniaxial and biaxial loading [18].
Biaxial loading can appear in different conditions, including equal biaxial tension, combined
tension and torsion, mixed-mode loading, and non-proportional. Proportional loading is when
the loads from the two axes are in-phase. The proportional loading condition is not as common
for offshore structures as non-proportional as they often experience loads in non-proportional
from the wind and wave forces acting at different times and in different directions as illustrated
in Figure 8. Proportional loading results in higher principal stress, while in non-proportional
loading, a less drastic increase in the principal stress occurs. Even though the principal stress
is higher in proportional loading, studies have shown that non-proportional loading results in a
lower fatigue life [1].

Figure 8: Proportional loading and non-proportional loading [19].
Uniaxial and biaxial loading reveals large differences in stress responses and material behavior.
In biaxial loading, the interaction of forces creates complex stress states and strain paths, clearly
influencing crack initiation and propagation compared to uniaxial conditions. Studies indicate
that under biaxial loading, the fatigue life of materials is reduced.
2.4
Zones of welded connections
Welding is commonly used in the offshore structures industry because of its economics and
efficiency [20]. There are many types of welding and different procedures and methods. The
methods include methods arc welding, gas welding, resistance welding, and laser welding. The
welding process involves heating, melting, and solidification, and in fusion welding, there are
three regions, which are the fusion zone, the heat-affected zone, and the base material [21]. In
Figure 9, the different zones are presented in a butt-welded connection. The fusion zone is the
region that experiences melting due to the source of heat. The heat-affected zone (HAZ) is the
region between the weld and the base material, and this zone experiences high temperatures
but below the metal’s melting point, which can cause changes in the microstructure [7]. The
HAZ is considered the most complicated region in the welded connection, as it influences the
microstructure, grain size of the weld bead, and the mechanical properties of the welded connec-
tion [8]. Some of the material properties that can change due to the welding are the hardness,
yield strength, and ultimate tensile strength of the connection. The welding parameters that
influence the microstructure changes are the welding current, welding speed, wire diameter, and
welding voltage [22]. The HAZ is the main reason for the degradation of strength in the welded
steel connections [9].
Figure 9: Fusion zone, heat-affected zone, and base metal of a butt-welded connection [23].

2.5
Effects of welding
When materials are welded, residual stresses are induced [24].
The stresses can cause the
material to deform and shrink, depending on different factors. The geometry of the material
has a large influence on the deformations. Bending and buckling often occurs in thin materials
and shrinkage in thicker materials. Another factor that influences this is the boundary conditions
and whether the material can move freely or is restrained [24]. In Figure 10, the different types
of deformations are illustrated.
Transverse shrinkage happens when the material contracts
perpendicular to the weld, and longitudinal shrinkage, when the material is contracted along
the weld if there are no constraints on the material. Angular shrinkage occurs when the material
is thin and makes bending deformations.

2.6
Residual stresses in welded connections
Residual stresses are defined accordingly in Eurocode as ”a permanent state of stress in a
structure that is in static equilibrium and is independent of any applied action”[25]. When
there is no external load imposed on a material, the residual stresses within the material have
to be in equilibrium. A material that has compressive residual stresses must also have tensile
residual stresses for it to be in equilibrium, which is illustrated in Figure 11.
Figure 11: Residual stress equilibrium in absence of external load [26].
Residual stresses are created during any manufacturing process. These stresses can also develop
9
==PAGE:18==
during the service life of a component. In response to the local strains, residual stresses form in
the material as an elastic response, which could result from non-uniform plastic deformation.
The material deforms elastically to maintain its shape and size despite internal stresses.
Residual stresses in structural steel are important to take into account when determining the
fatigue life of welded connections.
Tensile residual stresses can accelerate the fatigue crack
growth, which reduces the fatigue life [27]. A reason why tensile residual stresses accelerate
the fatigue crack growth is that any applied cyclic stresses are imposed on top of the existing
residual stress, and the range of the stress cycle does not change, which is illustrated in Figure 12
[28].
Figure 12: Superimposed residual stress on the cyclic stress [28].
The welding process creates residual stresses, which are caused by the difference in the thermal
expansion and contraction of the weld metal and the base metal. Since the material is heated
locally, the temperature distribution in the weld is non-uniform and changes throughout the
welding. During the welding process, nonelastic strains are created in the weld and base material
in regions close to the weld. These nonelastic strains create residual stresses after the weld cools
down and solidifies. The residual stress in and around the weld may even reach the material
yield strength in concentrated regions. Figure 13 illustrates the changes in temperature and the
how residual stresses is occurring during the welding process [29]. In Figure 13(a), the welding
is at the origin, and the hatched area illustrates where the plastic deformations occur during
welding. Figure 13(b) shows the temperature distribution throughout the welding. In Section
A-A where there has not been any welding, there are negligible changes in temperature and
residual stress due to the welding. In Section B-B, at the melting region, the stresses are zero
as molted metal can not support any load due to the reduction in the yield strength. On each
side of the arc weld (the HAZ), there are compressive residual stresses due to the expansion
of the metal, which is restrained by the colder metal that surrounds it. The residual stresses
away from the weld are tensile and are balanced by the compressive stresses near the weld. In
section C-C, the weld and base material have gradually cooled down and are now shrinking,
which creates tensile stress at the weld and in the region close to it (HAZ). In section D-D
the material is now cooled back to normal (∆T = 0), but there are now high tensile residual
stresses at the weld and at the heat-affected zone (HAZ), and it is in equilibrium with the
compressive residual stresses in the region away from the weld. The process explained here is a
very simplified example. These processes are very complex and are influenced by other factors
as well [29].

2.7
Post-weld treatments
Post-weld treatments are used to improve the weld strength and obtain longer fatigue life in
the welded connections. Many different treatment methods can be used to reduce local stress
concentrations and residual stresses with the use of thermal and mechanical processes. Some
of the fatigue life improvement methods include toe grinding, TIG remelting, shot peening,
needle peening and hammer peening [30]. The post-weld treatments are often costly and time-
consuming, however, they improve the weld strength and the fatigue life of a welded steel
connection [30].
2.8

assessment, notches are a critical subject to consider. Notches are introduced by the shape and

of cracks, which makes notched regions origins of fatigue failure.

Notch shapes

2.10
Fatigue assessment approaches
Fatigue assessment of welded steel connections can be performed using different approaches and
methods, which differ in how local stress concentrations is considered [32]. Global assessment
methods determine the fatigue life of welded connection based on internal forces and moments
and assumes a linear stress distribution, neglecting local stress effects [33]. Local assessment
methods and approaches, on the other hand, determine the fatigue life based on local parameters
such as stresses or deformations, considering the effects of how the geometry affects specific areas
[10]. Most guidelines and standards are based on a nominal stress approach, which is a global
approach, although fatigue failure of structures often results from local geometry and parameters
[32]. The most common fatigue assessment methods are the nominal stress approach, hot-spot
stress approach, and the effective notch stress approach. Figure 16 illustrates the different stress
regions and types used when assessing fatigue in welded steel connections. The nominal stress
is determined over a wide region, structural stress is determined near the welded joint, hot spot
stress is determined at critical points near the weld, and the notch stress is determined from
the highest stress at the weld edge [34].
Figure 16: Stress regions and types used when assessing fatigue in welded steel connections [34].

2.10.1
The nominal stress approach
When assessing fatigue life of materials, the nominal stress approach is widely used [32]. The
International Institute of Welding (IIW) and Eurocode 3 provide their own guidelines and rec-
ommendations. The nominal stress is calculated based on the average stress in the corresponding
cross-section and under the assumption of linear elastic theory. This method does not consider
the local stresses but is based on average stress in the corresponding cross-section [32]. IIW
and Eurocode 3 provide detailed classifications of connections and corresponding S-N curves
based on nominal stress. These classifications depend on the geometry of the element, loading
conditions, and the position of cracks [32]. The connection details considered in the guidelines
must, therefore, be chosen correctly to resemble the welded connection. The nominal stress
method is useful when assessing the fatigue life of simple element geometry, however for more
complex geometry and loading conditions, local approaches are more sufficient [32]. The S-N
curves are used to design connections by predicting the fatigue life. Using the provided S-N
curves ensures that the connections can withhold the cyclic loading. The detail category ∆σC
is the stress range (in MPa) which the specific detail can withstand for 2 million cycles with
a 95% probability of survival. Each detail category has their own S-N curve, which is used to
determine the fatigue life for a specific stress range.

welded connection. When the stress range and detail category are known, the number of cycles
until failure can be determined from the S-N curve or using the formulas provided by Eurocode
3. First of all, a construction detail is selected based on how suitable it is to the design category.

2.10.2
The effective notch stress approach
There are different approaches and guidelines for the effective notch stress approach. In the
following chapter, the method proposed by the International Institute of Welding (IIW) is
presented. The approach is a local approach which on contrary to the global nominal stress
approach, considers the weld geometry and changes in geometry in the toe and root [36]. The
idea behind the approach is to model the weld root or toe as a notch with a specific reference
radius as illustrated in Figure 18 [37]. Effective notch stress is the total stress in the root or
toe of the weld and is determined by the assumption of linear elastic behavior of the material
[32]. Geometric imperfections and notches in welded steel connections are the reason for high
stress concentrations which lower the fatigue life [32]. The radius and the sharpness of the notch
influence the stress. Sharper notches and smaller radii create high-stress concentrations [37].
15
==PAGE:24==
Figure 18: Reference radius [32].
The effective notch stress approach can be used to find the maximum stresses in the notch and
determine the fatigue life of the welded connection. This approach is applicable when facing
complex loading conditions or complex geometries. The effective notch stress approach was first
introduced by Radaj in the 1970s [38]. The suggestion was to use the microstructural support
hypothesis by Neuber [39]. To solve the problem of infinite stresses at the notch, a fictitious
enlargement of the notch radius, ρf, was introduced:
ρf = ρ + s · ρ∗
(11)
Where ρ is the actual notch radius, s is the constraint factor and ρ∗is the micro-structural
length.
By modifying the notch radius and incorporating a microstructural length ρ∗, the finite model
will be better at adjusting to different materials [40]. This parameter is highly important when
designing for fatigue, where the microstructural properties of a material are pivotal for the
fatigue performance of the welded connection. When designing complex welded connections,
standard design codes and guidelines often simplify the stress concentrations, which is where the
effective notch stress approach can take into account the complex geometries. This is employed
by modifying the constraint factor s to represent the shape of the notch, which will result in
more precise estimates of the fatigue life of the welded connection [40]. The reference notch is
calculated based on the worst condition, which would be where the actual radius was 0 (ρ = 0).
When ρ∗= 0.4mm and s = 2.5 for steel members. The final rounded fictitious radius wil be
1mm, for plate thickness of 5mm or above [38]. This is illustrated in Figure 19.

The effective notch stress approach has a lot of advantages when predicting fatigue life of
welded connections, but also has limitations The development of a finite element model needs
to accurately represent the welded connection, and factors such as mesh size, element type, and
boundary and load conditions are important. If not modeled correctly, the estimation of fatigue
life can be wrong. Developing a finite element model can be time and cost-consuming com-
pared to using the traditional and standard guidelines for fatigue assessment. This makes the
approach less practical for engineers to employ when designing for fatigue in welded connections.

2.12
Current state of research on biaxial fatigue
The current research on biaxial fatigue covers analytical, experimental, and numerical studies.
Through the past decades, progress in the field of biaxial fatigue has been reached [45]. The
investigations have provided knowledge in the fatigue behavior of welded steel connections,
including crack initiation and propagation and the influence of material properties [46]. Weld
geometry, post-weld treatments, and residual stresses are also investigated and their impacts on
the fatigue life [47]. Local approaches have been developed for assessing more accurate fatigue
life predictions for welded steel connections exposed to complex multiaxial loading compared
to the global approaches [48]. Although research on biaxial fatigue has been covered, there are
still areas that need to be further studied [32].
2.12.1
Biaxial loading and phase difference
Biaxial loading can either be in-phase or out-of-phase. In-phase, is where the principal stress
direction is constant and out-of-phase is where the principal stress direction varies over time. In-
vestigations has been conducted on the impact of phase differences in biaxial loading suggesting
same conclusions [2]. Archer (1987) and Siljander (1992) were the first researchers to study out-
of-phase loading in welded steel connections and both found that out-of-phase loading caused
more damage than in-phase loading and resulted in shorter fatigue life [2]. Takahshi came to the
same findings as Siljander and Archer et al. that out-of-phase 90◦loading decreased the fatigue
life compared to in-phase loading [1]. Backstr¨om and Marquises re-analyzed 233 experimental
results from eight different studies of welded connections. During out-of-phase loading, there
was an increase in damage compared to in-phase loading [2]. As many research papers have
focused on the 90◦out-of-phase cyclic loading, Winther, Jensen et. al. studied the effect of
phase difference of 0◦, 22.5◦, 45◦, 67.5◦, and 90◦[49]. The stress state was kept the same for
all the tests, and the tests were conducted with the four phase shift. The findings also found,
that out-of-phase loading causes more fatigue damage than in-phase loading. The fatigue life of
the different phase shifts was very close to each other, and the differences in fatigue life could
not be attributed to the phase shifts, suggesting that out-of-phase shifts less than 90◦did as
much damage as 90◦[49]. Sonsino et al. found that while in-phase loading aligns well with
traditional hypotheses (von Mises or Tresca) when assessing nominal, structural, or local strains
18
==PAGE:27==
or stresses, the impact of out-of-phase loading on fatigue life is overestimated by the methods
[50]. This indicates that there still is need for a local approach that can predict the fatigue life
more precise for out-of-phase loading.
2.12.2
Test of material level specimen
Material level testing is performed to understand of the fatigue life of the HAZ. The material
in the fusion zone and the HAZ are often assumed to have the same material properties as
the base material. This assumption can lead to inaccurate estimates because experiments show,
that cracks usually initiate in the HAZ due to its small defects and higher residual stresses. The
area of the HAZ can vary depending on the different temperature gradients during heating and
cooling cycles, and different materials will give different results. Due to these complexities in
the HAZ, it is important to take the mechanical properties of the HAZ into account to estimate
the fatigue strength accurately.
Uniaxial fatigue tests in the HAZ have been conducted on different steel qualities indicating
that the fatigue life of the HAZ is lower than the weld and base material [44, 51]. W Fricke et al.
conducted fatigue tests on specimens, were the V-and u-notches were placed in the HAZ of steel
S355. The v-notch specimens had an opening angle of ω = 135◦and a notch radius r = 0.15mm
and the u-notch had an opening angle of ω = 0◦and a notch radius r = 0.15mm. The tests were
conducted under tension-compression loading with a stress ratio R = −1. Figure 22 illustrates
the fatigue life of the weld material, HAZ, and base material which are plotted in S-N curves
with structural stress range and the number of cycles to fracture for the v-notch a) and u-notch
b).
The plots demonstrate for both the v-and u-notch, that the fatigue of the HAZ under
uniaxial tension, is lower compared to the weld and base material.
Figure 22: Uniaxial fatigue tests conducted on specimens were the V-and u-notches were placed in the
HAZ of steel S355.
The research on fatigue life of the HAZ have only been conducted for uniaxial loading, with
none in biaxial loading conditions. Biaxial fatigue represents more accurate loading conditions
in the real world, especially in offshore structures, where waves and wind subjects the structure

from more than one direction at the same time.
2.12.3
Fracture surfaces of materials
Experimental studies suggest that load magnitude, initial crack tip condition, load ratio, and
mean stress have a large effect on the crack growth mode [1, 2, 52]. In recent decades, there
has been increased interest in modeling and experimental investigations of small crack growth.
Researchers have focused on the fracture mechanics of small cracks within the field of fractog-
raphy [53]. In Figure 1, Section 2.1 the fatigue process was described as including both the
initiation and crack growth periods. The crack initiation period consists of crack nucleation
and micro-crack growth up to lengths of hundreds of micrometers. This is followed by the small
crack growth region, which occurs after the microscopic growth illustrated in Figure 23.
Figure 23: Different stages of crack nucleation and growth during the fatigue process [52].
Modelling the small crack will create a better understanding of the fracture mechanics of small
cracks.
This will improve the accuracy of current models in predicting small crack growth
behavior under multiaxial stress conditions and will eventually be useful for fatigue design. The
plasticity observed at the micro-scale is recognized as a local phenomenon in structural steel and
other metallic materials, which is characterized by deformations in small localized regions, while
the material globally remains in the elastic state [54]. Micro-plasticity is defined as a small-
scale deformation which arises before the material shows signs of overall plastic deformation.
Reis, Li, and Freitas studied the effects of multiaxial loading paths on the cyclic deformation
behavior, crack initiation, and crack path in three materials: Ck45 (medium carbon steel),
42CrMo4 (low alloy steel), and AISI 303 stainless steel.
The observations made were that
at the same strain, an out-of-phase loading path caused much more hardening than in-phase
loading paths, which had a degrading effect on fatigue life [46]. They also observed that the
three materials demonstrated different crack orientations under the same loading path, linked
to their varying cyclic plasticity behavior and their different responses to out-of-phase loading.
Lastly, they observed that critical plane models give good predictions of the orientation of the
crack initiation plane for structural steel. Cyclic plasticity refers to the material’s ability to
undergo irreversible, permanent deformation due to repeated or cyclic loading. Shamsaei et al.
studied small fatigue crack growth under multiaxial stresses during various multiaxial loading
conditions. They used steels including 1045 and 1050 (medium carbon steels), 304L (stainless
steel), and Inconel 718. Their results demonstrated that for all loading conditions and materials,
the cracks were on or about the maximum shear plane. They observed more cracks for in-phase
loading compared to out-of-phase loading at the same strain level, but the crack growth rate
was higher for out-of-phase loading [52].

2.12.4
Summary and research gaps
Research on biaxial loading and phase difference demonstrate consistent findings, on the fa-
tigue life of welded steel connections. Studies indicate out-of-phase loading conditions causes
a decrease in the fatigue life of welded connections. However findings also showed that phase
difference less than 90◦cause as much damage as 90◦. Research also indicates that fatigue life is
often overestimated by traditional hypotheses (von Mises or Tresca) under out-of-phase loading.
For the test of material level specimens studies of the heat-affected zone, found that the mate-
rial properties of the HAZ and the fusion zone often are assumed to be the same as the base
material, which leads to imprecise fatigue life predictions. The HAZ is a complicated region due
to the changes in the microstructure during welding and the high stress concentrations causes
the material to initiate cracks. Experimental fatigue testing in the HAZ of specimens subjected
to uniaxial loading conditions has been performed for different materials. However, studies on
the HAZ subjected to biaxial loading conditions are still in need of further research.
The research on fracture surfaces of materials, indicates that at the same strain, an out-of-phase
loading path caused much more hardening than in-phase loading paths, which had a degrading
effect on fatigue life.
It was observed that more cracks initiated for in-phase loading path,
compared to out-of-phase loading at the same strain level. However, the crack growth rate was
higher for out-of-phase loading. Further research on fracture surfaces in the HAZ is needed,
especially for biaxial loading conditions.

Tuning of the machine
The Instron biaxial fatigue testing machine was too sensitive, and tuning was necessary for
it to operate correctly. Tuning is done by changing the proportional-integral-derivative (PID)
settings. The reason why PID adjustment is necessary is that the loading is a sinus curve and
constantly oscillates. The machine’s response to this can be either too sensitive or too slow to
adjust. Without the calibration using PID, the force and torque would not produce a proper
sinus curve. Tuning was done for each uniaxial tension, uniaxial torsion, and biaxial tension
and torsion loading. When changing from the different loading, the machine had a tendency
to not produce a perfect sinus curve, and it required adjustment to the PID throughout the
testing process.

Element type
It is typically recommended to use a finer mesh or higher order elements to better predict the
stresses in areas where the stress gradient is high [62]. Figure 47 illustrates three-dimensional
element types, which can be used in the finite element model. The tetrahedra element is effective
to use when modeling complex geometries with curved surfaces. The triangular prisms (wedges)
elements are especially effective for creating good meshes of cylindrical and layer interfaces.
Hexahedra elements can be used for creating a fine mesh and are effective when the model has
high stress gradients, which is at notches or sharp edges [62].
Figure 47: Three-dimensional element types [62].
A convergence study has been conducted to analyze which element type fits the model best.
The study is presented in Section 4.3.1. The specimen has a notch, which creates a high stress
gradient around the notch. The high stress gradient makes the hexahedra elements ideal at
simulating the stresses around the notch. The different hexahedra element types considered
in the analysis are the first-order elements, C3D8 and C3D8R, and second-order elements,
C3D20 and C3D20R. The ”C” stands for continuum, which is for solid or structural mechanics
applications. ”3D” means that it is in three dimensions. 8 or 20 indicates the number of nodes
in the element. ”R” stands for reduced integration. The benefit of using elements with reduced
integration points is that they consume fewer computational resources.

Finite element analysis
4.3.1
Convergence analysis
A convergence analysis of the finite element model (FEM) was performed to ensure that the
approximated value is valid. The model must be as accurate as possible while staying within
the range of available nodes. The Abaqus academic license limits the number of available nodes
to 250,000. This is the upper limit for the number of nodes, and it is not wise to use that
many as the computational time would be unnecessarily long. Instead, a better mesh could
reduce the number of nodes used. A convergence study is conducted to determine the required
number of elements to achieve an acceptable result. Local mesh sizes are adjusted at the notch
to determine at what point the stresses converge. Some of the different meshes are illustrated
in Figure 53-Figure 55. the loadings for pure tension was 3491 N, for pure torsion the loading
was 3491 Nmm, for biaxial loading the tension component was 1742 N and torsion component
was 0.174 Nm
Figure 53 is a detailed visualization that presents the stress in a color gradient, with blue
representing the areas of low stress and red representing the area with high stress. The specimen
is held with grips at both ends, as illustrated in the figure, at this these two areas the stresses
are low. The mesh consists of 1mm spacing at the main body and 0.1mm spacing at the notch.
The C3D8R element is well suited to capture the stresses experienced on the main body, where
the stress gradient is lower. While the C3D8R is good for general purposes, it is not suited to
capture the complex stresses occurring at the notch. The elements are linear and are not well
suited to accurately model high stress gradient and sharp changes in geometry.
Figure 53: Mesh configuration of stress distribution with C3D8R brick element with 0.1mm spacing at
the notch and C3D8R elements with 1mm element spacing at the main body.
The C3D8R brick elements were not well suited for the high stress gradient experienced in the
material at the notch. The same analysis was conducted but now with the C3D8 brick elements
at the notch. This element is much better at capturing the changes in the stress at the notch,
but a very large number of elements with required to reach convergence.

Discussion
This section discusses the experimental results presented in the previous sections. Uniaxial tests
are firstly discussed followed by biaxial tests. In each series of tests, the nominal stress and the
effective notch stress are discussed. Either principal stress or the equivalent stress von Mises
stress at the critical locations (notch) is adopted in the analysis using the effective notch stress.
5.1
Uniaxial fatigue tests - Pure tension
Table 7 summaries the specimen number, load mode, maximum principal stress, von Mises
stress, predicted cycles to failure according to detail category 112 in Eurocode 3, and experi-
mentally obtained fatigue life. In total three tension tests were conducted with three different
stress ranges. The stress ranges, and tension amplitudes are provided in Table 6. The nomi-
nal stress is calculated using the applied force divided by the cross-sectional area. Figure 50a
illustrates the normal stress range (∆σnom) and the number of cycles to failure (Nf).
The
experimental data is plotted using the Eurocode 3 detail categories EC 112, 90, and 80 design
and mean curves (offset from the design value with the standard deviation of 0.3). The design
curves illustrates a 95% probability of survival, and the mean curve illustrates a 50% probability
of survival.
The nominal stress curve is above the design curve of the three detail categories and below
the mean curves. It can be observed that fatigue life decreases as the stress range increases.
Note that the nominal stress is primarily used to evaluate whether the obtained results are
within a reasonable range of the design and mean curves, which is the case for the tension tests.
Figure 50b illustrates the maximum principle stress and the von Mises stress range with the
number of cycles to failure. The design and mean curve for the FAT340 are plotted and the
figure shows, that the maximum principal stress falls within the FAT340 category. The FAT
curve for the von Mises is slightly lower compared to the FAT curve for the maximum principle
stress [66]. The von Mises stresses are lower than the maximum principle stresses, which is in
line with the FAT curve trend. The experimental curve of the maximum principle stress locates
between the design and mean curve, indicating that the ENS approach gives an understandable
prediction for the notch radius of 0.3 mm.
A potential reason for the nominal stress curve lies closer to the design compared to the mean
curve could be due to the notch effect. The notch causes the local stresses to be notably higher
than the nominal stress under tensile loading. Moreover, parallel misalignments may exist in
the tests. This occurs when the axis of the specimen is offset between the top and bottom
supports, meaning that the specimen is not perfectly aligned along the length. Instead of the
force being applied directly along the axis, it is off-centred, causing eccentric loads. Bending
is introduced which creates a non-uniform stress distribution, where the side of the specimen
furthest from the axis experiences tensile stresses. The maximum stresses are thus higher than
the hand calculated ones. Fatigue cracks, therefore, initiate and propagate earlier and faster,
which is detrimental to the fatigue performance.

==PAGE:61==
5.2
Uniaxial fatigue tests - Pure torsion
Results of pure torsion tests are given in Table 7 as well. Figure 51a illustrates the the shear
stress range (∆τnom) and the number of cycles to failure (Nf). The experimental results are
plotted with the design and mean curve of detail category EC100 (with a fixed slope of 5) in
Eurocode 3. The results from the pure torsion tests are slightly above the mean curve. This is
an indication, that the results are in a reasonable range and reliable. If there were a parallel
misalignment during the testing, it would not alter the distribution of stresses in torsion, as
torsion stresses are less sensitive to the axial alignment. The torsion tests depend more on
rotational symmetry, which is not an issue since the specimen is round. Figure 51a presents the
results for the maximum principal stress and von Mises stress range with the cycles to failure
with the FAT340 design and mean curve. Opposite from the pure tension, the von Mises stress
curve is obviously higher than the principal stress curve. The former locates between the mean
and design curves while the latter crosses the design curve. This indicates that the design curve
of principal stress may be different between tension and pure torsion. Calibration is needed to
cover both cases using the ENS.
5.3
Biaxial fatigue tests
Results of biaxial tension-torsion tests are summarized in Table 9. Figure 52a plots for in-phase
loaded Φ = 0◦or out-of-phase loaded Φ = 90◦the nominal stress results. The S-N curves
illustrate the stress range ∆σnom and ∆σeqv with the design and mean curves of Eurocode 3
detail categories 112, 90, and 80, with the slope m = 3. For the biaxial loading, the corre-
sponding shear stress amplitude τnom can be calculated using τnom = σnomλ. Note that in
Figure 52a, detail categories 112, 90, and 80 curves are exclusively used to compare the nominal
and equivalent stress. The in-phase and out-of-phase loaded nominal stress results are even
below the design curves of EC90 when 2 · 104 ≤Nf ≤1 · 105. The ∆σeqv curves are obviously
higher, which locate between the design and mean curves of detail categories at most points. It
can be concluded that ∆σeqv gives better predictions than ∆τnom for biaxial fatigue. It should
be clarified that nominal stress is determined as the force divided by the cross-sectional area.
However, due to the presence of a V-notch in the specimens, nominal stress is not a reliable in-
dicator for assessing the actual stress distribution. This is because it does not account for stress
concentrations caused by the notch. As this study’s focus was specifically to investigate the
fatigue life of HAZ. The welding process can affect the microstructure and material properties
in HAZ [8]. This makes the region more sensitive to fatigue loading and shows different fatigue
behaviours. Overall, the nominal stresses were plotted together with the detail categories for
comparison only. The equivalent stress from Eurocode 3 [25] is an assessment that combines
the shear and tension stress range. As the equivalent stress accounts for more complex stress
states, it provides a better representation of the stress states under biaxial loading. However,
the local approach is more suitable to study the notched specimens used in this study.
Figure 52b plots results of biaxial tests with the ENS using either the von Mises stress or
maximum principal stress failure criterion. The maximum principal stress criterion is often
used in brittle materials, where failure occurs at the highest tensile stress.
The von Mises
criterion is used to predict yield in materials under more complex loading conditions, which is
later adopted for multiaxial fatigue analysis by some researchers.

It can be observed that the 90◦out-of-phase biaxial loading curves are lower than the 0◦in-

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to study the fatigue life of the HAZ in butt-welded steel
connections. A total of 22 V-notched specimens were experimentally investigated. Two biaxial
loading conditions, in-phase 0◦and out-of-phase 90◦, with the stress ratio R = 0.1 and biaxiality
ratio λ = 1.0 were studied. Uniaxial loading, pure tension and pure torsion, were also applied.
The nominal stress approach and effective notch stress approach were used to evaluate the
fatigue of the HAZ. Based on the results from the uniaxial and biaxial tests and discussion,
conclusions can be drawn as follows:
1. For the uniaxial tension tests, the nominal stress curve is within a reasonable range be-
tween the EC design and mean curves. The curve lies closer to the design curve, which
could be due to the notch effect, causing the local stresses to be notably higher than the
nominal stress under tensile loading. Moreover, parallel misalignment could exist in the
tests. When the ENS with the max. principal stress failure criterion is applied, the re-
sults are between the design and mean curves, indicating that the ENS gives an acceptable
prediction for the notch radius of 0.3 mm.
2. For the uniaxial torsion tests, the shear stress curve is slightly above the mean curve,
indicating the results are in a reasonable range. Opposite from the pure tension, the von
Mises stress curve is higher than the max. principal stress curve and is the critical failure
criterion. The former locates between the mean and design curves while the latter crosses
the design curve. This concludes that the design curve with the principal stress failure
criterion may be different between tension and pure torsion. Calibration is needed to
cover both cases using the ENS.
3. For biaxial tests, the in-phase and out-of-phase loaded nominal stress results are even
below the design curves of EC90 when 2·104 ≤Nf ≤1·105. The ∆σeqv curves are higher
and located between the design and mean curves of detail categories at most points.
It can be concluded that ∆σeqv gives better predictions than ∆τnom for biaxial fatigue.
However, due to the presence of a V-notch in the specimens, the nominal stress may not
be a reliable indicator for assessing the actual stress distribution. As this study’s focus
was specifically to investigate the fatigue life of HAZ. The welding process can affect the
microstructure and material properties in HAZ [8]. This makes the region more sensitive
to fatigue loading and shows different fatigue behaviours. The 90◦out-of-phase biaxial
loading curves are lower than the 0◦in-phase curves for both max. principal stress and
von Misses stress failure criteria, which is consistent with findings from the literature [1,

three out of five points are below the FAT340 design curve. While more of the test data are
between the FAT mean and design curve for the in-phase biaxial loading. It, therefore, can
be concluded that the proposed FAT 340 may be unsuitable for the out-of-phase biaxial
loading. For the biaxiality ratio of λ = 1, the max. principal stress is higher than the von
Mises stress for both in-phase 0◦and out-of-phase 90◦biaxial loading, in line with the
uniaxial tension loading. It is opposite for the uniaxial torsion loading, where von Mises

stress, therefore, depends on the biaxiality ratio.
