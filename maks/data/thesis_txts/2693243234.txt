Abstract
The share of the wind energy in the energy system is constantly growing and will continue to do
so in the future. In the renewable-dominated energy systems the proﬁtability of the project will
be determined by the value of the energy and not only the cost of it. This study aims to ﬁnd a
way to design a new generation of wind farms, where the high energy value is prioritized over low
energy cost.
The optimal layouts were achieved by using the DTU optimization tool TOPFARM on two pairs
of metrics as objectives, where each pair consisted of one conventional and one value based metric.
First pair consisted of AEP (Annual Energy Production) and LROE (Levelized Revenue of Energy)
metric and the second, LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy) and COVE (Cost of Valued Energy). The
optimization was performed for the simple constrained layouts. The optimization was conducted
on a single site located at the west coast of Jutland in Denmark.
According to the results of the study, the chosen metrics created optimal layout designs that were
diﬀerent.
The slight diﬀerence was observed between the design pattern for AEP and LROE
objectives. However, for the LCOE and COVE objectives the diﬀerence was signiﬁcant.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Since the beginning of this century the wind capacity installed globally reached almost 750 GW.
Global installations has grown very fast as it is shown on the Figure 1.1. European Commission[1]
target is to cover at least one third of the energy consumption by 2030 with renewable energy. The
global scenario to achieve the target of net zero by 2050 as described in Global Wind Report 2021
(GWEC)[2] assumes the need of increase of new yearly installed wind capacity. That is illustrated
in the Figure 1.2. The mentioned targets clearly indicate continued growth in renewable energy
deployment and therefore wind energy deployment in coming years.

1.2. RESEARCH QUESTIONS
Figure 1.2: New global wind installations in GW to reach net zero by 2050.[2]
In the last decade, wind farm developers have developed advanced optimization capabilities to
optimized wind farm design for objectives of Annual Energy Production (AEP) and Levelized
cost of energy (LCOE). As a global design metric, LCOE metric includes the capital expenditures
(CAPEX) and operational expenditures (OPEX), together with AEP. However, already now and
in the future, where the share of energy from wind in the energy system will increase, the value
of energy and not just the cost of energy will determine a project’s proﬁtability. This occurs as
wind farms move from ﬁxed payment contracts to participation in electricity markets – a trend
that has been growing as shares of wind generation in systems across Europe and globally grow to
5%, 10% or more. For upcoming years, a new metric for wind farm design should be established
and implemented that will go beyond LCOE [3].
1.2
Research Questions
The following research question was formulated:
How can we design the next generation of wind farms that will be proﬁtable in
renewable-dominated energy systems – where high energy value is more important
than low energy cost?
2

1.3. THESIS OUTLINE
An additional set of questions were developed to answer the main research question:
• What metrics are available for the optimization of the wind farm layout design for value and
proﬁtability?
• What considerations apply while introducing a speciﬁc metric?
• What are the design variables of interest and how are they likely to impact the potential
metrics diﬀerently?
• How can each metric be applied in the farm design process?
• What is the best metric for the optimization of the wind farm layout design?
• What is the optimal way to design layout to improve the value of the wind energy for selected
metrics of interest?
• How does the diﬀerent choice of optimization metrics aﬀect the design of the wind farm
layout?

Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1
Metrics Review
The metrics presented in this report are classiﬁed similarly as in the literature [4] in three diﬀerent
groups.
The ﬁrst non-comprehensive group consist of metrics that include only cost or value
separately. The second group is the group of comprehensive metrics. The metric that is in this
group considers both cost and value sources. The third and the last group contain proﬁtability
metrics.
2.1.1
Non-comprehensive metrics
In this group of the metrics only one covers the cost component and four of them accounts just for
value of the energy. The sole cost metric is LCOE and the ones that consider the value are LACE,
LACEs, VF and LVOE. All of the mentioned metrics are compiled in the Table 2.1.
Levelized cost of energy (LCOE) is commonly-used metric, that calculates the cost of generat-
ing a unit of electricity for a certain technology. It is widely used in terms of comparing diﬀerent
energy generating technologies. Technologies having diﬀerent capital recovery period can be com-
pared unig LCOE, because all costs during the lifetime of a project are discounted to a present
value (expressed in e/MWh). As it is expressed in the Table 2.1 the LCOE is calculated by division
of total costs(capital costs, ﬁxed and variable Operation and Maintenance costs, and fuel costs)
by electrical energy production. Both total costs and electrical energy production are discounted
over the same period of time.
Although, LCOE metric is used very often to compare the energy technologies due to its advantage
of covering all of the costs, it is helpful to assess the economic competitiveness, but yet insuﬃcient
compared to other metrics [5]. LCOE is not a perfect metric due to its two limitations. First, it

information regarding system value of those energy services in money related terms[4]. Second the

2.1. METRICS REVIEW
The metric that is complementary to LCOE is Levelised value of energy (LVOE). It shows the
value of a unit of electricity for a certain technology. According to the literature [4][7] is it de-
ﬁned as total discounted sum of value for all services divided by the discounted sum of electrical
energy produced by the asset. The disadvantages of LVOE comparing it to comprehensive metrics
is the fact that it omits the technology costs and the value is subjected to uncertainty in the prices.
Another value based metric that is connected to LVOE and is complementary to LCOE is Value
Factor (VF). This measure is deﬁned as the ratio between market value (total revenues/production)
and the average electricity price[6][9][10]. In the quoted literature the VF is presented in situation
where the value of variable renewable energy (VRE) is decreasing with penetration. If there is a
source or assumption of prices and dispatch of certain technology, the VF can be calculated for
any technology.
The two last non-comprehensive metrics evaluated in this report are Levelized avoided cost of en-
ergy (LACE) published in [11] and simpliﬁed version of this metric simpliﬁed LACE (LACEs)[6].
The calculation of LACE returns the revenue that can be gained from a new power generation
system, levelized according to its expected AEP, and including capacity revenues. The simpliﬁed
LACE was developed to avoid the main disadvantage of LACE, which is the requirement of com-
plex site-speciﬁc electricity price models. The LACEs accounts for the revenue ignoring capacity
payments, but accounting for the variability of prices, therefore accounting for the value of the
energy[6].
2.1.2
Comprehensive metrics
The comprehensive group includes metrics that account for both the cost and also the value of the
energy. Similar to previously described non-comprehensive group of metrics all of the comprehen-
sive metrics are shown in the Table 2.2. In the this group the following measures are presented:
sLCOE, rsLCOE, NV, COVE, NVOE, NVOC and LRVE.
In the previous group LCOE and LACE metrics were reviewed and due to their drawbacks, alone
these two metrics are incomplete. In the literature[5][6] it was found that system LCOE (sLCOE)
metric covers both of the mentioned non-comprehensive metric and even more factors, which is
illustrated in the Figure 2.1. System LCOE measures the cost of the electricity including integration
cost felt by the related system. The integration costs are dependent on uncertainty, variability and
locational - constraints of the technologies [6][12]. In order to calculate the sLCOE for a speciﬁc
VRE system extensive modeling and analysis is required and benchmark price.

2.1. METRICS REVIEW
The benchmark price can be deﬁned applying diﬀerent methods[4], and in the literature numerous
ways of calculating it is presented. According to [5],the benchmark price is the result of the division
of the total cost of a system without any VRE by the electricity demand of the system.In the other
source [12] it is deﬁned as the annual-average marginal price of electricity of the system.
In the literature [4] [6] is addressed that after a review of set of metrics none of them were capable
to account for complete overview of the system value and cost in order to determine economic
viability with high ﬁdelity[6]. As an answer to this the revised system LCOE (rsLCOE) metric has
been introduced. The rsLCOE determines economic viability with high ﬁdelity, based on the ratio
of cost to value of the system and a benchmark price.This metric is characterised by its complexity,
and the fact that it requires the deﬁnition of an accurate benchmark price.

It is highlighted in the literature [4][7] that these two metrics have their own equivalents that

(2.2)

Decomposition of the LRVE components are presented in the Figure 2.3. The LROE describes

values. In the literature [13] the examples included in development value are environmental studies,
certiﬁcation, and consenting expenditures. For infrastructure value the electric grid connection and
use construction costs are referred.

computation of the metric might be limited.

2.2
Innovations to improve the value of energy
In this section the overview of the innovations in the design of the wind turbines is described. Most
of the innovations that are included in the Table 2.4 were reviewed in IEA Wind TCP workshop[16]
and [14]. Overall the reviewed innovations that improve the system value also increase the cost
based LCOE metric. These indicate a relationship between the value based objectives and LCOE.
In the Table 2.4 most of the improvements are concentrated on the design decision taken in the
phase before constructing the wind farm. The other listed improvements includes design choices
that are not related to wind turbine itself, however they focus on a higher level, where more than
one wind farm is considered (e.g. increase transmission build, geographic diversity).
Introducing low wind speed turbines and turbines with lower speciﬁc power can result in pro-
ducing more power, contrasted with a high speciﬁc power turbine at similar power rating. It is
due to fact the that, in systems with high shares of energy coming from wind, high electricity
prices are correlated to low wind speeds. At the same time, it inﬂuences the LCOE by increasing
its value [16]. In terms of low wind speed turbines a decrease of the generated power(AEP) will
be observed, and in case of low speciﬁc power turbines the CAPEX will increase per each unit of
rated power, due to the correlation between CAPEX and rotor size [16].
Although, overplanting of the wind farm by adding more turbines increases the LCOE through
increased CAPEX per each additional turbine, the system value increase as well. The strategy of
overplanting improves the dispatchability and predictability of wind energy [14].
Another design choice that improves the value of the system are the sensing technologies. The in-
vestment in forecasting models and data sets result in increase of LCOE due to increase of CAPEX
and OPEX. However, the improved capabilities of forecast models will result in cut of imbalance
payments, which are dependent on the error in the forecast.

wind and other VRE technologies.

2.3.1
TOPFARM
TOPFARM is Python based optimization software for wind farm design and operational strategies
optimization for onshore and oﬀshore projects, developed at DTU Wind Energy. TOPFARM is a
framework that integrates diﬀerent models and metrics in order to reach a ﬁnal optimal solution.
Annual Energy Production metric, as well as loads, site suitability and control strategy models
are the aspects from performance side of the software framework. The rest of the models related
to the system design includes infrastructure for onshore and oﬀshore (foundation, cabling, support
structures, roads, etc), operations(reliability, maintenance), ﬁnancial and market models, hybrid
systems.[20][21]

TOPFARM architecture includes basic elements that are used in a problem formulation for the
optimization problem. The organization of all of the components is presented in the Figure 2.4.
The central object is called TopFarmProblem and it gathers all of the information from design vari-
ables, constraints set for the problem and a chosen driver or optimizer which is problem dependent.
The Cost Component provides information in relation to objective function (cost function) of the

of design variables (described as a state). The recorder provides the information regarding the
performance of the optimization throughout the iteration process, recording each step. Moreover,
the software contains the plotting component that visualize the state of the wind farm as it is
being optimized[22].

3.3. INPUTS TO THE MODEL/ APPROACH
As presented in the optimization scheme in the Figure 3.5 the objective functions are to maximize
the AEP and LROE, and minimize the LCOE and COVE. The objective functions of each of the
metrics are described in the section 3.2 (equations 3.1 - 3.7). The optimization was performed
with the following constraints: spacing constraints around each of the turbines, and boundary
constraints limiting the space in which the turbines can move during the optimization.
In this project two diﬀerent optimizers/drivers (hybrid optimization) were used one after an-
other. First for the initial layouts the EasyRandomSearchDriver and afterwards the EasyScipy-
OptimizeDriver using the SLSQP (Sequential Least Squares Programming) optimizer was used.
The input to the SLSQP optimizer was the output of the Random Search Driver(RS). The idea
behind using the RS before the SLSQP is the diﬀerence in the way that these optimizers work.
In principle, the RS choose one wind turbine at random moves it into random direction with a
random step, checks whether the turbine movement fulﬁll the constraint requirements and also
remember and use the information regarding the good move for other turbines [33]. The SLSQP
was used as the second optimizer to improve the output of the ﬁrst driver that ﬁnds a diﬀerent
solution each time the optimization is performed no matter the initial positions of the turbines.
In SLSQP the steps are generated by solving quadratic subproblems of the objective function and
linear approximation is replacing the constraint functions or value [34]. The SLSQP is used as a
second optimizer to ﬁnd the local optima.
The optimization was executed for 20 cases for 4 diﬀerent metrics using 2 diﬀerent drivers for 2
diﬀerent boundary constraints(circular and quadrangular). That resulted in 160 simulations in to-
tal. The result of the optimization is the value of the metric (cost) and the the optimal positions of
the turbines (state). The problem formulation for both pairs of the metrics of interest is presented
below:

To analyze the results the "cross calculation" for the pairs of metrics was performed. The cross
calculation is done by using the state (optimal wind turbine positions) of one metric of the pair to

layout of LCOE optimization the COVE objective value is calculated and afterwards, for COVE

the optimization is error-free. For that the AEP based pair (AEP and LCOE) and revenue based
pair (LROE and COVE) were tested. Afterwards the cross calculation for the AEP vs LROE and
LCOE vs COVE pairs for further analysis were performed .

3.3.4
Assumptions and limitations
In this section the assumptions regarding the study and limitations related to the TOPFARM
optimization tool is described:
• Simple layouts considered: The layouts used in the model are very simple, especially the
boundary and the spacing constraints. Both are not realistic. The boundary constraints are
much more complex in reality, where the the constraints are more complicated ﬁgures than
used in the model and also for the whole wind farm they consist of more than one boundary.
The spacing constraint applied in the model is only considering the rotor diameter so that
the blades of the turbines does not hit each other. The spacing constraints does not consider
the multiplicity of rotor diameter as in the reality. However, this is an intentional decision
in order to allow the wind turbines to move more freely.
• Only one site / price trajectory considered: The study is conducted for just one site
- Bovling site.
That means that the optimal designs might diﬀer for other geographical
locations where the wind and price rose are diﬀerent.
• Highly simpliﬁed cost model: In the study the DTU cost model based on empirical
data calibrated for 2017 e integrated is used. The cost model is simpliﬁed since the the
calculations of the components of the model is based on the general inputs listed in the
Table 3.1. Another assumption in the model is using the constant discount rate for the
whole duration of the project.
• Very simple cable model: The model to create the optimal network of the cables is simple
and not realistic. The solution of the model result in one section of the cable that connects
all the turbines, which is not the case in reality. Another unrealistic feature of the model is
the assumption for the cost of the cable per meter that is not dependant on the capacity of
the cable. The value is constant for all the network.
• Data: The binning of the wind an price data is done shorten the computation time. However,
it is a signiﬁcant simpliﬁcation compared to the price data for the whole period of 8760 hours
in year. For the calculations the value that is chosen is the center of the bin, which is a
assumption that is characterized with low accuracy. For the wind rose the study takes does
not take into account the seasonal wind directions but only the representative for the wind
for the whole year.
• Modelling errors: During the second part of the hybrid optimization, while using the
SLSQP optimizer for the smaller circular boundary mostly for COVE but for other metrics
as well, however less frequent the error in optimization occurred. The problem was solved by

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Analysis of the results
Addressing the research questions described in section 1.2 the main ﬁndings are presented and
discussed in this section.
Firstly, as presented in section 4.1 the optimal layout designs for a quadrangular boundary, which
is wider (y-axis wise) perpendicularly to the dominant wind/price directions, resulted in more
proﬁtable layouts in all of the investigated cases for all the metrics.
Secondly, the optimal layouts resulted in diﬀerent designs with a very similar objective function
values, which is sign of local optima. In wind farm layout optimization, local optima are often ob-
served - especially when the problem is not heavily constrained. It can also be a sign of a very ﬂat
objective function landscape. For example, a valley that is wide with many designs characterized
by the same value.
Thirdly, chosen metrics aﬀect the design of the wind farm diﬀerently. In the case of the pair AEP
and LROE the diﬀerences are much less noticeable as in the second pair of the LCOE and COVE
metrics. The diﬀerences in main trends of optimal layouts for each of the metrics and also between
the boundaries resulted from the fact that the metrics are subjected to diﬀerent components. The
diﬀerence between the ﬁrst and the second metrics from the pair for both of the pairs of the metrics
is the revenue component. Value based metrics with this additional component were subjected to
not only the wind rose as the conventional AEP and LCOE metrics, but also the the price rose.
The AEP and LROE subjected to AEP and revenue components respectively resulted in optimal
designs quite similar to each other with minor diﬀerences. The designs from AEP resulted to create
more aligned set of turbines than in LROE case, where the turbines are positions mostly at the
49
==PAGE:59==
5.1. ANALYSIS OF THE RESULTS
edges of the boundaries. For both metrics the turbines are positioned in wake-free corridors and
also maintaining a signiﬁcant distance between the turbines to avoid the wake from other turbines.
In terms of LCOE subjected to AEP and cost component, and COVE additionally subjected to
revenue component the diﬀerence between the optimal layouts resulted to be more signiﬁcant. The
LCOE objectives are highly inﬂuenced by the cable cost component, which locates the turbines
very close to each other to minimize this cost. The optimal designs for COVE resulted to be a
hybrid layouts between LCOE and LROE optimal designs. The revenue component that diﬀers
the LCOE and COVE have signiﬁcant impact on the optimal design and it is prioritized over the
cost component.
Fourthly, despite the fact that the COVE is characterized by the higher wake losses than the
AEP and LROE, and lower values of the LROE (Figure 4.15c) it deserved to be named the best
metric for the optimization of the wind farm layout design among the ones that were investigated.
Especially, in the future, where the share from wind energy and other VRE will be increasing
as stated in the literature [1] and [2]. The COVE metric considers all three components - AEP,
cost and revenue, and creates optimal layouts that accounts for both cost and value of the energy.
As mentioned before COVE perform worse in the domain of other metrics. However, due to its
comprehensiveness the resulted designs balances the value and cost of energy.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Future work
6.1
Conclusions
Due to constantly increasing share of the energy from the wind in energy systems, the value and
not only the cost of energy will be vital in determining the proﬁtability of the project. In this
thesis a study of a two new metrics that account for the value of the energy (LROE and COVE)
is conducted in order to investigate the possibility of designing a wind farm layout of the new
generation. Other two well known metrics - AEP and LCOE were compared with LROE and
COVE, respectively to establish the performance of the new metrics. The study is performed by
optimizing the wind turbine layouts using the four previously mentioned metrics as the objectives.
Layout optimizations were performed for the Bovling site located at the west coast of Jutland in
Denmark. The historical wind data was binned to achieve the frequency distribution of the site, in
a form of wind and price roses. The optimization was conducted using DTU software - TOPFARM
by adding additional functions of the new metrics.
According to the results, the choice of the diﬀerent optimization metrics resulted in the diﬀerent
trends in the optimal layout designs. For the new metrics that account for the value of the energy
(LROE and COVE) - especially the COVE which consider also the cost of the energy, the observed
diﬀerence in the design will be very crucial when the shares of the energy in the system for the
wind energy will grow even more in the future. Since the COVE metric accounts for both value
and the cost of the energy, which is not the case in the LROE, it appears to be the best metric as
a base for the design of the next generation wind farm layouts.

6.2. FUTURE WORK
6.2
Future work
The simpliﬁcation that results from basing the calculations on the center of the bin introduce a
signiﬁcant inaccuracy of the calculations in the model. Future research could take that into account.
It would be interesting to see an optimization of a real case scenario, where the boundary is very
complex, dependent on the unavailable ares e.g. military areas, ship routes. Another thing from
the real life scenario would be to include in the model a new object that would appear in the
optimization layout plots and would act as substation. With that object the output from the
model, as a form of network of the cables would be optimized eﬃciently.
An interesting study would be to see how does the price of the cable cost per meter inﬂuence the
optimal layout design for the LCOE and COVE objectives. The usage of diﬀerent, more realistic
cable model would be beneﬁcial for the future research.
