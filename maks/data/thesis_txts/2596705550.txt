Abstract
More signiﬁcant rainfall events caused by Climate change and rapidly changed terrain surface by
urbanization have required more design and adjustment to urban drainage systems. Multiple runs
of simulations in a high-resolution model, for example, MIKE URBAN, are always needed to opti-
mize a drainage system, to ﬁnd the best adaption measures to fulﬁll environmental criteria including
Combined Sewer-system Overﬂow (CSO). However, the simulation speed of MIKE URBAN Long
Term Simulation (LTS) is usually slow, making it unacceptable to simulate enough times to do the
optimization.
For the ﬁrst time, this thesis proposed a method of building a conceptual model in Python script,
which can extracts parameters directly from physical characteristics to fast simulate ﬂow and CSO
at crucial locations of a drainage system. This conceptual model uses Storage-Outﬂow (S-Q) frame-
work to describe simpliﬁed network elements of a drainage system. The model calculates runoff
value into storage value, and then calculates the outﬂow based on the S-Q relationships.
Comparing with MIKE URBAN LTS, the simulation speed is improved by more than 200 times.
The quality of the conceptual model has been proved acceptable, with all ﬂow NSE scores larger
than 0.80, and errors of CSO volume less than 20%. With further model conﬁguration, the errors
of CSO simulation results decreased to 10%. Furthermore, the setup of the conceptual model has
shown a potential ability to be improved, and better estimation of S-Q relationships for the concep-
tual model is suggested.
This thesis concludes that the conceptual model is fast and with good quality, and it is reasonable to
set a conceptual model through the method that is proposed. When considering future applications,
we still need to make further adjustments and improvements to the conceptual model.

I
Introduction
Climate change has become one of the most server challenges globally and locally both nowadays
and will be in the future with more extreme events (Murray and Ebi, 2012; Lavell et al., 2012;
Hirabayashi et al., 2013). At the same time, urbanization is still happening, making rapid change to
the urban terrain (Fuller and Gaston, 2009; Cutter et al., 2016). Those two factors highly increase
the demand for designing new or adjust old drainage systems and climate adaption strategies to re-
duce the damage of extreme events (Zevenbergen et al., 2008). One of the most critical criteria for
creating a drainage system is the Combined Sewersystem Overﬂow (CSO) volume and frequency
(Zevenbergen et al., 2008). To optimize the drainage system considering the CSO regulations, eco-
nomic efﬁciency and uncertainties (Foster et al., 2011; Gersonius et al., 2013), a critical question
arises as
How to design a long-term eco-efﬁcient drainage system that fulﬁlls environmental regulations?
Since a couple of years ago, multiple models are introduced and used to simulate water ﬂows
and CSO in drainage systems. Among them, distributed physically-based models (DPM) are often
used in hydraulic modeling(Feyen et al., 2000; Harremo¨es and Madsen, 1999). It proves that it
is reasonable to use physical characteristics to build a model. Many models and software can be
categorized as DPM, such as the MIKE URBAN (MU) software.
MU is a software that models water level, water transportation, water discharge in drainage and
collection systems. Hydraulic mathematical models have been set in MU, and users can operate the
GIS-like interface of MU to run different simulations by changing parameters that are neededDHI
(2017). With those advantages, MU is widely used to design and optimize the drainage system.
To fulﬁll CSO regulations, optimizing the drainage system needs multiple runs of simulations to
test different scenarios of drainage structure settings (e.g., different basin volume, pipe diameter)
(L¨owe et al., 2017). However, the drawback of using MU in hydraulic modeling simulation is the
slow calculation speed. The hydrodynamic method, which MU software uses, calculates details
6
==PAGE:11==
of water level and ﬂow of every pipe at each time step. It makes calculation time up to days or
even weeks for a single run, especially for long term simulations (LTS) because of long model time
series(DHI, 2017).
It is often not necessary to simulate every structure in a drainage system. Instead, choosing speciﬁc
important points to simulate the input, output, and CSO behaviors at those points can be sufﬁ-
cient(Vanrolleghem et al., 2009). In this case, the Conceptual Model (CM) that reduces the details
in the hydrodynamic model (such as MU) is introduced to shorter the simulation time. Thus, the
critical question is modiﬁed as
How to build a fast-running model, to model water ﬂow and CSO at crucial points of a drainage
system?
Rational methods are often used to model how much runoff can be produced during one rain-
fall event, time-area method is also available(Schaake et al., 1967; Thompson, 2006; Wang and
Wang, 2018). Another widely used rainfall-runoff simulation conceptual model is the Model for
Urban Stormwater Improvement Conceptualization (MUSIC). MUSIC can calculate rainfall into
the runoff with a broad range of different catchment areas. It can also give the results of runoff qual-
ity, making it easy to have a general view of different strategies (Montaseri et al., 2015). However,
those methods are hard to simulate the CSO. An example of a more complex conceptual model
to model ﬂow and CSO is CITY DRAIN. It separates the extensive drainage system into several
sub-systems and models them separately using different equations and methods(Achleitner et al.,
2007). CITY DRAIN is developed in Matlab/Simulinkq environment, meaning it is more ﬂexible
to change based on different situations. It gives the idea that using code script to set a model is
reasonable.
The most applied conceptual model is based on Nash cascade of linear reservoir by assuming water
ﬂows in a continuous routing through a series of linear reservoirs(Te Chow, 2010). KOSIM is a con-
ceptual hydrological model software that represents catchments into linear reservoir(Bendel et al.,
2013). KOSIM uses rational methods to generate rainfall data into the input of those reservoirs,
7
==PAGE:12==
and calculate reservoir output using ordinary differential equations (ODE) of storage changes (S)
and outﬂows (Q), which is described as S-Q relationship method(Vanrolleghem et al., 2009; Ben-
del et al., 2013; Solvi et al., 2005). With the ability of fast calculating the runoff from catchments,
KOSIM is often combined with other integrated water management software, for example, WEST,
to simulate the CSO volume and quality (Solvi et al., 2005).
A possible faster way of setting a conceptual model under linear reservoir assumption is directly
using code script to calculate ﬂow results from linear reservoir storage volume. A simpliﬁed con-
ceptual model has been made by Morten Borup and Cecilie Thrysøe, which can simulate water
outﬂow from runoff data using a linear reservoir approach (Thrysøe et al., 2019). With the help of
the simpliﬁed model, this project proposes a method to build a conceptual model based on physical
structure parameters to fast simulate the ﬂow and CSO from rainfall data in Python script.
This method is ﬁrst ever introduced. Process of building a conceptual model is often case-sensitive(Wolfs
et al., 2013), this project uses a case study catchment to test the quality, sensitivity, and ﬂexibility
of the conceptual model.
The objective of this project is summarized as:
• Create a conceptual modeling framework for urban drainage systems using the S-Q function
principle.
• Identify the conceptual model parameters (S-Q speciﬁcations) from physical catchment and pipe
network characteristics so that it can be used without calibration against ﬂow data or HiFi model
results.
• Evaluate if the model identiﬁcation is sensitive to catchment speciﬁcs.
• Make potential conﬁguration and improvement of the model.

1
Conceptual Model
Hydrological conceptual models are usually deﬁned as a simpliﬁed real hydrology process in the
drainage system, on surfaces or in natural bodies, only to include conceptions or critical elements of
original model(Kavetski and Clark, 2010). In general, conceptual models are precipitation-runoff-
ﬂow models. It means that the conceptual model uses precipitation data as input to calculate runoff
and ﬂow in certain drainage systems(Kavetski et al., 2006).
The hydrodynamic models, such as MIKE URBAN, contain the hydrology details of every pipes,
basins, and weirs. On the other hand, conceptual models only select essential points of the system
and only focus on parts of hydrology results. It means that other than the critical points, all other
structures are either simpliﬁed to a whole or are disregarded(Kavetski and Clark, 2010). Figure 1
gives an example of building a hydrodynamic model (MU) and design a conceptual model for the
same catchment area.
9
==PAGE:14==
Figure 1: Excample of hydrodynamic model and conceptual model for same catchment.
Multiple methods can be used to calibrate the conceptual model and estimate the model quality,
while a conceptual model that based on physical structure parameters is potentially none cali-
brated(Kavetski et al., 2006; Jin et al., 2010). In this case, the choice of conceptual model parame-
ters is often an important part(Jin et al., 2010).

Linear Reservoir
The linear reservoir method models a pipe in a drainage system as a “black-box” model, meaning
the water transported in the pipe is described by a transfer function. This function assumes a linear
relationship between the storage volume of the pipe and the outﬂow. It focuses only on the ﬂow
calculation(Te Chow, 2010), any other physical processes, such as the effects of water level and
pressure, are not directly considered. The concept of a linear reservoir is shown in Figure 2.

tion of it uses a storage constant K to describe the relationship between storage volume and outﬂow,
as shown in Equation 2. The storage constant K is also called the retention constant, meaning how
long time water will last in one reservoir.

Nash Cascade of Linear Reservoir
The Nash cascade of linear reservoir is conceptually routing the ﬂow through a series of linear
reservoirs ??, as shown in Figure 3. It models a pipe into multiple numbers of reservoir, and
assumes those reservoirs are linked one by one. The numbers of reservoirs are usually deﬁned as N
to identify how much reservoirs are used in the method. By linking the Equation 2) of all reservoirs,
the outﬂow of the pipe (modeled by N reservoirs) can be calculated by the storage value of it.
11
==PAGE:16==
Figure 3: Concept of Nash Cascade of linear reservoir (left) with unit hydrograph of each reser-
voir(right).
In the Nash cascade, the inﬂow of the downstream reservoir is the outﬂow of the previous one. It
gives a later effect of ﬂows further downstream, as shown in the unit hydrograph in the right part
of Figure 3. The downstream reservoir has the outﬂow peaks coming later, representing the longer
time water stays in upstream reservoirs. The unit hydrograph is then possible to be used to calculate
outﬂows of the reservoirs.
The key method of modeling water ﬂow in a pipe using Nash cascade of linear reservoir is to
ﬁnd the relationship between the outﬂow value of the last reservoir Q and the storage value S in the
pipe. The outﬂow from the N-th (the last) reservoir can be used as the ﬁnal outﬂow from the pipe,
while the total storage value would be the summarized volume of all reservoirs, as shown below:
• Q = q of the last reservoir (qn)
• S = V1 + V2 + ... + Vn
12
==PAGE:17==
This theory ﬁrst splits the pipe to several linear reservoirs to generate outﬂow values, and then
combines those reservoirs back to a big, long linear reservoir, as shown in Figure 4. With known
input ﬂow, the relationship between S and Q can then be determined. The initial parameters or
those reservoirs, K and N, can be determined from the physical properties of the pipe.

By deﬁning corresponding events criteria, long term simulation (LTS) can be done by using the

for 240 minutes. Simulation and result saving time steps were set as 1 minute. LTS results were
used to understand CSO ﬂows during a long period.

1
Catchment and Network Characteristics Identiﬁcation
This section describes the ﬁrst step of the project method. The overall process is to identify the
characteristics of case study catchment and networks in order to derive a conceptual model using
those characterizations. The main process is:
• Subdivide catchments into compartments based on CSO locations
• Identify the transport pipe for each compartment
• Identify the CSO elements at each CSO location
The outcome of this section is making a simpliﬁed network model with runoff direction, transport
pipe and CSO elements. Figure 8 presents the main process of this section.

as an example.

1.1
Subdivide Catchments into Compartments
The CSO locations were ﬁrstly identiﬁed from MU in this project. All weirs were found and
analyzed in the MU model. Those weirs that have overﬂows out of the system were count as CSO
locations. Other weirs were disregarded. The CSO location identiﬁed in Bellinge catchments are
shown in Figure 9.

Conceptual Model Parameters Deﬁnition
After having built the simpliﬁed network for CM, this section described the method of determining
parameters and inputs of the CM. The principle of parameter deﬁnition was to collect parameters
of each SM and to generate S-Q function for it. The S-Q function for basin CSO was described
between storage volume (S) and CSO from weir (Qw). Three kinds of S-Q functions were described
as:
• Transport pipe S-Q function
• Basin CSO S-Q function
• Basin outﬂow pipe S-Q function
The outcome of this section was a ﬁnalized CM with all S-Q functions, and runoff results as model
input.
2.1

The S-Q function for pipes was described as the relationship between storage volume (S) and
outﬂow (Q). The S-Q relationship for the pipe was calculated based on the theory of Nash Cascade
of Linear Reservoir. This method assumed the pipe as a cascade of linear reservoir. The linear
reservoir assumption assumed a linear relation between storage (S) and outﬂow (Q), which is given

Runoff Generation
Water collected in each compartment was modeled as inﬂow to transport pipe in each compartment.
Runoff value was generated from Runoff Simulation results from MU. The MU runoff results gave
the runoff value of each catchment. Compartment runoff was calculated by aggregating the runoff
values of all catchments in that compartment.
A Python script ”ReadCRF.py” was used to generate the compartment runoff values. The script
read catchment IDs of each compartment and found the runoff value from the MU Runoff Simula-
tion result ﬁle. The matched runoff values were aggregated based on the time step to generate the
total runoff of that compartment. The Python script was given in PythonCode 1. The runoff ﬁle
generated from the MU Runoff Simulation ﬁle was then cleaned to a former format with 1 minute
time step. The Python script of cleaning runoff data was given in PythonCode 2.
In this project, the runoff was generated from MU software. It was also possible to calculate
the runoff directly from rainfall data. A time-area method could be included in CM in order to use
catchment information to calculate runoff. The catchment information with catchment areas and
imperiousness was able to be transferred into compartment parameters. In this way, the conceptual

The changes of pipe length in CM inﬂuenced both pipe length parameter and pipe slope param-

decrease in the slope. In this case, the changes of pipe length inﬂuence both storage constant value
k in the S-Q function of pipes and pipe outﬂow Q in the S-Q function. With this limitation, it could
be hard to identify if the changes of conceptual model results come from the change of k or the
change of outﬂow value.
As for MU, the changes in pipe length also widely inﬂuenced the whole structure of the drainage
system. Locations of physical structures in MU would be ”moved” due to the different distances
of the pipes. Flows traveled in different lengths of pipes would act differently. The slope of pipes
would also be changed, as MU also use pipe length and node level to calculate the slope.
Considering the two main limitations in CM and MU, the changes in pipe length would not make
much sense, without further improvement of the conceptual model.
Scenario 7 and 8 changed the pipe diameters. Decreasing pipe diameter was not tested, as decreas-
ing diameter would shorter the total volume of the drainage system of MU. The signiﬁcant change
could largely inﬂuence the simulation of the MU model. The change of diameter would change the
total storage volume of the system, making a big inﬂuence on both CM and MU models. Similar to
pipe length, only transport pipes were changed in MU, while other branch pipes remained the same.
Scenario 9, 10 had different pipe slopes, which increasing slope by 20% and decrease slope by
50%. It is unrealistic to change pipe slope in MU as MU use pipe length and upstream/downstream
levels to calculate the slope, thus only slope values in CM were changed in those two scenarios.

1
Method: Conceptual Model Setup
One of the key methods of this project is to set up a conceptual model based on physical character-
istics. We have successfully found a way to simplify catchment and deﬁne transport pipe and CSO
elements for the conceptual model.
1.1
Subdivision of Catchments to Compartments
Using CSO locations to divide catchments is proved to be reasonable and efﬁcient. The com-
partments are deﬁned correctly, with runoff volume received in compartments are matched with
compartment outﬂow, as shown in Table 12.
To the same catchment, different deﬁnitions of the compartment are possible by different users.
Considering the similar identiﬁcation of critical CSO structures, it is likely that the division of
compartments could be similar by a different person if all CSO elements are interested. However,
the users of this CM could be interested in speciﬁc CSO locations. In this case, decrease the com-
partment numbers to combine all catchments upstream of the speciﬁc CSO locations to a single
compartment may be the choice, and it is still reasonable.
In theory, the simulation results of CSO volume with fewer compartments can still be accurate
comparing to the MIKE URBAN result, as upstream water ﬂows are all aggregated in a big com-
partment. However, longer transport time in the transport pipe of the big compartment may in-
ﬂuence the ﬂow time series curve, as shown in Figure 33. The CM model ﬂow can be changed
by larger k, affecting the compartment outﬂow and basin outﬂow results. Different compartment
designs can be tested in the future to understand the inﬂuence of compartment numbers.
1.2
Transport Pipe and CSO Elements Identiﬁcation
It has been shown that transport pipes and CSO elements are successfully identiﬁed in this project.
Once the CSO locations have been decided, the transport pipe is usually decided based on the
upstream pipes in the drainage system. However, in reality, it is possible that we want to know
73
==PAGE:78==
where to design and install CSO structures without knowing CSO locations or existing CSO el-
ements. In this case, the identiﬁcation of transport pipe and CSO elements can be a combined
method, which needs to be processed at the same time. Multiple CSO elements can be modeled
and tested in different locations of the drainage system. The transport pipe, in this case, would be
deﬁned based on those CSO element locations. The results can be compared to decide where and
how to install CSO elements.
Sometimes the transport pipes can be hard to identify, as water ﬂows have multiple paths. In
this case, large pipes can be chosen as a transport pipe. This method is useful when the back trace
tool in MIKE URBAN is not able to identify a single path of the main water ﬂow. At the same time,
CSO elements can also be used to identify transport pipes. The key CSO elements should locate on
the transport pipe.
However, it may be a problem if the CSO element has no physical basin installed. This project
models upstream pipes into basins if there is no physical basin, as presented in Table 1. It has
shown that this method can simulate CSO from weirs without basin installed, but it could also in-
troduces more uncertainty. Pipe parameters would change not only pipes but also the basins that
are modeled by pipes. For example, pipe diameter changes ﬂow in pipes, and the basin volume is
also changed at the same time.
With changed pipe parameters, the CSO results are inﬂuenced by both basin volume and pipe
characteristics. It makes the CSO simulation at those locations unmatched with MIKE URBAN
model results under different pipe parameter scenarios. It is possible to separate those pipes apart
from transport pipes; however, it is hard to identify correct basin storage volume from pipe parame-
ters. It is difﬁcult to deﬁne when a pipe is ”full”, or how many pipes need to be regarded as ”basin”.
The method of modeling CSO without a basin needs to be further improved. One possible option is
to model the node that installs a weir as a basin. However, the storage volume of a node is usually
minimal; thus, this method requires a very precise calculation of inﬂow and outﬂow values of the
node.

Method: Key Elements in S-Q Function
One of the critical methods of this project is to use S-Q functions to describe transport pipes and
basins. Parameters that deﬁne transport pipe, CSO elements, and compartment affect the perfor-
mance of conceptual model by the deﬁnition of S-Q function. Parameter values have essential
effects on the two critical elements in S-Q function: storage constant k and full running pipe ﬂow
Qv.
2.1
Estimate Storage Constant k
One of the critical elements in the S-Q function is the storage constant k. With knowing the storage
value in the transport pipe or basin, its outﬂow can be calculated based on the k value. This method
generates the same among of outﬂow in the conceptual model and MIKE URBAN, however with
different ﬂow curves. It may be because the storage constant k in S-Q function may be underesti-
mated.
S-Q functions for pipes in the conceptual model use storage value to calculate outﬂow value, in-
stead of using water levels in pipes in MIKE URBAN. MU is a high-resolution model and simulate
water levels and ﬂows in every pipe. It means that for a downstream pipe, ﬂows from all upstream
pipes are aggregated. With different traveling time, peaks of those ﬂows arrive at different time and
they are also aggregated. It is the reason why the ﬂow in MU is ﬂatter in downstream. Furthermore,
the water peaks will decrease when ﬂow downstream because of energy loss. It is clearly shown in
Figure 27 that downstream ﬂow is ﬂatter than upstream in MIKE URBAN model results.
On the other hand, the conceptual model simulates the transport pipe as a single pipe. Once there
is water in the transport pipe, the conceptual model would generate outﬂow values based on the
storage value, regardless of water traveling through pipe links. It is the possible reason for the early
and high ﬂow peaks in the conceptual model, while the MIKE URBAN model has smooth ﬂows
with relevant low peak values.
A delay conﬁguration to SQ functions, as shown in Figure 25, is not successful. The outﬂows
75
==PAGE:80==
at small storage stages are decreased to simulate the fact that water outﬂow is smaller than it is
calculated. This method increase the k value at small storage stage, while fails to change the k in
large storage stage.
Longer pipe length shows a positive inﬂuence to ﬂow simulation as it increases k value in the
conceptual model, as shown in Table 17. The pipe slope parameter also has a similar effect. With
a larger k value, the ﬂow curve in the conceptual model becomes more smooth and has a smaller
difference with MU, as shown in Figure 38. The smoother ﬂow curve under larger k value improves
conceptual model quality. As shown in Table 20, longer pipe, which means larger k, has a larger
NSE of transport pipe outﬂow and basin outﬂow. The Length + 100% scenario has the largest NSE
value of all scenarios.

outﬂow pipe. Shown in Figure 40, pipe roughness and pipe diameter both change the full running

much the full running ﬂow are changed.
Furthermore, according to Figure 40, the smaller the full running ﬂow is, the more sensitive it
is to ﬂow and CSO simulation. It is because a smaller full running ﬂow is more comfortable to

On the other hand, pipe length is used to calculate both storage constant k and full running ﬂow,
while other parameters only change one. Pipe length changes full running ﬂow because the pipe
slope is calculated from length. The link of the two parameters, length and slope, maybe the reason
why the conceptual model and MIKE URBAN model give different results. Longer pipes cause a
more accurate ﬂow curve in transport pipe outﬂow. However, the basin outﬂow plays a bigger role
in affecting the conceptual model.
Unlike the conceptual model, the MIKE URBAN model does not sensitive to full running ﬂow
values. MIKE URBAN model is mainly controlled by water levels in pipes, while the ﬂow value
is the result of the water level. It is the reason why all parameters that change the full running ﬂow
have a large effect in the conceptual model, but unseen in MU. It also explains the excellent CM
model quality in Compartment A and C that they have the same full running ﬂow values in the
conceptual model and MIKE URBAN model, as the values are regulated.
When considering the sensitivity of parameters in the conceptual model, the parameter value of
CM should be deﬁned wisely, especially for the parameters that deﬁne the full running pipe ﬂow of
basin outﬂow pipes.

The method of deﬁning the length of transport pipe and basin outﬂow pipe can primarily affect

can be improved to only calculate storage constant k, instead of calculating both of k and full run-
ning ﬂow. The full running ﬂow value may only be calculated from roughness, diameter, and slope,
as shown in the Manning equation. It requires that the pipe slope is independent from pipe length.
However, the MIKE URBAN database uses length to calculate the slope. It may be wise to deﬁne
the slope of pipes from a real database, based on designed value or measured data.
3
Conceptual Model Quality
The key aim of this project is to fast-simulating CSO at important locations of a drainage system.
It can be concluded that the conceptual model has good quality to fast simulate CSO volume,
especially after the correct conﬁguration.
3.1
Simulation Speed
The conceptual model signiﬁcantly increases the simulation speed. Comparing to more than 40
hours of simulation time in MIKE URBAN, the conceptual model only need 5 to 10 minutes to
both generate the parameter ﬁle and run the main script and get results. The calculation speed is
improved by more than 200 times in the conceptual model.
Furthermore, the MIKE URBAN model may need an even longer time. Shown in Figure 27,
the ﬂow simulation result does not include all ﬂow values that we need; thus, more time is needed
to simulate each event. In this case, increasing the period of each event in the Job List ﬁle may
become necessary to have the correct ﬂow simulation results. The longest basin drain time may be
used to make sure there is no water remain in basins. The simulation time in MIKE URBAN may
be further increased.
Although we may need a certain time to do the data collection to identify parameter values, the
conceptual model is still in advantage in speed as we may need more time to build a MIKE UR-
BAN model from real catchment data. Even considering an existing MIKE URBAN model, the
79
==PAGE:84==
parameter identiﬁcation process still needs less time than an LTS simulation of MIKE URBAN.
3.2
CSO Simulation
The CSO volumes are correctly identiﬁed in most key CSO locations, with a small difference com-
pared to MIKE URBAN results.
Basin volume is the critical element of the conceptual model to simulate CSO correctly. It is
thus essential to set the basin volume as close as to the value in MIKE URBAN or reality. In this
case, the conﬁguration of upstream storage volume needs to be considered in the conceptual model,
as it adjusts basin volume and improves the quality of CSO simulation, shown in Table 18.
3.3
Flow Simulation
Flows in and out of basins, especially the basin outﬂow, are also proved to be the critical elements
of CSO simulation in the conceptual model. The NSE scores of ﬂows have shown that the overall
model quality of conceptual model ﬂow simulation is acceptable, with all NSE scores higher than
0.8. The ﬂow simulation quality can be further improved considering a better calculation of k,
including longer water traveling time. However, this potential improvement needs to be further
tested and evaluated.
4
Future Improvement and Application
4.1
Include Runoff Simulation in Conceptual Model
This project does not include the runoff simulation process but directly uses the runoff simulation
results from MIKE URBAN. The runoff generation method is possible to be included in the concep-
tual model with multiple methods. With the information on the catchment area, the compartment
can be modeled with more parameters to simulate runoff from rainfall. Runoffs in compartments
can be calculated by the time-area method, with the inputs of rainfall data and catchment informa-
tion. Other methods, like rational methods, can also be used to simulate runoff (Wang and Wang,
2018).
80
==PAGE:85==
4.2
Include Upstream Pressure in Pipe
Full running ﬂow values limit the maximum value of ﬂows in the conceptual model. However, it
should be considered that with higher pressure from upstream water, the outﬂow value of a pipe
can be larger than the full running ﬂow. It may happen when the pipe is already full running, while
the upstream pipes hold more water when the water level in the system keeps rising. In order to
include the upstream pressure in the conceptual model, it is possible to increase the ﬂow value after
reaching the full running stage by a factor. However, the inﬂuence of this method needs further
investigation.
4.3
Urban Flood Simulation Adjustment
This project identiﬁes CSO locations as the key location in the conceptual model. It is also possible
to identify the splitting node as a key location in order to simulate urban ﬂood from the splitting
nodes.
The compartment and transport pipe identiﬁcation can still be the same as the method in this project.
The splitting node can be modeled as a ”basin”. ”CSO” of the ”basin” will be the splitting water
ﬂow of the node when water volume in the node reaches the full storage volume. Instead of using
the weir discharge equation, the splitting ﬂow can be simply modeled as the difference between
inﬂow and outﬂow of the node. In this way, the splitting water from nodes can be calculated.
It is easy to adjust the conceptual model in this project to urban ﬂood simulation by changing
the deﬁnition of S-Q functions. However, modeling node as a ”basin” will meet the problem that
the storage volume of a node is minimal. The inﬂow and outﬂow value of the node needs to be
modeled correctly. Otherwise, the splitting water ﬂow can be wrongly calculated.
4.4
Application in Reality
In this project, the physical characteristics are extracted from the MIKE URBAN database. Since
the MIKE URBAN model is built based on a real database, it is also possible to identify catchment
and network characteristics directly from real drainage system data, without the help of MIKE UR-
81
==PAGE:86==
BAN. Furthermore, using the MIKE URBAN database may have the disadvantage that the slope is
linked to pipe length. Using real data may not have this problem.
In addition, instead of comparing to the MIKE URBAN model, it will be useful to compare the con-
ceptual model results with measured data when the conceptual model is built from a real database.
It will be interesting to evaluate the conceptual model quality comparing to the real data, making it
possible to be adjusted and applied in real situations.
At last, with the availability of an optimization package in Python, the conceptual model has the
potential to automatically give the optimized solution of drainage system design under different
runoff scenarios. With the advantage of fast simulation speed, this conceptual model can run hun-
dreds of simulations a day. Parameters can be set as variables in Python script, and use the least
CSO volume as an objective function. For each set of parameters, the conceptual model can give
a result of CSO volume until the smallest CSO volume is found. However, more accurate model
results may need to make sure the optimization makes sense.

Conclusion
This project mainly aims to answer the critical question:
How to build a fast-running model, to model water ﬂow and CSO at crucial points of a drainage
system?
In order to solve the question, in this thesis, we demonstrate a method to build a conceptual model
from physical characteristics to fast simulate ﬂow and CSO at critical locations in a drainage sys-
tem. The quality of the conceptual model is evaluated by comparing to MIKE URBAN LTS sim-
ulations results of the same case study catchment. The sensitivity of conceptual model parameters
is analyzed to evaluate the model performance with different physical characteristics.
The conceptual model increases simulation speed by 200 times, and the overall quality of the
conceptual model is acceptable, with all ﬂow NSE scores higher than 0.8 and the difference of
CSO volume smaller than 20%. The conﬁguration of basin volume improves the performance of
the conceptual model. The deﬁnition of pipe parameters, such as pipe length and slope, can be
adjusted to further improved the conceptual model, but more evaluations are needed. At last, po-
tential methods to apply the model in the real-world are suggested, and advanced model setups are
proposed.
The main conclusions of this project are:
• It is possible to deﬁne a conceptual model using the S-Q function framework. When simulate
CSO at key locations in a drainage system, three S-Q functions are needed for the location to
describe the inﬂow, outﬂow, and CSO, respectively.
• It is reasonable to generate parameter values in S-Q functions from physical characteristics. In
this way, no calibration against ﬂow data is needed.
• Additional data transformation is needed in the parameter generation process. Nash cascade of
linear reservoir can be used to estimate storage constant k in the S-Q function.
83
==PAGE:88==
• Conceptual model simulation speed is much faster than the MIKE URBAN model, increasing by
more than 200 times.
• Conceptual model simulates CSO volume in outstanding quality with additional basin volume
conﬁguration. CSO event simulation quality is also acceptable, but lower than CSO volume.
• CSO simulation in the conceptual model is mostly inﬂuenced by basin outﬂow, especially by the
full running pipe ﬂow value.
• Transport pipe ﬂow simulation is mostly inﬂuenced by storage constant k. The k value is mostly
deﬁned by pipe length and slope.
• The estimation process of storage constant k needs to be improved to get better performance of
ﬂow simulation.
• The most important process of building the conceptual model is correctly identifying the full
running ﬂow value of basin outﬂow pipe, as it largely inﬂuences the conceptual model results of
CSO.
• Method of deﬁne pipe length and pipe slope parameters may need to change in order to decrease
the effect from full running pipe ﬂow and increase the simulation performance of the ﬂow curve.
