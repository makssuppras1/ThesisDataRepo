Abstract
The rising threat of antimicrobial resistance necessitates a comprehensive exploration of
resistome dynamics within the context of Atlantic salmon sourced from European Union
(EU) countries. As part of the CIRCLES initiative, aimed at enhancing EU food system
sustainability through microbiome-centered strategies, this thesis work aims to characterize
antimicrobial resistance(AMR) in Atlantic salmon collected from Norway and Ireland across
diﬀerent salmon tissues and developmental stages, encompassing both farmed and wild spec-
imens from observation and intervention phases. More than 500 samples from salmon have
been shotgun metagenomics sequenced. Sequencing techniques were employed to decipher
the resistome evolution and assess the impact of probiotic interventions on AMR gene dy-
namics and composition. The metagenomics sequence data were analyzed to identify diver-
sity, richness, relative abundance of resistome in Atlantic salmon. Data analysis targeted
speciﬁc sample categories aﬀected by probiotic interventions, aiming to derive guidelines
for sustainable antibiotic reduction in salmon farming. The abundant antibiotic resistant
classes found in the Atlantic salmon samples were β-Lactam, Tetracycline, Streptogramin
A, Lincosinamide, Aminoglycosides, Macrolides, Sulfonamides, Fosfomycin. These antibiotic
resitance genes Isa(B), blaCME, blaOXA-493, vga(E), blaOXA-427, blaOXA-448, blaOXA-
85, fosA3, and tet44 were consistently present across the samples from both Ireland and
Norway. The results in general showed that fertilized egg have diﬀerent resistome composi-
tions compared to the other developmental stages. Similar patterns of abundance for ARGs
were found among samples from aquaculture feed and ﬁsh developmental stages, suggest-
ing that AMR genes could be from the feed which is provided to the salmon during their
growth. The resistome proﬁle during the intervention phase showed speciﬁc changes in the
digesta indicating potential interactions between the introduced probiotic and the microbial
community. However there is not much of signiﬁcant changes induced by the intervention
phase on the resistome dynamics. This work suggests that aquaculture feed could be a target
source for reducing AMR in aquaculture. The relationship between probiotic use and AMR
in aquaculture is an area of active research, and speciﬁc outcomes vary based on the type
of probiotics used, aquaculture practices, and local conditions. The transfer of antibiotic
resistance is a complex process inﬂuenced by various factors. Responsible and well-informed
use of probiotics, along with a comprehensive understanding of the genetic characteristics of
the introduced strains, can help minimize the risks associated with resistance gene transfer.
Sustainable aquaculture practices, responsible use of antimicrobials, and ongoing research
into regular monitoring and adherence to guidelines are critical in ensuring the sustainable
and safe use of probiotics in aquaculture. The insights garnered herein serve as a foundation
for developing targeted approaches aimed at curbing antibiotic resistance in salmon farming
practices, thereby contributing to the overarching goal of fostering sustainable food systems
within the EU.

Introduction
Aquaculture encompasses the production of a wide range of aquatic organisms, including
ﬁsh, molluscs, crustaceans and aquatic plants. While the products of aquaculture are di-
verse, ﬁsh production dominates the industry, accounting for a signiﬁcant portion of global
production and value. According to the Food and Agriculture Organization(FAO) of the
United Nations, ﬁsh production constitutes 47.7% of the global aquaculture production and
contributes to 56% of the total value [1].
Aquaculture is one of the fastest growing food production sectors in the world and is an
important contributor to global food supply and economic growth.
In fact, aquaculture
contributes to several of the UN Sustainable Development Goals(SDGs) for 2030, including
goal two, Zero Hunger[2]. In recent decades, increased demand for food along with depleted
wild ﬁsh stocks and intensiﬁcation of husbandry techniques made aquaculture the fastest
growing food production sector. Globally, total aquaculture production accounts for around
50% of overall seafood supply. In 2020, global aquaculture production reached a record 122.6
million tonnes, with a total value of USD 281.5 billion [3].
Global salmon production, including both farmed and wild, has doubled from 1.89 mil-
lion metric tons (mt) in 2000 to 3.8 million mt in 2020, where virtually all the growth is
due to farmed salmon. Europe is one of the major producers of Atlantic salmon world wide
[4]. The European salmon aquaculture sector was valued in 687 million euros in 2018. The
main producer countries of Atlantic salmon in the Europe are Norway and Ireland. In the
aquaculture sector, Ireland ranks 5th in value and 7th in volume in the EU, representing,
with its value of production of around EUR 36 million, 4% of total EU production [5]. Main
producer is Norway accounting for more than 50% share of the production [6].The main
importer of salmon is USA, and 6 of the top 10 importers are part of the EU [6]. Europe is
the highest consumer of salmon with an estimated consumption for 2017 of 1,058,000 Tn. It
is expected to increase as seafood consumption continuously increases.
Historically production of ﬁsh has been aﬀected by disease and parasite outbreaks, and
production cost is mainly driven by the cost of feed and varies with production volume and
market, while price to the public has been more stable although the price has increased
constantly. Almost all salmon production is done in sea cages, and mortality is a big cost
for the industry, which together with sea lice treatment reaches a 12% of the revenues [6].
The rapid development, intensiﬁcation and globalisation of the aquaculture sector have led
to many challenges, including the emergence and spread of diseases in aquatic organisms,
resulting in the reliance on antimicrobials to improve the aquaculture production.
1.1
Antibiotic Use in Aquaculture
Aquatic organisms live among an array of microbes, some of which are potential pathogens,
depending on a variety of factors speciﬁc to the host, pathogen and environment. The high

prevalence of bacterial infections in ﬁshes leads to frequent use of antibiotics and thus their
persistence in the aquatic environment, which in turn results in the proliferation of antibiotic
resistant bacteria. Most bacterial pathogens in aquatic animals are aerobic, gram negative
rods and therefore most of the antibiotics used in aquaculture are eﬀective against gram-
negative bacteria[7].
Many antibiotics such as beta lactam, tetracycline, amphenicol, ﬂuoroquinolone, sulfon-
amide, macrolide, quinolone, oxytetracycline, amoxycillin, and sulphadiazine-trimethoprim
are frequently used in aquaculture to treat or prevent ﬁsh diseases[8]. Oxytetracycline was
seen to reduce gut microbial diversity in Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar), while enriching
possible opportunistic pathogens belonging to the genus Aeromonas, and leading to a high
prevalence of multiple tetracycline resistance-encoding bacterial genes[9].
The use of antimicrobials in aquaculture increases when prophylactic alternatives such as
vaccines, probiotics, immunostimulants are not available or not eﬀective, or when health
management practices are deﬁcient. Given the remarkable growth of aquaculture worldwide,
and signs of misuse or overuse of antimicrobials, there is reasonable concern about the contri-
bution of aquaculture to AMR in the environment, and the consequences for environmental,
animal, and human health.
Antibiotics in aquaculture are mainly administered through feed to an aﬀected population.
In semi-open aquaculture systems, undigested medicated food and ﬁsh faeces, containing
un-absorbed antibiotics and excreted antimicrobial metabolites, end up in the waters and
sediments surrounding ﬁsh farms, where they can remain active for several months [10].
In semi-closed aquaculture systems, eventual wastewater discharges release high loads of
organic matter into the environment with bacteria exposed to antimicrobials during treat-
ments. Sub-therapeutic levels of antibiotics in the aquatic media exert selective pressure for
antimicrobial resistant bacteria and favour horizontal transmission of AMR genes, which can
persist in the environment for several years [11].
The negative consequences of AMR arising in aquaculture settings are the selection of an-
timicrobial resistant bacterial pathogens of aquatic animals, terrestrial animals, and humans
which may result in ineﬀective treatments and changes in the composition of environmental
bacteria[12].
All exposure to antimicrobials, either during treatment or chronic and sub-therapeutic level
exposure would select resistant mutants that may emerge spontaneously. Once a bacterial
strain is resistant, this resistance can be transferred to other bacterial species and strains
via horizontal gene transfer.
AMR in animal and human bacterial pathogens could occur either by the direct selection of
resistant variants or,indirectly, through the transmission of AMR genes within the aquatic
environment, from the aquatic to the terrestrial environment, or vice versa. The risk of
selecting AMR bacteria relevant to human health in the aquatic environment is limited, as

1.2
Resistome in Aquaculture
As the human population increases there is an increasing reliance on aquaculture to supply
a safe, reliable, and economic supply of seafood. Although food production is essential for a
healthy population, an increasing threat to global human health is antimicrobial resistance.
Extensive antibiotic resistant strains are now being detected; the spread of these strains
could greatly reduce medical treatment options available and increase deaths from previ-
ously curable infections [13]in other organisms.
AMR is a global concern and is now recognized as one of the greatest threats to pub-
lic health worldwide. In 2017, the FAO implemented a project FMM/RAS/298/MUL for
strengthening capacities, policies, and national action plans on the prudent and responsible
use of antimicrobials in ﬁsheries.[15]
Fish farms have been suggested as one reservoir of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) in
the environment due to the prophylactic and therapeutic use of antibiotics. Aquaculture
systems and farms have been designated as “genetic reactors” or “hotspots for AMR genes”
where signiﬁcant genetic exchange and recombination can occur, which can shape the evolu-
tion of future resistance proﬁles [16]. It has been estimated that 90% of bacteria originating
in seawater are resistant to one or more antibiotics and up to 20% of the bacteria are resis-
tant to at least ﬁve [17]. Once bacteria have acquired AMR genes, they may exist in the
environment for a long time, even after the selection pressure ceases [18].
Many studies have reported high frequencies of AMR in bacteria in the vicinity of open
aquaculture operations however, few studies have been adequately designed to determine
the actual impact of antimicrobial use on AMR prevalence [19].
1.3
Sustainable Aquaculture
With the rapid global expansion and intensiﬁcation of the aquaculture industry in recent
years, there has been a concomitant increase in aquatic disease outbreaks, challenging sus-
tainability of production. In view of the threat posed by injudicious or incorrect use of
antimicrobial agents that can lead to the development of ARGs [20]. Thus, antimicrobials
should only be used after careful consideration of the options for disease prevention and
control. Here, good aquaculture practices are key, as they lower disease risks and prevent
disease, reducing the need for antibiotics and antimicrobials. Additionally, vaccinations can
be used in aquaculture, again preventing diseases and reducing the need to rely on antibiotics
and antimicrobials. The therapeutic use of bacteriophages to combat bacterial infections is
being increasingly explored in aquaculture as an alternative to traditional antibiotics as are
probiotics and the immunostimulants[21].

By 2030, aquatic food production is forecast to increase by a further 15 percent and it is
widely acknowledged that this growth will come mainly from aquaculture. However, such
growth in aquatic food production must not come at the cost of aquatic ecosystem health,
increased pollution, animal welfare, biodiversity or social equality. This requires new, sus-

mented for sustainable aquaculture; Blue Transformation launched by FAO following the
2021 COFI Declaration for Sustainable Fisheries and Aquaculture is a priority programme

vision to expand aquatic food systems and increase their contribution to nutritious and af-
fordable healthy diets for the most vulnerable, while fostering equitable growth, especially
for those communities that depend on ﬁsheries and aquaculture.
1.4
CIRCLES Project
In addition to the economic signiﬁcance of ﬁsh production, it is important to consider the
environmental and social implications of aquaculture. The expansion of ﬁsh farming has
raised concerns about its impact on marine ecosystems, water quality, and biodiversity. Ef-
forts to address these concerns have led to the development of best management practices
and certiﬁcation schemes to ensure the sustainable and responsible production of farmed
ﬁsh[22].
Despite the economic importance of aquaculture and other food systems, such as agriculture,
their production is ineﬃcient and not long-term sustainable. In response to this concern, the
EU initiated the CIRCLES (Controlling mIcRobiomes CircuLations for bEtter food Systems)
project with a ﬁnancial allocation of 10 million euros. The primary objective of CIRCLES
project is to enhance the safety, production, sustainability and resilience of food systems.
CIRCLES aims to discover and translate innovative microbiomes-tailored circular actions

1.5
Atlantic Salmon
Atlantic salmon (Scientiﬁc name: Salmo salar) are a cold water migratory species that re-
quire diﬀerent habitats for each life stage. They are born in freshwater, migrate to sea to feed
and grow, and return to freshwater to reproduce or spawn. This type of migratory behavior
is referred to as anadromous. While at sea, salmon remain in coastal areas or travel 2,500
km across the Labrador Sea to Greenland. Atlantic salmon returning to freshwater have an
incredible homing instinct that guides them back to the river where they were born[24].
In freshwater, Atlantic salmon require clear, cold, fast moving water with a gravel bot-
tom for spawning and rocky areas for juvenile ﬁsh. Salmon are often found in pools that
oﬀer protection from predators and warm temperatures and where water ﬂow conditions
enable them to rest. Young Atlantic salmon in rivers, feed mainly on aquatic insect larvae
such as mayﬂies, blackﬂies and stoneﬂies. Marine salmon feed on a variety of organisms
including zooplankton, ﬁsh larvae, and small ﬁshes like capelin, sand lance and herring.
1.5.1
Atlantic Salmon life cycle
Figure 4: Anadromous life cycle of Atlantic Salmon.[25]
Atlantic salmon have a complex life cycle 4 consisting of distinct juvenile, adult, and repro-
ductive life history stages. The freshwater phase is characterized by embryonic development,
hatching, and growth until smoltiﬁcation[26]. Fertilization of eggs takes place externally in
gravel nests called redds excavated by females in river beds. The developing embryos spend
Page 7 of 47
==PAGE:18==
MSc. Thesis
1.5
Atlantic Salmon
a variable amount of time in the gravel bed before hatching the following spring as alevins.
Post-hatching, the alevins continue to gain nutrition from the yolk sac for several weeks
and remain in the relative safety of the redd. Once the yolk sac is fully absorbed several
weeks to months after hatching, alevins swim-up from the redd, becoming fry, and com-
mence feeding. Fry then develop into parr, shifting from a diet of microscopic invertebrates
to larger invertebrate prey. Parr remain in fresh water for a variable number of years before
transitioning to the marine phase. This morphological, physiological, and behavioral trans-
formation to migrate from the fresh water to the marine phase is known as smoltiﬁcation[26].
In the marine phase, salmon spend number of years feeding and growing at an acceler-
ated rate compared to the freshwater phase. The most common maturation strategy is to
obtain sexual maturity after a return migration to fresh water after a varying number of
years spent in the marine environment. Atlantic salmon can repeat the spawning process,
where a small portion of Atlantic salmons may return to spawn over multiple years[26].
Farmed Atlantic Salmon Life Cycle
Aquaculture imitates the anadromous life cycle of wild Atlantic salmon. The production
starts by fertilizing the eggs that will be introduced in a freshwater tank that mimics the
river streams. In the water tank, the yolk sac larvae emerge from the egg. The salmon larva
develops by consuming the yolk sac from its abdomen and becomes a fry capable of receiving
its ﬁrst feeding. During the parr stage, the juvenile salmon can receive treatments, such as
vaccination, bio enhancers and probiotics[27].
The juvenile life stages of farmed Atlantic salmon are crucial because the presence of oppor-
tunistic pathogens can cause higher mortality. For this reason, it is important to take special
attention to the microbial composition of live feed to avoid the appearance of opportunistic
pathogens. The supplementation of live feed with Lactobacillus spp. and Pediococcus spp.
has shown beneﬁcial eﬀects in mucosal immunity, increased growth and stress tolerance of
the ﬁsh larvae. Apart from this, the use of lytic bacteriophages has also proven to reduce
the prevalence of opportunistic pathogens[28].
When the salmon is developed enough, it will pass the smoltiﬁcation stage that it entails.
In the wild, the Atlantic salmon passes through a salinity gradient when it migrates from
the river to the sea. However, in production systems smolts are usually transferred directly
from freshwater to seawater without considering the possible impact of the salinity shock to
the Atlantic salmon microbiome. The ﬁnal habitat of Atlantic salmon in aquaculture is the
open-pen sea cages located in the seawater, where the salmon will live and grow until it is
slaughtered[27].
A complete cycle of Atlantic salmon production is accomplished usually in 2 years in Nord-
laks, Norway. In Ireland, the time needed to complete one full production cycle is longer,
around 12 months in freshwater, followed by another 12 to 18 months in seawater, resulting
in a total of 24 to 30 months[27].

Objective
Primary objective:
Being part of the CIRCLES project; the main objective of this master’s thesis is to character-
ize the resistome dynamics and evolution in Atlantic salmon collected from two EU countries:
Norway and Ireland. The diversity, richness and abundance of antimicrobial resistance genes
within Atlantic salmon populations collected from Norway and Ireland is studied. More than
500 samples from Atlantic Salmon have been shotgun metagenome sequenced. The samples
have been collected from ﬁsh’s diﬀerent developmental stage tissues, environment and feed
from farmed and wild ﬁsh at observation (C0) and intervention phase(C1).
Secondary Objectives:
The Atlantic salmon metagenomics data will be analyzed for the following :
• Identify variations across the developmental stages: Investigate potential diﬀerences in
AMR proﬁles among various developmental stages of Atlantic salmon, spanning from
early to mature stages, aiming to understand how the evolution of resistome changes.
• Compare farmed vs.
wild populations: Compare the resistome dynamics between
farmed and wild Atlantic salmon populations to assess the potential inﬂuence of aqua-
culture practices on AMR.
• Explore geographical diﬀerences: Investigate potential variations in ARGs patterns be-
tween samples collected from Norway and Ireland, considering diﬀerent farming prac-
tices, environmental factors, or geographical variations.
• Assess intervention phase impact: Analyze and compare antimicrobial resistance pro-
ﬁles in Atlantic salmon samples collected during observation and intervention phases
to understand the potential inﬂuence of probiotic intervention in resistome dynamics.
Research Questions:
1. Investigate the Impact of Probiotic Intervention on Resistome Dynamics
• How does the introduction of probiotics inﬂuence the dynamics of ARGs within
the resistome proﬁle of Atlantic salmon?
• Are there speciﬁc sample categories that underwent probiotic intervention com-
pared to the observation phase?
2. Develop Guidelines for Minimizing Antibiotic Usage in Farming
• How can the results contribute to a sustainable approach for antibiotic reduction
in farming of Atlantic salmon?

3.2.1
Observation Phase
In Aquaculture farmed Atlantic salmon, the sampling was performed during the most rele-

stages of development within the aquaculture farm.
These stages encompassed six time
points in freshwater, including fertilised eggs(T0), yolk sac larvae(T1), fry(T2), parr(T3),

The samples from wild Atlantic salmon were collected after the juvenile ﬁsh abandoned
the redds and emerged from the river gravel as fry(T2). Furthermore, samples were ob-
tained from more developed stages of juvenile salmon, namely parr(T3) and smolts still in
the river(T5). Returning adults(T8) is the last life stage at which samples were taken.
3.2.2
Probiotic Intervention Phase
For the intervention phase, cycles were repeated by using microbiome modulators based on
live microorganisms as inoculi for live ﬁsh. (cf. Stembiont, Previwo) was applied in Nor-
wegian salmon production. Fish are exposed to a probiotic bath from Previwo, and then
continue their lifecycle. Afterwards, microbiome data and metadata will be collected over the
production cycle to compare the ﬁsh with the control group[23]. Stembiont™is administered
to farmed Atlantic salmon from Norway by bath or dip by adding Stembiont™to the anes-
thetic water in connection with vaccination. This avoids unnecessary handling of the ﬁsh[29].
Stembiont™is a microbial product that strengthens ﬁsh health. The product is registered as
a microbial hygiene product with the Norwegian Environment and Climate Agency (Product
registration No. 620082) and thus documented safely for the ﬁsh, the environment and the
consumer. Stembiont™is composed of benign strains of probiotic Aliivibrio bacteria. Aliivib-
rio is a large and varied bacterial genus with a natural occurrence in marine environments
[29].
As a probiotic, the main function of Stembiont is to provide "good bacteria" to the At-
lantic salmon. Bacteria provided by probiotics can compete and prevent the colonization of
harmful bacteria and parasites. Also, probiotic microorganisms can interact with the host
and modify the host’s immune system [27].
Stembiont, has delivered good results both in the laboratory and in the ﬁeld, demonstrating
eﬀects on copepodite ﬁxation and impacting the ability of lice to reach maturity and develop
egg strings. The development of the probiotic by Norwegian company Previwo is based on
the theory that bacteria of the Aliivibrio genus are very prevalent and abundant in the ma-
rine environment, and many of the species have evolved together with aquatic animals in a
kind of symbiosis[30].

3.3
Sample Processing
The samples are collected from ﬁsh tissues, environment and aquaculture feed at diﬀerent
developmental timepoints. The CIRCLES deliverable D2.2 SOPs [27] explains in detail the
procedures for sampling, storage and transport of animal and human samples for micro-
biome analysis. A concise overview of the Atlantic salmon sample processing from Ireland
and Norway is explained in this section.
During the early life stages of Atlantic salmon, including fertilised eggs, yolk sac larvae
and fry, a total of 100 individual ﬁsh are randomly collected, transferred to 4 sterile stom-
acher bags (25 per bag) and frozen until delivered to the analytical laboratory. There, the
bag content is homogenized in a stomacher at normal speed for 1 minute. Until DNA ex-
traction, the homogenate is stored in a sterile plastic tube at -80°C. In the case of the other
successive developmental stages, 10 individuals are selected randomly from each tank, cage
or net. 0.1 g/L of Tricaine Methane sulfonate is used to anaesthetize the salmon and 0.3 to
0.5 g/L, of the same product is administered to euthanize it.
For skin sampling, a portion of approximately 1-2 cm ⇥1-2 cm is dissected from the left
side of each ﬁsh. It is recommended to minimize the inclusion of muscle tissue, avoiding the
removal of scales and mucus from the skin. The sample is stored in a sterile plastic tube at
-80°C until DNA extraction in the analytical laboratory.
The gastrointestinal tract is dissected from each ﬁsh and it is divided into pyloric caeca
(PC) or anterior intestine, the mid intestine and the distal or posterior intestine. 25 g of di-
gesta contained in each part of the gastrointestinal tract is separated from the tissue. When
the tissue is empty, it is washed 3 to 4 times in sterile PBS. The samples and digesta are
stored in sterile tubes at -80°C until DNA extraction.
The environmental samples are collected at all developmental stages.The samples of inlet,
tank and outlet water are taken independently. In tanks, the samples are taken at 0.5 meters
depth. The samples of upstream water are collected 10 meters upstream of the pens, and
water inside and outside pens are collected 2 meters inside the pens. 2 litters of water are
taken for each sample. The water is ﬁltered twice, ﬁrstly using a 20 m ﬁlter, and secondly
with a Sterivex ﬁlter with a 0.22 m pore size using a peristaltic pump. The Sterivex ﬁlter is
stored in a sterile plastic bag at -80°C until DNA extraction.
The bioﬁlms are collected at the farms by swapping the wall or net at the water surface
and at a one-meter depth close to the water inlet and outlet. In the tanks, the bioﬁlm is col-
lected from the bottom. The swabs collection and storage is done following the instructions
given by the sampling kit manufacturer.
The feed samples was taken before or when the salmon is being fed.a collection of ﬁve
random samples of 10g each is obtained from the feed that was most recently given to the
ﬁsh. The samples are stored in stomacher bags and are refrigerated until being delivered to

the analytical laboratory. There, the feed sample is mixed with 100 ml peptone water, the

DNA Extraction and Sequencing
Once the samples are processed, the DNA was extracted by partners of the CIRCLES project
and the DNA samples were delivered to Technical University of Denmark (DTU) for metage-
nomic sequencing. The DNA/RNA Shield Lysis Tube with Swab from ZymoResearch was
used for DNA extraction of swab samples using the manufacturer’s instructions. The DNeasy
PowerMax Soil Kit from Qiagen was used for the DNA extraction of tissue samples follow-
ing the manufacturer instructions with minor modiﬁcations, as indicated in CIRCLES D2.2
SOPs for sampling, storage and transport of human, environmental and animal samples, and
for microbiome analysis (M6)
Shotgun metagenomic sequencing
Shotgun metagenomics was performed on indexed DNA libraries with approximate frag-
ment length of 500 bp. Fragmentation was performed by means of physical or chemical
approaches, such as Covaris Focused Ultrasonicator followed by indexing using Illumina
Nextera XT Library Prep kit, or QIAseq FX DNA Library Kit (Qiagen), respectively. Li-
brary sequencing was performed using Illumina NextSeq instruments, with NexSeq High or
Mid Output Illumina sequencing kits, in order to obtain 2x150 pair end reads (300 cycles)
and >3 Gb per samples. Sequencing machine used for metagenomic sequencing was Illumina
NovoSeq S4.

Bioinformatics Analysis
3.5.1
Trimming and Quality Control
Data sequenced at the GenEpi group at DTU Food was processed through a QC pipeline
known as FoodQC pipeline [31], which trims low quality data and adaptor sequence. All
steps of bioinformatics analysis was performed on Computerome[32].
Quality control is done to trim low-quality bases and sequencing adapters to improve the
overall quality of the data. The reads were quality trimmed by removing the adapter se-
quences using bbduk2 part of the suite bbtools version 36.49[33]. bbduk2 uses a 19mer to
look for contaminating adapters, with the exception of the read tips where kmers down to
11 are used. The right end of each read is trimmed to remove bases with a phred score below
20. Reads shorter than 50 base pairs were discarded. BBDuk2 also trimmed adapters based
on where paired reads overlap. FastQC is run twice for quality check. Once before trimming
and once after, It provide a way to do quality control checks on raw sequence data coming
from high throughput sequencing pipelines.

The total bacterial fragment count was obtained by mapping samples to the genomic database
and extracting only reads that mapped to bacteria. Normalizing to FPKM accounts for vary-
ing sequencing depths and gene lengths across samples. It adjusts for total bacterial counts
to make samples comparable. Normalizing for gene length avoids bias in read counts due to
gene size. The result is a standardized unit for easier comparison and interpretation of gene
expression levels. This makes data more interpretable across diﬀerent conditions and sam-
ples. The aggregated FPKM values for resistome total gene abundance were then visualized
as boxplots.
3.6.2
Resistome Diversity and Richness
OTU tables with raw counts (KMA aligned fragments) were normalized to achieve relative
abundance.
This was accomplished by dividing each count by the sample’s total count,
facilitating the comparison of samples with diﬀering total counts. Using the normalized read
counts, the alpha diversity of Atlantic salmon resistome was assessed. Two diversity indices
(Shannon and Simpson) and one richness index (chao1) were calculated via the diversity()
and estimateR() functions from the vegan package in R.

Discussion
The quality report for Atlantic salmon samples showed that the reads after trimming had
a relatively good quality for the data analysis for both observation and intervention phase.
However, the presence of Atlantic salmon host DNA in trimmed reads might interfere with
the detection and quantiﬁcation of resistome genes. Therefore it is required to use bioinfor-
matics tools or methods to remove this host DNA from the metagenomic sequencing data.
This could be done by ﬁltering out reads/fragments that align to the host genome or using
speciﬁc techniques to enrich for microbial DNA. We had attempted to ﬁlter the Atlantic
salmon host DNA by using BMtagger[39] tool for “tagging” host sequences and removing
them from fastq ﬁles, however this tool was unsuccessful. So other techniques/ tools need to
be utilised after removing the host DNA and after ﬁltering out host DNA, we can proceed
with assembly and binning to analyze the impact of resistome and microbial components.
5.1
Resistome Diversity and Richness
Between Ireland and Norway observation phase samples the diversity and richness have a
similar pattern. Even though the tank water is ﬁltered to guarantee water quality and avoid
ﬁsh diseases, the presence of microorganisms in the water and bioﬁlms is high. This could
inﬂuence the richness and diversity levels of environment samples: inlet and tank water,
which have the same or higher Shannon diversity and Chao1 index than atlantic salmon
ﬁsh sample: skin, intestine tissues and aquaculture feed. however the variability in Atlantic
salmon ﬁsh samples is higher than the environment.
The wild samples have slightly lower resistome diversity than the farmed. Wild environ-
ments may experience exposure to natural antibiotics produced by other organisms. This
exposure can contribute to the selection of speciﬁc resistance mechanisms, potentially leading
to lower resistome diversity, whereas in the aquaculture setting the farmed Atlantic salmon
is exposed to antibiotics for disease prevention and growth promotion which can lead to
increased selection pressure for speciﬁc resistance mechanisms. This could contribute to a
higher diversity of resistance genes in farmed environments.
In Norway, the diversity and richness levels of the farmed Atlantic salmon are maintained
through all production stages with minimal variations at some sample types. The main
diﬀerence is observed between the diversity and richness of observation and intervention
phases, where the richness of samples from the intervention phase seem to be lower than the
observation stages except for higher in the digesta samples. This might be inﬂuenced by the
Stembiont probiotic modulating other microbial organisms’ presence and thus resistome.
5.2
Resistome Relative Abundance
The results revealed that the resistome composition in fertilized eggs diﬀered signiﬁcantly
from other developmental stages of the salmon. This indicates unique patterns of antibiotic

resistance genes in the early stages of development. Similar patterns of AMR gene abun-
dance were observed between samples from aquaculture feed and various ﬁsh developmental
stages. This suggests that AMR genes could originate from both the environment and the
feed provided to the salmon during their growth.
The resistome abundance distribution at the gene level shows that the developmental stage
is similar to the feed and the environment , except for the fertilized egg. Early life stages:
fertilized egg, yolk sac larvae, can acquire resistance genes vertically from the parents. If the
parents possess speciﬁc resistance genes, they can be passed on to the oﬀspring, leading to
diﬀerences in the resistome of embryos compared to the surrounding environment or feed.
When comparing the observation and intervention phase samples from Norway, it can also
be noted that the ARGs abundance seen in the ﬁsh developmental stages being originated
mainly from the feed rather than the environment, as in the intervention phase when looking
at the developed ﬁsh tissues: skin, intestine, digesta, ﬁlet it has similar ARGs seen in the
observation phase. However this is not present in the feed from the intervention phase. This
shows that the potential source of ARGs is from the aquaculture feed in the early develop-
mental stages.
Among the abundant resistome antibiotic classes β-Lactam, Tetracycline, Streptogramin A,
Lincosinamide, Aminoglycoside, Macrolide, Quinolone, Sulfonamide, Trimethoprim, Pheni-
col, Fosfomycin etc found in the Atlantic salmon samples; β-Lactam, Tetracycline, sulfon-
amides are the most reported AMR class in aquaculture as discovered in previous studies[40].
Most of the top 15 ARGs detected belong to AMR class for β-Lactam resistance based on
general classiﬁcation as certain genes may confer resistance to multiple classes of antimicro-
bials.
Beta-lactam Resistance Genes blaCME, blaOXA, blaOXA-427, blaOXA-448, blaVEB-1,
blaOXA-493, blaOXA-347, blaOXA-85: shows resistance to the widely used antibiotics β-
Lactam, which is commonly used in aquaculture. Aminoglycoside Resistance Genes (Isa(B),
ant(6)): shows resistance to aminoglycoside antibiotics and streptomycin which can im-
pact the treatment of bacterial infections in salmon. Tetracycline Resistance Genes (otr(C),
tet44): this is seen in ﬁsh starting from early life stages and the environment water samples,
suggesting that both OTC treatment in aquaculture facilities and the farms themselves may
be sources of Tetracycline resistance genes introduction to the environment and thus the
ﬁsh. Macrolide Resistance Gene (msr(D)): Macrolide resistance genes (macrolide-ARGs) in
seawater have hardly been detected even by metagenomic global analysis[41], so the presence
of msr(D)in the salmon tissues and ﬁlet , could mean that this is coming from the feed.
The probiotic used in the intervention phase is gram negative rod bacteria from the genera
Aliivibrio. It is commonly associated with marine environments and is known for its ability
to form symbiotic relationships with certain marine organisms. Some bacteria used as pro-
biotics naturally harbor ARGs. These genes may be part of the bacteria’s natural genetic
makeup, and their presence doesn’t necessarily result from exposure to antibiotics. Gram-

like conjugation, transformation, and transduction. If the introduced Aliivibrio strains carry

Resistome Proﬁle
The resistome proﬁle for the Atlantic salmon lacks a clear separation between the envi-
ronment, aquaculture and Atlantic salmon samples. There are no distinct clusters, which
indicates the dissimilarity in resistome composition. even when the bar plot for resistome
relative abundance for top 15 common ARGs show similar pattern between the samples.
This is because the resistome proﬁle might include low abundance genes that contribute to
the overall resistance landscape but are not abundant. The comprehensive resistome data
could reveal additional resitome genes that play a role in the resistance but are not captured
in the top 15 genes.
5.4
CAZy Metabolite Abundance
The consistent molecular function abundance across the diﬀerent sample types suggests a
stable metabolite proﬁle based on the Carbohydrate-Active enZymes metabolite abundance.
This implies that the distribution and prevalence of molecular functions like transferase ac-
tivity, antioxidant activity, carbohydrate binding, and metal ion binding is constant across
the samples regardless of whether the samples are environment, feed or ﬁsh.
For further analysis, we can perform assembly and binning of the Atlantic salmon data
from which the host DNA is ﬁltered out, and then investigate the resistome associated with
diﬀerent metabolites, to see the relation between the bacterial gene associated resistome and
the molecular function. Assembly and binning involves reconstructing the genomes from data
sequence after the ﬁsh DNA is removed and then grouping the sequences into bins. Then we
can analyze the resistome by identifying the ARGs associated with the microbial genome.
This can help in correlating the metabolites we found from CAZy with the resistance genes.

Conclusion
This master’s thesis has successfully characterized the resistome dynamics and evolution
in Atlantic salmon populations from Norway and Ireland through metagenomic analysis of
over 500 Atlantic salmon samples; spanning various developmental stages, environments,
and observation and intervention phases and unveiled distinctive patterns in antimicrobial
resistance genes. The observed patterns in resistome composition, diversity, and relative
abundance along with the metabolite abundance shed light on whether there is any inﬂu-
ence of probiotics on resistome evolution and emphasize the need for sustainable practices
aquaculture. The diﬀerences in diversity and richness between the observation and interven-
tion phases were minimal. And the resistome abundance distribution of sample types from
diﬀerent phases showed similar distribution for AMR class and ARGs, with few variations
in observation and intervention and between Ireland and Norway.
AMR genes associated with resistance to various AMR class were found in high abundance
in the salmon samples.
Genes linked to antibiotic class β-Lactam, Tetracycline, Strep-
togramin A, Lincosinamide, Aminoglycoside, Amphenicol, Macrolide, Quinolones, Sulfon-
amides, Trimethoprim, Phenicols, and Fosfomycin resistance were identiﬁed. Resistome for
these ARGs Isa(B), blaCME, blaOXA-493, vga(E), blaOXA-427, blaOXA-448, blaOXA-85,
fosA3, and tet44 genes are consistently present across the samples from both Ireland and
Norway, suggesting their prevalence and potential signiﬁcance in the resistome of Atlantic
salmon across diﬀerent countries and stages of development. The probiotic intervention with
Aliivibrio in the Atlantic salmon resulted in some shifts in the resistome composition. The
resistome proﬁle during the intervention phase showed speciﬁc changes in the digesta indi-
cating potential interactions between the introduced probiotic and the microbial community.
However there is not much of signiﬁcant changes induced by the intervention phase on the
resistome composition.
The study provides crucial insights into the resistome dynamics of farmed Atlantic salmon,
highlighting the potential sources of antibiotic resistance genes, such as aquaculture feed.
Understanding the resistome variations across developmental stages and intervention phases
contributes valuable information for developing sustainable guidelines. The ﬁndings sug-
gest that minimizing antibiotic usage can be achieved by addressing speciﬁc stages, such
as early developmental phases and feed sources. This work proposes that aquaculture feed
could be a target source for reducing AMR in aquaculture and this targeted approach aligns
with sustainable practices and emphasizes the need for responsible antibiotic use in aqua-
culture. In conclusion, the research outcomes oﬀer a foundation for understanding resistome
dynamics in Atlantic salmon, shedding light on the impact of probiotic intervention and pro-
viding insights for developing guidelines to minimize antibiotic usage in aquaculture. The
comprehensive analysis of resistome composition, diversity, richness and relative abundance
contributes to the broader context of antibiotic resistance management in the farming of
Atlantic salmon.
