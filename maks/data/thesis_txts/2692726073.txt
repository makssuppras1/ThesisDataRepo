Abstract
Automation has become an integral part of production with computer vision being an important part
of many of these systems and with a need for more products to be produced due to an increase in
population and an increase in economical standings, more and more people are in need of medicine such
as insulin.
Some of this medicine are kept in glass vials and bottles, thus production of these are also increasing.
Here, automation can help speed up the process and replace jobs that otherwise entail hard manual
labour. Additionally, due to an increase in education in developed countries, the amount of people
available for these menial jobs is becoming more limited.
This master thesis covers the design of a system that, through computer vision, can locate glass vials
in a bin and relay their positions to a robotic arm that moves said vials into a tray. The system also
identifies the orientation of the vials and places them in a specified way and align them with respect to
the orientation.
Furthermore, the thesis also investigates wavelengths outside the visual spectrum and shows that
a part of both the infrared and ultra-violet spectrums show great potential to ease the way we find
objects made out of glass.
The project ends with a test of a proof of concept system, that performs the aforementioned achieve-
ments.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Computer vision based bin-picking systems are becoming increasingly common in manufacturing pro-
cesses due to their uptime, speed, and low cost over time. Bin-picking systems, as a topic of research,
combine knowledge of computer vision, robotics, and industrial engineering; all areas of interest for
the parties involved in the dissertation. This project in particular concerns itself with the detection
and localization of glass vials of the type shown in figure 1.1. Additionally, the other major part of
the project aims to pick up the vials using a robotic arm, utilizing coordinate system transformations,
robotic arm command generation, and cross-platform communication.
Figure 1.1: Left: Golf ball for scale. Right: Glass vials used in the project.
Glass vials in particular provide an interesting challenge in terms of computer vision due to their
transparency. Additionally, as the vials are made of glass, typically used for pharmaceutical purposes,
extra care must be taken when handling the vials to prevent any damage. This imposes a requirement
of high precision localization and gentle method of picking them up. These challenges imply that simple
image morphology will likely not be enough to reliably detect the vials.
In an effort to explore solutions to the challenge, the project will also examine the effect of different
wavelengths of light in relation to strengthening the silhouette of the glass vials. Studies, such as the
one shown on figure 1.2 have shown that glass have lower transmittance at low wavelengths of light
which could aid the segmentation of the vials.

One also has to account for the fact that a regular camera lens will also absorb much of the light
emitted by e.g. a UV backlight thus requiring a special lens. Many lenses also have filters for both
Ultra Violet light, electromagnetic waves, ≈100 −400nm (UV) and Infrared light, electromagnetic
waves, about 800nm to 1mm (IR) light that prevents the Charged-Coupled Device (CCD) chips in the
camera from being affected by these wave lengths.
Given this short introduction to the topics of interest in the project, the project can be scoped to
fit the allotted time and budget of the project.
1.1
Motivation
The basis of the project was proposed by ProInvent, based in Hørsholm, specializing in development of
integrated manufacturing technology and product development.
They proposed a project that mainly revolved around using computer vision to detect randomly po-
sitioned glass vials. We proposed that this detection would be used to move the vials once detected,
using a robotic arm.
During the early phases of finalizing the project description, we began to wonder if visible light would
be a good choice to find an object made out of glass, so from this we wondered how other wavelengths
interact with glass. We already know that some wavelengths passes through some materials and not
through others and vice-versa for other wavelengths. We also know that glass can split light into various
colours as we see in a prism. This generated the question; are there wavelengths close to the visual
spectrum that gets blocked by glass? If the answer to question is yes, and there are a set of wavelengths
that gets absorbed by glass, then we might create a case where it gets easier to detect the glass vials
since they no longer appear as transparent when illuminated by the correct wavelengths (and using an
appropriate camera).

1.2
Problem description
In manufacturing, parts often have to go through several steps to complete the product. These steps
often need to move a part (or product) from one location to another. One of these steps can be from a
crate to a transport belt where the parts have to be aligned in a specific way. This can be done by the
use of a robotic arm in conjunction with a camera system to identify an object and its orientation.
Control of the movement of the robotic arm is a classical exercise within robotics but it has some
limitations regarding where it can react, even within the workspace of the arm. To compensate for this,
parts are often spread out on a conveyor or a table to give the robotic arm a way to reach every part
but the action of getting the parts on the table can be damaging to the parts and lead to scratches or
cracks in delicate parts such as glass vials.
Glass vials are highly used, including in the pharmaceutical industry and to identify these in a bin,
a camera setup and the corresponding image analysis has to be made. To identify the glass vials it will
be of interest to investigate different wavelengths of light to see what wavelengths yield the best results
when searching for a transparent object.
Therefore it is desired to design a system that can identify glass vials in a bin and orient a robotic
arm and its end piece to grab each individual glass vial and subsequently move the glass vials out of
the bin.
1.2.1
In bullet points
• Overall system requirements
– Identify the glass vials using classical image analysis tools
– Relay the coordinates and orientation found by the image analysis program to the robotic
arm
– Command the arm to move to the vial coordinates and pick it up and move it to a desired
location
• Object identification
– Identify a computer vision system that can detect glass and apply the appropriate solution
– Investigate if different wavelengths of light affect the recognition accuracy
– Identify vials laid out in a single layer
• Robotic arm
– The robotic arm should use its internal regulators and be controlled by programming the
movement
– Define a movement pattern for the arm such that obstacles are avoided
1.3
Project scope
The project is focused on solving the problem described above but is limited to proof of concept, mean-
ing that a full prototype (i.e. the building of a complete industrial machine) will not be done. However,
a setup will be made to demonstrate the developed solution, using computer vision and a robotic arm.
This will allow us to focus on finding a solution that is robust and allow us to use the tools at hand such
as our PCs/laptops, webcam, optical camera and the UR5 robotic arm available for experimentation
at DTU Elektro.

1.4 State of the art
4
1.4
State of the art
At the present time, no solution for the general bin picking system exist. This is outlined in [3] chapter
2, where Buchholz states, from his research, that even though many solutions to a specific bin-picking
problem have been solved, the general system has not.
He describes in his PhD. thesis, that from his findings, two general methods are used in finding the
object, correspondence matching and voting based matching (where Standard Hough Transform (SHT)
is the most prominent approach). Buchholz also finds that the object that needs to be picked from the
bin also has a large impact on the applied solution. Can the object be picked up from multiple places
(i.e. is part of the object better suited than other parts)? Is the object uniform? Are they stacked
in a particular way in the bin or are they mixed and jumbled together? These questions all need to
be answered before the system is designed as a part of the system description, resulting in making it
diﬀicult to make a general solution since each object in a system varies a lot from other systems.
When looking at the latest innovation within bin-picking, most works are concerned with finding
an unknown object and figure out its placement in 3D space. One solution for this is proposed by
Fischinger et. al. [5]. Even though the method shows good results compared to other methods, the
computational power required is high, but still taking a step in the right direction since it can deal
with multiple differently shaped objects. This method might not be the best solution for a case like
ours, since we know the object and can therefore speed up the process. The method does not test any
transparent objects, thus it contains a reliance on being able to ”see” objects in the images taken by
the system, but since this has not been tested, and with the additional knowledge about us only having
our objects in one layer, this method seems too excessive in the requirements in computational power
and also using machine learning which is out of the scope for this project.
In regards to the robot arm, there are multiple different arms that would be able to perform the task
of moving a vial from a box. Our choice of the UR5 arm is due to what robotic arms are available in
the lab at DTU Automation & Control department. This project focuses mostly on the vision part but
the desire to construct an experimental setup and to be able to expand our expertise and knowledge of
how a robotic arm can be used in combination with vision led us to include that part in the project.
In the literature different solutions for the kinematics of the UR5 arm are available, among others [1]-[8]
present solutions but the work by Parham M. Kebria et. al [8] include the complete forward and inverse
kinematics of the UR5. Thus, the modelling of the UR5 arm will not be developed in this project,
instead we will use the previously mentioned developed model.
In conclusion, there exists no general solution for the bin-picking problem yet, but various solutions
for different cases does exist where voting schemes and correspondence matching are among the two
most used. The literature shows that the UR5 arm is a good candidate for this type of work, being
known for its precision, speed and ease of use.

3.3.2
Moving the robot using Python
From the ’UR5 User Manual’ ([13]), different commands for moving the UR5 arm can be found. The
two functions used in this project are movel and movej. The ’movel’ command moves the robot in a
linear way, such that the manipulator’s orientation does not change during the movement, whereas the
’movej’ command moves in a non-linear way, thus not guaranteeing the orientation of the manipulator
during the movement. Furthermore, ’movel’ moves the tool in a linear path between waypoints whereas
’movej’ allows the robot to move more freely; this mainly allows ’movej’ to avoid singularities which
can happen during a ’movel’ command where the robot will occasionally try to reach infinite velocities
or move through itself.
Since the vials are empty we do not care if its orientation changes during
movement, only the end position is of interest. Furthermore, the only obstacle we have is the box itself
as the camera is mounted above the robot. This gives us a large room in which the arm can move
unimpeded.
The calculation of the inverse kinematics are done in the UR Control Box when using the movej[p]
position-command. This makes the robot move to a specified position whereas if the ’p’ is omitted the
command given should send the individual joint positions. The benefit of these two ways to move the
robot are very different. Given that the orientation and path of the manipulator largely does not matter
during movement, the arm can move arbitrarily to reach the end position (which is either a position in
space or in joint space), with the only obstruction being the box.
At the beginning we planned all movements as a position in space, using movej[p] for the majority of
the movements. However, this had the downside that since the joints have limitations on how far they
can turn (±360◦according to the specifications[14]), we saw that after a small number of iterations a
joint would be asked to move beyond its limits. Furthermore, with this we were not able to control
where the robot was positioned, when we wanted to capture a new image, thus it would at times block
the camera’s view.
Deciding a starting position and a hand-off position where the arm would not be interfering with the
camera was needed.
The starting position was chosen from a position where each joint would be rather close to the center
of its possible position, but in a manner where it would not be blocking the camera’s line of sight to the
box. As mentioned before, the movement was at first done using a tool position and not a joint position.
Since we now knew a position in world space, we just needed the joint positions in that configuration.
These joint positions were collected from data feedback from the arm itself.
For the hand-off position the same procedure was done since an implementation of the different hand-off
positions were already in place where the robot increments the hand-off position to place the vials in the
correct slots in the hand-off tray. The robot was moved to each slot in the tray and the joint positions
was noted and is then read from a Look-Up Table (LUT) depending on how many vials the robot has
already handed off.
These two changes had the effect that the errors/offsets we got using a position in world frame were
now minimized in comparison to what we saw using positioning.
We have two other fixed waypoints that we use during a move. These were also converted into joint
positions, using the same method as before. These two waypoints are used to make the manipulator
avoid hitting the box both when going to grab a vial and handing it off; one waypoint for each avoid-
ance. This resulted in four fixed waypoints in total, one used only as a start position1, one to center
the manipulator over the box to avoid hitting the box when moving to pick up a vial, a waypoint just
above the vial and lastly, a waypoint between box and tray, again for avoidance.
From the waiting position above the vial and down to the vial that has been chosen for pick-up, we
use movel[p], since the position of the vial is calculated using the point in world frame found in equation

3.5.1
Calibration tool
First, we designed a simple calibration tool to find the center of the end-piece when the gripper is closed,
giving a stable point that we would then move to the center of the three markers4 in order to calibrate
the robot to our workspace. The calibration tool is a simple rectangle with a half pyramid at the end
with one attached to each end-piece. The reason that the tool is designed in two halves, and not in one
solid piece, is that the 2F-140 does a calibration when first activated where it opens and closes fully.
Closing the gripper, puts the two fingers together, creating a sharp point that is used as the center of
the fingers, making it possible to do a precise calibration.
Figure 3.8: Calibration tool.
3.5.2
Gripper tool
The gripper tool was designed to have an inner curve such that the vials (that are circular in circum-
ference) would not slide out of the gripper once picked up. Furthermore, we also wanted to be able to
have the tool go between vials, without damaging any of them and also be able to gently push away
vials that are in the way of the ones that are being picked up.
This led to the design ending at a narrow tip, and with an outer slope that would push nearby vials
out of the way. The inside has a a semi-circular shape much larger than the vial, this helps the tool
grab the vial without it shifting. It was considered to have an indent on the inside corresponding to
the radius of the vial, but this would make the tool much thicker than desired, and this would in turn
call for a need to open the gripper more than what we wanted.
To aid in securing the vial in the tool once picked up, the inside was covered with duct tape. With
several layers, we got a surface with more friction than the plastic itself, and acting like a buffer around
the vial, creating a better fit.

3.6
Light box
A box was constructed to hold the Lumie lamp, such that it could be used as a backlight.
The box is made from wood and lifts the lamp 5 cm above the surface to provide some air such that
the lamp keeps relatively cool. The ripples seen on the lamp in figure 3.1a are ridges in the plastic
cover. This of course gave the problem that the lamp is not flat, having the impact that the vials had a
tendency to roll into the ”valleys”. This was of course not a desired thing to have since it would create
a setting where the vials would most of the time position themselves at a specific angle and furthermore
be situated against each other.
Furthermore, it was desired to diffuse the light more than what the lamp by itself did. It was found by
experiments that three layers which shape the light on an LCD screen (from an old laptop) did the job
well. Also, keeping two of the layers which polarize the light actually enhanced the vials in an image
while also having the benefit that these three layers provided a flat surface for the vials to lay a top.
The polarizing helps focus the light such that as much light as possible is directed strait out of the box
towards the camera.
On top of the lamp, inside the box, a frame has been set such that the vials cannot be placed at the
edge of the lamp since the lamp does not illuminate in a border around 1.5 cm along the lamp.

3.7.1
Glass and infrared light
Early research done by Francis W. Glaze et. al. from 1950 [6] shows that glass can absorb NIR where
the absorption rate is specific for each glass composition. We performed some experiments to see how
the vials would react when exposed to IR waves. From the experiments, we could see that the vials
became very distinct (blacker than the background) but the emitter (infrared source built into the
Microsoft Surface Pro 4) blinks in a noticeable frequency that made it diﬀicult to capture an image and
to be certain that the area was illuminated. Furthermore, the emitter did not illuminate an area big
enough and it had a ”flashlight effect”5 on the image.

4.3
Line detection
From the experimental images, after edge detection, we see that the sides of the vials are clearly visible,
thus these could be used to find where a vial is located on the image.
To be able to find these lines, a line detector is needed. From [12], the Standard Hough transform is
outlined. This is a classical way of detecting lines (the method has been extended over the years, to
include variations of the SHT to detect different types of shapes, we are only interested in the linear
Hough Transform) in a digital image, introduced by Paul Hough in 1962.
The SHT has been further developed over the years, resulting in a variety of Hough transforms, among
these are the PPHT.
4.3.1
Progressive probabilistic Hough transform
The PPHT (from [11]) has certain advantages over the SHT when the object is known. One of the
features that makes the PPHT favorable is the ability to discard lines that are too small and the ability
to determine how far we allow each pixel to be from another and still belong to the same line. We use
these features to detect only the sides of the vials with the PPHT, but also to guarantee that the lines
we find are only associated with one side so that we do not get lines that run across multiple vials.
From the experiments done in [11] we see that the PPHT uses substantial less votes in detecting a
line, thus minimizing the number of computations needed, therefore making the algorithm faster than
the SHT. Even though the speed of our developed program is not integral to the project, we desire to
have it running in real time in conjunction with the UR5 robotic arm. Thus, the speed of the program
is still of interest.

4.3.4
Sorting and grouping the lines
Looking in the documentation for the HoughLinesP function1 we see that the found lines are represented
as the coordinates for each end of the line. As a side note, the documentation does not describe in what
order the two sets of coordinates are given but through experiments we observed that the coordinates
are always given as the pair closest to the origin first. We use the knowledge that it is easier to find
and pickup vials at the center of the box so all lines that has one coordinate pair inside the boundary
are bundled together and the remaining lines are bundled together as well. Doing this provides a rough
sorting but we do not yet know which lines belong together to make out a side of a vial. It has to be
mentioned that a vial does not need both sides found by PPHT for the system to be able to find it and
pick it up. This is handled as a part of the template matching.
Figure 4.14: Bounding box for sorting found lines by PPHT.
After sorting for the bounding box, if there exists any lines within it, these lines are then sorted by
slope, otherwise the remaining lines are sorted by slope. As described before, each line consists of two
sets of coordinates where the first one is the closest to the origin. The slope can be negative as well as
positive depending on the orientation of the lines and the placement of the coordinates in relation to
the origin. The sorting is done with a margin/tolerance of ±0.07rad; if a line is within this tolerance
compared to the previous one, they are considered as belonging to the same vial. This is done with all
lines found, resulting in groups of lines associated with each other.
From this, the group with the lowest slope is then merged together with the lines that are present
at the same side of a vial. This is needed since a side of a vial might consist of several smaller line
segments. Here we again utilize that we know the slope of each line.
We construct new lines consisting of the first pair of coordinates from one line and the second pair from
another. Calculating the slope for this new line, we can now match the new slope to the original. If
the slope is within the boundary (against original slope ±0.07rad) the new line is defined as the first
coordinate pair of one line and the end coordinates from the other. The end-result from this is having
longer and fewer lines to describe a side of a vial. An example of this is shown in figure 4.15 where one
side consists of two lines and the other of one. The points P1,1 and P2,1 are made into a set of new lines
with P2,2 and P3,2 as new endpoints, respectively, P2,1 with P1,2 and P3,1 with P1,2 and P2,2.

4.3.4.1
The special case of vertical lines
Since the sorting and grouping are all dependent on the lines having a slope, a problem arose when a
vial was vertical. In this case the slope was infinite, which was handled as NaN by the software. This

lines, and both of them being vertical, they could be associated by distance (if other vertical vials where
present) and could then be assigned to one side or the other depending only on the x-value. With this
solution, the algorithm could handle lines without a slope as well.
It should be mentioned that there is no need for a special solution for horizontal lines since they have
a slope of 0.

4.4.1
Vial orientation
The way the vial orientation and location are found is by identifying the tip of the vial as well as
attributing a binary orientation (up or down) in the preprocessed binary image of the vials using a
template of the vial tip. In essence, this is a simplified approach to solving the correspondence problem,
which relates to finding a patch in an image which corresponds to a template of an element to be located.
To solve this problem, a template of the tip of the vial which includes a small amount of the sides of
a vial have been made. The template has been made based on the size of the binary images of the
vials. This template is shown below on figure 4.21 along with the template’s fit on a generic image of
an arbitrary vial tip.

(b) Vial template added to vial tip (template
shown in white, vial in red).

each positional increment is calculated. The number of matches for each template position increment

As mentioned, the size of the window in which the template-points (h0, w0) has a dynamic size. This
size is determined by multiple factors described below:
• An initial user-set size. The correspondence matching has an initial set of height and width
increments which define the search window and can be adjusted to search in a broader or narrower
part of the image.
• The number of iterations the program has had at finding a match. If the program does
not find a match initially, the window size is increased by a factor of tries/2 until a number of
tries have been made or until a suitable match is found.
• Whether one or two lines are found. If the program only finds a single line, the window
size is increased as the tip could be on either side of the line and there is no second line to aid in
determining the starting point of the matching process.
• The orientation of the vial.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The objective for this project was to find, pick up and hand off a glass vial, using a robotic arm and
classical image analysis. This objective have been completed, with a system that have an 82.54% success
rate.
The robotic arm moves in a pattern that are described by a set of waypoints, insuring that the arm
does not hit any obstacles, within the static environment.
An algorithm has been developed that can detect the glass vials using a multiple of image analysis
tools, such as template matching and PPHT. The algorithm uses a fixed camera and the light from a
back lit box, to capture images, where after the images are processed, supplying the aforementioned
two tools, to detect the vials.
The algorithm also finds where the detected vial are located in the world frame, and transforms and
generate the needed commands for the robotic arm to run a cycle, of picking up and placing down a
vial, from the box to the hand off tray.
The algorithm has only been applied to vials laying in a single layer.
Lastly, both infrared and ultra violet light have been investigated, for use in detection of glass.
Both investigated wavelengths showed promising results, that would ease the detection of glass. The
investigation was primary performed by gathering research done by Jack. M. Florence et. al. (1950)
and by F. L. Bouqet (1980), infrared and ultra violet light respectively, and be testing using IR.
Only infrared light have been tested, due to hardware constraints.
6.1
Future Work
On the hardware side, the system would mainly benefit from using components that are better suited
for the task. For instance, using an actual backlight with more evenly distributed light could rid us of
a lot of the faults related to vials near the edge of the box.
In addition to this, vials lying flush with the edge would be hard to pick up as the gripper would collide
with the bin. To remedy this, a bin with sloped sides could be made such that vials could be picked up
even when lying at the edge of the bin.
Using a vacuum gripper rather than a two-finger gripper would ensure that only a single vial would
be picked up at a time. However, as the vials are round, it would be hard for a vacuum gripper to get
proper suction on the vials. As an alternative, a gripper like the one used in this project with a smaller
stroke could be used such that two vials could not fit between the fingers. This would in turn require
very high accuracy to ensure that the gripper is perfectly centered above the vial.
While the UR5 robotic arm is also a good choice for a prototype project such as this one due to
its ease of programming and safety measures, a practical application of this system would require very
much faster speeds to be cost-eﬀicient. Using a faster industrial arm which might be a good optimization
to reduce the time it takes to pick up and hand off the vials. Further improvements could likely also be
made to the robot speed by inserting additional waypoints to reduce the travel distance of the robot
while not colliding with the bin.
==PAGE:77==
6.1 Future Work
60
Using a camera designed for interfacing with a computer, would provide some level of optimization.
Currently, the camera is set to automatically adjust ISO, shutter time, color temperature, etc. The
camera is set to this mode to avoid having to reset the camera every time the camera needed to
calibrate the bin location using the ArUco-markers.
This mode has still provided very acceptable
results but having a fixed camera setting would ensure a higher degree of consistency between images.
Another reason for using a different camera is that the camera used here is a DSLR camera designed
for photographers and not industrial use; thus, its life-span might be reduced by constantly recording,
various vibrations, and by the general degrading environment typically present in industrial production
lines.
Lastly, the software used to relay the image feed to the computer does not support higher
resolution than 1280 × 720. This is not a large issue as we downsize the images, but would have more
control over the operation receiving the full-resolution images. Regarding the vibration of the camera,
these vibrations have been observed to shake the camera out of focus over time which means that a
re-focusing is often required, this is mainly due to the UR5 and camera both being mounted on the
same aluminium structure.
Lastly on the hardware side, it would have been interesting to do further work on the IR images
as they did show promise but was unfortunately only available at a late point during the project. It is
possible that using the IR images, a simpler or more consistent preprocessing chain could be applied.
Also, no test was ever done with UV-light or X-rays, as no equipment was available, though it would
have made for an interesting test to see if performance could be increased by applying light from these
spectrums.
On the software side there are also some features that could be applied to increase performance. One
consistent issue is that the program might consistently detect a line near the edge of the bin although
the tip of the vial is not visible, thus not triggering a detection. To remedy this, the program could be
changed to discard that specific line and search elsewhere on the image such that the vials would not
have to be removed by hand.
Also, a difference image, which is a subtraction of a ”default” image with no vials from a ”test” image
taken by the camera, could possibly be used to enhance or act as an alternative to edge-detecting.
Lastly, another software improvement could be to introduce further sorting on which line(s) to select
by using additional features such as whether the vial is close to the center, whether the vial is lying
next to another vial, or how clear the edges of the vial is.
