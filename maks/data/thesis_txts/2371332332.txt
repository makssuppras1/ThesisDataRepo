Abstract
The historical market structure and traditional power system gave little incentives
for utilities to be customer-centric. However, the disruption of centralized power gener-
ation through an increasing penetration of rooftop PV panels among other sustainable,
but intermittent power technologies has initiated a structural shift in the electricity
system. Furthermore, the falling costs of battery storage and the rise of the era of
electric vehicles will inevitably require a better understanding of the changes in power
demand. Literature is rich in methodologies for ”aggregated” load forecasting which
has helped electricity network operators and retailers in optimal planning and schedul-
ing. The recent increase in the uptake of distributed generation and storage systems
has generated new demand for ”disaggregated” load forecasting for a single-customer
or even down at an appliance level. Access to high resolution data from smart meters
has enabled the research community to assess conventional load forecasting techniques
and develop new forecasting strategies suitable for demand-side disaggregated loads.
This thesis studies how calendar eﬀects, forecasting granularity and the length of
the training data set aﬀect the accuracy of a day-ahead load forecast for residential
customers. For deterministic load forecast models, root mean square error (RMSE)
and normalized RMSE were used as forecast error metrics. Regression Trees and Sup-
port Vector Regression (SVR) yielded better RMSE results than Neural Networks and
Multiple Linear Regression, but SVR had lower NRMSE values suggesting a slightly
better overall forecast accuracy.
The use of historical load proﬁles with daily and weekly seasonality, combined with
weather data, leaves the explicit calendar eﬀects a very low predictive power. In the
setting studied here, it was shown that forecast errors can be reduced by using a coarser
forecast granularity. It was also found that one year of historical data is suﬃcient to
develop a load forecast model for residential customers. Further increase in training
dataset has a marginal beneﬁt, but a shorter dataset may reduce the accuracy of
Regression Trees and Neural Networks.
To address the uncertainty of residential load demand, probabilistic forecasting
techniques were also investigated. Quantile Regression and Quantile Regression Aver-
aging were used to develop probabilistic forecasts. Pinball Loss, Continuous Ranked
Probability Score and Winkler Score were used to compare various probabilistic fore-
casting models. The results show that Quantile Regression Averaging based on point
forecasts outperforms Quantile Regression. The best probabilistic forecast model con-
sisted of 16 individual point forecast models.
As part of this thesis, a paper Short-term Residential Load Forecasting: Impacts of
Calendar Eﬀects and Forecast Granularity was submitted to Applied Energy journal
and is under review as on 24.04.2017.

Introduction
Electricity demand depends on several factors including weather, time, and socio-economic
constraints (Fahad & Arbab, 2014). Load forecasting considers these factors to facilitate the
decision making process of unit commitment, economic dispatch, and power system oper-
ation (Wan, Zhao, Member, & Song, 2015). The traditional centralized power generation
from conventional power plants involved little uncertainty. Utilities focused on the statisti-
cal accuracy of a cluster of loads rather than a single household (Wan et al., 2015). This
is changing due to the transition to distributed energy generation from intermittent energy
sources, the decentralization of the electricity market, and the rising number of demand
side control systems. The arrival of new power generation technologies have aﬀected our
approach to ensure the balance between energy supply and demand both in short-term and
long-term.
The changes in long-term energy demand are caused by policy, economic, behavioral changes,
and more recently by climate change. The policies in energy eﬃciency give an incentive to
reduce the power consumption and, thus, the energy bill.
In fact, the improvements in
energy eﬃciency and stricter building standards has resulted in reduced residential electric-
ity demand in Australia since 2010 (AEMO, 2016). As part of transition to sustainable
energy production, the renewable energy share in electricity production has been rapidly
growing. This reduces greenhouse gas emissions from power generation and diminishes de-
pendency on fossil fuels. From the beginning of clean energy development, its growth has
been almost entirely driven by appropriate policy incentives and support schemes. The his-
torical market structure and traditional power system gave little incentives for utilities to
be customer-centric. However, the disruption of centralized power generation through an in-
creasing penetration of rooftop PV panels among other sustainable, but intermittent power
technologies has initiated a structural shift in the electricity system.
Around 30% of the global electricity demand is related to the power consumption in the
residential sector (Swan & Ugursal, 2009).
The load forecasting in other sectors or for
the entire power network has reached relatively high accuracy. In contrast, the dynamics
of power consumption at household level change rapidly, inhibiting the forecasting eﬀorts
(Javed, Arshad, Wallin, Vassileva, & Dahlquist, 2012). The factors aﬀecting the residential
power demand are much more complex than in the industrial or commercial sector. While
the demand in non-residential sectors follow a regular pattern in a short-term, the residen-
tial sector consumption is linked to climate, household appliances, residents behavior and
the changes in households social-economic and social-demographic parameters. Households
energy demand changes quickly as appliances for washing, cooking and other activities are

As technology matures in terms of costs and reliability, renewables are becoming more com-
petitive with conventional power generation. Excellent solar resources have made Australia
well recognized for the deployment of distributed generation. Solar PV rooftops have become
increasingly popular solution to generate power locally and reduce the dependence on the

between 1-4kWp (Bacher, Madsen, & Nielsen, 2009). A steady increase in the system size is

However, the aggregated eﬀect of rooftops PV now reaches well beyond the household level
and must be taken into account when planning centralized unit commitment and dispatch.
Due to the stochastic nature of renewable energy sources, such as wind, there is always a
degree of uncertainty involved in the power forecast from renewables. This challenge adds
to the already diﬃcult task to forecast household demand at near-real-time. To ensure op-
timal battery operation, optimization process is required. This is largely based on available
historical data and the forecast model used.

Rooftop PV systems in the residential sector require new operational strategies to maxi-
mize households self-suﬃciency and minimize the negative eﬀect of the afternoon PV feed-in
spikes on the grid (Weniger, Bergner, & Quaschning, 2014).
The scale of management
has been moved down from utility level to micro-grid level and single households (Iwafune,
Yagita, Ikegami, & Ogimoto, 2014). To mitigate the adverse eﬀects of load ﬂuctuations and
voltage instability, as well as store excess PV output, the PV system can be combined with
battery storage (Song, Krishnamurthy, Kwasinski, & Sharma, 2013). The battery is charged
when the surplus energy from PV panels is available. When the battery is full and the local
demand is met, the surplus power is fed into the grid. Considering that this process occurs in
multiple households, a power feed-in peak occurs requiring the grid operator to pursue load
balancing procedures. As there is a growing solar power generation, it is necessary to ﬁnd a
trade-oﬀbetween the amount of the solar energy fed into the grid and optimal PV-battery
system operation from the household perspective (Weniger et al., 2014).
Figure 2 illustrates a distributed energy generation and storage system. Depending on the
battery state and solar irradiation, the power for electric appliances can be supplied from
PV panels, battery or the grid. The available solar energy would be ﬁrst used to meet the
household demand and charge batteries. Electricity is exported to the grid only if cannot
be consumed locally. In case of a grid failure or for safety or maintenance purposes, utilities
can disconnect the solar system from the grid through control system usually located in the
inverter.
The scheduling of batteries is strongly inﬂuenced by errors in input variables (Michiorri,
Bossavy, Kariniotakis, & Girard, 2013). Whether the household PV-battery system man-
agement is carried out locally or in the cloud environment by the third party, load forecasting
has an important role (Iwafune et al., 2014). The forecast accuracy is even more critical for
single stand-alone systems or microgrids, where unforeseen, high-frequency irradiance ﬂuc-
tuations or unexpected energy consumption can result in severe voltage ﬂuctuations (Habib,
Pecenak, Disfani, Kleissl, & Callafon, 2016). PV-battery system scheduling for residential
customers is optimized with complex mathematical programming algorithms or relying on
more eﬃcient geometrical methodologies. Case studies for PV-battery system optimization
are often based on historical data rather than solar irradiation and load forecasts (Zhong,
Khalilpour, Vassallo, & Sun, 2016). Since household load is less predictable than the overall
system forecast, excluding load forecast errors from PV-battery management gives a perfect,
but unrealizable solution.
With the challenges come opportunities. The need for ﬂexibility and faster response has
facilitated real-time control and monitoring technologies. Remotely reading digital meters

1.1
Understanding Forecasting
Since the beginning of dawn humans have wanted to peek into the future. Prediction is
related to future events that cannot be altered by the action in the present. However, we
live in the world, where most things involve a level of uncertainty. The main goal of fore-
casting is to identify the range of likelihood. Forecasting should be perceived as the most
likely outcome or the best estimate of a variable of interest (load in this study) at a given
time (Aprelkin, 2014). This is also known as deterministic forecasting or point forecasting.
However, often a numerical forecast is diﬀerent from the observation resulting in the forecast
error. All forecasting techniques are based on eﬀort to minimize this error.
At the foundation of forecasting is chronologically ordered sequence of observations on a
process of interest (Montgomery, Jennings, & Kulahci, 2011).
The forecasting approach
exercises historical data and is based on relationship between independent and dependent
variables. Statistical methods are used to ﬁnd patterns, cycles and trends between the vari-
ables. This link then is applied to the future events forming a forecast. Such forecasting
methods are combined under quantitative techniques. Mathematically, they can be cate-
gorized as forecast based on extrapolation, correlation or mix of both (Sallam & Malik,
2011). However, the forecast cannot always be made using quantitative techniques. Data
are not always available, for instance, when a new technology is launched. In this case,
qualitative techniques are used to predict the behavior and outcome of the process. Surveys,
experts knowledge and interviews are some of methods used to analyze the problem, identify
the alternative and form scenarios. Although such analysis involves some data, qualitative
forecasts are made on subjective judgment.
1.2
Forecasting in Power Systems
There is not a single forecasting method that perform well in all situations. The time hori-
zon of the desired process is probably the ﬁrst factor to consider before selecting a method.
Forecasting is often classiﬁed as short-term, medium-term and long-term. The boundaries
of these categories vary by author as well as by application. In power systems, short-term
load forecasting (STLF) is often interpreted as the forecast of the system conditions between
a few minutes ahead to a few days ahead (Weron, 2014). The load prediction for the in-
terval up to one hour ahead is sometimes referred as very short-term load forecasting and
facilitates a safe real-time system operation (Nataraja, Gorawar, Shilpa, & Harsha.J, 2012).
The medium-term load forecasting (MTLF) stretches up to several months, but long-term
load forecasts (LTLF) extend for a period up to 25 years and is used in decision making for
long term planning and capacity expansion (Weron, 2014). Forecasts with such long lead
times are required to account for the construction time of new generation capacity, allow the
responsible authority to implement energy policies and increase the conﬁdence of investors

1.2.1
Load Forecasting
Load forecasting is an integral part of the operation of power systems. A constant bal-
ance between supply and demand is required to avoid power disruption. The only matured
technology with a reasonable cumulative capacity and fast response time is pumped-hydro1,
yet limited by the capacity of a dam and the water level. The start-up time of conventional
power plants depend on the fuel used and the initial state. Considering that power plant
operators have been informed about the dispatch results, this process may still take up to 250
minutes (Parsons Brinckerhoﬀ, 2014). This clearly shows that the decisions about unit com-
mitment and dispatch have to be made in advance. In addition, some power plants may be
temporarily unavailable due to maintenance or experience a forced outage. This requires the
allocation of additional generation capacity and operational reserve that can substitute the
unavailable power plants. Considering these factors, the system operator must be equipped
with a load forecast in order to schedule the power plants.
STLF and LTLF have diﬀerent roles in the power system. The latter accounts for the grow-
ing energy consumption and the need of new generation capacity. Existing power plants
must be allowed to retire when their operation becomes economically infeasible. Capacity
expansion decisions have to be made well in advance considering economic, social, political
and technical factors. For example, in 2016 the UK approved the construction of Hinkley
Point nuclear power plant, but due to the long construction time, it is expected to come on-
line in 2025 (Gosden, 2016). The importance of demand forecasting cannot be overestimated
and so is the complexity of achieving a good forecast. Long-term forecasting often focuses
on macro-scale and is based on econometric approaches including factors such as population
growth, economic growth and changes in industry energy use. In a long-term, electricity
demand is non-linear and non-stationary. To certain extent demand has speciﬁc daily and
seasonal characteristics, aﬀected by the weather and calendar eﬀects.
STLF has a vital role to ensure the reliability and cost eﬀectiveness of the power system,
particularly in deregulated markets. An accurate forecast will assist system operators in unit
scheduling and allow them to deliver an optimum generator mix in terms of cost minimiza-
tion. Credible forecast can allow to reduce the required spinning reserve and reserve capacity
(Ceperic, Ceperic, & Baric, 2013). From distribution system operator’s perspective, a better
understanding about the characteristics of energy consumption in local areas can facilitate
the demand side management and reduce bottlenecks in the distribution network. From
retailers’ perspective, the knowledge about the customers behavior and expected power con-

1.2.2
Price Forecasting
A constant equilibrium between the power production and demand is required. This is
the main factor that makes the electricity market diﬀerent from other markets where imbal-
ance may be acceptable, at least in short-term. The power consumption, similarly to other
commodities is aﬀected by the price. An increase in price will reduce the demand and other
way around. However, electricity is a vital commodity ensuring that homes and businesses
run smoothly. This results in a slow response to an increase in price as manufacturing indus-
tries and commercial clients have to adjust their operations. Meanwhile, a ﬂat-fee tariﬀfor
residential customers gave little incentive to change the power consumption behavior. The
deregulation of electricity markets has enabled the competition for the rights to produce
electricity increasing the price volatility in short-term. Energy market participants try to
get a lower electricity price making decisions based on load and price forecasting (Ceperic
et al., 2013).
1.2.3
Renewables Generation Forecasting
Weather forecasts have always played a role in the energy demand forecasting. During
colder days people tend to use more heating, thus increasing the demand. Similarly, more air
conditioning is used in hot summer days. However, wind and solar irradiation forecasting at
minute-to-minute level is a relatively new ﬁeld in the energy forecasting due to the soaring
use of renewable energy sources in the power system. To cope with the intermittency of
renewables, extensive studies have been conducted to develop algorithms that could reduced
the uncertainty of renewables production. Here, more emphasis has been put on developing
probabilistic forecasting algorithms. Probabilistic forecasting relies on diﬀerent methods to
establish the probability of an event occurrence. In relation to load forecast, this approach
will explained more in Section 2.5 and applied for household load forecasting.
1.3
Exogenous Variables
For a forecasting model some predictor variables, also referred to as simply predictors or
features, must be deﬁned on which the variable of interest will depend. In this study the
variable of interest is household load. It may be also called the output or the forecast value.
The accuracy of the forecast model will be aﬀected by the quality of input data. Even the
best forecast model will not provide a good forecast if the data generation process contains
errors. This corresponds to the principle: ”Garbage in - garbage out”. The input variable for
a prediction model may be exogenous (external variables) or endogenous (forecast variable
itself). When examining an endogenous variable, one has to look into the past to see its

This makes time to be the most important factor in forecasting process (Fahad & Arbab,

1.3.1
Temperature
The dry bulb temperature is often simply recognized as the air temperature aﬀects our
level of comfort. If the temperature is below the individual comfort zone, we immediately
consider to turn on heating, thus increasing the demand. The same is true when temperature
is higher than our accepted levels and we turn on an air conditioner. Fahad et al. (2014)
found that there is high correlation between power demand and temperature above 25 de-
grees. Due to thermal storage eﬀects of buildings, the response to changes in the dry bulb
temperature is often lagged. When modeling the temperature eﬀect on load, the temperature
deviation from the historical average would only result in the load change (S. A.-h. Soliman
& Al-Kandari, 2010a). This is because the historical average is already considered in the
demand.
1.3.2
Wind and Humidity
The wind has a cooling eﬀect which increases with the wind speed and is inversely
proportional to the dry bulb temperature. Due to the this correlation between wind and
temperature, the wind has a stronger eﬀect on load demand in winter time and, thus, included
only in winter load models (S. A.-h. Soliman & Al-Kandari, 2010a). Other studies have
used wind chill factor to represent the eﬀect of wind to the load. The wind chill factor
is also related to the behavior as high wind chill factor makes people believe than the air
temperature is lower (Moghram & Rahman, 1989). Humidity, in contrary, becomes more
relevant during high temperatures. Higher humidity is associated with increased cooling

or as a dew-point temperature (S. A.-h. Soliman & Al-Kandari, 2010a). The dew point is
the highest temperature at which air can hold the water vapor with condensing it into liquid.
Precipitation has a dual eﬀect on the load. The direct consequences of rain is more people
staying indoors, thus, increasing the power consumption. At the same time, rain in a summer
day will reduce the air conditioning load. Fahad et al. (2014) stated that the exclusion of
precipitation from the short-term load forecast will increase the error.
1.3.3
Solar Irradiation and Cloud Cover
High cloud cover may increase the need for lighting and thus increase the electricity
consumption. Yet, the main reason of studying cloud cover on minute-by-minute bases is
to improve the solar irradiation forecast. While the amount of solar power can be easily
calculated for the given location, the weather, especially the type of clouds and the cover
rate, heavily aﬀects solar generation. Since this leads to voltage ﬂuctuations, a better forecast
of when the solar output ebbs or surges could increase the voltage stability. The challenge
with rooftop PV panels is that many installations are often made ”behind the meter”. This
conﬁguration does not allow utilities to distinguish between the power production of PV
panels and household consumption. Consequently, the availability of net generation data
from smart meters complicates the forecasting process if the goal is to provide load forecast
or solar forecast separately.
1.4
The Future Ahead
The falling costs of battery storage and the rise of the era of electric vehicles require
a better understanding of the changes in power demand these technologies deliver. While
the study by Australian Electricity Market Operator (AEMO) shows that the total resi-
dential power consumption will remain at current levels, it emphasizes the need for more
granularity in forecast models (AEMO, 2016). The falling costs of battery storage will make
batteries an attractive option in combination with solar PV, further reducing the household
power feed from the grid. More studies have recently focused on household load forecasting
questioning what methods and knowledge are required to improve the forecast (Javed et al.,
2012). A better forecast can be achieved only by aggregating the load of all households and
constructing a single forecasting model, instead of using disaggregated data (Javed et al.,
2012). However, today we are in the midst of a paradigm shift toward distributed energy
generation. The future belongs to the battery storage and distributed generators. In order
to optimize the operation of such system, load forecasting will play an important role.

1.4.1
Autonomous home energy systems
During afternoon, when the sun is at its highest, household with rooftop PV panels often
generate more electricity than consume. By feeding this electricity back into the grid, less
power is required from centralized conventional power plants. In addition, distributed gen-
eration has a network value since the generators are located in proximity to loads reducing
the distance for electricity transfers. Until the end of the year 2016, most solar PV own-
ers in Australia received a generous feed-in tariﬀof 60c/kWh. This was the Government’s
subsidy toward the Renewable Energy Target (Dundas, Hyslop, & Soderbaum, 2013). How-
ever, now solar customers are left under the retailers’ feed-in tariﬀof 11 c/kWh (Essential
Services Commission Victoria, 2016). This is signiﬁcantly lower than the retail electricity
price (Dundas et al., 2013) and can be even lower than the price per kWh in the wholesale
electricity market during peak hours. Several business models have gained more attention
recently that could provide a better economic deal for households. And they all involve some
level of a short-term load and power generation forecasting.
The anticipated drop in battery storage costs and rising electricity price will facilitate bat-
tery uptake. To maximize the cost and energy beneﬁts the storage can provide, it requires
the planning of the home energy system operation. The battery operation will be adjusted
depending on the expected solar generation and demand on the next day (Bacher et al.,
2009). An increased accuracy of forecasting will play a signiﬁcant role in optimizing utiliza-
tion of storage facilities. It is most likely that the power optimization will happen within the
household’s boundary requiring an energy management system. Regardless if the household
have an application to store data and run the computational model for every forecast or
the management system is cloud-based and run by the utility or retailer, this requires large
computational capability (Javed et al., 2012). Then, a trade oﬀbetween the computation
burden and the forecast accuracy is required. The costs of more expensive operation system
should not overweight the gains from an improved forecast accuracy (Iwafune et al., 2014).
Therefore, it is important to understand if forecasting at ﬁner granularity results in a better
forecast or a coarser granularity should be preferred. The questions remain how to maximize
the beneﬁt from the excessive power and here are two rising opportunities.

into a single asset. This provides greater ﬂexibility and improves operational decision mak-

called the Small Generator Aggregator (SGA), because it gathers the excessive energy from
rooftop PV customers and operates with this energy on behalf of households. One such

Another promising concept for household PV owners among other types of distributed

control the electron ﬂow physically, you can decide where it goes.

2
Literature Review
The importance of load forecasting has grown together with the development of new
forecasting methods and the combination of existing methods. It is diﬃcult to draw strong
lines between the methods and it can be found that various authors categorize them in
diﬀerent groups.
Techniques for forecasting have been progressed from linear regression
(LR) and autoregressive moving average methods to Neural Networks (NN) and support
vector machines (SVM). By progressive it is not necessarily meant better, rather progressive
describes the advantages and opportunities brought by information technology into the ﬁeld
of forecasting.
Soliman & Al-Kandari (2010) states that a good load forecasting model
should have ﬁve qualities:
• Adaptiveness - the forecasting model should be able update its dataset and recompute
the estimates;
• Recursiveness - as some input data for the forecast may change (such as weather), the
model should be able to correct the load prediction for the following steps;
• Computational Economy - depending on the application, there should be a determined
trade-oﬀbetween the accuracy of the forecast and the execution time;
• Robustness - forecast models should provide reasonable output even if erroneous data
are used as input or the model is predicting for the conditions it was not designed for;
• Accuracy - a perfect forecast for multiple forecasting intervals is very unlikely due to
the uncertainty of the future events. However, each forecasting model should attempt
to minimize the error and tend toward a higher accuracy.
The accuracy of forecast models plays the main role as it enables to juxtapose various
forecasting methods as well as compare diﬀerent situations and forecasting strategies. One
of thesis objectives is to implement various forecasting techniques, compare them and analyze
the performance for a day-ahead household load forecasts.
2.1
Short-Term Load Forecasting Techniques
Some forecasting techniques can be applied to diﬀerent problems and forecasting periods,
but, in general, diﬀerent forecasting horizons require diﬀerent methods (Weron, 2014). A
number of studies apply several forecasting models facilitating a better understanding of
pros and cons of each technique. The following section will introduce the basic principles of
commonly used methods. A persistence method is probably the simplest model of forecasting
as it assumes that the value of interest is equal to the last observation. Such approach uses a
persistence model and is relatively accurate in short-term forecasting (up to several forecast

Time series method consists of consecutive data points measured at usually uniform

2.1.4
Neural Networks
Neural Networks consists of an interconnected group of nodes, often called neurons. This
is a popular technique in load forecasting used by (Beccali, Cellura, Lo Brano, & Marvuglia,
2008), (Rodrigues, Cardeira, & Calado, 2014), and (Javed et al., 2012) among others. Al-
though the eﬀect that diﬀerent weather conditions have on load consumption is understood,
Neural Networks can analyze the nonlinear relationships between the exogenous variables
and load, as well as ﬁnd new patterns in historical load data. In short-term load forecast-
ing, a multilayer feed-forward model is often used due to the complex relationships between
exogenous and endogenous variables. The use of multiple layers enables extrapolation of
non-linear patterns from the input data. There are three types of layers:
• The input layer stores one or more independent variables that are processed in the
hidden layer.
• The output layer gives the results for the variable of interest, a load forecast in this
case.
• The main idea behind the Neural Networks is that independent variables are ﬁltered
through the hidden layer before they reach the output layer. The nodes of each adjacent
layer are directly connected via a link. This link has a weight factor that describes the
level of signiﬁcance between the connected nodes.
The term feed-forward is referred to each node in a layer receiving a certain level of input
from all nodes in the previous layer. The node input depends on the sum of weight factors
assigned to each node in the previous layer connected through links. While the number
of nodes in the input and output layers is known, it is required to estimate the number
of nodes in the hidden layer. Yang et al (2013) used Hetch-Nelson’s method presented in
Eq. 13, where a is the number of nodes in the input layer J.

Support Vector Regression Machines
Another artiﬁcial intelligence technique often applied in load forecasting is Support Vec-
tor Machines (SVM). The concept is based on supervised learning methods. Similar to neural
networks, the SVM can successfully solve linear and nonlinear problems. The advantage of
the SVR is that it always provide globally optimal solution for nonlinear problems, while
Neural Networks may converge within a local minima (Hussain, M, & Ali Shah, 2014). An-

risk minimization (SRM) principle that attempts to minimize the upper bound of the gener-
alization error (Hu, Bao, & Xiong, 2013), whereas Neural Networks minimize the empirical
risk. Yet, a high performance with SVR can be achieved only with an adequate parameter
setting. The SVM technique has been implemented in load forecasting by (Humeau, Wijaya,

2.5
Probabilistic Forecasts
Traditional forecasting methods are based on deterministic techniques that gives a single
value for each forecast period. Deterministic forecasts are also known as point forecasts
and consist of a single forecast which is the best estimate of the full range of potential
outcomes. A variety of decision-making problems today are addressed with probabilistic
forecasts (Pinson & Madsen, 2009). Probabilistic forecasting relies on diﬀerent methods to
establish the probability of an event occurrence. In probabilistic forecasting, the observation
is expected to be within the range of estimated probability called the nominal coverage rate.
While deterministic and probabilistic forecasting methods try to predict events, only proba-
bilistic forecasting considers the uncertainty of the prediction results. Probabilistic forecasts
have been utilized for applications such as weather and climate predictions, ﬂood risk assess-
ment as well as economic and ﬁnancial risk management for decades (Gneiting & Katzfuss,
2014). The intermittent nature of renewable energy generation has also drawn attention to
probabilistic forecasting. Probabilistic forecast ensemble was developed for wind power gen-
eration by (Pinson, Kariniotakis, Nielsen, Nielsen, & Madsen, 2006). Bootstrap statistical
ensemble for one-hour ahead PV-system output forecasting was developed by (Grantham,
Gel, & Boland, 2016).
The main components of probabilistic forecasting:
• reliability (or calibration) assesses the consistency between the forecast probability
and the corresponding distribution of observations. Ideally, the empirical coverage of
forecasts should tend to the nominal coverage (Pinson et al., 2006);
• sharpness (or dispersion) estimates the width of the probability variations.

The primary goal is to guarantee that the observation lies within the forecast interval and
the second goal is to maximize the sharpness of the predictive distribution, where sharpness
can be understood as the concentration of forecasts (Pinson et al., 2006).
In load forecasting studies, probabilistic approach has been applied to much lesser extent
than deterministic forecasting. Even less studies have investigated probabilistic load fore-
casting methods for smart meter data from residential customers. Since a single household
load consumption is more volatile than the aggregated load pattern, it is more challenging
to obtain an accurate forecast using deterministic forecasts. Thus, it may be beneﬁcial to
look at the entire distribution of the future load instead of giving a point forecast.
Probabilistic forecasts can be divided into parametric and non-parametric approaches. The
former assumes that the distribution of forecast follows some known distribution patterns
such as Gaussian and the associated model is based on ﬁxed number of parameters. Such
approach was used by (Gruber & Prodanovic, 2012) to develop energy consumption proﬁles
for households based on the energy demand contribution of each household’s appliances. In
reality, the density of load forecasts may not follow any distinct distribution as it was found
by (Grantham et al., 2016) studying very short-term forecasting on solar energy generation.
Then, non-parametric approach can be used instead to ﬁt the data applying methods such as
kernel density estimation and quantile regression (QR). When the data does not ﬁt distribu-
tion assumptions or the data shows that there are periods of low variance and high variance,
QR can give a better ﬁt because there is ﬂexible distributional assumptions (Taieb, Huser,
Hyndman, & Genton, 2016).
The majority of studies using probabilistic forecasting approach focus on renewable energy
generation, such as wind and solar generation forecasting. This can be explained by the
fact that wind and solar are intermittent energy sources and carry a notable level of uncer-
tainty. The operation of power systems can be negatively aﬀected if this fact is not properly
addressed. On the other hand, load generation, historically, has involved little uncertainty
when considering the aggregated demand. An increased eﬀort to facilitate demand side man-
agement at disaggregated level has fostered more research in load forecasting of individual
customers. At this level, the load ﬂuctuations are much higher as electric appliances are be-
ing turned on and oﬀ, sometimes in very irregular and even random pattern. To account for
this uncertainty, probabilistic load forecast can be applied to cover the nominal range of load.
The contribution of residential customers to voltage imbalance at low-voltage level was stud-
ied by (Moller & Meyer, 2016) using probabilistic analysis on peak load occurrence. Taieb
et al. (2016) state that most publications on forecasting energy demand have developed

2.6
Forecasting Strategies and Implementation
Choosing an application relevant forecasting strategy may improve the accuracy of the
forecasting model. There are various methods being used in the development process of fore-
casting models. The study by (T¨urkay & Demren, 2011) included seven dummy predictors
that indicated the day of week and seven predictors containing the load from each day of the
previous week. The same approach was used by (Ceperic et al., 2013) with the addition of
binary values for each month. The load for the same hour of the previous day, the previous
week and the previous hour of the same day are commonly used (Mazengia, 2008). Various
studies also try to combine and experiment with diﬀerent combinations of the historical load
(Kumar, 2016), (Samanta et al., 2014).
Other studies have added a larger variation of lagged load values including the same hour of
the previous day t-24 (when using 1-hour forecast intervals), the average of t-23, t-24, t-25,
the load of the same our from two days ago t-48, the previous week t-144 and the average
of previous seven days for the same hour of the day (T¨urkay & Demren, 2011). During
holidays the load tend to be lower, therefore if the forecast is made for a holiday, the average
load from previous holidays for each hour could be used (B.-J. Chen, Chang, & Lin, 2004).
An extensive study of how to implement holiday days and weekends was done by (Hong,
2010). In his work, it was found that there are two few holidays in the year to separate
them from weekends and weekdays. In addition, Hong (2010) stated that each holiday has
diﬀerent traditions surrounding it leading to diﬀerent load consumption. It can be expected
that for a single household level, this variation of load patterns during holiday days is even
more signiﬁcant than at the utility level. Further, three diﬀerent forecasting strategies are
described:
• Sliding Window approach considers the dataset as continuous time series and creates a
subset from the original dataset. The forecast model is ﬁtted to this subset (window),
validated, and evaluated against the test set. Then the subset is moved forward by a

user-set sliding length. If the goal is to provide a day-ahead load forecast, a sliding
window of one day is a good choice (Veit et al., 2014). For a given dataset this repeated
until all possible subsets have been tested against the test set. If new information
becomes available, it can be added to the initial dataset removing the same length of
the oldest data simultaneously (Fux, Benz, Ashouri, & Guzzella, 2013).
• Countrary to Sliding Window strategy, Similar Days approach builds subset based on
corresponding calendar eﬀects. Day type strategy uses cross-sectional data splitting
the data-set into the same days of the week and training the model for those days,
while leaving the last part of dataset for the evaluation. For example, a subset could
include only weekend days. Then, the forecast model is trained on this subset. If any
of the test set days occurs on a weekend, this forecast model will be used to provide
a load forecast. Similarly. another subset could be made using only weekdays. This
approach was used by (Veit et al., 2014). Similarly, the data could be split by the time
of day or the month of year.
• Hierarchical Forecasting strategy is used when load data are available at various levels,
for example, geographic hierarchy or temporal hierarchy.
The former utilizes load
measurements from multiple sources within a given area. Temporal hierarchy is based
on various sampling frequency of data (Hong & Shahidehpour, 2015). If smart meter
data are available for individual customers, this gives utilities a better understanding
of load composition at aggregated level (Hong & Shahidehpour, 2015).
Increasing
deployment of smart meters has provided researchers with data from single household
appliances. For household load forecasting, hierarchical day type strategy is based on
the bottom-up approach were the forecasts for individual appliances are combined to
form the aggregated household demand (Veit et al., 2014).

The periodic nature of load makes the past values of load the most important factor

forecast with 30-minute forecast intervals yt+1, ..., yt+48, made at time t. The following load
features were incorporated in the training process of the forecast model:
• L1 - load value from the same half-hour of the previous day of the same day type.
Hence, yt−48 for Tue, Wed, Thu, Fri, and Sun. yt−144 for Mon and yt−288 for Sat;
• L2 - the average of the same day and the same half-hour of the previous three weeks
(yt−336 + yt−672 + yt−1008)/3;
• L3 - the average of the same half-hour of the previous seven days (yt−48+...+yt−336)/7;
• L4 - the average load of the previous day (yt−1 + ... + yt−48)/48;

splits at each stage for a set of predictors. The best splitting point for each predictor is the
point that gives the lowest sum of square errors. The overﬁtting of the training data for a
single tree was avoided by creating a regression tree ensemble using bootstrap sampling with
replacement and using the mean of the ensemble predictions as the load forecast. Instead of
optimizing a single Regression Tree, bootstrap aggregated decision trees take the advantage

the cross-validation error and the number of leaf nodes. Cross validation error was minimized

4.4
Forecasting Granularity
All forecast models performed better when granularity is changed from 30-minute to 60-
minute to 120-minutes. This was true for all forecasting techniques. Between 30-minute and
120-minute granularities RMSE of scenario S1 for MLR dropped by 0.103 kWh, for Regres-
sion Trees by 0.117 kWh, for SVR by 0.116 kWh, and for Neural Networks 0.112 kWh. It
was also noticed that the forecast error reduced more between 60-minute and 120-minute
granularity than between 30-minute and 60-minute granularity.
This can be explained as being due to the smoothing of load ﬂuctuations. For example,
a power intensive electric oven may be used for 30 minutes in total each morning between
7am and 9am in a household with four students. At 30-minute forecast granularity the time
of occurrence and the magnitude of peak load will be diﬀerent for each 30-minute interval.
If 120-minute granularity is used instead, the peak will be always observed at the same
two-hour block. The forecast model will capture this load and learn to predict it. With 30-
minute forecast granularity this peak event may occur in any of adjacent half-hour intervals
from day to day hindering the predictability of the peak. To minimize the forecast error
at 30-minute granularity, all forecast techniques will act in a conservative manner. Hence,
the forecast models will issue a forecast value close to the average between a peak occurring
and not occurring for that forecast period. This explains why 60 and 120-minute granularity
gives better, but not much better results.

The forecast granularity had a similar eﬀect on NRMSE. Between 30-minute an 120-minute
granularities NRMSE of scenario S1 for MLR dropped by 0.766 (27.5%), for regression trees
by 0.881 (37%), for SVR by 0.551 (31%), and for neural networks 1.073 (39%). Neural
Networks has the largest improvement in accuracy at lower granularity. The smoothing of
low load values (mostly night time) reduced the complexity of forecasting for these periods.
4.5
Length of training period
The eﬀect of changing the length of training dataset can be seen in Figures 17 and 18. On
the contrary to the expected, a longer training dataset has a negligible eﬀect on the forecast
accuracy. Figure 17 shows that the RMSE of MLR with 3 years of data S5 reduced against
S59 only by 0.001 kWh, but for scenario S1 against S55 by 0.005 kWh. The diﬀerence
between the corresponding scenarios for SVR was 0.009 and 0.012 kWh respectively. For
Neural Networks the diﬀerence between the same scenarios was 0 and 0.01 kWh. The length
of the dataset play a more important role for Neural Networks at 120-minute granularity
where the error reduction is clearly seen with a growing length of the dataset. This suggests
that a critical point is reached where the training model simply do not have enough infor-
mation to provide an adequate forecast. This notion is further supported when looking at
the low RMSE diﬀerence between S18 and S27 for NN.
There is a potential notion toward a lower error when the training dataset is increased
for Regression Trees and SVR. As mentioned, the length below 1 year play a large role in
the accuracy for Regression Trees. The advantage of longer dataset tends to increase when
reducing the forecast granularity to 60 minutes and 120 minutes. This is better seen for
Neural Networks and SVR. It is very likely that additional historical information improves
the ability to predict the peak load. However, it is also fair to say that one year of historical
data is suﬃcient to develop a household load forecast.
Besides performing worse, MLR also did not show the same results when increasing the
length of the dataset. The lowest RMSE scores was achieved when using two years of data.
The importance of MLR lagged-load predictors were higher for some households with two
years of data. This is aﬀected by the changes in the average hourly power consumption as
in in households H 4 (-0.18 kWh, -15%), H 22 (-0.16 kWh, -27%), H 24 (-0.34 kWh, -24%) or
due to permanent changes in the daily load pattern as in H 16.
When analyzing how the length of dataset aﬀects NRMSE values (Figure 18), there is no
evidence that longer datasets reduce NRMSE score. It can be seen that the scores for Regres-
sion Trees and SVR remain ﬂat for diﬀerent lengths of dataset whether calendar variables

4.6
Calendar Eﬀects
The RMSE results show that calendar eﬀects have a very low predictive power for the
residential load forecast. In fact, none of the forecast models with dummy variables showed
a consistent forecast improvement against the scenario without calendar eﬀects. Assigning
dummy variables for weekdays and weekends as well as for diﬀerent seasons did not improve
the forecast. A slightly lower RMSE is achieved when including all calendar eﬀects (S1,
S10,S19). At 30-minute granularity, S1 scenario with all dummy variables were 0.007 kWh
lower than S5 that excludes calendar eﬀects. This diﬀerence was 0.01 kWh for Regression
Trees and Neural Networks. MLR performed worse when all calendar eﬀects were included.
This suggests that stepwise linear regression assigns too high coeﬃcients for dummy variables
overestimating their importance or it could be that the test weeks have some unique load
consumption characteristics that lead to a higher error. Essentially, the same pattern was
acquired for 60-minute and 120-minute granularity, except, at 120-minute granularity Neural
Networks performed worse with calendar eﬀects (S19 vs S23). While this may have hap-
pened due to over-ﬁtting during training process, it is more likely that the reduced number
of data points at this granularity negatively aﬀect how Neural Networks assigns the weights
for predictor variables.
The addition of all calendar eﬀects slightly improved NRMSE. Figure 17, scenarios S9,
S18, and S27 show that a forecast based on seasonal subsets has a higher RMSE for all
forecasting techniques and all granularities. Subsetting by time of the day and day of the
week yielded signiﬁcantly worse results than subsetting by season. While joining consecutive
input data into subsets according to a similar type of calendar eﬀects creates groups with
more distinguished load and weather characteristics, the irregularity of the peak loads led
to larger RMSE and NRMSE than when using a single dataset.

Reference Scenarios. Pref0 and Pref1 were expected to serve as benchmark models. How-

cannot be achieved without historical load data.
Quantile Regression (QR) Scenarios. Interesting results can be seen when looking at P2
in comparison with P3 to P5. Knowing that P2 is a model without weather predictors, its
score of 651.2 is better than expected. It is nearly as good as 647.7 for Hong’s model P3 and
650.7 for P4, which includes the weather data, but excludes calendar eﬀects. This raises a
question, why weather parameters have such a negligible role in probabilistic forecasting. It
has to be mentioned that Hong originally developed and applied his model for deterministic
forecasts at utility level. The use of 3rd order polynomials for predictors is a good approach
at utility-scale forecasting, where the relationship between the load and temperature is much
stronger. At single household level, the variety of consumption behavior hinder the ability to
see this eﬀect. In addition, several households showed no sign of an increased consumption
in winter for heating, suggesting that gas heating systems are present. (Hong, 2010) also
studied polynomial regression models including previous few load observations, but this was
not applicable for this study.
Comparing QR scenarios P4 and P5, it can be seen that calendar eﬀects in P5 are beneﬁcial
and work toward a lower error score. Calendar eﬀects implemented as dummy variables also
slightly outperformed the Hong’s model.

niﬁcantly outperformed QR. All scenarios P6 to P12 performed better. We start analysis
with P9 scenario that is an ensemble of MLR point forecasts. Since MLR structure is similar
to Quantile Regression, it could be said that QRA approach should be preferred for proba-
bilistic forecasts as it provides a better score. Considering ensembles made of diﬀerent point

Winkler score. This is in contrast to deterministic forecasting, where SVR had equally good
results with Regression Trees. It may be that one of ﬁve point forecasts that builds up the
ensemble for SVR is an outlier that aﬀects the average performance and, thus, damage the
results. In addition, following the success in deterministic forecasting, these results make
Regression Trees potentially the most desirable technique for household load forecasting in
given settings.

5
Discussion
Calendar eﬀects are implemented in most load forecasting models. At a utility scale a
lower power consumption during weekends can be observed in the industrial and commercial
sectors. Changes in heating and cooling demand can be associated with seasonal changes.
The residential sector experiences the peak in the evenings when people return home. Cal-
endar variables are usually implemented in the same manner regardless of the application.
Since the forecast error is higher for individual households, calendar eﬀects are often seen
as an addition that improves the forecast. In contrast to expectations, the use of dummy
variables for certain calendar periods, outlined in this study, added little or no predictive
power to the forecast model. An experiment with additional dummy variables for each hour
of day, day of week, and month of year reduced the NRMSE score for several households.
Since adding up to 33 new predictors (24 instead of 5 for hours, 7 instead of 2 for days,
and 12 instead 3 for months) requires longer computational time, the initial set of variables
can be used. However, if the goal is to provide a load forecast for only one household, both
variations of dummy variables should be examined.
Load aggregation reduces the load variance and unpredictable spikes. It was found that
clustering signiﬁcantly improved the forecast accuracy due to the load smoothing eﬀect.
However, calendar eﬀects still played a minor role. A case study could be developed to
observe if further increase in the number of households in a cluster would increase the im-
portance of calendar eﬀects, thus, contributing to a lower error.
In general, splitting the original dataset in subsets based on the part of week and the time
of day was not a success. This approach lead to a worse performance than scenarios with
dummy variables. The main contributors to the error were evening and morning periods.
However, the subsetting for night hours performed well. A suggestion could be to replace
the load forecast for night hours of any forecast scenario with the results from the subsetting
scenario. This approach would then slightly reduce the overall error for the given household.
More work could also be done to look on how the forecast techniques could be combined to
reduce the overall error. For example. SVR had the best prediction of low load values, while
Regression Trees had less and lower errors during peak load.
Forecast Granularity. As expected, the forecasting error reduced with coarsening forecast
granularity. This is an important ﬁnding for the management of a household PV-battery
system. Besides the traditional ﬂat electricity tariﬀfor residential customers, retailers and
utilities now oﬀer a time-of-use (ToU) tariﬀscheme for households load consumption as well
as for their power generation (Essential Services Commission Victoria, 2016). The decision
73
==PAGE:83==
Master Thesis
Peter Lusis
of when to charge and discharge the battery depends on the energy consumption and produc-
tion during each price-block and the price level for the corresponding block. Thus, the the
improved forecast accuracy from a lower granularity is adequate and desirable for optimal
battery utilization.
The amount of available data. The results showed that changing the length of the training
dataset between one and three years does not improve the forecast accuracy. This suggests
that one year of data is enough to develop a load forecast model for a residential customer,
but more data may be beneﬁcial for some households. For instance, if a household uses an
air conditioner a few times a year, its usage pattern may only become clear from multiple
years’ worth of data. Only MLR was sensitive to the changes in the training dataset. It was
found that in several households the mean and peak load had changed after the ﬁrst year.
The simpler MLR algorithm estimates predictor coeﬃcients using all data points. Thus, the
best length of training set with MLR should be determined manually. Correlation analysis
and visualization of historical load can help to choose the appropriate length of the dataset.
Other techniques were robust to the variations in the length of historical data between one
and three years. However, subsetting showed that there is a critical amount of data for Re-
gression Trees and Neural Networks when these techniques perform worse. Since the number
of data points depends on the length of dataset and the sampling rate, the minimum required
data could be determined for diﬀerent granularities. In practice, rarely less than one year of
data are available for the studies. But for new projects, the fact that there is no data at the
beginning cannot be avoided.
The results also showed that the current forecast models mispredicted the peak load. If
accurate peaks in individual houses are of interest, then new forecasting techniques must be
tailored to that task. However, it is very unlikely that much progress can be achieved in
household load forecasting due to frequent but irregular peaks. It may also not be impor-
tant to predict exact time of the occurrence of peak load, but the magnitude. For battery
optimization, the total load over several hours may be of importance only. (Haben, Ward,
Vukadinovic Greetham, Singleton, & Grindrod, 2014) looked at this problem suggesting a

some ﬂexibility in the timing of the load forecast when evaluating the forecast accuracy.

better for next hour load forecasting. The highest RMSE for MLR of 0.561 for 30-minute

Computation Time. Computational time of any forecast model is important for utilities serv-
ing large numbers of customers. If the online training is used, where the load observations
from each day are added to the training dataset and the model is retrained, computational
time also plays a crucial role (Cheng et al., 2013). When conducting a load forecast for mul-
tiple households, the computational time depends on the number of households, forecasting
horizon and the selected forecasting technique. In this study, parallel computations greatly
reduced the computational time. The load forecasting process in any household is not af-
fected by the forecast for other households, but depends on the predictors only. This makes
iterations independent of each other and enables the distribution of households among pro-
cessor cores. The computational time is reduced proportionally to the number of available
workers. This was true for all techniques except Neural Networks. It was not possible to
carry out parallel computing for Neural Networks. This is due to a fact that a unique Neural
Network with diﬀerent weights and bias factors was trained and applied for each household
and for each scenario. Matlab Neural Network Toolbox permits to train only one forecasting
model at time. The computation time of MLR for the reference model S1 was 14 seconds
versus 247 seconds for Regression Trees and 2015 seconds for neural networks conducted on
Intel i7-4712MQ 2.3GHz processor with 12GB of RAM. While computational time is not in
the focus of this study, the results show that model run-time may be an important factor
when choosing between forecasting techniques with a similar performance.
Probabilistic Forecasting. Providing probabilistic forecasts is important for a modern power
system with a large number of stochastic power production and energy storage. Probabilistic
forecasting also facilitates the load forecasting in the residential sector. The results showed
that deterministic forecasts based on the average estimated load can yield signiﬁcant errors
completely misrepresenting the actual situation. For example, this can result in suboptimal
battery system management. Two non-parametric approaches were implemented here to
achieve a good coverage rate of observations. When historical load data were included, QR
yielded lower error scores than reference models based on the historical load distribution.
However, QR was outperformed by QRA.
The advantage of QRA during extraordinary periods were presented in a case study with
household H 21. To address the issue with not covering low load values, the lowest QR quan-
tiles (like for 5% and 10]%) could be added to QRA model, but with reduced weight factor.
This would allow a QRA model to recognize the probability of low load values during day
when high load consumption is expected according to point forecasts.

solve QR was ineﬃcient for a large number of observations. The standard form of linear pro-

all households is load + weather parameters + dummies, close to 20. Using one year of data

various legth of training data and various granularity were not considered with QR. For large

approximated (C. Chen, 2007). Another method that could deal with the size of this prob-

Conclusions
Calendar eﬀects and historical load data are being deployed in most residential load
studies as a self-explanatory factor. This paper demonstrated how various calendar eﬀects
and load forecast granularity aﬀect the accuracy of the day-ahead household load forecast
considering diﬀerent forecasting techniques. It was found that certain dummy variables rep-
resenting calendar eﬀects have little predictive power. The approach with splitting of the
original dataset into subsets according to similar calendar periods yielded even lower fore-
cast accuracy. The two proposed implementation approaches failed here because the use of
historical load data as predictors extracted all the information, thus making calendar vari-
ables redundant. However, more sophisticated ways of using calendar information may still
yield improvements. In any forecasting model only signiﬁcant predictors should be used to
minimize the required computational power and time. While calendar eﬀects do not require
external data, this study shows that calendar eﬀects become more important if the historical
load is not available.
Increasing forecast intervals from 30-minutes to 60 and 120-minutes reduced the forecast
error.
Regression trees, support vector regression and neural networks yielded an aver-
age RMSE between 0.516 and 0.531 kWh at 30-minute forecast granularity. Support vector
regression had the lowest NRMSE value showing a better overall ability to predict the house-
hold load for the next 24 hours. Computational time should also be used when selecting
a forecasting technique. The scenario analysis also showed that one year of historical load
data can be suﬃcient to develop a load forecast since a longer dataset did not improve the
forecast accuracy. However, less data may be insuﬃcient to develop a good forecast model
based on Regression Trees and Neural Networks as seen from the week performance of sub-
setting approach.
Quantile Regression and Quantile Regression Averaging were used to provide probabilis-
tic forecasts for the same period. The latter yielded signiﬁcantly lower error score for all
scenarios.
This means that point forecasts are very beneﬁcial to develop a probabilistic
forecast model. Further, historical load by far is the most important factor in probabilistic
forecasting for residential customers. An ensemble consisting of multiple forecasting tech-
niques, but the same predictors performs better than an ensemble built on a single forecasting
technique, but with a combination of diﬀerent sets of predictors. Finally, increasing number
of point forecasts can further improve the accuracy of probabilistic forecasts.
It is also concluded that for a day-ahead household load forecast with given predictors
Regression Trees performed the best on overall and is better in capturing the erratic load

The visualization of the daily load pattern for each of study households facilitates the

and information about household demographics limits the information that can be extracted
