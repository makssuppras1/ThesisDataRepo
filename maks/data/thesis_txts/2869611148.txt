Abstract 
 
The growing need for private and protected online communications has spurred the 
widespread use of Anonymous Communication Networks (ACNs), such as Tor and I2P. 
However, these networks struggle with high latency issues, making them unsuitable for 
real-time applications. This thesis tackles the latency problem in ACNs by proposing 
Quantum Latency-Optimized Layered Routing (QLLR), a novel routing optimization 
technique that utilizes quantum-inspired networking principles to boost efficiency while 
preserving anonymity. The research commences with a thorough examination of existing 
ACN architectures, including Onion Routing, Garlic Routing, Mixnets, and blockchain-
based ACNs. It pinpoints the main sources of latency, such as multi-hop encryption, relay 
point congestion, and suboptimal path selection. To combat these issues, QLLR 
implements quantum fidelity-aware relay selection and adaptive routing, enhancing path 
efficiency while maintaining the essential probabilistic randomness for anonymity. A 
comprehensive theoretical framework is developed, incorporating quantum link quality, 
entanglement fidelity, and network congestion factors to dynamically adjust routing 
paths. Mathematical models grounded in Werner state fidelity and entanglement-assisted 
communication form the basis for calculating latency reductions in QLLR. The model is 
then implemented and tested using QuNetSim, a quantum network simulation platform, 
integrating real-world Tor network metrics to ensure practical relevance. Simulation 
outcomes reveal that QLLR substantially decreases latency compared to traditional ACN 
routing methods. This proposed approach achieves reduced average and median latencies, 
improves load distribution across relays, and enhances scalability in high-traffic 
networks. Unlike current methods such as ShorTor and QuicTor, which introduce 
vulnerabilities through predictable relay selection or require extensive protocol 
modifications, QLLR maintains strong anonymity guarantees while offering a scalable 
and gradual upgrade to existing Tor networks. This thesis establishes QLLR as a viable 
solution for future anonymous communication systems, addressing the balance between 
security, privacy, and efficiency in ACNs. Subsequent research will explore quantum 
error correction techniques, the integration of post-quantum cryptography, and the 
practical implementation of QLLR in decentralized ACN infrastructures.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
 
1.1 Background 
 
In the contemporary era of pervasive surveillance, the concept of digital privacy has 
become increasingly elusive. Nevertheless, certain technologies persist in safeguarding 
the integrity of transmitted information. One such approach is the utilization of 
Anonymous Communication Networks (ACN), which employ multi-layered encryption 
and indirect routing to protect user data and communications within compromised 
network infrastructures. The widespread presence of ACNs has increased substantially 
with the proliferation of digital monitoring. 
 
Although ACNs offer numerous advantages, they also present a significant limitation: 
high latency. This delay adversely affects real-time applications, including Voice over IP 
(VoIP) and video conferencing. Balancing security and efficiency remain a critical 
challenge in ACN design. 
 
The considerable latency in ACNs is primarily attributable to multi-hop encryption, 
network congestion, and suboptimal path-selection algorithms. Protocols, such as Onion 
Routing (Tor) and Garlic Routing (I2P), provide robust anonymity by utilizing multiple 
encryption layers and routing traffic through several nodes before reaching its 
destination. While these methods effectively conceal user identities, they result in 
substantial delays. These delays are caused by the increased computational and 
communication requirements associated with multiple encryption-decryption cycles and 
routing through various geographically dispersed relay nodes. 
 
1.2 Problem Statement 
 
The widespread adoption of ACNs is impeded by latency issues, particularly in contexts 
that necessitate rapid responses. The primary challenge lies in enhancing performance 
without compromising privacy. Elevated latency restricts the utility of ACNs to a limited 
set of applications, such as basic web browsing, electronic mail, and file transfers. 
However, their inability to manage real-time communications causes a conflict between 
privacy requirements and user experience demands. 
 
What strategies can be employed to optimize ACN routing, thereby reducing latency, 
while maintaining security and anonymity?

Addressing this challenge necessitates a comprehensive investigation of latency sources, 
an evaluation of the efficacy of current routing techniques, and the development of 
innovative optimization approaches that enhance performance without compromising the 
fundamental principles of user privacy and anonymity. 
 
1.3 Research Motivation 
 
With increasing limitations on digital rights and online privacy, there is a growing need 
for reliable and secure anonymous communication methods. Whereas traditional 
networks prioritize speed and efficiency, Anonymous Communication Networks (ACNs) 
must strike a balance between security measures and operational performance. Improving 
these networks would enhance accessibility for individuals concerned with data privacy, 
including journalists, activists, whistleblowers, and everyday users. 
 
Furthermore, the emergence of quantum computing technology and advanced traffic 
analysis techniques poses a new challenge to conventional privacy protection methods. It 
is essential to explore more efficient routing mechanisms to ensure that ACNs are 
protected against potential security vulnerabilities and can effectively support real-time 
applications.

2.2 Anonymous Communication Network Architectures 
 
Anonymous Communication Networks (ACNs) function by encrypting users' information 
and routing it through multiple points to obscure the connection between the origin and 
destination. Various ACN designs have been created over the years, each employing 
distinct techniques to maintain anonymity while addressing different speed and efficiency 
issues.

2.2.1 Onion Routing (Tor) 
 
Fig 2.1: A Tor circuit setup. Tor relays are represented as Onions. This is a three-node 
setup [13]. 
 
Among Anonymous Communication Networks (ACNs), Tor (The Onion Router) is the 
most widely used system. It employs a layered encryption method for transmitting 
messages through a series of at least three randomly selected relays [16]. As illustrated in 
Fig. 2.1, each relay in the circuit decrypts only the outermost layer of the encryption, 
revealing the next relay in the sequence before forwarding the message. This architecture 
ensures that no single relay has knowledge of both the origin and destination of the 
transmitted data [9]. 
 
When using the Tor browser, users may notice significant latency issues. This delay 
begins with the establishment of a circuit, which necessitates multiple rounds of key 
exchange and encryption negotiation, resulting in an initial lag. 
==PAGE:11==
9 | Please go to Appendix E for Simulation Codes. 
 
 
 
 
Fig 2.2: The Onion encryption and decryption visualized layer by layer [17]. 
 
Once the circuit is established, the subsequent source of delay stems from the encryption 
and decryption process. Each relay node performs these operations for a single layer at a 
time, as illustrated in Fig 2.2 [16]. This introduces additional computational latency, 
which is further exacerbated by network congestion [9]. Given that the network relies on 
volunteer-operated nodes, individual relay congestion varies significantly due to factors 
such as internet speed and geographic location [16]. Choosing suboptimal relays can lead 
to inefficient routing, further increasing round-trip times. 
 
2.2.2 Garlic Routing (I2P) 
 
The Invisible Internet Project (I2P) employs Garlic Routing, an enhanced version of Tor's 
design. This method bundles multiple messages into a single encrypted package called a 
Garlic Bulb. Each bulb contains numerous Garlic Cloves, which are distributed to 
different nodes, increasing the complexity of traffic flow [18]. As a result, Garlic routing 
intensifies the network congestion issues beyond those experienced in TOR circuits. 
==PAGE:12==
10 | Please go to Appendix E for Simulation Codes. 
 
 
 
Fig 2.3: The peer-to-peer networking using Garlic Routing [18].  
 
Unlike TOR, I2P operates on a distributed peer-to-peer system, enabling users to 
dynamically choose their relays. This results in a routing circuit that is not established 
during the initial phase of communication on either end. Consequently, the routing paths 
become less efficient. The additional encryption and bundling processes required for 
Garlic Routing at each node along the path introduce further processing delays. Due to 
the unfixed nature of the path, they tend to have short durations. The brief lifespan of 
these paths requires frequent rebuilding, leading to unusual latency spikes. As a result, 
the latencies become even more erratic and unusual. 
 
2.2.3 Mix Networks (Mixnets) 
 
Anonymity in mix networks is achieved through the process of grouping and shuffling 
encrypted messages before they are sent. This technique, known as mixing, introduces 
deliberate delays, making it challenging for potential attackers to correlate incoming and 
outgoing messages.  
 
 
Key Mixnet Implementations: 
 
 
Loopix: This platform offers enhanced privacy and security for online messaging through 
a secure communication system. It employs a mixed network architecture, which aims to 
obscure message origins and destinations by introducing delays, combining packets from 
different sources, and shuffling them before transmission [10]. Unlike centralized 
systems, this platform operates in a decentralized manner, improving its resilience against 
attacks. It achieves a compromise between anonymity and efficiency, making it suitable 
for practical applications such as messaging and internet browsing. The system employs 
Poisson mixing, where each packet undergoes random delays before forwarding, 
significantly complicating traffic analysis [10]. To further mask genuine communication 
patterns, it generates cover traffic. Messages are protected from interception using 
cryptographic methods.

Fig 2.4: Loopix Architecture [19]. 
 
 
Katzenpost: Katzenpost is a secure communication network designed to protect user 
privacy. It employs metadata-resistant transmission techniques, including message 
mixing and delaying, to counter traffic analysis attacks. Building upon mix network 
principles, Katzenpost improves on systems like Loopix by offering enhanced scalability, 
efficiency, and robustness. The network ensures that potential adversaries monitoring 
traffic cannot determine the identities of communicating parties [11]. Katzenpost utilizes 
cryptographic mixing techniques and ensures delays to prevent timing attacks. To further 
enhance anonymity, the system generates dummy packets that make it harder to identify 
genuine communications. Unlike traditional mix networks, Katzenpost is designed for 
improved scalability, allowing it to handle larger traffic volumes effectively. The network 
uses public-key cryptography to protect message integrity and prevent tampering. 
Katzenpost can be integrated into various applications, such as anonymous messaging 
platforms, email systems, and secure data transfer solutions that prioritize privacy 
protection. 
 
2.2.4 Decentralized ACNs (Blockchain-Based ACNs) 
 
The emergence of blockchain technology has paved the way for the creation of 
decentralized Anonymous Communication Networks (ACNs), which operate without the 
need for a central network administrator. Unlike conventional ACNs such as Tor and I2P, 
which rely on a structured relay system vulnerable to attacks on centralized control

points, blockchain-based ACNs utilize cryptographically protected public ledgers to 
maintain network integrity and security. This approach enhances their resistance to 
censorship and surveillance. These networks offer an alternative to traditional ACNs by 
incorporating decentralized incentives and cryptographic validation while preserving 
anonymity and security. 
 
ACNs powered by blockchain technology address security challenges like Sybil attacks, 
where malicious actors create multiple fake identities to manipulate network operations 
[12]. Through the use of public ledgers and proof-based authentication systems, these 
networks ensure that only genuine participants can contribute to routing and 
communication processes.

Nym stands out as a prominent example of blockchain-integrated Anonymous 
Communication Networks (ACNs), combining mix networks with blockchain 
technology. It strengthens privacy by employing mix nodes to anonymize user 
information while offering blockchain-based incentives for participation [12]. This 
approach ensures anonymity for relay nodes and provides economic motivation for 
secure traffic processing. Unlike conventional ACNs that depend on voluntary relays, 
Nym introduces a decentralized reward system, boosting network reliability and 
efficiency. By leveraging blockchain for managing identities and reputations, Nym 
strengthens security and impedes malicious infiltration.

Another noteworthy blockchain-based ACN is the Lightning Network by Blockstream, 
functioning as a secondary payment layer atop Bitcoin. This network incorporates onion 
routing, akin to Tor, within a decentralized payment system. The result is swift, 
anonymous transactions that safeguard users' financial privacy. By facilitating off-chain 
transactions that are ultimately settled on the Bitcoin blockchain, the Lightning Network 
minimizes transaction costs and processing duration while upholding cryptographic 
security. 
 
Nevertheless, blockchain-based ACNs face latency challenges despite the advantages of 
decentralization. Smart contract execution is one factor impacting performance, as these 
networks must validate transactions through distributed consensus mechanisms. This 
verification process introduces additional delays compared to traditional ACNs, where 
relay selection and packet forwarding are independent of consensus validation. Moreover, 
peer discovery overhead presents another latency bottleneck. In the absence of a 
centralized directory service, users in decentralized networks must rely on peer-to-peer 
discovery to locate available relays, a process that can be time-consuming and 
computationally demanding. 
 
Nevertheless, these latency issues, blockchain-based ACNs offer a promising alternative 
to centralized relay-based networks, enhancing resistance to censorship and bolstering 
security. By incorporating blockchain incentives, these decentralized networks can 
achieve efficient scaling while preserving privacy-centric communication mechanisms 
[12]. 
 
2.3 Existing Routing Mechanisms in ACNs 
 
The efficiency of communication and network latency in ACNs are significantly 
impacted by routing methods. Several routing approaches are commonly employed in 
ACNs, including: 
 
2.3.1 Randomized Relay Selection 
 
The default routing approach of Tor selects nodes randomly based on their bandwidth 
capacity [9]. While this method ensures fairness, it results in non-optimal paths and 
inconsistent latency times.

2.3.2 Congestion-Aware Routing 
 
Some ACNs employ routing strategies that consider current network congestion levels 
when choosing relay points. While this approach improves overall performance, it may 
inadvertently expose traffic patterns if not implemented carefully [5]. 
 
2.3.3 Multi-Hop Adaptive Routing 
 
I2P and Mixnets dynamically adjust routes based on observed network conditions. While 
this approach helps reduce congestion-related delays, it may compromise security by 
making routing decisions more transparent [11][15]. 
 
2.4 Approaches to Latency Optimization 
 
Addressing the latency issues in ACNs requires innovative solutions. Researchers have 
explored several techniques, including: 
2.4.1 Circuit Pre-Building 
Pre-built circuits reduce connection setup delays in Tor by maintaining pre-established 
paths for user traffic. 
2.4.2 Traffic Shaping and Padding 
Dummy traffic and padding techniques obscure traffic patterns but add bandwidth and 
processing overhead. 
2.4.3 Network Load Balancing 
Distributing network traffic across less congested relays improves performance but 
requires additional coordination between nodes. 
2.5 Security Implications of Latency Optimization 
 
Enhancing responsiveness in anonymous communication networks (ACNs) through 
latency optimization must be carefully weighed against potential security vulnerabilities. 
A key concern is that expedited routing may create discernible patterns that malicious 
actors can exploit. Prioritizing performance in routing decisions could make network 
traffic more traceable, facilitating traffic correlation attacks that jeopardize user 
anonymity by exposing communication patterns and possible network endpoints. 
 
Moreover, emphasizing speed in routing choices may diminish the randomness crucial 
for maintaining anonymity. ACNs typically employ probabilistic relay selection to 
==PAGE:17==
15 | Please go to Appendix E for Simulation Codes. 
 
conceal message origins and destinations. However, if routing based on latency favors 
only the swiftest nodes, the randomness in relay selection decreases, enabling adversaries 
to more easily predict which relays are likely to be used for a given transmission. This 
predictability can lead to network fingerprinting over time, allowing malicious actors to 
develop statistical models for inferring user activity patterns [6]. 
 
The tension between latency and security also affects network congestion management. 
In attempts to minimize delays, routing algorithms may favor less congested nodes, 
unintentionally creating load imbalances in the network. Attackers could take advantage 
of this by intentionally overwhelming specific relays to manipulate routing decisions, 
effectively channeling legitimate traffic through compromised nodes. This routing 
manipulation attack can expose sensitive metadata and undermine the network's overall 
anonymity guarantees. 
 
Additionally, relying on latency-aware routing may impact relay usage diversity. 
Consistently preferring lower-latency nodes could result in underutilization of higher-
latency relays, leading to centralization risks. Over time, the ACN may become 
dependent on a subset of fast relays, increasing vulnerability to node deanonymization 
attacks and reducing network resilience against node failures or adversarial takeovers. 
 
To address these risks, ACN designers must adopt a balanced approach that combines 
performance and security considerations. This can be achieved by incorporating 
controlled randomness into relay selection while still factoring in latency. Furthermore, 
implementing dummy traffic generation and traffic padding techniques can help obscure 
actual routing patterns, reducing the effectiveness of correlation attacks. Careful 
adjustment of relay selection algorithms and dynamic load balancing strategies can 
ensure that network security is maintained while still providing acceptable performance 
levels. 
 
2.6 Emerging Trends in ACN Optimization 
 
The field of Anonymous Communication Networks (ACNs) has seen remarkable 
progress, with cutting-edge technologies enhancing security, efficiency, and 
decentralization. A key innovation is the application of Machine Learning for Routing 
Optimization. While traditional ACNs use static or probabilistic methods for relay 
selection, which may be suboptimal in changing network conditions, AI-driven models 
can now forecast congestion, assess relay reliability, and determine the best paths in real-
time. These systems continuously adapt to network traffic patterns, adjusting routing 
decisions to boost speed and minimizing latency, thereby strengthening ACNs against 
performance issues and traffic analysis attacks.

Quantum-Resistant Encryption represents another crucial area of advancement. As 
quantum computing evolves, conventional cryptographic techniques employed in ACNs, 
such as RSA and ECC, risk becoming obsolete due to their vulnerability to quantum 
decryption methods. Scientists are investigating post-quantum cryptographic approaches, 
including lattice-based, hash-based, and multivariate polynomial schemes, to ensure 
enduring security [6]. The implementation of quantum-resistant encryption in ACNs will 
safeguard network security against potential quantum threats, maintaining anonymity and 
data integrity for years to come. 
 
Furthermore, the incorporation of Blockchain technology in ACNs is transforming 
network trust and decentralization. Blockchain provides an immutable ledger system that 
can be employed for authenticating relays, incentivizing participation, and managing 
nodes in a decentralized manner. By utilizing distributed ledger technology, ACNs can 
thwart Sybil attacks, where malicious actors create multiple fake identities to compromise 
network security. Blockchain-enabled ACNs also enable trustless interactions, allowing 
users to verify relay node integrity without relying on centralized authorities [12]. This 
decentralized approach not only enhances security but also offers economic incentives for 
network participants to contribute to the relay infrastructure, ensuring the network's 
sustainability and resistance to censorship [6]. 
 
These developments in machine learning, post-quantum encryption, and blockchain 
integration collectively boost the reliability, security, and efficiency of ACNs, laying the 
groundwork for a more robust and future-ready anonymous communication 
infrastructure.

3.1 Evaluating Perspective Solutions 
 
3.1.1 ShorTor: Multi-Hop Overlay Routing Optimization 
 
ShorTor represents an innovative approach to enhance multi-hop routing in the Tor 
network by implementing an intelligent relay selection strategy [13]. While conventional 
Tor routing employs a probabilistic method for choosing relays, prioritizing security but 
often leading to network bottlenecks and subpar performance, ShorTor refines this 
process. It does so by strategically identifying and utilizing low-latency relay nodes, 
thereby boosting user experience without sacrificing security measures. Through 
==PAGE:19==
17 | Please go to Appendix E for Simulation Codes. 
 
continuous monitoring of network conditions, ShorTor effectively addresses congestion 
issues and facilitates a more balanced distribution of traffic across the available relay 
infrastructure [13]. 
 
Fig 3.1: Multi-hop overlay routing as in a CDN [13]. 
 
ShorTor's primary function revolves around its sophisticated relay selection process, 
which utilizes real-time latency evaluations and network load distribution. In contrast to 
conventional Tor circuits that employ random relay selection, ShorTor dynamically 
assesses network conditions and adjusts routing decisions accordingly. 
 
A crucial aspect of ShorTor is its latency-driven relay selection. Rather than randomly 
choosing relays, the system evaluates the real-time latency of various relay nodes and 
favors those with lower congestion and higher available bandwidth. This approach 
ensures data transmission through the swiftest possible route while preserving Tor's 
anonymity safeguards [13]. 
 
Another essential feature is adaptive path optimization, which constantly monitors 
network traffic and dynamically modifies routing paths to circumvent overloaded or 
underperforming relays [7]. This helps alleviate bottlenecks and boosts the overall 
efficiency of the Tor network. Additionally, ShorTor implements multipath routing, 
allowing data packets to be distributed across multiple routes instead of relying on a 
single predetermined path. This enhances throughput and network reliability, making it 
more resilient against individual point failures and sudden congestion increases [13]. 
 
==PAGE:20==
18 | Please go to Appendix E for Simulation Codes. 
 
ShorTor offers several enhancements over traditional Tor routing methods, particularly in 
terms of reducing latency, balancing load, and improving usability for real-time 
applications. 
 
A key advantage is decreased latency. By choosing the least congested relays, ShorTor 
substantially reduces the end-to-end transmission time of data packets, resulting in faster 
browsing and improved performance for low-latency applications. 
 
ShorTor also enhances load distribution across the Tor network. By dynamically 
selecting relays based on congestion levels, it prevents traffic from concentrating on a 
few high-bandwidth nodes, ensuring more efficient utilization of all relays. This mitigates 
network congestion and improves the overall stability of Tor [13]. 
 
A particularly significant benefit of ShorTor is its impact on real-time applications. 
Traditional Tor's high latency makes it impractical for VoIP calls, video conferencing, 
and real-time gaming. ShorTor's optimized routing mechanism significantly improves 
Tor's viability for such applications, broadening its utility beyond conventional web 
browsing and email communication. 
 
While ShorTor offers advantages, it also presents several obstacles and constraints that 
require careful consideration. A primary worry is its vulnerability to traffic analysis. The 
selection of relays based on network conditions and latency means that a sufficiently 
capable adversary could deduce user locations by examining shifts in traffic patterns and 
relay choices. This could potentially reveal sensitive data, jeopardizing user anonymity. 
 
An additional constraint is the dependence on precise relay performance measurements. 
ShorTor relies on accurate and honest reporting of relay congestion levels, bandwidth, 
and latency. However, the system becomes vulnerable if malicious relays falsify their 
performance metrics, as they could draw more traffic and position themselves to execute 
traffic correlation attacks or intercept data. 
 
Scalability poses another hurdle. As Tor's user base expands, maintaining the availability 
of optimal relays for selection becomes increasingly difficult. A larger user population 
leads to higher demand for low-latency relays, potentially causing certain relays to 
become overburdened. Although ShorTor attempts to address this through adaptive path 
optimization, this approach necessitates ongoing monitoring and computational 
resources, which may restrict its effectiveness on a broader scale. 
 
3.1.2 QuicTor: Implementing QUIC Protocol in Tor 
 
QuicTor presents a novel approach to optimizing transport-layer operations in the Tor 
network by substituting the conventional TCP-based transport with QUIC (Quick UDP 
==PAGE:21==
19 | Please go to Appendix E for Simulation Codes. 
 
Internet Connections). This shift to UDP-based transport brings about substantial 
improvements in connection establishment speed, decreased latency, and enhanced 
overall network efficiency. QuicTor offers a more effective alternative to Tor's traditional 
multi-hop TCP routing by incorporating connectionless, multiplexed transport with 
integrated encryption capabilities [14]. 
 
Fig 3.2: QuicTor Protocol Stack [14]. 
 
QuicTor's main advancement is its utilization of QUIC as the transport protocol, offering 
several significant benefits compared to TCP-based routing. 
 
A key element of QuicTor is its UDP-based transport system. In contrast to conventional 
Tor circuits that depend on TCP handshakes, which cause delays due to multi-step 
connection establishment and retransmissions, QUIC eliminates these processes. By 
using connectionless communication, QUIC substantially decreases setup times, enabling 
more efficient Tor circuit establishment and enhancing overall system responsiveness 
[14]. 
 
Furthermore, QuicTor implements multiplexed streams, enabling concurrent transmission 
of multiple data streams within a single session. Traditional Tor routing using TCP is 
hindered by head-of-line blocking, where packet loss in one stream impedes all 
subsequent transmissions. QuicTor circumvents this issue by ensuring that separate data 
streams do not impact each other, resulting in improved network efficiency, particularly 
during high-traffic periods. 
 
QuicTor also features an enhanced congestion control mechanism. Unlike TCP's 
conservative approach, QUIC adapts bandwidth usage based on current network 
conditions. This allows QuicTor to sustain higher throughput, better packet transmission 
rates, and lower jitters, making it especially advantageous for large-scale Tor users and

enhancements. The investigation has offered comprehensive insights into the deficiencies

assurances inherent to the Tor network.

emphasizing the significance of mix networks, onion routing, and emerging blockchain-
integrated methodologies. The study identified the constraints of conventional relay 
selection mechanisms, particularly the inefficiencies in static routing that contribute to 
latency bottlenecks. This led to the creation of a novel model integrating quantum 
fidelity, link quality evaluation, and adaptive relay selection to dynamically optimize 
routing paths. A robust mathematical framework was developed to measure network 
latency in quantum-enhanced communication environments, incorporating variables such 
as quantum coherence, entanglement fidelity, and network congestion levels. 
 
The implementation stage involved simulating QLLR using Python and QuNetSim, 
ensuring an accurate representation of real-world quantum network behaviors. The 
experimental outcomes consistently showed that QLLR substantially decreases end-to-
end latency compared to Traditional Tor, especially in high-traffic scenarios where

This study introduces an innovative quantum-inspired routing approach that addresses the 
challenge of balancing anonymous network security with latency optimization. The 
researchers developed Quantum Latency-Optimized Layered Routing (QLLR), which 
incorporates quantum network principles into existing Tor infrastructure, enhancing 
efficiency without compromising anonymity. Unlike previous performance improvement 
attempts that often-introduced security vulnerabilities through predictable relay selection, 
QLLR maintains random node selection while utilizing real-time fidelity assessments for 
optimal routing choices. 
 
A key contribution of this work is the creation of a mathematical model for quantum 
network latency, which accounts for factors such as fidelity degradation, traffic 
congestion, and quantum decoherence effects. This framework provides a foundation for 
future studies in quantum-assisted anonymous communication, offering a structured

obstacles must be overcome for its practical implementation. A key challenge is its

Future Work and Applications 
 
 
Although this study has made substantial progress in enhancing latency in Anonymous 
Communication Networks (ACNs) through Quantum Latency-Optimized Layered 
Routing (QLLR), there are still numerous promising directions for future research, 
including practical applications, implementation strategies, and tackling the physical and 
hardware constraints that would be faced if deployed today. A crucial next step is the 
practical integration of QLLR into an operational ACN, such as Tor or I2P. While the 
current research utilizes QuNetSim simulations, implementing QLLR in real-world ACN 
environments would offer valuable insights into scalability, relay selection under actual 
traffic conditions, and resistance to malicious attacks. Partnering with open-source 
privacy initiatives could expedite the adoption of quantum-enhanced routing solutions in 
real-world scenarios. However, incorporating quantum-assisted routing into ACNs would 
necessitate changes to existing relay infrastructures, as current ACNs are built on 
classical cryptographic principles rather than quantum-enhanced routing mechanisms. 
Another vital area for enhancement is the durability of quantum entanglement in practical 
networks. Quantum communication is highly vulnerable to decoherence and noise, which 
can compromise entanglement fidelity over extended distances. Future studies should 
concentrate on incorporating quantum error correction techniques such as quantum 
repeaters, entanglement distillation, and fault-tolerant quantum communication protocols. 
These advancements would boost the reliability of QLLR for long-range secure 
communications, making it more suitable for deployment in real-world anonymous 
networks. Nevertheless, these solutions present their own challenges, as quantum 
repeaters are still in early development stages, and constructing a global-scale quantum 
internet would demand significant advancements in quantum memory and error-resistant 
entanglement distribution. Broadening QLLR to accommodate hybrid classical-quantum 
routing models would further improve its scalability and applicability. While this 
research focuses on optimizing latency using quantum-inspired relay selection, future 
studies could investigate how QLLR can function in partially quantum networks, where 
only certain nodes possess quantum capabilities. Hybrid approaches that combine 
classical ACN routing with quantum-assisted decision-making could facilitate a smoother 
transition toward fully quantum-optimized anonymous networks as quantum 
infrastructure matures. However, implementing such hybrid models would require 
substantial modifications to existing networking hardware and protocols, as current 
optical fibre networks are designed for classical data transmission rather than 
entanglement-based quantum communication.
