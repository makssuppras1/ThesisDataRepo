Abstract  
This thesis examines gender bias in recruitment, with a specific focus on 'Maybe Baby Bias ‚Äì 
a discriminatory practice affecting women of childbearing age in hiring or promoting 
decisions. The hiring company assumes that the woman applying for a job will soon fall 
pregnant and, therefore, hires a safer person. This could be someone older or someone who is 
not giving the impression that they will soon become pregnant.  
This research employs Grounded Theory methodology to investigate where Maybe Baby 
Bias occurs in the recruitment process in private firms and how this bias can be limited based 
on the company's perspective. Through semi-structured interviews conducted from four 
different companies, an in-depth exploration of this bias was conducted. The four companies 
represented varying recruitment approaches and were within different fields. Some 
companies had dedicated resources and specialized teams for recruitment, while others 
operated with fewer resources, leaving the responsibility of hiring to the team leader.  
None of the companies interviewed were familiar with the term Maybe Baby Bias or had 
experienced this bias within the companies. This leads to the discussion about where in the 
recruitment process it could happen. Three out of four companies assumed it happens both in 
the screening process of the application and during the interviews with the applicants, where 
family status, living situation, and other aspects are deeply explored. The last company 
assumed MBB mainly happened in the interview. All four companies emphasized that this 
bias exists within the recruiting process.  
Solutions were also discussed within the interviews, and a common response from the 
companies was that societal changes are needed for the companies and organisations to 
become better at managing bias. In addition, bias training was shown to be of interest. In 
relation to the reviewed literature, AI solutions, structured recruitment processes, combined 
with bias training, were shown to be part of the solution for making companies' recruitment 
processes more bias-free. This thesis demonstrates that while bias cannot be eliminated 
instantly, combining awareness with targeted initiatives can foster more inclusive hiring 
practices, improve team diversity, and enhance organizational performance.

1. Background and a Personal Introduction  
My passion for sports began at age five when I joined my first football team, introducing me 
to the complexities of teamwork. Being part of a team can be both a rewarding and a 
challenging experience. I quickly realised that being in a team requires communication and 
effort. Personally, I have experienced team dynamics being challenging. Sometimes, the 
dynamics just don't work out; other times, it works out very well. Sometimes, the individual 
performance level in a team is extremely high, but together, the achievements are very low. 
Other times, teams that don't have the same amount of talent can work extremely well 
together and achieve bigger goals. 
At my boarding school, I also did sports, and I had a course about philosophy, sports and 
teams. Here, we discussed the action of when something comes together as a greater whole 
and big goals are achieved. This can happen in a sports team, but also in other settings, for 
example, in a music band. My teacher called it ‚ÄúImagens‚Äù, a term he had invented himself. 
He explained it with when 2 + 2 = 5 ‚Äì when something magical happens, everything works 
out perfectly. Even though it can be hard to explain precisely why it happens and what the 
causes are. It can also be challenging to replicate when it does occur.  
How teams work has fascinated me for a long period of time. Because sometimes, it can be 
hard to tell exactly why the team dynamic worked out and how other times it didn't work out 
at all. When I saw that diversity in a team creates more innovation and performs better in 
finance (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021), it made sense for me to look at how a team can be put 
together in order to perform well and achieve goals. How can we create the best possible 
team that performs, where people enjoy being a part of it and where inclusion and well-being 
are key factors? 
In the course 38202 - Entrepreneurial Leadership Journey at DTU, I got familiar with the 
term Maybe Baby Bias. I realised that I had experienced this myself, in a recruiting setting, 
without paying attention. After this course, I started to pay attention and look into this 
subject. A whole world opened up to me about bias, especially bias in recruitment. Why is it 
happening, and where is it happening in the process of recruiting? Therefore, combining my 
interest in how to create a well-performing team with bias in recruiting seemed like an 
interesting topic to explore in depth. 
My goal with this thesis is to examine where ‚ÄúMaybe Baby Bias‚Äù occurs in recruitment 
processes and how companies and employees can address it. By interviewing four companies, 
I will dive deep into this subject and see how they recruit. At the end of this thesis, I will 
provide concrete examples of how a company can reduce bias, including Maybe Baby Bias 
and hopefully create more inclusiveness within their teams and the organisation. I personally 
don't think people can ever be 100% bias-free, but by establishing guidelines and raising 
awareness, the hope is that we all can become less biased and create strong teams that 
perform well.

1.1. Maybe Baby bias ‚Äì Terminology  
Before diving into the research, it is essential to provide clarity by defining the term Maybe 
Baby Bias. The following section will briefly explore the concept of Maybe Baby Bias.  
Maybe Baby bias is a central part of this thesis; therefore, an explanation is needed in order to 
understand and research where it occurs and how to limit it. Maybe Baby Bias represents a 
bias in recruitment and promotion processes, specifically targeting women of childbearing 
age (Gloor, J. L. et al., 2017). The bias concerns women who are applying for jobs or are 
searching for a promotion, and because of this bias, they are not getting the job/promotion 
due to misconceptions that they might fall pregnant soon. This bias is based on assumptions 
about a potential pregnancy rather than the woman's actual situation and planning regarding 
starting a family. It arises from stereotypes and misconceptions about women's roles as 
caregivers (Diehl, A. and Dzubinski, L.M., 2023). Because of this bias, companies often end 
up hiring a more ‚Äúsafe‚Äù person. This could, for example, be a man in the same age frame, a 
woman who already has children or a person with older kids. Later in this thesis, Maybe 
Baby Bias will be reviewed in detail in the literature review (section 3.4).  
Abbreviations will be used throughout this thesis (see section List of Abbreviations p. 5). 
Maybe Baby Bias will be referred to as MBB.  
1.2. Purpose and Research Question  
In order to establish a clear direction for the research in this thesis, a research question has 
been developed. The following section presents the topic explored and introduces the 
research question. 
Gender bias in recruitment processes, including Maybe Baby Bias, is a big subject to identify 
and research. To address this broad topic, it has been necessary to narrow down the focus. 
Research has shown that diversity within a team creates more innovation and better financial 
output (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021). In addition to this, creating a gender balance of at least 
30:70% in top management has a positive impact on the bottom line (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, C.L. 
,2021. p.165). However, despite the benefits, companies struggle to prioritise diversity in 
their teams and in their recruitment strategies. One of the reasons for this could be the bias 
that occurs in recruiting processes, such as MBB.  
Therefore, this thesis focuses on gender bias in recruitment with a focus on MBB. This bias 
not only limits women's opportunities but also limits diversity in a team, which can lead to a 
less attractive outcome (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021). It is, therefore, interesting to address bias in 
recruitment processes and dive into this theme. By being aware of the bias that occurs in 
recruitment, for example, in the selection process, a better starting point can be achieved to 
create more diversity within the team. While gender bias has been widely studied, MBB 
remains less explored, which leaves a gap in understanding its impact and how to minimise it.  
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
 p. 10 
Thus, the goal is to investigate when MBB occurs in the recruitment process from the 
perspective of businesses. Additionally, investigate what the companies perceive as a solution 
to limit bias in a recruitment setting. The goal of the research is to highlight MBB and create 
a conversation on how to reduce bias in recruitment processes. By exploring these questions, 
this study aims to make a meaningful impact on promoting diversity in the workplace. 
Therefore, the research question is:  
‚ÄúWhen do gender biases and particularly Maybe Baby Bias emerge during recruitment 
processes in private firms and what strategies can be implemented to create a more inclusive 
approach to hiring decisions.‚Äù

2. Methodology 
In the following section, the methodology will be presented. In this thesis, various methods 
have been used to approach the research systematically. These methods have been applied in 
different stages of the process. These stages include the process of creating a literature 
review, collecting data related to the problem and finally, analysing the data collected. Each 
method has been chosen and applied to ensure an extensive exploration of the topic. In the 
following subsections, the different methods are highlighted, and how they have been applied 
is explained in detail.  
2.1. Grounded Theory 
The following section will explain Grounded Theory and show how the method has been 
applied. 
Grounded Theory has been used as the primary research method in this thesis. The theory 
was originally developed by Anselm Strauss and Barney Glaser in 1967 and can be explained 
as both a straightforward yet complex method (Guv√•, G., Hylander, I, 2005, p.10). The 
method's goal is to generate a theory grounded in empirical data collected, making it suitable 
for research involving complex social or human behaviours. The point of this method is not 
only to collect and organise data but to develop theories based on the data collected (Guv√•, 
G., Hylander, I, 2005, p.9). Strauss and Glaser wanted researchers to create an understanding 
of the research field by exploring through the creation of new theories (Guv√•, G., Hylander, I, 
2005, p.12). Some describe the method as theory generation based on empirical data, as its 
purpose is both to create new theories and to anchor the ideas and theories generated in 
empirical evidence (Guv√•, G., Hylander, I, 2005, p.9).  
Grounded Theory typically begins with a question or a curiosity one wishes to explore. In this 
project, a curiosity about gender bias and MBB in recruiting processes, seen from the 
business side, was the starting point. Then, the existing literature is reviewed in order to 
understand the current knowledge base (Guv√•, G., Hylander, I, 2005, p.7-12). After 
reviewing existing research and identifying gaps in the literature, field observations are made. 
In this thesis, field observations were made in the form of conducting semi-structured 
interviews with four different companies. These interview findings are highlighted in section 
4.3. An important part of Grounded Theory is the key factor in choosing comparison groups 
for developing a theory, which is done by using theoretical sampling (Glaser, B.G., Strauss, 
A.L, 1967 p. 49). Theoretical sampling ensures that booth companies, stakeholders and 
experts interviewed are carefully selected and not predefined but rather selected as the project 
works forward. Another key part is that in Grounded Theory, data collection and analysis are 
interrelated processes (Corbin, J. and Strauss, A., 1990). In practice, this means that the 
collected data is analysed continuously instead of doing it at the end when all the data has 
been collected.  
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
 p. 12 
After the interviews were conducted, they were transcribed for analysis. The data collected 
from the interviews was then systematically coded to identify recurring patterns, similarities, 
and differences. The coding process is explained in the next section. From this, a 
conceptualisation has been made, and a new theory has been created based on the data 
collected.  
If the method should be described in steps, it could be done as followed:  
1. Define the research problem  
2. Collect data / create a field observation 
3. Analyse data by using coding 
4. Develop the theory based on the findings. 
This method was chosen because it offers flexibility when researching a topic. Since MBB 
has not been extensively researched, grounded theory helps gather insights directly from 
stakeholders, experts and company interviews without forcing the findings to fit a 
predetermined theory. This helps ensure that data creates the result rather than being limited 
by existing literature. Another important thing about Grounded Theories is that they originate 
from human behaviour and social actions, which is what this thesis has been researching. 
How humans act when recruiting, and how bias occurs in these situations. The last thing that 
fits within this thesis scope has been the allowance of an iterative process. The iterative 
process, involving data collection, analysis, and refinement of approaches, has contributed to 
a deeper understanding of this topic. The method is further discussed in the "discussion" 
section. 
2.1.1. Coding of data in Grounded Theory  
Coding is a part of Grounded Theory, and in the following section, an explanation of how 
coding has been applied in this project is provided.  
Coding of the data collected is an important step of Grounded Theory, as it identifies and 
creates patterns within the collected data. In Grounded Theory, coding can be done in various 
ways. The first step before coding is collecting qualitative data. This thesis consists of semi-
structured interviews that have been transcribed. The process of this will be explained in 
section 2.2. After the collection of data coding can be done. The coding process in this thesis 
consists of three phases: Open coding, axial coding and selective coding (Guv√•, G., Hylander, 
I, 2005).  
The process began with the creation and transcription of the four interviews. From the 
transcription, open coding was done. The purpose of this part of the process is to develop 
dynamic concepts that serve as guiding elements for continued exploration (Guv√•, G., 
Hylander, I, 2005.p 56). Thus, in this part of the coding, each interview was analysed by 
taking the interview and breaking it into smaller parts. From this, codes were created, and 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
 p. 13 
labelling was done. An example of this could, for example, be when speaking about the 
screening process, then screening was a code.  
The second phase is axial coding. In this part of the coding process, the concepts are 
transformed into categories to identify patterns and create classifications. This is also the part 
where the connections of the codes are identified (Guv√•, G., Hylander, I, 2005.p 58). This 
was done by using the codes created in open coding. These draw connections from the coded 
data developed in the open coding. And these categories become the axis that thighs the 
codes together. By doing axial coding, patterns and categories are identified. The last phase is 
selective coding. Selective coding creates core categories based on the categories made in 
axial coding (Guv√•, G., Hylander, I, 2005). Here, key themes are identified and selected 
based on the patterns created in the second phase. This could, for example, be the topic of 
bias, the company or the industry. This coding process has been made for each of the four 
interviews. In the appendix, an overview of each company and the coding process is 
provided, cheating an overview of the process.  
Lastly, a comparative analysis of the four interviews was conducted to identify patterns 
across the companies. This is also shown in the appendix 9.3. Some themes identified were 
expected due to the structured nature of the interview guidelines ‚Äì particularly those 
regarding MBB and the recruitment processes. The analysis revealed both expected and 
unexpected patterns. This will later be highlighted in the findings.  
2.1.2. Selection of Companies  
The selection of companies has been a big part of this project since the output of these 
interviews created a new theory. Choosing companies is also a chance to dive into different 
industries, producing a wide range of datasets. Therefore, an overview of the process is 
provided below. 
Before collecting data, a selection of companies to interview needs to be created. At the 
beginning of this project, the aim was to talk to small-medium sized companies with 50-249 
employees. The assumption was that small companies don't have a human resource 
department and may not have the resources to work with biases, including MBB, despite the 
fact that they may or may not benefit from it. Somehow, the same assumption was made for 
the bigger companies. The assumption was that they already had a recruitment system and, 
therefore, had bias on their radar. Thus, small-medium sized companies were the intended 
target group to interview to understand and work with this bias. The assumption was that 
small-medium sized companies were large enough to have resources to work with bias or at 
least had some reflections about it.  
Therefore, companies with 50-249 employees were contacted. In total, 17 companies were 
contacted, and out of them, no one wanted to participate. Since the goal was to involve

The Confederation of Danish Industry has made an organisation called the Diversity Pledge,

The 16 principles concern how a company can work with diversity and create more inclusion 
at work. When signing up for the organisation, the companies get a network and workshops

2.2. Semi-Structured Interview for Data Collection  
A semi-structured interview was chosen as the method for collecting data. Below is an 
explanation of how this method has added value to the process.  
Grounded Theory methodology employs various data collection methods, including 
observations, surveys, and interviews. For this study, semi-structured interviews were 
selected as the primary data collection method, as it allows the conversation to be structured, 
yet still offer the freedom to ask follow-up questions if needed (Naz, N., Gulab, F, & Aslam, 
N., 2022). 
Four companies have been interviewed and shared their knowledge and strategies about their 
recruiting process. When doing semi-structured interviews, an interview guide was made and 
followed. The guide will be explained in the following section. A semi-structured interview 
was chosen because it creates a frame yet offers the freedom to ask follow-up questions. For 
example, when the company has some interesting structures at work or says something that 
seems interesting, then this allows the interview style to offer the freedom to use follow-up 
questions. While all companies responded to the same core interview questions, enabling 
systematic comparison, the depth and focus of discussions varied naturally across interviews. 
Some questions have gotten more attention, where follow-up questions were needed. Follow-
up questions were, for example, made when one of the companies had an interesting 
promoting structure. The semi-structured interview was selected as the primary research 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
 p. 17 
method because it allows flexibility while sharing a deep dive into a company's strategy (Naz, 
N., Gulab, F, & Aslam, N.,2022).  
All interviews were conducted in Danish, and, therefore, the quotes presented in the findings 
chapter have been translated into English. The detailed transcription method will be discussed 
in section 2.3. 
2.2.1. Interview Guideline  
Since the choice was made to do semi-structured interviews, an interview guide needs to be 
prepared. Below is the interview guide explained.  
When interviewing the four companies, an interview guideline was made and followed, as 
mentioned in the section above. Since the subject of this study can be confidential and a bit 
personal, it was important to create a safe space when talking to the companies and their 
employees. It was preferred to go into a dialogue rather than strictly following an interview 
guide to create a safe space. Therefore, the questions within the interview guide were all 
answered in each interview but were not necessarily asked in the same order. The guidelines 
were based mainly on open-ended questions. This secured deeper insights and created a 
conversation (Naz, N., Gulab, F, & Aslam, N.,2022).  
The interview guideline was created based on four themes that investigated different aspects 
of how they worked with recruiting. This was done to identify the structure they followed 
within their company. The four themes were:  
1. Experiences with bias, MBB and diversity within the company 
2. Evaluating candidates, the recruiting interview and the overall recruiting strategy  
3. Strategies for gender bias in general within the company 
4. Solutions  
The first theme explored the participants' understanding and experience with bias and MBB. 
Here it was essential to know the participants' existing knowledge base and to evaluate how 
this was implemented within their organisation. The examination of their responses provided 
insights into both their theoretical understanding and practical application of these principles 
in their company.  
The second theme investigated the company's recruiting strategy. To identify if the 
participants had a strategy and, if so, how that was implemented in the business. Investigating 
how the company screened and evaluated candidates before offering a job interview was also 
important. This could provide insight into potential biases and how these emerged during the 
process. Questions about the job interview aimed to explore how sensitive topics were 
addressed and whether a structured procedure was used within the interviews. For instance, 
how were questions about family situations handled in a recruiting interview? 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
 p. 18 
The third theme was the company's overall strategy for bias in general. Did the company 
work with bias, and if so, how did they work with this? It was also important to investigate 
their reflections on diversity within their teams - was this something they worked with, and 
how? 
The final phase of the investigation focused on solution development. In alignment with the 
research objectives, discussions explored participants' perspectives on bias and MBB 
alongside potential solutions. When developing a solution, it is crucial to have a participatory 
approach to solution development. Involving and engaging the user will give valuable 
insights regarding implementation strategies to ensure that proposed solutions would be 
feasible for the organisations. The full interview guide is showcased in the appendix 9.4.  
2.3. Transcription of Interview  
Transcription of the interviews has been made to evaluate the data collected. Below is an 
explanation of each step in this process.  
Transcribing the interviews was a crucial step in data preparation for coding and the 
following analysis. The research generated qualitative data through interviews with both 
companies and experts. Data collection protocols included audio recordings of all interviews, 
with one conducted in person and three via Microsoft Teams. Different recording methods 
were used to ensure the data collection throughout the process, utilising both mobile device 
recording and Microsoft Teams platform's recording if the interview was conducted online. 
This was done to ensure if something happened, a backup was created. 
In the transcription phase, various artificial intelligence-based tools (AI tools) were tested 
with mixed success. Since the interviews were created in Danish, it was necessary for the tool 
to work within this language. After evaluating multiple platforms, Good Tape was selected as 
the optimal transcription tool for this project, mainly because it provides robust Danish 
language capabilities (Good Tape, 2024).  
When the AI tool had transcribed the files, an output was made. It clearly provided a useful 
starting point but also required some manual corrections along the way. Since the sound

3.1. Bias in General  
The first theme that has been explored is bias in general. To fully understand MBB, it is 
essential to explore bias in general and why humans are biased. Since MBB originates from 
bias, it is essential to explore this theme in depth.  
Bias is prejudice. A bias in our judgment that arises from human interactions in social 
contexts forms a culturally adapted social code that we learn from childhood (Muhr and 
Ottsen, 2021, p. 36). Bias can be conscious or unconscious. Conscious bias is attitude-based 
and can, for example, be our political beliefs. This can lead to discrimination such as racism, 
sexism and so on. While unconscious bias can also be discrimination, in this case, we don't 
realise that we are creating decisions based on an unstated basis (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p. 
36). Unconscious biases are also viewpoints and attitudes we may not be aware of, yet they 
shape our interpretations of people and situations ahead of a meeting (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, 
p. 14). If we are among people who look like us and act like so, our bias is rarely challenged.  
There are different kinds of bias. Some examples could be gender bias or affinity bias 
(Khallash, S., 2022). Gender bias is the tendency to prefer one gender over the other. For 
example, when a company assumes women are more interested in having a family than 
having a career and instead hires a man (Williams, J.C.,2023). Affinity bias is where humans 
prefer people who act and look like themselves (Khallash, S., 2022). These are examples of 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 p. 21 
different biases that can occur in different situations. The specific bias is not essential to 
understand, but the mechanisms behind it are important. The mechanism of bias can be found 
within cognitive psychology. In short, it is the theory of humanity's ability to recognise, 
perceive, and interpret the surrounding world (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p. 36). Therefore, bias 
is a fundamental concept in psychology that influences human decision-making. Among the 
leading researchers in the field of cognitive bias are Daniel Kahnemann and Amos Tversky. 
Daniel Kahnemann explained in the bestseller ‚ÄúThinking, fast and slow‚Äù, a theory about fast 
thinking and slow thinking (Kahneman, D.,2011). It combines cognitive psychology with 
economic thinking. Kahnemann describes two thinking systems:  
‚Ä¢ System 1 (fast-thinking): is used when people act on their routine, it could, for 
example, be when we are brushing our teeth or bicycling.  
‚Ä¢ System 2 (Slow thinking): Handles complex mental activities requiring focus and 
deliberation, such as studying or learning to drive.  
Both of these systems are constantly used to help people navigate within the world (Muhr and 
Ottsen, 2021, p. 39). By using these two systems, humans navigate the world. An example of 
this could be that research shows that leaders often make decisions solely based on their 
intuitive system 1, as a result of the hectic pace at the workplace, where they do not have the 
necessary time and resources for a more thorough assessment and decision-making process 
(Khallash, S.,2022. p.23). These cognitive systems evolved as survival mechanisms, 
essentially functioning as our intuition. Historically, quick decision-making was crucial for 
survival in natural environments. For example, years ago, when living in nature, people 
needed to be able to make decisions quickly (Kahneman, D.,2011). 
Kahnemann and Amos divide bias into heuristics. The term ‚Äúheuristic‚Äù was brought to worlds 
attention in psychology when Allen Newell and Herbert A. Simon used the word to describe 
simple processes that replace complex algorithms (Shah and Oppenheimer, 2008). In other 
words, heuristics are the mental shortcuts we use to make decisions quickly and efficiently. 
These can, for example, be used by leaders and employees when navigating a complex world, 
where heuristics make it possible to reduce the amount of data and simplify information 
(Khallash, S., 2022. p.28). Accessibility heuristic, anchoring heuristic and representativeness 
heuristic are some of the heuristics Kahnemann and Amos highlight when mentioning bias.  
The accessibility heuristic describes how human's ability to judge topics is influenced by 
information readily accessible in our memory. The more we encounter information about a 
topic, the more often a topic affects our perception of its likelihood. For example, studies 
have shown that people believe that the probability of dying from a shark attack is higher 
than dying from being hit by a falling part of an aircraft. Despite falling aircraft parts causing 
more deaths, shark attacks receive more media coverage, leading people to perceive them as 
more common (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p. 41).  
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
 p. 22 
The anchoring heuristic is our tendency to use the first information we get as an anchoring 
point. This means that significant weight is placed on the initial information provided. An 
example of this could be from an experiment based on real estate agents. In this study, real 
estate agents were shown a house and then asked to assess its value based on their judgment. 
Half of the agents were given a low listing price, while the other half were given a higher 
price as a reference. All the agents had access to an informational brochure about the 
property. Afterwards, they each provided an estimate of the house's value. When asked about 
their reasoning, the agents claimed that the listing price did not significantly influence their 
valuation. However, they were mistaken, as the anchoring effect was measured at 41%. In 
comparison, a similar experiment was conducted with business school students, where the 
anchoring effect was 48%. The only difference was that the students acknowledged that the 
listing price had influenced their estimates (Kahneman, D.,2011. p.143). This means that the 
price they were told had an impact on the price of the house.  
Representativeness heuristic is our tendency to find similarities between things to fit our 
schema-based perceptions. This could, for example, be that people believe that stress and 
stomach ulcers are connected because people have a perception that the two conditions occur 
simultaneously. It has been years since doctors found that stomach ulcers are caused by a 
bacterium and not stress. But people still connect these two things, and because of that, the 
myth lives on (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p. 41). These three heuristics are examples of how 
psychology and bias are coherent and how our minds play a big role when discussing bias.  
3.2. Stereotypes  
Another thing that is incorporated within us is stereotypes. Stereotypes are based on 
prejudices about others (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p. 42). It is part of the representativeness 
heuristic and illustrates how easily bias can trick our rational thinking. Stereotypes create a 
foundation for categorising people based on a shared understanding of characteristics, often 
linked to our external differences. This categorisation frequently results in false correlations 
between unrelated attributes, for example, assuming a connection between skin colour and 
educational level (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p 42). While stereotypes can foster a sense of 
community through shared understanding, they often lead to oversimplified assumptions. 
Stereotypes can also be practical and create a sense of community when we recognise each 
other's descriptions. This is also why people tend to use stereotypes when making jokes. The 
book ‚ÄúBias Bevidst Ledelse‚Äù offers an illustrative example: When asked to imagine a doctor 
in Denmark, most people imagine a male doctor, despite more female doctors being trained 
than male doctors in Denmark (Muhr and Ottsen, 2021, p. 43). Similarly, when thinking of 
nurses, people typically envision women. These automatic associations demonstrate how 
deeply ingrained stereotypes and gender stereotypes can be, even when they go against actual 
statistics.

3.3. Gender Bias  
Gender bias is, as already explained when people tend to favour one gender instead of the 
other (Williams, J.C., 2023). An example was the study that focused on selecting musicians 
for the American Symphony Orchestra. Symphony orchestras have historically been male 
dominated, with the belief that women lacked the skills or physical strength to perform as 
professional musicians. Goldin and Rouse, the researchers behind the study, demonstrated 
that changing the audition process significantly altered the outcomes. Instead of performing 
in front of a selection committee, musicians auditioned behind a curtain, ensuring that only 
their actual playing abilities were evaluated. This change led to a 25% increase in women 
being selected, as the evaluations were based solely on their skills (Goldin and Rouse, 2000).  
3.4. Maybe Baby Bias  
MBB is a form of gender bias that happens in recruitment and promotion processes, affecting 
women of childbearing age. This bias is based on assumptions that the women soon will fall 
pregnant, leading to maternity leave, which can lead to changes in the organisation. As a 
result of this bias, companies tend to favour candidates that are perceived as a safer choice. 
This could be an older person or a man (Gloor, J. L. et al. 2017). Maybe Baby Bias, Maybe 
Baby Expectations and Maybe Baby Effect are all biases that highlight the same bias, 
discrimination of women being in the childbearing age. This is an essential topic since 
discriminating against women based on their age and gender can affect their career 
opportunities and can lead to different salaries (Gloor, J. L. et al., 2017). It also creates 
stereotypes about women, such as the fact that women should take care of their families 
instead of pursuing careers (Diehl, A. and Dzubinski, L.M.,2023). 
Another related concept is the maternal wall. This is another form of bias directed at women, 
when they become mothers. Here, mothers face assumptions that they are less competent and 
less committed in their jobs (Williams, J.C.,2023. p. 27). This type of stereotype is directed 
against women and does not have the same impact on men. When men become fathers, it is 
often viewed as a positive thing in a professional context (Wynn, A.T and Corell, S.J, 2018).  
3.4.1. History and Maybe Baby Bias 
MBB as a term has not been explored in depth in research. However, the situation has been 
described in different ways throughout the years. In 2004, Godfrey Bloom from the European 
parliament said to BBC News, ‚ÄúNo self-respecting small businessman with a brain in the 
right place would ever employ a lady of child-bearing age‚Äù (Gloor, J. L. et al., 2017). 
Similarly, Donal Trump described pregnancy as: ‚Äú... pregnancy is... a wonderful thing for the 
woman, it's a wonderful thing for the husband, it's certainly an inconvenience for a business. 
‚Äì Donald Trump (2004)‚Äù (Gloor, J. L. et al., 2017). These two quotes illustrate how women, 
careers, and children were discussed 20 years ago and demonstrate the social attitude when 
discussing women at work.

3.4.2. The Business Perspective 
From the companies' perspective, the bias often originates from a focus on resources. 
Maternity leaves represent extra resources in terms of time and money. The task needs to be 
allocated, and maternity cover needs to be hired. If not, the task may need to be assigned to 
existing employees within the company. Another thing that companies could be afraid of is 
team dynamics. If an employee needs to be on maternity leave and a maternity cover is hired 
or tasks must be divvied, the team dynamic may change. If a maternity cover needs to be 
hired, it also requires time to onboard a new person, which means that the team‚Äôs 
effectiveness can be limited in a certain amount of time.  
3.4.3. Consequences of Maybe Baby Bias 
The consequences of Maybe Baby Bias can be seen from multiple angles. It can affect 
women's careers by not creating the same opportunities in the job market as, for example, 
men in the same age group (Verniers, C., 2020). This can lead to poorer conditions and lower 
pay. Furthermore, it also creates poorer conditions for the companies. By not hiring women 
and potentially compromising on the diversity within the team, the level of innovation 
delivered, and the bottom line may suffer (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, C.L.,2021). By assembling 
teams with diversity and differences, companies create a foundation for better analysis of 
challenges, leading to improved outcomes and better results. 
3.5. Leadership and Genders  
In the topic of leadership and gender, Sara Louise Muhr and Christina Lundsgaard Ottsen 
have been identified. They have written two books that have contributed with some key 
points in relation to leadership and bias. The first book, ‚ÄúBias Bevidst Ledelse‚Äù, explains how 
a leader can act with bias in focus (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, C.L.,2021). They explain how a leader 
can create decisions without being biased. The same thing goes for the book ‚ÄúLedelse af k√∏n‚Äù 
written by Sara Louise Muhr (Muhr, S.L. (2019).  
In the book ‚ÄúLedelse af k√∏n‚Äù by Sara Louise Murh, she tells a story about how people 
evaluate leaders based on their gender. The study examined how male and female managers 
are perceived and how people view their personalities. In 2003, two American professors 
conducted an experiment where they told students a story about a manager. For half the class, 
the manager was a woman named Heidi, while for the other half, it was a man named 
Howard. The experiment clearly showed that the students preferred Howard over Heidi. They 
were less willing to work for or hire Heidi, perceiving her as selfish. In contrast, they viewed 
Howard as a better manager and a more pleasant person (Muhr, S.L.,2019. p.94).  
Another example of how we see genders as leaders was when Corinne Moss-Racusin did an 
experiment in 2012 (Moss-Racusin et al., 2012). Here she sent a curriculum vitae (referred to 
as CV) out to a large group of people and asked them to evaluate it and were asked to assess 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
 p. 25 
the skills of the person behind the CV, determine whether they believed the person should be 
hired, and indicate if they would consider mentoring the individual. Half of the CVs had the 
male name John, while the other half had the female name Jennifer; otherwise, the CVs were 
identical. The participants who received John‚Äôs CV systematically rated him as more 
competent than those who received Jennifer‚Äôs CV, even though the CVs were identical. 
Participants were also significantly more likely to recommend hiring John, suggested a higher 
salary for him, and were more willing to offer mentorship opportunities. This applied to both 
male and female participants, indicating that both genders were influenced by systemic 
discrimination, which assigned more significant value to men than women. They evaluated 
the male CV more favourably than the female CV (Muhr, S.L.,2019. p.91). 
These two studies show how genders affect our evaluation of them as leaders. It's not 
necessarily their abilities that are being assessed, but rather your abilities based on your 
gender. Additionally, in the US and other Western societies, evaluators tend to value agentic 
leadership more highly ‚Äì traits that are culturally linked to men, such as assertiveness, 
dominance, initiative, decisiveness and independence (Wynn, A.T and Corell, S.J, 2018).  
3.6. Genders at work 
Understanding the dynamics of genders at work is essential to understanding some of the 
underlying reasons for MBB. Research on genders at work highlights differences in the job 
market, for example, regarding responsibilities, expectations and opportunities. The 
differences can impact opportunities, careers, income and how the diversity shows at work.  
Such studies are presented in ‚ÄúInvisible Women‚Äù by Caroline Criado Perez. In this book, she 
shows women‚Äôs unpaid work compared to men (Caroline Criado Perez, 2019). Worldwide, 
75% of all unpaid work is done by women, and they spend between three and six hours a day 
doing it, whereas men spend, on average, somewhere between half an hour and two hours per 
day (Caroline Criado Perez, 2019. p, 88). Unpaid work can be for example be; childcare, 
cooking and cleaning in the household. This means that women, on average work more than 
men, but are paid less for their labour. A consequence of this could be as shown in Great 
Britain. Here 42% of women work fewer hours than average at their job, compared to just 
11% of men (Caroline Criado Perez, 2019. p, 94). These unpaid responsibilities often limit 
women‚Äôs career advancement and perpetuate income disparities. 
Another way of seeing gender bias is described in the book ‚ÄúGlass Walls‚Äù by Amy Diehl and 
Leanne M. Dzubinski. In the book they identify six gender biases and explain why women 
are held back at work (Diehl, A. and Dzubinski, L.M.,2023). The six gender biases are: Male 
privilege, proportionate constraints, insufficient support, devaluation, hostility and 
acquiescence. Instead of talking about glass ceilings, it is explained as glass walls. Where 
bias occurs in all kinds of settings in the workplace. The book also talks about ‚Äúoffice work‚Äù 
where women are expected to help make the office work. It could for example, be tasks like: 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
 p. 26 
Taking notes in meetings, cleaning the meeting room, getting coffee, organising events, 
purchasing flowers or gifts for colleagues, etc. (Diehl and Dzubinski, 2023, p.97). The overall 
theme is how gender bias can damage women's career potential and lead to less income 
during their working years and retirement (Diehl and Dzubinski, 2023, p.190). But it also 
highlights why bringing diversity into a company is so important. For example, a study of 
21980 firms from ninety-one countries showed that ‚Äúthe correlation between women at the c-
suite level and firm profitability is demonstrated repeatedly‚Äù, and the gains are substantial. 
The survey found that ‚Äúfor profitable firms, a move from no female leader to 30 % 
representation is associated with a 15 % increase in the net revenue margin‚Äù (Diehl and 
Dzubinski, 2023, p.192). But it is not just enough to bring women into teams and then sit 
down. Their research shows that to eliminate gender bias, women must be fully accepted, 
supported, and promoted (Diehl and Dzubinski, 2023, p.192).  
Looking at the book more critically, it is clearly written from the American work culture 
perspective. It may have a limiting effect since some situations could appear different from 
other countries or cultures. One of the examples might look different if they were happening 
in Denmark. An example of that could be the Mike Pence rule and maternity leave. The Mike 
Pence rule is that he declared that he would not eat or meet alone with women other than his 
wife. This rule is an attempt to avoid the appearance of sexual impropriety as well as the risk 
of a sexual harassment lawsuit (Diehl and Dzubinski, 2023, p.23). The same thing shows 
when the authors talk about maternity leaves. Here, it is also clear that biases occurring on

and Dzubinski, 2023, p.165). In Denmark, there are clear rules for maternity leave, as shown 
later on in section 3.8.  
Showing how gender dynamics occur in workplaces can reveal how bias and stereotypes 
create structures at work, that is not beneficial, and by showcasing them it may be easier to 
create solutions.

As mentioned in the introduction, team diversity is essential to create innovation and a better

3.8. Maternity rules in Denmark  
One reason companies avoid hiring women can be their assumptions about pregnancy. Being 
pregnant leads to going on maternity leave, which means the company loses these employees 
for a period. This results in companies needing to hire a temporary maternity cover or 
redistribute tasks to other employees, which affects the company‚Äôs resources. Therefore, 
exploring maternity rules in Denmark is essential. The maternity rules in Denmark were 
changed in 2022, which made it more beneficial for both parents. The woman giving birth 
has the right to go on maternity leave four weeks before the baby arrives. The partner gets 14 
days of leave after the baby is born. After 14 days, both parents have 22 weeks each. The 
nine weeks are earmarked and need to be used otherwise, they will be forfeited. The last 13 
weeks are optional and can be transferred to the partner. This means that parents have 24 
weeks of parental leave if they use all of it (Besk√¶ftigelsesministeriet, 2022). Several shifts 
have occurred since Denmark changed the parental leave rules in 2022. According to 
Statistics Denmark, men take a larger share in parental leave. Men who had a child in the last 
five months of 2022 took, on average, 62 days, corresponding to 8.8 weeks of parental leave. 
This is an increase of 25 days compared to the corresponding group from 2021. These figures 
indicate that more men take parental leave for longer durations (Dansk Statistik, 2023).

The first part of the literature review has now been presented. However, the literature also

3.9.1.  Structure and Behavioural Design  
The first way to limit bias was found in the book ‚ÄúByrd Bias‚Äù by Joan C. Williams. Joan 
explained her take on recruiting people with a bias-free mindset. She highlights structure as a 
key factor when recruiting people. She suggests creating a structured approach using a 
spreadsheet/table to evaluate and rank candidates, ensuring the process is applied 
consistently. The key factor is to use this method consistently throughout the process. Bias 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
 p. 28 
thrives when you make decisions based on gut feelings or when you're on autopilot 
(Williams, J.C., 2023. p.59).  
Another proposed solution is highlighted in ‚ÄúBias Bevist Ledelse,‚Äù where behavioural design 
is identified as a key approach to reducing bias (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, C.L.,2021). Behavioural 
design supports diversity and inclusive leadership by challenging the cultural norms that 
reinforce memory biases and habitual thinking. Behavioural design is about making it easier 
to change habits by designing our surroundings to encourage more appropriate actions (Muhr, 
S.L., Ottsen, C.L.,2021. p.148). This can be achieved, for example, by using less transparent 
tools like nudging. A nudge is an unconscious shift in social norms aimed at changing 
behaviour patterns by turning routines and habits upside down and transforming them into 
advantages (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, C.L.,2021. p.149). Nudging can, for example, be when the 
plate is replaced with a smaller one in the canteen and people end up eating less.  
An example of this where nudging was used successfully was in public administration in the 
UK. Here is a group called BIT (Behavioural Insights Team) working primarily with what is 
called social proof, which is based on biases such as herd mentality‚Äîpeople's tendency to do 
what others are doing. Social mechanisms can be trained and used in behaviour design. BIT 
was asked to help increase gender balance in leadership among the UK‚Äôs largest companies in 
the FTSE index. In 2013, only 15% of executives in FTSE 100 companies were women, and 
the goal was for 25% of board members to be women by 2015 (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, 
C.L.,2021. p.164). BIT discovered, with the help of Harvard Professor Iris Bohnet‚Äôs research, 
that the problem needed to be approached differently. Instead of focusing on how few women 
were on boards, the narrative needed to highlight how many women were actually there. The 
solution became a communication strategy using social proof to show that successful 
organisations include both men and women. Rather than stating that there were 15% of 
women on boards, the main message was that over 50% of boards in successful organisations 
had at least one female representative. This norm turned out to be attractive to organisations 
and the goal of the FTSE 100 was achieved in 2015 (Muhr, S.L., Ottsen, C.L.,2021. p.165). 
3.9.2. Bias Training  
In research bias training has also been discussed as a solution or a part of the solution to limit 
bias. Unconscious bias training has been implemented in organizational settings for instance 
in academia to prevent discrimination based on biased decision-making in employment 
(Nentwich, Julia, et al.,2021). The idea is that if you educate people about bias, they will 
become aware of it and, as a result, be less biased. Research on bias training, in general, 
demonstrates that it is effective in increasing awareness (Nentwich, Julia, et al.,2021). But 
this is only for a short period. A report by Britain‚Äôs Equality and Human Rights Commission 
which examined 18 papers evaluating unconscious bias training, concluded that ‚Äúthe evidence 
for UBT‚Äôs [unconscious bias training] ability effectively to change behaviour is limited‚Äù 
(Nentwich, Julia, et al.,2021).  
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
 p. 29 
The same results were created by three sociologists Alexandra Kalev, Frank Dobbin and Erin 
Kelly. They analysed data from the US Equal Employment Opportunity and looked into data 
from 700 companies between 1971 and 2002. Here they researched if standard initiatives 
including bias training increased the diversity. The results showed that bias training within 
companies increased the representation of black men by 4,5% while the representation of 
white women was the same, and black women decreased by 6% (Williams, J.C.,2023, p.37). 
An essential key point to this research is that the data used is over 20 years old. This means 
that the training that was made at the time, also relied on older ways of training. As Joan C. 
Williams in the book ‚ÄúBryd Bias‚Äù points out, ‚ÄúKalve, Dobbin and Kelly showed that bad 
input created a bad outcome‚Äù (Williams, J.C.,2023, p.38).  
In the book ‚ÄúBryd Bias‚Äù Joan C. Williams explains how she and her team create bias training 
that creates an impact. They start out by showing the latest scientific research about bias. 
What do we know about bias at this moment? Then they ask the participants how they can 
work with it and try to make an impact. This form of teaching is working with the fact that 
people are more likely to act on what they've learned if they've committed to a specific action 
in advance (Williams, J.C.,2023, p.39) It has also been shown that bias training with social 
interaction between people creates a bigger impact (Williams, J.C.,2023, p.40). It shows a 
social aspect. People don't like to be told what to do; they like to have a say and be listened 
to. Instead of focusing on changing personal attitudes, bias training should aim to change 
practices, with an emphasis on interactions and processes as key areas for improvement, for 
example, by making visible and questioning the hierarchy in how skills and competencies are 
categorized as masculine or feminine (Nentwich, Julia, et al.,2021).  
3.9.3. AI Generated Tools  
AI can be one solution for limiting bias in recruitment and creating more diversity within 
teams. A recruiting process can vary depending on the company and their needs. Aspects 
such as industry, the HR department, size of the company and financial resources are all key 
aspects for the recruitment process. If the company has resources, AI can be an option to 
make the recruitment process smoother and less biased. The following highlights some of the 
current available options. Some of these have been identified through online research, while 
others have been suggested by experts and organizations interviewed during this study. 
An AI toll that could help with limit bias, was mentioned by one of the interviewed experts,

4.4.1. The Job Post and Screening

4.4.7. Promotion and Salary Negotiation 
Promotion is also a place where bias can occur. This was not a topic within the interview

system based on the assumption that a boss does not always know who does what and who is 
actually creating value for the company. A Zetland article about salary negotiation also 
mentioned the system they had invented. The system is quite simple: each employee lists 
whom they feel comfortable rating. After this, some of these people are selected, and you rate 
them, based on four parameters. The four parameters are in the headlines: `knowledge and 
experience‚Äô, ‚Äòproductivity and quality‚Äô, ‚Äòinnovation and change‚Äô and ‚Äòculture and colleague‚Äô 
(Dandanell, F., 2023). This rating is done twice yearly to ensure that it reflects the 
performance throughout the year. When the rating is done, the scores are placed in a software 
system that calculates which value the employee is creating. The score can be negative or 
positive, but no matter what, all employees will experience an increase in their salary. This 
system is created based on the assumption that your colleagues know you the best and, 
therefore, know what values you create. 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
 p. 44 
When talking to C4, the general feedback for this rating system was positive. Thus, they had 
experienced some feedback from employees concerned about the popular people in the 
workplace getting a better score than, for example, a more introverted person who did not 
have a high social status but still had a high level of performance. This was something they 
were aware of, but as they said: ‚ÄúSo, yes, our system can be a bit biased, but it can also be

just one person.‚Äù  
It was generally an interesting and alternative way of negotiating salary. As they mentioned 
themselves, the system may still be biased, but their argument is that more people are a part 
of this process, and this makes it less biased.  
4.4.8. The Diversity Pledge 
Some of the companies interviewed were selected because they are part of the Diversity 
Pledge from the Danish Industry. Diversity Pledge outlines 16 principles the Confederation 
of Danish Industry has developed to help companies promote diversity, inclusion and 
equality (Dansk Industri, 2024). Two out of the four companies interviewed are a part of this. 
Since the companies in The Diversity Pledge commit to work with diversity, it was 
interesting to talk about what initiatives they are working on and how they are doing it. C1 
and C2 were the two companies that were a part of the Diversity Pledge.  
When asked why they are a part of the Diversity Pledge, C1 answered they did not have a 
concrete answer on which part of the 16 principles they worked with. They just wanted to be 
a good company: ‚ÄúAnd we want to be a good company that attracts the best employees. You 
know, like 99 % of all other companies also say, right?" An addition to this was also made 
‚ÄúWe have this motto called 'People Behind.' It‚Äôs our company motto here‚Äù They simply just 
wanted to be a good company, but they lacked concrete actions towards this.  
The other company, C2 was also a part of the Diversity Pledge. They had a clear strategy 
when working with diversity, having allocated a dedicated team, consisting of different 
employees from different departments that worked within this field: ‚ÄúWe have created a 
working group. There are 11 people in the group, and they are working on diversity in the 
company. They have done all sorts of things to implement initiatives. For example, the 
recruitment team has also been involved, where they are engaging different parts of the 
organization where we need to make changes. [....] So, there are a lot of things they have 
looked into. Gender-neutral toilets at all major locations. They have also reviewed the 
recruitment process. How can we minimize gender bias? They have looked a lot at the 
management team and so on, in terms of how we ensure it is diverse.‚Äù 
It was clear that this company worked with diversity and inclusion. The interview was held at 
their head office, and in the building, there were posters of a campaign the diversity group

4.4.9. Industry in General  
An interesting part was what the companies knew about their industry, for example,

4.4.10. Diversity Within the Team  
Diversity within the team was interesting to dive into who they worked with and if they were

C1 did not have a strategy for bringing diversity to the team and said they should be careful

4.4.12. Solutions Discussed  
In the interview, solutions were discussed: what would they, from their perspective, assume 
could be a solution for them to limit bias in recruiting and MBB? The responses varied, with 
each offering unique answers and reflections on the topic. Since the question was framed 
from their individual perspectives, the answers naturally differed based on their experiences 
and viewpoints.  
C1 said that you should simply just treat people well and not be biased. He did not have any 
concrete examples of how this could be solved, but he just encouraged other companies to 
treat each other well, for example, when being in a recruiting system: ‚ÄúI try to approach 
recruitment processes without any particular bias or prejudice. That‚Äôs the mindset I aim for 
because sometimes you can be positively surprised by what candidates bring to the table.‚Äù  
C2 mentioned societal changes and mindset as things in order to change the situation: ‚ÄúI 
actually believe, to be completely honest, that it‚Äôs something societal that will change this. I 
think we need to take some steps. I see it a little bit like women on boards and in leadership. 
Quotas sound a bit drastic. You can‚Äôt bring in women on quotas who are about to go on 
maternity leave. That‚Äôs not what I mean. But we need to have a more open mindset.‚Äù  
Her point was that having a more open mindset, for example, with recruiting could benefit 
the company, and maybe not be limited by stereotypes and social structures. She gave an 
example from her own personal life: ‚ÄúI also have a girlfriend, and she‚Äôs married to a very,

5. Conclusion  
This thesis researches gender bias, with a particular focus on MBB in recruiting processes 
from the company's perspective. The research question asked was when MBB emerges 
during recruitment processes in private firms and what strategies can be implemented to 
create a more inclusive approach to hiring decisions from the business perspective.  
By conducting semi-structured interviews with four companies, this theme was highlighted. 
The four companies shared their overall recruiting strategy, which can lead to the conclusion 
that: all four companies had different strategies leading to different processes within their 
recruiting.  
Based on the perceptions and reported observations of the firms interviewed none of the four 
companies was familiar with the term MBB or had experienced this bias within the 
companies. When discussing where MBB could occur in the process, it was therefore based 
on their assumptions. Three out of four companies assumed it happens both in the screening 
process of the application and also during the interviews with the applicants, where family 
status, living situation, and other aspects are deeper explored. The last company assumed 
MBB mainly happened in the interview. All four company emphasized that this bias exists 
within the recruiting process.  
Solutions were also explored in the interview with the four companies. A recurring theme in 
their responses was the belief that societal structure, mindset, and open discussions could 
play a key role in increasing awareness of bias in the recruitment process and, by that, 
limiting bias. Bias training, along with creating awareness of bias, was also added as a 
suggestion on how bias can be limited in recruitment. None of the four companies expressed 
interest in using software and AI tools, for recruitment. However, they all emphasised the 
importance of treating applicants with respect and being mindful of their own biases during 
recruitment.

6. Discussion  
The findings from the interviews have now been presented, providing valuable insights about 
gender bias, particularly MBB, across various industries from the companies' point of view. 
The following section aims to discuss these findings, create patterns, and connect them to the 
reviewed literature. By looking at patterns and differences in the data, this discussion will 
examine the findings and how they connect to organisational practices, recruitment strategies, 
and efforts to improve gender equality. 
6.1. Companies Selection  
The first thing to discuss is the selection of the companies. The companies were selected 
based on the fact that they were part of the Diversity Pledge or that they had otherwise shown 
positive signs of wanting to contribute to the diversity debate. Therefore, the assumption was 
that their organisation had some sort of reflection or had made strategic moves to create more 
diversity within their company. Choosing companies was an important decision that 
influenced the angle of the project. It can be argued that companies that already have a 
positive lingo about diversity, for example, by being a part of the Diversity Pledge, may want 
to set themselves in a positive position and this can be a strategic choose.  
Another part in the selection of companies was the selection of the person being interviewed. 
As previously mentioned, it was up to the companies to select the person to be interviewed. 
Leading to one man and three women being interviewed. This was a coincidence since the 
specific title or position of the person was not important. The only condition was that the 
person had to be involved in the company's recruitment process and have knowledge about it 
that they could share. It was, therefore, up to the companies to select the person being 
interviewed. In relation to MBB, it was clear that the women interviewed had either had 
personal experiences with the topic or had reflected on it, either on their own behalf or on 
behalf of their companies. In comparison, the man interviewed had not done the same 
reflections about this theme before the interview. Whether this was a coincidence or 
structurally based on gender is impossible to say, but it was interesting to observe this 
difference in the reflection level.  
6.2. Recruitment Structure  
When looking at the four interviews and their recruiting process, they each stand out in their 
own way. C1 didn't have any clear recruiting structure but rather just went with the flow. 
There were no requirements regarding education or experience for employees they hired, 
which also affected their recruiting process, and it was also clear that even though they were 
part of the diversity pledge, they didn't have a concrete plan in order to create diversity within 
the team. C2, C3 and C4 had different variations of a dedicated group working within 
recruiting. This also affected their recruiting process, which all seemed more streamlined 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
 p. 50 
without being fixed. It was clear that these three companies had similarities in their 
recruitment practice.  
It was clear that the interviewed companies reflected on their own recruitment processes and 
how they dealt with bias in their organisation. In the companies, it seemed like there was a 
general understanding of gender bias and diversity, but it was not anchored in clear, 
knowledge-based processes or practices. Leading to great intentions but no output. Three of 
the companies had dedicated teams that, among other things, worked with inclusion and 
diversity within the company. This showed good intentions, but the resources of these teams 
were not allocated towards their recruitment structure.  
6.3. Transparency  
One of the things worth mentioning when discussing the findings is how the interviews have 
been conducted and how transparent the companies were during the interviews. It was clear 
that some of the companies were more inclined to answer the question than others. C2 was a 
special interview since the person being interviewed was in the process of leaving the 
company, having already secured a new job. As mentioned, it appeared to influence the 
honesty and transparency of the interview. This is, of course, only an assumption and can`t be 
proven in any way.  
When discussing transparency, it was also surprising that the three interviewees were familiar 
with MBB, not the exact term but the situation, but had not observed it within their current 
company. Instead, they had heard of it in other organisations or during their previous 
employment. C3 was honest and shared details about their recruitment process, with two 
candidates under consideration. One of the candidates, they assumed, was quite likely to 
become pregnant in the nearest future. However, due to other circumstances, they chose the 
other candidate for the position. This is always a difficult discussion to have because from 
their perspective, it was other criteria that made them choose the second candidate. But they 
did not say what these criteria were. They only said that if candidate number one didn't want 
the job, they would offer candidate number two the job.  
6.4. The Interview  
The semi-structured interview played a significant role in this project, making it essential to 
be well-prepared and have solid interview skills.  
A problem that occurred within the interviews was that it was hard to get direct answers to 
questions. When looking at the transcribed data and relisting to the interviews, it was clear 
that sometimes when a question was asked, the answer received didn‚Äôt quite answer the 
question. This was hard to do anything about since the interviews had been done. But it was 
noticed when doing the coding and comparison. It was also hard to say if it was me, the 
interviewer, who was not clear in asking the questions or should have been better at asking 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
 
 p. 51 
follow up questions. Something that has been noticed from previous projects is that 
sometimes it's easier to have two people interviewing. Ensuring that if one person loses track 
or something isn't answered, it can be noticed more easily.  
It was also noticed when listening to interviewees, my role as interviewer. It was clear that 
the first interview was a bit messy compared to the last one. Here, the experience of having 
already conducted interviews had clearly left its mark and made the interview more focused, 
and my role as an interviewer was better performed.  
6.5. Grounded Theory  
Grounded Theory has, as mentioned, been applied as the primary research method in this 
thesis, but has been applied with modifications. One modification has been the lack of an 
iterative process, which created constraints when analysing the final data. Usually in 
Grounded Theory collection data and analysis is an interrelated process. Which in practice 
means that data is collected and then analysed before collecting more data. Thus, an analysis 
should have been made after each of the interviews. In this thesis, after each interview, the 
audio file was listened to, and it was observed what could be emphasised more in the 
interview for the next interview, but the data was not fully analysed. Following Grounded 
Theory more structured could have lent to a different result. For example, by exploring 
follow-up interviews with the companies and this leading to even deeper insights.   
6.6. Solution Discussed in the Interviews  
In the literature review, different solutions were identified based on the literature and the 
expert interviewed. Structure and behavioural design, bias training and AI generated tools 
were highlighted from literature review. Heidi Rottb√∏ll Andersen, an expert interviewed, 
suggested bias training and highlighted doing tests in the recruiting process as a key element 
in limiting bias.  
The four companies highlighted societal structure, mindset, open discussions, and bias 
training when being aware of bias and creating a strategy to limit bias. None of them wanted 
help from software and AI tools, but they all emphasised how you should treat people well 
and be aware of the bias you have.  
The found in the literature conflicts a bit with what the companies highlight as their 
suggestion for a solution. First of all, social structures are of course difficult to change. 
Dealing with mindset change and open discussions in the companies show great intention, but 
considering that they are already in use and have not led to significant impact, it is 
challenging to view them as a viable part of the solution. The last solution mentioned was 
bias training; as already highlighted, bias training can't stand alone as a solution against bias 
and, if needed, needs to be linked to a tool.  
==PAGE:52==
 
 
 
 p. 52 
Therefore, a combination of different solutions might be preferable, but it also depends on the 
resources of the company. Structure and behavioural design could benefit if companies lack 
resources. Steps like lettering more people be a part of screening could be a useful tool to 
implement. Letting more people be a part of the recruiting process is a simple solution most 
companies can implement in the recruiting process. This can simply be done by letting a 
dedicated group read the applications instead of only one. This solution does not require a lot 
of resources compared to, for example, investing in AI solutions or bias training. A key 
takeaway from this project is that diversity creates better financial output and more 
innovation. When different people are part of a recruiting process, they have different 
opinions, and by having more people beeping a part of this, better solutions are made. Or at 
least different kinds of people would be in the position of being considerate. Another tool to 
implement is a more structured approach to evaluate candidates. This could be done as Joan 
Williams from the book ‚ÄúBryd Bias‚Äù mentions, where templates are created and used in the 
evaluation process, creating structure in the process.  
If more resources could be allocated, Developed Diverse, mentioned by Anne Sophie 
Sehested M√ºnster from Inspired Beyond Babies could help create job posts that appear less 
biased. AI could also be applied in the screening process. The only concern about AI tools in 
the recruitment process is that AI, like humans, can be biased. Another impact could be made 
by testing in the recruitment process. Heidi Rottb√∏ll Andersen, from Living Institute, 
suggested this as a part of the solution. Her idea was instead of people sending in CV and 
application she wanted the company to do a test of the applicants. The test should be around 
tasks with the position the application was made for. By testing people, a clear identification 
would be made in order to see how people would handle different situations and different 
tasks. And the hiring team would not be biased about the application or CV but only have an 
identification about how well the applicant scored on the test. An interesting observation was 
that none of the companies used this method in order to limit bias.  
When interviewing the companies and the experts a key takeaway was that there is not a 
quick fix in order to limit bias. There is no single solution that can stand alone and by that, 
solve the problem of people being biased in the recruitment process or in life in general. But 
by being aware of one's biases, combined with various tools such as behaviour design, bias 
training and AI tools, some of these biases can be eliminated, creating more diversity and 
better performing teams.

7. Further Investigation  
In this thesis, research has been limited to gender bias in recruitment and MBB, leading to 
more angles that could be exciting to explore or dive deeper into. Below are some of the 
suggestions that could be interesting to explore.  
One of the things that could be very interesting to dive deeper into would be to follow a 
company's recruiting process. An original thought with this thesis was to follow and observe 
a recruiting process from the beginning of the process from start to finish. The idea was to let 
one person in the recruiting team know about the research topic, but the actual recruiting 
team shouldn't know the precise topic. Then the process would be followed / observed to see 
if and when the bias occurs, and how the recruiting team handled this situation. It would 
hopefully show the recruiting process at a very transparent level, due to one of the concerns 
within this project being whether the companies were transparent and showed the real 
processes with the flaws that may follow along.  
Another theme that could be interesting to investigate and research more deeply is MBB from 
the perspective of men. In this thesis, the focus has been on MBB, specifically regarding 
women. However, since the maternity leave rules have been changed in Denmark, where men 
also have specific parental leave rights, this bias may also occur in men's recruiting processes. 
Since they now take a bigger part of the parental leaves, it could be interesting to look at 
whether this bias has changed from only being connected to women to also occurring with 
men in the recruitment process.
