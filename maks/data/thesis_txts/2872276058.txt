Abstract
This work examines the projected impacts of climate change on wind energy generation
across Europe and explores how these impacts can be integrated into long­term energy
system planning to enhance system resilience. Using an ensemble of 15 CMIP6 global
climate and Earth system models under the high­emission SSP5­8.5 scenario, this study
quantifies changes in annual and seasonal Capacity Factor (CF), and the frequency and
duration of wind droughts across six climatologically diverse European regions. A rigorous
two­stage bias correction with a constant scalling factor approach, leveraging ERA5 re­
analysis data and the Global Wind Atlas (GWA), ensures alignment with observational cli­
matology. Wind speeds are extrapolated using logarithmic vertical interpolation to turbine­
specific hub heights, followed by regionally differentiated power output modelling using
established turbine performance curves.
The analysis reveals a spatially heterogeneous but robust seasonal signal, with a signif­
icant decline in summer wind capacity factors across Central and Northern Europe, as
well as Southern Europe in autumn, and high inter­model agreement on these trends. In
contrast, winter and spring exhibit greater model disagreement and statistical variability,
reflecting a more uncertain climate response. Wind droughts, defined using seasonal,
model­specific fifth­percentile CF thresholds, are projected to increase in frequency and
duration in several regions, particularly during summer, although their emergence is less
consistent than the CF signal.
To assess the operational relevance of these projected changes, the Time of Emergence
(ToE) of climate signals is estimated using signal­to­noise ratios and statistical tests, pro­
viding insight into when changes in wind generation become distinguishable from histori­
cal variability. While some regions exhibit early emergence of declining CF signals, such
as the British Isles by mid­century, ToE estimates remain sensitive to methodological as­
sumptions and interannual variability.
By aligning the temporal scope of the projections with typical wind project development cy­
cles, this work addresses a key gap in current literature, namely the lack of medium­term,
operationally relevant projections for wind energy. The methodological framework, en­
compassing bias correction, spatial standardisation, seasonal and extreme­event charac­
terisation, and ensemble­based robustness assessments, is designed to be reproducible
and applicable to other regions or technologies. Findings from this study underscore the
importance of incorporating climate­aware wind generation estimates into energy mod­
els to ensure robust planning, particularly as Europe accelerates its transition towards
renewable­dominated power systems under increasingly non­stationary climate condi­
tions.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Climate change poses one of the most significant challenges of the 21st century, driving
the need for a global transition to sustainable energy systems. Renewable sources have
emerged as pivotal solutions to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions and reduce depen­
dence on fossil fuels. According to the International Energy Agency, renewable energy
must account for 65% of global electricity generation by 2030 to achieve net­zero emis­
sions by 2050 [1]. The European Union has set even more ambitious targets, aiming for
42.5% renewable energy in final consumption by 2030 under the REPowerEU plan [2].
In recent years, wind, solar, and hydropower have led the renewable energy market for
electricity generation. Technological advances in wind farm construction have increased
the economic attractiveness of this energy source [3]. In 2023, Europe installed 18.3 GW
of new wind power capacity. Moreover, the EU is expected to install 200 GW more to
meet its 2030 climate and energy targets [4].
Although these renewable sources are seeing increasing investment, their generational
potential is highly dependent on meteorological conditions. With climate change altering
regional wind and solar patterns, the reliability and predictability of renewable energy
systems are increasingly uncertain [5]. The energy system modeling community is thus
facing a transformation: models must now explicitly incorporate the impacts of climate
change on weather­dependent energy sources [6, 7].
Additionally, a growing body of research underscores the cost­effectiveness of early and
coordinated decarbonisation. [8] show that wind and solar technologies can form the
backbone of a fully decarbonized European energy system. These conclusions further
motivate the need to better understand not only long­term means but also variability and
extreme events under future climate scenarios.
1.2
Problem Statement
The increasing integration of Variable Renewable Energy Sources (VRE), such as wind
and solar, into the power system presents new challenges in the planning and operation
of energy systems. Traditional energy models often assume stationary climatic conditions
and rely on time periods too distant for practical planning. As identified in recent litera­
ture, most climate­wind studies focus on end­of­century projections (2080­2100) that have
limited relevance for current infrastructure decisions [9]. Additionally, wind drought anal­
ysis lacks standardized methodologies, and the concept of Time of Emergence remains
underexplored in renewable energy contexts [10].
Furthermore, climate change introduces changes in wind patterns, solar radiation, and
hydrological cycles, which affects the renewable energy generation potential. Inability to
accurately account for these climatic changes in energy models can lead to suboptimal
planning decisions, reduced system reliability, and inefficiencies in energy supply and
demand balance [11]. Therefore, it is crucial to develop methodologies that incorporate
climate change projections into energy modeling frameworks with appropriate temporal
relevance, ensuring resilience and adaptability in future energy systems.
1.3
Research Question
This study seeks to address the following central research question:

How will climate change affect the spatial and temporal characteristics of wind
energy generation in Europe, and how can these impacts be integrated into
long­term energy system modeling frameworks?
To address this problem, the following sub­questions are explored:
1. Climate Impact Quantification: How do bias­corrected CMIP6 projections indicate

2. Wind Drought Analysis: What standardized methodology can detect and quantify

3. Inter­model Uncertainty: How do the 15 CMIP6 models agree on wind energy
changes, and what does this uncertainty mean for planning decisions?
4. Time of Emergence: When do climate change signals in wind energy become
statistically detectable, and what are the implications for infrastructure investment
timing?
1.4
Scope
This study focuses on the impact of climate change on the potential for wind energy gen­
eration in Europe. The research leverages climate projections such as Coupled Model
Intercomparison Project Phase 6 (CMIP6) [12] from Global Circulation Model and Earth
System Models (ESM) to assess changes in meteorological parameters relevant to re­
newable energy. The study primarily considers mid­term projections (from 2035 to 2070),
in line with Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change scenarios.

Data Models
This chapter provides the theoretical foundation for the datasets used in this study, fo­
cusing on both climate model outputs and reanalysis data. Section 2.1 introduces the
CMIP6 models, highlighting their relevance for assessing the impact of climate change
on wind energy potential. Section 2.2 discusses reanalysis data, with a focus on ERA5,

2.1
CMIP6 Data Models
2.1.1
Importance of Data Selection
Choosing the appropriate dataset is a crucial step in climate impact studies. The quality
and relevance of the selected data directly determines the accuracy, reliability, and scope
of the analysis and projections. In the context of this study, which aims to assess the
impact of climate change on wind energy production in Europe, it is important to rely on
data that provides consistent long­term simulations, and broad international recognition.
The CMIP6 represents the latest and most comprehensive coordinated climate modelling
effort to date [15]. Its models are the result of a 20­year study across major climate re­
search institutions worldwide. CMIP6 data is suitable for this study because it combines
high scientific credibility, large ensemble diversity, and detailed future scenario coverage,
all of which are essential when investigating trends in wind resources under a changing
climate.
2.1.2
Overview of CMIP6
CMIP is a long­standing initiative led by the Working Group on Coupled Modeling (WGCM)
of the World Climate Research Programme (WCRP) [16] . It brings together climate mod­
eling centres across the world to run common climate experiments, share results, and
advance understanding of the Earth system. CMIP began over 20 years ago with a hand­
ful of coupled atmosphere­ocean models. Since then, it has grown into a major contributor
of global climate science and policy support [17]. Details of the previous phases of the
project can be seen in [18] [19].
CMIP6 is the most recent phase of this initiative. It is built around three key scientific
questions: (i) how does the Earth system respond to different types of external forcing, (ii)
what are the sources and impacts of systematic model biases, and (iii) how can we better
understand future climate changes given internal variability and scenario uncertainty. To
address these questions, CMIP6 defines a set of standardized experiments and protocols
that participating models must follow. These are organized into 21 sub­projects, known as
Model Intercomparison Projects (MIPs), each focused on a specific component or process
of the climate system [20].
The models participating in CMIP6 include both Earth System Models (ESMs) [21] and At­
mosphereOcean General Circulation Models (AOGCMs) [22]. ESMs explicitly represent
biogeochemical processes that interact with the physical climate, while AOGCMs rely on
prescribed greenhouse gas concentrations. This means that ESMs account for fluxes of
CO2 between atmosphere, ocean and biosphere, while AOGCMs show the changes in
CO2 concentration are prescribed and cannot vary in response to climate change. This
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
5
==PAGE:20==
distinction allows researchers to analyse a wide range of climate feedback mechanisms
and to investigate both natural and anthropogenic influences on the climate.
Historical simulations in CMIP6 typically cover the period from 1850 to 2014, capturing
observed variations due to natural and human­induced drivers such as solar radiation,
greenhouse gases, and land­use change.
CMIP6 data are freely available and widely used across climate research. They provide
the foundation for international climate assessments and negotiations, and their struc­
tured experimental framework ensures that results can be compared across models in a
consistent way. This makes CMIP6 not only a scientifically robust choice for this study,
but also one that aligns with global standards and expectations.
Bias on CMIP6
Nevertheless, CMIP6 models are not free of biases. In climate models,
biases refer to systematic differences between a model prediction and the actual observed
climate properties, implying that these differences are not due to random variations.
Bias can be induced by several factors, such as the simplification of Earth’s systems
in climate models and the output resolution of the models. GCM models have a coarse
resolution, typically around 100 km or 200 km per grid cell. Some regional climate features
may then not be represented, generating an incomplete understanding of the physical
process [23], [24].
Over the years, several bias­correction methods have been studied to understand which
technique achieves better results. The main assumption carried out in the studies, which
is also implemented in this project, is to consider bias as invariant over time. This assump­
tion considers that the historical bias is the same as the one affecting future climate model
simulations [25], [24]. Bias correction relies on observational reference data, which are
often model­generated, particularly for gridded datasets. Time­invariant bias corrections
imply calibrating an empirical transfer function between simulated and observed climate
data and then applying this function to future model outputs. The time­invariant assump­
tion simplifies the correction process, but it may not always be accurate, as the actual bias
can vary over time due to changes in climate dynamics and external influences. So, even
though this method is widely used, it’s important to be aware of its limitations and the fact
that in some cases, more advanced approaches that include time­varying bias might be
more appropriate.

2.2
Reanlaysis Data
Climate reanalysis datasets are built by combining physical models with a wide range
of observational data to reconstruct the state of the atmosphere, land, and oceans over
several decades [26]. Unlike direct observations, reanalysis provides a spatially and tem­
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
7
==PAGE:22==
porally complete dataset by blending measurements with short­range forecasts using a
technique known as data assimilation. This process allows for the creation of a contin­
uous and physically consistent global dataset, even in regions with limited or irregular
observations [27].
This project uses ERA5 [13] , the fifth­generation reanalysis dataset developed by the
European Centre for Medium­Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). ERA5 offers hourly
estimates of a wide set of climate variables at a horizontal resolution of 0.25◦× 0.25◦,
approximately 30 km, and spans from 1940 to the present. Of particular interest are
the wind components at 100 m and 200 m height, which are available in the form of u
(eastward) and v (northward) vectors. These components can be combined to compute
wind speed and direction, which are essential for wind energy assessments.
ERA5 is widely considered to be one of the most reliable sources for atmospheric reanal­
ysis data due to its high resolution, comprehensive assimilation system, and consistent
global coverage. However, as a reanalysis product, it is not free from error. It inherits
uncertainties both from the numerical weather prediction model and from the data assim­
ilation system used to ingest observational data. These uncertainties can lead to regional
biases, particularly in wind speed estimates [28].
To address this, ERA5 wind speeds are first bias­corrected using data from the Global
Wind Atlas (GWA) [29]. The GWA provides high­resolution (250 m) validated wind re­
source estimates derived from mesoscale modeling and observational datasets. By cor­
recting ERA5 with GWA, we reduce known regional biases, especially over areas where
wind energy analysis is highly sensitive to small­scale spatial variability.
After this correction, the GWA­adjusted ERA5 data are used as the reference dataset for
bias correction of the CMIP6 climate model outputs. A spatially­resolved correction factor
is calculated by comparing ERA5 and model wind speeds over the historical period. This
factor is then applied to the model projections for the End of Century period, ensuring that
the corrected data are more aligned with observational reality.
This two­step correction process, first from GWA to ERA5, and then from ERA5 to CMIP6,
ensures a more accurate foundation for evaluating wind energy potential under future
climate scenarios.
2.3
Global Wind Atlas
The Global Wind Atlas (GWA) is employed in this study to reduce regional biases in ERA5
wind speeds, particularly those arising from complex terrain and heterogeneous land sur­
face characteristics. Developed by DTU Wind Energy in collaboration with the World
Bank, the GWA provides high­resolution global wind resource data at a spatial resolu­
tion of 250 m, with coverage at multiple standard heights above ground level. It is based
on the Numerical Wind Atlas methodology, which integrates mesoscale simulations with
microscale corrections to enhance the representation of local wind climates [30].
Mesoscale modeling in the GWA is carried out using the Weather Research and Fore­
casting (WRF) model, while microscale downscaling is conducted with the WAsP model
[31], incorporating detailed terrain and land cover datasets. Successive versions, namely
GWA1, GWA2, and GWA3, have introduced incremental improvements to both the mesoscale
and microscale components. In particular, GWA3 includes updated topographic data, with
new sources for both surface roughness and elevation.
Compared to coarser reanalysis products, the GWA accounts for fine­scale features such
as orographic speed­up and surface heterogeneity, resulting in more spatially accurate

wind climatologies. In this study, long­term mean wind speeds from ERA5 are scaled to
match those reported by GWA3. This adjustment improves the spatial consistency of the
ERA5­based climatologies, thereby enhancing the reliability of bias correction procedures
applied to climate model outputs.
This method has been shown to improve both the statistical properties of wind speed
distributions and the temporal realism of simulated wind generation. As reported by [32],
combining GWA­based scaling with ERA5 reduces bias, narrows inter­model spread, and
increases agreement with measured country­level wind generation data, even in the ab­
sence of calibration against power production.

2.4.1
Data Collection Periods
In order to follow best practices and ensure that the analysis of results can be presented

approach.
First, it was decided that the Beginning of Century (BoC) and End of Century (EoC) periods
should be of equal length. This allows for a fair comparison between the two datasets and
ensures that both periods have similar statistical weight. Based on the availability of data

time, which was a limiting factor due to the time constraints of this project.
Taking this into account, the BoC period was defined as spanning from 1980 to 2015, and
the EoC period from 2035 to 2070. These periods are sufficiently distant from each other
to represent two distinct parts of the century. Although the EoC period technically falls
closer to the middle of the century, it will be referred to as EoC throughout the thesis for
clarity and consistency.
Although the climate models used in this study employ different calendar types, such
as NoLeap or 360­day calendars, this project focuses primarily on seasonal and annual
means. As a result, standardizing calendar types across models is not necessary for the
purposes of this analysis.

3
Literature Review
3.1
Introduction
Wind energy is a key component of Europe’s climate policy and is expected to play a
major role in the decarbonisation of the power sector [33]. However, its variability poses
challenges to system reliability, especially as climate change may alter wind patterns over
time. These changes can affect both the magnitude and temporal distribution of wind
energy generation, thereby increasing the risk of supply­demand mismatches.
Given the growing reliance on variable renewables and the long operational lifespans of
energy infrastructure, it is essential to assess how climate change could impact future
wind energy availability. In particular, understanding trends in seasonal variability and
extreme low­wind events is critical to ensure energy security and inform resilient planning
strategies.
This chapter reviews the state of the art in climate change impacts on wind energy, with
attention to modelling techniques, data sources, bias correction methods, and projections.
Special focus is given to the treatment of wind droughts and the emerging application of
Time of Emergence (ToE) to detect when changes become operationally relevant.
3.2
Data Sources and Study Scope
As detailed in chapter 2, studies investigating the impacts of climate change on wind en­
ergy typically rely on a combination of reanalysis data and outputs from global climate
models, especially those from the CMIP5 [34] and CMIP6 [35] ensembles. Reanalysis
datasets such as ERA5 [13] are commonly used for historical validation and bias correc­
tion [36]. Future projections are based on Global Circulation Model (GCM), often down­
scaled or bias­corrected [37], to provide finer spatial and temporal resolution.
Recent studies show a wide spatial domain coverage of geographical regions, in different
scales and different regions. Some studies assess the future wind resources on a global
scale [38], even though it is more common to focus on a local or regional scale. In fact,
the spatial domain coverage of most studies focuses on Europe [11] or subregions such
as the North Sea [9], the Mediterranean [39], or the Iberian Peninsula [40].
The temporal focus generally spans over multiple decades, often comparing a historical
reference period (e.g., 1980–2000) with a projection period far in the future (e.g., 2080
­ 2100). These far­in­the­future periods are relevant for theoretical studies, but less im­
pactful for feasibility studies for currently planned wind farms [9, 11].
The vast majority of studies rely on the raw wind speed output from climate models to
estimate turbine power production. These power estimates are then used to quantify
changes, typically by computing either the absolute difference or the relative change be­
tween future and historical periods [9]. Most studies focus on a single turbine type, usually
offshore, since offshore wind power can be easier to predict, since no surface roughness
and land use needs to be accounted for [41, 42].
An important step in this process is the accurate interpolation of wind speeds to the tur­
bine’s hub height. This requires careful consideration of ground­level characteristics such
as surface roughness and land­sea transitions, as these factors can significantly affect the
extrapolated wind speeds and, ultimately, the estimated power output [43, 44].

3.3
Wind Energy Modelling under Climate Change
3.3.1
Bias in Climate Models
Systematic bias is a persistent issue in climate­based wind energy assessments. Differ­
ences in surface roughness, resolution, and atmospheric dynamics cause discrepancies
between modeled and observed wind speeds [45]. Bias correction methods such as quan­
tile mapping [23] and linear scaling [46] are widely applied to address this. However, these
techniques often assume temporal stationarity, which may not hold in future projections
[24].
New studies include bias correction methods to correct the wind speeds, using reanalysis
data [24, 45]. Moreover, the use of a multi­model mean to reduce the bias of individual
models is very common. This approach creates a more robust representation of the effect
of climate change on wind speed. Typically, studies account for 5 to 15 different climate
models [47–49]. Bias correction methods require the reference and model dataset to be
in the same spatial resolution grid.
3.3.2
Regridding Methodology
Regridding or remapping is the process of interpolating from one grid to another. It can
involve spatial and temporal interpolations, but it is most commonly referred to for spatial
coordination. Regridding is a necessary step in model coupling and model comparison,
among others [50, 51]. The Earth System Modeling Framework (ESMF) [52] is a tool
that offers a common procedure for regridding operations. The tool does not assume 2D
coordinates, but takes into account the spherical geometry of the Earth. The first step of
regridding involves the creation of an interpolation weight matrix that shows how a point
in the source grid contributes to a point in the destination grid [53]. This tool is often used
to regrid datasets to relevan spatial grids [54–56].
The two most common remapping methods are bi­linear interpolation and first­conservative.
Bilinear interpolation is suitable for smoothly varying fields, as it uses the four nearest grid
cells to estimate new values [57]. However, this method can create errors for variables
with sharp changes, such as 3­hourly precipitation or wind speed, especially when regrid­
ding from high to low resolution.
In contrast, the first­order conservative method preserves the total quantity, such as flux
or energy, during regridding by averaging over the overlapping areas between source
and target grid cells [58]. This method is recommended for real­world data, particularly
when transitioning from high­resolution to coarser grids, as it ensures that key physical
properties are maintained, a crucial requirement for climate simulations [24]. It is important
to note, that all regridding methods will add systematic bias to the data [50].
3.3.3
Height Interpolation and Wind Speed Extrapolation
Wind speeds are typically provided at fixed model levels (e.g., 10 m or 100 m), neces­
sitating extrapolation to turbine hub heights. The chosen method for this process has a
significant impact on the results of the studies, as it can amplify or attenuate the find­
ings, specifically at higher altitudes [59]. The most common methods of extrapolation are
a power law with a shear exponent of 1/7 and a logarithmic wind profile using offshore
surface roughness [9, 24, 60]. Although there are studies presenting more innovative
methods, the aim of this section is to provide an insight into the most commonly used
ones.
The logarithmic wind profile is based on boundary layer theory and incorporates both sur­
face roughness and atmospheric stability, making it more physically grounded [61]. On
the other hand, the power law is an empirical approach that relates wind speed to height
12 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:27==
using a fixed shear exponent, most commonly 1/7 [62]. This value only fits the condi­
tions with neutrally­stratified atmosphere and smooth surface conditions, which may not
be valid for all regions or time periods [63, 64] . As highlighted in [60], the power law can
approximate the logarithmic profile under stable conditions and for very smooth surfaces,
but tends to diverge under neutral or unstable stratification. Relying on a constant shear
exponent can therefore introduce bias, particularly in offshore applications or when as­
sessing long­term changes in wind speed at hub height. Despite its limitations, the power
law remains the most used method due to its simplicity and ease of implementation [65].
Recent studies have chosen to define two shear exponents coefficients, one for onshore
(αL = 1/7) and one for offshore ( αO = 1/10) [66].
3.3.4
Capacity Factor and Power Output Estimation
The Capacity Factor (CF) is used to normalize power output across different turbines
and locations. It is calculated as the ratio between actual and rated power output, using
turbine­specific power curves. While some studies use the wind speed as output, it is
more relevant to study capacity factor, as it gives a contextualized approach to the energy
potential [67, 68]. As mentioned, it has been seen that the vast majority of studies assume
the same wind turbine, and thus the same power curve across the covered region. When
mixing land and sea areas, this approach can be misleading, as onshore and offshore
wind turbines have not only significantly different power curves, but also different hub
heights.
3.3.5
Temporal Resolution and Standardisation
Temporal aggregation (daily, monthly, seasonal, annual) is critical in analysing long­term
trends and extremes. While climate models differ in calendar types (e.g., 360­day vs.
Gregorian), this study avoids these issues by working with monthly or seasonal averages.
This simplifies inter­model comparisons and ensures consistency in trend detection.
3.3.6
Time of Emergence (TOE)
The time at which the signal of climate change rises from the noise of natural climate
variability (Time of Emergence, ToE) is a key variable for climate predictions and risk
assessments. In his paper, Hawkins laid the groundwork by introducing a methodology
to estimate ToE for surface air temperature, highlighting that the emergence of climate
signals varies by region and is influenced by both the magnitude of the signal and the
background variability[69]. Different methodologies to assess ToE of climate signals have
been used in the literature [70]. The most common one is to assess the ToE by estimating
the climate change signal (S) and the variability (or noise, N) of the climate metric of
interest [69], [71]. The degree of emergence is often categorized using qualitative terms
such as unusual (S/N>1), unfamiliar (S/N>2), and unknown (S/N>3), each reflecting a
greater departure from historically experienced conditions [72].
3.4
Seasonal Variability and Wind Droughts
3.4.1
Seasonality in Wind Energy Availability
Seasonal patterns in wind speed and power output are well documented in the literature
[73], [74]. Looking at the data season by season helps better understand how things
might change, because it puts the values into a context that reflects the yearly cycle. This
makes it easier to spot patterns or shifts that might not be visible when just looking at
long­term averages. Several studies highlight the risk of shifting seasonal cycles, which
could have implications for energy balancing and storage requirements [75].

==PAGE:28==
3.4.2
Wind Droughts: Definitions and Literature Approaches
Wind droughts are defined as extended periods of below­threshold wind energy avail­
ability. Though still an emerging topic, various definitions exist in the literature, typically
involving thresholds based on percentile values of the capacity factor and a minimum
duration criterion [76]. Some studies also consider spatial coverage, requiring a certain
fraction of the region to be below threshold for an event to qualify as a drought. Despite
growing interest, there remains a lack of standardized methodologies for detecting and
quantifying wind droughts in climate projections [10].
3.5
Summary and Research Gaps
The reviewed literature shows that most climate­based wind energy studies rely on re­
analysis data and GCMs, often using simplified methods for wind extrapolation and power
estimation. While there is increasing attention on seasonal variability and long­term pro­
jections, inconsistent methodologies, especially around wind droughts and temporal rel­
evance, limit comparability and application. Despite valuable advances, some important
issues are still not fully addressed.
Firstly, many studies look at time periods that are too far into the future to be useful for
current wind farm planning. Secondly, the way wind speeds are adjusted to turbine height
is often done using fixed values, without considering changes in the environment or tur­
bine type. There is also little focus on how to combine land and sea wind data properly,
which matters in regions where both onshore and offshore turbines are used. Third, wind
droughts are not always defined or studied in the same way, which makes it hard to com­
pare findings or apply them in practice. Lastly, this work aims to advance the emerging
concept of Time of Emergence (ToE) in the context of wind energy.
This work tries to fill those gaps by focusing on a time period that matches typical wind
project timelines. Moreover, it uses a more flexible approach for height interpolation, with
separate treatments for land and sea turbines. It also defines wind droughts using a
clear threshold that takes into account both the season and the region, helping to give
more useful and consistent results. Additoinally, by detecting when significant changes in
wind resource patterns first become distinguishable from historical variability, this study
tries to pinpoint the timeframes in which future changes may meaningfully impact wind
energy planning and operations. Finally, to ensure robustness, this work includes a large
ensemble of climate models.

4.3
Statistical Tests
This section presents the statistical methods employed to assess the robustness of pro­
jected changes in wind energy potential.
The goal is to evaluate both the consistency across climate models and the statistical
significance of observed changes in capacity factor and drought characteristics across
regions, time periods, and seasons. The limitations of statistical tests in climate datasets
are discussed later on.
4.3.1
Agreement Among Models
Model agreement is a useful metric for assessing the consistency of projections across
different climate models. It provides a quick overview of whether there is a general con­
sensus on the sign of change for a given climate indicator, such as capacity factor or
drought duration. High model agreement increases confidence in the projected trend,
whereas low agreement highlights uncertainty and variability among models, suggesting
that the signal may be weak or driven by model­specific assumptions.
This metric can be visualized through agreement maps, where a defined threshold (e.g.,
75%) determines whether the sign of change is consistent across models at each location.
Alternatively, heatmaps can summarize the fraction of models agreeing on the sign of
change across regions and seasons, offering a more aggregated view.
While model agreement offers an intuitive first impression of potential trends, it is essential
24 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:39==
to complement this with statistical testing. Assessing the statistical significance of the
projected changes ensures that the apparent agreement is not due to random variability
and helps identify cases where the signal is both consistent and robust.
4.3.2
Welch’s t­test: Mean Differences Between BoC and EoC
To evaluate the statistical significance of changes in seasonal capacity factor, Welch’s t­
test is applied independently for each combination of model, region, and season. This test
is chosen because it does not assume equal variances or equal sample sizes between
the two groups, which is often the case in climate simulations [83] [84].
The null hypothesis proposed is: The mean seasonal capacity factor in BoC and EoC is
equal. A p­value < 0.05 will then indicate that there is a statistically significant change in
the mean, and thus the hypothesis can be rejected.
Limitations of the t­test
Although Welch’s t­test is widely employed to compare mean
values across time series, its application to wind speed data involves several method­
ological limitations. First, wind speed time series typically exhibit temporal autocorrela­
tion, which reduces the effective sample size. If this is not accounted for, the estimated
variance can be underestimated, resulting in inflated Type I error rates [85]. Second, wind
speed distributions may deviate from normality, especially in small samples or when data
are seasonally aggregated. Such deviations can affect the validity of p­values if the stan­
dard assumptions of the test are violated [86]. To mitigate these concerns, the present
study applies the t­test to spatially aggregated regional time series rather than individ­
ual grid­point values. This reduces the influence of spatial dependence and sampling
variability and aligns the statistical inference with the scale of the regional analysis.
4.3.3
Boxplot Distribution Analysis
In addition, inter­model spread in seasonal projections is assessed using boxplots. For
each region and season, the relative change in capacity factor (∆CF) and the change
in mean drought duration between the BoC and EoC periods are calculated for all mod­
els. These distributions are summarized with boxplots to illustrate ensemble spread and
identify outliers.
These visualizations help quantify model disagreement within each region and season,
and support the statistical testing and robustness assessments presented in the results.
4.3.4
Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)
For each region, kernel density plots of annual mean capacity factor are produced for
both BoC and EoC periods. Each model’s annual capacity factor values are used to
create a Probability Density Function (PDF), grouped by time period. These distributions
help visualize not only the average change in capacity factor, but also the spread and
year­to­year variability [47].
The average interannual variability across all models in each region (denoted as σy) gives
an idea of how stable or variable wind resources are expected to be over time. To assess
the spread between models, the standard deviation of the model­mean capacity factors at
EoC (denoted σModels, EoC) is calculated. This metric reflects how different the projections
are between models.
4.3.5
Ensemble Distributions and Significance Masking
This step involves evaluating the statistical characteristics of the annual capacity factor
distributions at the BoC and EoC periods across all models and regions. For each case,
the distribution is first checked for normality using the Shapiro–Wilk test. This test helps

4.4
Time of Emergence (ToE)
As discussed in chapter 3, the concept of Time of Emergence (ToE) remains relatively
new and lacks a standardized methodological framework. Nevertheless, it is increasingly
recognized as a key metric for climate prediction and risk assessment, particularly in the
context of long­term planning for energy infrastructure. This section explores and tests
different approaches for estimating the Time of Emergence, to identify a robust and con­
sistent method to draw reliable conclusions.
To estimate the ToE, it is essential to work with a continuous time series of the metric under
consideration, in this case, the capacity factor. To achieve this, the temporal gap between
the BoC and EoC periods defined in this study will be addressed by generating capacity
factor time series for the intervening years (2015–2070). This additional “mid­century”
data enables the construction of a continuous temporal profile, which is necessary to track
when a climate­driven signal emerges from the background of natural variability. Such
continuity enhances the reliability of the Time of Emergence estimation and supports a
more nuanced interpretation of long­term trends in wind energy availability.
26 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:41==
4.4.1
Computation of ToE
The Time of Emergence is computed by identifying the first year in which the ensemble
median (across all models) departs from the baseline variability, defined over the BoC
period, and remains outside this range for a minimum number of consecutive years. An
emergence is considered statistically significant when the signal exceeds a threshold de­
fined as k × σs, where σs denotes the interannual standard deviation during the baseline
period. In this analysis, the threshold is set to k = 2, and the persistence criterion is set to
five years, which is sufficient given the time horizon of the study to confirm that the shift
is consistent and not just a temporary fluctuation.
On another perspective, the Time of Emergence can also be computed individually for
each model and then summarized using the IQR range (25th–75th percentiles), thereby
offering a robust ”ensemble spread” approach. This method enhances reliability by requir­
ing not just the ensemble median but also the lower or upper bounds of the inter­quartile
range to cross the threshold, ensuring that the emergence is not driven by outliers but is
a consistent feature across the model ensemble.
4.4.2
Signal­to­Noise Emergence
To characterize the rarity and significance of projected changes relative to baseline vari­
ability, the signal­to­noise (S/N) ratio of the ensemble mean time series for each region is
calculated. The S/N ratio is computed as the difference between the ensemble mean at
each year and the baseline mean (BoC), normalized by the baseline standard deviation.
Three S/N thresholds corresponding to increasingly rare categories: Unusual (S/N < −1),
Unfamiliar (S/N < −2), and Unknown (S/N < −3), following established conventions in
climate risk assessment.
For each category, the time of emergence (ToE) as the first year in which the S/N ratio
remained below the threshold for at least five consecutive years is identified, indicating a
persistent and statistically rare departure from historical conditions.
4.4.3
Statistical Relevance
To ensure robustness, the ToE can also be computed through statistical testing. In this
analysis, two tests are applied. The Welch’s t­test compares annual model values with the
baseline values, under the assumption of normality, and is robust to unequal variances. As
a non­parametric alternative, the Mann–Whitney U test is also employed, which does not
require the assumption of normality. For each test, the first sequence of consecutive years
(here, five years) in which the p­value remains below a specified significance threshold is
identified. The first year in this sequence is then recorded as the ToE.
The use of two statistical tests provides a higher degree of confidence that the identified
emergence is not the result of random variability, but reflects a consistent and significant
change in the underlying distribution.

5.4
Time of Emergence
This section explores the Time of Emergence (ToE) of climate change signals in wind en­
ergy Capacity Factors across Europe. ToE refers to the point in time when the signal of
climate change becomes statistically distinguishable from natural variability. As studied
in chapter 3, identifying this moment is critical for understanding when projected changes
may become operationally relevant for energy planning and infrastructure decisions. Mul­
tiple methods can be used to estimate ToE, including threshold­based deviation, signal­
to­noise ratio analysis, and statistical significance testing. The British Isles are used as
a case study to illustrate the application of these methods and highlight the variability in
emergence estimates depending on the approach used. The figures for the rest of the
regions can be seen in Appendix C.
5.4.1
Computation of ToE
As explained in subsection 4.4.1, the Time of Emergence (ToE) is computed by checking
when the time series crosses a set threshold for at least five consecutive years. In this
case, the threshold is defined as ±2 × σ. Figure 5.15 shows the annual Capacity Factor
time series for the British Isles from 1980 to 2070. While the ensemble median does
not cross the threshold, two individual models do, as shown by the faint vertical lines.
Even though there is no formal emergence at the ensemble level, the time series shows a
downward trend in Capacity Factor, pointing to a gradually strengthening climate signal.

Summer exhibits the most widespread and consistent decline in Capacity Factor (CF)

The results for wind drought do not reveal a clear or consistent pattern in the average

lack statistical robustness. The same applies to long droughts, defined as events lasting

Only in a few cases, such as in the British Isles and Eastern Europe during winter, does
the number of models indicating an increase rise above 70% , but even there, the signal
remains statistically inconclusive.

a high degree of agreement on decreasing capacity factors do not necessarily coincide
with those showing consistent increases in drought length. This disconnect suggests that
projected reductions in average wind output are not primarily driven by more frequent or
prolonged extreme low­wind events. Instead, they are more likely the result of a broad
weakening of mean wind conditions, rather than a shift in the statistical properties of wind

This distinction is important for interpreting climate impacts on wind energy. While ex­

here imply that the future challenge may be more subtle and systemic, characterized by

6.1.3
Statistical Significance and Model Agreement
The overlap between high model agreement and statistical significance, particularly in the
British Isles, Central Europe, and the North Sea during summer, provides strong evidence
for a robust decline in wind energy potential. This convergence is critical, as reliable
conclusions depend on the presence of both ensemble consensus and statistical support.
These regions should therefore be prioritised in adaptation planning.
In contrast, projections for spring and winter are less consistent. Individual models may
indicate changes, but without agreement or statistical significance, these signals remain
uncertain. This reflects both natural variability and the current limitations of climate models
in transitional seasons.
A key insight is that ensemble mean changes alone are insufficient. Robust findings
require both model agreement and significance. Where either is missing, results should
be treated probabilistically and used in broader risk assessments, not as deterministic
forecasts.
The choice of statistical test also plays a significant role. Parametric tests like Welch’s
t­test assume normality and independence, which are often violated in climate data. As
noted in [85, 86], this can lead to over­ or underestimation of significance due to autocorre­
lation and underestimated variance. Non­parametric tests, such as the Mann–Whitney U
test, offer a more robust alternative under these conditions. As shown in Figure 6.2, both
methods consistently identify significant summer declines in capacity factor, especially in
Central, Eastern, and Southern Europe. While the Mann–Whitney test tends to produce
slightly fewer significant areas, the overall patterns are consistent across methods.
In summary, credible signals emerge when high model agreement aligns with statistically
significant trends, using methods appropriate to the data. Ensemble means alone are not
sufficient. Energy planning should be based on consistent results across both consensus
and robust significance testing.

The Time of Emergence (ToE) analysis highlights an important temporal dimension of the
projected decline in wind energy potential. While the ensemble mean does not point to
a uniform or immediate emergence across Europe, several individual models begin to
show signs of statistically distinct behavior from the baseline as early as the mid­2020s.
This variation illustrates that emergence is not a singular, clearly defined event but a
probabilistic signal that may appear earlier in some model realizations or regions than
others.
This is further reinforced by the signal­to­noise evaluation, which shows capacity factor
values increasingly deviating from historical variability envelopes. Even in the absence
of clear emergence at the ensemble level, the tendency for values to drift into statistically
unusual or unfamiliar territory suggests a strengthening climate signal. In this context,
the concept of emergence becomes more of a risk threshold, where change is not yet
inevitable across all cases but increasingly likely, and thus relevant for long­term planning.
Different statistical approaches to estimating ToE, such as parametric and non­parametric
tests, return varying results, with some methods indicating emergence in the near term
and others delaying it by decades. This divergence reflects not only differences in test
assumptions but also the complexity of the underlying signal, which may be obscured by
internal variability or regional climate noise. The sensitivity of results to method choice
reinforces the need for a multi­method perspective when evaluating emergence timelines.
Critically, the uncertainty surrounding exact emergence timing does not undermine the
broader conclusion: there is a clear and gradual deterioration of wind energy potential,
especially during summer months, and this trend is detectable in several models even
before mid­century. As seen in Figure 6.3, the decreasing trend in capacity factor across
Europe is evident, even if the ensemble mean conceals some of the variability between
models. The challenge lies in deciding when this emerging signal becomes operationally

6.3
Uncertainty and Model Spread
Despite ensemble­mean trends, inter­model spread and interannual variability remain a
concern, particularly in summer and in regions like Eastern and Central Europe. This
variability limits confidence in regional projections and highlights the necessity of using
ensemble­based approaches rather than relying on single­model outputs.
Much of this spread stems from structural differences among models, such as surface­
layer parameterizations, horizontal and vertical resolution, and the treatment of land­sea
interactions, which all influence wind simulation, especially in areas with complex terrain or
sharp gradients. While bias correction improves comparability by addressing systematic
offsets, it cannot fully eliminate model­specific behavior or dampen internal variability,
which can obscure or exaggerate trends.
These uncertainties do not undermine the value of the projections but instead empha­
size the need for cautious interpretation. They support the adoption of ensemble­based
planning strategies that focus on robustness over precision, prioritizing resilience across
a range of plausible futures rather than optimal outcomes based on mean trends alone.

6.4
Comparison with Literature
Understanding how the findings of this study relate to existing research is essential for
contextualising their significance and evaluating their contribution to the broader scientific
discourse. This section reviews the current literature on climate change impacts on wind
energy in Europe, focusing on model biases, capacity factor projections, wind drought
characteristics, and the concept of time of emergence. It highlights areas of agreement
and divergence between this study and previous works, thereby situating the results within
the evolving knowledge on renewable energy resilience under climate variability.
Bias of CMIP6
The findings of this study align with previous research highlighting re­
gional variability in wind energy projections under climate change. Hahmann et al. [9]
observed similar bias characteristics in CMIP6 models for the North Sea, notably the
under­performance of the MIROC6 model, which was also excluded from this analysis
due to its significant deviations from observed wind patterns. Additionally, Hahmann et
al. confirmed the good quality and validity of the remaining CMIP6 models that are also
used in this study.
Capacity Factor
Analysis of the CMIP5 models concluded a general increase in wind
speed in Northern and Central Europe, and a general decrease in Southern Europe [88,
89]. On the other hand, studies using Euro­CORDEX models showed more mixed trends.
Tobin et al. [90] found that the change in energy production would mostly remain within
a ±5 % range across the century, suggesting limited overall impact. Moemken et al. [91],
however, reported only minor changes for annual and winter output but identified a more
pronounced decrease during summer months. These findings align more closely with the
conclusions reached in this study, especially in terms of seasonal behaviour.
A possible explanation for this divergence lies in the methodological differences. Moemken
et al. [91] applied vertical interpolation and bias correction, similar to the approach fol­
lowed here. In contrast, Tobin et al. [90] used extrapolation from 10 m, which can be less
reliable, particularly for estimating hub­height wind speeds and capacity factors. This
highlights the importance of data treatment choices, which can significantly influence the
results.
More recent work based on CMIP6 models [11], including those used in this study, projects
a stronger and more widespread decline in wind energy resources, often exceeding 10 %
in most European regions. The study emphasizes the differences between CMIP6 and
CMIP5 outcomes, underlining the importance of considering the model generation when
interpreting results. The findings in [11] are in strong agreement with the conclusions
of this thesis, particularly in confirming the expected downward trend in capacity factors
and the emerging seasonal asymmetry. Morover, the work in [92] using similar models
to the ones studied in this work, show similar results in model agreement across different
seasons, showing low percentage in winter and a higher agreement in summer.
Wind Droughts
Studies focusing on wind droughts remain less conclusive than those
examining wind energy variability. In [93], wind droughts during the winter season in Eu­
rope are investigated using a methodology closely aligned with that of the present study.
Drought events are defined based on a 10­year return period, using the mean load factor
as a threshold. The analysis is conducted with Euro­CORDEX regional climate models
and reveals a significant increase in the probability ratio of winter wind drought occur­
rences in future climate scenarios. The study also identifies clear regional disparities,
with northern European countries projected to experience a marked increase in drought

==PAGE:69==
frequency, while southern and central Europe show relatively stagnant trends. These
findings align with the ones discussed in this project.
Further evidence of current shifting of wind patterns is provided by [94], which analyses
ERA5 reanalysis data to assess extreme wind events. These are defined as wind speeds
falling below cut­in or exceeding cut­out thresholds relevant for wind turbine operation.
The study reports a higher occurrence of high wind events over northern Europe during
winter and an increase in low wind events both in northern Europe during summer and
throughout the year in the Mediterranean region, suggesting seasonal and spatial vari­
ation in offshore wind availability. Lastly, [95] examines the potential impact of climate
change on both solar and wind energy generation across Europe. The authors conclude
that average wind speeds are expected to decline in several regions, accompanied by
an increase in extreme events. The study underscores the necessity of understanding
these projected changes in order to support the planning and development of resilient
and efficient solar and wind energy systems.
In [92], extreme wind events are defined via typical turbine cut­in and cut­out thresh­

annual basis, with some regions showing winter decreases. These patterns resemble the
seasonal and regional variations found in this work, reinforcing the overall upward trend
in wind drought risk, even as localized discrepancies in magnitude and sign persist due
to model spread.
Time of Emergence
The existing literature on Time of Emergence of climate signals for
wind energy is scarce. Moreover, the existing papers reach the same conclusion found in
this study: ToE varies by model, and emergence shows a high inter­model variability [70].
This highlights the need for ensemble­based approaches and robust statistical methods
to ensure reliable assessments of wind­related climate risks in future energy planning.
6.5
Implications for Energy Planning
Climate change has a wide­ranging impact on energy systems, influencing both produc­
tion and consumption. As global temperatures rise, the demand for cooling increases,
especially during the summer months, along with the overall need for electricity [96]. The
projected seasonal asymmetry in wind energy potential, with declining summer capacity
factors and the possibility of more frequent winter wind droughts, raises concerns about
imbalances between supply and demand. In addition, changes in precipitation affect hy­
droelectric power generation [97], and variations in cloud cover can influence solar energy
output [98]. To address these challenges, two key strategies are needed: mitigation and
adaptation. Mitigation focuses on limiting future global warming by reducing greenhouse
gas emissions. Adaptation refers to the adjustments needed in response to the effects
of climate change on social, economic and natural systems, whether these effects are
already happening or are expected in the future.
These findings underscore the necessity of developing more flexible and adaptive power
systems. Expanding solar photovoltaic (PV) capacity can partially offset the anticipated
decline in summer wind energy production. At the same time, investments in energy
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System 55
==PAGE:70==
storage and demand­side flexibility are critical for ensuring grid stability during prolonged
periods of low wind availability. Notably, wind and solar resources tend to exhibit contrast­
ing seasonal patterns, particularly during summer months, which reinforces the value of
hybrid systems and spatial diversification as effective strategies to smooth variability and
reduce the risk of renewable energy droughts [99].
Including the concept of Time of Emergence (ToE) in energy planning can provide a strate­
gic advantage. Regions that are expected to reach ToE earlier should be prioritized for
adaptation efforts, such as adjusting investment strategies and revisiting siting policies.
The ToE approach enables planners to act before changes become irreversible, support­
ing a move from reactive to proactive decision­making.
This analysis also points to a structural disconnect between climate modelling and en­
ergy system analysis. As discussed in [89], global climate models often produce outputs
with limited temporal resolution and ensemble coverage, which restricts their usability in
energy planning. Current practices that rely on a single meteorological year risk under­
estimating both the frequency and severity of extreme low­wind events.
To close this gap, there is a need for multidisciplinary modelling frameworks, where cli­
mate scientists and energy planners collaborate from the start. This kind of integration
would improve the alignment of variables, such as wind measured at turbine hub heights,
allow for the use of ensemble­based climate data and lead to more robust energy sys­
tem planning under uncertainty. These steps are essential to ensure resilience as wind
patterns and climate conditions continue to change.
6.6
Limitations of the Study
This study adopts a rigorous and ensemble­based approach, but several methodological
and structural limitations must be acknowledged. Some of these stem from computational
and time constraints, which necessitate simplifying assumptions and trade­offs in model
resolution, data processing, and system representation.
Bias Correction and Input Data
The bias correction method uses the Global Wind At­
las as a reference dataset. Although the GWA is widely accepted, introduces uncertainty
through both mesoscale and microscale modelling, particularly due to limitations in ter­
rain representation, model resolution, and surface descriptions. In complex or rugged
terrain, where the orographic model exceeds its recommended range, wind speeds may
be overestimated, which users can assess using the provided ruggedness index (RIX)
layer [29].
In addition, the choice of bias correction method can significantly influence the corrected
wind speed distribution and, therefore the estimated capacity factors. Different techniques
may preserve or distort seasonal cycles, interannual variability, or extreme values, which
are especially relevant for drought or emergence analyses. Consequently, the results
presented here are contingent not only on the input climate model data, but also on the
structure and assumptions embedded in the bias correction procedure itself.
Resolution and Temporal Aggregation
The relatively coarse spatial resolution of CMIP6
models, downscaled here to approximately 1º × 1º, limits the ability to resolve mesoscale
features critical for wind dynamics, especially in coastal, mountainous, or heterogeneous
landscapes. Similarly, temporal aggregation to daily means smooths intra­day variabil­
ity and underrepresents short­lived wind droughts that can affect operational reliability.
56 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Wind Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:71==
These simplifications, while computationally necessary, reduce the model’s ability to cap­
ture extremes relevant to system operation.
Regridding and Interpolation
Dataset standarisation introduces additional uncertain­
ties. Regridding and vertical interpolation of wind speeds, particularly near geographic
boundaries or masked coastal zones, can distort gradients and wind speed magnitudes.
These effects propagate into the capacity factor estimates and influence derived statistics.
Definition of Wind Droughts
Wind droughts are defined here as days falling below the
5th percentile of the daily capacity factor distribution. While useful for isolating climate­
driven variability, this threshold does not account for real­world grid constraints or energy
demand profiles, limiting the operational relevance of this metric for system adequacy
assessments.
Statistical Testing
Significance testing is conducted using Welch’s t­test, which as­
sumes independent, normally distributed data. These assumptions are often violated in
climate model output, where autocorrelation and skewed distributions are common. As
discussed by Wilks [85, 86], such violations can bias variance estimates and overstate
significance. Non­parametric methods can offer more robust alternatives but are more
computationally time­consuming.
Wind Energy Modelling Assumptions
The energy yield estimates in this study rely
on a simplified approach using one representative power curve for onshore and one for
offshore turbines, without accounting for variations in turbine type, hub height, or tech­
nological specifications. Additionally, the analysis does not incorporate wake effects or
wind farm layout, both of which play a critical role in determining actual energy produc­
tion. As demonstrated by [9], these design factors can lead to significant differences in
projected outputs, particularly in offshore environments where wake losses and spatial
configuration are more pronounced.
Summary
These limitations reflect necessary trade­offs made to manage the scope,
data volume, and runtime of the analysis. While the projected climate signals appear
robust, the translation to wind power performance must be treated with caution. Future
work should incorporate higher spatial and temporal resolution, improved statistical frame­
works, and realistic turbine and system representations to increase the applicability of
climate­energy assessments.

Conclusion
This study explored how climate change may reshape wind energy potential across Eu­
rope using a CMIP6 ensemble and high­resolution capacity factor modelling. The findings
point to a consistent and robust decline in summer capacity factors across Central and
Northern Europe, with over 75% of models agreeing on statistically significant decreases
in these regions. Southern and Central Europe show strong autumn decline signals, par­
ticularly in the Mediterranean area, while spring and winter trends remain more heteroge­
neous with lower model agreement and less spatial coherence.
While changes in average capacity factor show clear seasonal patterns, the signals for
wind droughts are less coherent. The analysis reveals little systematic overlap between
regions showing strong capacity factor declines and those indicating longer drought du­
rations. This disconnect suggests that projected reductions in average wind output are
primarily driven by broad weakening of mean wind conditions rather than more frequent
or prolonged extreme low­wind events. Wind drought frequency and duration changes
show weak statistical robustness, with only isolated cases (such as the British Isles and
Eastern Europe during winter) showing model agreement above 70%.
The analysis of Time of Emergence shows that emergence is not a singular, clearly de­
fined event but a probabilistic signal that varies considerably across models and regions.
While ensemble medians remain mostly below emergence thresholds, several individ­
ual models indicate early emergence by the mid­2020s, with signal­to­noise evaluation
showing capacity factor values increasingly deviating from historical variability envelopes.
These early signals, even without ensemble consensus, carry operational relevance and
support the use of ToE as a planning tool for gauging confidence in trend detection rather
than a fixed benchmark for decision­making.

1. Climate Impact Quantification: How do bias­corrected CMIP6 projections indi­

Bias­corrected CMIP6 projections reveal significant regional and seasonal variations in
wind energy potential across Europe. The most robust signal is a consistent decline in
summer capacity factors, particularly affecting Central, Northern, and Eastern Europe,
with over 75% of models agreeing on statistically significant decreases. The seasonal
asymmetry is pronounced, with summer displaying both the highest inter­quartile range
and the largest difference between beginning and end­of­century means, indicating in­
creased variability during this critical period. Wind drought patterns show high spatial
and seasonal variability but lack the statistical robustness of capacity factor trends, with
most regions showing weak and inconsistent signals for drought duration and frequency
changes.

wind droughts in climate projections, and how do these events change under future

overall lower wind resource availability rather than more pronounced extremes. Time

signals become operationally relevant, supporting flexible planning frameworks that can
respond to early signals without overcommitting to specific outcomes given the substantial

Future Work
This study provides a foundational assessment of climate change impacts on wind energy
potential, but several aspects remain to be explored in future research.
First, wind farm­specific factors such as wake effects, turbine siting, and array layouts
deserve greater attention. These elements influence effective output and may interact with
climate­driven changes in wind regimes in non­linear ways. Including technical losses,
such as downtime, curtailment, and electrical inefficiencies, would further increase model
fidelity.
Turbine heterogeneity is another important aspect. Future work should investigate how
different technologies, hub heights, and control strategies respond to evolving wind con­
ditions, particularly under higher variability. Similarly, refined methods for vertical wind
extrapolation and more robust bias correction techniques (e.g., quantile mapping, multi­
variate correction) could enhance model accuracy.
Increasing the spatial resolution of input data, especially for complex terrain and coastal
regions, would better capture localised effects critical to site­level planning. High­resolution
regional modelling could also support better alignment with grid infrastructure and plan­
ning zones.
Incorporating electricity demand is also essential. Future work should explore how shifts
in wind availability coincide with load profiles, especially during extreme weather events or
seasonal peaks. Linking capacity factor trends with real­world balancing needs, storage
requirements, and dispatchability would support more grounded conclusions.
Finally, studies that couple climate impact projections with integrated energy system mod­
els or techno­economic planning tools could provide more actionable insights. This would
allow the translation of climate signals into system­level metrics such as reliability, flex­
ibility requirements, and investment needs, closing the gap between climate data and
policy­relevant outcomes.

A.2
Bias factors across Europe for all studied models
Figure A.2 shows the bias factor across Europe for all models. As explained in section 4.1,
the bias factor represents the multiplier applied to the wind speed of each model, obtained
by bias correcting against the already bias­corrected ERA5 dataset. Higher values, mostly
observed in mountainous regions, indicate an underestimation of wind speed and there­
fore require stronger correction. In general, most models have a bias factor close to one,
except in mountainous areas. However, MIROC6 exhibits a consistently high bias factor
across most of inland Europe, indicating a widespread underestimation of wind speeds.
