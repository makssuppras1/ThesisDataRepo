Abstract of Master Thesis, Submitted 01 January 2017:  
 
Decision making tool for future bicycle friendly cities 
 
This thesis concludes that it is possible to develop a decision-making tool for transport planners and 
decision makers, which helps cities to become a sustainable bicycle friendly city or remain their 
position as a sustainable bicycle friendly city in the future.  
 
The model is developed by a thorough understanding of the transport sector in a sustainable context. 
The transport sector is a fundamental key in developing societies and plays a big role in sustainable 
development. Transportation targets are included in 7 out of 17 goals set by the United Nations and 
it is shown that improvements within the transport sector can help to reach these targets and make 
the world more sustainable. 
 
The bicycle is categorized as strong sustainability, because it has extensive socio-economic benefits, 
but to obtain a bicycle friendly city, it is a necessity to improve transportation aspects within areas 
such as safety, infrastructure, density, culture and policy. The required city structure is a compact 
city with a high supply of services, jobs and schools within 3-4 km, which is flat with good 
connectivity and accessibility. Furthermore, it is also necessary with a good cycling culture, where 
cycling is the norm and not something temporary. In addition to this, people need to feel safe, which 
can be improved by safety regulations and reducing the number of people getting injured or killed in 
traffic. This also includes better cycle behavior, where cyclists must take other cyclist or drivers into 
consideration. At last it requires political support. National or local backup from the political system 
with clear strategies and plans is a necessity. Therefore, it is important that transport planners and 
decision makers prioritize cycling. This is done by following the green transportation hierarchy.  
 
To operationalize the concept and evaluate the performance of each city, a set of indicators are 
developed based on the indicators relevance and availability. Similar, a set of solutions are found, 
based on initiatives already launched and implemented in cities around Europe. Doing so, ensures 
the reliability and validity of the solutions. By combining indicators and solutions made it possible 
to come up with solutions for specific city requirements. 
 
The model will be sensitive to changes in the data and city types has been simplified as a consequence 
of this. If more data was available other city types, indicators and solutions could have emerged. 
Cities change over time, hence it is important to future prof the model. Technology continuously 
develop and new data will be available.  
 
The decision-making tool can be used by all cities around Europe and will help decision-makers and 
transport planners to make their city become a sustainable bicycle friendly city

1 Structure 
The scope of this master thesis is to come up with an evaluation tool for decision makers and 
transport planners to future-prof cities, who already have or wants to invest in cycling in order to 
become a sustainable bicycle friendly city.  
 
 
 
 
Figure 1 - Content  
This thesis will be using different methodologies to answer the stated problem formulation. This 
includes literature studies, theoretical methods, empirical methods and model building. This will be 
enrolled in the following way: 
 
The framework gives a quick overview of the process of how to fulfill a sustainable goal by 
understanding sustainable principles, collecting data to achieve sustainable development.   
 
The sustainability concept will be defined in order to clarify different sustainable theories. These will 
be used to give a deeper understanding of, how the transport sector is challenged and why bicycling 
is important in a sustainable context.  
 
Then, it is described what a bicycle friendly city is and explained what areas the focus can be 
increased to become a bicycle friendly city.  
 
Elaboration of different transport hierarchies will help to explain how political prioritization can 
influence modal share and the number of people using their bicycle on a daily basis.  
 
Categorization of cities is done in order to group cities to simplify the model. These groups will be 
formed based on basic parameters; GDP, density, climate, topography and modal share.  
 
A set of indicators is defined to give the concept an operational form. They will help to evaluate the 
performance of the city and in collaboration with a carefully chosen set of solutions and make 
guidance for proper recommendations in the model.  
 
The model will be build, based on the information and findings found in the sections explained above 
about categorization of cities, indicators and solutions.  
 
At last, a recommendation will be made to inform decision makers and transport planners about 
what initiatives to implement to become a sustainable bicycle friendly city

2 Motivation 
The United Nations has set up 17 sustainable development goals in order to improve efforts towards 
building an inclusive, sustainable and resilient future for people and the planet and can be seen in 
Figure 2 (UN, 2015). Several of these goals contains elements from the transport sector, because the 
transport sector is responsible for massive effects in these areas and it has an influence on our daily 
life. As an example, the transport sector is responsible for one quarter of energy-related greenhouse 
gas emissions worldwide (UN, 2016). Furthermore, the transport infrastructure will last for decades, 
which means that decisions made by local or national governments today, will have long-lasting 
impacts on urban development and form, as well as climate and the quality of life. For that reason, 
sustainable transport must be viewed and integrated as an important factor in sustainable 
development strategies.  
 
Figure 2 - Sustainable development goals (UN - SDG, 2015) 
Investigations shows that one very effective way of doing so is by improving cycling. As explained 
by the European Cyclists’ Federation (ECF, 2016a):  
 
The Global Goals, as stipulated in the preamble of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), seek 
to realize the human rights of all. Cycling is already delivering on these goals worldwide, and this is 
a good reason to invest more in cycling. Making transportation more sustainable is of critical 
importance for humanity and the planet. Moreover, active mobility is a human right on all scales – 
including the right to cycle. Governments at all levels should provide safe access to public space, 
protect those that walk and cycle, and ensure – through mobility – equal participation in society. 
 
The improvement of cycling initiatives has turned out to be very effective and costless compared to 
other initiatives (The City of Copenhagen, 2015). At the same time implementation of cycling 
initiatives will help cities too become more sustainable and to reach the sustainable development 
goals set by the United Nations.  
 
The Motivation behind this thesis is to future-prof cities, who already has or wants to invest in 
cycling to become a sustainable bicycle friendly city. There have been invested heavily in cycling in 
many European cities, which has caused a lot of both good and bad results, effects and experiences 
that have not been analyzed yet. Therefore, the purpose of this thesis is to develop a decision-making 
tool for transport planners in order to become or remain a sustainable bicycle friendly city in the 
future.  This will be done by investigating what a good sustainable bicycle friendly city is.

4 Method 
This chapter describes what kind of methods that has been used to investigate the thesis and 
strengthen the validity of our conclusions. It consists of approaches to collect and process information. 
It is essential for the objective to choose the best suited method for the study.  
4.1 
Pre-study 
The purpose of the pre-study is to narrow down the scope and create a frame for the thesis. The pre-
study is also initiated in order to eliminate misleading ideas. The first step was to get inspiration on 
how to form the study. The inspiration came from one decision maker and four experts in terms of 
bicycling. It was both interviews and email correspondences. Together with our own reflections it 
created a better understanding of our goal. 
4.2

A literature review has the purpose of studying the field of interest. It is the ability to seek

books. The topics in the literature review was about:

It is important to be critical when seeking and scanning for literature to be able to identify unbiased

There have been found lots of relevant, appropriate and useful material for most of the listed topics

Theoretical method 
The theory used in the empirical study is the foundation for the data analysis. The main purpose of 
using the listed theories is to analyze, interpret and create an overview of the data. The theories

Empirical method  
The empirical method includes data collection and data analysis. It is an essential part of the 
empirical method to explain how data have been collected and what it contains as well as how data 
are processed and analyzed to reach the conclusions.

Data collection

Data to describe cities was collected from different databases. First, information about modal share 
was collected from the European platform on mobility TEMS (European platform on mobility

5 Framework 
The framework is the foundation of the thesis and the purpose of the thesis should be reflected 
through the design and process flow of the framework. The motivation behind the framework is to 
take a complex problem and make it into an understandable and readable figure, which explains the 
steps in the process flow.  
5.1 
Theory 
A framework is a way to organize information according to an overall purpose or practice 
(Gudmundsson , et al., 2016) and frameworks comes in different shapes and forms that serves various 
needs (Assmuth & Hilden, 2008). The overall idea of a framework, is to define the statement of 
purpose and provide a useful context for making a set of indicators - The conceptual framework will 
help to focus and clarify what to measure, what to expect from measurement and what kinds of 
indicators to use. Without a conceptual framework and a realistic understanding of the total system, 
we cannot expect to find indicators that represent the viability of the system and the output can 
become inadequate.  
 
In this thesis, it will be a useful tool to clarify the concept of sustainable development (Becker, 2007) 
and how the selection of indicators will be driven by the goals of the agency/municipality in becoming 
a sustainable bicycle friendly city. This process of “framing” is therefore critical for how sustainability 
is made to “count” (Gudmundsson , et al., 2016) 
 
The framework will then connect the indicators together in comprehensive systems for planning and 
decision support, which enables decision makers to keep track on substance, measurement, intention 
and procedures for sustainability in transportation. Framework and indictors will together provide 
the necessary building block for measuring sustainability in transportation. Together they will also 
provide a picture of what is actually done in planning processes with the compromises and 
experimentation these involve.  
5.2 
Sustainable framework 
The framework, which is created, is the key to understanding the concept behind the process of 
becoming a sustainable bicycle friendly city. The framework is not intended to be for this purpose 
only. It is build up in such way that it can be used in many sustainable contexts. Every time a 
government, institution or a decision maker needs a framework to explain the process that a 
sustainable project is going through, this framework can be used – from understanding the basic 
principles within sustainability, to gather the right information, to go through a sustainable 
development and finally achieving the desired output.  
 
This means that the framework is not only suited to describe how to become a bicycle friendly city, 
but the framework is likewise applicable for other sustainability problems. E.g. to reduce the oil 
consumption or improve renewable energy sources.

top of the framework and the initial goal cannot be achieved in the best suited way. 
 
2. Gathering information - Data collection and data analysis – This step is very dependent on 
the problem and the sustainability approach. It is about collecting the right data with 
questionnaires, literature reviews etc. After the data collection, the next step is analyzing the 
data with the aim to answering the problem, with a focus on sustainability. The analysis can 
either be a qualitative or quantitative approach or elements from both of the methods.

Then decision makers must go back to this step and start all over with the data collection and data 
analysis. It can also be the case that decision makers understand the sustainability concept in the 
wrong way, hence they must go back to this step and rethink their approach. It is fundamental for 
the overall process to understand each of the steps and use the right methods and theories to achieve 
the defined goal.

6 The transport sector 
Transportation is a fundamental key in developing societies. It creates connection and links between 
humans and move goods across land. Transport is developing constantly and there is always a more 
sustainable way of creating links. Transport does not only support infrastructure, but is also part of 
a broad system. This system influence the environment, have huge impact on world economy, creates 
a social sphere and integrates people in society. Transport is fundamental in improving the social, 
economic and environmental parameters in society, but it likewise produce negative externalities 
such as increased CO2 emission, social inequity, urban transport challenges, noise and accidents 
(Gudmundsson, et al., 2016). This chapter gives a brief explanation of the transport sector, which 
helps to understand what role the transport sector plays in a sustainability context.  
 
 
 
Figure 5 - Seven sustainable transport targets within SDG’s (SLoCaT, 2015) 
 
The sustainable development goals, illustrated in Figure 2, are supported by 169 targets, where 
sustainable transport targets are included in 8 of the 17 goals as illustrated in Figure 5. Sustainable 
transport is covered directly by five targets and indirectly by six targets. These goals elaborate what

6.1 
Good health and well-being (CO2-emission) 
The energy use in the transport sector correspond to more than ¼ of the total CO2 emission. Figure 
6 illustrates the total share of the total CO2 consumption in the OECD countries from 2009. 28 % of 
the CO2 emission is consumed in the transport sector. 
  
Figure 6 - CO2 emissions in OECD countries in 2009 (NL - EAA, 2015) 
Since the sector is responsible for more than ¼ of the CO2 consumption, it is necessary to increase 
focus and money spent on research within this field. Politicians, researchers and daily users of 
motorized transport need to rethink the transport sector and invest in the right transport modes. 
The transport sector is essential to reduce the CO2 emission and the fuel consumption and if the 
transport sector does not make drastically changes in terms of emission, the world will experience a 
lot of difficulties. Private cars stand for the highest contribution of CO2 emission, within the transport 
sector on the road (EEA - Europa, 2013). Current and new technologies have the potential to deliver 
substantial reduction in CO2 emissions from transport. These technologies need to be introduced 
rapidly, but which of the new technologies that will show the greatest effect is still uncertain. Another 
contribution, can be achieved from travel shift to more efficient modes like the bicycle.

73 % of the total fuel use in the transportation sector stem from road transport. Road transport

The essential for the nested model is that the economic capital is nested inside the society, which is

social dimension respectively. Because the nested model follows the order from UN, which states that 
the environment is the most important dimension and it has the highest priority in order to save the

7.3 
Strong and weak sustainability 
The difference between strong and weak sustainability is large and the terms should not be 
misunderstood. The word strong has very powerful meaning compared too weak and it requires 
knowledge to understand the term and its meaning. The strong sustainability and weak sustainability 
concept is defined as follow:  
 
“strong sustainability implies carrying on to the next generation the same amount of natural capital, 
with human capital increases over time; whereas weak sustainability implies a declining natural 
capital over time while human capital increases” (Dasgupta, 2014)  
 
To obtain strong sustainability it is not allowed to decrease any of the three dimensions’ capital, 
(Natural, social, human, manufactured and financial capital) when increasing the amount of capital 
within another dimension. For instance, creating a new bicycle lane through a park would cause a 
decrease in natural capital in the environmental dimension (cutting trees and removal of green areas) 
but will increase human capital within the social dimension (people’s health or skills). This is not 
considered as strong sustainability, even if the total sum of capital is above the starting point, as 
long as there is a lack of capital in one of the dimensions. It can be difficult to follow the strong 
sustainability approach, due to the fact that no other dimension’s capital can be decreased, there 
will in most cases be a trade-off. The mindset behind the strong sustainability is the driving force. It 
is a theoretical approach and not a practical approach, but if the city is building a road through a 
forest they have to plant trees elsewhere.  
 
The weak sustainability mindset does also focus on improving the sustainability concept. Compared 
to the strong sustainability concept, the weak sustainability theory implies that one dimension can 
gain a decrease in capital if the total sum of capital is higher than the starting point. Though it can 
end up being difficult to value different initiatives and tradeoffs to a specific value. Weak 
sustainability is undoubtedly still a good approach, because it still benefits the society. 
 
Figure 12 illustrates the fundamental concept behind the strong and weak sustainability. The strong 
sustainability concept has to maintain both the natural and human capital through generations, 
while the weak sustainability concept has to improve one type of capital as long as the total sum of 
capital is equal or greater through generations. Some natural resources such as ozone layer and 
biodiversity cannot be substituted for human capital (Pryn, et al., 2015).

8 A bicycle friendly city 
The private car is the most dominant mode of transport in cities throughout the World. The car is 
even the most popular mode of transport for short trips, where it could easily be replaced by more 
sustainable modes of transport like the bicycle. However, it has not always been like that. From a 
historical perspective, the bicycle has been the main mode of transport in many countries for several 
years. This was before the late 1950’s where most people choose to buy a car, because cities were 
planned for motorized vehicles and cars became a more efficient mode of transport. In recent years, 
this development has turned around and the cycle share has started to increase again. However, the 
level is far from the historical level in 1950. The development over time can be seen in Figure 13.

The reason for this turnaround shall be found in environmental restrictions and regulations and 
because city planners begin to plan the city for people and their needs.  
 
So, what is a good cycling city? This question has several answers and the solution is different from 
city to city, but one of the central elements is that the city must be planned for people and not the 
other way around. Factors like infrastructure, density, culture, safety and policy will all influence 
the daily number of trips made by bicycle, as illustrated in Figure 14. It implies some difficulties if 
the structure does not invite bicycling. Therefore, city planning is important if a city wants to become 
a bicycle friendly city or wants to remain their position as a bicycle friendly city. Different cities 
need different strategies, but equal for all of them is to plan for the cyclist and pedestrian logic and 
without putting them inside the car world.

8.1 
City structure 
Different actors and countries have different ways of defining a cycling friendly city. According to 
Copenhagen University, a bicycle friendly city requires following city structure (Skov-Petersen & 
Nielsen, 2014): 
1. A dense traffic network with no more than 1.5 km between important routes. 
2. A compact city with a big range of services and workplaces within 4 km and schools within 
3 km. 
3. A flat city without hills. 
 
These results are based on a Danish study with Danish cities, but can be transformed and used by 
other cities as well. The majority of cycling in Danish cities takes place over short distances and it 
is often the individual city that make up the setting and sets the conditions for the resident cycling.  
 
First of all, bicycling will have the best conditions in high density cities with a big network consisting 
of a wide range of cycling routes within 1.5 kilometers. (Skov-Petersen & Nielsen, 2014). To obtain 
this, it is important with connectivity and accessibility.  Connectivity and accessibility occurs in the 
form of primary, secondary and tertiary cycle routes (Mušič, 2014): 
- 
Primary cycle routes are the artery of the city network, which ensure the fastest and safest 
way to and from the city center.  
- 
Secondary cycle routes enable access to all major stops or hubs and are forming the cycling 
network. They consist of longer routes, where cyclists can stop for different services or errands.  
- 
Tertiary cycle routes create accessibility and allow “door to door” trips. These types of routes 
represent the compaction of the cycle network and creates short cutes for cyclist.  
 
==PAGE:33==
26 
 
Besides that, 48% of the cyclists in Copenhagen say that the primary reason for choosing the bicycle 
is that it is the fastest and easiest mode for transportation (Københavns Kommune, 2011). In order 
to get more people to cycle, it is vital that travel time by bicycle is competitive with other modes of 
transport. However, travel time is not just about raising the speed but is also about making space 
for all types of users and building shortcuts, so users can get more easily from A to B. Many small 
jams, detours and the impossibility of overtaking increase travel time significantly. Alterations in the 
form of, new bicycle paths, traffic calming and bump for cars will help to improve cyclists' travel 
time but will also give a side effect in the form of safety and security improvements (Københavns 
Kommune, 2011).  
 
Second, citizens are not willing to cycle if there are no grocery stores, works spaces and educational 
offers nearby. It turns out that the supply of working places and retail offerings within 4 kilometers 
and availability of schools within 3 kilometers, has a positive effect on the bicycle share. Especially, 
service offerings are found to be essential for the use of bicycles as the primary mode of transport 
(Skov-Petersen & Nielsen, 2014). This reflects that the city shall be compact with short distance to 
all activities. However, if the distance to job, school and other services becomes less than 1.5 km, 
people tend to walk instead of cycling.  
 
There is a big difference in city structure and shape in European cities. Historically, some cities are 
built up in a way that attract cyclists and other cities has poor conditions for implementing cycling 
infrastructure. The importance of access to work places, schools, services and retail offerings reflects 
that a city with a specific function, type and structure is more enticement, compared to other cities 
where other city functions and structures are prioritized higher.

8.2 
Culture 
To grow a strong bicycle culture, it is necessary with a well-structured and well-developed cycling 
infrastructure, which do not focus on motorized vehicles. However, beside the physical surroundings 
it is necessary to get cycling to be the norm and not something temporary. A strong cycling culture 
refers to cities where large percentage of the citizens supports cycling. 
 
According to Jan Gehl (Kielgast, 2011), it is important to make a city people friendly to create a 
strong cycling culture. This means that it is important have both pedestrians and cyclist, because 
they are making the city more livable. From a cultural perspective, it will have a higher effect, if 
people is visible in the street and acting in the street life, instead of driving around in cars. A research 
(Ministry of Transport, 2009) has also shown that people associates bicycling with more happiness, 
compared to other transport modes. Figure 16 shows that people on bicycle are the happiest sort of 
people. This is despite the fact that people on bicycle are vulnerable in traffic. The fear factor is 
lower compared to public transport and car. Public transport is definitely not connected with positive 
emotions and has the lowest score of joy. Car drivers also has a high share of joy, but people fear 
and anger the car more than bicycles.

In order to become a bicycle friendly city, it is also necessary to create a bicycle culture, where the 
bicycle is used as part of social integration. It is important that people from all social classes, not 
just the lower class, uses the bicycle.  
 
It becomes a problem if the bicycle is associated with the lower class and the middle class and wealthy 
people becomes a society class that only will use the car, because they do not care about emission,

8.3 
Safety 
Cycling in a city can be hectic and there is a need of safety regulations to reduce the number of 
people getting injured or killed in traffic. One way to obtain a good cycling city is to build safe and 
secure infrastructure. Some cities that are designed for cars are characterized by many obstacles on 
side walk and space for car parking, instead of better facilities and infrastructure for pedestrians and 
cyclists. In car designed cities there are no cyclists, because it is unsecure and dangerous to be a 
cyclist. city planners have to make the streets attractive and safe with space enough on the bike path 
for the demand so the individual citizen has the impression that cycling is safe. A city that feel safe 
to cycle in, results in more people cycling. This include elderly, who find cycling during rush hour, 
an overwhelming experience, and parents cycling with their children in rush hour. People shall be 
able to ride differently and cycle tracks shall allow for this diversity. More people shall be able to 
ride alongside each other and chat, and other people shall be able to ride the speed that suits them 
best. Some people shall be able to take it easy, checking out shop windows and city life, without 
blocking the cycle path for them who wants to ride fast.

Another important factor is cycle behavior and the fact that people needs to take other cyclist or

8.4 
Policy 
The foundation for having a bicycle friendly city and a livable city, requires backup from the political 
system. In UK, pedestrians have not been seen as a transport mode, despite the fact they have many 
similarities with bicycle users. This is changing and in Europe some of the bigger cities are going 
through a paradigm shift. Walking is a mode of transport and bicycling has to be accepted, because 
it solves or reduce the congestion in the city. Despite all the benefits from increasing the cycling 
share, none of it is possible, without political aid and support (Tight, et al., 2011). The key for 
politicians is to understand the future problems urbanizations is causing and how to tackle it, without 
compromising on the workability and the city’s function.  
 
To tackle the urbanization and increase the cycling share and the good bicycle friendly city, guidelines 
is important. It can be on a local or national level. The national level strategies make the solutions 
and infrastructure uniform, which makes the traffic more understandable throughout the country. 
The national plans do not handle congestion problems in the biggest cities, but that is the benefit 
from the local plans. In order to create the better society, an allocated funds to cyclists every year is 
a strong statement from politicians and will create a better social environment. The focus shall be 
on the developing the city and politicians should not be afraid for making investments for cyclists 
instead of motorists. Study shows that culture benefits from political support by bicycling 
investments. 
 
Cycling culture is affected by policies (Goeverden & Godefrooij, 2011), but with the general increase 
of cycling share in Europe, creates other benefits besides the health perspective. An unseen benefit 
is the creation of new jobs in the cycling sector. The main share of people that works in cycling sector 
is in tourism. At the moment, there are 655,000 people working in cycling related sectors. ECF claims 
(ECF, 2013a) that in the nearby future it is possible to reach 1 million jobs in the cycling sector. 
Cycling does not decrease the society at any point and politicians have a responsibility to incorporate 
bicycling in solutions.

The green transportation hierarchy  
The green/ sustainable transportation hierarchy is the basic concept behind transportation 
reformations of groups all over the world. There are a number of variations on this theme, but the 
general idea remains the same – That the needs of more vulnerable transport users should be 
considered before the needs of those who travel by motorized transport (either private or public), see 
Figure 17. The hierarchy puts city friendly cyclist and pedestrians first, because it is space efficient, 
has low cost and zero environmental impact.

like it is done in the green transport hierarchy and then follow the transport hierarchy procedure, 
which is developed by the Sustainable Development Commission, see Figure 18.

Planning for cyclists 
It is important to plan for cyclists and not only in terms of infrastructure, but the right mindset has 
been used. There is a huge difference in planning for cities, where the bicycle plays a marginal role 
and cities that already have made great effort in implementing bicycling initiatives.  
 
A new requirement that has become a procedure in most cycling friendly countries/cities, is the

preference stated data to clarify the VOT6 for the individual on bicycle, but definitely important for 
the future sustainable perspective.  
9.3.1 
The logic and needs   
The transport hierarchy is a good way of illustrating how cities prioritize different transport modes. 
However, for the city to work and function in an optimal way. It can be necessary to focus on the 
city’s logic and needs when planning for the future.  
 
The different transport mode logic: 
- 
Car drivers’ logic: Is big roads with wide lanes and not necessarily has to be directly, but 
high speed lanes around cites with connected roads to the center of the city. Cars almost 
have the same size, same speed, same acceleration and stopping length and the size of the car 
and the limited vision, makes it difficult for car drivers to interact the other transport modes. 
Which is the reason for a strict infrastructure. (Harders, 2014) 
- 
Pedestrians logic: Everything at 5 km/h that is how the pedestrian flow works. Everything 
is noticeable at this speed and transport modes, which is faster than this is seen as not secure. 
Pedestrians takes the easiest/shortest way if possible, except if there are physical barriers or 
if it is too big of risk.  
- 
Public transport logic: Has to be the most direct way with lots of stops, high frequency and 
connections to other lines in a clear network. The metro system, is normally hidden due to 
the high speed and impossible to have it on the streets.  
- 
Cyclists logic: It is not logical for cyclists to be inside the car-driver’s world. It is not natural 
for cyclists to wait at traffic lights, because they are there to protect bicycles from cars, trucks 
and buses. The average speed is around 20 km/h which means it is possible to interact and 
brake before an accident would occur.  
 
In general, all the transport modes are placed in the car driver logistic, bicycle path and segregated 
path is made for car drivers so they do not hit the cyclist. The cyclist knows it is unsafe to use the 
travel lane, because the cyclist is the vulnerable road user. The problem is not at the straight 
stretches, but at the intersections, most of the accidents with cyclists is in the intersections 
(Vejdirektoratet-ulykkesstatstik, 2009-2013).  
 
Road was intentionally built for horses and pedestrians and cars came by later in the history. Traffic 
laws, stop signs and traffic lights were introduced, due to the danger posed by cars and other motor 
vehicles and the fact is that motor vehicles kills. Cyclists and pedestrians does not kill anyone in

10 Categorization of cities 
In order to evaluate the performance of different initiatives in a city and to give recommendations 
to other cities, it is important to understand what makes the specific city unique. A categorization 
of the cities, will help to differentiate cities from each other and help to find similarities among cities.   
 
It is important to do a categorization of the cities and divide them into groups, because cities that 
are similar in the way they are designed, placed or function, will automatically have greater chance 
of transferring, learn, copying and implementing strategy plans or initiatives from each other.   
 
A categorization will also simplify the model and make it more simple to use. Instead of analyzing 
one city at the time from scratch, it will become easier to position a specific city into a pre-determined 
group wherefrom it will be evaluated.  
 
If the model is not simplified, by grouping the cities, it will be too difficult to handle later in the 
process. The model will consist of too many parameters and be multiple case studies that individually 
must be evaluated.  
10.1 The selection of cities

relevant. It will also explain, where we data is from and what parameters have been used for the 
categorization.

cities consists of 46 cities from all over Europe, with a maximum of 5 cities from the same country,

11.1 Introduction to indicators   
An indicator is a variable, or combination of several variables that describes one indicator, selected 
to represent a certain wider issue or characteristic of interest (Gudmundsson , et al., 2016). In general 
indicators are selected to provide a clear measure of something that could be considered important, 
which means that indicators should be carefully selected in order to provide useful information. 
Indicators can be used in a bunch of different situations, from describing the conditions as they are 
right now, to make estimations and predictions for future outcomes, to evaluate the effect of policies, 
programs or projects over a specific time horizon.  
 
Indicators can lead to a better decisions and more effective actions by simplifying, clarifying and 
making aggregated information available to policy makers. In the sustainable transportation context, 
indicators can help incorporate physical and social science knowledge into decision-making, and they 
can help measure, calibrate and evaluate progress toward sustainable development goals (Litman, 
2005)  
 
It is important to understand the perspective of indicators and the limitations when using them. 
Different types of indicators reflect different perspectives and assumptions. Therefore, it is important 
to distinguish between different roles and function of indicators in the governance of transportation. 
Most of the indicators has a direct connection to the transport planners job. For some of the 
indicators it requires background information about the city and how to run the city.  
 
Planning for sustainability and transportation purposes are both areas that can benefit from the use 
of indicators. Sustainability is a concept that is hard to observe or measure directly. The ever-
changing world and the complex nature of sustainability, makes it difficult to make one measure 
where all aspects of the system are in focus all the time. For that reason, sustainability is a notion 
that call for the use of indicators and to make use of indicators, the field should be quantifiable. 
Likewise, transportation systems are complex and dynamic that can vary over time, but is more 
tangible due to the fact that transport is countable. It is necessary in transportation decision making 
to find relevant indicators in order to measure the number of people killed in traffic and time spent 
in queue to avoid effects like congestion, time delays and accidents. Transportation systems affect 
the social, environmental and economic conditions through their influence on landtake, air pollution, 
time savings etc. For that reason, it is important to predict and monitor these trends by using 
appropriate indicators in order to evaluate and prepare for future goals. The sooner an organized 
monitoring of indicators the easier it is to evaluate the transport system and other sectors. In some 
cases, they will also provide an early warning to prevent environmental, economic and social setbacks. 
11.2 Brainstorming and the initial set of indicators  
Brainstorming is a method which helps to give a quick overview of potential indicators. There are 
different ways to do brainstorming. It can be done by an open discussing or an individual assessment.  
 
In this thesis brainstorming is used as an inspiration tool to guide us in the right direction and make 
sure that we do not exclude potential indicators in advance, but at the same time ensure that we

11.3 Method to select the right indicators.  
This section will introduce a guideline to develop and select appropriate indicators for this purpose. 
The selection of individual indicators is determined by the purpose of the framework and the selected 
themes. In this context, it has to be a guideline for decision making, however the overall purpose is 
to inform politicians at the city level, such as the municipality of Copenhagen.   
 
In order to select indicators for sustainable development to describe cycling, the CSD (Commission 
on Sustainable Development) indicator guidelines and methodologies have recommended that 
indicators shall satisfy following criterions (UN, 2007) 
 
1. Primarily national scope 
2. Representative of an international consensus to the extent possible; 
3. Relevant to assessing sustainable development progress;  
4. Limited in number, but remaining open-ended and adaptable to future needs;  
5. Understandable, clear and unambiguous; within 
a. The capabilities of local governments to develop the city; and 
b. Dependent on cost effective data of known quality. (collection of data) 
The first criterion emphasizes that the indicators can be used for national level assessment. However, 
the second criterion emphasizes that if it is possible to develop or select indicators through a 
collaborative approach, which involves other intergovernmental organization or non-governmental 
organizations it will make the indicator set more broad and informal among international 
organizations.  Criteria three and four require that the indicators should be both limited and 
sufficiently comprehensive to embrace the multidimensional nature of sustainable development. This 
means that the indicators should reflect environmental, social and economic impacts and is selected 
because they are suitable for establishing a performance target which is usable (Hart, 2010) (Marsden, 
et al., 2006).  
 
If too many indicators are used, the result become unmanageable and difficult to interpret and 
concentrating on essential variables, will make the entire framework more viable. The development 
and selection of a small number of representative indicators, will be made based on a large set of 
potential indicators. These indicators will be developed and selected based on their ability to describe 
the chosen object and ease of collection. Normally, the set of indicators will decrease automatically, 
since the purpose of the indicators will narrow it down. (UN, 2007) The fifth criterion indicate that 
all indicators shall be understandable, clear and unambiguous. This means that indicators shall be 
easy to understand and clearly defined for both decision makers and the general public. It also means 
==PAGE:66==
59 
 
that all the data collected and analysis details shall be transparent and accessible and available to 
all stakeholders.  The sixth criteria, national capability may refer to the institutional capacity not 
only to monitor and collect data but also to interpret and synthesize it into information useful for 
decision-making. This regard especially in countries which is under development and the continuation 
and acceleration of these actions will lead to further understanding of the necessity for these measures 
(UN, 2007). The last criterion indicate that all data shall be consistent and accurate which require 
high standards when collecting the data. The collection of data shall be standardized so all the 
information and results are comparable across groups and jurisdictions. (Hart, 2010) (Marsden, et 
al., 2006). There will of course be a tension between convenience and comprehensiveness when 
selecting indicators. A smaller set of indicators using easily available data is more convenient to 
collect and analyze but may overlook important impacts. A larger set can be more comprehensive 
but have excessive data collection and analysis costs (Litman, 2005). However, it is preferred if the 
collection of indicators is cost-effective, so the benefit of collecting an indicator overweight the costs 
(Hart, 2010) (Marsden, et al., 2006). 
 
In adapting indicators to national sets, it may be useful to classify the level of availability and 
relevance for each indicator.  
 
Availability is divided into four categories: Fully available; potentially available; related data 
available; and not available (UN, 2007). The first category is the most preferred one, since all 
information is available. The second one, contains indicators with potentially available data. These 
indicators are all cases where information can be made available, within a reasonable timeframe if a 
reasonable amount of money is used. Therefore, this category also includes indicators for which there 
are plans to increase capacity for data collection, storage and management. As an example, it is 
possible to extend an already existing survey by adding a few extra questions. This will add additional 
information to the indicator. The third type of category contain those indicators, where there is a 
lack of important information, but data exist, which can be used to compute and create related 
indicators. For example, in the absence of data on the use of bicycles (such as kilometers traveled by 
cycling), other type of data such as bicycles sold, could be used to create a related indicator. Again, 
more information can be made available within a reasonable timeframe, if more investments in data 
collection and capacity building is done. The choice of investing in new information is a tradeoff 
between the extra cost on one side and the potential loss of information on the other side, which will 
lead to a loss in planning and decision making processes brought about by using a related but less 
accurate indicator. The last category embraces all those indicators where no data is available.  
 
Relevance is the second dimension of the adaptation matrix. Relevance is also divided into four 
categories in which the chosen indicators can be placed: Relevant; related indicator relevant; relevant 
but missing; irrelevant (UN, 2007).The first category contains the majority of the indicators since 
most of them should have a high level of relevance. The next category covers indicators, which is not 
found directly relevant in describing the purpose of the framework, but are closely related to relevant 
indicators. It can be the case when one country uses national indicators that address the same issue 
as in the framework, but measure it differently. It could also be the case where the underlying issue 
is closely related, but are not completely identical to the national issue. For example, if small 
communities or municipalities have similar strategies, but not completely identical to the national 
strategies. Here will there be a small variation in the relevance of specific indicators. The third 
category is expected to have few entries since this category contains indicators that are relevant but 
==PAGE:67==
60 
 
not covered by the framework. This is because they are relevant and available to a few countries but 
not to others and because there is a desire to keep the set of indicators relatively small. Irrelevant 
indicator belongs to the last category and covers issues that are not meaningful in this context 
 
As mentioned above, the majority of the indicators will belong to the first category. However, to find 
potential indicators which is relevant it is recommended to follow the guideline listed below (Hart, 
2010). Some of the statements has been modified to fit the purpose of a bicycle friendly city:      
 
Firstly, it is important to define if the indicator is relevant to describe the overall purpose of the 
stated problem. Here is it important to settle how well does the direction the indicator is pointing 
match the individual city’s definition and vision of sustainability. As an example, approaches to 
sustainability in urban and suburban areas can be different from the approaches used in rural town.  
Second, it is important that the indicator is based on information that is reliable, accessible, timely 
and accurate.  
 
Thirdly, it is important to define if the indicator focus is on local sustainability at the expense of 
global sustainability. If this is the case, the indicator shall be placed in the second category, as 
explained above.  
 
Fourthly, indicators can be disaggregated by demographic factors (gender, age, income), geographic 
factors (urban, suburban, rural), trip (commuting, tourism, shopping, commercial), time (day, night, 
peak hour, off-peak), but overall impacts should be evaluated per capita rather than per unit traveled.  
 
Fifthly, some indicators will overlap, by which it is most appropriate to use one of the indicators to 
avoid inconsistency. If several of these indicators are used, it would be necessary to give each of the 
indicators a smaller weight so they count for one indicator in the analysis.  
 
At last, it is important that the indicator provide a long-term view of the city. It is preferable to 
have information about where the city has been as well as where the city want to be in 20, 30 or 50 
years. In addition, it is also worth noticing that the indicator shall be understandable to the 
community at large, because if it is only understood by expert, it will only be used by experts.

11.4 The final set of indicators 
After the assessment of identifying the final set of indicators, the next step is to clarify how the 
chosen indicators help to explain what a bicycle friendly city is. If the final set of indicators is not 
assessed properly it cannot be used in a general model, because there will be missing links. This 
chapter gives a brief definition of how the indicators are measured, division and size of intervals and 
a short presentation of the core areas. Some indicators are divided into levels which covers from 
nothing to maximum.  
 
The chosen indicators can complement each other, but should not overlap in terms of information. 
“Daily kilometer” and “daily trips” explains two different things within the same sector. Daily 
kilometer does not provide anything about the number of trips, which is also important to create the 
most dynamic set of solutions.

Theme:  
Stolen bicycles (social) 
Brief description: Number of stolen bicycles per citizen 
 
Description:

bicycles that are stolen each year. Despite this fact, the high level of stolen 
bicycles will lead to other problems such as anger, economic lost, lack of

11.5.1 Indicator 18 – Reliability 
The measure of reliability is with regard to how reliable are the public transport compared to private 
cars and bicycles. Reliability is how much you can trust something and in this case, how often is 
travel time what you expect it to be. For many people the bicycle will be the most reliable transport 
mode, because it is possible control the speed, but the bicycle is not necessary more convenient, 
especially not in bad weather or safer compared to the other transport modes. The reliability 
indicator should be measured in an anthropologic study to encounter the level of satisfaction, but 
data is difficult to collect and reliability is different from person to person. 
11.5.2 Indicator 40 - VOT 
In transport economics value of time is the opportunity cost of time that a traveler spends on his 
journey. Said in other words, it is the amount of money that person is willing to accept as 
compensation for lost time. This amount of money will vary from person to person depending on 
parameters such as education, job, family income, etc. It is important to include value of time 
measures in the analysis because there are large economic gains associated with time savings in the 
transport sector. Individuals will spend less time in traffic and more time working or doing other 
things which create value for the society. Value of time will be calculated as a monetary value for 
each city/country. We assume that using the bicycle, will reduce the probability of sitting in queue 
which will reduce cost for the society. We also assume that people who has a higher value of time 
tend to add more cost to the society, than people with a low value of time. Because VOT is difficult 
to measure it is decided to neglect this indicator from the final set of indicators.

12 Solutions  
The purpose of this master thesis is to build a decision-making tool that helps decision makers to 
make important decisions about cycling, in order to become a sustainable bicycle friendly city. This 
chapter helps to identify different types of solutions within a field of different areas. This includes 
areas such as campaigns, climate, culture, experience, infrastructure, mobility, policy, safety, 
technical, travel time and tourism. All the chosen solutions within these areas contributes to a better 
understanding of how to overcome specific cycling problems and implementation difficulties. It is 
necessary to come up with a range of different solutions, because of the different city types and 
because each city is at different levels in the process towards becoming a sustainable bicycle friendly 
city.  
 
In the sections below, it will be explained why each of the chosen areas is important when defining 
solutions for this purpose. There will also be some examples of some of the solutions found. Most of 
the solutions found is inspired by initiatives already launched and implemented in cities around 
Europe. By doing so, ensure reliability and validity in the choice of solutions and in most cases a 
guarantee of a successful implementation. Despite that all the solutions will match the theory 
presented in chapter 8 - A bicycle friendly city. Solutions which is not presented in the sections below 
can be found in appendix 2 – Solutions.  
12.1 Campaign 
The purpose of campaigns and branding the bicycle is primarily to influence people’s attitude towards 
cycling to motivate and inspire people to use the bicycle. Other reasons for making campaigns is: to 
get people to use the bicycle for all kinds of trips, to get a safer cycling environment, to educate 
people and to make people aware of social benefits.  One of the oldest and largest types of campaigns 
are the “Bike to school” and “Bike to work” which focus on children, who cycle to school and adults 
who cycle to work. Other types of campaigns is “What do you know about traffic”, “Safer cycling for 
all”, etc. Some of these campaigns specialized with a narrower target group, while others are made 
on a national level. It can be difficult to determine the exact effect of campaigns, but the tendency 
is that cities who make a systematic effort at promoting cycling will lead to more people choosing 
the cycle (Cycling Embassy of Denmark, 2011).  
12.1.1 Solution 3 - “Bike to work”  
The “Bike to work” campaign have existed for many years and is now available in many different 
versions. The normal “Bike to work” campaign had the aim to reduce unnecessary congestion of 
towns with cars, reduce gas emissions and stimulate healthy lifestyle. Beside that it is a good way to 
solve problem with distance and increase the number of daily trips. The scheme was about 
encouraging employees to cycle to and from work. People are grouped in teams with the purpose of 
inspiring and motivating each other for one month of cycling.   
 
Today the aim of the campaign is still the same, but scheme exist in other versions. In Ireland, a 
scheme encourages employees to cycle to and from work by letting employers paying for their 
employees’ bicycles and bicycle equipment. Then the employee pays back through a salary sacrifice 
arrangement up to 12 months, where the employees is not liable for tax payment (Dutch Cycling 
Embassy, 2016). Another scheme in Milan will pay cyclist 25 cents per kilometer travelled, up to a 
monthly cap of 50 euros. This project uses smartphones to record the distance travelled each 
day(ibid.)

12.2 Climate 
Transport is a major contributor to greenhouse gas emission. One way to improve this is a committed 
and integrated climate plan with focus on greener transport. This will result in a functional city, 
with less noise and less pollution. Therefore, money must be available to support climate projects, 
generate investments for the promotion of better public transport, walking and cycling.

can be used for all types of trips and in all types of weather – even when it is snowing.

12.3.1 Solution 18 – Allow the Idaho stop 
The “Idaho stop” got its name from a law in Idaho, which allow 
cyclist to have a different set of rules than cars at traffic lights 
and stop signs. Because cyclist have a better ability to hear and 
see the surroundings on the road compared to drivers. Advocates 
have decided that cyclist can perceive stop sign as yield signs and 
red lights as stop signs. This will result in a more logic way of 
cycling as explained in section 9.3.1 - The logic and needs, and 
allow for new traffic behavior which is much more effective. This 
will make cycling more appealing and comfortable. However, it is 
important that cyclists show consideration for other transport 
modes and do not compromise on the transport hierarchy and 
should not ignore the signs completely, which is clearly unsafe 
(Fenwick, 2015).   
12.4 Experiences 
A big strength by cycling is the direct exposure of the surroundings.  
The good experience can be the amount of information, the beautifulness of the route and other 
users. Users of the bicycle will also experience the weather, life on the streets and happiness of 
another cyclist. Bicycle users can make a spontaneously stop, make errands and meet others while 
cycling. Experience is correlated with comfort since good comfort gives a good cycling experience. 
This include even road paving, good lightning, snow removal, cleaning and maintenance of cycle 
lanes, facilities and information along the route.  
12.5 Infrastructure 
Investments in bicycle infrastructure have generally a large impact on the number of bicycle users. 
For that reason, it is important to incorporate cycling in all infrastructure projects to make cycling 
inclusive infrastructure (Dutch Cycling Embassy, 2016). An excellent cycling infrastructure consist 
of:  
- 
Cycle paths 
- 
Segregated and wider cycle lanes 
- 
Protected intersections 
- 
Bicycle parking both at stations and bigger hubs 
- 
Surveillance of bicycle parking 
- 
Separation from pedestrians 
- 
Cycling and motorized transport modes 
- 
Routes dedicated to all types of cyclist, such as cycling highways, green routes and commuter 
routes. Good cycling routes are often completely separated from motor vehicle routes. 
Excellent infrastructure is also about making cycling routes shorter, quicker and more direct than 
car routes. At the same time, there has to be access to other kinds of infrastructure and a good 
connection between missing links and big areas, so potential bicycle users can see the benefits and 
time savings of cycling. Hence building a good cycling infrastructure is not only about cycle lanes, 
intersections and parking.  It is also about nudging. Nudging is a way to make people change behavior 
in traffic. It is infrastructure that encourage people to do specific things, when traveling on bicycles.

initiatives are build, it is also important to have a high level of maintenance. Cracks and uneven 
pavement in the surface must be renovated and in winter time snow has to be removed.   
12.5.1 Solution 37 – Cycling routes separated from motor vehicle routes 
Cycling highways in urban areas are a way to 
prioritize cyclists. Urban bicycle routes should 
preferably be traced through traffic-restrained areas 
because cyclists prefer cycling conditions involving 
less traffic stress and interaction. Cycling highways 
is an attractive alternative for the highways. Wide 
bicycle lanes can improve travel time and give a 
positive contribution to safety, comfort and a good 
cycling experience. At some places it is relevant to 
consider building bridges or tunnels, instead of 
creating wide bicycles lanes along the roads. By 
doing so is it possible to create shortcuts and routes 
where car traffic is not even possible, like Bryggebroen/ Cykelslangen in Copenhagen (Gössling, 
2013). Other places with green cycling routes can be more optimal, because it creates long and direct 
routes without traffic lights and keep users away from the motorized traffic.  
12.5.2 Solution 51 – More bike racks 
Bicycle parking is an important part of a city’s 
cycling infrastructure. When bicycle parking 
facilities are inadequate or scarce people tend to use 
nearby trees, parking meters or a fence instead. 
Cities need to offer more on better bicycle parking; 
offering better locations, security and shelters to 
cover the bicycles in bad weather. When bike 
parking is available at destinations, citizens are 
more likely to choose to ride to those places 
(Cycling Embassy of Denmark, 2012a). So instead 
of subsidizing parking for motorized vehicles, 
cyclist needs more places to lock up their bikes. As

be bicycle stations, bicycle hubs at bigger transit centers, bicycle basements and bicycle racks in 
several levels/floors. It can at the mean time creates a more attractive and decorated neighborhood.

Mobility solutions increase health and make transport more effective when moving around in a city. 
This require improvement of the entire transportation system in a city and the possibility to select 
a green mode of transport at any time. Such system will affect citizens, commuters and companies 
travel behavior and affect them to choose walking, cycling, public transport, electrical bicycles, 
electrical cars, kiss-and-ride, park-and-ride carpools, bicycle sharing and car sharing more often.

12.6.1 Solution 56 – Bicycle sharing 
Bicycle sharing programs, such as Bycyklen in 
Copenhagen, can also play a valuable role in fostering 
a bike-friendly city. These programs allow anyone to 
rent rides on standardized bicycles to and from hubs 
placed around the cities. They can be used by city 
residents on a causal basis or as a trial-run for owning 
a bike. Visitors also use them for cheap and convenient 
travel around the city. The largest bike sharing system 
in Europe is Vélib from Paris. The system was launched 
in 2007 and consist of a network of 23,600 specially 
designed bicycles located at 1,800 bike stations 
throughout Paris. This system contributes with an 
average of more than 110,000 daily ridership (Velib, 
2016).

12.6.3 Solution 61 – Park and ride 
Park-and-ride facilities are parking lots with public transport connections that allow commuters and 
other people heading to city centers to leave their vehicles and transfer to a bus, rail system or 
carpool for the remainder of the journey. The vehicle will stay at the car park during the day and 
retrieved when the owner returns. For that reason, Park-and-rides are generally located in the 
suburbs of metropolitan areas or on the outer edges of large cities. By doing so, will lead to a decrease 
in the number of cars in the city center, leaving more space for bicycles. The Park-and-ride solution 
can also be implemented in a cycling perspective. Like parking a car at the car park, it is possible 
for commuters to park a bicycle at a bigger transport hub and then continue the journey by public 
transport.   
12.7 Policy 
Policy decisions is affected by the size of founds, the number of private investors and the collaboration 
between departments. However, all political decisions and agreements derives from approved plans. 
To implement cycling initiatives, it is necessary with a national or local cycling strategy plan – a 
common agreement of what to do and how it shall be executed. Examples of political initiatives made 
in European cities in order to improve cycling is car free zones, cash-out programs, removal of parking 
lots, congestion pricing, charge more for street parking, improve enforcement of traffic laws and 
motor vehicle free city center. 
12.7.1 Solution 66 – National or local cycling strategy plan 
There are many examples of strategies and development plans 
when it comes to bicycling. One of them is made by the 
Danish government: “A new cycling strategy for Denmark: 
Denmark on you bike” (Ministry og Transport, Building and 
Housing, 2014). The main objectives in such plan is to 
introduce citizens to an inexpensive, healthy and environment 
friendly form of transport. Another example is the reduction 
of car traffic in Oslo. Oslo will reduce car traffic throughout 
the city by 20% in 2020 and by another 10% in 2030 and make 
the city center completely car free (Abbey-Lamertz, 2015). In 
return Oslo, will focus on the bicycle and get commuters onto 
the bicycle. They will make rush hour fee for motorist, make 
subsidies for e-bike sales, removing all investments in 
companies that produce fossil fuel energy and remove all free car parking spots in Oslo city center.  
12.7.2 Solution 71 – Charge more for street parking 
Many cities offer free or inexpensive parking on city streets. This is essentially a subsidy from the 
city to car owners, which makes driving a more lucrative option. Charging market rates for parking, 
or reducing the amount of parking spaces offered by the city, would make driving more expensive 
and could shift more people to use alternative means of transportation such as the bicycle.

12.7.3 Solution 75 – Embassy or Institution 
An Advocate is a group which aims to influence decisions within economic, political systems, social

12.8 Safety 
Making a good cycling city is about making the city safe. The negative sense of security will be 
removed by creating safety initiatives. This includes good intersections design with bicycle boxes 
(advance stop lines for cyclists at key intersections about 3-4 meters ahead of the stop line for cars, 
increasing both visibility and safety), colored pavement etc. Good design of intersections is essential 
because intersections are the most important cause for delays and cycling accidents. There must also 
be a focus on safe cycling routes to school and a reduction in speed limits on selected streets. To do 
so it is important with a road safety audit, who is a formal safety performance examination of new 
building or existing road or intersection, and a Road safety advisory council who make 
recommendations to the government about road safety, etc. 
12.8.1 Solution 76 – Colored pavement 
Colored pavement is an on-road marked lane, that is either 
fully painted (in red, green or blue) as shown in Figure 44 or 
just simply separated by a painted line alongside motorized 
traffic. Colored pavement bicycle lanes are implemented 
because of their effectiveness with regard to safety. This way 
of designing the road, helps drivers to recognize vulnerable 
road users and to define the underlined tracing and continuity 
of the route. Fully painted lanes in intersections will increase 
the number of cyclist and decrease the level of accidents. This 
type of pavement is considered best practice in the 30-50 kph 
band with low to medium traffic flows. 
12.8.2 Solution 78 - Road safety advisory council  
A road safety advisory council is a group of expert that has specialized in road safety. They will have 
a significant influence on the development of a road safety program. Their primary tasks are to make 
recommendations to Government about road safety, policy, expenditure of road safety advertising 
campaigns, school-based and public education programs.

New technology enables cities to use drones to track movements of cyclist. This will give a very good

Such kind of information can be used, when planning for new infrastructure or improving current 
infrastructure. Furthermore, GPS-tracking and counts can also be used for tracking cyclists.  
12.10 Tourism 
A study performed by the European Parliament in 2012 estimated that there are over 2.2 billion 
cycle tourism trips and 20 million over-night cycle trips made every year in Europe. These have an 
estimated economic impact of €44 billion (ECF, 2012). For that reason, it is important for cities to 
improve facilities for tourism trips on bicycle. This improvement includes better information, bicycle 
rent, bicycle routes and sale of cycling holidays and day trips such as company trips.  
12.11 Travel time  
Many cyclists choose the bicycle because it is the fastest and easiest way to get around as explained 
in chapter 8 - A bicycle friendly city. Therefore, it is important to minimize travel times between 
origins and destinations when designing and planning bicycle infrastructure. This will encourage 
citizens to use the bicycle, but only if the travel time on bicycle is competitive with other transport 
modes.   
 
One way to make the bicycle competitive is to use intelligent transport systems to optimize the city’s 
traffic signals to the benefit of bicyclists and busses. Another way is to secure a better integration 
between transport forms. Overall it is about reducing speed for busses and cars and raise the line 
speed (the speed that the individual user chose without any stops, congestion or other obstacles) for 
cyclists.

The purpose of the decision-making tool is to come with a set of recommendations, based on the 
entered input in connection with the specified data and the constructed solutions.  
 
The product, which is conducted in this thesis is a model that covers the first three steps in the 
conceptual flow chart (1. Input, 2, connection and 3. Recommendation), explained in section 5.2 - 
Sustainable framework. There will only be given any guideline for how to implement or evaluate the 
presented recommendations. This is the cities own responsibility.

It is possible not to choose an interval, but the solutions that correspond to the that specific indicator

already implemented solutions or unimportant solutions afterwards. All the indicators are described 
in detail and the intervals for each indicator are very precise and based on data from cities all around 
Europe. The purpose of using other cities as benchmark is that developing bicycling cities can aim 
high. Developed bicycling cities will not be located in the top interval for all indicators. This gives 
the developed bicycling cities the ability to aim even higher and makes the model more suited for 
future purposes.  
13.2 Link between indicators and solutions

of population. Each indicator helps in one or several cases to select potential solutions. For each of

low/high score, all the solution with that specific indicator will show up in the recommendation list.

input tab and the solution “Allow the Idaho stop” (number 18) will show up in the model if “bicycle 
accidents in traffic” has a high score from the input tab. This is illustrated in

14 Discussion 
The purpose of the thesis has been to explain what a sustainable bicycle friendly city is and analyze 
how to become a sustainable bicycle friendly city, by using the developed decision-making tool. 
However, a lot of questions have come to mind, when going through this process. This chapter will 
try to explain some of these questions and come up with some answers.  
14.1 Omitted methods 
The methods used in the thesis is evaluated to enhance the solution in the best possible way. 
However, other methods could have been used, but would have provoked another solution. Besides 
making a literature review of what a good cycling city is, a questionnaire or a focus group interview 
with experts could have been conducted.  
 
A questionnaire or a focus group interview could have given a deeper insight into the cities necessities 
and what indicators they find relevant in a bicycle context. They could have been involved in the 
process, which would have created a combination of our knowledge as experts and the decision 
maker’s knowledge about how the political system works in terms of improving and implementing 
bicycle initiatives. However, depending on the type of experts, the list of indicators, would not 
necessarily be with a sustainable focus. It would incontestable create a more goal-oriented set of 
indicators and easier to measure, but with a different focus. We would might have experienced that 
the suggestions from transport planners and decision makers are more economic oriented, compared 
to the scope of our list of indicators.  
 
Another approach could be a case study. A case study can provide additional information to the 
analysis and will make the model more detailed. By performing several case studies there could have 
been created a deeper insight into how cycling projects are actually structured and how the political 
system function. This practical approach could have resulted in a better process of selecting indicators 
and solutions for the model. It was the initial idea to use a case study, but was neglected due to the 
time limit.  
14.2 Delimitations of topic 
In the delimitation, we specified that the thesis focuses on optimizing cycling initiatives instead of 
optimizing the entire transport sector. However, we know that an optimization of the entire transport 
sector could cause a positive effect on the number of people using the bicycle. Investigating and 
optimizing the entire transport sector, is complex and require more time and data.  
 
One approach to optimize the entire transport system is to use the avoid-shift-improve approach. 
This is a sustainable approach that seek to deal with increased transport demand and motivate 
people to choose more sustainable modes of transport.  
- 
The avoid/reduce refers to the need to improve the efficiency of the transport system, 
through planning and transport demand management. Hence the need to travel and the trip 
length may be reduced.  
- 
The shift concept is about shifting people to more environmental transport modes as a 
consequence of better planning.  
- 
The improve concept focus on improving fuel and vehicle efficiency as well as optimizing 
the transport infrastructure.  
 
==PAGE:114==
107 
 
The main purpose of avoid, shift improve is the procedure of how to address problems. It furthermore 
depends on the influence it has on each transport mode. From a user perspective, the avoid procedure 
will result in better planning and reduce the need of travel, so trips will be made over shorter 
distances. The shift procedure will encourage to use non-motorized and public transport. If the user 
decides to choose the car anyway, they will be recommended to reduce car size, consider alternative 
fuels or use car-pools. Overall, this procedure will lead to an optimization of the entire transport 
sector and an improvement of the cycling environment, which will make more people chosen the 
bicycle as the preferred mode of transport.  
14.3 Framework 
Two different theories have been used to explain the concept of sustainability, but it was only the 
intersected model, which was used in the analysis. Will the analysis have been different if the Nested 
model was used instead? And would some solutions have been preferred over others if a group of 
bicycle advocates had to make the decisions?  
 
The intersected model was found most useable, because of the collaboration and the equally 
importance between the three areas; Environment, Social and Economic. In our model, all three 
dimensions have been weighted equally within the field of sustainability. The model has been build 
up in such way that no indicators or solutions are more important than others from a sustainable 
perspective. However, from an efficiency perspective, some solutions will take priority over others in 
order to achieve a good cycling city. In case all solutions are implemented at the same time, will any 
potential prioritization list withdrawn and all solution will be considered equal. If this is not the case 
our suggestion is to follow the recommended steps. This way of building up the model has 
characteristics of a case based model, where each city is unique in size, level of indicators and 
recommendations is customized for the specific city analyzed.   
 
By using the nested model instead of the intersected model, will lead to other results, because the 
indicators will be weighted differently in the two models. The most important dimension in the 
nested model, will be the biosphere we live in, (environment) which contains the human system 
(social and economic), where the environmental dimension will have the highest priority, as explained 
in section 7.2.1 - The nested model. The prioritization of indicators in the nested model, will be done 
by ROD weights (rank order distribution) (Roberts & Goodwin, 2002). The consequence of 
prioritizing the environmental dimension higher than the social and economic dimension, will be a 
recommendation that focus on environmental solutions. As mentioned above, this will not influence 
the chances of becoming a sustainable bicycle friendly city, but will affect the way and effectiveness 
of the process of becoming a sustainable bicycle friendly city. By using the nested model, will result 
in a more inefficient way of implementing the recommended solutions, since all cities do not 
necessarily need recommendations which has something to do with environmental impact, but do 
instead need recommendation which focus on infrastructure or social aspects. The idea of following 
the philosophy behind the nested model, will not necessarily result in more people using the bicycle. 
People do not have a tendency to cycle because of environmental reason, but because it is easier and 
faster, as explained in chapter 8 - A bicycle friendly city. Motivating people through an environmental 
agenda, will not necessarily result in a positive effect because people do not care.  
 
The last opportunity for a prioritization, is the approach of letting a group of stakeholders or experts 
expressing their preferences from a bicycle advocacy perspective. This can be performed by explicit

bicycle theories or be based on stakeholders’ and experts’ own opinion and understanding of what a 
bicycle friendly city is. This group of people do not need to have the sustainability concept in mind 
when making decisions. When making decisions, they will most likely focus on the local strategies 
and plans and what they find important for that specific city.

Governance must play a central role in bicycle planning. As illustrated in Figure 10 in section 7.2.1 
- The nested model, policy can have huge importance. Governance is very complex and requires a 
detailed investigation of the processes and how decisions is made. Furthermore, governance and 
political decisions in different cities are not based on the same set of values. All countries in Europe 
have some sort of democracy, which makes political decisions more complex, due to different agendas 
and standpoint from each side of the political system. A bicycle agenda can be removed from period 
==PAGE:116==

to period, hence it is necessary to build up a bicycle groundwork to keep it on the agenda through 
periods.  
 
However, it is out of the scope for this thesis to analyze the political system for all the cities in the 
database. It could have been interesting to analyze how different leaders have different ways of 
running a country or a municipality and how this would have affected the political process of 
implementing bicycle initiatives. Likewise, it would have been interesting to investigate how cultural 
barriers affect implementation processes. Therefore, the political implementation process is neglected 
in the solution and recommendation sections and implementation and evaluation is only described 
briefly. A suggestion for further investigation is to analyze these issues.  
 
Another issue is the lack of transport models, where bicycles are included. Transport models can help 
to clarify how investments in bicycle infrastructure and bicycle safety is a financial good investment. 
The problem is that cyclist benefits are at the current state not incorporated in the transport models 
and is only available in few countries. It can be difficult for politicians to defend bicycle initiatives, 
if social economic perspectives are not included in the models and projects looks like an expense for 
the city 
 
At last, it is not possible to cover all externalities. We are aware of the fact that politicians can affect 
the number of bicycle users by other legislations or laws, which not necessarily has something to do 
with bicycling. For example, by introducing a law that makes it more favorable to buy a car (reduce 
fees or taxes on cars) or by giving subsidies to public transport, which will transfer bicycle users to 
other transport modes.  
 
Strong/W eak sustain: Is it possible to have strong sustainability when planning for a bicycle 
friendly city? And is it necessary to be strong sustainability can weak be enough when planning for 
future? 
 
Sometimes the intersected model is referred to as the weak sustainability model, because a 
degradation of capital in one dimension can be compensated for, by improving the capital in another 
dimension. This means that some capital can be externalized and be left with marginal importance. 
To avoid weak sustainability, it is important not to end up in situations, where some indicators are 
overlooked and left out of the analysis or in situations, where some indicators have a negative impact 
on other indicators. However, this can be difficult even though bicycles are categorized and defined 
as a very effective tool to increase sustainability. Situations may arise where implementation of a 
solution, can have negative impact on indicators in the model such as: 
In case of building new infrastructure or improving existing infrastructure, there will be a higher level 
of noise, pollution and congestion during the construction period. There can also arise problems with 
safety and the level of comfort when building new infrastructure. This will affect people in traffic in 
the short run, but have positive effect in the long run, because of improved and better infrastructure.  
If more children start to cycle it can have a negative impact on the perception of safety for other 
people in traffic. Too many people on the streets can lead to people feeling unsafe and unsecure when 
cycling.  
 
==PAGE:117==
110 
 
It is assessed that these negative externalities will only have a minor effect in the short run and will 
become a benefit for the society in the long run. For that reason, it can be quantified that cycling is 
strong sustainability.  
14.4 A bicycle friendly city  
According to the literature a good bicycle friendly city is compact, dense, flat and has a lot of service 
offerings within 3-4 kilometers. However, is it possible to reach level with too many bicycles?  
 
Amsterdam is one of the leading countries when it comes to bicycling and has the highest bicycle 
share among all cities in the world. People in Amsterdam have been very good at making cycling the 
best transportation option the last decade. However, the city is facing problems – Amsterdam is very 
close to reach the critical mass of bicycles. This causes a lot of complications: Many old bicycle paths 
need to be reconstructed, because they do not measure up to current capacity level. Bicycle paths in 
Amsterdam are used by so many bicycle riders that the bicycle paths are no longer wide enough. 
Besides that, the city is also facing challenges with traffic behavior -  people cycling do not observe 
the basic traffic rules of priority and stick to their own rules instead.  
 
If this development continues without the government taking actions, Amsterdam will reach a level 
of too many bicycles. Thus, cyclist in Amsterdam will begin to face the same problems as motor 
vehicles experience: Traffic congestion, parking difficulties and road rage. The municipality of 
Amsterdam, must think of new ways to create even more space and make it more comfortable and 
safe for cyclists and pedestrians. To stay a bicycle friendly city and maintain their position as a 
cycling capital, Amsterdam needs a totally new kind of infrastructure that can handle these problems.  
Representatives from other cities around the world, will call it a luxury problem of having too many 
cyclists in the streets and it is better to have too many cyclists compared to too many cars. This 
lead back to the optimal transport hierarchy and regardless of the situation should still be aimed to 
retain it. If cyclists outnumber the other transport modes, pedestrians can start to feel unsecure and 
unsafe, which results in empty sidewalks. This scenario will reduce the social dimensions and decrease 
the level of sustainability.  
 
The city of Amsterdam has gone so far that they are thinking about to implement a cyclist tax 
(Holland Cycling, 2014) to pay for bicycle parking. Experts argue that I will be fair if all types of 
users (no matter if it is users of motorized vehicles or cyclist) shall contribute to the cost of congestion 
and parking.

We were able to collect data from 46 cities around Europe which is assumed enough, to make a

14.6 City types 
There are pros and cons by simplifying the model with a limited set of data.   
 
Pros:  
- 
We had better time to investigate and define each of the city types, which enabled us to draw 
more detailed conclusion and come up with a more precise set of solutions for each of the city 
types.   
Cons:  
- 
There were only six city types that matched our conditions in our model (out a potential of 
3 ∙2 ∙3 ∙2 ∙3 = 108 city types), because we neglected city types with less than three cities. If 
we have had more accessible data, it could have been easier to create more than six city 
types. It might be the case that some cities do not fit the model at all. As an example, the 
model consists of one city type which is hilly (Type 4).  It will benefit the model to have 
more than one city type within each category, because it creates the possibility to follow a 
development. 
- 
The five basic parameters (GDP, temperature, density, topography and cycle share) is 
evaluated on a local scale. If the European cities was evaluated on a global scale, they would 
probably belong to the same interval, because there is a huge difference between cities 
throughout the world. 
- 
It was difficult to make intervals for the basic parameters when making the city types. These 
intervals might be different, if we have had the full set of data. Small changes in the lower 
and upper limits would have made new intervals and other city types would have occurred.  
- 
There are not enough city types to handle the fact that cities will change over time. Density 
will increase if more people move to the city and GDP will decrease, if the city faces years 
with bad economy. In case a city experience a change in one of the five parameters, there will 
be a high chance for this city to be moved to another type. This in fact is not a bad thing, 
because it illustrates that the city has developed and the suggested solutions have had an 
effect. However, it is a bad thing if the city develops in a direction, where none of the 
presented city types fit theoretically.  
- 
If a city match one or more city types, despite it only match on one parameter, the model 
will suggest the different city types and it is due to the fact that there is a limited number of 
city types to choose from.  
- 
Some parameters have been left out of the analysis because of lack of data. Belonging to the 
group of parameters that are left out of the analysis it is worth mentioning city structure and 
shape. These parameters can give indications of the traffic flow: If bicycle users are able to 
make short cuts or if they are forced to make a lot of turns. It could also tell if streets/cycle 
paths were straight or contoured to secure a certain speed when cycling. Obviously some 
infrastructural and technical solutions will fit this one type of city structure and shape better 
than others.    
 
It could have been interesting to see what the recommendation would have looked like if there had 
been one or several changes in the basic parameters. To do so new city types have to be made. Lets 
assume that weather is not an important parameter and people will ride the bicycle no matter if it

is raining, snowing or the sun is shining. Is this case will there be a potential of 3 ∙2 ∙3 ∙2 = 36 city

14.7 Planning for individuals 
It is important to differentiate between solutions and motivation. How to motivate people is different 
between population and age groups. From this perspective, the challenge will not necessarily be to 
improve cycling facilities, but to approach the potential cyclist. It is about planning for the individual 
needs instead of planning for the city. Infrastructure needs to be designed, so people find it attractive 
and shops needs to be located just around the corner. Currently, cities are planned based on the 
requirements of economic benefit. Without an economic benefit, it is difficult to get the projects 
implemented from a political point of view. 
 
One possible solution can be to look at, why people move around and why some transportation modes 
is more important than others. This require a deeper understanding of different population groups 
and stereotypes and what needs they have. Because of cultural reason, motivation factors will be 
different from individual to individual and from city to city – In a city like Copenhagen, new 
infrastructure and downgrading of cars to ensure cyclists’ conditions and progress in traffic will be 
valued high, while other cities will the motivation be health benefits or the easiness of moving around. 
Therefore, it is of best interest that the city aims to target its citizens and target them by the right 
use of communication – make surveys or ask them directly on the street what needs they have and 
plan directly for that. To be able to plan better for individuals in the future, citizens needs to be 
more involved in decision making processes. Governments and institutions shall improve the level of 
citizen participation and listen more on citizens’ opinions.    
14.8 Indicators 
As shown in the framework in chapter 5 - Framework, indicators and collecting data is just one 
component in the overall planning process. This process also includes defining problems, identifying 
goals and objectives, consulting stakeholders, develop policies and development plans, implementing 
solutions, establishing performance targets, measuring impacts and evaluating performance.    
 
At the same time, it has been discussed that there are many possible ways of defining sustainability, 
sustainable development and sustainable transport. The literature agrees on that the sustainability 
concept refer to balancing social, environmental and economic goals. Therefore, the chosen set of 
indicators needs to reflect appropriate social, environmental and economic goals of impact. If the 
chosen indicator set focus too much on one impact dimension, it can result in suboptimal decisions. 
Because of this, there has been used a lot of time to understand the perspectives and limitations and 
make the right assumptions for each of the indicators.  
 
Even though there is not a standardized set of sustainable indicators within transportation, 
sustainable indicators have been criticized for their attempt to encapsulate complex and diverse 
processes in a relatively few simple measures (Bell & More, 2008).However, we find it fair to use 
simple sustainable indicators to gauge such a complex topic as a sustainability. The world is a 
==PAGE:120==
113 
 
complex place and the most obviously way of dealing with this complexity is, to divide the world 
into manageable bits. There is of cause a tradeoff between having meaningful sustainable indicators 
and the necessity of simplification, but by breaking the complex system down into components and 
studying how the indicators work one by one and together will to a certain level justify the 
simplification of the complex system. By testing the relevance and availability of data as done in 
chapter 11 - Indicators, and test how indicators interact enables us find the right set of indicators.  
 
Much of the required data for the chosen indicators, may already be available through existing 
sources such as surveys or other reports, while some indicators might need special data that requires 
additional resources to collect. Despite major advances and improvements of collecting available and 
reliable data many countries face problems with these procedures. Indicators often require data that 
are routinely collected either by the government, an embassy or a national statistical service. These 
methods can vary from country to country and cities might collect indicators, based on their specific 
needs and abilities. Hence it can be useful to define and standardize sustainable transport data and 
indicator sets and standardize transportation data selection and collection practice to make 
transparency and establish recommended indicator sets suitable for various planning applications.   
 
The model contains 30 indicators. A smaller indicator set using easily available data, would have 
been more convenient to use. The reason for chosen a relatively big set of indicators is because the 
solutions are dependent on the input data, because a smaller indicator set will overlook important 
impacts. At the same time, less indicators will make the recommendation be based on less 
information, which is not preferable in order to make the right recommendations. The high number 
of indicators creates a more dynamic set of variables and more independent recommendation. We 
are aware of, that a larger set of indicators can be more comprehensive, difficult to interpret and will 
have higher collection costs, but such costs are just an investment in the future.   
 
Even though the three sustainable dimension is equally weighted, will there be a difference in the 
number of indicators within each dimension. The social dimension consists of 18 indictors, 
environmental consists of 4 indicators and economic consists of 8 indicators. As described earlier, the 
social dimension is not more important than the other dimensions, but the social dimension consist 
of many different aspects, which has to be taken into consideration, when describing a sustainable 
bicycle friendly city. In comparison, the aspect within the environmental dimension is more an effect 
of the two other dimensions than it is a way to improve bicycling – Improving facilities, infrastructure 
etc. will lead to more people cycling, which will improve the environment by a reduction in CO2 and 
other pollutants and not the other way around. However, the importance of the environmental 
dimension shall not be neglected. These measures are still important in order to evaluate the 
performance of the city. 
 
Defining one indicator set for the model, which can fit all cities in Europe can be a problem. Cities 
have different agendas and strategies of how to develop in the best way. We cannot make sure that 
the chosen 30 indicators are the right ones to describe the exact problem that a specific city is facing. 
However, we have tried to choose those indicators that we found most valuable, according to the 
listed theory and our insight into city plan and strategies. Another solution could have been to use 
an existing set of indicators of sustainability already defined by cities or the UN.  
 
The number of intervals should likewise match the possibilities for the decision maker to choose an 
interval, which is the reason for a limited number of intervals and it is possible to see the description 
==PAGE:121==
114 
 
of each indicator. If the city planner not have the exact number of daily trips, the planner could 
have an indication of the current situation in the city and choose the closest interval.  
14.9 The model 
The model aims to create a bicycle friendly city and the model, does not provide dedicated solution 
against other transport modes. E.g. if city type 4 aims for a solution that are outside the bicycle 
field, the model has not incorporated solutions outside the bicycle field. If the city wants to limit 
other transport modes with restriction or other initiatives, it is outside the scope of the model, but 
can still improve the level of bicycling. This is a more complex situation, because it can be different 
from city to city. The reason for all the hierarchies for each of the cities is a snapshot of the present. 
If a city changes over time the solution would match better, because they are build up after the green 
transport hierarchy.  
 
The city types are created to simplify the model and due to a limited time. If more city types were 
added a development over time could be captured and the model could be used for a longer time. If 
some of the cities evolves to new city types, it is not incorporated in the model. With more time, it 
is possible to add all combinations of city types, but all solutions also have to be identified for each 
city type.    
 
The model is best suited for cities with a low bicycle share, because the solutions targets these cities. 
If a city is a bicycle friendly city, we do not see it necessary to show them a long list of solutions. 
City type 1 and 6 are the cities with a high cycle share and will most likely gets the lowest number 
of solution proposed. If the aim is being like Amsterdam or Copenhagen, it requires a very big 
investment and change in culture. It has to be done in steps, which our model can be used for.  
 
The overall purpose is a theoretical model and the amount of city types, makes the connection 
between indicators and solutions. The model can constantly be updated with new intervals and new 
solutions.  
14.10 Future perspective 
Cities change and more countries and cities have an increased focus on sustainability. The technology 
continuously develops and therefore city types also has to be updated over time and the intervals 
also needs to be adjusted to adopt the changing world.  
 
Some of the indicators have to be updated, due to technological development. In the future, it might 
be impossible to steal a bicycle, or driverless cars can reduce all accidents. It is impossible to predict 
the future and suit the indicators to the future. However, it is important that cities remember to 
evaluate performance and the national policies and monitor the success as the recommendations are 
implemented. If this is not the case it will be difficult to come up with further recommendations.

15 Conclusion 
This thesis has shown that it is possible to develop a decision-making tool for transport planners and 
decision makers, which helps cities to become a sustainable bicycle friendly city or remain their 
position as a sustainable bicycle friendly city.  
 
The transport sector is a fundamental key in developing societies and plays a big role in sustainable 
development. Transportation targets are included in 7 out of 17 goals and is covered directly by five 
targets and indirectly by six targets. It is shown that improvements within the transport sector, can 
help to reach these targets and make the world more sustainable.  
   
To achieve this, it was important with a deeper understanding of the sustainability concept and the 
complexity within sustainable development. The intersected model, was chosen as the most preferable 
theoretical tool to clarify the concept. It was shown that the bicycle is a strong sustainable tool to 
improve the transport sector and enforce sustainable development in cities.  
 
Five important areas helped to define how cities becomes a good cycling friendly city. This included 
aspects within safety, infrastructure, density, culture and policy. The required city structure is a 
compact city with a high supply of services, jobs and schools within 3-4 km, which is flat with good 
connectivity and accessibility. Beside a well-structured infrastructure, it is also necessary with a good 
cycling culture, where cycling is the norm and not something temporary. In addition to this, people 
needs to feel safe, which can be improved by safety regulations and reducing the number of people 
getting injured or killed in traffic. This also includes better cycle behavior, where cyclists need to 
take other cyclist or drivers into consideration. The last part in obtaining a good cycling city included 
political support. National or local backup from the political system with clear strategies and plans 
is a necessity. Therefore, it is important that transport planners and decision makers prioritize 
cycling. Seven transport hierarchies were defined to illustrate how cities prioritize transport modes 
differently. The green transportation hierarchy is identified as the most important hierarchy and 
cities becomes a perfect sustainable bicycle friendly city when they fulfill the green transportation 
hierarchy.  
 
To do so, a list of recommendations was made within the decision-making tool. It was important to 
understand what makes the specific city unique and what does cities have in common to simplify the 
model and to find the best suited set of solutions.  
 
This was done by categorizing cities. Cities was group by five parameters, GDP, temperature, 
topography, density and modal share, which resulted in six different city types with 3-4 cities within 
each group. The six city types represented different areas of Europe. The city groups were then 
connected with the transport hierarchies in order to find an optimal transport hierarchy for each of 
the groups. This made it possible to investigated the opportunity for each of the groups to obtain 
the green transportation hierarchy and become a perfect sustainable bicycle friendly city. All groups, 
except group 4, has the potential of reaching the green transportation hierarchy, within a short time 
horizon. Group 4 shall initially reach for the public driven hierarchy and after that the green 
transportation hierarchy.  
 
To operationalize the concept and evaluate the performance of each city a set of 30 indicators were 
developed. This was done through a qualitative assessment based on the indicators relevance and 
==PAGE:123==
116 
 
availability. By introducing a core set of indicators among a large set of indicators, helps to keep the 
indicator set manageable. The 30 indicators were divided into the three sustainable dimensions: 
Economic, environmental and Social corresponding to the intersected model. Several of indicators 
like VOT, affordability, city structure etc. was neglected from the analysis because of lack of available 
data or because they could not be modified.  
 
The set of solutions was found based on literature reviews and based on initiatives already launched 
and implemented in cities around Europe. Doing so ensured the reliability and validity of the choice 
of solutions which resulted in 95 solutions.  
 
By combining the 30 indicators with the city types and transport hierarchies, made it possible to 
suite the solutions for specific city requirements. This was implemented in the model and based on 
inputs entered by the cities, a list of recommendation becomes available. It is important that cities 
remember to evaluate performance and national policies and monitor the success as the measures are 
implemented to obtain the best results. If doing so it is possible to update inputs and run the model 
again.  
 
Implementation of these recommendations will help cities to become a sustainable bicycle friendly 
city and to reach the sustainable development goals set by the United Nations. 
 
It is important to notice that different recommendation may arise depending on the theory used to 
explain the concept of sustainability. Other results would have been obtained if the nested model or 
a bicycle advocacy approach was used instead. It is also worth noticing that the output is very 
sensitive to changes in the data and city types has been simplified as a consequence of this. If more 
data was available other city types, indicators and solutions could have emerged.  
 
Cities change over time, hence it is important to future prof the model. Technology continuously 
develop and new data will be available. Therefore, new city types have to be updated over time and 
intervals has to be adjusted to adopt the changing world.

Bike to work campaign
The “Bike to work” campaigns have existed for many years and is now available in many different versions. The classic “Bike to work” campaign had the aim to reduce unnecessary 
flooding of towns with cars, reduce gas emissions and stimulate healthy lifestyle. Beside that it is a good way to solve problem with distance and increase the number of daily trips. 
The scheme was about encouraging employees to cycle to and from work. People are grouped in teams with the purpose of inspiring and motivating each other for one month of 
cycling.  Today the aim of the campaign is still the same, but scheme exist in other versions. In Ireland, a scheme encourages employees to cycle to and from work by letting 
employers paying for their employees’ bicycles and bicycle equipment. Then the employee pays back through a salary sacrifice arrangement up to 12 months, where the employees 
is not liable for tax payment[1]. Another scheme in Milan will pay cyclist 25 cents per kilometer travelled, up to a monthly cap of 50 euros. This project uses smartphones to record 
the distance travelled each day

Another way to improve this is to have a city area/zone dedicated to transport modes following specific environmental rules. A low emission zone (LEZ) operates to encourage the

18 Culture

The “Idaho stop” got its name from a law in Idaho, which allow cyclist to have a different set of rules than cars at traffic lights and stop signs. Because cyclist have a better ability to 
hear and see the surroundings on the road compared to drivers, advocates have decided that cyclist shall be able to treat stop sign as yield signs and red lights as stop signs. This will 
result in a more logic way of cycling and allow for new traffic flows which is much more effective. This will make cycling more appealing and comfortable. However, it is important 
that cyclists show consideration for other transport modes and do not ignore the signs completely, which is clearly unsafe

Segregated cycle lanes improves safety, security and travel time. Generally separated cycle lane, fully-segregated and thus protected physically from road traffic by a kerb, parking,
