Preface
This master thesis is the work of Kathrine Breinstrup Butler as a finalisation of the master’s degree in
Architectural Engineering from the Technical University of Denmark, DTU.
This thesis is based on the data collected in the building at Amerika Plads 11 & 15 from IoT sensors
and BMS system, and it deals with data wrangling to analyse the data with focus on finding systems,
services or installations to optimise and thereby ensure a better building performance. All this is though
with focus on the continuous commissioning process after the handover of the building from contractor
to owner.
The project is credited 35 ECTS points and has been conducted in the period from the 1 September
2021 to 22 March 2022.
Kongens Lyngby, 22 March 2022

Abstract
Commissioning is a process, which takes place before, during and after the construction work or reno-
vation of a building. The purpose of the commissioning process is to clarify the demands related to the
performance of the building from the owner to the contractor as early as possible in the building process.
The benefits of this is thus, that the building installations and systems are adjusted to the specific needs
and thereby minimise the errors, which are often present in connection with occupancy.
In Denmark, no specific requirements for commissioning or similar initiatives apply, which in time might
defeat errors or correct systems before these might cause adverse incidents in the building. The only re-
quirement from the Danish Building Regulation is §298b, which states that before occupancy a functional
test might be made to ensure that the building automation is correctly installed and adjusted.
Regarding the renovation of the buildings of PensionDenmark at Amerika Plads 11 & 15, IoT sensors
and a BMS system have been installed. The sensors have since April 2021 collected data regarding
the indoor climate as well as the impact of technical installations in the buildings. The data include
i.a indoor temperatures and CO2 concentrations in the different zones of the building, consumption of
electricity and domestic water, as well as supply and return temperatures on risers related to space
heating, ventilation and domestic hot water from the boiler.
Based on the presented Key Performance Indicators, KPIs, data wrangling has been conducted and has
resulted in the following.
The analyses of the data show a huge circulation loss regarding the distribution of the domestic hot
water and followingly a poor utilisation of the heat of the water. This is i.a. caused by the use of the
building, and a proposal for optimisation of the utilisation could be to reduce the time of the circulation
to be active during working hours. Thereby, the return temperature will be reduced with 1-4◦C and
ensure a better building performance without compromising the quality of the indoor climate.
Analyses of the indoor CO2 concentration have shown that the ventilation system works hard by at-
tempting to keep down the concentration under 740 ppm - though in average around 460 ppm. By
regulating the system and thereby increasing the CO2 concentration within the requirement from the
Danish Building Regulation at maximum 1000 ppm, the energy consumption and thereby the electricity
consumption for ventilation can be reduced considerably.
Based on the results of the primary data it has been attempted to make an indication of the thermal
imbalance of the building. This showed a difference between heat losses and gains at 38% which might
be caused by the period of measured data and the minor heat losses, which has been left out from the
calculations but ought to be investigated further to presenting the real conditions.
With a view to simplify the work with the commissioning process at this stage, a commissioning assistant
has been created. It can be used as a guide to the data wrangling and processing for similar building
offices with opportunities to collect data.

Resume
Commissioning er en proces, der udspiller sig før, under og efter opførelsen eller renoveringen af et byggeri.
Commissioning-processen har til form˚al at klarlægge ejerens krav i forbindelse med bygningsperformance
til byggeriet over for entreprenøren p˚a et s˚a tidligt stadie af byggeprocessen som muligt. Fordelen herved
er, at bygningsinstallationerne og -systemerne tilpasses det bestemte behov, og dermed mindskes de fejl,
der ofte opst˚ar i forbindelse med ibrugtagningen.
I Danmark er der ingen krav til commissioning eller tilsvarende tiltag, der rettidigt skal kunne afhjælpe
fejl eller rette p˚a systemer, inden de for˚arsager utilsigtede hændelser i et byggeri. Det eneste krav fra
Bygningsreglementets side er §298b, som anfører, at der inden ibrugtagning skal udføres en funktion-
safprøvning af, om bygningsautomatikken er korrekt installeret og korrigeret.
I forbindelse med en gennemg˚aende renovering af PensionDanmarks bygninger p˚a Amerika Plads 11 & 15
er IoT-sensorer og BMS-anlæg blevet monteret og installeret. Sensorerne har siden april 2021 opsamlet
data omkring indeklimaforhold samt de tekniske installationers p˚avirkning p˚a byggerierne.
Dataene
omfatter bl.a.
indetemperaturer og CO2-koncentrationer i bygningernes forskellige zoner, forbrug af
elektricitet og brugsvand samt fremløbs- og returtemperaturer p˚a stigrør i relation til rumopvarmning,
ventilation og varmt brugsvand fra varmtvandsbeholderen.
P˚a baggrund af opstillede Key Performance Indicators, KPIer, er databehandlingen udført og har resul-
teret i følgende.
Analyserne af dataene viste et stort cirkulationstab i forbindelse med distribueringen af det varme
brugsvand med en d˚arlig udnyttelse af vandets varme. Dette skyldes bl.a. brugen af bygningen, og
´et forslag til at optimere udnyttelsen kan være at reducere cirkulationen til kun at være aktiv i arbe-
jdstimerne, hvorved cirkulationstabet reduceres med omkring 65%. Resultaterne viste ogs˚a, at det i
forbindelse med rumopvarmning er muligt at regulere p˚a installationerne og derved sænke returtempera-
turen med 1-4◦C og sørge for en bedre bygningsperformance uden at g˚a p˚a kompromis med indeklimaet.
Analyser af CO2-koncentrationen indendørs har vist, at ventilationssystemet arbejder h˚ardt ved at
forsøge at holde koncentrationen nede under 740 ppm - dog gennemsnitligt omkring 460 ppm.
Ved
at ændre p˚a systemet og derved øge CO2-koncentrationen - dog stadig holde den inden for Bygningsre-
glementets krav p˚a maksimalt 1000 ppm - vil energiniveauet og dermed elforbruget kunne reduceres
betragteligt.
Baseret p˚a resultaterne af den primære data er det forsøgt at lave en indikation af den termiske balance
i byggeriet. Denne viste en afvigelse p˚a 38% mellem varmetab og -tilskud, hvilket blandt andet kan
skyldes perioden, som dataene er m˚alt over, samt flere mindre varmetab, der ikke er medregnet, men
som bør studeres yderligere for at kunne klargøre de reelle forhold.
Med henblik p˚a at gøre commissioning-processen i dette stadie lettere at arbejde med er der udarbejdet
en commissioning-guide, der kan bruges til vejledning af databehandling for tilsvarende kontorbygninger
med mulighed for dataindsamling.

2.1
The Danish Building Regulation
The Danish Building Regulation 2018 states in §298b that
A functional test of building automation must be carried out before commissioning. The
functional test must demonstrate that the building automation is correctly installed and
regulated, performs as intended and provides the required energy efficiency.
[Bolig- og Planstyrelsen, 2018, §298b (translated)]
Functional test was implemented in the regulation with the new Building Regulation from 2018 (and as
an edition to BR15 the last half year, where it was valid), wherefore it is still a newer term. It is though
an extension of the already existing requirements of demonstration of the performance of installations.
§298b is the only section in the regulation, except the guidance, where functional test and commissioning
is mentioned. Thus they are important to distinguish as it is two different, technical terms.
Functional testing is a subject specific element, which has to be conducted in single installations and
not interdisciplinary. It includes ventilation systems, heating and cooling systems, lighting systems and
lifts. The purpose of this test is to demonstrate that the different systems in practice work and per-
form as intended and contribute with the necessary quantities which are used in the energy calculation
[Bolig- og Planstyrelsen, 2018]. The calculation needs to be respected as it documents that the building
obeys the regulated energy requirements. As for heating and cooling systems, the testing must prove
the minimum conditions used in the calculation of the energy consumption. Thereby the testing demon-
strates that the operation works as intended.
Likewise for lighting control, ventilation systems and
energy consumption for lifts. A functional test can be independent, which is a strength for the involved
[Bolig- og Planstyrelsen, 2018]. Furthermore, these are obligatory and the documentation must be sent
to the municipality which will access these, before issuing a possible occupation permit.
On the other hand is commissioning. Commissioning is a process which is conducted before, meanwhile
and after the functional testing.
The process is not a requirement or a part of the regulation, and
is thereby an additional choice to a building process. In 2014 a Danish Standard was made, DS3090
[Dansk Standard, 2014], as a guide to handle commissioning, section2.2.
Even though commissioning is not a requirement, more developers and project managers think that
functional testing should be incorporated in the commissioning process and think that functional tests
must be available as preconditions for an upcoming commissioning process [Jarløv, T. T., 2020] if the
data is useful in the specific situation.
2.2
Commissioning
DS3090 obeys the existing requirements from the Danish Building Regulation and other regulations
outlined by the Danish Environmental Protection Agency and the Danish Working Environment Au-
thority [Dansk Standard, 2014]. DS3090 is based on the requirements from the standards DS428, DS447,
DS469, DS700, and DS747, which all contain guidelines regarding ventilation, cooling and heating, light-
ing and indoor climate. Furthermore, the process in this standard corresponds to the one described in
the [Visier, J. C., Jandon, M., et al., 2010] and [Neumann, C., et al., 2012] of Energy Conservation for
Building and Community Systems Program (ECBCS), section 2.3.
6
==PAGE:21==

Commissioning is a process to verify and document the performance of a building system. The method
focuses on quality as it attempt to gain control over the operational performance in existing and future
buildings [Visier, J. C., Jandon, M., et al., 2010]. The objectives of using commissioning can be regarded
from four points of view: the building owner, the designer, the contractor, and the operator. They all
have different interests in the project, economical, political, and regarding personal publicity. These
interests might in some situation overrule the process of commissioning, but can often be minored by
clarifying expectations, requirements and agreements; using functional performance testing to verify that
the operating system is as expected; producing manuals and current reports to document the process
and to make decisions.
These stakeholders have to cooperate when the organisation of commissioning of a specific project is
established.
The building owner is responsible for the process of commissioning and accordingly he
is responsible for assembling the commissioning organisation. In general, three different combinations
of organisations exist [Visier, J. C., Jandon, M., et al., 2010], but the combination of stakeholders will
continuously be changed, so the stakeholders are adjusted in the respective phases, see section 2.3.
Currently, experts can be summoned and help with specific activities. Contractors, engineers, or suppliers
likewise [Dansk Standard, 2014].
The first example of an organisation for commissioning consists of the designers, contractors, engineers,
and operators. These stakeholders are already a part of the project and do not require additional people
or costs. Though, the building owner is not a part of this, and his knowledge about commissioning is
dependent on stakeholders, which he has not chosen by trust.
The second example consists of a commissioning authority, which is chosen by the company of the
building owner. His job is primarily to monitor the process. He is loyal to the building owner as he is
selected directly to conduct this inspection. This organisation is more expensive as an extra stakeholder
is hired to observe.
The third combination is a mix of the two above, where the designers, contractors, engineers, and
operators carry out their work, while the commissioning authority monitor the process.
To get the most effective commissioning organisation a horizontal organisational chart is preferable
[Dansk Standard, 2014]. It is defined by few levels of management between the managers and the staff
where the communication is flowing. The employees have more responsibility which might create moti-
vation and confidence and might result in a more productive organisation.
Figure 2.1 is an example of a horizontal commissioning organisation. The building owner has its own
operational staff who has to operate the systems when the building is occupied. The contractors etc.
have operational staff as well but these are the operating staff during construction. In this way the
decisions can be verified by the different experts.

Commissioning can be divided into four categories [Neumann, C., et al., 2012]:
• Initial commissioning
• Retro commissioning
• Recommissioning
• Lifetime commissioning or ongoing commissioning
Initial commissioning is a process, which systematically follows a new building or a new installed system
in a new building [Legris, C., et al., 2010]. In that way the performance will meet the expectations and
requirements from the building owner from the first step of the construction process.
Retro commissioning is commissioning implemented in an existing building, where a commissioning
process never before had been initiated.
The process of an already commissioned building, where the owner decides to begin a new commissioning
process, is called recommissioning. It is normal for commissioned buildings in its lifetime to have a
decreasing performance, which is measured on the reduced productivity from the occupants and reduced
energy efficiency. In such cases this type of commissioning can be used, when the building owner seeks
to improve and document the building system’s performance.
Finally, the lifetime commissioning, also called the ongoing commissioning, is a continuing process which
purpose is to improve and optimise the performance of the building systems from earlier attempts on
commissioning.

Commissioning process
Annex 47, [Neumann, C., et al., 2012], is a part of a bigger project ECBCS which was started by the
International Energy Agency (IEA), whose aim is to develop and implement an international energy
program. The mission with ECBCS is to develop and integrate processes and technologies for energy
efficiency, to ensure healthy, sustainable and low emission buildings [Neumann, C., et al., 2012]. This

specific program consist of 56 annexes regarding research and innovation on technologies for energy
efficiency. Annex 47 consists of 4 reports regarding effective commissioning. They describe and discuss
how existing and future buildings can improve the performance and how building commissioning can
affect this. Furthermore the purpose of these reports is to set a common standard for commissioning.
Right now the IEA countries have individual procedures for constructing and operating buildings which
are not sufficient to ensure optimal operation.
The building process starts with the predesign phase and ends in the occupancy and operations phase

Based on the initial commissioning process the building owner generate and send a request for proposals
to e.g. a commissioning authority in the predesign phase. If needed he searches for experts or advisors
with knowledge in technology, construction and economics.
The commissioning authority starts planning the process and initiate cooperation with e.g. construction
and facility managers, financial advisors, and maintenance staffs. Furthermore his job is to estimate the
cost of the project and to guide in regulations related to commissioning. The initial commissioning plan
is developed in this phase and requirements from the owner to the entire building are formulated. In
the design phase a time schedule is planned in more details. The documents for the design team are
submitted, so their work can start. Drafts will followingly be made and updated. If divergence in the
documents from the designer exists, the building owner needs to be informed.
The created design documents form the basis of the construction phase, where the system is tested and
adjusted to create the sufficient balance. If changes are necessary, the communication can be done by
the commissioning authority and followingly the performance is audited. The commissioning authority
verifies the testing, adjusting and balancing work and determines if the systems meet the requirements
for the building owner.
Between the design and construction phase is the elaboration phase where the official documents as bid
submission and assessment are sent as well as contractors are selected
When the handover has taken place and the building systems operates as dimensioned, the building
is started being used by the occupants in the occupancy and operation phase. As the season changes,
the design requirements needs to be confirmed by identifying the seasonal system performance. This

2.4
Guidelines for commissioning
As mentioned, the commissioning process is resource demanding and requires technical knowledge and
insight in the operational systems. Additional, it is a complicated economical calculation.
[Værdiskabende Byggeproces, værdibyg.dk., 2013] set up a list of advantages and disadvantages the com-
missioning process includes.
Before commissioning is a process the building owner plunges into, it has to create a value for him and the
entire project. The value can comprise energy consumption, economics, or sustainability. If the above-
mentioned can create a value for the owner or entrepreneur, they can expect a building ready for use from
the first day, a building with good indoor climate, where the consumption is documented, and a building
with optimised operation [Værdiskabende Byggeproces, værdibyg.dk., 2013]. The process is furthermore
a quality control of the operation and functional success of the entire building project. Because of the
milestones related to the commissioning activities, the time delays are decreased [´Ag´ustsson, R. ¨O., 2010].
Though, it is often seen that the finished building project deviates from the agreed requirements, which
might affect the occupants if the indoor climate is not as expected or the operation does not work
[Bygningsstyrelsen, 2021]. The outcome of the commissioning process is first visible in the occupancy and
operation phase, but the discussions about solutions and methods are taken in earlier phases than usual.
It can create tensions but often all involved are satisfied with the early discussions as the requirements are
obeyed and reconstructions are unnecessary. The operators and users are involved early in the process,
as the activities related to the commissioning process need to be implemented in the time schedule. The
proactive method demands resources and time but will in the long term ensure that the schedule will be
obeyed before the handover.
Consequently, Danish Building and Property Agency tries to develop a performance test method as a
part of the functional testing described in the Building Regulation, §298b and §450. The performance test
prepare the ground for commissioning as the process has to be verified during the phases of construction
and production. By these a part of the contract sum is paid out as a completion of a milestone. No
more requirements are demanded by the verification, but the new method has to connect how and when
the requirements are getting verified [Bygningsstyrelsen, 2021].
The commissioning process can variate in size dependent on the resources. Often it will create most
value if commissioning is used in relation with ventilation, plumbing and heating/cooling, but if the
budget for a project is big enough, more installations can be added.
Another way to keep down
the costs for commissioning is to make use of the already existing commissioning related activities.
If the cooperation with contractors works, the quality control, self-regulation and testing made in
the contracts can be reused in the commissioning process. Thereby, an economical saving is possible
[Værdiskabende Byggeproces, værdibyg.dk., 2013]
One of the leading nations to use and implement commissioning is the United States. [´Ag´ustsson, R. ¨O., 2010]
states that from a financial perspective, existing buildings have a median energy saving at 16% equalling
$3.23 per m2 in commissioning cost. This correspond to a median pay back time around a year for
existing buildings [´Ag´ustsson, R. ¨O., 2010].

2.5
Fault detection
Several factors come into play when it has to be considered how a building and its relating systems work.
As the building is in use most of the year, it has a lot of external impacts, which can results in several
detected faults. The reason for faults in building systems and regarding operation can i.a. be caused by
faulty equipment or construction as well as incorrect use and control of systems. Additionally, the faults
can be caused by missing maintenance of specific components [Neumann, C., et al., 2012].
Overall, fault detection has the purpose to determine specific fault types, their locations and times of
detection. It is done in a process of two stages; if a fault is present in the input-output measurements,
a signal is generated. If the residual signal has a zero value the system works without faults, and if
it varies from zero a fault is present in the system. The residual can be a single fault, a scalar signal
containing a single element, or multiple faults expressed by a vector. In the second step it is decided
what will happen with the fault(s) and what the process will be [Mouzakitis, A., 2013].
Fault detection is the main reason for the above mentioned regulations, guides, and articles having been
made. The detection of fault increases with the growth of complexity of existing buildings. Optimi-
sation and fault detection are essential in the development of energy savings and operational systems
[Neumann, C., et al., 2012]. The faults is handled by different fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) tools,
which mostly are based on predicted building performance and a knowledge-based system.
2.6
Building Management System and Internet of Things
The Building Management System is a controlled system installed in buildings that - based on computer
technology - can monitor the electrical and mechanical elements on the building [Pradeep, G., et. al., 2019].
The data is collected from the systems of heating, ventilation, water and electricity. This technical sys-
tem collects the data and automates the monitoring to ensure a well performed building. This is the
reason that a BMS system has to work as intended, efficient and stable.
The precocious systems had a central controller unit, which had minor functionality based on their
limited technological development and possibilities to control. Later, the interface of the systems was
developed and was far more user-friendly. This was the beginning of the present Building Management
Systems, which with the technology of today can monitor, control, and optimise the building based on
the pattern of use during different seasons of the year.
The traditional BMS are also named Building Automation Systems (BAS) and do as well consist of
servers, supervisory devices, inputs, outputs, and controllers. The primary function of BAS is to connect
the HVAC system with the different building equipment to work as a integrated unit, and in this way to
centralise the operations [Wattsense., 2021].
Additionally, Internet of Things (IoT) sensors exists which is a technology connected to the internet
where data can be transported through sensors. These collect data as BMS, but is not connected into
a network and does not control the system. Though, the collection and analysis are simple and cost-
effective as the data from disparate sources can be brought together, cataloged and analysed for insight
and following responded to on certain conditions and controls.
Installing BMS systems are an expensive solution as it requires specialized installation and programming
and following a competent, educated team to maintain it. Therefore, [Tracy, P., 2016] states, that BMS
is often installed in large buildings, as the return of investment is a challenge for all others. Often it

On the other hand, BMS systems can be a energy saving system, which can deliver energy efficiency

2.6.1
Visualisation of data from BMS and IoT
When using the data to analyse the building operation, different visualisation techniques can be used to
display this information [Neumann, C., et al., 2012]. Different diagrams and plots can present operation
patterns, which can be used to analyse correlations or other statistical information. By visualising the
data, the quality or operation can be evaluated and following identify potential optimisations possibilities.
This makes it easier when presenting the data for managers or other involved.
The visualisation can among others be used to show changes over time, relations between variables,
comparison of scalars or specific processes. Before visualising this a pre-processing might be carried out,
as it might be necessary to handle the BMS data before using it [Neumann, C., et al., 2012].
• Plausible data check: check and handle if there is outliers or ’not a number’-values in the data set.
These can be misrepresent in the visualisation, if e.g. a ratio between two variables have to be
shown. Additional, the time line can be changes, so the graphics present the data in a minor or
major scale.
• Data conversion: the collected data do in many cases have another unit than expected in a specific
analysis. Therefore, the unit might be convert to the wished, so new data is created.
• Data calculating: new variables can be created by calculate the ratio between two measured values.
This can streamline a process and a analysis. This is present in e.g. the temperature difference
between indoor and outdoor air.
• Filtering data: a tool to create a subset of data with certain conditions and thereby the data can
be studied under these boundaries. By doing this it can e.g. be possible to check a the indoor
temperature filtered by the external temperature have to by negative.
• Grouping data: Related to the filtering, data can be grouped to extract information from the data
set. In this way e.g. weekdays can be a separate group from weekends when looking at a specific
set of data, as well as working hours vs. outside working hours.
Above mentioned pre-processes are all affecting the raw data and have to be done in respect to the data
sets. By select specific data, other data is thus deselected. This means that the author have the power to
present the specific data, that he wants to be presented, and thereby the product can be misleading. It
is though important, that the pre-processing is careful considered and the result is evaluated in respect
of the building and data.

Key Performance Indicators
Key Performance Indicators are tools often used in management correlations where the effectiveness or
efficiency can be measured. KPIs can be used in the individual sections in workplaces to measure daily
performance or in major scales as long-termed objects, but common to the KPIs despite size is that they
direct the way of work.
KPIs are only effective if they are attempted to be respected. It is often seen, that a company focuses
on too many goals, which they want to achieve, and caused by the extent of the objectives none or only
a few of the KPIs will succeed. Therefore, it is important to choose the KPIs from a varied point of view
as well as to be aware that the measured parameters make sense for the company.
3.1
Lean and SMART
KPI is a part of the Lean methodology. Lean is a way of thinking where the practitioner seeks to find a
new way of optimising activities, people, effort, or resources e.g. in a company. The methodology relies
on the following basic elements: delivery of a requested value for costumers which have to have minor
consumption of resources. By this the elements which are not productive will be minimised. Finally, the
process has to be improved continuously to create new knowledge and insight in the process. Lean can be
incorporated in all business cases where the productivity and costumer or user satisfaction are in focus.
As the methodology is about improving work processes and people, Lean urges to share the responsibility
and leadership by respecting the people and improve continuously [Compass Academy, 2021].
If Lean is an incorporated part of an organisation, problems are a natural element in a process and are
welcomed as the company can improve and learn from them. By identifying these problems, it s easier
to solve the concrete problems and thereby be a better company and better than the competitor.
In 1990 James P. Womack and Daniel T. Jones published the book Lean Thinking which became a
bestseller. The term lean is not their invention but the term of lean thinking can be connected to the
two authors. They described the way of thinking as an activity with an amount of waste dependent on
the process and the related organisation. They defined furthermore the five principles behind the lean
thinking [Compass Academy, 2021], figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Lean thinking
14
==PAGE:29==
3

The value for the customer or user is the first principle. The value has to be clearly defined, otherwise
it is not possible to make a service or product which meets the intentions. The defined value will be
delivered by an value stream. In this stream more activities than necessary will be created. Therefore,
later waste (activities) need to be removed if they do not contribute to the value. To ensure the drive
in the value stream a continuous flow between activity and process has to be created. Information and
materials have to flow to avoid constantl stoppage. In a physical production the fourth step is most
relevant as the users have to create a demand on the materials and thereby create a pull which in the
long term will ensure no overproduction. If the material is information, it will be pushed throughout
the value stream. Finally, the most characteristic of lean is the ongoing evaluations and improvements,
where problems will be solved and the process optimized.
A tool to handle the thinking of lean is by using the SMART model [Jakobsen, A., Kolarik, J., 2018]. The
SMART model can be used when your company needs to measure the relevance of the KPIs. SMART
is a technique to set up KPIs and evaluate if they are relevant. A SMART goal have to be
• Specific
• Measurable
• Achievable
• Realistic
• Within time frame
A goal needs to be specific to drive an action. The KPI need to be concrete and refine to give the specific
knowledge about the progress. It is not enough to state, that e.g. the indoor climate has to be better.
Instead a goal could be, that at least half of the week, the Predicted Percentage of Dissatisfied, PPD,
should be less than 10% and thereby have a Predicted Mean Vote, PMV, between -0.5 and 0.5. To reach
such a specific goal, it might be considered what, how, and when the goal has to be achieved, and who
the participants are. Next, the KPIs should be measurable. The measurements are the metrics which
basically measure the performance. It is not enough to measure the indoor temperature in the entire
building to study the PPD - it would be more beneficial to study the temperature on the different levels
or in specific zones. The measurement needs to be done frequently to keep the entire project on track.
Therefore, the goals need to be assigned to a specific person or team making decisions, directly and
followed up. The assignable parameter is about making sure, the goal succeeds and not about criticising
a group of people.
All the above needs though to be relevant, therefore it is important to state what the purpose really is
and what should be accomplished. All sub-goals and KPIs need to be relevant to this, and if it leads in a
new direction, the sub-goal might be reconsidered. At least, all goals need a completion data, otherwise
they will be given a lower priority. Thus, milestones can be built into the KPIs to keep on track and
keep the deadlines [Grow, 2019].

3.3
Process of identifying KPIs
With a lot of data available there is a tendency of trying to measure and analyse everything, that is
measurable. Instead of focusing on, what can be measured, do focus on what is necessary to be measured.
When identifying KPIs it is therefore important to remove all noising data and find out if the performance,
you want to report, only can be presented with the specific KPI or if other KPIs have the same function.
The KPIs can be classified into different categories. It depends on which indicators need to be measured
[Perez, S., 2021]. The most common types of KPIs includes the following
• Quantitative KPI: a number to measure the progress. Two different quantitative indicators exist.
Continuous indicators includes values over a range that can go on in the future, where discrete
indicators are a specific amount of data.
• Qualitative KPI: a data set describing opinions or feelings, or is a description of a process.
• Process KPI: a way of measure the efficiency, effectiveness and productivity within a business
• Input KPI: an indicator to measure resources used or needed for a specific project or process. It
can be the time and resources.
• Output KPI: an indicator to measure the output, success or failure of a process. It includes often
revenues or profits.

• Leading KPI: a way of predicting the performance or outcome of a process. Summed up, the KPIs
will look into the future and what might happen.

3.4
Benchmark
A benchmark is a basis of comparison. It is a way to compare a specific business activities, processes,
materials, services, methods, and so on with other leading businesses, and measure the differences in
performance. The purpose of using benchmarks is to identify areas for improvements in a organisation.
Related to building work a benchmark is an index to evaluate e.g. if the result is positive or nega-
tive. Benchmarks are not requirements as the content of the Danish Building Regulation and Danish
Standards, but guidelines for desirable performance values.
Both benchmarks and Key Performance Indicators are methods to measure the performance of a system,
by using motivation to ensure a increased performance prospectively.
Benchmarks are reference points, that the performance can be compared to, meaning that when a
performance or process is compared to equals, we are dealing with benchmarking. The benchmark can
thus be individual for the specific situation and be a reference point on its own performance level. The
focus is to improve a process or technology with similar business cases or projects. Often the benchmarks
includes customer or user satisfaction, quality, cost, and time [Harper, M., 2019, Marr, B., 2021].
Overall four different types of benchmarks exist:
• Internal: compares processes or metrics to similar within the same company or project.
This
means e.g. that the temperature and CO2 concentration in different zones in the same building
are compared with each other. This results in an understanding of the current standard.
• External: compares processes or metrics to competitors or other equals.
Ensures an objective
understanding of the current state of own process, as well as an understanding of the competitors.
• Performance: gathers and compares quantitative data to find performance gaps. This is often the
first step in identifying missing parts.
• Practice: gathers and compares qualitative data about performance of specific activity. This results
in insight into performance gaps and best practice, which can be applied.
While KPI indicates the level of performance and in context shows if the progress is on track, benchmarks
can establish the relevant and specific target [Marr, B., 2021]. Therefore, benchmarking is a management
tool, which is used related to continuous improvement.

4.2.1
District heating
DH systems are systems supplying hot water to buildings across the country. The DH is transported
through and distributed via networks of insulated pipes to buildings. The end-user has a substation with
i.a. a heat exchanger to transfer the energy of DH to SH (HVAC system) and DHW system.
The difference between the temperature of supply and return water for DH is cooling, ∆T. The lower
the return temperature is when it returns to the plant of DH, the better system utilises the heating of
the water in the system. Thus, the economy of the system is better. When tapping hot water from the
domestic water system, the specific amount of water is removed from the pipes, and the return water is
thereby lower. Likewise for the SH; when adjusting the thermostat valves on the radiators to be opened,
the heating is delivered to the specific room, and the return temperature is lower.
The cooling is calculated as an average of the yearly consumption due to the difference in use during a
year. During Winter periods the temperature difference is highest because of the need for SH in most
buildings, and during Summer periods the SH is not necessary and the only need for DH is for DHW
[E.ON Danmark, 2020].
The utilisation of the heating is i.a. dependent on the single plant in the buildings connected to the DH,
as the supply temperature on the primary side of the plant depends on the location of the riser. The
closer to the DH plant the building is placed, the higher supply temperature and thereby a better cooling.
Most DH plants require a cooling temperature, ∆T, at 30◦C for 2022. Though, some facilitation goes
for districts with a low supply temperature. In these areas (e.g. Vesterbro in Copenhagen) the cooling
temperature is reduced to 25◦C [HOFOR, 2022]. If the cooling is lower than 30◦C, the water passes too
fast through the pipes and radiators so the heating is not utilised optimally.
Though, the return temperature may not be too low and thereby create a high cooling temperature. If
it is the case, the system in the building is not regulated correctly. In both situations a fee for return
water will be imposed to ensure a more sustainable heating plant.

4.2.3
Domestic Hot Water
According to the Danish Building Regulation §414 a water installation has to be planed and conducted
so that unnecessary water and energy consumption are avoided [Bolig- og Planstyrelsen, 2018, §414]. In
the following the data regarding water in AP15 is studied.
The building is supplied with water used for and divided between DH for SH and DHW. The DH used
for heating is described in section 4.2.2 and 6.5.2. The remaining part is water for domestic use.
A DH water tank can in general be constructed in two ways: a hot water storage tank with an internal
heat exchanger or with an instantaneous heat exchanger without a storage tank. The system at AP11
& AP15 is based on the first configuration with a DHW system with a hot water storage tank.
The system has a heat exchanger inside, known as a coil, which heats up the supplied water in the tank,
figure 4.10. From the top of the tank the supplied water is distributed to the building. The circulation
loop leads the water back to the top of the tank to ensure that the temperature is above 50◦C. When
hot water is needed it is distributed into the circulation loop and tapped. The tank is simultaneously
filled with cold water from the bottom which gradually is heated to the specific temperature by the heat
coil.
Figure 4.10: DHW configuration with a storage tank with an internal heat exchanger
In a hot water circulation system it is provided that the DHW is ready for use in taps around the building
within 10 seconds. Though, the temperature has to be at least 50◦C to prevent bacteria as Legionella
which in a period of years affected a lot of people by illness [Statens Serum Institut, 2000]. The 50◦C can
deviate in situations with peak demands where e.g 45◦C might be acceptable [Benakopoulos, T. et al., 2021].
To obtain the 50◦C in the pipes the supply water temperature is set to a higher value due to the heat
loss in the circulation loop. The supply temperature on the primary side is often 65◦C and 55◦C on the
secondary side.
The bottom of the tank is cooled with 10◦C water when the supply water leaves the tank. When the
DHW is not being tapped, the tank is charged to supply the circulation heat loss of the system. It results
in a return circulation flow with a temperature at 50◦C. As no more 10◦C cold water is added to the
tank, the cooling of the DH flow is not sufficient and the DH return temperature might be higher than
50◦C.
Often the DHW system with a hot water storage tank including an internal heat exchanger is visualised
as a storage tank connected to two heat exchangers coupled into a series on the primary side. This is

4.2.4
Ventilation
The ventilation system is a VAV, Variable Air Volume, system with a heating and cooling coil as well as
a rotary heat exchanger. The VAV system has the possibility of managing the volume of air in specific
zones. The VAV system is controlled by sensors in rooms or zones, which the system have to regulate.
The system is dependent on e.g. indoor temperature, concentration of CO2 and the relative humidity.
The registration is compared to a pre-setup with set points and is sent to the supply fittings which
regulates the area of opening, so that the ventilation rate reduces or decreases.
The system is programmed that the activation and deactivation times are determined by a time schedule.
When it is activated the valves get opened and the rotary heat exchanger starts. The extraction devices
open and are ready to exhaust deteriorate indoor air. After a while the supply fans start and supply the
indoor climate with fresh air.
The rotary heat exchanger and valve to the heating coil is a 100% open when the external temperature
is below 5◦C. As the external temperature rises, the port on the two devices reduces until the set point
of the supply temperature is reached. This is the regulating factor.
The calculated set point for the supply temperature is read off in the data from the BMS system. The
set point temperature is within the last half year of 2021 between 17◦C and 25.3◦C. This set point is
calculated based on the supply temperature and external temperature, figure 4.15.

7
Commissioning assistant
A commissioning assistant has the purpose of ensuring the optimal operation of the building from the day,
the building is handed over from contractor to owner, cf. AB92 [Boligministeriet, 1992]. Additionally, it
has to guide how the building performs and if it operates as intended as well as ensure that all functional
requirements from the owner are obeyed. The advantage of contracting related to the commissioning
process before initiation of the building project and potential before occupancy, is to avoid that wrong
use of the installations and systems and thereby utilise the opportunities, a the building brings along
and contributes with for the users. By contracting before the initiation of the building or renovation
it ensures that all requirements are obeyed, that the activities related to commissioning are planned in
time and that the logging of observations and potential data starts in accurate time. This note will be
based on the retro commissioning which is defined as commissioning for existing buildings which have
not been through a similar process [Bækmark, E. R., 2018].
The content of this project is a part of the occupancy and operation phase which in general can be
split into two periods: the precocious phase where the installations of the building are tested with load
concurrently with the occupancy of the building, and the late phase where the building has been used
for a period and where the commissioning process continues under leadership of the operating staff
instead of the selected commissioning leader in the previous commissioning phase. As the effective data
collection in this project has started one year after the occupancy, the 10 months - which is the first part
of the occupancy and operation process - have passed, and thereby the long-termed operation has been
implemented [NCC, 2021].
The method used in this part of the process can be described as a data driven functional analysis based
on surveillance of systems. The data collection happens via IoT sensors which log the data - and have
the opportunity to make it available via the internet so that the data can be accessed from all places with
web access. The IoT equipment is also used as a control of the BMS system which controls the technical
systems and installations. By installing IoT sensors it can be verified if the system works as intended
and if the data logging from the BMS system is similar to the measured values from the sensors. By
using this method (data driven functional analysis) a self-regulation of the actual building performance
can be made.
The surveillance of the system can be used by the operational staff to investigate of the technical instal-
lations and if these works as intended. The supervision is visualised in a dashboard which can create an
overview of the important parameters of the use and operation of the building.
IoT sensors are contrary to BMS systems a cheap solution of data collection, which makes the operational
phase easily accessible for the individual who wishes to understand and work with the performance. It
is not only reserved for organisations with a good economy to make data logging but is a possible
common household item which can be used to investigate buildings before designing technical systems
or maintenance of these. It is not only necessary to collect the data - it has to be processed before it
creates a value. The collected data ought to be analysed and presented so that it is possible to react if
correlations are unexplainable or if the data is wrong.
In the following the method in this project, continuous commissioning, is used as a guide to get insight
into data.

In some situations the data can be plain and easy to understand, but some data are measured during

has introduced new possibilities to work away from offices. Therefore, in the future, an adjustment of
the patterns of use of the workplace needs to be made to ensure that the collected data from sensors and
systems actually represents a routine. Based on this, the routine can form the basis of historical data on
which decisions related to changes or optimisations can be made.
The IoT and BMS data have been used in the section of results based on the assumption, that the data
is accurate for the actual situation during the period of logging in the buildings. In some cases, the
data have been adjusted or processed after simple plausible data check to increase the quality of the
data visualised in plots or presented in tables. Both data sets have been in use during the analysis as
they supplement each other where gaps in the respective data sets have had inadequate measurements.
However, it is seen that the data from the IoT sensors and the BMS system varies, even though they
ought to be identical. An example is the supply and return temperatures related to the domestic hot
water (DHW). The supply and return temperatures collected from the IoT sensors are in the interval
between 23◦C and 66◦C where the BMS data have measured temperatures between 51◦C and 59◦C
(figure 4.13 and 4.14) during week 46. When installing sensors and BMS in buildings the systems ought
to be calibrated with the actual conditions. Following the sensors are only re-calibrated if errors occurs
or if the data is analysed and it shows that something might be wrong with specific equipment. It is
therefore an assumption that the equipment works faultlessly unless other is proved so. This process
is not consistent to the commissioning process. The operating staff must reach on error measurements
or variations in the data to ensure the building performance. Thereby it can be assumed, that it is a
fundamental condition of the process that the systems are calibrated correctly and functions as intended.
This does not mean that the systems need to work perfectly, but if the data almost seems correct and does
not have deviations, the operating staff will not necessarily observe these minor errors which can affect
the entire building performance. Fault detection must thereby be the primary task for the maintenance
staff in order to detect and analyse the residual signals and decide how to correct faulting equipment to
keep up the building performance.
In the following the different systems related to the result will be processed and discussed with the insight
of the IoT sensors and BMS system, and in the view of the pandemic who has affected the pattern of
behaviour regarding working environment. Additional possibilities for optimisation will be discussed to
ensure that the divergence in the thermal imbalance can be reduced and thereby be within the acceptable
5-10% deviation.

8.1
Control of space heating
The DH supplying the building on the primary side has during the measured heating period had an
average water temperature of 76.9◦C where the return water temperature is 39.0◦C. This result in a
cooling temperature of 37.9◦C, which is above the limit of 30◦C ( in some districts, 25◦C) and above
the level to which a fee must be paid for cooling the DH return water. The building lives up to this
benchmark, but even though the utilisation of the DH water is within the limits from the DH plant, the
difference between supply and return temperature have been investigated.
The lower the return temperature in the secondary system, the better utilisation of the system though
without compromising with the comfort of personal. This factor is possible to regulate by utilising the
heat from the radiators better. The primary factor is the thermostats, which have to be set correctly
according to the zone and the occupancy.
The access to the temperature control is in many office
buildings limited to be adjusted by the operating staff. At Amerika Plads (AP) the individual employee
have the possibility to adjust a single radiator, if he feels the need. This individual adjustment can be
a reason for bad utilisation of the system, if the valve in the radiators e.g. are full opened meanwhile
windows are ventilating the zone.
If controlling these individual adjustment, by locking the temperature controls so only the technicians
can adjust these an optimisation of the return temperature can be made possible as a more homogeneous
heat will spread across the building. Now, it is possible to let one valve be fully opened and thereby
increase the temperature for this specific radiator simultaneous with other radiators are fully closed.
This irregular distribution of the DH for SH in the building can create unintended and poor utilisation
of the DH and thereby sent too hot water back to the boiler. Based on the results from section 6.2.2 the
return water temperature can be lowered with 1-4◦C compared to the measured return temperature and
thereby ensure a better building performance.
However, by depriving the employees the individual control of regulating the SH, the Predicted Percentage
of Dissatisfied might be affected. The adjusting of the return water temperature still ensures an indoor
climate with a comfortable indoor temperature at 21◦C, but it might be considered that the optimisation
and follower can result in a change in the PPD.
The advantages and disadvantages by adjust the control of the thermostats need to be considered before
making any decisions.
8.2
Circulation loss and domestic hot water
When looking at DHW in buildings the consumption of energy can be divided into four categories: heat
loss from circulation, heat loss from hot water tank and pipes, energy to heat up the domestic water
and energy for the circulation pump. From the calculations in section 6.3 it is seen that the circulation
loss is around 5-6 times higher than the consumption in the respective weeks. Immediately, it seems
like a big ratio between the two values. But as the system has to serve an entire office building at 6
levels each with a ground area at around 2,700 m2, where the occupants needs to have immediate access
to DHW when opening a tap regardless the position in the building, the circulation needs to be steady
and thereby the a huge circulation loss. [Bøhm, B., et. al., 2009] states that a typical circulation loss in
an office building is 50-90% of the energy consumption for DHW. Additionally, there is an example on
grouping of energy consumption where the circulation loss is 4.7 times higher than the energy to heat up
the domestic water. The circulation loss from AP15 is within this span and is assumed to be accurate
for the building. As the building is big and has many taps for DHW both in tea kitchens, toilets and
86
==PAGE:101==

canteen, the piping must similar be big. The boiler in the technical room is placed in the basement
of zone A and is additional placed away form the central of the building. These factors is the primary
reason for the huge circulation loss. But the circulation of DHW is necessary for the use of hot water.
However, the need for immediate access to DHW can be considered unnecessary outside working hours
and in the weekend.
The circulation is for now continuous all hours the entire week. If the circulation was reduced to be
working during working hours (from 4 to 20 Monday to Friday), the operative time will be reduced with
55.4%. This will reduce the circulation loss to 17.5-19.5 [-] per weekday and thereby the circulation loss
will be reduced to 2.2-2.6 times higher than the consumption. The utility company, Hofor, states that
a huge sum per year could be saved in office buildings if the DHW was turned off when the building
was not in use [Licitationen, 2015]. Hofor assumes that around 20% of the heating bill is reserved for
ensuring hot water in the taps.. Head of operations, Bjarne Sigvard Petersen from the Danish Building
and Property Agency have participated in projects where the operation of DHW has being optimised
based on this principle. In an office building at 4,400 m2, 50% of the heat for DHW have been saved per
year equalling 130,000 DKK.
The result from AP15 is still far higher than the optimal situation, where the majority of the energy
was reserved for the heating of water as it e.g. is in many single-family houses [Bøhm, B., et. al., 2009].
The DHW consumption is however different as the piping is shorter and the consumption is higher per
person.
The reason for having the circulation flow operating the entire week is to avoid legionella. Legionella
is a bacteria living in e.g. water pipes in buildings and in bathing water and can by ingestion lead
to critical pneumonia and influenza similar diseases [Styrelsen for patientsikkerhed, 2022]. To avoid the
growth of legionella in pipes the domestic cold water needs to be under 20◦C and the DHW over 50◦C.
When turning of for the circulation pump outside the working hours, the temperature of the water in the
pipes will reduce and become under 50◦C within short time (if the supply temperature is around 50◦C).
Therefore, the circulation is essential in the perspective of public health when using hot water tanks.
Because of the size of the building, the circulation loss will presumably never be lower than the con-
sumption when the system consists of a boiler. The hot water tank is not the most optimal solution
for office buildings, as most of the energy is lost in the distribution and circulation. Instead an electric
water heater could be a solution. An electric water heater is a system where cold water, needed in the
specific situation, will be heated - opposite a hot water tank, where the entire water in the system is
heated constantly. It ensures unlimited DHW. The standby time is not existing in this system, why the
loss is minor. This solution could be beneficial for an office building.

The data regarding occupancy is based on footfall entering and exiting the building. In the presentation
of the data in figure 4.23 and 4.24 it is seen, that the amount of employees in the building within a
day peaks around 10 and 14. This division of occupants corresponds to the general office culture with
a flexibility structure, where the central working hours are between 9 and 15, and where the employees
themselves are responsible for adjusting the surplus working hours around this time interval. Additionally
it is seen, that the biggest attendance occurs around 9, where more than 100 people enter the building,
followed by a huge occupancy at 8 and at 10 in the morning. Similarly, the biggest exiting from the
building happens at 16-17 except Friday, where the biggest part of the occupants exits the building before

The data seems very standardised compared to similar offices and cultures in the Danish working en-
vironment.
This assumption is however based on the fact, that the data before analysis have been
processed and that the method of pre-processing before calculation and visualization have been done.
This includes i.a. the filtering and grouping of data, but especially the selection of data. As mentioned,
some of the observations in the data are misleading in relation to the actual pattern of use. The sensors
have most days observed more footfall entering the building than exiting. An example is the 8 October
2021 at 18, where 92 employees are present in the building. One hour later only one employee has left
the building and the total number of occupants in the building is 91 which is continuous until at 4 the 9
October 2021 where the footfall entering counts resets. In all probability, 91 employees have not stayed
the night, and it might be assumed, that the sensors counting are faulty. The misleading counting could
be caused by the sensor cannot differentiate between persons passing too close next to each other and
eventual double counting. As the sensors are placed beside the reception, occupants waiting for help
might pass the sensor more than once. A way of solving the problem could be to move the sensor right
above the door, so that the sensor only register the amount people actually entering or exiting through
the door.

companies strive to give their employees 10-12 m2 per person. These areas is including printer facilities
but exclude toilets, canteens and tea kitchens [Arbejdstilsynet, 2022]. It is calculated, that in an average
situation each occupant has 712.6 m2 per occupant. Assumed that half of the floor area is reserved
for common facilities, the employees still have 350 m2 per person. This is though based on an average
workday. The maximum number of occupants at the same time is measured to 431, corresponding to
34.02 m2 including the common areas, which assumed without common areas will give a total of 17
m2 per person. This is only in a peak situation but must follow and fulfill the requirements from the
authority. The value is however still higher than the expected 10-12 m2 per person why a benchmark
for similar buildings is too high based on a realistic situation.
It might be taken into account that
the measured period can be affected by a backlog of the Covid-19 situation which affected the entire
population and their working habits. As the building was first operating during the pandemic, with no
supporting data to describe the working routines before the pandemic. It can be fairly assumed that
more people would be present in the building every day if the building had been commissioned before
the pandemic.
8.4
Ventilation, CO2 and electricity
The Danish Building Regulation states in the instructions for §420-§452, that the indoor CO2 concen-
tration might not obey 1000 ppm in schools and similar locations, which an office building might be
categorised under [Bolig- og Planstyrelsen, 2018]. This is not a problem in the office building, because
looking into the data presented above the indoor concentration has a peaking point at 740.6 ppm in zone
BE and in the remaining zones the maximum value is around 630 ppm. The average of the CO2 level
during the measured week is ≈460 ppm in the zones. The low concentration is a clear indication of the
massive ventilation and thereby a very high airflow in the zones, as the air flow rate is based on this
concentration.
Based on the requirements the Danish Building Regulation has presented for other buildings than hous-
ing, the air flow rate might not be lower than 0.35 l/s/m2 during working hours. In AP15 the air flow
rate is twice the demand. In many other cases this would not be a problem but the fact that the CO2
concentration never reaches the maximum 1000 ppm, the building uses a considerable amount of energy
to ventilate and even more than necessary. The ventilation system could with economical advantage go
through a change where the airflow rate was regulated in proportion to the part of the occupants and
the CO2 concentration. By doing so, energy could be saved on ventilation and as a result of a minor
flow stream, the ventilation loss would be reduced.
The statement can be substantiated by the presentation in figure 8.4 which presents the air flow rate
for the zones as a function of the measure indoor CO2 concentration, both data sets presented in the
sections above. The figure represents the concentration of the entire building and the related air flow
rates.

not change significant when more people occupy the building as this parameter is not dependent on

Thermal imbalance The result shows, that there is a thermal imbalance in the building as there is a
difference on the losses and the gains at ≈38%. If the variation was around 5-10% it could have been
assumed that there was a thermal balance based on the same principles as for the thermal comfort which
is evaluated on the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) index. Theoretical it is not possible to reach a higher
satisfaction than 95% (equalling the 5% variation) but in practice 90% satisfaction is a high number
(equalling the 10% variation)[Nielsen, T. R., 2014].
The deviation could have caused divergences in the data, and minor variations in the data as the gains
and losses are found as an average of different periods of the year. As the variation at far higher than
the 5-10% it can be assumed, that there is a unbalance and that there is a heat loss, which can not be
accounted for. The values included in this calculation is the values assumed have the hugest impact on
the balance. Though some assumed minor losses, e.g. the circulation loss in the distribution of domestic
hot water, not-utilised heat losses from hot water pipes and heat exchanger, the heat loss from the boiler,
transmission loss facing the ground.
Thermal balance is a prerequisite for thermal comfort within the building. As the occupants in the
building does not complain and based on a observation of the indoor climate by a visit in the building,
it is assumed, that the building does not have a thermal discomfort which is overshadowed for the
experience of the building.
However, it is observed through the data wrangling and analysing that the data and the measurements
varies a lot during the different weeks from April to December 2021. Solely on this assumption, the
factors within the thermal balance equation will during the measured year vary from week to week.
Based on this assumption and by including all the minor losses mentioned above, the equation could
possible drew closer to equivalence.

CONCLUSION
9
Conclusion
By the time PensionDenmark contracted NCC regarding renovation of the building of Nordisk Fjer, they
agreed upon installing IoT sensors and a BMS system to measure the building performance through data
from technical systems, equipment and sensors. In 2020, a month after the lockdown of Denmark due
to Covid-19, the tenants, LB Forsikring, moved into the building, and a year after, in April 2021, the
actual data collection started and is still ongoing. By means of the program Python studies and analyses
have been made by which the building performance has been investigated. Based on the actual data, it
is sought to find and present opportunities for possible optimisations of systems and physical conditions.
It is through data wrangling shown that there is a huge circulation loss in relation to the domestic hot
water (DHW). Due to the long distance of the risers through the building as well as the purpose of the
use of the building, the return water temperature from the risers is in average 6◦C lower than the supply
water temperature resulting in a return temperature at around 50◦C which is a poor utilisation of the
temperature of the DHW. Even though the part of the DHW is small compared to the total district
heating (DH) flow, a solution to the challenge could be to lead the return water to the space heating
system and thereby reuse the heated water internally.
Additionally, it is proved that the return temperature for space heating in average for all six zones can be
reduced with 1-4◦C. This result is based on calculations of the properties of the radiators in the building.
A way to utilise the hot water better in the risers for space heating (SH) is to deprive the occupants
the opportunity to regulate the thermostats at the radiators themselves. By adjusting the radiators
uniformly in the respective zones, the SH will be distributed better by which the DH and consequently
the SH is utilised as best as possible.
Regarding the ventilation, it is observed that the measured indoor CO2 concentration has an average
of 460 ppm in the entire building.
The measured maximum concentration is 740 ppm.
With this
observation follows a high airflow as the ventilation system uses huge amounts of energy to keep down
the concentration in this extent. As the requirements for indoor concentration is 1000 ppm, it is shown
that the building could economise with the consumption on ventilation - and thereby electricity - and
still ensure good indoor climate within the limit.
The calculation for thermal imbalance showed that there was a difference between the losses and gains
at ≈38%. This deviation seems huge but when investigating the data sets, that varies a lot within the
entire period, this could though be justified. However, this justification is only valid if the entire minor
losses are calculated in the equation. For now the heat losses from hot water pipes, exchanger and boilers
are not included, as well as the circulation loss from DHW and the transmission loss facing the ground.
This might though depend on a new study with this specific focus.
It is clear that the massive data collection creates the opportunity to scrutinise the zones of the building
by the knowledge given by the data, as well as it clarifies potential error measurements or diverging
data. It creates, thus, the opportunity to initiate further investigations of technical installations and
followingly correct errors and settings, if these occur.
By minor critical deviations the collection of
historical data can be used as justification for potential renovations of specific installations or plants,
and thereby unexpected interruptions or shutdowns can be avoided, which at worst can result in a bigger
economical cost. By maintaining of the system continually and ensuring that it works as intended, the
energy consumption of the daily service can be kept down and additionally ensure a good indoor climate
for the occupants. Based on the process, continuous commissioning, a commissioning assistant have been
introduced. It can be used as a guide in similar processes where the quantity of data is huge, and where
the owner wishes to use the collected data for potential improvements of the building.
