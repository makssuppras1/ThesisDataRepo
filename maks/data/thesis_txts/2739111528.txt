Abstract
Solid oxide co­electrolysis of H2O and CO2 offers an attractive method of producing syn­
gas ­ H2 and CO ­ due to its low environmental impact and heat integration possibilities.
However, it is unclear whether carbon monoxide is produced mainly via CO2 electrolysis
or the Reversible Water Gas Shift (RWGS) reaction. In this thesis, a COMSOL Multi­
physics model for steam electrolysis and co­electrolysis is developed and validated using
in­house data from the Technical University of Denmark. It is found that the RWGS re­
action is the main contributor to CO production from ­0.8 through 0.0 A/cm2. A series
of parametric sweeps are performed to determine the effect of the temperature, current
density, inlet molar fractions, and inlet volumetric flow rate on the gas species distribu­
tion, overpotentials, outlet syngas ratio and utilization factor, and the spontaneity of the
Boudouard reaction. It was found that the concentration of species is greatly impacted by
the current density and is largely temperature­independent in the validation range. Higher
temperatures are unfavorable to carbon deposition and diminish the overpotentials. In­
creasing the H2O/CO2 ratio at the inlet promotes the Boudouard reaction and decreases
the overpotentials. Lastly, limiting the volumetric flow rate at the cathode and anode in­
lets increases the affinity for carbon deposition and results in a higher utilization factor
and lower syngas ratio for the same current density.

Introduction
The European Union’s Green Deal entails the establishment of a climate­neutral and
resource­efficient EU­wide economy by 2050 [1]. Combined with heightened solar and
wind power production, electrification provides an effective pathway to decarbonization
for numerous industrial sectors. Certain fundamental sectors, such as air and marine
transport, are more technically and economically challenging to electrify. Additionally,
the variable nature of wind and solar power production necessitates the development of
scalable energy storage options. To that end, the amended EU Renewable Energy Direc­
tive (EU/2023/2413) provides a legislative framework for the promotion of low­carbon or
carbon­neutral energy carriers, known as green fuels [2]. These are intended as effective
and efficient substitutes for fossil fuels. At large scales, they can contribute toward the
flexibility of the energy system by converting excessive solar and wind energy to chemical
carriers.
Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cells (SOEC) are an attractive option for green fuel production,
largely due to their compatibility with downstream processes that enable heat integration.
This results in increased system efficiency and decreased levelized product cost (e.g., hy­
drogen). Additionally, SOECs enable high­temperature steam electrolysis toward hydro­
gen and necessitate a lower operating voltage and lower overall electricity consumption
when compared the their lower­temperature alternatives, such as alkaline electrolyzers
[3]. These benefits have driven industrial, academic, and legislative interest in scaling up
SOEC technology for hydrogen production.
For the production of green fuels, a mixture of carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H2),
known as syngas, is required as feedstock. The required CO can be produced via the Re­
versible Water Gas Shift reaction (RWGS) or by direct electrochemical reduction of carbon
dioxide (CO2). SOECs enable the production of CO and H2 in the same cell, as they can
operate in conditions conducive to steam electrolysis, carbon dioxide electrolysis, and the
RWGS reaction [4]. This process is called co­electrolysis and is thermodynamically de­
sirable considering the heat integration opportunities from excess downstream heat e.g.,
from methanol or jet fuel synthesis.

==PAGE:14==
While co­electrolysis provides a valuable pathway for syngas production and (CO2) val­
orization, the inner workings of the process have not been fully understood. In particular,
the extent of each reaction and their distinct contribution to the yielded products remains
relatively obscure. On one hand, the CO2 and H2 reduction reactions compete for the
same active catalyst sites. On the other, CO2 electrolysis and the RWGS reaction com­
pete for the consumption of carbon dioxide and the production of carbon monoxide, with
both reactions consisting of fairly complex mechanisms. Some experimental and com­
putational studies have been conducted in an attempt to develop an understanding of
co­electrolysis; however, a definitive consensus on the relative contributions of CO2 elec­
trolysis and the RWGS reaction has not been reached. This is discussed in detail in
section 1.2.
1.1
Co­electrolysis of steam and carbon dioxide
1.1.1
Chemical & electrochemical reactions
Co­electrolysis encompasses the concurrent electrochemical reduction of steam and car­
bon dioxide and the production of carbon monoxide via the RWGS reaction. A gas mixture
with a variable composition of steam, hydrogen, carbon dioxide, and carbon monoxide is
introduced to the cathode. Oxygen and nitrogen are introduced to the anode, typically at
the ratio of atmospheric air (21:79). A rudimentary schematic of the process is shown in
Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: 2­D schematic of the co­electrolysis process [4].

Across the gas channel, and given the appropriate temperature and catalyst, CO2 and H2
react toward CO and H2O, known as the Reversible Water Gas Shift (RWGS) reaction:
CO + H2O ↔CO2 + H2
(1.1)
The RWGS reaction does not involve the transfer of electrons and therefore does not re­
quire an applied current. It is, however, thermally activated and can occur at a significantly
higher pace at high temperatures i.e., over 1000 K [3][4][5].
The electrochemical reduction of CO2 and H2O happens at the cathode­electrolyte inter­
face, also called the Triple Phase Boundary (TPB) where the gas mixture, electrode, and
electrolyte meet. The following half­reactions take place, releasing oxygen ions into the
electrolyte:
H2O + 2e­ →H2 + O2­
(1.2)
CO2 + 2e­ →CO + O2­
(1.3)
The oxygen ions traverse the electrolyte and recombine into molecular oxygen at the TPB
around the anode­electrolyte interface:
2O2­ →O2 + 2e­
(1.4)
The electrons released in Equation 1.4 are funneled toward the cathode via an external
circuit. The complete reduction equations are as shown in Equation 1.5 and Equation 1.6
[3][4].

hydrogen and carbon monoxide toward methane formation (Equation 1.7). Prior modeling
studies indicate that the DIR reaction is not favored in co­electrolyzer cells [3][4]. Addi­
tionally, the experimental data used to validate the model in this thesis does not report
measurable methane production [6]. Therefore, methane production is not discussed fur­

2C(g) ↔CO2(g) + C(s)

to reliably control the outlet H2/CO ratio, as the target syngas composition varies depend­
ing on the downstream application. For instance, methanation requires a syngas ratio
of approximately 1, while the Fischer­Tropsch reaction requires values around 1.9­2.0
[12][13].
1.1.2
Cell voltage and overpotentials
The theoretical voltage necessary for the electrochemical reactions to take place is called
the Nernst voltage or Open Circuit Voltage (EOCV ). The Nernst equation predicts the
potential difference at thermodynamic equilibrium and depends on the temperature, pres­
sure, and gas composition at the TPB. Generally, the OCV decreases with increasing
temperature [14].
Co­electrolysis cells fail to operate at the Nernst voltage due to a series of losses. These
translate to a higher voltage requirement than predicted by the Nernst equation. The ex­
cess is called an overpotential or overvoltage, and typically consists of activation, concen­
tration, and Ohmic overpotentials. The total voltage can be described with the following

The activation overpotentials, ηact, arise from the energy barrier for electrochemical reac­
tions at the electrode­electrolyte interfaces. They are influenced by electrode properties
and reaction mechanisms, reflecting the kinetic limitations of the anodic and cathodic half­
reactions respectively. The concentration overpotentials, ηcon, reflect the concentration
gradients resulting from the diffusion of gas species. Finally, the Ohmic overpotential,
ηOhm, accounts for energy losses caused by Ohmic heating. It is proportional to element
thickness and inversely proportional to element conductivity; to wit, the thicker and less
conductive a cell component is, the more prominent the Ohmic contribution to the over­
voltage becomes [16][17][18].
1.1.3
Typical cell geometries and materials
Solid oxide cells are typically produced in a planar or tubular configuration. Tubular cells
have concave gas channels, resulting in different species and temperature profiles com­
pared to planar cells. Depending on the structure of the cell, a mechanical support layer
is added to either electrode. Figure 1.2 shows a cross­section of a cathode supported
cell. The anode and cathode are enclosed by metallic meshes. These layers connect the
electrodes to the external circuit or the rest of the SOEC stack, depending on whether the
connection is parallel or in series [19].

The electrolyte must demonstrate high ionic conductivity and low electrical conductivity to
promote oxygen ion diffusion and prevent electronic leakage. Therefore, the electrolyte
layer typically consists of Yttria­stablized Zirconia (YSZ) or Yttria­doped Ceria (YDC). The

support usually consist of a Ni­YSZ alloy. The anode is typically a solid solution consist­

(LSCF) or La0.8Sr0.2MnO3−x (LSM). If the anode and electrolyte functional materials are
incompatible in terms of mass or heat transport, a barrier layer can be interjected to avoid
long­term cell degradation [19][20][21].
1.2
Literature review
There is a wealth of publications on the computational modeling of co­electrolysis cells.
The key points of contention between works are primarily the modeling of chemical re­
actions and, to a lesser extent, the mass transport mechanism. Co­electrolysis models
can be 0­, 1­, or 2­dimensional depending on the research question. 0­D models are
geared toward balance­of­plant and thermodynamic analyses and thus forego the intrica­
cies of transport phenomena. Conversely, 1­D and 2­D models often provide innovative
couplings between the chemical and CFD domains.
Typically, modeling efforts focus on H2O electrolysis, CO2 electrolysis, and the RWGS
reaction. This excludes studies focusing on the functional materials and operating condi­
tions that enable direct methanation and discourage the Boudouard and nickel re­oxidation
reactions.
Most 0­D models leverage simple mass and energy balance equations to determine the
outlet composition and power consumption [22][23][24][25]. [26] treat the pre­TPB do­
main, where only the RWGS reaction occurs, as separate from the TPB. They initialize
the gas mixture composition at the entrance of the TPB by resolving elemental balance
equations for every species participating in the RWGS reaction. They then update these
balance equations to account for H2O and CO2 electrolysis at the TPB.
In 1­D and 2­D models, a parallel circuit approach is often used to describe the interplay
between the electrolysis reactions [27]. At the cathode­electrolyte interface, steam and
carbon dioxide compete for the active sites of the electrocatalyst. In electrical terms, this
means that a different portion of the applied current is dedicated to split H2O and CO2.

Further uncertainty surrounds the relative contribution of the CO2 electrolysis and RWGS
reactions to CO production. [3][4] developed a 1­D model to gauge the relevance of
the RWGS reaction and concluded that it contributes significantly to CO production at
relatively lower temperatures and applied voltages. The RWGS reaction accounts for
roughly 25% of CO production at 873 K, but consumes CO at 1073 K. They also found
that it does not reach equilibrium under the spectrum of conditions they simulated.
Conversely, [35] found that the RWGS reaction reaches equilibrium. They combined a
42­step heterogeneous catalysis model, a Dusty­Gas diffusion model, and a plug flow

RWGS reaction, as well as carbon deposition, steam reforming, methanation, dry re­
forming, and C1 species oxidation. By executing their simulation with the CO2 reduction
pathway turned on and off, they concluded that both CO­producing reactions contribute
to species distribution along the gas channel, albeit not along the depth of the electrodes.
The discourse over the relevance of the RWGS reaction extends to experimental stud­
ies. In experiments conducted by [36][37], the Area Specific Resistance (ASR) for co­
electrolysis was lower than for CO2 electrolysis, indicating that the latter does not occur.
[38] argues that this is due to the difference in conversion resistance between H2O and

1.3
Purpose
As shown in section 1.2, opinions on the significance of the RWGS reaction vary. This
thesis project purports to develop and validate a multi­physics model of a co­electrolysis
cell by leveraging previous experimental and computational efforts made at DTU Energy.
The model simulates the heat, mass, and momentum transport phenomena inherent to
the process and their coupling with the current distribution along the cell. After the model
is validated on in­house datasets for both steam electrolysis and co­electrolysis, the rele­
vance of each reaction is explored by simulating co­electrolysis under different operating
conditions.
Furthermore, co­electrolysis cells are prone to carbon deposition due to the significant
concentration of carbon monoxide. Pending thermodynamically favorable conditions, this
can trigger the Boudouard reaction and negatively influence the lifespan of the cell. A
parametric study is conducted to determine which combinations of operating conditions
make the Boudouard reaction spontaneous i.e., reduce its Gibbs energy to below zero.

2
Mathematical Modeling
This chapter details the mathematical model used to simulate a solid oxide co­electrolysis
cell. The modeling choices made hereafter comprise an extension of the model devised
for solid oxide steam electrolysis cells by researchers at the Department of Energy Con­
version and Storage at the Technical University of Denmark [39]. They are additionally
informed by the scientific literature on the subject of co­electrolysis.
2.1
Modeling scope
In this thesis, co­electrolysis is modeled on the macroscale cell level. The continuum
approximation is utilized, such that the porous materials and filling medium are assumed
to be homogeneous. This approach models cell performance sufficiently well under a wide
range of operating conditions while mitigating the computational complexity of resolving
a multi­physics problem [40].
2­dimensional symmetry is assumed along the cell depth. While 1­dimensional models
offer an acceptable initial estimate for the polarization curve at a relatively low compu­
tational cost, studies on steam electrolysis show that simplifying the cell geometry can
result in poorly defined heat flows, mass flows, and overpotentials [41][42]. Conversely, a
3­dimensional model best captures the distribution of species and heat across the cell ge­
ometry but significantly increases the degrees of freedom and complicates data analysis
and visualization. Simulating co­electrolysis in 2­D results in shorter computational times
while maintaining a sufficiently good representation of flow in the porous channel e.g., by
modeling velocity as a field rather than a vector [43]. This choice is in keeping with the
assumptions made in [39], which serves as the basis for the developed COMSOL model.
Furthermore, it is consistent with the majority of works cited in this thesis.
The modeling domain is a 2­D cross­section of a cathode­supported, planar cell. The cell
consists of layered sub­domains as shown in Figure 2.1; from the bottom up, a nickel
mesh, a mechanical support layer, the cathode, the electrolyte, a barrier layer, the anode,
and a gold mesh. The geometric and material parameters mirror the electrochemical cells
used in [39] and [6] and are summarized in Table 2.1­Table 2.5. The equations referenced

The process is modeled in the steady state i.e., the heat, current, and mass flow inputs

Lastly, CO2 electrolysis is consciously excluded so that the relevance of the reaction could
be ascertained compared to the RWGS reaction. The goal is to gain a better understand­

noble metals such as Au and Ag and Zn­based catalysts e.g., multilayer Zn nanosheets
with high edge site density or Zn electrodes enhanced with chloride ions. In the absence of
a highly selective electrocatalyst, most of the traversing electrons are dedicated to hydro­
gen evolution. At higher current densities, where there is higher availability of electrons,
CO2 reduction becomes possible [55][56].
Given the results in this chapter, the model developed during this thesis can confidently
approximate single co­electrolysis and steam electrolysis cells in the 927­1077K temper­

which bears significant implications for the relevance of the reaction at lower current den­

inputs rather than enabling and disabling the relevant physics nodes in COMSOL. While
there is no experimental data for lower temperatures at high current densities, it is safe
to assume that the model is valid up to at least ­0.8 A/cm2 between 927­1077 K. That is
because lowering the thermal input while maintaining the applied current is disadvanta­

RWGS reaction occur remains valid under 1077 K.
While the nonoccurence of CO2 electrolysis contradicts many of the studies summarized
in section 1.2, it also points to a larger discrepancy among experimental co­electrolysis
data. Such discrepancies can be attributed to the varying state of electrodes in different
studies. The migration of impurities from the cermet bulk to interfaces (e.g., the TPB),
grain boundaries, and external surfaces over time can greatly affect the reaction mecha­
nism [38][57][58]. It is plausible that the overall reaction mechanism in the cells used by
[6] does not favor the CO2 reduction pathway at lower current densities.
Whether this model is accurate for variable fuel compositions is up for debate. Prior work
has shown that the interplay between steam electrolysis, carbon dioxide electrolysis, and
the RWGS reaction is contingent upon the inlet composition [3][4][59]. In fact, the cell
parameters used in these studies make it so that CO2 reduction occurs irrespective of
the operating conditions. For the most part, no CO2 reduction is observed within the siz­
able range of temperatures and current densities tested by [6]. Finally, electrochemical
impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements by [38] highlight a higher polarization resis­
tance ­ and, concomitantly, a lower reaction rate ­ associated with CO2 electrolysis. The
authors point out that these measurements were not sensitive to the inlet concentration
of carbon monoxide and dioxide. Based on the information outlined in this paragraph, the
model developed for this thesis can conceivably be used for variable inlet compositions.
However, due to the stark deviations between co­electrolysis cells, the cell parameters
should be adopted from [39] or similar.

4.1
Introduction
In this chapter, the COMSOL model developed for this thesis is used to conduct different
parametric studies on co­electrolysis. The studies are conducted within the acceptable
validation range i.e., [927,1077] K and [­0.8,0.0] A/cm2, in 50 K and 0.1 A/cm2 intervals
respectively. The current density step is narrowed to 0.01 A/cm2 in the [­0.8­0.5] interval
to more accurately pinpoint the Boudouard threshold. Subsequently, the effects of inlet
species concentration are studied by altering the H2O/CO2 molar ratio. The validity of the
model at altered inlet compositions has been argued for in section 3.2. The relative inlet
H2 and CO fractions are not modified to circumvent the implications of separating and
recycling the products. As H2 also functions as a safe gas against nickel re­oxidation,
lowering its inlet concentration could lead to mass transport phenomena that are not ac­
counted for in this model [9][10]. An additional parametric sweep is performed based on
the inlet volumetric flow rate.
These parametric sweeps aim to investigate the effect of the operating and inlet conditions
on the species distribution along the cell, the outlet syngas ratio, the utilization factor, the
overpotentials, and the spontaneity of the Boudouard reaction. The species distribution
sheds light on the phenomena occurring within the different layers of the cell, as well
as the influence of the parameters on achieving a desirable outlet syngas composition.
The utilization factor and syngas ratio measure the extent to of H2O and CO2 conversion.
The overpotentials and the Boudouard reaction are indicators of carbon deposition and
long­term cell health.
Within the validation range, the furnace temperature does not have an impact on the
species distribution and, by extension, does not significantly affect the fuel utilization fac­
tor and the Boudouard reaction. This will be expanded upon in the following sections.
Consequently, only selected temperature sweep results will be displayed and commented
on in section 4.2.

4.2
The effect of temperature
It was found that the effect of temperature on species distribution is minimal. While the
steam electrolysis and RWGS reactions are both thermally activated, the simulated tem­
perature range is not large. The uniformity in species distribution is likely due to the rate
of the RWGS reaction being two orders of magnitude larger than the rate of H2O electrol­
ysis, as shown in Figure 4.1. Steam electrolysis can be thus considered rate limiting: the
ability of the RWGS reaction to produce CO is contingent upon H2 production, which in
turn depends more strongly on current density than temperature based on Faraday’s law
of electrolysis.

atures. Left: 927 K. Right: 1077 K.

skewed toward CO production throughout the cell and the entire 927­1077 K range, which

Conclusions & Outlook
In this thesis, a multi­physics model for steam electrolysis and co­electrolysis was devel­
oped and validated using experimental data from DTU Energy. The model successfully
simulates co­electrolysis with only the H2O reduction and RWGS reaction pathways en­
abled in the [927,1077] K and [­0.8,0.0] A/cm2 ranges. The disagreement with other
modeling works regarding the relevance of CO2 electrolysis in CO production can be at­
tributed to the unique reaction mechanisms of different SOE cells, caused by the migration
of impurities along the TPB.
It has been found that molar fractions at cell TPB, bulk, and outlet are not significantly
impacted by temperature in the validation range due to the RWGS reaction’s high rate
and tendency to restore equilibrium. The species distribution depends strongly on the
current density, and the amount of CO produced relative to H2 increases with the ap­
plied current. Likewise, the amount of H2O and CO2 molecules converted into syngas
also increases. The activation and concentration overpotentials make up for the largest
share of the overvoltage and decrease significantly at higher temperatures, suggesting
lower activation barriers and unencumbered diffusion. The affinity for and extent of the
Boudouard reaction are also proportional to the current density and inversely proportional
to the temperature. This implies that higher temperatures promote long­term cell health
without sacrificing Faradaic efficiency.
Increasing the H2O/CO2 inlet ratio does not visibly affect the syngas ratio or utilization
factor. This is likely due to the RWGS reaction being at equilibrium and the high volumetric
flow rate, which antagonizes diffusive transport. The activation overpotential decreases
slightly, owing to a higher chance of H2O molecules occupying an electrocatalyst site.
This also leads to a lower threshold for and bigger extent of the Boudouard reaction. It
might therefore be beneficial to keep the steam/carbon dioxide ratio below 1.
The cell’s ability to process steam and carbon dioxide improves drastically by limiting the
inlet volumetric flow rate. The outlet utilization factor increases, while the syngas ratio
decreases, indicating a more widespread consumption of CO2. At the base­case tem­
38

==PAGE:50==
perature (1027 K), this comes at the cost of higher affinity for carbon deposition. Seeing
as carbon deposition has been shown to be reversible when occurring to a lesser ex­
tent [43], it might be necessary to identify the golden balance between temperature, inlet
composition, and volumetric flow rate to achieve high yield while circumventing long­term
degradation from deposited carbon.
Future work would benefit from adding the carbon dioxide electrolysis reaction to the ex­
isting model. This would allow for the expansion of the validation range, as well as allow
for the model to be fitted to different functional materials and cell degradation states. Such
fits could encompass the anodic and cathodic transfer coefficients (aa, ac) and the CO2
exchange current density (iCO2,0). A larger, collaborative modeling study involving vari­
ous cells could provide a more complex understanding of these parameters as a function
of the cell materials and degradation indicators e.g., the Area Specific Resistance. The
incorporation of time­dependent studies on degradation and relevant experimental data
could be used to validate the Boudouard thresholds calculated in this work, as carbon de­
position has been shown to occur under thermodynamically unexpected conditions [45].
The Boudouard reaction as well as failures borne from thermomechanical stresses could
be studied further by transferring this model to the stack level. This would necessitate the
development of a Single Repeating Unit (SRU) abstraction for the reduction and RWGS
reactions, achieved through either traditional numerical means or machine learning (e.g.,
the surrogate model option available in COMSOL from version 6.2 onward). Stack­level
simulations could enable further R&D and scale­up efforts, which are particularly rele­
vant for achieving MW­level technology readiness for co­electrolysis and sparking interest
among industrial circles.

Solid oxide co­electrolysis of H2O and CO2 offers an attractive method of producing syngas ­ H2
and CO ­ due to its low environmental impact and heat integration possibilities. However, it is un­
clear whether carbon monoxide is produced mainly via CO2 electrolysis or the Reversible Water
Gas Shift (RWGS) reaction. In this thesis, a COMSOL Multiphysics model for steam electrolysis and
co­electrolysis is developed and validated using in­house data from the Technical University of Den­
mark. It is found that the RWGS reaction is the main contributor to CO production from ­0.8 through
0.0 A/cm2. A series of parametric sweeps are performed to determine the effect of the temperature,
current density, inlet molar fractions, and inlet volumetric flow rate on the gas species distribution,
overpotentials, outlet syngas ratio and utilization factor, and the spontaneity of the Boudouard reac­
tion. It was found that the concentration of species is greatly impacted by the current density and
is largely temperature­independent in the validation range. Higher temperatures are unfavorable to
carbon deposition and diminish the overpotentials. Increasing the H2O/CO2 ratio at the inlet pro­
motes the Boudouard reaction and decreases the overpotentials. Lastly, limiting the volumetric flow
rate at the cathode and anode inlets increases the affinity for carbon deposition and results in a
higher utilization factor and lower syngas ratio for the same current density.
