Abstract
Face recognition systems are a popular choice for biometric authentication. However,
they are known to be vulnerable to presentation and morphing attacks. To mitigate security
risks of these attacks, face impersonation detectors for detecting presentation or morphing
attacks are deployed in conjunction with face recognition systems. However, since these
detectors are based on deep neural networks they are prone to another class of attacks
known as adversarial attacks. The vulnerability of face impersonation detectors against
adversarial attacks is not well┬¡researched. This thesis aimed to assess this vulnerability
through a variety of black┬¡box adversarial attacks. The thesis also investigated the impact
of the impersonation attacks on multiple face recognition systems before and after the
adversarial attacks were applied to fool the impersonation detectors. A secondary aim
was to evaluate defence mechanisms to mitigate the risk of adversarial attacks.
It has been shown through experimental results that both presentation attack detectors
and morphing attack detectors are vulnerable to query┬¡based black┬¡box attacks. In this
regard, morphing attack detectors have been shown to be more vulnerable to adversarial
attacks than the tested presentation attack detectors. The vulnerability of face recognition
systems against attacks has also been shown. These vulnerabilities have been shown
through analysis of various standardised metrics such as detection equal error rates and
impostor attack presentation accept rates. Following this, it has been shown that both
detector types can be defended against the attacks with varying success rates. To this
end, defensive distillation was used on the presentation attack detectors while adversarial
training was used on the morphing attack detectors. The former in this case led to a
greater increase in model robustness.

Introduction
A biometric system is a system that carries out biometric recognition of individuals based
on their behavioural and biological characteristics [1]. Biometrics concerns itself firstly
with the security domain in the form of access control. Access control may be defined as
selective restriction to a place or a resource which may be physical or digital. This can
for example be, access to a smartphone, or border control at immigration checkpoints.
Biometrics is also used in the forensics domain for law enforcement. Camera surveillance
in shops, identifying suspects involved in a crime and maintaining a criminal database are
just a few examples.
For access control there exist many forms with which authentication can be achieved
[2]. The first category is something an individual knows, e.g.: a password or PIN. The
main drawbacks here are that passwords and PINs are hard to remember especially if
they are numerous which is often the case. Furthermore, there exist many techniques to
hack passwords especially if they are weak. The most common are brute┬¡force attacks,
dictionary attacks, and rainbow attacks [3]. Brute┬¡force attacks involve trying a variety of
possible passwords through permutations of characters in a sequence. Dictionary attacks
are similar but they work on the assumption that passwords are chosen using certain
patterns. Likely passwords are collected in dictionaries and an attack is launched by trying
out every entry in the dictionary. Stored passwords are also augmented with numbers,
letters or characters. Lastly, rainbow attacks involve compiling a list of passwords and
their corresponding hashed values. Once a data breach occurs, the hashes obtained are
compared to the stored ones. If they match the plain┬¡text password can be retrieved. The
second category is something an individual owns, e.g.: an access card, or a key. Here,
there is a risk of losing the token or having it stolen. Some individuals require several
tokens granting access to several resources. And although there is nothing to remember
it becomes cumbersome to safeguard and manage this list of ever┬¡growing tokens.
The last category is something an individual is, e.g.: body characteristics. This is es┬¡
sentially biometrics and comprises everything from facial features, to fingerprints and iris
patterns of an individual. The advantages of this form of authentication are obvious. With
the previous two categories there were risks of loosing or forwarding the token or forget┬¡
ting the password. With biometrics this is more challenging. It follows that it is also a lot
more convenient as a result.
When compared to PINs, biometrics are superior as biometric characteristics contain
more entropy than a corresponding 4 or 6┬¡digit PIN. For reference, a 6┬¡digit PIN has less
than 20bit entropy, whereas fingerprints, and iris have 84bit and 249 bit respectively [4],
[5]. Face and voice have 56bit and 127bit respectively [6], [7]. This higher entropy makes
biometrics less vulnerable to hacks than short PINs and objectively offer more security.
Finally, biometric authentication offers non┬¡repudiation of transactions which is not offered
in case of token based access, e.g. with PINs and passwords. This is so because au┬¡
thentication is tied directly to the userÔÇÖs unique biological characteristic. This characteristic
cannot be lent or stolen in the traditional sense.
Face recognition is one of the most popular form of biometric recognition. Ease of cap┬¡
turing the face with inexpensive hardware and the non┬¡intrusive nature of data capture
makes face recognition the preferred mode in many scenarios. The market size for face
recognition was valued at USD 5.15 billion in 2022 with an expected compound annual

growth rate of 14.9% from 2023 to 2030 [8]. Face recognition systems are also being
increasingly used for access control and for security & surveillance. In fact, in 2020 84%
of U.S. Customs and Border Protection airport biometrics were from facial recognition
systems [9]. Furthermore, face recognition models have surpassed humans in terms of
accuracy on certain benchmarks (e.g. ÔÇ£GaussianFaceÔÇØ model on LFW dataset) [10].
Traditionally, face recognition was done using hand┬¡crafted feature extractors and later
using machine learning techniques. However, they have since been replaced with deep
learning techniques based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) [11] which achieve
both high accuracy and good generalizability [12]. DeepFace released in 2014, was the
first breakthrough in deep learning based face recognition and has since been superseded
by state┬¡of┬¡the art models such as ArcFace and more recently FaceNet, [13], [14]. Deep
neural networks have propelled the use of face recognition systems and led to signifi┬¡
cant performance improvements over time. High recognition accuracy achieved by these
models has meant a sustained interest in the use of this technology moving forward.
However, the inherent complexity of the black┬¡box nature of deep neural networks makes
them prone to attacks. This not only has the effect of undermining the security and trust┬¡
worthiness of the systems but also the security of the areas and domains in which they
are deployed (border control, airports etc.).
Specifically, these models are vulnerable to presentation attacks (PA) and morphing at┬¡
tacks (MA) [15] (explained in chapter 2 below).
In this thesis, we focus on a categorization of PAs and MAs known as impostor attacks
(explained in more detail in chapter 2 below) where an adversary attempts to gain unau┬¡
thorized access by impersonating another individual.
To defend against these attacks, deep learning based presentation attack detectors (PAD)
and morphing attacks detectors (MAD) are employed. We use a general term: face im┬¡
personation attack detectors to refer to both of these types. A typical integration of the
detectors and face recognition systems is where these detectors filter the input. They
catch any image samples that may be impersonation attacks and pass the rest onto the
face recognition system.
However, since the detectors are also deep learning based they are vulnerable to a gen┬¡
eral set of attacks known as adversarial attacks [16] [15].
Adversarial attacks involve making small pixel┬¡level changes to an input image with the
aim of causing a misclassification in a detection model. The changes would be made to
a PA or MA image. The aim is then to cause the detectors to classify the image as a
genuine (bona fide) one and thus fool not only the detectors but also the face recognition
system by resulting in a successful biometric match. If the impersonation detectors (PAD
and MAD) are easily fooled with these attacks, they give a false sense of security. It is
therefore imperative to defend impersonation detectors against adversarial attacks.
Face recognition systems have been shown to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks. In
[17] the authors provide a taxonomy of various adversarial attacks against face recog┬¡
nition systems and possible defence mechanisms. However, the vulnerability of face
impersonation attack detectors against adversarial attacks is not well┬¡researched.
In this thesis the aim is thus to assess the vulnerability of existing face impersonation
attack detectors against adversarial attacks. Following that, a secondary aim is to explore
and implement defence mechanisms to defend against the attacks.

Background
In this chapter we provide an overview of key concepts related to the thesis including
biometric systems, face recognition systems, impersonation attacks, impersonation attack
detectors, adversarial attacks and defence mechanisms.
2.1
Biometric Systems
A biometric system consists of a combination of components that work together to achieve
the goal of recognition of individuals. We now briefly describe the components and work┬¡
flow of a biometric system. The components are as follows: Data Capture Subsystem,
Data Storage Subsystem, Signal Processing Subsystem, Comparison Subsystem, Deci┬¡
sion Subsystem. The workflow and interconnection with the components are shown in
fig. 2.1 [18]. We now give a brief description:
1. Enrolment: The biometric characteristic of the data subject is presented before the
Data Capture Subsystem and a digital representation of it is recorded and trans┬¡
mitted to the Signal Processing Subsystem. Here a variety of things may occur
depending on the mode of capture but in general features are extracted from the
image to create a biometric reference which is then stored in the Data Storage Sub┬¡
system. The biometric reference represents the ground┬¡truth and is used during
both two forms of biometric recognition (verification and identification).
2. Verification: In verification, the user presents their biometric data to the Data Capture
Subsystem along with his/her claimed identity. The data is passed onto the Signal
Processing Subsystem where again features are extracted known as the biometric
probe. At the same time, the stored biometric reference is retrieved from the Data
Storage Subsystem based on the provided claimed identity and both the features
from the reference and probe are sent to the Comparison Subsystem. Here, the
features are compared against each other and a comparison score is sent onward
to the Decision Subsystem. Finally, based on a predetermined threshold it is decided
whether the user really is who he/she claims to be. For similarity scores it is checked
whether the score is greater than the decision threshold.
3. Identification: In identification, the user presents their biometric data to the Data
Capture Subsystem. No identity is provided in this case. The procedure works in
much the same way as above except that all references from the Data Storage Sub┬¡
system are retrieved and one by one are compared to the biometric probe. A series
of comparison scores are then obtained and again based on a specific decision
threshold a decision is made as to the identity of the user.
In all of the above three procedures, impersonation attack detectors may also be em┬¡
ployed that reject samples if they are classified as an attack thereby stopping the current
procedure.

Face Recognition Systems
State of the art face recognition systems are based on convolutional neural networks
(CNNs) [19]. Pre┬¡trained weights for a majority of the most popular ones are available
online and these models can therefore be used in a straightforward manner. The following
three steps are commonly involved in face recognition [20]:

1. Face detection: This refers to the detection of faces amongst other objects in an
image. Variations in lighting and illumination can present challenges in detection.
There exist many techniques to accomplish this. Traditional methods are based
on handcrafted features and machine learning methods. These include techniques
such as local binary pattern (LBP), ViolaÔÇôJones detector and principle component
analysis (PCA) [20]. However, recently deep learning based approaches are more
common. Multi┬¡Task Cascaded Convolutional Neural Networks (MTCNN) [21] and
RetinaFace [22] are popular choices in this category.
2. Feature extraction: This is the next step and it involves encoding the features of a
face in a feature vector. The vector comprises all the information for a distinct face
and the features encoded are discriminative so that the similarity of two faces can
be quantified by comparing their corresponding vectors. Again, traditional methods
like Eigenface [23], linear discriminant analysis (LDA) [24] and local binary pattern
(LBP) [25] are a combination of handcrafted feature based and machine learning
approaches. More recently we have seen the rise of deep neural network based
techniques such as ArcFace [26], MagFace [27] and AdaFace [28].
3. Face recognition: This final step has two modes of operation depending on the use
case: 1) Verification and 2) Identification. In verification, a probe image is matched
against a specific reference image. The goal here is to affirm that the individual is
who he/she claims to be. The two feature vectors obtained from the previous step
are compared and a similarity score is obtained (typically using cosine similarity). If
the score is above a specified predetermined threshold the verification passes. In
identification the entire database of reference images is scanned and an attempt is
made to identify the individual by matching the probe to a stored reference image. If
a match is found the probe is identified as a particular identity in the stored database.
Here again, the feature vectors of each of the reference images is compared to
the vector for the probe and the similarity scores are stored. The highest score is
retrieved and compared to the threshold like before.

wearing physical masks, printing out a photo of an individual with the objective of fool┬¡
ing the face recognition system. A digital attack involves making alterations directly to
the digital representation of images. This could be via the use of editing software, post
processing programs, or programming scripts.
We now provide a brief overview of each of these.

this thesis, we focus exclusively on deep learning based software detection techniques.
Nevertheless we provide a brief categorisation of all techniques for completeness. They
are as follows [40]:
Hardware Based
There are further three categorisations of this:
ÔÇó Sensor Characteristics: This includes using specialised sensors to aid in detecting

spoofing attempts. An example here is using a light┬¡field camera that captures in┬¡

This depth information can then be used to discriminate between a live image and a

such as looking at a specific spot and following the gaze of the user. Obviously, the
response would be successful if a real user is involved whereas for a replay┬¡attack

Software Based
The challenge with hardware based techniques is computational complexity and cost as┬¡
sociated with the specialised hardware. Software based techniques are more readily
available and also easier to deploy. Moreover they have achieved high accuracy in recent
years. There exist many different categorisations of software based techniques because
they are so numerous. Here we provide a brief overview [15]:
ÔÇó Hand┬¡crafted methods: Local binary pattern (LBP) is a popular technique in this
subcategory. LBP captures textural changes in the spoofed sample that results
from the presentation mode, e.g. a print attack using a laser printer, display attack
using an iPad etc. Pigment print defects in laser printed photos are observed quite
clearly after a LBP operation on the printed photo [40]. Similarly, in a display attack,
unwanted frequencies emitted by the display and display reflections are highlighted

Morphing Attack Detection Techniques
There exist two major categorizations of morphing attack detection. They are as follows:
Single Image MAD (no┬¡reference or S┬¡MAD)

2.5.1
White┬¡Box Attacks
White┬¡box attacks are generally gradient┬¡based [45]. The key idea behind gradient┬¡based
attacks is to increase the loss on a particular input image by taking into account the gra┬¡
dient of the model. The fast gradient sign method (FGSM) [46] was one of the earliest
works in this category. Here, the gradient is computed with respect to a particular input
image. A perturbation is then added by multiplying a small constant ¤Á with the sign of the
gradient so as to move in the direction of the gradient that increases the loss instead of
minimizing it. This way, the addition of the perturbation causes the model to misclassify
the input.
Several enhancements have been done on top of FGSM resulting in a number of separate
attacks. Since FGSM uses only a single gradient update to craft perturbations sometimes
it is not enough for a successful attack. The basic iterative method [47] (BIM) solves this
by applying FGSM iteratively over a specified number of iterations so as to achieve good
attack success through several gradient updates. In this attack, the resulting perturbation
is also clipped to ensure the value of the perturbation falls below a certain threshold. Simi┬¡
larly, the projected gradient descent [48] (PGD) attack aims to improve BIM by introducing

perturbation of the original input which still causes a misclassification.

2.5.2
Black┬¡Box Attacks
Black┬¡box attacks can be broadly classified into query┬¡based and transfer based attacks
[51].
In query┬¡based attacks, the model is invoked and based on the response, a decision is
made as to how perturbations should be created. In transfer┬¡based attacks a surrogate
model is created to resemble as closely as possible the target model. Attacks are made
on this surrogate model with the hope that they will transfer to the target model.
Within the realm of query┬¡based attacks there exist two subcategories namely, score┬¡
based attacks and decision┬¡based attacks [52]. In score┬¡based attacks, the output of the
model needs to be a score vector (typically probabilities or pre┬¡softmax logits). Decision┬¡
based attacks are the most restricted as even a score vector is not required. Here, only
the hard labels from the model are required.
The zeroth┬¡order optimization (ZOO) [53] was one of the first score based attacks where
a derivative┬¡free optimization method is used to compute the gradient. Since then, there
have been several other attacks in this category such as TREMBA [54] and Square Attack
(SA) [55].
In terms of decision┬¡based attacks the boundary attack [56] was one of the first [52]. The
attack starts from a point that is already adversarial and moves along the decision bound┬¡
ary of the model such that the perturbation is minimized and the adversarial classification
is maintained. The perturbations are chosen by using rejection sampling to sample from
a given proposal distribution. In this subcategory also, there have been many advances
with attacks such as the HopSkipJumpAttack (HSJA) [57], qFool [58] and Surrogate Free
Attack (SurFree) [59] improving upon the basic boundary attack.

There exist many techniques on modifying input data.

goal is to smoothen the loss function so as to increase generalisability of the model to
unknown datasets and by extension to adversarial attacks.
2.6.2
Reactive Defence Approaches
The last defence category of employing detectors does not suffer from the drawback of
adversarial training. There exist some notable techniques in this subcategory.
AdvFAS [72] is a face anti┬¡spoofing framework that combines a detector coupled with a
corrector. The detector is responsible for detecting a spoofing attack. However, generally
detectors trained on spoofing attacks generalize poorly to adversarial samples. There┬¡
fore, a corrector is used to correct the errors incurred by the detector. Instead of treating
the output of the detector as the final decision, a combined coupled score known as the
expected score is used instead. This score takes into account the results of both the
detector and corrector. In other words, if the detector misclassifies an adversarial image
as non┬¡adversarial the corrector is responsible for recognizing that and adjust the score
accordingly.
The detector is a binary classifier based on convolution neural networks (CNN). The puri┬¡
fier on the other hand uses a feature recovery loss to retrieve the features of the perturbed
image and restore it.

AdaFace [28] like MagFace emphasizes the need to adjust the margin based on the quality

different importance is attached depending on the difficulty of the sample. If the quality
of the images is high then hard samples are emphasized during training. Conversely
if quality is low then very hard samples (unrecognizable images) are ignored so as not
to fit too much on that particular sample. In contrast to MagFace, by emphasizing hard
samples in this way more discriminative features are learned.

3.4.1
HSJA
HSJA begins with a sample that is already misclassified. This is done by either providing
a sample that is already in the target class (for untargetted attack) or with a sample that
is blended with noise (for targetted attack). For PAD we use the former approach while
for MAD the latter approach. Then it estimates the decision boundary of the model us┬¡
ing binary search. Following this, The sample is perturbed to the point at the boundary.
An estimation of the direction of the gradient is made and an iterative procedure begins
where the image is continuously perturbed such that it progresses geometrically bounc┬¡
ing between the edges of the boundary and the region where the sample is misclassified.
Eventually the sample converges to a point that is at a minimum distance to the original
unperturbed input sample while still at the boundary so that the perturbed sample is mis┬¡

3.4.3
Square Attack
Square Attack (SA) is simpler in the sense that it does not attempt to make gradient
estimations. Instead it uses random search to optimize the objective function. However,
it does require access to probability output and is therefore less restrictive than the two
previously mentioned attacks. Random search samples a random update at each iteration
and adds it to the image sample. If the objective function is improved, the update is
kept otherwise discarded. SA modifies random search by incorporating special sampling
distributions depending on if the attack is l2 or lÔê×based. A l2 based attack minimizes the
overall distance of a perturbed sample from the original one. A lÔê×based attack minimizes
the absolute maximum distance between a perturbed sample and original sample. The
updated samples are such that the pixels modified are chosen contiguously in the shape
of squares. A theoretical analysis is provided to motivate the choice of squares as the
shape in the paper.
3.4.4
SimBA
SimBA attack is the final score based attack implemented. This is yet again a very simple
attack that samples a random direction in each iteration and perturbs the input in this
direction for a fixed given perturbation size. The output probabilities are used to either
accept or reject the perturbation. If the update results in an increase in the probability
that the new sample belongs to the target class then it the update is kept, otherwise it is
rejected.

Methodology
This chapter aims to describe the steps undertaken to answer the research questions.
The sections are ordered in the sequence the various implementation steps were carried

The steps are outlined here for clarity:
ÔÇó Data Preprocessing: All data went through a general set of preprocessing steps.
Following this, the data corresponding to each model went through a set of model┬¡

ÔÇó Impersonation Detector Model Training: We trained the two PAD models and

It must be noted that an attack can either be l2 or lÔê×bounded (strictly speaking also l0
and l1 but that is beyond scope). The former is where the constraint on the perturbation is
represented as the euclidian distance between the original sample and perturbed sample.
The goal is then to minimize this distance while maintaining model misclassification. The
latter is where the constraint is represented as the maximum absolute difference between
vectors of the original and perturbed samples.
In this thesis the focus was not on a particular norm┬¡bounded attack constraint. It was
to assess overall attack impact. Therefore, default parameters were used during attack
generation some of which were l2 bounded and some lÔê×bounded.
In general regarding the attack algorithms, default parameters were used. In cases of
subpar attack performance it was attempted to adjust some parameters. The two PAD
models shared a set of parameters whilst the two MAD models shared a (different) set
of parameters. The reason for this is that the attacks generally fared better for the MAD
models so it was attempted to adjust the parameters for PAD.
The most notable difference was in the HSJA and SignOpt attacks. Both of these attacks
provided the option to specify a sample that is already adversarial which the algorithm
would use a starting point to generate perturbations from. This is contrast to starting
from random noise which in some cases can be a more difficult starting position. It was
observed that for the PAD models, by providing the algorithms with a bona fide image
from the RM test dataset attack performance was drastically improved. We took care to
provide a sample from the test set to still keep the attack as realistic as possible since an
attacker would most likely not have access to the training dataset. For the MAD models,
the default setting was used where no initial starting sample was provided.
After adversarial sample generation, an inverse normalisation was applied to the tensors
to ease visualization. For more details see appendix A.2
Following attack generation the performance was evaluated. Procedure for calculating
the D┬¡EER, IAPAR and and T┬¡EER scores is given below in section 4.5.
4.5
General Evaluation Procedure
In this section we describe how the metrics described in chapter 3 were implemented.
We name it a general procedure since the procedure was similar for both baseline per┬¡
formance evaluation and for evaluating the impact of the generated attacks. The main
difference being, in terms of baseline the presentation/morphing image was used as the
ÔÇ£attackÔÇØ sample while in terms of attack evaluation the adversarial sample was used.
4.5.1
Computing IAPAR
To compute the IAPAR we first needed to set the decision threshold for each face recog┬¡
nition model. Pretrained weights were used for each model.
The decision thresholds were set based on the FMR. We used thresholds corresponding
to FMR=0.1% and FMR=0.01% calculated for each face recognition model. The operat┬¡
ing thresholds were computed using bona fide non┬¡mated comparison trials (considering
zero┬¡effort impostor attack) against the FRGC database. Originally, the RM database
was used but very low thresholds were obtained possibly due to low intra┬¡class variance
therefore the FRGC database was used instead.
To compute the IAPAR we followed the procedure: We generated pairs of mated attack
samples for each PAD and MAD model. For each attack, the attack sample was paired

with the corresponding identity from the bona fide dataset. In the case of PAD the dataset
was RM. In the case of MAD the dataset was FRGC. The feature embeddings of these
pairs was then computed using each face recognition model and the cosine similarity be┬¡
tween each pair was computed. If the similarity score was above the operating threshold
for the respective face recognition model, the impostor attempt was considered success┬¡
ful. In case of the morphing attacks the first contributing subject was used to generate the
mated pairs.
It must be noted here that the IAPAR was computed first for baseline where the respective
presentation or morphing attack sample was used as the ÔÇ£attackÔÇØ sample. After this,
the IAPAR was calculated for each adversarial attack where the ÔÇ£attackÔÇØ sample was the
adversarial sample. This was necessary to assess the impact of adversarial attacks on
the recognition performance of the face recognition systems.
4.5.2
Computing BPCER scores
In addition to reporting the D┬¡EER, we also reported the BPCER scores for different
thresholds. Specifically, we reported BPCER100, BPCER20 and BPCER10. These are
computed by setting the APCER threshold to 1%, 5% and 10% respectively. This was
done using the pyeer Python package. Score files were generated by running model
evaluation against bonafide and attack images. In case of baseline scores, the ÔÇ£attackÔÇØ
images were presentation attacks, while for adversarial attacks it was the perturbed attack
images generated. The bonafide score file was generated by filtering and keeping only
the score where the corresponding image label was ÔÇØbonafideÔÇØ (label: 1) while the attack
score file was generated by keeping the score only where the image label was ÔÇØattackÔÇØ
(label: 0).
4.5.3
Computing T┬¡EER scores
In order to do a joint evaluation of the two separate systems (face impersonation and face
recognition) we used the D┬¡EER in addition to IAPAR scores. However recent literature
introduced a single parameter┬¡free evaluation metric to evaluate performance of the joint
systems. This is called the tandem equal error rate (T┬¡EER) [87]. This presents us with a
single scalar value that gives the performance of spoofing/adversarial attacks taking into
account both a face impersonation detector system as well as a face recognition system.
It consisted of computing two score files one containing scores evaluated against the
PAD (or MAD) model and the other containing scores computed using a face recognition
model (MagFace, ArcFace and AdaFace in┬¡turn). Both score files contained the same
three types of comparisons with the exception that in the face recognition case, the face
recognition model was used to compute the actual scores.
These three types of comparisons were:
1. mated comparison between probe and reference images
2. non┬¡mated comparison between probe and reference images
3. mated comparison between each attack and its respective bonafide
The reader is referred to the paper [87] for additional information.

The two defence techniques used were defensive distillation [71] and adversarial training

been briefly mentioned in chapter 2.

For the PAD models we used defensive distillation while for the MAD models we used
adversarial training. We now give an overview of the two techniques.
4.6.1
Defensive Distillation
The procedure for defensive distillation was as follows:
1. Trained teacher model using OULU┬¡NPU train dataset. The pre┬¡softmax logits were
divided by a scalar T.
2. Retrieved softmax probability vectors by running the teacher model in evaluation
mode on the same dataset. The same scalar T was used.
3. Trained student model using previously obtained probability vectors as soft┬¡labels.
The pre┬¡softmax logits were divided by the same scalar T.
4. To evaluate the defended (student) model, T was set to 1.0.
For DeepPixBiS a temperature (T) of 5.0 was used while for LMFD a temperature of 100.0
was used. These values were determined through experimentation. The temperature
was set to 5.0 and then gradually increased until an acceptable level of performance was
reached. The performance was evaluated using D┬¡EER scores (3).
4.6.2
Adversarial Training
For adversarial training we used the PGD┬¡AT method which is described to be the state┬¡
of┬¡the art in [99]. The procedure was as follows:
1. Generated perturbed samples using the PGD attack (ART) using original (unde┬¡
fended) base model.
2. Trained new model using a combination of generated adversarial samples and orig┬¡
inal training data (FERET). This was done by modifying the dataloader such that
every second morph image from the FaceFusion morphs should be PGD gener┬¡
ated. The training set consisted of 3525 image samples in total 529 of which were
FaceMorpher images. Therefore, the actual number of adversarial images used
during training was only 264.
3. The defended model was evaluated as usual.

We see peculiar behaviour for SA and HSJA amongst all four morphing datasets. We see
in fig. 5.8 the DET curves extend vertically upward. We take the SA plot in fig. 5.8a as an
example for this analysis. At a certain APCER percentage (71% in this case) the model
decision threshold is 1.0 (this is due to 71% of attack samples having value 1.0). Since
the threshold is already at a maximum level it does not change for lower APCER. As a
result, 71% of these image samples will never be classified as attacks as the threshold
cannot go higher. This has the consequence that the BPCER scores are 100.0 since no
bona fide image sample can be strictly greater than the threshold of 1.0. This analysis
extends to the other morphing cases as well where we see the curves extending vertically
upward.
The lack of a clear tradeoff between APCER and BPCER here means that enforcing
defence using solely a higher model threshold is not effective. In other words, for low
APCER thresholds (high security) the BPCER scores have a tendency to go to 100.0 for
SA and HSJA attacks. From a defence perspective this warrants the training of more
robust classifiers.

6
Defence Analysis
For defending against the attacks, we opted for a proactive approach (described pre┬¡
viously in section 2.6.1): training more robust models. The two most popular ways to
achieve this is through adversarial training and defensive distillation. Defensive distilla┬¡
tion was chosen as the primary defence mechanism as it offers advantages compared to
adversarial training. Firstly, it does not require access to perturbed inputs. So in theory it
should generalise well to all types of attacks and is pragmatic in implementation.
In adversarial training the model is trained on adversarial samples generated using a
single attack type. The primary disadvantage of adversarial training is therefore that it
is unrealistic to train on all known attack types [100]. Therefore generalisability suffers
and an attack the model has not been trained against might be highly effective against
that model. Furthermore, adversarial training is known to reduce baseline performance
(termed baseline performance and robustness trade┬¡off [99]).
The defence protocols and metrics used have been described previously and can be
reviewed in section 4.7 and section 3.5 respectively.
Since SimBA was the weakest attack as seen in the previous section chapter 5 and in the
interest of time defence analysis was omitted for this attack.
6.1
Impact of Defensive Distillation on PAD Models
We first analyze results for PAD. Two protocols were used (see section 4.7). In the first, U
ÔåÆD, the perturbed images generated against the baseline undefended model are used.
Whereas in the second D ÔåÆD, the perturbed images have been generated against the
defended model.
For PAD we used defensive distillation. We used a temperature T value of 5.0 in case
of DeepPixBiS. While for LMFD a temperature of 100.0 was used. These values were
chosen via empirical means through experimentation.
In table 6.1 below we see the results of defensive distillation. For the U ÔåÆD protocol
we see a reduction in D┬¡EER scores for all attacks except SA. Whereas for the second
protocol D ÔåÆD we see a reduction in D┬¡EER scores of all attacks. The reduction is
greater for the second protocol. This is to be expected since with a smoother loss function
it becomes harder to find adversarial samples. What is interesting to note here is that,
baseline performance is maintained for the defended model.
In table 6.2 below we see the results for the LMFD model. In this case, we see a reduction
in D┬¡EER scores of all attacks for both protocols. From a defence perspective this means
that the detection capabilities of the model has improved and it is able to better classify
adversarial inputs as attacks. The reduction is greatest in the second protocol as was
noted above. Again as before we see that baseline performance is largely maintained.
As noted earlier, a reactive defensive approach can be used against SA so the lack of
reduced D┬¡EER against SA for the defended model is dismissed. The main finding is
then that using defensive distillation we are able to defend the PAD models against the two
most effective black┬¡box attacks: HSJA and SignOpt. All this, without losing performance
against the baseline.

Discussion on Defence
Figure 7.1: From [101]: ÔÇ£... the defended model is very smooth in neighborhoods of
training points: i.e., gradients of the model outputs with respect to its inputs are zero and
the adversary does not know in which direction to look for adversarial examples. However,
the adversary can use the substitute modelÔÇÖs gradients to find adversarial examples that
transfer back to the defended model. Note that this effect would be exacerbated by models
with more than one dimension.ÔÇØ
This chapter focusses solely on discussion on defence results seen in the previous chap┬¡
ter.
Both defensive distillation and adversarial training are a form of gradient masking [102]
[103].
Gradient masking techniques result in models that are very smooth in specific directions
of training points making it difficult for attacks to find gradients which makes it harder to
craft adversarial examples.
However, based on [102] [103] models trained with these techniques should still be vul┬¡
nerable to attacks crafted using undefended models. This is due to high transferability
of adversarial samples. Samples generated against one model are highly likely to cause
misclassification when evaluated against a different model [101].
From the paper [102]: ÔÇ£We show a more general flaw in the category of gradient masking.
Even if the defender attempts to prevent attacks by not publishing the directions in which
the model is sensitive, these directions can be discovered by other means, in which case
the same attack can still succeed. We show that the black┬¡box attack based on transfer
from a substitute model overcomes gradient masking defenses.ÔÇØ.
The idea here is that the substitute model would have gradients and that the adversarial
samples generated against that, would transfer to the defended model. This can be seen
in fig. 7.1. In our case we do not use a substitute model but the adversarial samples are
generated against the undefended model itself. In any case, the transferability property
still holds.
The following excerpt from the article [103] asserts this as well: ÔÇ£If we transfer adversarial
examples from one model to a second model that was trained with either adversarial
training or defensive distillation, the attack often succeeds, even when a direct attack
on the second model would fail. This suggests that both training techniques do more to
Defending Face Impersonation Detectors against Adversarial Attacks
59
==PAGE:68==
flatten out the model and remove the gradient than to make sure it classifies more points
correctly.ÔÇØ.
In the context of this thesis, this implies three things:
1. High attack performance for U ÔåÆD protocol: D┬¡EER scores in the U ÔåÆD protocol
should be roughly the same as for the U ÔåÆU protocol for all attack types and all
models. In other words scores should be maintained.
2. Low attack performance for D ÔåÆD protocol for the HSJA and SignOpt attacks:
These two attacks make use of local gradient information. HSJA makes an estimate
of the direction of the gradient, while SignOpt an estimate of the sign. So crafting
adversarial examples should be more difficult against defended models. Therefore,
D┬¡EER scores in the D ÔåÆD protocol should be relatively low (compared to U ÔåÆU)
for these attacks.
3. High attack performance for D ÔåÆD protocol for the SA attack: Since SA makes no
use of local gradient information the gradient masking should be ineffective for this
attack. Therefore, we should see relatively high D┬¡EER scores.
We now analyze each of these implications in turn.
7.1
High attack performance for U ÔåÆD protocol
The results show that both forms of defensive training lead to an overall increase in robust┬¡
ness (lower D┬¡EER scores). There are only a few exceptions. For DeepPixBiS, MixNet
and OrthoMAD we see exceptions in SA, SignOpt and HSJA attacks respectively, where
high D┬¡EER scores are maintained. For LMFD there are no exceptions.
It is uncertain why high D┬¡EER scores are not maintained for all attack types. But it
seems that through the training process the decision surface and boundary is not only
smoothened but also changed in ways that make most existing adversarial attacks be┬¡
come correctly classified. It is also uncertain why the attack types in the exceptions men┬¡
tioned previously differ across the models. The three attacks: SA, SignOpt and HSJA are
exceptions for three separate models. Since the training procedure is invariable, these
differences must be attributed to differences in model architecture.
7.2
Low attack performance for D ÔåÆD protocol for the HSJA
and SignOpt attacks
We see indeed that relatively low D┬¡EER scores are obtained for the HSJA and SignOpt
attacks for both PAD models.
However, with respect to MAD results we see a lot of variation. For MixNet we see across
all four morphing types that the HSJA attack is defended against while SignOpt is not. For
OrthoMAD, SignOpt is defended against (for all four morphing types) while HSJA is only
defended against in two morphing types. Essentially we see roughly opposing trends.
Go into the why here?
7.3
High attack performance for D ÔåÆD protocol for the SA
attack
We see that high attack performance is not maintained and the SA attacks are defended
against (low D┬¡EER scores are obtained) across all models. The large size of the pertur┬¡
bations could be a reason for this. Especially since the D┬¡EER is abnormally high for the

undefended model, meaning making a reduction in D┬¡EER is more probable regardless
of which defensive mechanism is used. Furthermore, from the analysis in section 7.1,
we saw SA had only high D┬¡EER in one exceptional case for the U ÔåÆD protocol. Given
this knowledge and the fact that D ÔåÆD protocol is more challenging it is perhaps not
surprising to see low attack performance.

quite well. According to both implications 1 (7.1) and 2 (7.2), we should have expected

(higher D┬¡EER). However, the results in both cases show an increase in model robustness
and lower D┬¡EER scores. This is more profound for the PAD models while for MAD there
are some exceptions especially in 7.2.

Conclusion
In this chapter we sum up the main conclusions drawn from the previous chapters on
vulnerability analysis and defence evaluation. Finally, we answer the proposed research
questions.
For the PAD models we discovered that the LMFD model is most vulnerable to adversarial
attacks. Across both PAD models, MagFace was seen to be the most robust face recog┬¡
nition model followed by ArcFace. Regarding T┬¡EER scores the same trends in attack
performance were seen with the same conclusions drawn regarding categorisation of at┬¡
tacks based on attack performance. However, it was seen that the difference in scores
across different face recognition models was insignificant, making it difficult to argue over
the choice of face recognition system. Consequently, based solely on T┬¡EER results Mag┬¡
Face was seen to be the most robust model but the second most robust model could not
be concluded. We also saw that the T┬¡EER method was unable to produce a single con┬¡
current value for SA undermining the use of this evaluation metric for abnormally strong
attacks. Therefore, we conclude that the combination of D┬¡EER and IAPAR scores served
as a better evaluation metric. SA was seen to be the most impactful attack but this was
undermined by the high norm scores achieved by this attack.
For the MAD models we saw generally very high BPCER scores with some reaching
100.0, motivating the need to develop robust classifiers through proactive defence strate┬¡
gies. It was interesting to note for MixNet the HSJA attack achieving the lowest norm
scores despite resulting in the second highest D┬¡EER. This testified to the high impact of
this particular attack. We discovered the FaceFusion dataset to be most vulnerable to ad┬¡
versarial attacks. Also, MagFace was seen to be the most robust face recognition model
followed by ArcFace. Finally, the T┬¡EER evaluation metric failed to produce single con┬¡
current values for the most impactful attacks. For OrthoMAD, a value was not computed
for even baseline. These leads us to conclude that not only is T┬¡EER an inadequate met┬¡
ric in cases of abnormally impactful attacks but also in cases of models that have subpar
baseline classification performance (for OrthoMAD seen by the relatively high baseline
D┬¡EER (compare table 5.8 to table 5.7)).

Research Question 4: Can proactive defence mechanisms help protect face imperson┬¡
ation detectors against adversarial attacks?
Defensive distillation proved to be very successful for both PAD models. For the MAD
models, it did not and we had to opt for adversarial training.
First we summarize defensive distillation results:
For DeepPixBiS we saw that with the exception of SA on the U ÔåÆD protocol, defensive
distillation led to a decrease in D┬¡EER for all other attacks. Also, the baseline detection
performance was maintained.
For LMFD we saw that for all attacks, defensive distillation led to a decrease in D┬¡EER.
Furthermore, in contrast to DeepPixBiS, it was seen that HSJA (across both protocols)
led to a D┬¡EER even lower than the U ÔåÆU baseline. Baseline detection performance was
seen to be slightly worse (13.13 vs. 12.61) but still low enough to consider the defence
successful.
Adversarial training proved to also be a good defence strategy with the only drawback
being a reduction in baseline detection performance.
For MixNet, we saw a reduction in D┬¡EER across both protocols for all attacks except
SignOpt. Compared to defensive distillation, baseline detection performance proved to
be significantly worse post adversarial training.
For OrthoMAD, we saw again a reduction in D┬¡EER across both protocols for all attacks
with exceptions on HSJA (for the FaceMorpher and OpenCV morph types). In this case,
we again see a worsening of baseline detection performance post adversarial training.
We conclude that black┬¡box adversarial attacks can effectively impact face impersonation
detection performance. However, they can also be effectively defended against using
proactive defence strategies. In the context of this thesis, defensive distillation for PAD
models proved to be the most successful defence mechanism leading to both lower D┬¡
EER scores while maintaining baseline detection performance.

8.1
Future Work
8.1.1
Implementing Black┬¡box Transfer Based Attack
As mentioned in section 2.5.2 black┬¡box adversarial attacks can be classified into two
types: query┬¡based and transfer based. In this thesis, the focus was on query┬¡based
attacks. A natural next step would be to implement a transfer based attack and see if the
current defended models are able to defend against it.
Transfer based attacks are expected to be hard to defend against (based on [104] both
defensive distillation and adversarial training can be bypassed by transfer based attacks).
This is because since the surrogate model is chosen by the attacker, full knowledge of the
model is available paving the way to attack the surrogate using white┬¡box attacks. In addi┬¡
tion, white┬¡box attacks are more impactful than black┬¡box attacks [105]. Even though the
attacks would have been generated using a white┬¡box attack on a surrogate model which
could be different in architecture from the target model, the attacks are still expected to
succeed. This is due to adversarial example transferability, i.e. ÔÇ£the property that adver┬¡
sarial examples crafted to be misclassified by a model are likely to be misclassified by a
different modelÔÇØ [101]. The surrogate model used in this case could be a simple ResNet
based classifier.
8.1.2
Attack Performance Assessment Based on Query Efficiency
In this thesis, we looked at D┬¡EER scores and norm scores to assess attack performance.
However, most query┬¡based adversarial attacks are assessed on query efficiency. This is
because the faster an attack is able to craft an adversarial sample the greater the threat.
Also in real┬¡world scenarios where models are available through application programming
interfaces (APIs) an abnormally large number of API requests from a single source could
be suspicious. Especially in case of API throttling where the number of requests allowed
per user is limited.
As future work, it would therefore be interesting to also assess attack performance based
on query efficiency.
8.1.3
Implementing Reactive Defence Strategies
In this thesis, we implemented only proactive defence strategies. It would be interesting
to implement reactive approaches and see how well they fare in comparison.
